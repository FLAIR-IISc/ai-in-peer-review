Summary and relation to prior work
The paper introduces signal aggregate constraints (SACs) for blind source separation (BSS), instantiated in an additive factorial HMM (AFHMM) for energy disaggregation (non-intrusive load monitoring). The key idea is to encode domain knowledge about the total energy per source over a time horizon (e.g., daily kWh per appliance) via penalties that encourage each chain’s aggregate output to match a specified value µi0. The authors formulate MAP inference with these penalties and show how to transform the resulting objective into a convex quadratic program (CQP) by introducing transition variables Hit to linearize bilinear Markov terms. They also show the equivalence to the posterior regularization framework (Ganchev et al., JMLR 2010). Empirically, they evaluate on synthetic data and on the UK HES dataset (ultra-low-frequency 2–10 min sampling), reporting improvements over a plain AFHMM and over AFAMAP (Kolter & Jaakkola, AISTATS 2012), including ~45% NDE reduction on mains data in some settings. The approach is conceptually related to generalized expectation (Mann & McCallum), measurement-based learning (Liang et al.), and prior attempts to constrain AFHMMs (AFAMAP’s “one chain changes at a time,” duration models such as HSMMs), which the authors cite.

Strengths
- Conceptually simple and useful constraint class: Aggregate budgets are intuitive, often available (e.g., national appliance statistics), and directly address identifiability in BSS where interpretability of sources matters.
- Sound optimization formulation: Introducing edge variables Hit converts bilinear transition terms into linear ones; with Gaussian emissions the quadratic terms are concave in the relaxed variables. Maximizing a concave quadratic over a simplex polytope is a convex problem, enabling efficient global optimization with off-the-shelf solvers.
- Clear link to posterior regularization: The squared-aggregate penalties are shown to be equivalent to projecting the model posterior into a constrained family, providing a principled lens on the regularization.
- Empirical relevance: Results on low-frequency HES data (a challenging regime) show consistent gains over AFHMM and AFAMAP on both NDE and SAE, and the mains experiment reflects realistic deployment conditions.
- Practicality: The method is easy to combine with existing AFHMM pipelines and could generalize to other BSS domains.

Weaknesses and detailed comments (Quality, Clarity, Originality, Significance)
Quality
- Dependence on µi0: The approach assumes reasonably accurate per-source aggregate targets. While the toy study perturbs µ0 and shows graceful degradation, robustness on real data is not analyzed. Using “national total consumption” (NTC) as a proxy may be biased at the household level; negative transfer is possible if a house deviates from the national profile.
- Hyperparameters: λi are fixed to 1 throughout; there is no sensitivity analysis or cross-validation. This is important since λi controls the trade-off between fidelity to Y and the aggregates.
- Relaxation and decoding: The relaxed solution yields fractional Sit; the paper does not describe how these are used in practice (e.g., taking x̂it = µi^T Sit vs. projecting to discrete states). For applications where discrete state sequences matter (e.g., event-level interpretations), a rounding/projection step and its impact should be discussed.
- Baselines: The paper cites HSMM/semi-Markov approaches and other constraints but does not include a duration-model baseline, which is often competitive in NILM at low frequency. Including at least one explicit-duration model would strengthen claims.
- Scalability: The method is solved via CVX; claims of “similar” runtime to AFHMM/AFAMAP are not quantified and CVX’s scalability for long sequences and many chains is limited. Complexity and memory footprints as a function of T and total states should be reported, ideally with wall-clock times and problem sizes.

Clarity
- Generally clear, but some notational density could be reduced. It would help to explicitly state why the pre-Hit objective is non-convex (bilinear terms S_{t-1}⊙S_t) and why adding Hit restores concavity, and to clarify that maximizing a concave quadratic over linear constraints is a convex optimization problem. A brief statement on how outputs are computed from relaxed variables would improve reproducibility.
- The datasets, training protocol, and parameter estimation (e.g., σt) are described, but more detail on the learned K, µi, and transition matrices per appliance would aid replication.

Originality
- The specific SAC formulation and its integration into AFHMM via a convex relaxation are a modest extension of known ideas: posterior regularization with aggregate functionals, and linearization of pairwise terms via edge variables are standard. Nonetheless, applying these to AFHMM for NILM with a careful convex program is a useful contribution. The time-window generalization is natural but not evaluated.

Significance
- For NILM and similar BSS tasks where aggregate budgets exist, the approach is practical and likely to be adopted. However, the broader methodological novelty for the NeurIPS audience is limited; the main advance is an instantiation and careful engineering of known concepts. Stronger empirical breadth (more houses/datasets, more baselines, ablations) would raise the impact.

Suggestions for improvement
- Robustness: Evaluate performance as a function of µ0 error on real data by perturbing NTC (e.g., ±10–50%) and reporting NDE/SAE. Consider interval constraints or uncertainty-aware priors on µ0 (e.g., quadratic penalties with learned λi or hierarchical priors).
- Hyperparameter selection: Tune λi via cross-validation or optimize the dual variables in the posterior regularization framework. Report sensitivity curves.
- Decoding: Describe how fractional solutions are used. If a projection to discrete states is used (e.g., Viterbi on a surrogate HMM or randomized rounding), report its effect.
- Baselines: Include at least one explicit-duration HSMM baseline and, if possible, recent NILM methods beyond AFAMAP (e.g., sparse coding/NMF-based approaches) on HES.
- Windowed SAC: Evaluate the time-window SACs hinted in Section 4.2 (e.g., day/night) to demonstrate additional gains and address temporal usage patterns.
- Scalability: Provide absolute runtimes, memory usage, and scaling plots vs. T and number of chains/states. Consider a customized solver exploiting the problem structure rather than general-purpose CVX.

Pros
- Simple, principled constraint that addresses identifiability
- Convex relaxation with global optimum in the relaxed space
- Consistent gains on a realistic low-frequency dataset
- Clear connection to posterior regularization

Cons
- Relies on accurate aggregate targets; robustness insufficiently analyzed
- No tuning or sensitivity study for λi
- Missing key baselines (duration models), limited evaluation breadth
- Limited discussion of rounding and scalability

Recommendation
Weak reject. The idea is practical and results are promising for NILM, but the methodological novelty is limited and the empirical/analysis depth (robustness to µ0, λ selection, stronger baselines, scalability) needs strengthening. With the suggested additions, this could become a solid contribution.
