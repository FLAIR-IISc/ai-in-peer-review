This paper addresses the problem of identifiability in blind source separation (BSS), particularly within the context of energy disaggregation. The authors propose incorporating "Signal Aggregate Constraints" (SACs) into a standard Additive Factorial Hidden Markov Model (AFHMM). The core idea is to regularize the solution by constraining the total sum of each inferred source signal to be close to a known prior value (e.g., the average daily consumption of an appliance). As this constraint is non-Markovian and complicates inference, the authors formulate MAP inference as a penalized optimization problem. To make this tractable, they relax the integer state variables to be continuous within [0, 1], resulting in a convex quadratic program (CQP) that can be solved efficiently. The method is evaluated on a toy dataset and a real-world energy disaggregation task, where it is shown to outperform a baseline AFHMM and the AFAMAP algorithm, a recent state-of-the-art method.

**Strengths:**
1.  The paper addresses a well-known and critical challenge in BSS: the unidentifiability of separated sources. The application to energy disaggregation provides a clear and practical motivation.
2.  The proposed SAC is an intuitive and sensible form of prior knowledge. In many domains, aggregate statistics of the sources are indeed more readily available or stable than instantaneous signal characteristics.
3.  The formulation of the relaxed inference problem as a convex quadratic program is a methodologically sound approach to finding an approximate solution to an otherwise intractable integer programming problem.
4.  The empirical results are strong, showing significant error reduction over both a standard baseline (AFHMM) and a more advanced competitor (AFAMAP) on a challenging real-world dataset.

**Weaknesses:**
The paper presents an interesting idea but falls short in terms of methodological completeness and experimental rigor, which raises concerns about the soundness and generalizability of the conclusions.

1.  **Methodological Ambiguity:** The central technical contribution is the CQP formulation for the relaxed problem. However, the output of this CQP for the state indicator variables `Sitk` will be fractional values in the interval [0, 1], whereas the underlying HMM requires discrete states (i.e., {0, 1}). The paper completely omits any discussion of how a valid, discrete state sequence is recovered from the CQP's continuous solution. Is a rounding scheme used? Are the values interpreted as marginal probabilities for a subsequent inference step? This is a critical detail for both understanding and reproducing the work, and its absence constitutes a significant methodological gap. Without this information, the presented method is incomplete.

2.  **Lack of Rigorous Hyperparameter Evaluation:** The performance of the proposed AFHMM+SAC model depends on the penalty parameters `λi`. The authors state that they "simply set λi = 1 for all the experiments." This is not a rigorous approach. A proper evaluation would require tuning these hyperparameters, for instance via cross-validation on a held-out set, and reporting on the model's sensitivity to their values. Without such an analysis, it is difficult to assess whether the reported gains are robust or an artifact of a fortunate, un-tuned parameter choice that happens to work well for this specific dataset. It also raises questions about the fairness of the comparison, as it is unclear if the baselines were afforded a similar level of tuning.

3.  **Limited Scope of Baselines:** The paper compares against AFHMM and AFAMAP. While AFAMAP is a relevant baseline, the related work section itself mentions other established techniques for constraining HMMs, such as semi-Markov models [10, 11] which model state durations. These methods also serve to address the unidentifiability problem by encoding prior knowledge about appliance behavior. A more convincing demonstration of state-of-the-art performance would require comparison against a broader set of strong, alternative approaches.

4.  **Novelty:** The contribution, while useful, is arguably incremental. The core idea is the application of a quadratic penalty term to enforce a global constraint on an existing probabilistic model. This is a standard regularization technique. While its application to AFHMMs for this task may be new, the paper does not introduce a fundamentally new modeling or inference paradigm. The connection to posterior regularization in Section 6 further situates the work within an existing framework, highlighting it as a specific instance of a general principle rather than a new principle itself.

**Summary of arguments for and against acceptance:**

*   **For acceptance:** The paper tackles an important problem with a simple, intuitive idea. The reported empirical improvements over a state-of-the-art method are substantial.
*   **Against acceptance:** The paper contains a critical methodological omission regarding the handling of the CQP's fractional output, rendering the method irreproducible. The experimental validation is weakened by a complete lack of hyperparameter tuning and a limited selection of baseline models. The novelty is more evolutionary than revolutionary.

**Recommendation:**
While the idea of using signal aggregate constraints is promising, the methodological gaps and lack of experimental rigor are too significant for a paper at this venue. The ambiguity surrounding the conversion of the relaxed CQP solution back to a discrete state sequence is a major flaw. Furthermore, claims of superior performance cannot be fully trusted without a proper hyperparameter sensitivity analysis and tuning procedure. I recommend rejection, but I would encourage the authors to address these critical points, as the underlying concept has merit and could form the basis of a strong future submission.
