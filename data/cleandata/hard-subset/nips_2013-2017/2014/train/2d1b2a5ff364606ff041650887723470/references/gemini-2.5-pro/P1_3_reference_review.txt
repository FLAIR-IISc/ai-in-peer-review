This paper addresses the problem of identifiability in blind source separation (BSS), particularly within the framework of Additive Factorial Hidden Markov Models (AFHMMs). The core contribution is the introduction of Signal Aggregate Constraints (SACs), a novel form of prior knowledge that encourages the total sum of each separated source signal over a time period to be close to a known value. This is motivated by applications like energy disaggregation, where the total daily consumption of an appliance (e.g., a toaster) is often known or can be well-estimated. Because these constraints are non-Markovian, they make standard inference intractable. The authors overcome this by relaxing the MAP inference problem and formulating it as a convex quadratic program (CQP), which can be solved efficiently. The paper also provides a theoretical link between their penalized objective function and the posterior regularization framework. The method is evaluated on a challenging, real-world energy disaggregation dataset, where it demonstrates a significant performance improvement over a standard AFHMM and a state-of-the-art competitor, AFAMAP.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Novelty and Significance of the Core Idea:** The central idea of using signal aggregate constraints is simple, intuitive, and highly effective for the problem it aims to solve. The identifiability issue ("franken-appliances") is a well-known and critical barrier to the practical application of BSS models like AFHMMs. SACs provide a practical and powerful way to inject domain knowledge to resolve these ambiguities. This idea is general and could likely be applied to other BSS domains beyond energy disaggregation (e.g., audio, biology) where aggregate properties of the sources are known.

2.  **Technical Soundness:** The paper's technical execution is solid. The authors correctly identify that the SACs break the Markov property, rendering standard algorithms like Viterbi inapplicable. Their solution—relaxing the discrete state variables and formulating the problem as a Convex Quadratic Program—is a principled and well-established approach for handling such intractable inference problems. The connection drawn to the posterior regularization framework in Section 6 further strengthens the theoretical underpinnings of the proposed method, showing it is not just an ad-hoc penalty term but can be derived from a more general framework for incorporating constraints into posteriors.

3.  **Thorough and Convincing Experimental Evaluation:** The evaluation is a major strength of this work.
    *   **Dataset:** The use of the low-frequency HES dataset is commendable, as it represents a realistic and challenging scenario for energy disaggregation.
    *   **Baselines:** The paper compares against appropriate baselines: the basic AFHMM and the strong, closely related AFAMAP model.
    *   **Metrics:** The use of both Normalized Disaggregation Error (NDE) and Signal Aggregate Error (SAE) is well-justified. SAE is particularly relevant as it directly measures the model's ability to satisfy the very type of constraint being introduced.
    *   **Oracle Experiment:** The inclusion of the AFHMM+SAC (TTC) experiment is excellent scientific practice. It provides an "oracle" result that demonstrates the maximum potential benefit of the SACs, effectively isolating the performance gain from the quality of the prior.
    *   **Results:** The results are strong and statistically significant, showing a 45% decrease in NDE over the baseline AFHMM and a 29% improvement over AFAMAP on real mains data. This represents a substantial advance in the state of the art for this problem.

4.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is very well-written, clearly motivated, and easy to follow. The problem is introduced effectively, the proposed model is built logically upon the AFHMM, and the experiments are described in sufficient detail for the reader to understand the setup and conclusions.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Sensitivity to Hyperparameters:** The paper simplifies the experimental setup by setting the regularization parameter `λi = 1` for all experiments. While this demonstrates the method works "out of the box," it leaves open the question of sensitivity. A brief analysis or discussion of how performance varies with different choices of `λ` would strengthen the paper. For instance, what happens when the prior is trusted too much (large `λ`) or too little? How would one set this parameter in practice (e.g., via cross-validation)?

2.  **Robustness to Prior Quality:** The toy data experiment shows that performance degrades as the provided aggregate value (`µi0`) moves away from the truth, which is expected. However, a more in-depth discussion of this in the context of the real-world problem would be valuable. The NTC prior is derived from the same dataset (albeit from the training split). How would the model perform if the prior came from a less reliable source, such as a national survey with a different demographic profile? A more explicit analysis of the model's robustness to noisy or biased priors would be beneficial.

3.  **Scalability Discussion:** The paper states that the CQP has polynomial time complexity and that the runtime is similar to AFAMAP in practice. This is a good point, but a more detailed discussion of scalability could be included. CQP solvers can become slow for problems with very large numbers of variables, which would occur with very long time series (large `T`) or many appliances with many states. A brief mention of the practical limitations or how the runtime scales empirically with `T` and `I` would make the work more complete.

**Summary of Arguments:**

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Proposes a novel, intuitive, and highly effective type of constraint (SAC) that directly addresses the critical problem of identifiability in AFHMMs.
*   The technical approach (CQP relaxation) is sound, well-executed, and given strong theoretical justification through its link to posterior regularization.
*   The experimental results are comprehensive and demonstrate a significant, state-of-the-art performance improvement on a challenging real-world task.
*   The paper is exceptionally clear and well-written.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   The analysis of hyperparameter sensitivity (`λ`) is absent.
*   The robustness to errors or bias in the aggregate priors (`µi0`) could be explored more deeply.
*   The discussion on the practical scalability of the CQP approach is somewhat brief.

**Conclusion:**

This is a high-quality paper that presents a valuable contribution to the field of blind source separation and its applications. The proposed Signal Aggregate Constraints are a simple yet powerful idea, and the authors have developed a sound and effective inference method to incorporate them. The experimental results are impressive and convincingly demonstrate the utility of the approach. The weaknesses identified are relatively minor and relate to further analysis rather than fundamental flaws in the method or evaluation. This work is likely to be influential and built upon by others working on energy disaggregation and other BSS problems. I strongly recommend acceptance.
