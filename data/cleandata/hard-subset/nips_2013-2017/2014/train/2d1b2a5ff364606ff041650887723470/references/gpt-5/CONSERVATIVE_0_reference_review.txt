Summary
The paper tackles blind source separation in additive factorial HMMs (AFHMMs) by introducing signal aggregate constraints (SACs): priors on the total energy (or other aggregates) for each latent source over a time horizon. The authors cast MAP inference with SACs as a penalized objective equivalent to posterior regularization with quadratic penalties, and then derive a convex quadratic program (CQP) by relaxing the binary latent variables and introducing pairwise transition variables H. They solve the relaxed problem with CVX. Experiments on a synthetic toy dataset and on the UK HES energy dataset show improvements over AFHMM and AFAMAP (Kolter & Jaakkola, 2012), including a reported ~45% reduction in normalized disaggregation error in one setting. They also discuss an oracle setting where true aggregate totals are available and note further gains.

Relation to prior work
The work is positioned among methods that constrain factorial HMMs for NILM, including AFAMAP (one-chain-change constraint), non-homogeneous HMMs, semi-Markov variants, and k-segment constraints. The idea of incorporating prior knowledge via constraints/penalties is linked to posterior regularization (Ganchev et al.), generalized expectation (Mann & McCallum), and measurement-based learning (Liang et al.). The novelty here is the specific choice of global aggregate constraints, integrated into AFHMM inference via a quadratic relaxation.

Quality
- Modeling and optimization: The objective in Eq. (5) is well-motivated and its equivalence to posterior regularization is standard. The derivation of the quadratic form uses PSD matrices; because the paper maximizes and the quadratic terms have negative PSD curvature, this is a concave quadratic maximization over a convex polytope after relaxation. Calling it a “convex quadratic program” is acceptable if one clarifies it is maximization of a concave quadratic. However, two important issues need clarification:
  1) Rounding/feasibility: The method solves a relaxed problem with Sitk, hitjk in [0,1]. It is not stated how discrete, Markov-consistent sequences are recovered, if at all. Are fractional solutions used directly to compute x̂ (and thus NDE/SAE)? If so, comparisons to AFHMM/AFAMAP (which produce discrete sequences) may be biased; if rounding/projection is performed, the procedure and its effect on performance should be provided.
  2) Scalability: General-purpose CQP via CVX can be expensive (variables scale as O(T∑iKi + T∑iK^2_i)). The claim of “similar time” to AFHMM/AFAMAP needs more rigorous evidence: scaling plots vs T and number of appliances, solver settings, hardware, and whether specialized solvers were used. A polynomial-time statement is not informative without constants; practical complexity matters in NILM.
- Experimental design: The baselines are appropriate (AFHMM and AFAMAP), and using HES low-frequency data is commendable. However:
  - Hyperparameters: λi are fixed to 1 for all experiments, without tuning. Since λ controls the strength of SACs, sensitivity analyses and cross-validation (or at least a sweep) are needed to support claims of robustness and to rule out cherry-picked settings.
  - Aggregate priors: The “oracle” TTC constraint is useful as an upper bound, but practically unavailable. The NTC prior is computed from training data with per-appliance readings; the paper positions future work for unsupervised settings, but the current evaluations rely on supervised parameter estimation, which limits applicability. Please quantify performance degradation as µ0 moves away from the truth on real data (not only on the toy example).
  - Baseline fairness: Provide implementation details for AFAMAP (e.g., constraint enforcement, parameter estimation, and any global constraints), and ensure all methods use the same θ learned from the same data. Report state counts sensitivity (all appliances set to 3 states is simplistic).
  - Statistical reporting: Means and paired t-tests are mentioned, but detailed statistics (variance, per-house performance, effect sizes) and the exact number of test days are not clearly reported in the text provided.
- Theory: The connection to posterior regularization is correct; however, the paper would benefit from a short discussion of identifiability under SACs: to what extent do global aggregates reduce label-swapping and “Franken-appliance” issues, and under what conditions?

Clarity
The paper is generally understandable but notation is dense and occasionally inconsistent (e.g., switching between convex vs concave terminology; some typos). The derivation in Section 5 introduces many vectorizations at once; a brief outline explaining why the relaxation is concave and why CVX applies would improve readability. Missing procedural details include: how µi and transitions are estimated (beyond k-means for µ), how fractional solutions are used or rounded, and how λi are selected. Figures and tables are referenced but not visible here; ensure they include sufficient legends and ablations.

Originality
The high-level idea—imposing global expectation/aggregate constraints—is not new (posterior regularization, GE, measurement-based learning), and factorial HMM relaxations are well-studied. The contribution is an application-driven instantiation: SACs tailored to NILM and a quadratic relaxation for AFHMM with these constraints. I view the methodological novelty as incremental but reasonable.

Significance
Energy disaggregation is an active application area. If robust, SACs could be practically useful and may transfer to other BSS settings where aggregate priors are known. However, the current evidence is limited by the reliance on supervised training, lack of hyperparameter tuning analysis, unclear rounding, and uncertain scalability. Without addressing these, it is difficult to conclude a solid advance over established methods.

Pros
- Clear motivation for aggregate constraints in BSS/NILM.
- Coherent formulation via posterior regularization; concave quadratic relaxation is straightforward and implementable.
- Evaluations on real low-frequency HES data; comparisons to a strong baseline (AFAMAP); reported gains are promising.

Cons
- Unclear handling of relaxed (fractional) solutions and comparability to discrete baselines.
- No hyperparameter tuning or sensitivity to λ; limited analysis of robustness to misspecified µ0 on real data.
- Practicality/scalability of CVX-based inference not convincingly demonstrated.
- Modest novelty relative to existing constrained inference frameworks.

Questions for the authors
1) How do you obtain discrete sequences from the relaxed solution (if at all)? If you use fractional outputs directly, please justify fairness and report results after rounding/projection.
2) How sensitive are results to λi? Can you provide cross-validated results and a sensitivity plot?
3) How is θ (π, transitions) learned in practice? From per-appliance training data only? Are the same θ used across all methods?
4) What is the runtime as a function of T and number of appliances, compared to AFAMAP, using specialized solvers (not CVX)?
5) Can you report results with time-windowed SACs (Section 4.2) to show added value?

Recommendation: Weak reject. The idea is sensible and potentially useful, but the current paper lacks key experimental and methodological details (rounding, hyperparameter tuning, scalability) and the novelty over established posterior-regularization-based constraints is limited. With stronger empirical validation and clarity, this could become a solid application paper.

Confidence: High.
