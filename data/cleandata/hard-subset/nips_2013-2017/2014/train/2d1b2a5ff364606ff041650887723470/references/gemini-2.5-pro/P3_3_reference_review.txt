This paper addresses the problem of blind source separation (BSS), specifically in the context of energy disaggregation, where an aggregate time series must be decomposed into its constituent source signals. The authors identify the core challenge of identifiability, where standard models like the Additive Factorial Hidden Markov Model (AFHMM) can produce physically unrealistic "franken-appliance" signals. The key contribution is the introduction of Signal Aggregate Constraints (SACs), a novel form of prior knowledge that regularizes the total value of each source signal over a given time period. Because these global constraints break the Markov property of the HMM, making standard inference like Viterbi intractable, the authors formulate the MAP inference problem as a penalized objective function. They then relax the integer state variables to be continuous and solve the resulting Convex Quadratic Program (CQP). The work is evaluated on both synthetic data and a real-world energy disaggregation dataset, showing significant improvements over a standard AFHMM and the state-of-the-art AFAMAP model.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Novel and Intuitive Constraint:** The core idea of using signal aggregate constraints is simple, elegant, and powerful. It represents a form of domain knowledge that is often readily available (e.g., from national surveys or device specifications) but is difficult to incorporate into standard time-series models. Unlike constraints on state transitions (e.g., AFAMAP) or state durations (e.g., HSMMs), SACs provide a global, non-Markovian perspective that directly addresses the plausibility of the *entire* separated signal, which is a major step toward solving the identifiability problem.

2.  **Sound Technical Approach:** The formulation of the problem as a relaxed CQP is technically sound and builds effectively on prior work like Kolter & Jaakkola (2012). While the CQP relaxation technique itself is not entirely new, its application to this new class of global constraints is a valuable contribution. The connection drawn to the posterior regularization framework in Section 6 provides a nice theoretical grounding, showing that the penalized objective function is not just an ad-hoc heuristic but corresponds to finding a KL-minimal distribution within a constrained space.

3.  **Strong and Convincing Empirical Evaluation:** The experimental section is a major strength of the paper.
    *   The toy data experiment effectively demonstrates the model's behavior and its robustness to misspecified priors.
    *   The use of the HES dataset is appropriate, and the evaluation under two conditions (synthetically aggregated data and true mains data) provides a comprehensive picture of performance. The latter is particularly important as it reflects a more realistic and challenging scenario.
    *   The comparison against AFAMAP, a strong and relevant baseline, clearly demonstrates the superiority of the proposed approach. The reported 45% error reduction over the baseline AFHMM and 29% over AFAMAP on real mains data is a significant advancement for the field.
    *   The use of both "National Total Consumption" (NTC) as a realistic prior and "True Total Consumption" (TTC) as an oracle provides a clear upper bound on the potential of this method and shows that even with imperfect priors, the gains are substantial.

**Weaknesses and Areas for Improvement:**

1.  **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** A significant weakness is the lack of discussion regarding the choice of the complexity parameter `λ_i`. The authors state they "simply set `λ_i = 1` for all the experiments." This is a critical hyperparameter that balances the likelihood term against the aggregate constraint. Its optimal value would likely depend on the appliance, the length of the time series, and our confidence in the prior `μ_i0`. A more complete evaluation would include a sensitivity analysis of `λ_i` or a discussion of how it could be set in practice (e.g., via cross-validation). Without this, the reproducibility and practical applicability of the method are less certain.

2.  **Details of the CQP Solution:** The paper solves a relaxed CQP where the state indicator variables `S_itk` are in the range [0, 1]. However, it is not specified how these continuous values are converted back into discrete states to compute the final disaggregated signals `x̂_it` and the error metrics (NDE, SAE). Is it simple rounding? Or perhaps using the fractional values as expectations? This is a crucial missing detail needed for reproducibility and to understand potential sources of error introduced by the relaxation.

3.  **Scalability Concerns:** While the paper notes that the runtime is similar to baselines for the one-day experiments, the computational complexity of solving a CQP is polynomial in the number of variables, which is proportional to `T` (the number of time steps). The experiments are conducted on `T`=100 (toy) and single days (T ≈ 144 for 10-min data). It is unclear how the method would scale to longer disaggregation tasks, such as a month or a year, where `T` would be significantly larger. A brief discussion of the computational complexity and potential scalability limitations would strengthen the paper.

4.  **Originality of Technical Machinery:** While the *idea* of SACs is original, the technical machinery (relaxing the AFHMM inference problem into a CQP) is very similar to that used in the AFAMAP paper [12]. The paper is clear about its relation to this work, but it's worth noting that the novelty lies more in the formulation of the new constraint rather than in the optimization method itself.

**Summary of Review:**

*   **Quality:** The paper is of high quality. The methodology is sound, and the claims are well-supported by strong experimental evidence on a real-world task. The main weaknesses are the lack of hyperparameter analysis and missing details about the final discretization step.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is very clearly written and well-organized. The motivation is compelling, the model is described precisely, and the experiments are easy to understand.
*   **Originality:** The paper introduces a novel and significant idea: using signal aggregate constraints to address the identifiability problem in BSS. This is a genuinely new type of constraint for this problem class. The optimization framework builds upon existing work but its application here is novel and impactful.
*   **Significance:** The results are highly significant. The work presents a demonstrable and substantial improvement over the state-of-the-art in energy disaggregation. The core idea of SACs is generalizable and could inspire similar work in other BSS domains where global properties of the source signals are known (e.g., computational biology, audio processing).

**Recommendation:**
This is a strong paper with a novel, well-motivated idea and compelling empirical results. It clearly advances the state-of-the-art on an important practical problem. The weaknesses identified are minor and could be addressed in a final version. I strongly recommend acceptance.
