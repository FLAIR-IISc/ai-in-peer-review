Summary
The paper tackles blind source separation for additive factorial HMMs (AFHMMs), motivated by energy disaggregation. The central idea is to inject domain knowledge in the form of signal aggregate constraints (SACs): priors on the total energy (or other aggregates) contributed by each latent source over a horizon. This is encoded as a squared penalty on deviations between the aggregate implied by latent states and given targets µi0. Because this penalty is non-Markovian, the standard Viterbi-style MAP inference is not applicable. The authors introduce an auxiliary transition variable Hit and relax integrality to obtain a concave quadratic objective under linear constraints—i.e., a convex quadratic program (CQP) that can be solved via CVX. They show the objective can be derived from Posterior Regularization. Experiments on a toy example and on the UK HES low-frequency dataset show substantial gains over AFHMM and over AFAMAP (Kolter & Jaakkola 2012), including with realistic priors on µ0 derived from national appliance totals.

Relation to prior work
- AFHMM and variants for energy disaggregation are well established (e.g., AFAMAP’s one-change-at-a-time constraint, semi-Markov duration models, non-homogeneous HMMs). This paper differs by constraining global aggregates rather than local dynamics.
- The idea aligns closely with Posterior Regularization (Ganchev et al.) and measurement-based learning (Liang et al.), but here the “measurement features” are aggregates of per-state means across time. The connection is acknowledged and formalized.
- Aggregate constraints have appeared in probabilistic databases in much simpler models; this work brings them to factorial HMMs with an efficient convex relaxation.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The relaxation and convexity are plausible. Using Hit to linearize transitions and noting Λi and Vt are PSD makes the maximization a concave QP over a convex polytope (box plus linear consistency constraints). This is standard for relaxed HMM inference.
- However, some key details are missing:
  - Rounding/decoding: How are soft solutions S used to produce disaggregated signals? Are signals simply x̂it = µi^T Sit (expected values), or is there a discrete projection? Baselines (AFHMM/AFAMAP) should be evaluated under the same evaluation (soft vs. hard) for fairness.
  - Complexity and scalability: The relaxed problem introduces O(T∑iKi + T∑iKi^2) variables; off-the-shelf CVX solvers may not scale to long sequences or many appliances. The paper claims similar runtimes to AFHMM/AFAMAP but lacks concrete complexity analyses or precise timing breakdowns (problem sizes, solver settings, hardware).
  - Hyperparameter sensitivity: All experiments fix λi = 1. The toy experiment shows performance degrades as µ0 deviates from truth, but there is no sensitivity study to λi or robustness to mis-specified µ0 on real data.
  - Training/evaluation protocol: For the “with mains” setting, the mains includes unknown loads not modeled; clarify whether NDE/SAE are computed only over modeled appliances and how mismatch (residuals) are handled. Also clarify whether “AFHMM baseline” uses chainwise Viterbi or the relaxed CQP with λ=0.
- Empirics: Results are encouraging and include paired t-tests. Using national totals (NTC) is realistic, but more analysis of when SACs hurt (e.g., severe µ0 misspecification) would strengthen the claims.

Clarity
- Generally readable and well-organized, but some issues:
  - Notation is heavy; a compact restatement of the final CQP with all constraints collected would help reproducibility.
  - Provide explicit decoding procedure from relaxed S/H to signals/states.
  - The description of parameter learning (µi via k-means; transition/initial probabilities) is brief. For reproducibility, specify estimation for transitions and noise variances, and whether learned per-house/per-appliance or pooled.
  - Tables/Figures are referenced but not shown here; ensure they include error bars and exact problem sizes.
  - Several references are incomplete (missing years/pages); please fix.

Originality
- The notion of imposing per-source aggregate constraints is intuitive and, to my knowledge, novel in AFHMM-based disaggregation. The formalization as a convex relaxation and its link to Posterior Regularization are neat and of broader interest.
- Conceptually related to prior “measurement/expectation” constraints, but the specific choice of aggregate features and the CQP construction for AFHMM are new. Overall, moderate originality.

Significance
- The approach addresses a real pain point: identifiability in BSS, especially under ultra-low-frequency data. Gains over AFAMAP on HES data are practically relevant.
- The idea is general and could be adopted in other factorial latent sequence models or other BSS domains where aggregate priors exist (e.g., biologically constrained expression, audio with known per-source energy budgets).
- The main threat to impact is scalability (generic QP solver) and dependence on reasonable µ0; both could be mitigated with specialized solvers and uncertainty-aware priors.

Pros
- Simple, general idea with clear domain motivation.
- Sound convex relaxation; principled PR interpretation.
- Strong empirical gains on challenging low-frequency data.
- Straightforward to extend to multiple time-window constraints.

Cons
- Missing decoding/rounding details; fairness of baselines unclear.
- Scalability concerns with CVX; limited complexity analysis.
- Limited sensitivity analysis to λi and µ0; fixed λ across all experiments.
- Supervised per-appliance training reduces realism; limited discussion of unsupervised or cross-house generalization.
- References formatting and a few clarity gaps.

Questions for authors
1. How do you decode from relaxed S/H to states/signals? Are baselines evaluated comparably (soft vs. hard predictions)?
2. What are typical problem sizes (I, Ki, T) and variable counts, and how do runtimes scale? Could you provide per-house timing with hardware/solver details?
3. How sensitive are results to λi and to biased µ0 (e.g., ±25–100%) on the HES data?
4. In the mains setting, how do you handle unmodeled loads when computing NDE/SAE?
5. Could you implement a specialized first-order/ADMM solver exploiting chain structure to scale beyond CVX?
6. Can you incorporate uncertainty in µ0 (e.g., Gaussian priors) rather than fixed targets, and would that preserve convexity?

Minor comments/typos
- Ensure all references have complete bibliographic details and years.
- Clarify whether the local consistency constraints on H and S fully define the chain marginal polytope (they should for trees).
- The claim “similar runtime” would benefit from quantitative evidence.

Recommendation
A promising and practically useful contribution with a clean formulation and strong empirical results. I recommend acceptance if the authors address decoding, scalability, and sensitivity details; otherwise borderline but still positive due to impact and clarity of the core idea.
