**Summary of the Paper**

This paper introduces "Signal Aggregate Constraints" (SACs) as a form of prior knowledge to improve blind source separation (BSS) in the context of Additive Factorial Hidden Markov Models (AFHMMs). The primary motivation is the energy disaggregation problem, where the goal is to separate a household's total electricity consumption into the consumption of individual appliances. The key challenge in this domain is the non-identifiability of sources, which can lead to unrealistic "franken-appliance" signals. The authors propose that in many BSS applications, one has prior knowledge about the expected aggregate value of each source signal over a period of time (e.g., the average daily energy consumption of a toaster).

The paper formalizes this idea by adding a quadratic penalty term to the AFHMM's MAP inference objective, which encourages the sum of the inferred signal for each source to be close to a predefined target value. Because this constraint is non-Markovian, it breaks standard dynamic programming algorithms like Viterbi. To overcome this, the authors formulate a relaxed version of the MAP inference problem as a convex quadratic program (CQP), which can be solved efficiently. They also show a nice theoretical connection, demonstrating that their penalized objective can be derived from the posterior regularization framework. The proposed model, AFHMM+SAC, is evaluated on both toy data and a real-world energy disaggregation dataset (HES), showing significant improvements in accuracy over both a standard AFHMM and the state-of-the-art AFAMAP model.

**Strengths**

1.  **Novel and Well-Motivated Contribution:** The core idea of using signal aggregate constraints is simple, intuitive, and highly effective for the problem at hand. It directly addresses the critical issue of identifiability in BSS by incorporating a type of domain knowledge that is often available but underexploited. The "franken-appliance" example provides excellent motivation. This idea seems broadly applicable to other BSS domains.

2.  **Technically Sound Formulation:** The paper presents a clear and technically sound approach. The transformation of the non-Markovian MAP problem into a CQP by relaxing the integer constraints is a well-established and valid technique. The theoretical justification in Section 6, linking the approach to the principled framework of posterior regularization, adds significant weight and shows that the formulation is not merely ad-hoc.

3.  **Strong and Convincing Experimental Evaluation:** The empirical results are a major strength of the paper.
    *   The evaluation is performed on a challenging, real-world, low-frequency dataset (HES), which makes the results more compelling.
    *   The authors compare against appropriate and strong baselines, including AFHMM and the state-of-the-art AFAMAP.
    *   The experimental design is thoughtful. The use of an "oracle" setting with the True Total Consumption (TTC) is a clever way to demonstrate the upper bound of the method's potential.
    *   The results show substantial and statistically significant improvements over prior work (a 29% NDE improvement over AFAMAP in the most realistic setting is impressive).
    *   Reporting computational time and showing it is comparable to the baselines addresses practical concerns about the CQP approach.

4.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is very well-written, clearly organized, and easy to follow. The motivation, model, inference procedure, and experiments are all explained logically and concisely.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Discussion of the Relaxation:** The paper solves a relaxed version of the original integer quadratic program, where the state indicator variables `Sitk` are allowed to be in [0, 1]. However, there is no discussion of how the resulting fractional solutions are interpreted or used to generate the final discrete state sequences. Are they rounded? Does the solver typically return near-integer solutions for this problem? This is an important practical detail for reproducibility and understanding the gap between the relaxed problem and the original MAP problem.

2.  **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The regularization parameter `λi` is set to 1 for all experiments. While this simplifies the evaluation, the performance of regularized models often depends on this choice. A sensitivity analysis or at least a brief discussion on how to set this hyperparameter (e.g., via cross-validation) would strengthen the paper's practical utility.

3.  **Dependence on Prior Quality:** The method's success is contingent on having reasonably accurate prior information for the aggregate values (`µi0`). The toy data experiment shows graceful degradation, which is good. However, in the real-data experiments, the prior (NTC) is derived from the average over the training sets of the *same* overall dataset. A more challenging scenario would be applying a general prior (e.g., from a national survey) to a new set of homes not included in the survey. A discussion on the robustness to less accurate or mismatched priors would be beneficial.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Proposes a novel, intuitive, and effective form of constraint for an important and difficult problem in machine learning.
*   The technical approach is sound, well-executed, and theoretically justified.
*   The empirical results are strong, demonstrating a significant advance over the state of the art on a realistic benchmark.
*   The paper is clear, well-written, and likely to be impactful, inspiring follow-up work in energy disaggregation and other BSS domains.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   The lack of discussion on handling the fractional outputs of the CQP relaxation is a notable omission.
*   The analysis of hyperparameter tuning and robustness to prior quality could be more thorough.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a high-quality paper that presents a valuable contribution to the field. The core idea is elegant and practical, the technical execution is solid, and the experimental results are compelling. The weaknesses identified are relatively minor and relate more to completeness rather than fundamental flaws in the approach. They could be addressed in a final version of the paper. The work clearly advances the state of the art in energy disaggregation and introduces a constraint framework that could be useful in many other domains. I recommend acceptance.
