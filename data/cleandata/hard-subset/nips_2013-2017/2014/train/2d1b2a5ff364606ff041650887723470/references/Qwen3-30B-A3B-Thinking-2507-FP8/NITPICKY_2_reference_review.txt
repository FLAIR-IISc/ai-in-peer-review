

# Review of "Signal Aggregate Constraints in Additive Factorial HMMs, with Application to Energy Disaggregation"

## Summary
This paper introduces Signal Aggregate Constraints (SACs) for blind source separation problems, specifically applied to energy disaggregation using Additive Factorial HMMs (AFHMMs). SACs constrain the aggregate values of source signals to be close to specified values, addressing identifiability issues in BSS. The authors formulate a convex quadratic program to solve the constrained optimization problem, demonstrating significant improvements in energy disaggregation performance compared to baseline AFHMM and AFAMAP methods.

## Strengths
The paper presents a novel constraint mechanism (SACs) that addresses an important practical issue in BSS applications. The application to energy disaggregation is well-motivated, and the experimental results show promising improvements (45% NDE reduction over AFHMM). The theoretical connection to posterior regularization is well-established, and the technical approach is generally sound.

## Weaknesses

### Formatting and Typographical Issues
Multiple formatting inconsistencies undermine the paper's professionalism:
- Inconsistent capitalization of "AFHMM" (sometimes "AFHMM", sometimes "AFHMM")
- "franken-appliance" should be "Franken-appliance" (capitalized)
- "equavalent" (section 5) should be "equivalent"
- "inputted" (section 7.2.2) should be "input"
- Inconsistent spacing around mathematical symbols (e.g., "µi0" vs "µi0")
- Missing closing parentheses in equation (2) in section 4.1
- Inconsistent reference numbering format (e.g., [7,6] should be [6,7])

### Technical Omissions and Ambiguities
1. **Undefined Acronyms**: SAC is not defined until section 4, despite being used in the title and abstract. TTC (true total consumption) is used in section 7.2.1 without prior definition.

2. **Missing Critical Details**: The paper claims "45% decrease in normalized disaggregation error" but fails to provide the absolute NDE values for AFHMM and AFHMM+SAC. Without absolute values, it's impossible to assess the meaningfulness of the improvement.

3. **Hyperparameter Justification**: The paper states "we simply set λi = 1 for all the experiments" without justification. This critical hyperparameter choice should be explained or validated with sensitivity analysis.

4. **Statistical Significance**: While mentioning "paired t-test" in section 7.2.1, the paper doesn't provide p-values or confidence intervals to substantiate the claimed "significant improvement."

5. **Metric Ambiguity**: The paper uses "improved 33%" without clarifying whether this means NDE was reduced by 33% or performance improved by 33%. This is a critical ambiguity.

6. **Computational Complexity**: The paper claims "polynomial time" but doesn't specify the degree of the polynomial, which is essential for assessing practical applicability.

7. **Methodological Details**: The paper states "we simply assume 3 states for all the appliances" without justification for this specific choice of states, which is a critical hyperparameter.

8. **Reference Formatting**: Multiple references have inconsistent formatting (e.g., trailing commas, incomplete citations like "AAAI-,").

### Presentation Issues
1. **Figure and Table References**: The paper refers to "Figure 1" and "Table 1" but doesn't provide these figures/tables in the excerpt, making it impossible to verify the results.

2. **Redundant Phrasing**: "the national total consumption (NTC), was the average consumption..." should be "the national total consumption (NTC) was defined as..."

3. **Ambiguous Claims**: "AFHMM+SAC using NTC had improved 33% over state-of-the-art AFAMAP" - it's unclear whether this means NDE was reduced by 33% or something else.

4. **Missing Sensitivity Analysis**: The paper doesn't investigate how SACs perform when the assumed aggregate values (µi0) are not close to the true values, which is a critical practical limitation.

## Overall Assessment
While the paper presents a novel idea (SACs) with promising empirical results in energy disaggregation, the numerous formatting inconsistencies, technical omissions, and ambiguous claims significantly undermine the paper's quality. The paper fails to provide sufficient detail for reproducibility (critical for NIPS), doesn't properly justify key methodological choices, and contains multiple typos and formatting errors that suggest lack of attention to detail.

The technical contribution is modest - SACs represent a relatively straightforward extension to existing work. The paper doesn't sufficiently differentiate its approach from related work (e.g., the k-segment constraints mentioned in section 2) or demonstrate why SACs are superior to other constraint approaches.

The paper's claim of "45% decrease" without absolute error values, the unexplained hyperparameter choice of λi=1, and the lack of statistical significance details for claimed improvements are particularly concerning.

## Recommendation
I recommend rejection. While the paper has a reasonable technical foundation and promising results, the numerous minor flaws (formatting inconsistencies, technical omissions, ambiguous claims) collectively undermine the paper's quality and reproducibility. The paper reads as if it was not properly proofread or edited, which is unacceptable for a NIPS submission. The reviewers should not be required to decipher ambiguous claims or search for missing details in the paper. The paper's technical contribution is also not sufficiently novel or impactful to outweigh these significant presentation and methodological shortcomings.
