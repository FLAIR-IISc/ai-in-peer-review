

# Review of "Signal Aggregate Constraints in Additive Factorial HMMs, with Application to Energy Disaggregation"

This paper proposes "Signal Aggregate Constraints" (SACs) for Additive Factorial Hidden Markov Models (AFHMMs) to address identifiability issues in blind source separation, specifically for energy disaggregation. SACs constrain the aggregate values of source signals (e.g., total daily energy consumption per appliance) to be close to specified values. The authors formulate the inference problem as a convex quadratic program and demonstrate improvements over AFHMM and AFAMAP on energy disaggregation data.

The paper is well-written and clearly organized, with a strong motivation for addressing the identifiability problem in BSS. The technical approach is sound, and the connection to posterior regularization [4] is appropriately noted. The application to energy disaggregation is relevant, and the authors provide a reasonable justification for the constraints.

However, I have significant concerns about the paper's claims and validation that prevent me from recommending acceptance. The primary issues are:

**Overstated Results and Inadequate Baseline Comparison:** The paper claims a "45% decrease in normalized disaggregation error compared to the original AFHMM" and "29% improvement compared to a recent state-of-the-art approach [12]." These claims are misleading because the 45% improvement is based on using the oracle "true total consumption" (TTC), which is never available in practice. The actual improvement using the more realistic "national total consumption" (NTC) is only 33% over AFAMAP, not 45%. The paper's claim of 29% improvement compared to [12] is similarly based on an oracle scenario, as [12] is not the strongest baseline for energy disaggregation. The paper fails to compare against more recent state-of-the-art methods like [11, 16, 24], which would provide a more meaningful evaluation.

**Insufficient Robustness Analysis:** While the toy data experiment shows that performance degrades as the constraint deviates from the true value, this is only shown in a single figure without statistical significance testing. The paper lacks a thorough analysis of how sensitive the method is to errors in the constraint values, which is crucial for real-world applications where precise aggregate values are rarely known exactly. The authors acknowledge this limitation but don't provide evidence that SACs remain effective even with substantial errors in the constraint specification.

**Questionable Novelty:** The authors claim SACs are a new concept, but the approach is fundamentally similar to posterior regularization [4] and other constraint-based methods. The paper does not sufficiently differentiate SACs from existing approaches or demonstrate why this specific form of constraint is uniquely valuable. The connection to posterior regularization is acknowledged but not thoroughly explored, making the novelty claim weak.

**Inadequate Statistical Validation:** The paper states "AFHMM+SAC using NTC had improved 33% and 16% over state-of-the-art AFAMAP" but doesn't provide sufficient statistical evidence for these claims. While the paper mentions paired t-tests, it doesn't report the exact p-values or confidence intervals, making it difficult to assess the statistical significance of the improvements across multiple households.

**Limited Generalizability:** The paper only evaluates SACs on energy disaggregation data. There's no evidence that SACs would be beneficial for other BSS applications, despite the authors' claim that the constraints are "general across a wide variety of domains." The paper lacks experiments demonstrating the approach on other BSS tasks.

**Strengths:** The paper is technically sound, clearly written, and the convex optimization approach is well-motivated. The connection to posterior regularization is appropriately noted, and the energy disaggregation application is relevant.

**Weaknesses:** The paper's claims are overstated, the validation is insufficient, the novelty is questionable, and the generalizability is not demonstrated. The results depend heavily on the use of oracle constraints that would never be available in practice.

The paper's most significant contribution appears to be the demonstration that incorporating domain knowledge about aggregate consumption can improve energy disaggregation. However, the paper fails to provide convincing evidence that SACs represent a significant advance over existing constraint-based approaches, and the claimed improvements are not supported by proper validation.

I recommend rejection for the following reasons: The paper overstates its contributions, fails to provide adequate validation against realistic baselines, and doesn't sufficiently demonstrate the generalizability of its approach. The results are promising but not compelling enough to warrant publication at NIPS given the current state of the art in energy disaggregation and BSS. The authors would need to substantially revise the paper to provide more rigorous validation, better baseline comparisons, and stronger evidence of the method's novelty and general applicability before it could be considered for publication.
