

# Review of "Signal Aggregate Constraints in Additive Factorial HMMs, with Application to Energy Disaggregation"

This paper introduces a novel approach to addressing the identifiability problem in blind source separation (BSS) through signal aggregate constraints (SACs), which constrain the total aggregate value of each source signal (e.g., total daily energy consumption of an appliance) to be close to a specified value. The authors apply this to the additive factorial HMM (AFHMM) framework for energy disaggregation, a critical application where identifying individual appliance usage from household electricity consumption is essential.

The paper makes three key contributions: (1) proposing SACs as a general constraint for BSS problems, (2) developing a convex quadratic program for efficient inference with these constraints, and (3) demonstrating significant performance improvements on real-world energy disaggregation data. The authors show that SACs address the fundamental identifiability problem in BSS by incorporating domain knowledge about expected total signal values, which is particularly relevant for energy disaggregation where prior knowledge about appliance energy consumption exists.

The work is clearly situated within the literature on BSS and energy disaggregation, with a thorough discussion of related approaches in Section 2. The authors effectively differentiate their approach from posterior regularization and other constraint-based methods by focusing on aggregate signal values rather than state transitions or other properties.

The most compelling aspect of this paper is its novelty. While posterior regularization and other constraint methods have been proposed, SACs represent a specific, domain-relevant constraint that is general across many applications. The idea of constraining the total aggregate value of source signals is conceptually simple yet powerful, and the authors convincingly demonstrate its utility in energy disaggregation. The paper's core insight—that incorporating domain knowledge about expected total signal values can significantly improve identifiability—is both elegant and practical.

The experimental results are strong, showing a 45% decrease in normalized disaggregation error compared to the baseline AFHMM and a 29% improvement over a recent state-of-the-art approach. The evaluation on real-world energy data (Household Electricity Survey) with both national total consumption and true total consumption constraints demonstrates the practical value of SACs. The computational efficiency of the convex quadratic program approach is also well-documented, with similar runtime to existing methods.

The paper is well-written, clearly organized, and technically sound. The mathematical derivations are carefully presented, and the experimental setup is appropriate for the problem domain. The authors acknowledge limitations, such as the need for prior knowledge about aggregate values and the use of a single constraint per chain rather than more complex time-period constraints, which they note could be addressed in future work.

In terms of significance, SACs represent a promising direction that could be applied beyond energy disaggregation to other BSS problems where total signal values are known or can be estimated (e.g., audio source separation where total energy of each source is known, or biological signal separation where total protein expression is known). The paper effectively demonstrates how domain knowledge can be incorporated in a principled way to address identifiability, which is a fundamental challenge in many BSS applications.

While the method requires prior knowledge about expected aggregate values (which may not always be available), the authors provide a compelling argument for the practicality of this requirement in energy disaggregation through national surveys. The approach is also easily extensible to more complex constraints across time periods, as noted in Section 4.2.

The paper excels in novelty and significance, presenting a clear conceptual advance that addresses a fundamental problem in BSS. While the methodological approach is not entirely novel (it builds on convex optimization and posterior regularization), the specific application of aggregate constraints represents a significant new direction. The authors successfully demonstrate the value of this direction through compelling experimental results.

This paper makes a strong contribution to the field by introducing a novel constraint framework that can be applied broadly to BSS problems. The energy disaggregation application is well-motivated and serves as an excellent demonstration of the approach's value. The 45% improvement in normalized disaggregation error over the baseline AFHMM is particularly impressive and suggests this could be a valuable addition to the energy disaggregation toolkit.

As a reviewer who prioritizes novel ideas over methodological perfection, I find this paper to be a strong candidate for acceptance. The core idea of signal aggregate constraints is both novel and impactful, and the experimental results provide convincing evidence of its value. The paper makes a clear, significant contribution to the field of blind source separation and demonstrates how domain knowledge can be incorporated to address fundamental identifiability problems.

Strengths:
- Novel concept of signal aggregate constraints for BSS
- Strong experimental results with significant performance improvements
- Clear application to a meaningful real-world problem (energy disaggregation)
- Well-written and well-organized paper
- Demonstrates how domain knowledge can be incorporated in a principled way

Weaknesses:
- Requires prior knowledge about aggregate signal values (though the paper justifies this in the application domain)
- Uses a single constraint per chain rather than more complex time-period constraints (though the paper acknowledges this as a potential extension)
