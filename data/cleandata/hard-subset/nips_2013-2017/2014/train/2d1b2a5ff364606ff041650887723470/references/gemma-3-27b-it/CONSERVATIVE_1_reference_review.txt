This paper introduces signal aggregate constraints (SACs) within an additive factorial hidden Markov model (AFHMM) for blind source separation, motivated by the energy disaggregation problem. The core idea – incorporating prior knowledge about expected total energy consumption of appliances – is intuitively appealing. The authors formulate the problem as a convex quadratic program, enabling efficient computation. They demonstrate improvements over AFHMM and a recent state-of-the-art disaggregation method on real-world energy data.

While the motivation is sound and the proposed method shows promising empirical results, I find the paper ultimately lacking in rigor and sufficient justification for the proposed approach. My primary concern centers around the novelty and significance of the contribution. The concept of incorporating prior knowledge into probabilistic models is hardly new (as the authors acknowledge in the introduction, referencing posterior regularization, the generalized expectation criterion, and measurement-based learning). The specific contribution – *how* to identify “classes of constraints” – feels under-developed. The authors posit that aggregate constraints are useful, but provide limited theoretical justification *why* these constraints are generally applicable across diverse BSS domains beyond energy disaggregation. 

The empirical evaluation, while showing improvements, relies heavily on a single application – energy disaggregation. The authors acknowledge this limitation but do not adequately address whether the observed gains are specific to this domain or generalize to other BSS problems. The performance improvement of 45% over the original AFHMM and 29% over AFAMAP is noteworthy, but the impact of the chosen parameters (λi or δi) isn’t thoroughly explored; it's unclear how sensitive the results are to their selection.  The claim of a 45% decrease in normalized disaggregation error is significant, and a more detailed error analysis is warranted. The toy data example, while illustrating robustness to constraint specification error, feels contrived and doesn’t provide strong evidence for generalizability.

The technical contribution, the convex quadratic program, is presented clearly, but relies on relaxation. The implications of this relaxation – how much accuracy is lost – aren't fully discussed. The connection to posterior regularization (Section 6) feels tacked on and doesn’t substantially strengthen the paper. It simply re-frames the approach within an existing framework without offering new theoretical insight. 

The related work section is adequate but could be more thorough. The discussion of constraints on HMM states (AFAMAP, non-homogeneous HMMs, semi-Markov models) is brief and doesn’t adequately position SACs relative to these existing techniques.  It’s not clear why SACs offer a significant advantage over, say, simply constraining the number of state transitions per time step, as done in AFAMAP.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Demonstrated empirical improvement on a real-world dataset.
*   Clear presentation of the mathematical formulation.
*   Addresses a practical problem (energy disaggregation) with a potentially valuable approach.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   Limited theoretical justification for the general applicability of SACs.
*   Lack of evaluation on datasets beyond energy disaggregation.
*   Insufficient analysis of the impact of hyperparameters.
*   Incremental novelty; builds on existing techniques without a major theoretical leap.
*   The relaxation in the CQP formulation is not thoroughly discussed.



**Overall Assessment:**

I recommend **rejection**. While the paper presents a technically sound and clearly written approach, the lack of strong theoretical justification, limited scope of evaluation, and incremental novelty make it unsuitable for NIPS. The empirical results are encouraging, but further validation on diverse datasets and a more comprehensive analysis are needed to establish the significance of this work. The paper feels more appropriate for a more applied venue. I urge the authors to expand the scope of their evaluation, explore the theoretical foundations of SACs more deeply, and provide a more compelling argument for their general applicability before resubmitting.
