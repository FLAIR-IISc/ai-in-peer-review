Summary
This paper introduces signal aggregate constraints (SACs) for blind source separation with additive factorial HMMs (AFHMMs), motivated by energy disaggregation (non-intrusive load monitoring). The key idea is that in many domains the total contribution of each latent source over a window (e.g., a day) is easier to estimate than the precise activation times. The authors encode this as soft quadratic penalties that encourage the sum over time of each chain’s emission to match a target µi0. They derive a convex quadratic program (CQP) for a relaxed MAP problem by introducing transition indicator variables Hit, show that the objective can also be obtained via posterior regularization (PR), and solve the relaxation with CVX. Experiments on a toy dataset and the UK HES low-frequency electricity data show improvements over AFHMM and AFAMAP [Kolter & Jaakkola, AISTATS’12], including with real mains signals.

The work relates to: factorial HMMs [Ghahramani & Jordan], AFAMAP [Kolter & Jaakkola’12], semi-Markov/duration constraints in NILM [Johnson & Willsky; Kim et al.], and general frameworks for posterior constraints: posterior regularization [Ganchev et al.], generalized expectation [Mann & McCallum], and measurement-based learning [Liang et al.]. The idea of aggregate constraints has also been considered in probabilistic DBs [Yang et al.], but not with the FHMM structure or this application.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The SAC penalty is well-defined and integrates cleanly with AFHMMs. Introducing Hit variables linearizes the transition terms; the relaxed problem has a concave quadratic objective (negative PSD quadratic terms plus linear) with linear constraints, so maximizing it is a convex program. The PR derivation is correct and clarifies the objective as MAP under a constrained posterior family.
- Algorithmic details: The relaxation yields fractional S; the paper appears to evaluate on reconstructed signals µT Sit rather than discrete states, which is fine for NDE/SAE, but the rounding strategy (if any) should be described. Complexity scales as O(T Σi Ki^2) variables due to H, which is practical for small Ki (3 here) but could be limiting; this trade-off merits discussion.
- Empirics: On HES, gains over AFHMM and AFAMAP are consistent, including with mains (harder setting). The toy study sensibly shows sensitivity to misspecified µ0. However, λi are fixed to 1 (no tuning), which may be suboptimal; a sensitivity analysis for λ would strengthen the case. Comparisons do not include more recent duration/semi-Markov baselines or methods that could also benefit from SACs; combining AFAMAP with SACs would be informative. Reporting exact dataset sizes, T (time steps per day), and solver times would improve reproducibility.
- Fairness: AFHMM+SAC uses “national total consumption” (NTC) computed from training days across households. This is reasonable prior information, but its availability and robustness across demographics should be discussed; how performance degrades with biased priors on µ0 in the real data setting is important.

Clarity
The paper is generally clear and well organized. The motivation for identifiability and the NILM application are compelling. Notation is dense; the constraints on Hit would benefit from being written explicitly as ∑j hitjk = S_{i,t−1,k} and ∑k hitjk = S_{i,t,j}. Please clarify how S at test time is used (fractional vs. discretized) and how initial conditions are handled in the constraints. Minor typos and formatting issues exist (e.g., some citation formatting and equation references).

Originality
Using global aggregate constraints across time as soft priors in FHMM-based BSS, and casting the resulting relaxed MAP as a CQP, appears novel in this context. The PR perspective is a nice connection. While posterior constraints per se are not new, the particular form (sum-of-emissions constraints) and application to NILM seem new and practically useful.

Significance
The idea is simple, generic, and likely to transfer to other BSS settings where totals are known (audio source separation with known loudness budgets, biological assays with known total counts, etc.). The empirical gains on realistic low-frequency NILM data (a challenging regime) suggest practical impact. The broader ML audience may find the technique a useful addition to the toolbox for structured latent-variable models.

Pros
- Clear, intuitive prior (aggregate totals) with broad applicability.
- Sound convex relaxation; elegant link to posterior regularization.
- Consistent empirical improvements on a realistic, challenging dataset.
- Maintains similar runtime to strong baselines for small Ki.

Cons
- Limited comparison set; no evaluation of combining SACs with AFAMAP or semi-Markov/duration models.
- No tuning/sensitivity study for λ; robustness on real data to µ0 misspecification is not quantified.
- Scalability beyond small state spaces unclear; reliance on CVX may limit adoption.
- Evaluation focuses on NILM; no demonstration in a second domain.

Suggestions for improvement
- Include ablations: (i) AFAMAP+SAC; (ii) semi-Markov+SAC; (iii) sensitivity to λ and to biased µ0 on real data. Consider cross-validation or hierarchical priors over µ0 and λ.
- Clarify rounding/use of fractional solutions and provide a simple projection if discrete sequences are desired.
- Report exact problem sizes (I, Ki, T), solver settings, and wall-clock times; consider a custom first-order solver for scalability.
- Evaluate the proposed multiple-window SACs (day/night) to show added value.
- Discuss applicability beyond NILM; a small second-domain experiment (e.g., synthetic audio mixtures with known per-source energy) would bolster generality.
- Cite related convex/LP relaxations for MAP in graphical models (e.g., LP relaxations and TRW) and discuss differences.

Recommendation
Weak accept. The paper presents a simple, well-motivated, and generally applicable idea; the optimization is principled and practical for the considered regime; and the empirical results on a realistic dataset are compelling. Addressing the experimental and clarity suggestions would strengthen the contribution further.
