Summary
This paper addresses identifiability in blind source separation with additive factorial HMMs (AFHMMs) by introducing signal aggregate constraints (SACs): soft constraints that the total energy (sum over time) of each latent source should match a prior target. The authors incorporate SACs into AFHMM inference via a quadratic penalty on deviations from per-chain totals, derive a convex quadratic program (CQP) for a relaxed formulation using auxiliary transition variables, and show the objective arises naturally from posterior regularization. Experiments on toy data and the UK HES energy-disaggregation dataset (ultra-low frequency) demonstrate consistent reductions in normalized disaggregation error (NDE) and signal aggregate error (SAE) relative to AFHMM and AFAMAP (Kolter & Jaakkola, AISTATS’12). Gains are reported both with oracle aggregate targets and with realistic “national average” targets.

Relation to prior work: The paper builds on AFHMMs (Ghahramani & Jordan), additive disaggregation with constraints (AFAMAP), semi-Markov/duration-constrained NILM (e.g., Kim et al.; Johnson & Willsky), and general frameworks for incorporating constraints/prior knowledge such as posterior regularization (Ganchev et al.), generalized expectation (Mann & McCallum), and learning from measurements (Liang et al.). The proposed SACs align conceptually with these frameworks but target a ubiquitous, domain-agnostic form of side information—aggregate counts/totals—implemented here with a tractable convex relaxation.

Strengths
- Simple, broadly applicable idea: aggregate totals are often known or estimable in BSS problems; encoding them improves identifiability.
- Clean formulation and connection to posterior regularization; the CQP is a natural relaxation with linear constraints and concave quadratic objective.
- Empirical improvements on a realistic, low-frequency NILM dataset; comparisons to a strong baseline (AFAMAP) show meaningful gains (e.g., 29% SAE improvement reported).
- Sensitivity analysis on toy data illustrates robustness to mismatch in aggregate targets.
- Computational cost appears comparable to baselines at the reported scales.

Weaknesses
- Dependence on external targets µi0: When priors are inaccurate or household-specific variation is large, performance may degrade. Real-data sensitivity beyond “national average vs. oracle” is not thoroughly studied.
- Limited discussion of rounding/integrality: The CQP returns fractional S/H; it is unclear whether outputs are used directly as soft assignments or rounded, and how that affects metrics and interpretability.
- Parameter learning details are sparse: Transitions and noise parameters are assumed known; µi estimated via k-means. No joint learning with SACs (e.g., PR-EM) is explored, which could materially affect performance.
- Baselines: Duration/semi-Markov models and recent non-homogeneous/discriminative methods are mentioned but not compared empirically; this weakens the case that SACs are the key driver of improvements over other common constraints.
- Scalability: Reliance on generic CVX may limit applicability to longer sequences/more appliances; no custom solver or complexity constants are provided.
- Some presentation issues: Incomplete and inconsistent references; a few notational jumps and missing engineering details (λ selection, σt handling, appliance selection, K choice justification).

Quality
Technically sound overall. The convexity of the relaxed objective is correctly argued (maximization of a concave quadratic with linear constraints). The PR derivation is straightforward. However, the inference pipeline lacks clarity on post-relaxation discretization and on how θ is estimated; the choice to fix λi=1 without validation is questionable. Experimental evidence is positive but could be strengthened with more ablations (λ sweep, µ0 perturbations on real data) and additional baselines.

Clarity
Generally well written with clear motivation and derivations; the auxiliary Hit variables and vectorized notation are helpful once defined. Missing details on rounding, training of transition/noise parameters, and precise appliance sets. References need attention (years, venues, completeness).

Originality
Moderate. The use of aggregate constraints per se is not new (PR/GE/measurements literature), but instantiating them for AFHMMs with a tractable convex relaxation and demonstrating substantial gains for low-frequency NILM is a useful contribution.

Significance
For NILM and related BSS tasks with accessible aggregate priors (e.g., device usage statistics, biological pathway totals), this approach is practical and likely to be adopted. The improvement over AFAMAP on a challenging dataset is noteworthy. Broader ML impact is incremental but coherent with ongoing interest in weak supervision and constrained inference.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide a clear procedure for converting relaxed solutions to usable estimates (e.g., use fractional S to form expected signals vs. rounding). If rounding is used, describe it and report its impact.
- Analyze sensitivity to λi and propose a principled tuning strategy (cross-validation on held-out days, Bayesian selection, or per-appliance normalization reflecting expected variance).
- Extend the real-data sensitivity analysis by perturbing µ0 (e.g., scaling, additive noise) to quantify robustness when priors are misspecified.
- Compare against duration-constrained/semi-Markov baselines and/or combine SACs with “at-most-one-change” or duration priors to show complementarity.
- Discuss and, if possible, implement a specialized solver (e.g., block coordinate ascent, ADMM, projected Newton) that exploits structure to scale beyond CVX.
- Clarify parameter learning for θ and consider joint learning with SACs via PR-EM.
- Improve references and add missing details (σt estimation, appliance lists, K selection rationale).

Pros for acceptance
- Conceptually simple, broadly useful constraint class with a principled formulation.
- Solid empirical gains on a realistic and difficult dataset.
- Clear connection to established frameworks; convex relaxation is elegant.

Cons against acceptance
- Heavy reliance on external priors; limited robustness analysis on real data.
- Missing methodological details (rounding, θ learning, λ tuning).
- Limited baseline coverage and scalability discussion.

Overall recommendation: Weak accept. The idea is practical and the reported improvements meaningful for NILM; with clarified methodology and stronger empirical analysis, the contribution would be more compelling.
