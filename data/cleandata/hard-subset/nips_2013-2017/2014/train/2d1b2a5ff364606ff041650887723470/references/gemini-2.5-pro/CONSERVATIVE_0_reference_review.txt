This paper addresses the problem of identifiability in blind source separation, particularly within the context of Additive Factorial Hidden Markov Models (AFHMMs) applied to energy disaggregation. The central idea is to introduce "Signal Aggregate Constraints" (SACs), which leverage prior knowledge about the expected total value of each source signal over a given time period. This constraint is incorporated as a quadratic penalty term into the MAP inference objective for the AFHMM. As this penalty breaks the Markov property and complicates inference, the authors propose relaxing the integer optimization problem into a Convex Quadratic Program (CQP), which can be solved efficiently. The method is evaluated on a toy dataset and a real-world energy disaggregation dataset (HES), demonstrating significant improvements in disaggregation accuracy over a standard AFHMM and the state-of-the-art AFAMAP model. The paper also provides a theoretical connection to the posterior regularization framework.

**Strengths:**
1.  **Well-Motivated Problem:** The paper clearly articulates a significant and practical challenge in BSS and energy disaggregation: the identifiability of sources. Using interpretable, aggregate domain knowledge to address this is an intuitive and sensible approach.
2.  **Sound Formulation:** The translation of the domain knowledge into a quadratic penalty term within the MAP objective is straightforward and principled. The subsequent relaxation into a CQP is a standard and methodologically sound technique for making this type of problem tractable, building upon similar approaches used in prior work like AFAMAP [12].
3.  **Strong Empirical Results:** The experimental evaluation shows a clear and substantial performance improvement on a real-world task. The reported 29% and 33% decrease in normalized error compared to AFAMAP [12] on realistic data is significant and demonstrates the practical utility of the proposed constraints. The inclusion of an oracle experiment (using TTC) effectively illustrates the potential of this type of constraint.

**Weaknesses:**
1.  **Limited Originality:** While the application of aggregate constraints to AFHMMs is new, the core ideas are not. The use of regularization to incorporate prior knowledge is a fundamental concept in machine learning. Furthermore, the technical machinery for inference—relaxing the problem to a CQP—is heavily borrowed from the AFAMAP paper [12]. The contribution is thus more of an effective, incremental improvement on an existing framework rather than a fundamentally new approach.
2.  **Methodological Gaps:** The paper omits a critical detail regarding the CQP solution. The relaxation results in state indicator variables `Sitk` that lie in the interval [0, 1], not the discrete set {0, 1}. The paper does not explain how a final, discrete state sequence is recovered from this continuous solution. Is it rounded? Is a Viterbi-like step performed afterward? This lack of detail makes the method not fully reproducible and leaves questions about the theoretical properties of the final inferred sequence.
3.  **Insufficient Exploration of Hyperparameters and Priors:** The authors set the penalty weights `λi = 1` for all experiments. This is an arbitrary choice. A more rigorous evaluation would include a sensitivity analysis of this hyperparameter or discuss how it could be set via cross-validation. The performance of the method is critically dependent on the quality of the prior `µi0`. While the toy experiment shows some robustness, the paper does not sufficiently discuss the challenges of obtaining accurate priors in practice or the sensitivity of the model to errors in these priors in the real-data setting. The use of a national average (NTC) is a reasonable choice, but its limitations for atypical households are not explored.

**Detailed Comments and Questions:**
*   **Quality:** The paper is technically sound up to the CQP formulation. However, the lack of discussion on converting the relaxed solution back to a discrete sequence is a significant omission. The claims are well-supported by the provided experimental results, but the evaluation of the method's sensitivity to its own parameters (`λi`, `µi0`) is superficial.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The core idea is communicated effectively. The main point of confusion, as noted above, is the gap between the CQP solution and the final disaggregated signals.
*   **Originality:** The primary contribution is the specific form of the constraint (aggregate signal value) and its application to the AFHMM model for energy disaggregation. This is a novel combination of existing ideas rather than a groundbreaking new method.
*   **Significance:** The work is most significant for the energy disaggregation community, where it demonstrates a clear path to improving the performance of a popular class of models. The general idea of using aggregate constraints could potentially be useful in other BSS domains, but this is not explored. Given the strong performance improvement, the method is likely to be adopted by researchers in this specific application area.

**Arguments for acceptance:**
*   The paper addresses an important practical problem with an intuitive and effective solution.
*   The experimental results demonstrate a state-of-the-art performance on a challenging, real-world dataset.
*   The method is a solid, incremental contribution that builds upon and improves a well-established modeling framework.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
*   The novelty of the core technique is limited; it is largely an application of standard regularization and inference techniques to a specific problem.
*   The paper fails to describe a crucial step in the methodology (recovering a discrete solution from the CQP relaxation), which harms reproducibility and raises questions about the final output.
*   The experimental analysis lacks rigor concerning hyperparameter sensitivity and the quality of the required prior information.

In conclusion, this paper presents a sensible and effective extension to the AFHMM framework for energy disaggregation. The empirical results are compelling. However, the limited novelty and the missing methodological details detract from the overall quality of the contribution. The work is a good application paper but feels borderline for NIPS, which typically requires a higher degree of methodological or conceptual innovation. If the authors were to clarify the handling of the CQP output and provide a more thorough analysis of the model's sensitivity, the case for acceptance would be considerably stronger.
