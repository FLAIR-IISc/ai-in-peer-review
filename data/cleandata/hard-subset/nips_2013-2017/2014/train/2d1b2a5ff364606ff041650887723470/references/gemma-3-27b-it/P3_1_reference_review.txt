## Review of "Signal Aggregate Constraints in Additive Factorial HMMs, with Application to Energy Disaggregation"

**Summary:**

This paper tackles the challenging problem of blind source separation (BSS), specifically within the context of energy disaggregation. The authors identify the identifiability issues inherent in BSS and propose a novel approach: incorporating Signal Aggregate Constraints (SACs) into an Additive Factorial Hidden Markov Model (AFHMM). The core idea is to leverage prior knowledge about the expected total energy consumption of appliances to guide the model’s inference. They demonstrate a computationally efficient solution via a convex quadratic program relaxation and showcase significant performance gains on real-world energy disaggregation data, outperforming both a standard AFHMM and a state-of-the-art alternative.

**Relation to Previous Work:**

The paper builds on a substantial body of work in energy disaggregation (Hart, 1992; Zoha et al., 2014) and BSS using AFHMMs (Ghahramani & Jordan, 1997). It appropriately situates itself within the broader landscape of incorporating prior knowledge into probabilistic models (Ganchev et al., 2009; Liang et al., 2011).  The authors effectively differentiate their work by focusing on a specific *type* of prior – aggregate constraints – and proposing a tractable solution within the AFHMM framework. They acknowledge related constraints on HMM states (Kolter & Jaakkola, 2012; Kim et al., 2013) but highlight the novelty of their aggregate-level approach. The connection to posterior regularization (Ganchev et al., 2009) is well-articulated, demonstrating a deeper theoretical understanding.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty and Insight:** The introduction of SACs is a compelling idea. Recognizing that aggregate usage patterns are often known a priori is a practical and impactful contribution. The paper elegantly bridges the gap between domain knowledge and probabilistic modeling.
* **Technical Soundness:** The derivation of the convex quadratic program for the relaxed optimization problem is well-explained and appears mathematically sound.  The use of CVX for solving the program is appropriate.
* **Empirical Validation:** The experimental results on the HES dataset are convincing. A 45% reduction in normalized disaggregation error compared to the baseline AFHMM and a 29% improvement over AFAMAP (Kolter & Jaakkola, 2012) are substantial. The ablation study with TTC and NTC provides valuable insight into the sensitivity of the method to the accuracy of the constraints.
* **Clarity and Organization:**  The paper is generally well-written and logically organized. The mathematical notation is clear, and the explanations are accessible.
* **Computational Efficiency:** The authors acknowledge the computational complexity and highlight the fact that their method scales similarly to existing approaches, addressing a practical concern.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Sensitivity to Constraint Values:** While the ablation study using TTC and NTC is informative, it would be valuable to explore the sensitivity of the method to *incorrect* constraint values more systematically.  How does performance degrade as the discrepancy between the assumed aggregate value (µi0) and the true value increases?  A more nuanced analysis of this trade-off would be beneficial.
* **Limited Exploration of Constraint Generalizations:** The authors mention the possibility of using multiple, time-varying constraints (Section 4.2), but this is only briefly discussed.  Exploring the benefits of such constraints, especially in the context of appliance usage patterns (e.g., higher usage during daytime), could further enhance the method.
* **Parameter Tuning:** The paper states that λi was set to 1. While acceptable, some justification for this choice or a demonstration of sensitivity to λi would strengthen the argument. A cross-validation study to tune the regularization parameters would be beneficial.
* **Assumptions of Markov Structure:** The paper relies on the assumption that component signals can be modeled by HMMs.  While common in this field, it's worth acknowledging the limitations of this assumption and briefly discussing potential extensions to more flexible models.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound approach to address a critical challenge in BSS and energy disaggregation.
* The empirical results demonstrate a significant improvement in performance on a real-world dataset.
* The method is computationally efficient and scalable.
* The paper is well-written and clearly explains the technical details.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The sensitivity analysis regarding the accuracy of the aggregate constraints is limited.
* The potential for more complex constraint structures (time-varying, multiple constraints) is not fully explored.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with a clear contribution to the field. The idea of incorporating signal aggregate constraints is insightful and effectively addresses the identifiability problem in BSS. The technical execution is solid, and the empirical results are compelling. While there are minor areas for improvement, the strengths of the paper far outweigh the weaknesses.

**Recommendation:**

**Strong Accept.** I believe this paper is a valuable addition to the NIPS program and has the potential to influence future research in energy disaggregation and related areas. I encourage the authors to consider addressing the minor weaknesses identified in the review during the revision process.
