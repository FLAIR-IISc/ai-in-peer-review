## Peer Review: Signal Aggregate Constraints in Additive Factorial HMMs, with Application to Energy Disaggregation

**Summary:**

This paper introduces Signal Aggregate Constraints (SACs) as a method to improve the identifiability and performance of Additive Factorial Hidden Markov Models (AFHMMs), a common approach for blind source separation, specifically in the context of energy disaggregation. The authors observe that in many BSS applications, prior knowledge exists regarding the total expected values of source signals. They incorporate this knowledge by adding constraints to the AFHMM objective function, encouraging the aggregate values of the source signals to be close to specified values. A relaxed optimization problem is formulated and solved efficiently using a convex quadratic program. The proposed AFHMM+SAC is evaluated on both a toy dataset and real-world energy disaggregation data, demonstrating significant performance improvements over the baseline AFHMM and a recent state-of-the-art disaggregation approach.

**Relation to Previous Work:**

The paper correctly situates itself within the broader context of Blind Source Separation and the more specific field of Non-Intrusive Load Monitoring (NILM) / energy disaggregation. It acknowledges relevant work on AFHMMs and improvements to the model through state constraints (e.g., AFAMAP, semi-Markov models). The connection to posterior regularization and generalized expectation criteria is also well-established.  The authors distinguish their contribution by focusing on *what* types of prior knowledge to incorporate, rather than just *how* to incorporate it – a valuable distinction. The referencing is generally good, covering key papers in the field.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty & Insightful Observation:** The idea of leveraging aggregate signal constraints is a novel and intuitively appealing way to address the inherent identifiability issues in BSS, particularly in energy disaggregation where appliance-level consumption estimates are crucial for practical applications. The observation that aggregate values are often known *a priori* is a strong motivation.
* **Technical Soundness:** The formulation of the relaxed optimization problem and its conversion to a convex quadratic program appears technically sound. The use of CVX for solving the problem is a practical choice.  The connection to posterior regularization provides a theoretical grounding for the proposed approach.
* **Strong Empirical Results:** The results on both the toy data and the real-world energy disaggregation data are compelling. A 45% decrease in normalized disaggregation error compared to the original AFHMM, and a 29% improvement over a recent state-of-the-art method, are substantial gains.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is well-written and clearly organized. The mathematical notation is mostly accessible, and the explanations are generally understandable.
* **Computational Efficiency Discussion:** The authors acknowledge and discuss the computational complexity, noting that the CQP scales polynomially and that their implementation compares favorably to other methods in terms of runtime.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Sensitivity Analysis:** While the toy data experiments demonstrate the effect of constraint accuracy, a more comprehensive sensitivity analysis of the regularization parameter (λ) would be beneficial. How does performance vary with different values of λ, and is there a systematic way to choose an optimal value?  Cross-validation is mentioned but not detailed.
* **Real-World Data Detail:** The description of the real-world dataset (HES) is somewhat brief. More details about the specific households used, the appliances monitored, and the data preprocessing steps would be helpful for reproducibility.
* **Constraint Generalization:** The discussion of generalizing the constraints to multiple time periods is promising, but remains relatively brief.  Exploring the performance of this more flexible constraint structure would strengthen the paper.
* **Limited Comparison:** While the comparison to AFAMAP is valuable, including a broader range of state-of-the-art NILM algorithms (e.g., those based on deep learning) would provide a more comprehensive benchmark.
* **Identifiability Discussion:** The paper acknowledges the identifiability problem but doesn't deeply explore its implications within the SAC framework.  Are there specific scenarios where the constraints *exacerbate* identifiability issues, or are there theoretical guarantees regarding improved identifiability?



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* Addresses a significant challenge in BSS/NILM – improving identifiability.
* Proposes a novel and well-motivated approach (SACs).
* Provides strong empirical evidence of the effectiveness of the method.
* Technically sound with a practical and efficient implementation.
* Clearly written and well-organized.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* Sensitivity analysis of regularization parameter is limited.
* Lack of detailed data description and comparison to a wider range of benchmarks.
* Limited exploration of constraint generalization.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of blind source separation and energy disaggregation. The proposed SACs are a clever and effective way to incorporate prior knowledge and improve the performance of AFHMMs. While there are some areas for improvement (sensitivity analysis, data details, broader comparison), the strengths of the paper outweigh the weaknesses. 

**Recommendation:**

**Accept with Minor Revisions.**  I recommend accepting this paper with a request for the authors to:

1.  Expand the sensitivity analysis of the regularization parameter (λ).
2.  Provide more details about the real-world dataset and preprocessing steps.
3.  Consider adding a brief exploration of the generalized constraint structure described in Section 4.2.
4.  Discuss any potential limitations or scenarios where the constraints might not be beneficial.
