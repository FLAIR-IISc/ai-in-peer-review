### Summary of the Paper

This paper addresses the problem of blind source separation (BSS) in underdetermined systems, focusing on the application of energy disaggregation. The core challenge in this domain is the unidentifiability of the separated source signals, which makes interpretation difficult. The authors propose incorporating domain knowledge through a novel class of constraints, termed Signal Aggregate Constraints (SACs), into the Additive Factorial Hidden Markov Model (AFHMM), a common model for this task.

The central idea is that for many BSS problems, one has prior knowledge about the expected total aggregate value of each source signal over a given time period (e.g., the expected total daily energy consumption of a specific appliance). The proposed AFHMM+SAC model incorporates this knowledge by adding a quadratic penalty term to the MAP inference objective, which penalizes deviations of the inferred signal aggregates from their known prior values.

Because these aggregate constraints are non-Markovian, standard inference techniques like the Viterbi algorithm are not applicable. To overcome this, the authors formulate MAP inference as an integer quadratic program (IQP). They then relax the integer constraints on the state indicator variables, transforming the problem into a convex quadratic program (CQP) that can be solved efficiently. The paper also establishes a theoretical connection, showing that the penalized objective function can be derived within the posterior regularization framework (Ganchev et al., 2010).

The proposed method is evaluated on both synthetic data and a real-world energy disaggregation dataset (HES). The results demonstrate that AFHMM+SAC significantly outperforms both the standard AFHMM and a recent state-of-the-art method, AFAMAP (Kolter & Jaakkola, 2012), achieving a substantial reduction in disaggregation error with comparable computational cost.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound.
*   **Strengths:** The derivation of the CQP for the relaxed inference problem is clear and correct. Grounding the penalized objective function in the posterior regularization framework provides a solid theoretical justification for the approach. The experimental evaluation is a major strength of this work. It is comprehensive, including a synthetic experiment to demonstrate robustness to misspecified priors and two experiments on a challenging, low-frequency real-world dataset. The comparison against a strong baseline (AFAMAP) and the inclusion of an oracle experiment (using true total consumption as the prior) effectively demonstrate both the practical utility and the upper-bound potential of the proposed constraints. Claims of improvement are well-supported by quantitative metrics and statistical significance tests.
*   **Weaknesses:** The treatment of the regularization hyperparameter λi is brief. The authors state that λi is set to 1 for all experiments. While this simplifies the experiments, it leaves open questions about the sensitivity of the model to this parameter. A discussion on how λi might be set in practice (e.g., via cross-validation) or a sensitivity analysis would strengthen the paper's contribution. Additionally, the paper does not explicitly detail how the continuous solution of the CQP (where state indicator variables are in [0, 1]) is converted into the final discrete state assignments used for calculating the evaluation metrics. This is a minor but important detail for reproducibility.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized.
*   **Strengths:** The motivation for the problem and the proposed solution is laid out very clearly in the introduction. The model and inference algorithm are described with sufficient mathematical detail and clear notation, making the technical contribution easy to follow. The experimental setup and results are presented logically and are well-supported by clear tables and figures. The paper successfully communicates a complex idea in a straightforward manner.

**Originality:** The work presents a novel contribution to the field.
*   **Strengths:** The primary contribution—the concept of using signal aggregate constraints to improve identifiability in AFHMMs—is novel. While other forms of constraints have been explored for AFHMMs, such as the single-state-change constraint in AFAMAP, the proposed global, summary-based constraint is a new and distinct idea. The formulation of this specific problem as a CQP is a novel application of existing optimization techniques. The paper effectively situates this contribution within the context of prior work on both energy disaggregation and general probabilistic modeling with constraints.

**Significance:** The results presented are important and the ideas are likely to be influential.
*   **Strengths:** The paper addresses a significant and practical problem with broad relevance. The application to energy disaggregation is timely, and the substantial performance improvements (e.g., a 29% reduction in error over a state-of-the-art method) represent a demonstrable advance. The core idea of SACs is general and could be readily applied to other BSS domains where similar aggregate prior knowledge is available (e.g., computational biology, audio processing). The method's computational efficiency is comparable to existing approaches, making it a practical tool for researchers and practitioners.

### Arguments for and against acceptance

**Arguments for acceptance:**
1.  **Novelty and Intuition:** The paper introduces a novel, intuitive, and general type of constraint (SACs) for addressing the key challenge of identifiability in AFHMMs.
2.  **Technical Soundness:** The proposed inference method via CQP relaxation is technically sound and is theoretically justified through a connection to the posterior regularization framework.
3.  **Strong Empirical Results:** The method is shown to provide significant, statistically validated improvements over strong baselines on a challenging real-world task, demonstrating a clear advance in the state of the art.
4.  **Clarity and Readability:** The paper is very well-written, making its contributions accessible and easy to understand.
5.  **Potential for Impact:** The ideas are generalizable beyond the specific application domain and could inspire further work on incorporating diverse forms of prior knowledge into probabilistic models.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
1.  **Hyperparameter Treatment:** The method's sensitivity to the key hyperparameter λ is not explored, and a fixed, arbitrary value is used throughout the experiments.
2.  **Missing Implementation Detail:** The paper omits the step of converting the relaxed CQP solution back to a discrete sequence for evaluation, which is a minor gap in the description of the methodology.

Overall, the arguments for acceptance strongly outweigh the arguments against. The weaknesses are minor and could be addressed in a final version of the paper. The core contribution is a novel, well-motivated, and empirically validated method for a significant problem.
