### Summary of the Paper

This paper addresses the problem of blind source separation (BSS), with a specific focus on energy disaggregation. The authors identify the core challenge in this domain as the identifiability of the separated sources, where unconstrained models can produce "franken-appliance" signals that are physically meaningless. To address this, they propose a novel type of constraint for Additive Factorial Hidden Markov Models (AFHMMs), a standard model for this task. The proposed "Signal Aggregate Constraints" (SACs) enforce that the total sum of a source signal over a time window should be close to a known prior value (e.g., the average daily energy consumption of a specific appliance).

Since these constraints are non-Markovian and complicate inference, the authors formulate the MAP inference problem as a penalized objective function. To solve this, they relax the integer state variables to be continuous within [0, 1] and derive a Convex Quadratic Program (CQP), which can be solved efficiently. The authors also show a connection between their penalized objective and the posterior regularization framework. They evaluate their model, AFHMM+SAC, on a challenging, low-frequency real-world energy dataset (HES), demonstrating significant improvements in disaggregation accuracy over the standard AFHMM and a recent state-of-the-art method, AFAMAP.

### Strengths

1.  **Novel and Intuitive Contribution:** The core idea of using signal aggregate constraints is simple, intuitive, and highly relevant to the problem domain. While other forms of constraints for AFHMMs exist (e.g., AFAMAP's sparsity-of-change constraint), SACs leverage a different and readily available type of domain knowledge. This is a valuable contribution that directly tackles the critical identifiability problem in a practical manner.

2.  **Sound Technical Approach:** The methodology is well-founded. Recognizing that the non-Markovian SACs break dynamic programming, the authors' choice to formulate the problem as a relaxed CQP is an elegant and standard technique for this class of models (similar to the approach in Kolter & Jaakkola, 2012). This makes the proposed model computationally tractable and practical, with inference times shown to be comparable to existing methods.

3.  **Thorough and Convincing Experimental Evaluation:** The experimental section is a major strength.
    *   The use of the HES dataset is commendable, as its low sampling rate (2-10 minutes) represents a more challenging and realistic scenario than the high-frequency datasets often used in prior work.
    *   The comparison against both a standard baseline (AFHMM) and a relevant state-of-the-art method (AFAMAP) is appropriate and provides strong evidence for the proposed method's efficacy.
    *   The inclusion of an "oracle" experiment using the true total consumption (TTC) is excellent scientific practice. It effectively demonstrates the maximum potential of the SAC approach and validates that the constraint, when accurate, is indeed highly beneficial.
    *   The reported results, showing a 45% error reduction over AFHMM and a 29% reduction over AFAMAP on the most realistic task (with mains readings), are significant and clearly advance the state of the art in this application domain.

4.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is very well-written and easy to follow. The motivation is clearly established, the model is built logically upon the AFHMM, and the experimental design and results are presented transparently.

### Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement

1.  **Sensitivity to Hyperparameters:** The paper states that the penalty parameter `λi` was simply set to 1 for all experiments. While this simplifies the experiments, it leaves open the question of the model's sensitivity to this crucial hyperparameter. A sensitivity analysis, even on the toy dataset, showing how performance varies with `λ`, would significantly strengthen the paper. A brief discussion on how one might set this parameter in practice (e.g., via cross-validation) would also be beneficial.

2.  **Impact of Inaccurate Priors:** The toy data experiment shows that performance degrades as the prior `µi0` becomes less accurate, which is expected. However, this could be explored more deeply in the context of the real-world data. For instance, how does the model perform if an appliance's prior is taken from a different demographic, or if a "heavy user" household is given a "light user" prior? This would provide valuable insight into the robustness of the method for real-world deployment where priors may not be perfectly matched.

3.  **Discussion of the Relaxation:** The CQP solves a relaxed version of the original integer programming problem. The paper does not discuss the implications of this relaxation. For example, are the solutions to the CQP often integral, or do they require rounding? A brief comment on the nature of the relaxed solutions and whether this relaxation gap poses a practical problem would add technical depth.

4.  **Generalizability of SACs:** While the paper mentions that SACs are general, the discussion and evaluation are exclusively focused on energy disaggregation. To bolster the claim of generality, it would be helpful to briefly elaborate on how SACs could be applied in other BSS domains mentioned in the introduction, such as audio processing (e.g., constraining the total speaking time of a specific person in a mixed recording) or computational biology.

### Overall Assessment

This is a high-quality paper that presents a clear, novel, and effective contribution to the field of blind source separation and its application to energy disaggregation. The proposed Signal Aggregate Constraints provide a powerful and practical way to incorporate domain knowledge to overcome the key challenge of source identifiability. The technical approach is sound, and the experimental results are comprehensive and compelling, demonstrating a significant improvement over prior state-of-the-art methods on a challenging real-world dataset. The weaknesses identified are relatively minor and represent opportunities for future work rather than fundamental flaws in the current contribution. The paper is well-written, scientifically rigorous, and addresses a problem of significant practical importance. I strongly recommend acceptance.
