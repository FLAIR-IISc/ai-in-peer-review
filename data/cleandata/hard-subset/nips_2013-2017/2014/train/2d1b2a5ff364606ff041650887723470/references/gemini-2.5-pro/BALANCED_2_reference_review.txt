This paper addresses the problem of blind source separation (BSS) in the context of Additive Factorial Hidden Markov Models (AFHMMs). The authors correctly identify that a key challenge in applying AFHMMs to real-world problems like energy disaggregation is the inherent non-identifiability of the source signals, which makes the disaggregated components difficult to interpret. To address this, the paper introduces a novel form of prior knowledge called Signal Aggregate Constraints (SACs). These constraints encourage the sum of each source signal over a time period to be close to a known, expected value (e.g., the typical daily consumption of an appliance).

The authors formulate the maximum a posteriori (MAP) inference problem in an AFHMM with these SACs as a penalized optimization problem. Since the SACs are non-Markovian and break the standard dynamic programming structure, direct inference with algorithms like Viterbi is not possible. The main technical contribution is to show that a relaxed version of this integer programming problem can be cast as a Convex Quadratic Program (CQP), which can be solved efficiently. The paper also provides a nice connection to the posterior regularization framework [Ganchev et al., 2010], showing that their penalized objective corresponds to finding the MAP solution of a KL-regularized posterior.

The proposed AFHMM+SAC model is evaluated on a real-world energy disaggregation dataset and compared against the standard AFHMM and a strong, recent baseline, AFAMAP [Kolter & Jaakkola, 2012]. The results demonstrate a significant improvement in disaggregation accuracy, validating the utility of the proposed constraints.

### Strengths

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The core idea of using signal aggregate constraints is novel, intuitive, and highly relevant. While incorporating prior knowledge into latent variable models is a well-established research direction, the specific form of the constraint proposed here is well-suited for many BSS applications, particularly energy disaggregation, where such aggregate statistics are often available from surveys or domain expertise. This work provides a practical and effective way to tackle the critical identifiability problem in AFHMMs, which significantly enhances the applicability of these models.

2.  **Technical Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The formulation of the MAP inference problem with SACs is clear, and the relaxation into a CQP is an elegant and effective solution to the otherwise intractable inference problem. Grounding the approach in the established framework of posterior regularization adds theoretical weight to the proposed method.

3.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is well-written, clearly motivated, and logically structured. The authors do an excellent job of setting up the problem, explaining their solution, and presenting their results. The notation in Section 5 is dense, but the derivation is followable.

4.  **Thorough Empirical Evaluation:** The experimental validation is a key strength. The authors compare their method against appropriate and strong baselines on a challenging, low-frequency real-world dataset. The reported improvements are substantial (e.g., a 29% reduction in error compared to the state-of-the-art AFAMAP). The inclusion of an "oracle" experiment (using the true total consumption) effectively demonstrates the potential of the approach, while the main results using nationally averaged consumption data show its practical utility. The analysis on data both with and without unmodeled "mains" consumption adds to the robustness of the conclusions.

### Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement

1.  **Handling of the Regularization Hyperparameter (λ):** The paper states that λi was simply set to 1 for all experiments. The performance of any regularized model is typically sensitive to the choice of the regularization parameter. This simplification is a significant weakness, as it is unclear how the model would perform with different values of λ or how a user would choose an appropriate value in practice. The reported results may not be representative of the performance one could expect without careful tuning.
    *   **Suggestion:** The authors should include a sensitivity analysis for λ on a subset of the data. This would demonstrate how robust the performance is to this choice and provide guidance on how to set it (e.g., via cross-validation on a held-out set).

2.  **Computational Scalability:** The paper claims that the proposed method scales similarly to the baselines in practice, but solving a general CQP is typically more computationally intensive than the specialized inference algorithms used for AFHMMs and AFAMAP. The experiments are performed on single-day time series. It would be valuable to understand how the method's runtime scales to longer periods (e.g., weeks or months), as the number of variables in the CQP grows linearly with the time series length T.
    *   **Suggestion:** A more formal discussion of the computational complexity and empirical runtime plots as a function of the time series length (T) and the number of appliances (I) would strengthen the paper and clarify the practical limitations of the approach.

3.  **Generalization of Constraints:** The authors briefly mention in Section 4.2 that the SACs could be generalized to multiple constraints over different time windows (e.g., day vs. night). This is a powerful and interesting extension that is highly relevant to the energy domain. However, it is only mentioned in passing and not explored.
    *   **Suggestion:** While a full evaluation is not expected, elaborating on this generalization could significantly increase the perceived impact of the work. Discussing how multiple, overlapping constraints would be incorporated into the CQP framework would be a valuable addition.

4.  **Relationship to Other Constraints:** The comparison with AFAMAP is excellent. However, the constraint in AFAMAP (at most one state change at a time) and the SACs proposed here seem to capture orthogonal types of prior knowledge. It would be interesting to know if these constraints are complementary.
    *   **Suggestion:** A brief discussion on the possibility of combining SACs with other structural constraints (like those in AFAMAP) within the same CQP framework would be a nice addition, potentially pointing towards a promising direction for future work.

### Summary

**Arguments for acceptance:**
*   Proposes a novel, intuitive, and significant type of constraint (SACs) for AFHMMs.
*   Effectively addresses the critical problem of source identifiability in BSS.
*   Presents a technically sound and elegant inference method based on a CQP relaxation.
*   Demonstrates state-of-the-art performance with substantial improvements over strong baselines on a challenging real-world task.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
*   The treatment of the key regularization hyperparameter λ is overly simplistic and lacks a proper sensitivity analysis.
*   Potential scalability concerns for long time series are not fully addressed.

Overall, this is a high-quality paper presenting a solid and valuable contribution. The proposed idea is elegant, the technical execution is sound, and the empirical results are convincing. The weaknesses, while important, are addressable and do not undermine the core contribution of the paper. This work is likely to be of interest to researchers in probabilistic modeling and its applications, and it advances the state of the art in energy disaggregation. I recommend acceptance.
