- Introduces Signal Aggregate Constraints (SACs) for AFHMMs as quadratic penalties on per-chain aggregates and optimizes the MAP objective via a convex quadratic program after relaxing binaries and adding transition variables.  
- Frames the SAC formulation as equivalent to posterior regularization (log prior/regularizer).  
- Empirical results: AFHMM+SAC yields 29–45% reductions in NDE and improved SAE on UK HES and a toy dataset versus AFHMM and AFAMAP.  
- Technical soundness: relaxation and convexity arguments are largely valid, showing a concave quadratic in S and linear in H under linear constraints.  
- Missing clarity on post-processing: unclear whether authors output continuous estimates or round to discrete states and how rounding affects SAC feasibility.  
- Hyperparameter omission: λi fixed to 1 with no cross-validation or sensitivity analysis, despite λ controlling SAC strength.  
- Robustness gap: limited evaluation of sensitivity to μ0 misspecification on real data (only qualitative toy results).  
- Baselines limited: should compare to duration-constrained FHMMs/HSMMs and other recent NILM methods for broader context.  
- Computational claims need substantiation: “similar runtime” to AFHMM/AFAMAP is surprising for a generic CQP; request precise timing, problem sizes (I, T, Ki), solver tolerances, and memory.  
- Data/setting concern: unclear how SACs handle unmodeled mains/devices and whether modeled appliances absorb unknown loads (bias risk).  
- Theory/scope shortfall: no analysis of the relaxation gap or conditions guaranteeing integral solutions; empirical integrality rates would help.  
- Clarity: generally well organized but heavy notation in Sec.5; missing practical details (how μi, πi, transitions estimated, constraints on Sit, exact post-processing); suggest pseudo-code.  
- Originality: moderately novel—applying aggregate-sum constraints to AFHMMs and deriving a tailored CQP is a useful, nontrivial contribution.  
- Significance: directly relevant to NILM practitioners and potentially generalizable to other BSS domains where aggregate priors exist; reliance on credible priors is both strength and limitation.  
- Pros: addresses identifiability with practical priors, elegant convex relaxation, statistically significant gains on large realistic data, feasible for moderate problem sizes.  
- Cons: limited hyperparameter/robustness analysis, sparse solver/scaling details, assumption of reasonable μ0 priors, narrower baseline comparisons.  
- Concrete suggestions: report integrality/rounding procedure and rates, provide λ selection and sensitivity plots, test μ0 bias effects, add HSMM/duration baselines, report solver settings and runtimes, evaluate multi-interval SACs, and consider a slack/background chain for unknown loads.  
- Overall assessment: solid, well-motivated paper with convincing empirical benefits; would be stronger with more analysis of hyperparameters, robustness, and implementation details.
