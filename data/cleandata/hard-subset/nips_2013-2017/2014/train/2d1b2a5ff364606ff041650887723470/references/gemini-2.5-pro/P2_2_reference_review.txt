### Summary of the Paper

This paper addresses the problem of blind source separation (BSS), specifically in the context of a single aggregated time series, with a primary application in energy disaggregation. The authors correctly identify that a key challenge in this underdetermined problem is the lack of identifiability, which leads to solutions that are difficult for end-users to interpret (the "franken-appliance" problem). To mitigate this, the paper introduces Signal Aggregate Constraints (SACs), a novel form of prior knowledge that constrains the total value of each source signal over a given time period to be close to a predefined target.

The authors incorporate these constraints into the Additive Factorial Hidden Markov Model (AFHMM), a standard model for this task. As the SACs are non-Markovian and render exact MAP inference via dynamic programming intractable, the authors propose relaxing the integer optimization problem into a Convex Quadratic Program (CQP), which can be solved efficiently. The paper also demonstrates a principled connection between their penalized objective function and the posterior regularization framework. The proposed model, AFHMM+SAC, is evaluated on both synthetic data and a real-world energy disaggregation dataset (HES), showing significant performance improvements over the standard AFHMM and a state-of-the-art competitor, AFAMAP.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Quality:** The paper is of high technical quality.

*   **Soundness:** The proposed method is technically sound. The formulation of the problem, the introduction of the quadratic penalty term for the SAC, and the relaxation to a CQP are all well-established and appropriate techniques for this class of problem. The authors demonstrate a good understanding of the underlying optimization challenges.
*   **Completeness:** The work feels complete. It introduces a novel idea, provides a sound algorithmic solution, connects it to existing theoretical frameworks (posterior regularization), and validates it with a thorough empirical study.
*   **Evaluation:** The experimental evaluation is a major strength.
    *   The use of a toy dataset effectively demonstrates the mechanism and robustness of the SAC regularizer.
    *   The experiments on the real-world HES dataset are convincing. The comparison against both a standard baseline (AFHMM) and a strong, recent state-of-the-art method (AFAMAP) is appropriate.
    *   The inclusion of an "oracle" experiment using the True Total Consumption (TTC) is very insightful, as it establishes an upper bound on the potential benefit of the proposed constraints.
    *   The evaluation on data both with and without measured mains readings tests the method in both a controlled and a realistic, more challenging scenario. The reported performance gains (e.g., a 29% NDE improvement over AFAMAP on real mains data) are substantial.
    *   The authors are careful to report on computational time, showing their method does not introduce a significant overhead compared to related approaches.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized.

*   The motivation is clearly established in the introduction with the intuitive "franken-appliance" example.
*   The progression from the basic AFHMM model to the proposed AFHMM+SAC is logical and easy to follow.
*   The mathematical notation, while dense in Section 5, is clearly defined and standard for the field.
*   The description of the experiments and the presentation of results in tables and figures are clear and support the paper's claims effectively.

**Originality:** The paper presents a clear, original contribution.

*   While AFHMMs and their variants are well-studied, the specific idea of using *global aggregate constraints* on the source signals is novel in this context. It is a simple but powerful idea that differs meaningfully from prior work that constrains local state transitions (AFAMAP) or state durations (semi-Markov models).
*   The problem of energy disaggregation is not new, but the paper provides a new and effective tool for it. The application of this type of constraint seems particularly well-suited for this domain, where good priors on total consumption are often available from national surveys or historical data.
*   The work is well-situated within the existing literature, with clear citations and comparisons to related methods.

**Significance:** The work is significant and likely to have an impact.

*   **Practical Impact:** The proposed SACs offer a practical and intuitive way to incorporate domain knowledge into a difficult BSS problem. Given the substantial performance gains on the important application of energy disaggregation, practitioners in this field are likely to adopt or build upon this method. The constraint itself is general and could be applied to other BSS domains (e.g., transcriptomics, audio) where similar aggregate priors exist.
*   **Research Impact:** The paper advances the state of the art in energy disaggregation. The success of this global, non-Markovian constraint may encourage other researchers to explore similar types of structural priors that move beyond the limitations of traditional Markovian assumptions.
*   The results on low-frequency data (2-10 minutes) are particularly important, as this is the type of data most commonly available from deployed smart meters, making the work highly relevant to real-world systems.

### Suggestions for Improvement

While the paper is strong, a few points could be clarified or strengthened:

1.  **Hyperparameter Choice:** The paper sets the regularization parameter `λi = 1` for all experiments. While this simplifies the evaluation, a brief discussion on the sensitivity of the model to this parameter would be valuable. How would one set `λi` in practice (e.g., cross-validation)? A sensitivity analysis, perhaps in an appendix, would make the work more complete.
2.  **Relaxation Quality:** The CQP solves a relaxed problem where the state indicator variables `Sitk` are in [0, 1]. The paper does not mention how the final discrete state sequence is obtained from this continuous solution (presumably by rounding or taking the argmax). It would also be interesting to know how "integral" the solutions to the CQP are in practice. If the solutions are naturally close to {0, 1}, it strengthens the case for the relaxation being a tight approximation.
3.  **Generalization of SACs:** In Section 4.2, the authors briefly mention a generalization to multiple constraints over different time windows (e.g., day vs. night). This is a compelling idea. While a full exploration is beyond the scope of this paper, briefly re-emphasizing this potential in the conclusion could further highlight the flexibility and significance of the proposed framework.

### Recommendation: **Accept**

This is a strong paper that presents a novel, intuitive, and effective method for incorporating domain knowledge into AFHMMs. The contributions are clear: the introduction of Signal Aggregate Constraints, an efficient CQP-based inference algorithm, and a thorough empirical validation on a challenging and important real-world problem. The paper is well-written, technically sound, and demonstrates a significant improvement over the state of the art. The weaknesses are minor and do not detract from the core contribution. This work is a solid addition to the literature on probabilistic modeling and its applications and is well-suited for NIPS.
