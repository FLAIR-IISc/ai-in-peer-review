- Paper introduces signal aggregate constraints (SACs) for AFHMMs and derives a convex quadratic program (CQP) for relaxed MAP inference using edge variables.
- SACs are soft quadratic penalties encouraging each latent source's aggregate energy to match known/estimated values.
- The quadratic objective is concave (negative semidefinite) so the relaxed MAP becomes a concave maximization over linear constraints.
- Authors connect the formulation to posterior regularization with second-moment constraints.
- Empirical results on a toy dataset and UK HES low-frequency energy data show improved NDE and SAE over AFHMM and AFAMAP, with similar reported runtimes.
- SACs are a simple, general prior that can leverage widely available aggregate knowledge and extend naturally to multiple windows.
- Convexity and the use of the HMM flow polytope suggest the relaxation is likely tight; this point should be made explicit.
- The PR connection is clean and situates the method in a principled framework.
- Results demonstrate practical gains on challenging low-frequency NILM data, including significance testing.
- The paper omits discussion of how fractional S and H are rounded to discrete sequences or whether the relaxation is provably integral.
- Sensitivity to misspecification of µ0 is underexplored; only limited real-data sensitivity analysis is provided.
- Robustness to errors in µ0, miscalibrated λi (uniform λi=1), and the fixed 3-state assumption per appliance is not thoroughly evaluated.
- Baseline fairness/hyperparameter tuning is unclear—AFAMAP’s switching penalty and SAC λi may not have been comparably tuned.
- Use of national totals (NTC) estimated from the same dataset risks optimistic bias; external or disjoint-household µ0 evaluations are needed.
- SACs may absorb unmodeled loads in mains data, biasing modeled appliance estimates; residual and per-appliance bias analyses are missing.
- Scalability concerns: reliance on CVX/SDPT3/SeDuMi may not scale to long sequences; custom first-order solvers exploiting block structure are suggested.
- Runtime claims need concrete per-instance timings, sequence lengths, solver settings, and convergence criteria.
- Presentation could be clearer: derivations are compressed, notation dense, and the concavity/integrality arguments should be sketched explicitly.
- Overall technically sound with a plausible convexity argument, but the integrality/rounding gap weakens the claim.
- The aggregate-constraint idea for FHMMs is a novel and practically motivated contribution distinct from AFAMAP and duration constraints.
- Significance is moderate-to-high for NILM and other BSS applications where aggregate priors exist.
- Pros: practical prior improving identifiability, convex relaxation with likely tightness, PR interpretation, empirical gains.
- Cons: missing integrality discussion, limited hyperparameter/robustness analysis, potential µ0 bias, and scalability/evidence gaps.
- Suggested improvements: prove/argue relaxation tightness or provide rounding guarantees, run sensitivity sweeps over λi and µ0 (consider interval/hierarchical priors), clarify and tune baselines, report solver timings/settings, and analyze residuals/unmodeled loads.
- Recommendation: Weak accept — promising, well-motivated work that needs methodological and experimental clarifications.
