

# Review of "Signal Aggregate Constraints in Additive Factorial HMMs, with Application to Energy Disaggregation"

This paper proposes "Signal Aggregate Constraints" (SACs) for additive factorial hidden Markov models (AFHMMs) to address the identifiability problem in blind source separation, specifically for energy disaggregation. The authors argue that by constraining the aggregate values of source signals (e.g., total energy consumption per appliance) to be close to known values, they can improve the interpretability of results. They formulate SACs as a convex quadratic program and demonstrate improvements over baseline methods on energy disaggregation data.

**Strengths:**

The paper is well-organized and clearly written. The authors successfully demonstrate that SACs can be derived from the posterior regularization framework, providing a theoretical foundation for their approach. The experimental results on energy disaggregation data show consistent improvements over AFHMM and AFAMAP, with claimed 45% and 29% reductions in normalized disaggregation error compared to the baselines. The computational efficiency of their approach is also well-documented, with similar runtime to existing methods.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Lack of Novelty:** The core idea of constraining aggregate values is not particularly novel. The authors cite related work on posterior regularization and other constraints, but fail to convincingly distinguish SACs from existing approaches. For instance, the constraint on the sum of the time series is conceptually straightforward and similar to other domain knowledge incorporation methods. The paper would have been stronger if it had clearly articulated why this specific constraint is superior to alternatives.

2. **Insufficient Validation:** While the paper demonstrates improvements on one dataset (HES), the evaluation lacks breadth. The comparison is limited to AFHMM and AFAMAP, without comparison to other state-of-the-art methods in energy disaggregation. The paper claims to have a "significant improvement" (29%) over a recent state-of-the-art approach [12], but doesn't provide enough detail to verify this claim or assess its statistical significance beyond the paired t-test mentioned.

3. **Limited Scope of Validation:** The paper claims SACs are "general across a wide variety of domains," but only evaluates on energy disaggregation. Without demonstrating effectiveness in other domains, this claim remains unsubstantiated. The paper fails to provide any evidence that SACs would work well beyond the specific application domain.

4. **Oracle Assumption:** The most impressive results come from using "true total consumption" (TTC) as the constraint value, which is an oracle value that would never be available in practice. The paper acknowledges this but doesn't sufficiently address how to obtain realistic constraint values (like NTC) in practice, or how sensitive the results are to errors in constraint specification.

5. **Insufficient Theoretical Analysis:** The paper claims SACs address the identifiability problem but provides no theoretical analysis of how SACs affect identifiability. The paper also doesn't discuss the limitations of SACs or scenarios where they might fail.

6. **Implementation Details:** The paper mentions using λi = 1 for all experiments without discussing sensitivity analysis or hyperparameter tuning. This is concerning since the performance could be highly dependent on this choice.

**Critical Assessment:**

As a reviewer who prefers established methods and is skeptical of new approaches without thorough validation, I find this paper to be a modest extension of existing work rather than a significant contribution. The core idea of constraining aggregate values is conceptually simple and not sufficiently differentiated from prior work. The experimental validation is limited in scope and lacks sufficient detail to fully assess the claimed improvements.

The paper's main contribution appears to be a computational technique for incorporating a specific type of constraint into AFHMMs, rather than a fundamentally new approach to the blind source separation problem. While the convex quadratic program formulation is technically sound, it doesn't represent a significant methodological advance over existing approaches for handling constraints in HMMs.

The energy disaggregation application is important, but the paper doesn't convincingly demonstrate that SACs are necessary or superior to other constraint-based approaches for this problem. The 45% improvement claim is impressive, but without more detailed comparison to other methods and a clearer understanding of the constraint specification process, it's difficult to assess the true value of the approach.

**Recommendation:**

I recommend rejection of this paper. While the paper is technically sound and the results are positive, the contribution is incremental rather than novel. The paper fails to convincingly demonstrate how SACs differ from and improve upon existing constraint-based approaches. The evaluation lacks breadth and depth, and the claims about the generality of SACs across domains are not substantiated. The paper would benefit from significantly more rigorous comparison to a wider range of baselines and a more thorough analysis of the constraint specification process.

This paper represents a reasonable extension of existing work but doesn't advance the field in a way that meets NIPS' standards for significant contributions. The authors would need to substantially strengthen their claims about novelty, provide more comprehensive experimental validation, and better articulate how SACs differ from prior constraint approaches to make a compelling case for publication.
