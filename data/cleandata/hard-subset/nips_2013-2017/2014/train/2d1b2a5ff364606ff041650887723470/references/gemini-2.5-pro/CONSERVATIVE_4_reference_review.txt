**Review of "Signal Aggregate Constraints in Additive Factorial HMMs, with Application to Energy Disaggregation"**

**Summary**

This paper addresses the problem of identifiability in blind source separation, particularly within the context of energy disaggregation using additive factorial hidden Markov models (AFHMMs). The authors propose incorporating domain knowledge in the form of "signal aggregate constraints" (SACs), which enforce a prior belief that the total sum of each source signal over a given time period should be close to a known value. As this constraint is non-Markovian and complicates inference, the authors formulate the MAP inference problem as a penalized objective function. They then relax the integer constraints on the hidden states and solve the problem efficiently as a convex quadratic program (CQP). The proposed method, AFHMM+SAC, is evaluated on both synthetic data and a real-world energy disaggregation dataset, showing significant improvements over a standard AFHMM and the state-of-the-art AFAMAP model.

**Strengths**

1.  **Problem Motivation and Clarity:** The paper is well-written and clearly motivated. The "franken-appliance" example effectively illustrates the practical importance of the identifiability problem in energy disaggregation. The proposed solution—using prior knowledge about total energy consumption—is intuitive and directly targets this weakness.

2.  **Methodological Soundness:** The technical approach of relaxing the MAP inference problem into a convex quadratic program is sound. This is an established and rigorous way to find approximate solutions to otherwise intractable integer programming problems. The derivation is clear and builds upon related work (e.g., Kolter & Jaakkola, 2012).

3.  **Strong Empirical Evaluation:** The authors conduct a thorough set of experiments. The inclusion of the AFAMAP model as a baseline is commendable, as it represents a strong, recent competitor. The results demonstrate substantial and statistically significant performance gains on a real-world dataset (HES). The use of both synthetic aggregate data (7.2.1) and real mains readings (7.2.2) provides a comprehensive picture of the method's performance under both idealized and realistic conditions. The "oracle" experiment using True Total Consumption (TTC) is a clever way to establish a practical upper bound on the method's potential.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Limited Novelty:** While the application is new and the results are strong, the core technical components are largely established. The AFHMM is a standard model, the idea of incorporating prior knowledge via regularization is common, and the relaxation of MAP inference to a CQP has been seen before in related work (as the paper itself acknowledges). The primary contribution is the specific formulation of the *signal aggregate* constraint and its integration into this framework. While effective, the conceptual novelty is arguably incremental.

2.  **Lack of Hyperparameter Analysis:** A significant weakness is the handling of the complexity parameter `λ`. The authors state, "we simply set λi = 1 for all the experiments." This is a critical methodological omission. The performance of any regularized model is highly sensitive to its regularization parameter. Without a sensitivity analysis or a principled method for setting `λ` (e.g., cross-validation), the reported results are difficult to interpret fully. It is unclear if `λ=1` is coincidentally optimal for this dataset or if the method is robust to this choice. A rigorous evaluation would require demonstrating how performance varies with `λ` and justifying the chosen value.

3.  **Scope of Constraints:** The paper focuses on a single aggregate constraint per source over the entire time series `T`. While the authors mention that this could be extended to multiple time windows (e.g., day vs. night), this is not explored experimentally. This is a missed opportunity, as such constraints could potentially provide a much richer signal to resolve temporal ambiguities, which a single aggregate constraint cannot. The current constraint ensures the total energy is correct, but does little to prevent the model from assigning that energy to the wrong times of the day, a problem the NDE metric would capture but the constraint itself does not address.

4.  **Model Parameter Assumptions:** The experiments assume a fixed number of three states for all appliances, which were estimated via k-means. This simplification is understandable but may not be realistic. It would strengthen the paper to discuss the sensitivity of the results to the number of states or the method used to estimate the emission probabilities `µ_ik`.

**Overall Assessment**

This is a solid piece of engineering with strong empirical results on a practical problem. The approach is methodologically sound, and the paper is well-written. The main contribution lies in demonstrating that a simple, intuitive form of domain knowledge—total energy consumption—can significantly improve a standard BSS model when incorporated through a CQP-based inference scheme.

However, the work is let down by a lack of rigor in its experimental methodology, particularly concerning the arbitrary setting of the hyperparameter `λ`. This oversight calls into question the robustness and generality of the reported improvements. Furthermore, the conceptual novelty is somewhat limited, representing an effective combination of existing techniques rather than a fundamental new approach.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   The paper addresses a significant and practical problem.
*   The proposed method is simple, intuitive, and demonstrably effective.
*   The empirical results show a large and statistically significant improvement over a strong state-of-the-art baseline.
*   The CQP formulation is technically sound and well-executed.

**Arguments for Rejection:**
*   The core contribution is arguably incremental, combining well-known components.
*   The lack of any analysis or principled selection for the crucial hyperparameter `λ` is a serious methodological flaw that weakens the paper's conclusions.
*   The scope of the evaluation could be broader (e.g., exploring different time windows for constraints, sensitivity to model parameters).

**Recommendation:** Borderline Reject

While the performance gains are impressive, the failure to properly address the selection of the hyperparameter `λ` is a significant concern for a NIPS paper, where methodological rigor is paramount. If the authors were to include a thorough sensitivity analysis for `λ` and demonstrate a principled way of setting it (e.g., via a validation set), I would be inclined to raise my score. As it stands, the paper presents a promising idea but lacks the complete validation required for a top-tier conference.
