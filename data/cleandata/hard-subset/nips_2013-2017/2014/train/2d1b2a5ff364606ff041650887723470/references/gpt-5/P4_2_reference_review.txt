Summary and relation to prior work
The paper tackles blind source separation (BSS) for time series in additive factorial HMMs (AFHMMs), with a focus on energy disaggregation. The key idea is to incorporate signal aggregate constraints (SACs): prior information about the total energy (or other aggregate) each source is expected to consume over a period. These constraints are encoded as quadratic penalties on the sum of per-state means over time for each chain. Because these terms are non-Markovian, standard dynamic programming does not apply. The paper derives a convex quadratic program (CQP) by relaxing the binary state variables and introducing auxiliary transition variables; after relaxation, the objective is concave quadratic with linear constraints, solvable efficiently (e.g., via CVX). The authors also show the objective arises from posterior regularization (PR), linking to Ganchev et al. (2010). Experiments on synthetic data and the HES dataset demonstrate improvements over a plain AFHMM and AFAMAP (Kolter & Jaakkola, 2012), with notable reductions in normalized disaggregation error (NDE) and signal aggregate error (SAE). The work relates to prior constraint-based learning frameworks (PR, generalized expectation, measurement-based learning) but instantiates a specific, broadly applicable family of aggregate constraints integrated into AFHMM inference via a convex relaxation. It also complements prior disaggregation constraints (e.g., one-chain-change, semi-Markov duration models, k-segment constraints) by targeting identifiability through totals rather than transitions/durations.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The relaxation and optimization formulation appear correct. With Sitk, hitjk relaxed to [0,1], the objective is a sum of linear terms and negative quadratic terms with PSD matrices (−S^TΛS and −0.5 S_t^T V_t S_t), hence concave; maximizing a concave quadratic over a polytope is a convex optimization problem. The linear “flow” constraints for H tie transitions to states are standard. The PR derivation is consistent: the optimal constrained posterior is proportional to P(S|Y) exp(−∑ λi ϕi), matching the penalized objective.
- Empirical support: Results on HES show consistent gains versus AFHMM and AFAMAP, with reported paired t-tests at 5% significance. The toy study demonstrates sensitivity to mis-specified aggregates.
- Completeness: Some implementation details are missing or implicit. In the relaxed solution, Sitk ∈ [0,1]; the paper should explicitly state how xit is reconstructed (presumably xit = μ_i^T Sit) and whether any rounding is used. Parameter estimation for θ is only partially described (μ via k-means; transitions/initials unspecified). Hyperparameter λi is fixed to 1 without tuning; ablation on λi would strengthen conclusions. Scalability beyond small K (3 states) and modest T is not deeply analyzed; reliance on CVX may limit practical scale, although reported runtimes are comparable to baselines in the tested setting.
- Evaluation breadth: Comparisons to semi-Markov baselines or duration-constrained models would further contextualize gains. For the “with mains” setting, unknown appliances are present; this is realistic, but details on noise variance σt, mismatch handling, and robustness are limited.

Clarity
The paper is generally well organized and readable. The motivation for SACs is clear and grounded in domain knowledge. The optimization derivation is dense; defining all vectorizations and Kronecker products helps but could be streamlined. Minor notational issues (e.g., row/column sum constraints on H) could be clarified. Explicitly stating the reconstruction xit = μ_i^T Sit and how the relaxed S relates to interpretable outputs would improve clarity. Providing algorithmic steps or pseudocode for the CQP setup would aid reproducibility.

Originality
The specific form of aggregate constraints on totals per chain, integrated into AFHMM inference via a convex quadratic relaxation, is novel relative to prior work that focused on transition/duration constraints (AFAMAP, semi-Markov, interleaved FHMMs) or generic PR/GE/measurement-based frameworks. The PR connection is appropriate but the contribution lies in identifying a practically useful constraint family and showing how to make inference tractable in AFHMMs. This is a meaningful, if incremental, advance.

Significance
The approach addresses a central identifiability challenge in BSS when aggregate quantities are available or can be reasonably estimated (e.g., from national statistics). The demonstrated improvements on challenging low-frequency HES data indicate practical value in energy disaggregation, a recognized application area. The idea is general and could transfer to other additive-source domains (audio, biology) where aggregate priors exist. Impact is moderated by reliance on per-source aggregate priors and by scalability considerations; nonetheless, the method offers a useful tool in the disaggregation toolbox.

Strengths
- Clear, domain-motivated constraint class (totals) that addresses identifiability.
- Sound convex relaxation enabling efficient MAP inference without dynamic programming.
- Connection to posterior regularization provides a principled grounding.
- Empirical gains over strong baseline (AFAMAP) on realistic, low-frequency data; statistical significance reported.
- Straightforward to extend to multiple time-window constraints.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Relaxation yields fractional states; explicitly describe reconstruction and implications for interpretability.
- Limited discussion of scalability; consider specialized solvers or problem structure exploitation (block-diagonal Vt, rank-1 quadratics) and report complexity in terms of I, K, T quantitatively.
- Hyperparameter selection (λi) is not explored; include tuning or sensitivity analysis.
- Baseline coverage could include semi-Markov/duration models or k-segment constraints to better position gains.
- Reproducibility: provide more detail on θ estimation (initial/transition probabilities, σt), data preprocessing, and code availability.
- Generalization beyond energy disaggregation is argued but not empirically demonstrated; a small additional domain example would strengthen claims of generality.

Arguments for acceptance
- Addresses a well-known identifiability issue with a principled, practical constraint.
- Provides a convex optimization-based inference framework with clear mathematical grounding.
- Demonstrates consistent and significant empirical improvements on a realistic dataset of interest to the community.

Arguments against acceptance
- Limited methodological novelty beyond instantiating PR with aggregate constraints and a standard convex relaxation.
- Missing details on rounding/reconstruction and parameter estimation reduce reproducibility.
- Scalability and broader baseline comparisons are not thoroughly assessed.

Overall recommendation
The paper offers a technically sound and practically useful addition to AFHMM-based disaggregation by leveraging aggregate constraints, with convincing empirical benefits on realistic data. While the contribution is moderate in novelty and could be strengthened in clarity and evaluation breadth, it meets NeurIPS criteria for quality and significance.
