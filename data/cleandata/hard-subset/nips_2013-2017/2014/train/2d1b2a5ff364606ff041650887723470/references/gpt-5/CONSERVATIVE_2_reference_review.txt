Summary
The paper proposes adding signal aggregate constraints (SACs) to additive factorial HMMs (AFHMMs) for blind source separation, with a focus on energy disaggregation (NILM). The core idea is to encode prior knowledge about per-source totals (e.g., an appliance’s daily Wh) as quadratic penalties on the aggregate of each chain’s state means. To enable tractable inference, the authors introduce auxiliary transition variables and relax the binary constraints to obtain a convex quadratic program (CQP) solvable by CVX. They connect the formulation to posterior regularization. Empirically, they report improved normalized disaggregation error (NDE) and signal aggregate error (SAE) over AFHMM and AFAMAP on a toy dataset and the UK HES dataset (low-frequency), using either “national total consumption” priors or an oracle total.

Relation to prior work
The work sits within constrained inference/posterior regularization (Ganchev et al.), generalized expectation, and measurement-based learning, specifically applied to factorial HMMs for disaggregation (e.g., AFAMAP’s at-most-one-change constraint, HSMMs). The novelty is choosing aggregate signal constraints for BSS and casting AFHMM+SAC inference as a CQP. The paper cites relevant NILM/AFHMM literature and acknowledges related constrained HMMs and HSMMs.

Strengths
- Practical motivation: Aggregate priors are natural in NILM and potentially in other BSS applications.
- Clean formulation: The aggregate penalty integrates neatly with AFHMM via a concave quadratic term; transition terms are linearized via auxiliary variables.
- Posterior regularization connection clarifies the probabilistic interpretation.
- Empirical gains: On HES, reported improvements over AFHMM and AFAMAP in both NDE and SAE; significance tests are provided.
- The extension to multiple time-window constraints is natural and could be impactful in practice.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Reliance on priors: Performance depends critically on the quality of µ0. The toy experiment shows sensitivity to mis-specification, but the real-data sensitivity analysis is limited. Using national averages may vary widely across households and days; robustness across households with atypical usage is not examined.
- Hyperparameter tuning: λi are fixed to 1 throughout. No cross-validation, per-appliance tuning, or ablation to quantify the effect of λ and the strength of regularization.
- Relaxation and rounding: The method solves a relaxed CQP; it is unclear how fractional S/H solutions are converted into discrete states and how rounding impacts performance. No evidence that the relaxation is tight, nor theoretical or empirical analysis of integrality gaps.
- Baselines: Comparisons omit strong duration models (HSMM/Bayesian nonparametric HSMMs) and other contemporary NILM methods that explicitly model appliance durations or change constraints. AFAMAP is included, but broader comparisons would better support state-of-the-art claims.
- Data and potential leakage: For “without mains” experiments, aggregates are synthesized from measured appliances (model well-specified); impact on generalization to real mains is unclear. The NTC prior is computed “across all households”; it should be clarified whether test-house data contributes to the prior and whether there is any leakage. Results on public benchmarks (e.g., REDD) or controlled downsampling would aid comparability.
- Scalability: Using CVX/interior-point solvers for CQPs with variables O(T ΣKi + T ΣKi^2) is potentially costly. The claim of “similar runtime” to AFAMAP needs more rigorous reporting (hardware, solver tolerance, problem sizes) and discussion of scalability to longer horizons, more appliances, and finer time resolutions. CVX is typically not competitive at scale.
- Model realism: Assuming 3 discrete states per appliance and Gaussian observation noise is simplistic for NILM; no exploration of more realistic state counts, ramping, or duration distributions. Parameters are estimated by k-means; there is no joint learning with constraints, which posterior regularization typically supports.
- Clarity/notation: Some notation is heavy and inconsistent; the convexity argument is informal (maximization of a concave quadratic is fine, but the transition from non-convex MAP to convex CQP could be articulated more clearly). Typos and formatting issues in references.

Quality
Technically, the optimization formulation appears sound: the objective is concave in S with PSD quadratic terms and linear in H; constraints are linear after relaxation. However, the absence of a rounding strategy and analysis of relaxation tightness undermines the methodological completeness. The empirical evaluation is suggestive but not comprehensive: limited baselines, hyperparameter handling, and robustness analyses weaken the strength of the claims.

Clarity
Readable but notation-heavy; some derivations and definitions would benefit from clearer exposition and a concrete rounding procedure. Tables/figures referenced are not included here, but the text suggests missing details on run times and experiment settings.

Originality
Moderate. The idea of aggregate constraints is intuitive and, to my knowledge, not standard in AFHMM-based NILM, though conceptually close to posterior regularization and measurement-based learning. Casting AFHMM+SAC as a CQP via auxiliary variables is a reasonable engineering contribution, albeit with standard tools.

Significance
Potentially useful for practitioners who have reliable aggregate priors, especially in domains where totals are known or can be well-estimated. However, without stronger evidence of robustness, scalability, and comparisons to duration-aware models, impact is uncertain.

Questions for authors
- How are fractional S/H solutions mapped to discrete states? Does this post-processing affect NDE/SAE, and how often are solutions integral?
- How sensitive are results to λ? Please provide ablations and cross-validated tuning.
- How is NTC computed? Does it include test-house data? Can you show per-household breakdowns indicating robustness to households deviating from the national mean?
- Runtime details: problem sizes (T, I, Ki), number of variables/constraints, solver tolerances, hardware, average wall-clock vs AFAMAP/AFHMM, and scaling experiments.
- Comparisons to HSMM-based NILM and at-most-one-change models with duration priors?
- Any experiments with multiple time-window constraints, which seem natural for NILM?

Pros
- Well-motivated constraint class; clean optimization formulation.
- Statistically grounded via posterior regularization.
- Empirical improvements on a realistic low-frequency dataset.

Cons
- Heavy dependence on priors; limited robustness analyses.
- No rounding/integrality analysis; incomplete methodological pipeline.
- Limited baselines and unclear scalability; λ not tuned.

Recommendation
Lean reject. The idea is interesting and plausible, but the current evidence is insufficient: key methodological pieces (rounding, relaxation tightness), robustness to prior mis-specification, proper hyperparameter selection, and broader baseline comparisons are missing. Strengthening these aspects could make this a solid contribution.
