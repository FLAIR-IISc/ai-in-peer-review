## Review of "Signal Aggregate Constraints in Additive Factorial HMMs, with Application to Energy Disaggregation"

This paper introduces Signal Aggregate Constraints (SACs) as a method to incorporate domain knowledge into blind source separation problems, specifically within the context of Additive Factorial Hidden Markov Models (AFHMMs) for energy disaggregation. The core idea is to leverage prior knowledge about the expected total signal from unknown sources, encouraging the model to produce more interpretable results. The authors formulate this as a convex quadratic program for approximate inference and demonstrate significant improvements over both the baseline AFHMM and a recent state-of-the-art approach on a real-world energy disaggregation dataset.

This work builds upon a substantial body of research in blind source separation (BSS) [3, 17] and energy disaggregation [8, 12, 22]. The authors correctly identify the identifiability problem inherent in BSS and position SACs as a way to address this by incorporating prior knowledge, referencing related work in posterior regularization [4] and measurement-based learning [13]. The use of AFHMMs is also well-established in this domain [17], and the paper effectively situates its contribution within this existing framework. The connection to posterior regularization in Section 6 is a valuable addition, providing a theoretical grounding for the proposed approach.

**Strengths:** The paper is technically sound, presenting a clear mathematical formulation of SACs and a tractable optimization approach. The experimental results are compelling, demonstrating a substantial performance gain on a realistic dataset. The authors acknowledge limitations and provide a thorough comparison to existing methods. The writing is generally clear and well-organized, and the motivation for the work is well-articulated. The inclusion of both simulated and real-world experiments strengthens the validity of the findings.

**Weaknesses:** While the convex quadratic program is a significant contribution, the relaxation inherent in the approach is not fully explored. A more detailed discussion of the impact of this relaxation on the solution quality would be beneficial. The paper could also benefit from a more in-depth analysis of the sensitivity of the results to the choice of the constraint values (µi0) and the tuning parameter (δi). The discussion of related work, while comprehensive, could be more critical, highlighting the specific advantages of SACs over existing constraint-based methods [12, 21].

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novel approach to incorporating domain knowledge into BSS.
*   Strong empirical results on a real-world dataset.
*   Technically sound formulation and tractable optimization.
*   Clear writing and well-organized presentation.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   Relaxation in the optimization may impact solution quality.
*   Limited analysis of sensitivity to constraint parameters.
*   Could benefit from a more critical comparison to existing constraint methods.

**Overall:** This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of energy disaggregation and BSS. The proposed SACs offer a promising approach to improving the interpretability and accuracy of source separation models. Despite some minor weaknesses, the strengths of the work outweigh the concerns, and I recommend acceptance.
