- Paper proposes Signal Aggregate Constraints (SACs) to inject prior knowledge of per-source aggregates into AFHMMs for blind source separation, motivated by energy disaggregation.

- Incorporating SACs yields a non‑Markovian objective; authors derive a convex quadratic program (CQP) relaxation via transition indicator variables and integrality relaxation.

- The CQP is concave (negative semidefinite quadratic terms), making the relaxed problem convex and solvable by off‑the‑shelf solvers.

- The formulation is connected to posterior regularization: the penalized posterior corresponds to a KL projection with Lagrange multipliers λi.

- The claimed one-to-one correspondence between penalty weights δi and λi holds under convex duality in the relaxed setting.

- Inference optimizes the relaxed CQP and compares recovered continuous signals; no rounding procedure for discrete state sequences is provided.

- Markov consistency of relaxed marginals is maintained via transition variables H, but the impact of fractional states on interpretability is not assessed.

- Model parameters are learned with k-means and standard estimators assuming 3 states per appliance; this is a reasonable baseline but may not be optimal.

- Penalty weights λi are fixed to 1 in experiments; limited robustness analysis focused on µ0 variation rather than λi sensitivity.

- Experiments include synthetic toy data and the HES dataset; AFHMM+SAC with national totals (NTC) improves NDE and SAE over AFHMM and AFAMAP.

- Oracle experiments using true totals (TTC) demonstrate upper-bound performance; statistical tests show significant gains on real houses with mains.

- Runtime is reported similar across methods, but scalability concerns for generic CQP solvers are noted.

- Paper is generally clear and well organized; some notational density and lack of intuitive explanation in parts could be improved.

- Reproducibility details missing: how σt is set/estimated, parameter learning specifics, solver settings, convergence criteria, and post-processing of fractional solutions.

- The use of aggregate priors for per-source totals in AFHMMs is novel within NILM/BSS contexts.

- The convex relaxation connects to known HMM/CRF relaxations but is meaningfully extended by combining with SACs for NILM.

- The approach addresses a key identifiability challenge in BSS and shows practical impact on ultra‑low‑frequency energy data.

- Limitations include dependence on quality of µ0, fixed λi, lack of rounding/interpretability procedures, and potential scalability issues.

- Strengths: principled SAC incorporation with posterior-regularization view, convex relaxation avoiding local optima, empirical gains with statistical significance, and toy sensitivity analysis.

- Weaknesses/questions: robustness to µ0 misspecification on real data, hyperparameter selection for λi, rounding to discrete states, solver scalability, baseline tuning fairness, and missing ablations.

- Suggested improvements: provide principled λi selection (e.g., cross‑validation), include rounding and state‑accuracy evaluation, test robustness to perturbed µ0 and model uncertainty, report solver/runtime scaling and implement specialized solvers, and add ablations (λ=0, semi‑Markov, sub‑interval SACs).

- Arguments for acceptance: addresses identifiability in AFHMMs with a well‑motivated prior, solid convex formulation, clear theoretical grounding, and significant empirical improvements on realistic data.

- Arguments against acceptance: dependence on external priors with limited robustness analysis, scalability concerns with generic CQPs, missing rounding/interpretability details, and limited comparison to competing constrained models.

- Overall assessment: technically sound, clear, and a meaningful contribution that improves identifiability in AFHMMs with promising empirical results, but requires more robustness, scalability, and rounding details.
