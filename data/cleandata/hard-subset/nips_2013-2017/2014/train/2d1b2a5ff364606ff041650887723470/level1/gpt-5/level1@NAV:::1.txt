This paper proposes Signal Aggregate Constraints (SACs) for blind source separation (BSS), instantiated in an additive factorial HMM (AFHMM) for energy disaggregation. The key idea is to encode prior knowledge about total signal per source (e.g., daily Wh per appliance) as quadratic penalties on aggregate usage, addressing identifiability. The authors derive a convex quadratic program (CQP) for a relaxed MAP problem by introducing pairwise transition variables and show the objective can also be obtained via posterior regularization (Ganchev et al., 2010). Empirically, on the UK HES dataset (ultra-low-frequency), AFHMM+SAC improves substantially over AFHMM and AFAMAP (Kolter & Jaakkola, 2012), with statistically significant gains in normalized disaggregation error (NDE) and signal aggregate error (SAE). The work builds on factorial HMMs (Ghahramani & Jordan, 1997) and connects to constraint-based learning (posterior regularization, generalized expectation, measurement learning), as well as prior disaggregation constraints (e.g., “at most one change” in AFAMAP).

Quality: The technical contribution is sound: the SAC regularizer is well motivated; the CQP relaxation is carefully constructed, with PSD structure ensuring convexity of the relaxed maximization. The posterior-regularization derivation adds conceptual clarity. Experiments are thorough for the application, with multiple households, oracle and practical priors (TTC vs. NTC), significance testing, and runtime comparisons.

Clarity: Generally clear and well organized, though notation is dense. The optimization section would benefit from a brief discussion of integrality/rounding of relaxed solutions and solver specifics (tolerances, convergence criteria). A short algorithm box would help reproducibility.

Originality: While AFHMMs and constraint-based learning are established, encoding aggregate-signal priors as SACs and deriving an efficient convex relaxation for AFHMM inference is novel and broadly applicable across BSS domains.

Significance: Results show meaningful gains on a realistic, challenging dataset. The idea is general and could influence future constrained-inference formulations. However, dependence on aggregate priors and use of supervised parameter estimation may limit immediate deployability.

Weaknesses and suggestions: (1) Reliance on µi0 requires guidance for estimation under domain shift; ablations with noisy priors are limited beyond the toy study. (2) No comparison to duration-aware models (semi-Markov; Johnson & Willsky, 2013) or k-segment constraints (Titsias et al.). (3) Scalability of CVX to longer sequences and many devices is unclear; a custom solver or block-coordinate scheme would strengthen the case. (4) Rounding/integrality gap and impact on discrete states are not discussed.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Simple, general, and effective constraint that addresses identifiability.
- Solid convex relaxation with clear PR connection.
- Significant empirical improvements on realistic data.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Practical dependence on accurate aggregate priors; limited robustness analysis.
- Missing comparisons to strong duration-based baselines.
- Scalability and rounding details underexplored.
