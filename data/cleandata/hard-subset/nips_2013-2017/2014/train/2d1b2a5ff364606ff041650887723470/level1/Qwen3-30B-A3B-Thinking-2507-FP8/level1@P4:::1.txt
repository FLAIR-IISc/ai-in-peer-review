

# Review of "Signal Aggregate Constraints in Additive Factorial HMMs, with Application to Energy Disaggregation"

## Summary
This paper introduces Signal Aggregate Constraints (SACs) as a novel approach to address the identifiability problem in blind source separation (BSS), with specific application to energy disaggregation. SACs encourage the total signal for each unknown source to be close to a specified value, leveraging domain knowledge about expected total consumption. The authors incorporate SACs into an Additive Factorial HMM (AFHMM), transforming the constrained optimization problem into a convex quadratic program (CQP) for efficient inference. The method is evaluated on both synthetic data and real-world energy disaggregation data from the Household Electricity Survey (HES), showing significant improvements over the baseline AFHMM and a recent state-of-the-art approach (AFAMAP).

The paper relates to prior work on constraint-based learning for probabilistic models, including posterior regularization [4], generalized expectation criterion [14], and measurement-based learning [13]. Unlike these approaches, SACs specifically target aggregate values of source signals, which the authors argue are often known in advance for many applications (e.g., average appliance consumption in energy disaggregation). The paper also connects SACs to the posterior regularization framework, showing that the derived objective function matches the optimal solution under this framework.

## Quality
The paper demonstrates strong technical soundness. The authors clearly identify the identifiability issue in BSS and propose a well-motivated solution through SACs. The mathematical formulation of SACs within the AFHMM framework is precise and carefully derived. The transformation of the constrained optimization problem into a convex quadratic program is technically sound and justified. The experimental evaluation is thorough and well-designed, with appropriate metrics (NDE and SAE) and statistical validation (paired t-tests showing significance at the 5% level). The results on real-world energy data are convincing, showing a 45% decrease in normalized disaggregation error compared to the original AFHMM, and a 29% improvement over AFAMAP. The authors acknowledge the limitations of SACs, particularly the requirement for domain knowledge about expected total consumption values, and demonstrate the impact of constraint specification errors through their toy data experiments.

## Clarity
The paper is well-organized and clearly written. The problem statement, methodology, and experimental results are presented in a logical flow. The mathematical notation is consistent and well-defined throughout. The experimental setup is adequately described, including details about the HES dataset, evaluation protocols, and parameter settings. The authors provide sufficient technical detail to allow reproduction of their results, including the specific convex quadratic program formulation and implementation details (using CVX). The figures (particularly Figure 1 showing the impact of constraint specification errors) effectively illustrate key findings. The paper could benefit from slightly more detailed discussion of the computational complexity analysis, but the current presentation is sufficient for the intended audience.

## Originality
The concept of Signal Aggregate Constraints represents a novel contribution to the field of BSS. While the paper properly cites and relates to prior work on constraint-based learning (posterior regularization, generalized expectation criterion), SACs specifically target aggregate signal values, which appears to be a new direction not previously explored in BSS literature. The authors clearly differentiate their approach from related work in Section 2, particularly from AFAMAP [12] and other constrained HMM approaches. The specific application to energy disaggregation is well-motivated, and the authors demonstrate that SACs can be applied to other BSS problems where domain knowledge about total signal values is available. The connection to posterior regularization framework provides theoretical grounding for the approach, further strengthening its novelty.

## Significance
The paper addresses an important practical problem (energy disaggregation) with significant real-world implications for energy conservation. The demonstrated improvements over state-of-the-art approaches (29% reduction in error) are substantial in the context of this application domain. The approach is generalizable to other BSS problems where domain knowledge about total signal values is available, potentially benefiting applications in audio processing, computational biology, and other domains. The paper shows that even approximate domain knowledge (using national average consumption as a proxy for true consumption) can substantially improve performance, making the approach practically useful. The work advances the state of the art in energy disaggregation by providing a principled way to incorporate meaningful domain knowledge into the learning process.

## Strengths and Weaknesses

**Strengths:**
- Novel concept of Signal Aggregate Constraints for addressing identifiability in BSS
- Strong theoretical foundation connecting SACs to posterior regularization
- Comprehensive experimental evaluation on both synthetic and real-world data
- Clear demonstration of significant performance improvements (45% over AFHMM, 29% over AFAMAP)
- Well-organized presentation with sufficient technical detail for reproducibility

**Weaknesses:**
- Limited discussion of SACs' sensitivity to errors in constraint specification beyond the toy data experiment
- Brief computational complexity analysis; could provide more detailed complexity analysis of the CQP solution
- Does not compare with more recent energy disaggregation methods published after AFAMAP (2012)
- The unsupervised integration of SACs (mentioned as future work) is not explored in depth

## Recommendation
This paper makes a significant contribution to the field of blind source separation by introducing Signal Aggregate Constraints, a novel and practical approach to addressing identifiability issues. The technical development is sound, the experimental evaluation is thorough, and the results demonstrate clear improvements over existing methods. The work is well-positioned within the NIPS community, addressing an important application domain with a methodologically sound approach that could be generalized to other BSS problems.

The paper meets all the NIPS quality criteria: it is technically sound, clearly presented, original, and significant. While there are minor limitations regarding sensitivity analysis and computational complexity, these do not undermine the core contribution. The paper should be accepted for presentation at NIPS.
