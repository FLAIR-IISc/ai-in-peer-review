- Problem: Blind source separation for energy disaggregation — decompose household aggregate electricity into per-appliance signals, facing inherent unidentifiability.  
- Main idea: Introduce Signal Aggregate Constraints (SACs) that enforce each source's total signal over a period to match a known prior value.  
- Model/formulation: Integrate SACs into the Additive Factorial HMM (AFHMM), making MAP inference non-Markovian and intractable by DP.  
- Inference approach: Relax constrained MAP to a convex quadratic program (CQP) for efficient approximate inference.  
- Theoretical grounding: Connects to posterior regularization, justifying the penalized objective.  
- Empirical results: AFHMM+SAC outperforms AFHMM and AFAMAP, showing ~45% NDE reduction vs AFHMM and ~29% vs AFAMAP on real-world data.  
- Originality: SACs are novel, intuitive, and target readily available aggregate priors rather than fine-grained dynamics.  
- Significance: Addresses an important practical problem with results that materially advance the state of the art and generalize to other BSS settings.  
- Technical quality: Sound formulation, valid CQP relaxation, clear linkage to posterior regularization, and appropriate evaluation metrics/baselines.  
- Clarity: Paper is well-written, logically structured, and easy to follow.  
- Weakness — hyperparameters: λi regularizers were fixed to 1; sensitivity analysis or cross-validation strategy is missing.  
- Weakness — prior availability: Method depends on knowing accurate aggregate priors (µi0); applicability when such priors are unavailable or scarce is not fully addressed.  
- Weakness — scalability: CQP size grows with T, I, Ki; computational scaling and limits are not thoroughly analyzed.  
- Pro-acceptance: Novel, effective method with strong empirical gains and solid technical grounding.  
- Con-acceptance: Lacks hyperparameter sensitivity analysis, discussion of acquiring priors in low-data scenarios, and detailed computational complexity analysis.  
- Overall: Strengths (novelty, significance, results, clarity) outweigh weaknesses; SACs are a valuable, generalizable contribution.
