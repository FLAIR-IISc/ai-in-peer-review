This paper introduces Signal Aggregate Constraints (SACs) as a method for incorporating domain knowledge to address the identifiability problem in blind source separation, with a specific application to energy disaggregation. The core idea is to regularize the solution by constraining the total sum of each inferred source signal to be close to a known prior value. The authors incorporate these non-Markovian constraints into an Additive Factorial Hidden Markov Model (AFHMM) and derive a tractable approximate MAP inference algorithm via a convex quadratic programming (CQP) relaxation. This work extends the line of research on constrained factorial HMMs, such as the AFAMAP model of Kolter & Jaakkola (AISTATS 2012), but proposes a novel, global constraint based on domain expertise about signal totals rather than local transition dynamics.

The paper’s primary strengths lie in its originality, significance, and quality.

*   **Originality and Significance:** The concept of SACs is simple, intuitive, and novel. The results are highly significant, showing a 29% error reduction over a state-of-the-art baseline on a challenging, low-frequency energy disaggregation dataset. This substantial improvement highlights the value of the proposed constraint. Furthermore, the idea is general and could be readily applied to other BSS domains where aggregate source properties are known, making it likely that other researchers will build on this work.

*   **Quality and Clarity:** The work is technically sound and very clearly presented. The CQP formulation is well-derived, and the connection to the posterior regularization framework provides solid theoretical grounding. The experimental evaluation is thorough, using both synthetic and real-world data to convincingly demonstrate the method’s effectiveness and robustness. Comparing against an oracle alongside a realistic prior is a particularly strong aspect of the evaluation, clearly demonstrating the practical utility of the approach.

The main weakness is that the CQP inference technique is an adaptation of an existing method, though its application to this new class of constraints is well-executed. Additionally, the regularization hyperparameter was set heuristically rather than tuned, which could be explored in future work to further improve performance.

Overall, this is an excellent paper that introduces a novel and highly effective idea. The strong empirical results, clear presentation, and potential for broader impact make a compelling case for acceptance.
