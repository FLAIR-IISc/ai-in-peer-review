This paper addresses the problem of blind source separation (BSS), focusing on the application of energy disaggregation where a single time series of aggregate household electricity usage is decomposed into the consumption signals of individual appliances. The central challenge in this domain is the inherent unidentifiability of the source signals. To address this, the authors propose incorporating domain knowledge through a novel mechanism called Signal Aggregate Constraints (SACs). SACs enforce a prior belief that the total signal value for each source over a given time period should be close to a specified, known value.

This idea is formalized within the Additive Factorial Hidden Markov Model (AFHMM), a standard probabilistic model for BSS. The incorporation of SACs transforms the MAP inference problem into one with non-Markovian dependencies, rendering exact inference via dynamic programming intractable. The authors overcome this by formulating a relaxed version of the MAP inference problem as a convex quadratic program (CQP), which can be solved efficiently. This approach is related to the posterior regularization framework, providing theoretical grounding for the proposed penalized objective function. The proposed model, AFHMM+SAC, is empirically evaluated on a toy dataset and a real-world energy disaggregation dataset. The results demonstrate that AFHMM+SAC yields substantial improvements over both the standard AFHMM and AFAMAP, a state-of-the-art method from prior work that also introduces constraints to the AFHMM.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Originality:** The primary contribution, the introduction of Signal Aggregate Constraints, is a novel and intuitive idea. While the concept of using prior knowledge to constrain underdetermined problems is well-established, applying it to the *aggregate* signal of each source over a long time horizon is a simple yet powerful approach that has not been extensively explored in this context. It directly targets a key aspect of the energy disaggregation problem, where average consumption is often more readily available as prior knowledge than fine-grained temporal dynamics.

2.  **Significance:** The paper addresses a problem of significant practical and research interest. Energy disaggregation has clear applications for energy conservation and utility management. The empirical results are compelling, showing a 45% decrease in normalized disaggregation error compared to the baseline AFHMM and a 29% improvement over the state-of-the-art AFAMAP on a challenging, real-world dataset. This demonstrates that the proposed method provides a tangible advancement in the state of the art. The concept of SACs appears generalizable to other BSS domains where similar aggregate priors might exist.

3.  **Technical Quality:** The technical development is sound. The formulation of the constrained MAP inference problem and its transformation into a penalized objective function is clear and well-motivated. The core technical contribution—the relaxation of this problem into a CQP—is a valid and practical method for obtaining an approximate solution to an otherwise intractable problem. The connection drawn to the posterior regularization framework in Section 6 strengthens the theoretical basis of the approach. The experimental methodology is solid, using appropriate metrics (NDE and SAE) and comparing against relevant and strong baselines.

4.  **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow. The motivation for the problem is clearly articulated, the proposed model is introduced logically, and the inference procedure is explained in sufficient detail. The structure of the paper guides the reader effectively from the high-level concept to the technical implementation and empirical validation.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Hyperparameter Selection:** The model introduces a regularization parameter, λi, for each constraint, which controls the trade-off between the data likelihood and the constraint satisfaction. The paper states that λi was simply set to 1 for all experiments. While this simplifies the experimental setup, it leaves the question of the model's sensitivity to this parameter unexplored. A more thorough analysis, perhaps including a sensitivity study or a discussion of cross-validation strategies for setting λi, would strengthen the paper's claims of robustness.

2.  **Availability of Priors:** The effectiveness of AFHMM+SAC relies on the availability of accurate prior values for the signal aggregates (µi0). The experiments use two settings: an oracle value (True Total Consumption) and a more realistic one derived from aggregate statistics over the training set (National Total Consumption). While the latter shows strong performance, it raises questions about the applicability of the method in scenarios where such extensive, labeled training data is not available to derive these priors. A more detailed discussion of how to obtain or estimate these values in less data-rich environments would be beneficial.

3.  **Scalability of the CQP:** The paper notes that the CQP solver has polynomial-time complexity, but does not provide a detailed analysis. The number of variables and constraints in the CQP scales with the length of the time series (T), the number of sources (I), and the number of states per source (Ki). For very long time series or models with many states, the CQP could become computationally prohibitive. While the reported runtimes are comparable to the baseline for the specific dataset used, a brief discussion of the computational limitations and potential scaling challenges would be valuable.

**Summary of Arguments:**

*   **Pro-acceptance:** The paper introduces a novel, simple, and effective method for incorporating domain knowledge into a challenging BSS problem. The technical approach is sound, and the experimental results demonstrate a significant improvement over the state of the art on a relevant, real-world application. The paper is very clearly written and presents a complete piece of work.
*   **Con-acceptance:** The evaluation of hyperparameter sensitivity is limited. The practical application of the method is dependent on the availability of good prior information on signal aggregates, the acquisition of which is not fully addressed for all potential use cases. A more detailed analysis of computational complexity would be useful.

Overall, the strengths of this paper, particularly its novelty, significance, and strong empirical results, substantially outweigh its weaknesses. The proposed Signal Aggregate Constraints represent a valuable and generalizable contribution to the field of structured probabilistic modeling and its applications.
