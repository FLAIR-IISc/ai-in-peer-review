Summary and relation to prior work
The paper introduces Signal Aggregate Constraints (SACs) for blind source separation in additive factorial HMMs (AFHMMs), motivated by energy disaggregation (NILM). The key idea is to encode domain knowledge about the total signal per source (e.g., daily kWh of an appliance) as soft constraints penalizing deviations from specified aggregates. The authors incorporate SACs into AFHMM inference and derive a convex quadratic program (CQP) relaxation using auxiliary transition variables, enabling efficient MAP estimation. They further show the objective can be derived from the posterior regularization (PR) framework. Experiments on synthetic data and the UK HES dataset (including low-frequency data and real mains measurements) show that AFHMM+SAC improves normalized disaggregation error (NDE) and signal aggregate error (SAE) versus AFHMM and AFAMAP [Kolter & Jaakkola, AISTATS 2012].

This connects to a growing literature on constraining latent-variable models: posterior regularization [Ganchev et al., JMLR 2010], generalized expectation (GE) [Mann & McCallum], and measurement-based learning [Liang et al.]. In NILM, it relates to imposing temporal sparsity/transition constraints (e.g., at-most-one-change constraints in AFAMAP; interleaved/non-homogeneous FHMMs; semi-Markov models for durations). The novelty here is the use of aggregate, across-time constraints—common in many BSS tasks—and a practical convex relaxation for AFHMMs.

Strengths
- Conceptual simplicity and generality: SACs encode widely available domain knowledge (totals) that are orthogonal to temporal constraints; the approach is applicable beyond NILM.
- Sound convex relaxation: By introducing transition variables Hit and exploiting the PSD structure of quadratic terms (Gaussian emission and SAC penalties), the relaxed MAP objective is concave with linear constraints, solvable by off-the-shelf CQP solvers. The PR derivation clarifies the probabilistic semantics.
- Empirical value on real data: Consistent improvements in NDE and SAE on HES, including the more realistic “with mains” setting. The oracle (TTC) variant demonstrates headroom; the NTC variant shows gains from plausible priors.
- Computationally competitive (as reported) with AFHMM/AFAMAP for the scales considered.

Weaknesses and questions
Quality
- Effect of relaxation and rounding: The solution to the CQP is fractional in general. How are fractional S and H mapped to discrete appliance states for interpretability (or for evaluation)? If left fractional, the totals and NDE/SAE are computable, but interpretability (a central motivation) is compromised. Provide rounding or projection procedures and quantify any loss.
- Sensitivity to µ0 and λ: The toy experiment suggests performance degrades with mis-specified µ0. Real-world µ0 (NTC) can be far from household-specific totals. How robust is performance to mismatch? The paper fixes λi=1 without tuning; this risks over-/under-regularization across houses/appliances. A sensitivity analysis or cross-validated λ would strengthen the claims.
- Parameter learning: Means are set via k-means and transitions presumably estimated from limited training data; uncertainty is ignored. Joint learning with SACs (e.g., EM with PR) or at least a discussion of identifiability when parameters are mis-specified would be useful.
- Comparisons: AFAMAP is an appropriate baseline, but other strong NILM baselines (semi-Markov [Johnson & Willsky], Parson et al. AAAI, non-homogeneous FHMMs) are discussed but not compared empirically; this limits the breadth of the empirical claim. Also consider combining SACs with AFAMAP’s one-switch constraint, which seems natural and could further improve results.
- Scalability: While the formulation is polynomial, generic CVX may not scale to many appliances or long horizons. Provide complexity in terms of T and ∑ Ki, and report problem sizes solved (variables/constraints), iterations, and whether specialized solvers (e.g., ADMM) were needed.

Clarity
- Overall clear, but the notation is heavy. A concise summary of variables, dimensions, and constraints in one place would help reproducibility.
- Minor details missing: treatment of σt, numerical stability/regularization, initialization, stopping criteria, and whether bounds Sit∈[0,1] suffice to ensure local consistency with Hit without additional marginalization constraints.
- The extension to multiple time-window SACs is promising; a brief statement on preserved convexity and incremental computational cost would be helpful.

Originality
- Moderate. The core idea of aggregate constraints aligns with PR/GE/measurement-based learning, but the instantiation to AFHMMs with a clean convex relaxation and application to NILM is novel and practically meaningful.

Significance
- For NILM, aggregate priors are realistic (e.g., per-appliance kWh statistics, billing information), and the reported gains are non-trivial. Beyond NILM, the idea could benefit BSS problems where totals are known or bounded. However, dependence on accurate µ0 may limit applicability, and lack of broader baselines tempers claims about advancing the state of the art.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide a rounding scheme (e.g., per-time argmax, or projection to the nearest vertex) and report its effect.
- Explore combining SACs with transition constraints (AFAMAP) and/or duration models.
- Model uncertainty in µ0 via slack variables or priors (e.g., Laplace penalties or inequality constraints with confidence intervals), and learn λi (evidence maximization or cross-validation).
- Evaluate on an additional public NILM dataset (e.g., REDD) for comparability with prior work.
- Discuss household-specific priors learned from a small calibration period or meta-features (house size, occupancy).

Arguments for acceptance
- Simple, general idea with clear probabilistic grounding.
- Sound convex formulation; practical and implementable.
- Consistent empirical improvements on challenging, realistic data.
- Relevance to a timely application area (energy).

Arguments against acceptance
- Dependence on µ0 and lack of robustness/λ-tuning analysis.
- No discrete rounding/interpretability discussion despite motivation.
- Limited comparison set; not combined with other best practices (durations/one-switch).
- Scalability and implementation details not thoroughly addressed.

Overall
Borderline accept. The paper makes a useful, well-motivated contribution with a principled formulation and promising results in a relevant application. Addressing the outlined methodological and empirical gaps would strengthen its impact.
