Summary and relation to prior work
The paper addresses blind source separation (BSS) in additive factorial HMMs (AFHMMs) by introducing signal aggregate constraints (SACs): soft constraints that encourage the total signal (e.g., sum over time) of each latent source to match a specified prior value. The motivation is that aggregate usage often varies widely across sources and is easier to estimate from domain knowledge than detailed temporal dynamics (e.g., appliance-level daily Wh). The authors integrate SACs into an AFHMM, derive a relaxed convex quadratic program (CQP) for MAP inference using auxiliary variables that encode pairwise transitions, and show that the objective can also be derived from the posterior regularization framework. Experiments on real household electricity data (HES) compare AFHMM+SAC to AFHMM and AFAMAP (Kolter & Jaakkola, AISTATS’12) and report substantial improvements in normalized disaggregation error (NDE) and signal aggregate error (SAE), including with measured mains where unmodeled loads are present.

The work builds on FHMMs (Ghahramani & Jordan), AFAMAP’s structure (at-most-one-change), and general frameworks for incorporating side information such as posterior regularization (Ganchev et al.), generalized expectation (Mann & McCallum), and measurement-based learning (Liang et al.). The novelty here is identifying “aggregate over time” constraints as a practically useful prior for BSS, and a tractable convex relaxation for inference with such non-Markovian constraints.

Strengths
- Clear and compelling motivation: aggregate priors are realistic in domains like energy and potentially audio/bio.
- Sound convex relaxation with a careful construction that yields a CQP; link to posterior regularization clarifies the probabilistic interpretation.
- Realistic, challenging dataset (HES, very low sampling rate) where many disaggregation methods struggle; consistent gains over AFHMM and AFAMAP.
- Empirical evidence includes statistical significance tests and two scenarios (with/without mains).
- Simple to incorporate and orthogonal to other constraints; straightforward extension to windowed SACs is discussed.

Weaknesses
- Heavy reliance on accurate priors µi0; while a toy sensitivity analysis varies µ0, the real data fix λi=1 without tuning and do not study sensitivity to λ or systematic misspecification beyond the toy. Cross-validation or a Bayesian treatment of λ would strengthen the case.
- The CQP solves a relaxed (fractional) problem; the paper does not describe how fractional Si/Hi are converted into valid discrete state sequences, nor the impact on final signals. Is the output used directly as soft assignments, or is there a rounding/projection step?
- Scalability and solver details are limited. Using CVX is convenient but does not indicate scalability to longer horizons, more appliances, or higher Ki; per-iteration complexity and problem size (variables ~ O(T∑iKi + T∑iKi^2)) should be reported, and a specialized first-order/ADMM solver would be more convincing.
- Comparisons are limited. Prior work that encodes duration (HSMMs, Johnson & Willsky) or non-homogeneous transitions (Zhong et al.) is cited but not evaluated; combining AFAMAP’s at-most-one-change with SACs is an obvious baseline.
- Modeling assumptions are simplistic: all appliances have Ki=3 with k-means state means and no explicit duration modeling. Noise variance treatment (σt) and parameter estimation details are sparse, raising reproducibility concerns.

Quality
- The formulation is technically sound; the convexity of the relaxed objective follows from PSD quadratic terms and linear constraints. The posterior-regularization derivation is correct.
- Claims are supported by empirical results on a relevant dataset with significance tests. However, important implementation details (rounding from relaxed variables, λ selection, solver tolerances) are missing. The fairness of training across baselines should be clarified (e.g., do AFAMAP parameters come from the same k-means/EM?).
- The approach does not resolve identifiability per se; it biases solutions toward plausible aggregates. This is accurately conveyed but could be discussed more explicitly.

Clarity
- Generally well written, but the notation in Section 5 is dense. A schematic of the variable dependencies and a brief pseudocode/algorithm box would improve readability.
- Explain the post-processing from relaxed solutions to final estimates. Clarify whether outputs are continuous x̂it = E[µzit] under S or discretized.
- Provide concrete problem sizes (T, I, Ki) and average runtimes, and describe solver settings.
- Minor typos/grammar and missing years in references should be fixed.

Originality
- As an instantiation of posterior regularization/GE/measurement learning, the framework is not novel, but identifying aggregate-over-time constraints for BSS and producing a convex relaxation for AFHMM inference with such constraints is a useful and non-trivial contribution. I am not aware of prior FHMM disaggregation work that constrains total energy usage via convex inference.

Significance
- The reported ~29–45% improvements in NDE/SAE on low-rate HES data are practically meaningful for NILM. The idea is likely to transfer to other BSS domains with aggregate priors (e.g., total counts in biology, total energy per instrument in audio).
- Adoption may hinge on access to reliable µi0; use of national statistics is sensible, but guidance on robustness/tuning is needed.

Suggestions for improvement
- Detail rounding/projection from relaxed S,H to sequences or justify using soft assignments directly.
- Evaluate AFAMAP+SAC and/or HSMM/IFNHMM baselines, or at least discuss expected interactions.
- Report sensitivity to λ and an automatic selection strategy (CV on training, evidence maximization).
- Include a scalable optimization approach (e.g., ADMM with problem structure) and complexity analysis.
- Add a background “other” chain to absorb unmodeled loads in the with-mains setting and report residuals.

Arguments for acceptance
- Practical, domain-relevant prior that materially improves a hard real-world task.
- Clean, convex formulation with a principled connection to posterior regularization.
- Results on a challenging dataset with statistical significance.

Arguments against acceptance
- Limited methodological novelty beyond instantiating posterior regularization; comparisons to stronger structured baselines are missing.
- Incomplete details on rounding and scalability; reliance on CVX may not scale.
- Hyperparameter handling and robustness to misspecification are under-explored.

Recommendation
Lean toward accept. The paper identifies a useful class of constraints for BSS, provides a principled and tractable inference method, and demonstrates significant empirical gains on realistic data. With clarifications on rounding, scalability, and additional baselines/tuning, this would be a strong NIPS contribution.
