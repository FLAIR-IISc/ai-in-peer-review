Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper proposes Signal Aggregate Constraints (SACs) for blind source separation in additive factorial HMMs (AFHMM), with a primary application to residential energy disaggregation. The key idea is to incorporate domain knowledge about aggregate source totals (e.g., daily Wh per appliance) into inference by penalizing deviations of each source’s time-sum from a target µi0. The authors show that naively adding these non-Markovian constraints breaks dynamic programming, and derive a convex quadratic program (CQP) for a relaxed problem by introducing auxiliary pairwise variables Hit to linearize transition terms. They also connect the objective to posterior regularization, showing the constrained posterior form.

Empirically, the method (AFHMM+SAC) is evaluated on a toy dataset and the UK HES dataset, comparing against a basic AFHMM and AFAMAP (Kolter & Jaakkola, AISTATS’12), with both synthetic “no-mains” mixtures and real mains traces (9 households). Using national average consumption (NTC) vectors as µ0 yields consistent improvements over baselines, with stronger gains if an oracle µ0 (TTC) is used.

This work builds on factorial HMMs for BSS (Ghahramani & Jordan), prior constraints on AFHMM such as one-switch (AFAMAP), non-homogeneous/semi-Markov variants (Zhong et al.; Johnson & Willsky), and general frameworks for constrained learning (posterior regularization; generalized expectation; measurement learning). The proposed SACs are distinct from prior “local” constraints (switching, durations) because they act on aggregates across long horizons; they also relate to aggregate constraints studied in probabilistic databases, but in a much richer temporal graphical model.

Quality:
- Technical soundness: The formulation is well-motivated. The original MAP objective couples adjacent time steps bilinearly via STit log P Si,t−1; replacing those products with Hit and adding linear marginalization constraints yields a convex relaxation once combined with the concave quadratic data term and the concave SAC penalty (negative PSD quadratic). The convexity argument is sound. Using CVX for inference is reasonable for the problem scales studied. The posterior regularization derivation is correct and clarifies the probabilistic meaning of SACs.
- Completeness and caveats: The approach solves a relaxed CQP; the paper does not specify a rounding/projection procedure to obtain integral states (though in practice one can take argmax per time or decode via Viterbi conditioned on soft marginals). The effect of fractional solutions on evaluation is not discussed. Hyperparameters λi are fixed to 1; no sensitivity analysis or cross-validated tuning is reported, though a toy experiment explores µ0 misspecification. Parameter estimation for θ is only sketched (k-means for means; transitions implied from training), and the choice of 3 states for all appliances is simplistic; robustness to model order is not evaluated.
- Empirical evaluation: Results on 100 households (synthetic mains) and 9 households (true mains) show consistent, statistically significant improvements in NDE and SAE over AFHMM and AFAMAP; the oracle-TTC upper bound contextualizes possible gains. However, the use of NTC computed across all households could introduce a slight advantage not available to baselines unless framed explicitly as permitted prior knowledge; nonetheless, AFAMAP could in principle also accept such priors, so this comparison is fair if the goal is to assess the value of SAC information. Reporting per-appliance errors and failure cases would strengthen the evaluation.

Clarity:
The paper is generally clear and well organized. Section 5’s notation is dense; a schematic of variables S, H and constraints, or a small worked example over T=2, Ki=2 would aid readability. Details on solver settings, stopping criteria, and the post-processing of relaxed solutions are missing. The datasets and metrics are well described, and significance testing is reported.

Originality:
Introducing SACs—global sum constraints per source—within AFHMMs and deriving a tractable convex relaxation is novel relative to prior AFHMM constraints (one-switch, durations) and to general constrained learning frameworks. The posterior regularization perspective is known, but the specific choice of aggregate constraints and its CQP instantiation for AFHMM is an original and useful contribution.

Significance:
For energy disaggregation, aggregate consumption is exactly what users care about, so SACs align the objective with application needs. The reported improvements (e.g., 29–45% relative gains depending on setting) are meaningful for this domain, and the method should transfer to other BSS problems where aggregate priors exist (audio loudness totals, gene expression budgets, etc.). The reliance on approximate aggregate priors (e.g., national averages) is realistic and still helpful, per results. Broader impact is moderate-to-high given the generality of factorial models and posterior regularization.

Pros:
- Clear, well-motivated idea that encodes practically available knowledge (aggregate totals).
- Solid convex relaxation with an efficient and principled formulation; connection to posterior regularization clarifies the Bayesian interpretation.
- Empirical gains over strong baselines on realistic, low-frequency data; statistical significance reported.
- Method likely applicable beyond energy disaggregation.

Cons:
- No description/evaluation of rounding from relaxed to discrete states; potential mismatch between optimization objective and evaluated discrete outputs.
- Limited sensitivity analysis for λi and for misspecified µ0 in real data; robustness to poor priors is only shown on toy data.
- Scalability discussion is light; CVX-based CQP may struggle for long horizons or many appliances without specialized solvers.
- Model assumptions are simplistic (fixed K=3, k-means means); ablations on model order and training procedures are missing.
- Comparison set omits duration-based/semi-Markov baselines with which SACs could be combined, and no REDD or other public benchmarks for broader comparability.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Describe and evaluate a rounding strategy and report the fractionality of solutions; optionally enforce integrality via cutting planes or proximal rounding and assess trade-offs.
- Provide sensitivity to λi and robustness to noisy µ0 on real data; consider hierarchical priors over µ0 to adapt per household.
- Include per-appliance results and failure analysis; test multiple K and show how SACs interact with duration constraints.
- Discuss and, if possible, implement a scalable solver (e.g., ADMM) and provide runtime vs T, I, Ki.
- Evaluate on a public benchmark (e.g., REDD) for cross-paper comparability.

Overall recommendation:
This is a solid and relevant contribution with a clear idea, principled formulation, and promising empirical results. Despite some missing details (rounding, sensitivity, scalability), the paper meets the NIPS criteria well. I lean toward acceptance, contingent on addressing the noted clarity and experimental depth issues.
