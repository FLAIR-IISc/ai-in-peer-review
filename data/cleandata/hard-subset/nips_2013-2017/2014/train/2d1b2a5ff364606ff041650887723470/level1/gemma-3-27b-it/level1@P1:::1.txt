This paper introduces Signal Aggregate Constraints (SACs) to improve blind source separation, specifically within the context of Additive Factorial Hidden Markov Models (AFHMMs) for energy disaggregation. The core idea is to incorporate prior knowledge about the expected total energy consumption of individual appliances as constraints during inference, addressing the inherent identifiability issues in BSS. The authors formulate this as a convex quadratic program (CQP) for approximate inference and demonstrate significant performance gains on a real-world energy disaggregation dataset compared to the original AFHMM and a recent state-of-the-art method.

The paper builds on a well-established body of work in BSS and energy disaggregation (references [8, 12, 17, 22]). It correctly identifies the key challenge of identifiability and acknowledges existing approaches like posterior regularization [4] and measurement-based learning [13], positioning SACs as a specific class of constraints applicable to many domains. The introduction of SACs is a logical extension of incorporating domain knowledge into probabilistic models.  The use of AFHMMs as a baseline is also sensible, given their popularity in this field [17].  The connection to posterior regularization (Section 6) is a good addition, strengthening the theoretical foundation of the proposed method.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The introduction of SACs as a general constraint for BSS, especially their application to energy disaggregation, is a novel contribution. While incorporating prior knowledge isn't new, framing it as aggregate constraints provides a clear and effective approach.
*   **Technical Soundness:** The formulation of the problem as a CQP is well-motivated and allows for efficient computation. The derivation of the CQP (Section 5) appears mathematically sound, and the authors acknowledge the relaxation involved.
*   **Empirical Results:** The results on the real-world dataset are compelling, demonstrating a substantial improvement over existing methods.  The 45% decrease in normalized disaggregation error and 29% improvement over [12] are significant.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The motivation for the work is clear, and the key concepts are explained adequately.
*   **Thorough Evaluation:** The authors evaluate their method on both simulated and real-world data, and they compare it to relevant baselines. The inclusion of both NDE and SAE as evaluation metrics is appropriate.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Sensitivity to Constraint Value:** The toy data experiment (Section 7.1) shows the performance degrades as the constraint value (µ0) deviates from the true value. This suggests a sensitivity to the accuracy of prior knowledge. The paper doesn't thoroughly discuss how to handle uncertain or inaccurate prior knowledge.  Could robustness be improved with a more Bayesian treatment of µ0, perhaps incorporating a prior distribution over its possible values?
*   **Parameter Tuning:** The paper states λi=1 was used for all experiments, which could be suboptimal. The impact of different values of λi, and a more systematic method for tuning these parameters, isn’t explored. While cross-validation is mentioned as a possibility, it would be good to see some results of parameter tuning.
*   **Limited Discussion of Computational Cost:** While the authors mention similar computational time compared to other methods, a more detailed analysis of the scaling of the CQP solver with problem size (number of appliances, length of the time series) would be beneficial. This is particularly important for large-scale deployments.
*    **Generalizability beyond energy disaggregation:** While the authors position SACs as general, the evaluation is primarily focused on energy disaggregation. More discussion on the applicability of SACs to other BSS problems (e.g., audio processing, computational biology) would strengthen the argument for their broader relevance.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and technically sound approach to address a challenging problem in BSS.
*   The empirical results are strong and demonstrate a significant improvement over existing methods.
*   The paper is well-written and clearly explains the proposed method and its advantages.
*   The connection to posterior regularization provides a solid theoretical foundation.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   Sensitivity to the accuracy of the prior knowledge (SAC value) is a concern.
*   Lack of thorough parameter tuning and analysis of computational scalability.
*   Limited discussion of generalizability to other BSS applications.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with a clear contribution to the field of blind source separation and energy disaggregation. The proposed SACs offer a practical and effective way to incorporate domain knowledge into the inference process. While there are some minor weaknesses, the strengths of the paper outweigh them.  I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions. Specifically, the authors should:

1.  Discuss strategies for handling uncertainty in the prior knowledge (µ0).
2.  Include a brief analysis of the impact of different values of the complexity parameters (λi).
3.  Provide a more detailed discussion of the computational scalability of the CQP solver.
4.  Expand the discussion on the potential applications of SACs to other BSS problems.
