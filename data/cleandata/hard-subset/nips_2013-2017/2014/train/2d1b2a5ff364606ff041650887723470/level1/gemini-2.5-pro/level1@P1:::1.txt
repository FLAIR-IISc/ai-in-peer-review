This paper introduces Signal Aggregate Constraints (SACs), a novel method for incorporating domain knowledge into blind source separation (BSS) problems. The central idea is that for many BSS tasks, one has prior knowledge about the expected aggregate value of each source signal over a given period. The authors apply this idea to the problem of energy disaggregation, a specific BSS application where the goal is to separate a household's total electricity signal into the consumption of individual appliances. The proposed method incorporates SACs into an Additive Factorial Hidden Markov Model (AFHMM), a standard probabilistic model for this task. As the SACs introduce a non-Markovian dependency across the entire time series, standard inference algorithms like Viterbi are not applicable. To address this, the authors formulate the MAP inference problem as a penalized optimization problem and derive a convex quadratic program (CQP) for a relaxed version, which can be solved efficiently. This work builds upon the AFHMM framework popularised for source separation by Roweis (NIPS 2000) and Ghahramani & Jordan (1997), and provides a new way to constrain the model, differing from recent approaches like AFAMAP (Kolter & Jaakkola, AISTATS 2012) which constrains state transitions, or semi-Markov models which constrain state durations. The paper also provides a theoretical grounding for the approach by relating it to the posterior regularization framework of Ganchev et al. (JMLR 2010).

**Strengths:**

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The primary contribution—the concept of Signal Aggregate Constraints—is simple, highly intuitive, and novel for this problem class. It formalizes a type of domain knowledge that is often readily available (e.g., from national surveys of average appliance consumption) but has been difficult to incorporate into factorial models. By directly addressing the core issue of identifiability ("franken-appliances"), SACs provide a practical and powerful tool for making BSS solutions more meaningful. The reported results, showing a 29% error reduction over a recent state-of-the-art method on a challenging real-world dataset, demonstrate a significant advance for the energy disaggregation community. The idea is general enough that it could likely be applied to other BSS domains.

2.  **Technical Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The formulation of the non-Markovian MAP inference problem as a CQP by relaxing the integer constraints is a solid and appropriate technical approach, building on similar relaxations used in related work (e.g., AFAMAP). This makes an otherwise intractable problem computationally feasible. The connection drawn to posterior regularization in Section 6 is a nice addition that provides a principled Bayesian interpretation of the penalized objective function.

3.  **Experimental Evaluation:** The empirical validation is thorough and convincing.
    *   The use of a toy dataset to show the model's robustness to inaccurate priors for the SACs is a valuable analysis.
    *   The experiments are conducted on a large and very challenging real-world dataset (HES) that uses low-frequency data, which is more difficult for disaggregation than the high-frequency data used in some other studies (e.g., REDD).
    *   The method is benchmarked against appropriate baselines: the standard AFHMM and the state-of-the-art AFAMAP.
    *   The inclusion of an "oracle" experiment using the True Total Consumption (TTC) is an excellent way to demonstrate the potential of this constraint type under ideal conditions.
    *   The results are strong and statistically significant (validated with t-tests), showing clear improvement in both normalized disaggregation error (NDE) and the newly proposed signal aggregate error (SAE).

4.  **Clarity:** The paper is very well-written and organized. The motivation is clear, the model is built up logically from the base AFHMM, and the CQP formulation is laid out in sufficient detail. The experimental setup and results are presented clearly and are easy to interpret.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The penalty parameters λi are set to 1 for all experiments. The authors note that these could be tuned via cross-validation, but no analysis is provided. This feels somewhat ad-hoc. The performance of the model may be sensitive to this choice, and a brief sensitivity analysis or discussion of how λ relates to the variance of the prior belief about the aggregate would strengthen the paper's claims of robustness.

2.  **Generalizability:** While the paper claims SACs are general, the discussion and evaluation are entirely focused on energy disaggregation. To bolster the claim of generality, it would have been beneficial to briefly discuss other BSS domains (e.g., audio separation, gene expression analysis) where aggregate constraints would be meaningful and available.

3.  **Details on Model Training:** The paper focuses on inference, assuming model parameters are known. The description of how these parameters were learned is brief ("k-means clustering on each appliance’s signals"). Key details are omitted, such as how the number of states (Ki=3 for all appliances) was chosen. This simplification may not be optimal for all appliance types and providing more detail on the training phase would improve the paper's reproducibility.

**Summary of pros and cons:**

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Presents a novel, elegant, and highly effective method (SACs) for incorporating practical domain knowledge to tackle the key identifiability problem in BSS.
*   Provides a sound and tractable inference procedure based on a convex relaxation.
*   Demonstrates state-of-the-art performance with significant, statistically validated improvements over strong baselines on a challenging, large-scale, real-world application.
*   The paper is clear, well-structured, and the contributions are easy to understand.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   The treatment of the λ hyperparameter is oversimplified, lacking a sensitivity analysis.
*   The claims of the method's generality to other BSS domains are not substantiated with examples.
*   Some details regarding the learning of the underlying HMM parameters are sparse, slightly hindering full reproducibility.

Overall, this is a high-quality paper with a strong and significant contribution. The proposed idea is novel and impactful, the technical execution is sound, and the experimental results are compelling. The weaknesses are relatively minor and do not detract from the core contribution. This work is a valuable addition to the literature on constrained probabilistic models and energy disaggregation and is likely to be influential. I strongly recommend acceptance.
