## Review of “Signal Aggregate Constraints in Additive Factorial HMMs, with Application to Energy Disaggregation”

This paper addresses the challenging problem of blind source separation, specifically within the context of energy disaggregation. The authors introduce Signal Aggregate Constraints (SACs) – a method to incorporate domain knowledge by encouraging the total signal for each unknown source to align with expected values. They integrate SACs into an Additive Factorial Hidden Markov Model (AFHMM) and propose a convex quadratic program for approximate inference. The method is evaluated on a real-world energy disaggregation dataset, demonstrating significant improvements over both the baseline AFHMM and a recent state-of-the-art approach (Kolter & Jaakkola, 2012).

The core idea of incorporating aggregate constraints is intuitively appealing and a sensible approach to tackling the identifiability issues inherent in BSS problems. This builds upon previous work in incorporating prior knowledge into probabilistic models like posterior regularization (Ganchev et al., 2009) and measurement-based learning (Liang et al., 2011), but specifically focuses on a novel *type* of constraint – those relating to aggregate signal values. The technical contribution of formulating the inference as a convex quadratic program is significant, making the approach computationally tractable. The results presented on the HES dataset are compelling, showcasing a substantial reduction in disaggregation error.

However, the paper could benefit from a more thorough discussion of the limitations of the SAC approach. While the authors acknowledge the need for accurate prior knowledge of aggregate values (µi0), they don't delve into scenarios where these estimates are inaccurate or unavailable.  Additionally, the choice of a quadratic penalty in the optimization problem (5) is presented without strong justification; a sensitivity analysis exploring alternative penalty functions would be valuable. The connection drawn to posterior regularization (Section 6) feels somewhat forced and doesn't add substantial insight. While technically correct, it doesn't significantly enhance the understanding of the proposed method.  The presentation of results could also be strengthened by including error bars or confidence intervals.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novelty: Introduces a useful and intuitive approach (SACs) to address identifiability in BSS.
*   Technical Soundness: The CQP formulation provides an efficient inference method.
*   Empirical Results: Demonstrates substantial improvements on a real-world dataset.
*   Significance: Addresses a practical problem (energy disaggregation) with potential real-world impact.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   Limited Discussion of Limitations: Doesn't thoroughly explore sensitivity to prior knowledge accuracy.
*   Weak Connection to Posterior Regularization: Section 6 feels superficial.
*   Presentation of Results: Lacks detailed statistical analysis (e.g., error bars).



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a solid contribution to the field. While there are areas for improvement, the novelty of the approach, the technical soundness, and the strong empirical results justify acceptance. I recommend accepting this paper, with a request for the authors to address the limitations discussed above in a revised version.
