

# Peer Review: "Signal Aggregate Constraints in Additive Factorial HMMs, with Application to Energy Disaggregation"

This paper introduces Signal Aggregate Constraints (SACs), a novel approach for addressing the identifiability problem in blind source separation (BSS) problems. SACs constrain the total signal (sum over time) of each source to be close to a specified value, which is particularly relevant in energy disaggregation where domain knowledge about appliance energy consumption is often available. The authors incorporate SACs into an Additive Factorial HMM (AFHMM), transforming the constrained optimization problem into a convex quadratic program (CQP) for efficient inference. The paper demonstrates significant improvements over the baseline AFHMM and a recent state-of-the-art approach (AFAMAP) on real-world energy disaggregation data.

The work builds upon recent NIPS contributions on constrained inference, particularly connecting SACs to the posterior regularization framework (Ganchev et al., 2010). While posterior regularization has been used for various constraints, the authors identify a specific class of constraints (signal aggregates) that are both practically useful and computationally tractable. This complements the energy disaggregation literature (Kolter & Jaakkola, 2012; Parson et al., 2012) where identifiability has been a persistent challenge.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novel constraint type:** SACs represent a well-motivated and practical approach to incorporate domain knowledge into BSS problems. The paper clearly articulates why signal aggregates are a meaningful constraint in energy disaggregation (e.g., knowing a toaster typically uses 50Wh/day).

2. **Strong empirical results:** The 45% reduction in normalized disaggregation error over AFHMM and 29% over AFAMAP on real-world data is substantial and well-supported. The use of both normalized disaggregation error (NDE) and signal aggregate error (SAE) provides a comprehensive evaluation.

3. **Theoretical grounding:** The connection to posterior regularization (Section 6) provides a solid theoretical foundation, showing that SACs can be viewed as a specific case of prior distributions over the source signals.

4. **Clear presentation:** The paper is well-organized, with precise mathematical formulations and a logical flow from problem statement to solution to evaluation.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Practical constraint knowledge:** The paper's main limitation is that it assumes knowledge of the true total signal (µi0), which is rarely available in practice. While the authors use "national total consumption" (NTC) as a proxy, they don't adequately address how to obtain reliable estimates of µi0 for new households or appliances. The oracle "true total consumption" (TTC) results are misleading since they're not achievable in practice.

2. **Relaxation quality:** The CQP approach is a relaxation of the original problem, but the paper doesn't sufficiently analyze how this relaxation affects the quality of the solution. The authors state the solution "is not guaranteed optimal due to the identifiability problem," but don't quantify the gap between the relaxed solution and the true optimal solution.

3. **Comparison with related work:** The paper could better distinguish SACs from existing constraint approaches. For example, the k-segment constraints (Kolter & Jaakkola, 2012) and non-homogeneous HMMs (Parson et al., 2012) also constrain state transitions, though in different ways. The paper should more clearly articulate how SACs differ and complement these approaches.

4. **Computational complexity:** While the paper claims polynomial time complexity, it doesn't specify the exact complexity or how it scales with problem size (number of appliances, time steps, states). This is particularly important for real-world deployment where computational efficiency is crucial.

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper makes a valuable contribution by identifying a practically useful class of constraints (signal aggregates) for BSS problems and providing an efficient algorithm for incorporating them. The energy disaggregation application is well-motivated, and the empirical results are compelling. The main weakness is the reliance on known aggregate values, which limits practical applicability unless the authors provide a robust method for estimating these values from data.

However, the paper demonstrates significant potential for real-world impact in energy disaggregation and potentially other BSS applications. The connection to posterior regularization is theoretically sound, and the CQP approach provides a practical solution to an otherwise difficult inference problem. While the constraint knowledge issue is significant, it's a common challenge in constrained inference that could be addressed in future work (e.g., by using transfer learning or unsupervised estimation of aggregate values).

**Recommendation:** Accept. The paper's strengths in novelty, empirical results, and theoretical grounding outweigh its weaknesses. The constraint knowledge issue is important but not a fundamental flaw in the approach, and the paper provides a clear path for future work to address it. The results are significant enough to advance the state of the art in energy disaggregation, and the SAC concept could be valuable for other BSS problems where domain knowledge about signal aggregates exists.

**Pro Accept:**
- Novel constraint type addressing a real identifiability problem
- Significant empirical improvements over strong baselines
- Clear connection to posterior regularization framework
- Well-executed real-world evaluation

**Con Accept:**
- Reliance on known aggregate values (µi0) which is not practical
- Limited discussion of how to estimate µi0 in real applications
- Superficial computational complexity analysis
- Limited comparison with other constraint-based approaches
