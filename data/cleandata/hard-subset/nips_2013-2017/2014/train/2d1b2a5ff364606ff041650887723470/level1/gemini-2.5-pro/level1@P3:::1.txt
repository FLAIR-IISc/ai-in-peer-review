### Review of "Signal Aggregate Constraints in Additive Factorial HMMs, with Application to Energy Disaggregation"

#### Summary of the Paper

This paper addresses the problem of unidentifiability in single-channel blind source separation (BSS). The authors introduce a novel form of domain knowledge called Signal Aggregate Constraints (SACs), which posits that for many problems, one has prior knowledge about the expected total value of each source signal over a given time period. This idea is formalized within the context of an Additive Factorial Hidden Markov Model (AFHMM), a common choice for modeling BSS problems like energy disaggregation.

The core technical contribution is a method for performing MAP inference in an AFHMM subject to these SACs. Since the constraints are global over the entire time sequence, they break the Markov property, rendering standard dynamic programming algorithms like Viterbi inapplicable. The authors reformulate the constrained optimization problem as a penalized log-likelihood maximization. To make this tractable, they relax the integer state-indicator variables to be continuous on [0, 1] and formulate the problem as a Convex Quadratic Program (CQP), which can be solved efficiently. The authors also provide a nice connection, showing their penalized objective can be derived from the posterior regularization framework of Ganchev et al. (2010).

The proposed method, AFHMM+SAC, is evaluated on the task of energy disaggregation. It is compared against a standard AFHMM and the AFAMAP algorithm of Kolter & Jaakkola (2012), a strong baseline that uses a different type of constraint (sparsity of state transitions). On a real-world dataset, the proposed method demonstrates substantial improvements in disaggregation accuracy, reducing error by over 29% compared to the state-of-the-art AFAMAP.

#### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Quality:** The paper is of high technical quality. The proposed method is well-motivated, and the derivation of the CQP for approximate inference is sound. The experimental evaluation is thorough and provides convincing evidence for the method's effectiveness. The authors are careful in their evaluation, using both a realistic setting where aggregate priors are estimated from training data ("NTC") and an oracle setting to demonstrate the upper bound of performance ("TTC"). The use of two different error metrics (NDE and SAE) provides a more complete picture of performance. The connection to posterior regularization adds theoretical depth to the approach.

**Clarity:** The paper is very clearly written and well-organized. The motivation for SACs is presented intuitively, and the mathematical formalism is easy to follow. The progression from the AFHMM model to the constrained optimization problem and finally to the CQP relaxation is logical and well-explained. The experimental setup and results are presented transparently.

**Originality:** The primary contribution—the concept of Signal Aggregate Constraints—is novel and insightful. While incorporating prior knowledge into graphical models is not new, this specific form of constraint, based on the total sum of a signal, appears to be a new contribution to the BSS literature. It is a simple yet powerful idea that directly addresses a key weakness (label switching/unidentifiability) of factorial models. The application of CQP relaxation to this specific constrained AFHMM is also a solid technical contribution.

**Significance:** The results presented are highly significant for the energy disaggregation community. The demonstrated performance improvement over a strong, recent baseline (AFAMAP) is substantial. More broadly, the idea of SACs has the potential for impact beyond energy disaggregation. Any BSS problem where prior knowledge exists about the total contribution of different sources (e.g., total airtime of speakers in an audio mixture, total expression level of a gene pathway) could potentially benefit from this approach. The work provides a practical and effective tool for incorporating a very natural form of domain knowledge into a challenging class of inference problems.

Despite these significant strengths, there are a few weaknesses that could be addressed to improve the paper:

1.  **Hyperparameter Selection:** The choice of the regularization parameter `λ_i` is critical to the model's performance, as it balances the data likelihood term with the prior belief encoded in the SAC. The authors state they "simply set λi = 1 for all the experiments." This is an oversimplification. A sensitivity analysis showing how performance varies with `λ` would be highly valuable. While cross-validation is mentioned as a possibility, the lack of any exploration of this hyperparameter makes the results seem potentially fine-tuned and less robust.

2.  **Details of the CQP Solution:** The CQP relaxation results in fractional state assignments (`S_itk` in [0, 1]). The paper does not explicitly state how these fractional values are used to compute the final disaggregated signals (`x̂_it`) for evaluation. It is presumed that an expected signal value is calculated (i.e., `x̂_it = sum_k S_itk * µ_ik`), as is common in related work. However, this should be stated explicitly for clarity and reproducibility. Furthermore, a brief comment on the "peakedness" of the relaxed solution (i.e., how close the `S_itk` values are to 0 or 1) would be informative.

3.  **Scalability:** While the experiments show comparable runtimes for a single day of data, the computational complexity of solving a CQP can be a concern for larger problems. The number of variables and constraints scales with the length of the time series `T`. A discussion of the practical limits of this approach for longer time series (e.g., a month or a year of data) would be a useful addition.

4.  **Assumed Model Parameters:** The work focuses on inference, assuming the HMM parameters `θ` are pre-trained. This is a reasonable simplification, but a brief discussion of how SACs might be integrated into a parameter learning framework (e.g., EM) would strengthen the paper by outlining a path toward a fully unsupervised method.

#### Arguments for Acceptance:
*   Proposes a novel, intuitive, and powerful type of constraint (SACs) for BSS.
*   Provides a sound and tractable inference algorithm based on a CQP relaxation.
*   Demonstrates state-of-the-art performance with significant, statistically validated improvements on a challenging real-world problem.
*   The paper is well-written, clearly motivated, and the ideas have potential impact beyond the specific application domain.

#### Arguments against Acceptance:
*   The handling of the key hyperparameter `λ` is overly simplistic and lacks analysis.
*   Some implementation details regarding the output of the relaxed CQP are missing.

#### Conclusion

Overall, this is a strong paper with a novel core idea, a solid technical implementation, and impressive empirical results. The strengths significantly outweigh the weaknesses, which are primarily related to missing details that could be clarified in a final version. The introduction of Signal Aggregate Constraints is a valuable contribution to the field of blind source separation and machine learning more generally. I recommend acceptance.
