The paper "Signal Aggregate Constraints in Additive Factorial HMMs, with Application to Energy Disaggregation" proposes a novel approach to blind source separation problems by incorporating domain knowledge into the problem through signal aggregate constraints (SACs). SACs encourage the total signal for each of the unknown sources to be close to a specified value, which is often available in many applications. The authors incorporate SACs into an additive factorial hidden Markov model (AFHMM) and formulate the energy disaggregation problem where only one mixture signal is assumed to be observed. They employ a convex quadratic program for approximate inference to recover the source signals.

The main ideas of the paper are well-motivated and clearly explained, with a good balance between theoretical development and experimental evaluation. The authors provide a thorough review of related work, including previous approaches to energy disaggregation and blind source separation. The paper is well-organized, and the writing is clear and concise.

The strengths of the paper include:

* The introduction of SACs, which provides a novel approach to incorporating domain knowledge into blind source separation problems.
* The development of a convex quadratic program for approximate inference, which is computationally efficient and scalable.
* The experimental evaluation on real-world energy disaggregation data, which demonstrates the effectiveness of the proposed approach.

The weaknesses of the paper include:

* The assumption of a finite set of states for each Markov chain, which may not be realistic in many applications.
* The use of a simple Gaussian noise model, which may not capture the complexity of real-world noise.
* The lack of a thorough analysis of the computational complexity of the proposed approach, which is important for large-scale applications.

Overall, the paper makes a significant contribution to the field of blind source separation and energy disaggregation. The proposed approach is novel, well-motivated, and effectively evaluated on real-world data.

Arguments for acceptance:

* The paper proposes a novel approach to blind source separation problems, which is well-motivated and effectively evaluated.
