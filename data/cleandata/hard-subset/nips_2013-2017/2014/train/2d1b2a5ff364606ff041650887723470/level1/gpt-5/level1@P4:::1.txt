Summary and relation to prior work
The paper addresses blind source separation (BSS) in additive factorial hidden Markov models (AFHMMs), with a focus on energy disaggregation (non-intrusive load monitoring). The main idea is to incorporate domain knowledge about the aggregate magnitude of each source via signal aggregate constraints (SACs): penalties that encourage the total signal contributed by each chain over a time horizon to match a specified value. This prior is motivated by the observation that aggregate usage varies widely across sources and is often approximately known (e.g., daily energy usage of appliances). Incorporating SACs into AFHMMs yields a non-Markovian objective; the authors derive a convex quadratic program (CQP) for a relaxed version of MAP inference by introducing transition indicator variables and relaxing integrality, enabling efficient optimization with off-the-shelf solvers. They also connect their formulation to posterior regularization.

Related work includes AFHMMs and the AFAMAP algorithm (Kolter & Jaakkola, AISTATS 2012), which impose “one chain changes at a time” constraints; duration-constrained models (semi-Markov) applied to NILM; and general frameworks for incorporating constraints such as posterior regularization (Ganchev et al., JMLR 2010), generalized expectation, and measurement-based learning. Aggregate constraints have been explored in probabilistic databases but not in structured latent-variable models with HMM dependencies. The paper claims contributions in defining SACs for BSS, a convex relaxation for AFHMM+SAC, and empirical gains on low-frequency energy data (HES).

Quality (technical soundness)
- Formulation: The penalized objective adds squared deviations between each chain’s aggregate and a target µi0 to the AFHMM log posterior. The introduction of transition variables Hit, consistency constraints, and the relaxation to [0,1] makes the problem a concave quadratic maximization with linear constraints (equivalently a CQP). The quadratic terms are negative semidefinite due to the negative of PSD matrices, so the overall objective is concave. This is technically sound.
- Equivalence and interpretation: The link to posterior regularization is correctly established: the penalized log posterior corresponds to a KL projection onto posteriors satisfying expected aggregate constraints, with Lagrange multipliers λi. The “one-to-one correspondence” between δi and λi holds in the convex relaxed setting under standard duality assumptions.
- Inference and rounding: The paper optimizes the relaxed CQP and compares recovered signals directly. While valid for reconstructing continuous signals, the paper does not detail any rounding procedure to obtain discrete state sequences if desired, nor assess the impact of fractional states on interpretability. Markov consistency of relaxed marginals is ensured via H, but rounding might be needed in some applications.
- Learning: Parameters (µi, transitions) are learned via k-means and standard estimation on training data, assuming 3 states per appliance. This is a reasonable baseline, though not necessarily optimal for all appliances. The choice λi=1 for all experiments is simplistic; robustness is partially assessed on toy data by varying µ0 but not by varying λi.
- Experiments: Two settings are evaluated: synthetic toy data and the HES dataset. On 100 households without mains, aggregated from appliance submetering, AFHMM+SAC with national totals (NTC) improves NDE and SAE over AFHMM and AFAMAP; the oracle with true totals (TTC) gives an upper bound. On 9 houses with mains, AFHMM+SAC (NTC) improves over baselines with paired t-tests showing significance. Runtime is reported as similar across methods. The evaluation metrics (NDE, SAE) are standard in NILM; statistical testing is appropriate.

Clarity
The paper is generally clear and well organized. The derivation of the CQP is concise and includes key definitions. Some notational density in Section 5 could be alleviated with a brief intuition for the relaxation and a schematic of variable dependencies. Clarifications that would improve reproducibility: how σt is set/estimated; details of parameter learning (transition matrices, regularization); exact solver settings and convergence criteria; rounding or post-processing of fractional solutions.

Originality
The identification and use of aggregate constraints for source-specific totals in AFHMMs appears novel in the NILM/BSS context. The convex relaxation with transition variables is related to known relaxations for HMM/CRF MAP, but the specific combination with SACs and application to NILM is a meaningful extension. The connection to posterior regularization is appropriate and clarifies the conceptual grounding.

Significance
The method addresses a key identifiability challenge in BSS by exploiting practically available aggregate priors. The empirical improvements on a realistic, ultra-low-frequency dataset are substantial, suggesting practical impact for NILM. The approach is general and could transfer to other BSS domains where aggregate priors exist. Limitations include dependence on the quality of µ0 and scalability concerns with generic CQP solvers for larger I, Ki, or T.

Strengths
- Clear, principled incorporation of domain priors via SACs with a posterior-regularization interpretation.
- Convex relaxation enabling globally optimal solutions of the relaxed problem and avoiding local optima of chainwise Viterbi.
- Demonstrated gains over AFHMM and AFAMAP on realistic datasets; statistical significance reported.
- Sensitivity analysis on toy data clarifying the effect of µ0 mismatch.

Weaknesses and questions
- Dependence on µ0 quality: Results hinge on availability and accuracy of aggregate priors; robustness beyond toy sensitivity is not extensively characterized for real data.
- Hyperparameter handling: λi fixed to 1 without cross-validation; no analysis of its effect on performance or calibration.
- Rounding/interpretability: No description of converting relaxed solutions to discrete state sequences when needed.
- Scalability: Solving CQPs with CVX may not scale well; more details on complexity and specialized solvers exploiting problem structure would strengthen the case.
- Baseline fairness and parameterization: Uniform 3-state assumption and k-means parameter learning may not be optimal for all appliances; it is unclear whether baselines were tuned comparably (e.g., AFAMAP hyperparameters).
- Limited ablations: No comparison between AFHMM relaxed CQP (λ=0) vs chainwise Viterbi; no evaluation of sub-interval SACs despite being proposed.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide a principled procedure for setting λi (e.g., cross-validation, evidence maximization) and report sensitivity.
- Include a rounding procedure and evaluation of state accuracy, or justify continuous outputs for the target application.
- Explore robustness to misspecified µ0 on real data (e.g., perturb NTC) and consider modeling uncertainty over µ0 (Gaussian priors).
- Report more detailed runtime scaling and, if possible, implement a specialized solver exploiting block-structured quadratics and sparse constraints.
- Add ablations comparing AFHMM+SAC to AFHMM with convex relaxation (λ=0) and to semi-Markov/duration models.
- Evaluate the proposed sub-interval SACs on time-of-day constraints.

Arguments for acceptance
- Addresses a central identifiability issue in AFHMM-based BSS with a well-motivated, general prior class.
- Solid convex optimization formulation with a clear theoretical grounding.
- Empirical improvements on challenging, realistic datasets with statistical significance.
- Method likely of interest to the NIPS community working on structured prediction, convex relaxations, and applied ML for sustainability.

Arguments against acceptance
- Practical reliance on external priors µ0 with limited analysis of robustness and selection of λi.
- Scalability concerns with generic CQP solvers are not fully addressed.
- Missing details on rounding and interpretability of fractional solutions.
- Limited exploration of competing constrained models and ablations.

Overall, the paper is technically sound, clear, and contributes a useful and general mechanism to improve identifiability in AFHMMs, with promising empirical results on NILM.
