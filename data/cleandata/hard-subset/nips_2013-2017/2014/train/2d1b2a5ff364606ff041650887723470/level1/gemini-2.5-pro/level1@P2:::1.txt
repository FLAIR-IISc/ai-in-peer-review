### Summary

This paper introduces a novel method for incorporating domain knowledge into blind source separation (BSS) problems, specifically targeting the additive factorial hidden Markov model (AFHMM). The central idea is the "signal aggregate constraint" (SAC), which encourages the total sum of each unobserved source signal over a time period to be close to a known or expected value. This is motivated by applications like energy disaggregation, where one often has prior knowledge about the total daily consumption of an appliance (e.g., a refrigerator) but not its precise usage pattern.

The authors formulate a new model, AFHMM+SAC, by adding a quadratic penalty term to the AFHMM's log-posterior objective function, penalizing deviations from the target aggregate values. Since this penalty term breaks the Markov property and makes standard inference algorithms like Viterbi inapplicable, the authors propose an efficient approximate inference scheme. They relax the integer program for finding the maximum a posteriori (MAP) state sequence into a convex quadratic program (CQP), which can be solved efficiently. The paper also provides a nice connection to the posterior regularization framework, showing that their penalized objective can be derived as the solution to a KL-divergence minimization problem under expectation constraints.

The method is evaluated on a toy dataset and a real-world energy disaggregation dataset (HES). The results show that AFHMM+SAC dramatically reduces disaggregation error compared to a standard AFHMM (45% reduction) and significantly outperforms a recent state-of-the-art method, AFAMAP (29% reduction), demonstrating the practical value of the proposed constraints.

### Quality

The paper is of high technical quality. The proposed method is well-founded, and the claims are strongly supported by a thorough experimental evaluation.

**Strengths:**
*   The derivation of the CQP for approximate inference is sound and provides a practical way to solve an otherwise intractable problem. This builds upon similar relaxation techniques (e.g., in the AFAMAP paper by Kolter & Jaakkola) but adapts them successfully to the new non-Markovian objective.
*   The experimental design is excellent. The toy data experiment clearly demonstrates how the model's performance gracefully degrades as the provided aggregate information becomes less accurate, which is an important characterization.
*   The evaluation on real-world data is comprehensive, considering two scenarios: one with a synthetically aggregated signal (to control for unmodeled sources) and one with a real-world aggregate mains signal (a more realistic and challenging setting). The use of both an oracle constraint (True Total Consumption) and a practical one (National Total Consumption) effectively bounds the potential of the method and demonstrates its real-world utility.
*   The reported improvements are substantial and statistically significant, which leaves little doubt about the efficacy of the proposed approach.

**Weaknesses:**
*   The paper assumes the model parameters (transition and emission probabilities) are known or pre-trained. While this is a standard approach to isolate the inference problem, it would be valuable to discuss how SACs might be incorporated into a full EM-style learning procedure.
*   The hyperparameter `λi` is set to 1 for all experiments. While the authors note it could be tuned via cross-validation, the paper would be stronger if it included a sensitivity analysis for this parameter or a more principled method for setting it.
*   The CQP solves a relaxed version of the original MAP problem. It is not discussed how far the continuous solutions are from being integral in practice, which would give a better sense of the approximation quality.

### Clarity

The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized.

**Strengths:**
*   The introduction clearly motivates the problem of source identifiability in BSS and presents the core idea of SACs as an intuitive solution.
*   The model development proceeds logically from the baseline AFHMM to the proposed AFHMM+SAC. The notation, though dense in the CQP section, is consistent and well-defined.
*   The connection to posterior regularization in Section 6 is a valuable addition that places the work in a broader theoretical context.
*   The experimental setup and results are described clearly, making the paper's contributions easy to follow and appreciate.

**Weaknesses:**
*   Section 5, which details the CQP formulation, is quite dense. A slightly more high-level, intuitive explanation of the variable transformations before diving into the matrix notation could improve readability for a broader audience.

### Originality

The paper presents a novel and valuable contribution to the field.

**Strengths:**
*   The central idea of using signal aggregate constraints for regularizing factorial HMMs is, to my knowledge, new. It is a simple, interpretable, and powerful form of prior knowledge that seems particularly well-suited for many BSS problems.
*   While the paper builds on the AFHMM framework and related inference techniques, the specific formulation of the problem and its solution as a CQP is a novel contribution.
*   The work clearly distinguishes itself from prior work on constraining HMMs (e.g., AFAMAP's one-state-change-at-a-time constraint or semi-Markov models for duration modeling) by focusing on a different and complementary type of structural prior.

### Significance

The results presented in this paper are highly significant and likely to have a substantial impact.

**Strengths:**
*   The paper addresses a problem of significant practical importance: energy disaggregation. The demonstrated performance improvements over a strong state-of-the-art baseline are large enough to be meaningful for real-world deployment.
*   The idea of SACs is general. While demonstrated on energy data, it could easily be applied to other domains where aggregate statistics are available, such as audio source separation (e.g., total speaking time of different individuals) or bioinformatics (e.g., total expression of genes in a pathway).
*   The work provides a clear recipe for incorporating a very intuitive type of domain knowledge into a popular class of models, which is likely to inspire follow-up work and be adopted by other researchers and practitioners.

### Arguments For and Against Acceptance

**Pro:**
*   Introduces a simple, novel, and highly effective idea (SACs) for addressing the key challenge of identifiability in BSS.
*   Provides a sound and efficient convex relaxation for the resulting non-trivial inference problem.
*   Demonstrates state-of-the-art performance with substantial, statistically significant gains on a challenging and important real-world problem.
*   The paper is exceptionally clear, well-written, and well-structured.

**Con:**
*   The selection of the regularization hyperparameter `λ` is not thoroughly explored.
*   The evaluation is confined to a single application domain, although the method is broadly applicable.
*   The quality of the CQP approximation is not quantitatively assessed.

**Conclusion:**
The strengths of this paper far outweigh its minor weaknesses. It presents a novel, elegant, and impactful contribution that is both technically sound and empirically validated. The proposed Signal Aggregate Constraints provide a powerful new tool for a wide range of blind source separation problems. I strongly recommend acceptance.
