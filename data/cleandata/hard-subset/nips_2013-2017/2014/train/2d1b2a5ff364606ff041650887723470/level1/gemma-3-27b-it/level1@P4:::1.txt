This paper introduces Signal Aggregate Constraints (SACs) to improve blind source separation, specifically within the context of Additive Factorial Hidden Markov Models (AFHMMs) for energy disaggregation. The core idea is to incorporate prior knowledge about the total energy consumption of different appliances as constraints during inference. The authors formulate a convex quadratic program to solve the resulting optimization problem and demonstrate significant performance improvements on a real-world energy disaggregation dataset, outperforming both the original AFHMM and a recent state-of-the-art method.

The paper is well-motivated, addressing the inherent identifiability issues in blind source separation and the importance of incorporating domain knowledge. The introduction clearly explains the problem and the limitations of existing approaches. The related work section provides a good overview of existing methods for energy disaggregation and related techniques for incorporating prior constraints. The formulation of SACs is intuitive and reasonable, and the conversion to a convex quadratic program is a key contribution, enabling efficient inference. The experimental results are compelling, showing substantial improvements on a challenging real-world dataset. The connection to posterior regularization is also a valuable addition.

Strengths:
* **Novelty:** The introduction of Signal Aggregate Constraints is a novel approach to incorporating domain knowledge into blind source separation problems. While prior work explores adding constraints, the focus on aggregate values is a useful and practically relevant contribution.
* **Technical Soundness:** The formulation of the optimization problem and the derivation of the convex quadratic program appear sound. The use of CVX for solving the problem is appropriate. The discussion of the relaxed problem and its implications is clear.
* **Significance:** The problem of energy disaggregation is practically important, and the demonstrated performance improvements are significant. A 45% decrease in normalized disaggregation error compared to the original AFHMM and a 29% improvement over a state-of-the-art method are substantial gains.
* **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The mathematical notation is clear, and the explanations are concise. The connection between the SACs and the posterior regularization framework is also clearly explained.

Weaknesses:
* **Limited discussion of hyperparameter tuning:** While the authors state they simply set λi = 1, a more detailed discussion of hyperparameter tuning and sensitivity analysis would be beneficial. How does the performance vary with different values of λi and δi?
* **Assumptions:** The assumption of known aggregate values (µi0) may not always hold in practice. A discussion of methods for estimating these values from limited data or using alternative forms of prior information would be valuable. While the authors mention national consumption data, the paper does not explore this in detail.
* **Scalability:** While the authors claim that the computational complexity is polynomial, they don’t provide a detailed analysis of its scalability with increasing numbers of appliances and states. A more thorough assessment of computational performance on larger datasets would be helpful.
* **Comparison with other constraint approaches:** While the paper compares with AFAMAP, it would be valuable to also compare with other constraint approaches discussed in the related work section, such as those using k-segment constraints or non-homogeneous HMMs, to further demonstrate the benefits of SACs.

Arguments for acceptance:
* The paper presents a novel and technically sound approach to a practically important problem.
* The experimental results are compelling, demonstrating significant performance improvements over existing methods.
* The paper is well-written and clearly explains the proposed method and its advantages.
* The connection to posterior regularization provides a theoretical justification for the approach.

Arguments against acceptance:
* The paper lacks a detailed discussion of hyperparameter tuning and sensitivity analysis.
* The assumption of known aggregate values may limit the applicability of the method in some scenarios.
* A more thorough assessment of the scalability of the approach would be beneficial.



Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of blind source separation and energy disaggregation. The strengths of the paper outweigh its weaknesses, and I recommend acceptance.
