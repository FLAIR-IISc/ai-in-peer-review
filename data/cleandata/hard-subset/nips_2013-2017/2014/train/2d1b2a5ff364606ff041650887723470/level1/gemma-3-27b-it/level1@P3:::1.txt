This paper introduces Signal Aggregate Constraints (SACs) within an Additive Factorial Hidden Markov Model (AFHMM) framework, specifically targeting the challenging problem of energy disaggregation. The core idea – incorporating prior knowledge about expected total energy consumption of appliances – is intuitive and addresses a key weakness of standard BSS techniques: identifiability. The authors convincingly argue that in many real-world scenarios, approximate knowledge of aggregate signal values is available and can be leveraged to improve source separation.

The paper is well-motivated, clearly articulating the problem of unidentifiability in blind source separation and the need for domain-specific constraints. It builds on a solid foundation of related work in energy disaggregation (Hart, Zoha et al.) and probabilistic modeling (Ganchev et al., Kolter & Jaakkola). The connection to posterior regularization (Ganchev et al.) is insightful, providing a broader theoretical context for the proposed approach. The mathematical formulation, transforming the problem into a convex quadratic program (using CVX), is a significant contribution, enabling efficient inference. The experimental results on the HES dataset demonstrate a substantial improvement over both the baseline AFHMM and a recent state-of-the-art method (Kolter & Jaakkola), validating the effectiveness of SACs.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The introduction of Signal Aggregate Constraints is a novel approach to incorporating prior knowledge into BSS problems, particularly well-suited for applications like energy disaggregation.
* **Technical Soundness:** The mathematical formulation and the derivation of the CQP are rigorous and well-explained. The use of CVX provides a practical means for solving the optimization problem.
* **Empirical Validation:** The results on the real-world HES dataset are compelling, showing significant improvements in disaggregation accuracy compared to existing methods. The inclusion of both experiments with and without mains readings adds robustness to the evaluation.
* **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The authors clearly explain the motivation, methodology, and results.
* **Significance:** The problem of energy disaggregation is important, and improving its accuracy has practical implications for energy conservation and smart home applications. This approach potentially enables more accurate appliance identification and usage monitoring.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Sensitivity to Constraint Values:** The toy data experiment (Section 7.1) highlights a sensitivity to the accuracy of the aggregate constraint values (µ0). While acknowledged, the paper doesn’t deeply explore strategies for robustly estimating these values in real-world scenarios where perfect knowledge is unavailable. Simply using national average consumption (NTC) is a reasonable starting point but might not generalize well to individual households. A discussion of techniques for learning or adapting µ0 would strengthen the work.
* **Limited Exploration of Constraint Generalizations:** The authors mention the possibility of more complex constraints (e.g., time-varying constraints in Section 4.2), but these are not explored experimentally. Investigating these extensions could reveal further performance gains.
* **Computational Complexity Discussion:** While the authors state that AFHMM+SAC's computational cost is similar to other methods, a more detailed analysis of the scaling behavior with respect to the number of states (Ki) and the length of the time series (T) would be beneficial.
* **Comparison with Other Constraint Methods:** The paper primarily compares against AFHMM and AFAMAP. Including a comparison against other methods that incorporate constraints, such as those mentioned in Section 2 (e.g., semi-Markov models, k-segment constraints), would provide a more comprehensive benchmark.
* **Hyperparameter tuning:** The choice of λ (the constraint weight) is somewhat arbitrarily set. A more systematic approach to tuning λ, perhaps via cross-validation, would strengthen the experimental analysis.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and technically sound approach to a challenging problem.
* The experimental results are compelling and demonstrate a significant improvement over existing methods.
* The paper is well-written and clearly explains the methodology and results.
* The work has potential for significant impact in the field of energy disaggregation and beyond.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The sensitivity to constraint values requires further investigation.
* The limited exploration of constraint generalizations leaves room for future work.
* The analysis of computational complexity could be more detailed.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper is a strong contribution and should be accepted to NIPS. The introduction of SACs is a clever and effective way to incorporate prior knowledge into the BSS problem, and the experimental results demonstrate its practical value. While some areas could be strengthened (particularly regarding the sensitivity to constraint values and the exploration of constraint generalizations), the overall quality of the work is high. The paper addresses a relevant problem, presents a novel solution, and provides convincing empirical evidence of its effectiveness.
