

# Review: Signal Aggregate Constraints in Additive Factorial HMMs, with Application to Energy Disaggregation

This paper introduces "Signal Aggregate Constraints" (SACs) as a method to incorporate domain knowledge into blind source separation problems, specifically applied to energy disaggregation. The authors observe that in many BSS applications, the total signal across unknown sources varies widely and often has known expected values. SACs encourage the aggregate values of source signals to be close to specified values, which is incorporated into an Additive Factorial HMM (AFHMM) framework. The paper formulates the constrained optimization problem as a convex quadratic program, enabling efficient inference. The authors demonstrate significant improvements over the baseline AFHMM (45% decrease in normalized disaggregation error) and a state-of-the-art approach (29% improvement) on real-world energy disaggregation data.

The paper builds upon prior work in BSS and energy disaggregation, including the AFHMM framework introduced by Ghahramani and Jordan (1997) and the AFAMAP algorithm by Kolter and Jaakkola (2012). It distinguishes itself from previous constraint approaches (posterior regularization, generalized expectation criterion) by specifically addressing the question of "what types of domain knowledge to include" in BSS problems. The authors also connect their formulation to posterior regularization, showing that SACs can be derived from that framework.

**Strengths:**

1. **Quality and Technical Soundness:** The paper presents a well-motivated approach to a challenging problem. The mathematical derivation of the convex quadratic program is clear and correct. The experimental results are comprehensive, using both synthetic data (to demonstrate robustness to constraint errors) and real-world energy disaggregation data (HES dataset). The improvements over baselines are statistically significant (paired t-tests at 5% level), and the computational complexity is reasonable (polynomial time in number of states and time steps).

2. **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-organized and clearly written. The mathematical notation is consistent and well-defined throughout. The problem setting, model formulation, and experimental evaluation are presented in a logical flow. The authors provide sufficient detail for an expert to reproduce their results.

3. **Originality:** SACs represent a novel constraint type for BSS that addresses a specific gap in the literature (what domain knowledge to incorporate). The connection to posterior regularization provides theoretical grounding. While the application is specific to energy disaggregation, the SAC concept could be applied to other BSS problems, making it a potentially general contribution.

4. **Significance:** Energy disaggregation is an important real-world problem with practical applications in energy conservation and demand management. The demonstrated improvements over state-of-the-art methods (29% better than AFAMAP) are significant and could have tangible impact on real-world energy monitoring systems.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Generalization Demonstration:** While the paper claims SACs could be applied to "many application domains," it only demonstrates the approach on energy disaggregation. The paper would be stronger if it provided a brief example of how SACs might be applied to another BSS domain (e.g., audio processing or computational biology).

2. **Real-World Constraint Estimation:** The paper uses "true total consumption" (TTC) as an oracle in experiments, which isn't realistically available. While the national total consumption (NTC) is a reasonable approximation, the paper doesn't explore how robust the method is to errors in estimating µi0. The synthetic data experiments (Section 7.1) show some sensitivity to constraint errors, but this isn't thoroughly explored in real-world applications.

3. **Comparison with Recent Work:** The paper compares against AFHMM and AFAMAP, but doesn't compare against other recent energy disaggregation methods from NIPS 2013-2014 (e.g., approaches using deep learning or more sophisticated HMM variants). While the 29% improvement over AFAMAP is significant, it would be stronger to show this improvement over a broader set of baselines.

4. **Computational Details:** The paper mentions that AFHMM+SAC has similar computational complexity to AFHMM and AFAMAP, but doesn't provide detailed runtime comparisons or scalability analysis for larger problems (e.g., more appliances or longer time series).

**Pro and Con Acceptance Arguments:**

*Pro:*
- Introduces a novel constraint type (SACs) that addresses a specific, important gap in BSS literature
- Provides clear theoretical derivation and sound experimental validation
- Demonstrates significant performance improvements (29% over state-of-the-art) on real-world data
- Well-written, well-organized, and technically sound

*Con:*
- Limited demonstration of generalizability beyond energy disaggregation
- Doesn't thoroughly address the practical challenges of estimating constraint values in real-world applications
- Doesn't compare against the most recent energy disaggregation methods from the same conference series

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a clear technical contribution to blind source separation by introducing signal aggregate constraints. The theoretical development is sound, the experimental results are compelling, and the application to energy disaggregation is well-motivated and significant. While the paper could be strengthened by demonstrating broader applicability and addressing constraint estimation challenges more thoroughly, these are minor concerns given the paper's clear contribution and significant improvements over state-of-the-art.

The paper meets all the NIPS review criteria: it's technically sound (Quality), clearly written (Clarity), presents a novel constraint approach (Originality), and demonstrates significant improvements for an important application (Significance). The authors carefully address the strengths and weaknesses of their approach, acknowledging that the oracle constraint (TTC) isn't realistic but showing that the NTC approximation still yields substantial improvements.

I recommend acceptance with high confidence. The paper makes a valuable contribution to the field that would be of interest to both the energy disaggregation community and the broader BSS community. The method is generalizable, the theory is sound, and the empirical results are compelling.
