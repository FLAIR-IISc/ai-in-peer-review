

# Peer Review

## Summary and Relation to Previous Work

This paper introduces "signal aggregate constraints" (SACs) for blind source separation (BSS) problems, specifically applied to energy disaggregation. SACs constrain the total signal (sum over time) for each source to be close to a specified value, addressing the fundamental identifiability problem in BSS. The authors incorporate SACs into an additive factorial hidden Markov model (AFHMM), transforming the inference problem into a convex quadratic program (CQP) for efficient approximate inference. The approach is evaluated on both synthetic data and real-world energy disaggregation data, demonstrating significant improvements over the baseline AFHMM and the recent state-of-the-art AFAMAP approach.

This work builds on several key NIPS contributions: the AFHMM framework (Ghahramani & Jordan, 1997), posterior regularization (Ganchev et al., 2010), and the AFAMAP algorithm (Kolter & Jaakkola, 2012) for energy disaggregation. The authors correctly situate their work within the literature, noting that previous approaches to addressing identifiability in energy disaggregation (such as AFAMAP's constraint on state changes) are orthogonal to their SAC approach. While posterior regularization provides a general framework, this paper makes a specific, well-motivated contribution by identifying a particular class of constraints (signal aggregates) that are both practically useful and theoretically tractable.

## Strengths

1. **Novel and Well-Motivated Contribution**: The SAC concept is genuinely novel in the context of BSS and energy disaggregation. The observation that total energy consumption varies widely across appliances and is often known a priori is both insightful and practical. This addresses a fundamental weakness in BSS that has received limited attention.

2. **Strong Theoretical Foundation**: The derivation of the CQP formulation is mathematically sound and clearly presented. The connection to posterior regularization (Section 6) provides theoretical grounding that strengthens the contribution. The authors correctly note that SACs are a specific instance of the more general posterior regularization framework.

3. **Comprehensive Experimental Evaluation**: The paper provides a thorough evaluation using both synthetic data (to demonstrate robustness to errors in constraint specification) and real-world energy data. The use of multiple metrics (NDE and SAE) provides a more complete picture of performance than many prior works. The results are compelling, with a 45% reduction in NDE over AFHMM and 29% over AFAMAP on the energy disaggregation task.

4. **Practical Relevance**: The energy disaggregation problem has significant real-world applications for energy conservation, and the authors demonstrate how their method can be applied using existing national consumption data (NTC) rather than requiring perfect knowledge (TTC). This makes the approach immediately applicable.

5. **Computational Efficiency**: The authors carefully address the computational complexity, noting that AFHMM+SAC scales similarly to AFHMM and AFAMAP, making it practical for real-world applications.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited Analysis of SAC Sensitivity**: While the toy data experiments show that performance degrades as the constraint value diverges from the true aggregate, the paper doesn't provide a detailed analysis of the tolerance to errors in the constraint values. For real-world applications where the constraint values are approximate (as would be the case with NTC), it would be valuable to know how much error is tolerable before performance degrades significantly.

2. **Limited Discussion of Constraint Acquisition**: The paper acknowledges that the constraint values (µi0) must be known in advance, but doesn't explore how these might be obtained in domains where such prior knowledge is limited. While the energy disaggregation example uses national survey data, the paper doesn't discuss general strategies for acquiring such constraints in other BSS domains.

3. **Vague Computational Complexity Analysis**: While the paper states that the CQP has "polynomial time in the number of time steps times the total number of states," it doesn't provide the exact complexity (e.g., O(T^2K^2) or similar), which would be useful for practitioners.

4. **Limited Comparison with Other Constraint Types**: The paper contrasts SACs with AFAMAP's constraint on state changes, but doesn't explore how SACs might complement other constraint types. For example, could SACs be combined with the "at most one chain changes state" constraint to further improve performance?

## Overall Assessment

This is a strong paper that makes a meaningful contribution to the field of blind source separation, with a particularly compelling application to energy disaggregation. The SAC concept is novel, well-motivated, theoretically sound, and demonstrates significant practical improvements over existing approaches. The experimental evaluation is thorough and convincing.

The paper meets all four key criteria for a high-quality NIPS submission:

- **Quality**: The technical contributions are sound, the experimental results are well-supported, and the authors appropriately acknowledge limitations.
- **Clarity**: The paper is well-written and logically organized, with sufficient mathematical detail for replication.
- **Originality**: The SAC concept is distinct from previous work in BSS and energy disaggregation, as evidenced by the thorough literature review.
- **Significance**: The results represent a meaningful improvement in an important application domain with clear potential for real-world impact.

## Recommendation

I recommend acceptance of this paper. The contribution is significant, the methodology is sound, and the results are compelling. The weaknesses noted are not fundamental flaws but rather opportunities for future work that could be addressed in subsequent publications.

The paper would be strengthened by a more detailed analysis of the sensitivity to constraint values and a clearer discussion of how to obtain these constraints in practice. However, these are minor issues that do not detract from the overall quality and impact of the work. The authors have made a clear, novel contribution to a problem of both theoretical and practical importance.
