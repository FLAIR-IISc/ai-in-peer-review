

This paper introduces a novel generative model called Low-Rank Time-Frequency Synthesis (LRTFS) that extends the Gaussian Composite Model (GCM) to directly model the raw signal rather than its STFT. The authors build upon prior work in nonnegative matrix factorization (NMF) for audio processing, addressing a key gap in the literature where previous approaches only modeled the STFT coefficients. The LRTFS model provides a generative framework for signal synthesis with low-rank time-frequency structure, enabling multi-resolution representations that were previously unattainable with standard NMF approaches.

The paper makes several significant contributions. First, it presents a clear generative model that synthesizes signals from low-rank time-frequency components, properly connecting to sparse Bayesian learning while maintaining the low-rank structure. Second, it develops two EM-based algorithms for estimation (MJLE and MMLE), with the MJLE approach being computationally feasible for audio applications. Third, it demonstrates the natural extension to multi-resolution representations, which allows for different time-frequency resolutions across signal components - a capability that is particularly valuable in audio processing where transients and tonal components require different resolutions. The experimental results on music decomposition and speech enhancement show meaningful improvements over baselines, with 6.6 dB average SNR improvement in speech enhancement compared to 6 dB for IS-NMF and 4.6 dB for OMLSA.

The paper is well-organized and generally clear, with good connections to related work. The mathematical derivations are sound, and the authors carefully distinguish their approach from previous work. The experimental section provides sufficient detail to understand the methodology, though some additional information about the hyperparameter selection process would be helpful.

Strengths:
1. The paper addresses a clear gap in the literature by providing a generative model of the raw signal, not just the STFT.
2. The multi-resolution capability is a significant practical contribution that addresses a fundamental limitation of traditional NMF approaches.
3. The MJLE algorithm is computationally efficient and well-suited for audio applications, with clear implementation details.
4. The experimental results are convincing and demonstrate meaningful improvements over relevant baselines.
5. The paper properly contextualizes its contribution within the broader literature on NMF and signal processing.

Weaknesses:
1. The paper does not provide sufficient comparison to more recent approaches beyond the cited baselines (e.g., recent deep learning methods for audio source separation).
2. The justification for why the MJLE approach is preferred over MMLE could be stronger, especially given that the authors acknowledge MMLE's theoretical advantages.
3. The experimental section lacks detailed analysis of the multi-resolution components, making it difficult to fully assess the claimed advantage of different resolutions.
4. Some of the experimental details (e.g., specific choices of λ, training procedures) are not sufficiently described to enable replication.
5. The connection to the broader NIPS community could be strengthened by discussing how the model might generalize to other signal processing domains beyond audio.

The paper's main contribution is the LRTFS model, which successfully bridges the gap between time-frequency synthesis and traditional NMF approaches. The multi-resolution capability is particularly valuable, as it enables more natural modeling of audio signals where different components require different temporal resolutions. The experimental results support the claimed advantages, though more thorough comparisons to contemporary methods would strengthen the paper.

The paper is well-suited for NIPS as it presents a novel probabilistic model with clear theoretical foundations, practical algorithmic contributions, and demonstrated utility in real-world applications. The work is technically sound, well-written, and addresses a significant gap in the literature.

I recommend acceptance. The paper makes a clear, meaningful contribution to the field of signal processing with potential applications beyond audio. The multi-resolution aspect is particularly compelling, as it solves a fundamental limitation of traditional NMF approaches. While some experimental details could be improved, the core contribution is strong and well-executed. The paper would be valuable to the NIPS community as it provides a principled approach to modeling raw signals with low-rank time-frequency structure, with clear implications for audio processing and potentially other signal domains.
