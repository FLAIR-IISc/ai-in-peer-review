## Review of "Low-Rank Time-Frequency Synthesis"

This paper presents a novel generative model for audio signals, extending the Gaussian Composite Model (GCM) by directly modeling the raw signal via a low-rank time-frequency representation. The core idea – synthesizing signals from low-rank factors in the time-frequency domain – is a logical and promising step beyond existing methods that primarily focus on decomposing the STFT magnitude. The authors propose two Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithms for estimation and demonstrate the approach on music decomposition and speech enhancement tasks.

The paper is well-motivated, clearly articulating the limitations of existing NMF-based approaches and the GCM. The connection to sparse Bayesian learning is insightful, highlighting the differences and potential benefits of the proposed model. The extension to multi-resolution representations is a significant strength, addressing a key limitation of traditional spectral factorizations and opening doors to more nuanced signal modeling. The experimental results, while limited in scope, demonstrate promising performance in both music decomposition and speech enhancement, achieving competitive results compared to established methods like IS-NMF and OMLSA. 

However, the paper suffers from some weaknesses. The theoretical derivations, while present, could benefit from more detailed explanations, particularly regarding the connection to sparse Bayesian learning and the derivation of the EM algorithm update rules. The scalability of the MMLE approach is a significant limitation, acknowledged by the authors, and hinders broader applicability. The experimental evaluation is somewhat limited; a more comprehensive comparison with state-of-the-art methods and a more detailed analysis of the hyperparameter sensitivity (specifically λ) would strengthen the claims. The presentation of results could also be improved with more quantitative metrics beyond SNR improvement.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel and theoretically sound approach to signal synthesis.
*   Addresses a key limitation of existing methods by modeling the raw signal.
*   Demonstrates promising results in relevant audio processing tasks.
*   Clear presentation of the model and algorithms.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Limited experimental evaluation.
*   Scalability issues with the MMLE algorithm.
*   Some theoretical derivations could benefit from more detail.



**Overall:** This paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of audio signal processing. While the limitations regarding scalability and experimental evaluation are noteworthy, the novelty and potential of the proposed approach warrant acceptance, with encouragement for the authors to address these limitations in future work. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to improve clarity and expand the experimental analysis.
