

This paper introduces a Low-Rank Time-Frequency Synthesis (LRTFS) model that extends previous work on Gaussian Composite Models (GCM) by modeling the raw signal directly rather than its STFT representation. The authors propose two EM-based algorithms for estimation in their model, with a particular focus on the Maximum Joint Likelihood Estimation (MJLE) approach. The key contributions include a generative model that enables multi-resolution representations (e.g., different time resolutions for tonal and transient components in audio), which is not possible with traditional NMF approaches.

The paper is well-structured and clearly written, with a strong theoretical foundation connecting to prior work on GCM and sparse Bayesian learning. The mathematical derivations appear sound, and the connection to existing literature (e.g., [3, 4, 5]) is appropriately established. The multi-resolution capability is particularly compelling as it addresses a genuine limitation of traditional NMF approaches, where time resolution is fixed during feature computation. The authors convincingly demonstrate this advantage through experiments on music decomposition and speech enhancement.

The strengths of the paper include:
1. The clear conceptual advancement of modeling the raw signal rather than the STFT, which bridges a significant gap in the literature.
2. The well-motivated multi-resolution extension, which has clear practical implications for audio processing.
3. The thorough theoretical development of the LRTFS model and its relation to existing approaches.
4. The relevant experimental validation on two audio applications, with reasonable baselines.

However, the paper has several notable weaknesses that impact its significance and quality:

First, the paper lacks a sufficient comparison to state-of-the-art methods in audio processing. In the speech enhancement experiment, the authors compare to IS-NMF with a single intermediate window size and OMLSA, but these are relatively basic baselines. More recent and advanced methods for speech enhancement (e.g., deep learning approaches) would provide a stronger benchmark. The 0.6 dB improvement over IS-NMF is modest, and without comparison to stronger baselines, the significance claim is weakened.

Second, the computational complexity of the proposed methods isn't adequately addressed. The paper acknowledges that MMLE is computationally prohibitive for large-scale audio applications (Section 3.2), but doesn't provide a clear comparison of the computational requirements of MJLE versus other approaches. For audio applications where signals can be minutes long (T ~ 10^6), the computational cost of the proposed method remains a significant practical concern that isn't properly contextualized.

Third, the paper doesn't sufficiently differentiate its approach from existing signal synthesis methods. While the authors cite [15] (Elad's work), they don't clearly establish how LRTFS differs from or improves upon existing generative models for raw signals. The connection to prior work on generative audio models could be strengthened.

The paper's significance would be greatly enhanced by:
1. A more comprehensive comparison to recent state-of-the-art methods in audio processing.
2. A clearer discussion of the computational trade-offs between LRTFS and existing approaches.
3. A more detailed analysis of the multi-resolution capability's benefits beyond the two presented experiments.

The paper's clarity is excellent, with well-organized sections and clear mathematical notation. The experimental setup is appropriate for the claimed contributions, though the results could be more compelling with stronger baselines. The novelty claim is reasonable, though the paper could better establish how LRTFS differs from related work in generative signal modeling.

In conclusion, this is a technically sound paper that makes a genuine contribution to the field by modeling raw signals with low-rank time-frequency structure and enabling multi-resolution representations. However, the significance is somewhat limited by the modest performance gains over baselines and insufficient comparison to state-of-the-art methods. The paper would benefit from stronger baselines, a more thorough computational analysis, and a clearer differentiation from related work.

The paper is well-suited for NIPS as it presents a novel probabilistic model with clear theoretical grounding and practical applications in signal processing. While not groundbreaking, it makes a solid contribution to the field that should interest both signal processing and machine learning researchers. With the suggested improvements, this paper would be a strong candidate for acceptance.
