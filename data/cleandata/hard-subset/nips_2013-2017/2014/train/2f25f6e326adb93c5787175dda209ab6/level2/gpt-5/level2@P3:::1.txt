Summary
The paper introduces Low-Rank Time-Frequency Synthesis (LRTFS), a generative model of raw signals that imposes a low-rank structure on the variances of synthesis coefficients in a time-frequency frame. In contrast to the Gaussian Composite Model (GCM) for the STFT (which models analysis coefficients with low-rank variances and leads to IS-NMF on power spectrograms), LRTFS models x(t) directly via x = Φα + e with α ∼ Nc(0, diag(v)), where v = vec(WH) encodes a low-rank factorization. The work derives two estimation strategies: (i) maximum joint likelihood estimation (MJLE) using an EM algorithm that alternates Wiener-like shrinkage of α with IS-NMF updates for W and H; (ii) maximum marginal likelihood estimation (MMLE) akin to SBL/ARD with tied variances, which is principled but computationally demanding due to large matrix inversions. A key advantage is a natural multi-resolution extension by concatenating multiple frames (e.g., short/long Gabor frames) under separate low-rank priors. Experiments show qualitative hybrid music decomposition (tonal vs. transient layers) and quantitative speech enhancement, with SNR improvements compared to IS-NMF and OMLSA baselines.

Relation to prior work
The paper builds on IS-NMF and the GCM linking IS divergence to a complex Gaussian model of the STFT. It connects to Sparse Bayesian Learning (Tipping; Wipf & Rao) by viewing α as latent Gaussian coefficients but departs from SBL by tying variances through a low-rank WH, trading sparsity for structured low-rankness. Multi-resolution modeling relates to hybrid/morphological decompositions using sparse synthesis priors and mixed norms; here, the low-rank probabilistic synthesis perspective appears novel.

Quality
- The model is well-motivated and technically sound. The MJLE EM architecture is carefully derived, leveraging Figueiredo–Nowak’s split denoising trick to obtain simple Wiener-type updates for α and standard IS-NMF updates for W, H. The emergence of a log(|α|^2) term in the joint objective is correctly handled through the EM factorization.
- The MMLE derivation follows standard Gaussian identities and ARD-style EM, but is computationally prohibitive at audio scales; this limitation is acknowledged.
- Experimental validation demonstrates feasibility and some benefits, but evaluation choices weaken the evidence: λ is tuned to maximize output SNR in the music task (implying oracle knowledge); comparative baselines are limited; and metrics focus on SNR only.

Clarity
- The exposition is generally clear, with precise notation and connections to prior models (GCM, SBL). The multi-resolution extension is straightforward and easy to implement from the provided updates.
- Some clarifications would improve readability and reproducibility: how β is chosen in practice; the origin and role of the log(|α|^2) term in Eq. (11); initialization strategies and convergence behavior; per-iteration complexity and runtime; and how K is selected.

Originality
- Modeling low-rank variance on synthesis coefficients to obtain a generative time-domain model appears novel relative to prior work on IS-NMF/GCM (analysis coefficients) and SBL (independent variances). The multi-resolution extension under a low-rank synthesis prior is also original and appealing.

Significance
- The approach has potential impact in audio and more broadly in inverse problems where multi-resolution structure is beneficial. By operating directly in the signal domain, it opens paths to phase-consistent modeling and flexible multi-frame synthesis. Current experiments indicate promise but do not yet establish clear state-of-the-art performance.

Strengths
- Conceptually clean bridge from STFT/IS-NMF to a generative time-domain model.
- Simple, scalable MJLE algorithm using only analysis/synthesis operations and IS-NMF updates.
- Seamless multi-resolution extension with distinct low-rank factorizations per frame.
- Clear connection to SBL and discussion of MMLE as a principled alternative.

Weaknesses
- Evaluation is limited: modest number of signals, SNR-only metrics, and λ tuning based on oracle information in at least one experiment.
- Comparisons do not include GCM/IS-NMF with stronger baselines (e.g., multi-frame features or phase-aware models), nor ablations isolating the benefits of synthesis-domain vs. analysis-domain modeling.
- Practical guidance on setting λ, β, K, and initialization is minimal.
- MMLE is not scalable; no approximate scalable alternative is provided beyond pointers to future work.

Suggestions for improvement
Essential
- Adopt principled selection/estimation of λ (evidence-based λ update from EM, cross-validation, or an empirical Bayes prior), and avoid tuning with access to ground-truth SNR.
- Strengthen evaluations: report SDR/SIR/SAR (BSS Eval), PESQ/STOI for speech; include runtimes; test across more noise types and SNRs; provide statistical significance where appropriate.
- Add ablations: single- vs. multi-resolution; analysis-domain (GCM/IS-NMF with Wiener filtering) vs. synthesis-domain LRTFS at matched total dictionary size; effect of K and β.
- Include a GCM baseline with the same windows and Wiener reconstruction to isolate the contribution of synthesis modeling.

Nice-to-have (feasible)
- Provide initialization details and convergence diagnostics; discuss sensitivity to frame redundancy and window choices.
- Explore approximate MMLE variants scalable to long signals (e.g., conjugate gradient within EM, diagonal/low-rank approximations, message passing, or block-coordinate updates exploiting frame locality).
- Release code and pretrained dictionaries to aid reproducibility.

Overall assessment
The paper presents a well-motivated and technically solid framework that extends low-rank time-frequency modeling to a generative synthesis-domain setting and enables multi-resolution structure. The algorithmic contribution is practical and elegant. The main limitation lies in the experimental validation and parameter-setting methodology, which currently understate the potential impact. With strengthened empirical evidence and more principled hyperparameter handling, this work would make a valuable contribution to NeurIPS.
