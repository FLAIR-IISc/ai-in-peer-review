This paper proposes a low-rank time-frequency synthesis (LRTFS) model, extending the Gaussian Composite Model (GCM) [3] to a generative model of the raw signal itself rather than just its STFT. The core idea is to model the signal as a sum of atoms with low-rank structure on the synthesis coefficients, enabling multi-resolution representations. The paper details two expectation-maximization (EM) algorithms for estimation and demonstrates results on music decomposition and speech enhancement.

The work builds directly on previous NIPS publications [3] by addressing a limitation of the GCM – its inability to synthesize the original signal. It connects to broader research in matrix factorization, sparse Bayesian learning [4, 5], and signal decomposition techniques commonly used in audio processing [1, 8]. The extension to multi-resolution representations is a novel contribution, offering flexibility not available in traditional NMF settings.  The related work section is concise but appropriately cites relevant publications.

**Strengths:**

* **Technical Soundness:** The proposed LRTFS model appears technically well-motivated and grounded in probabilistic principles. The derivation of the EM algorithms seems logically sound, and the connection to sparse Bayesian learning provides valuable context.
* **Novelty:** Extending a generative model to operate directly on the raw signal rather than the STFT is a genuine advance. The ability to incorporate multi-resolution representations expands the applicability of this type of modeling.
* **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The notation is clear, and the mathematical derivations are presented in a reasonably accessible manner. The explanation of the relation to sparse Bayesian learning is particularly helpful.
* **Significance:** The ability to generate signals, rather than merely decompose them, has implications for various applications, including audio synthesis, editing, and source separation.  The demonstrated results on music decomposition and speech enhancement, while limited in scope, are promising.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Computational Complexity:** The MMLE approach, while theoretically interesting, is deemed impractical due to its computational cost.  This is acknowledged by the authors, but it’s a significant limitation. Further discussion on potential scalability improvements (e.g., approximations, subsampling) would be beneficial.
* **Limited Experimental Evaluation:** The experimental results are somewhat limited. While the provided examples are illustrative, a more comprehensive evaluation on a wider range of datasets and compared to state-of-the-art methods (beyond IS-NMF and OMLSA) would strengthen the claims of the paper.  Quantitative metrics beyond SNR improvement would also be valuable (e.g., perceptual audio quality metrics).
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:**  The performance appears sensitive to the choice of the λ parameter, which was treated as a hyperparameter and tuned empirically. A more robust method for determining this parameter (e.g., using cross-validation or a theoretical justification) would be desirable.
* **Lack of Theoretical Analysis on Multi-Resolution:** While the multi-resolution extension is presented, there’s a lack of theoretical justification or guidance on how to best select the basis functions and resolutions for different signals or applications.



**Arguments for acceptance:**

* The paper addresses a significant limitation of existing generative models for time-frequency representations.
* The proposed LRTFS model offers a novel and potentially powerful approach to signal synthesis and decomposition.
* The paper is technically sound, well-written, and clearly presented.
* The initial experimental results are promising and suggest the potential for real-world applications.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

* The computational cost of the MMLE approach limits its practical applicability.
* The experimental evaluation is somewhat limited and lacks a thorough comparison to state-of-the-art methods.
* The hyperparameter sensitivity and lack of theoretical guidance on the multi-resolution extension are weaknesses.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a solid and interesting paper with a novel contribution to the field of time-frequency signal processing. While the computational limitations and limited experimental evaluation are concerns, the potential benefits of the proposed LRTFS model outweigh these drawbacks.  

**Recommendation:**

Accept with minor revisions. Specifically, the authors should:

1.  Elaborate on potential strategies to address the computational complexity of the MMLE approach, even if a full-scale implementation is beyond the scope of the paper.
2.  Conduct a more thorough experimental evaluation, including comparisons to a wider range of state-of-the-art methods and the inclusion of perceptual audio quality metrics.
3.  Provide some guidance or theoretical justification for selecting the basis functions and resolutions in the multi-resolution setting.
4. Discuss the sensitivity of the results to the choice of λ and potentially investigate more principled methods for its estimation.
