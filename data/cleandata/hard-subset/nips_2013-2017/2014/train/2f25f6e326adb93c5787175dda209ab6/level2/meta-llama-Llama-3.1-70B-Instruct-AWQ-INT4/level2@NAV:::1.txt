This paper proposes a novel signal synthesis model with low-rank time-frequency structure, called Low-Rank Time-Frequency Synthesis (LRTFS). The authors aim to bridge the gap between time-frequency synthesis and traditional Nonnegative Matrix Factorization (NMF) approaches. The proposed model is an extension of the Gaussian Composite Model (GCM) and allows for multi-resolution representations, which is not possible in traditional NMF settings.

The paper is well-organized, and the authors provide a clear explanation of the proposed model, its relation to sparse Bayesian learning, and the estimation algorithms. The experiments on music decomposition and speech enhancement demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed approach.

**Quality**: The paper is technically sound, and the claims are well-supported by theoretical analysis and experimental results. The authors provide a thorough discussion of the model and its estimation algorithms.

**Clarity**: The paper is clearly written, and the authors provide enough information for the expert reader to reproduce the results. The notation is consistent, and the equations are well-explained.

**Originality**: The paper presents a novel signal synthesis model that extends the GCM and allows for multi-resolution representations. The authors also propose two estimation algorithms, Maximum Joint Likelihood Estimation (MJLE) and Maximum Marginal Likelihood Estimation (MMLE).

**Significance**: The results are important, as they demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed approach in music decomposition and speech enhancement. The model can be extended to various additional penalties on the matrices W or H, and future work will include the design of a scalable algorithm for MMLE.

**Arguments Pro and Con Acceptance**:

Pro:

* The paper proposes a novel signal synthesis model that extends the GCM and allows for multi-resolution representations.
* The authors provide a thorough discussion of the model and its estimation algorithms.
* The experiments demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed approach in music decomposition and speech enhancement.

Con:

* The MMLE algorithm is not scalable and requires the inversion of a large matrix, which can be computationally expensive.
* The authors do not provide a comprehensive comparison of MJLE and MMLE for LRTFS.

**Suggestions for Improvement**:

* Provide a more comprehensive comparison of MJLE and MMLE for LRTFS.
* Investigate the scalability of the MMLE algorithm and propose a more efficient solution.
* Consider applying the proposed approach to other applications, such as multichannel audio source separation.
