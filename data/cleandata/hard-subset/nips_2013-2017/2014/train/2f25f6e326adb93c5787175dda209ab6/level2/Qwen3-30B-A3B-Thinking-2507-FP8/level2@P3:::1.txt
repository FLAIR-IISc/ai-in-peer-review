

The paper introduces Low-Rank Time-Frequency Synthesis (LRTFS), a novel generative model that extends previous work on nonnegative matrix factorization (NMF) of spectrograms to provide a model for the raw signal rather than just its STFT. This work builds upon the Gaussian Composite Model (GCM) described in [3], which established a probabilistic framework for the STFT but did not model the raw signal directly. LRTFS proposes a synthesis model where the signal is represented as a sum of components with low-rank time-frequency structure, enabling multi-resolution representations that were previously infeasible with traditional NMF approaches. The paper presents two estimation methods: maximum joint likelihood estimation (MJLE) and maximum marginal likelihood estimation (MMLE), along with corresponding EM algorithms, and demonstrates the approach on music decomposition and speech enhancement tasks.

The paper demonstrates strong theoretical foundations, clearly connecting LRTFS to sparse Bayesian learning (SBL) and providing a rigorous derivation of the objective functions. The multi-resolution extension in Section 4 is particularly well-motivated and shows how LRTFS naturally accommodates different time-frequency resolutions without additional complexity. The experimental results in Section 5 show meaningful improvements over baselines in both music decomposition (where components correspond to tonal and transient elements) and speech enhancement (where the approach achieves 6.6 dB average SNR improvement compared to 6 dB for IS-NMF and 4.6 dB for OMLSA). The paper's mathematical exposition is clear, with well-structured derivations that should be reproducible.

The main strength of the work is the conceptual advancement in bridging the gap between time-frequency synthesis and traditional NMF approaches. The multi-resolution capability is a significant contribution that opens new possibilities for audio processing applications. The paper effectively demonstrates the practical utility of the approach through well-chosen experiments that showcase the model's ability to separate signal components with different temporal characteristics.

Several aspects could be improved to strengthen the paper. The MMLE approach, while presented, is noted to be computationally infeasible for typical audio processing applications due to the need to invert large matrices (Section 3.2). While this is acknowledged, a more detailed discussion of why MJLE is preferable for practical applications would be valuable. The paper mentions that λ was treated as a hyperparameter rather than estimated, but provides limited justification for this choice beyond noting "poor performance in practice." A more thorough analysis of parameter sensitivity would strengthen the experimental section.

The comparison with baselines in speech enhancement could be expanded. While IS-NMF and OMLSA are compared, the paper does not discuss how LRTFS relates to more recent deep learning approaches for speech enhancement, which have become increasingly dominant in the field since 2013. The paper would benefit from a brief discussion of how LRTFS might complement or differ from these more recent methods.

The paper makes a clear contribution to the signal processing literature, but its significance for the broader machine learning community at NIPS might be somewhat limited. While the mathematical framework is sound and the audio processing applications are well-executed, the paper primarily addresses a specialized signal processing problem rather than introducing a broadly applicable machine learning technique. The authors might strengthen the significance argument by discussing how the multi-resolution capability could be adapted to other domains beyond audio processing.

The paper's clarity is generally good, with clear mathematical notation and well-organized sections. The derivation of the EM algorithms is particularly well-explained, and the connection to sparse Bayesian learning provides helpful context. Some minor improvements could be made to the presentation of experimental details, particularly regarding the selection of hyperparameters and the specific implementation choices for the Gabor frames.

In terms of originality, the paper presents a clear extension of existing work that addresses a well-defined gap in the literature. The multi-resolution aspect represents a significant advance over traditional NMF approaches, and the mathematical formulation of LRTFS is novel. The connection to sparse Bayesian learning provides a useful theoretical bridge, though the paper could more explicitly discuss how LRTFS differs from recent work on structured sparse representations.

The quality of the paper is high, with sound theoretical foundations and well-executed experiments. The main limitation is the narrow scope of the applications, which are primarily focused on audio processing. While this is a valid domain, the paper might benefit from a brief discussion of potential extensions to other signal processing domains to strengthen its significance for the broader NIPS audience.

Overall, the paper presents a technically sound contribution with clear theoretical advances and practical applications in audio processing. The multi-resolution capability is a significant innovation that addresses a genuine limitation of traditional NMF approaches. While the scope is somewhat specialized, the mathematical framework and algorithmic contributions are substantial and would be of interest to researchers working on signal processing and generative models. The paper would be strengthened by more detailed discussion of the practical implementation choices and a broader comparison with recent methods, but the core contribution is solid and well-executed.
