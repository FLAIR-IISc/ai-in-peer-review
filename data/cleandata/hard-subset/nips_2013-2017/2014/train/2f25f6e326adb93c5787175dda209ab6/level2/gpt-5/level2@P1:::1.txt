Summary
This paper proposes Low-Rank Time-Frequency Synthesis (LRTFS), a generative model of raw time-domain signals whose synthesis coefficients have a low-rank time-frequency variance structure. In contrast to prior work (e.g., nonnegative matrix factorization (NMF) of magnitude/power spectrograms and the Gaussian Composite Model (GCM) that is generative for the STFT but not for the signal), LRTFS places a complex Gaussian prior on synthesis coefficients αfn with variance [WH]fn and generates x(t) via a linear combination of time-frequency atoms plus Gaussian residual. The authors connect LRTFS to sparse Bayesian learning (SBL): the variance parameters are tied through a low-rank factorization rather than being free per coefficient, promoting low-rank structure rather than sparsity. Two estimation strategies are presented: (1) Maximum Joint Likelihood Estimation (MJLE) with a computationally efficient EM algorithm using a Figueiredo–Nowak splitting, leading to alternating Wiener-like shrinkage on α and IS-NMF updates on W, H; and (2) Maximum Marginal Likelihood Estimation (MMLE) akin to type-II ML in SBL, but noted as impractical at current scale due to large matrix inversions. A key advantage of working in synthesis is a natural multi-resolution extension using multiple frames with different time-frequency resolutions; the authors derive an MJLE-EM algorithm for this setting. Experiments demonstrate qualitative hybrid decomposition (tonal/transient) on a music excerpt and semi-supervised speech enhancement (speech vs. applause) with moderate SNR improvements over IS-NMF and OMLSA.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The model is clearly defined, and the MJLE-EM derivation is careful. The link to IS divergence emerges naturally from the Gaussian assumptions, mirroring the GCM–IS connection. The multi-resolution extension is straightforward and well motivated. The MMLE formulation is correct, with a clear discussion of computational limitations.
- Algorithms: The MJLE-EM algorithm avoids large matrix inversions and reuses efficient analysis/synthesis operators, which is important for scalability. The requirement β ≤ λ/δΦ for the splitting is noted, though practical guidance is limited.
- Experiments: The demonstrations are relevant and support the claims to some extent. However, evaluations are relatively small-scale and rely mostly on SNR; more comprehensive metrics and baselines would strengthen the empirical case.

Clarity
- The paper is generally well written and organized. The distinction between analysis (STFT) and synthesis coefficients and the conceptual gap being bridged are clearly articulated. The role of the log(|α|^2) term in MJLE is explained and intuitively linked to sparsity.
- The multi-resolution section is clear and easy to follow.
- Some implementation details could be elaborated (e.g., initialization, β and λ selection/scheduling, computational cost per iteration).

Originality
- The main novelty is to move the low-rank statistical structure from analysis coefficients (GCM) to synthesis coefficients and to make the model generative for the raw signal, enabling multi-resolution modeling. The connection to SBL with structured/tied variances is insightful.
- While the ingredients (Gaussian priors, IS-NMF, Wiener filtering, EM splitting) are individually known, the combination and synthesis-centric perspective with low-rank variance is original and relevant.

Significance
- Modeling the raw signal and supporting multi-resolution layers addresses longstanding limitations of NMF/GCM operating on fixed-resolution STFTs. This has clear implications for audio decomposition and enhancement, and possibly other inverse problems.
- The presented results show improvements over standard baselines; the contribution is likely to be used and extended (e.g., to multichannel, additional priors).
- The MMLE direction, although not yet scalable, is promising.

Pros
- Conceptually clean bridge from STFT-domain GCM/IS-NMF to time-domain generative modeling with low-rank time-frequency structure.
- Practical, scalable MJLE-EM algorithm using only analysis/synthesis and NMF subproblems.
- Natural and elegant multi-resolution extension; fits well with audio phenomena (transients vs. sustained components).
- Clear derivations and connections to existing frameworks (GCM, SBL).
- Demonstrated benefits on music decomposition and speech enhancement, with reasonable gains over IS-NMF and OMLSA.

Cons
- Experimental evaluation is limited in scale and breadth; primarily SNR is reported. Lack of metrics such as SDR/SIR/SAR, PESQ/STOI, and statistical significance.
- Baselines could be stronger and better matched: e.g., GCM/IS-NMF with temporal continuity or multi-resolution features; structured NMF and MCA-based methods.
- λ is treated as a tuned hyperparameter and even annealed to a “negligible” value; implications for the β ≤ λ/δΦ condition and for generative correctness are unclear.
- MMLE is compelling but not evaluated due to scaling; no approximate scalable alternative (e.g., conjugate gradients, diagonalization via frame properties, message passing) is implemented.
- Limited discussion on sensitivity to initialization, identifiability across layers, and robustness of W/H estimates.

Questions and suggestions for the authors
- Please clarify how β and λ are chosen/updated in practice. If λ is decreased to near-zero, how do you maintain β ≤ λ/δΦ, and what are the consequences if the condition is violated?
- Provide runtime/complexity measurements for MJLE (single and multi-resolution) and compare to IS-NMF on the same hardware.
- Expand evaluation with standard audio metrics (e.g., BSS_EVAL SDR/SIR/SAR, PESQ/STOI) and report variability across runs (multiple initializations).
- Compare against stronger baselines: GCM/IS-NMF with temporal smoothness, multi-window STFT NMF, and MCA-type hybrid models. An ablation showing the benefit of modeling in synthesis vs. analysis domain under identical frames would be very informative.
- Discuss and, if possible, illustrate the impact of the log(|α|^2) term in MJLE (e.g., ablation or sensitivity study).
- Comment on identifiability and leakage between tonal/transient layers and whether regularizers on H (e.g., smoothness for tonal, sparsity for transient) improve separation.
- Consider approximate scalable MMLE (e.g., iterative solvers for Σ-vector products, Woodbury plus CG, or message passing) to bridge the gap between formulation and practice.

Verdict
This is a thoughtful and well-motivated contribution that advances probabilistic time-frequency modeling by moving to a synthesis perspective with low-rank structure and by enabling multi-resolution representations. While the experiments could be stronger and some implementation aspects clarified, the methodological contribution is solid and relevant to the NeurIPS community. I lean toward acceptance, contingent on addressing the evaluation breadth and parameterization clarifications.
