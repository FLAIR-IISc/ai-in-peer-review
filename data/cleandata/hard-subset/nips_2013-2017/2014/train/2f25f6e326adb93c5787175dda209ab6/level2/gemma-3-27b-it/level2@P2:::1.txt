This paper introduces a novel low-rank time-frequency synthesis (LRTFS) model, extending previous work on Gaussian Composite Models (GCMs) for signal decomposition. The key contribution lies in moving from a generative model of the STFT (as in GCM) to a generative model of the raw signal itself, allowing for multi-resolution representations. The authors propose two Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithms for estimation and demonstrate the approach with music decomposition and speech enhancement experiments.

The paper is well-structured and clearly written. It effectively builds upon prior work in NMF and GCMs, clearly articulating the limitations of existing approaches and positioning LRTFS as a logical next step. The connection to sparse Bayesian learning is insightful, and the discussion of multi-resolution representations is a significant advantage. The mathematical derivations seem sound, and the EM algorithms are presented with sufficient detail. The experimental results are promising, showing competitive performance on both music decomposition and speech enhancement tasks. The inclusion of sound examples in supplementary material is excellent.

A major strength is the novelty of directly modeling the raw signal. This opens doors to benefits that are difficult to achieve with STFT-based approaches, like the easily implemented multi-resolution capability. The authors demonstrate a good understanding of the related literature, referencing relevant works in NMF, GCM, and sparse Bayesian learning.  The connection between the proposed model and existing sparse recovery techniques (specifically its relationship to the first iteration of a LASSO solver) is a valuable insight.

However, the paper has some weaknesses. The scalability of the MMLE approach is a significant limitation, as acknowledged by the authors. While the MJLE algorithm is computationally efficient, the sensitivity to initialization and the potential for convergence to local optima is not thoroughly discussed. The experimental section, while demonstrating promising results, lacks a more comprehensive comparison to state-of-the-art methods, particularly in speech enhancement. The SNR improvements of 6.6 dB while better than OMLSA, are not substantial enough to definitively claim superiority without more rigorous testing across diverse noise conditions and signal types. Furthermore, the selection of hyperparameters (like λ) seems somewhat ad-hoc (“best output SNR”), and a more principled approach to hyperparameter tuning would strengthen the results. The discussion of why estimating λ within the EM framework fails is brief and could benefit from more detail. The phrase “unexpectedly” in section 3.1 feels unnecessarily weak – the appearance of the sparsity-inducing term is a natural consequence of the model formulation.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novelty: The direct synthesis model is a significant advance over existing STFT-based approaches.
*   Technical Soundness: The derivations and algorithms are well-presented and appear mathematically sound.
*   Potential Significance: The multi-resolution capability and the generative modeling approach have the potential to impact various signal processing applications.
*   Clear Writing and Organization: The paper is easy to follow and understand.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Scalability: The MMLE algorithm is impractical for large-scale problems.
*   Limited Comparative Evaluation: The experimental results lack a comprehensive comparison to state-of-the-art methods.
*   Hyperparameter Tuning: The hyperparameter selection process is somewhat ad-hoc.
*   Local Optima: The potential for convergence to local optima in the MJLE algorithm is not fully addressed.



Overall, this is a solid contribution with a clear and compelling idea. While the limitations regarding scalability and the lack of extensive comparative evaluation are concerns, the novelty and potential impact of the proposed approach outweigh these weaknesses.  I recommend acceptance with minor revisions, specifically requesting a more detailed discussion of the convergence properties of the EM algorithm and a more thorough evaluation of the approach against state-of-the-art methods in the experimental section.  A brief discussion of strategies for addressing the scalability issue of MMLE, even if only to acknowledge its complexity, would also be helpful.
