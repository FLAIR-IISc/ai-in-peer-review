- Introduces LRTFS, a generative model that extends NMF of spectrograms to synthesize raw signals with low-rank time-frequency structure.  
- Builds on the Gaussian Composite Model (GCM) but models the raw signal directly rather than only the STFT.  
- Enables multi-resolution time-frequency representations that traditional NMF cannot provide.  
- Proposes two estimation methods: maximum joint likelihood estimation (MJLE) and maximum marginal likelihood estimation (MMLE), with corresponding EM algorithms.  
- Connects LRTFS to sparse Bayesian learning (SBL) and provides rigorous derivations of objective functions.  
- Multi-resolution extension is well-motivated and naturally accommodates different time-frequency resolutions.  
- Experiments on music decomposition and speech enhancement demonstrate practical utility and meaningful improvements.  
- Shows better speech enhancement SNR (6.6 dB) compared to IS-NMF (6 dB) and OMLSA (4.6 dB).  
- Mathematical exposition and derivations are clear and likely reproducible.  
- Main conceptual strength: bridges time-frequency synthesis and traditional NMF, with notable multi-resolution capability.  
- MMLE is noted as computationally infeasible for typical audio applications due to large matrix inversions.  
- The paper insufficiently justifies treating λ as a hyperparameter and lacks thorough parameter sensitivity analysis.  
- Comparison to baselines omits recent deep learning approaches for speech enhancement and should be expanded.  
- Scope is somewhat narrow and primarily addresses signal processing rather than broadly applicable ML advances for NIPS.  
- Could strengthen significance by discussing extensions of multi-resolution capability to other domains beyond audio.  
- Minor presentation improvements needed in experimental details, hyperparameter selection, and Gabor frame implementation choices.  
- Originality is solid: novel LRTFS formulation and multi-resolution advance, but relation to structured sparse representations could be clearer.  
- Overall high quality—sound theory and experiments—though practical implementation discussion and broader comparisons would improve impact.
