Summary and relation to prior work: The paper proposes Low-Rank Time-Frequency Synthesis (LRTFS), a generative time-domain model where synthesis coefficients α over a TF frame have Gaussian priors with low-rank variances WH. This bridges NMF of the spectrogram (e.g., IS-NMF and the Gaussian Composite Model of the STFT) with a generative model of the raw signal, and naturally enables multi-resolution representations by combining multiple TF frames. Two estimation strategies are derived: a scalable EM algorithm for maximum joint likelihood (MJLE) via Figueiredo–Nowak splitting, and a type-II EM (MMLE) akin to Sparse Bayesian Learning (SBL), but with tied variances enforcing low rank rather than independent ARD. Experiments in music hybrid decomposition and semi-supervised speech enhancement show modest but consistent gains over IS-NMF and OMLSA. This connects to prior NIPS work on probabilistic NMF, SBL/ARD, and hybrid/morphological representations.

Quality: The model is well-posed and the MJLE-EM is carefully derived; leveraging Wiener-type updates within an EM loop is sound. The multi-resolution extension is technically straightforward and computationally compatible with standard TF toolboxes. However, the empirical evaluation is limited: small datasets, only SNR metrics, no statistical tests, and λ is tuned post hoc for best SNR (risk of optimistic bias). The MMLE path is acknowledged but not scalable; no complexity/throughput numbers are reported.

Clarity: Generally clear and well-organized; notation is dense but standard. Some implementation details are missing or under-specified (β choice, δΦ estimation, initialization, stopping criteria, handling real-signal Hermitian symmetry).

Originality: The shift from modeling the STFT to a synthesis-time-domain generative model with low-rank TF variance is a meaningful conceptual advance, distinct from both GCM and SBL (tied variances), and the multi-resolution formulation is elegant. The connection to IS-NMF/Wiener filtering as first-iteration analogues is insightful.

Significance: Potentially impactful for audio and more general inverse problems; enabling multi-resolution learning in a probabilistic framework is valuable. Current evidence of improvement (e.g., +0.6 dB over IS-NMF) is encouraging but not yet compelling at scale.

Pros for acceptance:
- Clear, principled generative model that fills a gap between NMF and time-domain synthesis.
- Scalable EM algorithm; straightforward multi-resolution extension.
- Solid grounding in and differentiation from GCM and SBL.

Cons for acceptance:
- Limited empirical scope and metrics; small gains.
- λ tuning via oracle-like selection; MMLE not demonstrated at scale.
- Missing ablations and runtime analyses.

Constructive suggestions:
- Provide ablations (single vs multi-resolution; MJLE vs IS-NMF; effect of K and λ) and report PESQ/STOI for speech.
- Replace oracle λ selection with validation or hierarchical modeling; analyze sensitivity.
- Report computational complexity and wall-clock benchmarks.
- Explore scalable MMLE via conjugate gradients and matrix-free solvers exploiting frame structure; consider variational approximations.
- Release code to improve reproducibility.

Overall: Borderline, leaning accept for its conceptual contribution and practical EM formulation; stronger empirical validation would solidify the case.
