

This paper proposes a novel generative model, Low-Rank Time-Frequency Synthesis (LRTFS), that bridges the gap between traditional nonnegative matrix factorization (NMF) approaches and raw signal modeling. The authors correctly identify that existing Gaussian Composite Model (GCM) approaches model the STFT, not the raw signal, and propose a synthesis model where the signal is represented as a sum of low-rank time-frequency components. The paper makes two key contributions: (1) a generative model that operates directly on the raw signal with low-rank time-frequency structure, and (2) a multi-resolution extension that naturally accommodates different temporal resolutions for different signal components.

The paper demonstrates strong technical quality with a solid theoretical foundation. The authors provide clear derivations of the model and two estimation approaches (MJLE and MMLE), with the MJLE algorithm being computationally feasible for audio applications. The experimental results on music decomposition and speech enhancement are well-designed, with the speech enhancement task showing 6.6 dB average SNR improvement over baselines (IS-NMF: 6 dB, OMLSA: 4.6 dB), demonstrating significant practical value. The multi-resolution capability is particularly compelling for audio applications where different components (tonal vs. transient) require different time resolutions.

The paper's clarity is excellent, with well-organized sections, clear mathematical formulations, and appropriate references to prior work (Févotte, Smaragdis, Elad, etc.). The novelty is well-established through careful comparison with GCM and traditional NMF approaches, and the model's connection to sparse Bayesian learning is insightful. The significance is strong, as the model enables more accurate audio representations while opening doors to multi-resolution analysis, which is difficult to achieve with previous approaches.

Weaknesses include the limited discussion of the computational complexity of MMLE (though the authors appropriately acknowledge it as future work), and the paper would benefit from a more detailed comparison with recent deep learning-based audio decomposition methods. The evaluation could also include perceptual metrics beyond SNR to better assess audio quality.

Strengths: Solid theoretical foundation, clear experimental validation, significant novelty in signal modeling, and practical audio applications. Weaknesses: Limited comparison with state-of-the-art deep learning approaches, and incomplete discussion of MMLE's computational limitations.

Overall, this is a strong contribution to the field of audio signal processing with clear technical merit and practical relevance. The work advances the state-of-the-art in low-rank time-frequency modeling and provides a valuable framework for audio applications. I recommend acceptance.
