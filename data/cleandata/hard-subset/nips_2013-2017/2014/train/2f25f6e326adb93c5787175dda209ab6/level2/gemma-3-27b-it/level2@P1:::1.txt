This paper proposes a novel low-rank time-frequency synthesis (LRTFS) model, extending the Gaussian Composite Model (GCM) [3] to directly model the raw signal instead of just the STFT. The core idea is to represent the signal as a sum of atoms with low-rank structure on the synthesis coefficients, enabling multi-resolution representations. The authors present two expectation-maximization (EM) algorithms for estimation and demonstrate results on music decomposition and speech enhancement.

The paper builds upon a strong foundation of prior work in matrix factorization, particularly NMF [1, 2] and its probabilistic extensions like the GCM [3]. The motivation for moving beyond STFT modeling is clearly stated, and the connection to sparse Bayesian learning [4, 5] is well-explained. The authors correctly identify the limitations of existing methods regarding multi-resolution analysis and demonstrate how LRTFS addresses this. The experimental section tackles relevant audio processing tasks and compares results to established techniques like IS-NMF and OMLSA.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The LRTFS model presents a genuinely novel approach to signal synthesis by directly modeling the raw signal with low-rank time-frequency structure. This is a significant step beyond the GCM, which operated on the STFT.
*   **Generative Model:** The development of a generative model is a valuable contribution. This allows for more principled signal processing and opens avenues for more sophisticated applications.
*   **Multi-Resolution:** The ability to incorporate multi-resolution representations is a key strength, addressing a crucial limitation of traditional NMF methods. This is particularly relevant for audio signals with varying time-frequency characteristics.
*   **Clear Presentation:** The mathematical formulation is clear and well-motivated. The connection to sparse Bayesian learning and the derivation of the EM algorithms are well-explained.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The experiments on music decomposition and speech enhancement demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed approach, with competitive results compared to existing methods.
*   **Computational Efficiency:** The MJLE algorithm's efficiency, avoiding complex matrix inversions, is a practical advantage.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Scalability of MMLE:** The authors acknowledge the scalability issues with the MMLE algorithm. While presented for completeness, its practical limitations hinder its applicability to larger datasets. More detailed discussion of these limitations and potential future remedies (e.g., online or stochastic versions) would be helpful.
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The sensitivity of the results to the choice of the hyperparameter λ isn’t fully explored. While the authors mention gradually decreasing λ, a more systematic analysis of its impact on performance would be valuable.
*   **Limited Baseline Comparison:** The comparison to OMLSA in speech enhancement is somewhat limited. Exploring other state-of-the-art speech enhancement algorithms would provide a more comprehensive evaluation.
*   **Lack of Theoretical Justification for Parameter Choices:** The choice of K (number of latent components) in both experiments is not explicitly justified.  A discussion on how to determine optimal K values would be beneficial.
*   **Reproducibility:** Providing code or detailed implementation instructions would significantly enhance reproducibility.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and well-motivated approach to signal synthesis with significant potential impact.
*   The LRTFS model addresses a key limitation of existing methods by enabling multi-resolution representations.
*   The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed approach on relevant audio processing tasks.
*   The paper is clearly written and well-organized, making it easy to understand.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The scalability issues with the MMLE algorithm limit its practical applicability.
*   The limited baseline comparison and lack of detailed analysis of hyperparameter sensitivity weaken the empirical evaluation.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with a novel and promising contribution to the field of time-frequency signal processing. While the scalability issues with MMLE and the limited baseline comparisons are valid concerns, the strengths of the paper outweigh the weaknesses. The proposed LRTFS model represents a significant step forward in signal synthesis and opens up new possibilities for multi-resolution audio processing.

**Recommendation:**

Accept. I recommend accepting this paper for NIPS 2014. I encourage the authors to address the weaknesses mentioned above in future work, particularly focusing on improving the scalability of the MMLE algorithm and providing more detailed experimental analysis.
