

This paper proposes Low-Rank Time-Frequency Synthesis (LRTFS), a novel generative model that extends the Gaussian Composite Model (GCM) by modeling the raw signal directly rather than its STFT. While previous work (Févotte et al., 2009) established GCM as a generative model for STFT with low-rank structure, LRTFS bridges this gap by formulating a synthesis model where the signal is expressed as a weighted sum of time-frequency atoms with low-rank variance structure. The paper builds on sparse Bayesian learning (SBL) frameworks while introducing a key distinction: LRTFS imposes a low-rank constraint on the synthesis coefficients' variance, unlike SBL's unstructured variances. The authors present two EM-based estimation algorithms (MJLE and MMLE), with the former being computationally efficient for audio applications. A significant contribution is the natural incorporation of multi-resolution representations, enabling different time-frequency resolutions for different signal components (e.g., transients vs. tonal elements in music).

Strengths: The paper addresses a meaningful gap in signal modeling, providing a theoretically sound generative model of the raw signal. The multi-resolution capability is well-motivated and demonstrated effectively in experiments, with clear improvements over baselines in both music decomposition (26 dB output SNR) and speech enhancement (6.6 dB average SNR improvement). The technical development is sound, with careful derivation of the EM algorithms and proper connection to related work in time-frequency analysis. The writing is clear and well-organized, with sufficient detail to reproduce results.

Weaknesses: The MMLE approach, while theoretically interesting, is not scalable for audio applications as acknowledged by the authors, limiting its practical utility. The paper could better differentiate its multi-resolution approach from existing techniques like the hybrid/morphological model in Smaragdis & Raj (2007) and Daudet & Torrésani (2006). While the experiments are convincing, the significance to the broader AI community could be strengthened by discussing potential applications beyond audio processing.

Arguments for acceptance: Significant theoretical contribution that fills a clear gap in signal modeling; strong empirical results with meaningful improvements; well-motivated multi-resolution capability; technically sound and well-written. Arguments against: Limited scalability of one estimation approach; potential over-reliance on audio domain examples for significance. Overall, this is a strong contribution to signal processing with clear relevance to NIPS through its generative modeling and optimization aspects.
