- Proposes Low-Rank Time-Frequency Synthesis (LRTFS): a generative time-domain model synthesizing signals from TF atoms with complex Gaussian synthesis coefficients whose variances have low-rank WH structure.  
- Bridges gap between NMF of spectrograms and time-domain modeling by making x(t) generative (unlike prior GCM on STFT coefficients).  
- Derives a scalable EM algorithm for maximum joint likelihood estimation (MJLE) using a latent-variable trick, yielding alternating “Wiener-like” shrinkage on α and IS-NMF updates on W,H.  
- Presents a maximum marginal likelihood (type-II/SBL-like) approach that is principled but currently not scalable.  
- Introduces a straightforward multi-resolution extension (multiple frames/short+long Gabor) keeping a probabilistic low-rank structure.  
- Empirical results (hybrid music decomposition, semi-supervised speech enhancement) show modest but consistent SNR gains over IS-NMF and OMLSA.  
- Modeling is mathematically sound and well-motivated; EM derivation is careful and exploits efficient Φ/Φ* operations.  
- MMLE is not demonstrated at scale; λ is treated as a tuned hyperparameter (even annealed toward zero) rather than robustly estimated.  
- Quantitative evaluation is limited: only SNR metric, small test set, no statistical tests, no modern baselines or ablation studies.  
- Convergence behavior, role/schedule of β, and initialization details for W,H,α are under-specified.  
- Paper lacks specified computational complexity, runtime, stopping criteria, and handling of Hermitian symmetry for real signals.  
- Novelty: moving low-rank modeling from analysis coefficients to synthesis coefficients in time domain and the clean multi-resolution probabilistic formulation is original.  
- Significance: could influence audio modeling by enabling coherent time-domain, multi-resolution low-rank synthesis with practical algorithms, but broader benchmarks are needed.  
- Arguments for acceptance: conceptual advance, elegant scalable EM linked to Wiener/IS-NMF, useful multi-resolution extension, empirical improvements over classical baselines.  
- Arguments against acceptance: limited evaluation and metrics, reliance on hand-tuned λ/β, undeveloped computational MMLE contribution, and missing details on complexity, initialization, and convergence diagnostics.
