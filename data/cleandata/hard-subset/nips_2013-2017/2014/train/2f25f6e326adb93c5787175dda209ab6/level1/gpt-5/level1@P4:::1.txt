Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes Low-Rank Time-Frequency Synthesis (LRTFS), a generative model of raw time-domain signals that imposes a low-rank structure on the variances of complex synthesis coefficients. Formally, the signal x(t) is synthesized as a linear combination of time-frequency atoms φfn(t) with complex Gaussian coefficients αfn ∼ Nc(0, [WH]fn), where WH is a low-rank nonnegative factorization. This bridges the gap between traditional IS-NMF of magnitude spectrograms and the Gaussian Composite Model (GCM) for STFT coefficients [3]: unlike GCM, which is a probabilistic model of analysis coefficients yfn, LRTFS is a generative model of x(t) itself. The paper provides two estimation schemes: (i) a scalable Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm for maximum joint likelihood estimation (MJLE) that uses an auxiliary-variable architecture akin to Figueiredo & Nowak [6], yielding simple Wiener-like updates for α and standard IS-NMF updates for W and H; (ii) a maximum marginal likelihood (MMLE, “type-II ML”) approach conceptually similar to sparse Bayesian learning (SBL) [4,5], but computationally heavy due to required matrix inversions, and not used in experiments. The model naturally extends to multi-resolution by stacking multiple time-frequency frames and learning separate low-rank structures per frame, which is difficult in conventional NMF pipelines.

Experiments demonstrate (1) a hybrid music decomposition into tonal/transient layers, and (2) semi-supervised speech enhancement using pre-trained speech dictionaries and noise dictionaries learned on-the-fly. Improvements over IS-NMF and OMLSA [14] are reported in SNR.

Relation to prior work is clear: IS-NMF and GCM [3], SBL [4,5], EM augmentation [6], hybrid/morphological audio models [8–10], supervised/semi-supervised source separation [11]. The key novelty is shifting low-rank modeling from analysis coefficients to synthesis coefficients of x(t), enabling multiresolution synthesis with the same low-rank prior.

Strengths
- Conceptual advance: a principled, probabilistic, signal-level counterpart to GCM/IS-NMF with a clear link to SBL, and a straightforward multiresolution extension.
- Algorithmic simplicity and scalability: the MJLE EM requires only Φ and Φ∗ operations (analysis/synthesis) and standard IS-NMF updates; no large matrix inversions.
- Clear derivations of MJLE updates, including the emergence of a log(|α|2) term in the objective that encourages shrinkage.
- Practical relevance: multi-resolution modeling is natural and well motivated for audio signals; results show competitive or better SNR than IS-NMF and OMLSA baselines.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Hyperparameter selection: in the music experiment, λ is selected to maximize output SNR, which uses ground truth and overestimates practical performance. Please adopt a validation criterion independent of test targets or report sensitivity analyses for fixed λ choices.
- MMLE scalability: while conceptually valuable, the MMLE algorithm is not scalable and is not evaluated. Either provide a reduced-scale demonstration or defer MMLE to a separate study.
- Limited evaluation: only SNR improvements are reported on small test sets. For speech, adding perceptual metrics (e.g., PESQ, STOI) and larger, statistically analyzed test suites would strengthen claims. For the music example, objective layer-separation metrics or ablations (single vs multi-resolution) would clarify the benefit.
- Missing details: initialization of W, H, and α; choice of β (e.g., β = λ/δΦ) in practice; normalization to address scale ambiguities between W and H; stopping criteria; runtimes and memory vs IS-NMF; training procedure for Wtrain (trained on what representation and with which objective?).
- Identifiability and convergence: no discussion of identifiability of W/H/α under redundant frames, nor convergence guarantees beyond monotonic likelihood decrease. Clarify whether column normalization or priors are used to mitigate scale/permutation ambiguities.
- Real-signal handling: the real-valued case and Hermitian constraints are mentioned but not detailed operationally in the algorithms; please specify the implemented procedure.
- Reference formatting is incomplete; several entries lack years/page numbers.

Evaluation per criterion
- Quality: The modeling and MJLE algorithm are technically sound, building on well-established Gaussian models, IS-NMF, and EM augmentation. Claims are supported by derivations and experiments. However, the λ selection protocol in the music example is problematic, and MMLE is not empirically validated. More comprehensive evaluation and complexity reporting would improve rigor.
- Clarity: Overall clearly written with explicit equations and algorithmic steps. Some notation is dense; minor typos exist; implementation details (initialization, β, normalization) should be added for reproducibility. The derivation leading to the log(|α|2) term could be summarized or referenced in an appendix.
- Originality: Modeling low-rank structure on synthesis coefficients of x(t) (vs analysis coefficients) and extending it to multi-resolution within a unified EM-NMF scheme is novel and nontrivial. The connection to SBL with tied variances is insightful.
- Significance: Potentially impactful for audio source separation and enhancement, especially for multi-resolution modeling. Demonstrated gains over IS-NMF/OMLSA are modest but meaningful; broader evaluations would better establish significance.

Pros for acceptance
- Introduces a principled, practical generative model at the signal level with clear advantages over STFT-only models.
- Scalable EM algorithm that integrates seamlessly with existing IS-NMF tooling.
- Natural multiresolution extension with demonstrated utility.
- Solid connections to established probabilistic frameworks (GCM, SBL, EM).

Cons against acceptance
- Evaluation is limited in scope and metrics; λ selection uses oracle information in one experiment.
- MMLE contribution is theoretical only; no scalable implementation or empirical study.
- Missing implementation details and complexity measurements hinder reproducibility.
- Some theoretical aspects (identifiability, convergence characterization) are not addressed.

Questions and requests for clarification
- How are W and H initialized and normalized to mitigate scale ambiguity? What stopping criteria are used?
- What β is used in practice? Is β adapted as λ is annealed in the speech experiment?
- How are real signals handled (Hermitian symmetry) in the implementation?
- How were the pre-trained speech dictionaries obtained (objective, data preprocessing, training model)?
- Can you report runtimes and memory vs IS-NMF, and provide sensitivity analyses for λ and K?
- Can you add perceptual metrics for speech, larger test sets, and an ablation isolating the benefit of multiresolution?

Overall, this is a technically solid and novel contribution with practical relevance. Strengthening the experimental methodology and adding implementation details would substantially improve the paper.
