Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes Low-Rank Time-Frequency Synthesis (LRTFS), a generative model of raw signals that imposes a low-rank structure on the variances of synthesis coefficients over a time-frequency (t-f) dictionary. In contrast to the Gaussian Composite Model (GCM) for the STFT (where the variance of the analysis coefficients is low-rank and the likelihood reduces to IS-NMF on the spectrogram), LRTFS models x(t) directly as a linear combination of t-f atoms with complex Gaussian synthesis coefficients whose variances factorize as WH. This closes the gap between NMF (typically applied to magnitude spectrograms) and a true generative model for the waveform and naturally supports multi-resolution representations by stacking multiple frames (e.g., short-window transient and long-window tonal dictionaries).

Two estimation strategies are proposed: (1) maximum joint likelihood estimation (MJLE) via an EM algorithm based on a denoising split trick (Figueiredo & Nowak), which alternates between a Wiener-like shrinkage for α and IS-NMF updates for W and H; and (2) maximum marginal likelihood estimation (MMLE, type-II ML akin to Sparse Bayesian Learning—Tipping; Wipf & Rao), where α is marginalized and the E-step computes posterior moments of α. The MMLE algorithm is principled but computationally heavy (involves inversion of large matrices), so experiments focus on MJLE. The paper demonstrates multiresolution modeling and reports audio experiments: (i) a tonal/transient/music decomposition with qualitative inspection and SNR; and (ii) semi-supervised speech enhancement using pre-trained speech dictionaries and learned noise, showing modest average SNR gains over IS-NMF and OMLSA.

Quality
Strengths:
- The generative formulation for the raw signal with low-rank variance over synthesis coefficients is technically sound and conceptually clean. It clearly connects to GCM/IS-NMF and to SBL.
- The MJLE EM is computationally practical: no large matrix inversions, only frame analysis/synthesis and standard IS-NMF multiplicative updates. The monotonic decrease argument is convincing.
- The multiresolution extension is straightforward and elegant, with joint estimation across layers.
- The link to Wiener reconstruction of latent components is well established, and the posterior splitting across k is exploited efficiently.

Weaknesses:
- The objective (Eq. 11) includes a log(|α|^2) term; although it arises from the Gaussian prior likelihood, the derivation is only sketched. An appendix with full derivation would improve rigor.
- The choice and impact of β in the E-step (requiring β ≤ λ/δΦ) is critical; guidelines on selecting β, estimating δΦ for redundant frames, and the sensitivity of convergence/performance are missing.
- The experiments are limited in scope and reporting. For denoising/separation, SNR alone is insufficient; standard metrics such as SDR/SIR/SAR (BSS_EVAL), PESQ, and STOI/ESTOI should be included.
- In speech enhancement, λ is annealed by hand; the schedule is not specified, and the paper notes that joint estimation of λ performed poorly. This raises concerns about robustness and hyperparameter sensitivity.
- Baselines are limited. A fairer comparison would include: supervised IS-NMF with Wiener reconstruction at similar dictionary sizes; complex NMF/phase-aware models; multi-resolution NMF or MCA-like baselines; and the GCM with time-domain reconstruction to assess the gain from synthesis vs. analysis modeling.

Clarity
- The paper is generally well written and organized. The synthesis vs. analysis distinction, the probabilistic graphical interpretation, and the EM steps are clearly presented.
- Some notation is dense (switching between (f,n) and m) and could be clarified with a concise mapping table. A brief recap of t-f frame properties (tightness, redundancy) would help readers outside audio.
- Provide practical details: initialization (W, H, α), stopping criteria, runtime per iteration, and implementation choices (window sizes are given; add frame redundancy and normalization).

Originality
- The core novelty is modeling low-rank structure on synthesis coefficients to obtain a generative model of the waveform, and enabling multiresolution synthesis under a common probabilistic umbrella. This is distinct from IS-NMF/GCM (analysis-coefficient modeling) and from SBL (independent variances per coefficient). The connection to EM denoising and to Wiener filtering is insightful.
- While multiresolution sparse synthesis exists (e.g., MCA), the low-rank variance coupling via WH in the synthesis domain appears new and valuable.

Significance
- The approach opens interesting avenues: phase-consistent generative modeling, seamless multi-resolution, and potential extensions to inverse problems and multichannel audio. The idea is likely to be reused.
- That said, empirical evidence is preliminary. Demonstrating clear, statistically significant gains over strong baselines on standard datasets would strengthen impact. A scalable MMLE (promised as future work) could further improve robustness and interpretability.

Pros for acceptance
- Conceptually neat bridge from IS-NMF/GCM to a generative time-domain model; multiresolution falls out naturally.
- Practical EM algorithm with affordable computation for long signals.
- Clear connections to SBL and Wiener filtering; potential broad relevance beyond audio.

Cons for acceptance
- Experimental validation is limited and uses ad hoc hyperparameter tuning; improvements over baselines are modest.
- Missing comparisons to stronger, phase-aware and/or supervised NMF baselines; no standard speech/intelligibility metrics.
- Some derivations and implementation choices (β, λ, initialization) are underspecified, affecting reproducibility.

Suggestions for improvement
- Add an appendix deriving Eq. (11), clarifying the origin of the log term and ensuring no hidden priors.
- Provide guidance and ablations for β and λ (including the annealing schedule), and report sensitivity.
- Include stronger baselines: supervised IS-NMF with Wiener reconstruction at matched K; complex NMF/phase-constrained models; multiresolution MCA; GCM with time-domain reconstruction.
- Report SDR/SIR/SAR, PESQ, and STOI/ESTOI; include confidence intervals across multiple runs.
- Provide runtime and scalability numbers; show how redundancy and window choice affect δΦ and convergence.
- Release code and trained dictionaries to foster reproducibility.

Recommendation: Weak accept. The paper presents a novel and technically solid generative synthesis model with a practical algorithm and a compelling multiresolution extension. With stronger empirical comparisons and clearer hyperparameter guidance, it could be a solid contribution to NIPS.
