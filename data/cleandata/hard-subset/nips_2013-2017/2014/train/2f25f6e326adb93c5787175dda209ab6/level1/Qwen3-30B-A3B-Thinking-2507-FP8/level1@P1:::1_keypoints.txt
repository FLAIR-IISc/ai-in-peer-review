- Introduces Low-Rank Time-Frequency Synthesis (LRTFS) that models the raw signal directly with low-rank time-frequency variance structure.  
- Extends the Gaussian Composite Model (GCM) framework from STFT coefficients to signal synthesis using basis elements and synthesis coefficients.  
- Presents two estimation approaches: Maximum Joint Likelihood Estimation (MJLE) and Maximum Marginal Likelihood Estimation (MMLE).  
- Demonstrates applications in music decomposition and speech enhancement with clear SNR-based evaluation.  
- Key innovation: natural multi-resolution representation enabling separation of transient and tonal components.  
- Builds on prior NMF/GCM work and relates to sparse Bayesian learning (SBL), though distinctions are not fully elaborated.  
- Theoretical derivations and EM algorithms are carefully developed and technically sound.  
- Experimental results are thorough with appropriate baselines and meaningful improvements (≈0.6 dB over IS-NMF).  
- Authors acknowledge computational limitations of MMLE for large-scale audio.  
- Main weakness: lack of theoretical analysis of convergence properties for the proposed EM algorithms.  
- Insufficiently detailed distinction between LRTFS and sparse Bayesian learning to fully establish novelty.  
- Paper does not clearly articulate broader significance beyond audio processing and lacks comparison to other recent low-rank synthesis models.  
- Clarity and organization are strong; mathematical derivations are accessible though some sections need more detail.  
- Supplementary materials (audio examples) would be valuable but are not provided here.  
- Strengths summarized: clear extension of GCM, novel multi-resolution capability, solid experiments, and careful model development.  
- Weaknesses summarized: limited convergence theory, superficial SBL connection, narrow articulation of broader impact, and impractical MMLE for large audio.  
- Arguments for acceptance: fills literature gap, practical multi-resolution contribution, meaningful empirical gains, and good positioning in prior work.  
- Arguments against acceptance: insufficient differentiation from related sparse synthesis work, limited general ML relevance, and need for stronger theoretical contributions.  
- Recommendation: accept with minor revisions—improve SBL comparison, provide convergence analysis, and better articulate broader machine learning significance.
