This paper proposes a novel generative model, Low-Rank Time-Frequency Synthesis (LRTFS), extending the Gaussian Composite Model (GCM) by directly modeling the raw signal instead of just its STFT. The core idea is to represent the signal as a sum of atoms with low-rank structure on the synthesis coefficients. The authors provide EM algorithms for estimation and demonstrate the approach on music decomposition and speech enhancement, showcasing its ability to handle multi-resolution representations.

The paper builds upon a strong foundation of prior work in matrix factorization, particularly NMF and the GCM [3]. It addresses a key limitation of the GCM—its inability to generate the raw signal—and presents a logical progression from spectral decomposition to signal synthesis. The connection to sparse Bayesian learning [4, 5] is well-articulated, highlighting the differences in coefficient structure and their implications. The multi-resolution extension is a valuable addition, addressing the varying time-frequency characteristics of audio signals, and builds upon existing hybrid models [8, 10].

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The LRTFS model is a significant extension to the GCM, addressing a fundamental limitation and opening up possibilities for signal synthesis and multi-resolution analysis.
* **Technical Soundness:** The mathematical formulation is clear and well-motivated. The derivation of the EM algorithms, while complex, appears logically sound and builds upon established techniques [6].
* **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The introduction clearly explains the motivation and contribution. The equations are well-presented and the notation is consistent.
* **Significance:** The ability to synthesize signals directly from low-rank time-frequency representations has potential applications in various audio processing tasks, beyond the demonstrated music decomposition and speech enhancement. The multi-resolution capability is particularly valuable.
* **Experiments:** The experiments, though somewhat limited in scope, provide initial evidence of the effectiveness of the approach. The comparison to IS-NMF and OMLSA is helpful in demonstrating the benefits of the proposed method.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Scalability of MMLE:** The authors acknowledge that the MMLE algorithm doesn't scale well to large dimensions, and the implementation isn't presented. While understandable, this limits the completeness of the work. The reliance solely on MJLE could be a concern, as MMLE is often more robust.
* **Hyperparameter Tuning:** The influence of the hyperparameter λ isn’t discussed in great detail. The claim that it was “gradually decreased” in the speech enhancement experiment could benefit from more precise explanation and justification.
* **Limited Experimentation:** The experiments are somewhat limited. A more extensive evaluation with different datasets, noise types, and performance metrics would strengthen the claims. The experiments focus primarily on audio, and exploring applications to other signal types could broaden the impact.
* **Lack of Theoretical Analysis:** While the technical formulation is sound, a more in-depth theoretical analysis of the properties of the LRTFS model (e.g., identifiability, convergence guarantees) would be beneficial.
* **Computational Complexity:** Though the MJLE algorithm appears efficient, a detailed analysis of its computational complexity compared to existing methods would be valuable.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and technically sound generative model for signal synthesis with strong theoretical motivations.
* It addresses a significant limitation of existing approaches (GCM) and opens up new avenues for research.
* The experiments, while limited, demonstrate the potential of the approach.
* The clarity of the writing and organization make the paper accessible to a broad audience.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The scalability issues with MMLE are a concern.
* The experimental evaluation could be more comprehensive.
* The lack of a deeper theoretical analysis limits the understanding of the model's properties.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a valuable contribution to the field of time-frequency signal processing. The proposed LRTFS model is a significant advancement over existing methods, and the authors have presented a convincing case for its potential. While there are some weaknesses, particularly regarding scalability and experimental evaluation, the strengths of the paper outweigh these concerns. 

**Recommendation:**

**Accept (with minor revisions)**. I recommend accepting this paper with the request that the authors address the following points:

1.  Provide more detail regarding the tuning of the hyperparameter λ and justify the chosen values.
2.  Expand the experimental evaluation with additional datasets, noise types, and performance metrics.
3.  Discuss the computational complexity of the MJLE algorithm in more detail.
4.  If possible, provide some theoretical insights into the properties of the LRTFS model.
