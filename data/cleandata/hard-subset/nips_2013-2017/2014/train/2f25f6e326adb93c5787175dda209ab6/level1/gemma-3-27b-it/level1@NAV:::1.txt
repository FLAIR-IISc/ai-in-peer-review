## Review of Low-Rank Time-Frequency Synthesis

This paper presents a novel generative model, Low-Rank Time-Frequency Synthesis (LRTFS), for single-channel signal decomposition, building upon previous work leveraging low-rank factorization of time-frequency transforms. Specifically, it extends the Gaussian Composite Model (GCM) [3] – which provides a probabilistic model for the STFT – to a generative model for the raw signal itself. The key contribution lies in formulating a synthesis model with low-rank time-frequency structure, enabling multi-resolution representations not possible with traditional NMF. Two Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithms are proposed for estimation, and the approach is demonstrated on music decomposition and speech enhancement tasks.

The paper clearly situates itself within the existing literature on matrix factorization, NMF, and probabilistic latent factor models for signal processing. It appropriately acknowledges the limitations of earlier NMF approaches (e.g., discarding phase information) and the GCM’s incompleteness as a signal generator. The connection to sparse Bayesian learning (SBL) is also well-articulated, highlighting the differences in variance modeling. The multi-resolution extension is a particularly interesting aspect, addressing a common need in audio processing for varying time-frequency resolutions. 

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The LRTFS model is a genuine extension of existing generative models for time-frequency representations, addressing a gap in the field.
* **Theoretical Soundness:**  The derivation of the model and EM algorithms appears mathematically well-founded, referencing relevant work in probabilistic modeling and optimization.
* **Multi-Resolution Capability:** The ability to handle multiple time-frequency resolutions is a significant advantage and opens up opportunities for more nuanced signal processing.
* **Empirical Validation:** Demonstrating the approach on both music decomposition and speech enhancement tasks provides initial evidence of its effectiveness.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Computational Complexity:**  The MMLE approach, while presented, is deemed impractical due to its computational cost. The authors acknowledge this, but it weakens the overall contribution.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The experiments reveal a dependence on hyperparameter tuning (λ), with limited discussion of strategies for choosing optimal values.
* **Limited Baselines:** The comparison to IS-NMF and OMLSA in the speech enhancement task is somewhat limited. More contemporary or state-of-the-art speech enhancement methods should be considered for a more thorough evaluation.
* **Clarity of Notation:** While generally clear, some of the notation (particularly related to indices and vector/matrix representations) could be made more consistent.




**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound generative model for signal synthesis.
* The multi-resolution capability is a significant contribution to the field.
* The empirical results, while preliminary, are promising.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

* The computational limitations of the MMLE approach.
* The limited scope of the experimental evaluation.
* The sensitivity to hyperparameter tuning requires further investigation.

**Overall:**

I believe this paper represents a solid contribution to the field. While the computational concerns and limited experimental scope are valid weaknesses, the novelty and potential of the LRTFS model are compelling.  I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions, specifically requesting a more thorough exploration of hyperparameter selection strategies and a broader comparison against state-of-the-art methods in the experimental evaluation.
