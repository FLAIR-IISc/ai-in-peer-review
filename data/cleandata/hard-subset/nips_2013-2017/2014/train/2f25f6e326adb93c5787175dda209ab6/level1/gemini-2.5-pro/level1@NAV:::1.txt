This paper introduces Low-Rank Time-Frequency Synthesis (LRTFS), a novel generative model for time-domain signals. The core idea is to represent a signal as a linear combination of time-frequency atoms, where the synthesis coefficients are modeled as complex Gaussian variables whose variances are constrained by a low-rank nonnegative matrix factorization (NMF). This work provides a principled extension to the Gaussian Composite Model (GCM), which established a probabilistic link between NMF with the Itakura-Saito divergence and a generative model of the Short-Time Fourier Transform (STFT). Crucially, LRTFS models the raw signal itself, not its transform, which is a significant conceptual step. The authors also draw a clear connection to Sparse Bayesian Learning (SBL), distinguishing their model through its structured low-rank prior.

The primary strength of the paper is its originality and significance. By shifting the low-rank model from the analysis domain (STFT coefficients) to the synthesis domain (synthesis coefficients), the authors create a more complete generative framework. A major practical benefit of this synthesis approach, which is well-argued and demonstrated, is the natural ability to incorporate multi-resolution representations. This is a difficult task for standard NMF approaches and highly relevant for signals like audio that contain both transient and tonal components. The paper is technically sound, providing clear derivations for two EM estimation algorithms (MJLE and MMLE) and correctly identifying the computational challenges of the latter. The writing is exceptionally clear and well-organized, making the model and its motivation easy to follow.

The main weakness lies in the experimental evaluation. While the speech enhancement results are positive and show an improvement over baselines, the margin over the single-resolution IS-NMF baseline (6.6 dB vs 6.0 dB SNR improvement) is modest. A more direct comparison, perhaps against a baseline that also uses multi-resolution features (e.g., by concatenating spectrograms), would have provided a stronger validation of the proposed model's unique advantages. Furthermore, while the derivation of the MMLE algorithm is appreciated, its computational intractability for the experiments leaves its practical benefits undemonstrated, making the work feel slightly incomplete in this regard.

Arguments for acceptance:
*   Presents a conceptually novel and significant bridge between synthesis models and NMF.
*   Provides a principled and elegant framework for multi-resolution signal decomposition.
*   Technically sound, with well-derived algorithms and insightful connections to related work.

Arguments against acceptance:
*   The experimental results, while promising, show a relatively small improvement over a simpler baseline.
*   The theoretically more robust MMLE estimation method is not experimentally validated due to scalability issues.

Overall, the paper’s strong conceptual contribution and the practical importance of its multi-resolution framework outweigh the limitations of the experimental section. I recommend acceptance.
