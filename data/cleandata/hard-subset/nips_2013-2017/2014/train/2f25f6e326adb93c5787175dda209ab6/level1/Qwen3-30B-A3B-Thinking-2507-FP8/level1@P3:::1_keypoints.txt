- Proposal: LRTFS models the raw audio signal as a sum of low-rank time-frequency components, extending GCM from STFT to raw signal.  
- Key innovation: synthesis coefficient model imposes low-rank structure on the variance of synthesis coefficients (eq.3) rather than analysis coefficients.  
- Algorithms: two estimation schemes presented — MJLE (practical) and MMLE (computationally intractable for typical audio due to large matrix inversions).  
- Multi-resolution: LRTFS naturally supports multi-resolution representations (e.g., transients vs tonal components), a strong advantage for audio.  
- Theoretical foundation: mathematically well-defined with clear connections to SBL, GCM, and use of Itakura-Saito divergence.  
- Experiments: demonstrated on music decomposition and speech enhancement, showing practical utility.  
- Empirical gain: speech enhancement yields modest improvement (6.6 dB vs 6.0 dB for IS-NMF), i.e., +0.6 dB.  
- Scalability weakness: MMLE’s computational intractability is a significant limitation for audio applications.  
- Marginal performance: modest gains relative to added complexity; comparison limited to IS-NMF and OMLSA.  
- Related work differentiation: insufficiently distinguishes LRTFS from other multi-resolution/time-frequency methods and doesn’t fully leverage SBL connections to show novelty.  
- Theoretical guarantees: lacks analysis of convergence properties or optimality for the proposed EM algorithms.  
- Quality assessment: technically sound and well-derived but practical scalability and modest gains weaken impact.  
- Clarity assessment: well-written and organized; some sections (notably SBL connection) could use more intuitive explanation.  
- Originality assessment: novel approach to modeling raw signal with low-rank TF structure; multi-resolution extension is particularly innovative.  
- Significance assessment: useful for audio processing and multi-resolution analysis, but limited perceived impact due to small performance improvements.  
- Recommendation: Accept.  
- For acceptance: fills a literature gap, enables multi-resolution analysis, provides sound algorithms, and shows practical utility.  
- Against acceptance: MMLE is intractable, performance improvement is small, and relation to existing multi-resolution work needs clearer distinction.
