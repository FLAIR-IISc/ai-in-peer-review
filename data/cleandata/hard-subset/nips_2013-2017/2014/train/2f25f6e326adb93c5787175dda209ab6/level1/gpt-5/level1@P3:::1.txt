Summary and relation to prior work:
This paper proposes a generative time-domain model, Low-Rank Time-Frequency Synthesis (LRTFS), that places a low-rank Gaussian prior on complex synthesis coefficients of a fixed time-frequency frame and models the observed signal as their synthesis plus Gaussian residual noise. This bridges the gap between IS-NMF/Gaussian Composite Models (GCM) of the STFT (e.g., Févotte et al., Neural Computation 2009) and a full generative model of the raw waveform. Two estimation strategies are presented: (i) maximum joint likelihood estimation (MJLE) via an EM algorithm using a Figueiredo–Nowak splitting trick to avoid large matrix inversions; (ii) maximum marginal likelihood (MMLE, “type-II ML”) akin to Sparse Bayesian Learning (SBL), but currently not scalable. The model naturally extends to multi-resolution by stacking multiple frames, enabling layered decompositions with different time supports, reminiscent of hybrid/morphological decompositions (e.g., Daudet & Torrésani).

Relation to prior work is carefully positioned: LRTFS differs from GCM by imposing the low-rank structure on synthesis coefficients rather than analysis coefficients, and differs from SBL by tying variances via the low-rank WH, which trades sparsity for low-rank structure. The estimation interleaves Wiener-like shrinkage of α and IS-NMF updates on |α|^2, connecting to standard audio NMF practice. Applications include multi-resolution music decomposition and semi-supervised speech enhancement; comparisons to IS-NMF and OMLSA are reported.

Quality:
- Modeling and derivations appear technically sound. The decomposition of −log p(x,α|W,H,λ) into a data-fidelity term and an IS-NMF penalty on |α|^2 is correct; expressing the prior via DIS(|α|^2|v) + log(|α|^2) is algebraically consistent with the complex Gaussian prior. However, the text’s interpretation that the added log(|α|^2) “favors sparsity” could be misleading, since that term cancels with the α-dependent component in DIS; the net effect is the original Gaussian prior, not an additional sparsity penalty.
- The MJLE EM algorithm is well-motivated. The β-splitting and requirement β ≤ λ/δΦ are important; practical guidance on choosing/scheduling β when λ is driven small (Section 5.2) is missing and should be clarified to ensure the validity of the surrogate likelihood.
- The MMLE route is standard and elegant but computationally prohibitive; the paper is honest about this limitation.

Clarity:
- The paper is generally clear and well-organized, with explicit equations and implementation notes (e.g., use of efficient Φ/Φ* via LTFAT). Some details that would improve reproducibility: initialization of W, H, α; stopping criteria; selection/schedule of β; runtime and memory footprint; and how K is chosen.
- The latent component reconstruction rule (α̂kfn = wfk hkn/[WH]fn α̂fn) is sensible, but its relation to the MMSE estimator and the approximation in lieu of inverting T × T matrices could be discussed more explicitly.
- The handling of real-valued signals via Hermitian symmetry is only briefly mentioned and could use a short implementation note.

Originality:
- Modeling low-rank structure directly on synthesis coefficients to obtain a time-domain generative model is a meaningful conceptual shift from GCM/IS-NMF on analysis coefficients and, to my knowledge, novel in this specific form.
- The multi-resolution extension is natural and valuable; while multi-resolution sparse models are common (e.g., MCA), a low-rank time-frequency synthesis with EM estimation is new.

Significance:
- Potential impact is strong for audio ML/signal processing: a principled way to operate in the waveform domain while retaining NMF-like interpretability, and a clean route to multi-resolution models. It could inspire extensions to multichannel and inverse problems.
- However, empirical evidence is limited. The music decomposition is illustrative, and the speech enhancement experiment (10 mixtures) shows modest average SNR gains over IS-NMF (6.6 vs 6.0 dB) and OMLSA (4.6 dB), but lacks ablations (single- vs multi-resolution, effect of K, effect of λ schedule), statistical significance, perceptual metrics (PESQ/STOI), and comparisons to a GCM baseline under matched conditions. Tuning λ for best output SNR in the music example uses ground-truth access and may overstate performance.

Specific strengths:
- Principled, coherent probabilistic formulation in the time domain with a clear link to IS-NMF/GCM.
- Efficient MJLE algorithm avoiding large inversions; practical to implement with existing transform toolboxes.
- Simple and elegant multi-resolution extension with layer-wise EM updates.
- Clear derivations and connections to SBL and Wiener filtering.

Specific weaknesses and suggestions:
- Experimental section is small-scale and not fully controlled. Include: (i) a single-resolution LRTFS baseline; (ii) a GCM/IS-NMF baseline with equivalent dictionaries; (iii) perceptual metrics; (iv) ablations on λ, β, K; (v) runtime/complexity; (vi) more noise types and speech conditions.
- Clarify the role and selection of β, especially when λ is warmed down; ensure the β ≤ λ/δΦ condition is maintained.
- The “sparsity” interpretation of log(|α|^2) should be tempered to avoid confusion.
- Discuss identifiability with redundant frames and the effect of the frame choice on decomposition quality.
- MMLE scalability: outline concrete approximate inference ideas (e.g., conjugate gradients with matrix-free ops, diagonal/low-rank approximations, AMP/EP) to make this path actionable.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Novel and principled generative model that unifies NMF-like low-rank structure with time-domain synthesis.
- Technically solid EM algorithm with practical computational profile and a neat multi-resolution capability.
- Potential to influence future work in audio modeling and beyond (inverse problems, multichannel).

Arguments against acceptance:
- Limited and partially confounded empirical validation; small improvements over strong baselines, no perceptual evaluation, and some hyperparameter peeking.
- Missing practical details (initialization, β/λ schedules, computational costs).
- MMLE path, while interesting, is left as future work; thus the most theoretically appealing estimator is not demonstrated at scale.

Overall recommendation:
Borderline accept. The modeling contribution is original and well-founded, and the multi-resolution formulation is elegant and practical. If the authors can strengthen the experimental section (more rigorous baselines/metrics/ablations) and clarify β/λ choices, the paper would merit acceptance.
