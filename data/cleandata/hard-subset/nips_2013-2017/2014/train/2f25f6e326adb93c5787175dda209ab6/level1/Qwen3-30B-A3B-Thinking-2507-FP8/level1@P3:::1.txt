

# Review of "Low-Rank Time-Frequency Synthesis"

This paper proposes a novel generative model for audio signals that extends the Gaussian Composite Model (GCM) from modeling the STFT to directly modeling the raw signal. The authors address a key limitation in previous work: while GCM (Févotte et al., 2009) provided a generative model for the STFT, it did not model the raw signal. The proposed Low-Rank Time-Frequency Synthesis (LRTFS) model directly models the signal as a sum of low-rank time-frequency components, while also enabling multi-resolution representations.

The paper builds upon previous NIPS work on NMF (e.g., Lee & Seung, 1999; Févotte et al., 2009) and sparse Bayesian learning (SBL; Tipping, 2001; Wipf & Rao, 2004). The LRTFS model is mathematically well-defined, with the key innovation being the synthesis coefficient model (equation 3) that imposes a low-rank structure on the variance of synthesis coefficients, rather than analysis coefficients as in GCM. The authors present two EM algorithms for estimation: Maximum Joint Likelihood Estimation (MJLE) and Maximum Marginal Likelihood Estimation (MMLE), with the latter being computationally intractable for typical audio applications.

**Strengths:**

1. **Clear conceptual contribution:** The paper successfully bridges a gap in the literature by providing a generative model for the raw signal, not just its STFT. This is a significant conceptual advance over previous work.

2. **Multi-resolution capability:** The extension to multi-resolution representations (Section 4) is a particularly strong contribution. Unlike traditional NMF approaches, LRTFS naturally accommodates different time resolutions for different signal components (e.g., transients vs. tonal components), which is highly relevant to audio processing.

3. **Solid theoretical foundation:** The mathematical derivations are sound, with clear connections to existing work (SBL, GCM) and appropriate use of the Itakura-Saito divergence.

4. **Relevant experiments:** The experiments on music decomposition and speech enhancement demonstrate practical utility, with the speech enhancement results showing a modest but consistent improvement (6.6 dB vs 6 dB for IS-NMF).

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited scalability of MMLE:** The paper correctly identifies that MMLE (Section 3.2) is computationally intractable for audio applications due to the need for large matrix inversions. The authors acknowledge this as "future work," but this represents a significant limitation for a paper claiming to address audio applications.

2. **Marginal performance gains:** The speech enhancement results show only a 0.6 dB improvement over IS-NMF, which is modest given the complexity of the proposed approach. The paper would benefit from a more thorough comparison with state-of-the-art methods beyond the baseline IS-NMF and OMLSA.

3. **Insufficient differentiation from related work:** While the paper cites relevant work (e.g., Smaragdis, Daudet & Torrésani), it could better articulate how LRTFS differs from existing multi-resolution approaches (e.g., morphological component analysis by Elad & Starck, 2005; Daudet & Torrésani, 2004). The connection to sparse Bayesian learning is mentioned but not fully leveraged to demonstrate novelty.

4. **Lack of theoretical guarantees:** The paper provides empirical results but lacks theoretical analysis of convergence properties or optimality of the proposed EM algorithms.

**Evaluation against criteria:**

*Quality:* The paper is technically sound with well-derived algorithms. The experiments are appropriately designed for the application domain. However, the limited scalability of MMLE and modest performance gains weaken the overall technical contribution.

*Clarity:* The paper is well-written and logically organized. The mathematical notation is clear, though some sections (particularly the connection to SBL) could benefit from more intuitive explanations for non-specialists.

*Originality:* The core idea of modeling the raw signal with low-rank time-frequency structure is novel and addresses a genuine gap in the literature. The multi-resolution extension is particularly innovative. However, the paper could better distinguish itself from related work on multi-resolution time-frequency analysis.

*Significance:* The approach has clear practical value for audio processing applications, particularly in scenarios requiring multi-resolution analysis. The multi-resolution capability represents a significant advance over traditional NMF approaches. However, the marginal improvement over baselines (0.6 dB) limits the perceived significance for the audio processing community.

**Recommendation:** Accept. The paper makes a meaningful contribution to the field by providing a generative model for the raw signal with low-rank time-frequency structure and enabling multi-resolution representations. While the performance gains are modest, the conceptual advance and practical utility for audio applications are substantial. The limitations noted (particularly MMLE scalability) are acknowledged by the authors and represent natural directions for future work.

**Arguments for acceptance:**
- Fills a genuine gap in the literature (generative model of raw signal vs. STFT)
- Introduces a useful multi-resolution capability not possible in traditional NMF
- Provides well-derived algorithms with sound theoretical foundation
- Demonstrates practical utility in two audio applications

**Arguments against acceptance:**
- MMLE approach is computationally intractable for audio applications
- Performance gains over baselines are modest (0.6 dB in speech enhancement)
- Could better differentiate from related work on multi-resolution time-frequency analysis

The paper represents a solid contribution to the field of audio signal processing and time-frequency analysis. While the performance improvements are modest, the conceptual advance and practical utility for multi-resolution audio processing make it a valuable addition to the literature. The authors have clearly identified the gap they're addressing and provided a well-structured solution.
