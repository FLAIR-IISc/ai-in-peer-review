This paper presents a novel generative model, Low-Rank Time-Frequency Synthesis (LRTFS), extending the Gaussian Composite Model (GCM) [3] to directly model the raw signal rather than just its STFT. The core idea is to represent the signal as a sum of time-frequency atoms with low-rank structure imposed on the synthesis coefficients, enabling multi-resolution representations. The authors propose two Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithms for estimation and demonstrate the approach on music decomposition and speech enhancement tasks.

The paper builds upon a strong foundation of work in matrix factorization and signal processing. NMF [2] has been widely used for spectral decomposition, but its limitations regarding phase information and probabilistic modeling led to the GCM [3]. This work naturally extends the GCM by addressing the crucial gap of raw signal synthesis. The connection to sparse Bayesian learning [4, 5] is insightful, highlighting the difference in variance treatment and its impact on sparsity. The multi-resolution extension elegantly leverages the synthesis approach, offering advantages over traditional methods fixed to a single resolution [8, 9].  The use of a hybrid model for music decomposition echoes prior work [10] and the speech enhancement application is related to semi-supervised source separation [11].

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The LRTFS model is a significant step forward by providing a generative model for the raw signal, addressing a limitation of the GCM and traditional NMF. The multi-resolution extension is particularly compelling.
* **Theoretical Soundness:** The derivation of the LRTFS model and the EM algorithms are well-presented and grounded in probabilistic principles. The connection to sparse Bayesian learning provides a valuable perspective.
* **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the model and algorithms. The notation is consistent, and the explanations are accessible to researchers familiar with signal processing and matrix factorization.
* **Practical Relevance:** The experiments demonstrate the potential of LRTFS in practical applications like music decomposition and speech enhancement, achieving competitive results compared to existing methods.
* **Complete Work:** While there is scope for future work, the presented work feels relatively complete, with well-defined algorithms and experimental validation.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Computational Complexity (MMLE):** The authors acknowledge the scalability issue with the MMLE approach, particularly for large-scale audio applications. The need for inverting a large matrix limits its practical applicability. While recognized, this is a significant drawback.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:**  The performance appears sensitive to the hyperparameter λ, requiring careful tuning.  The paper doesn't delve deeply into strategies for robustly setting λ, only noting that a decreasing strategy was used. This is a common issue with EM algorithms, but further discussion of robustness would be valuable.
* **Limited Baselines:** The comparison with other speech enhancement methods is somewhat limited. While OMLSA [14] is included, a wider range of state-of-the-art techniques (e.g., deep learning-based methods) would strengthen the evaluation.
* **Lack of Statistical Significance:** The speech enhancement experiments report average SNR improvements over 10 test signals. While promising, a more rigorous statistical analysis (e.g., t-tests or ANOVA) would be needed to confirm the significance of the improvements.
* **Missing Implementation Details:** While the authors mention the use of the LTFAT toolbox [7], more details on the specific implementation (e.g., optimization parameters, stopping criteria for the EM algorithm) would improve reproducibility.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound approach to signal synthesis with significant potential for advancements in audio processing.
* The multi-resolution capability is a distinct advantage over existing methods.
* The experiments demonstrate promising results in relevant applications.
* The paper is well-written and clearly explains the complex concepts.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The MMLE algorithm suffers from scalability issues, limiting its practical use.
* The hyperparameter sensitivity and limited robustness of the λ estimation are concerns.
* The experimental evaluation could be strengthened with more comprehensive baselines and statistical analysis.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with a clear contribution to the field of time-frequency analysis and signal processing. The proposed LRTFS model addresses a key limitation of existing methods and opens up new possibilities for multi-resolution signal representation and synthesis. While the computational complexity of MMLE and the hyperparameter sensitivity are weaknesses, they do not overshadow the novelty and potential of the work.  I believe this paper is worthy of acceptance to NIPS, contingent on the authors addressing the concerns regarding robustness and providing a more thorough experimental evaluation. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions, specifically focusing on a more detailed discussion of hyperparameter tuning strategies, and a more comprehensive comparison to state-of-the-art methods in the speech enhancement experiments.
