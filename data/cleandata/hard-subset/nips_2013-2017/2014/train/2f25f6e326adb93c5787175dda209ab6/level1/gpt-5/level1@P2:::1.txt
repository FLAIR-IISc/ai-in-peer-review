Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes a generative model of raw time-domain signals with low-rank time-frequency structure, termed Low-Rank Time-Frequency Synthesis (LRTFS). Unlike the Gaussian Composite Model (GCM) for the STFT [Févotte et al., 2011], which imposes a low-rank structure on the variance of analysis coefficients (STFT), LRTFS places the low-rank variance structure on synthesis coefficients that generate the signal as a linear combination of time-frequency atoms plus Gaussian residual. The variance of each synthesis coefficient αfn is parameterized as [WH]fn with nonnegative factors W and H, linking the model to IS-NMF but at the synthesis level. The authors derive two estimation strategies: (i) maximum joint likelihood estimation (MJLE) via an EM algorithm that alternates a Wiener-like shrinkage step for α with an IS-NMF step for W,H, and (ii) maximum marginal likelihood estimation (MMLE), akin to Type-II SBL, which marginalizes α but is computationally prohibitive for large-scale audio. A key advantage of the synthesis approach is a straightforward extension to multi-resolution modeling: multiple frames with different time-frequency resolutions contribute linearly to the signal, each with its own low-rank prior over synthesis coefficients. The paper presents experiments on hybrid music decomposition and semi-supervised speech enhancement, showing modest SNR improvements over IS-NMF and OMLSA.

Strengths
- Clear conceptual advance: bridges the gap between probabilistic factorizations of spectrograms (GCM/IS-NMF) and a generative model of the waveform. This is a principled extension that retains tractable inference.
- Elegant algorithmic design: the MJLE EM uses a splitting trick (after Figueiredo & Nowak) to avoid large matrix inversions; α-update is Wiener-like; W,H update reduces to standard IS-NMF with multiplicative rules.
- Natural multi-resolution extension: modeling multiple frames with distinct resolutions at the synthesis level is simple and compelling; this was awkward in analysis-based NMF frameworks.
- Practical results: for speech enhancement, the reported average SNR gain (6.6 dB) is competitive with an IS-NMF baseline (6 dB) and OMLSA (4.6 dB). The jazz decomposition qualitatively matches anticipated tonal/transient structure.
- Good connection to SBL: positions LRTFS as SBL with structured variances (tied by WH), clarifying differences in sparsity behavior and modeling assumptions.

Weaknesses
- Scalability of MMLE: the marginal-likelihood algorithm is not practically usable for typical audio sizes; only MJLE is evaluated. This limits the contribution on the inference side.
- Limited evaluation breadth: audio tasks are few; metrics are limited to SNR (no perceptual measures like PESQ/STOI for speech); statistical significance not reported. Baselines are relatively simple (single-resolution IS-NMF, OMLSA).
- Ablations missing: no direct ablation of (i) synthesis vs analysis modeling, (ii) single- vs multi-resolution within the same framework, or (iii) the effect of the sparsity-inducing log term from the joint likelihood.
- Sensitivity to hyperparameters: λ is treated as a tuned hyperparameter or annealed; the paper notes difficulties when estimating λ jointly. Robustness to λ and β (splitting parameter) is not systematically studied.
- Related work: the link to phase-aware/complex NMF and other probabilistic factor models that incorporate phase is only implicit. More discussion would help position LRTFS among models using complex Gaussians with structured variance on synthesis or analysis coefficients.

Quality
- The model is well-motivated and technically sound. Derivations for MJLE are consistent with the cited EM architecture; monotonicity of the negative log-likelihood is discussed, with a practical λ-update justified by prior work.
- The MMLE section is correct but incomplete from a practical standpoint because of computational bottlenecks; acknowledging this is honest, but it reduces the empirical impact.
- Experiments are reasonable and indicate benefits, especially for multi-resolution decomposition. However, stronger comparisons (e.g., multi-resolution analysis-based NMF baselines; phase-aware NMF; SBL on synthesis coefficients without low-rank tying) would more convincingly support claims.

Clarity
- The paper is generally clear and well organized. The distinction between analysis vs synthesis coefficients is explicit and helpful. The mapping between 2D indices (f, n) and vector index m is carefully handled.
- The appearance of the log(|α|^2) term in the joint objective is interesting; a brief derivation or pointer to supplementary material would improve clarity.
- Multi-resolution LRTFS is explained concisely with algorithmic updates; computational complexity considerations are appreciated.

Originality
- The core idea—imposing low-rank structure on synthesis coefficient variances to yield a generative waveform model and enabling multi-resolution—is novel relative to prior NMF/GCM work that models the STFT.
- The EM architecture for MJLE adapts known techniques but in a new setting; the structured SBL perspective is insightful.

Significance
- The approach is likely to influence audio ML work by providing a principled synthesis-based low-rank framework and a practical EM algorithm. The multi-resolution extension is especially valuable.
- Current empirical gains are modest; broader evaluations and stronger baselines would bolster significance. Nonetheless, the conceptual contribution is important and opens avenues (e.g., scalable MMLE, multichannel, inverse problems).

Suggestions for improvement
- Add ablations: single vs multi-resolution; synthesis vs analysis modeling on the same data; effect of the sparsity term.
- Include perceptual metrics (PESQ/STOI) and significance tests; diversify noise types and baselines (e.g., multi-resolution NMF/PLCA, phase-aware NMF).
- Discuss identifiability and initialization strategies; study sensitivity to β and λ; provide guidance or heuristics.
- Provide more detail or a supplement on the joint-likelihood derivation (source of log term), and computational cost per iteration.

Pros for acceptance
- Conceptual advance: principled generative synthesis model with low-rank structure.
- Simple, scalable MJLE algorithm leveraging standard IS-NMF machinery.
- Clean multi-resolution extension; practical audio benefits demonstrated.
- Clear connections to GCM and SBL; well-situated within NIPS topics.

Cons for acceptance
- MMLE is not scalable; only MJLE is practical and evaluated.
- Empirical evidence is limited in scope and metrics; modest gains over simple baselines.
- Missing comparisons to stronger or closer baselines and ablations.
- Sensitivity to hyperparameters and convergence behavior not fully characterized.

Overall assessment
I find the paper original, technically solid, and potentially impactful for audio ML. Despite limited experimental breadth, the modeling contribution and the elegant estimation method warrant publication. I lean toward acceptance, with the expectation that a revised version expands empirical validation and clarifies derivations.
