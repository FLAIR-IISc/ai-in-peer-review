**Summary of the Paper**

This paper introduces a novel signal synthesis model called Low-Rank Time-Frequency Synthesis (LRTFS) that bridges the gap between time-frequency synthesis and traditional Nonnegative Matrix Factorization (NMF) approaches. The LRTFS model represents a signal as a sum of time-frequency atoms with different temporal resolutions, allowing for multi-resolution modeling. The authors propose two estimation methods: Maximum Joint Likelihood Estimation (MJLE) and Maximum Marginal Likelihood Estimation (MMLE), both of which are based on Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithms. The paper demonstrates the effectiveness of LRTFS on music decomposition and speech enhancement tasks, showing improved results compared to traditional NMF approaches.

**Strengths of the Paper**

1. **Novelty**: The paper introduces a new signal synthesis model that combines the strengths of time-frequency synthesis and NMF approaches.
2. **Theoretical foundations**: The authors provide a solid theoretical foundation for the LRTFS model, including the derivation of the EM algorithms for MJLE and MMLE.
3. **Experimental results**: The paper presents experimental results on music decomposition and speech enhancement tasks, demonstrating the effectiveness of LRTFS.
4. **Comparison to state-of-the-art**: The authors compare LRTFS to traditional NMF approaches and other state-of-the-art methods, showing improved results.

**Weaknesses of the Paper**

1. **Computational complexity**: The MMLE algorithm has high computational complexity due to the inversion of a large matrix, which may limit its applicability to large-scale problems.
2. **Hyperparameter tuning**: The paper relies on manual tuning of hyperparameters, which may not be optimal and may require additional computational resources.
3. **Limited experimental evaluation**: While the paper presents experimental results on two tasks, a more comprehensive evaluation on a wider range of tasks and datasets would be beneficial.

**Originality and Significance**

The paper presents a novel signal synthesis model that combines the strengths of time-frequency synthesis and NMF approaches. The LRTFS model has the potential to advance the state-of-the-art in signal processing and audio analysis, particularly in applications where multi-resolution modeling is important.

**Quality and Clarity**

The paper is well-written, and the authors provide a clear explanation of the LRTFS model and the EM algorithms for MJLE and MMLE. The experimental results are presented in a clear and concise manner.

**Arguments For and Against Acceptance**

**For acceptance**:

* The paper presents a novel and well-motivated signal synthesis model that combines the strengths of time-frequency synthesis and NMF approaches.
* The authors provide a solid theoretical foundation for the LRTFS model and demonstrate its effectiveness on music decomposition and speech enhancement tasks.

**Against acceptance**:

* The MMLE algorithm has high computational complexity, which may limit its applicability to large-scale problems.
* The paper relies on manual tuning of hyperparameters, which may not be optimal and may require additional computational resources.

Overall, I recommend accepting the paper, but with the suggestion that the authors address the computational complexity of the MMLE algorithm and provide a more comprehensive evaluation of the LRTFS model on a wider range of tasks and datasets.
