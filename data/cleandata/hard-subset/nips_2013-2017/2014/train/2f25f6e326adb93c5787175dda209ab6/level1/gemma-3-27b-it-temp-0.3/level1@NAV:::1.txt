## Review of "Low-Rank Time-Frequency Synthesis"

This paper introduces a novel generative model for signal synthesis, extending the Gaussian Composite Model (GCM) presented in prior work (Févotte et al., NIPS 2013) to directly model the raw signal rather than just its STFT. The core idea is to leverage a low-rank structure on the *variance* of synthesis coefficients, enabling multi-resolution representations not achievable with traditional NMF. The authors present two Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithms for estimation and demonstrate the approach on music decomposition and speech enhancement tasks.

The paper builds logically upon existing work in time-frequency decomposition and probabilistic latent factor modeling. The motivation for moving beyond STFT-level modeling is clear, and the connection to sparse Bayesian learning (SBL) provides valuable context. The extension to multi-resolution representations is a significant contribution, addressing a limitation of standard NMF and opening doors to more flexible signal modeling. The experimental results, while limited in scope, demonstrate promising performance in both music decomposition and speech enhancement, achieving competitive results compared to IS-NMF and OMLSA.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The proposed LRTFS model is a genuine extension of existing work, addressing a key limitation of previous approaches.
*   **Theoretical Soundness:** The derivation of the model and EM algorithms appears mathematically rigorous.
*   **Multi-Resolution Capability:** The ability to handle multiple time-frequency resolutions is a significant advantage.
*   **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and logically organized, with a good explanation of the underlying concepts.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Scalability of MMLE:** The authors acknowledge the scalability issues with the MMLE algorithm, limiting its practical applicability. While understandable, this weakens the overall contribution.
*   **Limited Experimentation:** The experiments are somewhat limited in scope. More extensive evaluation on diverse datasets and with comparisons to state-of-the-art methods would strengthen the claims.
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The reliance on careful tuning of the λ parameter (treated as a hyperparameter rather than estimated) suggests potential sensitivity and a need for more robust parameter selection strategies.
*   **Lack of Detailed Ablation Studies:** The paper would benefit from ablation studies to isolate the impact of different components of the model (e.g., the low-rank constraint, multi-resolution capability).



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound approach to signal synthesis.
*   The multi-resolution capability is a significant contribution.
*   The experimental results are promising and demonstrate the potential of the approach.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The scalability issues with MMLE limit the practical applicability of one of the proposed algorithms.
*   The experimental evaluation is somewhat limited.
*   The hyperparameter sensitivity raises concerns about robustness.

**Overall:**

I believe this paper represents a solid contribution to the field. While the scalability limitations and limited experimentation are concerns, the novelty and theoretical soundness of the proposed approach, combined with the promising experimental results, outweigh these weaknesses. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions to address the points raised above, particularly regarding a more thorough discussion of hyperparameter sensitivity and potential strategies for improving the scalability of MMLE.
