

# Review of "Low-Rank Time-Frequency Synthesis"

This paper proposes a novel generative model for audio signal processing called Low-Rank Time-Frequency Synthesis (LRTFS), which extends the Gaussian Composite Model (GCM) framework to directly model the raw signal rather than its STFT. The authors build upon the GCM introduced in [3], which provided a generative model for the STFT coefficients, by developing a model for the actual time-domain signal. The key insight is that the synthesis coefficients (dual to analysis coefficients in STFT) can be modeled with a low-rank variance structure, enabling a complete generative model of the signal itself. The paper also introduces multi-resolution representations, allowing different signal components to have different time-frequency resolutions.

The paper makes a significant contribution by bridging the gap between traditional NMF approaches (which operate on STFT magnitude) and generative modeling of raw signals. Previous work, such as the GCM [3], only modeled the STFT, while LRTFS models the actual audio signal. The authors present two estimation approaches: Maximum Joint Likelihood Estimation (MJLE) and Maximum Marginal Likelihood Estimation (MMLE), with the former being computationally efficient and scalable for audio applications. The multi-resolution capability is particularly noteworthy, as it allows modeling of different signal components (e.g., transients vs. sustained tones) with appropriate time-frequency resolutions, something not possible with traditional NMF.

**Strengths:**

1. **Originality:** The paper clearly addresses a significant gap in the literature by providing a generative model for the raw signal rather than just the STFT. The multi-resolution extension is novel and directly addresses a limitation of traditional NMF approaches. The connection to sparse Bayesian learning (Section 2.2) is insightful and well-explained.

2. **Quality:** The theoretical framework is sound, with proper derivations of the EM algorithms. The paper demonstrates that the MJLE approach is computationally efficient (no matrix inversion required) and scales well with audio applications. The experimental results on music decomposition and speech enhancement support the claims, with reasonable SNR improvements (6.6 dB vs. 6 dB for IS-NMF).

3. **Clarity:** The paper is well-structured, with clear mathematical formulations and good organization. The connection to previous work (GCM, NMF, SBL) is well-articulated, and the paper provides sufficient detail for reproduction (referencing LTFAT for efficient implementation).

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Significance:** While the multi-resolution capability is promising, the paper doesn't convincingly demonstrate significant improvements over existing methods. The SNR improvement (6.6 dB vs. 6 dB) is marginal, and the paper doesn't compare with more recent state-of-the-art approaches for music decomposition or speech enhancement. The paper also doesn't discuss computational efficiency compared to other methods, which is important for audio applications.

2. **Experimental Evaluation:** The experiments are limited to two audio applications (music decomposition and speech enhancement) with only a few baselines. The paper doesn't provide ablation studies to isolate the contribution of the multi-resolution aspect or the generative model itself. The speech enhancement results don't clearly show how much of the improvement comes from the multi-resolution capability versus the generative modeling approach.

3. **Comparison with Recent Work:** The paper cites relevant prior work but doesn't adequately compare with more recent NIPS papers on audio processing or signal decomposition. For example, there's no comparison with recent deep learning approaches for audio separation, which have become increasingly popular in the field.

**Arguments for and against acceptance:**

*For Acceptance:*
- The paper addresses a clear gap in the literature (generative model for raw signal, not just STFT)
- The multi-resolution representation is a significant and novel contribution
- The MJLE approach is computationally efficient and well-motivated
- The paper is technically sound and well-written

*Against Acceptance:*
- The performance gains over existing methods are marginal (6.6 dB vs. 6 dB)
- Limited experimental evaluation with insufficient comparison to state-of-the-art
- Doesn't clearly demonstrate why the generative model approach is superior to existing non-generative NMF methods for the tasks considered
- Lacks analysis of computational complexity compared to alternative methods

**Overall Assessment:**

The paper presents a technically sound and novel approach to audio signal modeling by extending the GCM framework to the raw signal domain and introducing multi-resolution capabilities. The theoretical contributions are solid, and the computational approach (MJLE) is well-suited for audio applications. However, the significance of the work is somewhat diminished by the limited performance gains over existing methods and the lack of comprehensive experimental evaluation. While the multi-resolution capability is promising, the paper doesn't provide sufficient evidence to demonstrate that this is a major advance over previous work.

The paper would be stronger with a more thorough comparison to recent state-of-the-art methods (both generative and non-generative), ablation studies to isolate the contribution of the multi-resolution aspect, and a clearer discussion of the computational advantages over existing approaches. As it stands, the paper makes a valuable contribution to the literature but doesn't clearly demonstrate a significant advance over current state-of-the-art in audio signal processing.

Given the quality of the technical contribution and the importance of the problem addressed, I recommend acceptance, though the paper would benefit from addressing the limitations noted above in the camera-ready version. The work is a meaningful step forward in the field of generative models for audio signals and has potential for broader application in signal processing.
