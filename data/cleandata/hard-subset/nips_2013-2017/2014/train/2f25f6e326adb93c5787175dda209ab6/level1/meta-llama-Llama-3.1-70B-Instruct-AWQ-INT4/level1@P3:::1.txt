This paper introduces a novel signal synthesis model with low-rank time-frequency structure, which bridges the gap between time-frequency synthesis and traditional nonnegative matrix factorization (NMF) approaches. The proposed model, called Low-Rank Time-Frequency Synthesis (LRTFS), is a generative model that represents a signal as a sum of layers with their own time resolution and latent low-rank structure.

The paper is well-written and clearly organized. The authors provide a thorough introduction to the problem and the proposed solution, and they present a detailed derivation of the LRTFS model and its estimation algorithms. The experiments demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed approach on music decomposition and speech enhancement tasks.

The main contribution of this paper is the introduction of the LRTFS model, which offers several advantages over traditional NMF approaches. Firstly, LRTFS models the raw signal itself, rather than its spectrogram, which allows for more accurate representation and analysis of the signal. Secondly, LRTFS can accommodate multi-resolution representations, which is particularly useful in audio applications where different components of the signal may have different time-frequency resolutions.

The estimation algorithms proposed in the paper are based on maximum joint likelihood estimation (MJLE) and maximum marginal likelihood estimation (MMLE). The MJLE algorithm is an iterative shrinkage algorithm with an additional Itakura-Saito NMF penalty term, while the MMLE algorithm is based on the marginalization of the synthesis coefficients and is more robust to overfitting. However, the MMLE algorithm is more computationally expensive and may not be suitable for large-scale applications.

The experiments demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed approach on music decomposition and speech enhancement tasks. The results show that LRTFS can accurately separate the tonal and transient components of music signals and can improve the quality of speech signals corrupted by noise.

Overall, this paper presents a significant contribution to the field of signal processing and machine learning. The proposed LRTFS model and estimation algorithms offer several advantages over traditional NMF approaches and have the potential to be applied to a wide range of audio applications.
