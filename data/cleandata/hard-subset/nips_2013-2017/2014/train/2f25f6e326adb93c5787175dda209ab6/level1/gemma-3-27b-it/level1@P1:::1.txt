This paper introduces a novel low-rank time-frequency synthesis (LRTFS) model, extending the Gaussian Composite Model (GCM) [3] to directly model the raw signal rather than just its STFT. The core idea is to represent the signal as a sum of atoms with low-rank structure imposed on the synthesis coefficients, enabling multi-resolution representations not easily achievable with traditional NMF. The authors provide two expectation-maximization (EM) algorithms for estimation and demonstrate the approach on music decomposition and speech enhancement tasks.

The work builds upon a substantial body of research in matrix factorization for signal processing, particularly NMF [1, 2] and its probabilistic extensions like the GCM [3]. It addresses limitations of these methods by providing a true generative model for the raw signal and enabling flexible time-frequency resolution. The connection to sparse Bayesian learning (SBL) [4, 5] is insightful, highlighting the difference in variance modeling between LRTFS and SBL. The extension to multi-resolution representations is a significant contribution, addressing the inherent limitations of fixed-resolution methods in scenarios like audio processing [8, 9]. 

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The LRTFS model genuinely extends existing work by providing a generative model for the raw signal, a step beyond STFT-based approaches.
* **Multi-resolution capability:** The ability to handle signals with varying time-frequency characteristics is a significant advantage, particularly for audio applications. The discussion is clear and well-motivated.
* **Theoretical foundation:** The paper provides a clear derivation of the LRTFS model and the corresponding EM algorithms. The connection to SBL is also well-explained.
* **Experimental validation:** The experiments on music decomposition and speech enhancement demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed approach. The comparisons to IS-NMF and OMLSA for speech enhancement are valuable.
* **Clear writing:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The mathematical notation is clear, and the explanations are concise.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Computational complexity:** The MMLE algorithm is noted to be computationally expensive, especially for large-scale applications. While acknowledged, a discussion of potential approximations or scaling strategies would be beneficial. The paper mentions message passing [16] as a possible future direction, but details are lacking.
* **Hyperparameter sensitivity:** The experiments highlight the sensitivity to the hyperparameter λ, requiring manual tuning. A more robust approach to setting λ, perhaps through cross-validation or adaptive methods, would improve the practical applicability of the model.
* **Limited baselines:** While the comparisons to IS-NMF and OMLSA are useful, a wider range of state-of-the-art methods for speech enhancement (e.g., deep learning-based approaches) would provide a more comprehensive evaluation.
* **Lack of statistical significance:** The speech enhancement results are presented with an average SNR improvement, but without statistical significance tests (e.g., t-tests) it's difficult to definitively conclude the superiority of the proposed method.
* **Implementation details:** The paper lacks details on specific implementation choices (e.g., the specific parameters used for the Gabor frames).  Providing more information would improve reproducibility.



**Arguments for acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound approach to signal synthesis with clear advantages over existing methods.
* The multi-resolution capability is a significant contribution, opening up new possibilities for applications in audio processing and beyond.
* The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed approach on relevant tasks.
* The paper is well-written and clearly explains the core ideas.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

* The computational complexity of the MMLE algorithm limits its scalability.
* The sensitivity to the hyperparameter λ requires careful tuning.
* The limited baseline comparisons and lack of statistical significance weaken the strength of the experimental evaluation.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that introduces a valuable contribution to the field of signal processing. The LRTFS model is a well-motivated and theoretically sound extension of existing work, and the multi-resolution capability is particularly promising. While the computational complexity and hyperparameter sensitivity are concerns, they are acknowledged by the authors. The experimental evaluation is solid, although it could be strengthened with more extensive baseline comparisons and statistical analysis. 

I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions. Specifically, the authors should:

1.  Elaborate on potential strategies for addressing the computational complexity of the MMLE algorithm.
2.  Discuss more robust methods for setting the hyperparameter λ.
3.  Include statistical significance tests to support the experimental results.
4.  Provide more details on implementation choices to improve reproducibility.
