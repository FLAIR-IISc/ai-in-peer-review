This paper presents a novel signal synthesis model, Low-Rank Time-Frequency Synthesis (LRTFS), building upon previous work on Gaussian Composite Models (GCMs) for time-frequency representations. The core idea is to move from modeling the STFT coefficients (as in GCM) to directly modeling the raw signal using a low-rank structure on the synthesis coefficients. This enables multi-resolution representations, a key advantage over traditional NMF. The authors propose two Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithms for estimation and demonstrate the approach on music decomposition and speech enhancement tasks.

The paper builds directly on the NMF and GCM literature, particularly [3] (Févotte et al., 2013) which established the connection between NMF with the Itakura-Saito divergence and the GCM. It also draws connections to sparse Bayesian learning ([4, 5] Tipping & Rao) and highlights the differences arising from the low-rank constraints. The multi-resolution extension is related to morphological filtering and hybrid decomposition techniques ([8, 10] Daudet & Torrésani, Elad et al.). The experimental setup builds on established techniques in speech enhancement ([14] Cohen).

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** Moving beyond STFT modeling to raw signal synthesis is a clear contribution. The ability to natively support multi-resolution representations is a significant advantage over existing methods.
* **Technical Soundness:** The derivation of the LRTFS model and the EM algorithms appears mathematically sound. The connection to sparse Bayesian learning provides a useful perspective. The presentation of the EM algorithm with clear steps and explanations is a plus.
* **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The motivation is clearly stated, and the equations are well-presented.
* **Experiments:** The experiments provide some initial evidence of the effectiveness of the proposed approach, demonstrating improvements in both music decomposition and speech enhancement compared to baselines.  The supplementary material with sound examples is valuable.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Scalability of MMLE:** The authors acknowledge that the MMLE algorithm doesn't scale well to practical signal lengths due to the matrix inversion required. This limits the applicability of that portion of the work. The emphasis on MJLE is sensible, but the inability to demonstrate MMLE’s performance is a drawback.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The experiments show sensitivity to the choice of the regularization parameter λ. The justification for the chosen values is not fully explained, and a more systematic sensitivity analysis would be beneficial. The “warm-restart” strategy for λ in speech enhancement, while effective, could use more explanation.
* **Limited Baseline Comparison:** While the NMF baseline is reasonable, a more comprehensive comparison with state-of-the-art speech enhancement methods would strengthen the results. Particularly methods leveraging deep learning.
* **Theoretical Justification of MJLE:** While the authors mention that MJLE may only converge to a local optimum, there's no discussion of why this algorithm, rather than MMLE, was chosen for the main experimental evaluation. A clearer explanation of the trade-offs would be helpful.
* **Lack of Detailed Ablation Studies:** It would be useful to have ablation studies to understand the contribution of each component of the model, such as the low-rank constraint or the multi-resolution representation.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a genuinely novel approach to signal synthesis with a clear theoretical foundation.
* The multi-resolution capability offers a significant advantage over existing methods.
* The experimental results, while limited, are promising and demonstrate the potential of the proposed approach.
* The paper is well-written and clearly explains the technical details.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The scalability issues with the MMLE algorithm limit the scope of the work.
* The hyperparameter sensitivity and limited baseline comparison weaken the empirical evaluation.
* The lack of a deeper analysis of the model’s components and convergence properties leaves some questions unanswered.



**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a solid contribution to the field of time-frequency signal processing. The LRTFS model is a novel and promising approach with the potential to advance the state of the art in applications like music decomposition and speech enhancement. The weaknesses are not severe enough to warrant rejection, but addressing them would significantly strengthen the paper.  I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions to address the concerns regarding hyperparameter sensitivity, baseline comparison, and a more detailed discussion of the trade-offs between MJLE and MMLE.
