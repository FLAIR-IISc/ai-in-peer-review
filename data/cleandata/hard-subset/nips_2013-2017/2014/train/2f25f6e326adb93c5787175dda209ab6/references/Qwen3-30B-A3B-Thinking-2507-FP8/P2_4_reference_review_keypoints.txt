- Introduces Low-Rank Time-Frequency Synthesis (LRTFS), modeling raw audio as a sum of time-frequency atoms with low-rank synthesis coefficients, bridging NMF and synthesis approaches.  
- Extends Gaussian Composite Models (GCM) by tying variances with a low-rank structure rather than modeling analysis coefficients.  
- Presents a multi-resolution extension enabling different time-frequency resolutions for different components (e.g., transients vs tonal).  
- Provides two estimation methods (MJLE and MMLE) with detailed EM algorithms; MMLE noted as computationally intractable for large audio.  
- Connects LRTFS to sparse Bayesian learning (SBL), clarifying differences due to tied variance structure.  
- Experiments show strong performance: music decomposition (26 dB output SNR) and speech enhancement (6.6 dB average SNR improvement).  
- Demonstrates improvements over baselines (6 dB for IS-NMF, 4.6 dB for OMLSA) in the reported tasks.  
- Paper is technically sound, well-justified mathematically, and reproducible with clear notation and organization.  
- Multi-resolution extension is a novel, practically useful innovation not available in traditional NMF.  
- Claims potential applicability beyond audio signal processing.  
- Weakness: limited comparison to recent deep-learning methods (e.g., Conv-TasNet, DCCRN) and state-of-the-art separation techniques.  
- Weakness: insufficient analysis of MJLE computational complexity and runtime for large-scale (CD-quality) audio.  
- Weakness: assumes complex-valued signals without detailed practical guidance for real-valued audio implementation (Hermitian symmetry handling).  
- Weakness: lacks discussion of convergence properties or stronger theoretical guarantees for the iterative algorithms.  
- Recommendation: Accept — novel, well-executed contribution with meaningful experimental results and clear presentation.  
- Pro arguments: novel raw-signal low-rank model, multi-resolution capability, solid connections to SBL/GCM, strong experiments, clear writing.  
- Con arguments: limited comparisons to modern deep-learning baselines, incomplete complexity analysis, and practical justification for complex-signal assumption.
