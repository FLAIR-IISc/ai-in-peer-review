This paper proposes a low-rank time-frequency synthesis (LRTFS) model as a generative model for raw signals, building on the Gaussian Composite Model (GCM) which models the STFT. The core idea is to model synthesis coefficients as having a low-rank structure, contrasting with the GCM which imposes low-rank structure on the analysis coefficients. The authors present a maximum likelihood estimation approach with an EM algorithm, and discuss extending the framework to multi-resolution representations. Experiments are presented on music decomposition and speech enhancement.

While the motivation of bridging the gap between time-frequency synthesis and NMF is reasonable, and the multi-resolution extension is a potentially interesting direction, this paper falls short of convincing me of the novelty and significance of the proposed approach. My primary concern is the lack of compelling evidence that LRTFS substantially outperforms existing, well-established methods.

The paper relies heavily on existing work, particularly NMF and the GCM [3]. While building upon established foundations is not inherently negative, the authors do not clearly articulate *why* LRTFS is a necessary or superior alternative. The theoretical connection to sparse Bayesian learning (SBL) is noted, but the crucial difference – the lack of free variance parameters in LRTFS leading to a loss of sparsity-inducing properties – is acknowledged rather than addressed as a potential drawback. The authors state that the third term in the MJLE objective function naturally appears and favors sparse solutions, but this feels more like a fortunate byproduct than a designed advantage and its effects are not rigorously analyzed. 

The experimental evaluation is also weak. The music decomposition example lacks quantitative evaluation. Visual inspection of spectrograms (Fig. 1) is insufficient to demonstrate the benefits of LRTFS. The speech enhancement results show a modest improvement (6.6 dB SNR improvement) over IS-NMF (6 dB) and OMLSA (4.6 dB). While a slight improvement is observed, it’s not substantial enough to justify the introduction of a new model and associated complexity. Furthermore, the comparison to OMLSA, a well-known and optimized algorithm, is limited. A more thorough comparison against state-of-the-art speech enhancement techniques is needed. The statement that the MMLE implementation doesn’t scale is a significant issue, as it prevents a complete comparison to SBL-related approaches.

The clarity of the paper is acceptable, but could be improved. The notation is sometimes dense, and the discussion of the EM algorithm, while detailed, could benefit from more intuition. The connection between the equations and the underlying concepts isn’t always clear. 

**Strengths:**

*   The idea of a generative model for the raw signal, rather than just its STFT, is appealing.
*   The multi-resolution extension offers potential for modeling signals with varying temporal characteristics.
*   The connection to SBL provides a potential theoretical framework.

**Weaknesses:**

*   Lack of compelling evidence of superiority over existing methods.
*   Modest experimental results.
*   Limited comparison to state-of-the-art algorithms.
*   MMLE implementation scalability issues.
*   Theoretical discussion of the loss of sparsity-inducing properties isn’t fully addressed.
*   The rationale for choosing LRTFS over GCM isn’t strong enough.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The multi-resolution aspect is a potentially novel contribution.
*   The paper could serve as a starting point for further research in low-rank time-frequency modeling.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The lack of substantial performance gains compared to established methods.
*   The limited scope of the experimental evaluation.
*   The theoretical concerns regarding the loss of sparsity.
*   The paper feels incremental rather than groundbreaking.



**Overall Assessment:** Reject.

While the paper presents a technically sound approach, it lacks the originality and significance required for acceptance at NIPS. The experimental results do not convincingly demonstrate the benefits of LRTFS, and the theoretical concerns regarding sparsity are not adequately addressed. I believe that the paper would benefit from more extensive experimentation, a more thorough comparison to existing methods, and a more compelling justification for the proposed approach. I would suggest the authors focus on showcasing a clear advantage in a specific application and providing a more rigorous theoretical analysis.
