

# Review of "Low-Rank Time-Frequency Synthesis"

## Summary and Relation to Previous Work

This paper proposes a novel low-rank time-frequency synthesis (LRTFS) model that generates raw audio signals directly from low-rank time-frequency structures, rather than operating on STFT magnitude spectrograms as previous methods (like the Gaussian Composite Model). The authors frame their approach as filling a gap in the literature by providing a generative model of the raw signal rather than its STFT. The paper connects their work to sparse Bayesian learning (SBL) and presents two estimation approaches: maximum joint likelihood estimation (MJLE) and maximum marginal likelihood estimation (MMLE). The model is then extended to multi-resolution representations, which the authors claim is not possible in traditional NMF settings.

While the paper builds on previous work in audio signal processing (particularly the GCM model [3] and SBL [4,5]), it doesn't sufficiently distinguish its contributions from related work. For instance, multi-resolution representations have been explored in NMF-based audio source separation before [8,9], and the paper's claim that their approach "opens doors to multi-resolution representations, that were not possible in the traditional NMF setting" is inaccurate.

## Strengths

The paper presents a theoretically grounded generative model for audio signals that directly addresses the limitations of working with STFT magnitude spectrograms. The connection to sparse Bayesian learning is interesting, and the MJLE algorithm is well-motivated. The multi-resolution extension is conceptually sound and could be valuable for audio applications where different signal components have different temporal resolutions. The experimental results on music decomposition and speech enhancement demonstrate reasonable improvements over baselines.

## Weaknesses

### Technical and Methodological Issues

1. **Notational Inconsistency**: The paper uses inconsistent notation for matrix elements (e.g., [WH]fn vs. (WH)_{fn}). In equation (1), the notation is particularly ambiguous without clarification.

2. **Incorrect Mathematical Statement**: In section 2.2, the paper states "In the general unsupervised setting where both W and H are estimated, WH must be low-rank such that K < F and K < N. However, in supervised settings where W is known, we may have K > F." This is mathematically incorrect. If W is known and K > F, then rank(WH) ≤ rank(W) ≤ F, so WH cannot be low-rank with K > F.

3. **Misleading Complexity Claim**: In section 3.2, the paper states "M is typically at least twice larger than T, the signal length. Using the Woodbury matrix identity, the expression of Σ(i) can be reduced to the inversion of a matrix of size T, but this is still too large for most signal processing applications." This is misleading. The Woodbury identity would reduce the inversion to a K × K matrix (not T × T), which is feasible if K is small (as it typically is in low-rank models).

4. **Ambiguous Experimental Results**: In section 5.1, the authors claim "best output SNR (about 26 dB)" - SNR is a ratio, not an absolute value. They should clarify whether this is the SNR of the reconstructed signal or the SNR improvement.

5. **Inaccurate Warm-Restart Claim**: Section 5.2 states "The hyperparameter λ is gradually decreased to a negligible value during iterations (resulting in a negligible residual e), a form of warm-restart strategy [13]." This is not a warm-restart strategy (which typically refers to using previous solutions as starting points), but rather a parameter annealing approach.

### Formatting and Presentation Issues

1. **Footnote Formatting**: The footnote marker "1" is placed incorrectly in the text, and the footnote content appears to be cut off.

2. **Typographical Errors**: "Low-Rank Time-Frequency Synthisis" in the title is misspelled. The reference "Nonnegative matrix factorization with the Itakura- Saito divergence" has an unnecessary space before "Saito".

3. **Reference Inconsistency**: The reference list has inconsistent formatting (e.g., "IEEE Signal Processing Magazine,, , ," vs. "Nature, 401:788–791,, , ,").

4. **Contradictory Statements**: The paper states "all variables in Eq. (2) are assumed complex-valued" but then discusses the real case using Hermitian symmetry, creating confusion.

### Theoretical and Experimental Weaknesses

1. **Lack of Theoretical Guarantees**: The paper fails to provide theoretical analysis of the EM algorithm's convergence properties or the quality of the solutions it produces.

2. **Weak Experimental Comparison**: The comparison to IS-NMF is limited (only one window length tested), and the paper doesn't compare to more recent methods in the field. The 0.6 dB improvement over IS-NMF (6.6 dB vs. 6 dB) is marginal and not convincingly demonstrated to be significant.

3. **Misrepresentation of Previous Work**: The claim that "multi-resolution representations were not possible in the traditional NMF setting" is incorrect, as multi-resolution NMF approaches have been explored previously (e.g., [8,9]).

4. **Unjustified Methodological Choice**: The paper states that MMLE "is not yet a generative model of the raw signal x(t) itself, but of its STFT" but then proceeds to present an approach that does model the raw signal. This creates confusion about the novelty of their contribution.

## Recommendation

While the paper presents an interesting approach to audio signal modeling with some potential value for audio applications, it suffers from multiple technical inaccuracies, methodological weaknesses, and presentation issues that significantly undermine its contribution. The mathematical errors (particularly the incorrect statement about K > F) are particularly concerning as they suggest a lack of rigorous understanding of the underlying concepts. The experimental evaluation is also insufficient to support the claimed advantages over existing methods.

The paper's claim of "new doors to multi-resolution representations" is overstated, as similar concepts have been explored before. The marginal performance improvements shown in the experiments do not sufficiently justify the novelty of the proposed method.

I recommend rejection due to these significant technical flaws and the lack of compelling evidence supporting the paper's claims. The authors should address these issues before the work can be considered for publication at NIPS.
