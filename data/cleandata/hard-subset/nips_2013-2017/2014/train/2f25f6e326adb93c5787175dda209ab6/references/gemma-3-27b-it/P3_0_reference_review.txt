## Review of "Low-Rank Time-Frequency Synthesis"

**Overall Assessment:** This paper presents a novel and well-motivated generative model for signal synthesis with a low-rank time-frequency structure, building upon the Gaussian Composite Model (GCM). The approach offers a compelling alternative to traditional NMF methods, particularly in its ability to model the raw signal directly and facilitate multi-resolution representations. While the technical details are solid and the experiments demonstrate promising results, the lack of scalable MMLE implementation and limited exploration of more complex experimental setups (beyond audio) leave room for improvement. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions, leaning towards a strong accept.

**Summary:** The authors address limitations of existing time-frequency decomposition techniques, specifically the inability to generate the raw signal from spectral representations (as with NMF) and the arbitrary discarding of phase information.  They introduce the Low-Rank Time-Frequency Synthesis (LRTFS) model, which directly synthesizes the signal from latent components with a low-rank constraint on their variance.  This is achieved by modeling the synthesis coefficients as Gaussian distributed with a covariance matrix dictated by a low-rank factorization (W,H). The paper details two estimation approaches – Maximum Joint Likelihood Estimation (MJLE) and Maximum Marginal Likelihood Estimation (MMLE) – using Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithms. Finally, they demonstrate the benefits of LRTFS, particularly its adaptability to multi-resolution representations, through experiments in music decomposition and speech enhancement.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty & Theoretical Soundness:** The core idea of directly synthesizing signals from a low-rank time-frequency representation is novel and builds logically upon existing work like the GCM.  The formulation connects naturally to sparse Bayesian learning (SBL), providing a useful perspective. The derivation of the MJLE and MMLE estimators is clear and mathematically rigorous.
* **Generative Modeling:**  Unlike traditional NMF, LRTFS provides a true *generative* model of the signal, allowing for signal reconstruction and opening doors to more sophisticated applications.
* **Multi-Resolution Capabilities:** The inherent flexibility of the model to accommodate multiple time-frequency bases is a significant advantage, especially for signals with varying characteristics (e.g., transients vs. sustained tones in music). This is a clear improvement over standard NMF approaches.
* **Clear Presentation:**  The paper is generally well-written and organized.  The mathematical notation is standard and easy to follow. The authors clearly explain the motivation and connection to existing work.
* **Demonstrated Performance:**  The experimental results in music decomposition and speech enhancement show promising performance improvements over baseline methods (IS-NMF and OMLSA).

**Weaknesses:**

* **Scalability of MMLE:** The authors acknowledge that their MMLE implementation does not scale well to realistic signal dimensions. While they present the theory, the lack of a scalable MMLE implementation limits the broader applicability and potential benefits of the MMLE approach. Addressing this in future work is crucial.
* **Limited Experimental Scope:**  The experiments are focused primarily on audio applications. While these are relevant, demonstrating the effectiveness of LRTFS on other types of signals (e.g., time series data, images) would strengthen the paper's impact.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The performance is reportedly sensitive to the choice of λ, and the authors rely on a manual search to find suitable values. A more systematic approach to hyperparameter optimization (e.g., cross-validation) would be beneficial.
* **Limited Ablation Studies:** While the paper compares against baseline methods, a more thorough ablation study exploring the impact of individual components of the LRTFS model (e.g., the low-rank constraint, the multi-resolution framework) would provide deeper insights into its behavior.




**Originality:** The work is original in its formulation of a generative model for signal synthesis with a low-rank time-frequency structure. While building upon the GCM and SBL, it provides a novel synthesis framework. The authors correctly position their work within the existing literature and clearly highlight the differences.

**Significance:** The LRTFS model has the potential to significantly impact various signal processing applications. The ability to generate signals, adapt to different resolutions, and potentially handle more complex signal structures makes it a promising tool for tasks like audio processing, source separation, and time-series analysis.

**Clarity:**  The paper is generally well-written and clear. The mathematical notation is standard, and the explanations are concise.  Minor improvements could be made in the clarity of the EM algorithm descriptions (e.g., explicitly stating the conditions for convergence).

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* Novel and theoretically sound generative model.
* Addresses a significant limitation of existing time-frequency decomposition methods.
* Demonstrates promising results in audio applications.
* Well-written and clearly presented.
* Potential for broader impact in signal processing.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* Lack of scalable MMLE implementation.
* Limited experimental scope beyond audio.
* Hyperparameter sensitivity and lack of systematic optimization.



**Recommendation:** Accept with minor revisions.  

**Specific Suggestions for Revision:**

* **Discuss limitations of MMLE more thoroughly.** Explain why scalability is a major challenge and potential avenues for addressing it (e.g., approximate inference techniques).
* **Consider including additional experiments** with different types of signals to demonstrate the generality of the approach.
* **Perform a more systematic hyperparameter optimization** using cross-validation or a similar technique.
* **Include a more detailed ablation study** to assess the impact of individual components of the LRTFS model.
