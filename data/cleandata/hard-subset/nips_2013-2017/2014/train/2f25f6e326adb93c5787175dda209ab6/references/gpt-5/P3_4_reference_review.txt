Summary
The paper proposes Low-Rank Time-Frequency Synthesis (LRTFS), a generative time-domain model where a signal x(t) is synthesized as a linear combination of time-frequency atoms with complex Gaussian coefficients αfn whose variances are constrained by a low-rank factorization WH. This contrasts with the Gaussian Composite Model (GCM) that imposes low-rank structure on the variance of analysis (STFT) coefficients. Two estimation strategies are presented: (i) maximum joint likelihood (MJLE) via an EM algorithm that leverages the Figueiredo–Nowak splitting to avoid large matrix inversions and alternates Wiener-like shrinkage of α and IS-NMF updates on |α|^2; and (ii) maximum marginal likelihood (MMLE, type-II ML/SBL-style) which is principled but not scalable in the current implementation. The framework naturally supports multi-resolution modeling by stacking multiple frames. Experiments illustrate a hybrid tonal/transient decomposition and a semi-supervised speech enhancement task, showing moderate gains over IS-NMF and OMLSA baselines.

Relation to prior work
The work connects several literatures: probabilistic NMF with the IS divergence (e.g., Fevotte et al.), complex Gaussian modeling of STFT magnitudes/variances (GCM), sparse Bayesian learning (Tipping; Wipf & Rao), and multi-resolution/sparse synthesis in audio (Daudet & Torresani; MCA). The main novelty is to move low-rank modeling from the analysis STFT to the synthesis coefficients in a time-domain generative model, with an efficient EM procedure. The connection to SBL is clear: α has a diagonal Gaussian prior, but with variances tied through WH rather than free per-coefficient, so the model aims at low-rank structure rather than sparsity. The multi-resolution extension by stacking frames is straightforward and aligns with hybrid morphologies in audio, but is less common in NMF-based approaches.

Strengths
- Conceptual contribution: a clean, time-domain generative framework tying complex synthesis coefficients with low-rank variance. This bridges analysis-domain NMF/GCM and synthesis modeling.
- Algorithmic design: the MJLE EM is elegant and computationally practical for large signals, avoiding matrix inversions and relying on fast Φ/Φ* operations. The decomposition of the joint objective into a data-fit term plus DIS(|α|^2|WH) + log(|α|^2) is insightful and links Wiener shrinkage with IS-NMF.
- Multi-resolution modeling: natural, simple extension with shared estimation machinery; fits audio’s transient/tonal structure.
- Clarity of derivations: the EM steps are mostly correct and traceable, with appropriate references to Figueiredo–Nowak and SBL theory.

Weaknesses / concerns
- Experimental validation is limited. The first experiment is illustrative without quantitative comparisons; λ is selected to maximize output SNR on the test instance (not a fair protocol). The second experiment reports average SNR improvement over 10 mixtures, but:
  - Baseline fairness is unclear. LRTFS uses pre-trained speech dictionaries; the IS-NMF baseline appears unsupervised with a single window. A fair comparison should include (i) supervised/semi-supervised IS-NMF with the same speech dictionaries; (ii) a GCM/complex NMF baseline that exploits phase and probabilistic modeling; (iii) multi-resolution NMF baselines (stacked features).
  - Only SNR is reported; standard speech metrics (STOI, PESQ) and statistical significance are missing.
  - The 0.6 dB average gain over IS-NMF is modest without further analysis.
- Missing or thin related work: phase-aware/complex NMF (e.g., Kameoka et al.; Fevotte & Badeau) and multiresolution NMF/TF-factorization variants are not discussed; there is also related convolutional dictionary learning/time-domain sparse coding literature relevant to synthesis modeling.
- Model/algorithmic details:
  - The λ update in MJLE is noted not to be the true EM solution; monotonicity guarantees are not fully established. In practice λ is treated as a hyperparameter and even annealed to near-zero; this may violate the β ≤ λ/δΦ condition required by the splitting (unclear how β is chosen/updated).
  - Identifiability and layer competition in the multi-resolution setting are not addressed. With overlapping frames, redundancies across layers can occur; there is no explicit regularization to discourage duplicative explanations beyond the low-rank prior and the weak log(|α|^2) term.
  - Sensitivity to K, initialization, β, and λ is not studied. No ablation contrasting single- vs multi-resolution within LRTFS.
  - For real signals, the Hermitian constraint is mentioned but not integrated in the inference; implications for complexity/accuracy are not discussed.
- Scalability of MMLE is limited, and no partial or approximate scalable alternative (e.g., conjugate gradients with matrix–vector products, diagonal/low-rank approximations, message passing) is attempted, despite being proposed as future work.

Quality
Technically sound overall; the derivations for MJLE are consistent, and the connection to IS-NMF and Wiener filtering is correct. The MMLE formulation is standard but unimplemented at scale. The empirical section does not yet provide strong evidence of superiority due to fairness and metric limitations.

Clarity
Generally clear and well-organized. Some minor notational/typographical issues (e.g., missing mean 0 in Eqs. (29)-(30); “unexpectedly” for the log term could be clarified by showing the algebra DIS(|α|^2|v)+log|α|^2 = |α|^2/v + log v + const). Provide explicit choices for β and how the λ–β constraint is enforced during annealing.

Originality
Moderate to good. The synthesis-domain low-rank variance with efficient EM is a novel and valuable combination. Multi-resolution via stacking is conceptually simple but useful. Closer positioning vis-à-vis complex NMF and multiresolution NMF would help sharpen the novelty.

Significance
Promising, especially for audio tasks where phase and time-domain modeling matter. However, the current experimental evidence is insufficient to demonstrate clear state-of-the-art gains or broad applicability.

Suggestions and questions
- Strengthen experiments: supervised/semi-supervised baselines (IS-NMF, GCM/complex NMF), multiresolution NMF baseline, STOI/PESQ, confidence intervals, runtime.
- Clarify λ/β handling; report sensitivity and convergence behavior.
- Add ablations: single vs multi-resolution; effect of K; effect of the log term (e.g., replace with fixed prior) to assess its role.
- Discuss identifiability and cross-layer competition; consider simple cross-inhibition or group-sparsity between layers.
- Expand related work on complex/phase-aware NMF and multiresolution factorization.

Pros for acceptance
- Clear, principled time-domain generative model with efficient EM; good conceptual bridge to NMF/Wiener filtering.
- Natural multi-resolution extension; practical and elegant.
- Potentially impactful for audio processing where phase matters.

Cons for acceptance
- Limited, partially unfair experimental evaluation; modest gains.
- Missing comparisons to closest probabilistic/phase-aware baselines.
- Some algorithmic details (λ/β) and sensitivity analyses are under-specified.

Overall
A solid and interesting methodological contribution with good potential, but the empirical section needs to be substantially strengthened and positioned against stronger baselines before publication. I lean toward rejection in the current form, with clear paths to improvement.
