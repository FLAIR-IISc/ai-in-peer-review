## Review of "Low-Rank Time-Frequency Synthesis"

**Summary:**

This paper introduces a novel generative model for signal synthesis, termed Low-Rank Time-Frequency Synthesis (LRTFS).  Building upon the Gaussian Composite Model (GCM) which operates on the STFT, LRTFS directly models the raw signal *x(t)* with a low-rank structure imposed on the variance of synthesis coefficients. The authors present a maximum likelihood estimation framework, including an EM algorithm for practical implementation, and demonstrate the applicability of the model to multi-resolution representations. The work is motivated by the limitations of existing NMF-based spectral decomposition approaches, particularly their inability to model phase information and their lack of a true generative model for the raw signal.  The authors showcase the efficacy of their approach through music decomposition and speech enhancement experiments.

**Relation to Previous Work:**

The paper positions itself within the broader field of matrix factorization, specifically in the context of time-frequency analysis and signal processing.  It builds directly on the GCM [Févotte et al., 2011], addressing its limitation of operating solely on the STFT. The work also draws connections to sparse Bayesian learning (SBL) [Tipping, 2001; Wipf & Rao, 2004], highlighting similarities and differences in the variance structure of the coefficients. The multi-resolution aspect relates to work in hybrid time-frequency representations [Daudet & Torrésani, 2009], though LRTFS provides a principled generative framework for combining such representations.  The speech enhancement application draws parallels to established techniques like OMLSA [Cohen, 1994], providing a comparison point.

**Strengths:**

* **Generative Model:** A key strength is the development of a true generative model for the raw signal, offering advantages over approaches that focus solely on the STFT magnitude. This allows for more accurate signal reconstruction and opens avenues for more sophisticated applications.
* **Multi-Resolution Capability:** The natural extension to multi-resolution representations is a significant benefit.  This allows the model to adapt to signals with varying temporal characteristics, like music and speech, without requiring separate processing stages.
* **Theoretical Foundation:** The paper provides a sound theoretical basis for the proposed model, grounded in probabilistic principles and maximum likelihood estimation.  The derivation of the EM algorithm is clear and follows established methodologies.
* **Computational Efficiency:** The presented MJLE EM algorithm, despite its complexity, avoids costly matrix inversions, making it relatively computationally tractable for signal processing applications, as evidenced by the experiments.
* **Empirical Validation:**  The experiments on music decomposition and speech enhancement demonstrate the practical effectiveness of the proposed approach and show competitive performance compared to existing methods.

**Weaknesses:**

* **MMLE Scalability:** While theoretically presented, the MMLE approach suffers from scalability issues due to the required matrix inversion, hindering its application to larger-scale problems. The authors acknowledge this limitation, but a discussion of potential approximate inference techniques for MMLE would strengthen the paper.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper notes sensitivity to the regularization parameter λ, requiring careful tuning for optimal performance. A more thorough analysis of hyperparameter sensitivity and potential automated tuning strategies would be beneficial.
* **Limited Comparative Analysis:** While comparisons to OMLSA are provided, a more comprehensive evaluation against state-of-the-art methods in both music decomposition and speech enhancement would be desirable. This includes comparing against deep learning-based approaches.
* **Clarity of Notation:** The notation, while generally clear, occasionally becomes dense and could benefit from simplification. For example, consistently using (f,n) or *m* for indexing would improve readability.
* **Lack of Theoretical Justification for λ:** The choice of treating λ as a hyperparameter without estimation seems arbitrary and goes against the theoretical framework. A justification for this choice or a discussion of the challenges in estimating λ would be helpful.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and technically sound generative model for signal synthesis, addressing limitations of existing approaches.
* The multi-resolution capability is a significant contribution, opening up new possibilities for signal processing applications.
* The experiments demonstrate the practical effectiveness of the proposed approach.
* The paper is clearly written and well-organized, providing sufficient detail for reproducibility.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The scalability issues with the MMLE approach limit the potential impact of the work.
* The hyperparameter sensitivity and limited comparative analysis raise concerns about the robustness and generalizability of the results.
* The lack of theoretical justification for fixing λ feels incomplete.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper presenting a valuable contribution to the field of time-frequency analysis and signal processing.  The proposed LRTFS model is a significant advancement over existing techniques, offering a more principled and flexible approach to signal synthesis. While the limitations regarding scalability, hyperparameter sensitivity, and the lack of full theoretical justification for λ are notable, they do not fundamentally detract from the overall quality of the work. The strengths of the paper, particularly its novelty, theoretical soundness, and empirical validation, outweigh the weaknesses.

**Recommendation:**

**Accept with minor revisions.** 

I recommend accepting this paper with minor revisions addressing the points raised above, specifically: 1) Discussing potential approximate inference techniques for MMLE to address scalability concerns. 2) Providing a more thorough analysis of hyperparameter sensitivity. 3) Expanding the comparative evaluation to include more state-of-the-art methods. 4) Providing a justification for treating λ as a hyperparameter rather than estimating it.
