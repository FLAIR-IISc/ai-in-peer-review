## Review of "Low-Rank Time-Frequency Synthesis"

**Summary:**

This paper introduces a novel generative model for signal synthesis called Low-Rank Time-Frequency Synthesis (LRTFS). Unlike traditional Non-negative Matrix Factorization (NMF) approaches that operate on the magnitude spectrogram, LRTFS directly models the complex-valued STFT coefficients assuming a low-rank structure on their variance. This allows for a true generative model of the raw signal, and naturally extends to multi-resolution representations. The authors present two estimation approaches – Maximum Joint Likelihood Estimation (MJLE) and Maximum Marginal Likelihood Estimation (MMLE) – accompanied by EM algorithms.  Experiments on music decomposition and speech enhancement demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed approach.

**Relationship to Prior Work:**

The work builds directly upon Gaussian Composite Models (GCM) [3], extending them to a true signal synthesis model. It connects to sparse Bayesian learning (SBL) [4, 5], highlighting the differences stemming from the structured (low-rank) variances in LRTFS compared to the unstructured variances in SBL. The paper acknowledges and builds upon the concept of hybrid representations in audio processing [8, 9], and relates to existing semi-supervised source separation techniques [11]. The introduction clearly positions the work within the context of matrix factorization methods in machine learning and signal processing.

**Strengths:**

* **Generative Model:** The key strength lies in developing a generative model for the raw signal, addressing a limitation of prior NMF-based approaches that primarily focused on spectral magnitude. This opens up possibilities for more comprehensive signal manipulation and analysis.
* **Multi-Resolution Capability:**  The inherent ability to handle multi-resolution representations is a significant advantage, especially for signals like audio with components exhibiting varying time scales (transients vs. sustained tones).
* **Theoretical Foundation:** The derivation of the LRTFS model and the EM algorithms provide a solid theoretical grounding for the approach. The connection to SBL offers insightful comparisons.
* **Promising Experimental Results:** The experiments on music decomposition and speech enhancement demonstrate the potential of LRTFS to achieve competitive or improved performance compared to existing methods.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The mathematical notation is clear, and the explanations are understandable.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Scalability of MMLE:**  The authors acknowledge the scalability issues with the MMLE approach, which limits its application to large-scale problems. This is a significant limitation, as MMLE is theoretically appealing due to its sparsity-inducing properties.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The experiments reveal sensitivity to the hyperparameter λ, requiring careful tuning. While not uncommon, a more robust approach to hyperparameter selection or adaptation would strengthen the results.
* **Limited Comparative Analysis:** While the speech enhancement experiment includes a comparison to IS-NMF and OMLSA, a more comprehensive comparison with state-of-the-art methods would provide a stronger validation of the LRTFS approach.
* **Lack of Detailed Ablation Studies:** Investigating the impact of different components of the model (e.g., the low-rank constraint, multi-resolution framework) through ablation studies would provide deeper insights into their contributions.
* **Missing Error Bars/Statistical Significance:** The reported SNR improvements in the speech enhancement experiment would be more convincing with error bars or statistical significance tests.




**Qualitative Evaluation:**

* **Quality (8/10):** The paper presents a technically sound model with a clear theoretical justification. The EM algorithms appear well-designed, and the experimental results are promising. However, the scalability limitations of MMLE and the hyperparameter sensitivity introduce some reservations.
* **Clarity (8.5/10):**  The paper is well-written and organized. The mathematical notation is clear, and the explanations are generally easy to follow.  
* **Originality (8/10):**  The combination of low-rank modeling, a generative signal synthesis approach, and the explicit handling of phase information is novel. While building on existing work (GCM, SBL), the authors propose a distinct and worthwhile contribution.
* **Significance (7.5/10):**  The potential for multi-resolution signal representation and a generative model for raw signals is significant. The results on music decomposition and speech enhancement are encouraging, but further validation with more extensive experiments and comparisons is needed to fully establish the impact of the work.

**Arguments for and against acceptance:**

**Pro:**

* Presents a novel and theoretically sound generative model for signal synthesis.
* Introduces a flexible multi-resolution framework.
* Demonstrates promising results on relevant audio applications.
* Clear and well-written presentation.

**Con:**

*  MMLE suffers from scalability issues.
*  Hyperparameter sensitivity and lack of a robust selection strategy.
*  Limited comparative analysis against state-of-the-art methods.
*  Absence of ablation studies and statistical significance testing.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a solid contribution to the field of time-frequency signal processing. The proposed LRTFS model is a novel and theoretically grounded approach with the potential to advance the state-of-the-art in signal synthesis and analysis. While the scalability limitations of MMLE and the need for more comprehensive experimentation are weaknesses, the strengths of the work outweigh these concerns. 

**Recommendation:**

**Accept with Minor Revisions.** I recommend accepting the paper with minor revisions. The authors should address the following:

* Provide more detailed comparative analysis against state-of-the-art methods.
* Consider performing ablation studies to assess the contribution of different model components.
* Include error bars or statistical significance tests in the experimental results.
* Briefly discuss potential approaches for improving the scalability of MMLE (even if not implemented).
