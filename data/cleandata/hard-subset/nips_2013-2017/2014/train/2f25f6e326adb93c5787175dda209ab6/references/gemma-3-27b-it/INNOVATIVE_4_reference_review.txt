## Review of Low-Rank Time-Frequency Synthesis

This paper introduces a novel generative model for signals based on low-rank structure in the *synthesis* coefficients of a time-frequency representation, contrasting with existing approaches that impose low-rank constraints on analysis coefficients (like the STFT). The core idea – representing a signal as a sum of atoms with low-rank, complex-valued synthesis coefficients – is compelling and potentially unlocks new capabilities, particularly multi-resolution representations. The work builds upon the Gaussian Composite Model (GCM) and aims to address its limitations by modeling the raw signal directly. The paper presents maximum likelihood estimation approaches (MJLE and MMLE) and demonstrates the approach on audio decomposition and speech enhancement tasks.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The central idea of low-rank synthesis is genuinely novel and represents a significant departure from conventional time-frequency decomposition methods. This isn't merely incremental improvement; it's a rethinking of the fundamental structure of signal representation. The connection to sparse Bayesian learning is also insightful, framing the work within a well-established theoretical framework.
* **Generative Modeling:**  The paper explicitly aims for a generative model of the raw signal, which is a strong point. This allows for principled signal synthesis and opens the door to more sophisticated applications beyond mere decomposition.
* **Multi-resolution Capability:** The extension to multi-resolution representations is a key advantage and addresses a practical limitation of existing NMF-based approaches. The potential for adapting resolution to signal characteristics (e.g., transients vs. sustained tones) is exciting.
* **Clear Presentation:** The mathematical formulation is relatively clear, and the paper is well-organized. The connection between the LRTFS model and existing methods like sparse Bayesian learning and the GCM is effectively explained.
* **Promising Experimental Results:** The speech enhancement results, while not a dramatic improvement, are competitive with established techniques, and the demonstration of hybrid decomposition is visually appealing and provides initial validation.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Scalability of MMLE:** The acknowledged scalability issues with the MMLE approach are a serious concern. While theoretically interesting, its impracticality for larger datasets limits its impact.
* **Limited Theoretical Analysis:** While the connection to sparse Bayesian learning is noted, a deeper theoretical analysis of the properties of LRTFS (e.g., identifiability, statistical consistency) would strengthen the paper.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The dependence on careful tuning of the regularization parameter λ, without a robust method for its automatic selection, is a practical limitation.
* **Experimental Scope:** The experiments are somewhat limited in scope. While the audio examples are illustrative, a broader range of datasets and evaluation metrics would provide more conclusive evidence of the approach's effectiveness. The comparison to baseline methods could be more extensive.
* **Lack of Ablation Studies:** It's unclear how much each component (low-rank constraint, generative modeling, multi-resolution) contributes to the overall performance. Ablation studies would help pinpoint the key ingredients of the method.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* **High Novelty:** This paper presents a truly novel approach to time-frequency analysis and signal synthesis.
* **Potential Impact:** The multi-resolution capability and generative modeling framework have the potential to significantly advance the field of audio processing and beyond.
* **Sound Theoretical Foundation:** The connection to sparse Bayesian learning provides a solid theoretical basis.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* **Scalability Concerns:** The impracticality of the MMLE algorithm limits the wider applicability of the method.
* **Limited Validation:**  The experimental validation is somewhat limited and lacks extensive comparison to state-of-the-art techniques.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The reliance on manual hyperparameter tuning is a practical drawback.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with a highly original idea. While the scalability issues with MMLE and the limited experimental validation are weaknesses, the novelty and potential impact of the approach are compelling.  I believe the potential rewards of this line of research outweigh the current limitations. The paper presents a significant conceptual advance, and I'm willing to overlook the minor flaws in favor of its bold direction.

**Recommendation:** **Accept** (with encouragement to address scalability and conduct more comprehensive experiments in future work). I would recommend that the authors clearly articulate the limitations of the current approach and future research directions in the final version of the paper.
