

# Review of "Low-Rank Time-Frequency Synthesis"

## Summary
This paper introduces Low-Rank Time-Frequency Synthesis (LRTFS), a novel generative model that extends previous work on nonnegative matrix factorization (NMF) and the Gaussian Composite Model (GCM) by providing a model for the raw signal rather than just its STFT. The authors build upon the GCM framework, which models the STFT as a low-rank Gaussian process, but extend it to model the raw signal directly. The key innovation is a synthesis model where the signal is represented as a sum of time-frequency atoms with low-rank structure in the synthesis coefficients' variance. The paper also demonstrates how LRTFS naturally accommodates multi-resolution representations, a significant advantage over traditional NMF approaches. The authors present two estimation methods (MJLE and MMLE) with EM algorithms and demonstrate the approach on music decomposition and speech enhancement tasks.

## Strengths
1. **Theoretical Contribution**: The paper makes a clear and meaningful theoretical advancement by bridging the gap between time-frequency synthesis and traditional NMF approaches. The connection to sparse Bayesian learning (SBL) is well-explained and highlights the key difference in the low-rank constraint.

2. **Multi-resolution Capability**: The natural support for multi-resolution representations is a significant practical contribution that addresses a major limitation of previous NMF-based approaches. This is well-explained with clear examples from audio processing.

3. **Experimental Validation**: The experiments on music decomposition and speech enhancement demonstrate the practical utility of the approach. The speech enhancement results (6.6 dB SNR improvement) are promising and compare favorably to baselines.

4. **Clarity of Presentation**: The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with a logical flow from problem statement to solution to evaluation. The mathematical derivations are thorough and well-motivated.

## Weaknesses
1. **Limited Baseline Comparisons**: In the speech enhancement experiments, the comparison is limited to two baselines (IS-NMF and OMLSA). The paper would benefit from including more recent state-of-the-art methods in the field, such as deep learning-based approaches, to better establish the contribution.

2. **Computational Scalability**: While the paper acknowledges that MMLE doesn't scale well, it doesn't thoroughly discuss the computational complexity of the proposed MJLE approach for large-scale audio processing. This is a critical consideration for real-world applications.

3. **Insufficient Experimental Scope**: The experiments are limited to two applications (music decomposition and speech enhancement). More diverse experiments across different audio processing tasks would strengthen the paper's claim of general applicability.

4. **Lack of Theoretical Guarantees**: The paper presents an EM algorithm that is guaranteed to decrease the objective function at each iteration, but doesn't provide convergence guarantees or theoretical analysis of the solution quality.

## Recommendation

The paper presents a technically sound and novel approach to time-frequency modeling that advances the state of the art in audio signal processing. The multi-resolution capability is particularly compelling and addresses a significant limitation of previous work. While the experimental results are promising, the paper would benefit from more comprehensive comparisons with state-of-the-art methods and a deeper discussion of computational scalability.

I recommend **acceptance** with the following suggestions for improvement:

1. Expand the speech enhancement experiments to include more recent baselines, particularly deep learning-based approaches that have shown strong performance in this domain.

2. Provide a more detailed analysis of the computational complexity of the MJLE algorithm, including runtime measurements for different signal lengths.

3. Include additional experiments demonstrating the multi-resolution capability on different audio sources or with different window sizes to better showcase this key advantage.

4. Add a brief discussion of theoretical convergence properties of the proposed EM algorithm.

5. Consider discussing potential limitations of the approach, such as sensitivity to the choice of time-frequency basis or window parameters.

The paper makes a meaningful contribution to the field of time-frequency analysis and signal processing with clear potential for impact in audio applications. With the suggested improvements, it would be a strong contribution to NIPS.
