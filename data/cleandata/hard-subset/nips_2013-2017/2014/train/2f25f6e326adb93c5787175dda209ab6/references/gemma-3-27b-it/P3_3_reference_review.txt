## Review of "Low-Rank Time-Frequency Synthesis"

**Overall Assessment:** This paper presents a compelling and technically sound contribution to the field of time-frequency analysis and signal processing, specifically addressing limitations of existing methods like NMF and the Gaussian Composite Model (GCM). The proposed Low-Rank Time-Frequency Synthesis (LRTFS) model offers a more principled generative approach to signal representation, directly modeling the raw signal instead of its STFT. While the implementation of the MMLE approach isn't scalable at this time, the MJLE algorithm is well-defined and demonstrably effective in the presented experiments. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.

**1. Summary and Related Work:**

The paper tackles the problem of signal representation within a low-rank time-frequency framework. Traditional methods like NMF operate on the magnitude spectrogram, discarding phase information and lacking a true generative model. The GCM [3] improved upon this by providing a probabilistic model for the STFT coefficients, but still fell short of modeling the raw signal itself. LRTFS directly addresses this gap, formulating a generative model where the signal is synthesized as a sum of atoms with low-rank variance on their synthesis coefficients. This approach builds on concepts from sparse Bayesian learning [4, 5] but crucially distinguishes itself by imposing a structured low-rank constraint on the coefficient variances, rather than allowing for independent variances as in SBL. The paper also effectively connects the proposed model to existing techniques like Wiener filtering [7] and morphological component analysis [10].  The authors appropriately cite relevant work and position their contribution within the existing literature.

**2. Strengths:**

* **Principled Generative Model:** The LRTFS model represents a significant step forward by providing a true generative model for the raw signal, offering a more complete and potentially more accurate representation than existing methods.
* **Multi-Resolution Capability:** The inherent flexibility of the synthesis approach allows for multi-resolution representations, which is a significant advantage for signals with varying time-frequency characteristics (e.g., music with transients and sustained tones).  The authors illustrate this well in Section 4.
* **Sound Theoretical Foundation:** The derivation of the MJLE and MMLE algorithms is clearly presented, and the connection to existing techniques like SBL and Wiener filtering is well-explained.
* **Practical Algorithm (MJLE):**  The EM algorithm for MJLE is computationally efficient and practical for applications like audio processing, avoiding the scalability issues of the MMLE approach.
* **Demonstrated Effectiveness:** The experimental results in both music decomposition and speech enhancement show promising performance improvements over baseline methods. The provided sound examples (mentioned as supplementary material) are crucial for evaluating the perceptual quality of the results.
* **Clear Writing:** The paper is generally well-written and organized, making the core ideas accessible to readers familiar with time-frequency analysis and signal processing.

**3. Weaknesses:**

* **MMLE Scalability:** The authors acknowledge the scalability limitations of the MMLE algorithm, which hinders its application to larger datasets. While the theoretical motivation for including MMLE is sound, its impracticality limits its immediate impact.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The performance is sensitive to the choice of the regularization parameter λ. While the authors mention this and describe a warm-restart strategy, further investigation into automatic parameter selection methods would strengthen the work.
* **Limited Comparative Analysis:** While comparisons to IS-NMF and OMLSA are provided for speech enhancement, a more comprehensive comparison with state-of-the-art methods in both music decomposition and speech enhancement would be beneficial.  For example, comparing against deep learning-based source separation techniques would be insightful.
* **Lack of Theoretical Analysis of Convergence:** While the EM algorithm is presented, a more rigorous analysis of its convergence properties (beyond simply stating it may converge to a local optimum) would enhance the theoretical grounding of the work.



**4. Detailed Comments:**

* **Section 2.1 (Generative Model):**  The explanation of the hermitian symmetry exploitation in the real case could be slightly more detailed for readers less familiar with signal processing fundamentals.
* **Section 3.1 (MJLE):** The justification for using the modified update rule for λ (not strictly derived from the EM procedure, but empirically shown to improve performance) could be elaborated on.
* **Section 5.1 (Hybrid Decomposition of Music):**  The evaluation metric (output SNR) is a useful starting point, but a perceptual evaluation metric (e.g., PESQ or STOI) might provide a more meaningful assessment of the decomposition quality.
* **Section 5.2 (Speech Enhancement):** The choice of training data for the speech dictionaries (TIMIT) could be discussed in terms of its generalizability to other speech corpora.




**5. Arguments for and against Acceptance:**

**Pro:**

* Novel and theoretically sound generative model for signal synthesis.
* Addresses limitations of existing methods like NMF and GCM.
* Practical and efficient MJLE algorithm.
* Demonstrated effectiveness in audio applications.
* Clear and well-written presentation.

**Con:**

* MMLE algorithm is not scalable.
* Sensitivity to hyperparameter selection.
* Limited comparative analysis with state-of-the-art methods.



**6. Recommendation:**

I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions. Specifically, I suggest the authors:

1.  Expand the discussion of hyperparameter selection.
2.  Provide a more comprehensive comparison to state-of-the-art methods in both experiments.
3.  Consider adding a perceptual evaluation metric to the music decomposition experiment.
4.  Elaborate on the justification for the modified λ update rule in Section 3.1.



This paper presents a significant contribution to the field and has the potential to impact future research in time-frequency analysis, signal processing, and audio applications. The clarity of the presentation and the practical effectiveness of the MJLE algorithm make it a valuable addition to the NIPS proceedings.
