## Review of "Low-Rank Time-Frequency Synthesis"

**Summary:**

This paper presents a novel generative model for signals, termed Low-Rank Time-Frequency Synthesis (LRTFS), building upon the Gaussian Composite Model (GCM) [3].  While GCM models the variance of the *analysis* coefficients (STFT), LRTFS models the variance of the *synthesis* coefficients, offering a more direct approach to signal reconstruction. The authors propose maximum likelihood estimation via Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithms and demonstrate the model's applicability to multi-resolution representation and audio tasks (music decomposition and speech enhancement). A key benefit highlighted is the ability to perform synthesis directly, unlike methods focused solely on spectral decomposition.

**Relation to Prior Work:**

The work builds directly on NMF [2], GCM [3], and sparse Bayesian learning (SBL) [4, 5]. It addresses limitations of traditional NMF by providing a probabilistic generative model for the STFT and extends GCM to model the raw signal itself.  The connection to SBL is explicitly noted, but LRTFS distinguishes itself through the low-rank constraint imposed on the *variances* of the synthesis coefficients, rather than allowing free variance parameters as in SBL. The authors also relate the work to morphological component analysis [10] and existing speech enhancement techniques [14].

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** LRTFS provides a fresh perspective on time-frequency decomposition by directly modeling synthesis coefficients. This addresses a limitation of existing methods that primarily focus on analysis.
* **Generative Model:** The paper presents a complete generative model for the signal, moving beyond methods that only decompose the spectrogram. This opens doors to signal synthesis and more robust representations.
* **Multi-resolution Capability:**  The natural extension to multi-resolution representation is a significant strength, particularly relevant for audio signals where different components have varying temporal characteristics.  The proposed implementation of this is well-described.
* **Theoretical Foundation:**  The derivation of the EM algorithms, building on previous work in sparse learning [6], is clearly presented.
* **Empirical Validation:** The experiments, while limited, demonstrate promising results in both music decomposition and speech enhancement, comparing favorably to existing techniques.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Computational Complexity (MMLE):** The authors acknowledge the scalability issues with the MMLE approach, which limits its practical application.  The complexity analysis is appreciated, but it's a significant drawback given the potential benefits of marginal likelihood estimation.
* **Limited Experiments:** The experiments are somewhat limited in scope. While the results are encouraging, more extensive evaluations across diverse datasets and noise conditions would strengthen the claims.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The authors mention that the performance is sensitive to the hyperparameter λ and required a selection process. More discussion on hyperparameter tuning and robustness would be valuable.
* **Clarity in Notation:** While generally clear, the paper occasionally suffers from slightly dense notation, particularly when introducing multiple bases for multi-resolution analysis.
* **Incremental Advance:** While the model is novel, the performance gains in speech enhancement are modest (6.6dB vs 6dB for IS-NMF, 4.6dB for OMLSA). This raises the question of whether the increased complexity of LRTFS is justified by the marginal improvement.



**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a solid contribution to the field of time-frequency analysis. The LRTFS model is a conceptually sound and theoretically well-motivated approach. The authors clearly articulate the benefits of modeling synthesis coefficients directly and demonstrate the model’s potential through insightful experiments. While the MMLE algorithm faces scalability challenges and the experimental results show modest improvements, the novelty and generative nature of the model, coupled with its multi-resolution capabilities, make it a worthwhile contribution.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* Novel and theoretically sound generative model for signal synthesis.
* Addresses a known limitation of existing time-frequency decomposition methods.
* Demonstrates potential in audio applications, particularly multi-resolution modeling.
* Clear presentation of the model and EM algorithm derivations.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* Limited experimental evaluation and modest performance gains in speech enhancement.
* Scalability issues with the MMLE approach.
* Sensitivity to hyperparameter settings.



**Rating:**

**Accept (Borderline)** - I recommend acceptance, but with the caveat that the authors should address the concerns raised regarding the limited experimental validation and computational complexity in a future work.  Specifically, the authors should either provide a more convincing argument for the practical utility of MMLE (perhaps with approximations) or focus solely on MJLE and further improve its performance on more diverse datasets.
