Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes a generative, time-domain synthesis model with a low-rank time-frequency (t-f) structure for the synthesis coefficients. The signal x(t) is synthesized as a linear combination of t-f atoms φfn(t) with complex Gaussian synthesis coefficients αfn whose variances follow a low-rank structure vfn = [WH]fn, plus Gaussian residual e(t). This “Low-Rank Time-Frequency Synthesis” (LRTFS) contrasts with the Gaussian Composite Model (GCM) of [3], which places a low-rank variance model on analysis (STFT) coefficients yfn. The paper develops two estimation approaches: (i) maximum joint likelihood estimation (MJLE) using an EM algorithm derived via an auxiliary variable scheme (in the spirit of Figueiredo & Nowak [6]), leading to alternating updates resembling iterative shrinkage for α and IS-NMF updates for W,H; and (ii) maximum marginal likelihood estimation (MMLE), equivalent to type-II ML as in sparse Bayesian learning (SBL) [4,5], but noted to be computationally prohibitive at audio scales due to large matrix inversions. The framework naturally extends to multi-resolution modeling by stacking multiple t-f frames (e.g., short and long windows) with separate NMF priors on each set of synthesis coefficients.

The approach connects three lines of prior work: NMF/IS-NMF for spectrograms [1,3], phase-aware probabilistic modeling of complex STFTs (e.g., GCM [3]), and SBL [4,5]. Unlike standard NMF (often on magnitudes), LRTFS is a generative model of the raw waveform. Unlike SBL, LRTFS imposes structured variances via WH tying rather than independent ARD parameters. The multi-resolution extension relates to hybrid/morphological decompositions in audio [8–10], but with a probabilistic low-rank variance prior rather than sparsity penalties.

Quality (technical soundness)
- Model and objective: The joint negative log-likelihood decomposes into a data fidelity term (1/λ)‖x−Φα‖² plus a term DIS(|α|²|v) + log(|α|²), consistent with Gaussian priors on α with variances v and IS divergence between |α|² and WH. The derivation is internally consistent; the appearance of the log(|α|²) term follows from the relation between Gaussian likelihood and IS divergence.
- EM for MJLE: The auxiliary-variable EM using z = α + √β e1 and x = Φz + e2 (with β ≤ λ/δΦ) is soundly adapted from [6]. The E-step implies a gradient-like correction z = α + (β/λ)Φ*(x−Φα); the M-step gives closed-form Wiener-like updates α←(v/(v+β)) z and standard IS-NMF updates for W,H. No matrix inversion is required beyond transforms, which is appropriate for large-scale audio.
- MMLE: The type-II ML approach is standard and well-derived, but computationally infeasible at presented scales; this limitation is transparently acknowledged.
- Reconstruction of components: The Wiener-type decomposition for αk given α is coherent with the model structure.
- Implementation choices: For λ, the paper departs from the exact EM update and uses a simpler residual-based update (or treats λ as a hyperparameter), noting practical issues; this is a reasonable engineering compromise but weakens end-to-end likelihood optimization guarantees.
- Potential gaps: Convergence properties are local only (nonconvexity). The conditions and selection of β are not detailed beyond the upper bound, and the impact of β on convergence speed/accuracy is not experimentally analyzed. The generative assumptions (circular complex Gaussians, frame normalization) are not fully specified, which may matter in practice.

Clarity
The paper is generally clear and well-organized, with precise equations and algorithmic steps. The multi-resolution extension is straightforward and well-explained. Suggestions:
- Specify the normalization of frames Φ and the exact STFT/frame implementation assumptions to ensure reproducibility (tight frame vs. general frame).
- Clarify initialization strategies for α, W, H and sensitivity to initialization.
- Detail β selection and its practical range. State whether β is fixed or adapted during iterations.
- Provide algorithmic complexity per iteration and wall-clock times for typical audio signals.
- The references in the provided list lack years and complete details; ensure proper citation formatting.

Originality
Modeling low-rank structure directly on synthesis coefficients in the time domain, rather than on analysis coefficients (GCM), appears novel and conceptually distinct. The auxiliary-variable EM architecture is adapted from known work [6], and the type-II ML formulation follows SBL [4,5]; these are appropriate reuses rather than novel contributions per se. The multi-resolution extension is natural and elegant within LRTFS and arguably simpler than ad hoc multi-window setups in STFT-NMF. While related to phase-aware/complex NMF and hybrid sparse models [8–10], the specific probabilistic synthesis formulation with low-rank variance tying is a meaningful new combination.

Significance
The ability to model waveforms directly with multi-resolution low-rank structure is potentially impactful for audio decomposition and enhancement. The MJLE algorithm is scalable, relying only on forward/backward transforms and NMF updates. However, the experimental evidence, while promising, is limited:
- Experiment 1 is illustrative and selects λ post hoc for best SNR (introducing selection bias).
- Experiment 2 shows a modest average SNR improvement over a single-window IS-NMF baseline (6.6 vs. 6.0 dB) and OMLSA (4.6 dB) on 10 signals. No perceptual metrics (e.g., STOI/PESQ) or statistical tests are reported. Comparisons to phase-aware NMF/complex NMF or multi-resolution baselines are absent.
More comprehensive evaluations would strengthen the case for broad adoption.

Pros
- Clear, principled generative model bridging time-domain synthesis and low-rank t-f structure.
- Scalable EM algorithm for MJLE with simple, interpretable updates.
- Natural, effective multi-resolution extension with separate low-rank priors per resolution.
- Conceptual connection to SBL with structured variances is insightful.
- Demonstrated applicability to hybrid decomposition and speech enhancement with competitive results.

Cons
- Experimental section is limited in scale and breadth; baselines and metrics could be stronger (e.g., complex/phase-aware NMF, perceptual metrics, ablations).
- Hyperparameter λ tuning is ad hoc in Experiment 1; annealing to near-zero in Experiment 2 reduces the role of residual modeling.
- No runtime/complexity benchmarks; MMLE remains impractical without scalable approximations.
- Some notational/implementation details (frame normalization, β selection, initialization) are under-specified.

Suggestions for improvement
- Add comparisons to phase-aware/complex NMF and to multi-window NMF baselines to isolate the benefit of synthesis modeling and multi-resolution.
- Report STOI/PESQ (or DNSMOS) alongside SNR; include confidence intervals across more test cases.
- Provide ablations: single vs. multi-resolution within LRTFS; MJLE vs. GCM; effect of β and λ schedules.
- Include computational cost per iteration and total runtime.
- Clarify implementation details for reproducibility and complete the references.

Overall recommendation
The paper is technically sound, clearly written, and proposes a novel and potentially significant modeling viewpoint with a practical algorithm. The main weakness is modest and somewhat limited empirical validation. With expanded experiments and stronger baselines, this work would be a solid contribution to NeurIPS at the intersection of probabilistic modeling and audio signal processing.
