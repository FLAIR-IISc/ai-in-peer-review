This paper introduces a novel generative model, Low-Rank Time-Frequency Synthesis (LRTFS), for signal decomposition, particularly focusing on audio. It builds upon the Gaussian Composite Model (GCM) [3] by directly modeling the raw signal x(t) rather than its STFT. The core idea is to represent the signal as a sum of weighted atoms from a time-frequency basis, with the synthesis coefficients having a low-rank structure enforced through a probabilistic model. The paper details the model formulation, two estimation approaches (MJLE and MMLE using EM algorithms), and demonstrates its ability to handle multi-resolution representations. It concludes with experimental validation on music decomposition and speech enhancement tasks.

The paper addresses a relevant and challenging problem in signal processing and machine learning – effective signal decomposition and representation. The motivation for moving beyond magnitude spectrograms and incorporating phase information is well-articulated. The connection to sparse Bayesian learning is insightful, highlighting the differences and advantages of LRTFS. The extension to multi-resolution analysis is a significant contribution, enabling adaptive time-frequency representations. The experiments presented offer initial evidence of the method's effectiveness, particularly in speech enhancement where it outperforms a standard IS-NMF baseline and a traditional OMLSA approach.  The theoretical foundation is solid, and the mathematical derivations appear correct. The authors also demonstrate awareness of related work, citing key publications in NMF, sparse representation, and time-frequency analysis.

However, the paper suffers from several weaknesses. Firstly, while the mathematical formulation is complete, the explanation surrounding the motivation for certain design choices (e.g., why choose a Gaussian prior, the specific form of the low-rank constraint) could be more intuitive. A more thorough discussion of the statistical assumptions would be beneficial.

Secondly, the MMLE approach is presented but ultimately deemed impractical due to computational complexity, which feels like wasted space. While acknowledging the limitations is honest, a more focused presentation would have streamlined the paper. The computational bottleneck is identified (matrix inversion of size *T*), but a more in-depth discussion of potential solutions or approximations would be valuable, even if only briefly mentioned as future work.

Thirdly, the experimental section, while promising, lacks depth. The descriptions of the experimental setup are somewhat terse.  Specifically, the parameter settings (e.g., window sizes, number of components, λ values) are mentioned but not justified.  A more comprehensive ablation study investigating the sensitivity of the method to these parameters would significantly strengthen the results. Furthermore, the quantitative evaluation primarily relies on SNR improvement. Including other metrics (e.g., perceptual audio quality measures like PESQ or STOI for speech enhancement, or more musically relevant metrics for music decomposition) would provide a more comprehensive assessment. The sound examples are mentioned, but access to these examples should be directly provided or a clear indication of where to find them (beyond "supplementary material"). Finally, the comparison with other state-of-the-art methods besides the listed baselines is missing.

Fourthly, the writing, while generally clear, could benefit from more concise phrasing. The repetition of ideas across sections sometimes makes the paper feel longer than necessary.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel generative model for signal synthesis with low-rank time-frequency structure.
*   Addresses a significant limitation of traditional NMF by modeling phase information.
*   Enables multi-resolution representations, a key advantage for adapting to signal characteristics.
*   Demonstrates promising results in audio applications, outperforming existing baselines in speech enhancement.
*   Solid theoretical foundation and careful mathematical derivations.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Limited depth in the experimental section, lacking a thorough ablation study and comprehensive evaluation metrics.
*   The inclusion of a computationally intractable MMLE approach adds length without substantial contribution.
*   The motivation behind some design choices could be more clearly explained.
*   Writing style could be more concise.
*   Missing comparison to stronger state-of-the-art methods.



**Overall Assessment:**

I rate this paper as **Weak Reject**. While the core idea is novel and the theoretical work is sound, the lack of thorough experimentation and the inclusion of impractical methods detract from its overall impact. The presented results are promising, but they are not sufficiently convincing to warrant acceptance to NeurIPS.

**Recommendations for Improvement:**

*   Significantly expand the experimental section with a more thorough ablation study, including sensitivity analysis of key parameters.
*   Incorporate additional evaluation metrics beyond SNR improvement, particularly those relevant to perceptual audio quality.
*   Remove or significantly condense the discussion of MMLE, focusing instead on a more detailed analysis of MJLE.
*   Provide a more intuitive explanation of the statistical assumptions underlying the model.
*   Include a comparison to stronger state-of-the-art methods in both music decomposition and speech enhancement.
*   Make the sound examples easily accessible.
*   Revise the writing for conciseness and clarity.
