Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes Low-Rank Time-Frequency Synthesis (LRTFS), a generative, time-domain model that imposes a low-rank structure on the variances of synthesis (not analysis) time-frequency coefficients. Concretely, the signal is synthesized as x(t) = Σ_{f,n} α_fn φ_fn(t) + e(t), with α_fn ∼ Nc(0, [WH]_{fn}), thereby tying the variances of synthesis coefficients through a nonnegative low-rank factorization WH. This contrasts with the Gaussian Composite Model (GCM) for STFT analysis coefficients (e.g., Fevotte et al.) that models the STFT directly (often discarding phase). The authors develop two estimation strategies: (i) maximum joint likelihood estimation (MJLE) with an EM algorithm leveraging a Figueiredo–Nowak auxiliary-variable split, yielding scalable updates that alternate Wiener-like shrinkage of α and IS-NMF on |α|^2; and (ii) maximum marginal likelihood estimation (MMLE) in the spirit of Sparse Bayesian Learning (SBL), where α is marginalized and variances are updated via type-II ML. They highlight that, unlike standard SBL, LRTFS ties the per-coefficient variances via WH instead of learning each independently, thus inducing structured low rank rather than sparsity. A key advantage of working in synthesis is a straightforward multi-resolution extension by stacking multiple frames with different time supports; the paper provides the corresponding EM updates. Experiments demonstrate (a) a tonal/transient decomposition of a jazz clip and (b) single-channel speech enhancement with applause, where the multi-resolution LRTFS performs slightly better than an IS-NMF baseline and an OMLSA denoiser.

Strengths
- Conceptual novelty: Shifts low-rank modeling from analysis/STFT space to synthesis/time-domain with a coherent probabilistic formulation. This is a meaningful new direction that bridges NMF-style low-rank modeling and signal synthesis.
- Handles phase implicitly and naturally, avoiding phase-reconstruction issues endemic to magnitude-only NMF.
- Elegant multi-resolution extension: stacking frames with different window lengths is seamless in the synthesis model and leads to a simple, scalable EM algorithm.
- Solid algorithmic design: the MJLE EM scheme is computationally light (no large matrix inversions), reusing efficient TF operators and standard IS-NMF updates; clear link to Wiener filtering and to the first-iteration view of shrinkage methods.
- Promising empirical signals: speech enhancement gains over IS-NMF and OMLSA, and illustrative hybrid decomposition.

Weaknesses
- Empirical validation is limited: small-scale, narrow metrics (SNR only), and modest improvements. No ablations (e.g., single vs multi-resolution LRTFS, with/without residual, dependence on K or λ).
- The noise variance λ is treated as a tuned hyperparameter (and even decreased to near-zero during training), which weakens the generative story and may bias comparisons; the reported “best SNR” selection suggests a degree of ex post tuning.
- The MMLE route is not scalable and thus untested in realistic audio settings; no approximate scalable alternative (e.g., iterative solvers, low-rank preconditioners, AMP/EP) is explored beyond a brief outlook.
- Baselines are limited: multi-resolution counterparts for NMF or phase-aware/complex NMF are not considered; no runtime comparisons.
- Minor clarity issues: a few typos (e.g., missing mean “0” in Nc(0, [W H]_{fn}) in Sec. 4) and formatting glitches in references; hermitian symmetry and real-signal handling are only briefly mentioned.

Quality
Technically sound overall. The MJLE EM derivation is clear and leverages well-understood components (IS-NMF, Wiener filtering, proximal-like splitting). The MMLE formulation aligns with SBL but is computationally heavy; still, it’s correctly positioned as future work. Claims are mostly supported, though the λ handling and limited baselines weaken the empirical case.

Clarity
The paper is generally well written and organized. The model, its relation to GCM/SBL, and the multi-resolution extension are clearly presented. Some notational slips and reference formatting should be fixed. Providing more details on λ selection and algorithmic complexity would help reproducibility.

Originality
High. Modeling a low-rank structure on synthesis coefficient variances in a time-domain generative model is a notable conceptual shift relative to GCM and magnitude NMF. The structured-variance view versus standard SBL is insightful. The multi-resolution synthesis treatment is clean and compelling.

Significance
Moderate to high. The approach opens a new line of work for phase-aware, multi-resolution, probabilistic time-domain modeling with low-rank structure. The immediate empirical gains are modest but the formulation is general and likely to spur follow-up research in audio, inverse problems, and structured Bayesian modeling.

Suggestions for improvement
- Automate λ: adopt hierarchical priors/evidence maximization or robustified likelihoods; avoid hand-tuning or post-hoc selection.
- Scalable MMLE: explore conjugate-gradient with implicit operators, low-rank preconditioners, diagonal/expectation approximations, or AMP/EP message passing as suggested.
- Stronger experiments: include ablations (multi- vs single-resolution LRTFS), additional baselines (multi-window NMF; complex/phase-aware NMF), metrics beyond SNR (PESQ, STOI), runtime, and more diverse noises/datasets.
- Clarify real-signal handling, hermitian symmetry, and provide code/audio for reproducibility.
- Fix minor typos and reference formatting.

Pros
- Clear, bold new modeling direction (synthesis-domain low-rank variance)
- Natural multi-resolution capability with simple, scalable EM updates
- Conceptually unifies IS-NMF and Wiener filtering in a generative time-domain model
- Promising initial results

Cons
- Limited and somewhat hand-tuned empirical evaluation
- MMLE not yet practical; no approximate scalable alternative demonstrated
- Missing stronger baselines and ablations
- Minor clarity/notation issues

Recommendation
Weak accept. Despite imperfect validation and some engineering gaps, the core idea is fresh and potentially influential. Given its conceptual novelty and elegant formulation, I would favor acceptance to encourage exploration of synthesis-domain low-rank generative models and multi-resolution extensions.
