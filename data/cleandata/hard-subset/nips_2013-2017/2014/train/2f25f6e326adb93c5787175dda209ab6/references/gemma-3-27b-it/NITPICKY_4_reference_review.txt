This paper introduces a low-rank time-frequency synthesis (LRTFS) model, extending probabilistic latent factor models like the Gaussian Composite Model (GCM) to directly model the raw signal rather than just its STFT. The core idea is to express the signal as a sum of atoms with low-rank structure imposed on the synthesis coefficients. The authors propose maximum likelihood estimation approaches (MJLE and MMLE) and demonstrate the method’s potential for multi-resolution representations and applications in music decomposition and speech enhancement.

The paper is generally well-written and the motivation is clear. The connection to existing work in NMF, probabilistic latent factor models, and sparse Bayesian learning is appropriately established. However, a meticulous examination reveals numerous issues, ranging from notational inconsistencies to questionable methodological choices and insufficient experimental validation. 

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The direct synthesis approach, aiming to model the raw signal, is a genuine extension of existing generative models for time-frequency representations. The concept of multi-resolution LRTFS is promising.
*   **Theoretical Foundation:** The derivation of the LRTFS model and the MJLE/MMLE estimation procedures demonstrate a solid understanding of probabilistic modeling and signal processing.
*   **Potential Applications:** The application to music decomposition and speech enhancement demonstrates the applicability of the approach to relevant real-world problems.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Notational Inconsistencies:** The notation is confusing. For example, the indices `f` and `n` are used for frequency and time frames, but also for component indexing (e.g., α<sub>fn</sub>).  The inconsistent use of `m` and `(f,n)` to index coefficients is needlessly confusing. The definition of `v` is also unclear, shifting between representing `WH` and its vectorized form without explicit clarification.
*   **Lack of Rigor in Model Justification:** The claim that assuming a centered STFT is equivalent to a valid assumption in the time domain for many signals feels unsubstantiated.  A more detailed justification or citation is needed.
*   **Implementation Details & Scalability:** While the MJLE algorithm avoids explicit matrix inversions, the MMLE approach is deemed impractical due to its computational cost (inversion of a T x T matrix).  This is a significant limitation, and the paper doesn’t offer strategies for addressing this scalability issue beyond mentioning future work. The justification of ignoring the low-rank constraint in MMLE is not very convincing.
*   **Insufficient Experimental Detail:** The experimental section lacks crucial details. The choice of hyperparameters (beyond mentioning λ being treated as a hyperparameter) is not discussed. The criteria used to select the "best" results for the music decomposition are not clearly defined (e.g., output SNR is mentioned, but its calculation is not described). There is no ablation study to demonstrate the benefit of the LRTFS model *specifically* versus simpler approaches.
*   **Baseline Comparison:** The comparison to IS-NMF is limited. The window size for IS-NMF is arbitrarily chosen as an intermediate length, with no explanation. A more thorough comparison with established methods and careful parameter tuning would be necessary.
*   **Missing Error Analysis:** No discussion of failure cases or limitations of the method.
*   **Minor Errors:** Equation (19) for updating λ appears to be incorrect, as it doesn't precisely follow the EM procedure as indicated in the text. (It is mentioned that this update rule still reduces the negative log-likelihood, but the lack of a clear derivation is concerning.)

**Pro/Con Acceptance:**

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The idea of directly synthesizing the signal from low-rank time-frequency components is novel and potentially impactful.
*   The theoretical framework is sound and builds upon well-established probabilistic methods.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   Numerous notational inconsistencies and lack of rigor create confusion.
*   The scalability issues with MMLE represent a significant practical limitation.
*   Insufficient experimental detail and weak baseline comparison make it difficult to assess the true benefits of the proposed method.
*   The many small issues demonstrate a lack of meticulousness in the development and presentation of the work.

**Overall Assessment:**

Despite the novelty of the approach, the numerous flaws, both large and small, significantly detract from the paper’s overall quality. The lack of thorough experimental validation and the scalability limitations raise serious concerns. While the ideas presented are interesting, the paper does not meet the high standards expected of a NIPS publication.

**Recommendation:** **Reject**. The authors should address the notational issues, provide more detailed experimental validation with robust baseline comparisons, and explore scalable alternatives to MMLE before resubmitting. A more rigorous justification of key modeling assumptions (e.g., the centered STFT assumption) is also required.
