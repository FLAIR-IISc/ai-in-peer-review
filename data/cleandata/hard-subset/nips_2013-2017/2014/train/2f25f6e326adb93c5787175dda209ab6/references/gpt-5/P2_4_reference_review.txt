Summary
This paper proposes a generative, low-rank time–frequency synthesis (LRTFS) model for raw time-domain signals. Instead of modeling the STFT magnitudes or the STFT itself (as in IS-NMF or the Gaussian Composite Model, GCM), the authors synthesize the signal as a linear combination of time–frequency atoms with complex Gaussian synthesis coefficients αfn whose variances are constrained to have a low-rank structure via WH. This bridges nonnegative matrix factorization with a time-domain generative model and preserves phase in a principled way. Two estimation strategies are presented: (i) Maximum Joint Likelihood Estimation (MJLE) with an EM algorithm inspired by Figueiredo & Nowak’s variable-splitting trick to avoid large matrix inversions; this reduces to an analysis–synthesis step plus Wiener-like shrinkage for α and an IS-NMF update on |α|^2. (ii) Maximum Marginal Likelihood Estimation (MMLE), analogous to SBL “type-II” ML, with EM treating α as hidden; while elegant, it is not scalable due to large inversions. The framework naturally extends to multi-resolution by stacking multiple frames (e.g., short/long windows) with separate low-rank structures. Experiments illustrate a tonal/transient decomposition on music and a semi-supervised speech enhancement task, reporting modest gains over IS-NMF and OMLSA.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The probabilistic formulation is coherent and relates cleanly to prior work in IS-NMF, GCM, and SBL. The MJLE objective derivation and the EM updates are plausible; the use of the Figueiredo–Nowak architecture is appropriate to ensure scalable iterations without large inversions. The link between the objective and an IS divergence on |α|^2 plus a log(|α|^2) sparsity term is interesting, though the derivation of the log term is only sketched and would benefit from an explicit derivation. The MMLE derivation closely mirrors SBL and is standard.
- Algorithmic correctness and convergence: The monotonicity/decrease arguments for MJLE rely on established majorization/minimization for NMF and on properties of the variable-splitting EM; however the λ update used is heuristic (authors note it still decreases the objective per [6]). In practice, the experiments fix or anneal λ, so full ML is not performed.
- Empirical evaluation: The experiments are illustrative but limited. The music demo shows plausible layer separation and SNR improvement (selected by tuning λ on the test signal), but is qualitative. The speech enhancement study on 10 signals shows a +6.6 dB SNR improvement vs +6 dB with IS-NMF (single resolution) and +4.6 dB with OMLSA; this is encouraging but the margin over a strong IS-NMF baseline is modest and evaluation is narrow (SNR only; no STOI/PESQ; no statistical analysis). There is no runtime/complexity report, no ablations (e.g., effect of β, initialization, K, multi-resolution vs single-resolution within LRTFS), and no comparison to phase-aware/complex NMF or probabilistic TF models that reconstruct waveforms via inverse STFT.
- Reproducibility: Key implementation choices are under-specified (β selection, initialization, stopping criteria, training of speech dictionaries, computational cost). The real-signal hermitian handling is mentioned but not detailed. Notably, λ is tuned or annealed ad hoc. No code is provided.

Clarity
- The paper is generally well organized, with clear equations and algorithms. The multi-resolution extension is elegantly presented.
- However, there are issues: some derivations are omitted (e.g., the appearance of the log(|α|^2) term); MMLE scalability discussion could give practical approximations; and the reference list appears scrambled/incomplete (e.g., TIMIT is cited as [12] but the bibliography entry is unrelated). These should be fixed for readers to verify claims and reproduce results.

Originality
- The central idea—imposing a low-rank variance structure on synthesis coefficients in a time-domain generative model—differs meaningfully from GCM (which operates on analysis coefficients) and from SBL (which uses independent variances without low-rank coupling). The multi-resolution extension is natural and attractive.
- The EM algorithm combines known ingredients (Wiener-like shrinkage, IS-NMF, Figueiredo–Nowak splitting) in a novel way for this problem. To my knowledge, the synthesis-side low-rank Gaussian model with multi-resolution and scalable MJLE is not standard in prior NIPS/SP literature.

Significance
- For audio ML/signal processing, this is a promising direction: a principled time-domain generative model that integrates phase, supports multi-resolution, and yields scalable estimation. It could spur follow-on work (e.g., scalable MMLE, multichannel extensions, structured priors).
- That said, the current empirical gains over strong baselines are modest and the evaluation breadth is limited. As written, the impact is more methodological than demonstrably state-of-the-art.

Pros
- Clear and coherent probabilistic model at the waveform level; principled treatment of phase.
- Scalable MJLE EM algorithm avoiding large inversions; leverages efficient TF operators.
- Elegant multi-resolution extension with separate low-rank structures per frame.
- Well-motivated connections to IS-NMF, GCM, and SBL; interpretable Wiener-like updates.
- Empirical results show feasibility and some gains over IS-NMF and OMLSA.

Cons
- Limited and partly ad hoc evaluation: small test sets, SNR-only metrics, λ tuned/annealed without a principled scheme.
- No comparisons to phase-aware/complex NMF or recent probabilistic TF synthesis models.
- Missing implementation details, ablations, runtime, and sensitivity analyses.
- MMLE remains unscalable; no approximate alternatives (e.g., diagonal/low-rank approximations, conjugate gradients) explored.
- Reference list is inconsistent/incomplete; some derivations are only sketched.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide a principled approach to λ (e.g., proper ML update, cross-validation, or Bayesian treatment), and report sensitivity.
- Add ablations: single- vs multi-resolution within LRTFS; effect of K, β, initialization; convergence and runtime.
- Compare against complex/phase-aware NMF and STFT-domain probabilistic models that reconstruct waveforms.
- Use perceptual/intelligibility metrics (PESQ/STOI) and larger, more diverse test sets.
- Clarify derivation of the log(|α|^2) term, real-signal handling, and dictionary training; fix references. Consider releasing code.

Recommendation
Borderline accept (weak accept). The paper offers a novel and well-motivated generative framework with a scalable algorithm and a compelling multi-resolution story. The empirical validation is not yet fully convincing for NeurIPS standards; strengthening experiments and fixing clarity/reproducibility issues would substantially improve the case.
