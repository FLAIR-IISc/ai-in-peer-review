

# Peer Review: Low-Rank Time-Frequency Synthesis

## Summary

This paper introduces the Low-Rank Time-Frequency Synthesis (LRTFS) model, which extends previous work in time-frequency analysis by providing a generative model of the raw signal x(t) rather than just its time-frequency transform (STFT), as done in the Gaussian Composite Model (GCM) [3]. The authors present a novel framework where the signal is synthesized from low-rank time-frequency components, with a key distinction from previous approaches being that LRTFS models the raw signal directly rather than the STFT. The paper develops two estimation methods (MJLE and MMLE), a multi-resolution extension allowing for different time-frequency resolutions for different components, and validates the approach on music decomposition and speech enhancement tasks. The work connects to sparse Bayesian learning (SBL) [4,5] by imposing a low-rank structure on the variance of synthesis coefficients, contrasting with SBL's unstructured variances.

## Strengths

1. **Originality**: The core contribution—modeling the raw signal with low-rank time-frequency structure—is novel within the context of NMF-based approaches. Previous work focused on modeling either magnitude spectrograms (NMF) or STFT coefficients (GCM), but not the raw signal. This bridges an important gap in the field.

2. **Technical Soundness**: The theoretical development is rigorous, with proper connections to existing work in matrix factorization and sparse Bayesian learning. The derivation of the MJLE objective and EM algorithm is well-supported, and the multi-resolution extension is naturally incorporated. The paper carefully addresses the limitations of MMLE for large-scale applications.

3. **Experimental Results**: The experiments demonstrate clear practical utility. The speech enhancement results (6.6 dB average SNR improvement) outperform both IS-NMF baselines (6 dB) and standard OMLSA (4.6 dB), providing strong evidence of the approach's value. The music decomposition results are also compelling, with clear separation of tonal and transient components.

4. **Clarity**: The paper is exceptionally well-written and logically organized. The connections to prior work are clearly articulated, and the mathematical derivations are accessible to the signal processing community. The multi-resolution extension is particularly well-explained.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited Comparative Baselines**: While the speech enhancement results are promising, the comparison to recent deep learning approaches (e.g., neural audio processing methods) is limited. Including such comparisons would better position the work in the current landscape and strengthen the significance argument.

2. **Scalability Concerns**: The paper acknowledges that MMLE is impractical for large-scale audio applications due to matrix inversion requirements (M > T). While this is noted, the paper could better address how to scale the approach to modern audio processing tasks, which is increasingly important in the field.

3. **Lack of Theoretical Guarantees**: The paper is technically sound but lacks theoretical guarantees about the convergence or optimality of the proposed EM algorithm. While this is common in applied signal processing, it would strengthen the contribution.

4. **Hyperparameter Sensitivity**: The paper mentions that λ was treated as a hyperparameter rather than estimated, with results depending on its selection. More detail on how λ was chosen and its sensitivity to results would be valuable.

## Quality, Clarity, Originality, Significance Assessment

**Quality**: The paper is technically sound with well-supported claims. The experimental results are convincing, though more thorough comparisons would strengthen the evaluation. The authors are careful to acknowledge limitations, particularly regarding MMLE's scalability.

**Clarity**: The paper is clearly written, well-organized, and provides sufficient detail for experts to reproduce the results. The connections to prior work are well-explained and appropriately contextualized.

**Originality**: The core idea of modeling raw signals with low-rank time-frequency structure is novel within the NMF framework. While multi-resolution approaches exist [8,9], the synthesis approach with low-rank structure is new, and the paper clearly articulates how this differs from previous contributions.

**Significance**: The work addresses an important gap in signal processing literature and provides a practical approach with demonstrated improvements in speech enhancement. The multi-resolution capability is particularly valuable for audio applications where different signal components require different temporal resolutions.

## Recommendation

This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field. The core idea is novel, the technical development is sound, and the experimental results are convincing. While the comparison to recent deep learning approaches could be expanded, the paper makes a clear contribution to the signal processing community and advances the state of the art in time-frequency analysis.

**Pros for acceptance**:
- Novel approach to modeling raw signals with low-rank time-frequency structure
- Practical implementation with clear experimental validation
- Well-written with clear connections to prior work
- Multi-resolution capability is a significant practical advantage

**Cons for acceptance**:
- Limited comparison to recent deep learning approaches
- MMLE scalability issue not fully addressed for large-scale applications
- Lack of theoretical convergence guarantees

Overall, I recommend acceptance. The paper advances the field in a meaningful way, provides a useful new tool for signal processing applications, and is well-suited for NIPS as it combines signal processing with machine learning concepts in a novel and impactful manner. The work demonstrates both theoretical soundness and practical utility, making it a valuable contribution to the conference.
