Summary
This paper proposes Low-Rank Time-Frequency Synthesis (LRTFS), a generative model of raw time-domain signals that imposes a low-rank structure on the variance of synthesis (not analysis) coefficients in a time-frequency frame. Specifically, the signal x is synthesized as a linear combination of atoms with complex Gaussian coefficients αfn whose variances vfn are tied through a nonnegative factorization v ≈ WH. This contrasts with the Gaussian Composite Model (GCM), which places a low-rank structure on the variances of STFT analysis coefficients. Two estimation strategies are presented: (i) maximum joint likelihood estimation (MJLE) via an EM algorithm that leverages a Figueiredo–Nowak splitting to avoid matrix inversions and alternates Wiener-like shrinkage on α with IS-NMF updates on W,H; and (ii) maximum marginal likelihood estimation (MMLE), akin to SBL type-II ML, also via EM, but currently not scalable due to large matrix inversions. A key advantage of the synthesis view is a natural multi-resolution extension obtained by stacking multiple frames with different time-frequency resolutions. Experiments on hybrid music decomposition and semi-supervised speech enhancement show qualitative separation and modest SNR gains over IS-NMF and OMLSA.

Relation to prior work
The work bridges NMF/IS-NMF and complex Gaussian modeling of STFT magnitudes/phases (e.g., GCM) with sparse Bayesian learning ideas, but crucially moves the low-rank structure to the synthesis side. The connection to SBL is made explicit: the variances are tied via WH, removing the sparsity-inducing per-coefficient free variances but enabling low-rank structure and multi-resolution. The multi-resolution construction relates to morphological component analysis but uses a probabilistic low-rank synthesis prior instead of sparsity.

Strengths
- Conceptual novelty: A compelling shift from analysis-side low-rank modeling (GCM, NMF on spectrograms) to a synthesis-side generative model with low-rank variance. This is a meaningful new direction that directly models the waveform and sidesteps arbitrary phase handling.
- Elegant multi-resolution extension: Stacking frames with different resolutions is straightforward in the synthesis framework, enabling layered representations for transients vs tonal content.
- Technically sound derivations: The MJLE EM scheme is well-motivated, computationally efficient (no large inversions), and connects to Wiener filtering and IS-NMF. The emergence of a log(|α|^2) sparsity-promoting term in the joint objective is insightful.
- Practical applicability: Implementable with standard t-f toolboxes; experiments demonstrate interpretable decompositions and consistent improvements on real audio tasks.

Weaknesses
- Limited empirical depth: Gains over strong baselines are modest (e.g., +0.6 dB over IS-NMF on speech). No perceptual metrics (PESQ/STOI), ablations (choice of frames, K, β, λ schedule), or runtime/scalability analysis are provided.
- MMLE not scalable: The type-II approach is left as future work; practical large-scale solution strategies (e.g., iterative solvers exploiting frame structure) are not explored.
- Hyperparameter handling: λ is treated as a tuned hyperparameter or annealed, and the paper notes poor joint estimation. The choice/sensitivity of β (ensuring λI − βΦΦ* ≽ 0) is not discussed empirically.
- Missing comparisons: No direct comparison to complex NMF/GCM with phase-aware reconstruction or to multi-window NMF variants; unclear how much of the gain comes from multi-resolution vs synthesis modeling.
- Clarity and completeness: Some references are incomplete; details on initialization, convergence criteria, and real-signal handling (Hermitian symmetry) could be strengthened.

Quality
The modeling and derivations are sound and clearly connect to established methods (IS-NMF, SBL, Wiener filtering). The MJLE algorithm appears correct and practical. The experimental methodology is reasonable but not exhaustive; more rigorous evaluations would strengthen claims.

Clarity
The paper is generally clear and well-organized. The EM updates are explicit, and the multi-resolution extension is easy to follow. A few implementation details (β selection, initialization, computational costs) should be added. References need completion and polishing.

Originality
High. The synthesis-side low-rank generative model and its seamless multi-resolution variant are novel and intellectually appealing, distinct from GCM/NMF and standard SBL.

Significance
Moderate to high. The approach opens a new line of work in generative time-frequency modeling that can influence audio separation, enhancement, and multiresolution analysis. Despite modest quantitative gains in the current experiments, the conceptual advance and algorithmic tractability make it a promising direction.

Questions/suggestions for authors
- How do you set β in practice and assess sensitivity? Do you fix β=λ/δΦ or tune it?
- Provide runtime and memory benchmarks vs IS-NMF and GCM on your tasks.
- Add comparisons to complex NMF/GCM with consistent phase reconstruction and to multi-window NMF baselines.
- Include ablations on K, window lengths/overlaps, and the λ annealing schedule; report robustness across noises.
- Consider iterative linear solvers (CG) with fast Φ,Φ* to approximate MMLE updates, or variational/message-passing approximations.
- Report perceptual metrics (PESQ/STOI) on speech; include more diverse noise types and datasets.

Pros
- Novel and bold modeling idea (synthesis-side low-rank variance).
- Simple, scalable EM algorithm; clear connection to known tools.
- Natural multi-resolution framework; interpretable decompositions.
- Demonstrated applicability on real audio tasks.

Cons
- Limited and modest empirical validation; missing perceptual and runtime results.
- MMLE currently impractical; some hyperparameters hand-tuned.
- Missing comparisons to strongest phase-aware and multi-resolution baselines.

Recommendation
Accept (borderline positive). The paper introduces a significant new concept—low-rank time-frequency synthesis—that is both elegant and practical, with a compelling path toward richer multi-resolution generative audio models. While the empirical section could be stronger, the novelty and potential impact justify acceptance. Confidence: 0.7.
