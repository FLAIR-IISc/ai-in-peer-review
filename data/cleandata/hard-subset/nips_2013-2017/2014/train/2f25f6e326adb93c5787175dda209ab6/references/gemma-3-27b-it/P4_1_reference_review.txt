This paper introduces a novel generative model, Low-Rank Time-Frequency Synthesis (LRTFS), aiming to address limitations of existing time-frequency decomposition techniques like Nonnegative Matrix Factorization (NMF) and the Gaussian Composite Model (GCM). Specifically, it models the raw signal directly, allows for multi-resolution representations, and incorporates a low-rank structure on the variance of synthesis coefficients. The authors present Maximum Joint Likelihood Estimation (MJLE) and Maximum Marginal Likelihood Estimation (MMLE) for parameter estimation, with an EM algorithm for MJLE. Experiments on music decomposition and speech enhancement demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed approach.

The paper builds upon a strong foundation of prior work in matrix factorization, NMF, and probabilistic latent factor models for signal processing, referencing key papers such as [1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6]. The connection to sparse Bayesian learning [4, 5] is clearly articulated, and the differences between LRTFS and SBL are well explained. The extension to multi-resolution representations is a novel contribution, addressing a known limitation of traditional spectral factorizations [8, 9].  The experimental setup leverages established techniques like Gabor frames and compares against relevant baselines such as IS-NMF and OMLSA [14].

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The direct synthesis model of the raw signal is a significant departure from existing approaches that typically operate on the spectrogram.  The multi-resolution capability is a clear strength, allowing for adaptive time-frequency representations.
* **Technical Soundness:** The mathematical formulation of the LRTFS model is clear and well-motivated. The derivation of the EM algorithms for MJLE and MMLE appears to be correct, based on standard EM principles. The discussion of the computational complexity and limitations of MMLE is honest and helpful.
* **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The notation is consistent, and the explanations are relatively easy to follow. The connection between the LRTFS model and related techniques (NMF, GCM, SBL) is clearly articulated.
* **Experimental Validation:** The experimental results on music decomposition and speech enhancement demonstrate the potential of the proposed method. The comparison with established baselines (IS-NMF, OMLSA) provides a reasonable assessment of performance.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Scalability of MMLE:**  The authors acknowledge the computational limitations of MMLE, particularly for large-scale problems. While this is a valid limitation, it restricts the applicability of one of the proposed estimation methods.  The future work section mentions addressing this with message passing, but it's a critical bottleneck.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:**  The performance appears sensitive to the choice of the regularization parameter λ, as noted in the speech enhancement experiment. The paper doesn’t detail a robust method for selecting this parameter beyond trial-and-error, or discuss its theoretical implications.
* **Limited Scope of Experiments:** While the experiments are illustrative, they are relatively limited in scope. Expanding the experimental evaluation to a wider range of audio data, noise types, and performance metrics would strengthen the conclusions. The comparisons with existing methods are focused on SNR, but other perceptual metrics might be more relevant for audio applications.
* **Lack of Theoretical Justification for MJLE’s Local Optima:** The paper acknowledges that the MJLE objective function is non-convex.  There is no discussion of the properties of the local minima, or any guidance on initialization strategies to improve convergence to desirable solutions.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The LRTFS model presents a novel and promising approach to time-frequency synthesis with clear advantages over existing techniques.
* The technical formulation is sound, and the derivation of the EM algorithms is correct.
* The experimental results, although limited, demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method.
* The paper is well-written and clearly explains the underlying concepts.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The scalability limitations of MMLE restrict the practical applicability of one of the proposed estimation methods.
* The sensitivity to the hyperparameter λ and the lack of a robust selection method are concerning.
* The experimental evaluation is relatively limited, and further validation is needed.
* The non-convexity of the MJLE objective function and the potential for convergence to undesirable local minima are not adequately addressed.

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of time-frequency analysis and signal processing. The LRTFS model is a novel and technically sound approach with the potential to improve performance in a variety of audio applications. While the scalability limitations of MMLE and the sensitivity to hyperparameters are weaknesses, they do not outweigh the strengths of the work.  The paper is of sufficient quality for NIPS, particularly given the novelty of the approach.  I recommend **acceptance**, with a suggestion to the authors to address the scalability issue and provide more guidance on hyperparameter selection in future work.
