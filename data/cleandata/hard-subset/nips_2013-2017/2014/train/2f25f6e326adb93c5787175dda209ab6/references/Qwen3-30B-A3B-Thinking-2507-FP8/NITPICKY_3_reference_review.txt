

# Review of "Low-Rank Time-Frequency Synthesis"

This paper introduces a new generative model called Low-Rank Time-Frequency Synthesis (LRTFS) that extends previous work on the Gaussian Composite Model (GCM) by modeling the raw signal x(t) rather than its STFT. The authors propose a generative model where the signal is represented as a sum of time-frequency atoms with low-rank variance structure, and they present two estimation approaches (MJLE and MMLE) along with multi-resolution extensions.

## Technical Issues and Inconsistencies

The paper contains numerous technical inconsistencies and ambiguities that undermine its technical soundness:

1. **Notation and Dimensional Confusion**: In Section 2.1, the authors write "x(t) = ∑ fn αfnφfn(t) + e(t)" without specifying the summation range for f and n, which is critical for understanding the model. In equation (5), they define Φ as T × M with M = FN, but in audio applications, T (number of time samples) is typically much larger than FN (number of time-frequency bins), making the dimensionality confusing.

2. **Ambiguous Mathematical Expressions**: 
   - In equation (11), the term "log(|α|2)" is problematic as |α|2 is a vector/matrix, not a scalar. The authors should clarify if they mean the sum of element-wise logs.
   - In equations (7) and (9), the Wiener filtering expressions are written as products (e.g., "wfkhkn [WH]fn yfn") rather than ratios, which is mathematically incorrect (should be (wfkhkn / [WH]fn)yfn).
   - In equation (17), the Wiener filter expression "v(i)fn / v(i)fn + β" is ambiguous without parentheses, and the standard Wiener filter formula is missing the proper denominator structure.

3. **Inconsistent Matrix Dimensions**: 
   - In Section 3.2, the authors claim "the complexity of this algorithm can be problematic as it involves the computation of the inverse of a matrix of size M," but M = FN (number of time-frequency bins), which is typically smaller than T (number of time samples). The matrix to invert is M × M, not T × T, so the complexity claim is misleading.
   - In the multi-resolution section, the dimensionality of the bases Φa and Φb is inconsistent with the number of time-frequency bins.

4. **Experimental Design Omissions**: 
   - The paper states that λ was treated as a hyperparameter without explaining how values were selected, which is a significant omission in experimental design. The authors should justify their choice of λ values.
   - The window lengths for the Gabor frames in the speech enhancement experiment (512 samples for tonal layer, 32 samples for transient layer) are not justified, and the relationship between window length and the number of latent components is unclear.

5. **Formatting and Typographical Errors**: 
   - In the introduction, "the so-called Gaussian Composite Model (GCM) introduced in [3] writes simply" should be "is written simply" or "writes simply."
   - The reference for [3] lacks page numbers, which is unusual for a citation.
   - The paper inconsistently uses DIS and dIS for the Itakura-Saito divergence.

## Strengths and Weaknesses

**Strengths**:
- The paper makes a clear connection to sparse Bayesian learning and provides a novel perspective on time-frequency synthesis.
- The multi-resolution extension is conceptually interesting and potentially useful for audio applications.

**Weaknesses**:
- The paper fails to convincingly demonstrate why modeling the raw signal directly is superior to modeling the STFT (the GCM).
- The experimental results show only marginal improvements over baselines (6.6 dB vs. 6 dB for IS-NMF), with no clear explanation for the small gain.
- The paper doesn't adequately address the computational complexity of the proposed method for large-scale audio applications.

## Quality and Significance

The paper lacks sufficient technical rigor to support its claims. The mathematical formulations contain critical ambiguities that would prevent reproduction of the results. The experimental section is underdeveloped, with insufficient justification for parameter choices and limited demonstration of the model's advantages.

While the multi-resolution aspect is interesting, the paper doesn't convincingly demonstrate that this is a significant advantage over existing approaches. The claimed "improved accuracy of representation" isn't quantified beyond the marginal SNR improvements in the experiments.

## Conclusion

Despite the potentially interesting concept, the paper's numerous technical inconsistencies, ambiguous mathematical formulations, and underdeveloped experimental section prevent it from meeting NIPS standards for technical soundness. The authors should address these fundamental issues before the work can be considered for publication.

**Recommendation**: Reject

**Summary of Key Issues**:
- Ambiguous mathematical expressions (equations 7, 9, 11, 17)
- Dimensional inconsistencies in model formulation
- Missing justification for parameter choices (λ, window sizes)
- Inconsistent notation and formatting
- Marginal experimental improvements without clear explanation

The paper has potential but requires substantial revision to address these fundamental technical issues before it can be considered for publication.
