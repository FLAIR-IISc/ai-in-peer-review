Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes Low-Rank Time-Frequency Synthesis (LRTFS), a generative probabilistic model that operates directly in the time domain. A signal x(t) is synthesized as a sum over time-frequency atoms φfn(t) with complex Gaussian synthesis coefficients αfn, whose variances are constrained to have a low-rank structure via WH. A Gaussian residual models unstructured noise. This bridges a gap between: (i) deterministic NMF on magnitude spectrograms (discarding phase), and (ii) the Gaussian Composite Model (GCM), which is a probabilistic model on the complex STFT but not on x(t). LRTFS moves the low-rank modeling to synthesis coefficients (dual to STFT analysis coefficients) and thus yields a full generative model of the waveform. The paper develops two estimation schemes: a scalable maximum joint likelihood EM (MJLE) based on a Figueiredo–Nowak variable splitting that alternates “Wiener-like” shrinkage of α and IS-NMF on |α|^2, and a maximum marginal likelihood EM (MMLE) in the spirit of SBL/type-II ML that is presently computationally prohibitive at audio scales. A multi-resolution extension accommodates multiple time-frequency frames with different time scales. Experiments on hybrid music decomposition (tonal/transient) and semi-supervised speech enhancement show qualitative interpretability and modest quantitative gains over IS-NMF and OMLSA.

This work is well-positioned with respect to prior art: it builds on IS-NMF and GCM for phase-aware probabilistic spectrogram models, connects to SBL by viewing α as latent Gaussian with structured variances, and relates to multi-resolution sparse synthesis (MCA). The key novelty is imposing low-rank structure on synthesis coefficient variances and estimating them efficiently with an EM that avoids large matrix inversions.

Evaluation against NeurIPS criteria

Quality
- Technical soundness: The model is well-specified. The MJLE objective is derived correctly: combining −log Nc(α|0, diag v) with the split-likelihood yields DIS(|α|^2|v) plus a log(|α|^2) term that induces shrinkage. The EM steps are consistent with the variable-splitting architecture: the E-step computes a denoised proxy z by a linear residual correction; the M-step applies Wiener-like shrinkage and then IS-NMF updates for W,H. The monotonicity argument is plausible (each substep decreases the negative log-likelihood) though the λ update is acknowledged as approximate.
- Scalability: A strong point for MJLE—no large matrix inversion; analysis/synthesis uses efficient transforms (LTFAT). MMLE is principled but not scalable; the authors are transparent about this limitation.
- Empirical support: The experiments demonstrate feasibility and some gains. However, the quantitative evaluation is limited: small number of signals, single metric (SNR), and modest improvement over IS-NMF (6.6 dB vs 6.0 dB). Importantly, in the music demo λ is tuned by selecting the best output SNR, which is not a fair or reproducible setting without oracle access. More rigorous tuning or cross-validation is needed. No ablations (e.g., effect of multi-resolution, β, K, initialization) and no runtime/convergence plots are provided.

Clarity
- The paper is generally clear and well organized. The relation to GCM and SBL is articulated and helpful. The multi-resolution extension is straightforward and clearly derived.
- Missing details hamper reproducibility: choice and scheduling of β relative to λ and δΦ; initialization and normalization of W/H to avoid scale ambiguities; stopping criteria; dictionary training details (for speech); number of iterations; complexity per iteration; implementation choices for Φ and Φ*. Adding pseudocode would help.

Originality
- The central idea—low-rank Gaussian prior on synthesis coefficients with EM that alternates Wiener-like shrinkage and IS-NMF—is, to my knowledge, novel and nontrivial. The tie to SBL but with tied variances through WH (as opposed to independent v_m) is a meaningful conceptual shift.
- The multi-resolution instantiation is not new in spirit (multi-scale sparse synthesis is well-studied), but embedding low-rank variance structure within a probabilistic synthesis model and deriving a practical EM is original.

Significance
- Modeling x(t) directly while retaining a low-rank time-frequency structure and allowing multi-resolution layers is compelling for audio modeling and potentially other 1D signals. The approach could influence future work at the interface of probabilistic modeling, dictionary learning, and source separation.
- That said, demonstrated gains over strong baselines are modest, and the empirical scope is narrow. The fact that λ had to be treated as a tuned hyperparameter—and even warm-started to negligible values—weakens the end-to-end generative story. The MMLE path, while promising theoretically, is deferred due to scalability.

Pros
- Clear, principled generative model of the waveform that unifies synthesis with low-rank TF structure.
- Scalable EM algorithm (MJLE) with simple, interpretable steps and minimal linear-algebra burden.
- Natural multi-resolution extension; model can decompose signals into layers with different time scales.
- Good connections to GCM, IS-NMF, and SBL; the log(|α|^2) shrinkage term is an interesting byproduct.
- Demonstrates feasibility on real audio tasks; modest improvements over IS-NMF and OMLSA.

Cons
- Limited and partially oracle-tuned empirical evaluation; modest quantitative gains; small test suite.
- No comparison to complex NMF/phase-aware baselines beyond [3], nor to multi-window NMF variants.
- Missing ablations, convergence diagnostics, and runtime/complexity reporting.
- λ estimation is fragile; resorting to manual tuning contradicts the fully probabilistic narrative.
- Reproducibility details (β, initialization, normalization, code) are insufficient.

Questions and suggestions for authors
- Please report how β is set relative to λ and δΦ, and provide sensitivity analysis. Does β scheduling affect convergence or quality?
- Clarify initialization of α, W, H and normalization to resolve scale ambiguities between α and WH.
- Provide runtime and iteration counts, and convergence plots of the objective. How does MJLE scale with K and number of frames?
- Compare against: (i) complex NMF/phase-constrained NMF variants; (ii) multi-window NMF on STFT magnitudes; (iii) GCM with multi-resolution front-ends, if applicable.
- Evaluate with standard speech metrics (SDR/SIR/SAR, PESQ, STOI) and larger test sets; avoid oracle tuning of λ.
- Include ablations: single- vs multi-resolution, effect of K, β, λ strategy, and dictionary size/training.
- Consider a small-scale MMLE experiment to highlight potential benefits beyond MJLE.

Overall recommendation
Borderline accept (leaning weak accept). The modeling contribution and scalable EM are interesting and relevant to the NeurIPS audience working on probabilistic models and signal processing. The paper would be strengthened with more rigorous and broader empirical validation and clearer implementation details.
