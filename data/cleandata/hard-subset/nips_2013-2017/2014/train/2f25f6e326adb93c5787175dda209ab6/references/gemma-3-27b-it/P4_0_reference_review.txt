This paper introduces Low-Rank Time-Frequency Synthesis (LRTFS), a generative model for signal reconstruction based on low-rank structure in the variance of synthesis coefficients. The core idea is to model a signal as a sum of atoms drawn from a time-frequency basis, with synthesis coefficients possessing a low-rank structure enforced via a Gaussian Composite Model (GCM) framework, extending previous work [3] which imposed low-rank structure on *analysis* coefficients. The authors present maximum likelihood estimation approaches (MJLE and MMLE) and demonstrate multi-resolution capabilities, showcasing applications in music decomposition and speech enhancement.

The paper is well-structured, beginning with a clear motivation and connection to existing work on matrix factorization, NMF, and probabilistic latent factor models. The extension from analysis-based low-rank structure (GCM) to synthesis-based structure is a logical progression and the multi-resolution aspect is a valuable contribution. The mathematical derivations appear sound, and the EM algorithms are described adequately. The experimental section provides initial validation of the approach with illustrative examples.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The synthesis-based low-rank modeling is a distinct contribution, addressing a limitation of prior GCM-based approaches. The extension to multi-resolution representations further enhances novelty.
*   **Technical Soundness:** The mathematical framework is well-defined, and the proposed EM algorithms seem logically derived. The connection to sparse Bayesian learning (SBL) provides useful context.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and easy to follow, especially for readers familiar with NMF and signal processing concepts. Equations are clearly presented and explained.
*   **Potential Significance:** The ability to model signals directly, rather than their STFT, opens up possibilities for more accurate and flexible representations. The multi-resolution capability is particularly promising for applications with varying time-frequency characteristics.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Scalability of MMLE:** The authors acknowledge the scalability issues with MMLE due to matrix inversion. While this is admitted, the inclusion of MMLE in detail feels somewhat incomplete without addressing this issue further, or potentially omitting it altogether.
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The experiments show sensitivity to the choice of the regularization parameter λ, which was not estimated by the EM algorithm but instead selected based on output SNR. This introduces a degree of arbitrariness and could limit generalization.
*   **Limited Baselines:** The comparison to IS-NMF and OMLSA in the speech enhancement experiment is relatively limited. Including more state-of-the-art speech enhancement algorithms would strengthen the evaluation.
*   **Lack of Theoretical Justification:** While the connection to SBL is mentioned, a more thorough theoretical analysis of the properties of LRTFS (e.g., identifiability, consistency) would be beneficial.
*   **Experimental Depth:** The experiments are somewhat limited in scope. Exploring a wider range of datasets and conditions would provide a more robust assessment of the method’s performance.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and technically sound approach to signal modeling that addresses a clear limitation of existing methods.
*   The multi-resolution capability is a significant contribution with potential for a wide range of applications.
*   The experimental results, while limited, demonstrate the feasibility and effectiveness of the proposed method.
*   The paper is well-written and clearly explains the core ideas and technical details.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The scalability issues with MMLE and sensitivity to hyperparameter selection raise concerns about the practical applicability of the method.
*   The experimental evaluation is somewhat limited and lacks a comprehensive comparison to state-of-the-art baselines.
*   A deeper theoretical analysis of the properties of LRTFS would be beneficial.



**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of time-frequency signal processing. The proposed LRTFS model offers a novel and promising approach to signal reconstruction with several advantages over existing methods. While some weaknesses remain, particularly regarding scalability and experimental depth, the strengths of the paper outweigh the weaknesses. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions to address the points raised above, particularly a discussion of strategies to mitigate the scalability issue of MMLE and more comprehensive experimentation. It would also be useful to clearly justify the choice of the hyperparameter λ and potentially explore methods for its automatic estimation.
