This paper introduces Low-Rank Time-Frequency Synthesis (LRTFS), a generative model for signals leveraging low-rank structure in the synthesis coefficients, unlike previous approaches like the Gaussian Composite Model (GCM) which impose low-rank constraints on the *analysis* coefficients. The model is formulated as a sum of weighted atoms from a time-frequency basis, with synthesis coefficients having a low-rank covariance matrix. The authors propose two estimation methods – Maximum Joint Likelihood Estimation (MJLE) and Maximum Marginal Likelihood Estimation (MMLE) – along with corresponding EM algorithms. They demonstrate the potential of LRTFS for multi-resolution representations and showcase its performance on music decomposition and speech enhancement tasks.

The paper builds upon a strong foundation of related work in matrix factorization, NMF, and probabilistic latent factor models for signal processing, citing key papers like [1, 2, 3]. The authors correctly position their work as a logical extension of the GCM [3], addressing its limitation of being a model of the STFT rather than the raw signal. The connection to sparse Bayesian learning [4, 5] is also insightful, highlighting the differences stemming from the structured variance in LRTFS compared to the unstructured variances in SBL.  The reference to morphological component analysis [10] in the music decomposition experiment demonstrates awareness of relevant prior art.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** LRTFS represents a novel approach to signal modeling by directly modeling the synthesis process with a low-rank constraint on the synthesis coefficients. This is a conceptually different and potentially advantageous approach compared to existing methods focusing on analysis.
*   **Generative Model:** The LRTFS framework provides a full generative model of the signal, opening possibilities for various applications beyond just decomposition and enhancement, such as signal synthesis and missing data imputation.
*   **Multi-resolution Capability:**  The ability to easily incorporate multi-resolution representations is a significant strength, particularly relevant for signals like audio where different components have varying temporal characteristics. This is a distinct advantage over traditional NMF approaches.
*   **Clear Mathematical Formulation:** The mathematical derivations of the model and EM algorithms are presented clearly and concisely, facilitating understanding and potential implementation.
*   **Experimental Validation:** The experiments demonstrate the potential of LRTFS for both music decomposition and speech enhancement, achieving competitive results compared to baseline methods.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Scalability of MMLE:** The authors acknowledge the scalability issues with the MMLE algorithm due to the matrix inversion step, limiting its applicability to large-scale problems. While acknowledged, this is a significant limitation of one of the proposed estimation methods.
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The performance relies on careful selection of the regularization parameter λ. The paper doesn’t provide sufficient guidance on how to choose an appropriate value for λ, and the chosen values were determined empirically rather than via a principled approach.
*   **Limited Comparative Analysis:** The comparative analysis in the speech enhancement experiment is relatively limited.  While LRTFS outperforms IS-NMF and OMLSA, a more comprehensive comparison with state-of-the-art deep learning-based speech enhancement methods would strengthen the evaluation.
*   **Lack of Theoretical Analysis:** While the paper provides a clear mathematical formulation, a more in-depth theoretical analysis of the convergence properties of the EM algorithms and the conditions for achieving a good solution would be valuable.
*   **Presentation of Results:** Fig. 1, illustrating the music decomposition, lacks proper labeling of the axes and could benefit from clearer visualization of the estimated components.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound approach to signal modeling with a clear potential to advance the field.
*   The multi-resolution capability is a significant advantage for applications involving signals with varying temporal characteristics.
*   The experimental results demonstrate the practical effectiveness of the proposed method, achieving competitive performance.
*   The paper is well-written and clearly explains the technical details of the proposed approach.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

*   The scalability issues with MMLE significantly limit the applicability of one of the proposed estimation methods.
*   The hyperparameter sensitivity and lack of a principled approach for choosing λ are potential drawbacks.
*   The limited comparative analysis does not fully establish the superiority of LRTFS over existing state-of-the-art methods.




**Overall Assessment:**

The paper presents a promising new approach to signal modeling with LRTFS. The novelty of directly modeling the synthesis process with a low-rank constraint, coupled with the multi-resolution capability, makes it a valuable contribution to the field. While the scalability issue with MMLE and the hyperparameter sensitivity are limitations, the overall strengths of the paper outweigh these weaknesses.  I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions addressing the clarity of figure labeling and suggesting a more extensive analysis of hyperparameter selection strategies.  Future work focusing on scaling MMLE or exploring alternative estimation methods would be beneficial.
