

# Review of "Low-Rank Time-Frequency Synthesis"

This paper introduces Low-Rank Time-Frequency Synthesis (LRTFS), a novel generative model that bridges the gap between traditional nonnegative matrix factorization (NMF) approaches and direct modeling of raw time-domain signals. The authors build upon the Gaussian Composite Model (GCM) from [3], which models the STFT as a low-rank Gaussian process, by extending it to model the raw signal directly. The key innovation is the generative formulation where the signal is expressed as a sum of time-frequency atoms with low-rank variance structure. The paper presents two estimation approaches (MJLE and MMLE) and demonstrates multi-resolution capabilities, enabling different temporal resolutions for different signal components (e.g., transient vs. tonal in audio).

**Quality:** The paper presents a technically sound model with clear theoretical derivations. The connection to sparse Bayesian learning (SBL) is well-explained, and the EM algorithms for estimation are properly justified. The experimental results on music decomposition and speech enhancement demonstrate the model's effectiveness, with a 6.6 dB average SNR improvement in speech enhancement over IS-NMF's 6 dB. However, the authors acknowledge that MMLE doesn't scale to large audio applications, and they don't provide a detailed comparison between MJLE and MMLE, which would strengthen the evaluation. The paper is careful to acknowledge limitations, such as the need for λ to be treated as a hyperparameter rather than estimated.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with logical progression from problem statement to model, estimation, extensions, and experiments. The mathematical notation is consistent and well-explained, making the technical content accessible. The connection between the proposed model and existing work (GCM, SBL) is particularly well-handled. The supplementary materials provide additional evidence (sound examples), which is helpful for reproducibility.

**Originality:** The paper makes a clear contribution by modeling the raw signal directly (not just the STFT) while maintaining low-rank structure, which addresses a limitation of previous approaches. The multi-resolution capability is particularly novel and well-motivated, as it enables different temporal resolutions for different signal components—a feature not possible with traditional NMF approaches. The paper correctly contextualizes its contribution against relevant prior work, including [3], [4], [5], and [8].

**Significance:** The model has clear practical significance for audio signal processing applications, with demonstrated improvements in music decomposition and speech enhancement. The multi-resolution capability opens new possibilities for modeling signals with varying temporal characteristics. While the paper focuses on audio applications, the underlying principles could potentially extend to other time-series analysis domains. The work advances the state of the art by providing a unified framework that connects time-frequency synthesis with low-rank modeling.

**Strengths:**
- Clear conceptual contribution: Direct modeling of raw signals with low-rank time-frequency structure.
- Novel multi-resolution capability with practical implementation.
- Strong theoretical foundation with connections to existing methods (SBL, NMF).
- Relevant experimental results showing clear improvements over baselines.
- Well-organized and clearly written.

**Weaknesses:**
- MMLE approach is acknowledged as not scalable for large audio applications, but the authors don't provide sufficient justification for why MJLE is preferred in practice beyond computational considerations.
- The computational complexity of MJLE for very long audio signals (e.g., 3+ minutes at CD quality) isn't thoroughly analyzed.
- The paper could better connect to broader machine learning literature beyond signal processing (e.g., how this relates to other low-rank models).
- Hyperparameter selection process for λ is not detailed, making it harder to reproduce results.
- The limitations of the model (e.g., when it might not work well) are not thoroughly discussed.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
- The paper presents a novel model with clear theoretical contributions.
- The multi-resolution capability is a significant advancement over previous work.
- The experimental results are relevant and show measurable improvements over baselines.
- The paper is well-written and well-organized.

**Arguments for Rejection:**
- The computational scalability of the main approach (MJLE) for large-scale audio processing isn't fully addressed.
- The comparison between MJLE and MMLE is insufficient, leaving important questions about the optimal estimation approach.
- The paper doesn't sufficiently connect to broader machine learning literature beyond audio processing.

**Recommendation:** I recommend **weak acceptance**. The paper makes a clear and significant contribution to the field of audio signal processing, with a novel model that bridges important gaps in the literature. The multi-resolution capability is particularly compelling. However, the paper would be stronger with more thorough analysis of computational complexity for large-scale applications and a more detailed comparison between estimation approaches. The authors should also better connect their work to broader machine learning literature.

**Suggestions for Improvement:**
1. Add a section analyzing the computational complexity of MJLE for large audio signals (e.g., 3 minutes at 44.1kHz), including wall-clock times for different signal lengths.
2. Provide a more detailed comparison between MJLE and MMLE, including a small-scale experiment where MMLE is feasible.
3. Include a discussion of the model's limitations (e.g., when might the low-rank assumption fail for certain types of signals?).
4. Expand the discussion to connect the work more explicitly to broader machine learning concepts (e.g., how this relates to other low-rank modeling approaches).
5. Provide more detail on hyperparameter selection (e.g., how λ was chosen, sensitivity analysis).

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a meaningful contribution to audio signal processing. With the suggested improvements, it would be an excellent fit for NIPS.
