Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes Low-Rank Time-Frequency Synthesis (LRTFS), a generative time-domain model that imposes a low-rank structure on the variance of complex synthesis coefficients associated with a time-frequency (t-f) dictionary (e.g., Gabor frame). Specifically, the signal x(t) is synthesized as a linear combination of atoms φfn(t) with Gaussian coefficients αfn ∼ Nc(0, vfn), where the variances vfn are constrained to factorize as [WH]fn with W ≥ 0, H ≥ 0. This shifts the low-rank prior from the variance of analysis coefficients (as in the Gaussian Composite Model (GCM) for STFT magnitudes) to the variance of synthesis coefficients of x itself. The model is related to sparse Bayesian learning (SBL) in that α has a Gaussian prior with diagonal covariance, but differs crucially because LRTFS ties variances via a low-rank WH rather than learning independent variances.

Two estimation strategies are presented. (1) Maximum joint likelihood estimation (MJLE): minimize −log p(x, α | W, H, λ), yielding an objective consisting of a data term (1/λ) ||x − Φα||^2 and an IS-NMF penalty on |α|^2 relative to v = WH, plus a log(|α|^2) term arising from the Gaussian prior. An EM algorithm based on the Figueiredo–Nowak variable-splitting architecture avoids large matrix inversions and leads to iterative “Wiener-like” updates for α and IS-NMF updates for W, H. (2) Maximum marginal likelihood estimation (MMLE, type-II ML): marginalize α and optimize −log p(x | W, H, λ) via EM with α as latent variables, resulting in updates involving Σ = (Φ*Φ/λ + diag(v)−1)−1; this is not computationally scalable for long signals.

A multi-resolution extension stacks multiple frames (e.g., short-window and long-window Gabor frames) and assigns independent low-rank priors to each set of synthesis coefficients, enabling decomposition into layers with different time scales.

Experiments show: (i) hybrid decomposition of a noisy jazz excerpt into tonal/transient layers (K=3 each) with qualitative interpretability; (ii) semi-supervised speech enhancement using pre-trained speech dictionaries and learned noise dictionaries in two resolutions, with an average SNR improvement of 6.6 dB over 10 mixtures, compared to 6.0 dB for IS-NMF (single resolution) and 4.6 dB for OMLSA.

Strengths
- Conceptual advance: a generative time-domain model with low-rank structure on synthesis coefficients, bridging classical NMF/GCM (analysis-side) and synthesis modeling.
- Clear connection to SBL: demonstrates how tying variances via WH trades sparsity for low-rank structure while retaining tractable EM algorithms.
- Computationally practical MJLE algorithm: no large matrix inversions; relies on fast Φα and Φ* operations available in standard toolboxes.
- Natural multi-resolution extension by stacking frames; leverages the synthesis formulation to mix time scales.
- Latent component reconstruction via Wiener-like formulas on α enables interpretable decompositions.

Weaknesses
- Experimental evaluation is limited in scope and scale; gains over a strong IS-NMF baseline are modest (0.6 dB SNR on average) and reported on 10 examples.
- Hyperparameter handling is ad hoc: λ is treated as a tuned hyperparameter in one task and annealed to near-zero in another, deviating from the probabilistic model and complicating interpretability.
- No comparison to the GCM operating directly on complex STFTs with time-domain reconstruction; only a single-resolution IS-NMF magnitude baseline is considered.
- Sensitivity to initialization, choice of β (must satisfy β ≤ λ/δΦ), and frame parameters is not analyzed; convergence guarantees are not provided beyond monotonicity claims and a heuristic λ update.
- Some clarity issues: minor notational slips (e.g., missing zero-mean in Eqs. 29–30), and limited details on computational cost, runtime, and initialization strategy.

Evaluation by criteria
- Quality: The modeling and derivations appear technically sound. The MJLE objective correctly recovers the IS divergence and log term from the Gaussian prior over α. The EM algorithm using variable splitting is standard and avoids intractable inversions; the constraint on β is correctly noted. The MMLE derivation is consistent with SBL. However, the use of a heuristic λ update (and annealing in experiments) weakens the statistical coherence, and there is no convergence rate or global optimality analysis. Empirical evaluation lacks ablations (e.g., effect of multi-resolution vs single-resolution LRTFS, effect of λ).
- Clarity: Generally well-written and organized, with a clear statement of the model, algorithms, and multi-resolution extension. A compact summary of the EM steps is provided. A few notational typos and missing details (choice of β, initialization, run time, criteria for stopping, real-signal handling in practice) should be clarified.
- Originality: The combination is novel: imposing low-rank variance structure on synthesis coefficients of a time-domain generative model, with an MJLE algorithm that couples iterative shrinkage with IS-NMF. The explicit multi-resolution extension within this framework is natural and useful. Relation to SBL and GCM/NMF is clearly articulated.
- Significance: Potentially meaningful for audio and other time-series domains where multi-resolution synthesis is valuable. The demonstrated improvements are modest; nonetheless, the framework could stimulate follow-up work (e.g., scalable MMLE, structured priors, multichannel extensions). Current empirical evidence is not yet strong enough to claim broad impact.

Pros for acceptance
- Clear, principled generative modeling contribution with a practical algorithm.
- Bridges established areas (NMF/GCM, SBL, t-f synthesis) in a coherent way.
- Multi-resolution capability is elegant and easy to implement.
- Code feasibility implied via standard t-f toolboxes; method likely reproducible.

Cons against acceptance
- Limited and small-scale empirical validation; modest quantitative gains.
- Heuristic handling of λ and lack of systematic hyperparameter study.
- Missing comparisons to closer baselines (e.g., GCM with multiresolution STFTs).
- No runtime/complexity benchmarks; sensitivity and robustness not assessed.

Questions and suggestions for the authors
- Provide guidance on choosing β and λ; report sensitivity analyses and the effect of annealing λ on performance and convergence.
- Compare against a GCM-based method operating on complex STFTs with time-domain reconstruction, and against multiresolution variants where applicable.
- Include ablations: single- vs multi-resolution LRTFS; effect of K; effect of dictionary size; influence of initialization (W,H,α).
- Report computational cost (per-iteration time, total time) and stopping criteria; discuss scalability for long signals.
- Clarify Eq. (29)–(30) to include the zero mean; discuss handling of real signals and Hermitian symmetry in implementation.
- Consider additional metrics for speech enhancement (e.g., SDR/SIR/SAR, PESQ, STOI) and larger test sets.

Overall recommendation
Borderline. The paper presents a technically sound and novel synthesis-side low-rank model with a practical EM algorithm and a compelling multi-resolution extension. However, empirical validation is limited, and some modeling choices (λ) are handled heuristically. Strengthening experiments and comparisons would significantly improve the case for acceptance.
