

# Peer Review: Low-Rank Time-Frequency Synthesis

## Summary
This paper introduces the Low-Rank Time-Frequency Synthesis (LRTFS) model, which directly models the raw signal x(t) with low-rank time-frequency structure, bridging a gap left by previous approaches like the Gaussian Composite Model (GCM) that modeled the STFT instead. The core innovation is a generative model where the signal is expressed as a sum of time-frequency atoms with synthesis coefficients αfn that have a low-rank variance structure (vfn = [WH]fn). This differs fundamentally from GCM, which imposed low-rank structure on analysis coefficients. The paper presents two estimation methods (MJLE and MMLE), with MJLE being computationally feasible for audio applications. A key contribution is the multi-resolution extension, allowing different time-frequency bases for different signal components (e.g., tonal vs. transient audio elements), which traditional NMF approaches cannot naturally accommodate.

The paper relates LRTFS to sparse Bayesian learning (SBL), noting that while SBL induces sparsity through free variance parameters, LRTFS ties variances together through the low-rank structure WH. The authors demonstrate the approach on two audio applications: music decomposition (separating tonal/transient components) and speech enhancement (removing applause noise), showing 6.6 dB average SNR improvement over a baseline IS-NMF approach.

## Strengths

**Originality (High):** The paper's core contribution—direct modeling of the raw signal with low-rank time-frequency structure—is novel. Previous work (GCM, NMF) operated on the STFT, which forces a fixed time resolution. LRTFS naturally accommodates multi-resolution representations without additional complexity, addressing a clear limitation of traditional approaches. The connection to sparse Bayesian learning is insightful but not the main contribution.

**Significance (High):** The multi-resolution capability has clear practical value in audio processing where different signal components (transients vs. sustained tones) require different time-frequency resolutions. The experiments demonstrate tangible improvements over baselines (6.6 dB vs. 6 dB SNR gain), suggesting real-world utility. The generative nature of the model could extend to other signal processing domains beyond audio.

**Quality (High):** The technical presentation is rigorous, with clear mathematical formulations and a well-designed EM algorithm for MJLE that avoids computationally prohibitive matrix inversions. The authors acknowledge limitations (e.g., MMLE's scalability issues) and provide a thoughtful comparison with related work. The experimental design is sound for the applications tested.

**Clarity (Good):** The paper is well-organized and clearly written. The mathematical derivations are precise, and the multi-resolution extension is logically presented. The experimental results are adequately described, though some deeper analysis of the decomposition quality would strengthen the presentation.

## Weaknesses

**Limited Theoretical Analysis:** While the EM algorithm is well-derived, the paper lacks theoretical guarantees about convergence or optimality. The connection to sparse Bayesian learning is mentioned but not leveraged to establish theoretical advantages of LRTFS over SBL.

**Experimental Comparison:** The comparison with baselines is limited to SNR improvement without qualitative analysis of the decomposed components (e.g., are the tonal/transient components perceptually meaningful?). The paper doesn't explore how LRTFS performs against more recent audio decomposition methods beyond the specified baselines.

**Practical Implementation Details:** The paper doesn't address how to select key hyperparameters (like the number of latent components K) in practice, which is crucial for real-world adoption. The choice of λ as a fixed hyperparameter (rather than estimated) is noted but not thoroughly justified.

**Multi-resolution Limitations:** While the multi-resolution extension is promising, the paper doesn't discuss how the approach scales with more than two bases or how to automatically determine the appropriate number of bases for a given application.

## Recommendation

I recommend **acceptance**. The paper makes a significant contribution to the intersection of signal processing and machine learning by introducing a novel model that directly handles raw signals with low-rank time-frequency structure. The multi-resolution capability is particularly compelling and addresses a well-known limitation of traditional NMF approaches.

While the paper has some minor weaknesses in experimental depth and theoretical analysis, these don't undermine the core contribution. The technical quality is high, the approach is novel, and the results demonstrate meaningful improvements over baselines. The work aligns well with NIPS' focus on novel machine learning approaches with strong theoretical and practical grounding.

The authors should consider adding a brief discussion of hyperparameter selection for K and λ in the final version, and perhaps provide more qualitative analysis of the decomposition results to strengthen the experimental section.

This work represents a valuable advance in time-frequency signal processing, with clear potential for broader application in audio and other signal processing domains. It addresses a meaningful gap in the literature and offers a practical, computationally feasible approach that could benefit both researchers and practitioners in the field.
