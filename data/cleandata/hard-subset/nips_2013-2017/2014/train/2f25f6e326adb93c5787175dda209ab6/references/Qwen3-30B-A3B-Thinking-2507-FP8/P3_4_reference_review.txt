

# Peer Review: "Low-Rank Time-Frequency Synthesis"

## Summary and Relation to Previous Work

This paper introduces the Low-Rank Time-Frequency Synthesis (LRTFS) model, which bridges a critical gap between traditional NMF-based spectral decomposition and generative modeling of raw signals. While previous work like the Gaussian Composite Model (GCM) [3] modeled the STFT directly, LRTFS models the raw signal x(t) as a sum of low-rank time-frequency components. The authors position their work as a natural extension of NMF and SBL, with the key innovation being a generative model that operates directly on the time domain while maintaining low-rank structure in the time-frequency domain. The paper effectively connects to relevant prior work in audio signal processing [1, 8, 10] and sparse Bayesian learning [4, 5], though it could better contextualize how LRTFS differs from recent advances in deep learning-based audio decomposition.

## Quality

The paper demonstrates strong technical quality in its methodological development. The MJLE approach is well-motivated, with the EM algorithm avoiding expensive matrix inversions (a significant practical advantage over MMLE). The theoretical derivation of the objective function (Eq. 11) correctly identifies the natural appearance of the IS divergence penalty, which is a nice insight connecting to existing work [3]. The multi-resolution extension (Section 4) is particularly compelling, as it solves a well-known limitation in traditional NMF-based approaches where time resolution is fixed at feature extraction.

However, the paper has notable weaknesses in its evaluation. The speech enhancement experiment (Section 5.2) uses only 10 test samples, which is insufficient for robust statistical claims. The comparison to IS-NMF with an intermediate window size (256 samples) lacks justification for why this specific window size was chosen over optimal settings. More critically, the paper doesn't compare against recent deep learning approaches for speech enhancement (e.g., [18, 19]), which have become the new state-of-the-art in this domain. While the authors acknowledge MMLE's scalability issues, they don't provide sufficient empirical evidence that MJLE is indeed superior to MMLE in practice for audio applications.

## Clarity

The paper is generally well-organized and clearly written. The mathematical notation is consistent, and the key equations are well-explained. The connection to sparse Bayesian learning (Section 2.2) is particularly helpful for readers familiar with that literature. However, the paper could improve by:
1) Clarifying why λ is treated as a hyperparameter rather than estimated (Section 5), as this is a significant practical choice that affects reproducibility
2) Providing more detail on the implementation of the Gabor frames (window types, overlaps) in the experiments
3) Including more visualizations of the time-domain components in Figure 1 (beyond the spectrograms)

## Originality

The core idea of modeling the raw signal directly with low-rank time-frequency structure is genuinely novel for NMF-based approaches. The multi-resolution capability is particularly original and addresses a practical limitation of prior work. While the connection to SBL is interesting, the paper doesn't fully leverage this to develop new theoretical insights about the relationship between LRTFS and sparse representations. The authors correctly position their work as filling a gap between GCM and raw signal modeling, but could better distinguish from recent work on deep generative models for audio (e.g., WaveNet [20], sample-level generative models).

## Significance

The significance of this work is substantial for signal processing applications, particularly in audio where multi-resolution modeling is crucial. The paper successfully demonstrates practical benefits in music decomposition (Fig. 1) and speech enhancement (Table 1), with 6.6 dB SNR improvement over baselines. However, the significance would be strengthened by:
1) More extensive experiments showing consistent performance across diverse audio types
2) Analysis of computational complexity and scalability for longer audio signals (e.g., full songs)
3) Discussion of how LRTFS might integrate with deep learning approaches for improved performance

## Key Strengths
- Natural extension from GCM to raw signal modeling
- Efficient MJLE algorithm without matrix inversions
- Clear multi-resolution capability (critical for audio)
- Strong experimental results showing practical benefits

## Key Weaknesses
- Insufficient comparison with recent deep learning baselines
- Limited experimental validation (small test set for speech enhancement)
- MMLE dismissal lacks sufficient empirical justification
- Theoretical analysis of convergence properties is limited

## Recommendation

This paper presents a technically sound and practically useful extension to time-frequency modeling in audio processing. The core idea is novel and addresses a genuine limitation in existing approaches. While the experimental validation is somewhat limited, the methodological contribution is significant and the multi-resolution capability is particularly valuable.

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions. The authors should:
1) Expand the speech enhancement experiments to include more diverse audio samples
2) Compare against recent deep learning baselines for speech enhancement
3) Justify their choice of λ as a hyperparameter more thoroughly
4) Clarify the relationship between LRTFS and recent deep generative models

The paper makes a meaningful contribution to the field of time-frequency analysis and signal processing, and would be of interest to the NeurIPS community working on structured representations, audio processing, and probabilistic modeling. The method is well-motivated, technically sound, and demonstrates clear practical advantages over existing approaches for audio applications.
