This paper introduces a novel time-frequency synthesis model (LRTFS) building upon Gaussian Composite Models (GCMs) [3] and Nonnegative Matrix Factorization (NMF). The key idea is to model the raw signal directly, rather than the STFT, with a low-rank structure imposed on the variance of synthesis coefficients. This allows for multi-resolution representations, a significant advantage over traditional NMF. The authors present two estimation approaches, Maximum Joint Likelihood Estimation (MJLE) with an EM algorithm and Maximum Marginal Likelihood Estimation (MMLE), although the latter is deemed computationally prohibitive for their audio applications. Experiments on music decomposition and speech enhancement demonstrate promising results.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The direct synthesis approach is a clear advancement over existing methods that operate on the STFT magnitude. Modeling the raw signal allows for more accurate representation and enables multi-resolution analysis.
* **Theoretical Soundness:** The model is well-motivated, grounded in probabilistic principles (Gaussian distributions) and clearly relates to existing techniques like NMF and Sparse Bayesian Learning (SBL). The derivation of the MJLE objective function and the EM algorithm appear solid.
* **Multi-Resolution Capability:** The ability to combine different time-frequency resolutions within a single framework is a significant strength, particularly relevant for audio signals with transient and sustained components.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and organized, with a logical flow of ideas. Equations are clearly presented, and the connection to previous work is well-established.
* **Empirical Validation:** The experiments, although limited in scope, provide initial evidence of the effectiveness of the proposed method on both music decomposition and speech enhancement tasks. The comparison with IS-NMF and OMLSA provides a useful baseline.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Computational Complexity:** While MJLE is presented as computationally feasible, the authors acknowledge the scalability issues with MMLE, which is a potentially more robust estimation approach.  The complexity analysis could be more detailed, and it’s unclear how well MJLE scales to very large datasets or higher-dimensional signals.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The reliance on manual tuning of the regularization parameter λ is a limitation.  A more systematic approach to hyperparameter selection (e.g., cross-validation) is needed. The paper mentions poor performance when λ is estimated during optimization, but provides limited insight into *why* this occurs.
* **Limited Experimental Evaluation:** The experiments are somewhat limited in scope.  The evaluation is primarily based on SNR improvement, which doesn’t capture all aspects of perceptual quality. Subjective listening tests and comparison with state-of-the-art methods are lacking.  The choice of datasets is also limited; evaluating on more diverse audio material would strengthen the conclusions.
* **Connection to SBL Could Be Expanded:** The paper briefly draws parallels to SBL [4, 5], highlighting the differences in variance treatment.  However, a more in-depth discussion of how LRTFS relates to and potentially benefits from techniques developed in the SBL literature could be valuable.  For instance, could sparse priors be incorporated into the LRTFS framework?
* **Minor Clarifications:** The notation can be slightly confusing at times (e.g., the use of ‘m’ and (f, n) interchangeably). More consistent notation would improve readability.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound approach to time-frequency synthesis with the potential to advance the state-of-the-art in signal processing.
* The multi-resolution capability is a significant contribution, addressing a limitation of traditional NMF methods.
* The MJLE algorithm is computationally feasible and provides promising results in preliminary experiments.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The experimental evaluation is limited in scope and lacks a comprehensive comparison with state-of-the-art methods.
* The computational complexity of the proposed approach, particularly for MMLE, remains a concern.
* The sensitivity to hyperparameter tuning and the lack of a systematic selection strategy are drawbacks.


**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a solid contribution to the field of time-frequency analysis and signal processing.  The proposed LRTFS model is a novel and theoretically well-grounded approach with the potential to address important limitations of existing methods.  However, the limited experimental evaluation and computational concerns prevent me from recommending unconditional acceptance.

**Recommendation:**

**Reject with Strong Encouragement to Resubmit.**

I recommend rejecting the paper in its current form, but strongly encourage the authors to address the weaknesses outlined above and resubmit a revised version. Specifically, I suggest:

1. **Expanded Experimental Evaluation:** Conduct more comprehensive experiments with a wider range of datasets, including subjective listening tests and comparisons with state-of-the-art methods.
2. **Computational Analysis:** Provide a more detailed analysis of the computational complexity of MJLE and explore potential optimizations.
3. **Hyperparameter Optimization:** Implement a systematic approach to hyperparameter selection, such as cross-validation.
4. **Explore SBL Connections:** Investigate how techniques from the SBL literature could be incorporated into the LRTFS framework.
5. **Address Scalability:** Briefly discuss future work plans to tackle the scalability issues of MMLE.

Addressing these points would significantly strengthen the paper and increase its likelihood of acceptance in a future review cycle.
