## Review of "Low-Rank Time-Frequency Synthesis"

**Summary:**

This paper introduces a novel generative model, Low-Rank Time-Frequency Synthesis (LRTFS), for signal representation, building upon the Gaussian Composite Model (GCM) [3]. Unlike GCM which models the STFT coefficients as having low-rank variance, LRTFS models the variance of the *synthesis* coefficients, directly linking it to the original signal. This allows for a truly generative model, enabling signal reconstruction from low-rank latent factors and naturally lending itself to multi-resolution representations. The authors present two estimation approaches - Maximum Joint Likelihood Estimation (MJLE) via EM and Maximum Marginal Likelihood Estimation (MMLE) – and demonstrate its application in music decomposition and speech enhancement.

**Relation to Prior Work:**

The work builds strongly on the foundation laid by NMF [1, 2] and the GCM [3]. It addresses the limitations of NMF by providing a probabilistic generative model and overcomes the GCM's restriction to the STFT domain.  The connection to Sparse Bayesian Learning (SBL) [4, 5] is insightful, highlighting the difference in variance structure as a key distinction.  The multi-resolution approach draws upon ideas from hybrid signal representations in audio processing [8, 9, 10] but integrates them seamlessly within a low-rank synthesis framework.

**Strengths:**

* **Generative Model:**  The key strength is the development of a truly generative model for the raw signal, a significant step beyond methods that only operate on spectral representations. This opens doors to more robust and flexible signal processing applications.
* **Multi-resolution Capability:** The natural integration of multi-resolution analysis within the LRTFS framework is a valuable contribution, particularly relevant for signals with varying temporal characteristics like audio.
* **Theoretical Foundation:** The connection to SBL provides a solid theoretical justification for the model and its estimation procedures. The derivation of the EM algorithm is well-presented.
* **Empirical Validation:** The experimental results, despite being relatively simple, demonstrate the potential of LRTFS. The SNR improvement in speech enhancement is competitive with established methods.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the core concepts, the model formulation, and the estimation procedures.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Scalability of MMLE:** The authors acknowledge the scalability issues with MMLE, which is a significant limitation. The computational complexity makes it impractical for larger-scale audio applications.  While mentioning potential solutions like message passing [16], these are left as future work.
* **Limited Experimental Scope:** The experiments are somewhat limited in scope.  While the music decomposition example is illustrative, the performance is not rigorously compared to state-of-the-art hybrid decomposition methods. The speech enhancement experiment focuses on a single noise type, and the improvement is marginal over a standard approach (OMLSA).  More extensive evaluation, including different datasets and noise conditions, is needed.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The reliance on careful tuning of the regularization parameter λ, without a principled method for selection, is a concern.  The discussion on why directly estimating λ via the EM update rule performs poorly is helpful, but a more robust approach is desirable.
* **Lack of Theoretical Analysis of Convergence:** While the EM algorithm is presented, a formal analysis of its convergence properties is missing.  Acknowledging potential convergence to local optima is insufficient.
* **Connection to Wavelet-based methods:** The paper could benefit from a more thorough discussion of its relation to existing wavelet-based methods for signal decomposition.

**Arguments For/Against Acceptance:**

**Pro:**

* The LRTFS model represents a novel and theoretically sound approach to signal representation.
* The generative nature of the model and the multi-resolution capability are significant contributions.
* The paper is well-written and clearly explains the underlying concepts.

**Con:**

* The scalability issues with MMLE are a major practical limitation.
* The experimental evaluation is limited and doesn't provide strong evidence of superior performance over existing methods.
* The hyperparameter sensitivity and lack of convergence analysis are concerns.



**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a promising new direction in time-frequency signal modeling. The LRTFS model is a valuable contribution, offering a generative framework with inherent multi-resolution capabilities. However, the scalability limitations of the MMLE algorithm and the limited scope of the experimental evaluation prevent it from being a definitively strong paper.  The hyperparameter tuning and lack of convergence analysis are also weaknesses. 

**Recommendation:**

I recommend **accepting** this paper with minor revisions. The core idea is innovative and holds significant potential. The authors should:

1. **Expand the experimental evaluation:** Include comparisons to state-of-the-art methods in both music decomposition and speech enhancement, using a wider range of datasets and noise conditions.
2. **Investigate alternative regularization techniques:** Explore more robust methods for selecting the regularization parameter λ.
3. **Discuss scalability solutions:** Expand on the potential approaches for improving the scalability of MMLE, even if a full implementation is beyond the scope of the current work.
4. **Add a discussion regarding the connections to wavelet-based methods.**



I believe that addressing these points will significantly strengthen the paper and make it a more valuable contribution to the NIPS community.
