The paper proposes a low-rank time-frequency synthesis (LRTFS) model, extending probabilistic latent factor models like the Gaussian Composite Model (GCM) to directly model the raw signal rather than its STFT. The core idea is to express the signal as a sum of weighted atoms from a time-frequency basis, with low-rank constraints imposed on the variance of the synthesis coefficients. The authors present maximum likelihood estimation approaches (MJLE and MMLE) and demonstrate the method on music decomposition and speech enhancement tasks.

While the idea of directly synthesizing the signal with low-rank time-frequency structure is conceptually appealing and builds upon existing work in NMF and GCMs, the paper suffers from a significant number of shortcomings, ranging from minor notational inconsistencies to deeper concerns regarding the practical scalability and justification of certain methodological choices.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The LRTFS model represents a logical extension of existing probabilistic latent factor models, addressing the limitations of previous approaches that operate solely on the STFT.
*   **Multi-resolution potential:** The framework naturally lends itself to multi-resolution representations, offering a potential advantage for signals with varying temporal characteristics.
*   **Theoretical grounding:** The connection to sparse Bayesian learning (SBL) is clearly articulated, providing a theoretical basis for the proposed model.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Notational inconsistencies & Clarity:** The indexing of coefficients (using 'm' vs. '(f, n)') is confusing and frequently abused, impacting clarity. The explanation of the EM algorithm is terse and assumes a high level of familiarity with the cited work ([6]). The transition between equations (2) and (3) isn’t fully clear and the explanation of ‘loosely speaking’ is unnecessary.
*   **MJLE Algorithm Details:** The EM algorithm description lacks sufficient detail. For instance, the update rule for λ (Eq. 19) is stated as "not exactly deriving from the EM procedure" – this is a significant omission and raises concerns about the algorithm’s theoretical justification. Why is it still decreasing the negative log-likelihood?
*   **MMLE Scalability:** The authors acknowledge the scalability issues with MMLE but present it anyway for “completeness.” Given its impracticality for the target applications, including it feels superfluous. The discussion regarding the Woodbury matrix identity feels like a post-hoc rationalization rather than a careful consideration during the design of the algorithm.
*   **Experimental Evaluation:** The experiments, while demonstrating potential, are limited. The hybrid decomposition example lacks a quantitative evaluation and relies on subjective listening tests. The speech enhancement results show only a modest improvement over a basic IS-NMF baseline, and the improvement over OMLSA is also small. The experiment parameters are not thoroughly justified. Why K=500?
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The reliance on manual tuning of λ (without any principled method for selection) is a weakness. The authors admit that λ was selected by maximizing the output SNR, which introduces a potential bias and lacks generality.
*   **Lack of Comparative Analysis:** The paper does not adequately compare LRTFS to other state-of-the-art signal synthesis or source separation methods.
*   **Minor Errors and Imprecision:** The sentence, “Unexpectedly, a third term that favors sparse solutions of α, thanks to the log function, naturally appears from the derivation of the joint likelihood” is not precise. The term arises from the Gaussian prior on α, not a direct promotion of sparsity.
*  **Reference Format:** The list of references is not uniformly formatted.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

*   The core idea is novel and potentially impactful.
*   The theoretical connection to SBL is well-established.
*   The multi-resolution framework is a valuable contribution.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The paper suffers from numerous methodological and presentation flaws.
*   The scalability of MMLE is a major limitation.
*   The experimental evaluation is insufficient to demonstrate significant advantages.
*   The unclear explanations and notational inconsistencies hinder understanding.
*   The overreliance on hyperparameter tuning and lack of principled selection criteria detract from the robustness of the results.



**Overall Assessment:**

I recommend **rejection**. While the LRTFS model presents a promising direction for signal synthesis, the numerous weaknesses in the paper, particularly regarding clarity, scalability, and experimental validation, outweigh its strengths. The paper feels incomplete and lacks the rigor expected of a NIPS submission. Significant revisions addressing the concerns outlined above would be necessary for reconsideration. The work, as presented, is more akin to a preliminary investigation than a fully developed and validated contribution.
