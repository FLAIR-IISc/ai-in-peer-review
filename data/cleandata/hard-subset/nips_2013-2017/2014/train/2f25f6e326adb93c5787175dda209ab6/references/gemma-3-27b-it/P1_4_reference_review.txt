## Review of "Low-Rank Time-Frequency Synthesis"

**Summary:**

This paper introduces a novel generative model for signals, termed Low-Rank Time-Frequency Synthesis (LRTFS), building upon the Gaussian Composite Model (GCM) [3]. Unlike the GCM, which models the STFT coefficients as having a low-rank variance structure, LRTFS models the *synthesis* coefficients as having a low-rank structure. This allows for direct signal synthesis and facilitates multi-resolution representations, a feature absent in traditional NMF approaches. The authors present two estimation approaches: Maximum Joint Likelihood Estimation (MJLE) and Maximum Marginal Likelihood Estimation (MMLE) with corresponding EM algorithms. Experiments on music decomposition and speech enhancement demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed approach.

**Relation to Previous Work:**

The work builds directly on the popular field of matrix factorization, specifically NMF [1, 2] and its probabilistic extension, the GCM [3]. It differentiates itself by moving away from analyzing the STFT (as in NMF and GCM) towards a true signal synthesis model. The connection to Sparse Bayesian Learning (SBL) [4, 5] is highlighted, noting the difference in variance structure between LRTFS and SBL. The multi-resolution aspect leverages ideas from morphological filtering and hybrid representations in audio processing [8, 9, 10]. The speech enhancement experiment draws parallels to semi-supervised source separation techniques [11]. 

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The LRTFS model presents a genuinely novel approach to signal representation and synthesis, moving beyond traditional spectral decomposition methods. The focus on modeling synthesis coefficients rather than analysis coefficients is a key contribution.
* **Generative Model:**  Developing a generative model for the raw signal x(t) is a significant step forward, allowing for signal reconstruction and, crucially, multi-resolution representations.
* **Multi-Resolution Capabilities:** The ability to incorporate multiple time-frequency bases opens up possibilities for representing signals with varying characteristics (e.g., transients and sustained tones) more effectively.
* **Theoretical Foundation:** The derivation of the MJLE and MMLE approaches, along with the associated EM algorithms, provides a solid theoretical foundation for the proposed model. The connection to SBL adds further insight.
* **Experimental Validation:** The experiments, while limited in scope, demonstrate promising results in both music decomposition and speech enhancement, showing potential practical applicability.
* **Clear Writing:** The paper is generally well-written and logically organized, making it relatively easy to follow the key ideas.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Computational Complexity:** The MMLE approach, while presented, is acknowledged as not scalable to practical signal dimensions.  This limits the exploration of its potential benefits. The MJLE implementation is computationally feasible, but scalability to extremely long signals remains a concern.
* **Limited Scope of Experiments:** The experimental results, while positive, are based on relatively small datasets and a limited set of hyperparameters. A more thorough evaluation with diverse datasets and rigorous hyperparameter tuning would strengthen the conclusions.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The performance appears sensitive to the choice of the regularization parameter λ, which was treated as a hyperparameter rather than estimated.  Further investigation into automated parameter selection strategies would be beneficial.
* **Lack of Comparison to Strong Baselines:** While a baseline NMF approach is used for speech enhancement, comparison to more state-of-the-art speech enhancement algorithms (beyond OMLSA [14]) would provide a stronger assessment of the method’s performance.
* **Limited Theoretical Analysis:** While the authors present the EM algorithm, a more in-depth theoretical analysis of convergence properties and the impact of different parameter settings would be valuable.




**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper introduces a novel and theoretically sound generative model for signal synthesis.
* The multi-resolution capability is a significant advancement over existing NMF-based approaches.
* The experimental results demonstrate the potential of the proposed method in practical applications.
* The connection to SBL provides valuable insights into the underlying principles of the model.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The computational complexity of the MMLE approach limits its practical applicability.
* The experiments are relatively limited in scope and hyperparameter sensitivity is a concern.
* A more comprehensive comparison to state-of-the-art baselines is needed.

**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a significant contribution to the field of time-frequency analysis and signal processing. The LRTFS model offers a novel and promising approach to signal representation and synthesis, with the potential for applications in various domains.  While the computational complexity and limited scope of experiments are weaknesses, the strengths of the paper outweigh these concerns.  I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions, specifically addressing the following points:

1.  **Expand Experimental Evaluation:** Conduct more extensive experiments with larger datasets, diverse signals, and rigorous hyperparameter tuning.
2.  **Strengthen Baseline Comparisons:** Compare the proposed method to more state-of-the-art baselines in both music decomposition and speech enhancement.
3.  **Discuss Computational Trade-offs:** Elaborate on the computational trade-offs between MJLE and MMLE, and explore potential strategies for improving the scalability of the MMLE algorithm.
4.  **Provide more detail on hyperparameter selection:** Explain how the value of lambda was selected and provide sensitivity analysis of performance to lambda.

I believe addressing these points will further strengthen the paper and enhance its impact on the field.
