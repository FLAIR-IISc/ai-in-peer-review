Summary
The paper proposes Low-Rank Time-Frequency Synthesis (LRTFS), a generative model that operates directly in the signal (time-domain) via a linear synthesis using a time-frequency dictionary. The synthesis coefficients are complex Gaussian with variances constrained to be low-rank via WH, analogous to NMF structure but imposed on the synthesis coefficients rather than STFT analysis coefficients (GCM). Two estimation strategies are discussed: maximum joint likelihood (MJLE) with an EM algorithm leveraging a Figueiredo–Nowak splitting, and maximum marginal likelihood (MMLE, SBL-like), which the authors acknowledge is currently not scalable. A multi-resolution extension allows modeling with multiple time-frequency frames (e.g., short/long windows). Experiments include a small illustrative tonal/transient decomposition and a semi-supervised speech enhancement task, showing modest SNR improvements over IS-NMF and OMLSA.

Strengths
- Clear conceptual bridge between synthesis modeling and low-rank factorization, relating to NMF/GCM and SBL.
- The MJLE-EM procedure avoids large matrix inversions; implementation appears feasible using standard TF toolboxes.
- Multi-resolution formulation is natural and cleanly integrated.
- Preliminary results suggest the approach can be competitive with established baselines.

Weaknesses
- Empirical validation is limited (very small-scale, few baselines, modest gains, no statistical analysis); λ is tuned to maximize test SNR, which is not proper experimental protocol.
- MMLE is not scalable in its current form; the main novelty rests on MJLE which resembles alternating shrinkage and IS-NMF, raising questions about practical advantage vs established methods.
- Inconsistency in the MJLE objective: the claim that a “sparsity-favoring log(|α|^2) term” arises is at odds with the algebra because DIS(|α|^2|v) already contains −log|α|^2; adding +log(|α|^2) cancels it. This needs correction/clarification.
- No ablations isolating the value of synthesis vs analysis modeling, or of multi-resolution vs single-resolution.
- No runtime/memory profiling; reproducibility details and robust hyperparameter selection (dev set) are missing.

Quality
- The modeling framework and EM derivations for MJLE are mostly sound and build on well-known Gaussian latent-variable machinery. However:
  - The stated sparsity-inducing effect from the log term appears incorrect; the objective as written simplifies to terms without log(|α|^2), undermining the stated interpretation.
  - Choice of β and its effect on convergence/estimation is not discussed beyond a spectral bound; no convergence guarantees beyond monotonic decrease are provided.
  - λ is not estimated in experiments, and is instead hand-tuned to optimize outcome SNR; this undermines claims of probabilistic soundness and fair comparison.
- The MMLE approach is standard SBL-style type-II ML but is deemed impractical by the authors; without a scalable routine, the paper’s stronger claims about marginal-likelihood benefits remain untested.

Clarity
- Generally well written and organized; the relation to GCM and SBL is helpful.
- Some notational overload (m vs (f,n)) is acknowledged; acceptable.
- The claim about the log term and sparsity should be corrected, and the derivation in Eq. (11) clarified.
- References are incomplete (missing years/venues) and could be strengthened, especially regarding phase-aware TF factorization and multi-resolution NMF variants.

Originality
- Imposing a low-rank structure on variances of synthesis coefficients (rather than analysis coefficients) is a reasonable twist, but close in spirit to probabilistic NMF/GCM with Gaussian priors and to structured SBL. The multi-resolution extension is straightforward once the synthesis model is adopted.
- The methodological novelty seems incremental rather than fundamentally new; the experimental section does not demonstrate a clear qualitative advantage.

Significance
- Potentially useful for applications needing multi-resolution time-domain modeling; however, demonstrated improvements over strong, established baselines are small (0.6 dB over IS-NMF on a tiny test suite).
- Without broader and more rigorous evaluation (standard datasets/metrics, multiple noises, ablations, significance tests), impact is uncertain.

Detailed comments and suggestions
- Correct Eq. (11) and the interpretation of the log term; if sparsity is intended, consider explicit priors (e.g., scale mixtures or Laplace) and analyze their effect.
- Provide a principled strategy for λ (and β) selection (e.g., dev set, evidence maximization), and avoid tuning on test data.
- Include stronger baselines: phase-aware NMF/complex NMF variants, multi-window NMF, and modern speech enhancement baselines. Report standard metrics (e.g., SDR/SIR/SAR, PESQ/STOI) and statistical significance.
- Add ablations: single vs multi-resolution; analysis vs synthesis modeling (GCM vs LRTFS) under identical constraints; effect of K; effect of fixing vs learning W.
- Report computational costs (per-iteration time, memory) and convergence behavior.
- For MMLE, outline or prototype a scalable approximation (e.g., conjugate gradients with matrix–vector products, diagonal/low-rank approximations, message passing) to substantiate feasibility.

Pros
- Conceptually coherent synthesis-side low-rank model.
- Efficient MJLE algorithm; easy multi-resolution extension.
- Preliminary competitive results.

Cons
- Experimental validation is weak and potentially biased by test-time hyperparameter tuning.
- Questionable sparsity claim in objective; limited theoretical analysis.
- MMLE contribution undeveloped; modest gains over strong baselines.

Recommendation: Reject

Confidence: High (familiar with NMF/SBL/phase-aware TF models; concerns hinge on correctness and evaluation rigor rather than taste)
