## Review of Low-Rank Time-Frequency Synthesis

This paper introduces a novel generative model for signals, the Low-Rank Time-Frequency Synthesis (LRTFS) model, extending the Gaussian Composite Model (GCM) from operating on the STFT to directly synthesizing the raw signal. The core idea – modeling synthesis coefficients as having a low-rank structure, similar to how GCM imposes low-rank structure on analysis coefficients – is compelling and represents a potentially significant shift in time-frequency representation learning. The paper clearly articulates the motivation, relates the model to sparse Bayesian learning, and proposes EM algorithms for estimation. The extension to multi-resolution representation is a particularly appealing feature.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The direct synthesis approach, going beyond STFT-domain decomposition, is genuinely novel and addresses a fundamental limitation of previous NMF-based methods. This is a bold move, attempting to build a truly generative model at the signal level.
* **Theoretical Foundation:** The connection to sparse Bayesian learning and the derivation of the EM algorithms provide a solid theoretical underpinning. The authors correctly identify the differences and similarities with SBL, highlighting the constraint imposed by the low-rank structure.
* **Multi-Resolution Capability:** The extension to multi-resolution representations is a significant advantage, opening doors to modeling signals with varying temporal characteristics. This is a practical and valuable addition.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the model and algorithms. The mathematical notation is generally accessible.
* **Promising Experimental Results:** While not exhaustive, the experiments demonstrate the potential of the approach in both music decomposition and speech enhancement, achieving competitive results.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Scalability of MMLE:** The authors acknowledge the scalability issues with the MMLE algorithm, which limits its application to larger-scale problems.  The reliance on MJLE, while practical, may lead to suboptimal solutions.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The experiments reveal sensitivity to the choice of the regularization parameter λ. More thorough investigation of hyperparameter tuning strategies would be beneficial.
* **Incomplete Validation:** The experimental section, while demonstrating promise, lacks comprehensive evaluation. Comparison with state-of-the-art methods beyond the listed baselines would strengthen the claims. The subjective nature of evaluating decomposed components (music) and SNR improvement (speech) necessitates more rigorous quantitative analysis.
* **Lack of Theoretical Analysis of Local Optima:** The authors acknowledge that the EM algorithm may converge to a local optimum.  Further analysis of the loss landscape and the impact of initialization on the solution quality would be helpful.
* **Limited Exploration of Prior Choices:** While the Gaussian prior is a natural choice, exploring alternative priors on the synthesis coefficients could potentially improve performance.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper introduces a fundamentally new approach to time-frequency representation learning.
* The model is theoretically sound and offers a principled framework for signal synthesis.
* The multi-resolution extension significantly expands the applicability of the approach.
* The experimental results, though preliminary, are promising and suggest potential for real-world applications.
* The authors clearly identify limitations and potential avenues for future work.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The scalability limitations of MMLE restrict the applicability of a potentially powerful estimation method.
* The hyperparameter sensitivity requires careful tuning and may limit the robustness of the approach.
* The experimental validation is not extensive enough to fully demonstrate the superiority of LRTFS over existing methods.
* The potential for convergence to local optima remains a concern.



**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a highly innovative and potentially impactful contribution to the field of time-frequency analysis and signal processing. While the paper is not without its limitations, the novelty of the approach and its theoretical soundness outweigh the weaknesses. The authors have identified a significant gap in existing methods and proposed a compelling solution. I believe that the potential benefits of LRTFS are substantial enough to warrant acceptance, even with the acknowledged limitations. I am willing to overlook the incomplete validation and scalability issues, given the boldness and originality of the idea. I encourage the authors to address the reviewer comments in a revised version, particularly focusing on improving the experimental validation and exploring strategies for scaling up the MMLE algorithm.

**Recommendation:** Accept.
