

# Review of "Low-Rank Time-Frequency Synthesis"

This paper introduces the Low-Rank Time-Frequency Synthesis (LRTFS) model, which extends the Gaussian Composite Model (GCM) to directly model the raw signal rather than its STFT. The key contribution is a generative model that bridges the gap between time-frequency synthesis and traditional NMF approaches, while enabling multi-resolution representations. The authors present two estimation methods: Maximum Joint Likelihood Estimation (MJLE) and Maximum Marginal Likelihood Estimation (MMLE), with the latter being deemed impractical for audio applications due to computational constraints.

While the paper addresses a meaningful gap in the literature and presents a theoretically interesting extension of existing work (particularly in relation to SBL and GCM), it contains several critical technical flaws that undermine its scientific contribution. The following points highlight major concerns:

1. **Mathematical Errors in Core Formulation**: Equation (1) states "yfn ∼ Nc(0, [WH]fn)" but the Nc distribution definition given later shows Nc(x|µ, λ) = (πλ)⁻¹ exp⁻(|x−µ|²/λ), which implies the variance parameter is λ, not [WH]fn. This is a fundamental inconsistency. The paper later uses [WH]fn as the variance parameter, but the notation is never properly reconciled with the distribution definition.

2. **Confusing Notation in EM Algorithm**: In equation (11), the term "log(|α|²)" appears, which is mathematically nonsensical as |α|² would be a matrix. This should be clarified as log(|α|₂²) or similar. The inconsistent use of v(i)fn versus [W(i)H(i)]fn throughout the paper creates significant confusion about the model parameters.

3. **Invalid Covariance Matrix**: In equation (13), the authors state "e2 ∼ Nc(0, λI − βΦΦ*)", but λI − βΦΦ* is not guaranteed to be positive semi-definite, which is required for a valid covariance matrix. The paper fails to establish conditions under which this expression represents a valid distribution.

4. **Incorrect Rank Constraint Statement**: The paper claims "In the general unsupervised setting where both W and H are estimated, WH must be low-rank such that K < F and K < N. However, in supervised settings where W is known, we may have K > F." This is mathematically incorrect. The rank of WH is at most min(F, N, K), so WH can be low-rank even when K > F (as long as min(F, N) is small).

5. **Inconsistent Treatment of λ**: The paper provides an update rule for λ in equation (19) but states "this parameter was not estimated in our experiments, but instead treated as an hyperparameter." This is inconsistent with the entire EM algorithm framework, which should estimate all parameters. The authors cite [6] for justifying their λ update, but fail to implement this in their experiments.

6. **Formatting and Presentation Issues**: The paper uses inconsistent notation for the Itakura-Saito divergence (DIS vs dIS), and the complex normal distribution notation Nc is not clearly explained for readers unfamiliar with this convention. The statement about exploiting Hermitian symmetry for real signals is vague and lacks sufficient detail.

These issues collectively represent significant technical flaws that prevent the paper from making a credible contribution to the field. The mathematical errors undermine the core theoretical framework, while the inconsistent notation and implementation choices create confusion about the model's actual behavior. The paper's claims about multi-resolution capabilities are promising, but the technical shortcomings prevent readers from assessing whether these claims are valid.

While the paper's motivation is sound and the problem addressed is meaningful, the technical execution is flawed. The authors have not properly verified their mathematical derivations, leading to errors that would likely cause problems for anyone attempting to implement or build upon their work. The paper would benefit from a thorough mathematical revision to resolve these inconsistencies.

The experimental results are presented but suffer from the same technical inconsistencies (e.g., λ not being properly estimated despite being part of the model). The speech enhancement results (6.6 dB improvement) are modest compared to the baseline (6 dB), and the improvement over OMLSA (4.6 dB) is not clearly explained.

In conclusion, while the paper addresses an interesting problem, the technical errors and inconsistencies are too severe to recommend acceptance. The paper would need a complete mathematical revision to address these issues before it could be considered for publication. The authors should carefully verify all mathematical derivations and ensure consistent notation throughout the paper.

Recommendation: Reject
