This paper introduces a low-rank time-frequency synthesis (LRTFS) model, aiming to provide a generative model for the raw signal itself, unlike previous approaches focusing on the STFT magnitude. The core idea is to represent the signal as a sum of time-frequency atoms with low-rank constraints on the synthesis coefficients, building upon the Gaussian Composite Model (GCM) [3]. The authors present maximum likelihood estimation approaches (MJLE and MMLE) via EM algorithms and demonstrate the applicability of LRTFS to multi-resolution representations, with experiments in music decomposition and speech enhancement.

The paper’s strength lies in its attempt to address a fundamental limitation of traditional NMF-based spectral decomposition: the disregard for phase information and the lack of a true generative signal model. The extension to multi-resolution representations is a potentially valuable addition, aligning with known signal characteristics in domains like audio. The authors correctly identify the connection to sparse Bayesian learning (SBL) and attempt a principled probabilistic framework. The presented experimental results, while limited in scope, show competitive performance in speech enhancement compared to established methods.

However, several weaknesses raise significant concerns. The primary issue is the lack of a compelling demonstration of superiority over existing, well-established methods. While the speech enhancement results are comparable to IS-NMF and OMLSA, they do not definitively showcase the advantages of the more complex LRTFS model. The improvement of 6.6 dB over the baseline is not substantial enough to justify the increased complexity. The music decomposition example feels illustrative rather than a rigorous evaluation; a quantitative assessment is missing.

The theoretical justification, while present, feels somewhat underdeveloped. The connection to SBL is acknowledged, but the implications of tying the variances in LRTFS (unlike the free variances in SBL that enable sparsity) are not fully explored. The authors note the potential for sparsity due to the log term in the objective function, but this is presented as an "unexpected" result rather than a deliberate design choice and doesn't appear strongly leveraged. 

The scaling issues with MMLE are a significant limitation. While acknowledged, the authors state that large-scale algorithms are “left as future work.” This significantly weakens the contributions, as MMLE is presented as a theoretically desirable alternative to MJLE. Furthermore, the decision to treat λ as a hyperparameter rather than estimating it within the EM framework, despite providing update rules, is questionable. The rationale provided (poor performance with joint estimation) needs more justification, and a sensitivity analysis to the choice of λ would be beneficial.

The writing quality is generally good, but the paper could benefit from more concise explanations and clearer motivation for certain design choices. For example, the justification for using a Gabor frame is somewhat superficial.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Attempts to address a fundamental limitation in spectral decomposition methods.
*   Introduces a principled probabilistic model for signal synthesis.
*   Demonstrates a potential for multi-resolution representation.
*   Achieves competitive results in speech enhancement.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Lacks a compelling demonstration of superiority over established methods.
*   Theoretical justification feels underdeveloped.
*   MMLE is not scalable, limiting the practical impact of the work.
*   The hyperparameter λ is not estimated, raising concerns about robustness.
*   The music decomposition example lacks quantitative evaluation.



**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents an interesting idea, but its contribution falls short of NIPS standards. The LRTFS model has potential, but the current presentation lacks the rigorous evaluation and demonstrable advantage needed to justify acceptance. The claims of novelty are somewhat overstated, and the paper feels more like a preliminary investigation than a complete piece of work. I recommend **rejection**. I would encourage the authors to focus on more extensive evaluation, particularly in demonstrating a clear benefit over existing methods. Addressing the scaling issues with MMLE and performing a thorough sensitivity analysis on λ would also significantly strengthen the paper.
