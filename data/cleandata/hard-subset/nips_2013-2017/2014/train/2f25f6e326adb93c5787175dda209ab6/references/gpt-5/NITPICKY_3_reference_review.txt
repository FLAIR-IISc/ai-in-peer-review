Summary
This paper proposes Low-Rank Time-Frequency Synthesis (LRTFS), a generative time-domain model in which a signal x(t) is synthesized from complex time-frequency atoms with Gaussian synthesis coefficients αfn whose variances factorize as [WH]fn (low-rank in the time-frequency plane). This contrasts with prior Gaussian Composite Models (GCM) that place a low-rank variance on analysis (STFT) coefficients. The authors develop two estimation strategies: (i) maximum joint likelihood (MJLE) with an EM algorithm that avoids matrix inversions via a Figueiredo–Nowak splitting trick; and (ii) maximum marginal likelihood (MMLE) following SBL-type Type-II ML, which is acknowledged as not scalable due to large matrix inversions. They also extend LRTFS to multi-resolution by combining multiple frames (e.g., short/long windows). Experiments on hybrid music decomposition and semi-supervised speech enhancement show moderate SNR improvements over IS-NMF and OMLSA.

Relation to prior work
The model is closely related to NMF with IS divergence on spectrograms [Févotte et al.], probabilistic PLCA/GCM on complex STFT magnitudes, and SBL [Tipping; Wipf & Rao]. The novelty is to move the low-rank variance model from the analysis coefficients to the synthesis coefficients of a time-domain generative model, with multi-resolution handled naturally by stacking frames. This also connects to hybrid/morphological decompositions (MCA) and multi-window/time-frequency representations in audio. However, relations to phase-aware/complex NMF (e.g., Sawada et al.), Bayesian NMF variants, Gaussian scale mixture priors, and sparse/mixed-norm multiresolution models could be discussed more thoroughly.

Strengths
- Clear conceptual bridge from spectrogram-domain probabilistic factorization to a time-domain generative model with low-rank variance structure.
- Practical MJLE algorithm avoiding matrix inversions; using frame analysis/synthesis operators makes it computationally plausible for audio-length signals.
- Simple multi-resolution extension with clean EM updates.
- Derivation links to Wiener filtering interpretations and standard IS-NMF machinery.

Weaknesses and detailed comments
Technical correctness and clarity
- Equation (11) and the “sparsity” claim: Writing the joint negative log-likelihood as DIS(|α|^2|v) + log(|α|^2) + const suggests a sparsity-promoting term via log(|α|^2). But DIS(x|y) = x/y − log x + log y − 1; adding +log x exactly cancels the −log x, leaving x/y + log y − 1. Thus, there is no net sparsity-inducing effect in α from this decomposition. The text’s claim that the log term favors sparsity is misleading and should be corrected.
- Inconsistency on inversion dimensions: Sec. 2.3 states that α̂MMSE requires inversion of a T×T matrix; Sec. 3.2 uses Σ = (Φ*Φ/λ + diag(v)^{-1})^{-1} (M×M). These should be reconciled (e.g., both forms exist via Woodbury; please state which is used and when).
- Equations (29)-(30): Missing the zero-mean in the Gaussian notation (should be Nc(0, [W H]fn)).
- Sec. 4 Eq. (32) indexing appears inconsistent: z(i)fn should be z(i)` ,fn for layer `∈{a,b}.
- The Figueiredo–Nowak split requires λI−β ΦΦ* ≽ 0 (β ≤ λ/δΦ). How is δΦ computed in practice for the chosen frames? Is Φ tight? This is important for correctness and reproducibility.
- The M-step update for λ in MJLE is not the exact EM update; the text claims it still decreases the negative log-likelihood, but no proof or reference beyond [6] is provided in this context. Please clarify conditions under which monotonicity holds when (i) W,H are updated via MM for IS-NMF and (ii) λ is updated approximately.

Experimental methodology
- Hyperparameter tuning leakage: In Sec. 5.1, “We experimented several values for λ and selected the results leading to best output SNR.” This uses test ground truth to tune λ and overstates performance. A validation set or principled selection (or the proposed estimator for λ) should be used.
- Speech enhancement uses a “warm-restart” schedule for λ decreased to a negligible value; the schedule is unspecified and appears tuned heuristically. Provide the schedule, rationale, and sensitivity analysis.
- Baselines are weak and incomplete: Only IS-NMF with a single window and OMLSA are compared. Missing: complex/phase-constrained NMF, multi-window IS-NMF baselines, GCM/STFT-domain generative models with Wiener reconstruction, modern supervised/bayesian NMF variants, and even a single-resolution LRTFS ablation. The reported 0.6 dB gain over IS-NMF may be within variance.
- Metrics: Only SNR is reported; perceptually and separation-relevant metrics (SDR/SIR/SAR via BSS_EVAL, STOI, PESQ) and standard deviations over trials are missing. No statistical significance.
- Reproducibility: Missing initialization details for W,H,α; β selection; number of iterations/stopping criteria; frame redundancy and overlap; computational time/memory; code availability. For dictionary training (K=500), details (optimizer, epochs, normalization, hop sizes) are absent.

Formatting/Presentation
- Numerous typos/usage: “hit-hat” (hi-hat), “mentionned,” “hermitian” (capitalize), colloquial phrasing, inconsistent notation between m and (f,n).
- Figures referenced (Fig. 1/2) but no details; results rely on supplementary audio.
- References are incomplete (missing years/venues/pages, inconsistent styles); this is below NeurIPS standards.

Originality and significance
- The idea to parameterize synthesis-coefficient variances with a low-rank WH and to work in the time domain is interesting and may open doors to multiresolution modeling; however, it is fairly close to SBL with tied variances and to prior phase-aware/complex NMF generative models. The MMLE route (a potential strength) is left as future work due to scalability, limiting immediate impact. Empirical gains are modest and not convincingly demonstrated against strong baselines.

Pros
- Conceptually clean generative time-domain model bridging NMF and synthesis modeling.
- Practical EM algorithm without matrix inversions; multi-resolution extension is simple.
- Potentially useful for future multi-resolution/audio tasks.

Cons
- Mathematical misstatement about sparsity effect in Eq. (11); inversion size inconsistency; small typos in equations.
- Experimental methodology issues (hyperparameter tuning on test, underspecified schedules), weak baselines, limited metrics.
- Incomplete references and several presentation/notation issues.
- Reproducibility details and compute/scaling analysis are missing.
- MMLE contribution not realized at scale.

Recommendation
Reject. While the core idea is interesting and the MJLE algorithm is appealing, the paper contains multiple technical and presentation issues (including a substantive misinterpretation of the objective), weak/arguably biased experimental methodology, and insufficient comparisons. Addressing these points, strengthening baselines/metrics, and clarifying derivations could make this a solid contribution.

Confidence
High.
