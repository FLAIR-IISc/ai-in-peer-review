

# Review of "Low-Rank Time-Frequency Synthesis"

## Summary and Relation to Previous Work

This paper introduces the Low-Rank Time-Frequency Synthesis (LRTFS) model, which extends traditional nonnegative matrix factorization (NMF) approaches by modeling the raw signal directly rather than its time-frequency representation. The authors build upon the Gaussian Composite Model (GCM) [3], which models the STFT as having a low-rank variance structure, but extend it to model the actual signal as a linear combination of time-frequency atoms with low-rank variance structure. This bridges a key gap between traditional NMF approaches (which work on transformed data) and signal synthesis.

The paper makes several important connections to prior work: it relates to sparse Bayesian learning (SBL) [4,5], noting that while SBL induces sparsity through independent variance parameters, LRTFS imposes a low-rank constraint on these variances. The paper also connects to multi-resolution approaches in audio processing [8,9,10] but extends them by allowing for a unified probabilistic framework rather than ad-hoc combinations of different time-frequency representations.

## Quality

The paper demonstrates strong technical quality. The mathematical formulations are sound, with clear derivations of the generative model, the two estimation approaches (MJLE and MMLE), and the multi-resolution extension. The authors provide detailed EM algorithms with explicit update rules, and the theoretical connections to existing work (like SBL) are well-established.

The experimental results are well-supported. The music decomposition task successfully separates tonal and transient components, and the speech enhancement experiment shows a 6.6 dB SNR improvement over a baseline IS-NMF approach (6 dB) and a standard OMLSA method (4.6 dB). The authors are careful to acknowledge limitations, such as the scalability issues with MMLE for large audio applications, and they appropriately treat λ as a hyperparameter rather than estimating it.

## Clarity

The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The logical flow from problem statement to model definition to estimation to experiments is clear. The authors use effective notation, provide helpful explanations of key concepts (e.g., the relationship between synthesis and analysis coefficients), and include well-structured equations. The supplementary material is mentioned as containing audio examples, which would be valuable for readers.

The paper's clarity is particularly impressive given the mathematical complexity of the content. The authors make a concerted effort to explain concepts in an accessible way, such as distinguishing between the synthesis and analysis coefficients and clearly explaining the multi-resolution extension.

## Originality

LRTFS represents a significant step beyond existing time-frequency modeling approaches. While previous work like GCM [3] modeled the STFT directly with low-rank structure, LRTFS models the raw signal with the same low-rank structure in the time-frequency domain. This is a meaningful conceptual advance that allows for more natural signal synthesis and multi-resolution modeling.

The multi-resolution extension is particularly novel. The paper shows how LRTFS can naturally accommodate different time-frequency bases for different components (e.g., short windows for transients, longer windows for tonal components), which is not possible with traditional NMF approaches that work on a single transformed representation. This addresses a real limitation in audio processing where different signal components require different resolutions.

## Significance

The significance of this work is substantial. By modeling the raw signal rather than its STFT, LRTFS provides a more accurate representation that preserves phase information (unlike traditional NMF approaches that discard phase). The multi-resolution capability has immediate practical applications in audio processing, where transients (e.g., drum hits) require higher time resolution than sustained sounds (e.g., vocals).

The results demonstrate clear practical advantages: the 0.6 dB SNR improvement over the IS-NMF baseline may seem modest, but in speech enhancement, such improvements are meaningful. The ability to naturally decompose signals into components with different time resolutions is a significant contribution to the field.

## Strengths

1. Clear conceptual advance over previous time-frequency modeling approaches
2. Well-justified mathematical formulation with sound theoretical connections
3. Practical multi-resolution capability addressing a real limitation in audio processing
4. Strong experimental validation with meaningful improvements over baselines
5. Excellent writing and organization

## Weaknesses

1. The paper doesn't adequately compare with state-of-the-art deep learning approaches for audio processing, which have become dominant in recent years. This is a notable omission given the field's current trajectory.

2. The computational complexity discussion for the MMLE approach could be more detailed. While the authors acknowledge scaling issues, they don't explain why the matrix inversion in MMLE is problematic for large audio applications, nor do they suggest potential solutions.

3. The experimental section is somewhat brief and could include more detailed analysis of the decomposition results (e.g., visualizations of the reconstructed signals, more discussion of component identification).

## Overall Recommendation

This is a high-quality paper that makes a meaningful contribution to the field of time-frequency analysis and audio processing. The LRTFS model is conceptually novel, technically sound, and demonstrates practical advantages over existing approaches. The paper is well-written, clearly organized, and provides a solid foundation for future work in the area.

The main weaknesses (lack of comparison with deep learning approaches and limited computational complexity analysis) are important but not severe enough to outweigh the paper's significant contributions. The authors have provided a clear path for future work, including scalable algorithms for MMLE and extensions to multi-channel audio.

I believe this paper makes a strong contribution to the field and would be of interest to the NeurIPS community, particularly those working in audio processing, signal modeling, and probabilistic machine learning. The paper advances the state of the art in time-frequency representations and offers a more complete generative model for audio signals.

**Recommendation: Accept**
