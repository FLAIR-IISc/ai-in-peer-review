## Peer Review: Low-Rank Time-Frequency Synthesis

**Summary:**

This paper introduces a novel generative model, Low-Rank Time-Frequency Synthesis (LRTFS), for signal representation, specifically building upon the Gaussian Composite Model (GCM) by directly modeling the raw signal rather than its STFT. LRTFS represents a signal as a sum of weighted atoms from a time-frequency basis, with the synthesis coefficients having a low-rank structure enforced via a probabilistic model. The authors present maximum likelihood estimation approaches (MJLE and MMLE) using Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithms, and demonstrate the potential of LRTFS for multi-resolution representation. The paper concludes with experimental validation on music decomposition and speech enhancement tasks.

**Relation to Previous Work:**

The work builds directly upon Non-negative Matrix Factorization (NMF) and its probabilistic extension, the GCM [3]. It acknowledges the limitations of traditional NMF regarding phase information and generative modeling.  The authors position LRTFS as addressing these limitations by providing a complete generative model of the signal itself.  The connection to Sparse Bayesian Learning (SBL) [4, 5] is explicitly drawn, highlighting the differences in variance modeling that lead to distinct properties. The use of a multi-resolution approach draws inspiration from related work in audio processing [8, 9].

**Strengths:**

* **Generative Model:** The primary strength is the introduction of a true generative model for the raw signal, going beyond STFT-based decompositions. This offers potential benefits for synthesis tasks and a more complete signal representation.
* **Multi-Resolution Capability:**  The framework naturally supports multi-resolution analysis, a significant advantage over traditional NMF, as demonstrated by the hybrid decomposition example. This adaptability to different signal characteristics is highly valuable.
* **Theoretical Foundation:** The derivation of the LRTFS model and the EM algorithms are well-presented, with clear mathematical formulations. The connection to SBL provides a solid theoretical grounding.
* **Practical Algorithm:** The MJLE algorithm is computationally efficient, avoiding expensive matrix inversions, making it feasible for real-world applications.
* **Empirical Validation:** The experiments on music decomposition and speech enhancement demonstrate the effectiveness of the approach, with competitive performance compared to established methods like IS-NMF and OMLSA.
* **Clear Writing:** The paper is generally well-written and organized, making the core concepts accessible.

**Weaknesses:**

* **MMLE Scalability:**  The authors acknowledge that the MMLE implementation doesn’t scale to practical signal lengths, limiting its applicability. This is a significant drawback, as MMLE is often preferred for its robustness.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The experiments indicate sensitivity to the choice of λ. While the authors mention a warm-restart strategy, a more systematic hyperparameter optimization approach would strengthen the results.
* **Limited Comparative Analysis:**  While performance is compared to IS-NMF and OMLSA, a more comprehensive evaluation against state-of-the-art methods in each task (music decomposition and speech enhancement) would be beneficial.
* **Lack of Ablation Study:** A clear ablation study to demonstrate the contribution of each component of the LRTFS model (e.g., low-rank constraint, multi-resolution) would be valuable.
* **Simplified Residual Model:** The assumption of a white Gaussian residual (Eq. 4) might be limiting for complex signals. Investigating more sophisticated residual models could improve performance.
* **Limited Novelty in Algorithm:** While the model is novel, the EM algorithm is a standard application of existing techniques [6]. The core algorithmic contribution lies in applying these techniques to the specific LRTFS framework.




**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound generative model for signal representation.
* The multi-resolution capability is a significant advancement over existing methods.
* The MJLE algorithm is computationally efficient and practically applicable.
* The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the approach.
* The connection to SBL provides a strong theoretical foundation.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The MMLE algorithm is not scalable.
* The experiments lack a comprehensive comparative analysis and ablation study.
* The sensitivity to the hyperparameter λ could be a practical limitation.




**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of signal processing and machine learning. The introduction of LRTFS offers a novel and theoretically grounded approach to signal representation, with the potential for significant impact in various applications. While the scalability of MMLE and the lack of a comprehensive experimental evaluation are weaknesses, the strengths of the paper outweigh these concerns. 

**Recommendation:**

**Accept with Minor Revisions.** 

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions. The authors should:

1. **Expand the experimental evaluation:** Include a more comprehensive comparison to state-of-the-art methods in music decomposition and speech enhancement.
2. **Perform an ablation study:**  Investigate the contribution of each component of the LRTFS model.
3. **Provide a more detailed discussion of hyperparameter selection:**  Explain the rationale behind the chosen λ values and explore systematic optimization strategies.
4. **Consider exploring more sophisticated residual models.**
