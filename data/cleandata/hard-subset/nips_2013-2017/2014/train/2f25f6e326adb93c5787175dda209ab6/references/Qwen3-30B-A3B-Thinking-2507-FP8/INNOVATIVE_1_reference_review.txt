

# Review of "Low-Rank Time-Frequency Synthesis"

This paper introduces a novel generative model called Low-Rank Time-Frequency Synthesis (LRTFS) that bridges the gap between traditional nonnegative matrix factorization (NMF) approaches and time-frequency signal synthesis. The authors correctly identify a key limitation of existing methods: while NMF and the Gaussian Composite Model (GCM) operate on time-frequency representations (STFT), they don't model the raw signal directly. The LRTFS model addresses this by defining a generative process for the raw signal x(t) with low-rank time-frequency structure, where synthesis coefficients αfn follow a complex Gaussian distribution with a low-rank variance structure [WH]fn. This represents a significant conceptual shift from previous work, as it enables modeling at the raw signal level rather than the transformed domain.

The paper's most compelling contribution is its natural extension to multi-resolution representations. Traditional NMF approaches operate on a fixed time-frequency grid, but LRTFS allows combining different time-frequency bases (e.g., short-window for transients and long-window for tonal components) within the same framework. This capability is demonstrated effectively in the music decomposition and speech enhancement experiments.

The technical development is sound. The authors derive two estimation approaches (maximum joint likelihood estimation and maximum marginal likelihood estimation) with corresponding EM algorithms. The MJLE approach is computationally efficient (no matrix inversions required), making it practical for audio applications. The connection to sparse Bayesian learning is insightful, particularly the observation that LRTFS imposes structure on the variance parameters that differs from standard SBL.

The experiments demonstrate practical utility. The music decomposition example successfully separates tonal and transient components, while the speech enhancement results (6.6 dB average SNR improvement) outperform both IS-NMF (6 dB) and OMLSA (4.6 dB). The multi-resolution capability is particularly valuable for audio applications where different signal components require different time-frequency resolutions.

**Strengths:**
1. **Novel Core Concept:** Direct modeling of raw signals with low-rank time-frequency structure represents a genuine conceptual advance over existing NMF and GCM approaches.
2. **Multi-resolution Capability:** The natural extension to multi-resolution representations is a significant practical advantage not possible with traditional NMF.
3. **Theoretical Soundness:** The probabilistic model and EM algorithms are well-derived and theoretically grounded.
4. **Practical Relevance:** The approach demonstrates clear benefits in audio applications with meaningful performance improvements.

**Weaknesses:**
1. **Limited Baseline Comparison:** The speech enhancement comparison includes only two baselines (IS-NMF and OMLSA), which doesn't fully establish the significance of the improvement.
2. **MMLE Scalability Issue:** While the authors acknowledge that MMLE is not scalable for audio applications, they don't provide a clear justification for why MJLE is preferred over MMLE in practice.
3. **Marginal Performance Gains:** The 0.6 dB improvement over IS-NMF in speech enhancement, while positive, may be considered marginal by some practitioners.
4. **NIPS Relevance:** The paper is primarily a signal processing contribution; while it has ML connections, the review committee may question its fit with NIPS' typical scope.

**Pro and Con Acceptance Arguments:**

**Pro:**
- Introduces a genuinely novel modeling approach that addresses a clear gap in the literature
- Enables multi-resolution representations, a significant practical advantage
- Well-written theoretical development with sound algorithms
- Demonstrates meaningful improvements in audio applications

**Con:**
- Limited experimental comparison to establish significance of results
- Marginal performance gains in speech enhancement (0.6 dB over IS-NMF)
- Primarily a signal processing contribution with limited ML novelty
- MMLE scalability issue not fully addressed

**Recommendation:**

This paper presents a conceptually novel approach that significantly advances the modeling of time-frequency representations. While the performance gains in the experiments are moderate, the core idea of directly modeling raw signals with low-rank time-frequency structure represents a meaningful step forward. The multi-resolution capability is particularly compelling and opens new possibilities for audio signal processing.

As a reviewer who values novelty over methodological perfection, I believe this paper's conceptual contribution outweighs its minor weaknesses. The authors have made a clear, significant advance in the field that should interest the NIPS community. The paper would be strengthened by more extensive comparisons to establish the significance of the results, but the core contribution is sufficiently novel to warrant acceptance.

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions (primarily to better contextualize the significance of the results within the broader ML community). The paper's conceptual novelty and practical utility in audio processing make it a valuable contribution to the field.
