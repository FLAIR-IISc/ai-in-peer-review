

# Review of "Low-Rank Time-Frequency Synthesis"

**Summary**: The paper introduces Low-Rank Time-Frequency Synthesis (LRTFS), a novel generative model that directly models raw signals while maintaining low-rank structure in the time-frequency domain. The authors build upon the Gaussian Composite Model (GCM) from [3] by extending it from modeling STFT coefficients to modeling the raw signal itself. The key innovation is the ability to represent signals as a sum of layers with different time resolutions (multi-resolution representation), which is not possible in traditional NMF approaches. The paper presents two estimation methods (MJLE and MMLE) and demonstrates the model's effectiveness on music decomposition and speech enhancement tasks.

**Strengths**:
1. **Significant contribution**: The paper fills an important gap by bridging time-frequency synthesis with NMF approaches. The multi-resolution capability is particularly valuable, as it enables more flexible signal representations that were not possible in prior work.
2. **Technical soundness**: The mathematical derivations are well-structured and clearly explained, with appropriate connections to related work (GCM, sparse Bayesian learning).
3. **Practical relevance**: The experimental results on music decomposition (identifying tonal/transient components) and speech enhancement (6.6 dB SNR improvement) demonstrate clear practical utility.
4. **Clarity**: The paper is well-organized and clearly written, making complex concepts accessible to readers familiar with signal processing.

**Weaknesses**:
1. **Theoretical limitations**: The paper lacks thorough theoretical analysis of the convergence properties of the proposed EM algorithms. While the authors mention that the algorithms "may only ensure convergence to a local solution," they don't provide guarantees or discuss how to mitigate this limitation.
2. **Limited experimental comparison**: The speech enhancement experiments compare only three methods (LRTFS, IS-NMF, and OMLSA), which is insufficient to establish the model's relative advantages. A comparison with recent deep learning approaches would strengthen the claims.
3. **Narrow scope**: The paper focuses exclusively on audio applications without clearly articulating how the work contributes to broader machine learning themes beyond signal processing. The connection to core NIPS themes needs strengthening.
4. **Practical limitations**: The paper acknowledges that MMLE is computationally infeasible for large-scale audio applications but doesn't provide sufficient discussion of how this limitation affects the practical adoption of the method.

**Arguments for acceptance**: The paper presents a novel framework with significant practical value for audio processing, particularly through its multi-resolution capabilities. The technical contributions are sound, and the experimental results demonstrate clear improvements over baseline methods.

**Arguments against acceptance**: The theoretical contributions are limited compared to the technical novelty. The experimental validation is somewhat narrow, and the paper doesn't sufficiently address how this work advances the broader field of machine learning beyond audio processing.

**Overall assessment**: This is a strong technical contribution with clear practical applications in audio processing. The multi-resolution capability is a particularly compelling aspect that advances the state of the art. However, the paper would benefit from stronger theoretical analysis, more comprehensive experimental validation, and clearer articulation of broader significance.

**Recommendation**: Accept with minor revisions.

**Suggestions for improvement**:
1. Provide theoretical analysis of the convergence properties of the proposed EM algorithms, possibly with convergence guarantees or discussion of practical strategies to mitigate local minima.
2. Expand the experimental section to include more baseline methods, particularly recent deep learning approaches for audio processing, to better establish the model's relative advantages.
3. Include additional visualizations (e.g., spectrograms showing multi-resolution decomposition) to better illustrate the model's capabilities.
4. Clarify the broader significance of the work by discussing potential applications beyond audio processing (e.g., in other signal processing domains or machine learning tasks).
5. Address the computational limitations of MMLE more thoroughly, including potential strategies for scaling the approach to larger datasets.

The paper makes a valuable contribution to the field of time-frequency analysis and audio processing. With the suggested revisions, it would make an excellent contribution to NIPS.
