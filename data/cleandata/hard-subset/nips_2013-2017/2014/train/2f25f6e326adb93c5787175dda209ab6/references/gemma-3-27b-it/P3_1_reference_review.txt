## Review of "Low-Rank Time-Frequency Synthesis"

**Overall Assessment:** This paper presents a novel and technically sound generative model for signal synthesis with a low-rank time-frequency structure. It builds logically upon prior work in NMF and the Gaussian Composite Model (GCM), addressing limitations of both. The model is well-motivated, and the proposed estimation methods (MJLE and MMLE) are clearly described. The experimental results, while limited in scope, demonstrate promising performance in both music decomposition and speech enhancement. However, the scalability of the MMLE approach remains a significant concern, and the experiments could benefit from more comprehensive evaluation. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.

**Summary:**

The authors introduce Low-Rank Time-Frequency Synthesis (LRTFS), a generative model that directly models the raw signal x(t) as a sum of complex-valued time-frequency atoms, with a low-rank constraint imposed on the variance of the synthesis coefficients. This contrasts with the GCM, which imposes a low-rank constraint on the *analysis* coefficients (STFT).  The paper details the LRTFS model, proposes two estimation approaches – Maximum Joint Likelihood Estimation (MJLE) and Maximum Marginal Likelihood Estimation (MMLE) – utilizing EM algorithms, and demonstrates the potential for multi-resolution representations.  Experiments on music decomposition and speech enhancement showcase the approach’s effectiveness.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty & Originality:** The LRTFS model represents a significant step forward in time-frequency decomposition. Directly modeling the raw signal, rather than its STFT, and incorporating a low-rank structure on the synthesis coefficients is a clever and innovative idea. The extension to multi-resolution analysis is a particularly strong contribution.
* **Technical Soundness:** The derivation of the LRTFS model and the proposed EM algorithms for MJLE and MMLE are well-explained and appear theoretically sound.  The connection to sparse Bayesian learning is clearly articulated, highlighting the differences and rationale behind the proposed approach.
* **Clarity & Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The mathematical notation is clear, and the explanations are concise and informative. The connection to existing work (NMF, GCM, SBL) is well-established.
* **Potential Significance:** The ability to model signals at the lowest level (raw signal) and incorporate multi-resolution representations has significant potential for a wide range of applications in audio processing, signal processing, and potentially beyond.  The demonstrated improvements in speech enhancement are encouraging.
* **Practical Algorithm:** The MJLE algorithm is computationally efficient and practical, as it avoids complex matrix inversions.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Scalability of MMLE:**  The authors acknowledge that the MMLE approach doesn’t scale well due to the matrix inversion required. While presented for completeness, its limited scalability diminishes its practical impact. A more detailed discussion of potential approaches to address this (e.g., using approximations or stochastic methods) would be beneficial.
* **Limited Experimental Evaluation:** The experiments, while demonstrating promising results, are somewhat limited in scope. The comparison with baseline methods (IS-NMF and OMLSA) is reasonable but could be more extensive.  A more thorough evaluation using standardized datasets and metrics would strengthen the claims.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The performance is reported to be sensitive to the choice of the hyperparameter λ.  A more systematic investigation of the impact of λ and strategies for setting it automatically would be valuable.  The authors mention a gradual decrease of λ, but the reasoning behind this approach is not fully explained.
* **Lack of Theoretical Analysis of Multi-Resolution:** While the concept of multi-resolution LRTFS is exciting, the theoretical justification for its advantages isn't thoroughly explored. Providing some analysis of the benefits of combining different time-frequency resolutions would enhance the contribution.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a genuinely novel and technically sound contribution to the field of time-frequency decomposition.
* The proposed LRTFS model addresses important limitations of existing methods (NMF and GCM).
* The MJLE algorithm is practical and efficient.
* The multi-resolution extension offers significant potential for real-world applications.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The scalability of the MMLE algorithm is a significant limitation.
* The experimental evaluation could be more comprehensive.
* The hyperparameter sensitivity needs further investigation.




**Detailed Comments:**

* **Page 3, Equation (1):** Clarify that the complex normal distribution is centered.
* **Page 4, Section 2.2:** The discussion of the relationship to sparse Bayesian learning is excellent.
* **Page 6, Section 3.1:** Explain the motivation for using the slightly different update rule for λ than what would directly result from the EM procedure.
* **Page 7, Section 3.2:** The authors rightly point out the computational bottleneck. Perhaps a brief discussion of existing techniques for approximate matrix inversion could be added.
* **Page 9, Section 5.1:** Provide more details on the selection of K (number of latent components) and the window sizes used for the Gabor frames.
* **Page 10, Section 5.2:** A more detailed explanation of the warm-restart strategy for λ would be helpful.
* **Figures:** The figures are clear and informative.  Consider adding error bars to the SNR improvement results in the speech enhancement experiment.



**Overall Recommendation:**

Accept with minor revisions. The authors should address the concerns regarding the scalability of MMLE, expand the experimental evaluation, and provide a more thorough investigation of the hyperparameter sensitivity.  Addressing these points will further strengthen this already promising contribution.
