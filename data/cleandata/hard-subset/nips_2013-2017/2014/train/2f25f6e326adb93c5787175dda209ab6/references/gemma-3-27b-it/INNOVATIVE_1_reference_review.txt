## Review of Low-Rank Time-Frequency Synthesis

This paper presents a novel generative model for signals, dubbed Low-Rank Time-Frequency Synthesis (LRTFS), extending the Gaussian Composite Model (GCM) to directly model the raw signal rather than its STFT. The core idea—imposing a low-rank structure on the *variance* of synthesis coefficients—is a compelling and elegant extension of existing techniques like NMF and GCM. The authors correctly identify the limitations of existing methods regarding phase information and generative modeling, and position LRTFS as a significant step towards addressing these. The potential for multi-resolution representation, naturally arising from the synthesis approach, is particularly exciting.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The core idea of directly modeling the signal with low-rank time-frequency structure is genuinely novel. While building upon NMF and GCM, it represents a significant conceptual leap. The ability to easily incorporate multi-resolution representations, a direct consequence of the synthesis approach, is a substantial advantage over existing methods.
* **Theoretical Soundness:** The derivation linking LRTFS to sparse Bayesian learning, while not fully exploited in the implementation (MMLE is computationally prohibitive), provides a valuable theoretical underpinning and highlights the relationship to established techniques. The EM algorithms are clearly described.
* **Potential Significance:** If scalable, this model could have broad implications for audio processing tasks like music decomposition, source separation, and speech enhancement. The multi-resolution capability is particularly promising for handling non-stationary signals.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and organized, providing a clear explanation of the model and its derivation.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Computational Scalability:** The MMLE approach, though theoretically interesting, is computationally intractable for practical audio signal lengths. The reliance on MJLE and the lack of a scalable MMLE implementation is a significant limitation. The authors acknowledge this, but it weakens the overall contribution.
* **Limited Validation:** While experiments on music decomposition and speech enhancement demonstrate *some* benefit, the comparisons are relatively limited. The SNR improvements, while positive, are modest (particularly in the speech enhancement experiment – only 0.6dB improvement over IS-NMF). A more thorough evaluation against state-of-the-art methods and a wider range of datasets would strengthen the claims.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper mentions sensitivity to the hyperparameter λ, and notes that careful tuning is required. This suggests the method might be difficult to apply in practice without significant experimentation. A more robust analysis of hyperparameter selection or adaptation would be beneficial.
* **Lack of theoretical justification for λ choice:** The choice of λ feels somewhat arbitrary and the lack of a clear theoretical reason behind the adopted procedure feels a bit unsatisfactory.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The core idea is genuinely novel and presents a significant shift in perspective within the time-frequency decomposition field.
* The theoretical connections to sparse Bayesian learning are insightful.
* The multi-resolution capability is a substantial advantage.
* The preliminary experimental results, despite being modest, are encouraging.
* The paper is well-written and clearly presents a complex idea.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

* The computational limitations of the MMLE approach are significant.
* The experimental validation is limited and the SNR improvements are relatively small.
* The method appears sensitive to hyperparameter tuning.



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite the limitations, I believe this paper represents a significant contribution to the field. The novelty of the LRTFS model and its potential for multi-resolution representation are compelling. While the computational challenges and limited validation are concerns, I am willing to overlook them in light of the innovative nature of the work. This paper introduces a genuinely new concept and a promising direction for future research. I lean towards acceptance. 

**Recommendation:** Accept (with encouragement for future work addressing the scalability and validation concerns)

**Rating:** 7.5/10
