Summary
This paper proposes a generative time-domain model (“Low-Rank Time-Frequency Synthesis,” LRTFS) that ties the variances of complex-valued synthesis coefficients α over a time-frequency dictionary Φ via a low-rank structure v = WH. The model is complementary to the Gaussian Composite Model (GCM) where the low-rank structure is imposed on the analysis (STFT) coefficients. Two estimation strategies are presented: (i) maximum joint likelihood (MJLE) via an EM scheme that alternates a Wiener-like shrinkage update for α and IS-NMF updates for W,H, and (ii) maximum marginal likelihood (MMLE), akin to SBL, which is acknowledged to be computationally impractical at audio scale. The framework naturally extends to multi-resolution modeling by stacking multiple time-frequency dictionaries (e.g., short and long windows). Experiments on a toy hybrid decomposition and on semi-supervised speech enhancement show modest SNR gains over an IS-NMF baseline and OMLSA.

Relation to prior work
- The work is closely related to IS-NMF and probabilistic models of spectrograms (e.g., Fevotte et al., IS-NMF/GCM). The novelty is to impose the low-rank variance on synthesis coefficients rather than on analysis coefficients.
- The MMLE connection to SBL is clear; tying variances via v = WH relates to structured/blocked SBL variants, though this connection is not fully explored.
- Multi-resolution modeling via multiple dictionaries builds on ideas from MCA and multi-resolution sparse coding; similar multi-window extensions of NMF and complex-valued factorization in TF domains have also been explored in the audio literature, which could be discussed and compared.
- The paper correctly notes the link between IS-NMF and Wiener filtering and positions the proposed EM as a unifying iteration.

Quality
- The MJLE derivation is plausible; the presence of the log(|α|^2) term follows from the joint likelihood decomposition via DIS(|α|^2|v). The EM architecture using an auxiliary z (Figueiredo & Nowak) is standard and avoids costly inversions.
- However, theoretical guarantees are limited: no convergence proof beyond monotone decrease is provided; the update for λ is approximate; and stability/sensitivity to β (with the constraint β ≤ λ/δΦ) is not examined. In particular, the speech experiment anneals λ to “negligible,” potentially violating the β constraint or forcing β to be extremely small, which may degrade the E-step—this interaction is not analyzed.
- The MMLE method is largely a sketch; given that scalability is left to future work, its inclusion is informative but not experimentally substantiated.
- Component reconstruction is done via a heuristic conditioning on α̂ (Eq. 9), not the full MMSE; the induced bias vs. MMSE is not quantified.
- Experimental validation is limited: the hybrid demo tunes λ to maximize test SNR (risk of overfitting) and lacks baselines (e.g., MCA with pursuit, multi-window NMF, complex NMF). The speech enhancement task uses only 10 mixtures with a single noise type; improvements over IS-NMF are modest (6.6 vs. 6.0 dB), with no variance/error bars or perceptual metrics (STOI/PESQ). No ablations (synthesis vs. analysis modeling, multi-resolution vs. single, λ schedules) or runtime comparisons are provided.

Clarity
- The paper is generally well written and the main ideas are understandable. The matrix notation and the link to IS-NMF are explained clearly.
- Some important details are omitted or could be clarified: how β is chosen in practice, the effect of frame redundancy on δΦ, initialization, stopping criteria, handling of near-zero α in the log(|α|^2) term, and how real signals exploit Hermitian symmetry in implementation. The MMLE section defers key derivations and does not provide any small-scale experiment to validate correctness.
- The multi-resolution section is clear but light on practical guidance (e.g., how to select windows, K per layer, and whether joint normalization is needed).

Originality
- Modeling the low-rank structure directly in synthesis coefficients and casting estimation as an alternation between Wiener-like shrinkage and IS-NMF is a neat twist on established probabilistic NMF. The multi-resolution extension is natural but not in itself novel.
- The connection to SBL with tied variances (low-rank structure) is interesting; however, structured SBL/group-sparsity analogs exist, and the work would benefit from a more precise positioning relative to block/structured SBL.

Significance
- The conceptual bridge from analysis to synthesis modeling could be impactful if it yields clear empirical advantages or new capabilities. Currently, the empirical gains are small and the evaluation narrow. Without stronger and broader evidence, it is hard to assess significance for the NeurIPS community.
- Practical adoption is further limited by the reliance on manual/annealed λ and the lack of computational benchmarks and ablations.

Pros
- Clear probabilistic formulation that unifies IS-NMF and Wiener-like shrinkage in a time-domain generative model.
- Efficient MJLE algorithm that avoids large matrix inversions and extends readily to multi-resolution.
- Conceptually appealing link to SBL and potential for broader inverse problems.

Cons
- Limited and weak empirical validation: small gains over strong baselines, narrow datasets, no perceptual metrics, no ablations, λ tuned on test.
- MMLE is presented but not scaled or demonstrated; convergence and parameter-sensitivity analyses are missing.
- Missing comparisons to closely related established methods (multi-window NMF, complex NMF, MCA), and no runtime/complexity measurements.
- Practical choices (β, λ schedule, redundancy) and stability are under-discussed.

Questions for authors
1. How is β selected in practice, and how do you ensure β ≤ λ/δΦ during λ annealing? What is δΦ for your frames?
2. Can you provide runtime per iteration and compare to IS-NMF on the same hardware/dictionaries?
3. How sensitive are results to K, initialization, and the λ schedule? Any automatic selection?
4. Can you report STOI/PESQ and standard deviations across runs for the speech task, and include more noise types?
5. Can you add baselines: multi-window IS-NMF (single analysis-domain factorization over concatenated STFTs), complex NMF, and MCA-style sparse coding for the hybrid demo?

Recommendation
At present, I am not convinced the paper meets the NeurIPS bar in terms of empirical rigor and demonstrated significance. The methodological contribution is incremental on established probabilistic NMF/SBL ideas and is not sufficiently validated. I recommend rejection in its current form, with the hope that a strengthened experimental evaluation, additional baselines, and analysis of parameter sensitivity and convergence would make a stronger case.
