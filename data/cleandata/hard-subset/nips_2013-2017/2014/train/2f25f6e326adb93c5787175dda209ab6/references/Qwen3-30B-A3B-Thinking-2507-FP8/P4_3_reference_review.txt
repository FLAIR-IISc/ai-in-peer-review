

# Review of "Low-Rank Time-Frequency Synthesis"

This paper introduces Low-Rank Time-Frequency Synthesis (LRTFS), a novel generative model that extends the Gaussian Composite Model (GCM) [3] by modeling raw signals directly rather than their STFT representations. While GCM models the STFT as low-rank, LRTFS synthesizes the original signal from low-rank time-frequency components, providing a more direct generative approach. The work builds upon nonnegative matrix factorization (NMF) [1, 2], Gaussian Composite Models [3], and sparse Bayesian learning [4, 5], while addressing key limitations of previous approaches that either modeled magnitude spectrograms or required phase recovery.

The paper's core contributions include: (1) a generative model for raw signal synthesis with low-rank time-frequency structure; (2) two maximum likelihood estimation approaches (MJLE and MMLE) with corresponding EM algorithms; and (3) a multi-resolution extension that naturally accommodates different time-frequency resolutions for different signal components. The multi-resolution capability is particularly valuable for audio applications where transients (short duration) and tonal components (longer duration) require different resolutions.

**Quality:** The paper demonstrates strong technical soundness. The mathematical derivations are clear and well-justified, with proper attention to the probabilistic foundations of the model. The MJLE approach is presented with a computationally efficient EM algorithm that avoids matrix inversions, making it practical for audio applications. The authors appropriately acknowledge the computational limitations of MMLE (requiring inversion of an M×M matrix, where M is typically larger than signal length T) and focus their experiments on MJLE, which is more scalable. The experimental validation on music decomposition and speech enhancement is thorough, with appropriate baselines (IS-NMF, OMLSA) and meaningful metrics (SNR improvement). The paper carefully addresses the strengths and limitations of their approach, including the computational constraints of MMLE.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-organized and clearly written. The generative model is introduced logically, followed by estimation methods and experimental validation. The connection to sparse Bayesian learning is well-explained, though it could have been more thoroughly developed. The mathematical notation is consistent and well-defined, with sufficient detail for an expert reader to understand and reproduce the methods. The figures illustrating the music decomposition results effectively demonstrate the multi-resolution capability.

**Originality:** The paper makes a clear and significant contribution by modeling raw signals directly with low-rank time-frequency structure, which was not possible with previous approaches. The multi-resolution extension is particularly novel, as it allows for different time-frequency resolutions in different signal components without additional estimation complexity. The paper effectively differentiates its approach from related work: unlike traditional NMF [1, 2] that only models magnitude spectrograms, and unlike GCM [3] that models STFT rather than raw signals, LRTFS provides a direct generative model of the raw signal. The connection to multi-resolution analysis [8, 9] is acknowledged, but the paper's specific contribution to this area is well-defined.

**Significance:** The results demonstrate clear improvements in music decomposition and speech enhancement tasks compared to baselines. The multi-resolution capability provides a practical advantage for audio processing where different signal components require different temporal resolutions. The paper's approach could be applied to other signal processing tasks beyond audio, and the generative model provides a foundation for future work in signal synthesis and source separation. The work advances the state of the art by providing a direct model of the raw signal with low-rank time-frequency structure, which was not possible with previous methods.

**Strengths:**
- Clear motivation for modeling raw signals directly rather than STFT
- Solid theoretical foundation with well-justified EM algorithms
- Novel multi-resolution extension with practical value for audio applications
- Good experimental validation with appropriate baselines
- Strong differentiation from previous work (GCM, traditional NMF, SBL)

**Weaknesses:**
- MMLE approach is presented but not implemented due to computational constraints, with limited discussion of why MMLE is infeasible for audio applications
- Some experimental details are missing (e.g., specific EM algorithm convergence thresholds, training details for speech enhancement)
- The connection to previous multi-resolution analysis work [8, 9] could be more thoroughly discussed
- The relationship to sparse Bayesian learning is mentioned but not deeply explored

**Pro/Con Acceptance:**

Pro:
- Novel approach to modeling raw signals with low-rank time-frequency structure
- Strong theoretical foundation with well-justified algorithms
- Clear demonstration of multi-resolution capability
- Good experimental results showing improvements over baselines
- Well-written and well-organized paper

Con:
- MMLE approach is not implemented due to computational constraints, limiting completeness of the estimation framework
- Some experimental details are missing, potentially affecting reproducibility
- Connection to prior multi-resolution work could be more thoroughly discussed

Overall, this paper presents a technically sound, novel, and significant contribution to the field of signal processing and machine learning. The LRTFS model fills an important gap in the literature by providing a direct generative model of raw signals with low-rank time-frequency structure, while the multi-resolution extension offers practical advantages for audio applications. The paper is well-written, clearly differentiates itself from previous work, and provides strong experimental validation. The minor weaknesses noted do not detract significantly from the paper's overall contribution, and the work represents a meaningful advancement in the field.
