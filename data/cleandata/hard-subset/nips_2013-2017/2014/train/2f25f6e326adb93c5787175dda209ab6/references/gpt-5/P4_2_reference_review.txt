Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes Low-Rank Time-Frequency Synthesis (LRTFS), a generative model of raw time-domain signals whose synthesis coefficients (in a chosen time-frequency basis, e.g., a Gabor frame) follow zero-mean complex Gaussian distributions with variances constrained to have a low-rank Itakura–Saito NMF structure WH across frequency-time. This contrasts with the Gaussian Composite Model (GCM) [3], which imposes the low-rank variance structure on analysis (STFT) coefficients. The model yields a likelihood comprising a data-fidelity term ‖x − Φα‖2 and an IS-NMF divergence between |α|2 and WH, plus a log-penalty on |α|2 arising from the joint likelihood.

Two estimation strategies are developed. (i) Maximum Joint Likelihood Estimation (MJLE) uses an EM architecture inspired by Figueiredo–Nowak [6], introducing an auxiliary z so that the E-step becomes a simple analysis/synthesis update and the M-step alternates Wiener-type shrinkage for α with IS-NMF multiplicative updates for W,H. This avoids large matrix inversions and scales to audio lengths. (ii) Maximum Marginal Likelihood Estimation (MMLE), akin to type-II ML in sparse Bayesian learning (SBL) [4,5], marginalizes α and performs EM with α as hidden data. It requires inverting large matrices and is deemed impractical for typical audio sizes; scalable MMLE is left for future work.

The framework naturally accommodates multi-resolution modeling by stacking multiple frames Φℓ with different time supports (e.g., short/long Hann windows), each with its own low-rank variance WH. This yields a hybrid decomposition similar in spirit to prior morphological audio models [8,9] but with low-rank Gaussian structure instead of sparsity.

Experiments include: (1) a hybrid decomposition of a 6 s jazz excerpt into transient and tonal layers (K=3 each), showing interpretable components and denoising gains; and (2) semi-supervised speech enhancement with two frames and pre-trained speech dictionaries (K=500) vs small learned noise dictionaries, reporting average SNR improvement of 6.6 dB across 10 mixtures, compared to 6.0 dB with IS-NMF and 4.6 dB with OMLSA [14].

Strengths
- Conceptual contribution: shifts low-rank modeling from analysis to synthesis coefficients, yielding a generative model of the waveform rather than the STFT. This bridges synthesis frameworks common in sparse coding with NMF-style variance modeling [1,3].
- Algorithmic practicality: the MJLE EM scheme is simple, uses only Φ/Φ∗ operations and standard IS-NMF updates, and is compatible with efficient toolboxes [7]. No large inversions are needed.
- Multi-resolution extension: stacking frames with different time supports is natural in this synthesis setting and operationally straightforward, addressing a known limitation of STFT-based NMF where the resolution is fixed [8,9].
- Empirical indications: on speech enhancement, LRTFS outperforms a comparable IS-NMF baseline and a classical enhancement method (OMLSA) by a modest margin; qualitative hybrid decomposition results are plausible.

Weaknesses
- Limited empirical validation: experiments are small-scale (one music clip; 10 speech mixtures). There is no ablation to isolate the benefits of synthesis-domain modeling vs analysis-domain NMF, nor controlled evaluation of the multi-resolution advantage.
- Hyperparameter handling: λ is treated as a tuned hyperparameter and even annealed to a negligible value in speech experiments, deviating from the probabilistic model and potentially biasing comparisons. The paper notes instability when estimating λ jointly but does not provide a principled remedy (e.g., priors or cross-validation).
- Baselines: comparisons are limited. For audio, phase-aware or complex NMF variants and multi-resolution sparse models [8,9] would be informative. The 0.6 dB gain over IS-NMF, while positive, is relatively small given added model complexity.
- Theory and guarantees: no convergence proof beyond EM monotonicity (and even λ update is acknowledged not to be the exact EM step); no analysis of identifiability or conditions under which synthesis vs analysis modeling improves estimation.
- MMLE scalability: while included for completeness, it is not practically usable in presented settings; no approximate or matrix-free strategies (e.g., conjugate gradients, exploitation of frame structure) are attempted.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The MJLE derivation is consistent with the Figueiredo–Nowak split [6] and produces a coherent alternating scheme combining Wiener-like shrinkage and IS-NMF. The appearance of a log(|α|2) term in the joint likelihood is correctly noted; its practical impact (e.g., sparsity) is not analyzed. The MMLE formulation aligns with SBL [4,5] but is computationally heavy.
- Experimental methodology: SNR is a reasonable metric, but more comprehensive evaluation (SDR/SIR/SAR for source separation, PESQ/STOI for speech) and statistical significance would strengthen claims. Tuning λ “to best SNR” risks overfitting; a validation protocol should be described.

Clarity
- The paper is generally well-written and organized. The model and algorithms are clearly specified, with concise matrix notation and implementation notes. Some details are sparse (initialization, β selection, stopping criteria), and references are incompletely formatted in the provided text.

Originality
- Moderate to good. Prior work has modeled STFT magnitudes with NMF [1,3] and synthesis with sparsity [8,9]; tying synthesis-coefficient variances via a low-rank NMF and operating directly on the waveform is a novel combination. The multi-resolution extension is natural but has precedents in sparse/morphological models.

Significance
- Potentially useful for audio representation, separation, and enhancement, especially when multi-resolution structure matters. Demonstrated gains are modest; broader evidence and comparisons would be needed to establish clear state-of-the-art impact.

Questions and suggestions for the authors
- Provide an ablation comparing: (i) LRTFS single-resolution vs multi-resolution; (ii) LRTFS vs GCM/IS-NMF with identical frames and training; (iii) effect of the log(|α|2) term (e.g., remove it to assess contribution).
- Clarify selection of β and its relationship to frame bounds (δΦ). Describe initialization and stopping criteria.
- Address λ estimation more robustly (e.g., Gamma prior; empirical Bayes with safeguards), and adopt a validation split to avoid tuning on test SNR.
- Include comparisons to phase-aware or complex NMF methods, and to multi-resolution sparse coding baselines [8,9].
- Discuss computational complexity and runtime versus IS-NMF, and memory usage.
- Consider approximate MMLE using conjugate gradients with matrix-vector products exploiting Φ/Φ∗ and diagonal structure, or message passing [16].

Arguments for acceptance
- Clear, implementable modeling idea bridging NMF and synthesis; naturally supports multi-resolution.
- Sound EM algorithm with practical scalability.
- Positive empirical trend versus reasonable baselines.

Arguments against acceptance
- Limited experimental scope and modest gains; tuning choices undermine strict generative evaluation.
- Missing stronger baselines and ablations to substantiate the claimed advantages.
- MMLE contribution is not practically realized; theoretical analysis is limited.

Overall, this is a technically sound and clearly presented contribution with an appealing idea and practical algorithm, but the empirical validation is currently too limited to fully support strong claims of significance.
