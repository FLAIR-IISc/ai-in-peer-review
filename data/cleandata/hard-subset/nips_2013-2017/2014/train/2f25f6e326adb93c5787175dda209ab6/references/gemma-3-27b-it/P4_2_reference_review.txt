This paper introduces a novel signal synthesis model, Low-Rank Time-Frequency Synthesis (LRTFS), building upon the Gaussian Composite Model (GCM) [3]. The core idea is to model the raw signal x(t) directly, assuming a low-rank structure in the variance of synthesis coefficients, unlike GCM which imposes low-rank structure on analysis coefficients. The authors present maximum likelihood estimation approaches via Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithms and demonstrate the benefits of this approach in multi-resolution representations, with applications to music decomposition and speech enhancement.

The paper is well-structured and clearly written. The motivation is clearly stated, and the connection to prior work (NMF, GCM, sparse Bayesian learning) is well established. The mathematical derivations are generally easy to follow, with sufficient detail provided to understand the key steps. The organization into sections addressing the model, estimation, multi-resolution extensions, and experiments is logical and effective. The inclusion of references to relevant tools like the LTFAT toolbox is helpful.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The direct synthesis approach with low-rank structure on synthesis coefficients is a novel contribution, addressing a limitation of existing methods like GCM. The extension to multi-resolution representations is also a valuable addition.
*   **Technical Soundness:** The mathematical formulation of the LRTFS model appears sound. The EM algorithms for maximum likelihood estimation are well-described, and the connection to sparse Bayesian learning is insightful. The treatment of the multi-resolution case is also well-defined.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is very well written and easy to understand, even for readers not intimately familiar with all the underlying techniques. The notation is consistent and well-explained.
*   **Experimental Validation:** The experiments on music decomposition and speech enhancement demonstrate the practical utility of the proposed method. The comparison with existing techniques (IS-NMF, OMLSA) provides a good baseline for evaluating performance. The provision of supplementary audio examples is a positive aspect.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Computational Complexity:** The paper acknowledges computational challenges with the MMLE approach, particularly the matrix inversion required for larger dimensions. While MJLE is presented as more tractable, the scalability of even MJLE to very large-scale problems is not thoroughly discussed.
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The experiments show sensitivity to the choice of the regularization parameter λ. The paper mentions a "warm-restart" strategy for λ, but a more systematic exploration of hyperparameter tuning and its impact on performance would strengthen the results.
*   **Limited Scope of Experiments:** While the experiments are illustrative, they are limited to specific audio applications and datasets. Expanding the experimental evaluation to other domains or more diverse datasets would broaden the impact of the work.
*    **Lack of Theoretical Analysis of Convergence:** The paper mentions that the EM algorithm may converge to a local solution, but there is no theoretical analysis of the convergence properties of the proposed algorithms.



**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and technically sound approach to time-frequency synthesis, addressing a gap in the existing literature.
*   The multi-resolution extension is a significant contribution, enabling applications that are not possible with traditional NMF-based methods.
*   The experimental results demonstrate the practical effectiveness of the proposed method in relevant audio applications.
*   The paper is well-written and clearly presented, making it accessible to a broad audience.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The computational complexity of the proposed methods, particularly MMLE, may limit its applicability to large-scale problems.
*   The hyperparameter sensitivity and lack of systematic hyperparameter tuning could be a concern.
*   The experimental evaluation is limited in scope, and further validation on more diverse datasets would be beneficial.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that presents a novel and well-motivated approach to time-frequency synthesis. The technical contributions are significant, and the experimental results are promising. While there are some limitations regarding computational complexity and experimental scope, the strengths of the paper outweigh the weaknesses. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions, specifically requesting a more detailed discussion of scalability and a more thorough exploration of hyperparameter tuning. A theoretical discussion of the convergence properties of the proposed algorithm would also be a valuable addition.
