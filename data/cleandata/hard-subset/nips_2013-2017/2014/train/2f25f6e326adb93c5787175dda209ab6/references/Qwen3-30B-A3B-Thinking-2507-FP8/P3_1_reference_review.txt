

# Peer Review: Low-Rank Time-Frequency Synthesis

This paper introduces Low-Rank Time-Frequency Synthesis (LRTFS), a novel generative model that bridges the gap between time-frequency synthesis and traditional nonnegative matrix factorization (NMF) approaches. The authors extend the Gaussian Composite Model (GCM) [3] to model the raw signal directly rather than its time-frequency transform, while maintaining a low-rank structure in the time-frequency domain. The key innovation lies in the direct modeling of the signal through synthesis coefficients with low-rank variance structure, enabling natural multi-resolution representations that were previously impossible in traditional NMF settings.

## Quality and Methodology

The paper presents a technically sound approach with clear mathematical derivations. The authors derive a Maximum Joint Likelihood Estimation (MJLE) approach with a corresponding EM algorithm, which is computationally feasible for audio applications. The connection to sparse Bayesian learning (SBL) [4,5] is well-established, and the paper correctly identifies that the key difference between LRTFS and SBL is the structured (low-rank) variance parameters rather than unstructured ones. The multi-resolution extension (Section 4) is particularly compelling, as it naturally accommodates different temporal resolutions for different signal components (e.g., transients vs. tonal components in audio).

However, the paper lacks sufficient theoretical analysis of the proposed EM algorithm. While the authors acknowledge that the algorithm may converge to local minima, they don't provide convergence guarantees or discuss sensitivity to initial conditions. The computational complexity analysis is also insufficient—while the authors correctly identify that MMLE is infeasible for large-scale audio applications, they don't provide detailed complexity analysis for MJLE.

## Clarity and Presentation

The paper is well-written and logically organized, with clear mathematical notation and good explanations of the model and algorithms. The relationship to prior work (GCM, SBL) is well-established, and the multi-resolution extension is clearly motivated. The experimental section (Section 5) effectively demonstrates the model's capabilities for music decomposition and speech enhancement.

Minor clarity issues include inconsistent notation for synthesis coefficients (sometimes using α, sometimes α̂) and insufficient explanation of how the synthesis coefficients relate to standard STFT analysis coefficients. The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion of why traditional NMF cannot naturally support multi-resolution representations, as this is a key claim of the paper.

## Originality

The core idea of modeling the raw signal directly with low-rank time-frequency structure is novel and addresses a genuine gap in the literature. The multi-resolution capability is particularly significant, as the paper correctly identifies that traditional NMF approaches work on transformed data with fixed time resolution. While the paper acknowledges prior work on multi-resolution approaches [8,9], it does not sufficiently differentiate its generative synthesis framework from these earlier methods that used sparse synthesis models.

The paper could strengthen its originality claim by more explicitly contrasting with recent deep learning approaches to audio source separation, which have also been applied to multi-resolution modeling. The lack of comparison with state-of-the-art deep learning methods (e.g., Conv-TasNet, DPRNN) is a notable omission in the experimental section.

## Significance

The paper makes a meaningful contribution to audio signal processing, with clear experimental results showing improvement over baselines (6.6 dB vs. 6 dB SNR improvement for speech enhancement). The multi-resolution capability has clear practical value in audio applications where different components require different time resolutions.

However, the significance could be better articulated by discussing how the computational cost of LRTFS compares to other methods, especially deep learning approaches that have become dominant in audio source separation. The paper states that MMLE is computationally prohibitive but doesn't provide similar comparisons for MJLE.

## Strengths and Weaknesses Summary

**Strengths:**
- Clear mathematical formulation of a novel model addressing a genuine gap in the literature
- Well-motivated multi-resolution extension with practical audio applications
- Good experimental results demonstrating clear improvements over baselines
- Strong connection to existing work (GCM, SBL)

**Weaknesses:**
- Limited theoretical analysis of the proposed EM algorithm
- Insufficient comparison with state-of-the-art deep learning methods
- Incomplete discussion of computational complexity
- Limited explanation of why traditional NMF cannot support multi-resolution representations

## Recommendation

This paper presents a technically sound and well-motivated approach that addresses an important gap in the literature. The multi-resolution capability is particularly valuable for audio applications, and the experimental results demonstrate clear improvements over baselines. However, the paper would be significantly strengthened by addressing the theoretical and comparative limitations noted above.

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the following:
1. Provide more detailed comparison with recent deep learning methods for audio source separation
2. Include a more thorough theoretical analysis of the MJLE algorithm
3. Clarify the computational complexity of MJLE and its comparison to other methods
4. Better articulate the limitations of traditional NMF regarding multi-resolution representations

The paper makes a meaningful contribution to the field and would be a valuable addition to NeurIPS, particularly for the signal processing and audio applications communities.
