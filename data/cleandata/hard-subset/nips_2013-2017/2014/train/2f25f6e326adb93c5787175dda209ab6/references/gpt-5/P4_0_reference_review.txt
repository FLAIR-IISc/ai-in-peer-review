Summary
The paper proposes Low-Rank Time-Frequency Synthesis (LRTFS), a generative model of the raw time-domain signal that imposes a low-rank structure on the variance of complex synthesis coefficients defined over a time-frequency (t-f) frame. Concretely, the signal is synthesized as x = Φα + e with αfn ∼ Nc(0, [WH]fn) and e ∼ Nc(0, λ), where Φ is a (possibly redundant) t-f dictionary, and W,H are nonnegative factors whose product WH gives the per-coefficient variances. This contrasts with prior models (e.g., the Gaussian Composite Model, GCM) that place the low-rank structure on the variance of analysis (STFT) coefficients. The authors develop two estimation strategies:
- Maximum joint likelihood estimation (MJLE): an EM algorithm using an auxiliary variable z (Figueiredo–Nowak style splitting), alternating a Wiener-like shrinkage update for α and IS-NMF multiplicative updates for W,H. A simple residual-variance update for λ is also given.
- Maximum marginal likelihood estimation (MMLE): a type-II ML EM that integrates out α (akin to Sparse Bayesian Learning, SBL), updating the posterior mean/covariance of α, then W,H via IS-NMF on the expected squared coefficients v. This is noted to be computationally prohibitive for typical audio sizes.

The framework naturally extends to multi-resolution by summing multiple frames (e.g., short and long windows), each with its own low-rank variance model. Experiments include: (i) a music decomposition into tonal/transient layers (illustrative), and (ii) semi-supervised speech enhancement versus applause using pre-trained speech dictionaries and small noise dictionaries, with average SNR improvements of 6.6 dB (LRTFS), 6.0 dB (IS-NMF baseline), and 4.6 dB (OMLSA).

Relation to prior work
- NMF with IS divergence on spectrograms and Wiener filtering for component reconstruction [1,3] are closely related; LRTFS generalizes these ideas to synthesis modeling in time domain and provides multi-resolution capability.
- The probabilistic GCM [3] models STFT coefficients with variances constrained by WH; LRTFS imposes this structure on synthesis coefficients instead, making the model generative for x(t).
- The MMLE approach and its interpretation relate to SBL/RVM [4,5], but here variances are tied via low rank (WH) rather than independently per coefficient.
- The EM splitting for MJLE builds on Figueiredo–Nowak [6].
- The multi-resolution setup connects to hybrid/morphological decompositions and structured sparse models in audio [8,9,10].

Strengths
- Conceptual novelty: shifting low-rank modeling to synthesis coefficients yields a generative model for the raw signal and enables principled multi-resolution modeling with shared low-rank structure.
- Clear linkage to established frameworks (IS-NMF, GCM, SBL) with well-motivated derivations and interpretable updates (Wiener-like shrinkage; IS-NMF subproblem).
- Practical, scalable MJLE algorithm avoiding large matrix inversions; leverages efficient Φ/Φ* implementations.
- Multi-resolution extension is simple and natural in the synthesis view.
- Empirical results indicate competitive or better performance than IS-NMF and OMLSA on speech enhancement; illustrative music decomposition shows plausible layer separation.

Weaknesses
- Experimental evaluation is limited in scope: small number of test cases, modest improvement over a strong IS-NMF baseline (0.6 dB), and no statistical analysis or ablations (effects of K, β, λ, initializations).
- Comparisons omit closely related probabilistic t-f models that incorporate phase or other structure at the STFT level, and do not compare to multiresolution baselines (e.g., MCA/structured sparse coding).
- The residual variance λ is not estimated in practice (reported to perform poorly), and a warm-restart schedule is used in the second experiment. This raises questions about model calibration and robustness.
- Convergence properties beyond monotonic decrease are not discussed; the MJLE λ update is acknowledged as approximate.
- Some implementation details are missing: initialization schemes, stopping criteria, normalization/scaling to address W/H ambiguities, choice and impact of β, and complexity per iteration.
- MMLE is presented but not made scalable; no empirical comparison between MJLE and MMLE.

Quality
- The modeling assumptions and derivations appear sound. The EM updates are consistent with the references; the IS-NMF subproblem is standard. The auxiliary-variable EM is appropriate and avoids expensive inversions.
- Technical caveat: the approximate λ update and failure to estimate λ effectively suggest either identifiability issues or sensitivity; further analysis would strengthen the work.
- Component reconstruction via αk posterior mean proportional to wfkhkn/[WH]fn is reasonable given α̂, but exact MMSE reconstruction is avoided due to computational burden.

Clarity
- Generally clear and well-organized, with equations and algorithm steps explicit. The relation to prior art is well articulated.
- Minor notational overload (switching between (f,n) and m) and some implementation details are deferred or omitted.

Originality
- The shift to synthesis-domain low-rank variance modeling with multi-resolution capability is a novel and meaningful contribution relative to NMF/GCM and SBL.

Significance
- Potentially impactful for audio representation, separation, and enhancement, especially where multi-resolution modeling is advantageous. Current empirical gains are modest; broader evaluation would better establish significance.

Questions and suggestions
- Provide a more systematic study of λ and β: sensitivity analyses, criteria for selection, and a discussion of why λ estimation degrades performance.
- Include ablations (K, frame choices, redundancy) and report initialization/stopping/normalization details for reproducibility.
- Compare against additional multiresolution or phase-aware baselines; include more datasets/noise types and metrics (e.g., PESQ/STOI for speech).
- Discuss identifiability/scaling ambiguities between W,H, and α; consider constraints or normalizations.
- Explore scalable approximations to MMLE (e.g., conjugate gradients with matrix–vector products, variational/message passing) to validate benefits over MJLE.

Arguments for acceptance
- Clear and principled modeling contribution with novel synthesis-domain perspective.
- Practical algorithm with natural multi-resolution extension.
- Demonstrated applicability to real audio tasks with competitive results.

Arguments against acceptance
- Limited and modest empirical validation; missing comparisons to several relevant baselines.
- Reliance on hyperparameter tuning (λ schedule) and lack of robust λ estimation.
- MMLE method not scalable; no empirical contrast between MJLE and MMLE.

Overall, the paper presents a technically sound and original modeling idea with a practical algorithm and promising results, though the empirical evaluation and analysis could be strengthened.
