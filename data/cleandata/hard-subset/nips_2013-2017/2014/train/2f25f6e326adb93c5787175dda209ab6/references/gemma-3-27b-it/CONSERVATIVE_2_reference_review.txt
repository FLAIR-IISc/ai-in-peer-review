This paper proposes a low-rank time-frequency synthesis (LRTFS) model for signal representation, extending previous work on Gaussian Composite Models (GCMs) by directly modeling the raw signal rather than its STFT. The core idea is to express a signal as a sum of weighted atoms from a time-frequency basis, with the synthesis coefficients having a low-rank structure enforced through a probabilistic model. The authors present two estimation approaches, maximum joint likelihood estimation (MJLE) and maximum marginal likelihood estimation (MMLE), with accompanying EM algorithms. A multi-resolution extension is also introduced. The experiments focus on music decomposition and speech enhancement.

While the idea of directly synthesizing the signal is a natural progression from GCMs, and the multi-resolution aspect is potentially valuable, the paper suffers from a lack of compelling evidence for the advantages of this approach over established methods. My primary concern lies with the limited novelty and thoroughness of the evaluation. The proposed LRTFS builds heavily on existing techniques (NMF, GCM, SBL) but doesn’t demonstrate a substantial improvement in performance or offer a fundamentally new perspective. 

The technical soundness appears reasonable, given the reliance on well-established principles. The derivation of the EM algorithms seems correct, and the connection to sparse Bayesian learning is appropriately noted. However, the lack of detailed analysis regarding the identifiability of the model and the sensitivity to parameter choices (particularly λ) is a weakness. The authors acknowledge that λ was treated as a hyperparameter tuned empirically, which is suboptimal. Moreover, the claim that MMLE doesn’t scale is a significant limitation, given its potential benefits. While they mention complexities associated with matrix inversion, this seems like a solvable problem with appropriate approximations or algorithmic techniques.

Clarity is generally good. The paper is reasonably well-written and organized, with a clear exposition of the LRTFS model and estimation procedures. However, the notation can be dense at times, and a more intuitive explanation of the relationship between the synthesis coefficients and the STFT coefficients would be helpful. The discussion of the EM algorithm could benefit from more details on the computational cost of each step. 

Originality is the weakest aspect of this work. The core idea, while a logical extension of GCM, doesn’t represent a major conceptual breakthrough. The use of low-rank constraints and EM algorithms is standard practice in this field. The connection to sparse Bayesian learning is acknowledged but doesn’t lead to a novel algorithmic approach. The multi-resolution extension is a valuable addition, but it’s not entirely novel—multi-resolution representations have been explored in signal processing for decades. The related work section feels incomplete, particularly regarding recent advances in time-frequency decomposition and source separation.

Significance is questionable. The experimental results, while demonstrating competitive performance on the chosen tasks, do not convincingly establish a significant advantage over existing methods like IS-NMF or OMLSA. The SNR improvements are modest, and the subjective evaluation (mentioned but not presented) is insufficient to support strong claims of superiority. The potential benefits of the multi-resolution approach are not fully realized in the experiments. The application scenarios seem limited, and it's unclear how widely applicable this model will be to other signal processing problems.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a logically consistent extension of existing time-frequency decomposition models.
*   The multi-resolution aspect is a potentially valuable addition.
*   The technical execution is reasonably sound.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Limited novelty and significant overlap with existing work.
*   Lack of compelling experimental results demonstrating substantial improvement over established methods.
*   Insufficient analysis of model identifiability and parameter sensitivity.
*   Unresolved scalability issues with the MMLE approach.
*   Weak justification of the model’s significance and broader applicability.



**Overall Assessment:** Reject. The paper is technically sound and clearly written, but it lacks the novelty and empirical validation required for acceptance at NIPS. The authors have identified an interesting direction for research, but the presented work feels incomplete and doesn’t convincingly demonstrate a significant contribution to the field. More extensive experimentation, a more thorough comparison with state-of-the-art methods, and a more detailed analysis of the model’s properties are needed before this work could be considered for publication.
