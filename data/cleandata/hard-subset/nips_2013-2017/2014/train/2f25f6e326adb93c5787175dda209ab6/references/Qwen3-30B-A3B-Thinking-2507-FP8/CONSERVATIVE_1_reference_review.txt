

# Review of "Low-Rank Time-Frequency Synthesis"

This paper introduces a "Low-Rank Time-Frequency Synthesis" (LRTFS) model that extends previous work on nonnegative matrix factorization (NMF) and the Gaussian Composite Model (GCM) for audio signal processing. The authors propose a generative model that operates directly on the raw signal x(t) rather than its STFT, as in the GCM, and claim their approach naturally accommodates multi-resolution representations. The paper develops two estimation methods: Maximum Joint Likelihood Estimation (MJLE) and Maximum Marginal Likelihood Estimation (MMLE), with the latter being computationally infeasible for large-scale audio applications.

The paper builds on a well-established line of research in audio processing, including the GCM [3] and sparse Bayesian learning (SBL) [4,5]. The connection to SBL is particularly relevant, as the authors note that their model differs from SBL by imposing a low-rank constraint on the variance parameters rather than allowing free variance parameters for each coefficient. The multi-resolution aspect is presented as a key innovation, allowing different time-frequency bases for different signal components.

The paper demonstrates the approach on two audio tasks: music decomposition and speech enhancement. In the music decomposition experiment, the authors decompose a jazz excerpt into tonal and transient components. For speech enhancement, they compare their method against IS-NMF (using a single Gabor transform) and the standard OMLSA method, reporting a modest 6.6 dB SNR improvement compared to 6 dB for IS-NMF.

The mathematical development is technically sound, with proper derivations of the EM algorithms. The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with good connections to previous work. The authors acknowledge limitations of their approach, such as the computational infeasibility of MMLE for large-scale audio applications.

However, I have significant concerns about the paper's contribution and validation. The most troubling aspect is the lack of substantial experimental validation against established baselines. The reported gains (0.6 dB in speech enhancement) are marginal compared to the complexity of the proposed method. The paper doesn't demonstrate why this approach would be preferable to simpler, well-established methods in practical applications. For instance, why would a practitioner choose LRTFS over standard NMF-based approaches when the performance gains are so minimal?

The multi-resolution claim is overstated. Previous work has already explored multi-resolution audio representations using different time-frequency bases [8,9,10], and the paper doesn't clearly articulate how LRTFS differs from these approaches in a meaningful way. The authors mention these papers in their introduction, but fail to demonstrate a clear technical distinction or advantage.

The computational limitations of the MMLE approach are substantial, with the paper explicitly stating it's infeasible for most audio applications due to the need to invert large matrices. The MJLE approach, while computationally feasible, doesn't demonstrate significant advantages over simpler methods. The paper doesn't provide a thorough analysis of when LRTFS would outperform existing approaches, nor does it include ablation studies to isolate the contribution of the proposed innovations.

The paper also lacks a proper comparison with state-of-the-art methods for the tasks it addresses. For speech enhancement, the comparison against IS-NMF with a single Gabor transform (256 samples) is insufficient, as more sophisticated approaches exist that would provide a fairer benchmark. The paper cites OMLSA as a baseline, but doesn't provide sufficient context on why this particular method was chosen or how it compares to other modern speech enhancement techniques.

In summary, the paper presents a technically sound extension of existing work but fails to demonstrate significant advantages over established methods. The claimed benefits of modeling the raw signal and enabling multi-resolution representations aren't sufficiently validated with compelling experimental evidence. The marginal performance gains reported don't justify the additional complexity of the proposed approach.

The paper's main weakness is its lack of convincing evidence that LRTFS offers meaningful improvements over existing methods. In the context of NIPS, where we expect substantial advances in methodology or significant improvements in performance, this paper falls short. The contribution appears incremental rather than transformative.

Strengths: 
- Clear mathematical formulation
- Good connection to prior work
- Well-structured and well-written
- Acknowledgment of limitations (e.g., MMLE infeasibility)

Weaknesses:
- Marginal performance gains over baselines
- Insufficient experimental validation against state-of-the-art methods
- Overstated claims about multi-resolution capabilities
- Lack of clear differentiation from previous multi-resolution approaches
- No demonstration of when LRTFS would be preferable to simpler methods

I recommend rejection because the paper doesn't provide sufficient evidence to justify its novelty and claimed advantages. The contribution appears incremental without compelling evidence of significant improvements over established methods. The marginal performance gains reported don't justify the additional complexity of the proposed approach, which is a critical concern for a NIPS submission that should demonstrate substantial methodological advances.
