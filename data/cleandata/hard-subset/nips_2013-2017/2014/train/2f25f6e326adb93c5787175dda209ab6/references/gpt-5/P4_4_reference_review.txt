Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes Low-Rank Time-Frequency Synthesis (LRTFS), a generative probabilistic model for time-domain signals that imposes a low-rank structure on the variance of complex synthesis coefficients. Concretely, the signal x(t) is modeled as a linear combination of time-frequency atoms φfn(t) with complex Gaussian synthesis coefficients αfn ∼ Nc(0, [WH]fn), where W ∈ R+F×K and H ∈ R+K×N define a low-rank variance v = WH over the time-frequency grid, and e(t) is Gaussian residual noise. This contrasts with the Gaussian Composite Model (GCM) [3], which assumes a low-rank variance on the analysis STFT coefficients yfn and is therefore a generative model of the STFT rather than the time-domain signal.

The paper develops two estimation strategies:
- Maximum joint likelihood estimation (MJLE), minimizing −log p(x, α | W, H, λ), with an EM algorithm based on a variable-splitting trick inspired by Figueiredo & Nowak [6]. The M-step alternates Wiener-like shrinkage of α and IS-NMF updates of W, H; the updates only require fast Φ/Φ* operations and no large matrix inversions.
- Maximum marginal likelihood estimation (MMLE), minimizing −log p(x | W, H, λ), akin to “type-II” SBL [4,5], with an EM algorithm where α is latent. This requires inversion of large matrices and is not scalable for typical audio lengths.

A natural multi-resolution extension is proposed by stacking multiple frames (e.g., short/long Gabor windows), each with its own low-rank variance model, and applying the same MJLE scheme.

Experiments demonstrate: (i) a hybrid tonal/transient decomposition of a noisy jazz excerpt using two resolutions and K=3 components per layer, and (ii) semi-supervised speech enhancement with pretrained speech dictionaries and learned noise dictionaries across two resolutions. LRTFS shows an average SNR improvement of 6.6 dB, outperforming an IS-NMF baseline (6 dB) and OMLSA (4.6 dB).

Strengths
- Conceptual advance: moves from modeling STFT analysis coefficients (GCM) to directly modeling the time-domain signal via synthesis coefficients with low-rank variance. This bridges NMF-like low-rank modeling with time-domain synthesis.
- Efficient and practical MJLE algorithm: EM scheme uses only Φ, Φ* and standard IS-NMF updates; applicable to large-scale audio signals.
- Natural multi-resolution extension: clean formulation for multiple frames with shared synthesis formulation; algorithmic reuse without added complexity.
- Clear connection to SBL: highlights the difference between unstructured variances (SBL) and low-rank-structured variances (LRTFS), clarifying expected sparsity/structure trade-offs.
- Promising empirical results: meaningful decompositions and consistent SNR gains over standard baselines.

Weaknesses
- Scalability of MMLE: the marginal-likelihood approach is not practical at the scales considered, and no small-scale empirical comparison of MJLE vs MMLE is provided.
- Hyperparameter handling: λ is tuned by hand or annealed, which weakens the probabilistic story; the paper notes that joint estimation of λ behaved poorly, but provides limited analysis.
- Evaluation scope: experiments are limited in breadth and do not include ablations (e.g., single-resolution vs multi-resolution, effect of K, β, initialization) or comparisons to phase-aware or time-domain probabilistic baselines beyond IS-NMF/OMLSA.
- Clarity point on the MJLE objective: writing −log p(α|v) as DIS(|α|2 | v) + log(|α|2) may be misinterpreted as adding an extra sparsity-inducing term; algebraically, DIS(|α|2 | v) + log(|α|2) equals |α|2/v + log v + const, i.e., the standard complex Gaussian prior. Making this identity explicit would avoid confusion.

Quality
- The modeling assumptions are standard and technically sound: complex Gaussian priors, low-rank variance via WH, Gaussian residuals, conditional independence of coefficients. The EM derivations are consistent with prior work [6] and SBL architectures [4,5].
- The MJLE algorithm is well-justified and monotonic (up to the approximate λ update following [6]); the NMF subproblem uses established IS-NMF updates [3].
- The MMLE approach is correct but not scalable. The paper acknowledges this and leaves scalable MMLE as future work.

Clarity
- Overall clear and well-organized. The synthesis-versus-analysis distinction and the role of low-rank variance are articulated well.
- Some implementation details that would aid reproducibility are missing or brief: initialization of W/H/α, choice and scheduling of β, convergence criteria, computational cost/timings. Clarifying the objective decomposition (see above) would prevent misinterpretation.

Originality
- Modeling low-rank variance on synthesis coefficients for time-domain generative modeling, and showing an efficient EM procedure, is novel relative to GCM/NMF. The multi-resolution extension is a clean and useful contribution.

Significance
- The approach has potential impact for audio decomposition, source separation, and enhancement, particularly when multi-resolution is advantageous. While the improvements over IS-NMF/OMLSA are modest, the framework is general and could be extended (e.g., structured priors on W/H, multichannel).

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide a small-scale comparison of MJLE vs MMLE (runtime, convergence, performance) to validate the benefits of marginal likelihood.
- Report sensitivity to λ and β, and describe a principled strategy (e.g., empirical Bayes for λ, continuation for β) or regularization to stabilize λ estimation.
- Add ablations: single vs multi-resolution; varying K; different frames; initialization strategies; impact on SNR and perceptual metrics.
- Expand baselines to include phase-aware STFT models (e.g., GCM-based Wiener reconstructions) and/or time-domain factor models where applicable.
- Include computational complexity and wall-clock timings for typical audio segments.
- Clarify the identity leading to DIS(|α|2|v) + log(|α|2) and avoid implying an additional sparsity penalty beyond the Gaussian prior.

Arguments for acceptance
- Clear and principled time-domain generative model with low-rank structure on synthesis coefficients.
- Efficient, scalable EM algorithm with strong practical relevance.
- Natural multi-resolution modeling with demonstrated benefits.
- Solid connections to NMF/GCM and SBL that situate the contribution.

Arguments against acceptance
- Limited experimental breadth and absence of MMLE evaluation.
- Manual hyperparameter tuning and limited analysis of its effect.
- Modest empirical gains over strong baselines and lack of ablation studies.
- Minor clarity issue in the objective decomposition that could mislead readers.

Overall, this is a technically sound and well-motivated contribution with practical algorithms and a clean multi-resolution extension. Strengthening the empirical section and clarifying the objective terms would further improve the work.
