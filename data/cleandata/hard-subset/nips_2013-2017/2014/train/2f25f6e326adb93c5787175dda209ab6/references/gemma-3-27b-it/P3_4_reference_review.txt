## Review of Low-Rank Time-Frequency Synthesis

**Summary:**

This paper presents a novel generative model, Low-Rank Time-Frequency Synthesis (LRTFS), for representing signals with a low-rank structure in their time-frequency representation. Building upon the Gaussian Composite Model (GCM) [3], which imposes low-rank constraints on the *analysis* coefficients (STFT), LRTFS models the low-rank structure of the *synthesis* coefficients.  This allows for a truly generative model of the raw signal, and enables multi-resolution representations, a significant advantage over traditional NMF-based approaches. The authors propose both Maximum Joint Likelihood Estimation (MJLE) and Maximum Marginal Likelihood Estimation (MMLE) for parameter estimation, leveraging Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithms. The work is demonstrated on audio applications – music decomposition and speech enhancement – showing promising results.

**Relation to Prior Work:**

The paper builds directly on the success of NMF [1, 2] and GCM [3] in spectral decomposition. It positions itself as filling the gap between these approaches and a fully generative signal model.  The connection to Sparse Bayesian Learning (SBL) [4, 5] is explicitly drawn, highlighting the key difference being the tied variances in LRTFS versus the free variances in SBL. The multi-resolution aspect draws upon prior work in hybrid time-frequency representations [8, 9, 10] but introduces a principled generative framework.  The speech enhancement application relates to existing semi-supervised approaches [11], and the baseline comparison to OMLSA [14] provides context.

**Strengths:**

* **Generative Model:** The core contribution – a truly generative model of the raw signal with a low-rank time-frequency structure – is significant.  This is a natural progression from existing spectral decomposition methods.
* **Multi-Resolution Capability:** The ability to seamlessly incorporate multiple time-frequency resolutions is a substantial advantage.  This is particularly relevant for signals with varying characteristics (e.g., transients and sustained tones in music).
* **Theoretical Foundation:**  The derivation of the LRTFS model and the MJLE/MMLE estimation procedures are well-presented, grounded in probabilistic principles.  The connection to SBL is insightful.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The equations are clearly presented, and the explanations are concise.
* **Practical Demonstrations:** The experimental results on music decomposition and speech enhancement demonstrate the potential of the method. The performance improvement in speech enhancement over a standard approach like OMLSA is encouraging.
* **Efficient MJLE Algorithm:** The proposed MJLE algorithm doesn't require matrix inversions, making it computationally efficient and scalable.

**Weaknesses:**

* **MMLE Scalability:** The authors acknowledge that the MMLE implementation does not scale to the problem sizes encountered in the audio applications.  This is a significant limitation, as MMLE is often preferred for its robustness and potentially sparser solutions.  While acknowledging this, and indicating future work, the paper would benefit from a more detailed discussion of *why* MMLE doesn't scale and what specific algorithmic challenges are involved.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The experiments mention sensitivity to the hyperparameter λ. The limited exploration of its impact and the somewhat *ad hoc* adjustment ("gradually decreased") are concerns. A more systematic investigation of λ’s role, potentially through cross-validation, would strengthen the results.
* **Limited Comparison:** While a comparison to OMLSA is included for speech enhancement, a more comprehensive comparison to state-of-the-art speech enhancement techniques (e.g., deep learning based methods) would be valuable. Similarly, for music decomposition, comparing against more recent source separation algorithms would position the work more effectively.
* **Lack of Theoretical Analysis on Identifiability:** While the model is well-defined, there is limited discussion on the identifiability of the learned factors (W and H). This is a known issue in NMF and related methods, and addressing it, even briefly, would add to the theoretical rigor.
* **Missing Details on Training Data:** While the TIMIT database is mentioned, details regarding the specific selection of speech excerpts for training and testing are lacking.

**Arguments For/Against Acceptance:**

**For Acceptance:**

* The LRTFS model presents a novel and theoretically sound approach to signal synthesis with a low-rank time-frequency structure.
* The multi-resolution capability is a significant advancement over existing methods.
* The MJLE algorithm is efficient and demonstrates promising results in practical applications.
* The paper is well-written and clearly presents a complex method.

**Against Acceptance:**

* The MMLE algorithm's scalability issue is a significant drawback.
* The hyperparameter sensitivity and limited comparison to state-of-the-art methods raise concerns about the robustness and generalizability of the results.
* The lack of discussion on identifiability is a minor but noticeable omission.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this is a strong paper with significant potential. The proposed LRTFS model is a valuable contribution to the field of time-frequency analysis and signal processing. While the scalability issues with MMLE and the limited comparative analysis are concerns, they do not outweigh the strengths of the work. The novelty of the generative model and its multi-resolution capabilities are compelling. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions.  Specifically, the authors should:

1.  Elaborate on the scalability challenges of MMLE and outline potential approaches for addressing them in future work.
2.  Conduct a more systematic investigation of the hyperparameter λ and report the results.
3.  Expand the comparative analysis to include more state-of-the-art methods in both music decomposition and speech enhancement.
4.  Briefly discuss the identifiability of the learned factors.
