

# Peer Review: Low-Rank Time-Frequency Synthesis

## Summary and Relation to Previous Work

This paper introduces Low-Rank Time-Frequency Synthesis (LRTFS), a novel generative model that directly models the raw audio signal rather than its STFT representation, as done in previous approaches like the Gaussian Composite Model (GCM). The key insight is that by modeling the synthesis coefficients α with low-rank variance structure (α~Nc(0, WH)), the model can naturally accommodate multi-resolution representations where different time-frequency atoms have different temporal resolutions. This differs from traditional NMF approaches that operate on magnitude spectrograms and from GCM that operates on STFT coefficients but still doesn't model the raw signal. The paper extends previous work on sparse Bayesian learning (SBL) by introducing structured variance constraints on the synthesis coefficients, rather than the unstructured variances in SBL.

## Strengths

1. **Theoretical Contribution**: The paper makes a clear and well-motivated contribution by bridging the gap between time-frequency analysis and signal synthesis. The probabilistic formulation is elegant and well-grounded, with careful derivation of the EM algorithms for both maximum joint likelihood (MJLE) and marginal likelihood (MMLE) estimation.

2. **Multi-resolution Capability**: The natural extension to multi-resolution modeling is a significant strength. The paper convincingly demonstrates that LRTFS can represent different signal components with different temporal resolutions (e.g., tonal vs. transient components in audio) without requiring complex modifications to the estimation procedure, unlike traditional NMF approaches.

3. **Experimental Validation**: The experiments on music decomposition and speech enhancement are well-designed and demonstrate clear improvements over baselines. The 6.6 dB SNR improvement in speech enhancement is notable, particularly given the simplicity of the approach compared to deep learning methods.

4. **Clarity and Organization**: The paper is exceptionally well-written with clear mathematical formulations and logical flow. The connection to prior work (GCM, SBL) is well-explained, and the technical details are presented accessibly.

## Weaknesses and Critiques

1. **Limited Comparison to State-of-the-Art**: The most significant weakness is the lack of comparison to recent deep learning approaches for audio processing, which dominate the field. The paper compares to IS-NMF and OMLSA, but these are older methods. Recent deep learning models (e.g., TasNet, Conv-TasNet) typically achieve >10 dB SNR improvements in similar speech enhancement tasks, making the reported 6.6 dB improvement appear modest in context. The authors should address this gap to better position their contribution.

2. **Computational Scalability**: While the authors acknowledge MMLE's computational infeasibility for large audio signals, they don't propose practical solutions. For MJLE, the complexity is manageable but could be further optimized. The paper doesn't explore whether the approach could be accelerated using modern hardware or approximations.

3. **Ambiguity on Multi-resolution Advantage**: The claim that multi-resolution representations "were not possible in the traditional NMF setting" needs stronger justification. Traditional NMF approaches could potentially use multiple STFTs with different resolutions, though this would be less natural. The paper should clarify why LRTFS's approach is fundamentally better.

4. **Lack of Parameter Sensitivity Analysis**: The paper mentions using λ as a hyperparameter but doesn't discuss how sensitive the results are to this choice. A brief sensitivity analysis would strengthen the experimental section.

5. **Theoretical Limitations**: The paper lacks convergence guarantees for the EM algorithm and doesn't discuss the sample complexity of the model. These are important theoretical considerations for a NIPS submission.

## Overall Assessment

This is a technically sound paper that makes a meaningful contribution to audio signal processing. The core idea of modeling the raw signal with low-rank time-frequency structure is novel and well-executed. The multi-resolution capability is particularly compelling and represents a genuine advancement over previous approaches.

However, the paper's relevance to NIPS is somewhat limited by its focus on signal processing rather than machine learning methodology. While the probabilistic model has strong theoretical foundations, the field has increasingly shifted toward deep learning approaches for audio tasks. The lack of comparison to modern deep learning baselines is a significant drawback for a NIPS submission.

## Recommendation

**Accept** with minor revisions.

The paper makes a valuable contribution to audio signal processing with a novel generative model and clear experimental validation. To strengthen the paper for NIPS, I recommend:

1. Adding a comparison to at least one recent deep learning approach for speech enhancement (e.g., Conv-TasNet)
2. Clarifying the advantage of LRTFS over multi-resolution NMF approaches
3. Including a brief sensitivity analysis for the hyperparameter λ
4. Discussing potential computational optimizations for large-scale applications

These revisions would better position the work within the broader machine learning landscape that NIPS emphasizes, while preserving the paper's strong theoretical and experimental contributions.

The paper is well-written, technically sound, and makes a meaningful contribution to the field. With the suggested revisions, it would be a strong candidate for NIPS.
