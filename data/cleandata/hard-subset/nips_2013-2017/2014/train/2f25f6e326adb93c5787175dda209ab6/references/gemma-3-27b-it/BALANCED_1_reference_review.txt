This paper proposes a novel generative model for signal synthesis, termed Low-Rank Time-Frequency Synthesis (LRTFS), extending upon previous work on Gaussian Composite Models (GCMs). The core idea is to model the raw signal directly with a low-rank time-frequency structure, offering advantages over existing methods that primarily operate on spectrograms. The authors present maximum likelihood estimation approaches (MJLE and MMLE) and demonstrate the method’s applicability to multi-resolution representations and audio applications – music decomposition and speech enhancement.

The paper is well-motivated, clearly outlining the limitations of traditional NMF and GCM approaches and positioning LRTFS as a natural progression addressing those limitations. The connection to sparse Bayesian learning is insightful, and the extension to multi-resolution representations is a valuable contribution. The mathematical formulation is generally clear and concise, and the inclusion of the EM algorithm derivations provides sufficient detail for reproduction.  The experimental results, while limited in scope, show promising performance in both music decomposition and speech enhancement, outperforming a baseline NMF approach.  Referencing related work, like [8] on hybrid decomposition, demonstrates awareness of the relevant literature.

However, the paper also suffers from several weaknesses.  Firstly, the MMLE approach is deemed computationally intractable for the presented audio applications, despite being presented in detail. This feels somewhat incomplete; if a method is introduced, a more thorough discussion of its limitations and potential scalability solutions would be beneficial. The justification for excluding λ from the EM optimization (treating it as a hyperparameter) is relatively weak ("performed poorly in practice") and lacks a more rigorous explanation or sensitivity analysis. The experimental section feels somewhat shallow. While the results are promising, the comparisons are limited, and the parameters chosen (e.g., window sizes, number of components) lack detailed justification.  The provided sound examples in the supplementary material are crucial for evaluating the perceptual quality of the results, and their inclusion is appreciated, but a more quantitative evaluation of the results, such as a formal user study, would strengthen the claims.  Furthermore, the relationship between the proposed LRTFS and existing time-frequency decomposition methods beyond NMF and GCM could be explored more thoroughly. For instance, a discussion of connections to matching pursuit or wavelet transforms would enrich the context.  The notation is occasionally dense, and a glossary or more frequent definition of variables could improve readability. Finally, the concluding remarks are somewhat repetitive and could benefit from a more forward-looking discussion of potential research directions.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** The LRTFS model presents a novel approach to signal synthesis with a low-rank time-frequency structure, filling a gap between traditional NMF and direct signal modeling.
*   **Theoretical Soundness:** The mathematical formulation is rigorous and well-supported by derivations, demonstrating a strong understanding of the underlying principles.
*   **Potential Significance:** The multi-resolution capability opens up opportunities for applications in audio processing where signals exhibit varying time-frequency characteristics.
*   **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the core concepts, even if some sections are mathematically dense.
*   **Promising Experimental Results:** The preliminary results in music decomposition and speech enhancement show competitive performance.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

*   **Computational Limitations:** The MMLE approach is deemed intractable for the target applications, limiting the completeness of the proposed methodology.
*   **Weak Justification for Hyperparameter Choices:** The rationale for treating λ as a hyperparameter is not sufficiently explained.
*   **Limited Experimental Evaluation:** The experiments lack detailed analysis and comprehensive comparisons to state-of-the-art methods.
*   **Lack of Thorough Contextualization:** The relationship to other time-frequency decomposition techniques could be more thoroughly explored.
*   **Shallow Conclusion:** The conclusion is somewhat repetitive and lacks a strong vision for future research.



**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a promising new model for signal synthesis. The theoretical foundations are strong, and the multi-resolution extension is a significant contribution. However, the computational limitations of MMLE, the limited experimental evaluation, and the lack of deeper contextualization raise concerns about its readiness for NIPS publication. 

**Recommendation:**

I recommend **rejection** with encouragement to revise and resubmit. The authors should address the weaknesses outlined above, specifically by:

1.  Providing a more detailed discussion of the limitations of MMLE and exploring potential scalability solutions.
2.  Offering a more rigorous justification for treating λ as a hyperparameter.
3.  Conducting a more comprehensive experimental evaluation with a wider range of comparisons and quantitative metrics.
4.  Expanding the discussion to include related time-frequency decomposition techniques.
5.  Strengthening the conclusion with a more forward-looking perspective on future research directions.
