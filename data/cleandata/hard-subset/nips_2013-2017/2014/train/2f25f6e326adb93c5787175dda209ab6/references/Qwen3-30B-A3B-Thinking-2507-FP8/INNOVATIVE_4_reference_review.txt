

# Review of "Low-Rank Time-Frequency Synthesis"

This paper introduces a novel approach to time-frequency signal modeling called Low-Rank Time-Frequency Synthesis (LRTFS), which fundamentally shifts the paradigm from modeling spectrograms to modeling raw signals directly. The authors address a clear gap in the literature: while previous approaches like the Gaussian Composite Model (GCM) model the STFT, LRTFS models the actual time-domain signal x(t) as a sum of low-rank time-frequency components. This is a significant conceptual leap from traditional NMF approaches, which typically operate on magnitude spectrograms without a generative model of the raw signal. The key innovation is the ability to naturally support multi-resolution representations, where different signal components (e.g., tonal vs. transient parts of audio) can have different time-frequency resolutions—a capability impossible in traditional NMF frameworks.

The paper's main contribution is the generative model (Eqs. 2-4), which represents x(t) as a linear combination of time-frequency atoms with low-rank variance structure in the synthesis coefficients. This differs fundamentally from the GCM (Eq. 1), which imposes low-rank structure on the analysis coefficients (STFT). The authors show that this model leads to a natural multi-resolution extension (Section 4) that can represent signals with components having different temporal resolutions (e.g., short transients vs. sustained tones in audio). The experiments in Sections 5.1 and 5.2 demonstrate the practical utility of this approach in music decomposition and speech enhancement, showing consistent improvements over baselines.

**Strengths:**
1. **Novel Core Idea**: The shift from modeling STFTs to modeling raw signals with low-rank time-frequency structure is genuinely novel and addresses a fundamental limitation in the field. This is the kind of bold conceptual advance that NIPS values.
2. **Multi-resolution Capability**: The natural integration of multi-resolution representations is a major strength that opens new research directions in audio processing and potentially beyond.
3. **Clear Technical Presentation**: The mathematical formulation is well-structured, with clear connections to related work (e.g., sparse Bayesian learning in Section 2.2).
4. **Practical Results**: The experiments demonstrate consistent improvements in audio applications, with 6.6 dB SNR improvement in speech enhancement over a strong baseline.

**Weaknesses:**
1. **Limited Comparison with Modern Approaches**: The paper doesn't adequately compare with recent deep learning approaches to audio source separation, which have become dominant in the field. The baseline comparisons (IS-NMF, OMLSA) are somewhat dated.
2. **Theoretical Depth**: While the connection to sparse Bayesian learning is mentioned, the theoretical justification for the model's structure could be stronger. The paper would benefit from more discussion about why low-rank structure in synthesis coefficients is preferable to other representations.
3. **Phase Handling**: The paper doesn't fully address how phase information is handled, which is critical for audio signal reconstruction. The GCM paper (reference [3]) notes the importance of phase, but LRTFS doesn't elaborate on this key aspect.
4. **MMLE Discussion**: The paper mentions that MMLE doesn't scale well but doesn't provide sufficient explanation or suggest potential solutions (beyond future work).

**Overall Assessment:**
This paper presents a compelling and novel approach to time-frequency modeling that significantly advances the field. The conceptual shift from modeling spectrograms to modeling raw signals with low-rank time-frequency structure is bold and valuable, and the multi-resolution capability is a particularly strong practical contribution. While the paper has some limitations in experimental comparisons and theoretical depth, these don't undermine the fundamental novelty of the approach.

As a reviewer who prioritizes novel ideas over methodological perfection, I find this paper's core contribution to be sufficiently significant to warrant acceptance. The authors have identified a meaningful gap in the literature and proposed a conceptually sound approach to fill it. The multi-resolution capability alone would be enough to make this paper noteworthy, and the practical results in audio applications demonstrate its utility.

**Recommendation:** Accept. This paper makes a significant conceptual contribution to time-frequency analysis with clear potential for impact in audio processing and beyond. The novelty of the approach justifies its acceptance despite minor weaknesses in experimental comparisons and theoretical depth. The paper opens up new research directions that will likely inspire follow-up work, which is exactly what NIPS seeks to publish.
