

# Peer Review: Low-Rank Time-Frequency Synthesis

## Summary and Relation to Prior Work

This paper introduces Low-Rank Time-Frequency Synthesis (LRTFS), a generative model that bridges the gap between traditional nonnegative matrix factorization (NMF) approaches and time-domain signal synthesis. The authors build upon the Gaussian Composite Model (GCM) by modeling the raw signal directly rather than its STFT, while maintaining a low-rank structure in the synthesis coefficients. This represents a meaningful extension of previous work, particularly the GCM (Févotte et al., 2009) and sparse Bayesian learning (SBL) (Tipping, 2001; Wipf and Rao, 2004), by operating at the signal level rather than the transformed domain. The paper's multi-resolution extension, allowing different time-frequency resolutions for different signal components (e.g., transient vs. tonal components in audio), is particularly noteworthy compared to standard NMF approaches that fix resolution during feature extraction.

## Strengths

1. **Theoretical Soundness**: The paper presents a well-structured generative model with clear mathematical formulation. The connection to SBL is insightful, highlighting how the low-rank constraint differs from unstructured variance parameters in SBL. The derivation of the joint and marginal likelihoods is technically sound.

2. **Novel Multi-Resolution Capability**: The ability to represent signals with multiple time-frequency resolutions (e.g., short windows for transients, longer windows for tonal components) is a significant contribution. This addresses a fundamental limitation of traditional NMF approaches where time resolution is fixed at feature computation.

3. **Practical Demonstration**: The experiments on music decomposition and speech enhancement demonstrate clear advantages over baselines. The 6.6 dB average SNR improvement in speech enhancement compared to IS-NMF (6 dB) and OMLSA (4.6 dB) is compelling evidence of the method's practical value.

4. **Clear Algorithmic Presentation**: The EM algorithms for MJLE are well-described, with efficient update rules that avoid expensive matrix inversions (unlike MMLE). The computational considerations for audio applications are appropriately addressed.

## Weaknesses and Limitations

1. **Limited Discussion of MMLE**: While the paper acknowledges MMLE's scalability issues for audio applications, it doesn't sufficiently explore why this approach fails to scale, or how it might be adapted for larger problems. The reference to "message passing" as a potential solution (citing [16]) feels underdeveloped for a NIPS paper.

2. **Comparative Analysis Gap**: The paper compares to IS-NMF and OMLSA but doesn't address more recent audio processing methods that might be relevant to NIPS readers, such as deep learning approaches to source separation (e.g., [17] in the references). A more comprehensive comparison would strengthen the significance claim.

3. **Theoretical Limitations**: The paper doesn't sufficiently address whether the low-rank assumption is truly valid for natural signals. While the experiments show good results, a more thorough theoretical justification of the low-rank structure in synthesis coefficients would be valuable.

4. **Practical Implementation Details**: The paper states that λ was treated as a hyperparameter rather than estimated, but doesn't explain how this choice was made or its impact on the results. For a NIPS paper, more details on hyperparameter sensitivity would be expected.

## Overall Assessment

This is a technically sound paper that makes a meaningful contribution to the intersection of signal processing and machine learning. The LRTFS model addresses a genuine gap in the literature by modeling raw signals with low-rank time-frequency structure, and the multi-resolution capability is particularly valuable for audio applications. The methodology is well-justified and the experiments demonstrate clear practical advantages.

However, the paper would benefit from:
1) A more thorough discussion of the MMLE limitations and potential solutions
2) A more comprehensive comparison to recent deep learning approaches in audio processing
3) Deeper theoretical justification for the low-rank assumption
4) More details on hyperparameter selection

The paper aligns well with NIPS topics in machine learning for signal processing and representation learning. While not groundbreaking in the theoretical sense, it makes a solid practical contribution that advances the state of the art in audio processing applications.

## Recommendation

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions. The paper's contributions are significant for audio signal processing, and the methodology is technically sound. The minor weaknesses noted above could be addressed with additional discussion in the revision.

**Pro Acceptance Arguments**:
- Clear extension of existing work to model raw signals directly
- Novel multi-resolution capability with practical significance
- Strong experimental results demonstrating clear advantages
- Well-structured theoretical foundation

**Con Acceptance Arguments**:
- Limited discussion of MMLE scalability beyond the stated limitations
- Insufficient comparison to recent deep learning approaches
- Some theoretical justification for low-rank assumption could be stronger

The paper makes a valuable contribution to the field of audio signal processing and would be a useful addition to the NIPS proceedings. With minor revisions addressing the points above, it would make a strong contribution.
