Summary and relation to prior work:
This paper proposes Low-Rank Time-Frequency Synthesis (LRTFS), a generative model for time-domain signals that places a low-rank structure on the variances of complex synthesis coefficients associated with a time-frequency (t-f) frame. Concretely, the signal x is generated by a linear synthesis x = Φα + e, with α ∼ Nc(0, diag(v)), and the variance vector v is constrained to be low-rank via v = vec(WH), with W,H ≥ 0 and the Itakura-Saito (IS) divergence serving as the fit between |α|^2 and WH. This contrasts with the Gaussian Composite Model (GCM) for STFT analysis coefficients y ∼ Nc(0, WH), which models the STFT directly [Févotte et al.], and with classical NMF that operates on magnitude/power spectrograms. The model is connected to Sparse Bayesian Learning (SBL) via a Gaussian scale mixture perspective, but differs in that variances are tied by the factorization WH rather than being independent per-coefficient, trading sparsity for low-rank structure. The paper develops two estimation strategies: (i) maximum joint likelihood estimation (MJLE) via an EM algorithm using the Figueiredo–Nowak splitting with an auxiliary variable z, yielding alternating updates that look like Wiener shrinkage for α and IS-NMF for W,H; (ii) maximum marginal likelihood estimation (MMLE, “type-II” ML) with α integrated out, whose E-step requires inverting large matrices and is not yet scalable for long signals. A multi-resolution extension stacks multiple frames (e.g., short and long windows) into the synthesis, enabling decomposition into layers with different time scales. Experiments demonstrate a hybrid tonal/transient decomposition on music and a semi-supervised speech enhancement task, with modest SNR improvements over an IS-NMF baseline and OMLSA.

Quality:
- The generative formulation is technically sound and clearly derived. The joint objective naturally yields an IS divergence on |α|^2 with an additional log(|α|^2) term; the EM updates are correct and computationally practical (no large inverses).
- The MMLE derivation is standard for Gaussian linear models; the scalability limitation is candidly discussed.
- However, experimental methodology raises concerns: λ is tuned post hoc for best SNR in the music case and annealed to near-zero in speech, which compromises the generative story and fair comparison. Baselines are limited (single-resolution IS-NMF and OMLSA); no comparisons to phase-aware/complex NMF (e.g., GCM), MCA/structured-sparse synthesis methods, or multi-resolution NMF variants. Metrics are narrow (SNR only), and there are no run-time or convergence analyses, ablations (e.g., single vs multi-resolution), or sensitivity studies (β, K, window sizes).

Clarity:
- The paper is generally well written and organized. The link to IS-NMF in the M-step and the Wiener-like α update are clearly explained. The multi-resolution extension is straightforward.
- Some details are missing: how β is set relative to λ/δΦ; how frames are normalized; handling of real signals (Hermitian symmetry) in practice; criteria for K; computational cost and memory; convergence diagnostics.
- A brief intuitive discussion of the role of the log(|α|^2) term (sparsity promotion vs low-rank) would help.

Originality:
- The main novelty is shifting the low-rank variance model from analysis coefficients (STFT/GCM) to synthesis coefficients and doing so in a way that enables multi-resolution representations within a coherent generative framework. The EM architecture combining Wiener-like shrinkage with IS-NMF is an appealing synthesis of known tools.
- Conceptually, “analysis vs synthesis” modeling is known in sparse modeling and MCA; SBL and complex-NMF/GCM are established. The multi-resolution extension is natural. Overall, the contribution is incremental but interesting.

Significance:
- Modeling at the synthesis level with multi-resolution capability is potentially impactful for audio, where transients and tones differ in time scale. However, empirical gains over strong baselines are modest (≈0.6 dB over IS-NMF) and limited in scope. Without broader comparisons, perceptual metrics (e.g., STOI/PESQ), and larger-scale evaluations, it is hard to conclude that LRTFS advances the state of the art.
- The MMLE strand is not yet practical; MJLE seems to be the workhorse but would benefit from a deeper analysis.

Pros:
- Clear, principled generative model in the time domain with low-rank t-f structure.
- Efficient EM algorithm with practical updates; no large matrix inversions for MJLE.
- Natural and elegant multi-resolution extension.
- Connections to GCM/NMF and SBL are insightful.

Cons:
- Experimental evaluation is limited and somewhat methodologically weak (λ tuning/annealing; few baselines; only SNR).
- No comparison to phase-aware complex NMF/GCM on STFT, which seems the closest alternative.
- Modest quantitative gains; no ablations or sensitivity analyses.
- MMLE is not scalable; no approximate large-scale approach is attempted.
- Some missing implementation details and no runtime/convergence reporting.

Recommendation: Reject (borderline). The paper presents a neat and potentially useful modeling perspective, but the empirical validation and comparisons are not yet at the level expected for NeurIPS.

Constructive suggestions:
- Compare directly against GCM/complex NMF on STFT (with Wiener reconstruction), and against structured-sparse synthesis/MCA baselines in single- and multi-resolution settings. Include multi-resolution NMF baselines if applicable.
- Use standard speech quality metrics (STOI, PESQ), SDR/SIR/SAR for separation, and report runtime and convergence behavior. Avoid tuning λ on the test set; either learn it with a principled prior or cross-validate on a validation set. Clarify β selection and sensitivity.
- Provide ablations: single vs dual-resolution; effect of K; different window lengths; with/without the log(|α|^2) term (e.g., via variational surrogates).
- Discuss identifiability and the interplay between low-rank variance and sparsity. Elaborate on the proximal interpretation and provide monotonicity guarantees (empirical/analytical).
- Explore scalable MMLE approximations (e.g., conjugate gradient with matrix-free multiplications using Φ/Φ*, Woodbury with preconditioning, variational Bayes, or message passing) and report on medium-scale problems.
- Include more diverse datasets/noises and perceptual listening tests, and release code/audio to facilitate reproducibility.
