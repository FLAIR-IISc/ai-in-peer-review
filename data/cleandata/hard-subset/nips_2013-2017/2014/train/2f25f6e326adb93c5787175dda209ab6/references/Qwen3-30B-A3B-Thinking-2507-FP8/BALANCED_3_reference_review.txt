

# Review of "Low-Rank Time-Frequency Synthesis"

This paper introduces Low-Rank Time-Frequency Synthesis (LRTFS), a novel generative model that bridges the gap between time-frequency synthesis and traditional nonnegative matrix factorization (NMF) approaches. The authors address a key limitation of previous work (such as the Gaussian Composite Model [3]) by modeling the raw signal directly rather than its STFT, while maintaining a low-rank structure in the time-frequency domain. The model naturally accommodates multi-resolution representations, enabling decomposition of signals into components with varying temporal resolutions (e.g., separating tonal and transient elements in audio).

## Strengths

**Originality and Significance (Strong):** The paper identifies and addresses a clear gap in the literature. While previous work (like GCM) modeled the STFT with low-rank structure, LRTFS models the raw signal itself, which is a significant conceptual advancement. The multi-resolution capability is particularly compelling, as it enables natural decomposition of signals into components with different temporal resolutions (e.g., transient vs. tonal elements in audio), which was not possible with traditional NMF approaches. This has clear practical implications for audio processing applications.

**Quality (Strong):** The theoretical foundation is solid, with well-justified probabilistic formulations and clear connections to sparse Bayesian learning (Section 2.2). The MJLE estimation approach is technically sound, computationally efficient, and well-suited for audio applications. The experimental results in Sections 5.1 and 5.2 demonstrate clear improvements over baselines (6.6 dB vs. 6 dB for IS-NMF and 4.6 dB for OMLSA), providing strong empirical support for the approach. The authors are careful to acknowledge limitations (e.g., MMLE's computational complexity).

**Clarity (Strong):** The paper is well-organized and clearly written. The mathematical notation is consistent and well-explained, with sufficient detail for reproducibility. The connection to previous work is thorough and appropriately cited (e.g., [1-3, 8-10, 15-17]). The experimental setup is described in sufficient detail, and the figures (Fig. 1 and 2) effectively illustrate the model's capabilities.

## Weaknesses

**Limited Comparison with State-of-the-Art (Moderate):** While the paper compares with IS-NMF and OMLSA, it does not compare with more recent deep learning approaches for audio processing (e.g., convolutional neural networks or transformer-based architectures). Given that deep learning has become dominant in many audio processing tasks, this omission weakens the significance claim. The authors should consider adding such comparisons to better position their method within current research trends.

**Theoretical Convergence Analysis (Moderate):** The paper acknowledges that the MJLE algorithm converges to a local solution but provides no theoretical analysis of convergence properties or conditions under which convergence is guaranteed. A brief discussion of these aspects would strengthen the theoretical foundation.

**Lack of Failure Cases and Limitations (Moderate):** The paper presents strong results but does not discuss potential failure cases or limitations of the approach. For instance, how does the model perform with highly complex audio signals or in the presence of severe noise? A more balanced discussion of limitations would enhance the paper's credibility.

**Connection to Deep Learning (Moderate):** The paper does not discuss how LRTFS might complement or integrate with deep learning approaches, which is a missed opportunity given the current landscape of audio processing research. A brief discussion of potential synergies would strengthen the paper's relevance.

## Overall Assessment

This is a well-written, technically sound paper with a clear contribution to the field of time-frequency analysis and audio processing. The LRTFS model is novel, well-motivated, and demonstrates significant practical advantages over previous approaches, particularly through its multi-resolution capability. The experimental results are convincing and well-presented.

The paper would be strengthened by:
1. Including comparisons with recent deep learning-based audio processing methods
2. Adding a brief discussion of convergence properties for the MJLE algorithm
3. Addressing potential failure cases and limitations
4. Exploring connections to deep learning approaches

Despite these limitations, the paper makes a valuable contribution that advances the state of the art in time-frequency modeling. The core idea of modeling the raw signal with low-rank time-frequency structure is compelling and has clear applications in audio processing. The computational efficiency of the MJLE approach makes it particularly suitable for practical applications.

## Recommendation

I recommend acceptance. The paper presents a significant, well-executed contribution that advances the field. The strengths of the paper (originality, technical quality, clear experimental results) substantially outweigh the weaknesses, which are relatively minor and addressable with minor revisions. The multi-resolution capability is a particularly strong point that distinguishes this work from previous NMF approaches. The authors have clearly identified a gap in the literature and provided a compelling solution that is both theoretically sound and practically useful.
