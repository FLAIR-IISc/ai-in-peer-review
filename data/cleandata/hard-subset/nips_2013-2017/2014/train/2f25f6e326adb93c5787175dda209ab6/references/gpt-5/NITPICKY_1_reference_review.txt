Summary
The paper proposes Low-Rank Time-Frequency Synthesis (LRTFS), a generative, time-domain probabilistic model where a signal x(t) is synthesized from a redundant time-frequency dictionary Φ with complex synthesis coefficients αfn drawn from a zero-mean circular complex Gaussian whose variance is constrained to have a low-rank structure v = WH (as in Itakura–Saito NMF). This contrasts with the Gaussian Composite Model (GCM), which imposes low-rank structure on the variance of analysis (STFT) coefficients. The authors develop two estimation procedures: (i) a scalable maximum joint likelihood (MJLE) EM algorithm based on the Figueiredo–Nowak splitting that avoids matrix inversions and alternates Wiener-like shrinkage of α and IS-NMF multiplicative updates for W,H; and (ii) a maximum marginal likelihood (MMLE, SBL-like) EM algorithm that is more principled but computationally prohibitive. They extend LRTFS to multi-resolution (stacked frames with different windows) and present two experiments: a jazz hybrid decomposition into tonal/transient layers and single-channel speech enhancement with pre-trained speech dictionaries and learned noise.

Strengths
- Clear conceptual shift: generative modeling at the signal level using synthesis coefficients with low-rank-structured variances. This bridges NMF/PLCA-style variance modeling and time-domain synthesis.
- EM formulation for MJLE that is computationally friendly (no large inversions; uses analysis/synthesis operators).
- Natural multi-resolution extension by stacking frames, keeping estimation simple.
- Relation to SBL (MMLE) is informative; shows how LRTFS trades coefficient-wise sparsity for low-rank structure.
- Audio use cases are appropriate; results show modest gains over IS-NMF and OMLSA in the reported setup.

Weaknesses

Quality/Technical rigor
- Hyperparameter handling undermines validity: In Sec. 5.1, λ is tuned to maximize output SNR using the ground truth (oracle selection). In 5.2, λ is annealed “to a negligible value,” effectively forcing near-perfect reconstruction without a principled criterion. This compromises the fairness and reproducibility of the evaluation.
- Many key implementation details are missing: choice of β (subject to β ≤ λ/δΦ), how δΦ is computed for single and stacked frames, initialization of α,W,H, normalization strategies for WH scale ambiguity, stopping criteria, number of iterations, and regularization (if any).
- Potential identifiability issues in multi-resolution: with highly redundant, coherent frames, nothing prevents components from duplicating across layers; no constraints (e.g., exclusivity, group penalties, priors) are imposed to separate layers. No analysis or ablations on leakage/identifiability.
- The reconstruction of latent components uses α̂kfn = (wfk hkn / [WH]fn) α̂fn (Eq. 9), which is a heuristic conditional on α̂, not the MMSE for c = Φα; the actual MMSE would require a T×T inversion (acknowledged), but the performance impact of this approximation is not discussed.
- Convergence claims are informal: while the Figueiredo–Nowak split can yield monotonicity under certain conditions, the paper uses a simplified λ update (Eq. 19) and extends to multi-resolution (Eqs. 31–34) without a formal monotonicity or convergence guarantee in the stacked-frame case.
- No run-time or complexity measurements, and no scalability evidence beyond claims that Φα/Φ∗x are efficient.

Clarity/Presentation
- Notation/typos:
  - Eqs. (29)–(30): distributions are written as Nc([W H]fn); the mean “0” and variance parameter are missing (should be Nc(0,[W H]fn)).
  - Multiple typos: “hit-hat” (hi-hat), “mentionned,” “other the 10 test signals,” inconsistent norms (Frobenius vs ℓ2 for vectors), and inconsistent use of indices in Eq. (32).
  - In Sec. 2.1, footnote markers and the “2” footnote about supervised settings are awkwardly placed.
  - The reference list is severely incomplete and inconsistent (missing years/venues/pages, inconsistent styles).
- Important practical aspects for real-valued signals (Hermitian symmetry, how it is enforced in code) are not described for experiments.
- Figures are referenced but not included; no quantitative tables.

Originality
- The core idea—low-rank variance on synthesis coefficients in a time-domain generative model—appears novel relative to GCM and SBL. However, related work is underdeveloped. There is no discussion of complex NMF/phase-aware NMF, time-domain probabilistic models with structured priors, or multi-resolution NMF baselines. The multi-resolution idea with redundant frames and probabilistic modeling has partial precedents in sparse synthesis and MCA; stronger positioning relative to that literature is needed.

Significance/Empirical evidence
- Experimental scope is limited. The “hybrid decomposition” is illustrative; the speech enhancement uses 10 clips and only SNR improvement. Missing standard metrics (PESQ/STOI/SI-SDR), confidence intervals, and ablations (single vs multi-resolution, effect of K, effect of β/λ schedules, impact of diagonal e vs structured noise).
- Baselines are weak for NeurIPS 2025 standards: no comparisons to modern DNN speech enhancement, nor to phase-aware/complex NMF or multi-resolution NMF baselines. Reported gains over IS-NMF (0.6 dB) are small and could be within variance.

Additional minor issues
- EM M-step for W,H does not mention normalization to address scale indeterminacy; implementationally crucial.
- The condition β ≤ λ/δΦ is nontrivial for stacked frames; δΦ can be large. No guidance is given.
- Lack of code/data for reproducibility.

Pros
- Conceptually interesting synthesis-side probabilistic modeling with low-rank structure.
- Scalable MJLE algorithm leveraging standard TF toolboxes.
- Clean extension to multi-resolution.

Cons
- Evaluation methodology issues (oracle tuning of λ; ad hoc annealing; weak baselines/metrics).
- Missing details for reproducibility and convergence.
- Numerous presentation/notation/reference problems.
- Limited empirical support for claimed advantages of multi-resolution LRTFS.

Recommendation: Reject

Confidence: High

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide rigorous, reproducible experiments: fixed validation set for hyperparameters, report PESQ/STOI/SI-SDR, ablations (single vs multi-res; effect of β/λ/K), run-times, and variance across seeds.
- Compare against stronger baselines (complex/phase-aware NMF variants, multi-resolution NMF, recent DNN denoisers).
- Clarify and justify β and λ strategies; avoid oracle tuning; provide convergence diagnostics and, if possible, a monotonicity proof for the multi-resolution EM variant.
- Fix notation/typos; complete and standardize references; detail real-signal handling and initialization/normalization procedures.
- Analyze identifiability/leakage between layers and consider constraints/priors to mitigate it.
