Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes a generative, time-domain model—Low-Rank Time-Frequency Synthesis (LRTFS)—that models a signal x(t) as a sum of time-frequency atoms with complex synthesis coefficients whose variances follow a low-rank structure v = WH (with W ≥ 0, H ≥ 0). In contrast to the Gaussian Composite Model (GCM) [3], which imposes a low-rank variance on STFT analysis coefficients and effectively models the complex STFT, LRTFS imposes the low-rank structure on synthesis coefficients and thereby becomes a generative model of the raw signal itself. The paper develops two estimation strategies: (i) a scalable maximum joint likelihood estimation (MJLE) via an EM algorithm using an auxiliary variable z and leading to alternating updates for α and an IS-NMF (W,H) step; and (ii) a maximum marginal likelihood estimation (MMLE, “type-II ML” à la SBL [4,5]) that marginalizes α but is presently not scalable due to large matrix inversions. The model naturally extends to multi-resolution by stacking multiple time-frequency frames (e.g., short and long Gabor frames), enabling layered decompositions (tonal vs transient). Experiments demonstrate illustrative music decomposition and a semi-supervised speech enhancement application, with modest SNR improvements over IS-NMF and OMLSA.

The work is grounded in the NMF/IS-NMF literature [1–3], SBL [4–5], EM for linear-Gaussian models with splitting [6], and multi-resolution representations in audio [8–10]. Conceptually, it bridges analysis-based low-rank models (complex NMF/GCM) and synthesis modeling with tied variances, and formalizes multi-resolution low-rank synthesis in a probabilistic framework.

Strengths
- Conceptual contribution: A clear, principled synthesis-domain generative model that mirrors GCM but at the raw signal level, preserving complex phases by construction and enabling multi-resolution composition.
- Algorithmic design: The MJLE-EM is simple and scalable, relying on Φ/Φ* operations and IS-NMF subproblems. The appearance of a log(|α|^2) sparsity-promoting term in the joint objective is insightful and links to nonconvex shrinkage.
- Multi-resolution extension: The framework naturally supports multiple frames with separate low-rank structures, an appealing advantage over standard single-resolution NMF pipelines.
- Soundness of derivations: The probabilistic formulation, EM steps, and component reconstruction draw coherently on Gaussian identities and Wiener filtering analogies.

Weaknesses and detailed evaluation

Quality
- Estimation of λ: In Sec. 5.1, λ is tuned post hoc to maximize output SNR; in Sec. 5.2, λ is annealed to a negligible value. Both deviate from a proper probabilistic treatment and can bias comparisons. An ablation on λ and its principled selection (evidence maximization, CV, or hierarchical prior) is needed.
- MMLE scalability: While the derivation is correct, dismissing MMLE as future work limits the empirical assessment of the claimed robustness advantages of marginal-likelihood approaches. There are practical workarounds (CG with matrix–vector products using Φ and diagonal precision; Woodbury with iterative solvers; preconditioning exploiting frame structure) that could make MMLE feasible for moderate-length signals.
- Experimental scope: The experiments are small-scale, use only SNR, and include limited baselines. No statistical variability, runtime, or convergence behavior are reported. The semi-supervised enhancement task uses only 10 utterances from TIMIT and a single noise type for the main result. The multi-resolution benefit is suggested qualitatively but not isolated quantitatively via controlled ablations.
- Reproducibility: Details on initialization (W,H,α), β setting, δΦ estimation, stopping criteria, and code/data availability are insufficient for full reproduction.

Clarity
- The paper is generally well written, but some notational and methodological choices could be clearer:
  - Provide a short derivation of Eq. (11) to justify the DIS(|α|^2|v) + log(|α|^2) decomposition and explain its shrinkage effect relative to standard ℓ1/ℓ2 penalties.
  - Explicit pseudocode for MJLE (and multi-resolution MJLE) with all hyperparameters and recommended defaults (β choice, number of NMF inner iterations per outer loop).
  - Clarify the reconstruction strategy in Sec. 2.3: the practical ĉk uses α̂, not the full MMSE; comment on consequences for separation accuracy.

Originality
- The idea of imposing a low-rank variance on synthesis coefficients and doing probabilistic synthesis is novel within the NMF/GCM tradition. The multi-resolution extension is elegant and natural in this synthesis view. That said, the components (Gaussian scale mixtures, IS-NMF updates, Wiener filtering) are individually known; the originality lies in their combination and shift from analysis to synthesis with a tied-variance factorization.

Significance
- Potentially impactful for audio processing: modeling at the signal level with multi-resolution structure can benefit separation, enhancement, and layered representations. However, the current empirical evidence is limited (modest gains over IS-NMF and OMLSA on small tests), and the practical advantages over strong baselines (e.g., complex NMF variants, multi-resolution NMF, structured SBL, or modern supervised approaches) are not convincingly demonstrated.

Questions for the authors
- How sensitive is performance to β in the MJLE splitting? How is δΦ estimated in practice for large frames?
- What are the initialization strategies for W,H, and α, and how do they affect convergence/local minima?
- Can you report runtime and scaling with signal length and redundancy of Φ?
- How do results change if λ is treated as a parameter and updated per the EM rule, or with a hierarchical prior on λ?

Suggestions for improvement
- Strengthen experiments:
  - Include ablations: single-resolution vs multi-resolution LRTFS; effect of K; effect of λ selection strategy; impact of using α̂k vs full MMSE where feasible.
  - Use standard speech metrics (STOI, PESQ, SI-SDR) and larger, diverse datasets/noises (e.g., NOISEX/CHiME). Provide statistical variability across runs.
  - Add baselines: complex NMF/phase-aware models, multi-resolution NMF, MCA with sparse coding, and a structured SBL baseline with group/low-rank priors.
- Make MMLE practical: implement iterative solvers for Σ-vector products with CG, exploit Φ structure (tight frames, fast transforms), and compare MJLE vs MMLE on moderate-size problems.
- Theoretically analyze identifiability/scaling ambiguities between W and H under synthesis modeling, and discuss regularization to avoid degenerate solutions.
- Release code and detailed hyperparameter settings.

Pros for acceptance
- Clear, principled formulation of a synthesis-domain low-rank probabilistic model.
- Scalable MJLE algorithm with a neat link to IS-NMF and Wiener filtering.
- Natural and useful multi-resolution extension.

Cons for acceptance
- Limited and partially non-rigorous empirical evaluation (λ tuning, small datasets, few baselines).
- MMLE contribution remains mostly theoretical; no practical demonstration.
- Reproducibility and implementation details are insufficient.

Recommendation
Reject (borderline). The modeling idea and algorithm are appealing and relevant to NeurIPS audiences, but the current empirical validation and practical demonstrations are not yet strong enough. With more rigorous experiments, stronger baselines, a principled λ treatment, and a scalable MMLE or well-justified preference for MJLE, this work could become a solid contribution.
