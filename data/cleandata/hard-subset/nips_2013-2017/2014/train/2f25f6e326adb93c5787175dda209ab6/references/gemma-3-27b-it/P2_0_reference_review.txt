## Review of "Low-Rank Time-Frequency Synthesis"

**Summary:**

This paper introduces a novel generative model for signal synthesis, termed Low-Rank Time-Frequency Synthesis (LRTFS). Unlike traditional Non-negative Matrix Factorization (NMF) approaches which operate on magnitude spectrograms, LRTFS directly models the complex-valued STFT coefficients, leveraging a low-rank structure on the variance of synthesis coefficients. The authors present two estimation approaches – Maximum Joint Likelihood Estimation (MJLE) via an EM algorithm and Maximum Marginal Likelihood Estimation (MMLE) – and demonstrate the application of LRTFS to multi-resolution signal representation.  Experiments on music decomposition and speech enhancement showcase the model's efficacy.

**Relation to Previous Work:**

The work builds upon the Gaussian Composite Model (GCM) [3], extending it to a truly generative model of the raw signal *x(t)* rather than just its STFT. It also draws connections to Sparse Bayesian Learning (SBL) [4, 5], highlighting the differences in variance structure and sparsity-inducing properties. The multi-resolution aspect is reminiscent of approaches in audio processing [8, 9], but LRTFS provides a principled probabilistic framework for such decompositions.  The speech enhancement application relates to existing semi-supervised source separation techniques [11].

**Strengths:**

* **Generative Model:** A key strength is the development of a complete generative model for the signal, directly operating on the complex STFT.  This is a significant advancement over magnitude-only NMF or models limited to the STFT domain.
* **Multi-Resolution Capability:** The seamless integration of multi-resolution analysis is a notable advantage. This allows the model to adapt to different signal characteristics (transients vs. sustained tones) which is particularly relevant for audio processing.
* **Theoretical Foundation:** The paper provides a solid theoretical grounding, relating the proposed model to established techniques like GCM and SBL, and offering a clear derivation of the EM algorithms.
* **Empirical Validation:**  The experiments, while limited, demonstrate promising results in both music decomposition and speech enhancement, and compare favorably to baseline approaches.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and logically organized. The mathematical derivations are presented clearly, and the connection to existing methods is well-explained.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Computational Complexity (MMLE):** The authors acknowledge that the MMLE approach is computationally prohibitive for large-scale problems, and was not implemented for their audio applications. While the theoretical development is valuable, the lack of scalability limits its practical impact.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The experiments indicate sensitivity to the choice of the regularization parameter λ, requiring tuning for optimal performance. A more systematic investigation of hyperparameter selection strategies would strengthen the results.
* **Limited Scope of Experiments:** The experiments are somewhat limited in scope. Further evaluation on diverse datasets and with more sophisticated metrics would be beneficial.  A more detailed ablation study examining the impact of different design choices (e.g., window sizes, number of components) would also be useful.
* **Lack of Comparison to State-of-the-Art:** While baseline comparisons are provided, the speech enhancement results should be compared with more recent and sophisticated state-of-the-art methods.
* **MJLE Convergence:** The paper notes that the MJLE EM algorithm may only converge to a local solution. Discussion of strategies to mitigate this, such as multiple restarts, would be helpful.



**Review Criteria Assessment:**

* **Quality:** The paper is technically sound and the derivations appear correct. The claims are generally supported by the experiments, although more comprehensive evaluation would be beneficial. The discussion of limitations (e.g., local optima in MJLE) demonstrates carefulness. (Score: 8/10)
* **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and easy to follow. The mathematical notation is clear and the explanations are concise. (Score: 9/10)
* **Originality:** The combination of a generative model for the raw signal, a low-rank structure on synthesis coefficients, and multi-resolution capability is novel. The work clearly differentiates itself from existing NMF and GCM approaches. (Score: 8.5/10)
* **Significance:** The proposed LRTFS model has the potential to impact signal processing applications, particularly in audio. The multi-resolution capability is a valuable contribution, and the generative nature of the model opens up possibilities for more advanced signal manipulation and synthesis. (Score: 7.5/10)

**Arguments For and Against Acceptance:**

**For:**

* Novel generative model for signal synthesis.
* Seamless integration of multi-resolution analysis.
* Solid theoretical foundation and clear presentation.
* Promising initial experimental results.

**Against:**

* Scalability limitations of MMLE.
* Sensitivity to hyperparameters.
* Limited scope of experiments and comparison to state-of-the-art.
* Potential for convergence to local optima in MJLE.

**Overall Recommendation:**

I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions. While the computational limitations of MMLE and the somewhat limited scope of the experiments are weaknesses, the novelty and potential significance of the proposed LRTFS model outweigh these concerns. The authors should address the reviewer concerns by:

1.  Providing a more thorough discussion of hyperparameter selection.
2.  Adding comparisons to more recent state-of-the-art methods for speech enhancement.
3.  Discussing strategies to mitigate the risk of convergence to local optima in MJLE.
4. Consider providing results of additional experiments.
