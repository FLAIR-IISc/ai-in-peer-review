Summary
This paper proposes Low-Rank Time-Frequency Synthesis (LRTFS), a generative time-domain model for signals expanded on a time-frequency frame. Unlike the Gaussian Composite Model (GCM) that places a low-rank structure on the variance of STFT analysis coefficients, LRTFS places a low-rank structure (via WH) on the variance of synthesis coefficients α in x = Φα + e with α ∼ Nc(0, diag(v)), v = vec(WH). Two estimation strategies are derived: (i) Maximum Joint Likelihood Estimation (MJLE) via an EM algorithm using the Figueiredo–Nowak auxiliary variable trick, yielding an efficient iteration alternating a Wiener-like shrinkage step and an IS-NMF update on |α|^2; and (ii) Maximum Marginal Likelihood Estimation (MMLE), akin to SBL Type-II ML, which is elegant but currently not scalable due to large matrix inversions. A multi-resolution extension is natural by stacking multiple frames with independent low-rank priors. Experiments on hybrid music decomposition and semi-supervised speech enhancement show modest gains over IS-NMF and OMLSA in SNR.

Relation to prior work
The work is closely related to probabilistic NMF for complex STFTs (GCM; Févotte et al.) and sparse Bayesian learning (Tipping; Wipf & Rao). The main conceptual shift is imposing the low-rank structure on synthesis rather than analysis coefficients, enabling time-domain generative modeling and seamless multi-resolution formulations, which is difficult in standard NMF. The multi-resolution aspect connects to morphological component analysis and hybrid audio models. However, the experimental baselines omit phase-aware NMF/complex NMF variants and time-domain probabilistic models that also aim to model complex STFT with phase.

Quality
- Methodology: The MJLE EM derivation based on the two-stage noise model z = α + √βe1, x = Φz + e2 is sound, and the resulting algorithm is practical (no large inversions; leverages fast analysis/synthesis). The MMLE derivation mirrors SBL, but scalability is a known challenge.
- Objective function: The decomposition of the joint negative log-likelihood as a sum of an IS divergence term and log(|α|^2) is an algebraic identity: DIS(|α|^2|v) + log(|α|^2) ≡ |α|^2/v + log v − 1. Thus, the “sparsity-promoting” log term does not act as an independent penalty and does not, by itself, induce sparsity beyond the low-rank variance structure. The current text suggests it favors sparsity, which is misleading; please clarify.
- Convergence: The algorithm is EM-like with a surrogate, but the λ update is not the exact M-step. The paper claims it still decreases the likelihood (citing [6]); a brief argument or explicit reference to a monotonicity proof would strengthen the paper. Also, guidance on selecting β (beyond β ≤ λ/δΦ) and sensitivity analyses are missing.
- Identifiability: The usual NMF scale ambiguity persists, and multi-frame decompositions may be non-unique. A short discussion of identifiability, normalization, and regularization (e.g., smoothness/temporal continuity on H) would help.

Clarity
The paper is overall clear and well organized; the connection to GCM and SBL is well explained, and the EM updates are explicit. Some minor issues:
- Eq. (29)–(30) appear to omit the zero-mean in Nc(0, [W H]fn).
- In Sec. 4, Eq. (32) indexing of z should likely be z(i)`,fn (not z(i)fn).
- Clarify that the log(|α|^2) term is not an extra sparsity regularizer.

Originality
Moderate. Modeling in the time domain with a low-rank prior on synthesis coefficients rather than on STFT analysis coefficients is conceptually straightforward but nontrivial, and the EM architecture is nicely adapted to large-scale audio by avoiding inversions. The multi-resolution extension is elegant and practical. However, the modeling assumptions (Gaussian priors, low-rank variance via WH) and IS-NMF updates are standard components recombined in a new place.

Significance
Promising for audio modeling where multi-resolution representations are important. By synthesizing directly in the time domain, the approach avoids some issues with phase handling and can combine frames with different resolutions. The experimental gains over IS-NMF and OMLSA are modest but consistent. Wider impact would be clearer with stronger baselines and ablations.

Empirical evaluation
- Strengths: Two applications; quantitative SNR improvements; plausible qualitative decomposition (tonal/transient).
- Limitations: Small-scale experiments; λ is treated as a hyperparameter or annealed, which reduces the end-to-end probabilistic appeal. Missing comparisons to complex NMF/phase-aware NMF, PLCA variants that model complex STFT, and modern speech enhancement metrics (STOI, PESQ/SI-SDR). No runtime or scalability reporting. No ablation on multi-resolution vs single-resolution LRTFS, choice of frame lengths, or K. No uncertainty estimates.

Pros
- Clear, principled generative time-domain model with low-rank time-frequency structure.
- Scalable MJLE EM algorithm; simple to implement using standard TF toolboxes.
- Natural multi-resolution extension; demonstrated on relevant audio tasks.
- Good connection to GCM, SBL, and IS-NMF; helps unify viewpoints.

Cons
- Novelty is incremental; advantages over phase-aware/complex NMF not established empirically.
- Misleading interpretation of the log(|α|^2) term as sparsity-inducing.
- MMLE not scalable; λ estimation unstable; practical recipe relies on hyperparameter tuning/annealing.
- Evaluation limited: few baselines, metrics, and ablations; no runtime analysis.

Suggestions for improvement
- Clarify the role of the log(|α|^2) term and correct the sparsity claim.
- Provide guidance for choosing β and discuss convergence/monotonicity.
- Add baselines with complex/phase-aware NMF and report STOI/PESQ/SI-SDR; include ablations (single- vs multi-resolution LRTFS, frame choices, K).
- Report computational complexity and wall-clock times.
- Discuss identifiability and propose regularizers (e.g., smoothness on H) with references.
- Consider approximate MMLE via low-rank approximations, conjugate gradients with matrix-free ops, or message passing as suggested.

Recommendation
Weak accept. The paper presents a coherent and practically useful shift from analysis- to synthesis-side low-rank modeling, with an efficient algorithm and a compelling multi-resolution extension. Strengthening clarity on the objective, expanding empirical comparisons, and adding ablations would make it a solid NeurIPS contribution. Confidence: high.
