- Proposes LRTFS: a generative time-domain synthesis model with complex Gaussian synthesis coefficients whose variances follow a low-rank WH factorization.  
- Presents two estimation strategies: MJLE via an EM algorithm (practical) and MMLE/type-II ML (elegant but computationally heavy).  
- Multi-resolution extension by concatenating frames (short/long windows) enables morphological decompositions (transient/tonal).  
- Experiments show modest but consistent SNR gains over single-resolution IS-NMF and OMLSA for music decomposition and semi-supervised speech enhancement.  
- Builds on IS-NMF/GCM by shifting low-rank modeling from analysis to synthesis coefficients and from STFT to time-domain generation.  
- MMLE resembles SBL/type-II ML but uses tied low-rank variances (WH) instead of independent ARD variances, yielding low-rank rather than sparse solutions.  
- Extends hybrid/morphological decomposition frameworks by replacing sparse priors with low-rank Gaussian priors and provides a scalable EM variant using fast frame operators.  
- Related to phase-aware/complex NMF but differs by modeling in the synthesis domain; direct comparison to those methods is missing.  
- Conceptually novel: synthesis-domain low-rank variance model with a natural multi-resolution formulation.  
- Algorithmic contribution: practical EM for MJLE using splitting tricks to avoid large matrix inversions, alternating Wiener-like shrinkage and IS-NMF.  
- Clear derivations and explanation of IS divergence connection and per-component Wiener reconstruction.  
- Time-domain generation and multi-resolution modeling are practically relevant for audio applications.  
- Experimental scope is limited: small datasets, only SNR reported for speech, and insufficient noise/type diversity.  
- Hyperparameter handling is ad hoc (oracle λ); needs principled selection and sensitivity analysis (β, λ).  
- Lacks comparisons to stronger baselines: multiresolution NMF, complex/phase-aware NMF, morphological component analysis variants, and GCM+Wiener baselines.  
- MMLE is presented but not made scalable; paper should include approximate scalable methods or timing/memory estimates.  
- The claim that the objective “favors sparse solutions of α” is misleading—prior is Gaussian and not sparsity-inducing; clarify interpretation.  
- Missing practical details: initializations, iterations, runtimes, convergence diagnostics, frame normalization, and multiresolution regularization against energy leakage.  
- Minor notational/typo issues (e.g., Eqs. 29–30) and a request for pseudocode/practical guidance to improve reproducibility.  
- Technically solid EM derivations; MJLE algorithm appears correct and scalable, MMLE is correct but incomplete in practice.  
- Overall clarity is good but requires correction of the sparsity remark and added practical instructions.  
- Originality: novel cohesive framework combining synthesis-domain low-rank modeling and multi-resolution; builds on existing tools but offers new implications for time-domain modeling.  
- Significance: potentially impactful for audio ML and signal processing, though empirical gains are modest and broader evaluation is needed.  
- Pros for acceptance: novel modeling perspective, practical EM leveraging fast operators, useful multi-resolution extension, and clear literature links.  
- Cons for acceptance: limited experiments and ad hoc hyperparameter tuning, missing comparisons to close baselines, MMLE left theoretical, and some overstated interpretations.  
- Key questions for authors: sensitivity and principled selection of β and λ; runtimes and iteration counts; single-resolution vs multi-resolution ablation; perceptual and separation metrics (PESQ/STOI/SDR/SIR/SAR).  
- Recommendation: Weak accept contingent on clarifying objective interpretation, adding practical guidance, and strengthening empirical evaluation or baselines/ablations.
