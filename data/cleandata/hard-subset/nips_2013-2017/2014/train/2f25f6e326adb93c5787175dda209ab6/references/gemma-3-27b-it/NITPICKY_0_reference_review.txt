This paper introduces a low-rank time-frequency synthesis (LRTFS) model for signal representation, extending previous work on generative probabilistic latent factor models like the Gaussian Composite Model (GCM). The core idea is to directly model the raw signal as a sum of weighted time-frequency atoms with low-rank structure imposed on the synthesis coefficients, rather than operating on the STFT as in GCM. The authors propose maximum likelihood estimation approaches (MJLE and MMLE) and demonstrate the method’s applicability to multi-resolution representations with experiments on music decomposition and speech enhancement.

The paper is generally well-structured and mathematically formulated. The connection to sparse Bayesian learning is clearly articulated, highlighting the difference in variance structure between LRTFS and SBL. The multi-resolution extension is a natural and potentially useful addition. However, despite the novelty of the approach, the paper suffers from numerous minor flaws and inconsistencies that detract from its overall quality.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The direct synthesis approach, modeling the raw signal instead of its STFT, is a valuable contribution.
*   **Multi-Resolution Capability:** The ability to naturally integrate multiple time-frequency resolutions is a significant advantage, especially for audio signals.
*   **Theoretical Foundation:** The derivation of the LRTFS model and the EM algorithms is presented with a good level of mathematical rigor.
*   **Experimental Validation:** The experiments, though limited, demonstrate the potential of LRTFS in practical applications.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Minor Technical Inconsistencies:** Equation (1) states `yfn ∼ Nc(0, [WH]fn)`, yet the surrounding text claims the GCM imposes a low-rank structure on the *variance* of the analysis coefficients.  [WH]fn is not a variance; it’s a covariance matrix, and the variance is the diagonal of that matrix. This demonstrates a lack of careful attention to detail.
*   **Unjustified Assumptions:** The assumption of complex-valued variables and the subsequent discussion of Hermitian symmetry seem unnecessarily complex. The paper states one only needs to consider positive frequencies, but doesn't fully explain how the real signal is constructed from the complex one – a crucial detail.
*   **Ambiguous Notation:** The indexing of coefficients using 'm' and '(f, n)' is confusing and could be more clearly defined.  The phrase "slightly abuse notations" is a red flag.
*   **Implementation Details:** The fact that MMLE doesn't scale and wasn’t fully implemented is concerning, especially since it's presented as a theoretical alternative. The mention that λ was not estimated but treated as a hyperparameter diminishes the impact of the MJLE approach. The lack of detail about hyperparameter selection (beyond "best output SNR") is problematic.
*   **Limited Experimental Evaluation:** The experiments lack rigorous quantitative comparisons against state-of-the-art methods. The SNR improvements are modest, and the analysis is superficial. The supplementary material is essential to truly assess results, which isn’t ideal.
*   **Formatting/Typos:** The writing quality is uneven. For example, the phrase “Unexpectedly, a third term that favors sparse solutions of α, thanks to the log function, naturally appears from the derivation of the joint likelihood,” is awkward and imprecise. The last sentence in the conclusion is also poorly phrased.
*   **Redundancy:** The explanation of the EM algorithm is overly detailed and repetitive. The conclusion reiterates information already presented in the introduction.
* **Citation Style:** The referencing is inconsistent and several citations seem unnecessarily broad (e.g., “[1] for a review”).

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

*   The LRTFS model presents a novel approach to signal synthesis with potential advantages over existing methods.
*   The multi-resolution capability is a significant contribution.
*   The theoretical framework is sound.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   Numerous minor technical inaccuracies and inconsistencies demonstrate a lack of meticulousness.
*   The experimental evaluation is insufficient to convincingly demonstrate the superiority of LRTFS.
*   The implementation limitations (MMLE) and hyperparameter choices raise concerns about the robustness of the approach.
*   The writing quality is uneven and contains unnecessary complexities and redundancies.



**Overall Assessment:**

While the LRTFS model is an interesting contribution, the numerous minor flaws and the limited experimental validation lead me to recommend **rejection**. The paper feels incomplete and lacks the polish expected of a NIPS publication. The authors would significantly benefit from addressing the technical inconsistencies, providing a more comprehensive experimental evaluation, and refining the presentation of their work. Addressing these issues would elevate the paper to a level suitable for consideration. The attention to detail simply isn’t sufficient for a top-tier conference like NIPS.
