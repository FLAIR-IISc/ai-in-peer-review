Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes Low-Rank Time-Frequency Synthesis (LRTFS), a generative model of raw time-domain signals that expresses x(t) as a sum over time-frequency atoms with complex Gaussian synthesis coefficients αfn. The variances of αfn are constrained to be low-rank via an NMF factorization vfn = [WH]fn and a residual Gaussian noise term models the mismatch. This contrasts with the Gaussian Composite Model (GCM) that imposes a low-rank structure on the variance of analysis coefficients (STFT) rather than synthesis coefficients. The authors derive two estimation strategies: (i) maximum joint likelihood (MJLE) via an EM algorithm using a Figueiredo–Nowak variable-splitting trick, yielding simple Wiener-type shrinkage for α and IS-NMF updates for W,H; and (ii) maximum marginal likelihood (MMLE) in the spirit of Sparse Bayesian Learning (SBL), integrating out α with an EM algorithm, though not scalable as presented. They also extend LRTFS to multi-resolution by combining multiple time-frequency frames with separate low-rank structures per layer. Experiments illustrate a tonal/transient hybrid decomposition on music and semi-supervised speech enhancement with pretrained speech dictionaries; LRTFS improves SNR by ~6.6 dB on average, slightly outperforming single-resolution IS-NMF (6 dB) and OMLSA (4.6 dB).

The work is related to: NMF with IS divergence for audio (Fevotte et al.), GCM (probabilistic model on STFT magnitudes), SBL/RVM (Tipping; Wipf & Rao) for variance learning, and hybrid/morphological component analysis using sparse synthesis models (Daudet/Torrésani). The key novelty is moving the low-rank variance prior to the synthesis domain and showing how to estimate it efficiently and in multi-resolution.

Strengths
- Conceptual novelty: A clear and well-motivated shift from analysis-domain (STFT) modeling to synthesis-domain modeling with a structured (low-rank) Gaussian prior. This bridges NMF/GCM and synthesis-based models.
- Algorithmic practicality: The MJLE-EM updates are simple, require no large matrix inversions, and leverage standard tools (IS-NMF, Wiener-type updates), making the approach scalable and implementable.
- Multi-resolution extension: Natural and elegant; reuses the same EM structure to handle multiple frames/layers without extra complexity.
- Empirical indications: On two audio tasks, LRTFS is competitive and slightly better than reasonable baselines, supporting the practical relevance.

Weaknesses
- Experimental rigor: The music experiment tunes λ by selecting the value that yields best output SNR, which uses ground-truth at test time and biases results. The speech enhancement setup lacks ablations (e.g., effect of multi-resolution vs single-only within LRTFS; effect of λ schedule), runtime, and statistical significance. Baselines are limited (no GCM-based time-domain reconstruction or multiresolution sparse MCA comparison).
- Clarity and derivations: The decomposition of the joint objective into DIS(|α|^2|v) + log(|α|^2) is potentially confusing; the “sparsity-favoring” log term cancels with a term inside DIS, leaving the standard quadratic shrinkage from a Gaussian prior. This should be clarified to avoid suggesting a true sparsity prior where there is none.
- MMLE scalability: The MMLE route is intellectually interesting but currently impractical; no approximate or structured solutions (e.g., diagonal plus low-rank, conjugate gradients, message passing) are developed, leaving a key advertised contribution unvalidated.
- Limited scope of significance: Gains over IS-NMF are modest (0.6 dB on average), and the paper does not show broader advantages (e.g., phase-aware reconstruction benefits, robustness, or clear artifacts reduction) beyond SNR.

Evaluation by criteria
- Quality: Generally sound. The MJLE EM derivation follows established techniques; the updates are correct and consistent with the model. However, some steps are only sketched (e.g., convergence guarantees, β selection criteria), and the objective decomposition could be more precise. The experimental methodology needs stronger controls (fair hyperparameter selection, broader baselines).
- Clarity: The paper is well structured and readable. The relation to GCM and SBL is well explained. Two areas need clarification: (1) the role and effect of the log(|α|^2) term (as noted), and (2) practical guidance for λ and β (initialization, scheduling, sensitivity). Reproducibility would benefit from more implementation details and reporting of computational costs.
- Originality: Good. Imposing low-rank variance on synthesis coefficients and tying it to NMF/IS divergences is novel, as is the clean multi-resolution extension under this low-rank Gaussian prior. The connection to SBL with tied variances (via WH) is insightful.
- Significance: Moderate. The idea is likely to interest audio ML/signal processing researchers and could inspire further work on structured priors in synthesis models and scalable MMLE. However, current empirical gains are modest and the stronger MMLE variant is not yet practical, somewhat limiting immediate impact.

Pros for acceptance
- Clear conceptual contribution connecting NMF/GCM and synthesis-domain modeling.
- Practical, simple algorithm with multi-resolution capability.
- Solid grounding in probabilistic modeling; likely to stimulate follow-up work.

Cons for acceptance
- Experimental evaluation is not fully rigorous (oracle λ selection, limited baselines, modest gains).
- Some claims/derivations (sparsity effect) are potentially misleading.
- MMLE contribution remains largely theoretical without a scalable instantiation.

Suggestions for improvement
- Clarify the objective decomposition and explicitly state that the net prior is Gaussian shrinkage (not sparsity), or introduce a true sparsity-promoting prior if that is desired.
- Provide fair hyperparameter selection (e.g., cross-validation or fixed protocol) and add ablations (single vs multi-resolution; effect of K; effect of λ schedule).
- Compare against GCM with phase-aware reconstruction and multi-resolution sparse MCA, and report computational times.
- Consider scalable approximations for MMLE (Woodbury + iterative solvers, diagonal plus low-rank structure, variational/message passing).

Overall recommendation
Borderline accept. The paper is original and technically sound with a practical algorithm and a compelling modeling perspective, but the empirical evaluation and some clarity issues hold it back. Strengthening the experiments and clarifying the derivations would make it a strong contribution.
