This paper introduces a novel generative model for signal synthesis called Low-Rank Time-Frequency Synthesis (LRTFS), building upon previous work on Gaussian Composite Models (GCMs) [3]. The core idea is to model the raw signal directly, rather than its STFT, by representing it as a sum of atoms with a low-rank structure imposed on the synthesis coefficients. This approach allows for multi-resolution representations and potentially overcomes limitations of traditional NMF-based spectral decompositions. The authors present two estimation approaches – Maximum Joint Likelihood Estimation (MJLE) and Maximum Marginal Likelihood Estimation (MMLE) – with accompanying EM algorithms. Experiments on music decomposition and speech enhancement demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The direct synthesis approach, as opposed to analyzing the STFT as in GCM, is a significant step forward and addresses a limitation of existing methods. Modeling the raw signal directly opens possibilities for more accurate representations and multi-resolution analysis.
* **Generative Model:** The LRTFS model provides a principled generative framework, which is more robust and informative than purely discriminative approaches. This is a key strength, enabling applications beyond simple decomposition.
* **Multi-Resolution Capability:** The ability to seamlessly integrate different time-frequency resolutions is a valuable feature, particularly relevant for audio signals with varying characteristics (transients vs. sustained tones).  The explanation and demonstration of this capability are well done.
* **Clear Derivation:** The mathematical derivations, especially for the EM algorithms, are presented clearly and logically, building upon existing work in sparse Bayesian learning and NMF.
* **Experimental Validation:**  The experiments demonstrate the effectiveness of LRTFS in both music decomposition and speech enhancement tasks, comparing favorably to standard methods like IS-NMF and OMLSA. The inclusion of supplementary sound examples is valuable.
* **Connection to Existing Work:** The paper appropriately connects the proposed method to existing literature in NMF, GCMs, and sparse Bayesian learning, providing sufficient context.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Computational Complexity (MMLE):** The authors acknowledge the scalability issues of the MMLE approach due to the matrix inversion step. While this is honestly addressed, it limits the applicability of that specific method and would benefit from a more detailed discussion of potential solutions (e.g., approximate inference techniques).
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The experiments indicate sensitivity to the hyperparameter λ. A more systematic investigation of its influence on performance and a potential strategy for adaptive selection would be beneficial. The justification for using a gradually decreasing λ is somewhat weak.
* **Limited Scope of Experiments:** While the chosen applications are relevant, the experiments are relatively limited in scope.  Exploring additional datasets, noise types, and evaluation metrics would strengthen the conclusions.
* **Lack of Comparison to State-of-the-Art:** The comparison to existing methods in speech enhancement, while showing improvement, doesn't include the most recent and sophisticated deep learning-based approaches. A comparison to these methods would better position the contribution of LRTFS.
* **Minor Clarity Issues:** Some notation could be clarified (e.g., the meaning of subscripts for α and v). More explicitly stating the differences between analysis and synthesis coefficients could be helpful.  The use of "atoms" is a bit vague; clarifying that these are basis functions would be beneficial.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound generative model for signal synthesis with promising results.
* The multi-resolution capability addresses a significant limitation of existing methods.
* The mathematical derivations are clear and well-presented.
* The experimental validation provides evidence of the effectiveness of the proposed approach.
* The paper is well-written and adequately references related work.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

* The computational complexity of MMLE limits its practical applicability.
* The experiments are relatively limited in scope and lack comparison to state-of-the-art deep learning approaches.
* The sensitivity to hyperparameters could pose challenges in real-world applications.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of time-frequency analysis and signal processing. The proposed LRTFS model is novel, well-motivated, and has the potential to advance the state of the art in various applications. While the computational complexity of MMLE and the limited scope of experiments are weaknesses, they do not overshadow the overall quality and significance of the work. I believe the paper is worthy of acceptance, albeit with minor revisions addressing the clarity issues and suggestions for future work. The authors should consider expanding the experimental evaluation to include comparisons with more recent deep learning-based methods and investigating more robust hyperparameter selection strategies.



**Recommendation:** Accept (with minor revisions)
