## Review of Low-Rank Time-Frequency Synthesis

This paper proposes a novel generative model for signals based on low-rank structure in the time-frequency domain, extending previous work on Gaussian Composite Models (GCMs). The core idea is to model the raw signal as a sum of atoms with low-rank synthesis coefficients, offering advantages over methods operating solely on the STFT magnitude, and enabling multi-resolution representations. The authors present maximum likelihood estimation approaches via Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithms, and demonstrate the method on music decomposition and speech enhancement tasks.

While the motivation for a generative model of the raw signal is sound, and the extension to multi-resolution analysis is a potentially interesting direction, I find the paper lacking in sufficient novelty and rigorous validation to warrant acceptance to NIPS. The proposed Low-Rank Time-Frequency Synthesis (LRTFS) model feels like a natural, albeit incremental, extension of existing sparse Bayesian learning (SBL) and NMF techniques, rather than a truly groundbreaking contribution. The connection to SBL is explicitly acknowledged by the authors, highlighting the limited originality.

**Strengths:**

*   **Clear Motivation:** The paper identifies a clear limitation of existing time-frequency methods – lack of a generative model for the raw signal – and proposes a solution.
*   **Multi-resolution capability:** The extension to multi-resolution representations is a valuable addition and distinguishes the approach from traditional NMF.
*   **Well-defined mathematical framework:** The model is presented with mathematical clarity, and the EM algorithms are described in sufficient detail.
*   **Demonstration on relevant tasks:**  Experiments on music decomposition and speech enhancement provide some initial validation.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Limited Originality:** The core idea builds heavily on established techniques like SBL and NMF. The main contribution appears to be adapting these existing methods to a slightly different formulation.
*   **Insufficient Baseline Comparison:** The experimental evaluation is weak.  The comparison with IS-NMF in speech enhancement is a reasonable starting point, but lacks depth.  The choice of OMLSA is also good, but the comparison is limited. More importantly, a direct comparison with existing state-of-the-art SBL-based methods for signal reconstruction/enhancement is missing. This is crucial given the acknowledged relationship with SBL.
*   **Scalability Concerns:** The authors acknowledge scalability issues with the MMLE approach, which is particularly troubling given the computational demands of audio signal processing. The dependence on inverting large matrices renders the MMLE method impractical for many real-world applications.  The fact that the experiments rely solely on MJLE, with λ treated as a hyperparameter instead of being estimated, further highlights this limitation.
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The performance appears heavily dependent on the choice of the regularization parameter λ, and the methodology to select it is not thoroughly described. This raises concerns about the robustness of the approach.
*   **Lack of Theoretical Analysis:** While the mathematical framework is clear, a more in-depth theoretical analysis of the properties of the LRTFS model would be beneficial. For example, what conditions guarantee the recovery of the underlying factors?  How does the choice of the time-frequency basis (e.g., Gabor frame) affect performance?
*   **Limited Discussion of Failure Cases:** The paper lacks a discussion of potential failure modes or limitations of the LRTFS model.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

*   The method offers a potentially useful framework for multi-resolution signal processing.
*   The EM algorithm provides a practical approach for estimating the model parameters.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The originality is limited; it is an incremental improvement on existing methods.
*   The experimental evaluation is insufficient to demonstrate a clear advantage over established techniques.
*   The scalability concerns related to MMLE and the hyperparameter sensitivity raise doubts about the practical applicability of the method.
*   The lack of theoretical analysis limits the understanding of the model's properties.



**Overall Assessment:**

I rate this paper as **reject**. While the ideas are reasonably well-presented and the technical work is solid, the lack of substantial novelty, limited experimental validation, and scalability concerns prevent me from recommending acceptance to NIPS. The paper reads more like a preliminary investigation than a complete, impactful contribution.  Significant revisions, including a more thorough comparison to state-of-the-art methods, a more robust treatment of hyperparameter selection, and a detailed discussion of limitations, would be necessary for reconsideration.
