Summary
The paper proposes Low-Rank Time-Frequency Synthesis (LRTFS), a generative model of time-domain signals in which the signal is synthesized from a time-frequency dictionary Φ with complex Gaussian synthesis coefficients α whose variances are constrained to be low-rank via an NMF parameterization v = WH. This shifts the probabilistic low-rank modeling from analysis coefficients (as in the Gaussian Composite Model, GCM) to synthesis coefficients. Two estimation routes are presented: (i) maximum joint likelihood (MJLE) using an EM scheme that leverages a Figueiredo–Nowak auxiliary variable trick to avoid large matrix inversions and alternates Wiener-like shrinkage of α with IS-NMF on |α|^2; and (ii) maximum marginal likelihood (MMLE, type-II ML/SBL-style), which the authors acknowledge is not scalable due to required inversions. A multi-resolution extension stacks multiple frames (e.g., short and long Gabor windows) with separate low-rank structures. Experiments illustrate a tonal/transient decomposition on noisy jazz and a semi-supervised speech enhancement task, with a reported average SNR improvement of 6.6 dB versus 6.0 dB for an IS-NMF baseline and 4.6 dB for OMLSA.

Relation to prior work
The approach is closely related to probabilistic NMF/PLCA on STFT magnitudes and the GCM (complex Gaussian with IS divergence) but differs in modeling synthesis coefficients directly. It also connects to Sparse Bayesian Learning (SBL/ARD), except that LRTFS ties variances through WH rather than independent vm parameters, and to hybrid/morphological models and multiresolution TF representations (e.g., MCA, structured sparse models). The links to Wiener filtering and iterative shrinkage/thresholding are made explicit.

Strengths
- Conceptual bridge: a coherent generative model at the time-domain level with low-rank TF structure on synthesis coefficients; the connection to GCM and SBL is thoughtful.
- Algorithmic practicality: the MJLE EM scheme avoids large inversions and is implementable with standard TF toolboxes; the multiresolution extension is simple and natural within the framework.
- Clarity of the main estimation steps and the use of IS-NMF on |α|^2 aligns with established audio practice.
- Empirical illustrations show plausible decompositions and some improvement over a strong classical baseline (OMLSA) and a simple IS-NMF configuration.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Empirical evidence is limited and not fully convincing:
  - Baselines are weak for the proposed setting. Comparing multi-resolution LRTFS to single-resolution IS-NMF is not equitable; multiresolution NMF or multiframe variants, complex NMF/phase-aware NMF, supervised NMF with temporal continuity/smoothness priors, or structured sparse synthesis (e.g., MCA) are more appropriate baselines. The 0.6 dB gain over a single-window IS-NMF is modest and may be within variance.
  - The experiments are small-scale (10 speech mixtures) with no statistical testing, no runtime/memory report, and no ablations (e.g., single-resolution vs multi-resolution within LRTFS, effect of K, sensitivity to λ or β, or MJLE vs MMLE when feasible).
  - λ is treated as a hyperparameter, tuned for best SNR in the music example, and annealed to near-zero in speech enhancement. This weakens the claimed probabilistic grounding and raises concerns about overfitting or optimistic tuning. The paper notes that ML updates for λ performed poorly but offers no principled alternative selection scheme.
- Methodological gaps:
  - The EM construction requires λI − βΦΦ* to be PSD; while the β ≤ λ/δΦ condition is stated, the algorithm also updates λ (and in practice fixes or anneals it). It is unclear how this constraint is enforced across iterations, and what happens if violated.
  - Convergence guarantees are not discussed beyond monotonicity for certain steps; the λ update used in MJLE is heuristic (not the exact EM update), and the IS-NMF subproblem only reaches local minima.
  - MMLE, arguably the more statistically robust route, is declared impractical and deferred to future work. This limits the contribution to a variant of joint ML with heuristic choices.
- Novelty is incremental:
  - Modeling synthesis coefficients with Gaussian priors and low-rank variance is a natural variant of GCM/IS-NMF and SBL with tied hyperparameters. The multi-resolution extension (stacked frames) is straightforward and has close analogs in TF dictionary learning and MCA.
- Clarity and reproducibility issues:
  - Some notational slips (e.g., Section 4 uses Nc([W H]fn) without the mean 0). Real-signal Hermitian constraints are largely hand-waved.
  - Reference list is incomplete (missing years/venues for many entries). Figures are referenced but not deeply analyzed.
  - Training protocol details (e.g., how Wtrain was learned, regularization, initialization, stopping) are sparse; code/data availability is not stated.

Quality
Technically competent overall, but key choices (λ handling, β constraint) are not fully justified, and the more rigorous MMLE path is not operational. Claims are supported by limited experiments with small margins over a basic NMF baseline.

Clarity
The core model and MJLE algorithm are described clearly. However, some notation/typos and incomplete references detract. More detail on implementation choices and constraints would aid reproducibility.

Originality
Moderate. The idea of imposing low-rank structure on synthesis coefficients and stacking frames is a reasonable variation on established probabilistic TF models and SBL; the main novelty is in the specific factor-variance prior over synthesis coefficients and the associated EM workflow.

Significance
Currently limited by the empirical scope and modest gains. Potential impact in audio is plausible if stronger, fairer comparisons and scalable MMLE are developed.

Questions for the authors
- How sensitive are results to λ and β? Can you provide an automatic, principled selection (evidence maximization, SURE, or cross-validation) rather than tuning to best SNR?
- How does single-frame LRTFS compare to multi-frame LRTFS, controlling for total dictionary size? What is the gain attributable to multiresolution vs the synthesis modeling choice?
- Can you compare against multiresolution NMF baselines and phase-aware NMF? What about MCA or structured sparse synthesis with learned dictionaries?
- Do you enforce the λI − βΦΦ* PSD condition during iterations? What happens if it is violated?
- Any small-scale demonstration of MMLE (e.g., short signals) to substantiate the claimed robustness advantages?
- Runtime and memory usage vs IS-NMF and OMLSA?

Minor comments
- Fix notation in Section 4: α·,fn ∼ Nc(0, [W·H·]fn).
- Clarify handling of real-valued signals and Hermitian symmetry.
- Complete bibliographic entries and ensure all cited methods are properly referenced.

Recommendation
Lean toward rejection in the current form. The modeling idea is reasonable and the algorithm is practical, but the paper lacks rigorous, fair empirical validation against established multi-resolution and phase-aware baselines, relies on tuned hyperparameters that undermine the probabilistic story, and provides only modest gains on small test sets. Strengthening the empirical section, clarifying methodological details, and providing at least a small-scale MMLE study would substantially improve the case for acceptance. Confidence: high.
