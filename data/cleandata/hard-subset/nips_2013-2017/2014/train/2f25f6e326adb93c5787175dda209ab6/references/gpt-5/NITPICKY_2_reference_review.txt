Summary
This paper proposes Low-Rank Time-Frequency Synthesis (LRTFS), a probabilistic time-domain generative model in which a signal x(t) is synthesized from time-frequency atoms φfn(t) with complex Gaussian synthesis coefficients αfn whose variances follow a low-rank structure vfn = [WH]fn. This contrasts with the Gaussian Composite Model (GCM), which imposes low-rank structure on STFT analysis coefficients. Two estimation strategies are given: (i) maximum joint likelihood estimation (MJLE) via an EM algorithm inspired by Figueiredo & Nowak’s variable-splitting, avoiding large matrix inversions; and (ii) maximum marginal likelihood estimation (MMLE) akin to SBL that marginalizes α but is computationally heavy. A multi-resolution extension stacks multiple frames (e.g., short/long Gabor frames). Experiments on hybrid music decomposition and semi-supervised speech enhancement show modest SNR improvements over IS-NMF and OMLSA.

Strengths
- Clear conceptual contribution: a synthesis-domain probabilistic model with low-rank variance, bridging NMF-like structure with time-domain generative modeling and implicitly handling phase.
- EM algorithm for MJLE is computationally practical (no large inversions), using only Φ/Φ* operations and standard IS-NMF updates.
- Multi-resolution extension is straightforward and attractive for audio (transient vs tonal).
- Relation to SBL is insightful; MMLE formulation aligns with type-II ML principles even if not scalable here.

Weaknesses and detailed concerns
Technical correctness/derivations:
- Eq. (11): The decomposition −log p(α|W,H) as DIS(|α|^2|v) + log(|α|^2) + const is algebraically equivalent to the Gaussian prior (since DIS(s|v)+log s = s/v + log v − 1). However, the text claims this “unexpected” log term “favors sparse solutions.” That is misleading: the net objective is exactly the Gaussian prior; no additional sparsity beyond Gaussian is introduced. Please correct this interpretation.
- EM architecture (Sec. 3.1): You state “The hidden variable posterior simplifies to p(z|x,θJL) = p(z|x,λ),” but Eq. (16) uses α(i) in the posterior mean. As written, the statement is contradictory. Clarify which posterior quantities depend on α and which depend only on λ, β, Φ.
- Choice of β and δΦ: The algorithm requires β ≤ λ/δΦ for positive semidefiniteness. There is no guidance on selecting β, computing δΦ, or step-size sensitivity. This affects convergence and reproducibility.
- MMLE E-step indexing: In Eq. (25), v(i) is defined as E{|α|^2|x, v(i), λ(i)}; conditioning should use v(i−1). As written, it is circular.
- Multi-resolution model (Sec. 4): Eq. (29)–(30) omit the zero-mean: Nc([W H]fn) should be Nc(0, [W H]fn). Eq. (32) uses z(i)fn without the layer index ` (likely z(i)`,fn).
- Real signals: While Sec. 2.1 mentions Hermitian symmetry for real signals, experiments do not document how this is enforced in implementation.

Novelty and relation to prior work:
- The move from analysis-domain low-rank modeling (GCM) to synthesis-domain is interesting, but the paper overstates that multi-resolution “was not possible” in traditional NMF settings. Multi-window and multi-resolution NMF variants exist (e.g., stacking spectrograms/multi-scale dictionaries). Related work on complex/phase-aware NMF and multiresolution NMF should be referenced and contrasted.
- Connections to earlier synthesis-domain Gaussian scale mixtures (e.g., wavelet-domain GSMs) and structured sparsity models could be better situated.

Experiments and evaluation:
- Hyperparameter tuning: Sec. 5.1 selects λ to maximize output SNR on the test example; this is not a fair or reproducible protocol. In Sec. 5.2, λ is annealed to a negligible value via a warm restart; the schedule is unspecified. These choices bias results and limit reproducibility.
- Baselines: The IS-NMF baseline uses a single middle-length window, whereas the proposed method uses two resolutions; this is not apples-to-apples. A stronger and fairer baseline would be multi-resolution NMF on magnitudes (stacked features) and/or complex NMF. Also, a variant of GCM with similar multi-resolution features would be appropriate.
- Metrics: Only SNR improvements are reported, without statistical variability. For speech enhancement, perceptual metrics (PESQ/STOI/SI-SDR) would be more informative.
- Scale: The improvements over IS-NMF (6.6 dB vs 6.0 dB) are modest; ablations (single-resolution LRTFS, single-layer only, effect of MJLE vs MMLE on small problems) are missing, so the source of gains (synthesis vs multi-resolution vs dictionary supervision) is unclear.
- Computational cost: No wall-clock timings or complexity vs. NMF. MMLE is declared impractical; MJLE scalability beyond the toy problems is not quantified.

Clarity and organization:
- Some inconsistencies in notation (Nc(·), vectorization/indexing by m vs (f, n), missing zero means).
- The claim that MJLE decreases the negative log-likelihood each iteration while using an inexact λ update is made by appeal to [6]; a brief derivation or explicit conditions would help.
- Figures are referenced but not described in enough detail to reproduce; no code or parameter details (dictionary normalization, K selection, initialization, number of iterations) are given.

Formatting and references:
- References are incomplete or malformed (missing years/volume/pages for many entries). Please fix.
- Minor typos: “hermitian” → “Hermitian”; “hit-hat” → “hi-hat”; “other the 10 test signals” → “over the 10 test signals”; “mentionned” → “mentioned”; “Nc([WaHa]fn)” missing mean; several equation indices and subscripts inconsistent.

Pros
- Conceptually clean synthesis-domain probabilistic model with low-rank variance.
- Practical EM algorithm avoiding large inversions.
- Natural multi-resolution extension, relevant to audio.

Cons
- Misinterpretation of the objective’s sparsity properties; several technical/notation errors.
- Limited, partially unfair experimental validation; modest gains; weak baselines.
- Reproducibility concerns (λ tuning/annealing unspecified, no code).
- Overclaims regarding multi-resolution novelty; incomplete related work.
- Poorly formatted/incomplete references.

Recommendation: Reject
While the idea is interesting and potentially impactful for audio, the paper contains multiple technical and clarity issues, fairness concerns in evaluation, and significant formatting/referencing problems. I encourage the authors to correct the derivations/claims (esp. Eq. 11 interpretation), strengthen and fairly benchmark experiments (multi-resolution NMF/complex NMF baselines, proper tuning protocols, perceptual metrics, ablations), document implementation choices (β, λ schedule, real-signal handling), and fix the references.
