Summary
This paper proposes a generative, time-domain model—Low-Rank Time-Frequency Synthesis (LRTFS)—where the waveform x(t) is synthesized from a time-frequency dictionary Φ with complex Gaussian synthesis coefficients α whose variances are constrained to have a low-rank structure WH. This transposes the low-rank Gaussian Composite Model (GCM) from analysis coefficients (STFT) to synthesis coefficients. Two estimation strategies are discussed: (1) a maximum joint likelihood (MJLE) approach using an EM algorithm adapted from Figueiredo & Nowak to avoid large matrix inversions, and (2) a maximum marginal likelihood approach (MMLE) à la SBL that is not scalable for the intended audio dimensions. The framework naturally extends to multi-resolution by stacking multiple frames. Experiments on a short music hybrid decomposition and semi-supervised speech enhancement show modest SNR gains over IS-NMF and OMLSA.

Relation to prior work
- The model is closely related to GCM/IS-NMF (Fevotte et al.): it differs mainly by imposing the low-rank structure on synthesis coefficients instead of analysis coefficients. The estimation includes an IS-NMF step on |α|^2 and a Wiener-like shrinkage, which mirrors “NMF + Wiener filtering” known in GCM.
- The MJLE EM architecture follows Figueiredo & Nowak (wavelet denoising) and the MMLE derivation follows SBL (Tipping; Wipf & Rao). Multi-resolution modeling by stacking dictionaries is standard in MCA/hybrid models (Daudet & Torresani; Elad et al.), albeit here the structure is low-rank rather than sparse.
- Complex/phase-aware NMF and related time-domain generative formulations (e.g., complex NMF, phase-constrained models) seem relevant and should be discussed and compared.

Strengths
- Clear, principled generative formulation connecting NMF/GCM, SBL, and time-domain synthesis.
- The MJLE EM algorithm avoids expensive inversions, is implementable with existing TF toolboxes, and each step is interpretable (shrinkage/Wiener-like update + IS-NMF on |α|^2).
- Multi-resolution extension is straightforward and computationally feasible.
- The connection showing that −log p(α|v) can be written as IS(|α|^2|v) + log(|α|^2) is insightful and explains the implicit sparsity pressure.

Weaknesses
- Novelty/significance: Moving the low-rank variance from analysis to synthesis is incremental; the estimation essentially alternates “coefficient shrinkage” and IS-NMF on |α|^2. The multi-resolution stacking is a well-trodden idea in MCA; the claimed advantage over traditional NMF is under-argued (multi-resolution NMF or multi-window GCM are feasible).
- Empirical validation is limited and not rigorous:
  - Very small-scale experiments; no statistical significance or variability reported.
  - Hyperparameter λ is tuned to maximize output SNR on the test in the music demo; for speech, λ is annealed to near zero. This undermines fairness and generality.
  - Baselines are weak: IS-NMF uses a single window; no multi-resolution NMF/GCM, no complex/phase-aware NMF, no modern speech enhancement baselines (e.g., Wiener-PLCA variants). Gains over IS-NMF are small (~0.6 dB) and may not be significant.
  - Metrics restricted to SNR; no perceptual (PESQ, STOI) or artifact measures; no runtime or memory.
- The “more principled” MMLE approach is not usable at the advertised scales; leaving scalability to future work weakens the technical contribution.
- Theoretical guarantees are minimal: no convergence guarantees beyond heuristic monotonicity claims; the λ update is acknowledged as not exact EM; no identifiability or consistency results.
- Clarity/rigor issues:
  - Some notation slips (e.g., missing mean in Nc in Eqs. 29–30), and reference formatting is incomplete.
  - The claim that traditional NMF “cannot” do multi-resolution is overstated; concatenated multi-window features or multi-dictionary NMF exist and should be engaged with more directly.
  - The role and selection of β, and computing/approximating δΦ, are not fully specified.

Quality
- The modeling and derivations are mostly sound, and the MJLE algorithm is coherent. However, the empirical support is weak and comparisons insufficient. The MMLE section is incomplete as a practical method.

Clarity
- Generally readable and well-structured; connections to GCM/SBL are helpful. Needs cleaner notation, complete references, and more explicit algorithmic settings (β, δΦ, stopping criteria, runtime).

Originality
- Moderate. The synthesis-side low-rank Gaussian model and the EM construction are natural extensions of known ideas. The multi-resolution extension is straightforward.

Significance
- Modest at present. The empirical gains over strong, fair baselines are not convincingly demonstrated, and the scalable MMLE is left for future work.

Questions for the authors
- How does LRTFS compare to multi-resolution NMF/GCM or complex/phase-aware NMF on the same tasks?
- How sensitive are results to λ and β? Can you provide a principled selection or cross-validation protocol not peeking at test SNR?
- What are the runtimes and memory vs IS-NMF and MCA? Does convergence exhibit monotonic decrease with the inexact λ update?
- Can you provide ablations: single-resolution vs multi-resolution; low-rank vs unstructured variances; effect of K?
- Can you include perceptual metrics and confidence intervals?

Pros
- Principled generative model and EM algorithm with interpretable updates.
- Natural multi-resolution extension.
- Bridges NMF/GCM and SBL perspectives.

Cons
- Incremental novelty; weak empirical validation; small and potentially unfair gains.
- MMLE not scalable; missing comparisons to closer/stronger baselines.
- Hyperparameter tuning on test; limited metrics; incomplete references.

Recommendation
- Score: 5 (weak reject)
- Confidence: 0.7

With stronger and fairer experimental validation (multi-resolution NMF/GCM baselines, phase-aware models, robust hyperparameter protocols, perceptual metrics, runtime), and either a scalable MMLE or a more compelling MJLE advantage, this line could merit acceptance. As is, the contribution is interesting but insufficiently validated for NeurIPS.
