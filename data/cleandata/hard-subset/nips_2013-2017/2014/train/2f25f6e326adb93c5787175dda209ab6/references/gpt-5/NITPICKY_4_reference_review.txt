Summary and relation to prior work:
This paper proposes a generative probabilistic model for raw signals that imposes a low-rank structure on the variances of complex time-frequency synthesis coefficients. Unlike the Gaussian Composite Model (GCM) of [Févotte et al., Neural Computation] which models the STFT analysis coefficients yfn ∼ Nc(0, [WH]fn), the proposed low-rank time-frequency synthesis (LRTFS) model writes x = Φα + e with αfn ∼ Nc(0, [WH]fn) and e ∼ Nc(0, λ), thereby pushing the low-rank modeling down to the synthesis layer. The authors provide two estimation strategies: (1) a maximum joint likelihood estimation (MJLE) solved by an EM algorithm inspired by Figueiredo & Nowak’s “hidden z” architecture, yielding iterative Wiener-like shrinkage of α and IS-NMF updates for W, H; and (2) a maximum marginal likelihood estimation (MMLE) akin to SBL “type-II” ML, which is acknowledged as computationally impractical in typical audio settings. They also extend the model to multi-resolution by stacking multiple frames and show experiments on music layer decomposition and speech enhancement.

The work relates to, and is positioned against, NMF/IS-NMF on magnitude spectra, probabilistic models of complex STFTs (GCM, complex NMF), sparse Bayesian learning (SBL), and hybrid/morphological models in audio. The idea of variance low-rank structure on synthesis coefficients appears to be a novel perspective relative to prior analysis-coefficient models; multi-resolution via multiple frames with a joint probabilistic model is in the spirit of prior hybrid/sparse-coding work, but here with a low-rank variance prior.

Strengths:
- Conceptual novelty: modeling the synthesis coefficients’ variance via WH is a clean, phase-aware generative alternative to analysis-side models.
- EM algorithm for MJLE is elegant and computationally practical for long signals (no large matrix inversions; Φ and Φ* can be implemented efficiently).
- Multi-resolution extension integrates naturally and is easy to implement in the same EM framework.
- Clear connection to SBL and a useful contrast: variance tying via low-rank constraints vs free variances in SBL.
- The iterative form (gradient-like step on α followed by Wiener-type shrinkage and IS-NMF) is intuitive and may interest practitioners familiar with shrinkage and IS-NMF.

Weaknesses:

Quality (technical soundness and empirical support):
- Estimation of λ is problematic. Although update rules are provided, all experiments treat λ as a hand-tuned hyperparameter (music) or annealed “to negligible” (speech) without a specified schedule. Selecting λ to maximize output SNR on test data is an oracle-like procedure and undermines the validity of the comparison.
- The MMLE section contributes little practically: the provided algorithm is acknowledged as non-scalable and is not used in experiments. No approximate scalable MMLE (e.g., CG solves, diagonally preconditioned iterations, Krylov/subspace methods, or message passing approximations) is attempted.
- Baselines are weak/unbalanced. The IS-NMF baseline uses a single-resolution STFT (intermediate window) and no pre-trained speech dictionary, whereas the proposed method uses multi-resolution frames and a 500-component pre-trained speech dictionary. OMLSA is a classical baseline but again mismatched in supervision/priors. This makes the 0.6 dB advantage over IS-NMF difficult to interpret.
- Metrics are limited to SNR. No perceptual metrics (PESQ/STOI) or user studies are reported for speech enhancement, and no separation metrics (e.g., SDR/SIR/SAR) or ablations (e.g., effect of K, window sizes, λ schedule) are provided.
- The claim that the “log(|α|^2)” term “favors sparse solutions” is potentially misleading. This term arises from an algebraic decomposition of the Gaussian prior −log p(α|v) into DIS(|α|^2|v) + log(|α|^2) + const; it does not introduce an additional sparsity prior beyond Gaussianity. The observed shrinkage in Eq. (17) is ridge-like (v/(v+β)), not sparsity-inducing.
- Component reconstruction in Sec. 2.3 via E{αk|α̂,W,H} is a heuristic; the true MMSE component estimate would be E{αk|x, W,H}, which the authors note is intractable at T×T inversion scale but could be approximated iteratively. No validation of the heuristic vs the true posterior mean is provided.

Clarity (writing/organization/reproducibility):
- Several notational inconsistencies/typos:
  - Eq. (19) uses ‖·‖2F for a vector; should be ‖·‖22. Elsewhere ‖·‖22 is used.
  - Sec. 4, Eqs. (29)-(30): Nc([W H]fn) is missing the mean/variance parameters; should be Nc(0, [WH]fn).
  - Sec. 4, Eq. (32): z(i)fn appears without the layer subscript `; likely z(i)`,fn.
  - Sec. 3.1: choice of β (≤ λ/δΦ) is not operationalized; δΦ needs to be estimated (e.g., power iteration). No guidance or stopping criteria provided.
  - Hermitian vs hermitian; several typos: “mentionned,” “hit-hat” (should be hi-hat), “companion EM,” “sum of latent components” phrasing.
- Derivations are sketched; the EM Q functions and the exact form of the λ update that “still decreases the NLL” are not shown (references are fine, but key steps could be summarized).
- Reproducibility: No details on initialization, iteration counts, convergence monitoring, runtime, or code availability.

Originality:
- The synthesis-side low-rank variance model is a fresh angle compared to GCM and complex NMF variants that model analysis coefficients. The EM structure is adapted from known tools (Figueiredo & Nowak; IS-NMF multiplicative updates), and the multi-resolution extension is a straightforward stacking. Related work on phase-aware/complex NMF and multiresolution NMF/PLCA could be discussed more comprehensively.

Significance:
- Potentially impactful for phase-aware, multi-resolution modeling. However, the present experimental evidence is limited and comparisons are not sufficiently controlled to demonstrate a clear advantage. The scalability of MJLE is attractive, but without more extensive evaluations (including longer signals and broader tasks), the practical significance remains uncertain.

References and formatting:
- The reference list is severely incomplete/inconsistent (years, venues, volumes, pages often missing; inconsistent capitalization and punctuation; hyphenation errors). This does not meet NeurIPS standards and impedes verification.
- Some key related works (phase-aware NMF/PLCA, complex Gaussian models of STFT beyond [3], multiresolution NMF) are either missing or under-cited.

Pros:
- Novel synthesis-side low-rank variance model with a simple and efficient EM algorithm.
- Natural and practical multi-resolution extension.
- Clear connection to SBL/Gaussian modeling; intuitive shrinkage interpretation.

Cons:
- Hyperparameter λ tuned with knowledge of test SNR; unclear annealing schedule; weak empirical rigor.
- MMLE contribution is theoretical only and non-scalable; no approximate scalable alternative.
- Baselines and experimental design are not rigorously matched; small gains.
- Limited metrics, no ablations, no perceptual evaluation; reproducibility details missing.
- Numerous typos/notation issues and poor references formatting.

Recommendation: Reject.

While the core idea is interesting and the MJLE algorithm is elegant and potentially useful, the cumulative methodological, experimental, and presentation shortcomings (especially the tuning of λ on test outcomes, weak/mismatched baselines, and inadequate references/formatting) prevent me from recommending acceptance at NeurIPS in its current form. I encourage the authors to strengthen the empirical section with fair baselines, proper hyperparameter selection, broader metrics, ablations, and to revise the manuscript for clarity and reference quality.

Confidence: High.
