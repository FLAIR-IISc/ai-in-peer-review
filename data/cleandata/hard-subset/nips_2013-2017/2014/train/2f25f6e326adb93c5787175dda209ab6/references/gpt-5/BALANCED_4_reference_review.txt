Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes a generative time-domain model, Low-Rank Time-Frequency Synthesis (LRTFS), where a signal x(t) is synthesized as a linear combination of time-frequency atoms with complex Gaussian synthesis coefficients whose variances are tied through a low-rank structure WH. This departs from the Gaussian Composite Model (GCM) and conventional NMF approaches that model the STFT magnitudes or power directly: instead of imposing a low-rank structure on analysis coefficients, LRTFS imposes it on synthesis coefficients and models the raw signal, enabling multi-resolution representations by stacking multiple frames. Two estimation strategies are presented: (i) maximum joint likelihood estimation (MJLE) via an EM algorithm using an auxiliary variable (following Figueiredo & Nowak), which interleaves Wiener-like shrinkage of α and IS-NMF updates on |α|^2; and (ii) maximum marginal likelihood estimation (MMLE) in the spirit of sparse Bayesian learning (SBL), where α is marginalized, but whose naive implementation is computationally prohibitive for large signals. A multi-resolution extension is derived by stacking multiple frames and running essentially the same EM scheme on each layer. Experiments on hybrid tonal/transient music decomposition and semi-supervised speech enhancement show modest gains over an IS-NMF baseline and OMLSA.

Relative to prior work, this paper connects the probabilistic NMF/GCM line (e.g., Fevotte et al.) with SBL (Tipping; Wipf & Rao) by treating synthesis coefficients as Gaussian with low-rank variances, and leverages EM architectures previously used for wavelet denoising (Figueiredo & Nowak). The multi-resolution modeling relates to hybrid sparse representations (e.g., MCA, Daudet & Torresani; Elad et al.) but with a low-rank, probabilistic structure rather than sparsity per se.

Strengths
- Conceptual contribution: a clear, principled generative model in the time domain that ties variance across synthesis coefficients via WH, bridging NMF/GCM with SBL-style hierarchical modeling.
- Algorithmic insight: the MJLE-EM scheme is simple and efficient, requiring only analysis/synthesis operations and an IS-NMF subproblem; the connection to Wiener filtering and IS-NMF as “first iteration” is illuminating.
- Natural multi-resolution extension: stacking frames with different resolutions is straightforward and well-motivated for audio, where transients and sustained components require different time-frequency trade-offs.
- Component reconstruction: the model supports reconstruction of latent components via Wiener-like formulas on α, analogous to GCM, which is useful for source separation tasks.
- Promising empirical indications: in speech enhancement, the proposed approach yields a small but consistent SNR improvement over an IS-NMF baseline with a single window and over OMLSA.

Weaknesses and questions
Quality
- Experimental scope is limited. Only two tasks are shown, with small-scale, single-channel data, and evaluation uses SNR only. For speech enhancement, perceptual metrics (e.g., STOI, PESQ) would be more convincing, and statistical variability across speakers/noises should be reported.
- Hyperparameter handling is problematic. In 5.1, λ is selected to maximize output SNR (not feasible at test time). In 5.2, λ is annealed to near zero; rationale and sensitivity are not studied. More principled strategies (e.g., cross-validation, evidence maximization) or robust defaults are needed.
- The MMLE route is largely “future work,” with no scalable implementation or empirical comparison. Given the motivation from SBL (fewer local minima, robustness), the paper would be stronger with either a scalable approximation (e.g., message passing, diagonal/low-rank approximations, Krylov solvers) or a more thorough justification for focusing on MJLE.
- Baselines are limited. For audio, comparisons to stronger NMF variants (e.g., temporal continuity/smoothness constraints, complex/phase-aware NMF, PLCA) and to multi-window NMF baselines would better isolate the contribution of time-domain modeling and multi-resolution stacking.
- Complexity/timing not reported. Although the algorithm is “computationally friendly,” wall-clock times, memory use, and scaling with T, M, K, and number of frames should be quantified.

Clarity
- Generally well-written, but some implementation-critical details are missing:
  - Choice of β and ensuring β ≤ λ/δΦ: how is δΦ computed or bounded in practice for large frames?
  - Initialization for W, H, and α; stopping criteria; handling of scale ambiguities across layers.
  - Real-valued signal handling via Hermitian symmetry: how implemented in code to avoid redundancy?
- The assertion that the λ update “still decreases the negative log-likelihood” could use a brief justification or citation (beyond [6]) specific to this setting; empirical behavior of the exact vs. approximate update would be informative.

Originality
- The key novelty is placing the low-rank structure on synthesis coefficients and operating in the time domain with multi-resolution frames. While related ideas exist (GCM on STFT, SBL for synthesis sparsity, hybrid multi-resolution sparse models), the combination and the derived EM scheme are original and interesting for the NeurIPS audience.

Significance
- Potentially impactful for audio and broader inverse problems, but current empirical evidence is modest. Demonstrating clear, consistent gains over strong phase-aware baselines or showing advantages in tasks where phase and temporal consistency matter (e.g., music source separation, inpainting) would raise the significance.

Pros and cons for acceptance
Pros:
- Clear, principled model bridging established lines of work (NMF/GCM and SBL).
- Simple, scalable EM algorithm with interpretable updates and multi-resolution capability.
- Promising results and qualitative component interpretability.

Cons:
- Limited and somewhat ad hoc experimental evaluation and hyperparameter selection.
- No scalable MMLE implementation despite being a central motivation.
- Missing comparisons to stronger baselines and lack of timing/complexity reporting.

Suggestions for improvement
- Add stronger baselines (e.g., complex NMF/phase-constrained NMF; NMF with temporal smoothness; multi-window NMF) and perceptual metrics for speech.
- Provide ablations: single- vs multi-resolution LRTFS; effect of λ and β; role of K; fixed vs learned noise dictionaries.
- Include runtime and memory comparisons to IS-NMF on comparable frames.
- Explore scalable approximations to MMLE (e.g., conjugate gradients with matrix-free multiplies, diagonal/low-rank covariance approximations, expectation propagation or AMP variants).
- Clarify implementation details and release code to enhance reproducibility.

Recommendation: Weak reject
The paper presents a neat and potentially useful modeling/algorithmic contribution with a natural multi-resolution extension, but the empirical validation and comparison to strong baselines are not yet sufficient for NeurIPS. With a more thorough experimental section, clearer hyperparameter handling, and either a scalable MMLE or stronger justification for MJLE, this could become a solid contribution.

Confidence: Moderate.
