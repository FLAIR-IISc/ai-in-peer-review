## Review of Low-Rank Time-Frequency Synthesis

This paper introduces a novel generative model for signals, dubbed Low-Rank Time-Frequency Synthesis (LRTFS), which extends the Gaussian Composite Model (GCM) by directly modeling the signal itself rather than its STFT. The core idea – imposing a low-rank structure on the *variance* of synthesis coefficients – is compelling and a logical next step from existing probabilistic latent factor models in signal processing. The connection to sparse Bayesian learning is insightful, highlighting the difference in variance structure as a key distinction. The extension to multi-resolution representations is particularly exciting, addressing a limitation of traditional NMF-based approaches.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The direct synthesis approach and extension to raw signal modeling represent a significant departure from existing methods focusing on spectral decomposition. The multi-resolution extension is a genuinely novel contribution with potential for practical impact.
* **Theoretical Soundness:** The derivation of the LRTFS model and the connection to sparse Bayesian learning are clearly presented and theoretically justified. The proposed EM algorithms seem well-motivated and follow established procedures.
* **Potential Significance:** The ability to model signals directly, coupled with multi-resolution analysis, opens doors for more accurate representations and potentially superior performance in tasks like music decomposition and speech enhancement. The model’s generative nature provides a strong foundation for future work.
* **Clear Writing:** The paper is generally well-written and logically organized. Equations are clearly presented, and the notation is consistent.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Empirical Validation:** While the experiments demonstrate promising results, they feel somewhat limited in scope. The jazz decomposition example is illustrative but lacks a quantitative evaluation. The speech enhancement results are better, but the SNR improvements are marginal compared to established baselines. A more extensive comparison with state-of-the-art methods across a wider range of datasets would strengthen the claims.
* **Scalability Concerns (MMLE):** The acknowledged scalability issues with the MMLE approach are a concern, especially given its potential benefits. While the authors are upfront about this limitation, it hinders a complete assessment of the method.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The reliance on careful hyperparameter tuning (particularly λ) without a robust method for its automated selection could limit practical applicability. The mention of gradually decreasing λ suggests a degree of ad-hoc procedure.
* **Lack of Theoretical Justification for λ Update:** While presented as improving convergence, the λ update rule deviates from standard EM derivation. More justification would be valuable.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper introduces a fundamentally new and promising approach to signal modeling.
* The theoretical contributions are solid and well-presented.
* The multi-resolution extension is particularly innovative and addresses a significant limitation of existing methods.
* Despite limited validation, the preliminary results are encouraging.
* The paper clearly identifies future research directions.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

* The empirical validation is somewhat weak, lacking a thorough comparison with state-of-the-art methods.
* The scalability issues with MMLE are a significant concern.
* The hyperparameter sensitivity and ad-hoc λ update rule limit practical applicability.
* Incremental improvements in speech enhancement alone do not fully justify publication at NIPS.

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a bold and potentially impactful new direction in signal modeling. While the empirical validation could be stronger and scalability is a concern, the novelty and theoretical contributions are significant. I am inclined to accept this paper, prioritizing its innovative nature and potential for future research over its current limitations. The potential to move beyond spectral decomposition and directly synthesize signals with a low-rank time-frequency structure is a compelling contribution to the field. The introduction of a flexible multi-resolution framework is a noteworthy achievement.  I believe the ideas presented here warrant further exploration and could lead to substantial advancements in signal processing and related areas. I recommend acceptance, with the caveat that the authors address the scalability concerns and perform more extensive validation in future work.
