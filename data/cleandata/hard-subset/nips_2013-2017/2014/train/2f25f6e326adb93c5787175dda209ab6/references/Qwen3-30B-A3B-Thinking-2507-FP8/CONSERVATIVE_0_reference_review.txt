

# Review of "Low-Rank Time-Frequency Synthesis"

This paper proposes a "Low-Rank Time-Frequency Synthesis" (LRTFS) model that extends traditional NMF approaches by modeling the raw signal directly rather than its STFT, as done in the Gaussian Composite Model (GCM) referenced in the introduction. The authors present a generative model where the signal is expressed as a sum of synthesis coefficients with a low-rank variance structure, and introduce two estimation approaches: Maximum Joint Likelihood Estimation (MJLE) and Maximum Marginal Likelihood Estimation (MMLE). The paper demonstrates the model on two audio tasks: music decomposition and speech enhancement.

**Strengths:** The paper is well-written with clear mathematical formulations and provides a logical connection to existing work (particularly the GCM and sparse Bayesian learning). The multi-resolution extension is conceptually appealing and well-motivated for audio applications. The MJLE approach is computationally efficient without matrix inversions, which is a practical advantage.

**Weaknesses:** Despite the promising concept, the paper has several significant shortcomings that undermine its contribution.

First, the novelty is overstated. The core idea of modeling synthesis coefficients with low-rank structure is a natural extension of the GCM (Eq. 1), which already models the STFT as low-rank. The authors claim their approach "fills in this ultimate gap" by modeling the raw signal, but they fail to convincingly demonstrate how this leads to meaningful improvements over existing approaches. The paper cites relevant work but doesn't sufficiently differentiate LRTFS from existing methods like the hybrid decomposition approaches mentioned in [8, 10].

Second, the experimental validation is inadequate. The paper presents only two experiments (music decomposition and speech enhancement) with limited baselines. In the speech enhancement experiment, the proposed method achieves 6.6 dB SNR improvement compared to 6 dB for IS-NMF, but this marginal gain (0.6 dB) doesn't justify the complexity of the proposed method. The paper doesn't compare to more recent state-of-the-art speech enhancement methods, and the "baseline" IS-NMF is applied with an intermediate window size, which may not be the most appropriate comparison.

Third, the paper lacks sufficient theoretical analysis. While the authors derive the EM algorithm for MJLE, they don't provide convergence guarantees or analysis of how the low-rank constraint affects the solution space. The paper acknowledges that MMLE is computationally intractable for large-scale audio applications (Section 3.2), but doesn't adequately address how this limitation affects the practical utility of their approach.

Fourth, the paper fails to demonstrate the claimed advantages of their method. They state that "modeling at lowest level" leads to "improved accuracy," but provide no evidence that LRTFS outperforms GCM or other direct signal modeling approaches in terms of reconstruction quality or downstream task performance. The multi-resolution capability is presented as a major strength, but the paper doesn't show how this translates to measurable improvements over existing multi-resolution methods.

Finally, the paper's claims about "opening doors to multi-resolution representations" are unsubstantiated. The multi-resolution extension (Section 4) appears to be a straightforward application of existing ideas to their framework, but the paper doesn't provide evidence that this approach offers significant advantages over established multi-resolution techniques in audio processing.

**Recommendation:** The paper presents a technically sound extension of existing time-frequency modeling approaches, but the claims of novelty and significance are not adequately supported by the evidence provided. The marginal improvements demonstrated in the experiments don't justify the complexity of the proposed method, especially given the computational limitations noted in Section 3.2. Without more comprehensive validation against state-of-the-art methods and stronger theoretical justification, the contribution appears incremental rather than transformative.

**Pro/Con for Acceptance:**

Pro:
- Well-structured and clearly written
- Presents a logical extension of existing work
- MJLE algorithm is computationally efficient
- Multi-resolution extension is conceptually interesting

Con:
- Novelty claims are overstated
- Experimental validation is insufficient and lacks appropriate baselines
- Marginal performance gains don't justify proposed complexity
- Fails to demonstrate clear advantages over existing methods
- MMLE infeasible for practical applications
- Limited theoretical analysis

This paper would be more compelling with more extensive experiments against state-of-the-art methods, stronger theoretical justification for the approach, and a clearer demonstration of how the proposed method advances the field beyond existing techniques. As currently presented, the contribution appears incremental rather than transformative, and the paper doesn't meet the high bar for significance expected at NIPS.
