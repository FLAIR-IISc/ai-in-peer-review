

# Review of "Low-Rank Time-Frequency Synthesis"

This paper proposes a "Low-Rank Time-Frequency Synthesis" (LRTFS) model that extends previous work on Gaussian Composite Models (GCM) by modeling the raw signal directly rather than its STFT. The authors build on established methods (NMF, SBL, GCM) to propose a generative model where the signal x(t) is expressed as a sum of synthesis coefficients αfn with low-rank variance structure. The key innovations claimed are: (1) modeling the raw signal (not just STFT), and (2) enabling natural multi-resolution representations.

The paper is well-structured and technically sound in its mathematical derivation. The connection to previous work (GCM, SBL) is appropriately established, and the EM algorithms for MJLE are presented with sufficient detail. The multi-resolution extension (Section 4) is a useful feature for audio applications where different components require different time-frequency resolutions.

However, I have significant concerns about the paper's quality and significance, particularly regarding the validation of the proposed approach. While the authors acknowledge that MMLE is computationally infeasible for audio applications (Section 3.2), they primarily rely on MJLE for their experiments. The experimental validation is insufficiently comprehensive to support their claims of superiority over established methods. 

Specifically, the speech enhancement experiment (Section 5.2) shows only marginal improvement (6.6 dB vs. 6 dB for IS-NMF) over a baseline that uses a single Gabor transform with intermediate window length. The authors do not compare against more recent state-of-the-art audio processing methods (e.g., deep learning-based approaches), nor do they provide ablation studies to isolate the contribution of the proposed low-rank structure versus the multi-resolution capability. The claimed "improved accuracy" in music decomposition (Section 5.1) is not quantitatively substantiated with metrics beyond visual inspection of spectrograms.

The paper's originality is questionable. The core idea of modeling raw signals with time-frequency structure builds directly on prior work (e.g., [8, 9, 10] cited in the paper), and the multi-resolution aspect has been explored previously in the audio processing literature. The authors do not sufficiently differentiate their contribution from these existing approaches, particularly since the key innovation (modeling raw signals) is limited to the audio domain without clear generalization potential.

Furthermore, the paper fails to address critical questions about practical applicability. The authors acknowledge that MMLE is computationally infeasible for large-scale audio applications, yet they don't provide a thorough comparison of MJLE's computational complexity versus established methods. The paper also lacks analysis of how the method scales with signal length or dimensionality, which is crucial for real-world deployment.

The significance of the work is limited by these validation gaps. While the multi-resolution capability is a useful feature for audio processing, the paper doesn't convincingly demonstrate that LRTFS provides a meaningful advancement over existing approaches. The marginal performance gains reported (6.6 dB vs 6 dB) may not justify the added complexity of the proposed method, particularly when compared to simpler alternatives like IS-NMF.

In summary, while the paper presents a technically sound extension of previous work, it falls short of NIPS standards in several key areas:

Strengths:
- Clear mathematical exposition
- Well-organized presentation
- Appropriate connection to prior work
- Useful multi-resolution extension for audio applications

Weaknesses:
- Insufficient experimental validation against established baselines
- Marginal performance improvements that don't clearly justify the new method
- Lack of comparison with state-of-the-art methods
- Failure to adequately differentiate from related work
- Limited discussion of computational scalability

I'm concerned that the paper presents a new method without sufficient evidence of its advantages over established approaches. The skepticism I maintain toward new methods without extensive validation is reinforced by the paper's limited experimental scope and lack of compelling evidence that LRTFS provides a significant advancement over current practice.

While the idea has potential, the paper as submitted does not meet the high standards for acceptance at NIPS. The authors should substantially strengthen their experimental validation by comparing against more recent methods, providing quantitative metrics for all results, and demonstrating clear advantages of their approach over simpler alternatives. Without these improvements, the paper's contribution remains incremental rather than transformative.
