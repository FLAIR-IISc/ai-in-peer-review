This paper proposes a low-rank time-frequency synthesis (LRTFS) model for signal reconstruction, extending previous work on probabilistic latent factor models like the Gaussian Composite Model (GCM). The core idea is to model the raw signal directly as a sum of low-rank components in the time-frequency domain, enabling multi-resolution representations. The authors present a maximum joint likelihood estimation (MJLE) approach with an EM algorithm and briefly discuss a maximum marginal likelihood estimation (MMLE) approach, though implementation limitations prevent thorough evaluation of the latter. The paper concludes with experiments on music decomposition and speech enhancement.

While the idea of directly synthesizing the signal, rather than operating on the STFT magnitude, is conceptually sound and potentially beneficial, the paper suffers from a multitude of minor flaws and a lack of thoroughness that, cumulatively, weaken its impact.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The direct signal synthesis approach and the extension to multi-resolution representations represent a conceptually novel contribution.
*   **Generative Modeling:** Framing the problem as a generative model offers a more principled approach than traditional NMF.
*   **Connection to SBL:** The link to Sparse Bayesian Learning (SBL) provides a useful perspective and potential avenues for future work.
*   **Clear Presentation of MJLE:** The EM algorithm for MJLE is clearly described.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Lack of Rigorous Justification:** The motivation for imposing a low-rank structure on the *synthesis* coefficients (α) rather than the analysis coefficients (as in the GCM) is not adequately justified. The paper vaguely suggests mirroring the assumptions in the time domain, but lacks a detailed explanation.
*   **Insufficient Detail in MMLE:** While MMLE is mentioned, the authors acknowledge its limited scalability. However, even a more detailed theoretical discussion of the challenges and potential solutions would have strengthened the paper. Simply stating it doesn’t scale isn’t sufficient.
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The sensitivity of the MJLE to the hyperparameter λ is acknowledged but not thoroughly investigated. The authors state they selected results based on output SNR, but the range of values tested and the rationale for the selection are not provided.
*   **Experimental Limitations:** The experiments, while demonstrating feasibility, are not sufficiently comprehensive.
    *   The music decomposition experiment lacks quantitative evaluation beyond a subjective assessment.
    *   The speech enhancement experiment compares LRTFS to only two baselines, and the improvement over the IS-NMF baseline is marginal (0.6dB). A more extensive comparison with state-of-the-art speech enhancement algorithms is needed.
    *   The parameters for the baseline methods are not explicitly stated, hindering reproducibility.
*   **Minor Technical Issues:**
    *   The notation is inconsistent. For example, the use of 'v' for both the vectorized WH and the variances is confusing.
    *   Equation (17) lacks clarity regarding the dimensions of the involved variables.
    *   The justification for the choice of Gabor frames (window size, overlap) is not provided.
    *   The claim that the update rule for λ is derived from the EM procedure, despite not being the standard derivation, is misleading. It should be explicitly stated that it’s a heuristic.
*   **Formatting issues:** The citation style is inconsistent, with some citations using brackets ([1]) and others appearing as inline references.
* **Missing comparison with GCM:** A direct comparison of LRTFS with the original GCM (Eq. 1) on the same datasets would have been essential to demonstrate the benefits of the proposed approach.



**Arguments for/against acceptance:**

**Against:**

*   Numerous minor technical flaws and inconsistencies.
*   Lack of thorough experimental evaluation.
*   Insufficient justification for key modeling choices.
*   Limited scalability of MMLE and lack of a comprehensive discussion of its challenges.

**For:**

*   The core idea of direct signal synthesis is novel.
*   The MJLE algorithm is clearly presented.
*   The paper demonstrates initial feasibility on two applications.

**Overall Assessment:**

While the paper presents a potentially interesting idea, the numerous shortcomings significantly detract from its overall quality. The lack of rigor, insufficient experimentation, and minor technical issues suggest that the work is not yet ready for publication in a highly selective venue like NIPS. The paper reads more like a preliminary study or proof-of-concept than a complete and polished contribution.

**Recommendation:** **Reject**.

The authors should address the weaknesses outlined above, conduct more thorough experiments, and improve the clarity and rigor of the presentation before resubmitting. A stronger emphasis on theoretical justification and a more comprehensive comparison with existing methods are crucial.
