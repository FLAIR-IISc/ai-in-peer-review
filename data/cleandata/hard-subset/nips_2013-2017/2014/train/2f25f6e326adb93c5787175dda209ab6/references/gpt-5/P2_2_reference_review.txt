Summary
The paper proposes Low-Rank Time-Frequency Synthesis (LRTFS), a generative model of raw time-domain signals that imposes a low-rank structure on the variances of complex synthesis coefficients over a time-frequency (t-f) frame. Unlike prior probabilistic NMF formulations that model the analysis coefficients (e.g., Gaussian Composite Model, GCM), LRTFS models α in x = Φα + e with αfn ∼ Nc(0, [WH]fn), thereby “bridging” synthesis modeling with low-rank time-frequency structure. Two estimation strategies are developed: (i) Maximum Joint Likelihood Estimation (MJLE) via an EM scheme leveraging the Figueiredo–Nowak trick to avoid large matrix inversions; the updates interleave a Wiener-like shrinkage on α and IS-NMF updates on |α|^2. (ii) Maximum Marginal Likelihood Estimation (MMLE), akin to “type-II” SBL, which is elegant but computationally prohibitive at audio scale. A multi-resolution extension allows multiple frames (e.g., short/long windows) to be combined with shared EM updates. Experiments on a toy music hybrid decomposition and semi-supervised speech enhancement show modest but consistent gains over IS-NMF and OMLSA, and qualitative interpretability of separated components.

Relation to prior work
- Extends NMF/GCM by moving the low-rank Gaussian variance prior from analysis STFT coefficients to synthesis coefficients of a redundant frame—novel relative to IS-NMF and phase-aware GCM.
- Connects to Sparse Bayesian Learning (SBL): same linear Gaussian form, but with variances tied via WH (low-rank) rather than free, so LRTFS is not sparsity inducing in the SBL sense, but enforces structured variance.
- Multi-resolution modeling builds on hybrid/morphological decompositions (Daudet, Torrésani) but replaces sparsity with low-rank Gaussian priors and a coherent generative likelihood.

Quality
- The modeling and derivations are technically sound. The decomposition of −log p(α|v) into DIS(|α|^2|v) + log |α|^2 (plus constants) is correct and clarifies the implicit sparsity-promoting log term. The EM updates for MJLE are reasonable: the E-step applies a gradient-like correction using Φ*, and the M-step combines Wiener shrinkage α ← v/(v+β) z with IS-NMF on |α|^2. The approach avoids matrix inversions and is scalable with FFT-based Φ operations.
- The MMLE derivation mirrors SBL and is correct, but the authors are candid that it is not currently scalable. That limits exploration of potential statistical benefits (fewer local minima) that MMLE could bring.
- Experimental validation is limited. The jazz demo is illustrative but small; λ is tuned for best SNR, raising fairness concerns. The speech enhancement study is stronger (10 utterances, pretrained speech dictionaries), but the performance gain over a reasonable IS-NMF baseline is modest (6.6 vs 6.0 dB SNR improvement). No run-time comparisons, variance across runs, or ablations (single- vs multi-resolution, analysis vs synthesis model) are reported. No comparison to phase-aware NMF/GCM baselines, which seems the most direct prior.
- Some notational slips exist (e.g., missing “0,” in Nc(0, [W H]fn) in Sec. 4), but they do not affect the main results.

Clarity
- Generally clear and well organized. The high-level motivations and contrasts with GCM/SBL are explained well. The EM steps are explicit and implementable with standard IS-NMF updates.
- Areas to improve: specify how β is chosen in practice and its effect on convergence; provide pseudocode and complexity per iteration; clarify frame normalization and overlap; describe stopping criteria; and detail how λ is set (or annealed) consistently across experiments to ensure fair comparisons.
- Figures and qualitative descriptions are helpful; more quantitative metrics (e.g., SDR/SDRi) would aid replicability.

Originality
- The core idea—low-rank variance priors on synthesis coefficients for a generative time-domain model—is novel relative to prevalent analysis/STFT-based NMF and GCM. The EM scheme that couples iterative shrinkage on α with IS-NMF on |α|^2 is an elegant algorithmic contribution.
- The multi-resolution extension is conceptually straightforward but practically important, and the proposed updates remain simple.

Significance
- Conceptually significant: it provides a principled synthesis-side probabilistic model that naturally handles multi-resolution frames, something not easily accommodated in traditional NMF pipelines. This could influence subsequent work in probabilistic modeling of redundant dictionaries and t-f representations.
- Empirical significance is modest in the current form: improvements over strong baselines are small, and experiments are limited in scope. Broader adoption would benefit from more extensive benchmarking and code.

Pros
- Clear, principled generative model that bridges NMF and synthesis modeling.
- Scalable MJLE algorithm with simple, well-motivated updates; no large matrix inversions.
- Natural, elegant multi-resolution extension with the same EM machinery.
- Explicit connections to SBL and GCM; good positioning in the literature.
- Promising qualitative decompositions; consistent, if modest, quantitative gains.

Cons
- Limited and small-scale experiments; λ tuning strategy may bias results.
- No direct comparison to phase-aware GCM or other time-domain generative approaches.
- MMLE not scalable; thus the most statistically appealing estimator is not demonstrated.
- Lack of ablations (e.g., single vs multi-resolution, effect of the log term, comparison to analysis-side modeling under identical settings).
- Some missing implementation details and notational typos.

Suggestions for authors
- Add comparisons to GCM/phase-aware NMF with Wiener filtering under single- and multi-resolution settings to isolate the benefit of synthesis vs analysis modeling.
- Report run-time, memory, and convergence behaviors; provide pseudocode and parameter settings (β, λ schedules).
- Include ablations (e.g., fix single frame to match IS-NMF) and more metrics (SDR/PESQ/STOI for speech).
- Discuss identifiability/scaling of W and H, and the role of the log(|α|^2) term empirically.

Overall assessment
A solid and well-motivated methodological contribution with a clean, scalable algorithm and a compelling multi-resolution extension. The empirical section is not yet strong enough for a definitive claim of state-of-the-art performance, but the conceptual advance is clear and relevant to the NeurIPS community interested in probabilistic latent factor models and signal processing. I lean toward weak accept, contingent on strengthening empirical comparisons and clarifying hyperparameter choices.

Confidence: medium-high.
