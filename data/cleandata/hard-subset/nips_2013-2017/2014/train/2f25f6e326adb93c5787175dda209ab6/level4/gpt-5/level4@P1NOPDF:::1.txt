This paper presents an extension to the family of low-rank spectrogram factorization models for sound decomposition. It generalizes the latent factors to an arbitrary (potentially overcomplete) set of bases, enabling decomposition on “multiresolution” bases (e.g., tonal plus transient). This is argued to benefit source separation and denoising. The work builds on the Gaussian Composite Model (GCM) of Fevotte et al., which models the variance of each spectrogram time–frequency bin via a low-rank matrix factorization. The paper then makes explicit the implicit convolution with multiple Gabor impulse responses associated with each spectrogram row, yielding a purely time-domain expression for the estimated signal. This formulation can be adapted to different sets of impulse responses, including nonorthogonal ones. An EM procedure is derived, with convergence to a local optimum.

Quality: The central insight is interesting, though its full implications are not entirely clear to me. At first glance, it reads as another perspective on Fevotte’s GCM, but the ability to accommodate overcomplete dictionaries is appealing. However, this generalization would seem to introduce complications, and it was not obvious to me why these do not cause greater difficulties in practice. I will assume the estimation procedure is correct—it is an impressive piece of analysis—although I could not follow all details.

Clarity: The paper is generally well explained and well motivated. The evaluation, however, is very superficial: there is one purely illustrative result and one SNR improvement measurement. It is hard to draw meaningful conclusions from a single example. I appreciated the included audio examples, but they were not especially convincing. For instance, the OMLSA-enhanced example appears to be much longer (about 7 seconds versus 2 seconds) than the new result, which raises concerns about the fairness of the comparison. Moreover, although OMLSA includes more background noise (in part because it is longer), the speech itself sounds of much higher quality to me. In the music example, the denoising seems to introduce substantial artifacts; I would expect conventional wavelet shrinkage or Wiener filtering to yield higher perceived quality in this case.

Originality: The work leans heavily on the foundation laid by Fevotte et al., but the time-domain interpretation did make me think about the model in a new way. The extension to arbitrary, potentially overcomplete bases within this probabilistic framework is a meaningful conceptual step.

Significance: In principle, enabling matrix-factorization techniques over overcomplete bases could have significant impact on source separation and denoising. However, the empirical evidence presented here is too thin and anecdotal to substantiate that potential. As it stands, rating the work as highly significant would be an act of faith rather than a conclusion supported by rigorous evaluation. Reformulating probabilistic matrix-factorization-based sound analysis in terms of time-domain kernels is a valuable idea and enables an EM analysis for multiresolution (overcomplete) bases, but the sparse evaluation and noticeable artifacts in the examples do not demonstrate its importance in its current form.
