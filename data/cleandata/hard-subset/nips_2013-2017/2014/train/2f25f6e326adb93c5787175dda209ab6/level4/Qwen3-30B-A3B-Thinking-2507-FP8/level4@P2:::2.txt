

This paper introduces Low-Rank Time-Frequency Synthesis (LRTFS), a novel generative model that directly synthesizes time-domain signals rather than modeling only their short-time Fourier transform (STFT) as previous approaches like the Gaussian Composite Model (GCM) did. The authors build on the GCM framework from [3] by extending it to a full generative model of the raw signal x(t) itself, rather than just its STFT. This is achieved by modeling synthesis coefficients αfn with a low-rank variance structure [WH]fn, rather than the analysis coefficients yfn modeled by GCM. The paper presents two expectation-maximization algorithms for estimation in this new framework and demonstrates its utility for multi-resolution signal processing, which was previously impossible in traditional NMF settings.

The paper makes a significant theoretical contribution by bridging a critical gap between time-frequency signal processing and generative modeling. While NMF and GCM have been widely used in audio applications, they've only modeled the STFT magnitude or complex coefficients, not the underlying signal itself. LRTFS fills this gap by providing a principled generative model of x(t), enabling more accurate signal representation and novel multi-resolution capabilities. This work extends the NIPS tradition of theoretical advances in signal processing, building on previous NIPS papers like [3] on GCM and [4,5] on sparse Bayesian learning.

Strengths:
1. Theoretical novelty: The LRTFS model represents a substantial conceptual advancement over previous work. It properly models the raw signal rather than just its transform, providing a more complete generative framework. The connection to sparse Bayesian learning (SBL) is insightful and well-explained.

2. Multi-resolution capability: The paper's most compelling contribution is the natural integration of multi-resolution representations, which is particularly valuable in audio applications where different signal components (e.g., transients vs. tonal components) require different time-frequency resolutions. This capability is demonstrated effectively in the music decomposition and speech enhancement experiments.

3. Experimental validation: The paper provides compelling results on two audio applications (music decomposition and speech enhancement) that demonstrate the practical utility of the approach. The speech enhancement results (6.6 dB average SNR improvement) outperform both IS-NMF (6 dB) and OMLSA (4.6 dB).

4. Clear exposition: The paper is well-organized, with precise mathematical formulations and clear explanations of the relationships between the new model and previous work.

Weaknesses:
1. Limited comparison with single-resolution baselines: While the paper demonstrates multi-resolution capabilities, it doesn't sufficiently compare the single-resolution performance of LRTFS with traditional IS-NMF. It's unclear whether LRTFS with a single resolution (i.e., one t-f basis) would provide similar or better results than IS-NMF, and how the computational complexity compares.

2. MMLE implementation limitations: The paper presents a Maximum Marginal Likelihood Estimation (MMLE) approach but notes it's computationally infeasible for typical audio applications due to large matrix inversions. This is a significant limitation that should be addressed more thoroughly.

3. Algorithmic efficiency: The paper doesn't provide detailed comparisons of computational efficiency between the proposed MJLE algorithm and standard IS-NMF multiplicative updates. It would be valuable to know if the proposed approach offers comparable speed or stability.

4. Broader impact: The paper doesn't fully explore how this approach might impact other signal processing domains beyond audio, though the authors mention potential applications in multichannel source separation.

Pro/Con Acceptance:

Pro:
- Significant theoretical advancement in bridging the gap between time-frequency analysis and generative modeling
- Novel multi-resolution capability with clear practical applications in audio processing
- Strong experimental validation on relevant audio tasks
- Well-written with clear connections to prior work

Con:
- Limited comparison with single-resolution baselines
- MMLE approach is computationally impractical for typical applications
- Insufficient discussion of computational efficiency compared to standard methods
- Limited exploration of broader applications beyond audio

Overall, this is a high-quality contribution that makes a meaningful theoretical advance in signal processing. The multi-resolution capability is particularly compelling and addresses a genuine limitation of previous NMF approaches. While the paper could strengthen its evaluation by comparing single-resolution performance with IS-NMF and providing more detailed computational analysis, these are relatively minor concerns for a paper of this caliber. The work is technically sound, clearly presented, and makes a significant contribution to the field of time-frequency analysis. I believe it would be a valuable addition to the NIPS proceedings and would interest many researchers in machine learning and signal processing.
