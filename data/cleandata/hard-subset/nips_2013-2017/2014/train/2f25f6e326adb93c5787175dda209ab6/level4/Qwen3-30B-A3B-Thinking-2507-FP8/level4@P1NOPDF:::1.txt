

Quality: This paper extends low-rank spectrogram-factorization models for sound decomposition by generalizing latent factors over arbitrary (potentially overcomplete) bases, enabling multiresolution decomposition (e.g., tonal + transient components). While the core idea builds on Fevotte et al.'s Gaussian Composite Model (GCM), the key contribution is reinterpreting the model in the time domain. Specifically, the authors replace the implicit convolution with Gabor impulse responses (used in GCM) with a purely time-domain expression, which can then accommodate nonorthogonal bases. An EM-based inference procedure is proposed for this formulation. The insight is interesting—it offers a novel perspective on GCM—but the precise implications remain unclear. The approach primarily reinterprets existing work, though the ability to handle overcomplete bases is a valuable extension. The inference procedure appears sound, though its details were difficult to follow; the rigorous analysis is commendable.

Clarity: The paper is well-motivated and clearly explained. However, the evaluation is severely underdeveloped. Only one illustrative example and a single SNR metric are provided, which is insufficient to draw meaningful conclusions. While the included audio examples are helpful, they fail to convincingly support the claims. For instance, the OMLSA comparison example is 7 seconds long versus 2 seconds for the proposed method, raising concerns about a fair comparison. Additionally, the OMLSA example includes more background noise (partly due to its length), yet the speech quality is perceptually superior to the proposed method. In the music denoising example, the proposed approach introduces significant audible artifacts, whereas conventional methods like wavelet shrinkage or Wiener filtering would likely yield higher perceived quality. These artifacts undermine the claimed benefits.

Originality: The work builds substantially on Fevotte et al.'s GCM framework. While the time-domain reinterpretation offers a fresh perspective, the novelty is incremental rather than transformative. The core modeling approach does not depart significantly from prior work.

Significance: The potential to apply matrix-factorization techniques to overcomplete bases is promising for source separation and denoising. However, the current evaluation fails to substantiate this impact. The single SNR result and superficial audio examples provide no evidence that the method outperforms established baselines in practical scenarios. Without robust experiments demonstrating consistent improvements across diverse data, the significance claim remains speculative. The reformulation of probabilistic matrix-factorization analysis via time-domain kernels is technically sound, but the lack of rigorous evaluation and the presence of artifacts in the examples prevent the work from demonstrating tangible importance. As presented, the significance is overstated relative to the evidence provided.
