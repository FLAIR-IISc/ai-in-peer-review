

This paper introduces a novel framework for time/frequency factorizations that significantly extends beyond conventional STFT-based approaches. The core contribution lies in its flexibility: by decoupling factorization from specific transform analysis coefficients, the method applies to arbitrary transforms while maintaining a direct, interpretable link to the time-domain signal. This theoretical shift offers a compelling alternative to existing formulations, particularly for scenarios where transform adaptability is crucial. The exposition is exceptionally clear, with well-structured arguments and precise mathematical formulation, making the work accessible to researchers in signal processing and related fields. The originality of the theoretical approach is above average, addressing a meaningful gap in the literature.

The primary weakness lies in the insufficient demonstration of practical significance. While the theoretical development is sound and well-motivated, the paper fails to adequately articulate *why* this framework matters in real-world applications. The claimed advantages—such as adaptability to non-standard transforms or improved interpretability—remain largely unverified. Crucially, the experimental section and supplemental material lack convincing empirical validation. There are no comparisons against baseline methods (e.g., traditional STFT factorizations or competing factorization techniques), and the limited results provided do not quantify the method's benefits in terms of reconstruction accuracy, computational efficiency, or robustness. For instance, without benchmarks against established approaches, it is unclear whether the proposed framework offers tangible improvements over simpler alternatives. The supplemental material’s additional experiments appear superficial and fail to address these gaps, reducing their persuasive value.

This evaluation shortfall is the paper’s most significant shortcoming. While the theoretical contribution alone is substantive enough to merit interest, the absence of comparative results weakens the paper’s overall impact. In a venue like NIPS, where methodological rigor and empirical validation are paramount, the lack of direct comparisons to prior work—especially the methods the framework builds upon—undermines the claim of practical relevance. The authors acknowledge the framework’s flexibility but provide no evidence that this flexibility translates to measurable gains in performance or applicability. A more rigorous evaluation, including ablation studies on transform choices or comparisons on standard datasets, would have strengthened the case for adoption.

That said, the paper’s theoretical foundation is robust and well-executed. The derivation of the factorization conditions, the connection to time-domain signal properties, and the generalization to arbitrary transforms are logically presented and mathematically sound. This theoretical depth compensates for the evaluation limitations to a degree, as the framework itself represents a meaningful conceptual advance. The writing quality is excellent, with no ambiguities in the core arguments, and the paper avoids unnecessary technical clutter. The authors successfully establish a new lens for understanding time-frequency representations, which could inspire future work in adaptive signal processing.

In summary, this is a technically strong paper with a clear theoretical contribution that advances the field. However, the lack of empirical validation and comparative analysis prevents it from fully realizing its potential impact. The significance of the method is not convincingly demonstrated, and the evaluation section falls short of NIPS standards for methodological papers. While the theoretical novelty alone makes the paper worthy of consideration, the absence of rigorous experimental support is a notable drawback. To elevate the work, the authors should address these gaps in revision by adding comparative experiments and clarifying the practical implications of their framework.

Given the strong theoretical core but underdeveloped empirical component, I recommend **weak acceptance**. The paper would be significantly improved with the addition of comparative results and a clearer articulation of the method’s practical value. As submitted, it is a solid contribution that would interest the signal processing community, but the current evaluation limits its broader appeal and impact.
