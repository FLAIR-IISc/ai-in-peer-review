This paper presents an extension to low-rank spectrogram factorization models for sound decomposition, generalizing latent factors over an arbitrary, potentially overcomplete, set of bases. This enables decomposition based on “multiresolution” representations, such as tonal and transient components, with the goal of improving source separation and denoising performance. The work builds upon the Gaussian Composite Model (GCM) of Févotte et al., which models spectrogram cell variances using low-rank matrix factorization. However, it expands the implicit convolution inherent in GCM – representing it with multiple Gabor impulse responses – to derive a purely time-domain expression for the estimated parameters. This reformulation facilitates the application of the model to different, including non-orthogonal, sets of impulse responses. An Expectation-Maximization (EM) procedure for parameter estimation, and an analysis of its local optima, are also presented.

**Quality:** This is an interesting and insightful contribution, although the full implications remain somewhat unclear. While the work initially appears as a reinterpretation of Févotte’s GCM, the ability to incorporate overcomplete dictionaries is a valuable extension. However, expanding to overcomplete bases naturally introduces potential complications, and the paper doesn’t fully address why these don’t manifest more significantly. I assume the estimation procedure is mathematically sound – the analysis is impressive, even if I was unable to follow all the details.

**Clarity:** The work is well-explained and clearly motivated. The primary weakness lies in the evaluation, which feels very preliminary. The paper provides a single illustrative result alongside one Signal-to-Noise Ratio (SNR) improvement measurement, which is insufficient to draw robust conclusions. While the included sound examples are appreciated, they are not particularly compelling. Specifically, the comparison between the proposed method and OMLSA for speech enhancement is problematic. The OMLSA example is substantially longer (7 seconds versus 2 seconds), raising concerns about a fair comparison. Furthermore, despite the OMLSA example including more background noise (potentially due to its greater length), the resulting speech quality appears superior. The denoising of the musical example introduces significant audible artifacts. I would expect conventional signal processing techniques like wavelet shrinkage or Wiener filtering to achieve higher perceived quality.

**Originality:** The work is strongly grounded in the foundation laid by Févotte et al. However, the time-domain interpretation provides a novel perspective on the underlying model.

**Significance:** The potential to apply matrix factorization techniques to overcomplete bases is promising and could have a significant impact on the field. However, the limited and superficial evaluation prevents a strong assessment of its practical value. Currently, the claims regarding significance rely largely on conjecture. The reformulation of probabilistic matrix factorization for sound analysis in terms of time-domain kernels, and its subsequent EM analysis for multiresolution (overcomplete) bases, are noteworthy. Nonetheless, the thin evaluation and noticeable artifacts in the provided examples fail to demonstrate its importance in its current form. 

**Overall:** This paper presents a theoretically interesting extension to existing spectrogram factorization models. However, the lack of a rigorous empirical evaluation limits its impact. I recommend the authors significantly expand the experimental section with more comprehensive and comparative results to convincingly demonstrate the benefits of their approach, and to address the artifacting issues observed in the current examples. A more thorough investigation of the computational complexity of the proposed method would also be beneficial.
