This paper presents a thoughtful and novel interpretation of time-frequency factorizations. Rather than building upon existing formulations based on the analysis coefficients of Short-Time Fourier Transforms (STFTs), the authors propose a more flexible framework directly linked to the time-domain signal. This allows for a generative model of the raw signal itself, a step beyond previous work focused on the STFT.

The paper is well-written and clear, particularly for readers familiar with signal processing and matrix factorization techniques. It effectively positions the work within the context of related research, referencing key papers in NMF, the Gaussian Composite Model (GCM) [3], and sparse Bayesian learning. The authors demonstrate a strong understanding of prior work and clearly articulate how their approach differs. The originality of the work is above average, introducing a new synthesis model with low-rank time-frequency structure and opening doors to multi-resolution representations not achievable with traditional NMF.

The primary strength of this paper lies in its theoretical contribution. The derivation of the LRTFS model and the associated Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithms are well-explained and appear technically sound. The connection to sparse Bayesian learning and the discussion of multi-resolution representations are particularly insightful. The potential for representing signals as a sum of layers with varying time resolutions is a significant advantage.

However, the paper’s significance is somewhat diminished by the limited scope of the experimental evaluation. While the music decomposition example provides a visual demonstration of the approach, the results presented are not particularly compelling. The lack of quantitative comparison to established methods (e.g., the methods it builds upon such as NMF and GCM [1, 3]) is a notable weakness. The speech enhancement experiment shows a modest improvement over a baseline IS-NMF approach, but a more thorough comparison to state-of-the-art speech enhancement techniques would strengthen the claims of effectiveness. The reliance on supplemental material for sound examples, while appreciated, doesn’t fully address this concern. The computational challenges associated with the MMLE approach are acknowledged, but a discussion of potential solutions (e.g., message passing [16]) could be beneficial.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** The proposed LRTFS model represents a significant advance in time-frequency signal decomposition by providing a generative model of the raw signal.
*   **Theoretical Soundness:** The mathematical derivations and EM algorithms are well-presented and appear technically robust.
*   **Potential Impact:** The multi-resolution capabilities of LRTFS open up new possibilities for signal processing applications.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and easy to follow for researchers in the field.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Limited Evaluation:** The experimental results are not comprehensive enough to fully demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed approach.
*   **Lack of Comparison:** The paper does not adequately compare LRTFS to existing methods, hindering the assessment of its practical benefits.
*   **Significance Not Fully Demonstrated:** While the theoretical contributions are valuable, the paper could more effectively communicate the practical implications of the work.

**Overall Assessment:**

Despite the weaknesses in the experimental evaluation, the theoretical contributions of this paper are substantial and warrant acceptance. The proposed LRTFS model is a significant step forward in time-frequency signal decomposition, and the potential for future research and applications is considerable. I recommend accepting this paper with the caveat that the authors should consider expanding the experimental evaluation and providing a more thorough comparison to existing methods in future work.
