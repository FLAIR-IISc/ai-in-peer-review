This paper presents an extension to low-rank spectrogram factorization models for sound decomposition, generalizing latent factors over an arbitrary – and potentially overcomplete – set of bases. This enables decomposition using “multiresolution” bases, such as those representing both tonal and transient characteristics, with the aim of improving source separation and denoising performance. The work builds upon the Gaussian Composite Model (GCM) of Fevotte et al., which models spectrogram cell variances with low-rank matrix factorization. However, it innovatively expands the implicit convolution involving multiple Gabor impulse responses to derive a purely time-domain expression for the estimated parameters. This reformulation allows the method to be readily adapted to various sets of impulse responses, including non-orthogonal ones. An Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm is presented for parameter estimation, alongside an analysis of its local optima.

**Quality:** This paper presents an interesting and potentially valuable insight, though the full implications aren’t immediately clear. While initially appearing as a reinterpretation of Fevotte’s GCM, the ability to incorporate overcomplete dictionaries is a significant advantage. However, I anticipated that introducing overcompleteness would introduce complexities, and the paper didn’t fully elucidate why these didn’t pose greater challenges. I assume the estimation procedure is mathematically sound and am impressed by the analytical rigor, though the details were difficult to follow completely.

**Clarity:** The work is generally well-explained and clearly motivated. The primary weakness lies in the evaluation, which is remarkably superficial. The presented results consist of a single illustrative example and a single Signal-to-Noise Ratio (SNR) improvement measurement, insufficient for drawing meaningful conclusions. While the included sound examples are appreciated, they were not particularly convincing. Specifically, the comparison between the proposed method and OMLSA enhancement is questionable; the OMLSA example is significantly longer (7 seconds versus 2 seconds), raising concerns about a fair comparison. Moreover, despite the OMLSA example containing more background noise (potentially due to its length), the speech quality appears superior. The denoising of the music example introduces substantial and noticeable artifacts; conventional techniques like wavelet shrinkage or Wiener filtering would likely yield higher perceptual quality.

**Originality:** The work relies heavily on the foundational work of Fevotte et al. Nevertheless, the time-domain interpretation provides a fresh perspective and encourages a new way of thinking about the original model.

**Significance:** The potential impact of applying matrix-factorization techniques to overcomplete bases is considerable. However, the limited and superficial evaluation presented fails to demonstrate this potential. As such, assessing the true significance of this work currently requires a degree of faith. The reformulation of probabilistic matrix factorization for sound analysis in terms of time-domain kernels, and its subsequent enabling of EM analysis for multiresolution (overcomplete) bases, is promising. However, the thin evaluation and the presence of significant artifacts in the examples prevent a strong endorsement of its importance in its current form. Further, more comprehensive evaluation is crucial to validate these claims.
