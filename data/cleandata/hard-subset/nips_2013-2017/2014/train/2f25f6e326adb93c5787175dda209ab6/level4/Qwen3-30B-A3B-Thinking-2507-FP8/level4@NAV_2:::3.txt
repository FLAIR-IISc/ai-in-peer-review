

This paper introduces Low-Rank Time-Frequency Synthesis (LRTFS), a novel generative model that extends the Gaussian Composite Model (GCM) [3] from modeling the STFT to directly modeling the raw audio signal. The authors bridge a critical gap in signal processing literature by formulating a probabilistic framework where the signal is synthesized as a sum of low-rank components (Eq. 2-4), enabling multi-resolution representations unattainable in traditional NMF approaches. The work builds upon NIPS 2009 GCM [3] and advances beyond prior STFT-centric factorizations [1, 8, 9] by providing a coherent time-domain generative structure. Two efficient EM algorithms (MJLE and MMLE) are derived for estimation, with MJLE being computationally feasible for audio applications (Section 3.1), while MMLE is noted as scalable only for smaller problems (Section 3.2). The multi-resolution extension (Section 4) allows adaptive temporal resolution for transients and tonal components, validated through music decomposition (Section 5.1) and speech enhancement (Section 5.2).

Strengths:  
- **Quality**: The theoretical development is rigorous, with clear derivations of the generative model, EM algorithms, and connections to sparse Bayesian learning [4,5]. The MJLE algorithm avoids matrix inversions, making it practical for audio.  
- **Originality**: The shift from STFT modeling to raw-signal synthesis is a significant conceptual advance over GCM [3] and standard NMF. Multi-resolution capability is explicitly novel for low-rank factorizations in audio.  
- **Clarity**: The paper is exceptionally well-organized, with intuitive notation and effective use of equations to explain the model. The relation to prior work (e.g., [8,9]) is well-articulated.  

Weaknesses:  
- **Significance**: The experimental evaluation lacks sufficient comparison to the foundational GCM framework [3] and state-of-the-art methods (e.g., IS-NMF [3] in Section 5.2 only compares to a single baseline). The speech enhancement SNR gain (6.6 dB vs. 6 dB) is modest, and the supplemental audio examples do not include perceptual evaluation or ablation studies on resolution trade-offs.  
- **Evaluation**: Results (Section 5) are presented without statistical significance testing or analysis of computational complexity relative to baselines. The claim that multi-resolution "opens doors" lacks quantitative demonstration of its advantage over fixed-resolution NMF.  

Pro: Strong theoretical contribution, clear exposition, multi-resolution capability, practical MJLE algorithm.  
Con: Underwhelming empirical validation, insufficient comparison to core related work [3], and limited demonstration of real-world impact.  

This paper makes a valuable theoretical contribution to time-frequency modeling, but the significance is not fully justified by the current evaluation. The authors should strengthen the experimental section by comparing to GCM-based baselines [3] and providing deeper analysis of multi-resolution benefits. As is, the theory is compelling, but the empirical case for broader adoption remains incomplete.
