This paper proposes an extension of low-rank spectrogram-factorization models for sound decomposition that generalizes the latent factors over an arbitrary (potentially overcomplete) set of bases, enabling decomposition on multiresolution (e.g., tonal + transient) bases. The approach is argued to benefit source separation and denoising. It builds on the Gaussian Composite Model (GCM) of Févotte et al., which models the variance of each spectrogram cell via low-rank matrix factorization, and then makes explicit the implicit convolution with the multiple Gabor impulse responses corresponding to each spectrogram row. This yields a purely time-domain expression for the estimated signal that can be adapted to different sets of impulse responses, including nonorthogonal ones. An EM analysis (converging to a local optimum) is presented.

Quality: This is an interesting insight, though the exact implications remain somewhat unclear to me. At first glance, it reads as another way to understand Févotte’s GCM, but the ability to incorporate overcomplete dictionaries is appealing. At the same time, this generalization seems likely to introduce complications, and it is not immediately clear why these do not cause more difficulties. I assume the estimation procedure is correct (and I am impressed by this kind of analysis), although I could not follow it in full detail.

Clarity: The method is well explained and motivated. However, the evaluation is very superficial: there is one purely illustrative result and one SNR improvement measurement, which is insufficient to support strong conclusions. I appreciated the included sound examples, but they were not especially convincing. In particular, the OMLSA-enhanced example appears much longer (approximately 7 seconds compared with about 2 seconds) than the new result, raising concerns about the fairness of the comparison. Although OMLSA includes more background noise (in part because it is longer), the speech sounds of much higher quality to my ear. For the music example, the denoising seems to introduce very noticeable artifacts; I would expect conventional wavelet shrinkage or Wiener filtering to yield higher perceived quality in this case.

Originality: The work relies heavily on the foundation laid by Févotte et al., though the time-domain interpretation did prompt me to think about the model in a new way.

Significance: On the face of it, enabling matrix-factorization techniques over overcomplete bases could have substantial impact. However, the examples and evaluation are too thin and superficial to substantiate this potential; as it stands, rating the significance highly would be an act of faith. Reformulating probabilistic matrix-factorization sound analysis in terms of time-domain kernels enables an EM analysis for multiresolution (overcomplete) bases, but the limited evaluation and significant artifacts in the examples do not yet demonstrate the approach’s importance in its current form.
