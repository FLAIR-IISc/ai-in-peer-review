This paper presents an extension to the family of low-rank spectrogram-factorization models for sound decomposition, generalizing the latent factors over an arbitrary set of bases, including potentially overcomplete and multiresolution bases (e.g., tonal and transient). This extension is argued to improve source separation and denoising capabilities. The authors build upon the Gaussian Composite Model (GCM) proposed by Fevotte et al., which models the variance of each spectrogram cell using a low-rank matrix factorization. However, they expand the implicit convolution with multiple Gabor impulse responses corresponding to each spectrogram row, resulting in a purely time-domain expression that can be modified to accommodate different sets of impulse responses, including non-orthogonal ones. An Expectation-Maximization (EM) analysis procedure is also presented.

Quality: The idea is interesting, although its implications are not immediately clear. At first glance, it appears to be another interpretation of Fevotte's GCM, but the ability to incorporate overcomplete dictionaries is a welcome addition. However, it is surprising that this extension does not introduce more complications. The estimation procedure seems correct, and the analysis is impressive, although some details are difficult to follow.

Clarity: The authors provide a clear explanation and motivation for their work. However, the evaluation is superficial, consisting of only one illustrative result and a single SNR improvement measurement, making it challenging to draw meaningful conclusions. The inclusion of sound examples is appreciated, but they are not entirely convincing. For instance, the OMLSA-enhanced example is significantly longer (7 seconds vs. 2 seconds) than the new result, raising concerns about the fairness of the comparison. Furthermore, although the OMLSA example includes more background noise, the speech quality appears higher. The denoising of the music example comes at the cost of introducing significant artifacts, which are likely to be perceived as lower quality compared to conventional wavelet shrinkage or Wiener filtering.

Originality: The work heavily relies on the foundation laid by Fevotte et al., although the time-domain interpretation offers a fresh perspective.

Significance: The ability to apply matrix factorization techniques to overcomplete bases has the potential for significant impact. However, the evaluation and examples presented in this work are too limited and superficial to demonstrate this potential. As a result, attributing substantial significance to this work requires an act of faith. The reformulation of probabilistic matrix factorization sound analysis in terms of time-domain kernels allows for an EM analysis for multiresolution (overcomplete) bases, but the thin evaluation and significant artifacts in the examples fail to demonstrate its importance in its current form.
