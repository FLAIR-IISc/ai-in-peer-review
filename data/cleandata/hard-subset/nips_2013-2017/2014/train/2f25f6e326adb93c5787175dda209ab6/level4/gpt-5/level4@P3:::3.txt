Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes Low-Rank Time-Frequency Synthesis (LRTFS), a generative model for time-domain signals whose synthesis coefficients are endowed with a low-rank time–frequency variance structure. In contrast to the widely used Gaussian Composite Model (GCM) for NMF with the Itakura–Saito (IS) divergence, which is generative only for the STFT analysis coefficients, LRTFS moves the probabilistic modeling to the synthesis side: the signal is generated as a sum of time–frequency atoms with complex Gaussian coefficients whose variances factorize as WH. This bridges the gap between probabilistic NMF/GCM and true signal synthesis.

The paper presents two estimation strategies. The first, maximum joint likelihood estimation (MJLE), uses an EM algorithm built on the Figueiredo–Nowak variable-splitting scheme, leading to scalable updates that rely only on analysis/synthesis operations (no large matrix inversion). The objective comprises a data-fidelity term, an IS-NMF penalty on |α|^2, and a log(|α|^2) term that promotes sparsity. The second, maximum marginal likelihood estimation (MMLE), integrates out the synthesis coefficients, connecting the method to sparse Bayesian learning (SBL) with structured variances tied by WH rather than independent hyperparameters. MMLE is elegant but computationally heavy in the current implementation.

A key advantage of the synthesis view is a natural multi-resolution extension: the model can sum multiple frames (e.g., short-window transient and long-window tonal frames), each with its own low-rank structure. Experiments demonstrate (i) a hybrid music decomposition into tonal/transient layers and (ii) semi-supervised speech enhancement with pre-trained speech dictionaries and on-the-fly noise dictionaries. The latter yields mean SNR improvements of 6.6 dB, outperforming IS-NMF (6 dB) and OMLSA (4.6 dB) on the reported setup.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The modeling is principled and clearly grounded in the probabilistic NMF/GCM literature and SBL. The EM derivations for MJLE are consistent, and the connection to Wiener filtering is well explained. The MMLE formulation is standard for type-II ML but is acknowledged as computationally challenging.
- Empirical support: The experiments are illustrative and show encouraging gains, especially in the speech task. However, the evaluation is relatively limited in scope and ablation: there is little analysis of sensitivity to λ, β, K, frame choices, or of the benefits of multi-resolution versus single resolution on the same data. A direct comparison to the GCM (IS-NMF on STFT with Wiener filtering) on identical setups would be particularly informative.
- Limitations are stated candidly (e.g., MMLE scalability, practical issues estimating λ jointly).

Clarity
The paper is clearly written and well organized. The modeling choices and algorithmic steps are easy to follow for readers familiar with NMF and time–frequency analysis. Minor issues:
- The bibliography entries are incomplete (missing years/volumes/pages for many references).
- It would help to state explicitly how β is chosen to satisfy β ≤ λ/δΦ and to discuss its effect on convergence and reconstruction quality.
- A brief complexity analysis with operation counts for the main loop would strengthen the practical message.

Originality
The move from a low-rank model on analysis coefficients (GCM) to synthesis coefficients with tied low-rank variances is a noteworthy conceptual shift. The structured-variance SBL interpretation and the clean multi-resolution extension are original and valuable. While related ideas exist in hybrid time–frequency modeling and SBL, the specific formulation and its algorithmic realization appear novel.

Significance
This work has the potential to influence future audio modeling by providing a principled time-domain generative alternative to STFT-based NMF, with a clear path to multi-resolution modeling. The demonstrated gains in speech enhancement and the qualitative hybrid decomposition are promising. That said, broader and deeper evaluation would better substantiate impact:
- More datasets/noise types, metrics beyond SNR (e.g., PESQ/STOI for speech, source-to-distortion ratios for separation).
- Ablations: single- vs multi-resolution, effect of dictionary size, joint vs fixed λ, and comparisons to recent probabilistic STFT models and NMF variants with temporal priors.

Pros
- Conceptually clean generative synthesis model that connects NMF/IS divergence to the time domain.
- Scalable EM algorithm (MJLE) using only analysis/synthesis operators.
- Natural and elegant multi-resolution extension.
- Clear links to GCM and SBL; insightful discussion of structured variances.
- Promising results for speech enhancement; qualitative interpretability in hybrid music decomposition.

Cons
- Experimental evaluation is limited; few quantitative comparisons and no direct head-to-head with GCM under matched conditions.
- MMLE is currently not scalable, and the paper does not provide approximations to make it practical.
- Hyperparameter handling (λ, β) and sensitivity are under-explained; λ is treated as a heuristic in experiments.
- Bibliography entries are incomplete; some implementation details (e.g., window choices, overlap, K selection) could be better justified.

Suggestions for improvement
- Add controlled comparisons against GCM/IS-NMF with Wiener filtering under identical conditions, including single- and multi-resolution baselines.
- Provide ablation studies on the impact of λ, β, K, and window sizes, and report convergence diagnostics.
- Discuss identifiability and conditions under which synthesis components are recoverable; clarify treatment of real-valued signals (Hermitian symmetry).
- Include a brief complexity section and runtime measurements.
- Strengthen the reference list with complete citations.
- Consider approximate MMLE variants (e.g., conjugate gradient with implicit multiplications, message passing, diagonal/low-rank approximations) or variational alternatives to bridge the theory–practice gap.

Overall assessment
A well-written and technically solid paper that makes a meaningful conceptual advance by formulating low-rank time–frequency structure directly in a generative synthesis model, with a practical EM algorithm and a compelling multi-resolution extension. While the empirical section could be more comprehensive, the theoretical contribution is substantial and relevant to NIPS. I recommend acceptance, with the above suggestions aimed at improving clarity and strengthening the empirical validation.
