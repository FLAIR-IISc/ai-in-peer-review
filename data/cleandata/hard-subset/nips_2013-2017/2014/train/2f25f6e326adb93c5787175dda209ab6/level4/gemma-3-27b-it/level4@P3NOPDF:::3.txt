This paper offers a compelling and insightful reinterpretation of time-frequency factorizations. Moving beyond existing formulations based on Short-Time Fourier Transform (STFT) analysis coefficients, the authors present a more general and flexible framework applicable to arbitrary transforms. Notably, this framework establishes a direct connection to the time-domain signal, representing a significant conceptual advance.

The paper is exceptionally well-written and readily accessible to researchers familiar with time-frequency analysis. The theoretical development is of high quality, exhibiting a clear and logical progression. The originality of the work is above average, introducing a valuable new perspective on a fundamental problem. However, the paper’s significance isn’t fully realized due to limitations in the empirical evaluation.

My primary concern lies with the presented results and their justification. The supplemental material, while providing some supporting evidence, is not entirely convincing. Critically, the paper lacks a comparative analysis against existing methods upon which it builds. While the theoretical contribution is substantial enough to make this paper interesting in its own right, demonstrating practical advantages over established techniques would significantly strengthen its impact. Specifically, a clear articulation of *how* this more general framework outperforms or complements existing approaches (e.g., in terms of reconstruction quality, computational efficiency, or applicability to different signal types) is missing. 

The absence of such comparisons hinders a full assessment of the method’s practical value. The authors should consider including at least a baseline comparison to standard STFT-based approaches on representative datasets. Furthermore, clarifying the scenarios where the proposed framework offers distinct benefits would substantially enhance the paper's contribution.

Despite this, the theoretical soundness and clarity of the presentation are commendable. The authors have identified a crucial area for improvement in time-frequency analysis and presented a solid foundation for future work. The paper is a valuable contribution to the field, and I believe it has the potential to stimulate further research.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a good paper with a strong theoretical contribution. While the lack of extensive experimental validation and comparative analysis prevents it from being truly outstanding, the originality and clarity of the work make it a worthwhile addition to the NIPS conference. I recommend acceptance, with the caveat that the authors consider addressing the concerns regarding empirical evaluation in future work or a revised version.
