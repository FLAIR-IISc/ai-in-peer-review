This paper presents an extension in the family of low-rank spectrogram-factorization models of sound decomposition that generalizes the latent factors over an arbitrary (potentially overcomplete) set of bases, meaning that decomposition can be performed on "multiresolution" (e.g. tonal + transient) bases. This is argued to help with source separation and denoising. It starts from the Gaussian Composite Model of Fevotte et al, which models the variance of each cell of a spectrogram with a low-rank matrix factorization, but then expands the implicit convolution with the multiple Gabor impulse responses corresponding to each row of the spectrogram to give a purely time-domain expression for what is being estimated, that can then be modified to work for different sets of impulse responses, including nonorthogonal ones. An EM (local optimum) analysis procedure is presented.

Quality: The paper presents an interesting insight, although the implications are not immediately clear. The idea appears to be an extension of Fevotte's GCM, and the ability to include overcomplete dictionaries is a notable contribution. However, the potential complications arising from this extension are not thoroughly addressed. The estimation procedure seems correct, but the details are difficult to follow.

Clarity: The work is well-explained and motivated, but the evaluation is superficial. Only one illustrative result and one SNR improvement measurement are provided, which is insufficient to draw conclusions. The included sound examples are appreciated, but the comparison with OMLSA seems unfair due to the difference in duration. Additionally, the denoising of the music example introduces significant artifacts, which may be addressed using conventional methods like wavelet shrinkage or Wiener filtering.

Originality: The work relies heavily on the Fevotte et al foundation, although the time-domain interpretation offers a new perspective.

Significance: The ability to apply matrix-factorization techniques to overcomplete bases seems to have significant potential impact. However, the thin evaluation and significant artifacts in the examples fail to demonstrate this importance. The reformulation of probabilistic matrix-factorization sound analysis in terms of time-domain kernels allows for an EM analysis for multiresolution (overcomplete) bases, but the current evaluation does not convincingly show its importance.

Arguments pro acceptance:

* The paper presents a novel extension of the Gaussian Composite Model, allowing for multiresolution analysis.
* The time-domain interpretation offers a new perspective on probabilistic matrix-factorization sound analysis.
* The EM analysis procedure is presented, which is a notable contribution.

Arguments con acceptance:

* The implications of the proposed extension are not thoroughly addressed.
* The evaluation is superficial and does not convincingly demonstrate the importance of the proposed method.
* The comparison with OMLSA seems unfair, and the denoising of the music example introduces significant artifacts.
* The reliance on the Fevotte et al foundation may limit the originality of the work.
