This paper presents a novel and insightful framework for time-frequency factorizations. Rather than building upon existing formulations that operate on the analysis coefficients of the Short-Time Fourier Transform (STFT), the authors propose a more flexible approach directly linked to the time-domain signal and applicable to arbitrary transforms. This represents a significant shift in perspective, moving towards a true signal synthesis model.

The paper is clearly written and accessible to researchers familiar with signal processing and matrix factorization techniques. The technical quality is strong, with a well-developed theoretical foundation. The originality of the work is above average, offering a compelling alternative to traditional methods. However, the paper’s significance could be more strongly emphasized. While the theoretical contribution is substantial, the connection to broader applications and potential impact isn’t fully articulated.

A potential weakness lies in the presentation of results and evaluation. The experiments, while illustrative, are not particularly comprehensive. The supplemental material lacks sufficient detail to be fully convincing, and crucially, the paper does not include a direct comparison to existing methods upon which it builds – such as Gaussian Composite Models [3]. Establishing a clear performance advantage over established techniques would significantly strengthen the paper’s contribution. 

Despite this, the theoretical development presented is compelling enough to make this an interesting and valuable paper. The introduction of a multi-resolution framework, naturally accommodated by the proposed approach, is a particularly noteworthy aspect, opening possibilities for applications where adapting time-frequency resolution is critical (e.g., audio processing with transients). 

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** The work presents a fresh perspective on time-frequency decomposition, moving from analysis-based to synthesis-based modeling.
*   **Theoretical Soundness:** The mathematical framework is well-developed and appears rigorous.
*   **Potential Impact:** The multi-resolution capability offers advantages for applications requiring adaptive time-frequency resolution.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and easy to understand.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Limited Empirical Evaluation:** The experiments are limited in scope and lack comparison to relevant baselines.
*   **Significance Not Fully Demonstrated:** The potential impact of the proposed method isn’t fully conveyed, and a clear articulation of advantages over existing methods is missing.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a good paper with a solid theoretical foundation. While the lack of comprehensive experimental validation and detailed comparisons weakens the presentation, the originality and potential of the proposed framework make it a worthwhile contribution. I recommend acceptance, but with the caveat that future work should prioritize a more rigorous empirical evaluation and a clearer articulation of the method’s advantages.
