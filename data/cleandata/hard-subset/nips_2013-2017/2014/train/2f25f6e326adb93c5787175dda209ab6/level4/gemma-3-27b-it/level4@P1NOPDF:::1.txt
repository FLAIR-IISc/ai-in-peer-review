This paper presents an extension to low-rank spectrogram factorization models for sound decomposition, generalizing latent factors over an arbitrary (potentially overcomplete) set of bases. This allows for decomposition on “multiresolution” bases – for example, combining tonal and transient representations – and is motivated by applications in source separation and denoising. The work builds upon the Gaussian Composite Model (GCM) of Fevotte et al., which models spectrogram cell variances with low-rank matrix factorization. However, it expands the implicit convolution with multiple Gabor impulse responses (corresponding to each spectrogram row) to derive a purely time-domain expression for the estimated parameters. This formulation allows the model to operate with different sets of impulse responses, including non-orthogonal ones. An Expectation-Maximization (EM) procedure for parameter estimation, potentially subject to local optima, is also presented.

Quality: I find this work presents an interesting insight, though I am still considering its full implications. Initially, it appears as a refined understanding of Fevotte’s GCM, but the ability to incorporate overcomplete dictionaries is a definite advantage. However, I expected this increased flexibility to introduce complications, and the paper didn’t immediately clarify why these weren't more substantial. I assume the estimation procedure is correct – I’m impressed by this level of analysis, even if I couldn’t follow all the details.

Clarity: The work is well explained and motivated. However, the evaluation feels very superficial. We are presented with only one illustrative result and a single Signal-to-Noise Ratio (SNR) improvement measurement; it’s difficult to draw firm conclusions from such limited evidence. I appreciated the inclusion of sound examples, but wasn't particularly impressed by them. For instance, the OMLSA-enhanced example is considerably longer (7 seconds compared to 2 seconds for the new result), raising questions about the fairness of the comparison. Furthermore, despite the OMLSA example including more background noise (partly due to its longer duration), the speech quality sounds noticeably higher. The denoising of the music example introduces significant artifacts – I would anticipate conventional techniques like wavelet shrinkage or Wiener filtering to yield better perceived quality in this scenario.

Originality: The work relies heavily on the foundation laid by Fevotte et al., although the time-domain interpretation does offer a novel perspective.

Significance: The potential to apply matrix-factorization techniques to overcomplete bases appears significant. However, the examples and evaluation presented are too limited and superficial to demonstrate this convincingly. Currently, assessing the work’s substantial significance requires a degree of faith. The reformulation of probabilistic matrix-factorization for sound analysis in terms of time-domain kernels enables EM analysis with multiresolution (overcomplete) bases. Nevertheless, the thin evaluation and noticeable artifacts in the examples prevent a clear demonstration of its practical importance in its current form.
