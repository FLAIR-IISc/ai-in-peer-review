This paper proposes an extension to the family of low-rank spectrogram-factorization models for audio decomposition. The key contribution is a reformulation that generalizes the latent factors to operate over an arbitrary, and potentially overcomplete, set of basis functions. This enables "multiresolution" decomposition, combining, for example, tonal and transient bases, which the authors argue is beneficial for applications like source separation and denoising.

The method builds upon the Gaussian Composite Model (GCM) of Févotte et al., which models the variance of each spectrogram cell using a low-rank matrix factorization. The authors expand the implicit convolution associated with the Short-Time Fourier Transform (STFT) into a purely time-domain expression. This reformulation allows the STFT basis to be replaced with other sets of impulse responses, including non-orthogonal and overcomplete dictionaries. The paper presents an Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm to find a local optimum for the model parameters.

**Quality and Originality**

The core technical insight—reformulating probabilistic matrix factorization in terms of time-domain kernels to accommodate overcomplete bases—is novel and interesting. It offers a new perspective on the GCM and related models. The ability to apply these powerful factorization techniques to multiresolution dictionaries is a valuable contribution with significant theoretical potential.

However, the paper does not fully explore the implications of this extension. For instance, the use of overcomplete bases can introduce challenges related to the uniqueness and stability of the decomposition. The manuscript would be strengthened by a more thorough discussion of these potential complications and how the proposed framework addresses them. While the derivation of the EM estimation procedure appears rigorous, its complexity makes it difficult to verify completely on a first read.

The work is heavily based on the foundation laid by Févotte et al., but the time-domain interpretation represents a clear and original conceptual advance.

**Clarity**

The paper is generally well-written, and the motivation for the proposed model is clearly explained. The theoretical development is presented in a logical manner.

**Evaluation and Significance**

The primary weakness of this paper is its superficial evaluation, which fails to substantiate the claims of the method's practical utility. The experimental results are too thin to draw any meaningful conclusions. The evaluation consists of a single illustrative result and one quantitative signal-to-noise ratio (SNR) improvement measurement. This is insufficient evidence to demonstrate the general effectiveness of the proposed approach.

The provided audio examples, while appreciated, are not compelling and raise several concerns:

1.  **Unfair Comparison:** In the speech denoising example, the result from the proposed method is approximately 2 seconds long, while the baseline OMLSA result is 7 seconds. This discrepancy in duration raises questions about the fairness of the comparison, as processing different segments of a signal can lead to different outcomes.
2.  **Perceptual Quality:** Subjectively, the speech in the OMLSA-enhanced example sounds clearer and of higher quality than the speech processed by the proposed method, even though the OMLSA result retains more background noise.
3.  **Significant Artifacts:** The denoising result for the music example suffers from very significant, perceptually distracting artifacts. It is likely that conventional methods, such as wavelet shrinkage or Wiener filtering, would achieve a higher perceived quality in this scenario.

**Conclusion**

The paper introduces a clever and potentially impactful reformulation of spectrogram factorization models that enables their use with overcomplete, multiresolution bases. However, the significance of this contribution is not demonstrated due to a severely limited experimental evaluation. The unconvincing results and the presence of strong artifacts in the audio examples undermine the paper's claims.

As it stands, the work presents an interesting theoretical idea but fails to provide the empirical evidence necessary for publication. The potential significance of the method can only be accepted on faith. To be convincing, the paper would require a comprehensive evaluation against state-of-the-art baselines on a standard dataset, including both objective metrics and a more rigorous subjective assessment.
