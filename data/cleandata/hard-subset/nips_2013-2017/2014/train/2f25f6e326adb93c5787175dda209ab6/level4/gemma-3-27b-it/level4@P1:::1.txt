This paper presents an extension to low-rank spectrogram factorization models for sound decomposition, generalizing latent factors over an arbitrary (potentially overcomplete) set of bases, enabling decomposition with “multiresolution” representations (e.g., tonal and transient components). This is motivated as a potential improvement for source separation and denoising. The work builds upon the Gaussian Composite Model (GCM) of Févotte et al., which models the variance of each spectrogram cell with a low-rank matrix factorization. It then expands the implicit convolution with the multiple Gabor impulse responses corresponding to each row of the spectrogram, resulting in a purely time-domain expression for estimation that can be modified to accommodate different, including non-orthogonal, sets of impulse responses. An Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm, acknowledging potential for local optima, is presented for analysis.

**Quality:** This is an interesting and insightful contribution. While initially appearing as a reinterpretation of Févotte et al.’s GCM, the ability to incorporate overcomplete dictionaries is a notable extension. The implications of this flexibility are not immediately obvious, and the paper could benefit from a more thorough discussion of the potential challenges and advantages. I assume the estimation procedure is technically correct—the analysis is impressive—although I was unable to follow all the details.

**Clarity:** The work is well-explained and clearly motivated. However, the evaluation is somewhat superficial. The illustrative result lacks sufficient detail, and the SNR improvement measurement is based on a single example, limiting the ability to draw firm conclusions. The inclusion of sound examples is appreciated, but they are not particularly compelling. For instance, the OMLSA-enhanced example uses a longer segment (7 seconds versus 2 seconds for the proposed method), raising concerns about a fair comparison. Moreover, the OMLSA output exhibits higher speech quality despite including more background noise. The denoising of the music example introduces noticeable artifacts, and I suspect that conventional wavelet shrinkage or Wiener filtering might yield better perceptual quality.

**Originality:** The work builds heavily on the foundation laid by Févotte et al., but the time-domain interpretation offers a new perspective. Reformulating probabilistic matrix factorization for sound analysis in terms of time-domain kernels allows for EM analysis with multiresolution (overcomplete) bases.

**Significance:** The potential to apply matrix factorization techniques to overcomplete bases appears promising. However, the limited and superficial evaluation presented in this work makes it difficult to assess its true significance. Currently, judging its substantial impact requires an act of faith. 

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel extension of existing GCM framework to support overcomplete bases.
*   Provides a time-domain interpretation which might lead to further development.
*   Clear theoretical framework and EM algorithm for estimation.
*   Potential for improved performance in source separation and denoising tasks.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Evaluation is weak and based on limited examples.
*   Artifacts present in the illustrative music decomposition example.
*   Comparison with baseline methods appears potentially unfair (e.g., different signal lengths).
*   Lack of thorough discussion on challenges and limitations of the approach.

**Overall Recommendation:**

I believe this paper presents a technically sound and potentially valuable contribution. However, the weak evaluation and lack of compelling results weaken its case for acceptance. I recommend a **weak accept** contingent on the authors significantly strengthening the experimental validation and addressing the concerns regarding the fairness of the comparisons. Specifically, providing a more thorough evaluation with multiple datasets, more sophisticated evaluation metrics (beyond SNR), and a careful comparison with state-of-the-art methods would substantially improve the paper's impact.
