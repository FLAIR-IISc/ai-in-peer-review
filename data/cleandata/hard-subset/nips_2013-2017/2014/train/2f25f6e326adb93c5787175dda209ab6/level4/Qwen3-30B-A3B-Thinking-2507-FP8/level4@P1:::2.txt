

This paper introduces a novel generative model for audio signal processing called Low-Rank Time-Frequency Synthesis (LRTFS), which extends traditional nonnegative matrix factorization (NMF) approaches by modeling the raw time-domain signal directly rather than just its short-time Fourier transform (STFT). The authors build upon the Gaussian Composite Model (GCM) framework introduced in [3], which provides a generative probabilistic model for STFT coefficients, by extending it to a generative model for the underlying time-domain signal. This represents a significant conceptual advancement as it bridges the gap between time-frequency analysis and signal synthesis.

The core contribution is the LRTFS model, which expresses the signal as a sum of synthesis coefficients modulated by time-frequency atoms, with the synthesis coefficients following a low-rank variance structure. The authors present two estimation approaches: maximum joint likelihood estimation (MJLE) and maximum marginal likelihood estimation (MMLE). The MJLE approach is particularly well-motivated and computationally efficient, leveraging an EM algorithm that avoids matrix inversions and can be implemented efficiently using standard signal processing toolboxes. The paper also demonstrates how LRTFS naturally enables multi-resolution representations, which is a significant advantage over traditional NMF approaches that fix time resolution at the feature extraction stage.

The authors effectively relate their work to previous literature. They clarify how LRTFS differs from the GCM by modeling the signal itself rather than its STFT, and they explicitly connect their approach to sparse Bayesian learning (SBL) while highlighting the key difference in how variances are structured. The paper also properly situates the work within the context of audio processing, referencing relevant applications like music decomposition and speech enhancement.

The paper's strengths are numerous. First, the theoretical development is sound, with clear mathematical formulations and proper derivation of the EM algorithms. Second, the multi-resolution capability is genuinely novel and addresses a significant limitation of previous approaches. Third, the experimental results on music decomposition and speech enhancement are well-designed and demonstrate clear practical value. The speech enhancement results (6.6 dB average SNR improvement) outperform both IS-NMF (6 dB) and the standard OMLSA method (4.6 dB), which provides compelling evidence of the approach's practical utility.

The authors also thoughtfully discuss the limitations of their approach. They acknowledge that the MMLE approach, while theoretically appealing, is computationally prohibitive for large-scale audio applications due to the need to invert large matrices. They appropriately focus their experiments on the MJLE approach, which is computationally feasible for practical audio processing.

However, the paper could benefit from a more thorough comparison with existing single-resolution approaches. While the authors present multi-resolution results, they do not provide a comprehensive comparison of their single-resolution LRTFS approach with IS-NMF (which is the standard approach for single-resolution audio decomposition). Specifically, it would be valuable to know:
1. How the estimated W and H matrices compare between LRTFS and IS-NMF in single-resolution settings
2. Whether the MJLE algorithm achieves similar convergence speed and stability as the standard IS-NMF multiplicative updates
3. How the computational complexity of MJLE compares to IS-NMF for equivalent signal lengths

The paper also doesn't fully explore the potential for extending LRTFS to other domains beyond audio processing, though this is perhaps reasonable given the focus on audio applications in the experiments. The authors briefly mention potential extensions to multichannel audio source separation in the conclusion, but this could be expanded.

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a meaningful contribution to the field of audio signal processing. The theoretical framework is sound, the multi-resolution capability is genuinely novel and valuable, and the experimental results demonstrate clear practical advantages over existing methods. The writing is generally clear, though the mathematical notation could be slightly more consistent in places.

The paper meets all NIPS quality criteria:
- Quality: The paper is technically sound with well-supported theoretical analysis and experimental results. The authors carefully consider both strengths and limitations of their approach.
- Clarity: The paper is well-organized and generally clear, though some mathematical details could be presented more consistently.
- Originality: The LRTFS model represents a significant conceptual advance beyond traditional NMF approaches, with the multi-resolution capability being particularly novel.
- Significance: The approach addresses a practical limitation of existing methods and demonstrates clear improvements in audio signal processing applications.

Pro-con acceptance list:
PROS:
- Novel generative model that bridges the gap between time-frequency analysis and signal synthesis
- Genuine multi-resolution capability that was not possible with traditional NMF
- Strong experimental results showing clear performance improvements over baseline methods
- Well-motivated and computationally efficient MJLE algorithm
- Clear connection to relevant prior work in signal processing and machine learning

CONS:
- Insufficient comparison with single-resolution IS-NMF approach
- Limited discussion of computational complexity comparison with IS-NMF
- MMLE approach, while theoretically interesting, is not implemented for large-scale applications

Given the paper's strong theoretical contribution, clear practical advantages demonstrated in experiments, and the significance of the multi-resolution capability, I recommend acceptance. The minor limitations identified do not detract from the paper's overall contribution to the field.
