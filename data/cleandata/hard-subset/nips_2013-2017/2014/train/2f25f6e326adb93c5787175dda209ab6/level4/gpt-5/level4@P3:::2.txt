Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes Low-Rank Time-Frequency Synthesis (LRTFS), a generative model of the time-domain signal that places a low-rank prior on time-frequency synthesis coefficients. Concretely, the signal is modeled as x(t) = sum_{f,n} α_fn φ_fn(t) + e(t), with complex Gaussian synthesis coefficients α_fn ∼ Nc(0, [WH]_{fn}), where W and H are nonnegative factors, and e(t) is Gaussian residual noise. This contrasts with the Gaussian Composite Model (GCM) for NMF with Itakura–Saito (IS) divergence [Févotte et al.], which assumes a low-rank variance structure on STFT analysis coefficients y_fn. By modeling synthesis rather than analysis coefficients, LRTFS provides a generative model for the raw signal and naturally accommodates multi-resolution representations by combining multiple frames.

The paper develops two estimation strategies: (i) maximum joint likelihood estimation (MJLE) via an EM algorithm that leverages a Figueiredo–Nowak auxiliary-variable “noise-splitting” trick to avoid large matrix inversions and alternates Wiener-like shrinkage of α with IS-NMF updates of W, H; and (ii) maximum marginal likelihood estimation (MMLE), in the spirit of sparse Bayesian learning (SBL) [Tipping; Wipf & Rao], which marginalizes α but requires inverting large matrices and is not used at scale here. The multi-resolution extension stacks multiple frames (e.g., short and long Gabor windows) with independent low-rank priors on each set of synthesis coefficients.

Experiments illustrate (a) a jazz mixture decomposed into tonal/transient layers and (b) semi-supervised speech enhancement in applause noise, using pre-trained speech dictionaries and noise dictionaries learned from the mixture. Average SNR improvement is 6.6 dB for LRTFS, versus 6.0 dB for an IS-NMF baseline (single mid-length window) and 4.6 dB for OMLSA [Cohen]. The noise variance λ is treated as a hyperparameter/warm-start schedule rather than fully estimated.

Strengths
- Conceptual contribution: A clean bridge from traditional IS-NMF/GCM (on analysis coefficients) to a generative model of the waveform via synthesis coefficients with low-rank variance. This closes the gap between probabilistic factorization and signal synthesis.
- Multi-resolution capability: Modeling at the synthesis level makes combining multiple frames straightforward, addressing a longstanding limitation of single-resolution NMF pipelines and aligning with prior audio representations that exploit hybrid/morphological decompositions [Daudet & Torrésani; Kowalski & Torrésani].
- Algorithmic design: The MJLE-EM avoids large inversions, uses efficient analysis/synthesis operations (e.g., via LTFAT), and has clear, interpretable update steps (IS-NMF on |α|^2, Wiener-like shrinkage). The appearance of a log-sparsity term in the joint likelihood is insightful and ties to SBL intuitions.
- Empirical results: Reasonable gains over a strong IS-NMF baseline and OMLSA on speech-in-noise; qualitative layer separation in the music example.
- Potential impact: The framework is flexible (e.g., multi-resolution, supervised/semi-supervised settings) and suggests broader applications (e.g., multichannel).

Weaknesses and questions
- Comparative scope: The empirical evaluation is limited. A controlled single-resolution comparison against IS-NMF/GCM (same window/frame, same rank K, same computational budget) is needed. Do W and H learned under LRTFS (via |α|^2) resemble those from IS-NMF on the corresponding STFT power? How does output quality (SNR, SDR/SIR/SAR) and perceptual quality compare?
- Efficiency and stability: Runtimes, memory usage, and convergence behavior are not reported. How does MJLE’s speed and stability compare with standard IS-NMF multiplicative updates? How sensitive is the EM to initialization (W, H, α) and to the auxiliary parameter β?
- Treatment of λ: The paper notes poor performance when estimating λ jointly and resorts to hand-tuning/warm-start schedules. This weakens the probabilistic story. Can the authors provide guidance or rationale (e.g., identifiability issues, scale ambiguities between λ and WH), and practical heuristics for setting λ?
- MMLE scalability: The MMLE path is theoretically appealing but impractical for the reported scales. Are there structured approximations (e.g., diagonal/Toeplitz approximations, conjugate gradients with Woodbury, message passing as suggested) that could make MMLE viable in the audio sizes considered?
- Reconstruction details: For component reconstruction, the paper uses a plug-in estimator based on α̂ rather than the exact MMSE; a brief discussion of the bias introduced and its practical impact would be helpful.
- Reproducibility and robustness: More details on parameter choices (K per layer, β, number of EM iterations, stopping criteria), ablations (single vs multi-resolution, effect of K), and robustness to frame choices (window length/overlap) would strengthen the empirical section.

Quality
The model and EM derivations appear technically sound and well-grounded in prior work on IS-NMF, SBL, and EM with auxiliary variables. The MJLE algorithm is plausible and practical; the MMLE derivation is standard but currently not scalable. The empirical evaluation supports the method’s promise but is not comprehensive.

Clarity
The paper is generally clear, with precise notation and good linkage to related work. Suggestions:
- Clarify initialization strategies and parameter settings (β, λ schedule).
- Provide pseudo-code with complexity per iteration and practical runtime.
- Summarize how real-valued signals are handled (Hermitian symmetry) in experiments.

Originality
The shift from low-rank modeling of analysis coefficients to synthesis coefficients for a generative time-domain model is novel and meaningful. The multi-resolution extension is a natural but nontrivial advantage over analysis-based NMF. The connection to SBL and the emergence of a log-sparsity term enrich the perspective.

Significance
For audio ML and signal processing, the work is significant: it advances generative modeling fidelity, offers an elegant route to multi-resolution factorization, and shows competitive performance on speech enhancement. With stronger empirical baselines and efficiency reporting, the impact could extend to broader inverse problems.

Suggestions for improvement
- Add a single-resolution head-to-head comparison with IS-NMF/GCM, matching frame and rank, including quality and runtime.
- Report convergence diagnostics, sensitivity to initialization, and parameter-setting guidelines (especially for λ and β).
- Include ablations on multi-resolution choices (window lengths, K per layer) and the effect of the log-sparsity term.
- Provide wall-clock times and memory footprint for the reported experiments.
- Release code or sufficient details for reproduction.
- Outline a concrete plan to scale MMLE (e.g., iterative solvers with matrix–vector products exploiting Φ structure).

Pros
- Principled generative model of the waveform with low-rank time–frequency structure
- Natural, effective multi-resolution extension
- Scalable MJLE-EM without large inversions
- Empirical gains over IS-NMF and OMLSA

Cons
- Limited comparative evaluation and no single-resolution head-to-head
- Lack of runtime/stability analysis
- Reliance on hyperparameter tuning for λ
- MMLE not yet practical at scale

Overall, this is an interesting and promising contribution. Strengthening the empirical comparisons and providing practical guidance on parameter selection and computational cost would substantially improve the paper.
