This paper presents an extension to the family of low-rank spectrogram factorization models for sound decomposition. The key innovation is to generalize the latent factors over an arbitrary, and potentially overcomplete, set of bases. This allows decomposition to be performed on "multiresolution" bases (e.g., combining tonal and transient components), which the authors argue is beneficial for source separation and denoising.

The work builds upon the Gaussian Composite Model (GCM) of Févotte et al., which models the variance of each time-frequency bin in a spectrogram as a low-rank matrix factorization. The authors expand the implicit convolution associated with the Short-Time Fourier Transform (STFT) to derive a purely time-domain expression for the estimated signal. This time-domain formulation, in turn, can be adapted to arbitrary sets of impulse responses, including non-orthogonal and overcomplete bases. The authors present an Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm to find a locally optimal solution for this model.

**Quality**

The core insight of this paper is interesting, although its full implications are not immediately apparent. At first glance, the method appears to be a re-interpretation of Févotte's GCM. However, the ability to incorporate overcomplete dictionaries is a valuable extension. On the other hand, using overcomplete bases typically introduces complications, and the paper does not fully address why these issues do not pose a greater challenge in this framework. While I could not follow the derivation in complete detail, the EM-based estimation procedure appears sound, and the analytical work is impressive.

**Clarity**

The paper is well-motivated and clearly written. However, the evaluation is superficial. It is based on a single illustrative result and one SNR improvement measurement, and it is difficult to draw meaningful conclusions from such a limited example.

The inclusion of sound examples is appreciated, but they were not particularly convincing.
1.  The OMLSA-enhanced baseline example is significantly longer (approx. 7s vs. 2s) than the example processed by the proposed method, raising questions about the fairness of the comparison.
2.  While the OMLSA example contains more residual background noise (perhaps due to its longer duration), the speech itself sounds perceptually higher in quality.
3.  Similarly, the denoising of the music example introduces significant audible artifacts. I suspect that conventional methods like wavelet shrinkage or Wiener filtering would yield higher perceived quality on this particular example.

**Originality**

The work relies heavily on the foundational GCM of Févotte et al. However, the re-interpretation in the time domain is a novel contribution that provides a new perspective on this class of models.

**Significance**

In principle, the ability to apply matrix factorization techniques to overcomplete bases could have a significant impact on audio processing. The paper's main contribution—a reformulation of probabilistic matrix factorization in terms of time-domain kernels that enables EM-based analysis for overcomplete, multiresolution bases—is a strong theoretical idea. Unfortunately, the thin evaluation and the significant artifacts in the provided examples fail to demonstrate the practical importance of this contribution in its current form.
