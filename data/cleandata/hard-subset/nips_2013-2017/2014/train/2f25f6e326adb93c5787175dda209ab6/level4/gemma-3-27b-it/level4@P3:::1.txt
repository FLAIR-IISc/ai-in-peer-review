This paper presents a novel extension to low-rank spectrogram factorization models for sound decomposition, generalizing latent factors over arbitrary (potentially overcomplete) bases, enabling “multiresolution” representations (e.g., tonal and transient components). The work builds upon the Gaussian Composite Model (GCM) of Févotte et al. (2013), which models spectrogram cell variances with a low-rank factorization, and reformulates this into a time-domain expression suitable for estimation with different sets of basis functions, including non-orthogonal ones. An Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm is proposed for parameter estimation.

The core idea is compelling: extending the GCM to operate directly in the time domain and accommodate overcomplete bases opens possibilities for more flexible and accurate signal representation, particularly for tasks like source separation and denoising. While the extension appears logically sound, and the mathematical derivation of the EM algorithm seems robust (although detailed verification is beyond my expertise), the paper’s impact is currently limited by its empirical evaluation. The authors correctly position their work within the existing literature on NMF and probabilistic latent factor models for signal processing, citing key contributions from Lee & Seung (1999), Smaragdis et al. (2006), and Févotte et al. (2013), among others. The connection to sparse Bayesian learning (SBL), as highlighted in Section 2.2, is also interesting and provides valuable context.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The time-domain reformulation of the GCM and the extension to overcomplete bases represent a clear contribution to the field. The ability to directly model signals with different time resolutions (as demonstrated with tonal and transient components) is a significant advantage over traditional approaches.
*   **Theoretical Soundness:** The derivation of the LRTFS model and the EM algorithm appear mathematically well-founded. The authors demonstrate a strong understanding of the underlying principles of probabilistic modeling and signal processing.
*   **Potential for Broad Applicability:** The proposed framework has the potential to be applied to a wide range of audio processing tasks, including music analysis, speech enhancement, and source separation. The multi-resolution capability is particularly promising for scenarios where signals exhibit varying temporal characteristics.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Limited Empirical Evaluation:** The experimental results presented are rather superficial. The “illustrative” music decomposition example, while visually interesting, lacks quantitative evaluation. The speech enhancement experiment, while showing a modest SNR improvement compared to baseline methods (OMLSA and IS-NMF), relies on a single test case and the comparison isn't entirely fair due to differences in signal length.
*   **Artifacts in Music Decomposition:** The music decomposition example suffers from noticeable artifacts, suggesting that the current implementation may not be fully optimized for perceptual quality. Conventional signal processing techniques, such as wavelet shrinkage or Wiener filtering, might yield more pleasing results.
*   **Lack of Ablation Studies:** It would be beneficial to include ablation studies to assess the contribution of different components of the proposed model. For instance, how much does the overcompleteness of the basis functions contribute to the performance gains?
*   **Scalability Concerns:** The discussion of MMLE in Section 3.2 highlights scalability concerns, particularly for large-scale audio applications. While the authors acknowledge this limitation, further investigation into efficient implementation strategies (e.g., using message passing, as suggested) would be valuable.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound extension to existing low-rank matrix factorization models for audio processing.
*   The ability to incorporate overcomplete bases and multi-resolution representations is a significant advance.
*   The potential for broad applicability across various audio processing tasks is substantial.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

*   The empirical evaluation is limited and does not convincingly demonstrate the benefits of the proposed approach.
*   The presence of artifacts in the music decomposition example raises concerns about perceptual quality.
*   The lack of thorough ablation studies makes it difficult to assess the contribution of different components of the model.



**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents an interesting and potentially valuable contribution to the field of audio signal processing. However, the limited empirical evaluation and the presence of artifacts in the example results raise concerns about its practical impact. While the theoretical framework is strong, more extensive experiments and a more careful consideration of perceptual quality are needed to fully justify its significance.

**Recommendation:**

I recommend **weak reject**. While the ideas presented are compelling and the theoretical work is sound, the lack of a robust empirical evaluation prevents me from confidently recommending acceptance. The authors should significantly expand their experimental section with more thorough quantitative and qualitative analyses, including comparisons to a wider range of baseline methods and a more detailed investigation of the artifacts observed in the music decomposition example. Addressing the scalability concerns related to MMLE would also strengthen the paper.
