- Proposes Low-Rank Time-Frequency Synthesis (LRTFS), placing a low-rank prior on synthesis coefficients to model raw time-domain signals.  
- Contrasts with IS-NMF and Gaussian Composite Model (GCM) by tying synthesis coefficient variances via a WH factorization instead of modeling analysis variances.  
- Derives two estimation strategies: a scalable EM algorithm for maximum joint likelihood (MJLE) that avoids large matrix inversions.  
- Presents a maximum marginal likelihood (MMLE) approach inspired by sparse Bayesian learning (SBL) that is conceptually appealing but not currently scalable.  
- Naturally supports multi-resolution time–frequency representations by combining multiple frames in one generative model.  
- Demonstrates applications in hybrid music decomposition and semi-supervised speech enhancement.  
- Technical development is solid: clear probabilistic formulation, well-motivated links to IS divergence and Wiener filtering, and sound EM derivations.  
- MJLE algorithm is computationally practical for typical audio tasks by leveraging fast analysis/synthesis operators.  
- MMLE derivation is correct but the implementation does not scale to long signals.  
- Paper is clearly written, well organized, and explains relationships to NMF, GCM, and SBL and the roles of synthesis vs. analysis coefficients.  
- The shift from analysis-domain to synthesis-domain low-rank modeling is a meaningful and original conceptual contribution.  
- Unification with EM/Wiener-style updates and natural multi-resolution handling distinguishes this work from prior NMF approaches.  
- Potentially significant for signal decomposition in audio and beyond, especially where time-domain generative modeling and multi-resolution structure matter.  
- Empirical evaluation is limited in scope and depth, understating the practical gains over established baselines.  
- Missing ablation studies isolating benefits of synthesis-domain modeling versus GCM/IS-NMF and multi-resolution effects.  
- MMLE scalability issues are not addressed and no large-scale approximations are proposed.  
- Sensitivity to noise hyperparameter λ is not systematically analyzed.  
- Suggestions: expand quantitative comparisons, include ablations (resolution, domain, K, λ), report computational costs/convergence, and explore scalable MMLE approximations.  
- Arguments for acceptance: solid, original theory; practical algorithm; clean extensible multi-resolution framework.  
- Arguments against acceptance: insufficient experimental evidence across tasks and limited discussion/solutions for MMLE scalability and hyperparameter sensitivity.  
- Overall: technically strong and well-written paper that advances synthesis-domain low-rank modeling and multi-resolution representations; recommend acceptance with stronger empirical validation.
