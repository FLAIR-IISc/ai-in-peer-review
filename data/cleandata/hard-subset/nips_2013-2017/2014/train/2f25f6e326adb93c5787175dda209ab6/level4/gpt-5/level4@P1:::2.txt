Summary
This paper proposes a low-rank time-frequency synthesis (LRTFS) model that, unlike standard NMF approaches, is generative for the time-domain signal itself. Instead of modeling the variance of analysis coefficients in the STFT domain (as in the Gaussian Composite Model, GCM, which leads to IS-NMF), the authors impose a low-rank structure on the variance of synthesis coefficients, tying them through W and H. This shift enables a natural multi-resolution extension: the signal can be synthesized as a sum of layers using different time-frequency bases (e.g., Gabor frames with different window lengths), which is not possible in traditional NMF settings that operate on a fixed-resolution transform. The paper presents two estimation strategies—maximum joint likelihood (MJLE) with an EM algorithm that avoids large matrix inversions, and maximum marginal likelihood (MMLE), which is elegant but currently not scalable for long audio signals. Experiments on music decomposition and speech enhancement illustrate the approach.

Strengths
- The synthesis-side generative model is a clear conceptual advance over STFT-domain models (e.g., GCM/IS-NMF), bridging the gap between probabilistic modeling and direct signal generation.
- The multi-resolution extension is particularly appealing: it fits the structure of many audio signals (transients vs tonal content) and integrates naturally within the proposed framework.
- The MJLE-EM algorithm is computationally practical for large signals because it relies on efficient analysis/synthesis operations rather than large matrix inversions.
- The experimental results on hybrid music decomposition and semi-supervised speech enhancement are promising and show the potential of the approach.

Weaknesses and questions
- The paper focuses on multi-resolution decompositions, but I would like to see a single-resolution comparison to the standard IS-NMF baseline. Specifically:
  - Does LRTFS (with a single time-frequency basis) recover W and H similar to those learned by IS-NMF on the corresponding STFT (e.g., in terms of factor interpretability and reconstruction quality)?
  - How do the resulting decompositions compare in terms of standard metrics (e.g., reconstruction error, separation quality) when both methods use the same single-resolution Gabor frame?
  - Is the proposed MJLE algorithm as fast and stable in practice as the multiplicative updates for IS-NMF? A side-by-side comparison of convergence speed, robustness to initialization, and runtime would be helpful.
- More detail on the computational complexity per iteration (for MJLE) relative to IS-NMF would clarify practical trade-offs.

Qualitative evaluation
- Quality: The modeling choices are technically sound, and the MJLE algorithm is well-motivated. The MMLE path is principled but currently impractical for large-scale audio; acknowledging this limitation is appropriate.
- Clarity: Overall the paper is clearly written and well-organized. A brief complexity analysis and an explicit single-resolution comparison would further improve clarity for readers familiar with IS-NMF.
- Originality: Modeling low-rank structure on synthesis coefficients and enabling multi-resolution decomposition is a novel and meaningful departure from analysis-domain NMF/GCM.
- Significance: The approach is likely to be useful for audio analysis, separation, and enhancement, especially where multi-resolution structure matters.

Pros
- Conceptually strong shift from analysis to synthesis modeling.
- Natural and effective multi-resolution extension.
- Practical EM algorithm for large-scale signals.
- Demonstrated utility on music and speech tasks.

Cons
- Lack of a direct single-resolution comparison to IS-NMF (factors W, H; accuracy; speed/stability).
- Limited discussion of computational complexity relative to IS-NMF.

Overall assessment
This is an interesting and promising contribution. I particularly like the multi-resolution extension. To strengthen the paper, I encourage the authors to add a single-resolution comparison to IS-NMF addressing whether LRTFS yields similar W and H, and whether the proposed algorithm is as fast and stable as the standard multiplicative updates.
