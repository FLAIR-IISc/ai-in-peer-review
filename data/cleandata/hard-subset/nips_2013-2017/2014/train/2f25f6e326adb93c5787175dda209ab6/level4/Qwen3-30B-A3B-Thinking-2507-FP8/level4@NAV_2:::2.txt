

The paper introduces a novel low-rank time-frequency synthesis (LRTFS) model that extends traditional nonnegative matrix factorization (NMF) approaches by modeling the raw time-domain signal directly rather than its short-time Fourier transform (STFT). This work builds upon the Gaussian Composite Model (GCM) introduced in [3], which provided a generative model for STFT coefficients, but extends it to model the time-domain signal itself through synthesis coefficients with low-rank variance structure. The authors present two expectation-maximization algorithms for estimation in this new model and demonstrate its application to multi-resolution audio processing, which was previously unattainable in standard NMF frameworks.

The paper makes a significant contribution to the field by bridging the gap between time-frequency synthesis and traditional NMF approaches. The multi-resolution capability is particularly noteworthy, as it allows for modeling different temporal resolutions in different components of a signal (e.g., transients vs. tonal components in audio), which is not possible with conventional NMF-based methods that operate on transformed data with fixed time resolution.

Regarding quality, the theoretical foundation is sound, with clear connections to previous work including the GCM [3] and sparse Bayesian learning [4,5]. The mathematical derivations are well-presented, and the two proposed algorithms (MJLE and MMLE) are theoretically grounded. However, the evaluation could be strengthened by including a thorough comparison with IS-NMF in the single-resolution case, particularly regarding the quality of the estimated W and H matrices and computational efficiency.

The clarity of the paper is excellent. The introduction provides a clear motivation for the work by identifying the gap between existing generative models of STFT (GCM) and the raw signal. The mathematical formulations are well-organized, and the connection to previous work is appropriately contextualized. The multi-resolution extension (Section 4) is particularly well-motivated and clearly explained.

The originality of the work is strong. The core contribution—modeling the time-domain signal directly rather than the STFT—represents a meaningful advance beyond the GCM framework. The multi-resolution capability is a novel application that was not possible within traditional NMF settings, and the paper clearly demonstrates how LRTFS enables this capability.

The significance of the work is well-supported by the experimental results. The music decomposition and speech enhancement experiments demonstrate practical utility, with the multi-resolution approach showing clear advantages over single-resolution baselines. The 6.6 dB average SNR improvement in speech enhancement compared to 6 dB for IS-NMF (and 4.6 dB for OMLSA) provides evidence of the method's practical value.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Novel generative model that directly models the time-domain signal
- Multi-resolution capability represents a significant advancement over traditional NMF
- Strong theoretical foundation with clear connections to previous work
- Promising experimental results in audio applications
- Well-written and well-organized paper

Arguments against acceptance:
- Lack of comprehensive comparison with IS-NMF in single-resolution settings
- Insufficient discussion of computational complexity and scalability
- Limited analysis of how the estimated W and H matrices compare with IS-NMF in single-resolution scenarios

Overall, this is a strong contribution to the field with clear potential for impact in audio processing applications. The multi-resolution extension is particularly compelling, and the paper effectively demonstrates its advantages. With minor improvements to the evaluation section, this would be an excellent paper for NIPS.
