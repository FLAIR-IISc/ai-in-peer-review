Summary and relation to prior work:
This paper introduces a low-rank time–frequency synthesis (LRTFS) model that is generative in the time domain. Instead of imposing a low-rank structure on the variance of STFT analysis coefficients (as in the Gaussian Composite Model underlying IS-NMF), LRTFS places a low-rank variance model on complex synthesis coefficients used to reconstruct the signal from a time–frequency dictionary. This shift enables a true generative model of the waveform and naturally supports multi-resolution representations by combining multiple frames.

The authors derive two estimation strategies: (1) a maximum joint likelihood EM algorithm (MJLE) using an auxiliary variable that alternates a Wiener-like shrinkage step on the synthesis coefficients with an IS-NMF update on their squared magnitudes, and (2) a maximum marginal likelihood EM (MMLE) akin to type-II SBL that integrates out the coefficients but currently does not scale due to large matrix inversions. They also show how to reconstruct latent components and extend the model to multiple resolutions by stacking frames.

Experiments demonstrate a tonal/transient/music residual decomposition and a semi-supervised speech enhancement setup, with average SNR gains over IS-NMF and OMLSA baselines. The work connects to IS-NMF and the GCM (Févotte et al.), NMF (Lee & Seung), sparse Bayesian learning (Tipping; Wipf & Rao), and hybrid/morphological audio models (Daudet; Elad).

Strengths:
- Conceptual novelty: modeling low-rank structure in synthesis (time-domain generative) rather than analysis space, bridging signal synthesis and NMF.
- Clean probabilistic formulation with clear links to IS-NMF, Wiener filtering, and SBL; the MJLE EM is simple and avoids matrix inversions.
- Multi-resolution extension is elegant and practical; stacking frames requires minimal changes to the algorithm.
- Empirical results indicate benefits for audio decomposition and speech enhancement; reconstruction of components is straightforward.

Weaknesses and questions:
- Missing single-resolution head-to-head against IS-NMF on the same data: Do W and H learned from |α|^2 in LRTFS resemble those from IS-NMF on STFT power? How do quality, speed, and stability compare when using a single frame?
- Computational aspects: Please report per-iteration complexity and wall-clock times for MJLE (and multi-resolution MJLE), number of iterations, and stopping criteria. How sensitive is runtime to the redundancy of Φ?
- Hyperparameters and stability: The EM requires β ≤ λ/δΦ. How are β and λ chosen in practice, especially when λ changes during iterations (warm-restart)? Is δΦ recomputed or bounded for overcomplete frames?
- Learning λ: You note that joint learning of λ performed poorly (as in SBL). Could you provide a short analysis or guideline on when to fix λ, schedules that work, and the effect on reconstruction and overfitting?
- MMLE scalability: Any thoughts on iterative solvers (conjugate gradients with matrix–vector products using Φ and Φ*), diagonal/low-rank approximations, Krylov subspace methods, or variational/message-passing approximations to avoid explicit inverses?
- Evaluation scope: Results rely mainly on SNR. Consider adding perceptual measures (PESQ/STOI), more noise types and conditions, and ablations quantifying the gain from multi-resolution vs. the best single-resolution setting. A comparison to a “stacked-STFT IS-NMF” baseline would isolate the effect of modeling in synthesis space.
- Reproducibility: Several practical details would help readers reproduce results: initialization (W, H, α), K selection and sensitivity, β and λ values/schedules, frame redundancy, and code/data availability.
- Presentation: There are minor typos (e.g., “mentionned”), some reference entries are incomplete, and notation occasionally shifts between (f, n) and single indices. Clarifying real-signal handling (Hermitian symmetry) and assumptions on Φ would improve readability.

Quality:
The model and MJLE derivations appear sound, with careful links to known EM architectures (Figueiredo & Nowak). The λ update used in practice is heuristic but justified to decrease the objective. Convergence is to local optima as expected. A more explicit discussion of identifiability and the effect of initialization would strengthen the technical assessment.

Clarity:
The paper is generally well written and organized. Proof sketches or references for key steps (e.g., equivalence to IS divergence on |α|^2) are appropriate. Cleaning up notation, typos, and references, and adding more implementation details would further improve clarity.

Originality:
The synthesis-side low-rank variance modeling and its seamless multi-resolution extension are, to my knowledge, new and nontrivial. The connection to SBL with tied variances is interesting and distinguishes LRTFS from standard sparse synthesis models.

Significance:
The approach is promising for audio decomposition and enhancement, and the multi-resolution capability is valuable. If MMLE can be scaled, the framework could impact broader inverse problems (e.g., multichannel separation).

Specific requests to authors:
- Provide a controlled single-frame comparison to IS-NMF: quality, runtime, convergence robustness, and similarity of learned W/H.
- Report runtime/complexity and iteration counts; include ablations on K, β, λ, and frame choices.
- Add metrics beyond SNR and more diverse test conditions; quantify multi-resolution gains.
- Clarify β/λ selection and stability; discuss initialization and identifiability.
- Fix typos and reference formatting; consider releasing code.

Pros:
- Principled, time-domain generative model with low-rank structure in synthesis coefficients.
- Efficient EM for MJLE with practical multi-resolution extension.
- Demonstrated gains over common baselines.

Cons:
- Limited comparative evaluation in the single-resolution regime.
- MMLE currently impractical; scalability not demonstrated.
- Several implementation choices (β, λ) are heuristic without clear guidelines; limited reproducibility details.

Overall, I find the contribution original and relevant to NIPS, with solid technical grounding and clear potential impact in audio modeling. I lean toward acceptance, contingent on strengthening the empirical comparison and clarifying practical aspects.
