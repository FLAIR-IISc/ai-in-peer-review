Summary
The paper presents a thoughtful reinterpretation of time–frequency factorizations. Rather than relying on analysis coefficients of STFT-based formulations, it proposes a more general framework that can operate on arbitrary transforms while maintaining a direct link to the time-domain signal. This design choice increases modeling flexibility and offers a clearer connection between the factorization and the original signal.

Strengths
- Clarity and readability: The paper is well written and accessible to readers familiar with the area. The main ideas are conveyed clearly, and the exposition is well structured.
- Technical quality: The theoretical development appears sound and carefully executed. The framework’s generality and its direct time-domain linkage are appealing and potentially impactful.
- Originality: Above average. While it builds on familiar concepts, the move beyond STFT analysis coefficients to a transform-agnostic, time-domain-connected formulation is a meaningful advance.

Weaknesses
- Communication of significance: The potential implications of the framework are not fully articulated. The paper could better explain what new capabilities are enabled (e.g., handling diverse transforms, improved reconstruction, or better modeling of structure) and in which scenarios this leads to tangible benefits.
- Empirical evaluation: The most notable shortcoming is the limited and unconvincing experimental evidence. The supplemental material does not sufficiently demonstrate the practical advantages, and the main paper does not compare against the methods it extends or supersedes. This gap makes it hard to assess the real-world value of the contribution.

Suggestions for improvement
- Comparative baselines: Include direct comparisons with established time–frequency factorization approaches, especially STFT-based models (e.g., NMF/CNMF variants) and other transform-domain factorization methods. Use standard benchmarks and datasets to enable fair evaluation.
- Quantitative metrics: Report commonly used metrics relevant to the application domain (e.g., for audio source separation: SDR/SIR/SAR; for denoising: SNR, PESQ/STOI; or task-appropriate alternatives). Provide runtime and convergence statistics to assess computational trade-offs.
- Ablations and sensitivity: Analyze the effect of key design choices, such as the choice of transform, regularizers, constraints, and model hyperparameters. Show when the time-domain linkage provides measurable improvements (e.g., phase handling, reconstruction fidelity).
- Scope and assumptions: Clarify conditions on the transforms (invertibility, frame properties, redundancy), identifiability assumptions, and any requirements for stability. Summarize optimization details and state any convergence guarantees or practical heuristics used.
- Practical guidance: Offer criteria for choosing transforms for specific tasks and discuss how the framework integrates with existing pipelines. If possible, provide code or pseudocode to enhance reproducibility.
- Expanded supplement: Strengthen the supplement with additional experiments, qualitative illustrations (e.g., spectrograms, signal reconstructions), and case studies that highlight where the proposed framework excels or fails.

Overall assessment
This is a good paper with a solid and interesting theoretical contribution. Its originality and clarity are clear strengths. The primary weakness lies in the limited empirical validation and lack of comparisons to closely related methods, including those it builds upon. While this is a significant area for improvement, it is not a fatal flaw given the quality and potential of the theoretical framework. I lean toward acceptance, especially if the authors can bolster the empirical section with comparative results and clearer articulation of the framework’s practical significance.
