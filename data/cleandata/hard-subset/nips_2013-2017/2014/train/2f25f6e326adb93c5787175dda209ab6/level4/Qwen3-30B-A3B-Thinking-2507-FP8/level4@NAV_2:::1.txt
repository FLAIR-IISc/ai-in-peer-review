

This paper extends the family of low-rank spectrogram factorization models for sound decomposition by generalizing latent factors over arbitrary (potentially overcomplete) bases, enabling multiresolution decomposition (e.g., tonal + transient components). Building upon the Gaussian Composite Model (GCM) introduced by Fevotte et al., which models spectrogram variance using low-rank factorization, the work provides a time-domain formulation of the estimated signal that accommodates different impulse response sets, including nonorthogonal ones. An EM-based estimation procedure (converging to a local optimum) is presented for the new model.

Quality: This represents a meaningful conceptual extension that provides a novel perspective on the GCM framework. The ability to incorporate overcomplete dictionaries is a valuable contribution that could improve source separation and denoising. While the estimation procedure appears technically sound, the reviewer found the detailed analysis challenging to follow in full detail, though the approach is impressive.

Clarity: The paper is well-motivated and clearly written with a solid theoretical foundation. However, the experimental evaluation is overly limited, consisting of only one purely illustrative decomposition example and a single SNR improvement measurement. The comparison with OMLSA is questionable as the OMLSA example appears significantly longer (7 seconds vs. 2 seconds), potentially affecting the fairness of the comparison. Additionally, the music denoising results exhibit noticeable artifacts, suggesting conventional approaches like wavelet shrinkage or Wiener filtering might achieve better perceptual quality.

Originality: The work builds upon the strong foundation of Fevotte et al.'s GCM, but the time-domain interpretation provides a fresh perspective on the problem. The extension to overcomplete bases through the time-domain formulation represents a noteworthy conceptual contribution.

Significance: The potential to apply matrix factorization techniques to overcomplete bases could have significant impact in audio processing. However, the current evaluation is insufficient to demonstrate this potential. The limited examples and superficial analysis prevent a strong assessment of significance. While the reformulation of probabilistic matrix factorization for sound analysis in terms of time-domain kernels enables EM-based analysis for multiresolution (overcomplete) bases, the lack of robust evaluation means the significance of the work cannot be properly established in its current form.
