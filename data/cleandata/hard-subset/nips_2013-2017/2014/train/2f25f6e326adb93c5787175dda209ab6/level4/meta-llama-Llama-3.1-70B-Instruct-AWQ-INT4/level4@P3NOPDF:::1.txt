This paper presents an extension to the family of low-rank spectrogram-factorization models of sound decomposition, generalizing the latent factors over an arbitrary (potentially overcomplete) set of bases. This allows for decomposition to be performed on "multiresolution" bases, such as tonal and transient bases, which is argued to improve source separation and denoising. The approach builds upon the Gaussian Composite Model (GCM) of Fevotte et al., which models the variance of each cell of a spectrogram using a low-rank matrix factorization. However, it expands the implicit convolution with multiple Gabor impulse responses corresponding to each row of the spectrogram, resulting in a purely time-domain expression for the estimated quantities. This expression can be modified to accommodate different sets of impulse responses, including non-orthogonal ones. An Expectation-Maximization (EM) analysis procedure is also presented.

In terms of quality, the idea is interesting, although its implications are not immediately clear. At first glance, it appears to be an alternative interpretation of Fevotte's GCM, but the ability to incorporate overcomplete dictionaries is a welcome addition. However, it is unclear why the potential complications arising from this approach do not cause more issues. The estimation procedure appears to be correct, and the analysis is impressive, although the details are difficult to follow.

The work is well-explained and motivated, but the evaluation is superficial, consisting of a single illustrative result and a Signal-to-Noise Ratio (SNR) improvement measurement. It is challenging to draw conclusions from a single example, and the included sound examples, while appreciated, are not convincing. The comparison between the proposed method and the OMLSA enhanced example is questionable, as the latter is significantly longer (7 seconds vs. 2 seconds), which may affect the fairness of the comparison. Furthermore, although the OMLSA example includes more background noise, the speech quality appears to be higher. The denoising of the music example comes at the expense of significant artifacts, which may be mitigated using conventional wavelet shrinkage or Wiener filtering.

The originality of the work relies heavily on the foundation laid by Fevotte et al., although the time-domain interpretation offers a new perspective on their work. The significance of the work is potentially substantial, as the ability to apply matrix-factorization techniques to overcomplete bases could have a significant impact. However, the evaluation and examples presented in this work are too limited to demonstrate this significance, making it difficult to rate the work as highly significant in its current form. The reformulation of probabilistic matrix-factorization sound analysis in terms of time-domain kernels allows for an EM analysis for multiresolution (overcomplete) bases, but the thin evaluation and significant artifacts in the examples fail to demonstrate its importance.
