This paper presents an extension to the family of low-rank spectrogram-factorization models for sound decomposition, generalizing the latent factors to an arbitrary, potentially overcomplete set of bases. This allows for decomposition on "multiresolution" bases, such as tonal and transient bases, which is argued to improve source separation and denoising. The approach builds upon the Gaussian Composite Model of Fevotte et al, which models the variance of each spectrogram cell using a low-rank matrix factorization. However, it expands the implicit convolution with multiple Gabor impulse responses corresponding to each spectrogram row, resulting in a purely time-domain expression that can be modified to work with different sets of impulse responses, including non-orthogonal ones. An Expectation-Maximization (EM) analysis procedure is also presented.

Quality: I find this insight interesting, although I am still grappling with its exact implications. Initially, it appears to be another interpretation of Fevotte's Gaussian Composite Model, but the ability to incorporate overcomplete dictionaries is a welcome addition. However, this extension seems to introduce potential complications, which I do not fully understand. I assume the estimation procedure is correct, and I am impressed by the analysis, although I was unable to follow it in detail.

Clarity: The authors have done a good job explaining and motivating their work. However, the evaluation is superficial, consisting of only one illustrative result and a single SNR improvement measurement, which is insufficient to draw meaningful conclusions. I appreciate the inclusion of sound examples, but I was not impressed. The OMLSA-enhanced example is significantly longer (7 seconds vs. 2 seconds) than the new result, raising concerns about the fairness of the comparison. Furthermore, although the OMLSA example includes more background noise, the speech quality appears higher, and the denoising of the music example comes at the expense of significant artifacts. I would expect conventional wavelet shrinkage or Wiener filtering to yield much higher perceived quality in this case.

Originality: The work relies heavily on the foundation established by Fevotte et al, although the time-domain interpretation offers a new perspective.

Significance: The ability to apply matrix-factorization techniques to overcomplete bases seems to have significant potential impact. However, the examples and evaluation presented in this work are too limited and superficial to demonstrate this potential, making it difficult to assess the work's significance. The reformulation of probabilistic matrix-factorization sound analysis in terms of time-domain kernels allows for EM analysis with multiresolution (overcomplete) bases, but the thin evaluation and significant artifacts in the examples fail to demonstrate its importance in its current form.
