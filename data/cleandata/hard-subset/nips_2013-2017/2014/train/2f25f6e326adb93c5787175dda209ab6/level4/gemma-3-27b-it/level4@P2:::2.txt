This paper introduces a novel generative model for time-frequency synthesis, extending the commonly used Gaussian Composite Model (GCM) – which models the STFT coefficients – to directly model the raw signal. This allows for a more complete generative approach and, crucially, opens the door to multi-resolution signal representations, a capability absent in traditional NMF-based methods. The core idea is to model synthesis coefficients, rather than analysis coefficients, within a low-rank framework.

The work builds on previous research in matrix factorization for signal processing, particularly Nonnegative Matrix Factorization (NMF) of spectrograms [1]. It addresses a limitation of earlier probabilistic latent factor models like the GCM [3], which, while providing a generative model for the STFT, didn’t extend to the raw signal. The authors propose a Low-Rank Time-Frequency Synthesis (LRTFS) model and derive Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithms for parameter estimation. They demonstrate the approach with music decomposition and speech enhancement experiments.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The approach of directly modeling the raw signal through synthesis coefficients is a significant extension to existing generative models in this area. The ability to naturally incorporate multi-resolution representations is a particularly strong contribution.
*   **Theoretical Foundation:** The paper provides a clear theoretical formulation of the LRTFS model, grounding the method in probabilistic principles. The connection to sparse Bayesian learning [4, 5] is insightful.
*   **Potential for Multi-Resolution Analysis:** This is a significant advantage, particularly for audio signals where different components may require different time-frequency resolutions. The examples provided demonstrate the feasibility of this approach.
*   **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and logically structured, making the key concepts accessible.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Experimental Comparison:** The comparison to IS-NMF in the single-resolution case is lacking. The paper states a curiosity about the similarity of resulting W and H matrices, and relative speed/stability, but does not provide results. Demonstrating competitive performance (or clearly outlining the trade-offs) against established methods like IS-NMF is crucial.
*   **Computational Complexity:** While the authors acknowledge the computational challenge of the MMLE approach and do not present results for it, the scalability of the MJLE algorithm, particularly for large signals, isn't thoroughly discussed. A more detailed analysis of computational cost would be beneficial.
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The dependence on the hyperparameter λ and the use of a “warm-restart” strategy suggest potential sensitivity. A more rigorous investigation of hyperparameter tuning and robustness is needed.
*    **Limited Baselines:** The speech enhancement experiment includes a comparison to OMLSA [14] which is good, but adding more recent state-of-the-art speech enhancement methods would strengthen the evaluation.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a genuinely novel approach to signal modeling that addresses a limitation of existing methods.
*   The theoretical formulation is sound, and the proposed algorithms are clearly described.
*   The multi-resolution capability is a significant contribution with practical potential.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The lack of a thorough comparative evaluation against established methods (especially in the single-resolution case) weakens the claim of improvement.
*   The computational scalability concerns need to be addressed.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a promising paper with a novel contribution to the field of time-frequency signal processing. The extension to multi-resolution representations is particularly exciting. However, the lack of a comprehensive experimental comparison to existing methods and the limited discussion of computational scalability are significant concerns. 

**Recommendation:**

I recommend **weak accept** with the expectation that the authors address the weaknesses outlined above, particularly by providing a more thorough comparison to IS-NMF and a discussion of the algorithm's scalability. Addressing these points would significantly strengthen the paper and solidify its contribution to the field.
