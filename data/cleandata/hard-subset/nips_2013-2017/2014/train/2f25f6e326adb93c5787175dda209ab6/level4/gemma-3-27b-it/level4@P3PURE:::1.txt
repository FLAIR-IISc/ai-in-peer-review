This paper presents an extension to low-rank spectrogram factorization models for sound decomposition, generalizing latent factors over an arbitrary (potentially overcomplete) set of bases, enabling decomposition on “multiresolution” bases (e.g., tonal and transient components). This generalization is motivated by potential improvements in source separation and denoising. The work builds upon the Gaussian Composite Model (GCM) of Fevotte et al., which models spectrogram cell variances using low-rank matrix factorization. However, it expands the implicit convolution with multiple Gabor impulse responses – corresponding to each row of the spectrogram – to derive a purely time-domain expression for the estimated parameters. This formulation allows for modifications to accommodate different, including non-orthogonal, sets of impulse responses. An Expectation-Maximization (EM) procedure, with analysis of its local optima, is presented for parameter estimation.

Quality: This is an interesting insight, though the full implications are still unfolding. Initially, it appears as an alternative understanding of Fevotte’s GCM, but the incorporation of overcomplete dictionaries is a valuable addition. However, this generalization seems likely to introduce complications, and the paper did not immediately clarify why these are mitigated. I assume the estimation procedure is correct – and I am impressed by this level of analysis – although the detailed derivation was beyond my ability to follow.

Clarity: The work is well-explained and motivated. However, the evaluation is superficial. We are presented with only one illustrative result and a single Signal-to-Noise Ratio (SNR) improvement measurement, which is insufficient to draw meaningful conclusions. While I appreciated the included sound examples, I was not particularly impressed. For instance, the OMLSA-enhanced example is significantly longer (7 seconds versus 2 seconds) than the proposed method’s result, raising concerns about a fair comparison. Furthermore, despite the OMLSA example containing more background noise (partly due to its greater length), the speech quality is noticeably higher. The denoising of the music example introduces substantial artifacts; conventional wavelet shrinkage or Wiener filtering would likely achieve superior perceived quality.

Originality: The work heavily relies on the foundation laid by Fevotte et al., though the time-domain interpretation offers a fresh perspective.

Significance: The potential of applying matrix-factorization techniques to overcomplete bases is significant. However, the limited and superficial examples and evaluation provided do not adequately demonstrate this potential. Currently, assessing substantial significance requires an act of faith. The reformulation of probabilistic matrix-factorization-based sound analysis in terms of time-domain kernels enables EM analysis for multiresolution (overcomplete) bases, but the thin evaluation and prominent artifacts in the examples fail to convincingly demonstrate its importance in its current form.
