

This paper extends the family of low-rank spectrogram-factorization models for sound decomposition by generalizing the latent factors over an arbitrary (potentially overcomplete) set of bases, enabling decomposition on "multiresolution" bases (e.g., tonal + transient). The authors argue this approach improves source separation and denoising. Starting from Fevotte et al.'s Gaussian Composite Model (GCM), which models spectrogram cell variances via low-rank factorization, the work reinterprets the implicit convolution with multiple Gabor impulse responses as a purely time-domain expression. This reinterpretation allows modification for arbitrary impulse responses, including nonorthogonal ones, and introduces an EM-based estimation procedure (converging to a local optimum).  

**Quality**: The insight is interesting, though the precise implications require further clarification. While the time-domain reinterpretation offers a novel perspective on GCM, the reliance on Fevotte's foundation and the potential complications from overcomplete bases warrant deeper analysis. The estimation procedure appears theoretically sound, but the technical details are not fully transparent.  

**Clarity**: The core methodology is well-motivated and clearly explained. However, the evaluation is superficial: only one illustrative example and a single SNR metric are provided, which is insufficient to draw meaningful conclusions. The included audio examples are helpful but unconvincing. The OMLSA-enhanced example (7 seconds) is notably longer than the proposed method's example (2 seconds), raising fairness concerns in comparison. Additionally, while OMLSA includes more background noise due to its length, the speech quality is perceptually higher. The music denoising example exhibits severe audible artifacts, suggesting conventional approaches like wavelet shrinkage or Wiener filtering would yield superior perceived quality.  

**Originality**: The work builds substantially on Fevotte et al.'s GCM. The time-domain reinterpretation provides a fresh perspective but does not constitute a major departure from prior work.  

**Significance**: The potential to apply matrix-factorization techniques to overcomplete bases holds promise. However, the current evaluation lacks depth and evidence—superficial examples and significant artifacts in the results fail to demonstrate tangible impact. The significance claim remains unsubstantiated; the work currently represents a theoretical reformulation without compelling empirical validation.
