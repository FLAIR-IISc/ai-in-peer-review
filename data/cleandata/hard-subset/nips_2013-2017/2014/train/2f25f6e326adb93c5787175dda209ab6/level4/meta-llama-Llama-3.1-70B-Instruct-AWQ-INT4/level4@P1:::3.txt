This paper presents a thoughtful interpretation of time/frequency factorizations. Instead of using existing formulations that are built on the analysis coefficients of STFT transforms, it presents a more flexible framework that can be applied on an arbitrary transform and is directly linked to the time domain signal.

The paper is easy to read for people in this area and of good quality. The originality is above average, although the significance is not fully communicated in the paper. Perhaps the most disappointing part of this paper is in terms of the results and the evaluation. The supplemental material is not particularly convincing and the paper itself doesn't compare this work to the methods it builds on. I don't think that this is a significantly weak point (the theoretical development is itself enough to make this an interesting paper), but I think it would have made the paper a lot better.

The paper provides a clear and concise summary of the main ideas and relates them to previous work at NIPS and elsewhere. The authors demonstrate a good understanding of the topic and provide a thorough explanation of the proposed model, including its strengths and weaknesses. The writing is clear and well-organized, making it easy to follow the authors' arguments.

One of the strengths of the paper is its ability to bridge the gap between time-frequency synthesis and traditional NMF approaches. The proposed model, LRTFS, offers a more flexible framework that can accommodate multi-resolution representations, which is not possible with traditional NMF methods. The authors also provide a clear explanation of the estimation procedures, including the EM algorithm, which is well-suited for the problem at hand.

However, the paper could benefit from more comprehensive results and comparisons to existing methods. While the supplemental material provides some examples, it is not particularly convincing, and the paper itself does not provide a thorough comparison to other methods. This makes it difficult to fully evaluate the significance of the proposed model.

In terms of quality, the paper is well-written, and the theory is solid. The authors demonstrate a good understanding of the topic and provide a thorough explanation of the proposed model. However, the lack of comprehensive results and comparisons is a weakness that prevents the paper from being truly outstanding.

Overall, I would recommend this paper for publication, but with the suggestion that the authors provide more comprehensive results and comparisons to existing methods. This would strengthen the paper and make it more convincing.

Arguments for acceptance:

* The paper presents a thoughtful interpretation of time/frequency factorizations.
* The proposed model, LRTFS, offers a more flexible framework that can accommodate multi-resolution representations.
* The authors provide a clear explanation of the estimation procedures, including the EM algorithm.
* The paper is well-written, and the theory is solid.

Arguments against acceptance:

* The paper lacks comprehensive results and comparisons to existing methods.
* The supplemental material is not particularly convincing.
* The significance of the proposed model is not fully communicated in the paper.
