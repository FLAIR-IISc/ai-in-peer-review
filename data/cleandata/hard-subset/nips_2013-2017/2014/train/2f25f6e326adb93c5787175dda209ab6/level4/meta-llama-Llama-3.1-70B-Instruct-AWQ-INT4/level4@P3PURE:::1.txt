This paper presents an extension to the family of low-rank spectrogram factorization models for sound decomposition, generalizing the latent factors over an arbitrary, potentially overcomplete set of bases. This enables decomposition on "multiresolution" bases (e.g., tonal + transient), which is argued to aid in source separation and denoising. The work builds upon the Gaussian Composite Model (GCM) of Fevotte et al., which models the variance of each cell of a spectrogram using a low-rank matrix factorization. However, it expands the implicit convolution with multiple Gabor impulse responses corresponding to each row of the spectrogram, resulting in a purely time-domain expression for the estimated quantities. This expression can then be modified to accommodate different sets of impulse responses, including non-orthogonal ones. An Expectation-Maximization (EM) analysis procedure is presented to find a local optimum.

Quality: I find this insight interesting, although I am still trying to grasp its exact implications. At first glance, it appears to be another interpretation of Fevotte's GCM, but the ability to incorporate overcomplete dictionaries is a welcome addition. On the other hand, this extension seems like it should introduce some complications, and I did not immediately understand why these did not cause more issues. I assume the estimation procedure is correct, and I am impressed by this type of analysis, although I was unable to follow it in detail.

Clarity: The work is well-explained and motivated. However, the evaluation is very superficial, consisting of one purely illustrative result and a single SNR improvement measurement. I do not think it is possible to draw any meaningful conclusions from a single example. I appreciated the inclusion of sound examples, but I was not particularly impressed. The OMLSA-enhanced example seems to be significantly longer (7 seconds vs. 2 seconds) than the new result, which raises concerns about the fairness of the comparison. Furthermore, although the OMLSA example includes more background noise (partly due to its length), the speech sounds much higher quality to me. The denoising of the music example comes at the expense of very significant artifacts, and I would expect conventional wavelet shrinkage or Wiener filtering to yield much higher perceived quality in this case.

Originality: The work appears to heavily rely on the foundation laid by Fevotte et al., although the time-domain interpretation did offer a new perspective.

Significance: At first glance, the ability to apply matrix factorization techniques to overcomplete bases seems like it could have a significant impact. However, the examples and evaluation presented in this work are too thin and superficial to demonstrate this potential. As it stands, it is an act of faith to rate this work as having substantial significance. The reformulation of probabilistic matrix factorization sound analysis in terms of time-domain kernels does allow for an EM analysis for multiresolution (overcomplete) bases. However, the thin evaluation and significant artifacts in the examples fail to demonstrate its importance in its current form.
