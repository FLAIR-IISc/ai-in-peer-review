Many single-channel signal decomposition techniques rely on a low-rank factorization of a time-frequency transform. In particular, nonnegative matrix factorization (NMF) of the spectrogram – the (power) magnitude of the short-time Fourier transform (STFT) – has been considered in many audio applications. This paper builds upon previous work [1, 3] by proposing a novel signal synthesis model with low-rank time-frequency structure, moving beyond a generative model of the STFT to one of the raw signal itself. This allows for multi-resolution representations, a capability lacking in traditional NMF settings. The authors describe two expectation-maximization algorithms for estimation within the new model and demonstrate results on music decomposition and speech enhancement.

This is a compelling paper that addresses an important limitation of existing methods. The idea of directly modeling the signal synthesis coefficients, rather than the analysis coefficients, is insightful and opens up interesting possibilities, particularly the multi-resolution extension, which I found particularly appealing. The paper is well-motivated, clearly outlining the limitations of previous approaches and positioning the contribution effectively within the existing literature.

The strengths of the paper include the theoretical grounding of the proposed model, the clear description of the EM algorithms, and the demonstration of the benefits in two relevant audio applications. The connection to sparse Bayesian learning is also well-articulated. The experimental section provides a good starting point for evaluating the performance of the method.

However, there are some areas where the paper could be strengthened. While the authors present results on multi-resolution signal decompositions, a more direct comparison to IS-NMF in a single-resolution scenario would be valuable. Specifically, it would be useful to know if the proposed method yields comparable W and H matrices to IS-NMF, and whether the proposed algorithm achieves similar speed and stability as the well-established multiplicative update algorithm for IS-NMF. Additionally, the justification for the choice of λ is somewhat limited; a more systematic exploration of its impact on performance would be beneficial. The discussion of the MMLE approach is somewhat brief, and the authors acknowledge scalability issues that prevent its full evaluation in the experiments. A more detailed discussion of these limitations would be helpful.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novel approach to signal synthesis with a generative model of time-frequency coefficients.
*   Enables multi-resolution representations, extending the capabilities of traditional NMF.
*   Solid theoretical foundation with well-defined EM algorithms.
*   Demonstrated performance on relevant audio applications.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   Lack of a direct performance comparison to IS-NMF in a single-resolution setting.
*   Limited discussion of the impact of the hyperparameter λ.
*   Scalability issues with the MMLE approach.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with a clear contribution to the field. While there are some minor weaknesses, the novelty of the approach, the theoretical rigor, and the promising experimental results outweigh these concerns. I believe this paper would be a valuable addition to NIPS. I recommend acceptance, contingent upon the authors addressing the points raised regarding the comparison with IS-NMF and the discussion of the hyperparameter λ.
