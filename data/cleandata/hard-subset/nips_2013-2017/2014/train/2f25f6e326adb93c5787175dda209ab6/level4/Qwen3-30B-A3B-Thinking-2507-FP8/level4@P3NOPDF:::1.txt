

This paper extends low-rank spectrogram-factorization models for sound decomposition by generalizing latent factors over arbitrary (potentially overcomplete) bases, enabling multi-resolution analysis (e.g., separating tonal and transient components). The core contribution reinterprets Fevotte et al.’s Gaussian Composite Model (GCM) in the time domain, replacing the implicit convolution with Gabor impulse responses with a purely time-domain formulation. This allows the framework to accommodate nonorthogonal bases and supports EM-based estimation for multiresolution decomposition.  

**Strengths.** The time-domain reinterpretation is conceptually elegant and technically sound, offering a fresh perspective on GCM that clarifies how latent factors interact with arbitrary basis sets. The EM procedure for overcomplete bases represents a nontrivial extension of prior work, and the derivation is well-motivated. The paper’s clarity in explaining the model’s structure and the time-domain shift is commendable.  

**Weaknesses.** The evaluation is severely underdeveloped and undermines the paper’s potential. Only a single illustrative example and a solitary SNR metric are provided, which is insufficient to draw meaningful conclusions about performance. Crucially, the comparison with OMLSA (a baseline method) is unfair: the OMLSA example spans 7 seconds versus the proposed method’s 2-second example. This discrepancy introduces more background noise in the OMLSA output, yet the speech quality appears perceptually superior. The music denoising example further weakens the case—the proposed method introduces severe artifacts (e.g., audible distortion and phase smearing), while conventional approaches like wavelet shrinkage or Wiener filtering would likely achieve higher perceptual quality without such artifacts. The paper fails to address why overcomplete bases do not exacerbate issues like non-uniqueness or convergence instability, despite acknowledging these as potential complications.  

**Originality.** The work builds heavily on Fevotte et al.’s GCM but introduces a novel time-domain perspective that enables overcomplete bases. This reinterpretation is the paper’s primary novelty, though it remains tightly coupled to the foundational model. The extension to nonorthogonal bases is technically significant but not sufficiently distinguished from prior work on overcomplete dictionaries in audio processing.  

**Significance.** The potential impact of applying matrix-factorization techniques to overcomplete bases is substantial for source separation and denoising. However, the current evaluation provides no evidence that this potential translates to real-world gains. The lack of rigorous experiments—beyond a single SNR metric and uncomparable examples—leaves the significance speculative. The paper asks readers to accept its promise without empirical validation, which is insufficient for a venue like NIPS.  

**Overall.** The paper presents an interesting theoretical extension with a clean time-domain formulation. However, the evaluation is inadequate to support its claims, and the examples fail to demonstrate practical advantages over existing methods. The artifacts in the music example and the unfair OMLSA comparison particularly undermine confidence in the results. While the core idea merits further exploration, the current work lacks the empirical rigor required to justify its significance. A revision with comprehensive experiments—including multiple audio examples, standardized comparisons, and perceptual evaluations—would be necessary to strengthen the contribution. As presented, the paper does not sufficiently demonstrate that its approach outperforms or even matches conventional techniques in practice.
