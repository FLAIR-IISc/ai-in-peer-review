Summary
The paper proposes Low-Rank Time-Frequency Synthesis (LRTFS), a generative model that extends low-rank spectrogram factorization to the time domain. Building on the Gaussian Composite Model (GCM) for STFT coefficients (e.g., Févotte et al.), the authors shift the low-rank prior from analysis coefficients to synthesis coefficients of a time-frequency (t-f) frame. The waveform is modeled as a linear combination of atoms with complex Gaussian synthesis coefficients whose variances admit a low-rank factorization WH. This formulation enables the use of arbitrary, potentially overcomplete, t-f dictionaries and naturally supports multi-resolution modeling by stacking multiple frames (e.g., short for transients, long for tonals). The paper presents two estimation strategies: (1) maximum joint likelihood estimation (MJLE) via an EM algorithm that avoids large matrix inversions by using an auxiliary variable trick; and (2) maximum marginal likelihood estimation (MMLE, type-II ML), closely related to sparse Bayesian learning (SBL), but not currently scalable. Experiments include an illustrative tonal/transient music decomposition and a semi-supervised speech enhancement task, with reported SNR gains and comparisons to IS-NMF and OMLSA.

Quality
- The modeling move from STFT-domain GCM to a waveform-level synthesis model is technically sound and well motivated. The link to SBL is insightful: LRTFS ties variances through WH rather than assigning free variances per coefficient, trading explicit sparsity for structured low-rankness.
- The MJLE/EM derivation is carefully constructed and computationally practical (no T×T inversions; efficient use of Φα and Φ* operations). The MMLE derivation is consistent with SBL literature, though not yet practical at audio scale.
- That said, some aspects need clarification: identifiability and conditioning with overcomplete frames; the practical selection and effect of β (subject to β ≤ λ/δΦ); the role and influence of the implicit log(|α|²) term (sparsity) in the joint objective; and the sensitivity to λ (which is treated as a hyperparameter or warm-restarted).

Clarity
- The paper is generally well written and logically organized. The connection to prior work (IS-NMF/GCM, SBL) is clear and helpful.
- The experimental section is thin. Results are largely illustrative, with limited quantitative evaluation and few baselines. Some comparison details are unclear (e.g., segment durations differ in the OMLSA example), making it hard to assess fairness.

Originality
- The work is a natural but nontrivial extension of GCM/NMF: it provides a generative model for the raw signal with low-rank time-frequency structure and straightforwardly accommodates overcomplete and multi-resolution dictionaries.
- The synthesis viewpoint and the EM algorithm that mirrors “Wiener filtering after IS-NMF” at its first iteration offer a fresh perspective on the relationship between NMF-style models and shrinkage-type estimators.

Significance
- The ability to combine low-rank modeling with overcomplete, multi-resolution frames is promising for audio decomposition, source separation, and enhancement. The conceptual bridge between time-domain synthesis and low-rank TF structure could influence future work.
- However, the current evaluation is too limited to substantiate impact. The examples show potential but also audible artifacts, and the superiority over strong baselines is not convincingly demonstrated.

Pros (arguments for acceptance)
- Conceptual advance: a coherent generative waveform model with low-rank TF variance and a clean link to GCM and SBL.
- Practical EM algorithm for MJLE that scales to real audio without large matrix inversions.
- Natural extension to multi-resolution modeling with multiple frames, a capability difficult to realize in standard NMF settings.
- Clear mathematical development and connections to Wiener filtering/IS-NMF.

Cons (arguments against acceptance)
- Experimental evidence is sparse: few datasets, limited metrics, and potential issues with fair comparison (e.g., mismatched durations).
- Audible artifacts remain; the paper lacks perceptual evaluations (e.g., PESQ, STOI) and standard separation metrics (SDR/SIR/SAR).
- No ablation to quantify the benefit of multi-resolution versus single-resolution, or of synthesis versus standard GCM on identical conditions.
- MMLE is presented but not usable at scale; the practical section thus depends solely on MJLE.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide broader, controlled experiments on multiple datasets with standardized metrics (PESQ/STOI for speech; SDR/SIR/SAR for separation; MOS or listening tests if feasible).
- Ensure fair comparisons (identical durations, frame parameters, and preprocessing). Include baselines beyond IS-NMF and OMLSA, such as PLCA/GCM variants with phase reconstruction, morphological component analysis, and sparse coding in multi-resolution dictionaries.
- Add ablations: single vs multi-resolution; orthonormal vs overcomplete frames; effect of K; effect of λ and β; impact of the implicit sparsity term.
- Discuss identifiability/conditioning with redundant frames, and give practical guidance for choosing frames and β. Report runtimes and memory usage.
- If possible, release code and data splits to improve reproducibility.

Overall assessment
This is a thoughtful and well-motivated modeling contribution that unifies low-rank TF structure with time-domain synthesis and enables multi-resolution representations. The methodological development is solid and potentially impactful. However, the empirical validation is currently too limited to convincingly demonstrate the claimed advantages. Strengthening the evaluation and clarifying practical aspects would substantially improve the paper’s case for acceptance.
