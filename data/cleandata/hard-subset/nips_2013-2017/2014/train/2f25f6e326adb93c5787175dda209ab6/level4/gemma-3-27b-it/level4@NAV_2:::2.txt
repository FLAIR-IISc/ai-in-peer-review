This paper introduces a novel low-rank time-frequency synthesis (LRTFS) model, extending traditional nonnegative matrix factorization (NMF) approaches by providing a generative model of the raw signal, rather than just its spectrogram. This is a significant step, as it enables multi-resolution representations not possible with conventional NMF. The work builds upon prior research establishing the connection between NMF with the Itakura-Saito divergence and a Gaussian Composite Model (GCM) of the STFT [3], and addresses the limitation of the GCM being solely a model of the STFT itself. 

The core idea – modeling synthesis coefficients directly – is compelling and logically sound. The authors describe two expectation-maximization (EM) algorithms for estimation, demonstrating a strong theoretical understanding of the problem. The experimental results, including music decomposition and speech enhancement, provide initial validation of the approach. The multi-resolution extension presented in Section 4 is particularly noteworthy and opens up interesting possibilities for applications where signals possess varying time-frequency characteristics. The connection to sparse Bayesian learning (SBL) [4, 5] is also well-articulated, highlighting the relationships to existing methods.

Strengths of the paper include its clear mathematical formulation, the novelty of the synthesis-based generative model, and the potential for multi-resolution analysis. The presentation is generally well-organized, and the authors acknowledge the limitations of their approach, such as the computational complexity of the MMLE method. The citations are relevant and appropriately used.

However, the paper would benefit from a more thorough comparative evaluation. While the authors mention comparing to IS-NMF in the speech enhancement experiment, a detailed analysis of performance in the single-resolution case, compared with standard IS-NMF, is missing. Specifically, it would be valuable to know if the proposed algorithm yields comparable dictionary matrices (W and H), and whether its computational speed and stability are comparable to the well-established multiplicative update rules for IS-NMF. The discussion of the limitations of MMLE (scalability) is valid, but further elaboration on potential solutions (e.g., exploiting sparsity structures) would be beneficial. Additionally, the experimental section, while providing initial results, could be strengthened with more comprehensive quantitative evaluations and comparisons to state-of-the-art methods.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** The LRTFS model represents a significant advance over traditional NMF approaches by providing a generative model of the raw signal.
*   **Theoretical Soundness:** The mathematical formulation is clear and rigorous, and the EM algorithms are well-defined.
*   **Potential Impact:** The multi-resolution capability opens up new avenues for research in areas such as audio processing and speech enhancement.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized, making it easy to understand the key concepts.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Limited Comparative Evaluation:** The lack of a thorough comparison to standard IS-NMF, particularly in the single-resolution case, weakens the claim of improvement.
*   **Scalability Concerns:** The computational complexity of MMLE poses a practical limitation for large-scale applications.
*   **Experimental Scope:** The experiments, while illustrative, could benefit from more extensive quantitative evaluation and comparison to other state-of-the-art methods.

**Overall:**

This is a promising paper with a novel contribution to the field of time-frequency analysis. While some areas could be strengthened, the originality and potential impact of the work warrant acceptance, conditional on the authors addressing the concerns regarding comparative evaluation and scalability in a revised version.
