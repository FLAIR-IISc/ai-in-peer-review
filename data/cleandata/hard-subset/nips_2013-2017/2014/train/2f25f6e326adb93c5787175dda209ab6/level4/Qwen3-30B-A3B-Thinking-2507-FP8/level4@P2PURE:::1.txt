

This paper introduces an extension to the family of low-rank spectrogram-factorization models for sound decomposition, generalizing the latent factors over an arbitrary (potentially overcomplete) set of bases. This enables decomposition using "multiresolution" bases (e.g., tonal + transient components), which is argued to improve source separation and denoising. The work builds upon Fevotte et al.'s Gaussian Composite Model (GCM), which models spectrogram cell variances via low-rank matrix factorization. It then reformulates the implicit convolution with multiple Gabor impulse responses (corresponding to each spectrogram row) into a purely time-domain expression for the estimated signal. This reformulation allows adaptation to arbitrary impulse response sets, including nonorthogonal ones, and supports an EM-based optimization procedure.

Quality: The insight is interesting, though the precise implications require further clarification. While the time-domain interpretation offers a novel perspective on Fevotte's GCM, the ability to incorporate overcomplete dictionaries is a valuable contribution. However, the potential complications arising from using overcomplete bases are not adequately addressed, and it is unclear why these do not significantly degrade performance. The estimation procedure appears correct (and the analytical approach is impressive), though its details remain somewhat opaque.

Clarity: The paper is well-motivated and clearly explained. The evaluation, however, is superficial: it presents only one illustrative result and a single SNR improvement metric, which is insufficient for drawing meaningful conclusions. While audio examples are included, they are unconvincing. The OMLSA-enhanced example (7 seconds) is significantly longer than the proposed method's result (2 seconds), raising fairness concerns in the comparison. Additionally, despite OMLSA including more background noise (partly due to its length), the speech quality appears higher. The music denoising example exhibits severe artifacts, suggesting conventional methods like wavelet shrinkage or Wiener filtering would yield superior perceptual quality.

Originality: The work heavily relies on Fevotte et al.'s foundation, though the time-domain reinterpretation provides a fresh perspective.

Significance: The potential to apply matrix-factorization techniques to overcomplete bases could be impactful. However, the current evaluation is too thin and superficial to substantiate this claim, rendering the significance rating an unsubstantiated assertion. While the reformulation of probabilistic matrix-factorization sound analysis via time-domain kernels enables EM-based analysis for multiresolution (overcomplete) bases, the thin evaluation and significant artifacts in the examples fail to demonstrate its practical importance.
