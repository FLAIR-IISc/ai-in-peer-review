This paper presents an extension to the family of low-rank spectrogram factorization models for audio decomposition. Building upon the Gaussian Composite Model (GCM) of Févotte et al. [3], which models the variance of each time-frequency bin in a spectrogram using a low-rank matrix factorization, this work reformulates the model directly in the time domain. This key shift recasts the problem from modeling a signal's Short-Time Fourier Transform (STFT) to a generative model of the raw time-domain signal itself. The primary advantage of this new formulation is its ability to generalize the decomposition over an arbitrary, and potentially overcomplete, set of basis functions. This enables multi-resolution analysis (e.g., using separate "tonal" and "transient" bases) within a unified probabilistic framework. The authors derive an Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm for parameter estimation to find a local optimum of the joint likelihood.

**Quality:**
The paper's core insight—reformulating the GCM as a time-domain synthesis model—is interesting and technically sound. The derivation of the MJLE EM algorithm appears correct and provides an elegant estimation procedure. A significant strength of this formulation is the ability to incorporate overcomplete dictionaries. However, the use of overcomplete bases often introduces complications, and the paper would be strengthened by a more detailed discussion of why these potential issues (such as identifiability or stability) do not seem to cause problems within this framework. While the theoretical work is well-presented, the experimental validation is not yet a complete piece of work, as detailed below.

**Clarity:**
The paper is well-written, clearly motivated, and effectively explains the transition from the STFT-based GCM to the proposed time-domain synthesis model. The connection to Sparse Bayesian Learning is also well-articulated.

The primary weakness of the paper lies in the superficiality of the experimental evaluation. The results presented are insufficient to draw strong conclusions about the method's effectiveness. The music decomposition is described as purely illustrative, and the speech enhancement experiment provides only a single average SNR improvement metric over a limited test set. While the inclusion of audio examples is appreciated, they raise further questions and concerns:

1.  In the speech enhancement task, the provided OMLSA baseline example appears to be significantly longer (approx. 7s) than the result from the proposed method (approx. 2s). This discrepancy raises concerns about whether the comparison was conducted fairly on the same segment of audio.
2.  Subjectively, the speech quality in the OMLSA result seems higher and more natural, despite containing more audible background noise. The speech recovered by the proposed method sounds processed and less clear.
3.  The denoising of the music example introduces very significant and audible artifacts. It is plausible that conventional methods, such as wavelet shrinkage or Wiener filtering, could achieve a much higher perceived quality on this specific task.

A more thorough and convincing evaluation, with multiple objective metrics and clearer comparisons against relevant baselines, is needed to support the paper's claims.

**Originality:**
The work is heavily based on the foundational GCM proposed by Févotte et al. [3]. However, the time-domain interpretation and the subsequent generalization to multi-resolution, overcomplete bases is a novel and valuable contribution. This reframing provides a new and powerful perspective on probabilistic source separation models.

**Significance:**
The potential to apply matrix factorization techniques to overcomplete bases in a principled, probabilistic manner could have a significant impact on audio processing and other signal processing fields. The multi-resolution capability addresses a well-known limitation of standard spectrogram-based methods. However, the significance of this work is currently more prospective than proven. The thin experimental evaluation and the audible artifacts in the examples fail to convincingly demonstrate the practical importance or superiority of the proposed approach in its current form.

**Summary:**
The paper introduces a clever and elegant reformulation of probabilistic matrix factorization for audio analysis in terms of time-domain synthesis kernels. This allows for a principled EM analysis for multi-resolution decomposition using overcomplete bases, which is a significant conceptual advance. The primary weakness is the unconvincing and insufficient experimental evaluation, where the provided examples contain significant artifacts and the comparisons to baselines are not clearly established. While the theoretical contribution is noteworthy, the lack of strong empirical evidence currently limits the paper's overall impact.
