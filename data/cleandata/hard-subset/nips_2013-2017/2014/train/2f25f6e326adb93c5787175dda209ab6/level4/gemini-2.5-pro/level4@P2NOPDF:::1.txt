### Review

**Summary**

This paper presents an extension to the family of low-rank spectrogram-factorization models for audio decomposition. Building upon the Gaussian Composite Model (GCM) of Févotte et al., which models the variance of each spectrogram cell with a low-rank matrix factorization, the authors introduce a key innovation. They reformulate the model from the frequency domain to the time domain by making the convolution with the analysis filters explicit. This time-domain expression allows the latent factors to be generalized over an arbitrary, and potentially overcomplete, set of basis functions (impulse responses). This enables "multiresolution" decompositions, such as using combined tonal and transient bases, which the authors argue is beneficial for applications like source separation and denoising. The paper provides an Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm for estimating the model parameters.

**Quality and Originality**

The core contribution of this work is the time-domain re-interpretation of probabilistic spectrogram factorization. This is an elegant and insightful idea that provides a new perspective on this class of models. The ability to incorporate overcomplete dictionaries is a natural and powerful consequence of this reformulation, representing a valuable extension to the existing framework. While the work is heavily based on the foundation laid by Févotte et al., the proposed generalization is non-trivial and genuinely novel.

The derivation of the EM estimation procedure appears sound, though its complexity makes it difficult to verify in full detail during a review. A key question that arises from the use of overcomplete bases concerns the potential for non-uniqueness or instability in the decomposition. The paper would be strengthened by a more thorough discussion of these potential challenges and how the proposed framework addresses them.

**Clarity**

The paper is generally well-written, and the motivation behind the proposed model is clearly explained. The theoretical development is presented in a logical manner.

**Weaknesses and Evaluation**

The primary weakness of this paper is its superficial and unconvincing experimental evaluation. The empirical evidence provided is too thin to substantiate the claims of the model's utility. Specifically:

1.  **Insufficient Data:** The evaluation is limited to a single illustrative result and one quantitative SNR improvement measurement. It is impossible to draw meaningful conclusions about the general performance and robustness of the method from a single example.

2.  **Unconvincing Audio Examples:** The provided audio examples, while appreciated, do not effectively demonstrate the superiority of the proposed method.
    *   The comparison with the Optimal Modified Log-Spectral Amplitude (OMLSA) estimator for speech denoising is questionable. The audio clip processed by the proposed method is significantly shorter (approx. 2 seconds) than the OMLSA example (approx. 7 seconds), which calls into question the fairness of the comparison.
    *   From a perceptual standpoint, the results are not compelling. In the speech example, while OMLSA leaves more residual background noise, the speech itself sounds of a much higher quality and is more intelligible. The proposed method seems to degrade the speech signal along with the noise.
    *   In the music denoising example, the reduction in noise comes at the cost of introducing very significant and musically unpleasant artifacts. It is highly likely that established techniques, such as conventional Wiener filtering or wavelet shrinkage, could achieve a better trade-off between noise reduction and signal fidelity, resulting in higher perceived audio quality.

**Significance**

In principle, the ability to apply matrix-factorization techniques to overcomplete bases for audio analysis could have a significant impact on the field. The concept is powerful and opens up new possibilities for designing flexible audio models.

However, the potential of this idea is not realized in the current manuscript. The work, as presented, is an interesting theoretical proposal that lacks the empirical validation necessary to demonstrate its practical value. The thin evaluation and the prominent artifacts in the audio examples fail to show that the proposed method offers a tangible improvement over existing techniques. Therefore, while the core idea is significant, its importance is not demonstrated by the results provided. The paper makes an act of faith for the reader to believe in its significance, which is not backed up by sufficient evidence.
