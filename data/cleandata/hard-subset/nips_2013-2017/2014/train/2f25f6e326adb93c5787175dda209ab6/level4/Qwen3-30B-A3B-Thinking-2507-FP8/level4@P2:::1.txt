

This paper extends the family of low-rank spectrogram factorization models by introducing a novel time-domain synthesis framework that generalizes latent factors across arbitrary (potentially overcomplete) time-frequency bases. Building on Fevotte et al.'s Gaussian Composite Model (GCM), which models spectrogram variances via low-rank factorization, the authors derive a purely time-domain formulation where the synthesis coefficients' variance structure is constrained by a low-rank matrix. This enables multi-resolution decomposition (e.g., tonal + transient components) without the limitations of traditional NMF approaches. The paper presents two EM-based estimation algorithms for maximum joint likelihood and marginal likelihood, with the former implemented for experiments.  

**Quality**: The technical contribution is sound and well-motivated. The extension from GCM to a generative time-domain model represents a meaningful step forward, and the EM algorithms are rigorously derived. The connection to sparse Bayesian learning (Section 2.2) is insightful, though the paper could better clarify how the low-rank constraint avoids the pitfalls of standard SBL. The estimation procedure appears correct, though the derivation of Eq. (11) and the role of the log(|α|²) term require more intuitive explanation. The work is technically complete, with clear theoretical grounding and implementation details.  

**Clarity**: The paper is exceptionally well-written, with a logical flow from problem statement to solution. The generative model (Section 2.1) and relation to GCM (Section 2.3) are clearly articulated. However, the experimental evaluation is underdeveloped. The music decomposition (Section 5.1) is purely illustrative, with only one example and no quantitative comparison to established methods beyond SNR. The speech enhancement results (Section 5.2) report an average SNR gain of 6.6 dB (vs. 6 dB for IS-NMF and 4.6 dB for OMLSA), but the comparison lacks statistical significance testing, ablation studies (e.g., impact of resolution choices), or perceptual analysis. While sound examples are provided, the claims about quality improvements are not substantiated—e.g., the music decomposition exhibits noticeable artifacts (e.g., distorted bass/piano), and the OMLSA comparison appears unfair due to differing signal lengths (the paper states both use "7 s-long" examples, but the supplementary material shows OMLSA's output is shorter). The evaluation fails to demonstrate whether the multi-resolution capability genuinely outperforms single-resolution baselines.  

**Originality**: The core innovation—extending GCM to a time-domain synthesis model enabling multi-resolution decomposition—is novel. While the paper heavily leverages Fevotte et al.'s GCM, the time-domain reinterpretation and multi-resolution extension are distinct contributions. The ability to handle nonorthogonal bases (Section 4) is particularly valuable, as prior work (e.g., [8, 9]) used sparse synthesis models without the low-rank variance constraint. However, the originality is somewhat diminished by the reliance on established NMF techniques (e.g., IS divergence minimization). The paper could better differentiate itself from related work like [10] (which uses morphological components) and [15] (on sparse representations).  

**Significance**: The potential impact is promising—enabling multi-resolution decomposition in audio applications (e.g., separating transients from tonal components) addresses a key limitation of traditional NMF. However, the current evaluation does not convincingly demonstrate this. The single music example lacks perceptual validation, and the speech enhancement results show only marginal gains over IS-NMF (0.6 dB) with no evidence that the multi-resolution aspect drove this improvement. Without stronger empirical evidence (e.g., ablation studies on resolution trade-offs, comparison to other multi-resolution methods), the significance claim remains speculative. The work would be significantly stronger if the authors demonstrated how multi-resolution decomposition specifically improves downstream tasks (e.g., source separation accuracy, perceptual quality).  

**Summary of Strengths and Weaknesses**:  
*Pro*:  
- Novel time-domain formulation bridging GCM and synthesis.  
- Rigorous derivation of EM algorithms (Section 3).  
- Multi-resolution capability (Section 4) is theoretically sound and practically relevant.  
- Clear exposition and well-structured presentation.  

*Con*:  
- Experimental evaluation is superficial: one illustrative example, no ablation studies, and questionable SNR comparisons.  
- Claims about superiority over baselines (e.g., OMLSA) lack robust validation.  
- Potential artifacts in music decomposition (Fig. 1) undermine the practical utility.  
- Significance not sufficiently demonstrated by current results.  

**Recommendation**: The paper presents a technically solid extension of GCM with a clear path to multi-resolution audio processing. However, the weak evaluation prevents a strong endorsement. To strengthen the submission, the authors should: (1) provide additional experiments comparing multi-resolution vs. single-resolution settings; (2) include perceptual evaluations (e.g., MUSHRA tests); (3) clarify the OMLSA comparison (ensuring consistent signal lengths); and (4) better quantify the contribution of multi-resolution decomposition. As-is, the significance is overstated relative to the evidence provided. I recommend *weak acceptance* if the authors address these points in revision, but as submitted, the work lacks the empirical rigor expected for NIPS.
