This paper introduces a novel signal synthesis model with low-rank time-frequency structure, which bridges the gap between time-frequency synthesis and traditional nonnegative matrix factorization (NMF) approaches. The proposed Low-Rank Time-Frequency Synthesis (LRTFS) model considers a generative model of the time-domain signal itself by describing a generative model of time-frequency synthesis coefficients, allowing for multi-resolution representations that were not possible in the traditional NMF setting.

The paper is well-organized and clearly written, making it easy to follow the authors' reasoning and methodology. The introduction provides a thorough background on NMF and its limitations, setting the stage for the proposed LRTFS model. The authors then describe the LRTFS model, its relation to sparse Bayesian learning, and the estimation procedures for the model parameters.

The experiments presented in the paper demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed approach on music decomposition and speech enhancement tasks. The results show that the LRTFS model can successfully decompose signals into their constituent components, and the multi-resolution extension allows for more flexible and accurate representations.

Strengths of the paper include:

* The proposed LRTFS model is a significant contribution to the field of signal processing, offering a new perspective on time-frequency synthesis and analysis.
* The authors provide a thorough theoretical foundation for the LRTFS model, including its relation to sparse Bayesian learning and the estimation procedures for the model parameters.
* The experiments presented in the paper demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed approach on real-world tasks.

Weaknesses of the paper include:

* The paper could benefit from a more detailed comparison with existing methods, such as IS-NMF, in terms of performance and computational complexity.
* The authors mention that the estimation of the noise variance parameter λ can be problematic, but do not provide a clear solution to this issue.
* Some of the mathematical derivations and proofs could be more detailed and explicit, to facilitate understanding and verification by readers.

In terms of the conference reviewing guidelines, the paper scores high on originality, technical quality, and relevance to the field. However, the paper could be improved by addressing the weaknesses mentioned above and providing more detailed comparisons with existing methods.

Arguments for acceptance:

* The proposed LRTFS model is a significant contribution to the field of signal processing.
* The paper provides a thorough theoretical foundation for the LRTFS model and demonstrates its effectiveness on real-world tasks.
* The multi-resolution extension of the LRTFS model offers a new perspective on time-frequency synthesis and analysis.

Arguments against acceptance:

* The paper could benefit from a more detailed comparison with existing methods, such as IS-NMF.
* The estimation of the noise variance parameter λ can be problematic, and a clear solution to this issue is not provided.
* Some of the mathematical derivations and proofs could be more detailed and explicit.

Overall, I recommend accepting the paper, but with the suggestion that the authors address the weaknesses mentioned above and provide more detailed comparisons with existing methods.
