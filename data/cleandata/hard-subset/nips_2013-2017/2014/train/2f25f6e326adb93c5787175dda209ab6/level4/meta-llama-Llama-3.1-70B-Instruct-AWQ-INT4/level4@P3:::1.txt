This paper presents an extension in the family of low-rank spectrogram-factorization models of sound decomposition that generalizes the latent factors over an arbitrary (potentially overcomplete) set of bases, meaning that decomposition can be performed on "multiresolution" (e.g. tonal + transient) bases. This is argued to help with source separation and denoising. The paper starts from the Gaussian Composite Model of Fevotte et al, which models the variance of each cell of a spectrogram with a low-rank matrix factorization, but then expands the implicit convolution with the multiple Gabor impulse responses corresponding to each row of the spectrogram to give a purely time-domain expression for what is being estimated. This expression can then be modified to work for different sets of impulse responses, including non-orthogonal ones. An EM (local optimum) analysis procedure is presented.

Quality: The idea presented in this paper is interesting, although its implications are not immediately clear. The approach can be seen as a new way of understanding Fevotte's GCM, and the ability to include overcomplete dictionaries is a welcome addition. However, it is not clear why the potential complications arising from this approach do not cause more trouble. The estimation procedure appears to be correct, and the analysis is impressive, although some of the details are difficult to follow.

Clarity: The work is well-explained and motivated, but the evaluation is superficial. The paper presents only one purely illustrative result and one SNR improvement measurement, which is not enough to draw any conclusions. The included sound examples are helpful, but they do not demonstrate a clear advantage of the proposed approach. In fact, the OMLSA enhanced example sounds much higher quality, despite including more background noise. The denoising of the music example comes at the expense of significant artifacts, which could be avoided with conventional wavelet shrinkage or Wiener filtering.

Originality: The work relies heavily on the Fevotte et al foundation, although the time-domain interpretation is a new and interesting perspective.

Significance: The ability to apply matrix-factorization techniques to overcomplete bases could have significant impact. However, the examples and evaluation presented in this work are too thin and superficial to demonstrate this significance. To fully appreciate the importance of this work, more thorough evaluation and better-demonstrated results are needed.

Arguments For Acceptance:

* The paper presents a new and interesting perspective on sound decomposition using low-rank spectrogram-factorization models.
* The approach has the potential to improve source separation and denoising.
* The estimation procedure is correct and the analysis is impressive.

Arguments Against Acceptance:

* The evaluation is superficial and does not demonstrate a clear advantage of the proposed approach.
* The included sound examples do not show a clear improvement in quality.
* The denoising of the music example comes at the expense of significant artifacts.
* The work relies heavily on the Fevotte et al foundation, and it is not clear what new insights are gained from this approach.

Recommendation: The paper has some interesting ideas, but the evaluation is not thorough enough to demonstrate its significance. I would recommend rejection, but with an invitation to resubmit with more thorough evaluation and better-demonstrated results.
