

**Review of "Low-Rank Time-Frequency Synthesis"**

This paper introduces a novel generative model that bridges the gap between traditional nonnegative matrix factorization (NMF) approaches and time-domain signal synthesis. The authors present a Low-Rank Time-Frequency Synthesis (LRTFS) framework that extends the Gaussian Composite Model (GCM) by modeling the raw time-domain signal directly rather than its STFT representation. This represents a significant conceptual advancement from previous work, including the GCM (Févotte et al., 2009) which modeled the STFT coefficients directly but not the underlying time-domain signal. The LRTFS model is particularly notable for its ability to naturally support multi-resolution representations through different time-frequency bases, a capability that traditional NMF approaches cannot accommodate due to their fixed-time-resolution analysis.

**Quality:** The technical quality of the paper is strong. The authors rigorously derive a generative model that properly accounts for the signal's time-domain structure, providing a theoretically sound alternative to previous approaches. The development of two estimation algorithms (MJLE and MMLE) is well-motivated, with clear derivations of the EM update rules. The experimental validation on music decomposition and speech enhancement demonstrates practical utility, though the comparison to IS-NMF in single-resolution settings is limited. The authors acknowledge that MMLE is computationally prohibitive for audio applications, which is a thoughtful assessment of limitations. However, the paper would benefit from more comprehensive comparisons to IS-NMF in single-resolution scenarios, particularly regarding reconstruction quality and computational efficiency. The experimental section could also better address the sensitivity of the method to hyperparameter choices like λ.

**Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and well-organized, with a clear progression from theoretical development to practical implementation. The mathematical notation is consistent and well-defined, though the paper occasionally assumes familiarity with complex Gaussian distributions that might confuse readers unfamiliar with signal processing. The relation to sparse Bayesian learning is well-explained, though the connection to previous NMF work could be more explicitly articulated. The multi-resolution extension (Section 4) is particularly well-explained, with clear algorithmic descriptions that would enable reproduction. The figures (not included in the draft) appear to be well-chosen to illustrate the decomposition results.

**Originality:** This work makes a significant contribution to the field by addressing a fundamental gap in the generative modeling of audio signals. While previous work (e.g., Smaragdis & Raj, 2004; Févotte et al., 2009) has focused on modeling spectrograms, LRTFS models the raw signal directly, which is conceptually novel. The multi-resolution capability represents a particularly valuable extension, as it allows for different temporal resolutions across different signal components (e.g., transients versus tonal elements), a feature impossible to achieve with traditional NMF approaches. The paper adequately references previous work in NIPS and related venues, though it could better distinguish its contributions from related work on multi-resolution analysis (e.g., Daudet & Torrésani, 2005; Kowalski & Torrésani, 2007).

**Significance:** The significance of this work is high for audio processing applications. By providing a generative model of the raw signal rather than the STFT, the authors enable more accurate signal reconstruction and open new possibilities for multi-resolution analysis. The experimental results on music decomposition and speech enhancement are promising, with the proposed method achieving 6.6 dB SNR improvement in speech enhancement compared to 6 dB for IS-NMF and 4.6 dB for OMLSA. The multi-resolution capability is particularly valuable for audio applications where transients and tonal components have different temporal characteristics. While the paper focuses on audio applications, the framework could potentially be extended to other signal processing domains, increasing its broader significance.

**Strengths:** 
- Conceptual advancement from modeling STFT to modeling raw signal
- Natural support for multi-resolution representations
- Well-developed theoretical framework with two estimation algorithms
- Promising experimental results in relevant audio processing applications
- Clear connection to sparse Bayesian learning

**Weaknesses:**
- Limited comparison to IS-NMF in single-resolution settings
- MMLE implementation is computationally prohibitive without detailed explanation
- Sensitivity to hyperparameter choices (λ) is not thoroughly explored
- The practical implementation details for large-scale audio processing need more elaboration

**Recommendation:** I recommend acceptance of this paper. The work represents a meaningful theoretical advance with clear practical applications in audio signal processing. The multi-resolution capability is particularly compelling and represents a significant extension of traditional NMF approaches. While the experimental comparison to IS-NMF could be more comprehensive, the results presented are convincing and demonstrate the practical value of the proposed framework. The paper makes a clear contribution to the field that would be of interest to the NIPS community, particularly those working on generative models for signals. I would encourage the authors to include a more detailed comparison to IS-NMF in single-resolution settings in the camera-ready version, as this would strengthen the paper's claims about the advantages of their approach.
