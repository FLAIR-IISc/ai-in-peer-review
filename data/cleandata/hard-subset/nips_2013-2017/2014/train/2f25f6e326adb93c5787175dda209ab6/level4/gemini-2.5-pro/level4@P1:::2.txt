This paper proposes a novel generative model for time-domain signals, called Low-Rank Time-Frequency Synthesis (LRTFS). Whereas standard Nonnegative Matrix Factorization (NMF) frameworks, such as those based on the Itakura-Saito divergence, implicitly define a generative model for the time-frequency *analysis* coefficients (i.e., the STFT), the proposed framework models the time-domain signal directly. It achieves this by defining a generative model for the time-frequency *synthesis* coefficients, imposing the low-rank structure on their variance. A key advantage of this synthesis-based approach is that it naturally allows for multi-resolution representations—combining different time-frequency bases—which is not possible in the traditional NMF setting where the analysis resolution is fixed. The authors derive two EM algorithms for parameter estimation and demonstrate the model's effectiveness on audio decomposition and speech enhancement tasks.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The paper's main contribution is conceptually elegant and novel. By shifting the probabilistic model from the analysis domain to the synthesis domain, it successfully bridges the gap between low-rank NMF models and time-domain signal synthesis models. This provides a more complete and principled generative model that begins from the raw signal itself.

2.  **Multi-Resolution Capability:** The multi-resolution extension is a significant practical advantage of the proposed framework. Many real-world signals, particularly audio, contain components with varying time-frequency characteristics (e.g., sharp transients and sustained tonal parts). The ability to model these components simultaneously with appropriate time-frequency bases is a powerful feature that is not easily accommodated by standard NMF methods. The experiments effectively illustrate this capability.

3.  **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and clearly organized. The authors do a good job of positioning their work relative to the Gaussian Composite Model (GCM) for IS-NMF and related techniques like Sparse Bayesian Learning, making the model's motivation and novelty easy to understand.

**Weaknesses and Questions for Clarification:**

The primary weakness of the paper lies in its experimental evaluation, specifically in the lack of a direct comparison to the established baseline in a single-resolution setting. While the authors present compelling results for the multi-resolution case, this makes it difficult to understand the fundamental trade-offs of the LRTFS model compared to standard IS-NMF. To strengthen the paper and clarify its contribution, I would be very interested to see answers to the following questions:

1.  **Performance in the Single-Resolution Case:** How does the proposed method perform against standard IS-NMF when restricted to a single resolution? For a given task like speech enhancement, if both LRTFS and IS-NMF are configured with identical time-frequency analysis parameters (e.g., the same window size), what are the comparative results? This direct, "apples-to-apples" comparison is crucial for understanding whether the proposed model offers benefits beyond its multi-resolution capability.

2.  **Factor Interpretation:** In such a single-resolution comparison, do the learned factors W and H from LRTFS resemble those learned by IS-NMF? Understanding the similarities or differences would provide valuable insight into the behavior of the new model.

3.  **Computational Cost and Stability:** The proposed MJLE algorithm is iterative and involves synthesis and analysis steps within its E-step. How does its computational cost and convergence speed compare to the standard, highly efficient multiplicative update algorithm for IS-NMF? Is the algorithm stable and robust to initialization? A discussion of these practical aspects would be essential for researchers and practitioners considering adopting this new model.

In summary, this is an interesting paper with a strong and well-motivated core idea. The multi-resolution extension is a clear and valuable contribution. However, the paper would be significantly improved by a more thorough comparison to the standard IS-NMF baseline in the single-resolution case. This would allow readers to better assess the performance, computational, and qualitative trade-offs of this promising new approach.
