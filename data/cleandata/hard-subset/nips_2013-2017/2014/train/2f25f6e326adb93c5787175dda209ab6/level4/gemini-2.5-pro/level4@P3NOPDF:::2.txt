This paper presents a novel generative framework for Non-negative Matrix Factorization (NMF). Unlike conventional NMF approaches that formulate a generative model for the time-frequency (TF) analysis coefficients of a signal, the proposed framework models the time-domain signal directly by describing a generative model of its TF synthesis coefficients. The primary contribution of this reformulation is that it naturally enables multi-resolution representations, a capability not easily achieved within the traditional NMF setting.

The paper is well-conceived and interesting. The core idea of shifting the generative model from analysis to synthesis coefficients is both elegant and powerful. The resulting extension to multi-resolution signal decomposition is a significant and valuable contribution, and the authors present compelling initial results demonstrating its potential.

While the focus on the multi-resolution extension is understandable, the paper would be significantly strengthened by a more thorough analysis of the proposed method's performance in the single-resolution case, particularly in relation to established benchmarks. To this end, I have the following questions and suggestions for the authors:

1.  **Comparison with IS-NMF:** The evaluation would benefit greatly from a direct comparison with a standard baseline like Itakura-Saito NMF (IS-NMF) in a single-resolution setting. This comparison is crucial for establishing a performance baseline and understanding the potential trade-offs (e.g., in reconstruction accuracy or factor quality) introduced by the new synthesis-based model.

2.  **Factor Similarity:** How do the learned factors (W and H) derived from the proposed algorithm compare to those obtained from a standard IS-NMF decomposition on the same data? An analysis of whether the methods converge to similar dictionaries and activation patterns would provide valuable insight into the fundamental properties of the new model.

3.  **Computational Performance:** The paper should include an analysis of the proposed algorithm's computational efficiency and stability. How do its convergence speed and numerical stability compare to the widely-used and well-understood multiplicative update algorithm for IS-NMF? An empirical comparison of runtime and convergence behavior would be an important addition for assessing the practical viability of the method.

Addressing these points would provide a more complete picture of the proposed framework's characteristics, not only showcasing its novel capabilities but also grounding its performance within the context of existing, widely-used methods. Overall, this is a promising direction of research, and with a more comprehensive evaluation, it could make a strong impact.
