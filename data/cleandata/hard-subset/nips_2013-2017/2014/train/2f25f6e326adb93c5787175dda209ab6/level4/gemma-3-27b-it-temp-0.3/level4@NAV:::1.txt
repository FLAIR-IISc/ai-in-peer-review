This paper introduces an extension to low-rank spectrogram factorization models for sound decomposition, generalizing latent factors across an arbitrary, potentially overcomplete, basis set – enabling decomposition on “multiresolution” bases like tonal and transient components. The authors posit this will improve source separation and denoising performance. Starting from the Gaussian Composite Model (GCM) of Fevotte et al., which utilizes low-rank matrix factorization to model spectrogram cell variances, the work expands the implicit convolution using multiple Gabor impulse responses, resulting in a purely time-domain estimation expression. This formulation facilitates application to diverse, including non-orthogonal, impulse response sets, and is coupled with an Expectation-Maximization (EM) analysis procedure.

Quality: The core insight presented is interesting, though the full implications require further consideration. While initially appearing as a reinterpretation of Fevotte’s GCM, the inclusion of overcomplete dictionaries is a valuable addition. However, this extension introduces potential complexities that were not fully clarified. I assume the estimation procedure is sound and commend the analytical rigor, although a detailed understanding eluded me.

Clarity: The work is well-explained and effectively motivated. The evaluation, however, is limited. The presented results consist of a single illustrative example and one SNR improvement measurement, insufficient for drawing firm conclusions. The included audio examples were appreciated, but did not demonstrate compelling improvements. Specifically, the OMLSA enhanced example is substantially longer than the proposed method’s result, raising concerns about a fair comparison. Furthermore, despite the OMLSA example containing more background noise due to its length, the speech quality appears superior. The music denoising example introduces noticeable artifacts; conventional techniques like wavelet shrinkage or Wiener filtering would likely yield better perceptual quality.

Originality: The work builds heavily upon the foundation laid by Fevotte et al., although the time-domain interpretation offers a novel perspective.

Significance: The potential impact of applying matrix factorization techniques to overcomplete bases is significant. However, the limited and superficial evaluation prevents a clear demonstration of this potential. Currently, assessing substantial significance requires a degree of speculation. The reformulation of probabilistic matrix-factorization sound analysis using time-domain kernels enables EM analysis for multiresolution (overcomplete) bases, but the weak evaluation and prominent artifacts in the examples currently limit demonstrable impact.
