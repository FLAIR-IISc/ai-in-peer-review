This paper presents an extension to the family of low-rank spectrogram factorization models for sound decomposition. The proposed method generalizes the latent factors over an arbitrary, and potentially overcomplete, set of bases. This allows the decomposition to be performed using "multiresolution" bases (e.g., combining tonal and transient components), which the authors argue is beneficial for source separation and denoising. The model builds upon the Gaussian Composite Model (GCM) of Fevotte et al., which models the variance of each spectrogram cell with a low-rank matrix factorization. The authors expand the implicit convolution with the Gabor impulse responses into a purely time-domain expression. This reformulation allows the model to be adapted for different sets of impulse responses, including non-orthogonal ones. An Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm is derived to find a local optimum for the model parameters.

**Quality:** The core insight of this work is interesting, though its full implications are not immediately apparent. At first glance, the model appears to be a reinterpretation of Fevotte's GCM; however, its key contribution is the ability to incorporate overcomplete dictionaries, which is a valuable extension. On the other hand, the use of overcomplete dictionaries would typically be expected to introduce complications, and the paper does not fully address why these are not more problematic in this framework. While the reviewer could not follow the derivation of the estimation procedure in complete detail, the analysis appears to be sound.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-explained and motivated. The experimental evaluation, however, is superficial. It consists of only one illustrative result and a single SNR improvement measurement, which is insufficient for drawing general conclusions. The provided audio examples are a welcome addition, but they raise several concerns. First, the audio example for the OMLSA baseline is significantly longer (approx. 7s) than the one for the proposed method (approx. 2s), which calls into question the fairness of the comparison. Second, while the OMLSA example contains more audible background noise, the perceptual quality of the speech itself sounds higher. In the music example, the denoising comes at the expense of significant artifacts; it is plausible that conventional methods, such as wavelet shrinkage or Wiener filtering, could achieve higher perceived quality in this case.

**Originality:** The work is heavily based on the foundation laid by Fevotte et al. The primary contribution to originality is the novel time-domain interpretation, which offers a new perspective on this class of models.

**Significance:** The potential to apply matrix-factorization techniques to overcomplete bases is significant. However, the thin and superficial evaluation fails to substantiate this potential. As it stands, assessing the work's significance relies more on the promise of the core idea than on demonstrated results. While the reformulation of probabilistic matrix-factorization for sound analysis in terms of time-domain kernels is a notable contribution that enables EM for multiresolution bases, the limited evaluation and significant artifacts in the examples fail to demonstrate its practical importance in its current form.
