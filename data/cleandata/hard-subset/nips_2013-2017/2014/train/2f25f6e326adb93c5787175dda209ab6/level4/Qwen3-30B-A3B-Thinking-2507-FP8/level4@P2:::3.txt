

This paper introduces a novel low-rank time-frequency synthesis (LRTFS) framework that bridges a critical gap in signal processing literature by providing a generative model of the raw signal rather than just its STFT. The authors build upon the Gaussian Composite Model (GCM) [3], which established a probabilistic foundation for STFT-based nonnegative matrix factorization (NMF), by extending it to directly model the time-domain signal. This advancement enables multi-resolution representations that were previously impossible with traditional NMF approaches, as the authors demonstrate through applications in music decomposition and speech enhancement.

The paper makes a significant theoretical contribution by formulating a generative model where the signal x(t) is expressed as a sum of time-frequency atoms with low-rank variance structure. The authors derive two expectation-maximization algorithms for estimation in this model, one based on maximum joint likelihood (MJLE) and another on maximum marginal likelihood (MMLE). The theoretical development is sound and well-justified, with appropriate connections to sparse Bayesian learning [4,5] and the Itakura-Saito divergence [3].

**Quality:** The technical content is strong and well-executed. The paper provides clear mathematical formulations, rigorous derivations of the EM algorithms, and thorough theoretical analysis. The MJLE algorithm is particularly well-motivated and computationally efficient, avoiding matrix inversions that would hinder practical application. The MMLE approach is presented as a theoretical extension, with a clear acknowledgment of its computational limitations for large-scale signal processing tasks.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The authors provide clear motivation for their model, carefully explain the relationship to prior work, and present the mathematical development in a logical sequence. The introduction of the LRTFS model and its relation to GCM is particularly effective, and the multi-resolution extension in Section 4 is well-motivated and clearly explained. The paper would benefit from more detailed pseudocode for the algorithms, but the mathematical exposition is sufficient for expert readers to reproduce the results.

**Originality:** The paper makes a genuinely novel contribution by shifting from STFT-based modeling to direct time-domain modeling with low-rank structure. While the paper builds on the GCM framework [3], the authors clearly articulate how their approach differs and advances the field. The multi-resolution capability represents a significant conceptual advance over traditional NMF approaches. The paper's originality is high, as it addresses a clear limitation in the existing literature and provides a theoretically grounded solution.

**Significance:** This is where the paper falls short. While the theoretical contribution is strong, the paper does not sufficiently demonstrate the practical significance of the approach. The experimental results (Sections 5.1 and 5.2) are limited and lack meaningful comparisons to state-of-the-art methods. The music decomposition example is illustrative but lacks quantitative evaluation beyond SNR. The speech enhancement results (6.6 dB average improvement) are not convincingly compared to relevant baselines; the authors mention IS-NMF and OMLSA as baselines, but provide insufficient context about their relative performance in similar settings. The supplemental material does not address these concerns. The significance would be greatly enhanced by demonstrating how LRTFS outperforms existing methods in challenging scenarios or by providing more detailed analysis of the multi-resolution benefits.

**Pro:** 
- Strong theoretical contribution that addresses a clear gap in the literature
- Well-written with excellent organization and mathematical clarity
- Novel multi-resolution capability that extends traditional NMF approaches
- Practical algorithm (MJLE) that avoids computational bottlenecks
- Clear connections to prior work and proper citation of relevant literature

**Con:**
- Limited experimental evaluation with insufficient comparison to state-of-the-art methods
- Significance of the approach not adequately demonstrated through quantitative results
- Supplemental material does not provide convincing additional evidence
- The paper does not fully articulate how LRTFS improves upon existing STFT-based approaches in practical applications

This paper makes a valuable theoretical contribution to the field of time-frequency analysis and signal processing. The core ideas are sound, well-motivated, and represent a meaningful advancement over the GCM framework. However, the lack of sufficient experimental validation and comparison to relevant baselines weakens the paper's significance claim. The theoretical contribution is strong enough to warrant acceptance, but the paper would be significantly stronger with more comprehensive experimental evaluation.

I recommend acceptance with the understanding that the authors should address the significance concerns in future work, particularly by providing more thorough comparisons to existing methods in their experiments. The paper is well-suited for NIPS as it presents a theoretically grounded approach to an important problem in signal processing, though it would benefit from stronger empirical validation.
