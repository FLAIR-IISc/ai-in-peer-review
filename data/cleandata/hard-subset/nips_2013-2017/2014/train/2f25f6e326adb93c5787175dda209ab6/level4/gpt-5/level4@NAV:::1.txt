Review- This paper proposes an extension to the family of low-rank spectrogram factorization models for audio decomposition that generalizes the latent factors to an arbitrary (potentially overcomplete) set of bases, enabling decomposition on multiresolution (e.g., tonal plus transient) bases. This is argued to benefit source separation and denoising. The method begins from the Gaussian Composite Model of Fevotte et al., which models the variance of each spectrogram cell via a low-rank matrix factorization, and then makes explicit the implicit convolution with the multiple Gabor impulse responses associated with each spectrogram row to derive a purely time-domain expression for the estimate. This, in turn, can be adapted to alternative sets of impulse responses, including nonorthogonal ones. An EM-based (local optimum) analysis procedure is provided.

Quality: I find the central idea compelling, though I am still working out the full implications. At first glance, it reads as a re-interpretation of Fevotte’s GCM, but the ability to incorporate overcomplete dictionaries is attractive. However, this would seem to introduce complications, and it wasn’t immediately clear why these do not pose greater difficulties. I am willing to assume the estimator is correct (the analysis is impressive), although I could not follow every detail.

Clarity: The presentation and motivation are clear. The evaluation, however, is quite minimal—one purely illustrative example and a single SNR improvement figure—insufficient to support strong conclusions. I appreciated the audio demos, but found them unconvincing. The OMLSA-enhanced clip appears much longer (about 7 s vs. 2 s) than the proposed result, raising concerns about the fairness of the comparison. Moreover, while OMLSA preserves more background noise (partly due to its longer duration), the speech sounds higher quality to me. For the music denoising case, the improvements come with substantial artifacts; I would expect conventional wavelet shrinkage or Wiener filtering to deliver better perceived quality here.

Originality: The work leans heavily on the Fevotte et al. framework, though the time-domain reframing did offer a new way to think about it.

Significance: In principle, enabling matrix-factorization methods to operate on overcomplete bases could be impactful. However, the examples and evaluation provided are too limited and superficial to demonstrate this convincingly; at present, rating the work as highly significant feels more like an act of faith. Reformulating probabilistic matrix-factorization-based audio analysis in terms of time-domain kernels enables an EM analysis for multiresolution (overcomplete) bases, but the sparse evaluation and noticeable artifacts in the demos do not yet establish its importance.
