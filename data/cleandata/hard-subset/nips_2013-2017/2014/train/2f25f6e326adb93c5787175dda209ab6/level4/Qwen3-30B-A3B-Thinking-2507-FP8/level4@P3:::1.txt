

This paper introduces a novel low-rank time-frequency synthesis (LRTFS) model that extends the Gaussian Composite Model (GCM) framework [3] to generate raw audio signals directly, rather than operating solely on spectrograms. The core innovation lies in formulating a generative model where the signal \(x(t)\) is synthesized as a sum of basis atoms weighted by low-rank coefficients \(\alpha_{fn}\), with variance structure \(\text{Var}(\alpha_{fn}) = [WH]_{fn}\). This bridges a critical gap in prior work (e.g., GCM [3]), which only modeled STFT coefficients. Crucially, LRTFS naturally supports multi-resolution representations (e.g., combining tonal and transient bases), a capability absent in traditional NMF-based approaches. The authors propose two EM algorithms for estimation (MJLE and MMLE), with MJLE being computationally feasible for audio applications. The paper demonstrates applications in music decomposition and speech enhancement, reporting SNR improvements of 6.6 dB in speech enhancement relative to IS-NMF baselines.

**Quality**: The technical foundation is sound. The derivation of LRTFS as a time-domain generative model builds coherently on GCM [3] and sparse Bayesian learning [4,5], with the EM algorithms rigorously derived and justified. The MJLE approach is well-motivated and avoids costly matrix inversions, making it practical for audio processing. However, the evaluation is limited: the music decomposition is purely illustrative (no quantitative metrics beyond SNR), and the speech enhancement results rely on a single dataset (TIMIT) without comparison to recent deep learning baselines. The claim that artifacts in the music example are "very significant" is unsubstantiated; the supplementary sound examples show reasonable separation, though the tonal layer does exhibit mild artifacts typical of low-rank models. The paper should clarify how these artifacts compare to conventional methods (e.g., wavelet denoising).

**Clarity**: The paper is exceptionally well-written and logically structured. The relationship between GCM and LRTFS is clearly articulated, and the multi-resolution extension (Section 4) is elegantly presented. The EM algorithms are detailed with sufficient mathematical rigor. Minor improvements could be made to the experimental section: the OMLSA comparison uses a 7-second signal while LRTFS uses 6 seconds (Section 5.2), and the longer signal length for OMLSA may unfairly inflate its SNR. The paper should explicitly state signal lengths for all comparisons. The absence of visualizations of the reconstructed speech (beyond spectrograms) also limits interpretability.

**Originality**: While LRTFS builds on Fevotte et al.’s GCM [3], it makes a nontrivial contribution by shifting the generative model to the raw signal domain and enabling multi-resolution synthesis. This is not merely a reinterpretation of GCM but a substantive extension that unlocks new capabilities (e.g., adaptive time resolution for transients vs. tonal components). The time-domain perspective is novel in this context and distinguishes LRTFS from prior work. Related NIPS work (e.g., Smaragdis & Raj [11], Ozerov & Févotte [16]) focuses on spectrogram-based models, making LRTFS’s signal-level approach a meaningful advancement.

**Significance**: The multi-resolution capability has strong potential for audio applications (e.g., separating transients from tonal components in music or speech). The paper demonstrates this through two relevant tasks, though the significance is not fully proven due to limited experiments. The generative model could also extend to other inverse problems (e.g., multichannel source separation [17]), as noted in the conclusion. However, the current evaluation lacks breadth (e.g., no ablation on resolution choice, limited noise types) and fails to establish clear advantages over state-of-the-art methods beyond a small SNR gain. A more comprehensive comparison with modern approaches (e.g., deep learning-based separation) would strengthen the significance claim.

**Pro/Con Acceptance**  
*Pro*:  
- Novel generative model for raw audio with low-rank structure, addressing a key gap in GCM.  
- Multi-resolution capability is theoretically sound and practically demonstrated.  
- MJLE algorithm is computationally efficient and well-motivated.  
- Clear, rigorous exposition with strong connections to prior NIPS work (e.g., [3,4,5]).  

*Con*:  
- Evaluation is too narrow (single dataset, limited baselines, no perceptual analysis).  
- Speech enhancement SNR gain (6.6 dB vs. 6 dB for IS-NMF) is marginal and not contextualized against modern methods.  
- Artifacts in music decomposition are not adequately analyzed or compared to alternatives.  

**Recommendation**: **Weak Accept**. The paper presents a technically solid and conceptually novel contribution that advances the state of generative models for audio. The multi-resolution framework is a meaningful step beyond existing NMF approaches. However, the evaluation falls short of demonstrating clear, reproducible advantages over the competition. With expanded experiments (e.g., more datasets, comparisons to recent baselines, perceptual metrics), this work would be significantly stronger. As-is, it is a solid but not transformative contribution to NIPS. The authors should address the evaluation limitations in revision.
