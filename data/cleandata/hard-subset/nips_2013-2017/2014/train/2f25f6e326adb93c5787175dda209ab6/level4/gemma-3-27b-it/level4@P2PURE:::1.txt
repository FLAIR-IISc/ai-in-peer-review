This paper presents an extension to low-rank spectrogram factorization models for sound decomposition, generalizing latent factors over an arbitrary, potentially overcomplete, set of bases – enabling decomposition on “multiresolution” bases such as tonal and transient components. The authors argue this generalization improves source separation and denoising performance. Starting from the Gaussian Composite Model (GCM) of Fevotte et al., which models spectrogram cell variances with low-rank matrix factorization, the paper expands the implicit convolution with multiple Gabor impulse responses (corresponding to each spectrogram row) into a purely time-domain expression. This formulation allows the estimation procedure to be adapted for different sets of impulse responses, including non-orthogonal ones. An Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm, subject to local optima, is presented for parameter estimation.

Quality: This work offers an interesting insight, though the full implications remain unclear. While initially appearing as a reinterpretation of Fevotte’s GCM, the ability to incorporate overcomplete dictionaries is a valuable addition. However, this extension seems likely to introduce complications, and the paper did not fully clarify why these are mitigated. I assume the estimation procedure is correct, and I commend the analysis, though I was unable to follow the details closely.

Clarity: The work is well explained and motivated. However, the evaluation is superficial; a single illustrative result and one SNR improvement measurement are insufficient to draw firm conclusions. While the included sound examples are appreciated, they are not particularly compelling. Specifically, the OMLSA enhanced example is considerably longer (7 seconds versus 2 seconds) than the proposed method's result, raising concerns about a fair comparison. Furthermore, despite the longer duration and increased background noise in the OMLSA example, the speech quality appears superior. The denoising of the music example introduces significant artifacts; conventional wavelet shrinkage or Wiener filtering would likely yield better perceived quality. 

Originality: The work builds heavily on the foundation laid by Fevotte et al., although the time-domain interpretation offers a novel perspective.

Significance: The potential impact of applying matrix-factorization techniques to overcomplete bases is significant. However, the current examples and evaluation are too limited to demonstrate this potential, making a positive assessment largely speculative. The reformulation of probabilistic matrix-factorization for sound analysis in terms of time-domain kernels facilitates EM analysis for multiresolution (overcomplete) bases, but the lack of thorough evaluation and the presence of noticeable artifacts in the examples prevent a strong claim of importance in its current form.
