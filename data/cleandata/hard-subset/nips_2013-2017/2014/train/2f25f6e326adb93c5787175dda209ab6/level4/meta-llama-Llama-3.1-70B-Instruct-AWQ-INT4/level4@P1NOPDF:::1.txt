This paper presents an extension to the family of low-rank spectrogram factorization models for sound decomposition, generalizing the latent factors to an arbitrary, potentially overcomplete set of bases. This enables decomposition to be performed on "multiresolution" bases, such as tonal and transient bases, which is argued to improve source separation and denoising. The approach builds upon the Gaussian Composite Model of Fevotte et al., which models the variance of each spectrogram cell using a low-rank matrix factorization. However, the authors expand the implicit convolution with multiple Gabor impulse responses corresponding to each row of the spectrogram, yielding a purely time-domain expression for the estimated quantities. This expression can be modified to accommodate different sets of impulse responses, including non-orthogonal ones. An Expectation-Maximization (EM) analysis procedure is presented to estimate the model parameters.

Quality: I find this insight intriguing, although I am still grappling with its exact implications. Initially, it appears to be another way of understanding Fevotte's Gaussian Composite Model, but the ability to incorporate overcomplete dictionaries is a welcome addition. On the other hand, it seems that this extension should introduce some complications, and I did not immediately understand why these did not cause more issues. I assume the estimation procedure is correct, and I am impressed by the level of analysis, although I could not follow it in detail.

Clarity: The authors do a good job of explaining and motivating their work. However, the evaluation is very superficial, consisting of a single illustrative result and a SNR improvement measurement. I do not think it is possible to draw any conclusions from a single example. I appreciated the inclusion of sound examples, but I was not impressed by them. The OMLSA-enhanced example appears to be significantly longer (7 seconds vs. 2 seconds) than the new result, which raises questions about the fairness of the comparison. Furthermore, although the OMLSA example includes more background noise, partly due to its longer duration, the speech sounds much higher quality to me. The denoising of the music example comes at the expense of significant artifacts, which I would expect conventional wavelet shrinkage or Wiener filtering to avoid, resulting in higher perceived quality.

Originality: The work relies heavily on the foundation laid by Fevotte et al., although the time-domain interpretation offers a new perspective on their model.

Significance: At first glance, the ability to apply matrix factorization techniques to overcomplete bases seems to have significant potential impact. However, the examples and evaluation presented in this work are too limited and superficial to demonstrate this potential. As it stands, it requires an act of faith to rate this work as having substantial significance. The reformulation of probabilistic matrix factorization sound analysis in terms of time-domain kernels allows for an EM analysis for multiresolution (overcomplete) bases. Unfortunately, the thin evaluation and significant artifacts in the examples fail to demonstrate its importance in its current form.
