

This paper introduces Low-Rank Time-Frequency Synthesis (LRTFS), which extends the Gaussian Composite Model (GCM) framework of Fevotte et al. (NIPS 2009) to directly model the raw audio signal rather than its spectrogram. While the GCM models the variance of STFT coefficients as a low-rank matrix factorization, LRTFS provides a time-domain generative model where the signal is represented as a sum of components with low-rank time-frequency structure. The authors propose two EM algorithms for estimation and demonstrate applications in music decomposition and speech enhancement, with particular emphasis on the ability to handle multi-resolution representations (e.g., tonal and transient components with different time-frequency resolutions).

The paper makes a technically sound contribution that builds upon the GCM framework while addressing a significant limitation of previous work. The core insight—that the STFT-based GCM can be "inverted" to produce a time-domain generative model with low-rank structure—provides a mathematically rigorous foundation for direct signal synthesis. The authors carefully relate their model to sparse Bayesian learning, highlighting the key difference that their approach imposes structured low-rank constraints on the variances rather than allowing free variance parameters as in SBL.

Quality: The technical content is sound and well-supported. The derivation of the EM algorithms is thorough, and the connection to existing work (particularly Fevotte et al. and SBL) is well-articulated. The authors correctly acknowledge the limitations of their current implementation (MMLE is not scalable for audio applications) and focus on the MJLE approach, which is computationally feasible. The paper demonstrates proper care in evaluating both strengths (multi-resolution capability) and weaknesses (computational complexity of MMLE).

Clarity: The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with a logical progression from problem statement to model definition, estimation algorithms, and applications. The mathematical notation is consistent and well-explained. The authors provide sufficient detail for an expert to reproduce their results, though the connection between the multi-resolution formulation and practical implementation could be slightly clearer.

Originality: While the work builds substantially on the GCM framework, the time-domain interpretation and extension to multi-resolution representations constitutes a meaningful contribution. The authors clearly articulate how their approach differs from previous work (e.g., the ability to handle non-orthogonal bases for multi-resolution analysis), and the references to prior NIPS work (Fevotte et al., 2009) are appropriate. The paper does not claim to be a radical departure from the GCM but rather a natural extension that addresses a specific limitation.

Significance: The potential significance of LRTFS is substantial, as multi-resolution representations are particularly valuable in audio processing (e.g., separating transients from sustained components). However, the current evaluation is limited in scope. The music decomposition example is purely illustrative, and the speech enhancement results show only marginal improvements (6.6 dB vs. 6 dB for IS-NMF and 4.6 dB for OMLSA) on a single test set. The authors acknowledge this limitation by stating "more extensive experimental studies are planned," but the current evidence does not sufficiently demonstrate the practical significance of their approach.

Strengths:
- Rigorous mathematical foundation extending the GCM framework
- Novel time-domain interpretation of low-rank spectrogram factorization
- Clear demonstration of multi-resolution capability
- Well-structured and technically sound EM algorithms
- Appropriate connection to related work

Weaknesses:
- Evaluation is limited to one illustrative example and one speech enhancement experiment
- Comparison to baseline methods lacks sufficient detail (e.g., the OMLSA comparison appears to use different signal lengths)
- No discussion of computational complexity for large-scale audio applications
- Perception of artifacts in the music decomposition example is not adequately addressed

The paper makes a technically interesting contribution to the field of audio signal processing, extending a well-established framework to address a meaningful limitation. The core idea is sound and well-motivated, and the authors have provided a rigorous mathematical foundation for their approach. However, the current evaluation does not sufficiently demonstrate the practical significance of the proposed method. While the multi-resolution capability is theoretically compelling, the empirical results fail to convincingly show that LRTFS provides meaningful advantages over existing approaches in real-world applications. The marginal SNR improvements reported (0.6 dB over IS-NMF) are not compelling evidence of significant impact, especially given the additional complexity of the proposed approach.

The authors should address these evaluation limitations in future work, particularly by providing more extensive experimental results across multiple audio domains and longer signal segments. The current paper presents a promising theoretical advance but does not yet provide sufficient empirical evidence to justify its significance for the NIPS community.
