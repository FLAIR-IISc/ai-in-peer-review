This paper presents an extension to the family of low-rank spectrogram factorization models for sound decomposition. The key contribution is a generalization that allows the latent factors to be defined over an arbitrary, potentially overcomplete set of bases, enabling decomposition using "multiresolution" representations (e.g., combining tonal and transient bases). The authors argue this is beneficial for tasks like source separation and denoising. The proposed method builds upon the Gaussian Composite Model (GCM) of Fevotte et al., which models the variance of each spectrogram cell with a low-rank matrix factorization. The authors reformulate this model by expanding the implicit convolution with the Gabor impulse responses into a purely time-domain expression. This time-domain perspective is what allows the framework to be adapted for different, including non-orthogonal, sets of impulse responses. An EM algorithm for finding a locally optimal solution is also presented.

**Quality**

The central idea is interesting, though its full implications are not immediately clear. While it can be viewed as an alternative interpretation of Fevotte's GCM, the key advantage is the ability to incorporate overcomplete dictionaries. However, the use of overcomplete bases would typically be expected to introduce complications, and the paper does not fully clarify why these are not more problematic in this framework. While the details of the estimation procedure were difficult to follow, the derivation appears rigorous, and I assume it to be correct.

**Clarity**

The paper is well-written, and the motivation is clear. However, the experimental evaluation is superficial. Only a single illustrative result and one SNR improvement measurement are provided, which is insufficient to draw meaningful conclusions about the method's performance. The provided audio examples are appreciated but are not compelling. First, the comparison with OMLSA may not be fair, as the provided OMLSA example (7 seconds) is significantly longer than the result from the proposed method (2 seconds). Second, despite containing more background noise (partially due to its longer duration), the speech in the OMLSA example subjectively sounds higher in quality. The music denoising example exhibits significant artifacts; it is likely that conventional methods like wavelet shrinkage or Wiener filtering would yield higher perceptual quality in this case.

**Originality**

The work is heavily based on the foundation laid by Fevotte et al. However, the reformulation and interpretation in the time domain is novel and provides a new perspective on this class of models.

**Significance**

The ability to apply matrix factorization techniques to overcomplete bases is potentially highly significant. However, the superficial examples and thin evaluation presented in the paper fail to demonstrate this potential. As it stands, assessing the work as having substantial significance would require a leap of faith. The reformulation of probabilistic matrix factorization for sound analysis in terms of time-domain kernels is an elegant theoretical contribution that enables an EM analysis for multiresolution bases. Unfortunately, the weak evaluation and significant artifacts in the examples fail to demonstrate its practical importance in its current form.
