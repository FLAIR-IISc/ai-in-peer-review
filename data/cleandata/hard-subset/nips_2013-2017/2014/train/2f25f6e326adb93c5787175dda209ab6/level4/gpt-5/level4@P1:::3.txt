Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper introduces a low-rank time-frequency synthesis (LRTFS) model that directly generates the time-domain signal rather than modeling only its short-time Fourier transform (STFT). The key idea is to impose a low-rank structure on the variances of synthesis coefficients αfn in a chosen time-frequency frame Φ, with x(t) reconstructed as a linear combination of atoms φfn(t) plus Gaussian residual noise. This contrasts with the Gaussian Composite Model (GCM) used to justify Itakura–Saito (IS) NMF on spectrograms, which imposes low-rank structure on the variances of analysis coefficients (the STFT). The authors derive two EM-based estimation schemes: maximum joint likelihood estimation (MJLE), which alternates Wiener-like shrinkage on α with IS-NMF updates on W and H, and maximum marginal likelihood estimation (MMLE), in the spirit of sparse Bayesian learning (SBL), which integrates out α but is currently not computationally scalable for the audio-sized problems considered. A notable advantage of the synthesis formulation is a natural extension to multi-resolution modeling by combining multiple time-frequency frames (e.g., short and long Gabor windows) within the same generative model.

The paper relates LRTFS to SBL by showing that, ignoring the low-rank constraint, the model reduces to a diagonal Gaussian prior on α. The novelty here is that the variances are “tied” through WH, enforcing structured low-rank behavior rather than independent sparsity. The authors provide component reconstruction via Wiener-type formulas on α and demonstrate applications in hybrid (tonal/transient) music decomposition and semi-supervised speech enhancement.

Strengths:
- Conceptual advance: bridges the gap between analysis-based NMF/GCM and a truly generative time-domain synthesis model with low-rank structure on synthesis coefficients.
- Flexibility: accommodates arbitrary time-frequency frames and easily extends to multi-resolution decompositions, an important practical feature for audio (e.g., transients vs. sustained harmonics).
- Algorithmic clarity: the MJLE EM scheme is simple and practical (no large matrix inversions), leveraging well-known IS-NMF updates and efficient Φ/Φ* operations.
- Solid theoretical grounding: clear derivations, connections to IS divergence, Wiener filtering, and SBL.

Weaknesses:
- Empirical evaluation is limited. The results and supplemental material are not particularly convincing, and the paper does not adequately compare with the very methods it builds on (e.g., GCM/IS-NMF variants) to quantify practical gains.
- The significance of the contribution is not fully communicated through experiments; more comprehensive benchmarks and ablations would strengthen the case.
- MMLE, while conceptually appealing, is not scalable in its present form; the paper leaves this as future work.
- Some estimation choices (e.g., treating λ as a hyperparameter, warm-restart scheduling) are practical but reduce the completeness of the generative treatment.

Quality:
- The technical development is sound. The MJLE objective and EM updates are correct and connect to known tools (IS-NMF, Wiener shrinkage). The link to SBL is appropriate.
- There is an acknowledged gap between the “exact” EM update for λ and the implemented one, and the overall nonconvexity means convergence is to local optima; these limitations are standard in this literature.
- The MMLE path is carefully outlined but not yet computationally practical for large-scale audio; this limits immediate applicability of that variant.

Clarity:
- The paper is well written and easy to follow for readers in this area. Notation and modeling choices are generally clear.
- Suggested clarifications: briefly summarize computational costs and memory footprints; specify how β is set relative to λ and δΦ; provide guidance on initialization; and more explicitly discuss the choice of Φ and its impact.

Originality:
- Above average. Moving the low-rank structure from analysis coefficients (STFT magnitude) to synthesis coefficients of a time-domain generative model is a meaningful shift that enables multi-resolution synthesis within the same framework. The connections drawn to SBL and to classical Wiener filtering are insightful.
- While related ideas exist in hybrid/morphological sparse models, the low-rank synthesis probabilistic formulation is a novel and coherent contribution.

Significance:
- Potentially significant for audio signal processing and related inverse problems, especially due to the multi-resolution capability and direct modeling in the time domain.
- However, the current empirical section does not fully substantiate practical advantages over established baselines; stronger, broader comparisons are needed to demonstrate impact.

Pros for acceptance:
- Clear, elegant formulation that unifies synthesis modeling with low-rank structure.
- Practical EM algorithm (MJLE) with standard building blocks.
- Natural and useful multi-resolution extension.

Cons against acceptance:
- Limited and not fully convincing experimental validation; insufficient comparative analysis with strong baselines.
- MMLE remains a compelling idea but is not yet scalable.
- The paper undersells its significance by not demonstrating clear empirical gains across diverse tasks.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Add thorough comparisons to GCM/IS-NMF variants and other competitive methods on standard datasets; report quantitative metrics (e.g., SNR gains, SDR/SIR/SAR, PESQ/STOI for speech).
- Include ablations (single vs. multi-resolution, effect of K, impact of λ scheduling) and sensitivity to initialization.
- Provide runtime and scaling analyses, and implementation details for reproducibility.

Overall assessment:
A good paper: well written, theoretically solid, and offering a thoughtful, flexible interpretation of time–frequency factorizations that directly links to the time-domain signal. While I would have liked to see more results and stronger comparisons, this is not a fatal flaw given the quality of the theoretical contribution. I recommend acceptance, with encouragement to strengthen the empirical evaluation.
