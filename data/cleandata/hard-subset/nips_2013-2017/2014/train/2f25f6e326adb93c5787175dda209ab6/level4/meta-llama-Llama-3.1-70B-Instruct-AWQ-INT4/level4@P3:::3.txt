This paper presents a thoughtful interpretation of time/frequency factorizations. Instead of using existing formulations that are built on the analysis coefficients of STFT transforms, it presents a more flexible framework that can be applied to an arbitrary transform and is directly linked to the time domain signal.

The paper is easy to read for people in this area and of good quality. The authors have done a great job in making the content accessible to readers familiar with the subject matter. The writing style is clear, and the organization of the paper is logical and well-structured.

The originality of the paper is above average. The proposed Low-Rank Time-Frequency Synthesis (LRTFS) model offers a novel approach to signal synthesis, and the authors have successfully bridged the gap between time-frequency synthesis and traditional Nonnegative Matrix Factorization (NMF) approaches. The introduction of a generative model that can accommodate multi-resolution representations is a significant contribution to the field.

However, the significance of the paper is not fully communicated. While the authors have provided some experimental results, they are limited, and the paper lacks a comprehensive evaluation of the proposed method. The supplemental material is not particularly convincing, and the paper does not compare this work to the methods it builds on. This is a disappointing aspect of the paper, as it would have been beneficial to see a more thorough comparison with existing approaches.

Despite this limitation, the theoretical development in the paper is robust and interesting. The authors have provided a solid foundation for the LRTFS model, and the derivation of the Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithms for estimation is well-explained. The discussion on the relation to sparse Bayesian learning is also informative.

In terms of quality, the paper meets the criteria. The authors have demonstrated a good understanding of the subject matter, and the paper is well-written. The use of mathematical notation is clear, and the figures are relevant and well-captioned.

In conclusion, this is a good paper with solid theory. While the results and evaluation could be improved, the theoretical contribution is significant, and the paper is well-written. With some additional experimental results and comparisons, this paper could have been even stronger.

Arguments For Acceptance:

* The paper presents a novel approach to signal synthesis, bridging the gap between time-frequency synthesis and traditional NMF approaches.
* The proposed LRTFS model offers a flexible framework that can be applied to an arbitrary transform and is directly linked to the time domain signal.
* The theoretical development is robust and interesting, providing a solid foundation for the LRTFS model.

Arguments Against Acceptance:

* The paper lacks a comprehensive evaluation of the proposed method, and the experimental results are limited.
* The supplemental material is not particularly convincing, and the paper does not compare this work to the methods it builds on.
* The significance of the paper is not fully communicated, and the authors could have done a better job in highlighting the importance of their contribution.

Rating: 7/10

Recommendation: Accept with minor revisions. The authors should consider adding more experimental results and comparisons to strengthen the paper. Additionally, they could provide more context on the significance of their contribution and its potential impact on the field.
