This paper proposes a novel low-rank time-frequency synthesis (LRTFS) model, extending traditional nonnegative matrix factorization (NMF) by directly modeling the raw signal instead of its spectrogram. This allows for a generative model of synthesis coefficients, opening the door to multi-resolution representations not previously achievable with standard NMF approaches. The core idea builds upon recent work establishing a connection between NMF with the Itakura-Saito divergence and a Gaussian Composite Model (GCM) of the STFT [Févotte et al., 2013], and further generalizes this to a complete signal generation process.

The paper is well-motivated and clearly articulates the limitations of existing methods. The proposed LRTFS model is a logical progression from the GCM, addressing the gap in creating a fully generative model for the raw signal. The inclusion of multi-resolution capabilities is a significant strength, potentially enabling more effective signal decomposition for complex audio signals with varying temporal characteristics. The authors present two expectation-maximization (EM) algorithms for estimation and demonstrate results on music decomposition and speech enhancement.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The approach of directly modeling the signal synthesis process is a significant contribution, moving beyond spectrogram-based NMF.
*   **Multi-resolution Representation:** The ability to incorporate multiple time-frequency resolutions is a key advantage, particularly for audio signals with transients and sustained components.
*   **Generative Model:** Establishing a generative model for the raw signal is theoretically appealing and offers potential benefits for downstream tasks.
*   **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and the mathematical derivations are presented in a clear and concise manner.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Limited Comparative Evaluation:** While the authors present results, a more thorough comparison to existing methods, specifically IS-NMF in the single-resolution case, is needed. It remains unclear if the proposed approach offers advantages in simplicity, speed, or stability compared to established techniques.  Do the learned W and H matrices align with those obtained from IS-NMF?
*   **Computational Complexity:** The MMLE approach, while theoretically interesting, appears computationally prohibitive for large-scale audio applications due to matrix inversion requirements. The authors acknowledge this, but a discussion of potential scalability improvements (e.g., using approximation techniques) would be valuable.
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The reliance on a fixed (rather than estimated) λ parameter raises questions about the robustness of the results.  Further investigation into the sensitivity of performance to λ would strengthen the conclusions.
*   **Lack of Theoretical Justification for λ Fixation:** The justification provided for fixing λ (poor performance with simultaneous estimation) is not elaborated upon. A deeper analysis, perhaps through ablation studies, would be beneficial.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound approach to signal decomposition.
*   The multi-resolution capability is a significant contribution with potential for a wide range of applications.
*   The experimental results, although limited, demonstrate the feasibility and potential effectiveness of the proposed method.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

*   The lack of a comprehensive comparative evaluation limits the assessment of the practical benefits of the LRTFS model.
*   The computational complexity of the MMLE approach restricts its applicability to large-scale problems.
*   The sensitivity to the fixed hyperparameter λ raises concerns about robustness.

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a promising new approach to time-frequency signal decomposition. The novelty of directly modeling signal synthesis and the inclusion of multi-resolution capabilities are significant strengths. However, the limited comparative evaluation and concerns regarding computational complexity and hyperparameter sensitivity prevent a strong recommendation for acceptance in its current form. I recommend **weak accept** contingent upon the authors addressing the weaknesses outlined above, specifically by providing a more thorough comparison to IS-NMF, discussing potential scalability improvements for MMLE, and justifying the choice of fixing λ.
