This paper proposes an extension to the family of low-rank spectrogram factorization models for sound decomposition that generalizes the latent factors to an arbitrary—potentially overcomplete—set of bases. This enables decomposition on “multiresolution” dictionaries (e.g., jointly modeling tonal and transient components) and is argued to benefit source separation and denoising. The approach builds on Févotte et al.’s Gaussian Composite Model (GCM), which models the variance of each spectrogram bin via low-rank matrix factorization. The authors make explicit the implicit convolution with multiple Gabor impulse responses associated with each spectrogram row, yielding a purely time-domain formulation that can be adapted to different, including nonorthogonal, impulse-response sets. They then derive an EM procedure that converges to a local optimum.

Quality: I find the core idea interesting, though the implications are not entirely clear to me. At first glance it reads as an alternative perspective on Févotte’s GCM, but the ability to incorporate overcomplete dictionaries is appealing. That said, I would expect overcompleteness to introduce complications, and it was not immediately clear why these do not become more problematic in practice. I assume the estimation procedure is correct (and I am impressed by the technical development), although I could not follow every step in detail.

Clarity: The paper is generally well motivated and clearly written. However, the evaluation is very limited: there is one illustrative demonstration and one SNR improvement figure, which is insufficient to support strong claims. I appreciated the audio examples, but they did not convincingly showcase the method. In particular, the OMLSA-enhanced example appears much longer (about 7 s vs. 2 s) than the proposed result, raising concerns about the fairness of the comparison. Moreover, although the OMLSA output contains more background noise (partly due to its greater duration), the speech quality is perceptually higher. For the music example, the denoising seems to introduce substantial artifacts; my expectation is that conventional wavelet shrinkage or Wiener filtering would yield better perceived quality in this case.

Originality: The contribution is strongly rooted in the framework of Févotte et al., but the time-domain reinterpretation prompted me to view the model in a new light. The generalization to nonorthogonal and overcomplete bases within this probabilistic factorization framework is a noteworthy angle.

Significance: In principle, enabling matrix factorization techniques to operate over overcomplete bases could be impactful. However, the current empirical evidence is too sparse and superficial to substantiate this promise. As it stands, assessing the significance of the contribution requires a degree of faith not yet supported by the experiments. Reformulating probabilistic matrix-factorization–based sound analysis in terms of time-domain kernels is a compelling step that permits an EM analysis for multiresolution (overcomplete) bases, but the thin evaluation and noticeable artifacts in the examples do not yet demonstrate the method’s practical importance.
