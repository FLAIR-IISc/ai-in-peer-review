This paper introduces a novel extension to low-rank spectrogram factorization models for sound decomposition, building upon the Gaussian Composite Model (GCM) of Févotte et al. The core idea is to generalize latent factors across arbitrary, potentially overcomplete, basis sets, enabling multi-resolution decomposition (e.g., separating tonal and transient components). The authors move from modeling the variance of spectrogram cells with low-rank factorization to a time-domain expression, facilitating the use of diverse impulse responses and non-orthogonal bases. An Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm is presented for parameter estimation.

This work offers an interesting perspective on existing spectral factorization techniques. While initially appearing as a reinterpretation of Févotte's GCM, the inclusion of overcomplete dictionaries is a valuable contribution. The authors successfully bridge the gap between time-frequency synthesis and traditional NMF approaches, presenting a flexible framework for signal representation. The theoretical development, particularly the EM algorithm, is impressive, although the detailed derivations were challenging to fully follow. 

The paper is well-written and clearly motivated. However, the experimental evaluation is a significant weakness. The provided results are superficial, consisting of a single illustrative example and one SNR improvement measurement. Drawing strong conclusions from such limited evidence is difficult. The inclusion of audio examples is appreciated, but they did not demonstrate compelling improvements. The comparison to OMLSA appears potentially unfair due to differing signal lengths; the OMLSA example’s longer duration could contribute to its perceived higher quality. Furthermore, the denoising of the music example introduces noticeable artifacts, suggesting that simpler methods like wavelet shrinkage or Wiener filtering might yield better perceptual quality. 

The originality lies in the time-domain interpretation of the GCM and the consequent ability to incorporate overcomplete bases. The work builds directly on the foundation laid by Févotte et al., but introduces a novel and potentially powerful generalization. This reformulation of probabilistic matrix factorization for sound analysis in terms of time-domain kernels and subsequent EM analysis for multiresolution (overcomplete) bases is a noteworthy contribution.

The potential significance is substantial. Applying matrix factorization techniques to overcomplete bases could have a significant impact on signal processing tasks. However, the current evaluation is too limited to substantiate this claim. The thin experimental validation and the presence of artifacts in the examples prevent a strong assessment of its importance at this stage. 

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel extension of established NMF/GCM techniques.
*   Theoretical soundness of the proposed model and EM algorithm.
*   Potential for improved performance in applications benefiting from multi-resolution analysis (e.g., source separation, denoising).
*   Clear and well-motivated presentation of the work.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Weak and superficial experimental evaluation.
*   Artifacts present in the example results.
*   Limited demonstration of practical benefits compared to existing methods.
*   Reliance on a local optimum EM algorithm without thorough investigation of convergence properties.



**Overall Assessment:** This paper presents a theoretically sound and potentially significant contribution to the field of audio processing. However, the lack of robust experimental validation raises concerns about its practical impact. I recommend **weak reject** with encouragement to significantly expand the experimental evaluation, address the observed artifacts, and provide a more compelling demonstration of the method’s advantages. A more thorough comparison against state-of-the-art techniques, using standardized datasets and evaluation metrics, is crucial.
