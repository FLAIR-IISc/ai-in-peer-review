This paper proposes an extension of low-rank spectrogram factorization for single-channel audio that models the raw waveform via a low-rank time–frequency synthesis (LRTFS). The key idea is to move from the Gaussian Composite Model (GCM) of Févotte et al.—which places a low-rank variance model on STFT coefficients—to a generative time-domain formulation in which the signal is synthesized from atoms of an arbitrary time–frequency frame, with synthesis coefficients whose variances follow a low-rank WH structure. This shift enables the use of arbitrary (including overcomplete and nonorthogonal) dictionaries and naturally supports multiresolution representations (e.g., separate tonal and transient layers). The paper presents EM-based estimation procedures (with a scalable MJLE scheme and a less scalable MMLE variant) and illustrates applications to music decomposition and speech enhancement.

Quality: The conceptual move to a synthesis-domain, low-rank variance model is interesting and potentially useful, and the ability to accommodate overcomplete frames is appealing. At first glance, much of the framework can be viewed as a reinterpretation of the GCM within a time-domain generative setting, but the multiresolution capability goes beyond standard NMF practice. I assume the estimation procedure is correct—derivations appear careful—although I was not able to verify every step in detail. I would have liked more discussion of potential pitfalls (e.g., conditioning/identifiability with highly redundant frames) and how the algorithm mitigates them.

Clarity: The model and its motivation are clearly presented, and the EM scheme is explained coherently. However, the experimental section is quite thin: one illustrative music decomposition and a single SNR-improvement result for speech. This is insufficient to draw strong conclusions. The audio examples are helpful, but I was not convinced by them. For speech, the OMLSA output provided appears much longer (about 7 s vs. ~2 s for the proposed result), raising concerns about whether the comparison was performed on matched segments. Moreover, while OMLSA leaves more background noise (partly due to length), the speech quality sounds higher. For music, the denoising seems to introduce noticeable artifacts; I would expect simple baselines such as wavelet shrinkage or Wiener filtering to yield higher perceived quality in that scenario. Stronger, controlled, and matched evaluations would substantially improve clarity of the empirical claims.

Originality: The work builds directly on the GCM and IS-NMF lineage, but the synthesis-domain formulation that supports overcomplete and multiresolution frames is a fresh angle. The connection enabling EM-based estimation in this more general setting is a useful addition to the literature.

Significance: In principle, bringing low-rank factorization to overcomplete, multiresolution synthesis frames could impact source separation and enhancement methods. As presented, though, the empirical evidence is too limited to substantiate this potential: the examples are few, comparisons are not clearly matched, and artifacts are prominent in the demos. Thus, any assessment of significance rests more on the promise of the formulation than on demonstrated gains. Reformulating probabilistic matrix factorization through time-domain synthesis kernels does enable EM estimation for multiresolution (overcomplete) bases, but the current evaluation and audible artifacts do not yet convincingly establish practical importance.
