This paper proposes an extension to the family of low-rank spectrogram factorization models for sound decomposition, allowing the generalization of latent factors across an arbitrary set of bases, including "multiresolution" bases that combine tonal and transient components. This extension is expected to improve source separation and denoising capabilities. The approach builds upon the Gaussian Composite Model of Fevotte et al, which represents the variance of each spectrogram cell using low-rank matrix factorization. However, it expands the implicit convolution with multiple Gabor impulse responses corresponding to each spectrogram row, resulting in a time-domain expression that can be adapted for different sets of impulse responses, including non-orthogonal ones. An Expectation-Maximization (EM) analysis procedure is also presented.

Quality: The idea presented in this paper is intriguing, and I am still exploring its implications. At first glance, it appears to offer an alternative perspective on Fevotte's Gaussian Composite Model, with the added benefit of accommodating overcomplete dictionaries. However, I anticipate potential complications arising from this approach, which are not immediately apparent. While I assume the estimation procedure is correct and appreciate the analytical effort, I was unable to follow the details.

Clarity: The authors provide a clear explanation and motivation for their work. However, the evaluation is limited, consisting of a single illustrative result and a measurement of SNR improvement, which is insufficient to draw meaningful conclusions. The inclusion of sound examples is appreciated, but the comparison between the proposed method and OMLSA is questionable, as the lengths of the examples differ significantly (7 seconds vs. 2 seconds). Furthermore, although OMLSA includes more background noise, the speech quality appears higher, and the denoising of the music example is compromised by significant artifacts, which could be mitigated using conventional techniques such as wavelet shrinkage or Wiener filtering.

Originality: The work heavily relies on the foundation established by Fevotte et al, although the time-domain interpretation offers a fresh perspective.

Significance: The ability to apply matrix factorization techniques to overcomplete bases has the potential for significant impact. However, the evaluation and examples presented in this work are too limited to demonstrate this potential, making it difficult to assess the true significance of the proposed approach. The reformulation of probabilistic matrix factorization sound analysis in terms of time-domain kernels allows for EM analysis with multiresolution (overcomplete) bases, but the thin evaluation and significant artifacts in the examples undermine the demonstration of its importance.
