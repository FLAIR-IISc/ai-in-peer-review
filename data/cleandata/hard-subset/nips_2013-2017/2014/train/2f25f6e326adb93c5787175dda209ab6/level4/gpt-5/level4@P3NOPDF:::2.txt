Summary:
The paper departs from the conventional NMF framework that models time–frequency (TF) analysis coefficients (e.g., spectrogram magnitudes) and instead proposes a generative model for the time-domain signal via TF synthesis coefficients. This shift enables multi-resolution representations that are difficult or impossible to realize with standard NMF formulations. The approach is interesting and, in particular, the multi-resolution extension is a compelling contribution.

Strengths:
- Conceptual novelty: Modeling the time-domain signal through synthesis coefficients provides a fresh perspective compared to traditional analysis-based NMF. This perspective is well-motivated and opens the door to richer representations.
- Multi-resolution capability: The framework naturally accommodates multiple resolutions, a feature that is highly relevant for audio and other nonstationary signals. This is an appealing and potentially impactful extension over standard NMF.
- Empirical demonstrations: The paper presents results on multi-resolution decompositions that suggest practical utility.

Questions and points for clarification:
- Single-resolution comparison with IS-NMF: How does the proposed method perform when restricted to a single resolution, relative to a standard IS-NMF baseline? Specifically:
  - Do the learned basis and activation matrices (W and H) resemble those obtained by IS-NMF under comparable conditions (same data, same initialization if applicable, same model rank)?
  - Are the reconstructions (both in TF magnitude space and in the time domain) comparable in quality?
- Algorithmic efficiency and stability:
  - How do convergence speed and computational cost compare to multiplicative updates for IS-NMF in the single-resolution setting?
  - Is the optimization as numerically stable and robust to initialization as IS-NMF? Do you observe similar monotonic decrease of the objective and absence of pathological behaviors (e.g., stalled updates, component collapse)?
- Practical guidance:
  - Are there hyperparameters or design choices (e.g., window sizes, number of resolutions, regularization) that critically affect performance or stability?
  - How sensitive is the method to the choice of synthesis dictionary or frame? Any recommendations for practitioners?

Suggestions for strengthening the paper:
- Provide a controlled single-resolution experiment that matches IS-NMF as closely as possible (same data, rank, initial factors, number of iterations), and report:
  - Objective values and reconstruction errors (time-domain and TF-domain).
  - Similarity between learned W and H (e.g., correlation or permutation-aligned distance).
  - Convergence traces and wall-clock time.
- Include an ablation or quantitative study isolating the benefits of multi-resolution (e.g., reconstruction quality versus computation, or task-specific metrics such as source separation scores), to demonstrate clear gains over single-resolution baselines.
- Discuss the relationship between analysis- and synthesis-based modeling: when do they coincide, and when do they diverge in practice? This would help readers interpret differences in learned factors and reconstructions.
- Briefly address identifiability and invariances in the synthesis setting (even qualitatively), to frame expectations about uniqueness of W and H relative to standard IS-NMF.
- Add implementation details sufficient for reproduction: optimization steps, step sizes or update rules, stopping criteria, and any tricks that improve stability.

Overall assessment:
This is an interesting and promising paper. The synthesis-based generative perspective is well-motivated and the multi-resolution extension is particularly attractive. The work would be significantly strengthened by a careful single-resolution comparison with IS-NMF and a more thorough analysis of computational efficiency and stability. With these additions, the paper would offer a clearer picture of both the conceptual advantages and the practical trade-offs of the proposed framework.
