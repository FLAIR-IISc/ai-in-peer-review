

This paper introduces an extension to low-rank spectrogram-factorization models for sound decomposition, generalizing latent factors over arbitrary (potentially overcomplete) bases to enable decomposition using "multiresolution" bases (e.g., tonal + transient). It argues this approach improves source separation and denoising. The work builds upon Fevotte et al.'s Gaussian Composite Model (GCM), which uses low-rank matrix factorization to model spectrogram cell variances. It then reformulates the implicit convolution with multiple Gabor impulse responses (corresponding to spectrogram rows) into a purely time-domain expression, allowing modification for diverse impulse response sets, including nonorthogonal ones, and presents an EM-based (local optimum) estimation procedure.  

Quality: The insight is intriguing, though the precise implications remain unclear to me. Initially, it appears to be merely an alternative interpretation of Fevotte’s GCM, though the capability to incorporate overcomplete dictionaries is valuable. However, I question why potential complications from this generalization are not more evident, as they seem likely to arise. I assume the estimation procedure is correct (and commend the analytical depth), but I could not fully follow its technical details.  

Clarity: The work is well-motivated and clearly explained. The evaluation, however, is superficial: only one illustrative result and a single SNR improvement metric are provided, which is insufficient to draw meaningful conclusions. While the included audio examples are appreciated, they are unconvincing. The OMLSA comparison example is 7 seconds versus the new method’s 2 seconds, raising fairness concerns. Additionally, the OMLSA example—despite longer duration and more background noise—presents noticeably higher speech quality. The music denoising example exhibits severe artifacts, whereas conventional methods like wavelet shrinkage or Wiener filtering would likely yield superior perceptual quality.  

Originality: The work leans heavily on Fevotte et al.’s foundation, though the time-domain reinterpretation offers a novel perspective.  

Significance: The potential to apply matrix-factorization techniques to overcomplete bases holds promise, but the current evaluation is too sparse and the examples too flawed to demonstrate tangible impact. As presented, the significance rests on an act of faith rather than empirical evidence. While the reformulation enables EM analysis for multiresolution bases, the thin evaluation and significant artifacts in the examples prevent a meaningful assessment of its importance.
