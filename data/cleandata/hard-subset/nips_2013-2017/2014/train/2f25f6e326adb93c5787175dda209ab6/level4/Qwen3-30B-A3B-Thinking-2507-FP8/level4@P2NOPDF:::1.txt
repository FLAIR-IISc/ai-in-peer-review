

This paper extends low-rank spectrogram factorization models for sound decomposition by generalizing latent factorization over arbitrary (potentially overcomplete) bases, enabling multiresolution analysis (e.g., combining tonal and transient components). Building on Fevotte et al.'s Gaussian Composite Model (GCM), which uses low-rank factorization to model spectrogram variances, the authors derive a time-domain formulation by replacing the implicit convolution with Gabor impulse responses. This reformulation allows the model to accommodate arbitrary impulse response sets—including nonorthogonal bases—and supports a time-domain EM estimation procedure. The approach is framed as a generalization of GCM that facilitates decomposition on flexible basis dictionaries.

**Quality**: The core insight—reinterpreting GCM in the time domain to enable overcomplete bases—is technically sound and represents a meaningful conceptual advancement. While the method builds directly on Fevotte et al., the time-domain perspective offers a novel lens for understanding and extending the model. The EM analysis appears rigorous, though the paper does not fully address potential complications from overcompleteness (e.g., identifiability issues or computational overhead), which would be critical for practical adoption. The estimation procedure is likely correct, but the lack of detailed justification for why these complications are manageable weakens confidence.

**Clarity**: The motivation and technical exposition are clear and well-structured. However, the evaluation is severely underdeveloped. Only one illustrative example and a single SNR metric are provided, which is insufficient to substantiate claims about source separation or denoising performance. The included audio examples are helpful but misleading: the OMLSA comparison uses a 7-second clip versus the proposed method’s 2-second clip, raising fairness concerns. Additionally, while the OMLSA example includes more background noise (due to its length), the speech quality appears perceptually superior. The music denoising example exhibits severe artifacts (e.g., musical noise), where conventional methods like wavelet shrinkage or Wiener filtering would likely yield higher perceptual quality. These issues undermine the paper’s core claims.

**Originality**: The work is heavily grounded in Fevotte et al.’s GCM, but the time-domain reinterpretation and extension to overcomplete bases constitute a nontrivial contribution. This reframing provides a new theoretical pathway for applying matrix factorization to multiresolution sound analysis, though the novelty is incremental rather than transformative.

**Significance**: The potential impact of applying matrix factorization to overcomplete bases is promising, particularly for tasks requiring flexible basis decomposition (e.g., source separation with hybrid tonal/transient models). However, the current evaluation fails to demonstrate this impact. The superficial results—limited to one example with questionable comparisons and perceptual artifacts—do not justify the significance claim. Without robust, multi-metric validation across diverse datasets, the method’s practical value remains speculative. The reformulation enables EM analysis for overcomplete bases, but the absence of compelling evidence prevents this from being rated as a substantial contribution.  

**Recommendation**: The paper presents a technically interesting extension of existing work, but the weak evaluation and unresolved practical concerns prevent it from being accepted in its current form. The authors should substantially strengthen the experimental section by: (1) providing multiple quantitative metrics (e.g., SDR, STOI) across diverse audio datasets; (2) ensuring fair comparisons (e.g., equal-length clips); and (3) addressing perceptual artifacts through detailed listening tests. Without these, the significance and practical relevance remain unproven.
