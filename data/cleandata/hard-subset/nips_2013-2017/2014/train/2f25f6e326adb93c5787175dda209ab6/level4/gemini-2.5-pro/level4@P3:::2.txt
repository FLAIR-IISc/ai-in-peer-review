### Review

**Summary**

This paper introduces a novel probabilistic generative model for time-domain signals, termed Low-Rank Time-Frequency Synthesis (LRTFS). Traditional approaches, such as Nonnegative Matrix Factorization (NMF) with the Itakura-Saito divergence, operate on a time-frequency representation of the signal (e.g., the spectrogram). These methods can be interpreted as a generative model for the complex Short-Time Fourier Transform (STFT) *analysis* coefficients, where their variance is constrained to have a low-rank structure (WH).

In contrast, the proposed LRTFS model provides a generative model for the time-domain signal itself. It represents the signal as a linear combination of time-frequency atoms from a synthesis frame, where the *synthesis* coefficients are modeled as complex normal random variables whose variances are governed by a low-rank WH structure. This shift from an analysis-based model to a synthesis-based one is the paper's core contribution. A key advantage of this formulation is that it naturally accommodates multi-resolution representations by allowing the signal to be synthesized from a combination of different time-frequency bases, a feature not readily available in standard STFT-based NMF. The authors derive two estimation procedures based on Expectation-Maximization (EM): one for Maximum Joint Likelihood Estimation (MJLE), which is computationally tractable, and another for Maximum Marginal Likelihood Estimation (MMLE), which is currently not scalable. The model's effectiveness is demonstrated on audio tasks, including music decomposition and speech enhancement.

**Strengths (Arguments for acceptance)**

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The conceptual shift from modeling analysis coefficients to modeling synthesis coefficients is novel and elegant. It provides a more fundamental generative model that operates directly on the raw signal, bridging a gap between synthesis-based signal models and low-rank factorization techniques like NMF.

2.  **Multi-Resolution Capability:** The most significant practical contribution is the model's inherent ability to handle multi-resolution analysis. By defining the signal as a sum of components synthesized from different time-frequency frames (e.g., one with high temporal resolution for transients, another with high frequency resolution for tonal parts), the model overcomes a major limitation of standard NMF methods that are tied to a single, fixed STFT resolution. This is a highly valuable feature for complex signals like audio.

3.  **Technical Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The probabilistic model is well-formulated, and the derivations of the EM algorithms are clear. The connection drawn to Sparse Bayesian Learning (SBL) is insightful and correctly positions the work within the broader machine learning literature. The authors are also commendably transparent about the scalability limitations of the MMLE approach.

4.  **Clarity:** The paper is very well-written and organized. The motivation is clearly stated, the model is introduced logically, and its relationship to prior work is well-explained. This makes the core ideas accessible despite their technical nature.

**Weaknesses (Arguments against acceptance / Questions for authors)**

1.  **Insufficient Ablation in Experiments:** The experiments effectively demonstrate the multi-resolution capabilities of the model. However, they do not sufficiently disentangle the benefits of the LRTFS framework itself from the benefits of using multiple resolutions. A crucial comparison is missing: the performance of single-resolution LRTFS against the standard single-resolution IS-NMF baseline. This would help answer the question of whether the synthesis model offers inherent advantages even when the resolution is fixed. The speech enhancement experiment shows a modest 0.6 dB improvement over IS-NMF, but the LRTFS method uses two resolutions while the baseline uses one, making it an unequal comparison.

2.  **Lack of Comparison on Computational Cost and Stability:** The draft raises some excellent questions that remain pertinent. How does the computational cost of the proposed MJLE algorithm compare to the standard multiplicative updates for IS-NMF in a single-resolution setting? Is the algorithm as stable? A discussion of convergence speed and computational complexity per iteration relative to the baseline would strengthen the paper's practical claims.

3.  **Ambiguity in Hyperparameter Setting:** The estimation of the residual variance `λ` is noted to be difficult in practice, and it is instead treated as a hyperparameter. In the speech enhancement experiment, it is "gradually decreased to a negligible value." This strategy, while pragmatic, could be described in more detail to improve reproducibility. Clarification on how `λ` was selected or scheduled would be beneficial.

**Detailed Comments and Suggestions**

To strengthen the paper, I would suggest the authors consider the following:

*   **Add a single-resolution experiment:** Please include a direct comparison of LRTFS against IS-NMF where both methods use the exact same single time-frequency representation (e.g., the 256-sample window used for the IS-NMF baseline). This would provide a clearer picture of the new model's performance and potentially highlight other advantages, such as the quality of the learned factors W and H. Do the learned dictionaries differ significantly from those learned by IS-NMF?

*   **Provide complexity analysis:** A brief analysis of the computational complexity of the MJLE algorithm, especially in relation to the highly efficient multiplicative updates for IS-NMF, would be a valuable addition.

Overall, this is an interesting and well-executed paper presenting a novel and promising framework. The multi-resolution extension is a significant strength. The primary weakness lies in an experimental evaluation that, while positive, does not fully isolate the contributions of the core modeling idea from the multi-resolution capability. Addressing the points above would substantially improve the paper.
