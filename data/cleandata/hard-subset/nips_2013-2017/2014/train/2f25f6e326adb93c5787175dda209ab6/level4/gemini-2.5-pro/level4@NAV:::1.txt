This work introduces an extension to low-rank models for spectrogram factorization by generalizing the latent factors across an arbitrary, and potentially overcomplete, dictionary of basis functions. This enables decomposition using multiresolution bases, such as those combining tonal and transient components, which the authors argue is beneficial for tasks like source separation and denoising. The proposed model builds upon the Gaussian Composite Model (GCM) by Fevotte et al., which represents the variance of each spectrogram bin via a low-rank matrix factorization. The key contribution is reformulating the implicit convolution associated with the spectrogram's Gabor impulse responses into an explicit time-domain expression. This reformulation permits the use of diverse, including non-orthogonal, sets of impulse responses. The paper provides an Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm to find a locally optimal solution.

Quality: The core insight of this paper is intriguing, though its full implications are not immediately apparent. While it can be viewed initially as a reinterpretation of Fevotte's GCM, the capacity to incorporate overcomplete dictionaries is a valuable addition. However, the use of overcomplete bases typically introduces challenges, and the paper does not fully clarify why these issues are not more problematic in this framework. The derivation of the estimation procedure is impressive; while I did not verify every step, I trust in its correctness.

Clarity: The paper's motivation and explanation of the method are clear. In contrast, the experimental evaluation is quite limited. The results consist of a single illustrative example and one SNR metric, which is insufficient to support any general conclusions. While the inclusion of audio examples is appreciated, their quality was not compelling. Specifically, the comparison to OMLSA may be inequitable, as the provided OMLSA example is significantly longer (approximately 7 seconds vs. 2 seconds) than the result from the proposed method. Furthermore, despite containing more residual noise, the speech in the OMLSA example possesses a subjectively higher perceptual quality. In the music denoising task, the proposed method introduces substantial processing artifacts. It is likely that standard techniques like Wiener filtering or wavelet shrinkage would yield superior perceived quality on this example.

Originality: This work is heavily dependent on the foundational model of Fevotte et al. Nevertheless, the novel time-domain interpretation offers a valuable new perspective on the problem.

Significance: The central idea of applying matrix factorization techniques to overcomplete bases has the potential for high impact. Unfortunately, the limited and superficial evaluation presented here fails to substantiate this potential. As a result, assessing the work as highly significant at this stage would be speculative. In summary, while the reformulation of probabilistic matrix factorization using time-domain kernels enables EM-based analysis for multiresolution bases, the work's importance is not demonstrated due to an insufficient evaluation and the presence of significant artifacts in the provided examples.
