Summary
This paper extends low-rank time–frequency decomposition by moving from an analysis model on STFT coefficients (the Gaussian Composite Model, GCM) to a synthesis model on the raw signal. Specifically, it imposes a low-rank structure (via WH) on the variances of complex synthesis coefficients α over a time–frequency dictionary Φ, rather than on the STFT (analysis) coefficients. This yields a generative model of the waveform x(t) itself, not only of its STFT, and naturally accommodates overcomplete and multi-resolution dictionaries (e.g., separate tonal and transient Gabor frames). The authors provide two estimation strategies: a practical EM algorithm for maximum joint likelihood estimation (MJLE) that alternates between Wiener-like shrinkage of α and IS-NMF updates for W,H; and a maximum marginal likelihood approach (MMLE), analogous to SBL “type-II” ML, which is elegant but computationally prohibitive at audio scale due to large matrix inversions. The formulation also connects to component reconstruction via Wiener filtering and to SBL, with the key difference that LRTFS ties coefficient variances through a low-rank WH rather than learning independent variances per coefficient.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The modeling move from analysis to synthesis is well-motivated, and the EM derivation for MJLE appears careful. The use of an EM “plug-in” architecture to avoid large inversions is a practical contribution. The connection to IS divergence via the Gaussian model is consistent with prior work.
- Scalability: The MMLE route is acknowledged to be impractical for typical audio signals, which limits the empirical comparison between MJLE and MMLE. The MJLE algorithm is computationally friendly (relying on analysis/synthesis operations and IS-NMF updates), which is a strength.
- Potential complications: Using overcomplete, nonorthogonal dictionaries can introduce identifiability and scaling ambiguities between α and W,H, as well as heavier local-minima issues. The paper does not fully discuss how these are mitigated (e.g., normalization of Φ, W columns, constraints on H, or regularization beyond IS-NMF). The condition β ≤ λ/δΦ is stated; guidance on choosing β and its impact on convergence and estimation quality would help.
- Clarity of derivations: I could not follow every detail of the estimation derivation; an appendix with more explicit steps, assumptions, and convergence guarantees (or lack thereof) would be welcome. Similarly, practical choices (initialization, stopping, normalization of frames, handling of Hermitian symmetry in the real case) should be specified.
- Experiments: The evaluation is very limited. One illustrative hybrid music example and a single SNR-improvement figure for speech enhancement are not sufficient to support strong claims. The supplementary audio was useful, but in my listening the proposed method introduced noticeable artifacts. The OMLSA comparison audio appears longer than the LRTFS result; please ensure same segments, time alignment, and identical pre/post-processing. Moreover, although OMLSA left more background noise, the speech quality seemed higher. For the music denoising, I would expect baseline methods like wavelet shrinkage or simple Wiener filtering to deliver higher perceptual quality; these should be included for a fair comparison.

Clarity
The paper is generally well written and the motivation is clear. The distinction between analysis (STFT) and synthesis coefficients, and the move of the low-rank prior from the former to the latter, is explained well and is thought-provoking. The multi-resolution extension is straightforward and nicely presented. To improve clarity:
- Provide algorithm pseudocode and explicit complexity per iteration and per frame.
- Clarify the role of λ: although update rules are given, in practice it is treated as a hyperparameter or annealed; discuss sensitivity and how to set it.
- Expand on the SBL connection, especially on how tying variances via WH affects sparsity and what that implies for interpretability of α.

Originality
The work builds directly on the GCM/IS-NMF line of research but contributes a distinct synthesis perspective that enables multi-resolution, overcomplete dictionaries. The explicit generative model for the waveform, the EM architecture for MJLE using IS-NMF subproblems, and the relation to SBL with tied variances provide a new angle. Conceptually, this reframing made me think about GCM in a new way.

Significance
The ability to combine low-rank factor models with overcomplete, multi-resolution synthesis dictionaries could be impactful for audio decomposition, denoising, and source separation. However, the current empirical evidence is too thin to substantiate the claimed benefits. To demonstrate significance, I recommend:
- Evaluation on larger benchmarks with multiple signals, reporting standard metrics (e.g., SDR/SIR/SAR, PESQ/STOI) and statistical variability.
- Ablations: single-resolution vs multi-resolution; overcomplete vs complete frames; effect of K; influence of λ and β; MJLE variants; impact of pretraining W vs learning W.
- Baselines beyond OMLSA and single-resolution IS-NMF: wavelet shrinkage, Wiener filtering, supervised/semi-supervised NMF variants, PLCA, and other relevant methods.
- Runtime and memory comparisons.

Pros for acceptance
- Conceptually clean synthesis reformulation that bridges NMF/GCM with overcomplete, multi-resolution dictionaries.
- Practical EM algorithm (MJLE) leveraging IS-NMF subproblems, avoiding large inversions.
- Clear link to component-wise reconstruction and to SBL with tied variances.

Cons for acceptance
- Very limited and partially inconclusive empirical validation; audible artifacts; fairness concerns in comparisons.
- MMLE path is not scalable; no empirical comparison between MJLE and MMLE.
- Insufficient discussion of identifiability, normalization, and convergence in overcomplete settings.

Overall
Reformulating probabilistic matrix factorization of spectrograms in the time-domain synthesis setting is a valuable idea, and the multiresolution extension is appealing. At present, though, the experimental section is too thin and the perceptual quality not convincing enough to substantiate impact. Strengthening the evaluation as suggested above would significantly improve the case for this paper.
