This paper presents an extension to low-rank spectrogram-factorization models for sound decomposition, generalizing latent factors over an arbitrary (potentially overcomplete) set of bases, enabling decomposition with "multiresolution" (e.g., tonal + transient) bases. This approach is motivated by potential improvements in source separation and denoising. The work builds upon the Gaussian Composite Model (GCM) of Févotte et al., which models the variance of spectrogram cells with a low-rank matrix factorization, and expands this into a time-domain expression for the estimated quantities, allowing for flexibility in the choice of bases, including non-orthogonal ones. An Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm, though potentially converging to a local optimum, is presented for parameter estimation.

Quality: This paper presents a compelling and insightful extension of existing work in low-rank time-frequency analysis. While the connection to Févotte et al.’s GCM is clear, the authors demonstrate a significant step forward by extending the framework to accommodate overcomplete bases. This generalization has the potential to address limitations of traditional methods. The theoretical development and proposed estimation procedure appear sound, and the authors demonstrate a strong understanding of the underlying mathematical principles. While a detailed verification of the estimation procedure was beyond my capacity, the presented derivation appears logically consistent.

Clarity: The paper is well-written and clearly motivated, effectively contextualizing the proposed method within the existing literature. However, the empirical evaluation is limited. The illustrative example, while helpful for understanding the concept, lacks sufficient rigor. The SNR improvement measurement in the speech enhancement experiment, based on a single example, provides limited evidence of the method's effectiveness. The inclusion of audio examples is appreciated, but their impact is diminished by the superficial evaluation. The comparison to OMLSA is potentially problematic due to differing signal lengths and perceived audio quality, with OMLSA yielding seemingly superior results. The music decomposition example suffers from noticeable artifacts, raising concerns about the practical impact of the proposed method compared to established techniques like wavelet shrinkage or Wiener filtering. 

Originality: The work builds directly upon the foundation laid by Févotte et al., but the time-domain interpretation and the ability to leverage overcomplete bases represent a novel contribution. The formulation of the probabilistic matrix-factorization framework in terms of time-domain kernels and the resulting EM analysis for multiresolution bases are original and potentially impactful.

Significance: The ability to apply matrix-factorization techniques to overcomplete bases holds significant promise for improving sound decomposition and related tasks. However, the current evaluation is insufficient to fully demonstrate this potential. The lack of comprehensive experiments and the presence of artifacts in the provided examples necessitate a more thorough investigation of the method's performance. The reformulation of probabilistic matrix-factorization sound analysis, enabling EM analysis for multiresolution (overcomplete) bases, is a valuable contribution. However, a more robust evaluation and mitigation of the observed artifacts are crucial to establishing the method’s practical significance.

Arguments for acceptance:
* Novel extension of existing low-rank modeling techniques.
* Theoretically sound and well-motivated.
* Potential to address limitations of traditional methods by leveraging overcomplete bases.
* Clear and well-written presentation.

Arguments against acceptance:
* Limited and superficial empirical evaluation.
* Artifacts observed in example results raise concerns about practical performance.
* Comparison to existing methods (e.g., OMLSA) requires careful consideration of experimental setup.
* Lack of comprehensive experimentation to demonstrate significant improvements over established techniques.
