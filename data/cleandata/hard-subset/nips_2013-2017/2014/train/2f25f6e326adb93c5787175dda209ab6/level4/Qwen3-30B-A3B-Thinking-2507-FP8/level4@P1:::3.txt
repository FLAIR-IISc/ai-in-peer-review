

This paper introduces a novel generative model called Low-Rank Time-Frequency Synthesis (LRTFS) that bridges a significant gap in the signal processing literature. While previous work such as the Gaussian Composite Model (GCM) [3] provided a probabilistic framework for the STFT coefficients, LRTFS directly models the raw time-domain signal, offering a more principled generative approach. The authors correctly identify that existing NMF-based methods operate on magnitude spectrograms and treat the STFT as a black box, whereas their model explicitly connects the time-domain signal to its low-rank time-frequency structure. This represents a meaningful extension of the GCM framework, which itself was a significant step beyond empirical NMF approaches by providing a valid generative model for the STFT.

The paper is technically sound and well-structured, with clear theoretical development that builds logically on prior work. The authors establish a solid connection to sparse Bayesian learning (SBL) [4,5], highlighting both the similarities and critical differences between LRTFS and SBL, particularly regarding the structured variance parameters versus the unstructured ones in SBL. The theoretical contributions are substantial, with the derivation of two estimation approaches (MJLE and MMLE) and the extension to multi-resolution representations being particularly noteworthy. The paper also correctly identifies the limitations of MMLE for large-scale audio applications, which is a thoughtful and honest assessment of the method's practical applicability.

The clarity of the paper is excellent. The authors present complex mathematical concepts in a well-organized manner, with appropriate notation and clear explanations of the model's components. The writing is accessible to researchers in audio signal processing and machine learning, with sufficient detail for experts to understand and potentially reproduce the results. The figures and supplementary material would further aid in understanding the model's practical implementation.

Regarding originality, the paper makes a clear and significant contribution to the field. While NMF-based approaches for audio processing are well-established [1,2,3], the shift from modeling the STFT to modeling the raw signal itself represents a non-trivial conceptual advance. The multi-resolution extension, which allows for different temporal resolutions in different signal components, is particularly novel and addresses a limitation of traditional NMF approaches that cannot adapt time resolution during decomposition. This is well-documented in the literature [8,9,10] and represents a meaningful contribution to the field.

The significance of the work is somewhat limited by the experimental evaluation. While the paper presents two audio applications (music decomposition and speech enhancement), the results are not sufficiently comparative. The authors do not adequately benchmark their method against state-of-the-art approaches in either application domain, nor do they provide sufficient quantitative comparison to the methods they build upon (e.g., IS-NMF). The supplemental material appears to contain limited results that do not convincingly demonstrate the superiority of their approach. The paper would have been strengthened by more comprehensive experiments, including comparisons with relevant baselines such as the hybrid decomposition approach in [8,10] and standard speech enhancement methods like OMLSA [14].

The theoretical contribution is strong enough to stand on its own, and the paper makes a valuable contribution to the theoretical understanding of time-frequency representations. The MJLE algorithm is well-motivated and implemented efficiently without requiring matrix inversions, which is particularly important for practical audio applications. However, the lack of sufficient experimental validation diminishes the impact of the theoretical advances.

In summary, this paper makes a solid theoretical contribution to the field of time-frequency analysis with a novel generative model that directly operates on the raw signal. The quality of the theoretical development is high, and the paper is clearly written. The originality is significant, though the significance is somewhat limited by the insufficient experimental evaluation. The authors should address the lack of comparative results in future work to better demonstrate the practical value of their approach.

The strengths of this paper are its strong theoretical foundation, clear exposition, and novel multi-resolution capabilities. The weaknesses are the limited experimental validation and lack of comprehensive comparison with relevant baselines. Given the strong theoretical contribution and clear novelty, this paper would be a good fit for NIPS, but the authors should address the experimental shortcomings in a revised version to maximize the paper's impact.
