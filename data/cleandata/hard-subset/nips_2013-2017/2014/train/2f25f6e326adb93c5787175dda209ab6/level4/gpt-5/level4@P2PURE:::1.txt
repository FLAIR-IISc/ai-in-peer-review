This paper introduces an extension to the family of low-rank spectrogram-factorization models for sound decomposition that generalizes the latent factors to an arbitrary (potentially overcomplete) set of bases. This enables decomposition on multiresolution bases (e.g., tonal + transient), which is argued to improve source separation and denoising. Starting from the Gaussian Composite Model (GCM) of Févotte et al.—which models the variance of each spectrogram cell via low-rank matrix factorization—the paper makes explicit the implicit convolution with multiple Gabor impulse responses corresponding to each spectrogram row, yielding a purely time-domain formulation of the estimator. This formulation can then be adapted to other sets of impulse responses, including nonorthogonal ones. An EM algorithm (converging to a local optimum) is presented.

Quality: I find the core idea interesting, though I am still working out its full implications. At first glance it appears to be another way to understand Févotte’s GCM, but the ability to incorporate overcomplete dictionaries is appealing. However, this flexibility seems likely to introduce complications, and it was not immediately clear to me why these do not pose greater problems. I assume the estimation procedure is correct—and I am impressed by this kind of analysis—although I could not follow all the details.

Clarity: The exposition and motivation are generally clear. The evaluation, however, is very superficial: there is one purely illustrative example and a single SNR improvement measurement, which is insufficient to support firm conclusions. I appreciated the included audio examples, but they were not especially convincing. The OMLSA-enhanced example appears much longer (about 7 s vs. 2 s) than the new result, raising concerns about whether the comparison is fair. Moreover, although OMLSA includes more background noise (in part because it is longer), the speech itself sounds of higher quality. In the music example, the denoising introduces very noticeable artifacts; I would expect conventional wavelet shrinkage or Wiener filtering to yield higher perceived quality in this case.

Originality: The work leans heavily on the foundation of Févotte et al., but the time-domain interpretation did prompt me to think about the model in a new way.

Significance: On the face of it, enabling matrix-factorization techniques for overcomplete bases could be impactful. However, the examples and evaluation are too limited and superficial to substantiate this potential; at present, rating the work as substantially significant would be an act of faith. Reformulating probabilistic matrix-factorization sound analysis in terms of time-domain kernels enables an EM analysis for multiresolution (overcomplete) bases, but the thin evaluation and the significant artifacts in the examples fail to establish its importance in its current form.
