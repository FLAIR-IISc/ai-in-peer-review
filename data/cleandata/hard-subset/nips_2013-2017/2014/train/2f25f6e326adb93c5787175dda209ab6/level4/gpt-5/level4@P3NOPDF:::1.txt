Summary
This paper extends low-rank spectrogram-factorization models by allowing the latent factors to act over an arbitrary, potentially overcomplete, set of time-domain kernels. Starting from the Gaussian Composite Model (GCM) of Fevotte et al., which models spectrogram-cell variances via low-rank matrix factorization, the authors derive an explicit time-domain formulation that interprets the spectrogram modeling as a convolution with multiple impulse responses (e.g., Gabor atoms). This reformulation enables an EM algorithm to handle multiresolution and nonorthogonal bases, with the aim of improving source separation and denoising.

Strengths
- Conceptual contribution: The time-domain reinterpretation of GCM is insightful and provides a unifying perspective that naturally accommodates multiresolution and overcomplete dictionaries.
- Generality: Allowing nonorthogonal impulse responses broadens applicability beyond fixed STFT-like settings to tonal/transient mixtures and other structured bases.
- Methodology: The EM derivation appears carefully constructed and, if correct, is a valuable technical contribution.

Weaknesses
- Evaluation is too limited to support the claims. The paper presents a single illustrative example and one SNR improvement, which are insufficient to establish clear benefits over strong baselines.
- Audio demonstrations raise concerns about fairness and quality. The OMLSA-enhanced sample is longer than the proposed method’s sample, complicating comparisons, and the perceived speech quality seems higher for OMLSA despite more background noise. The music example exhibits noticeable artifacts; conventional wavelet shrinkage or Wiener filtering may yield better perceptual quality in that scenario.
- Limited discussion of complications arising from overcompleteness and nonorthogonality. There is little analysis of identifiability, stability, regularization needs (e.g., sparsity or smoothness), or sensitivity to initialization when dictionaries are highly coherent.
- Missing or weak baselines. There is no thorough comparison against standard NMF/PLCA on STFT spectrograms, convolutional NMF, sparse coding/K-SVD, or conventional MMSE/Wiener/OMLSA approaches under matched conditions.
- Practical considerations are underdeveloped. The paper does not adequately discuss computational complexity, memory demands, and scalability for large dictionaries or long signals.

Quality
The technical insight is promising, and the EM framework is likely sound, although the derivation is dense and difficult to verify in detail from the current exposition. The experimental evidence, however, is not commensurate with the ambition of the claims. In particular, the examples do not convincingly demonstrate that the proposed generalization provides a consistent advantage over well-established methods, and the presence of audible artifacts suggests the need for additional constraints or post-processing.

Clarity
The motivation and high-level idea are clearly presented. The derivations are compact and could benefit from more guidance (e.g., a schematic linking spectrogram modeling to time-domain convolutions, precise conditions under which the model reduces to GCM, and a short pseudocode summary of EM updates). The evaluation section needs a clearer, standardized protocol to ensure fair and reproducible comparisons.

Originality
While the work builds directly on Fevotte et al.’s GCM, the time-domain reinterpretation and its use to accommodate arbitrary, potentially overcomplete, impulse-response sets is a fresh and thought-provoking angle. Positioning the contribution relative to convolutional NMF and time-domain factorization approaches would further clarify novelty.

Significance
In principle, extending probabilistic matrix factorization to multiresolution and overcomplete bases could have substantial impact on separation and denoising. However, the current empirical evidence is too thin to substantiate this potential, and the audible artifacts temper the practical significance as presented. With a stronger and more systematic evaluation, the contribution could become more compelling.

Questions for the authors
- How are the impulse-response sets chosen or learned? Are they fixed (e.g., Gabor, wavelets) or adapted from data? How sensitive are results to this choice?
- What regularization is used to mitigate issues from overcompleteness (e.g., sparsity penalties, temporal smoothness, group structure)? How does it affect artifacts?
- Does the proposed EM reduce exactly to the GCM when the impulse responses correspond to a standard STFT basis? An explicit proof or empirical equivalence would help.
- What are the per-iteration time and memory costs, and what optimizations (e.g., FFT-based convolution, batching) are employed?
- How are hyperparameters (number of components, kernel redundancy, noise parameters) selected?

Suggestions for improvement
- Substantially expand the evaluation with multiple datasets and tasks (speech denoising, music separation, mixtures with transients/tonals), and report standard metrics (e.g., SDR/SIR/SAR, SI-SDR, PESQ, STOI) alongside SNR.
- Ensure matched and fair comparisons: same input segments, durations, target SNRs, and evaluation windows for all methods, including OMLSA, Wiener filtering, wavelet shrinkage, standard NMF/PLCA, and convolutional NMF baselines.
- Provide ablations isolating the benefit of overcompleteness and multiresolution: orthogonal vs overcomplete dictionaries, varying redundancy, and analysis of identifiability and stability.
- Address artifacts by exploring appropriate priors/constraints and by analyzing failure modes. A small perceptual listening study (e.g., MOS) would strengthen claims about audio quality.
- Clarify derivations with intermediate steps, algorithm pseudocode, and a brief discussion of convergence properties and initialization strategies. Share code and audio with matched conditions.

Overall assessment
The paper offers a compelling conceptual extension of GCM to arbitrary, potentially overcomplete, time-domain kernels and a corresponding EM algorithm. Unfortunately, the current empirical validation is too limited and, in places, unfavorable in perceived quality to established baselines. I see promise in the idea, but as presented I cannot recommend acceptance. With a more rigorous and comprehensive evaluation and clearer treatment of overcomplete-model challenges, this work could have meaningful impact.
