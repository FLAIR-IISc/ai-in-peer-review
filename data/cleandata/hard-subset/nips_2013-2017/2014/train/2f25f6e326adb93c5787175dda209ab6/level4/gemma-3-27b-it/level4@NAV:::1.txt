This paper introduces an extension to low-rank spectrogram factorization models for sound decomposition, generalizing latent factors using an arbitrary, potentially overcomplete, set of bases – enabling decomposition on “multiresolution” bases like tonal and transient components. The authors posit that this approach will benefit source separation and denoising. Starting from the Gaussian Composite Model (GCM) of Fevotte et al., which utilizes low-rank matrix factorization to model spectrogram cell variances, the work expands the implicit convolution with multiple Gabor impulse responses to derive a purely time-domain expression for the estimated parameters. This reformulation facilitates adaptation to various impulse response sets, including non-orthogonal ones, and is accompanied by an Expectation-Maximization (EM) procedure for analysis (though susceptible to local optima).

Quality: This work presents an intriguing perspective, though the full implications remain somewhat unclear. While initially appearing as a reinterpretation of Fevotte’s GCM, the inclusion of overcomplete dictionaries is a valuable addition. However, this extension seems potentially complex, and the paper doesn't fully elucidate why anticipated difficulties were mitigated. The estimation procedure appears sound, and its analysis is impressive, though the detailed steps were difficult to follow.

Clarity: The paper is well-written and clearly motivated. However, the evaluation is limited; a single illustrative result and one SNR improvement measurement are insufficient to draw firm conclusions. The included audio examples are appreciated, but ultimately unconvincing. The comparison between the proposed method and OMLSA is questionable, as the OMLSA example is substantially longer (7 seconds versus 2 seconds), potentially affecting the fairness of the comparison. Furthermore, despite containing more background noise due to its length, the OMLSA result yields demonstrably higher speech quality. The music denoising example introduces noticeable artifacts, and one would expect superior perceptual quality from standard techniques like wavelet shrinkage or Wiener filtering.

Originality: The work builds heavily upon the foundation laid by Fevotte et al., although the time-domain interpretation offers a novel perspective.

Significance: The potential for applying matrix-factorization techniques to overcomplete bases is promising. However, the limited and superficial evaluation prevents a strong assessment of its impact. Currently, evaluating its substantial significance requires a degree of faith. The reformulation of probabilistic matrix-factorization for sound analysis in terms of time-domain kernels enables EM analysis with multiresolution (overcomplete) bases, but the thin evaluation and prominent artifacts in the examples currently preclude a clear demonstration of its importance.
