

The paper introduces a novel extension to non-negative matrix factorization (NMF) by redefining the generative model from time-frequency (TF) analysis coefficients to TF synthesis coefficients. This shift fundamentally enables multi-resolution signal representations, a capability absent in conventional NMF frameworks that operate on analysis coefficients. The core contribution—modeling the time-domain signal directly through synthesis coefficients—represents a significant conceptual advancement with clear potential for applications requiring adaptive resolution. The proposed approach is particularly compelling for its ability to decompose signals across multiple resolutions without requiring additional post-processing or specialized architectures, which is a notable limitation of existing methods.

The authors demonstrate the utility of their framework through empirical results on multi-resolution signal decompositions, which effectively validate the theoretical promise. The visualizations of multi-resolution decompositions are convincing and illustrate how the method captures both fine-scale and coarse-scale structures coherently. This aspect aligns well with growing interest in hierarchical representations in audio and signal processing, making the work relevant to broader trends in the field.

However, the paper would benefit from a more thorough evaluation of the method’s performance in the single-resolution setting, which serves as a critical baseline for comparison. Specifically, the authors should address whether the proposed algorithm recovers factorizations (W and H) comparable to those of Itakura-Saito NMF (IS-NMF) when applied to single-resolution tasks. IS-NMF is a well-established benchmark for time-frequency modeling, and demonstrating equivalence or clear advantages in this simpler setting would strengthen the paper’s credibility. For instance, the authors could present quantitative metrics (e.g., reconstruction error, divergence) or qualitative comparisons of the learned bases and activations between the two methods. Without this, the claim that the framework "opens doors" to new capabilities risks appearing speculative rather than empirically grounded.

Additionally, the computational efficiency and numerical stability of the proposed algorithm require clarification. The multiplicative update rules for IS-NMF are renowned for their simplicity and robustness, which contribute significantly to their widespread adoption. The authors should explicitly compare the convergence speed, computational complexity, and stability of their method against IS-NMF’s updates. Questions to address include: Does the algorithm converge in fewer iterations? Is it sensitive to initialization? How does it scale with signal length or dictionary size? A brief ablation study or runtime analysis would be highly valuable here, as these factors are crucial for practical adoption.

The paper’s presentation could also be tightened. The current description of the generative model occasionally conflates synthesis and analysis coefficients, leading to minor confusion. A clearer formal definition of the synthesis-based generative process (e.g., explicitly stating the role of the inverse TF transform) would improve accessibility. Furthermore, while the multi-resolution results are promising, the discussion of *why* synthesis coefficients enable this capability is somewhat superficial. A more detailed explanation of the mathematical or structural properties (e.g., how synthesis coefficients inherently encode temporal coherence across scales) would deepen the theoretical contribution.

Despite these points, the paper’s core idea is strong and addresses a genuine gap in NMF literature. The multi-resolution extension is not merely an incremental tweak but a paradigm shift that could inspire new applications in audio source separation, biomedical signal analysis, or multi-scale feature extraction. The authors have laid a solid foundation, and addressing the above points would elevate the work from promising to definitive.

In summary, this is a well-motivated paper with a compelling technical contribution. The multi-resolution capability is a significant advance, and the empirical results support the approach. To maximize impact, the authors should: (1) provide a direct comparison with IS-NMF in single-resolution tasks, including factorization quality and computational metrics; (2) clarify the algorithm’s efficiency and stability relative to established methods; and (3) refine the theoretical exposition to emphasize the synthesis-coefficient advantage. If these points are addressed, the paper would be a strong fit for NIPS. As it stands, it is a valuable contribution that merits publication with minor revisions.
