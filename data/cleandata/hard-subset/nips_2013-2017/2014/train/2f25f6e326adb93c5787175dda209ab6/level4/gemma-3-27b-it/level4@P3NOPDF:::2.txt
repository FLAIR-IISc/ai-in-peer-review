This paper presents a novel Non-negative Matrix Factorization (NMF) framework that shifts the generative model from the time-frequency (TF) analysis coefficients to the time-domain signal itself, by modeling the TF synthesis coefficients. This key distinction enables the development of multi-resolution signal representations, a capability not readily available within the traditional NMF paradigm. The core idea is compelling and represents a potentially significant extension to NMF.

The strength of this work lies in its theoretical foundation and the potential for novel applications unlocked by the multi-resolution decomposition. The authors clearly articulate the motivation for this alternative generative modeling approach and demonstrate its applicability. I particularly appreciate the exploration of multi-resolution representations; this extension is a valuable contribution.

However, the paper would benefit from a more thorough empirical evaluation. While the results demonstrate the method’s capability in multi-resolution scenarios, a comparative analysis against existing single-resolution NMF techniques, specifically Iterative Shrinkage-NMF (IS-NMF), is crucial. Specifically, it would be insightful to understand how the learned weight (W) and height (H) matrices compare between the proposed method and IS-NMF when applied to the same data at a single resolution. Furthermore, a discussion of computational efficiency and stability is warranted. Does the proposed algorithm exhibit similar performance characteristics – speed and convergence – to the well-established multiplicative update rules used in IS-NMF? Addressing these questions would provide a more comprehensive understanding of the method’s strengths and weaknesses relative to existing approaches.

Repetition of the core concept – shifting the generative model to the time domain for multi-resolution analysis – appears twice in the review draft and also within the paper itself (as I recall). While emphasizing the novelty is important, this repetition could be streamlined for greater conciseness. 

Overall, this is an interesting and potentially impactful contribution to the field of NMF. The concept of generating multi-resolution representations through a novel generative model is a significant advancement. Addressing the questions regarding single-resolution performance and computational efficiency would significantly strengthen the paper and further solidify its contribution. I recommend considering these points for revision.
