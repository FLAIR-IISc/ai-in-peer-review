This paper presents an extension in the family of low-rank spectrogram-factorization models of sound decomposition that generalizes the latent factors over an arbitrary (potentially overcomplete) set of bases, meaning that decomposition can be performed on "multiresolution" (e.g., tonal + transient) bases. This is argued to help with source separation and denoising. It starts from the Gaussian Composite Model of Fevotte et al, which models the variance of each cell of a spectrogram with a low-rank matrix factorization, but then expands the implicit convolution with the multiple Gabor impulse responses corresponding to each row of the spectrogram to give a purely time-domain expression for what is being estimated, that can then be modified to work for different sets of impulse responses, including non-orthogonal ones. An EM (local optimum) analysis procedure is presented.

Quality: The paper presents an interesting insight, although it may benefit from a more detailed explanation of its implications. The ability to include overcomplete dictionaries is a significant advantage. However, it is not entirely clear why the complications arising from this approach do not cause more trouble. The estimation procedure appears to be correct, and the analysis is impressive, although some parts may be difficult to follow in detail.

Clarity: The work is well-explained and motivated, making it easy to understand the underlying concepts. However, the evaluation could be more comprehensive. The provided results, including one purely illustrative example and one SNR improvement measurement, are not sufficient to draw conclusive results. The inclusion of sound examples is appreciated, but the comparison between the proposed method and other approaches, such as OMLSA, may not be entirely fair. The denoising of the music example comes at the expense of significant artifacts, which may be perceived as lower quality than conventional methods.

Originality: The work relies heavily on the Fevotte et al foundation, although the time-domain interpretation offers a new perspective on the existing model.

Significance: The ability to apply matrix-factorization techniques to overcomplete bases could have a significant impact. However, the presented examples and evaluation are too limited to demonstrate this significance. A more comprehensive evaluation, including multiple examples and comparisons with other approaches, would be necessary to fully appreciate the importance of this work.

Arguments pro acceptance:

1. The paper presents a novel extension to the existing Gaussian Composite Model, allowing for the use of overcomplete bases.
2. The proposed EM analysis procedure is a significant contribution to the field.
3. The work has the potential to impact source separation and denoising applications.

Arguments con acceptance:

1. The evaluation is limited, and the provided results are not sufficient to draw conclusive results.
2. The comparison between the proposed method and other approaches may not be entirely fair.
3. The denoising of the music example comes at the expense of significant artifacts, which may be perceived as lower quality than conventional methods.
