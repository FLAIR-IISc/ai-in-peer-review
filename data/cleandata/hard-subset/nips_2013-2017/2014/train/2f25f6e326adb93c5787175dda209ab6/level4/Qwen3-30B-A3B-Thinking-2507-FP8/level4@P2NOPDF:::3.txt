

This paper offers a novel perspective on time-frequency factorizations, departing from conventional approaches that rely on STFT analysis coefficients. The authors propose a more flexible framework applicable to arbitrary transforms, with explicit connections to the time-domain signal reconstruction. The exposition is clear and accessible to researchers in the field, and the theoretical development is sound and well-executed.  

The originality of the approach is above average, though the paper does not sufficiently justify its broader significance or practical impact. The most substantial limitation lies in the experimental evaluation. The results presented lack compelling evidence of performance gains, and the supplemental material fails to provide convincing comparative analysis. Crucially, the paper does not benchmark its method against foundational techniques it extends or builds upon, leaving key questions about its relative advantages unanswered.  

While the absence of thorough empirical validation is disappointing, it does not undermine the paper's core theoretical contribution. The framework itself is mathematically rigorous and conceptually significant, providing a valuable alternative to existing formulations. The strong theoretical foundation alone suffices to make this an interesting contribution to the field. However, the omission of comparative results represents a missed opportunity to strengthen the paper's impact.  

Overall, this is a well-written paper with solid theoretical grounding. The proposed framework advances the understanding of time-frequency decompositions and offers a useful generalization beyond standard STFT-based methods. To elevate the work further, the authors should address the evaluation gap in future revisions by including meaningful comparisons with relevant baseline approaches and demonstrating tangible benefits in practical scenarios. As it stands, the paper presents a noteworthy theoretical advance, but the lack of empirical support limits its immediate applicability and broader appeal. The contribution is valuable, yet the current evaluation falls short of fully realizing its potential.
