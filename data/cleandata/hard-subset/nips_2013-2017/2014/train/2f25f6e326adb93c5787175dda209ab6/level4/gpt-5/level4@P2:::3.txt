This paper proposes Low-Rank Time-Frequency Synthesis (LRTFS), a generative model that places a low-rank prior directly on synthesis coefficients of a time–frequency representation, thereby modeling the raw time-domain signal rather than only its STFT. In contrast to prior work such as IS-NMF on magnitude spectrograms and the Gaussian Composite Model (GCM), which imposes low-rank structure on the variance of analysis coefficients, LRTFS ties the variances of synthesis coefficients through a WH factorization. The authors derive two estimation strategies: a scalable EM algorithm for maximum joint likelihood (MJLE) that avoids large matrix inversions, and a maximum marginal likelihood (MMLE) approach in the spirit of sparse Bayesian learning (SBL), which is conceptually appealing but currently not scalable for long signals. A notable strength of the formulation is that it naturally enables multi-resolution representations by combining multiple time–frequency frames (e.g., short/long windows) within the same generative framework. The paper demonstrates applications to hybrid music decomposition and semi-supervised speech enhancement.

Quality: The technical development is solid and carefully executed. The probabilistic formulation is clear, the connection to IS divergence and Wiener filtering is well motivated, and the EM derivations are sound. The MJLE algorithm is computationally practical for typical audio problems because it leverages fast analysis/synthesis operators rather than explicit inversions. The MMLE derivation is correct, though the current implementation does not scale.

Clarity: The paper is clearly written and well organized. The relationship to NMF, GCM, and SBL is articulated, and the roles of synthesis vs. analysis coefficients are explained. The multi-resolution extension is straightforward and nicely presented.

Originality: The shift from analysis-domain low-rank modeling (GCM/IS-NMF) to synthesis-domain low-rank modeling is a meaningful conceptual contribution. The unification with EM/Wiener-style updates and the natural accommodation of multiple resolutions distinguish this work from prior NMF-based approaches.

Significance: The proposed framework has the potential to influence signal decomposition methods in audio and beyond, particularly where time-domain generative modeling and multi-resolution structure are advantageous. However, the empirical section underplays this potential. The current evaluations are limited in scope and depth, making it harder to assess practical gains over established baselines on standard tasks.

Strengths
- Clear, principled generative model that bridges NMF/GCM and synthesis modeling.
- Convincing theoretical grounding; elegant EM algorithm with practical complexity.
- Natural support for multi-resolution expansions, difficult to achieve in standard NMF settings.
- Useful connections to SBL and Wiener filtering that clarify the role of priors and updates.

Weaknesses
- Empirical evaluation is limited. Comparisons to strong baselines are sparse and not comprehensive, particularly for music decomposition.
- The significance of modeling in the synthesis domain is not fully quantified; ablations isolating the benefits over GCM/IS-NMF are missing.
- MMLE, while appealing, is not scalable in the current form, and no approximate large-scale alternative is provided.
- Sensitivity to the noise parameter λ (treated as a hyperparameter) is not systematically analyzed.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide broader quantitative comparisons to GCM/IS-NMF and other strong baselines on common datasets, including metrics beyond SNR (e.g., SDR/SIR/SAR, PESQ/STOI for speech).
- Include ablations: single-resolution vs. multi-resolution; synthesis vs. analysis-domain modeling; effect of K; effect of λ and warm-start strategy.
- Report computational costs and convergence diagnostics to substantiate scalability claims.
- Discuss or prototype scalable approximations for MMLE (e.g., iterative solvers, matrix-free methods, or message passing).
- Clarify when and why the synthesis-domain model yields tangible gains, with concrete case studies.

Arguments for acceptance
- Solid and original theoretical contribution with a clear link to established models.
- Practical algorithm that can be adopted by the community.
- Clean and extensible framework enabling multi-resolution modeling.

Arguments against acceptance
- Experimental evidence is not yet strong enough to demonstrate consistent, significant advantages across tasks.
- Limited discussion of scalability for MMLE and of sensitivity to hyperparameters.

Overall assessment: A well-written and technically strong paper that advances low-rank modeling by moving to the synthesis domain and enabling multi-resolution representations. While the evaluation could be more thorough, the theoretical contribution alone is compelling. I recommend acceptance, with encouragement to strengthen the empirical section and expand comparisons.
