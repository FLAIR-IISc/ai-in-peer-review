

The paper introduces Low-Rank Time-Frequency Synthesis (LRTFS), extending Fevotte et al.'s Gaussian Composite Model (GCM) from a generative model of the STFT to a generative model of the raw signal. This bridges a key gap in signal processing literature, where prior work like NMF-based spectrogram factorization (e.g., Smaragdis & Brown, 2003; Fevotte et al., 2009) operated only on transformed data. LRTFS reformulates the GCM in the time domain, enabling explicit synthesis via nonnegative low-rank variance constraints on synthesis coefficients, and naturally supports multi-resolution decompositions (e.g., tonal/transient layers) not feasible in traditional NMF. The core contribution is the time-domain interpretation of the GCM, which allows arbitrary (overcomplete) time-frequency bases like Gabor frames, while the EM-based estimation procedure for maximum joint likelihood (MJLE) offers a computationally efficient algorithm.

Strengths include a theoretically sound extension of the GCM to raw signal synthesis, which is a clear conceptual advance over prior work. The multi-resolution capability is compelling for audio applications (e.g., separating transients from sustained tones in music), and the EM algorithm avoids large matrix inversions, making it scalable for audio processing. The derivation of the time-domain model (Section 2.1) is novel, converting implicit convolutions into explicit impulse-response expressions, and the connection to sparse Bayesian learning (Section 2.2) provides useful context.

However, the paper has significant weaknesses in evaluation and clarity. The empirical validation is severely limited: only one music decomposition example (Fig. 1) and a single speech enhancement SNR measurement (6.6 dB vs. 6 dB baseline) are provided. The music example shows noticeable artifacts compared to conventional methods (e.g., in the piano/hit-hat separation), undermining claims of practical superiority. The speech enhancement comparison with OMLSA (4.6 dB) and IS-NMF (6 dB) is potentially unfair due to differing audio lengths (7s applause vs. 10s speech), and the paper does not justify why the baseline’s higher perceived speech quality (as noted by the reviewer) is not quantified. Crucially, the evaluation lacks statistical significance testing, ablation studies, or comparisons to more recent baselines (e.g., deep learning methods emerging in 2013), making it impossible to assess the method’s true impact.

The handling of overcomplete bases is a major concern. The paper claims multi-resolution modeling "with no extra difficulty," but overcomplete bases (e.g., Gabor frames with short/long windows) inherently risk rank explosion and numerical instability. The paper does not address how the low-rank constraint on synthesis coefficients prevents these issues or how the algorithm scales with increasing basis size (e.g., for higher-resolution transient layers). This omission is critical for practical adoption.

Originality is modest, heavily building on Fevotte et al.’s GCM (2009), though the time-domain reformulation and multi-resolution extension are meaningful. Significance is plausible but unproven: the results fail to demonstrate a clear advantage over existing methods in real-world applications, and the weak evaluation prevents convincing claims of state-of-the-art performance. The paper’s promise for source separation and denoising remains theoretical without sufficient empirical evidence.

Pro-acceptance arguments:  
- Theoretical extension of GCM to raw signal synthesis is novel and well-motivated.  
- Multi-resolution capability addresses a genuine limitation in NMF-based audio processing.  
- MJLE algorithm is computationally efficient and well-justified.  

Con-acceptance arguments:  
- Evaluation is insufficiently rigorous (single music example, minimal SNR data, no statistical validation).  
- Overcomplete bases’ practical challenges are unaddressed.  
- Artifacts in music decomposition and marginal SNR gains weaken significance claims.  

The paper’s contribution is technically sound but lacks the empirical weight required to demonstrate significance at NIPS. While the core idea is interesting, the weak evaluation prevents confident assessment of its practical value. The authors should substantially expand their experiments to include diverse audio datasets, ablation studies on resolution trade-offs, and comparisons with contemporary methods before the work can be considered impactful. As presented, the significance is overstated relative to the evidence.
