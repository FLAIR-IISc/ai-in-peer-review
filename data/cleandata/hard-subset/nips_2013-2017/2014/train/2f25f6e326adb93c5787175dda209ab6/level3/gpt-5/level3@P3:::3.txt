Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes Low-Rank Time-Frequency Synthesis (LRTFS), a generative model that imposes a low-rank (NMF-like) structure on the variance of synthesis coefficients in an arbitrary time-frequency frame, and links that model directly to the time-domain signal. This departs from the widely used STFT-domain Gaussian Composite Model (GCM) and IS-NMF approaches that model the distribution of analysis coefficients or spectrogram magnitudes, not the waveform. The authors derive two estimation procedures: a scalable EM-based maximum joint likelihood estimator (MJLE) requiring no large matrix inversions, and a maximum marginal likelihood estimator (MMLE) in the spirit of sparse Bayesian learning (SBL), which is theoretically appealing but currently not scalable. A notable extension is a multi-resolution LRTFS that combines multiple frames (e.g., short and long windows) in a single synthesis model, enabling per-layer low-rank structure. The paper connects LRTFS to SBL (variance-tied vs. variance-free priors), discusses component reconstruction (Wiener-like), and provides two audio use cases: a hybrid decomposition (tonal/transient) and semi-supervised speech enhancement.

Relative to prior work, this is a conceptual shift: instead of low-rank structure on STFT magnitudes or analysis coefficients (e.g., IS-NMF, GCM), the low-rank structure is placed on synthesis coefficients with a direct likelihood on the waveform. It also relates to multi-resolution sparse synthesis (e.g., MCA) but replaces sparsity with low-rank variance structure and accommodates arbitrary frames.

Strengths
- Clear, well-motivated formulation that directly models the waveform via a synthesis operator; flexible enough to handle arbitrary transforms and multi-resolution frames.
- Solid and careful theoretical development, including an EM architecture for MJLE that is computationally practical for audio, a principled MMLE derivation, and insightful links to SBL and Wiener filtering.
- Thoughtful interpretation that moves beyond standard STFT-based formulations; the variance tying via WH over synthesis coefficients is an interesting modeling choice.
- Writing quality is high; the paper is easy to follow, with relevant references and a coherent progression from model to inference to applications.

Weaknesses
- Experimental section is limited and underwhelming; the supplemental material does not convincingly demonstrate advantages commensurate with the conceptual contribution.
- Missing empirical comparisons to core baselines the work builds upon: GCM/IS-NMF with equivalent frames, multi-resolution NMF or MCA-style decompositions, and modern speech enhancement baselines beyond OMLSA.
- MMLE, though interesting, is not demonstrated; scalability is left for future work.
- The significance of modeling in the time domain versus STFT domain is not fully substantiated empirically; the paper undersells when and why this matters in practice.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The model and inference are well-grounded. The MJLE EM steps are derived cleanly; the appearance of a log(|α|^2) term and its sparsity-inducing effect is well explained. The MMLE derivation mirrors SBL literature appropriately.
- Assumptions: Zero-mean Gaussian residual; Gaussian synthesis coefficients with tied variances via WH; independence across TF points. These are standard but could be discussed vis-à-vis audio non-Gaussianity and phase correlations in redundant frames.
- Algorithms: MJLE is attractive computationally (no large inversions; uses Φ and Φ*). MMLE scalability limitations are acknowledged; leaving this unresolved weakens the empirical validation of the most principled estimator.
- Evaluation: The hybrid decomposition is illustrative but anecdotal. The speech enhancement results (average SNR improvement 6.6 dB vs. 6.0 dB for IS-NMF and 4.6 dB for OMLSA) are encouraging but modest; key design choices (λ tuning to the best SNR, warm-restarts) raise fairness and robustness concerns. Broader metrics (SDR/SIR/SAR, STOI, PESQ), error bars, and statistical tests are missing.

Clarity
- The manuscript is well organized and readable. Notation is largely consistent. The relation between analysis and synthesis coefficients is clearly explained.
- Suggestions: Clarify the choice and impact of β in the EM architecture, discuss initialization strategies and convergence behavior, and explicitly state assumptions on frames (tightness, redundancy) and their practical consequences. A complexity/runtime table would help.

Originality
- Above average. The shift from STFT/analysis-space modeling to time-domain synthesis modeling with a low-rank variance prior is novel and conceptually clean, and the multi-resolution extension is natural and useful.
- The link to SBL is insightful; tying variances through WH is a meaningful departure from variance-free SBL.

Significance
- Potentially significant for audio and other signal domains where multi-resolution structure and phase handling matter. However, the current experiments are too limited to convincingly demonstrate practical gains or robustness across tasks/datasets. The paper does not fully communicate when the time-domain synthesis perspective provides tangible benefits over STFT-domain GCM/IS-NMF.

Detailed suggestions for improvement
- Add strong baselines: GCM/IS-NMF with identical frames and multi-resolution setups; MCA-style sparse synthesis methods; modern speech enhancement baselines (e.g., Wiener filtering with NMF variance models, supervised NMF variants).
- Provide ablations: Single- vs multi-resolution LRTFS; effect of λ and β; role of the implicit sparsity term; dictionary size K; supervised vs unsupervised W.
- Report standard audio metrics (SDR/SIR/SAR, STOI, PESQ) and statistical variability; include more noise types and longer test sets.
- Fair hyperparameter selection protocols; avoid picking λ post hoc by best SNR; provide cross-validation or fixed schedules.
- Discuss identifiability and interactions between low-rankness and sparsity; comment on potential overfitting and local minima; provide initialization heuristics.
- Include computational cost and runtime comparisons to NMF/GCM; discuss memory/computation scaling with frame redundancy.
- If possible, include a small-scale MMLE experiment to illustrate its potential advantages or trade-offs.

Pros for acceptance
- Solid, well-executed theory with a clean and flexible modeling framework that generalizes beyond STFT-based NMF.
- Clear writing and insightful connections to SBL and Wiener filtering.
- Multi-resolution synthesis modeling is elegant and timely.

Cons for acceptance
- Empirical validation is thin; improvements over strong baselines are modest and not rigorously established.
- No MMLE demonstration; scalability left open.
- The practical significance of modeling the waveform vs. STFT is insufficiently evidenced.

Overall evaluation
A good, well-written paper with a valuable and technically sound modeling contribution that departs from STFT-based formulations and enables multi-resolution, arbitrary-frame synthesis factorization. However, the experimental section is disappointing and lacks key comparisons, which blunts the paper’s impact. I lean borderline/weak accept for the novelty and solid theory, conditional on strengthening the empirical evaluation; otherwise, it risks being cited primarily for the idea rather than demonstrated utility.
