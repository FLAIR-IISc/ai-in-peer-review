The paper "Low-Rank Time-Frequency Synthesis" presents a novel approach to signal decomposition by modeling the time-domain signal via generative time-frequency synthesis coefficients rather than traditional time-frequency analysis coefficients. This approach enables multi-resolution representations that traditional nonnegative matrix factorization (NMF) cannot provide. The authors propose a low-rank time-frequency synthesis (LRTFS) model, which is a generative model of the raw signal itself, rather than just its short-time Fourier transform (STFT).

The LRTFS model is defined by a set of equations that describe the signal as a sum of time-frequency atoms with low-rank latent structure. The authors present two expectation-maximization (EM) algorithms for estimation in the LRTFS model: maximum joint likelihood estimation (MJLE) and maximum marginal likelihood estimation (MMLE). The MJLE algorithm is computationally friendly and does not require matrix inversion, while the MMLE algorithm is more robust but has a higher computational complexity.

The authors also present a multi-resolution extension of the LRTFS model, which allows for the representation of signals as a sum of time-frequency atoms with different temporal resolutions. This approach is particularly relevant in audio applications, where transients and sustained parts of musical notes have different time resolutions.

The paper presents two experiments that demonstrate the effectiveness of the LRTFS approach. The first experiment decomposes a jazz excerpt into two layers (tonal and transient) plus a residual layer, while the second experiment enhances a speech signal corrupted by a texture sound. The results show that the LRTFS approach outperforms traditional NMF and other speech enhancement methods.

The strengths of the paper include the novelty of the approach, the clarity of the presentation, and the effectiveness of the experiments. The authors provide a clear and concise introduction to the LRTFS model and its estimation algorithms, and the experiments demonstrate the potential of the approach in audio applications.

However, there are some weaknesses in the paper. The authors do not provide a comparison of the LRTFS approach with other state-of-the-art methods in audio signal processing, such as independent component analysis (ICA) or sparse representations. Additionally, the MMLE algorithm is not scalable to large dimensions, which limits its applicability in practice.

In terms of quality, the paper is technically sound, and the claims are well-supported by theoretical analysis and experimental results. The authors are careful and honest about evaluating both the strengths and weaknesses of the work.

In terms of clarity, the paper is well-written and easy to follow. The authors provide clear and concise explanations of the LRTFS model and its estimation algorithms, and the experiments are well-described.

In terms of originality, the paper presents a novel approach to signal decomposition that combines the strengths of NMF and time-frequency synthesis. The multi-resolution extension of the LRTFS model is also a new contribution.

In terms of significance, the paper has the potential to impact the field of audio signal processing, particularly in applications such as music decomposition and speech enhancement. The LRTFS approach could also be applied to other signal processing problems, such as image processing or biomedical signal processing.
