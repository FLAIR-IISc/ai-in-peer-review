### Summary of the Paper

This paper proposes a new generative model for time-domain signals called Low-Rank Time-Frequency Synthesis (LRTFS). The model is a direct extension and time-domain re-interpretation of the Gaussian Composite Model (GCM) proposed by Févotte et al. (2009), which operates on the Short-Time Fourier Transform (STFT) of a signal. Where GCM models the variance of the *analysis* coefficients (the STFT) with a low-rank non-negative matrix factorization (NMF), LRTFS instead models the variance of the *synthesis* coefficients in a time-frequency dictionary representation. A key advantage of this synthesis-based approach is that it is not restricted to the orthogonal basis of the STFT; it can accommodate arbitrary, non-orthogonal, and potentially overcomplete time-frequency bases. This flexibility is exploited to create multi-resolution models, for instance by combining a basis with long time windows (for tonal components) and one with short time windows (for transient components). The authors derive two EM algorithms for parameter estimation—one based on maximum joint likelihood (MJLE) and another on maximum marginal likelihood (MMLE)—and provide experimental results for music decomposition and speech enhancement using the more scalable MJLE approach.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Strengths:**

1.  **Conceptual Novelty:** The core idea of shifting the low-rank probabilistic model from the analysis (spectrogram) domain to the synthesis (time) domain is elegant and insightful. It provides a principled generative framework for a class of signal representations that have often been treated with more ad-hoc sparse modeling techniques.

2.  **Enables Multi-Resolution Analysis:** The most significant practical contribution is that the LRTFS framework naturally supports multi-resolution decompositions. The ability to combine multiple time-frequency bases (e.g., Gabor frames with different window sizes) within a single probabilistic model is a powerful feature for audio signal processing, where signals often contain components with different time-frequency characteristics (e.g., sharp percussive attacks and sustained harmonic notes).

3.  **Clarity of Model Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written. The model itself, its relationship to the GCM, and its connection to sparse Bayesian learning are clearly articulated in Section 2.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Insufficient Experimental Evaluation:** The primary weakness of the paper is the very thin empirical validation of the proposed model. The claims of the benefits of LRTFS are not sufficiently supported by the experiments.
    *   The music decomposition example is presented as purely illustrative, with no objective metrics or comparisons to baseline methods. The provided audio samples for this task exhibit noticeable artifacts, and it is unclear if the performance is superior to conventional methods.
    *   The speech enhancement experiment reports only a minor average SNR improvement (0.6 dB) over a baseline IS-NMF method. This small gain may not be statistically significant and is insufficient to demonstrate a clear advantage. Furthermore, the baseline itself may not be optimally configured; using a single intermediate window length for IS-NMF is a compromise that might not represent its best possible performance against a multi-resolution method. In listening to the provided audio, the subjective quality of the OMLSA baseline was arguably higher than the proposed method.
    *   The evaluation is limited to one specific type of noise (applauses), and it is not clear if the results generalize.

2.  **Unaddressed Technical Questions:** The move to overcomplete bases is a central part of the paper's contribution, but the potential complications arising from this are not discussed. Overcomplete representations are inherently non-unique, which could introduce identifiability issues or additional local minima challenges for the estimation algorithm. A discussion of how the model's structure and the EM algorithm handle this redundancy would significantly strengthen the paper's technical contribution.

3.  **Clarity of Derivations:** While the model is presented clearly, the derivation of the MJLE EM algorithm in Section 3.1 was difficult to follow in detail. The reviewer had to take the correctness of the update equations on faith. Expanding on the steps to arrive at the Q-function and its subsequent optimization would improve the paper's clarity and reproducibility.

### Detailed Comments and Questions for the Authors

*   **Quality & Significance:** The idea is promising, but its practical significance is unproven by the current results. The paper would be much stronger with a more thorough experimental section. This should include:
    *   Comparisons against multiple state-of-the-art baselines for both music decomposition and speech enhancement.
    *   Objective metrics beyond SNR (e.g., SDR/SIR/SAR for source separation, PESQ/STOI for speech enhancement).
    *   Experiments on a wider variety of signals and noise types.
    *   An ablation study to demonstrate the specific benefit of the multi-resolution aspect. For instance, how does the multi-resolution LRTFS compare to single-resolution LRTFS models using either the short or long window alone?

*   **Originality:** The work builds heavily on the GCM framework of Févotte et al., and can be seen as a reformulation and generalization of that work. While the time-domain perspective is a fresh and valuable contribution, the originality is incremental rather than groundbreaking.

*   **Clarity:** Could you please elaborate on the choice of `β` in the MJLE EM algorithm? The condition `β ≤ λ/δΦ` is mentioned, but it is not specified how `β` was set in the experiments and how sensitive the algorithm is to this choice. Similarly, the estimation of `λ` was avoided in experiments; a discussion on the practical difficulties of its estimation would be welcome.

In summary, this paper presents an interesting and potentially impactful extension of low-rank models to the time domain, enabling principled multi-resolution analysis. However, the current manuscript feels more like a proof-of-concept than a complete piece of work. The experimental evidence is too weak to substantiate the claims of the model's benefits. Without a more convincing empirical demonstration of its value, the significance of the contribution remains unclear.
