This paper presents a novel low-rank time-frequency synthesis (LRTFS) framework, extending previous work on Gaussian Composite Models (GCMs) by directly modeling the raw signal rather than its STFT. The approach leverages low-rank factorization to constrain the variance of synthesis coefficients, offering potential benefits for multi-resolution representations. The paper is well-written and easy to follow, demonstrating a clear understanding of relevant prior work, particularly in NMF and probabilistic latent factor models for signal processing (e.g., [1, 3]). The theoretical development is solid and interesting, offering a thoughtful interpretation that moves beyond traditional STFT-based formulations.

The originality of the work is above average. Building upon the GCM [3], the authors address a significant limitation by proposing a generative model for the raw signal, rather than just its spectrogram. This is a logical and valuable extension. The multi-resolution aspect is also a novel contribution, opening possibilities not easily attainable with conventional NMF. However, the paper falls short in fully conveying the *significance* of this advance. While the technical contribution is sound, the broader implications and potential impact aren't emphasized sufficiently. The introduction could be strengthened by more explicitly articulating why modeling the raw signal is a crucial step and what new possibilities this unlocks beyond multi-resolution analysis.

Unfortunately, the empirical evaluation is the major weakness of this paper. The results presented are disappointing, and the supplemental material (mentioned but not reviewed here) is described as unconvincing. The music decomposition example (Section 5.1) provides a visual demonstration, but lacks quantitative metrics for assessing performance. The speech enhancement experiment (Section 5.2) shows a modest SNR improvement (6.6 dB) compared to baselines, but the gain over standard IS-NMF is minimal (0.6 dB). A more rigorous comparison against state-of-the-art speech enhancement techniques is necessary.

Critically, the paper lacks empirical comparisons to the existing methods it builds upon. While it references SBL [4, 5], it doesn't demonstrate a clear advantage over these established approaches. Similarly, a more detailed comparison to standard NMF implementations, particularly focusing on situations where the LRTFS model is expected to outperform them, would strengthen the claims. The authors acknowledge the scalability issues with MMLE, but the sole reliance on MJLE and limited experimental evaluation raise concerns about the practical applicability of the proposed framework. The choice of hyperparameters (especially λ) feels somewhat arbitrary without a more systematic exploration of its impact.

Despite the limited empirical validation, the theoretical contributions are valuable. The derivation of the LRTFS model and the EM algorithms for estimation are well-presented. The connection to sparse Bayesian learning is insightful, highlighting the differences and potential trade-offs between the two approaches. The extension to multi-resolution representations is a particularly strong theoretical contribution.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Solid theoretical foundation and interesting mathematical development.
*   Novel extension of GCMs to a generative model of the raw signal.
*   Well-written and clear presentation of the material.
*   Potential for multi-resolution signal representation.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Disappointing and limited empirical evaluation.
*   Lack of comparisons to existing, well-established methods (SBL, NMF).
*   Insufficient demonstration of the significance and practical impact of the work.
*   The results do not demonstrably advance the state-of-the-art.



**Overall:** This is a good paper with a potentially valuable theoretical contribution. However, the limited experimental validation and lack of comparative results significantly detract from its impact. I recommend acceptance contingent upon the authors significantly expanding the experimental evaluation, including comparisons to relevant baselines and a more thorough analysis of the model's performance. A stronger articulation of the significance of the work is also recommended. A revised version addressing these points would be a strong contribution to the NIPS proceedings.
