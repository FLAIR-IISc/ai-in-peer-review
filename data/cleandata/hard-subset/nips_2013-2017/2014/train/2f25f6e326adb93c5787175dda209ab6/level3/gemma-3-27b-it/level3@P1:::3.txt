This paper introduces a novel low-rank time-frequency synthesis (LRTFS) model, offering a generative approach to signal decomposition that bridges the gap between traditional time-frequency analysis and nonnegative matrix factorization (NMF). The authors build upon their previous work establishing a link between NMF with the Itakura-Saito divergence and a Gaussian Composite Model (GCM) of the STFT, and extend this to a generative model of the raw signal itself. A key strength of the proposed method is its flexibility – it’s applicable to various time-frequency transforms and enables multi-resolution representations, a capability lacking in conventional NMF.

The LRTFS model is clearly presented, grounded in a probabilistic framework. The authors appropriately connect their work to related areas such as sparse Bayesian learning (SBL), highlighting both similarities and crucial differences.  The theoretical development is robust, deriving expectation-maximization (EM) algorithms for parameter estimation, and thoughtfully acknowledging the non-convexity of the optimization problem.  The inclusion of an EM algorithm for maximum joint likelihood estimation (MJLE) and a discussion of maximum marginal likelihood estimation (MMLE) demonstrates a thorough exploration of potential estimation strategies.  The connection made to soft-thresholding and LASSO in the conclusion offers a valuable perspective on the model’s properties.

The paper’s clarity is a significant strength. The notation is consistent, and the explanations are generally accessible. The organization is logical, progressing smoothly from the introduction of the model to the estimation procedures and finally to experimental results.  The consideration of multi-resolution representations, and their potential benefit for audio signals with varying time-frequency characteristics (e.g., transients vs. sustained tones), is a particularly insightful contribution. 

The originality of the work is above average. While building upon established concepts like NMF and GCMs, the LRTFS model represents a genuine advancement by providing a generative model for the raw signal, directly addressing a limitation of previous approaches.  The multi-resolution capability further enhances its originality. The authors correctly reference related work, including papers from NIPS (e.g., Smaragdis et al. on NMF, Tipping on SBL) and other relevant publications. 

However, the significance of the work is not fully conveyed. While the theoretical framework is sound, the experimental results are somewhat disappointing. The reported SNR improvements in the speech enhancement experiment (6.6 dB) are modest, and only slightly outperform a basic IS-NMF baseline and a traditional OMLSA approach.  The supplementary material, which the authors invite the reader to consult for audio examples, does not seem to provide compelling evidence of a substantial improvement over existing methods.

A critical weakness is the lack of more comprehensive empirical comparisons. The authors compare their method to only a few baselines, and do not demonstrate a clear advantage over more sophisticated state-of-the-art techniques in either music decomposition or speech enhancement.  It would be beneficial to see comparisons to other recent approaches for source separation or time-frequency decomposition, especially those based on deep learning or more advanced probabilistic models.  Further, the parameters λ are chosen via manual tuning without mentioning any methodology, potentially impacting reproducibility.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Solid theoretical foundation and insightful connection to existing work.
*   Clear and well-written presentation.
*   Original approach that addresses a limitation of existing methods.
*   Potential for multi-resolution representations.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Limited and unconvincing experimental results.
*   Lack of comprehensive comparisons to state-of-the-art methods.
*   Significance of the work is not fully demonstrated.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a good paper that presents a theoretically sound and potentially valuable contribution to the field of signal processing. However, the limited experimental evaluation weakens its impact. While the theoretical developments are interesting and potentially useful, the lack of strong empirical evidence makes it difficult to assess the true significance of the work.  I would recommend acceptance with minor revisions, contingent on the authors strengthening the experimental section with more thorough comparisons and providing more compelling evidence of the benefits of their approach. Specifically, exploring other noise types and comparing against other strong baselines such as deep learning models would be worthwhile.
