This paper presents a novel signal synthesis model, called Low-Rank Time-Frequency Synthesis (LRTFS), which extends low-rank spectrogram factorization to arbitrary bases, enabling multiresolution decompositions. The authors derive a time-domain interpretation of the Gaussian Composite Model, expanding implicit convolutions into explicit impulse-response expressions, and enabling non-orthogonal bases. They also present an EM-based local-optimum estimation procedure.

The paper is well-written, and the authors provide a clear explanation of their approach, including the mathematical derivations. The idea of applying matrix factorization to overcomplete bases is interesting and potentially impactful. However, the reviewer is unsure of the full implications of this approach and would like to see more discussion on the potential limitations and challenges.

One concern is that the overcomplete bases should introduce complications, but the reviewer did not see these issues addressed or clarified in the paper. Additionally, the evaluation section is very thin, with only one illustrative result and one SNR measurement, which is insufficient to support the claims made in the paper.

Specifically, the reviewer notes that the comparison between the proposed method and other approaches is not entirely fair, as the audio lengths between methods are different. Furthermore, the speech quality of the baseline method (OMLSA) appears to be higher, and the proposed method introduces significant artifacts in music denoising compared to conventional methods.

In terms of originality, the paper builds heavily on the work of Fevotte et al., and while the time-domain reformulation offers a fresh perspective, the overall originality is limited. The significance of the paper is plausible but unproven due to the weak empirical demonstration, and the current results fail to convincingly show the practical importance of the proposed approach.

In conclusion, while the paper presents an interesting idea, it requires more development and evaluation to demonstrate its potential impact. The reviewer suggests that the authors provide more discussion on the limitations and challenges of their approach, as well as more comprehensive evaluation results to support their claims.

Arguments for acceptance:

* The paper presents a novel and potentially impactful idea.
* The mathematical derivations are clear and well-explained.
* The authors provide a good introduction to the background and related work.

Arguments against acceptance:

* The evaluation section is too thin and does not provide sufficient evidence to support the claims.
* The comparison with other methods is not entirely fair.
* The paper builds heavily on previous work, and the originality is limited.
* The significance of the paper is plausible but unproven due to the weak empirical demonstration.
