

# Review of "Low-Rank Time-Frequency Synthesis"

This paper introduces a novel generative model called Low-Rank Time-Frequency Synthesis (LRTFS), which extends the Gaussian Composite Model (GCM) from Fevotte et al. (NIPS 2009) to the time domain. The authors propose a time-domain interpretation of the GCM by explicitly expressing the synthesis coefficients as a sum of low-rank components, enabling the use of arbitrary (potentially overcomplete) bases. This extension allows for multi-resolution representations (tonal + transient) that were previously impossible in traditional NMF approaches, with potential applications in source separation and denoising.

The paper makes three key contributions: (1) a time-domain reformulation of the GCM that enables nonorthogonal bases; (2) an EM-based estimation procedure for the model parameters; and (3) a multi-resolution extension that naturally accommodates different temporal resolutions for different signal components. The authors relate their work to sparse Bayesian learning (SBL) and demonstrate its application to music decomposition and speech enhancement.

**Quality:** The technical content is sound, with the authors providing a clear mathematical formulation of the LRTFS model and two estimation approaches (MJLE and MMLE). The derivation of the MJLE algorithm appears correct, though the reviewer found the detailed derivation challenging to follow. The paper acknowledges limitations (e.g., MMLE's computational complexity) and appropriately sets aside MMLE for future work. However, the evaluation is severely lacking: only one illustrative result (Fig. 1) and a single SNR measurement (6.6 dB) for speech enhancement are provided. This is insufficient to support the claims of practical importance. The comparison with OMLSA appears unfair as the authors use different audio lengths (the paper states 7-second applause samples for their method versus unspecified lengths for OMLSA), and the perceived higher speech quality of the baseline contradicts the SNR results. The music denoising results show significant artifacts compared to conventional methods, raising questions about practical utility.

**Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and well-organized. The mathematical formulation is clear, and the relation to previous work is well-explained. However, the derivation of the EM algorithm (Section 3.1) is difficult to follow in detail, particularly the connection between the EM steps and the underlying Gaussian assumptions. Some technical details about the implementation of the Gabor frames (e.g., handling of hermitian symmetry) would benefit from more explicit discussion.

**Originality:** The core idea builds heavily on Fevotte et al.'s GCM, which was presented at NIPS 2009. While the time-domain reformulation offers a fresh perspective, the novelty is limited by this strong reliance on prior work. The multi-resolution extension is a valuable application of the model but doesn't constitute a fundamentally new approach to the problem. The paper properly cites relevant prior work, but the original contribution is more incremental than transformative.

**Significance:** The potential significance of the model is plausible, as multi-resolution audio processing is an important problem in signal processing. However, the current empirical demonstration fails to convincingly show practical importance. The weak evaluation (only one SNR measurement across 10 test signals) and questionable comparison with OMLSA make it impossible to assess whether this approach actually improves upon existing methods in practice. The paper doesn't address potential complications of using overcomplete bases (e.g., increased computational complexity, potential for overfitting), which is a significant concern given the authors' claim that "overcomplete bases should introduce complications" but don't clarify how their approach addresses these issues.

**Strengths:** The paper presents a theoretically sound extension of the GCM to the time domain with a clear mathematical formulation. The multi-resolution capability is an interesting application that could be valuable in audio processing. The time-domain interpretation of the GCM provides a fresh perspective on the model.

**Weaknesses:** The evaluation is severely inadequate for a NIPS paper, with insufficient experiments to support the claims. The comparison with baselines appears biased. The derivation of the EM algorithm is hard to follow. The paper doesn't address potential complications of overcomplete bases. The originality is limited by heavy reliance on prior work.

**Recommendation:** While the paper presents an interesting theoretical extension with plausible applications, the current empirical demonstration is insufficient to support the claims of practical importance. The evaluation is too thin to convince reviewers of the method's effectiveness. I recommend rejection due to inadequate empirical validation, though the theoretical contribution is sound. The authors would need to substantially strengthen the evaluation with more comprehensive experiments (including multiple metrics, more diverse audio examples, and fairer comparisons) before the paper could be considered for acceptance. The paper's significance is plausible but currently unproven.
