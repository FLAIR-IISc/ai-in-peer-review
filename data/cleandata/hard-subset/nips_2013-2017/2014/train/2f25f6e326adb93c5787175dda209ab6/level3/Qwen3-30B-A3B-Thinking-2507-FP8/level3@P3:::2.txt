

# Review of "Low-Rank Time-Frequency Synthesis"

This paper introduces Low-Rank Time-Frequency Synthesis (LRTFS), a novel approach that models time-domain signals directly through generative time-frequency synthesis coefficients, rather than working with time-frequency analysis coefficients (spectrograms) as in traditional nonnegative matrix factorization (NMF). The authors build upon the Gaussian Composite Model (GCM) introduced in [3], which provides a generative model for the STFT (analysis coefficients), but extend it to model the raw signal directly. The key insight is that by using synthesis coefficients (dual to analysis coefficients), they can naturally support multi-resolution representations—where different signal components can have different temporal resolutions—which was not possible in traditional NMF frameworks. The paper presents two EM algorithms for estimation and demonstrates applications in music decomposition and speech enhancement.

The paper is well-written and clearly organized, with strong connections to previous work in audio signal processing (e.g., [8, 9, 10]). The authors effectively explain how their model differs from the GCM in [3] and how it relates to sparse Bayesian learning (SBL) in [4, 5]. The mathematical derivations are sound, and the connection between the proposed approach and existing techniques like IS-NMF is well-motivated.

**Quality:** The technical content is strong, with two well-designed EM algorithms for estimation. The experiments in Section 5 demonstrate promising results in both music decomposition and speech enhancement, showing improvements over baseline IS-NMF (6.6 dB vs. 6 dB SNR improvement). However, the paper lacks sufficient evaluation of the multi-resolution capabilities. While the authors present decomposition results in Figure 1, they do not provide quantitative comparisons to assess how much the multi-resolution approach contributes to performance gains. The reviewer requests more thorough evaluation of the multi-resolution extension, including comparisons with single-resolution IS-NMF to determine whether the proposed approach truly outperforms standard methods when multi-resolution capabilities are not needed.

**Clarity:** The paper is clearly written, well-structured, and adequately explains the technical content. The connection between the synthesis coefficients and traditional analysis coefficients is well-articulated, and the mathematical derivations are accessible to the target audience. The authors provide sufficient detail for experts to reproduce their results, though the supplementary material would benefit from more implementation details.

**Originality:** The paper makes a significant contribution by shifting the paradigm from modeling analysis coefficients (spectrograms) to modeling synthesis coefficients for the raw signal. This enables multi-resolution representations, which is a novel capability not present in traditional NMF. The authors clearly differentiate their work from previous contributions, particularly from [3] and [8]. The idea of using synthesis coefficients for generative modeling represents a meaningful step beyond previous approaches.

**Significance:** The work has potential impact in audio signal processing, where multi-resolution representations could improve performance in applications like music decomposition and speech enhancement. The ability to model different signal components at different resolutions (e.g., transients vs. tonal components) is particularly valuable. However, the significance would be stronger if the paper provided more convincing evidence that the multi-resolution approach consistently outperforms single-resolution methods, especially in the speech enhancement experiments where the improvement over IS-NMF was marginal (6.6 dB vs. 6 dB).

**Major Strengths:**
- Novel shift from analysis to synthesis coefficients enables multi-resolution representations
- Clear theoretical connection to previous work in GCM and SBL
- Well-designed EM algorithms with computational efficiency
- Promising experimental results in audio applications

**Major Weaknesses:**
- Insufficient evaluation of multi-resolution capabilities (lacks quantitative comparison with single-resolution baselines)
- Does not address whether the proposed model's W and H matrices match those of IS-NMF in the single-resolution case
- Does not compare the speed and stability of the proposed algorithm with IS-NMF's multiplicative updates
- Limited discussion of computational complexity compared to existing methods

**Recommendation:** The paper presents an interesting and novel approach that advances the state of the art in time-frequency modeling. The multi-resolution capability is particularly appealing and represents a meaningful contribution. However, the lack of thorough evaluation of this key feature and the absence of direct comparisons with IS-NMF in the single-resolution case weaken the paper's claims about the advantages of the proposed method. The authors should address these points in a revision, including quantitative comparisons with IS-NMF for the single-resolution case and a more detailed analysis of the multi-resolution benefits.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
- Novel approach with clear theoretical foundation
- Enables multi-resolution representations not possible in traditional NMF
- Promising experimental results in audio applications
- Well-written and clearly organized

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
- Insufficient evaluation of the key multi-resolution feature
- Missing comparison with IS-NMF in single-resolution case
- Unclear whether the proposed algorithm matches IS-NMF's speed and stability

Overall, this is an interesting paper with potential impact in audio processing, but it requires stronger empirical validation of its key claims before it can be accepted for publication. The authors should address the requested comparisons and provide more thorough evaluation of the multi-resolution capabilities.
