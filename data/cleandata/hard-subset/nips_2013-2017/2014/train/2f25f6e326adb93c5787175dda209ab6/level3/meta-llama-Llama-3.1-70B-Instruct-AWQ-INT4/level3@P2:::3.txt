This paper proposes a novel signal synthesis model with low-rank time-frequency structure, referred to as Low-Rank Time-Frequency Synthesis (LRTFS). The model fills the gap between traditional nonnegative matrix factorization (NMF) approaches and generative models of the raw signal itself. The authors introduce two expectation-maximization (EM) algorithms for estimation in the new model and report audio signal processing results with music decomposition and speech enhancement.

The main idea of the paper is to propose a flexible time/frequency factorization framework that applies to arbitrary transforms and is directly linked to the time-domain signal. The authors offer a thoughtful interpretation that departs from STFT-based formulations. The paper is well-written and easy to read, making it a pleasure to follow.

The originality of the paper is above average, as it presents a new approach to signal synthesis. However, the paper does not fully communicate the significance of the proposed method. The results and evaluation are somewhat disappointing, and the supplemental material is unconvincing. Furthermore, the paper lacks empirical comparisons to the existing methods it builds on.

The theoretical development is solid and interesting, making the paper valuable despite limited experiments. The authors provide a clear and concise explanation of the LRTFS model and its relationship to sparse Bayesian learning. The EM algorithms for estimation are well-described, and the application to multi-resolution representations is a nice touch.

The experiments, although limited, demonstrate the potential of the proposed method. The hybrid decomposition of music and speech enhancement examples show promising results, but more extensive experiments are needed to fully evaluate the performance of the method.

In terms of quality, the paper is well-written and well-organized. The authors provide a clear and concise explanation of the proposed method, and the notation is consistent throughout the paper. The figures and tables are well-presented and help to illustrate the main points.

Overall, this is a good paper that would benefit from more results and comparisons. The proposed method is interesting and has potential applications in audio signal processing. With more extensive experiments and comparisons to existing methods, this paper could be even stronger.

Arguments pro acceptance:

* The paper proposes a novel signal synthesis model with low-rank time-frequency structure.
* The theoretical development is solid and interesting.
* The paper is well-written and easy to read.
* The experiments demonstrate the potential of the proposed method.

Arguments con acceptance:

* The paper does not fully communicate the significance of the proposed method.
* The results and evaluation are somewhat disappointing.
* The paper lacks empirical comparisons to the existing methods it builds on.
* The supplemental material is unconvincing.

Rating: 7/10

Recommendation: Accept with minor revisions. The authors should address the limitations of the paper by providing more extensive experiments and comparisons to existing methods. Additionally, they should clarify the significance of the proposed method and provide more convincing supplemental material.
