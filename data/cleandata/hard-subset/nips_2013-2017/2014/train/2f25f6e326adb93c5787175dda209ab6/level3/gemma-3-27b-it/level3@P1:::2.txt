This paper introduces a novel low-rank time-frequency synthesis (LRTFS) model, shifting the focus from decomposing the spectrogram (as in traditional Non-negative Matrix Factorization – NMF) to directly modeling the raw signal using low-rank synthesis coefficients. This is a significant step, as it offers a generative model of the signal itself, rather than merely its time-frequency representation. The authors leverage the Gaussian Composite Model (GCM) as a foundation, addressing its limitation of only modeling the STFT and extending it to the raw signal domain. A key strength of the proposed approach is its natural ability to facilitate multi-resolution representations, a capability absent in standard NMF methods. Two Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithms are presented for estimation, and results are shown for music decomposition and speech enhancement.

The work builds upon a substantial body of research in matrix factorization for signal processing, particularly NMF [1, 2]. The authors rightly point out the limitations of traditional NMF, specifically its empirical nature and its disregard for phase information. The introduction of the GCM [3] is effectively positioned as a step towards a more principled probabilistic framework. This paper extends that work by tackling the final gap – generating the raw signal. The connection to sparse Bayesian learning (SBL) [4, 5] is thoughtfully discussed, highlighting the differences in the coefficient structure and its implications for sparsity. The authors also acknowledge and relate their work to prior art in multi-resolution analysis [8, 9], providing context for the novelty of their approach.

The strengths of the paper lie in its theoretical grounding and its potential to overcome limitations of existing methods. The proposed LRTFS model appears well-motivated and the derivation of the EM algorithms is clear. The multi-resolution capability is genuinely interesting and represents a significant potential advantage over traditional NMF. The preliminary experimental results in music decomposition and speech enhancement, while not exhaustive, demonstrate the feasibility of the approach. The connection to the existing body of work on morphological component analysis [10] and other related techniques is also a positive aspect.

However, several weaknesses warrant attention. While the paper outlines the theoretical framework, a more rigorous comparison to existing techniques, specifically IS-NMF, is needed. The authors mention a comparison in the speech enhancement experiment but it’s not clear if the single-resolution case was tested – do the estimated W and H matrices match when using the proposed method versus IS-NMF? This would be a crucial benchmark.  Furthermore, the computational cost and stability of the proposed EM algorithm are not thoroughly addressed. The authors allude to the potential for local optima and mention the use of a warm-restart strategy for the λ parameter, but a more detailed analysis of the algorithm’s convergence properties and its computational complexity relative to the well-established multiplicative updates of IS-NMF is crucial. Do they exhibit comparable speed and stability?

The experiments, while promising, are somewhat limited. The music decomposition example lacks a clear quantitative evaluation metric beyond subjective listening. The speech enhancement results show a modest improvement over IS-NMF and a standard OMLSA method. A broader range of noise types and speech datasets, along with more rigorous statistical testing, would strengthen the conclusions. Additionally, the reliance on a fixed λ parameter, despite acknowledging its potential impact, is a limitation.  The claim that MMLE is impractical due to computational cost is reasonable, but further elaboration on attempts to scale it would be helpful.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novelty: The LRTFS model represents a genuine advancement over existing methods by directly modeling the raw signal.
*   Theoretical Soundness: The paper is well-grounded in probabilistic principles and establishes a clear connection to related work.
*   Multi-resolution capability: This feature opens up new possibilities for signal processing applications.
*   Potential Impact: The framework has the potential to address limitations of traditional NMF and provide improved performance in various applications.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Limited experimental evaluation: The experiments are somewhat limited in scope and lack comprehensive quantitative analysis.
*   Missing comparison to IS-NMF: A more direct and thorough comparison to IS-NMF is needed, particularly in the single-resolution case.
*   Algorithm complexity: A deeper analysis of the EM algorithm’s computational cost and stability is required.
*   Fixed λ parameter: The reliance on a fixed λ parameter is a limitation and should be addressed.



**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents an interesting and potentially impactful contribution to the field of signal processing. The LRTFS model offers a novel approach to signal decomposition with the attractive feature of multi-resolution analysis. However, the limited experimental evaluation and the lack of a comprehensive comparison to existing methods, particularly IS-NMF, raise concerns.  I recommend **acceptance with minor revisions**.  Specifically, the authors should: (1) Provide a detailed comparison to IS-NMF in the single-resolution case, focusing on estimated parameters and performance. (2) Analyze the computational complexity and stability of the EM algorithm relative to IS-NMF's multiplicative updates. (3) Expand the experimental evaluation with more diverse datasets and quantitative metrics. Addressing these points would significantly strengthen the paper and increase its impact.
