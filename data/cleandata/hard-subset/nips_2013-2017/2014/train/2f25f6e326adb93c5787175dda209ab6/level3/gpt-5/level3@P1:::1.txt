This paper proposes a generative time-domain model, Low-Rank Time-Frequency Synthesis (LRTFS), that imposes a low-rank structure on the variances of synthesis coefficients in an arbitrary (possibly overcomplete) time-frequency dictionary. Concretely, the signal x(t) is generated as a linear combination of atoms φfn(t) with complex Gaussian coefficients αfn ∼ Nc(0, [WH]fn) and additive Gaussian residual. This “synthesis” view complements Fevotte et al.’s Gaussian Composite Model (GCM) for the STFT [Neural Computation 2009], which imposes low-rank structure on analysis-coefficient variances. The authors derive two estimation strategies: (i) a scalable EM algorithm for maximum joint likelihood estimation (MJLE) using an auxiliary variable that avoids large matrix inversions and leads to alternating Wiener-like shrinkage of α and IS-NMF updates on |α|2; and (ii) a maximum marginal likelihood estimation (MMLE) akin to Sparse Bayesian Learning (SBL) [Tipping 2001; Wipf & Rao 2004], which they deem impractical at audio scale due to required matrix inversions. A key benefit of the synthesis model is a clean multi-resolution extension by stacking multiple frames (e.g., short for transients and long for tonals), with separate low-rank structures per frame, estimated with essentially the same EM. The paper presents two applications: an illustrative tonal/transient decomposition of a jazz excerpt and a semi-supervised speech enhancement task, reporting modest SNR gains over IS-NMF and OMLSA.

Relation to prior work: The model bridges NMF/IS divergence-based probabilistic models for spectrograms [Fevotte et al.] and synthesis models used in sparse coding/MCA [Daudet; Elad et al.], offering a probabilistic low-rank alternative to sparsity priors. The MMLE formulation follows SBL but differs by tying variances through WH rather than independent vm, which removes sparsity-inducing behavior but enforces low-rank structure. The multi-resolution construction connects to morphological component analysis and mixed-norm models [Kowalski & Torrésani], but with a low-rank Gaussian prior instead of sparse penalties.

Strengths
- Clear modeling idea that unifies low-rank factorization and synthesis in the time domain; provides a principled way to handle non-orthogonal/overcomplete frames.
- Simple, scalable MJLE-EM with no large matrix inversions; leverages standard IS-NMF updates and efficient TF toolboxes.
- Natural multi-resolution extension that is harder to formulate in analysis-only NMF; the tonal/transient decomposition example is elegant and easy to build upon.
- Useful connections to GCM and SBL that situate the contribution within well-studied probabilistic frameworks.

Weaknesses
Quality
- Derivations are dense; some key steps (e.g., origin and role of the log|α|2 term; conditions on β ≤ λ/δΦ; convergence guarantees with redundant Φ) are hard to follow or insufficiently justified.
- Overcompleteness brings non-uniqueness and conditioning issues; the paper does not analyze identifiability, the effect of frame redundancy on δΦ and EM stability/speed, or sensitivity to initialization.
- MMLE is presented but not made practical; no approximate scalable alternative (e.g., conjugate gradients, message passing, diagonal/low-rank covariance approximations).
- The experiments tune λ to maximize output SNR and then fix it; this risks optimistic results and undermines a fair protocol.

Clarity
- Overall writing is good, but the probabilistic implications of moving from analysis to synthesis (and when the synthesis model reduces to GCM, e.g., unitary Φ) could be clarified.
- Multi-resolution section would benefit from an explicit comparison against a single-resolution LRTFS control to quantify the added value.

Originality
- The step from analysis-GCM to synthesis with tied low-rank variances is a fresh perspective, especially for multi-resolution. However, the core ingredients (IS-NMF, Wiener filtering, SBL-style EM) are established, and the contribution leans more on reformulation than on new theory.

Significance
- Potentially impactful for audio decomposition and source separation, but the empirical evidence is thin. The music example is illustrative only; the speech enhancement experiment reports a small average SNR gain over a single IS-NMF configuration and an OMLSA baseline, without broader metrics or ablations. Subjectively, artifacts in the music example and perceived higher speech quality with OMLSA call the practical advantage into question.

Specific empirical concerns and suggestions
- Provide stronger baselines and controls: IS-NMF with multiple window sizes and multi-resolution features; complex/phase-aware NMF; PLCA variants; sparse synthesis methods (e.g., K-SVD/MCA) with comparable frames.
- Report standard metrics (SDR/SIR/SAR, PESQ, STOI) with confidence intervals; include runtime and memory; ensure all methods use identical test segments and model capacities.
- Ablations: single- vs multi-resolution LRTFS; effect of redundancy in Φ; number of components K; learned vs fixed dictionaries; role of the residual e and λ; use of MMSE component reconstruction vs the proposed proxy.
- Avoid tuning hyperparameters on test data; use validation splits.

Questions for the authors
- Under what conditions does LRTFS reduce to GCM/IS-NMF? Can you characterize behavior for unitary vs highly redundant frames?
- How sensitive is MJLE to β and λ, and how should they be set without peeking at test labels?
- Do you observe multiple local minima; how do different initializations affect results?
- Can you provide a scalable approximation to MMLE (e.g., diagonal Σ, iterative solvers)?

Pros for acceptance
- Conceptually neat synthesis reformulation of low-rank TF modeling.
- Scalable EM algorithm; easy to adopt for practitioners already using NMF.
- Natural and flexible multi-resolution modeling with promising qualitative results.

Cons for acceptance
- Limited and potentially biased empirical validation; modest gains over baselines and noted artifacts.
- Insufficient analysis of overcompleteness-related issues and algorithmic robustness.
- MMLE contribution is incomplete at scale; novelty is moderate relative to prior probabilistic NMF/SBL work.

Recommendation: Weak reject. The idea is interesting and likely useful, but the current paper does not yet provide convincing empirical support or sufficient analysis to demonstrate practical significance. Strengthening the experiments, clarifying derivations and robustness in overcomplete settings, and providing a scalable MMLE approximation would substantially improve the case.
