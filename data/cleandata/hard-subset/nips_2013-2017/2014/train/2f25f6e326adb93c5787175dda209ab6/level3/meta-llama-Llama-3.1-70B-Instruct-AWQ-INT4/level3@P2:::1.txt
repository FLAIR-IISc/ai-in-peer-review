This paper proposes a novel signal synthesis model with low-rank time-frequency structure, referred to as Low-Rank Time-Frequency Synthesis (LRTFS). The authors aim to bridge the gap between time-frequency synthesis and traditional Nonnegative Matrix Factorization (NMF) approaches. The proposed model extends low-rank spectrogram factorization to arbitrary bases, enabling multi-resolution decompositions and potentially benefiting source separation and denoising.

The authors derive a time-domain interpretation of the Gaussian Composite Model (GCM), expanding implicit convolutions into explicit impulse-response expressions and enabling non-orthogonal bases. They present an Expectation-Maximization (EM)-based local-optimum estimation procedure, which is computationally friendly and does not require matrix inversion.

The paper is well-organized, and the authors provide a clear explanation of their model and estimation procedure. The writing is generally good, making it easy to follow the authors' reasoning. However, the evaluation section is thin, with only one illustrative result and one SNR measurement, which is insufficient to support the claims made in the paper.

One of the main concerns is that the paper relies heavily on previous work by Fevotte et al., which limits its originality. Although the time-domain reformulation offers a fresh perspective, the paper's significance is plausible but unproven due to the weak empirical demonstration. The current results fail to convincingly show the practical importance of the proposed model.

The experiments presented in the paper are limited, and the comparison with other methods is not comprehensive. For example, the baseline method (OMLSA) seems to produce higher speech quality, and the music denoising results show significant artifacts compared to conventional methods. Furthermore, the comparison is not fair, as the audio lengths between methods are different.

Despite these limitations, the paper presents an interesting and potentially impactful idea. The application of matrix factorization to overcomplete bases is a novel approach that could have benefits for source separation and denoising. However, the authors should address the complications that arise from using overcomplete bases and provide a more comprehensive evaluation of their method.

In conclusion, while the paper presents a promising idea, it requires more development and evaluation to demonstrate its significance and practical importance. The authors should provide more comprehensive experiments, address the limitations of their method, and clarify the implications of using overcomplete bases.
