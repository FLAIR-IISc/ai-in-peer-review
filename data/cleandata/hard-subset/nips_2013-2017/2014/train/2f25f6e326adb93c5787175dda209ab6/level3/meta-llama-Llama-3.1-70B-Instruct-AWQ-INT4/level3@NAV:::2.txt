This paper proposes a novel signal synthesis model with low-rank time-frequency structure, called Low-Rank Time-Frequency Synthesis (LRTFS). The model is an extension of traditional Nonnegative Matrix Factorization (NMF) approaches, which only model the magnitude of the Short-Time Fourier Transform (STFT) of a signal. In contrast, LRTFS models the raw signal itself, allowing for multi-resolution representations that are not possible with traditional NMF.

The authors present two estimation methods for the LRTFS model: Maximum Joint Likelihood Estimation (MJLE) and Maximum Marginal Likelihood Estimation (MMLE). The MJLE method is based on an Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm that iteratively updates the model parameters to minimize the negative log-likelihood of the observed signal. The MMLE method, on the other hand, marginalizes out the synthesis coefficients and estimates the model parameters by maximizing the marginal likelihood of the observed signal.

The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of the LRTFS model on two experiments: music decomposition and speech enhancement. In the music decomposition experiment, the authors use the multi-resolution LRTFS model to decompose a jazz excerpt into two layers (tonal and transient) plus a residual layer. The results show that the model is able to capture the different components of the signal with high accuracy. In the speech enhancement experiment, the authors use the LRTFS model to separate a speech signal from a noisy background. The results show that the model is able to improve the signal-to-noise ratio (SNR) of the speech signal by 6.6 dB on average.

Strengths of the paper:

* The LRTFS model is a novel extension of traditional NMF approaches that allows for multi-resolution representations of signals.
* The authors present two estimation methods for the LRTFS model, which are based on different optimization criteria.
* The experiments demonstrate the effectiveness of the LRTFS model on two different applications: music decomposition and speech enhancement.

Weaknesses of the paper:

* The MMLE method is not scalable to large datasets, as it requires the computation of a large matrix inverse.
* The authors do not provide a comparison of the LRTFS model with other state-of-the-art methods in the field.
* The paper could benefit from more theoretical analysis of the LRTFS model, such as convergence guarantees and stability analysis.
