This paper proposes Low-Rank Time-Frequency Synthesis (LRTFS), a generative time-domain model in which a signal is synthesized as a linear combination of time-frequency atoms with complex Gaussian coefficients whose variances have low-rank structure WH. The work reinterprets Févotte et al.’s Gaussian Composite Model (GCM) in the time domain by expanding the implicit STFT-domain convolutions into explicit impulse-response expressions, thereby accommodating arbitrary, potentially overcomplete and nonorthogonal bases and enabling multiresolution (tonal/transient) decompositions. Two estimation strategies are presented: a computationally friendly EM procedure for maximum joint likelihood (MJLE) and a less scalable marginal-likelihood (MMLE/SBL-style) variant. The paper illustrates applications to source decomposition and denoising.

Strengths: The time-domain reformulation is conceptually appealing, unifying NMF/GCM-style low-rank variance modeling with synthesis in arbitrary frames. Allowing overcomplete, multiresolution dictionaries directly in the generative model is intriguing and could be impactful for audio separation/denoising. The MJLE-EM is simple and avoids large matrix inversions; the connection to Wiener filtering/NMF is insightful.

Weaknesses: The implications of overcomplete, nonorthogonal bases are underexplored. Identifiability, scaling/rotation ambiguities (W-H-α-λ), conditioning of ΦΦ*, and how redundancy affects EM convergence and variance tying are not addressed. The MMLE route is acknowledged as intractable at audio scale; the practical method thus relies on MJLE with heuristic handling of λ, but the consequences for bias/variance and artifacts are not analyzed. The derivations, while likely correct, are hard to follow; key steps in the EM construction (e.g., β ≤ λ/δΦ, monotonicity guarantees, initialization sensitivity) would benefit from clearer exposition or an appendix.

Evaluation is too thin to substantiate the claims. Only one illustrative music example and a small speech-denoising study are reported, mostly with SNR. There are concerns about fairness (different audio lengths across methods), perceived higher speech quality for OMLSA despite lower SNR, and audible artifacts for music denoising relative to conventional approaches. Absent are standard objective metrics (PESQ/STOI/SDR), controlled ablations (single- vs multi-resolution, overcomplete vs orthonormal bases, effect of λ), and stronger baselines (e.g., STFT-GCM+Wiener with matched windows, SBL/MCA).

Quality: Technically promising but incomplete empirically. Clarity: generally good, but derivations dense. Originality: moderate—builds closely on GCM/SBL; the time-domain, overcomplete extension is a fresh angle. Significance: plausible but unproven.

Pros for acceptance:
- Elegant, potentially general framework; enables multiresolution synthesis
- Practical EM algorithm; insightful link to Wiener/NMF

Cons against acceptance:
- Weak, potentially unfair evaluation; lacks robust metrics and listening tests
- Overcomplete-basis complications not analyzed
- MMLE contribution not scalable; λ handling ad hoc
- Derivations hard to follow without supplementary details

Overall: Interesting idea with promise, but current evidence is insufficient; I lean toward rejection pending a stronger, more rigorous evaluation and clearer treatment of overcompleteness.
