

This paper introduces Low-Rank Time-Frequency Synthesis (LRTFS), a novel approach that models the raw time-domain signal directly rather than its time-frequency transform, addressing a key limitation in traditional nonnegative matrix factorization (NMF) approaches for audio signal processing. The authors build upon the Gaussian Composite Model (GCM) framework [3], which models the STFT as a low-rank complex Gaussian process, but extend it to model the signal itself through a generative synthesis framework. This represents a significant conceptual advancement over previous work where NMF was applied to spectrograms (magnitude of STFT) without a direct generative model of the raw signal.

The main contribution is the LRTFS model, defined by equations (2)-(4), which expresses the signal as a linear combination of time-frequency atoms with synthesis coefficients that have low-rank variance structure. This framework naturally enables multi-resolution representations where different signal components can have different time-frequency resolutions, a capability not possible with traditional NMF approaches that work on a fixed time-frequency representation. The authors present two EM-based algorithms for estimation: Maximum Joint Likelihood Estimation (MJLE) and Maximum Marginal Likelihood Estimation (MMLE), with MJLE being computationally feasible for large-scale audio applications.

Strengths of the paper include:
1) The theoretical soundness of the generative model, which properly models the raw signal rather than just its transform, providing a more principled approach than prior work.
2) The multi-resolution capability is genuinely novel and useful for audio applications where different signal components (e.g., transients vs. tonal components) require different time resolutions.
3) The connection to sparse Bayesian learning (SBL) is well-explained, showing how the low-rank constraint differs from standard SBL's sparsity-inducing property.
4) The experimental results on music decomposition and speech enhancement demonstrate practical utility, with the speech enhancement experiment showing 6.6 dB average SNR improvement compared to IS-NMF's 6 dB.

Weaknesses and concerns:
1) The paper lacks a thorough comparison with IS-NMF in the single-resolution case. The authors claim their approach enables multi-resolution, but it's unclear whether the proposed MJLE algorithm matches IS-NMF's performance when constrained to single resolution. The authors should provide this comparison to establish that their framework doesn't degrade performance in the single-resolution case.
2) The paper doesn't sufficiently address whether the proposed algorithm matches IS-NMF's multiplicative updates in speed and stability. The EM algorithm described in Section 3.1 appears to have different convergence properties than standard IS-NMF, but this is not explicitly discussed.
3) While the multi-resolution results are promising, the paper could provide more systematic evaluation of the multi-resolution advantage. For instance, how much does the multi-resolution capability specifically contribute to the performance gains compared to using a single resolution with the same model?
4) The MMLE approach is dismissed as computationally infeasible for audio applications, but the authors don't clearly justify why the MMLE approach can't be made scalable for these applications (e.g., through approximate inference).

The paper's significance is evident in its potential to bridge the gap between time-frequency analysis and generative modeling, with clear applications in audio processing. The multi-resolution capability is particularly compelling, as it addresses a fundamental limitation of traditional NMF approaches that cannot adapt resolution during decomposition.

The quality of the paper is high technically, with clear mathematical formulations and well-justified algorithmic choices. The clarity is good, with the model and algorithms well-explained. The originality is strong, as the direct modeling of the raw signal with low-rank time-frequency structure represents a meaningful extension of prior work. The significance is demonstrated through both theoretical advancement and practical audio applications.

The paper would be strengthened by:
1) A direct comparison of MJLE with IS-NMF in the single-resolution setting
2) Analysis of the computational complexity and convergence properties compared to IS-NMF
3) More systematic evaluation of the multi-resolution advantage
4) Discussion of whether the multi-resolution capability could be incorporated into existing NMF-based approaches

In conclusion, this is a strong paper with significant theoretical contributions and practical relevance. The multi-resolution capability is particularly novel and useful for audio applications. While the paper would benefit from more thorough comparisons with established baselines and clearer justification for the computational choices, the core contribution is valuable and well-executed. The work advances the state of the art in time-frequency modeling for audio processing by properly modeling the raw signal rather than its transform, with the added benefit of natural multi-resolution capabilities. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the comparative analysis concerns.
