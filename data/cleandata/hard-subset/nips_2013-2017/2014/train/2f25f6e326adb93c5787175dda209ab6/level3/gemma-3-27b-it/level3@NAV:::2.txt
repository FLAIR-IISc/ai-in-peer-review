## Review of “Low-Rank Time-Frequency Synthesis”

This paper introduces a novel generative model for single-channel signal decomposition, moving beyond traditional time-frequency (TF) analysis approaches like NMF of the spectrogram. The core idea – modeling the time-domain signal via generative TF *synthesis* coefficients rather than analysis coefficients – is a compelling one, directly addressing a limitation of existing methods that operate on the STFT magnitude only. The authors correctly position their work within the context of existing probabilistic latent factor models like the Gaussian Composite Model (GCM), highlighting that their approach finally achieves a generative model for the raw signal itself. 

The potential for multi-resolution representations is a particularly appealing strength. Traditional NMF lacks this flexibility, and the ability to adapt the time-frequency resolution based on signal characteristics (transients vs. sustained tones, for example) is a significant advance. The presentation of the LRTFS model is clear and mathematically sound, and the derivation of the EM algorithms is well-explained. The connection to sparse Bayesian learning is also a valuable insight.

However, while the authors demonstrate the multi-resolution decomposition on a jazz excerpt and a speech enhancement task, the evaluation feels somewhat limited. The multi-resolution results are intriguing but require more thorough quantification. It would be helpful to see a more rigorous comparison of the proposed method against state-of-the-art alternatives for both music decomposition and speech enhancement. 

Crucially, the paper lacks a comparison to IS-NMF in the single-resolution case. Given the close relationship, it’s important to establish whether the proposed method’s W and H matrices converge to similar solutions as IS-NMF under identical conditions. Furthermore, the authors mention the speed and stability of IS-NMF’s multiplicative updates and it would be insightful to know if their proposed EM algorithm matches this efficiency. Initial implementations suggest that, while mathematically sound, the proposed approach is computationally more expensive. 

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novelty: The approach of modeling synthesis coefficients directly is a significant departure from existing methods.
*   Potential Significance: The multi-resolution capability opens up new avenues for signal processing applications.
*   Technical Soundness: The mathematical formulation and algorithm derivations are well-presented.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   Limited Evaluation: More thorough quantitative results and comparisons are needed.
*   Missing Baseline: A comparison to IS-NMF in the single-resolution case is crucial.
*   Computational Cost: Potential efficiency concerns compared to existing methods.



Overall, this paper presents a promising new direction for signal decomposition. Addressing the concerns outlined above, particularly with a more comprehensive evaluation and comparison to established techniques, would significantly strengthen the contribution and increase its likelihood of acceptance. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions, contingent on the authors addressing the evaluation and baseline comparison points.
