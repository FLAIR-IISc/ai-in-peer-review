- Proposes LRTFS, a novel generative synthesis model that models raw signals as sums of time-frequency atoms with low-rank structure on synthesis coefficients.  
- Motivated by limitations of spectrogram-based NMF and builds on the Gaussian Composite Model (GCM) to enable true generative modeling and multi-resolution representations.  
- Derives EM algorithms for parameter estimation and connects the model to sparse Bayesian learning, highlighting differences (tied variances vs. free variances).  
- Claims multi-resolution capability as a key advantage, allowing components with different temporal characteristics to be modeled naturally.  
- Demonstrates applicability on music decomposition and speech enhancement, comparing to IS-NMF as a benchmark.  
- Strength: clear theoretical derivation and sound probabilistic foundation.  
- Strength: novelty in shifting from analysis to synthesis and enabling multi-resolution TF representations.  
- Strength: experimental results show promise in relevant audio tasks and the paper is well-written.  
- Weakness: computational scalability concerns for the MMLE approach, especially due to inversion of large matrices.  
- Weakness: limited comparative analysis — more thorough comparison to IS-NMF (and alignment of learned W and H) is needed.  
- Weakness: unclear algorithm speed and stability trade-offs compared to IS-NMF multiplicative updates; convergence and robustness need discussion.  
- Weakness: multi-resolution decomposition evaluation is superficial; needs quantitative metrics and subjective listening tests.  
- Weakness: sensitivity to regularization parameter λ is mentioned but not systematically explored.  
- Argument for acceptance: novel, theoretically sound contribution with significant multi-resolution advances and promising experiments.  
- Argument against acceptance: scalability, limited comparisons, and insufficiently rigorous multi-resolution evaluation.  
- Overall assessment: strong paper recommended for acceptance with minor revisions addressing comparative analysis, algorithm speed/stability, multi-resolution evaluation, and MMLE computational limitations.
