## Review of “Low-Rank Time-Frequency Synthesis”

This paper introduces a novel generative model for single-channel signal decomposition, moving beyond traditional Non-negative Matrix Factorization (NMF) of the spectrogram. The key innovation lies in modeling the time-domain signal directly via generative Time-Frequency (TF) synthesis coefficients, rather than analyzing the TF representation itself. This approach naturally enables multi-resolution representations, a significant advantage over conventional NMF which operates at a fixed resolution. The work builds upon previous research establishing connections between NMF and the Gaussian Composite Model (GCM) [3], and extends it to a truly generative signal model.

The authors present two Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithms for parameter estimation and demonstrate the multi-resolution capability with music decomposition and speech enhancement experiments. The multi-resolution extension is indeed a compelling contribution, offering flexibility in representing signals with varying temporal characteristics. The experimental results are promising, showing comparable or slightly improved performance in speech enhancement compared to standard IS-NMF and OMLSA. 

However, the paper would benefit from more thorough evaluation. While multi-resolution decomposition results are presented, a more systematic comparison across different resolutions and signal types is needed. A crucial point is the comparison with IS-NMF [3] in the *single-resolution* case. Do the estimated W and H matrices align with those obtained from IS-NMF? Establishing this connection would strengthen the paper's position within the existing literature. 

Furthermore, the authors should address the computational efficiency of their proposed algorithm. They mention the speed and stability of IS-NMF’s multiplicative updates and it is important to know if their EM algorithm matches this performance. The MMLE approach, while theoretically interesting, is acknowledged to be computationally expensive and not fully implemented for the presented applications. 

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel generative model for signal decomposition.
*   Enables multi-resolution representations, a significant advancement.
*   Promising experimental results in audio applications.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Limited comparative evaluation, particularly against IS-NMF in the single-resolution case.
*   Lack of detailed analysis of computational complexity and speed compared to existing methods.
*   MMLE approach is not fully scalable and remains largely theoretical for the presented applications.



**Overall:** This paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of signal decomposition. The proposed model is well-motivated and the multi-resolution capability is a significant strength. However, addressing the concerns regarding comparative evaluation and computational efficiency would substantially strengthen the paper and increase its impact. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the points raised above.
