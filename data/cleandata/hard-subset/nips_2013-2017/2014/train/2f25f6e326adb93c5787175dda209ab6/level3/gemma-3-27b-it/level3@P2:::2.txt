## Review of Low-Rank Time-Frequency Synthesis

This paper presents a novel generative model for signal synthesis based on low-rank time-frequency representations. The core idea is to move beyond decomposing the spectrogram (as in traditional Nonnegative Matrix Factorization – NMF) and instead directly model the raw signal as a sum of time-frequency atoms with low-rank structure on the synthesis coefficients. This allows for a true generative model and, importantly, naturally supports multi-resolution representations, a capability lacking in standard NMF approaches. The authors propose Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithms for estimation and demonstrate the approach on music decomposition and speech enhancement tasks.

The work builds upon a strong foundation in signal processing and machine learning, particularly leveraging the Gaussian Composite Model (GCM) introduced in Févotte et al. (2004) as a probabilistic latent factor model of the STFT.  The motivation for moving to a synthesis model, rather than working solely with analysis coefficients, is clearly articulated and convincingly addresses limitations of previous approaches. Related work in sparse Bayesian learning (Tipping, 2001; Wipf & Rao, 2007) is appropriately acknowledged and contextualized, highlighting the differences between the proposed method and SBL – specifically, the tying of variances in the LRTFS model versus the free variances in SBL.  The paper is well-aware of existing techniques such as morphological component analysis (Elad et al., 2006) and hybrid decomposition approaches (Daudet & Torresani, 2008), positioning the contribution within the broader landscape of signal decomposition methods.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The proposed model represents a significant step forward in time-frequency signal decomposition, shifting from analyzing the spectrogram to synthesizing the signal directly. This unlocks new possibilities, most notably the inherent support for multi-resolution representations.
* **Theoretical Soundness:** The derivation of the LRTFS model and the EM algorithms appears mathematically sound, building upon established probabilistic frameworks. The connection to sparse Bayesian learning is insightful.
* **Multi-Resolution Capability:** The ability to readily incorporate multiple time-frequency resolutions is a compelling advantage, particularly for signals with components exhibiting varying temporal characteristics.
* **Experimental Validation:** The experiments demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method in both music decomposition and speech enhancement scenarios. The comparison to IS-NMF in the speech enhancement task provides a reasonable benchmark.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the core concepts and algorithmic details.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Computational Complexity:** While the authors mention the EM algorithm being computationally friendly, the discussion regarding the MMLE approach highlights significant scalability issues. The inversion of large matrices presents a practical barrier to applying MMLE to real-world problems, and this limitation should be more explicitly addressed.
* **Limited Comparative Analysis:** The comparison to IS-NMF in the speech enhancement task is somewhat limited. A more thorough comparison, particularly in the single-resolution case (where LRTFS should, in theory, converge to a similar solution), would be valuable. Do the learned W and H matrices align with those obtained via IS-NMF?
* **Algorithm Speed and Stability:** The authors claim the MJLE algorithm's iterative steps are efficient and benefit from optimized packages. However, a discussion comparing the speed and stability of the proposed EM algorithm to the well-established multiplicative update rules of IS-NMF would be beneficial.  Are there any trade-offs in convergence speed or robustness?
* **Evaluation of Multi-Resolution Decomposition:** While the multi-resolution decomposition example is intriguing, the evaluation is somewhat superficial. More quantitative measures and subjective listening tests would strengthen the demonstration of its effectiveness.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The sensitivity of the results to the choice of the regularization parameter λ is mentioned but not thoroughly investigated. A more systematic exploration of the impact of λ on performance would be informative.



**Arguments for acceptance:**

* The paper presents a genuinely novel and theoretically sound approach to time-frequency signal decomposition.
* The multi-resolution capability is a significant advancement over existing methods.
* The experimental results demonstrate the potential of the proposed approach in relevant applications.
* The paper is clearly written and well-organized.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

* The scalability issues with the MMLE algorithm are a concern.
* The comparative analysis with existing methods is somewhat limited.
* The evaluation of the multi-resolution decomposition is not as rigorous as it could be.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that presents a valuable contribution to the field of signal processing. The proposed LRTFS model offers a compelling alternative to traditional NMF and opens up exciting new avenues for research. While there are some minor weaknesses, the strengths of the work outweigh these concerns. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions. The authors should address the points raised regarding comparative analysis, algorithm speed/stability, and the evaluation of the multi-resolution decomposition. Further discussion of the computational limitations of the MMLE approach would also be helpful.
