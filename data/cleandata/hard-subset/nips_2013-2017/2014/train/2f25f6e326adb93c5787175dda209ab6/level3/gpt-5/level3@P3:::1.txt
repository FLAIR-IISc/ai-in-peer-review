Summary
This paper proposes Low-Rank Time-Frequency Synthesis (LRTFS), a generative time-domain model in which a signal is synthesized as a linear combination of time-frequency atoms with complex Gaussian coefficients whose variances follow a low-rank structure WH. This extends the Gaussian Composite Model (GCM) view of Itakura–Saito (IS) NMF from the STFT domain to the raw waveform, and it allows the synthesis dictionary Φ to be an arbitrary (possibly overcomplete and nonorthogonal) frame. The authors derive two estimation procedures: a scalable EM algorithm for maximum joint likelihood estimation (MJLE) that alternates Wiener-like shrinkage of synthesis coefficients and IS-NMF on |α|^2, and a maximum marginal likelihood (MMLE) approach akin to SBL that is not yet computationally tractable at audio scale. They also show how to stack multiple frames (e.g., short/long windows) to enable multiresolution, tonal/transient decompositions directly in the time domain. Experiments illustrate music decomposition and single-channel speech enhancement.

Relation to prior work: The work builds directly on Févotte et al.’s GCM interpretation of IS-NMF for the STFT, and on sparse Bayesian learning (SBL) methodologies (Tipping; Wipf & Rao) for type-II ML with Gaussian scale mixtures. It also relates to hybrid multiresolution approaches such as morphological component analysis (Daudet & Torrésani) and to Wiener-filtered NMF-based source separation.

Quality
- Theory: The time-domain reformulation of the GCM is interesting and, to my reading, sound in spirit. The MJLE EM scheme using the Figueiredo–Nowak splitting is plausible and computationally attractive (no large inversions), and the appearance of a log(|α|^2) term is a neat connection to sparsity. That said, I found the derivation hard to follow in places and could not verify all steps; clearer intermediate steps (especially for Eq. 11 and the role of β) would help.
- Overcomplete bases: Allowing general, nonorthogonal frames is a key selling point, but it raises nontrivial issues that the paper does not address: dependencies among α induced by Φ’s coherence, identifiability (scale/rotation of W,H versus energy distributed across overlapping atoms), and how tight/non-tight frames affect estimation and reconstruction. The MJLE update treats α’s prior as diagonal; some discussion of the posterior covariance structure and its implications when Φ is redundant would strengthen the work.
- Algorithms: MJLE scales; MMLE does not. There is no convergence analysis beyond monotone decrease, and the choice/sensitivity of β (constrained by λ/δΦ) and the handling of λ (ultimately treated as a hyperparameter) deserve more justification.
- Empirics: The evaluation is very thin. There is one illustrative music result and one SNR-type comparison for speech, which is insufficient to support broad claims about source separation/denoising. Some comparisons appear potentially unfair (e.g., different time supports/windows and possibly different effective audio lengths across methods). In listening, OMLSA seems to yield cleaner speech, while LRTFS introduces audible artifacts in the music denoising example.

Clarity
The paper is generally well written and well organized, with helpful context to GCM and SBL. However, some derivations are terse (MJLE objective; MMLE updates), and key implementation choices are under-specified (β selection, initialization, frame normalization/tightness, λ scheduling). A simple algorithm box and a small “practitioner’s guide” would help reproducibility.

Originality
Moderate. The work leans heavily on the GCM interpretation of IS-NMF and on standard Gaussian latent-variable machinery. The primary novelty is shifting the low-rank variance model to synthesis coefficients in the time domain and exploiting arbitrary (overcomplete) frames to enable multiresolution decompositions. This is a fresh and potentially useful perspective, but conceptually adjacent to existing GCM/SBL/NMF threads.

Significance
Potentially impactful if validated: enabling low-rank structure with overcomplete, multi-scale bases could unify NMF-style modeling with hybrid/morphological decompositions and benefit audio separation/denoising. However, with the current limited and partly subjective evaluation, the practical importance remains unproven.

Specific comments and suggestions
- Overcompleteness: Provide analysis or experiments on how redundancy and frame coherence affect recovery (e.g., varying redundancy factors; tight vs. non-tight frames). Clarify identifiability and scaling ambiguities among Φ, W, and H.
- MMLE: Offer a path to scalability (e.g., approximate inference, conjugate gradients, message passing), or drop it from claims and focus on MJLE.
- Algorithmic details: Explain β selection and its effect; report runtime and memory; detail initialization of α, W, H; discuss sensitivity to K and λ scheduling.
- Evaluation: Substantially expand. Use standard objective metrics (BSS Eval SDR/SIR/SAR for separation; PESQ/STOI for speech), more datasets/noise types, and matched conditions across methods (same signal duration, same STFT parameters where applicable). Include listening tests or MUSHRA/MOS. Compare against:
  - IS-NMF with Wiener filtering on STFT (single- and multi-window variants).
  - Morphological component analysis / sparse multiresolution baselines.
  - Convolutional/temporal smooth NMF variants.
  - Strong speech denoising baselines (OMLSA tuned, MMSE-LSA).
- Ablations: Single- vs multi-resolution; orthonormal vs overcomplete Φ; effect of nonnegativity/low-rank alone vs combined with sparsity (the log term).
- Clarify the “time-domain interpretation” by explicitly connecting to the GCM’s implicit convolution view and by providing impulse-response intuition and limits where LRTFS reduces to GCM.

Arguments for acceptance
- Conceptually appealing bridge from STFT-domain NMF to time-domain synthesis with arbitrary frames.
- Clean EM scheme with practical scalability for MJLE.
- Natural multiresolution extension enabling tonal/transient decompositions.
- Fresh perspective on GCM that may inspire follow-on work in audio modeling.

Arguments against acceptance
- Empirical evidence is too weak to substantiate claims; limited, potentially unfair comparisons and mixed perceptual quality.
- Key complications of overcomplete, nonorthogonal bases are not analyzed or clarified.
- MMLE contribution is not yet practical; derivations are hard to follow in places.
- Originality is incremental relative to Févotte et al.’s GCM and SBL literature.

Recommendation
Lean reject in current form due to insufficient experimental validation and unaddressed implications of overcomplete bases. I would be enthusiastic about a revised version with stronger, fair evaluations, clearer derivations, and a substantive treatment of redundancy/coherence effects.
