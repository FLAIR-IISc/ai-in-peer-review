## Review of “Low-Rank Time-Frequency Synthesis”

This paper introduces a novel low-rank time-frequency synthesis (LRTFS) model, offering a generative approach to signal decomposition that moves beyond traditional STFT-based methods like NMF. The core idea – directly modeling the raw signal with a low-rank structure in its time-frequency representation – is a compelling departure from existing work, building upon the Gaussian Composite Model (GCM) presented in Févotte et al. (2011) by completing the generative modeling pipeline. The authors present two Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithms for estimation and demonstrate the potential for multi-resolution representations, a significant advantage over standard NMF. 

The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow, presenting a complex theoretical development with clarity. The mathematical derivations are sound, and the connection to sparse Bayesian learning is thoughtfully discussed. The originality of the approach is above average, offering a fresh perspective on time-frequency decomposition. However, while the theoretical contribution is strong, the paper falls short in convincingly demonstrating the *significance* of this approach. 

The primary weakness lies in the disappointing experimental results. The music decomposition example, while illustrative, lacks quantitative evaluation and relies on subjective assessment in the supplemental material. The speech enhancement experiment shows modest improvements over a baseline IS-NMF and OMLSA, but these gains are not substantial enough to establish a clear advantage for LRTFS. Critically, the paper lacks a thorough comparison to existing state-of-the-art methods that it builds upon, such as those leveraging deep learning for similar tasks. The authors acknowledge the computational limitations of the MMLE approach, but this further weakens the empirical validation.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Solid theoretical foundation and interesting novel approach.
*   Clear and well-written presentation.
*   Potential for multi-resolution analysis is a valuable contribution.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Disappointing and limited experimental results.
*   Lack of comparison to relevant state-of-the-art methods.
*   Significance of the work is not fully communicated.



**Overall:** This is a good paper with a valuable theoretical contribution. However, the limited and unconvincing experimental evaluation prevents a strong recommendation for acceptance. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions, contingent on the authors significantly expanding the experimental section with more comprehensive comparisons and quantitative results. Addressing the limitations in evaluation would substantially strengthen the paper’s impact and demonstrate the true potential of the proposed LRTFS model.
