This paper presents an interesting extension of low-rank spectrogram factorization, moving beyond traditional NMF to enable multi-resolution time-frequency decompositions. The core idea – formulating a generative model directly for the raw signal, rather than the STFT, and allowing for overcomplete bases – has the potential to improve source separation and denoising performance. The derivation of a time-domain interpretation of Fevotte et al.’s Gaussian Composite Model, extending it to non-orthogonal bases, is a notable contribution, offering a fresh perspective on existing work.

The paper is well-written and generally clear. The motivation is well-explained, and the connection to previous work (particularly Fevotte et al., Lee & Seung, and sparse Bayesian learning) is appropriately acknowledged. The mathematical notation is largely accessible, and the proposed EM algorithms are presented in a structured manner. The introduction of multi-resolution analysis builds logically on the core LRTFS model.

However, despite the promising idea, several weaknesses limit the paper’s impact. The originality, while present in the combination and extension of existing techniques, is somewhat constrained by the heavy reliance on Fevotte et al.’s GCM. The time-domain reformulation is valuable, but the overall contribution feels incremental. 

A significant concern is the potential complications introduced by using overcomplete bases, which the authors don’t fully address. While the paper states generality is achieved, it doesn't explore issues like increased ambiguity, computational cost, or sensitivity to noise that commonly arise with overcompleteness. This omission weakens the argument for the method's robustness. The discussion of the connection to sparse Bayesian learning also feels incomplete; while similarities are noted, a deeper exploration of the differences and consequences of the tied variances (compared to the free variances in SBL) would strengthen the analysis.

Crucially, the empirical evaluation is extremely thin. Only one illustrative music decomposition example and one SNR measurement for speech enhancement are provided. This is insufficient to support the broad claims made about improved accuracy, multi-resolution benefits, and applicability to various signal processing tasks. The evaluation setup also raises concerns. The comparison to OMLSA and IS-NMF isn't entirely fair, as the audio lengths differ. Moreover, the authors acknowledge perceiving higher speech quality from the baseline OMLSA method, and report significant artifacts in music denoising compared to conventional approaches—these are serious concerns that need thorough investigation and discussion. The lack of statistical significance tests and a wider range of test data further undermines the claims.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel extension of low-rank factorization to overcomplete bases.
*   Time-domain interpretation of the GCM offers a valuable alternative perspective.
*   Potential for multi-resolution analysis provides flexibility for diverse signal types.
*   Theoretically sound EM-based estimation procedure (although the derivation is hard to fully verify).

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Weak empirical validation with insufficient results and a potentially unfair comparison.
*   Lack of discussion regarding the challenges and implications of using overcomplete bases.
*   Limited originality, heavily building upon previous work by Fevotte et al.
*   The significance of the contribution is unproven due to the inadequate experimental demonstration.
*   Reported artifacts and subjective quality concerns in experimental results.

**Overall Assessment:**

The paper presents a potentially valuable idea, but its impact is currently limited by the lack of compelling empirical evidence and a thorough exploration of the implications of its design choices. While technically sound, the paper feels incomplete. I recommend **rejection** in its current form. The authors should significantly expand the experimental evaluation, address the concerns regarding overcomplete bases, and provide a more in-depth analysis of the connection to sparse Bayesian learning. A stronger empirical demonstration is crucial to establish the practical significance of the proposed LRTFS model.
