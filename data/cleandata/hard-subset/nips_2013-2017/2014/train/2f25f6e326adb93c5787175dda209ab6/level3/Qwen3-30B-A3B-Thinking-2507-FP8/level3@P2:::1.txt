

# Review for "Low-Rank Time-Frequency Synthesis"

This paper proposes a novel signal synthesis model that extends nonnegative matrix factorization (NMF) approaches for audio processing by moving from the spectrogram domain to the raw signal domain. The authors build upon Fevotte et al.'s Gaussian Composite Model (GCM), which provided a generative probabilistic model for the STFT, by introducing a low-rank time-frequency synthesis (LRTFS) model that directly generates the raw audio signal. The key innovation is the derivation of a time-domain interpretation of the GCM, enabling the use of arbitrary (potentially overcomplete) time-frequency bases, which naturally supports multi-resolution representations (e.g., tonal and transient components).

The paper makes several theoretically sound contributions. First, it provides a clear generative model for the raw signal that explicitly incorporates the low-rank structure of the synthesis coefficients, rather than the analysis coefficients as in GCM. Second, it establishes a connection to sparse Bayesian learning (SBL), showing how the low-rank constraint in LRTFS differs fundamentally from SBL's sparse variance structure. Third, it derives two expectation-maximization algorithms for estimation in the new model, with the maximum joint likelihood estimation (MJLE) approach being computationally efficient and avoiding matrix inversions.

The multi-resolution capability is particularly promising, as it allows the model to represent different components (e.g., tonal and transient) with their own temporal resolutions. This is a significant advantage over traditional NMF approaches, which operate on the transformed data and cannot adapt resolution during decomposition. The authors demonstrate this capability in two audio applications: music decomposition (tonal + transient + residual) and speech enhancement.

**Strengths:**
1. The time-domain reformulation of the GCM is theoretically elegant and provides a fresh perspective on existing work.
2. The multi-resolution capability is a genuine conceptual advance with potential to address real audio processing challenges.
3. The MJLE algorithm is computationally efficient, avoiding the expensive matrix inversions required by the alternative MMLE approach.
4. The paper is well-written with clear mathematical derivations and good organization.

**Weaknesses:**
1. **Insufficient empirical evaluation:** The paper presents only one illustrative music decomposition example and a single speech enhancement result with average SNR improvement of 6.6 dB (vs. 6 dB for IS-NMF and 4.6 dB for OMLSA). This minimal evaluation fails to convincingly demonstrate the practical significance of the approach. The music decomposition results appear to have significant artifacts compared to conventional methods, and the speech enhancement results don't clearly show advantages over existing approaches.
2. **Unfair comparison:** The speech enhancement comparison uses different audio lengths between methods (the paper's method uses 7-second segments while the baseline uses 16ms window, which is not comparable).
3. **Lack of discussion on overcomplete bases:** The paper claims to work with arbitrary bases but doesn't address potential complications that overcomplete bases might introduce (e.g., non-uniqueness, numerical instability), which is a significant concern in signal processing.
4. **Theoretical claims not fully supported:** While the theoretical development appears sound, the paper doesn't provide sufficient evidence that the multi-resolution capability actually leads to better performance in practical applications.

**Originality:** The paper builds substantially on Fevotte et al.'s work, though the time-domain reformulation and extension to raw signal synthesis does offer a fresh perspective. The multi-resolution capability is a meaningful extension that differentiates this work from previous NMF approaches for audio.

**Significance:** The potential significance of the approach is plausible, as multi-resolution representations could be valuable for many audio processing tasks. However, the current empirical results fail to convincingly demonstrate this significance, making it difficult to assess the practical impact of the work.

**Quality:** The theoretical development appears technically sound, though the paper would benefit from more detailed discussion of the practical challenges with overcomplete bases. The empirical evaluation is the main weakness, as it is insufficient to support the claims made.

**Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and clear, with good mathematical presentation. However, some derivations (particularly the connection to SBL) could be more accessible.

**Recommendation:** The paper presents an interesting theoretical extension to existing work with potential practical implications. However, the extremely limited empirical evaluation fails to demonstrate the claimed advantages over existing methods. The paper would require substantial additional experiments to properly evaluate the practical significance of the proposed approach, particularly addressing the concerns about overcomplete bases and providing fairer comparisons with baselines.

**Arguments for acceptance:**
- The theoretical extension of GCM to raw signal synthesis is novel and well-motivated.
- The multi-resolution capability is a genuine conceptual advance with clear potential benefits.
- The MJLE algorithm is computationally efficient and avoids the pitfalls of MMLE.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
- The empirical results are insufficient to support the claims of improved performance.
- The comparison with baselines appears unfair due to inconsistent experimental setups.
- The paper doesn't adequately address potential challenges with overcomplete bases.
- The significance of the approach is unproven with the current evidence.

Overall, this paper presents an interesting theoretical contribution that could be valuable if properly validated. However, the current empirical evaluation is too weak to support the claims made, and the paper would need substantially more experiments to demonstrate the practical significance of the proposed approach before it could be accepted at NIPS.
