Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes Low-Rank Time-Frequency Synthesis (LRTFS), a generative model of the raw time-domain signal whose synthesis coefficients α are endowed with a low-rank variance structure v = WH. This contrasts with Itakura–Saito NMF (IS-NMF) and the Gaussian Composite Model (GCM), which posit a low-rank variance on STFT analysis coefficients y. The authors develop two estimation strategies: (i) a scalable maximum joint likelihood EM algorithm (MJLE) with a Wiener-like update for α and an IS-NMF subproblem for W,H; and (ii) a maximum marginal likelihood approach (MMLE) akin to sparse Bayesian learning (SBL), which is elegant but computationally heavy here. Crucially, the synthesis viewpoint naturally enables multi-resolution modeling: the signal is a sum of synthesis layers with different time-frequency bases (e.g., short and long Gabor windows), each with its own low-rank structure. Experiments illustrate tonal/transient music decomposition and semi-supervised speech enhancement.

The work builds on: IS-NMF/GCM links (Fevotte et al.), SBL/type-II ML (Tipping; Wipf & Rao), EM-based shrinkage (Figueiredo & Nowak), and hybrid/morphological TF decompositions. The novelty lies in moving the low-rank probabilistic modeling from analysis to synthesis, thus bridging generative modeling of the STFT and of the waveform, and enabling multi-resolution within a single probabilistic factorization framework.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The model is clearly specified; the MJLE objective combines a reconstruction term, an IS divergence penalty on |α|^2 vs v, and a surprising log(|α|^2) sparsity term arising from the joint likelihood. The EM derivation for MJLE is convincing and computationally practical (no large inversions; uses Φ and Φ*). The MMLE route is standard for SBL but currently impractical at audio scale; this is candidly acknowledged.
- Empirical support: Multi-resolution decomposition examples are appealing, and the speech enhancement experiment shows SNR gains over a single-resolution IS-NMF baseline and OMLSA. However, evaluation is limited: small number of signals, a single metric (SNR), hand-tuned λ, and no runtime or convergence analysis.
- Correctness/clarity nitpicks: Eq. (29)-(30) seem to miss the “0” mean (Nc(0, [WH]fn)). Please clarify β selection (β ≤ λ/δΦ) and how λ is set or scheduled in practice.

Clarity
The paper is generally well written and organized. The relation to GCM and SBL is clear, and the multi-resolution extension is well motivated. Some suggestions:
- Unify the m versus (f,n) indexing early; define all shapes at first use; restate assumptions on Φ (frame tightness, redundancy).
- Provide a compact algorithm box for MJLE (single- and multi-resolution) with complexities per iteration.
- Explain initialization and permutation/scale handling for W,H; discuss identifiability.
- Discuss the practical choice/sensitivity of β and λ; the manuscript notes that joint λ estimation performs poorly—give guidelines.

Originality
Modeling low-rank structure on synthesis coefficients, rather than analysis coefficients, and doing so for multi-resolution TF frames is novel and conceptually neat. The EM architecture that reduces to IS-NMF on |α|^2 is elegant. The connection to SBL with structured variances (tied via WH) is also interesting.

Significance
If adopted, LRTFS could influence how we factorize signals directly at the waveform level and enable principled multi-resolution decompositions. The approach has potential beyond audio (e.g., biomedical signals, radar). At present, the experimental validation is promising but not sufficient to fully establish practical advantages over strong single-resolution baselines across tasks.

Strengths
- Clear, principled generative model of the time-domain signal.
- Multi-resolution capability not available in traditional IS-NMF/GCM.
- Scalable EM algorithm; per-iteration steps reuse standard IS-NMF solvers and efficient TF operators.
- Appealing qualitative decompositions and measurable speech enhancement gains.

Weaknesses
- Limited quantitative evaluation and ablations; λ is tuned post hoc for best SNR.
- No runtime or convergence-speed comparison to IS-NMF; no stability study.
- No single-resolution sanity check demonstrating alignment with IS-NMF factors when using the same frame.
- MMLE remains theoretical here; scalability left to future work.

Requested clarifications and experiments
- Single-resolution comparison to IS-NMF: Using the same STFT frame (Φ tight/Parseval), compare LRTFS (MJLE) to IS-NMF on the magnitude spectrogram. Do W and H align up to scaling/permutation? Report correlation or Hungarian-aligned similarities between columns of W, rows of H, and reconstruction error. Show cases across different redundancies and λ→0.
- Algorithm speed and stability: Plot objective versus wall-clock time and iterations for MJLE versus standard multiplicative IS-NMF on matched problems. Report average iteration cost (Φ/Φ* FLOPs, NMF updates), number of iterations to convergence, and monotonicity of the likelihood. Comment on numerical stability and sensitivity to initialization.
- Multi-resolution gains: Provide ablations comparing (i) single-resolution LRTFS; (ii) IS-NMF; (iii) multi-resolution LRTFS with 2+ frames. Use metrics beyond SNR (e.g., BSS Eval, PESQ/STOI for speech). Show when and why multi-resolution helps (transients vs sustained content).
- Hyperparameters: Provide a principled λ schedule (e.g., warm-start annealing used in Section 5.2) and effect on quality; sensitivity to β; robustness across datasets. Clarify whether λ is per-sample or global.
- Complexity and reproducibility: Report runtimes on the given tasks; release code/audio to support adoption.

Pros for acceptance
- Novel and elegant synthesis-side probabilistic factorization with a natural multi-resolution extension.
- Sound derivations and a practical EM algorithm with clear links to well-known methods.
- Potentially impactful idea for waveform-level modeling.

Cons for acceptance
- Experimental section is not yet thorough enough to establish superiority or parity in single-resolution settings and to quantify multi-resolution advantages.
- Missing runtime/stability comparisons to canonical IS-NMF multiplicative updates.
- Some practical aspects (λ, β, initialization) under-specified.

Overall
An interesting and appealing contribution. I lean toward acceptance if the authors can (i) add a careful single-resolution comparison to IS-NMF (including factor alignment), (ii) provide speed/stability evidence relative to multiplicative IS-NMF, and (iii) strengthen evaluation with broader metrics and ablations to substantiate the multi-resolution benefits.
