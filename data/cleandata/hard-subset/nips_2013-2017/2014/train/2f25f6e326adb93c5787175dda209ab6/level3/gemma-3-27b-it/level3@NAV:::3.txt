## Review of “Low-Rank Time-Frequency Synthesis”

This paper introduces a novel low-rank time-frequency synthesis (LRTFS) model, extending previous work on generative Gaussian Composite Models (GCMs) for signal decomposition. The core idea is to directly model the raw signal as a sum of time-frequency atoms with low-rank structure on their variances, moving beyond STFT-based approaches and enabling multi-resolution representations. The authors present two Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithms for estimation and demonstrate applications to music decomposition and speech enhancement.

The work builds logically on the existing literature surrounding NMF and GCMs, notably Févotte et al. [3], and positions itself as a step towards a fully generative model of the signal. The theoretical development is solid and offers an interesting departure from traditional spectral decomposition formulations. The paper is very well-written and clearly explains the model and algorithms. The inclusion of multi-resolution analysis represents a potentially valuable extension, addressing limitations of standard NMF. 

However, the paper’s impact is hindered by disappointing experimental results. While the provided examples are illustrative, the SNR improvements in speech enhancement are modest and comparable to existing methods (like OMLSA [14]). The supplementary material, alluded to for listening examples, is crucial but not wholly convincing, as the improvement over baselines isn’t substantial based on the reported SNR figures. Critically, the paper lacks empirical comparisons to existing methods *directly building* on the GCM framework – a key point of reference given the work’s foundation. Furthermore, the authors acknowledge limitations with the MMLE approach due to computational complexity, reducing the scope of the practical evaluation. 

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Solid theoretical foundation and interesting novel approach.
*   Clear and well-written presentation.
*   Potential for multi-resolution representations.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Weak experimental validation and limited SNR improvements.
*   Lack of comparison with related methods building on the GCM.
*   Unconvincing supplemental material.
*   Significance isn’t fully communicated – the “ultimate gap” filled doesn't translate to a demonstrably significant improvement in performance.



**Overall:** This is a good paper with a novel contribution. Despite the theoretical strengths and clarity, the limited and underwhelming empirical results prevent a strong recommendation for acceptance. The authors should substantially strengthen the experimental evaluation, including rigorous comparisons to related work, to fully demonstrate the benefits of their proposed approach. A stronger articulation of the significance of the work beyond simply "filling a gap" would also be beneficial. I recommend acceptance with major revisions.
