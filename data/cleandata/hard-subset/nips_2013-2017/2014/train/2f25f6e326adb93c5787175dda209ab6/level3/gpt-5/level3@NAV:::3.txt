This paper proposes Low-Rank Time-Frequency Synthesis (LRTFS), a generative, time-domain model in which synthesis coefficients over arbitrary time-frequency frames are Gaussian with a low-rank variance parametrized by WH. This departs from the common STFT-domain formulations (e.g., IS-NMF and the Gaussian Composite Model, GCM) that model analysis coefficients or magnitude spectra, and connects to sparse Bayesian learning (SBL) by marginalizing synthesis coefficients (MMLE) or jointly estimating them (MJLE). The framework naturally supports multi-resolution representations by combining multiple frames. Two EM algorithms are derived; MJLE scales via analysis/synthesis operations, while MMLE is principled but currently impractical. Applications to music decomposition and speech enhancement are presented.

Quality: The theoretical development is solid. The probabilistic construction, EM derivations, and the relation to Wiener filtering and IS-NMF are sound and clearly argued. The MJLE algorithm is elegant and computationally reasonable. However, the empirical section is underpowered: datasets are small, metrics limited, gains modest (e.g., 6.6 vs 6.0 dB over IS-NMF), and the supplementary audio evidence is not compelling. Key choices (e.g., λ scheduling, β, dictionary sizes) lack ablations, and convergence/runtime analysis is missing. Comparisons omit strong baselines that the paper builds upon (e.g., GCM/STFT-domain Wiener filtering with identical dictionaries, SBL variants).

Clarity: The paper is well written and easy to follow; notation and connections to prior work (IS-NMF, GCM, SBL, multiresolution MCA) are carefully laid out. The significance and “when to use” guidance could be argued more convincingly.

Originality: Above average. Shifting low-rank modeling from analysis to synthesis coefficients and decoupling from the STFT to arbitrary frames is a meaningful conceptual contribution; the multi-resolution extension is natural but useful.

Significance: Potentially impactful for generative audio, multiresolution modeling, and inverse problems. Current evidence does not yet demonstrate clear superiority over STFT-domain methods or modern speech enhancement pipelines.

Suggestions: Provide rigorous comparisons to GCM/IS-NMF with Wiener filtering under matched settings; include SBL-type baselines; add ablations (λ, β, K, frame choices), complexity/convergence plots, and objective metrics (SDR/SI-SDR/PESQ/STOI) on larger datasets; highlight cases where time-domain synthesis is decisively advantageous; release code.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Solid, elegant theory with clear links to NMF/GCM/SBL
- Flexible, frame-agnostic, multi-resolution synthesis perspective
- Well written; contributes a fresh viewpoint

Arguments against acceptance:
- Limited and unconvincing empirical validation
- Missing key comparisons and ablations
- MMLE not yet scalable; practical gains unclear
