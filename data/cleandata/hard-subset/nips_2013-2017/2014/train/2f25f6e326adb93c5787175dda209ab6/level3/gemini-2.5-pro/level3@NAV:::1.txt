This paper presents Low-Rank Time-Frequency Synthesis (LRTFS), a novel generative model for time-domain signals. The work extends the well-established framework of low-rank spectrogram factorization, building directly upon Fevotte et al.'s Gaussian Composite Model (GCM). The key contribution is a time-domain reformulation of the GCM, which enables the use of arbitrary, potentially overcomplete synthesis bases. This generalization is a significant step, as it naturally accommodates multi-resolution analysis, allowing for the simultaneous decomposition of signal components with different time-frequency characteristics, such as tonal and transient parts of an audio signal. The paper derives an EM-based procedure for parameter estimation.

The primary strength of this work is its conceptual elegance. The idea of imposing a low-rank structure on synthesis coefficients over flexible, overcomplete bases is interesting and potentially impactful for signal processing. The reformulation of a frequency-domain model into an explicit time-domain impulse-response model offers a fresh and valuable perspective. The manuscript is also generally well-written and easy to understand.

However, the paper suffers from several major weaknesses that undermine its contribution. While the EM derivation is presented, it was difficult to follow in detail. More importantly, the paper fails to address or discuss the theoretical complications that should arise from using overcomplete bases, such as the non-uniqueness of the resulting decompositions.

The most critical flaw is the empirical evaluation, which is insufficient to support the claimed benefits for source separation and denoising. The experiments are limited to one illustrative example and a single SNR measurement for speech enhancement. This thin validation fails to demonstrate the practical importance of the proposed method. Specific issues further weaken the results: the comparison to a baseline may be unfair due to differing audio lengths, the baseline OMLSA method subjectively produced higher-quality speech, and the music denoising example exhibited significant audible artifacts not present in conventional methods.

In conclusion, while the originality lies in the clever time-domain reformulation, the work's significance is unproven. The plausible theoretical benefits are not backed by convincing empirical evidence. As it stands, the paper does not demonstrate a clear advance over the state of the art and requires a much more thorough experimental validation to be considered a complete contribution.
