This paper proposes a novel signal synthesis model with low-rank time-frequency structure, referred to as Low-Rank Time-Frequency Synthesis (LRTFS). The authors aim to bridge the gap between time-frequency synthesis and traditional Nonnegative Matrix Factorization (NMF) approaches. The LRTFS model is an extension of the Gaussian Composite Model (GCM) that was previously proposed for modeling the Short-Time Fourier Transform (STFT) of a signal. The key difference between LRTFS and GCM is that LRTFS models the raw signal itself, rather than its STFT.

The LRTFS model is defined by a set of equations that describe the generative process of the signal. The signal is represented as a linear combination of time-frequency atoms, where each atom is associated with a synthesis coefficient. The synthesis coefficients are assumed to be conditionally independent and have a low-rank latent structure. The authors also propose two estimation methods for the LRTFS model: Maximum Joint Likelihood Estimation (MJLE) and Maximum Marginal Likelihood Estimation (MMLE).

The MJLE method is based on the optimization of the joint likelihood of the signal and the synthesis coefficients. The authors employ an Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm to minimize the negative log-likelihood function. The EM algorithm consists of an E-step and an M-step, where the E-step computes the posterior mean of the hidden variable, and the M-step updates the model parameters.

The MMLE method, on the other hand, is based on the optimization of the marginal likelihood of the signal. The authors also employ an EM algorithm to minimize the negative log-likelihood function. However, the MMLE method is more computationally expensive than the MJLE method, and the authors do not use it in their experiments.

The authors also propose a multi-resolution extension of the LRTFS model, which allows for the representation of signals with different time-frequency resolutions. The multi-resolution LRTFS model is based on the use of multiple time-frequency bases, where each basis is associated with a different time-frequency resolution.

The authors evaluate the performance of the LRTFS model on two audio signal processing tasks: music decomposition and speech enhancement.
