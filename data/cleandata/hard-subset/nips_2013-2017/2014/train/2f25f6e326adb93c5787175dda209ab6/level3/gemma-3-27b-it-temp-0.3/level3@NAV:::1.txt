## Review of “Low-Rank Time-Frequency Synthesis”

This paper presents a novel approach to signal decomposition, extending low-rank spectrogram factorization to arbitrary (potentially overcomplete) bases, enabling multi-resolution representations. The core idea builds upon Févotte et al.’s Gaussian Composite Model (GCM) by deriving a time-domain interpretation, effectively moving from a model of the STFT to a model of the raw signal itself. This is achieved by explicitly representing implicit convolutions as impulse responses, allowing for non-orthogonal bases. The authors propose EM-based algorithms for estimation, and demonstrate the approach with music decomposition and speech enhancement examples.

The work addresses a significant problem in audio processing – effectively representing and decomposing signals – and the extension of low-rank methods to overcomplete bases is a potentially impactful contribution. The time-domain reformulation of the GCM is a clever step, offering increased flexibility and opening the door to multi-resolution analysis, which is particularly relevant for signals with both tonal and transient components. The connection to sparse Bayesian learning is also insightful, providing a broader context for the proposed model.

However, the paper suffers from several weaknesses. While the core idea is interesting, the implications of using overcomplete bases are not thoroughly discussed. Overcompleteness can introduce redundancy and potential instability, and the paper doesn’t adequately address how these issues are mitigated or their impact on performance. The clarity of the EM algorithm derivations is also a concern; the reviewer notes difficulty following the details. 

More critically, the empirical evaluation is extremely thin. Only one illustrative music decomposition and a single SNR measurement for speech enhancement are presented. This is insufficient to support the broad claims made about the method’s benefits. The evaluation also raises concerns: the comparison to OMLSA appears potentially unfair due to differing audio lengths, and the authors acknowledge perceived higher speech quality with the baseline. Furthermore, the music denoising results reportedly exhibit significant artifacts compared to conventional methods. 

The originality is somewhat limited by the strong reliance on Févotte et al.’s work, though the time-domain reformulation does offer a fresh perspective. The significance is plausible, but remains unproven due to the weak empirical demonstration. The current results do not convincingly establish the practical importance of the proposed approach.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novel extension of low-rank methods to overcomplete bases.
*   Insightful time-domain reformulation of the GCM.
*   Potential for multi-resolution signal representation.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   Insufficient empirical evaluation to support claims.
*   Potential issues with overcomplete bases not adequately addressed.
*   Unclear EM algorithm derivations.
*   Concerns regarding fairness of experimental comparisons.



**Overall Recommendation:** Reject. The paper presents an interesting idea, but the lack of robust empirical validation and the unresolved concerns regarding overcompleteness and clarity prevent a positive recommendation. Substantial revisions, including a more thorough evaluation and a clearer explanation of the theoretical aspects, are needed before this work could be considered for publication.
