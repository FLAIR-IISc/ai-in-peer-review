Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes Low-Rank Time-Frequency Synthesis (LRTFS), a generative model of the raw signal that imposes a low-rank time–frequency structure on synthesis coefficients. Specifically, a signal is modeled as a linear combination of atoms from an arbitrary (possibly overcomplete) time–frequency frame, with complex Gaussian synthesis coefficients whose variances factorize as WH. This shifts the low-rank modeling assumption from the short-time Fourier transform (STFT) analysis coefficients (as in the Gaussian Composite Model, GCM, of Févotte et al., Neural Computation 2009) to the synthesis coefficients, thereby making the model directly generative in the time domain and enabling the use of multiple frames for multi-resolution representations. The authors derive two estimation procedures: (i) a scalable EM algorithm for maximum joint likelihood estimation (MJLE), inspired by Figueiredo & Nowak’s EM architecture for Gaussian denoising, which alternates a Wiener-like shrinkage of α and IS-NMF on |α|^2; and (ii) a maximum marginal likelihood (MMLE) algorithm akin to Sparse Bayesian Learning (Tipping 2001; Wipf & Rao 2004) that is not currently scalable. They demonstrate two applications: a tonal/transient music decomposition and a semi-supervised speech enhancement experiment, reporting modest SNR gains over IS-NMF and OMLSA (Cohen 2003).

The work builds clearly on: IS-NMF/GCM for spectrogram modeling (Févotte et al.), SBL-type type-II ML, and hybrid/morphological decompositions using multi-resolution frames (Daudet & Torrésani; Elad et al.; Kowalski & Torrésani). The contribution is a synthesis-domain, low-rank probabilistic formulation that cleanly accommodates multiple, nonorthogonal frames.

Strengths
- Conceptually clean bridge from STFT-domain GCM to a time-domain generative model with low-rank structure on synthesis coefficients.
- EM algorithm for MJLE is elegant and practical: no large matrix inversions, relies on standard analysis/synthesis operators (e.g., LTFAT).
- Natural and flexible handling of multi-resolution (tonal/transient) decompositions within a single probabilistic framework.
- Clear connection to Wiener filtering and to SBL; good positioning within the literature.

Weaknesses
- Empirical evaluation is thin: one illustrative decomposition and one speech enhancement study with limited metrics, no statistical analysis, and few baselines.
- Overcompleteness introduces identifiability and leakage issues that are not analyzed; conditions on frames (e.g., frame bounds, coherence) and their effect on estimation are not discussed.
- The claimed practical advantages are plausible but not convincingly demonstrated; several audible artifacts remain, and in some cases the OMLSA baseline sounds better despite lower SNR.
- MMLE is presented but not scalable; λ estimation is acknowledged as problematic; guidance on β, λ, and convergence/stopping is limited.
- Derivations are terse in places; the “unexpected” log term and its practical effect are not fully elucidated.

Quality
- The modeling and MJLE algorithm appear technically sound, but some steps are hard to verify from the main text. A more detailed derivation (supplementary) would help, especially for the auxiliary-variable EM construction, the origin of the log(|α|^2) term, and the precise conditions on β ≤ λ/δΦ.
- Lack of analysis on identifiability and component leakage across overlapping frames is a methodological gap, especially important with overcomplete bases and multi-resolution layers.
- Experimental design leaves room for confounds: choice of windows and K, pretraining for speech, and warm-restart schedules may advantage the proposed method. Hyperparameter sensitivity is not explored.

Clarity
- Generally well written and well organized. The relation to GCM and SBL is useful, and the algorithmic steps are stated clearly.
- Some notation and derivations are dense; adding pseudo-code, explicit complexity analysis, and a discussion of convergence criteria would help.
- The “multi-resolution not possible in NMF” claim is too strong; prior NMF work has used multi-window or multiresolution features, though the present synthesis-domain formulation is arguably more principled.

Originality
- Moderate. The core ingredients (IS-NMF/GCM, SBL/type-II ML, EM with auxiliary variables, multi-resolution frames) are known, but their combination in a synthesis-domain low-rank generative model is novel and interesting. The reformulation offers a fresh perspective on low-rank structure and phase handling.

Significance
- Potentially significant: a unified, scalable, multi-resolution, time-domain low-rank model is attractive for audio analysis, source separation, and denoising. However, the present experiments do not yet establish a clear practical advantage over strong baselines across diverse conditions.

Questions for the authors
- What conditions on Φ (frame bounds, redundancy, coherence) affect identifiability and leakage across components/layers? Any guidance on designing Φa/Φb?
- How sensitive are results to β, λ, K, and the warm-restart schedule? Can you provide ablations?
- Can you quantify convergence behavior (objective vs. iterations, wall-clock) and provide complexity scaling with T, F, N, K?
- Why is λ estimation unstable, and are there principled regularizations that fix it?

Suggested additional experiments/analyses
- Broader benchmarks: more noise types and SNRs; report SDR/SIR/SAR (BSS Eval), STOI/PESQ for speech; include confidence intervals.
- Ablations: single- vs multi-resolution; analysis- vs synthesis-domain (GCM vs LRTFS); effect of redundancy; different K values.
- Baselines: multi-resolution NMF variants; MCA/structured sparsity hybrids; supervised/semi-supervised NMF with identical training.
- Perceptual evaluation (MUSHRA-like) or at least blind listening tests; publish code and audio.

Arguments for acceptance
- Clear, principled modeling contribution that extends GCM to the time domain and enables multi-resolution synthesis.
- Scalable EM algorithm with simple building blocks; likely to be useful to practitioners.
- Conceptually impactful and relevant to NIPS communities working on probabilistic models and audio.

Arguments for rejection
- Insufficient empirical evidence; improvements over strong baselines are small and not consistently corroborated by perceptual quality.
- Missing analysis of key issues introduced by overcomplete, multi-frame synthesis (identifiability, leakage, sensitivity).
- MMLE remains a promise; λ estimation instability unresolved; practical guidance limited.

Overall recommendation: Weak reject. The idea is interesting and potentially impactful, but the current empirical and analytical support is not strong enough for acceptance at NIPS. Strengthening the evaluation, clarifying theoretical aspects of overcomplete synthesis, and demonstrating clear gains over strong baselines would significantly improve the case.

Reviewer confidence: Moderate.
