

This paper proposes a novel Low-Rank Time-Frequency Synthesis (LRTFS) model that extends Fevotte et al.'s Gaussian Composite Model (GCM) to a time-domain generative framework. The authors derive a time-domain interpretation of the GCM, reformulating it to enable nonorthogonal (potentially overcomplete) bases for multi-resolution audio decomposition. The work builds on prior NMF-based approaches in audio processing, particularly the GCM framework presented in [3], but extends it to model the raw signal directly rather than its STFT. The paper introduces an EM-based estimation procedure for the model and demonstrates applications in music decomposition and speech enhancement.

The paper's main strength lies in its theoretical contribution: the time-domain reformulation of the GCM provides a fresh perspective on low-rank spectrogram factorization and enables natural multi-resolution representations (tonal + transient) that were not possible in traditional NMF settings. The idea of extending factorization to arbitrary bases is interesting and potentially impactful for audio processing.

However, the paper suffers from significant weaknesses in empirical evaluation. The results section provides only a single illustrative example and a single SNR measurement, which is insufficient to support the claimed benefits for source separation and denoising. The comparison with OMLSA appears potentially unfair due to different audio lengths, and the paper fails to address concerns about complications introduced by overcomplete bases. The music denoising results show significant artifacts compared to conventional methods, while the speech enhancement results show minimal improvement (6.6 dB vs. 6 dB for IS-NMF).

The derivation of the EM algorithm is technically sound but difficult to follow in detail, and the evaluation lacks sufficient depth to demonstrate practical significance. While the time-domain reformulation offers a novel perspective, the paper's originality is limited by heavy reliance on prior work. The significance claims are plausible but unproven due to weak empirical demonstration.

In summary, while the theoretical contribution is promising, the paper's weak empirical evaluation and unclear presentation of the EM derivation prevent it from convincingly demonstrating practical importance. The work would benefit from more comprehensive experiments and clearer exposition of the algorithm.
