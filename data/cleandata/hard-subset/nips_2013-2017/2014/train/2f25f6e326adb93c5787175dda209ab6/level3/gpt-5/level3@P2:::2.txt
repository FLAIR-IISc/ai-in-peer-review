Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes Low-Rank Time-Frequency Synthesis (LRTFS), a generative model of the raw time-domain signal where the synthesis coefficients over a time-frequency dictionary are Gaussian with low-rank variances parameterized by WH, with W,H ≥ 0. This contrasts with prior work such as the Gaussian Composite Model (GCM) underlying IS-NMF, which imposes low-rank variance on analysis coefficients (STFT). The paper develops two estimation strategies: (i) a scalable EM algorithm for maximum joint likelihood estimation (MJLE) using an auxiliary variable scheme inspired by Figueiredo–Nowak; and (ii) a type-II maximum marginal likelihood estimator (MMLE) akin to sparse Bayesian learning (SBL), which is currently not scalable for audio-length signals. A key contribution is that the synthesis view naturally accommodates multi-resolution modeling by summing over multiple frames (e.g., short and long Gabor frames). The paper demonstrates applications to hybrid music decomposition (tonal/transient) and to semi-supervised speech enhancement, with comparisons to IS-NMF and OMLSA.

Strengths
- Conceptual advance: moves the low-rank modeling from analysis to synthesis coefficients, yielding a fully generative model of the waveform, not just the STFT. This bridges NMF/GCM and synthesis modeling, with a clear probabilistic formulation.
- Multi-resolution capability: formulation over a union of frames is neat and practically appealing for audio, where transients and steady-state components live at different time scales. The extension and EM updates are simple and computationally reasonable.
- Algorithmic clarity for MJLE: the EM updates are explicit, require no large matrix inversions, and leverage standard IS-NMF multiplicative updates for W,H. The latent “Wiener-like” update for α is intuitive and connects to classical shrinkage/filtering.
- Empirical promise: music hybrid decomposition produces interpretable components; speech enhancement shows average SNR improvements over an IS-NMF baseline and OMLSA.

Weaknesses and questions
- Limited quantitative evaluation of the novel synthesis vs analysis modeling: beyond the speech enhancement SNR comparison, there is little controlled assessment of what is gained by modeling synthesis coefficients (e.g., reconstruction fidelity, robustness to phase, sensitivity to window choice).
- Missing single-resolution comparison against IS-NMF on the same transform: If the frame Φ corresponds to the STFT synthesis operator used by IS-NMF, how do the learned W,H compare (qualitatively and quantitatively) between LRTFS and IS-NMF? Do they converge to similar dictionaries/activations on the same data, and under what conditions (tight frames, λ → 0, etc.)?
- Speed and stability: While MJLE is described as computationally friendly, the paper does not provide runtime or convergence curves. How do iterations/time to convergence and stability compare to standard IS-NMF multiplicative updates on comparable problems?
- MMLE scalability: The MMLE route is well motivated but currently inapplicable at audio scales; the paper would benefit from at least small-scale numerical results to illustrate potential advantages (e.g., better model selection, robustness).
- Hyperparameters and identifiability: λ is treated as a hyperparameter or annealed to near zero; this can bias estimates and risks overfitting. How sensitive are results to λ and β? How are W,H initialized and how is the scale ambiguity handled across layers in the multi-resolution setting?
- Evaluation breadth: For music decomposition, the evaluation is mostly qualitative. For speech enhancement, only SNR is reported on 10 files; perceptual metrics (STOI, PESQ), BSS-Eval (SDR/SIR/SAR), and statistical significance would strengthen claims.

Quality
The model and MJLE derivations appear sound, with appropriate connections to IS-NMF and SBL. The EM steps are consistent, and the computational considerations are realistic. However, the empirical section is relatively small, and ablations (e.g., single-resolution LRTFS vs IS-NMF, one vs two frames) are missing. The decision to largely fix/anneal λ is practical but should be justified with sensitivity analyses.

Clarity
The paper is generally clear and well organized. The core ideas and updates are presented succinctly. Suggestions:
- Add pseudocode for MJLE (single- and multi-resolution) with explicit stopping criteria.
- Report per-iteration complexity and wall-clock timings vs IS-NMF.
- Clarify handling of real signals via Hermitian symmetry and any constraints on Φ (tight frames, normalization).
- Detail initialization of W,H,α and the choices of β, λ, K.

Originality
Moderately high. Modeling low-rank structure in the synthesis coefficients of a TF frame, with a coherent EM procedure and a simple multi-resolution extension, is a novel and timely twist on NMF/GCM. The connection to SBL is insightful, highlighting the structured variance tying via WH instead of per-coefficient variances.

Significance
Potentially significant for audio representation, separation, and enhancement, especially for signals with multi-scale structure. The model could influence subsequent work in multichannel and inverse problems. Impact would be strengthened by broader, more rigorous empirical validation.

Requested comparisons and analyses
- Single-resolution head-to-head with IS-NMF on the same signal and TF parameters: Do W and H match (up to scaling/permutation) when Φ corresponds to the STFT frame used by IS-NMF? If not, characterize differences and when they arise (effects of λ, β, tight vs non-tight frames).
- Speed/stability: Provide runtime per iteration, number of iterations to convergence, and relative decrease per iteration for MJLE vs IS-NMF multiplicative updates; include convergence plots and sensitivity to initialization.
- Ablations: One-frame LRTFS vs two-frame LRTFS; effect of λ and β; effect of K.
- Metrics: Add STOI/PESQ and BSS-Eval metrics; expand test sets.

Pros for acceptance
- Clear, principled generative model of the waveform with low-rank TF structure.
- Elegant and practical multi-resolution extension not easily achievable with standard IS-NMF.
- Scalable EM algorithm with intuitive updates and plausible results on two tasks.
- Conceptual bridge between NMF/GCM and synthesis/SBL communities.

Cons for acceptance
- Empirical evaluation is limited in scope and depth; missing key head-to-heads with IS-NMF in single-resolution settings and runtime/stability analyses.
- MMLE contribution is undeveloped experimentally due to scalability.
- Heavy reliance on manual λ setting/annealing without sensitivity study.

Overall assessment
A well-motivated and technically solid paper that introduces a compelling synthesis-centric alternative to IS-NMF and naturally enables multi-resolution modeling. With strengthened experimental validation—particularly the requested single-resolution comparisons to IS-NMF, runtime/stability analysis, and broader metrics—the paper would make a valuable contribution. I find the multi-resolution extension especially appealing; more thorough evaluation would substantially increase confidence in the practical benefits.
