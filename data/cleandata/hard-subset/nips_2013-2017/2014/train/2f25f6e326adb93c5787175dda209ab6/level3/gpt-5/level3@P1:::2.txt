This paper proposes a generative, time-domain model of single-channel signals whose time-frequency (TF) synthesis coefficients have low-rank covariance structure. Unlike standard NMF/IS-NMF and the Gaussian Composite Model (GCM), which place a low-rank model on the variance of STFT analysis coefficients, the Low-Rank Time-Frequency Synthesis (LRTFS) model draws complex synthesis coefficients αfn ∼ Nc(0, [WH]fn) and generates the waveform as x(t) = ∑fn αfn φfn(t) + e(t). The authors give two estimation schemes: (i) maximum joint likelihood estimation (MJLE) via an EM algorithm that alternates Wiener-type shrinkage of α and an IS-NMF M-step on |α|2; (ii) maximum marginal likelihood estimation (MMLE) in the spirit of sparse Bayesian learning (SBL), which integrates out α but is currently computationally prohibitive for audio-scale problems. A notable contribution is a multi-resolution extension that stacks multiple TF frames (e.g., short and long Gabor windows) in the synthesis, enabling joint modeling of transients and sustained components—something the traditional NMF-on-spectrogram pipeline cannot natively do. Experiments on hybrid music decomposition and semi-supervised speech enhancement show qualitative component separation and SNR improvements over IS-NMF and OMLSA.

Relation to prior work: The paper builds directly on IS-NMF and its GCM interpretation, extending the generative modeling level from the STFT to the waveform. It also connects to SBL/RVM by showing that LRTFS is a structured (non-separable) Gaussian scale model where coefficient variances are tied by WH rather than independently learned, thereby trading sparsity for low-rank structure. The multi-resolution decomposition relates to MCA and hybrid audio models, but here the low-rank priors replace sparsity priors and are embedded in a coherent generative synthesis model.

Quality: The modeling is sound and the MJLE-EM derivation is convincing. The “unexpected” log(|α|2) term in the joint objective is interesting; a concise derivation in an appendix would help readers verify it. The EM architecture avoids large matrix inversions and is practical with fast analysis/synthesis routines. The MMLE route is principled but currently unscalable; acknowledging this and scoping it to smaller problems is appropriate. Empirical validation is promising but limited in scope and depth; several critical baselines and diagnostics are missing (see requests below).

Clarity: Overall well written. The switch between indexing by m and (f, n) could be streamlined. It would help to (a) summarize the per-iteration complexity and memory cost vs IS-NMF; (b) provide pseudocode for MJLE (and multi-resolution MJLE) with explicit choices for β and λ; (c) clarify treatment of real signals via Hermitian symmetry in code; (d) state initialization and stopping criteria. A short discussion on identifiability and scale ambiguities (WH, α, window gains) would also help.

Originality: Modeling low-rank structure on synthesis coefficients to generate the raw signal, together with a computationally viable EM procedure and a clean multi-resolution extension, is novel with respect to IS-NMF/GCM and to sparse synthesis models. The observation that IS-NMF + Wiener filtering corresponds to the first MJLE iteration is insightful and conceptually unifying.

Significance: The ability to directly model the waveform while retaining low-rank TF structure and enabling multi-resolution layers is valuable for audio source separation, enhancement, and representation learning. If supported by broader evaluation and runtime evidence, the approach could be impactful beyond audio (e.g., biomedical signals, seismic).

Strengths
- Conceptual advance: bridges analysis (GCM/IS-NMF) and synthesis modeling at the waveform level.
- Practical EM algorithm (MJLE) with only analysis/synthesis calls; no large inversions.
- Natural multi-resolution extension with separate low-rank structures per layer.
- Clear connections to SBL illuminate modeling trade-offs.
- Promising results on music decomposition and speech enhancement.

Weaknesses / questions
- Limited evaluation and diagnostics; comparisons to IS-NMF/GCM are not comprehensive.
- No single-resolution head-to-head study to assess whether W, H and convergence properties align with IS-NMF when using the same frame.
- Computational aspects (speed, stability, sensitivity to β and λ) are not quantified vs IS-NMF’s multiplicative updates.
- λ is treated as a hyperparameter due to instability; more guidance and robustness analysis are needed.
- MMLE remains impractical at audio scale; no approximate scalable alternative is presented.

Requested clarifications and additional experiments
- Single-resolution equivalence: Using a single tight Gabor frame, compare LRTFS (MJLE) to IS-NMF/GCM on identical data. Do learned W and H (up to permutation/scale) match? Show factor alignment metrics and reconstruction error.
- Convergence and runtime: Report per-iteration cost, wall-clock time, and iteration counts to reach comparable objective decreases, versus IS-NMF multiplicative updates. Include convergence plots and sensitivity to initialization.
- Stability and hyperparameters: Explain how β is chosen (relation to λ/δΦ) and demonstrate robustness to β, λ choices. Since λ estimation performed poorly, provide a practical selection heuristic and show performance sensitivity.
- Multi-resolution gains: Ablate single- vs two-resolution with equal total K. Quantify gains (SNR/SDR/STOI/PESQ) and show which classes of signals benefit most.
- Additional baselines: Include GCM+Wiener filtering, and a “concatenated-spectrogram” IS-NMF baseline if feasible. For speech enhancement, add standard supervised/semi-supervised IS-NMF with identical dictionaries.
- Real-signal implementation: Detail enforcement of Hermitian symmetry and any impact on complexity.
- Reproducibility: Provide pseudocode and parameter settings (windows, hops, K per layer, stopping criteria).

Arguments for acceptance
- Novel and well-motivated generative model that closes a conceptual gap between spectrogram-factorization and waveform modeling.
- Elegant, scalable EM algorithm with clear links to established methods (IS-NMF, Wiener filtering, SBL).
- Multi-resolution modeling capability is compelling and broadly useful.
- Initial results are promising and indicate practical benefits.

Arguments against acceptance
- Empirical evaluation is not yet thorough enough to establish clear superiority or parity in single-resolution scenarios versus IS-NMF/GCM.
- Lack of quantitative runtime and stability comparisons to strong baselines.
- Key hyperparameter (λ) requires ad hoc tuning; guidance and robustness analyses are limited.
- MMLE contribution remains largely theoretical for the intended application scale.

Overall: A strong, conceptually significant paper with practical methodology. With the requested comparative studies and computational analysis, it would make a compelling NIPS contribution. I lean toward acceptance contingent on strengthening the experimental section.
