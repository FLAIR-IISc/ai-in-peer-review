Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes Low-Rank Time-Frequency Synthesis (LRTFS), a generative model of the time-domain signal whose synthesis coefficients are Gaussian with a variance constrained to be low-rank via an NMF (W,H) factorization. This contrasts with prior work that models the STFT analysis coefficients (e.g., the Gaussian Composite Model, GCM). The authors derive two estimation strategies: (i) a scalable maximum joint likelihood EM algorithm (MJLE) that alternates Wiener-like coefficient shrinkage and IS-NMF updates; and (ii) a maximum marginal likelihood EM algorithm (MMLE), akin to sparse Bayesian learning (SBL), which is currently computationally prohibitive at audio scale. A straightforward multi-resolution extension allows multiple frames (e.g., short vs long windows) to be used simultaneously, a key practical advantage over standard NMF on a fixed-resolution spectrogram.

This work sits at the crossroads of IS-NMF for audio (e.g., Fevotte et al.), probabilistic NMF of the STFT (GCM), and SBL/RVM. The main conceptual step is to move the low-rank variance structure from STFT analysis coefficients to synthesis coefficients in the time domain, yielding a bona fide generative model of the waveform and enabling multi-resolution representations. The paper demonstrates music decomposition and speech enhancement, comparing to IS-NMF and OMLSA.

Strengths
- Conceptual novelty: a clean, principled synthesis model that unifies low-rank structure and time-domain signal generation, clearly related to IS-NMF/GCM and SBL.
- Solid derivations: the MJLE-EM algorithm is well motivated and computationally practical; the MMLE-EM ties to “type-II” ML and SBL, with transparent limitations.
- Multi-resolution extension is elegant and easy to implement within the same framework.
- Writing is clear and organized; the connections to prior work are generally well explained.

Weaknesses
- Empirical evaluation is limited. Improvements over a strong IS-NMF baseline are modest (e.g., 6.6 dB vs 6 dB SNR gain), and the supplemental material does not convincingly demonstrate qualitative advantages.
- Insufficient comparative breadth: no head-to-head against GCM/Wiener filtering on STFT, PLCA variants, or multi-resolution sparse synthesis baselines (e.g., MCA, mixed-norm models).
- MMLE, while interesting, is not scalable in current form; no approximate large-scale solution is provided, making the comparison to MJLE incomplete.
- Practical choices (e.g., λ treated as a hyperparameter and tuned to maximize output SNR) risk optimistic assessment; clearer validation procedures are needed.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The modeling and EM derivations are correct to the best of my reading. The link between IS-NMF, Wiener filtering, and the first EM iteration is insightful. The appearance of a log(|α|^2) term in MJLE is interesting; a short discussion of its effect (sparsity vs shrinkage) and sensitivity would be helpful.
- Algorithmic robustness: MJLE is scalable and uses Φ/Φ* only, which is important for audio. MMLE’s complexity is a limitation; discussing approximate solvers (CG on the T×T system with implicit ΦΦ*, diagonal plus low-rank preconditioning, variational approximations, AMP/message passing) would strengthen the contribution.
- Empirical methodology: Results are reproducible in principle, but evaluation lacks breadth, ablations, and standardized metrics beyond SNR.

Clarity
- The paper is easy to follow. The relation to SBL and GCM is well articulated. Minor clarifications: explicitly summarize ambiguities/identifiability (scaling between W and H, redundancy in Φ), and provide guidance on choosing β (in the EM splitting) and λ (beyond hand tuning).

Originality
- Above average. Moving low-rank structure to synthesis coefficients and enabling multi-resolution in a single generative framework is a meaningful departure from STFT-based NMF/GCM. The algorithms combine known ingredients in a novel way tailored to time-domain modeling.

Significance
- Potentially significant, especially for applications needing multi-resolution modeling and for inverse problems where a time-domain prior is preferable. However, the current experiments do not fully demonstrate clear, practical advantages over strong baselines.

Detailed suggestions for improvement
- Comparisons: Include STFT-domain GCM with IS-NMF+Wiener filtering using matched windows; complex/phase-aware NMF variants where applicable; multi-resolution sparse synthesis baselines (e.g., MCA). Report BSS_eval metrics, PESQ/STOI (for speech), and perceptual listening tests with basic statistics.
- Ablations: (a) single vs multi-resolution; (b) analysis vs synthesis model; (c) effect of K and window sizes; (d) impact of λ schedules and β; (e) with/without the log(|α|^2) term (or analyze its influence).
- Scalability of MMLE: Provide an approximate solver (e.g., iterative inference with matrix-vector products via Φ, Φ*, conjugate gradients with Hutchinson trace estimates for the λ update) to enable at least medium-scale comparisons to MJLE.
- Hyperparameter selection: Choose λ via validation or an unsupervised criterion rather than oracle SNR. Discuss sensitivity and provide default heuristics.
- Clarity: Add a short appendix deriving the equivalence between IS-NMF+Wiener and the first EM iteration; discuss identifiability and convergence diagnostics.

Arguments for acceptance
- Clear, principled model that bridges a conceptual gap between STFT-domain NMF and time-domain generative modeling.
- Scalable MJLE algorithm with straightforward multi-resolution extension.
- Solid theoretical development and well-written presentation.

Arguments against acceptance
- Limited and only modestly positive empirical gains over strong baselines; supplemental audio not compelling.
- Missing comparisons to key related baselines and lack of ablations weaken the empirical case.
- MMLE contribution is incomplete without a scalable implementation.

Overall recommendation
A good, well-written paper with a solid theoretical contribution and a promising modeling idea. I lean toward weak accept, contingent on strengthening the experimental section with broader comparisons, ablations, and more rigorous evaluation.
