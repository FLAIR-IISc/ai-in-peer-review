This paper introduces a novel probabilistic framework for signal decomposition called Low-Rank Time-Frequency Synthesis (LRTFS). The main contribution is a conceptual shift from traditional Nonnegative Matrix Factorization (NMF) models, which impose a low-rank structure on the variance of time-frequency *analysis* coefficients (i.e., the spectrogram), to a model that imposes this structure on generative time-frequency *synthesis* coefficients. This allows LRTFS to serve as a generative model for the raw time-domain signal itself. A key advantage of this synthesis-based approach, as highlighted by the authors, is its natural ability to incorporate multi-resolution representations by combining different time-frequency frames, a capability that is not straightforward in standard NMF. The authors derive an EM algorithm for parameter estimation and demonstrate its use in audio applications.

**Strengths:**
The primary strength of this work lies in its originality and significance. The proposed synthesis-based model provides a more principled and complete generative story for low-rank signal decomposition than prior work like the Gaussian Composite Model for IS-NMF. The multi-resolution extension is particularly interesting and appealing. It addresses a well-known limitation of fixed-window STFT-based NMF and opens up promising avenues for more flexible modeling of complex signals, such as music, which contain both transient and tonal components requiring different time-frequency trade-offs. The paper is clearly written, and the model derivation is easy to follow.

**Weaknesses and Questions for the Authors:**
While the conceptual contribution is strong, the experimental validation could be more thorough.
1.  The multi-resolution decomposition results are compelling but largely illustrative. The paper would be stronger if it included a more quantitative evaluation demonstrating the performance gains of the multi-resolution approach compared to single-resolution methods on a benchmark source separation or enhancement task.
2.  In the single-resolution setting, the model is a direct alternative to IS-NMF. A crucial comparison is missing from the evaluation. Do the learned factors W and H from LRTFS converge to the same solutions as those obtained from standard IS-NMF on the power spectrogram? An experiment addressing this would help situate the proposed model relative to the established state-of-the-art.
3.  The paper should discuss the practical aspects of the proposed EM algorithm. How do its convergence speed and stability compare to the standard multiplicative updates for IS-NMF? This information is essential for assessing the algorithm's viability for large-scale signal processing tasks.

Overall, this paper presents an elegant and significant idea, but its practical advantages over existing methods require more rigorous empirical support.
