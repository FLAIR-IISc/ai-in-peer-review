The paper "Low-Rank Time-Frequency Synthesis" proposes a novel signal synthesis model that extends low-rank spectrogram factorization to arbitrary bases, enabling multiresolution decompositions. This approach has potential benefits for source separation and denoising. The authors derive a time-domain interpretation of the Gaussian Composite Model (GCM), expanding implicit convolutions into explicit impulse-response expressions and allowing for non-orthogonal bases.

The paper presents an Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm for local-optimum estimation, which is a significant contribution. However, the reviewer found the derivation challenging to follow in detail. The idea of applying matrix factorization to overcomplete bases is intriguing and potentially impactful, but the reviewer is uncertain about its full implications.

A concern is that overcomplete bases should introduce complications, which the reviewer did not see addressed or clarified in the paper. The clarity of the paper is generally good, but the evaluation is very thin, with only one illustrative result and one SNR measurement, which is insufficient to support the claims.

Specific evaluation issues include a possible unfair comparison (different audio lengths between methods), a perceived higher speech quality of the baseline (OMLSA), and significant artifacts in music denoising compared to conventional methods. The originality of the paper is limited by its heavy reliance on Fevotte et al., although the time-domain reformulation offers a fresh perspective.

The significance of the paper is plausible but unproven due to the weak empirical demonstration. The current results fail to convincingly show the practical importance of the proposed approach.

Strengths:

* The paper proposes a novel signal synthesis model that extends low-rank spectrogram factorization to arbitrary bases.
* The authors derive a time-domain interpretation of the Gaussian Composite Model (GCM), expanding implicit convolutions into explicit impulse-response expressions and allowing for non-orthogonal bases.
* The EM algorithm for local-optimum estimation is a significant contribution.

Weaknesses:

* The derivation of the EM algorithm is challenging to follow in detail.
* The paper does not address or clarify the complications introduced by overcomplete bases.
* The evaluation is very thin, with only one illustrative result and one SNR measurement.
* The comparison with the baseline (OMLSA) may be unfair due to different audio lengths.
* The paper relies heavily on Fevotte et al., which limits its originality.

Arguments for acceptance:

* The paper proposes a novel and potentially impactful approach to signal synthesis.
* The EM algorithm is a significant contribution to the field.
* The paper has good clarity and is well-organized.

Arguments against acceptance:

* The evaluation is insufficient to support the claims.
* The paper does not address the complications introduced by overcomplete bases.
* The comparison with the baseline may be unfair.
* The paper relies heavily on previous work, which limits its originality.

Recommendation:

The paper has potential, but the evaluation needs to be significantly improved to support the claims. The authors should address the complications introduced by overcomplete bases and provide a more thorough comparison with the baseline. Additionally, the paper could benefit from a more detailed explanation of the EM algorithm derivation. With revisions to address these concerns, the paper could be a strong contribution to the field.
