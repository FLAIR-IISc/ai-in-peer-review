This paper proposes a novel low-rank time-frequency synthesis (LRTFS) model, shifting the focus from decomposing the STFT magnitude (as in traditional NMF) to directly modeling the raw signal via generative time-frequency synthesis coefficients. This allows for a true generative model of the signal, unlike previous approaches that only modeled the STFT, and crucially opens the door to multi-resolution representations. The work is well-motivated, building upon the Gaussian Composite Model (GCM) [3] and addressing its limitations. The connection to sparse Bayesian learning is insightful, and the presentation is generally clear.

The core idea of modeling the signal synthesis directly is a significant and original contribution. Traditional NMF, even with probabilistic extensions like the GCM, operates on the transformed data. This work tackles the inverse problem directly, offering more flexibility and potential for improved accuracy. The multi-resolution extension is particularly appealing, as it allows the model to adapt to the varying time-frequency characteristics of signals, crucial for audio processing tasks where transients and sustained tones require different resolutions. This is a clear advance over existing methods that fix the time-frequency resolution during decomposition. The paper appropriately cites related work in NMF, sparse Bayesian learning, and time-frequency analysis.

However, the paper suffers from several weaknesses. While the authors present multi-resolution decomposition results, the evaluation feels limited. The jazz example (Fig. 1) is illustrative but lacks quantitative assessment. Demonstrating the benefits of the multi-resolution approach more convincingly requires a comparative study against single-resolution methods on a standardized dataset. The speech enhancement experiment is more substantial, showing SNR improvements comparable to IS-NMF and OMLSA, but a more rigorous comparison with a wider range of state-of-the-art speech enhancement techniques would strengthen the claims. 

A key request from the reviewers was a comparison to IS-NMF in the single-resolution case, specifically regarding whether the learned W and H matrices match. The authors do not address this directly. Demonstrating equivalence or highlighting the differences would be valuable for understanding the benefits of the proposed LRTFS model.  

Furthermore, the authors mention the computational cost of the MMLE approach and its limitations in scaling. While understandable, the claim that the MJLE algorithm is “rather computationally friendly” needs more nuance. The EM algorithm still involves iterative updates and potentially expensive operations, particularly for large signals.  The review requests whether the proposed algorithm matches IS-NMF’s multiplicative updates in speed and stability. This is also not addressed. A detailed computational complexity analysis and a timing comparison to IS-NMF would be beneficial.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* **Novelty:** The direct signal synthesis approach is a significant departure from traditional NMF-based methods.
* **Multi-resolution Capability:** The ability to handle multi-resolution representations is a valuable contribution, particularly for audio processing.
* **Theoretical Foundation:** The connection to sparse Bayesian learning provides a solid theoretical grounding.
* **Promising Results:** The speech enhancement results are competitive with existing methods.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

* **Limited Evaluation:** The experiments lack rigorous quantitative evaluation and comparison to state-of-the-art methods.
* **Missing Comparisons:** The comparison to IS-NMF is not provided.
* **Computational Complexity:** The paper lacks a detailed discussion of computational complexity and performance compared to existing methods.
* **Lack of Ablation Studies:** It isn’t clear what benefits the proposed method brings compared to simpler variations, like standard NMF.



**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents an interesting and potentially significant contribution to the field of time-frequency signal processing. The proposed LRTFS model offers a novel approach to signal decomposition with the key advantage of multi-resolution representation. However, the limited evaluation and lack of direct comparison to existing methods raise concerns about the practical benefits of the approach. I believe the paper has merit and could be accepted with major revisions, contingent on addressing the weaknesses outlined above. Specifically, the authors should:

1.  Provide a detailed comparison to IS-NMF, including a discussion of the learned W and H matrices.
2.  Conduct a more comprehensive evaluation of the multi-resolution capability, including quantitative results on a standardized dataset.
3.  Include a computational complexity analysis and timing comparison to IS-NMF.
4. Add ablation studies.
