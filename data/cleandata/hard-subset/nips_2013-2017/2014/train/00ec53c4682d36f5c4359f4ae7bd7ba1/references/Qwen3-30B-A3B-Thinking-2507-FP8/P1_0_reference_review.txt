

# NIPS Peer Review: "Two-Stream Convolutional Networks for Action Recognition in Videos"

## Summary

This paper proposes a two-stream convolutional network architecture for action recognition in videos, addressing a key limitation of previous deep learning approaches that attempted to learn spatio-temporal features directly from stacked RGB frames. The authors decouple the problem into two streams: a spatial stream processing individual video frames (with pre-training on ImageNet), and a temporal stream processing optical flow to capture motion information. The streams are combined through late fusion of their softmax scores. The paper makes several key contributions: (1) a novel temporal stream using optical flow as input to a ConvNet (rather than trying to learn motion features directly from RGB), (2) a multi-task learning framework to combine training data from multiple datasets (UCF-101 and HMDB-51), and (3) extensive experimental validation showing their approach outperforms previous deep learning methods and is competitive with state-of-the-art hand-crafted shallow representations.

The work builds on the two-streams hypothesis in visual neuroscience (ventral/dorsal streams) and relates to previous work on optical flow-based action recognition [8, 26, 29], while addressing a limitation of [14] who found that stacked-frame ConvNets performed worse than shallow representations. The paper also connects to recent deep learning work on video recognition [11, 12, 14, 16], positioning their approach as a more effective way to incorporate motion information.

## Strengths

1. **Technical Soundness**: The paper presents a well-motivated approach that addresses a clear limitation in prior work. The experimental design is comprehensive, with careful ablation studies of different input configurations for the temporal stream (optical flow stacking, trajectory stacking, bi-directional flow, etc.). The results consistently show that the two-stream architecture outperforms previous deep learning approaches.

2. **Clarity and Reproducibility**: The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The architecture is clearly described with supporting figures (Fig. 1, 3), and the implementation details (Section 5) provide sufficient information for reproduction. The authors carefully explain their choices (e.g., why they removed the second normalization layer in the temporal network).

3. **Significant Results**: The paper demonstrates that their two-stream approach achieves state-of-the-art performance for deep learning methods on standard video action recognition benchmarks (UCF-101 and HMDB-51), matching the performance of hand-crafted shallow representations. This is particularly impressive given they train on relatively small datasets compared to [14] which used Sports-1M.

4. **Novel Contributions**: The key innovation is using optical flow as explicit input to a ConvNet for the temporal stream, which avoids the difficulty of learning motion features directly from RGB frames. The multi-task learning framework to combine datasets is also a valuable contribution for video action recognition where datasets are limited.

5. **Thoughtful Evaluation**: The authors acknowledge limitations of their approach (e.g., missing local feature pooling over spatio-temporal tubes, reliance on mean flow subtraction for camera motion compensation) and compare against multiple baselines, including both deep learning and shallow methods.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited Theoretical Justification**: While the paper provides strong empirical results, it lacks theoretical justification for why optical flow as input to a ConvNet works better than stacked RGB frames. The authors mention that "the network does not need to estimate motion implicitly" but don't provide deeper analysis of why this representation is more effective.

2. **Comparison with Recent Deep Learning Methods**: The paper compares to [14] but doesn't address more recent deep learning approaches for video recognition that have emerged since their work (e.g., [27, 30, 32]). The paper was written before some of these methods were published, but it would be helpful to acknowledge this limitation.

3. **Computational Cost**: The authors mention they pre-computed optical flow (reducing storage from 1.5TB to 27GB for UCF-101), but don't discuss the computational cost of this preprocessing step. For practical applications, this could be a significant barrier, and the paper doesn't address how this impacts real-world deployment.

4. **Lack of Ablation on Fusion Methods**: While the paper compares averaging versus SVM fusion, it doesn't explore more sophisticated fusion methods (e.g., learned fusion layers), which might further improve performance.

5. **Limited Analysis of Failure Cases**: The paper shows strong overall performance but doesn't analyze where the model fails, which would help identify remaining challenges in video action recognition.

## Overall Assessment

This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of video action recognition. The two-stream architecture with optical flow as input to a ConvNet for the temporal stream is a simple yet highly effective approach that significantly outperforms previous deep learning methods. The results are well-supported by extensive experiments and comparisons to both deep learning and shallow hand-crafted methods.

The paper is technically sound, well-written, and addresses a challenging problem with a clear and novel solution. While there are some limitations (primarily the lack of theoretical justification and limited comparison to very recent deep learning methods), these do not significantly undermine the paper's contributions.

## Arguments for and against Acceptance

**Arguments for Acceptance**:
- The two-stream architecture with optical flow as input to a ConvNet is a novel and effective approach
- The method significantly outperforms previous deep learning approaches for video action recognition
- The results are well-supported by comprehensive experiments on standard benchmarks
- The paper is well-written and provides sufficient details for reproducibility
- The multi-task learning framework for combining datasets is a valuable contribution

**Arguments against Acceptance**:
- Limited theoretical justification for why optical flow input works better than stacked RGB frames
- Does not compare to some very recent deep learning approaches for video recognition
- Doesn't address computational cost of optical flow precomputation for practical deployment
- Limited analysis of failure cases

## Recommendation

I recommend **acceptance** of this paper. The paper makes a significant contribution to video action recognition, with a clear, novel approach that produces state-of-the-art results for deep learning methods. The technical soundness, clarity, and significance of the work outweigh the minor limitations noted above. The paper is well-suited for NIPS as it presents a novel approach to a challenging problem with strong experimental validation.

## Specific Suggestions for Improvement

1. In the introduction, briefly acknowledge the emergence of more recent deep learning approaches for video recognition (post-[14]) and discuss how their method compares to these.

2. In the discussion of limitations (Section 7), elaborate on the computational cost of optical flow precomputation and suggest potential ways to address this (e.g., integrating flow computation into the network).

3. Consider adding a figure or table showing example failure cases to provide deeper insight into the model's limitations.

4. In Section 3.2, provide a bit more explanation of why the temporal stream outperforms the spatial stream, beyond just stating "motion information is important."

5. Consider adding a brief analysis of the computational complexity of the two-stream approach compared to previous methods, especially in terms of training and inference time.
