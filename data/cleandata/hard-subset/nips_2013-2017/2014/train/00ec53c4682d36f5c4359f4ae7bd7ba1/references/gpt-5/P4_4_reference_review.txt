Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes a two-stream convolutional architecture for action recognition in videos: a spatial stream that operates on individual RGB frames (appearance) and a temporal stream that operates on dense optical flow stacks (motion). The two streams are trained separately and fused by averaging or linear SVM over softmax scores. The spatial stream is pre-trained on ImageNet and fine-tuned on the target video datasets, while the temporal stream is trained from scratch on stacked optical flow inputs with several variants: (i) stacking dx, dy fields across L frames; (ii) “trajectory stacking” sampling flow along estimated trajectories; (iii) bi-directional flow; and (iv) mean flow subtraction. To counter limited video data, the paper employs multi-task learning with dual softmax heads to train on UCF-101 and HMDB-51 jointly.

This work builds on two main lines of prior work: (1) shallow hand-crafted video representations such as dense trajectories with HOG/HOF/MBH descriptors and Fisher vectors (e.g., Wang & Schmid; Peng et al.), and (2) deep learning for video, including stacked-frame ConvNets (Karpathy et al.), 3D ConvNets (Ji et al.), and HMAX-style two-stream systems (Jhuang et al.). The paper argues that explicitly providing motion via optical flow simplifies learning compared to inferring it from raw RGB stacks. The authors further note connections between their first-layer filters and traditional HOF/MBH features. Empirically, on UCF-101 and HMDB-51, the temporal stream surpasses a single-frame spatial stream and a “slow fusion” RGB stack baseline, and the fused two-stream model is competitive with state-of-the-art shallow methods.

Strengths
- Clear architectural decomposition that mirrors established appearance/motion separation; simple and effective late score fusion.
- Strong empirical evidence: comprehensive ablations on flow stacking depth (L), mean subtraction, trajectory sampling, bi-directionality, and fusion strategies. The temporal stream clearly benefits from stacked flows and mean subtraction; two-stream fusion yields substantial complementary gains.
- Principled multi-task learning to leverage multiple datasets without manual class filtering; shows improvements over pretraining or partial data merging.
- Thoughtful implementation details (data augmentation, pretraining, learning schedules, multi-GPU training). Practical handling of flow precomputation and storage (quantization and compression) is described.
- Relates to and generalizes classic descriptors (HOF/MBH, kinematic features), situating the method within existing literature.

Weaknesses
- Not end-to-end: heavy reliance on an external optical flow algorithm (Brox et al.) introduces significant computational overhead and a dependency on flow quality. Flow computation dominates runtime and must be precomputed; test-time efficiency and throughput are not quantified end-to-end.
- Limited analysis of failure modes and sensitivity: while L is varied, sensitivity to flow quality/algorithm choice, crop sampling, and number of test frames is not thoroughly explored. JPEG quantization of flow is used; potential accuracy impact is not measured.
- Fusion: only late fusion of softmax scores is feasible due to overfitting; attempts at deeper joint fusion are not fully developed. Calibration of softmax scores across streams is handled via L2 normalization, but alternatives (e.g., temperature scaling) are not discussed.
- Dataset scale: results are on UCF-101 and HMDB-51, which are relatively small; while multi-task helps, there is no demonstration on larger-scale benchmarks to validate generalization and stability.
- Camera motion handling is rudimentary (mean flow subtraction). No explicit modeling or compensation of global motion beyond this heuristic.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The approach is well-motivated. The use of optical flow stacks is a reasonable design to expose motion to ConvNets. Ablations support the key design choices (stack length, mean subtraction, two-stream complementarity). The multi-task learning setup is standard and appropriately implemented with separate softmax heads and summed losses.
- Empirical support: Comparisons include strong baselines (single-frame ConvNets, stacked RGB “slow fusion”) and state-of-the-art shallow methods. The evaluation protocol follows standard splits; the paper reports average accuracies and provides ablation studies. Some important practical aspects (compute cost, latency) are not quantified.
- Completeness: The work is a complete system with training and testing details. The only notable missing analysis is on alternative flow algorithms and end-to-end considerations.

Clarity
- The paper is clearly written and well-organized. The two-stream concept and temporal input configurations are explained with equations and figures. Training/testing pipelines and pretraining are documented. Tables (referenced) summarize ablations and final comparisons. Minor suggestions: explicitly report end-to-end inference time per video (including flow), quantify effect of flow compression on accuracy, and provide more detail on SVM fusion (e.g., regularization, calibration).

Originality
- The two-stream ConvNet with an explicit optical-flow-based temporal stream and late fusion is novel relative to prior stacked-RGB ConvNets and shallow two-stream HMAX systems. The multi-task setup is standard but its application to jointly train video ConvNets across datasets is useful. The input variations (trajectory stacking, bi-directional flow, mean subtraction) are incremental; the main contribution is the temporal ConvNet on flow and its integration with a spatial stream.

Significance
- The approach addresses a central challenge in video recognition—leveraging motion effectively in deep models—and achieves performance competitive with top handcrafted pipelines while offering a learnable alternative. The modular design (pretrained spatial + flow-based temporal) is practical and likely to be adopted and extended. While not end-to-end, it provides a strong baseline and a stepping stone toward deeper integration of motion in ConvNets.

Pros for acceptance
- Clear, simple, and effective architecture with strong empirical gains and thorough ablations.
- Demonstrated complementarity of appearance and motion, improving over both individually.
- Solid methodological and implementation details that others can reproduce and build upon.
- Advances the state of the art on standard benchmarks, matching or approaching top shallow methods.

Cons for acceptance
- Dependence on external optical flow and lack of end-to-end training; compute and deployment costs not fully characterized.
- Limited analysis of robustness to flow quality, camera motion, and domain shift; results confined to relatively small datasets.
- Fusion limited to late score-level due to overfitting; no successful joint representation learning across streams.

Suggestions for improvement
- Report full runtime and memory footprint, including flow computation, and explore faster flow methods or approximations.
- Evaluate sensitivity to the choice of flow algorithm and to flow quantization/compression; include ablations on number of test frames/crops versus accuracy.
- Explore light-weight global motion compensation beyond mean subtraction, and/or trajectory-aware pooling in later layers.
- Investigate regularization schemes to enable deeper cross-stream fusion (e.g., shared intermediate layers, dropout strategies, or parameter tying).
- Consider semi-supervised or larger-scale training to further reduce overfitting in the temporal stream.

Overall, this is a technically sound and well-presented contribution that offers a practical and effective deep architecture for video action recognition, with strong evidence of novelty and significance relative to existing deep baselines.
