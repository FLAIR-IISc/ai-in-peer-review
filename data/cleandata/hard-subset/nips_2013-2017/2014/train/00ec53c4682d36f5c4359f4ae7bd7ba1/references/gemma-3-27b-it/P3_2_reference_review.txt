## Review of "Two-Stream Convolutional Networks for Action Recognition in Videos"

**Overall Assessment:** This paper presents a significant and well-executed contribution to the field of video action recognition. It proposes a simple yet effective two-stream convolutional network architecture, leveraging the complementary strengths of spatial and temporal information. The results demonstrate a substantial improvement over previous deep learning approaches and achieve competitive performance with state-of-the-art hand-crafted features. While not a radical departure in core concepts, the successful application and analysis of this architecture, coupled with clear writing and thorough experimentation, makes this a strong submission worthy of acceptance to NIPS.

**1. Summary and Relation to Prior Work:**

The paper addresses the challenge of action recognition in videos. It builds upon the recent advancements in deep convolutional networks for image classification and extends them to the temporal domain. The key innovation lies in decoupling spatial and temporal information into separate convolutional streams, trained on RGB frames and dense optical flow respectively, and then fusing their predictions.  The authors explicitly connect their work to the “two-stream hypothesis” from visual neuroscience, although they do not delve into the neuroscientific implications.  

The paper effectively positions itself within the existing literature. It clearly differentiates its approach from stacking frames as input to a single CNN (which proved ineffective in [14]), and contrasts it with previous trajectory-based methods ([20, 26]) which rely on hand-crafted features. The authors accurately highlight the limitations of existing deep learning approaches for video and argue for a more explicit modeling of motion via optical flow. The comparison to HMAX-based two-stream architectures [16] is also well-placed, emphasizing the advantages of using deeper, discriminatively trained ConvNets.

**2. Strengths:**

* **Effective Architecture:** The two-stream CNN is a conceptually simple but demonstrably powerful architecture.  Separating spatial and temporal processing allows each stream to specialize and capture complementary information.
* **Strong Performance:** The paper achieves state-of-the-art or competitive results on UCF101 and HMDB51, demonstrating a clear improvement over prior deep learning methods and a strong showing against established hand-crafted feature approaches.
* **Thorough Experimentation:** The authors perform a comprehensive set of experiments to analyze the impact of various design choices, including network configurations, input representations (optical flow stacking vs. trajectory stacking), and fusion methods.
* **Multi-task Learning:** The incorporation of multi-task learning to leverage data from multiple datasets is a practical and effective technique for improving generalization, particularly given the limited size of the available video datasets.
* **Clear Writing and Presentation:** The paper is well-written, well-organized, and easy to follow.  The figures and tables are clear and informative.
* **Pre-training Strategy:** Leveraging pre-trained models on ImageNet for the spatial stream is a standard but crucial technique for successful deep learning on limited data.

**3. Weaknesses:**

* **Optical Flow Dependency:** The reliance on pre-computed optical flow is a potential bottleneck.  While the authors address computational cost, integrating optical flow computation directly into the network (e.g., differentiable optical flow estimation) could be a future direction.
* **Limited Exploration of Fusion Strategies:** While the authors compare averaging and SVM-based fusion, more sophisticated fusion techniques (e.g., attention mechanisms) might further improve performance.  The rationale for choosing the final fusion method isn't deeply discussed.
* **Lack of Ablation Study on Network Depth:** While many configurations are tested, the depth of the networks is relatively fixed. Exploring different network depths for each stream could reveal optimal configurations.
* **Limited Discussion of Failure Cases:** While overall performance is strong, a more detailed analysis of the types of actions where the network struggles would provide valuable insight.
* **Connection to Neuroscience is Superficial:** The mention of the ventral/dorsal stream hypothesis feels somewhat tacked on and isn't further explored. While not essential, a deeper engagement with this connection could add theoretical value.



**4. Originality:**

While the core idea of separating spatial and temporal processing is not entirely new (as acknowledged by the authors regarding [16]), the application of *deep* convolutional networks to this task, with optical flow as a primary temporal input, is a significant contribution.  The effective combination of pre-training, multi-task learning, and careful experimental analysis elevates this work beyond a simple application of existing techniques. The key originality lies in demonstrating the power of this decoupled architecture with modern deep learning tools.

**5. Significance:**

The paper's findings have the potential to significantly impact the field of video action recognition.  The proposed architecture provides a strong baseline for future research and offers a practical approach for building high-performance action recognition systems.  The demonstrated improvement over existing methods, particularly in the realm of deep learning, is likely to encourage further exploration of similar architectures. The clear, well-documented experimental setup will facilitate replication and extension of this work.

**6. Arguments for and Against Acceptance:**

**Pro:**

*  Strong experimental results and competitive performance.
*  Clear and concise writing.
*  Thorough analysis of design choices.
*  Addresses a challenging problem with a practical and effective solution.
*  Well-positioned within the existing literature.

**Con:**

*  Reliance on pre-computed optical flow.
*  Limited exploration of more advanced fusion techniques.
*  Superficial connection to neuroscience.

**7. Recommendation:**

**Accept.**  The strengths of this paper significantly outweigh its weaknesses.  It represents a valuable contribution to the field of video action recognition and is well-suited for presentation at NIPS. The clear presentation, thorough experimentation, and strong results make it a compelling and impactful work. I would encourage the authors to address the minor weaknesses raised in the review in a future version, particularly exploring more advanced fusion methods and potential integration of optical flow computation within the network.
