This paper presents a two-stream convolutional network architecture for action recognition in videos, decoupling spatial and temporal information processing. The spatial stream utilizes a ConvNet pre-trained on ImageNet, while the temporal stream operates on dense optical flow. The streams are fused via averaging or an SVM. While the core idea of separating spatial and temporal streams is not entirely novel (related to the two-streams hypothesis and prior work [16]), the authors demonstrate a significant performance improvement over previous deep learning approaches ([14]) on standard benchmarks (UCF101, HMDB-51). However, the paper suffers from a surprising number of minor flaws in implementation detail and presentation, which, while individually small, accumulate to a concerning level.

**Strengths:**

*   **Clear Motivation:** The paper clearly articulates the problem and the rationale behind the proposed architecture. The decoupling of spatial and temporal streams is a logical approach.
*   **Strong Empirical Results:** The results demonstrate significant improvement over [14] and achieve competitive performance with state-of-the-art hand-crafted features.
*   **Multi-task Learning:** The introduction of multi-task learning is a valuable contribution, showing a clear performance gain, especially with limited data.
*   **Detailed Implementation:** The implementation details are relatively comprehensive, including information about training procedures, network configurations, and optical flow computation.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Numerous Typos & Grammatical Errors:** Throughout the paper, there are frequent typographical errors ("mult-task" instead of "multi-task," inconsistent capitalization, missing articles). While not critical individually, they detract from the paper’s professionalism.
*   **Inconsistent Notation:** The notation used for optical flow components (dx, dy) is inconsistent with standard conventions. The use of τ as a frame index is also unusual and less intuitive than t.
*   **Lack of Justification for Design Choices:** While the architecture is described, there’s limited justification for specific design choices (e.g., the number of layers in the ConvNets, the choice of ReLU, the specific pooling strategy). Why CNN-M-2048 specifically?
*   **Insufficient Ablation Study:** The ablation study (Table 1b) lacks clarity. The impact of bi-directional flow is described as “not beneficial,” yet the reported numbers show a slight *decrease* in performance—this deserves a clearer explanation. Why does mean subtraction help, specifically?
*   **Redundant Information:** Some sections (e.g., optical flow computation) reiterate well-known methods without adding substantial insights. The descriptions of established techniques (e.g., HOF, MBH) are overly brief and assume the reader is intimately familiar with them.
*   **Inconsistent Discussion of Related Work:** The related work section occasionally misrepresents prior contributions. The claim that [16] used “shallow (3-layer) HMAX models” seems inaccurate; the architecture included more complex processing than just three layers.
*   **JPEG Compression of Optical Flow:** While described as a space-saving technique, the JPEG compression of optical flow introduces artifacts and potentially impacts performance. This is not adequately discussed as a limitation.
*   **Missing Details on SVM Fusion:** The SVM-based fusion method is mentioned, but crucial details (kernel type, regularization parameter) are missing.
*   **Figure 1 is Unclear:** The diagram of the two-stream architecture (Fig. 1) is overly simplistic and does not clearly illustrate the connections between the streams and the fusion layer.
*   **Overly Strong Claims:** Statements like “training a temporal ConvNet on optical flow…is significantly better than training on raw stacked frames” are overly strong and lack sufficient justification. The experiment comparing to [14] used a different dataset (Sports-1M vs. UCF101/HMDB51).

**Arguments for/against acceptance:**

**Pro:**

*   Novel architecture combining pre-trained spatial networks with temporal flow processing.
*   Significant performance gains over previous deep learning methods.
*   Effective use of multi-task learning to address data limitations.

**Con:**

*   The sheer number of minor flaws suggests a lack of meticulousness in the preparation of the manuscript.
*   Lack of rigorous justification for design choices.
*   Insufficient ablation study to understand the contribution of individual components.
*   Overstated claims and inconsistent presentation.

**Overall Assessment:**

Despite presenting a novel and reasonably effective architecture, the pervasive minor flaws in this paper are concerning. The lack of rigor in the presentation and the insufficient justification for design choices diminish the overall impact of the work. While the empirical results are positive, the numerous issues suggest the authors did not conduct a sufficiently thorough evaluation or proofreading. I would lean towards **rejection** due to the cumulative effect of these shortcomings. A major revision addressing these points is needed before this paper would be suitable for NIPS. The potential is there, but the current version falls short of the high standards expected at NIPS.
