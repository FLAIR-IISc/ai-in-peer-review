Summary
The paper proposes a two-stream architecture for video action recognition: a spatial stream ConvNet operating on individual RGB frames (pre-trained on ImageNet) and a temporal stream ConvNet operating on stacked dense optical flow fields. The temporal input explores several variants: stacking L flow fields, trajectory stacking, bi-directional flow, and mean flow subtraction. The two streams are combined by late fusion (averaging or linear SVM). To mitigate data scarcity for the temporal stream, the authors use a multi-task setup with two softmax heads for UCF101 and HMDB51, summing losses. Experiments on UCF101 and HMDB51 show the temporal stream substantially outperforms the spatial stream, the streams are complementary under late fusion, and the approach is competitive with trajectory-based shallow methods; it also outperforms prior deep baselines that take stacked RGB frames as input.

Relation to prior work: The method builds on deep ConvNets for images and on traditional video features based on optical flow (HOF, MBH, dense trajectories). Conceptually it echoes the dorsal/ventral (“two streams”) hypothesis and prior two-stream HMAX designs, but here both streams are learned, deeper ConvNets. Compared to Karpathy et al. (2014), the key distinction is to provide motion explicitly via optical flow rather than expecting early layers to learn it from RGB stacks.

Strengths
- Clear, simple decomposition of appearance vs. motion; temporal stream on flow is intuitive and empirically effective.
- Solid empirical gains over deep baselines on UCF101/HMDB51; ablations (L, mean subtraction, stacking variants) illuminate design choices.
- Multi-task learning across datasets is practical and improves the temporal stream under data scarcity.
- Implementation details like multi-GPU training and input sampling policy are helpful to practitioners.
- The paper convincingly argues that explicit motion is currently superior to stacked RGB for end-to-end ConvNets on small datasets.

Weaknesses and concerns (technical and methodological)
- Reproducibility gaps:
  - Critical temporal-data augmentation detail is missing: when horizontally flipping flow inputs, is the horizontal component dx sign-inverted? If not, flipping is invalid and may harm learning; if yes, this must be stated.
  - Weight decay, initialization, and any gradient clipping are not specified. Given sensitivity of training to these, they should be reported.
  - The exact Brox flow parameters (e.g., alpha, gamma, pyramid scales, iterations) are not given; results can vary substantially with these settings.
  - For multi-task training, the sampling policy and loss weighting are unclear. Are batches balanced across tasks? Are losses weighted equally? Are class priors matched?
  - For SVM-based late fusion, how is the regularization parameter selected? Is there a held-out validation set per split, or is test data inadvertently used? Feature normalisation is mentioned only briefly.
- Optical flow storage/compression raises validity questions:
  - Flow components are “linearly rescaled to [0,255] and compressed using JPEG.” What range is used for linear scaling (global, per-video, or per-frame), and is clipping applied? JPEG is lossy and not designed for vector fields; quantisation and compression artifacts can bias learning. No analysis is provided on the accuracy impact of this choice.
  - When is mean flow subtraction applied—before or after compression and cropping? If after compression with unknown offsets, zero-centering may be inconsistent.
- Fairness of comparisons and sensitivity:
  - The “slow fusion” RGB stack baseline is trained “from scratch” on UCF101; however, the spatial stream benefits from ImageNet pretraining. A fairer comparison would include an RGB-stack model initialised from ImageNet (e.g., inflating filters or pretraining per-frame) to probe whether the gap is due to motion representation vs. pretraining/data size.
  - No sensitivity analysis to the choice of flow estimator (e.g., Brox vs. TV-L1 vs. DeepFlow). Since the method depends on an external optical flow algorithm, robustness to the flow choice should be reported.
  - L is only explored up to 10; longer windows and temporal strides are not studied.
- Evaluation and reporting:
  - No statistical variability (std/CI) or per-class analysis. Given small datasets, error bars would help assess significance of improvements and fusion gains.
  - Fusion of fully connected features was dismissed due to overfitting; more rigorous regularization (e.g., stronger dropout, L2, early stopping, or using a small validation set) could be attempted and reported.
  - Potential cross-dataset leakage: Using multi-task learning across UCF101 and HMDB51 may risk overlap or near-duplicates across datasets. The paper should state that deduplication checks were performed.
- Clarity/notation issues:
  - Ambiguity in equations (ranges written as [1;w] etc.) and in the definition of bi-directional stacking (temporal indexing and alignment could be clearer).
  - It is unclear whether mean subtraction is per-flow-field vector mean over the full frame, per-crop, or per-sample volume.
  - Some claims (e.g., “video provides natural data augmentation”) are vague and not substantiated.
- Formatting and references:
  - Numerous reference formatting errors (missing years/venues, typos, inconsistent titles; e.g., “D convolutional neural networks,” “classication,” incomplete bibliographic fields).
  - Typos and minor inconsistencies (e.g., “mult-task,” “finetuned”) and footnote placement issues.
  - Figures are central to understanding but the text relies on them; ensure captions and figure numbers are consistent and self-contained.

Quality: The empirical section is extensive, but several important details are missing for full reproducibility and to exclude confounds (flow compression, flipping, hyperparameters, fusion tuning). The external dependency on flow without sensitivity analysis is a weakness.

Clarity: Generally readable and well organized, but key implementation details are omitted, and some notation/wording is imprecise. Reference and formatting issues detract from professionalism.

Originality: The two-stream idea is not new conceptually; the novelty lies in instantiating the temporal stream as a ConvNet on stacked flow with simple, effective design choices and multi-task training. Incremental but valuable.

Significance: Results are competitive with strong hand-crafted baselines and substantially better than prior deep baselines, suggesting impact. However, the reliance on precomputed flow and missing analyses limit generality claims.

Questions for the authors
- Do you invert dx on horizontal flips? Please specify all temporal augmentation details (temporal jitter, stride, start/end handling).
- Exact Brox parameters and whether any flow post-processing (median filtering, flow clipping) is used?
- Details of flow scaling to [0,255]: chosen min/max, clipping policy, and empirical effect of JPEG compression on accuracy.
- Multi-task batching and loss weighting; any deduplication across datasets?
- SVM fusion: training/validation protocol and hyperparameter selection.
- Weight decay, initialization, and any gradient clipping used.

Pros for acceptance
- Simple, effective architecture with strong empirical gains over existing deep baselines.
- Clear demonstration that explicit motion (optical flow) benefits ConvNets.
- Useful ablations and a practical multi-task training recipe.

Cons for acceptance
- Insufficient methodological detail for reproducibility; several ambiguous or missing training details.
- Heavy reliance on external flow with lossy compression, without sensitivity or ablation.
- Limited novelty relative to prior two-stream ideas; fairness of baselines could be improved.
- Numerous formatting/reference issues.

Recommendation: Weak reject. The paper is promising and likely impactful, but the accumulation of methodological ambiguities and formatting errors prevents confident assessment and reproduction. With clarifications, additional analyses (flow robustness, compression impact, fairer baselines), and cleaned-up presentation, this would be a strong submission.

Confidence: 0.7 (familiar with video recognition and optical flow; did not re-run experiments).
