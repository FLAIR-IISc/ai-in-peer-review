Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes a two-stream convolutional architecture for video action recognition that decouples appearance and motion: a spatial stream processes individual RGB frames, and a temporal stream processes stacked dense optical flow fields. The outputs are combined by late fusion (average or linear SVM on softmax scores). The spatial stream leverages pre-training on ImageNet (ILSVRC-2012) and fine-tuning on UCF101/HMDB-51. The temporal stream is trained from scratch given that large-scale labeled flow data are not available.

For the temporal stream, the authors encode motion by stacking L consecutive displacement fields (dx, dy) computed by dense optical flow [2]. They explore: (i) optical-flow stacking at fixed spatial locations; (ii) trajectory stacking sampling flow along estimated point trajectories; (iii) bi-directional flow (forward and backward); and (iv) mean displacement subtraction to reduce camera motion. They argue the temporal ConvNet generalizes prior hand-crafted features (HOF, MBH, kinematic features) since such operations can be expressed by early convolutional layers.

To mitigate overfitting on small video datasets, they introduce multi-task learning with two classification heads (UCF101 and HMDB-51) sharing the convolutional backbone, with total loss as the sum of task losses [5]. Implementation uses a CNN-M-2048-style network [3], standard data augmentation, multi-GPU training, and precomputation/compression of optical flow.

Experiments on UCF101 and HMDB-51 include ablations on L, trajectory vs optical-flow stacking, mean subtraction, and bi-directional flow. Main findings: (1) temporal nets on stacked optical flow substantially outperform RGB-only spatial nets when trained from scratch; (2) stacking multiple flow fields (L up to 10) and mean subtraction improve accuracy; (3) optical-flow stacking slightly outperforms trajectory stacking; (4) bi-directional flow yields marginal gains; (5) two-stream late fusion yields sizable improvements over either stream alone; (6) multi-task learning improves temporal nets, especially on HMDB-51. The approach is competitive with contemporary state-of-the-art shallow pipelines based on improved dense trajectories (e.g., [26]) and surpasses prior deep video models that stacked raw frames (e.g., [14]).

Quality
- Technical soundness: The architectural choices are well-motivated. Using stacked optical flow cleanly exposes motion to the network, avoiding the need to learn motion implicitly. The mean-displacement subtraction is a simple, effective normalization akin to global motion compensation. The multi-task setup is standard and appropriate. The evaluation is thorough for the proposed components, with clear ablations.
- Empirical support: The paper reports results on standard splits of UCF101 and HMDB-51, compares to strong hand-crafted baselines [20, 21, 26] and deep baselines [14, 16], and includes informative ablations (L, trajectory vs flow stacking, mean subtraction, fusion strategy). The training/testing protocols are described with sufficient detail to reproduce. One limitation is the absence of statistical variability analysis (e.g., multiple runs), but the improvements are large enough to be convincing.
- Limitations: The approach depends on precomputed optical flow, which adds computational cost and a reliance on a particular flow method [2]. The fusion is only at the score level; attempts at deeper joint fusion reportedly overfit, but more analysis of regularization/architectural variants would strengthen the claim. There is limited analysis of failure modes or per-class behavior. Efficiency trade-offs relative to improved trajectories [26] are not quantified.

Clarity
The paper is clearly written and well-structured. The description of the temporal input formulations (equations (1)–(2)) is precise. Implementation details (training schedules, augmentation, multi-GPU, flow compression) are explicit. The relation to prior work is well articulated, including how the temporal net subsumes HOF/MBH. Figures aid understanding. A few areas could benefit from added clarity: explicit compute/runtime comparisons; precise hyperparameters for SVM fusion; and a brief discussion on protocol compliance when using multi-task training across datasets.

Originality
The key novelty lies in combining two discriminatively trained ConvNets—one on RGB, one on stacked optical flow—and demonstrating that explicit optical flow input enables substantially better temporal modeling than stacked RGB frames. While two-stream ideas and HMAX-based dual pathways exist [16], and stacked-frame ConvNets have been explored [12, 14], the specific optical-flow-as-input ConvNet, the analysis of stacking/mean subtraction/bidirectional flow, and the multi-task training across action datasets represent a clear, differentiated contribution at the time. The argument that the temporal ConvNet generalizes popular hand-crafted motion descriptors is insightful.

Significance
Results show strong, competitive performance on widely used benchmarks, narrowing the gap between deep and hand-crafted pipelines and providing a simple, effective baseline that influenced subsequent work on video understanding. The method is practical and reproducible. The reliance on precomputed flow is a deployment caveat, but the demonstrated gains and clarity of the approach indicate high potential impact and adoption.

Pros
- Clear, modular two-stream design with well-justified decoupling of appearance and motion.
- Strong empirical gains from temporal ConvNets on stacked optical flow; comprehensive ablations.
- Late fusion yields substantial improvements; SVM fusion beats averaging.
- Multi-task learning effectively regularizes training on small datasets.
- Implementation details are sufficient for reproduction.

Cons
- Dependence on external optical flow computation; limited analysis of computational cost and sensitivity to the chosen flow algorithm.
- Limited exploration of joint fusion beyond softmax-level; overfitting claim would benefit from more systematic attempts (e.g., stronger regularization, weight sharing).
- No statistical significance or multiple-run variability reported.
- Only UCF101 and HMDB-51 evaluated; cross-dataset generalization beyond multi-task training is not analyzed.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide compute/resource analysis: preprocessing time for flow, inference throughput (videos/sec), and comparison to improved trajectories [26] in wall-clock and memory.
- Evaluate robustness to different optical flow methods or quality (e.g., coarser flow, noisier estimates) to understand sensitivity.
- Explore regularized joint fusion: e.g., low-rank bottlenecks between streams, shared early layers, or attention/gating, with stronger dropout, batch norm, or weight decay to mitigate overfitting.
- Include per-class error analysis and qualitative failure cases to guide future improvements (e.g., sensitivity to camera motion, small/fast actions).
- Clarify dataset protocol issues: explicitly state that multi-task training uses only training partitions and no test leakage; report results with and without auxiliary data for fair comparisons.
- Consider lightweight motion encodings (e.g., motion vectors) as an efficiency baseline.

Overall recommendation
The paper is clear, technically sound, original in its temporal modeling via optical flow ConvNets and two-stream fusion, and significant for action recognition. The empirical evaluation supports the claims. The main weakness is the reliance on precomputed flow and limited analysis of efficiency and joint fusion. On balance, it merits acceptance.
