Summary
The paper proposes a two-stream ConvNet architecture for video action recognition that explicitly decouples spatial appearance and temporal motion. The spatial stream is a standard image ConvNet (pretrained on ImageNet) applied to individual frames. The temporal stream is a ConvNet applied to stacked dense optical flow fields across multiple frames, with several design variants: optical-flow vs trajectory stacking, uni- vs bi-directional flow, and mean-flow subtraction to mitigate camera motion. The two streams are fused by late score fusion (averaging or a linear SVM). To alleviate data scarcity for training the temporal stream, the authors introduce a simple multi-task learning setup to jointly train on UCF-101 and HMDB-51 with separate softmax heads. On UCF-101 and HMDB-51, the temporal stream substantially outperforms RGB-only ConvNets trained on stacked frames; fusing the two streams further improves performance to be competitive with trajectory-based shallow pipelines, while clearly surpassing prior deep approaches.

Strengths
- Clear, bold idea: explicitly model motion via optical flow as input to a dedicated ConvNet, rather than hoping motion emerges from stacked RGB. The two-stream decomposition is conceptually clean and well-motivated (and related to the ventral/dorsal hypothesis).
- Solid empirical evidence: ablations on flow stacking length, bi-directionality, trajectory vs spatial stacking, and mean-flow subtraction; comparisons to single-frame and “slow fusion” RGB stacks; analysis of fusion schemes; and a practical multi-task learning strategy to use multiple datasets.
- Substantive advance over prior deep models on standard benchmarks; strong single-stream temporal results and complementary gains from fusion.
- Pragmatic engineering details (multi-GPU training, input sampling, pretraining) that enhance reproducibility and show careful experimentation.
- The temporal model arguably generalizes classical HOF/MBH and trajectory descriptors, providing a learnable alternative to shallow pipelines.

Weaknesses
- Dependence on an external, precomputed optical flow front-end prevents end-to-end learning and adds computational overhead; the paper does not quantify training/inference time or resource costs with/without precomputation.
- The approach remains “competitive” rather than decisively surpassing the best trajectory-based shallow methods; analysis of remaining gaps (e.g., trajectory-tube pooling, stronger camera motion compensation) is mostly qualitative.
- Feature-level fusion is abandoned due to overfitting; more rigorous attempts (regularization, sharing, bottlenecks) are not explored, likely due to data size, but still leaves open whether tighter joint modeling would help.
- Camera motion handling is rudimentary (mean subtraction). Comparisons to explicit global motion estimation/compensation would strengthen claims.
- Limited exploration of design choices: only one flow algorithm is used; sensitivity to L (beyond 10), stride, normalization, or different flow methods is not studied; robustness to flow errors is unreported.
- No runtime/storage report on the JPEG-compressed flow pipeline; potential artifacts from lossy compression are not analyzed.

Quality
Technically sound, with enough architectural detail and ablations to support the core claims. The multi-task setup is straightforward and effective. Missing are computational metrics and deeper analysis of failure modes or sensitivity to the flow front-end, but the overall empirical evidence is convincing for the stated scope.

Clarity
Well written and organized. The relation to prior work is thorough, including a useful discussion of how the temporal ConvNet can subsume HOF/MBH/trajectory ideas. Some figures/tables (referenced but not shown here) presumably support clarity. Minor suggestions: report computational cost (FLOPs, throughput, storage), and provide per-class gains to better illustrate where motion helps most.

Originality
High. The explicit two-stream decomposition with a learnable ConvNet on stacked optical flow is a novel and impactful direction relative to stacked-RGB ConvNets and prior shallow descriptors. The multi-task training to leverage small video datasets is a sensible and useful contribution.

Significance
High. The approach offers a new conceptual and practical template for video recognition and clearly improves over prior deep baselines. It is likely to stimulate follow-up work (e.g., end-to-end motion learning, better fusion, larger-scale training) and be adopted by practitioners.

Suggestions for improvement
- Quantify compute/storage and report training/inference speed; discuss deployment trade-offs.
- Explore stronger camera motion compensation and/or compare flow algorithms.
- Investigate end-to-end variants (e.g., differentiable flow or warping, learned motion representations).
- Revisit feature-level fusion with stronger regularization, bottlenecks, or gating/attention for stream weighting.
- Analyze robustness to flow quality, class-wise performance, and failure cases.
- Consider trajectory-aware pooling layers to bring in benefits of tube pooling from shallow pipelines.

Arguments for acceptance
- Conceptually novel and elegant decomposition; practically effective.
- Strong empirical gains over prior deep models with clear ablations.
- Likely to open a fruitful research direction and be widely adopted.

Arguments against acceptance
- External optical flow dependency and lack of end-to-end training.
- Does not decisively outperform the best shallow baselines.
- Missing compute/efficiency analysis and deeper design sensitivity studies.

Recommendation: Accept (strong). Confidence: High.
