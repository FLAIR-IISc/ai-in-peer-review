

# Peer Review: "Two-Stream Convolutional Networks for Action Recognition in Videos"

## Summary

This paper proposes a two-stream convolutional neural network architecture for action recognition in videos, where one stream processes spatial information from individual frames (using a ConvNet pre-trained on ImageNet) and the other stream processes temporal information from optical flow (using a ConvNet trained on video data). The authors demonstrate that combining these two streams via late fusion significantly outperforms single-stream approaches and achieves competitive results with state-of-the-art hand-crafted methods on UCF101 and HMDB-51 datasets. The paper makes several contributions: (1) a novel two-stream architecture for video action recognition that decouples spatial and temporal processing, (2) an analysis of different optical flow input configurations for the temporal stream, (3) a multi-task learning approach to handle limited video datasets, and (4) a detailed experimental comparison showing the effectiveness of the approach.

The paper builds on prior work in video action recognition, particularly addressing the limitations of previous deep learning approaches that stack RGB frames as input [14], which the authors argue fail to adequately capture motion information. The work also relates to the two-stream hypothesis in neuroscience [9], though this connection is not explored in depth.

## Strengths

1. **Strong Technical Contribution**: The two-stream architecture is well-motivated and effectively addresses the key challenge of video action recognition - how to properly incorporate temporal information. The authors provide a clear explanation of why their approach differs from previous deep learning attempts (e.g., [14]) and why it works better.

2. **Comprehensive Experimental Evaluation**: The paper provides thorough ablation studies on different input configurations for the temporal stream (optical flow stacking vs. trajectory stacking, bi-directional flow, etc.) and demonstrates the effectiveness of multi-task learning for handling limited training data. The comparison with multiple baselines (including both deep learning and hand-crafted methods) is particularly strong.

3. **Practical Implementation Details**: The paper provides sufficient technical details for reproducibility, including network architecture, training procedures, and optical flow computation methods. The discussion of computational considerations (e.g., flow pre-computation, GPU training) is valuable for practitioners.

4. **Clear Demonstration of Complementarity**: The authors convincingly show that spatial and temporal streams are complementary (with 6% improvement over temporal stream alone), which is a key insight for future work in video recognition.

## Weaknesses and Concerns

1. **Lack of Theoretical Justification for Key Design Choices**: The paper doesn't adequately explain *why* the temporal stream using optical flow works better than stacked RGB frames (as in [14]). While the authors claim the stacked RGB approach is "too challenging," they don't provide deeper analysis or theoretical justification for this claim. The paper would benefit from a more thorough discussion of why optical flow as input is superior for motion recognition compared to raw RGB frames.

2. **Limited Analysis of Failure Modes**: The paper doesn't provide any analysis of failure cases or limitations of the approach. For instance, how does the method perform on actions where motion is less discriminative (e.g., "drinking" vs. "eating")? How does it handle videos with significant camera motion (despite the mean flow subtraction)? This would strengthen the paper's scientific contribution.

3. **Inadequate Comparison with Recent Work**: The paper references [14] as the most recent deep learning approach, but misses the opportunity to compare with more recent works that have built upon [14], such as [12] and [25]. The paper also doesn't discuss how their approach relates to the "two-stream HMAX" model in [16], which also uses separate spatial and temporal streams (though implemented as shallow hand-crafted models).

4. **Overstated Claims about "State-of-the-Art"**: The paper claims to be "competitive with the state of the art shallow representations" but doesn't explicitly state how much it falls short of the best hand-crafted methods (e.g., [26]). The paper would be stronger with a more precise comparison of absolute performance metrics.

5. **Computational Cost Consideration**: The paper doesn't adequately address the computational cost of optical flow computation, which is a significant bottleneck for real-world applications. The authors mention pre-computing flow to avoid on-the-fly computation, but don't discuss how this affects the practical usability of their approach.

## Technical Concerns

1. **Multi-task Learning Implementation**: While the multi-task learning approach is well-motivated, the paper doesn't provide sufficient detail on how the two softmax layers are combined during training. Specifically, how are the losses weighted for the two tasks? The paper states "the overall training loss is computed as the sum of the individual tasks' losses" but doesn't specify if the losses are normalized by the number of samples per task.

2. **Optical Flow Input Configuration**: The paper claims that "optical flow stacking performs better than trajectory stacking," but the results in Table 1b don't show a statistically significant difference between these two approaches (only a small margin). This claim might be overstated based on the presented results.

3. **Comparison with Recent Deep Learning Approaches**: The paper doesn't discuss how their approach relates to more recent deep learning methods for video recognition, such as the 3D ConvNets proposed in [25] or the spatio-temporal feature learning approaches in [12]. These methods have since shown promising results and would provide a more complete context for their contribution.

4. **Lack of Ablation on Network Architecture**: The paper doesn't provide an ablation study on the network architecture itself (e.g., varying the number of layers or filter sizes) for either the spatial or temporal streams. This would help clarify the contribution of the two-stream architecture versus the specific network design.

## Recommendations for Improvement

1. Add a more detailed analysis of why optical flow as input works better than stacked RGB frames, perhaps with visualizations of the learned filters or intermediate representations.

2. Include an analysis of failure cases and limitations of the approach, particularly for actions where motion is less discriminative.

3. Expand the related work section to include recent deep learning approaches for video recognition (post-2014) and clarify how their work relates to these.

4. Provide more precise comparison with the best hand-crafted methods, including absolute performance metrics rather than just stating "competitive."

5. Discuss the computational cost implications of their approach more thoroughly, including inference time and memory requirements.

## Overall Assessment

This paper makes a valuable contribution to the field of video action recognition by demonstrating that a two-stream deep learning architecture can achieve competitive results with hand-crafted methods. The experimental evaluation is thorough and well-executed, and the approach is well-motivated. However, the paper would be significantly strengthened by addressing the technical concerns outlined above, particularly regarding the theoretical justification for key design choices and a more comprehensive comparison with recent work.

## Reviewer Recommendations

**Quality**: The paper is technically sound with well-supported claims, but lacks deeper analysis of why certain design choices work better than others. The experimental evaluation is strong, but could be improved with more thorough analysis of failure cases.

**Clarity**: The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with sufficient detail for reproducibility. Some sections (e.g., the connection to the two-stream hypothesis in neuroscience) could be clarified.

**Originality**: The two-stream architecture for deep video recognition is novel in the context of deep learning, though the concept of separate spatial and temporal processing has precedents. The specific implementation using ConvNets for both streams with optical flow for the temporal stream is a meaningful contribution.

**Significance**: The results are significant as they demonstrate that deep learning can be competitive with hand-crafted methods for video action recognition, which is an important step toward more general video understanding systems. However, the paper doesn't claim to significantly outperform the state-of-the-art, so its impact is more incremental than transformative.

**Overall Recommendation**: Accept. This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field. With minor revisions addressing the concerns outlined above, it would be an excellent addition to the NIPS proceedings. The paper addresses an important problem in video recognition, provides a well-motivated solution, and demonstrates strong empirical results. While not revolutionary, it represents a significant step forward in applying deep learning to video action recognition.

**Pro Accept Arguments**:
- Well-motivated two-stream architecture that effectively separates spatial and temporal processing
- Thorough experimental evaluation with ablation studies on key design choices
- Demonstrates that deep learning approaches can be competitive with hand-crafted methods
- Provides practical implementation details for reproducibility
- Introduces multi-task learning to handle limited video datasets

**Con Accept Arguments**:
- Lacks deeper theoretical justification for key design choices
- Inadequate comparison with more recent deep learning approaches
- Doesn't sufficiently analyze limitations and failure cases
- Overstates the "state-of-the-art" claim without precise comparison metrics

I believe this paper would be a valuable contribution to NIPS, and with the suggested revisions, it would be a strong candidate for acceptance.
