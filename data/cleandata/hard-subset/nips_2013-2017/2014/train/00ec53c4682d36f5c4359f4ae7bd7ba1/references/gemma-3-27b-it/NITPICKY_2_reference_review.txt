This paper presents a two-stream convolutional network architecture for action recognition in videos, decoupling spatial and temporal information processing into separate networks. While the idea of utilizing separate streams for spatial and temporal information is not novel (drawing connections to the two-streams hypothesis and prior work like [16]), the authors demonstrate a significant performance improvement over directly stacking frames as input to a single ConvNet [14] and achieve competitive results with state-of-the-art hand-crafted features [20, 21, 26]. However, the paper suffers from a number of minor, yet persistent, issues that collectively detract from its overall quality.

**Strengths:**

*   **Clear Motivation:** The paper clearly articulates the motivation for decoupling spatial and temporal streams, highlighting the limitations of previous approaches.
*   **Competitive Performance:** The proposed architecture achieves competitive performance on standard benchmarks (UCF101 and HMDB51), demonstrating its effectiveness.
*   **Thorough Experimental Evaluation:** The authors conduct a comprehensive experimental evaluation, exploring various input configurations for the temporal stream and fusion strategies.
*   **Detailed Implementation Details:** The implementation details are described thoroughly, facilitating reproducibility.
*   **Multi-task Learning:** The incorporation of multi-task learning provides a practical method for leveraging data from multiple datasets.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Lack of Novelty:** The core idea of two-stream networks is not entirely new. The authors acknowledge this, but don’t sufficiently emphasize *how* their specific implementation significantly advances the field beyond existing two-stream approaches. A clearer delineation from [16], for example, is needed.
*   **Minor Technical Inconsistencies & Imprecision:** Several technical details are not fully clarified or exhibit inconsistencies. For example, the description of trajectory stacking (Section 3.1) is unclear about how the trajectories are initialized and terminated, and how boundary conditions are handled. The equation (2) appears to require initial conditions not defined in the text. Similarly, the discussion of mean flow subtraction feels ad-hoc without a deeper justification.
*   **Overly Detailed & Sometimes Redundant Description:** The paper is excessively verbose in certain sections, particularly Section 3. The detailed explanation of different optical flow representations, while thorough, often repeats concepts and could be more concise. Figures 2 and 3 are somewhat redundant, and the explanation of Fig. 3 is unclear.
*   **Formatting and Typographical Errors:** Numerous minor formatting and typographical errors exist throughout the paper (e.g., "mult-task" in Section 1, missing period at the end of sentence in section 3.1, inconsistent use of equations and parentheses). While individually minor, these errors accumulate and create a sense of carelessness.
*   **Limited Discussion of Limitations:** The paper briefly touches upon limitations in the conclusion, but a more comprehensive discussion of the drawbacks of the approach (e.g., computational cost of optical flow, sensitivity to optical flow estimation errors) would be valuable.
*   **Insufficient Comparison to More Recent Work:** While the authors compare to a good set of baselines, the inclusion of more recent action recognition methods, particularly those utilizing 3D convolutional networks, would provide a more comprehensive comparison.

**Specific Concerns & Questions:**

*   The justification for using ReLU activation is lacking. Why ReLU and not other activations?
*   The choice of the specific ConvNet architecture (CNN-M-2048) is not fully justified. What alternatives were considered?
*   The impact of different dropout rates on performance is not thoroughly analyzed.
*   The description of the SVM fusion method lacks details (e.g., kernel type, regularization parameter).
*   The statement "temporal ConvNets significantly outperform the spatial ConvNets" needs quantification. What is the exact performance difference?

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Demonstrates a viable and competitive approach to action recognition using deep learning.
*   Provides a thorough experimental evaluation.
*   Offers valuable insights into the benefits of decoupling spatial and temporal information.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

*   The lack of significant novelty.
*   The numerous minor technical issues and inconsistencies.
*   The excessive verbosity and lack of conciseness in certain sections.
*   The cumulative effect of formatting and typographical errors, suggesting insufficient attention to detail.



**Overall Assessment:**

While the paper presents a technically sound and reasonably effective approach to action recognition, the persistent presence of minor flaws, coupled with the lack of substantial novelty, makes it difficult to recommend acceptance. The paper feels more like a well-engineered implementation of an existing idea, rather than a groundbreaking contribution. The attention to detail, while appreciated in some aspects (implementation details), is conspicuously absent in other areas (formatting, technical precision). I recommend **rejection** at this time, with encouragement for the authors to address the identified issues and resubmit to a future conference. A thorough revision focusing on clarity, conciseness, and technical rigor is crucial.
