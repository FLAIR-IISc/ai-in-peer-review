

# Review of "Two-Stream Convolutional Networks for Action Recognition in Videos"

This paper proposes a two-stream architecture for video action recognition, with one stream processing spatial information (individual video frames) and the other processing temporal information (dense optical flow). The authors claim this approach significantly outperforms previous deep learning methods for video action recognition (which typically process stacked frames) and is competitive with state-of-the-art shallow representations. The work builds on the two-stream hypothesis in visual neuroscience and extends previous work on video recognition, including the recent [14] which explored ConvNets for video action recognition.

## Strengths

The paper presents a well-structured experimental evaluation on two standard benchmarks (UCF101 and HMDB-51), with appropriate ablation studies on different temporal input configurations. The implementation details are thorough, including the use of multi-task learning to combine datasets and the careful handling of optical flow computation. The authors demonstrate that their two-stream approach significantly outperforms single-stream deep learning methods (like [14]) and achieves competitive results with state-of-the-art shallow representations.

## Weaknesses

The primary weakness of this paper is its lack of sufficient evidence to support the claim that the two-stream architecture is fundamentally superior to alternative approaches. The authors compare their method against [14] which was trained on a much larger dataset (Sports-1M, 1.1M videos), while their temporal stream is trained on relatively small datasets (UCF101 and HMDB-51). This makes the comparison unfair, as the performance difference could simply reflect the difference in training data size rather than the architecture.

The paper also fails to adequately address why the two-stream approach is better than using optical flow directly as input to a single stream (which is essentially what they're doing with their temporal stream). The spatial stream's contribution is minimal (only 14% improvement over the temporal stream alone), and the paper doesn't provide sufficient justification for why combining the streams is necessary.

The novelty of the approach is questionable. The use of optical flow as input to ConvNets for video recognition is not new, and the two-stream architecture is directly inspired by neuroscience, not a novel technical contribution. The paper doesn't sufficiently distinguish its method from previous work, particularly [16] which also used two streams but with hand-crafted HMAX models.

## Evaluation

**Quality**: The experimental design is sound, but the comparisons are not sufficiently rigorous. The authors fail to address the critical issue of dataset size differences in their comparisons, which undermines the validity of their claims about the superiority of their architecture. The paper lacks theoretical justification for the two-stream approach, relying solely on empirical results.

**Clarity**: The paper is well-written and organized, with clear figures and detailed implementation descriptions. The relation to previous work is well-explained, making it easy to understand the paper's contribution within the broader context of video action recognition.

**Originality**: The core idea of using two streams for video recognition is not novel. While the application of deep learning to both streams is an extension of previous work, the paper doesn't sufficiently differentiate its approach from existing two-stream methods like [16] or [14]. The optical flow input to ConvNets has been explored before, and the multi-task learning approach is standard practice.

**Significance**: The results are competitive with state-of-the-art, but the improvements are marginal compared to previous work. The paper doesn't present a compelling argument for how this approach will advance the field beyond the current state. The method doesn't address the key challenge of scaling to larger datasets (despite acknowledging this limitation), and the improvements over existing methods don't represent a major breakthrough.

## Recommendation

I cannot recommend acceptance of this paper for NIPS. While the work is technically sound and the experiments are well-executed, the paper fails to provide sufficient evidence to support its claims of a fundamentally better approach. The lack of rigorous comparison with methods trained on comparable dataset sizes, combined with the questionable novelty of the two-stream architecture, undermines the paper's contribution. The authors have presented an incremental improvement rather than a significant advancement in the field.

The paper would benefit from a more careful comparison with methods trained on similar-sized datasets, a clearer theoretical justification for the two-stream approach, and a more thorough discussion of how this work differs from previous two-stream architectures. Without these improvements, the paper does not meet NIPS's standards for significant contributions to the field.

The authors' claim that "our proposed architecture significantly outperforms that of [14]" is misleading without proper context about the difference in training data size. This is a critical flaw that needs to be addressed before the paper can be considered for publication at NIPS.
