Summary
The paper proposes a two-stream ConvNet architecture for action recognition in videos: a spatial stream operating on RGB frames (pretrained on ImageNet) and a temporal stream operating on stacked dense optical flow fields. The streams are fused by averaging or a linear SVM over softmax scores. The authors explore several temporal input configurations (optical-flow stacking, trajectory stacking, bi-directional flow, mean-flow subtraction) and introduce a simple multi-task training setup to leverage UCF-101 and HMDB-51 jointly. Experiments on UCF-101 and HMDB-51 show that (i) temporal ConvNets with optical flow substantially outperform RGB-only models trained on frames or stacked frames, (ii) spatial and temporal streams are complementary, and (iii) multi-task learning improves temporal stream performance on small datasets. The work relates to trajectory-based shallow pipelines (HOF/MBH/Fisher) and prior deep video models (e.g., Karpathy et al.’s slow/late fusion), arguing that explicit motion (optical flow) as input simplifies learning.

Strengths
- Clear, sensible decomposition into spatial and temporal streams; the temporal stream’s explicit optical flow input is well motivated and effective.
- Systematic investigation of temporal inputs (L, mean subtraction, trajectory vs. location stacking, bidirectionality), with strong empirical gains over frame- or stacked-RGB inputs.
- Practical multi-task training trick to mitigate data scarcity in video tasks.
- Demonstrated complementarity of streams; late fusion yields solid improvements.
- Convincing performance on UCF-101 and HMDB-51; temporal stream alone is competitive with strong shallow baselines; fusion further improves.

Weaknesses and concerns
Methodological/detail omissions affecting reproducibility:
- Optical flow details are underspecified: parameters of [2] (pyramid levels, regularization, warping iterations), pre/post-processing, handling of occlusions, boundary conditions for bi-directional stacks. No ablation across flow algorithms (e.g., [30]) or parameter sensitivity.
- Flow quantization/compression: Displacement fields are linearly rescaled, JPEG-compressed, then rescaled back. No analysis of quantization artifacts on accuracy; unclear clipping/scale limits; potential bias across datasets.
- Mean-flow subtraction: Is the mean computed per full frame or per crop? Before or after JPEG quantization? Consistency between train/test is unclear.
- Multi-task learning: Loss weighting between tasks, dataset sampling strategy within mini-batches, and class-imbalance handling are not specified. The text mentions sampling uniformly across classes for single-dataset training; the multi-task regime likely alters sampling but is not described.
- Fusion: Using an SVM on L2-normalized softmax scores raises calibration questions. Why not train a shallow learned fusion layer end-to-end with proper regularization? No statistical test of fusion gains; hyperparameters for SVM (C, class weights) are missing.

Evaluation/protocol issues:
- Most ablations appear to be on UCF-101 split 1; variance across splits is not reported. Statistical significance or confidence intervals are absent.
- Fairness of comparisons: Slow fusion replication (11 frames) differs from [14] and is trained from scratch on UCF only; not strictly comparable. Similarly, comparisons to trajectory-based methods should control for camera-motion compensation; your approach only subtracts mean flow (a weak proxy).
- HMDB/UCF cross-dataset training: When training HMDB with “all splits combined” UCF data (or vice versa), clarify whether any test videos from one dataset were used as auxiliary supervision when evaluating on the other (acceptable but should be stated explicitly), and whether class overlaps could leak information.
- Runtime/compute: The approach precomputes flow; no inference-time throughput or wall-clock comparisons, nor resource requirements (beyond training). For practitioners, flow computation often dominates. Report end-to-end inference speed and memory.
- Sensitivity to L, number of frames sampled at test time (25), and cropping strategy (10 crops) is not explored beyond L=5/10; no temporal stride ablation.

Clarity and organization:
- Figures and tables are repeatedly referenced (Fig. 1–3, Tables 1–4) but not visible in the submission; without them, critical architectural and numeric details are missing. Equations (1)–(2) are helpful but do not substitute for the absent illustrations/tables.
- Architecture details are too sparse: exact layer shapes, kernel sizes/strides/padding, places where LRN is used/removed, dropout placements, and which layers are fine-tuned vs. frozen. “CNN-M-2048” alone is insufficient for reproduction.
- Dataset protocols are described, but per-class results, confusion matrices, and failure cases are not provided.

Originality
- The two-stream idea and the explicit optical-flow input for the temporal ConvNet are a valuable, pragmatic reframing relative to learning motion implicitly from stacked frames. The multi-task training application to video action recognition is reasonable though not novel. The connection to hand-crafted motion descriptors is well articulated.

Significance
- The empirical improvements on established benchmarks and the clear finding that flow-as-input is crucial should influence subsequent architectures. However, the reliance on precomputed flow and several missing experimental details limit immediate reproducibility and practical adoption.

Questions for the authors
- Specify optical flow parameters and provide an ablation on flow algorithm choice and quantization (with/without JPEG).
- Clarify mean-flow subtraction timing/scope (global vs. crop).
- Detail multi-task sampling and loss weighting; are tasks mixed within a batch? How is class imbalance handled?
- Report end-to-end inference speed (fps) including flow, and model parameter counts.
- Provide variance across splits and statistical tests for fusion gains.
- Why not learn fusion end-to-end (e.g., a small FC layer) with regularization?

Minor issues and formatting
- Numerous reference entries are incomplete or inconsistent (missing years/venues; duplicated commas). “D convolutional neural networks” likely “3D”. “mult-task” appears once. “finetuned” vs. “fine-tuned” inconsistent. “sub-image” vs. “subimage” inconsistent. Ensure consistent hyphenation (bi-directional/bidirectional).
- A footnote-like sentence in Section 6 (“Very recently, [14] released...”) appears mid-paragraph; consider proper footnotes or moving to Related Work.

Pros
- Simple, well-motivated architecture with strong empirical gains.
- Clear evidence that explicit motion input helps.
- Complementarity of streams demonstrated; multi-task helps data-scarce settings.

Cons
- Missing critical implementation/evaluation details; limited ablations.
- Questionable effects of flow compression unaddressed.
- Comparisons not strictly controlled; no runtime analysis.
- Absent figures/tables in submission hinder assessment.

Recommendation
Borderline reject. The contribution is promising and likely impactful, but the submission, as written, omits too many methodological details and analyses needed for a NeurIPS-standard, reproducible paper. A revision addressing the above could warrant acceptance.
