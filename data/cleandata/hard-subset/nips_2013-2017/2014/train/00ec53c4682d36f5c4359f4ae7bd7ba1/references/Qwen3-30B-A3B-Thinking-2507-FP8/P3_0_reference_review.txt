

# NIPS Review: "Two-Stream Convolutional Networks for Action Recognition in Videos"

## Summary of Main Contributions

This paper proposes a two-stream convolutional network architecture for video action recognition, addressing a key limitation of previous deep learning approaches to video classification. The authors decompose video recognition into two complementary streams: a spatial stream that processes individual video frames (leveraging pre-training on ImageNet), and a temporal stream that processes optical flow information between frames. The key innovation is using optical flow as explicit input to the temporal stream, rather than trying to learn motion implicitly from raw frame stacks as in previous work [14]. The paper also introduces multi-task learning to effectively combine training data from multiple datasets (UCF-101 and HMDB-51), and demonstrates that the two streams are complementary, with their fusion significantly outperforming both individual streams and previous deep learning approaches.

The work builds on the two-stream hypothesis in neuroscience [9] but implements it using deep convolutional networks rather than hand-crafted features. The authors thoroughly evaluate different optical flow input configurations for the temporal stream and demonstrate that stacking multiple flow fields (L=10) with mean subtraction provides the best results. Their two-stream architecture achieves competitive results with state-of-the-art hand-crafted methods [20, 21, 26] on standard benchmarks (UCF-101 and HMDB-51), while being a more unified deep learning approach.

## Strengths

1. **Strong Motivation and Novel Architecture**: The paper effectively argues why previous deep learning approaches for video action recognition [14] failed (they implicitly tried to learn motion from raw frames, which is challenging). The two-stream architecture is well-motivated by both neuroscience and practical considerations, and the use of optical flow as explicit input to the temporal stream is a significant improvement over stacking RGB frames.

2. **Comprehensive Experimental Evaluation**: The paper provides thorough ablation studies on the temporal stream input configurations (optical flow stacking vs. trajectory stacking, bi-directional flow, etc.), demonstrating that stacking multiple flow fields (L=10) with mean subtraction is optimal. The comparison between spatial and temporal streams alone and their fusion is particularly convincing.

3. **Effective Multi-Task Learning**: The multi-task learning approach to combine UCF-101 and HMDB-51 datasets is clever and practical, especially given the limited size of video action recognition datasets. The results show that multi-task learning outperforms other data combination strategies, making a strong case for this approach.

4. **Competitive Results**: The paper demonstrates that their two-stream architecture achieves results comparable to state-of-the-art hand-crafted methods [20, 21, 26], which is significant because previous deep learning approaches [14] were substantially worse. This represents a meaningful advance in video action recognition with deep learning.

5. **Technical Detail and Reproducibility**: The implementation details (including training procedures, data augmentation, and flow computation) are provided with sufficient detail to allow reproducibility. The use of pre-computed optical flow (with efficient storage via JPEG compression) is a practical consideration that addresses a major bottleneck in video action recognition.

## Weaknesses and Criticisms

1. **Lack of Integration with End-to-End Learning**: The paper pre-computes optical flow using a standard method [2] rather than integrating flow computation into the end-to-end learning process. This is a significant limitation compared to more recent approaches (e.g., [30]) that combine optical flow computation with deep learning. The authors acknowledge this as a direction for future work, but it's a notable gap in the current methodology.

2. **Insufficient Analysis of What the Temporal Stream Learns**: While the paper visualizes the first-layer filters of the temporal network, it doesn't provide deeper insights into what the network actually learns at higher layers. Understanding the representations learned by the temporal stream would strengthen the paper's theoretical contribution.

3. **Limited Comparison to More Recent Work**: The paper compares to state-of-the-art hand-crafted methods from 2012-2013 [20, 21, 26], but doesn't compare to more recent deep learning approaches that might have improved on the state of the art since the paper was written. For instance, the paper doesn't compare to the work of [14] which was published around the same time (though it does mention [14] in the introduction).

4. **No Analysis of Computational Cost**: The paper mentions that pre-computing optical flow for UCF-101 required 27GB (from 1.5TB), but doesn't discuss the computational cost of this preprocessing step relative to training the networks. This is a practical consideration for real-world applications.

5. **Overlooking the Potential of 3D Convolutions**: The paper briefly mentions the "slow fusion" architecture of [14] (which uses stacked RGB frames), but doesn't explore the potential of 3D convolutional networks that can learn spatio-temporal features directly from raw video. This is a significant omission given that 3D CNNs have since become the dominant approach for video recognition.

## Quality and Technical Soundness

The paper is technically sound, with well-designed experiments that thoroughly evaluate the proposed architecture. The ablation studies are particularly strong, providing clear evidence for the benefits of each component (e.g., stacking multiple flow fields, mean subtraction). The multi-task learning approach is well-justified and effective. The results are well-supported by experimental evidence on standard benchmarks.

However, the paper would benefit from more theoretical analysis of why the two-stream architecture works well. While the authors mention the two-stream hypothesis in neuroscience [9], they don't explore this connection in depth, which could provide valuable insights into why their approach is successful.

## Clarity

The paper is well-organized and clearly written. The methodology is explained in sufficient detail, and the figures (particularly Figure 3 showing the optical flow input configurations) are helpful. The paper would benefit from a more detailed explanation of the multi-task learning approach (Section 4), including a diagram showing how the two softmax layers work together.

## Originality

The core idea of a two-stream architecture for video action recognition is novel in the context of deep learning. While the two-stream hypothesis in neuroscience [9] provides inspiration, the implementation using deep convolutional networks for both streams is a significant contribution. The use of optical flow as explicit input to the temporal stream is particularly novel and distinguishes this work from previous deep learning approaches to video recognition.

The paper does a good job of distinguishing its approach from related work, particularly [14] and [16]. However, it could have more explicitly connected to the recent work on 3D convolutional networks (though these were less common at the time of submission).

## Significance

This paper represents a meaningful advance in video action recognition with deep learning. By demonstrating that a two-stream architecture using optical flow can achieve results competitive with state-of-the-art hand-crafted methods, the authors show that deep learning can be successfully applied to video action recognition with the right architecture. This work has had a significant impact, as it directly inspired the development of more advanced two-stream architectures (like the later "Two-Stream I3D" model) and established a paradigm that is still widely used today.

The paper addresses a difficult problem (video action recognition) in a better way than previous research by introducing a novel architecture that explicitly separates spatial and temporal processing. It advances the state of the art in a demonstrable way, as shown by the comprehensive experimental results.

## Overall Assessment

This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of video action recognition. The two-stream architecture with optical flow input is a clever and effective solution to a challenging problem. The paper is well-written, technically sound, and provides thorough experimental validation. While there are some limitations (particularly the pre-computation of optical flow), these are acknowledged by the authors and represent natural directions for future work.

The paper is clearly within the scope of NIPS, as it presents a novel deep learning approach to a challenging computer vision problem with significant theoretical and practical implications.

## Recommendation

**Accept** - This paper makes a significant contribution to the field of video action recognition with deep learning. It presents a novel architecture that effectively combines spatial and temporal information, and demonstrates competitive results with state-of-the-art methods. The experimental evaluation is thorough and well-designed, and the paper is well-written and clearly organized.

## Specific Suggestions for Improvement

1. The paper could benefit from a more detailed analysis of what the temporal stream learns (beyond the first-layer filters), perhaps through visualization of higher-level features or feature attribution techniques.

2. A comparison to more recent deep learning approaches (like 3D CNNs) would strengthen the paper, even if only as a brief discussion of how the two-stream approach relates to these methods.

3. The paper should explicitly discuss the computational cost of the optical flow pre-computation step and how it compares to the cost of training the networks.

4. The authors could provide more insight into why the temporal stream outperforms the spatial stream (beyond the obvious importance of motion information), perhaps by analyzing which types of actions benefit most from motion information.

5. A diagram illustrating the multi-task learning architecture would improve clarity, particularly for readers unfamiliar with this approach.

This is an excellent paper that makes a meaningful contribution to the field. I believe it would be a valuable addition to the NIPS proceedings and would be of significant interest to the machine learning and computer vision communities.
