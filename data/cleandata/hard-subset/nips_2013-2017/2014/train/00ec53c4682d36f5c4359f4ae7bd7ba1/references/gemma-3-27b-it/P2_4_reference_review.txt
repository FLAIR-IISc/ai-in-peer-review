## Review of "Two-Stream Convolutional Networks for Action Recognition in Videos"

**Overall Assessment:** This paper presents a strong contribution to the field of video action recognition. It proposes a simple yet effective two-stream convolutional network architecture that significantly outperforms previous deep learning approaches and achieves competitive results with state-of-the-art hand-crafted features. The work is well-motivated, clearly written, and demonstrates a good understanding of the existing literature. I believe this paper is a strong accept, leaning towards a high confidence score.

**1. Summary & Relation to Prior Work:**

The paper addresses the challenge of action recognition in videos by proposing a two-stream convolutional network: a spatial stream processing individual frames and a temporal stream processing stacked optical flow.  This effectively decouples the representation of *what* is happening (spatial) from *how* it is happening (temporal). This is motivated by the biological “two-streams” hypothesis of visual processing.  Prior work in deep learning for video primarily focused on stacking frames as input, which the authors argue is ineffective. They correctly point out that existing hand-crafted features (trajectory-based methods) still outperform these early deep learning attempts.  The authors build upon recent advances in image recognition (specifically, pre-training on ImageNet) and extend them to the video domain, creating a system that leverages both spatial and temporal information in a principled manner.  They appropriately position their work in relation to earlier attempts at two-stream architectures (e.g., [16]), highlighting the key difference: the use of *deep* convolutional networks instead of shallow HMAX models.

**2. Strengths:**

*   **Strong Performance:** The results presented in the paper are compelling. The two-stream architecture demonstrably outperforms the single-stream approach of [14] and achieves competitive accuracy on UCF101 and HMDB51 compared to hand-crafted features.
*   **Clear and Principled Approach:** The idea of decoupling spatial and temporal streams is intuitive and well-justified. The use of optical flow for the temporal stream is a smart choice as it explicitly represents motion.
*   **Leveraging Pre-training:**  Effectively utilizing pre-training on ImageNet for the spatial stream is a significant contribution, allowing the network to benefit from large-scale image data.
*   **Thorough Evaluation:** The authors conduct extensive experiments exploring different input configurations for the temporal stream (stacking, trajectories, bidirectional flow, mean subtraction) and fusion methods (averaging, SVM).
*   **Clear Writing:** The paper is well-organized and clearly written, making it easy to understand the proposed approach and results. The figures are helpful.
* **Multi-task learning:** The inclusion of multi-task learning helps to regularize and improve performance, demonstrating a good understanding of practical training considerations.

**3. Weaknesses:**

*   **Computational Cost:**  While the authors mention the computational cost of optical flow, it remains a significant practical limitation. Pre-computing the flow helps, but it's still an additional step and storage requirement.
*   **Limited Discussion of Ablation Study Justification:** While many ablation studies are performed, the rationale behind choosing specific parameter settings (e.g., L=10 for stacked frames) isn’t always explicitly discussed. A more thorough justification would be helpful.
* **Lack of comparison with more recent methods**: The comparison to state-of-the-art is somewhat dated now, given the rapid advancement in the field. While the paper was submitted a while ago, more recent comparisons would strengthen the impact.
* **Optical Flow Implementation Details:** While they cite the optical flow implementation [2], details regarding parameter choices in that implementation would be beneficial for reproducibility.



**4. Addressing Review Criteria:**

*   **Quality:** The paper is technically sound.  The experiments are well-designed, and the results are statistically meaningful. The ablation studies provide strong support for the design choices. The inclusion of multi-task learning further demonstrates the authors’ technical competence.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally clear. The architecture is well-illustrated (Fig. 1), and the explanations are concise and easy to follow.
*   **Originality:** The combination of deep convolutional networks with a two-stream architecture and the explicit use of optical flow for motion representation is novel and represents a significant improvement over existing approaches.
*   **Significance:** This work has the potential to be highly influential in the field of video action recognition. The proposed architecture is relatively simple to implement and has demonstrated promising results. It provides a strong baseline for future research.

**5. Arguments For and Against Acceptance:**

**For Acceptance:**

*   Strong empirical results demonstrating significant improvement over existing methods.
*   Clear and principled approach with strong theoretical motivation.
*   Thorough experimental evaluation and ablation studies.
*   Well-written and easy to understand.

**Against Acceptance:**

*   Computational cost of optical flow.
*   Limited discussion of parameter selection.
*   Comparison against more recent state-of-the-art methods would be beneficial.



**6. Recommendation:**

**Accept** with high confidence. This paper represents a significant advance in video action recognition and is a valuable contribution to the NIPS community. While there are minor weaknesses, the strengths of the work far outweigh them. I would recommend a presentation slot if possible, given the potential impact of this work.
