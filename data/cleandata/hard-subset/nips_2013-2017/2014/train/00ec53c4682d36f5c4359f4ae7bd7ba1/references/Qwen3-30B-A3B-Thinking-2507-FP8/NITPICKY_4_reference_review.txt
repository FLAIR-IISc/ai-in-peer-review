

# Review of "Two-Stream Convolutional Networks for Action Recognition in Videos"

This paper proposes a two-stream architecture for video action recognition, where one stream processes spatial information from individual frames (using a pre-trained ConvNet) and the other processes temporal information from optical flow (using a ConvNet trained on optical flow). The authors claim their method outperforms previous deep learning approaches for action recognition and is competitive with state-of-the-art shallow representations.

## Strengths
The paper presents a clear and logical approach to video action recognition by decoupling spatial and temporal processing, which is conceptually sound. The authors provide thorough experimental validation across two standard benchmarks (UCF-101 and HMDB-51), and the multi-task learning framework for handling small video datasets is a useful contribution. The architecture is well-motivated, and the paper demonstrates that temporal information is crucial for action recognition.

## Weaknesses

### Technical Inconsistencies
The paper contains several technical inconsistencies that undermine its credibility. In the introduction, the authors state that their method "is competitive with the state of the art shallow representations [20, 21, 26]" but earlier claim that [14] "found that a network, operating on individual video frames, performs similarly to the networks, whose input is a stack of frames" and "turned out to be 20% less accurate than hand-crafted state-of-the-art trajectory-based representation [20, 27]." This creates confusion about how the authors' method compares to [20, 21, 26] versus [20, 27].

### Missing Methodological Details
The paper lacks critical implementation details required for reproducibility. The architecture of the ConvNets is described as "similar to the network of [31]" but no specific layer configuration is provided. The paper mentions "the supplementary material" but doesn't include the visualizations of learned filters as promised. The exact implementation of the optical flow computation (beyond citing [2]) is insufficient for replication.

### Incomplete Comparisons
The paper claims to be "competitive with the state of the art shallow representations" but fails to provide a direct comparison with the best shallow methods. For instance, the paper cites [26] as a state-of-the-art shallow method but doesn't report results from [26] for fair comparison. The paper also fails to compare with other contemporary deep learning approaches such as 3D ConvNets (e.g., [12, 31]) which were emerging around the same time.

### Experimental Flaws
The paper reports results for "temporal ConvNets significantly outperforming the spatial ConvNets" but provides no statistical significance tests to support this claim. The paper also makes inconsistent claims about the effectiveness of multi-task learning. In Table 2, it states that "multi-task learning performs the best," but in Table 3, it claims that "using bi-directional flow is not beneficial in the case of ConvNet fusion," which contradicts the earlier claim that bi-directional flow was "only slightly better than a uni-directional forward flow."

### Formatting and Presentation Issues
The paper contains multiple formatting inconsistencies. It uses "Sect. 1.1" instead of "Section 1.1" throughout. The references are inconsistently formatted (e.g., "Large scale visual recognition challenge (ILSVRC), A. Berg, J. Deng, L. Fei-Fei, , , " has missing information). The paper mentions "Fig. 1" but the figure is not included in the provided text. The paper also uses inconsistent notation for optical flow (e.g., "dxτ+k-1" without proper mathematical formatting).

### Unjustified Claims
The paper claims that "the temporal ConvNet, trained using multi-task learning, performs the best both alone and when fused with a spatial net" but provides no ablation study to support this claim. The paper also states that "using the bi-directional optical flow is only slightly better than a uni-directional forward flow" but then later claims "bi-directional flow is not beneficial in the case of ConvNet fusion," which is contradictory.

### Technical Errors
In section 3.1, the optical flow stacking equation (1) states "Iτ(u, v, 2k - 1) = dxτ+k-1(u, v)," which would imply that for k=1, it's dxτ(u, v), for k=2, it's dxτ+1(u, v), etc. However, the description claims "the channels Iτ(u, v, c), c = [1; 2L] encode the motion at that point over a sequence of L frames," which would require the frames to be τ to τ+L-1, not τ to τ+L. This indexing error affects the clarity of the method.

## Conclusion

While the two-stream architecture is conceptually sound and the results are promising, the paper suffers from multiple technical inconsistencies, missing implementation details, and formatting errors that significantly undermine its quality as a NIPS submission. The paper would benefit from a complete revision to address these issues, including providing all necessary implementation details, resolving contradictions in the results, and including direct comparisons with the best shallow methods.

The paper's technical flaws are substantial enough that they overshadow its conceptual contribution. For a NIPS submission, the paper must provide complete, reproducible methodology with rigorous experimental validation. These requirements are not met in the current version.

I recommend rejection due to the numerous technical inconsistencies and insufficient methodological details that prevent proper evaluation of the work.
