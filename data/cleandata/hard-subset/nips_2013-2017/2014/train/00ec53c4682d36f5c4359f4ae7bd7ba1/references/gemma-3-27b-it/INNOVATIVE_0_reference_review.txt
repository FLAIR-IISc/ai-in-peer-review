This paper presents a two-stream convolutional network architecture for action recognition in videos, decoupling spatial and temporal information processing into separate streams. The core idea – leveraging dedicated ConvNets for spatial (frame-level) and temporal (motion-level) information, and fusing their outputs – is a compelling and conceptually clean approach. It’s a strong departure from simply stacking frames as input to a single ConvNet, an approach the authors rightly point out suffers in performance. The connection drawn to the ventral/dorsal stream hypothesis in the visual cortex, while not explored in depth, provides a biologically plausible motivation.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The two-stream architecture is a genuinely novel idea in the context of deep learning for video action recognition at the time of submission. It’s a significant architectural shift from prior work that attempted to learn spatio-temporal features implicitly within a single network.
* **Strong Performance:** The results demonstrate a clear improvement over the stacked frame approach of [14] and achieve competitive performance with state-of-the-art handcrafted features. This validates the effectiveness of the proposed architecture.
* **Clear Writing & Organization:** The paper is well-written and logically organized, making it easy to understand the proposed method and its evaluation.
* **Insightful Analysis:** The ablation studies (different input configurations for the temporal stream, fusion methods) provide valuable insights into the components driving performance. The discussion of the limitations of previous approaches is also well-reasoned.
* **Practicality:** The pre-training of the spatial stream on ImageNet is a pragmatic step that addresses the limited size of available video datasets.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Optical Flow Reliance:** While the use of optical flow explicitly encodes motion, it introduces a dependency on accurate and robust optical flow computation. The paper doesn’t deeply investigate the sensitivity of the method to optical flow errors.
* **Limited Exploration of Fusion:** While SVM fusion slightly outperforms averaging, the exploration of fusion strategies feels somewhat limited. More sophisticated fusion mechanisms could potentially yield further improvements.
* **Depth of Biological Motivation:** The connection to the ventral/dorsal stream hypothesis is mentioned but not explored further. While not essential, a deeper investigation could provide additional justification for the architectural choices.
* **Dataset Limitations:** Although the multi-task learning addresses this to some extent, the experiments are still performed on relatively small datasets (UCF101 & HMDB51). Scaling to larger datasets (like Sports-1M, as acknowledged by the authors) is an open question.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a genuinely novel architecture for video action recognition that demonstrably improves upon existing approaches.
* The results are compelling and suggest a promising direction for future research in this area.
* The paper is well-written, clearly organized, and provides a thorough evaluation of the proposed method.
* The identified weaknesses are not critical and do not detract significantly from the overall contribution.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The reliance on optical flow introduces an external dependency.
* The exploration of fusion strategies and the biological motivation could be more in-depth.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that introduces a significant and innovative contribution to the field of video action recognition. The two-stream architecture is a conceptually elegant and effective approach. While there are some minor weaknesses, the novelty and demonstrated performance outweigh these concerns. I believe this paper is well-suited for acceptance at NIPS. The paper prioritizes a novel idea and its demonstration over extensive methodological polishing, which aligns with my reviewing preferences. The potential impact on the field, even with the identified limitations, is considerable. I recommend **acceptance**.
