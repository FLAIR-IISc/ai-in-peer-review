## Review of "Two-Stream Convolutional Networks for Action Recognition in Videos"

**Summary:**

This paper presents a two-stream convolutional neural network architecture for action recognition in videos. The key idea is to decouple spatial and temporal information into separate streams, a spatial stream processing individual frames and a temporal stream processing stacked optical flow. These streams, implemented as CNNs, are then fused for final classification. The authors demonstrate improvements over previous deep learning approaches for action recognition and achieve competitive performance with state-of-the-art hand-crafted features on the UCF101 and HMDB51 datasets. They also explore multi-task learning to leverage larger datasets.

**Relation to Previous Work:**

The work builds upon the growing trend of applying deep learning to video understanding. It addresses limitations of previous attempts using stacked frames as input to a single CNN [14], which struggled to effectively capture motion. The two-stream approach is motivated by the "two-streams hypothesis" in visual neuroscience [9], and relates to earlier two-stream architectures using hand-crafted features [16]. The paper positions itself as a deep learning generalization of successful hand-crafted feature pipelines, particularly those leveraging dense trajectories and optical flow [20, 26].  

**Strengths:**

* **Novel Architecture:** The two-stream architecture, separating spatial and temporal processing, is a clear and effective contribution. This allows for targeted learning of each modality and leverages the complementary nature of appearance and motion information.
* **Strong Empirical Results:** The paper demonstrates significant improvements over previous deep learning approaches [14] and achieves competitive results with hand-crafted features on standard benchmarks.  The ablation studies are thorough, demonstrating the benefits of different architectural choices (stacking optical flow, mean subtraction).
* **Clear and Well-Written:** The paper is generally well-written and logically organized. The explanation of the architecture and the motivation behind design choices are clear. 
* **Multi-Task Learning:**  The inclusion of multi-task learning is a practical and effective approach to mitigate the limited size of available video datasets.
* **Connection to Hand-Crafted Features:** The authors successfully link their convolutional network layers to commonly used hand-crafted features, providing intuition and validation for the learnt representations.
* **Pre-training:**  Leveraging pre-training on ImageNet significantly boosts performance, demonstrating the power of transfer learning in this domain.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Optical Flow Dependency:** The temporal stream relies heavily on the quality of optical flow computation. While standard methods are used, the performance is inherently limited by the accuracy and computational cost of optical flow. The paper doesn't deeply explore the sensitivity to optical flow errors.
* **Limited Discussion of Computational Cost:** While the implementation details mention GPU usage, a more thorough discussion of the computational complexity, especially concerning the optical flow calculation, would be beneficial.
* **Lack of Comparison to More Recent Work:** The paper was written prior to a wave of more recent advances in video understanding (e.g., 3D CNNs, Transformers for video). A brief discussion of how this work relates to these more recent models would provide valuable context.
* **Limited Analysis of Learned Filters:** While the paper *mentions* visualizing filters, a more detailed analysis of what the temporal stream's convolutional filters are learning could provide further insights into the effectiveness of the architecture.
* **Fusion Method:** The choice of averaging or SVM for fusion seems somewhat arbitrary. Exploring more sophisticated fusion techniques could potentially yield further improvements.



**Qualitative Evaluation:**

* **Quality:** Technically sound, claims are well-supported by experimental results. The architecture is a complete piece of work, although it would benefit from discussion of limitations like optical flow dependency. The authors are careful to evaluate the impact of different design choices.
* **Clarity:**  Excellent clarity in both the description of the architecture and the presentation of the results. 
* **Originality:** The two-stream architecture is a novel combination of existing techniques, offering a significant improvement over previous approaches. The paper is well-referenced and positions itself clearly within the existing literature.
* **Significance:** The results are important, demonstrating a significant advance in video action recognition. The architecture is likely to be useful for other researchers and practitioners in the field. This work has indeed influenced later research in video understanding.

**Arguments for and against acceptance:**

**Pro:**

* Demonstrates a significant improvement in video action recognition using a novel architecture.
* Thorough experimental evaluation with ablation studies.
* Clear and well-written presentation.
* Addresses a challenging problem with a practical solution.

**Con:**

* Reliance on optical flow introduces a potential bottleneck.
* Limited discussion of computational complexity.
* Lack of comparison to more recent advancements in the field.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of video action recognition. The two-stream architecture is a clever and effective approach, and the empirical results are compelling. While the reliance on optical flow and the lack of comparison to more recent work are minor weaknesses, they do not detract significantly from the overall quality of the paper. 

**Recommendation:**

**Accept with minor revisions.**  I recommend acceptance.  The authors could strengthen the paper by addressing the limitations noted above, particularly by discussing the computational cost and relating their work to more recent advancements in video understanding.
