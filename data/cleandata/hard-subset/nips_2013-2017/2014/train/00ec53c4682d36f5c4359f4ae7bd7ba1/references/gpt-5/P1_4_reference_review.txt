Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes a two-stream convolutional architecture for video action recognition that explicitly decouples appearance and motion. The spatial stream is a ConvNet applied to individual RGB frames and is pre-trained on ImageNet; the temporal stream is a ConvNet applied to stacks of dense optical flow fields across multiple frames. Scores from the two streams are fused (averaging or linear SVM). The authors further introduce a simple, effective multi-task learning scheme to leverage multiple video datasets (UCF-101, HMDB-51) by sharing the ConvNet trunk and using dataset-specific softmax heads.

Relative to prior deep approaches that feed stacks of raw RGB frames to learn motion implicitly [Ji et al. (3D CNN), Karpathy et al.], this work explicitly represents motion via optical flow and shows this is crucial for performance. The design also echoes the two-stream hypothesis from neuroscience and earlier two-stream HMAX-based systems (Jhuang et al.), but here both streams are learned deep ConvNets. The temporal stream input generalizes popular hand-crafted video features (HOF, MBH, kinematic features, dense trajectories), and the paper offers a clear mapping from these to early ConvNet layers. The multi-task training builds on established ideas (Collobert & Weston) but is adapted to video recognition to combat overfitting on small datasets.

Quality
The paper is technically sound and experimentally careful. Key ablations are provided for the temporal stream: number of stacked flows (L), trajectory vs. location stacking, bidirectional vs. forward flow, and mean-flow subtraction. The comparison to raw multi-frame RGB “slow fusion” baselines is informative and favors optical-flow input. Fusion strategies are compared, and the case for multi-task learning is supported both when HMDB-51 is the target and when it regularizes UCF-101. Implementation details (network, training schedule, data augmentation, GPU setup, precomputing/compressing flow) are sufficient for reproduction. The evaluation on UCF-101 and HMDB-51 follows standard protocols with three splits; the method is competitive with state-of-the-art shallow pipelines and outperforms prior deep baselines by a large margin.

Some limitations of the evidence: the study focuses on two datasets and does not include larger-scale training (e.g., Sports-1M) though this is discussed as future work; runtime and computational cost are only briefly described (precomputing flow is necessary and large-scale storage is a bottleneck); analysis of failure modes and class-wise performance is missing; joint end-to-end learning of motion estimation is not attempted.

Clarity
The paper is well written and organized. The description of optical-flow-based inputs is precise, with clear notation and motivation (including mean subtraction and trajectory stacking). The architectural choices and training protocol are described in enough detail for an expert to reimplement. The relation to prior shallow and deep methods is appropriately discussed. Minor suggestions:
- Include more explicit numbers in the main text from Tables 1–4 to improve readability without flipping pages.
- Report inference speed (frames/s) and memory footprint for each stream and for fusion, including the cost of optical flow.
- Provide a brief qualitative analysis of typical failure cases.

Originality
The core novelty lies in (i) explicitly separating spatial and temporal streams and feeding dense optical flow stacks to ConvNets; (ii) simple but effective mean-flow subtraction; and (iii) multi-task training across datasets for temporal ConvNets. While optical flow, two-stream processing, and multi-task learning are not new individually, their combination in a deep, discriminatively trained architecture and the systematic study of flow-based inputs are novel and impactful. The connection to hand-crafted features and the argument that the temporal ConvNet generalizes them are insightful.

Significance
The paper addresses an important problem—video action recognition—and demonstrates a substantial improvement over previous deep attempts, achieving results on par with state-of-the-art hand-crafted pipelines. The two-stream design and optical-flow input have clear practical implications and are likely to influence subsequent work. The simplicity and effectiveness of the proposed multi-task strategy make it broadly useful when labeled video is scarce. The work advances the field in a demonstrable way and offers a strong baseline and blueprint for future architectures.

Strengths
- Clear, principled decomposition into spatial and temporal streams with strong empirical complementarity.
- Convincing ablations demonstrating why optical flow stacks work better than raw RGB stacks.
- Practical tricks that matter (mean flow subtraction, bi-/uni-directional comparison, number of stacked flows).
- Multi-task training that materially improves performance on small datasets.
- Thorough implementation details and standard evaluation protocols.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Dependence on precomputed optical flow: the approach is not end-to-end and inherits flow estimation cost and errors. Suggestions: explore learning motion features end-to-end (e.g., differentiable flow or learned warping), or approximate flow with cheaper learned representations.
- Limited scale: no experiments on truly large-scale video pretraining; unclear how performance scales with more data and categories.
- Fusion is done at score level; attempts at joint fusion over feature layers reportedly overfit. Suggestions: explore lightweight regularized fusion (e.g., bottleneck layers, dropout, weight decay), or sequence models (temporal pooling, recurrent layers) on top of both streams.
- Trajectory information is not exploited in pooling; only in input stacking. Suggestions: add trajectory-aligned pooling/tubes or attention along motion paths.
- No explicit camera motion compensation beyond mean-flow subtraction; more sophisticated global motion modeling could help.

Questions for the authors
- What is the test-time throughput (videos per second) for each stream and for the fused system, including flow computation? Any accuracy-speed trade-offs with fewer frames/crops?
- How sensitive is performance to the optical flow algorithm and its parameters? Have you tested faster/approximate flow?
- Did you try temporal augmentation beyond uniform sampling (e.g., random temporal crops) at training time for the temporal stream?

Pros for acceptance
- Strong empirical results; clear improvement over prior deep baselines and competitive with SOTA shallow methods.
- Simple, elegant architecture with solid ablations; practical and reproducible.
- Conceptually impactful: establishes optical-flow-based temporal ConvNets and two-stream fusion as a strong paradigm.
- Multi-dataset multi-task training addresses a key bottleneck (data scarcity).

Cons for acceptance
- Not end-to-end with respect to motion; relies on a computationally heavy pre-processing step.
- Limited exploration of larger-scale data and more advanced fusion/temporal modeling.
- Some missing analyses (runtime, failure cases, class-wise breakdown).

Overall evaluation
This is a high-quality, clearly written, and practically significant paper. Despite relying on precomputed optical flow, it convincingly demonstrates that explicit motion input is crucial for deep video recognition and provides a strong, influential baseline. I recommend acceptance.
