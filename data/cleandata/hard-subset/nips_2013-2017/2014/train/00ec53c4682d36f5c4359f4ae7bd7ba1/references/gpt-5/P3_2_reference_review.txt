Summary
This paper proposes a two-stream ConvNet architecture for video action recognition, with a spatial stream operating on RGB frames and a temporal stream operating on stacked dense optical flow fields. The key ideas are: (1) decouple appearance and motion into separate networks, (2) explicitly provide motion via optical flow rather than expecting the network to learn it from raw frames, (3) fuse the softmax scores of the two streams at test time, and (4) mitigate overfitting for the temporal stream via multi-task learning across UCF-101 and HMDB-51. The authors systematically study several temporal input configurations (flow stacking, trajectory stacking, bi-directional flow, mean-flow subtraction), and show that stacking multiple optical flow fields with mean subtraction yields strong performance. They report substantial gains over single-frame and “slow fusion” multi-frame RGB baselines, and competitive accuracy against then state-of-the-art hand-crafted trajectory-based methods.

Relation to prior work is well discussed: the temporal input generalizes HOF/MBH and trajectory stacking, and the two-stream decomposition draws inspiration from ventral/dorsal pathways, but is implemented with modern ConvNets. The empirical comparisons to Karpathy et al. (Sports-1M) and improved dense trajectories are appropriate and informative.

Strengths
- Clear, well-motivated architectural decoupling of appearance and motion, with a simple and effective training protocol. The finding that explicit flow input is markedly superior to stacked RGB frames is important and actionable for the community.
- Solid and careful ablations: number of stacked flows, trajectory vs. fixed-location stacking, mean subtraction, bi-directional flow, and fusion strategy. The study convincingly supports the design choices.
- Practical multi-task learning to exploit limited labeled video data, yielding consistent improvements on HMDB-51 and UCF-101. The formulation is simple, reproducible, and likely to generalize.
- Strong empirical results: both streams individually outperform prior deep baselines; fusion improves a further 6–14%, approaching or matching hand-crafted pipelines while using much smaller training sets than Sports-1M.
- Good implementation details (data augmentation, learning schedules, multi-GPU training, flow precomputation/compression), aiding reproducibility.

Weaknesses
- The dependency on precomputed optical flow (Brox) is a significant computational bottleneck and breaks end-to-end learning. While practical, the approach shifts complexity upstream and limits real-time applicability; the paper does not quantify test-time cost relative to IDT pipelines.
- Camera motion handling is limited to mean displacement subtraction. Prior work shows that explicit global motion compensation (e.g., homography estimation) can be critical; an empirical comparison would strengthen the case.
- The bi-directional flow and trajectory stacking variants are explored, but the analysis of why they underperform is limited. For example, trajectory stacking intuitively aligns motion, yet performs worse—diagnostics (e.g., visualization, receptive field alignment) would be valuable.
- Limited analysis of failure modes and class-wise contributions of spatial vs. temporal streams; per-class results or qualitative examples would clarify complementarity and guide future improvements.
- Multi-task learning details are sparse: no discussion of loss weighting, sampling strategy across datasets, or sensitivity to class imbalance. This may matter for broader adoption.
- Generalization to larger-scale datasets is acknowledged but not attempted; while understandable, the claims about scalability remain somewhat speculative.

Quality
Technically sound overall, with appropriate baselines and ablations. The empirical support for claims is strong on UCF-101 and HMDB-51. Some missing analyses as noted above (runtime, camera motion compensation, deeper diagnosis of variants), but the core contributions are substantiated.

Clarity
The paper is clearly written and well organized. Architectural and training details are sufficient for reproduction, including data augmentation, learning schedules, and multi-GPU training. Figures (not included here) and supplementary visualizations of learned filters are mentioned; per-class/error analyses would further improve clarity.

Originality
The two-stream ConvNet with explicit optical flow input and late score fusion is a novel and influential design at the time, distinct from prior stacked-frame ConvNets and shallow HMAX/IDT pipelines. The multi-task learning application to video action recognition is not novel per se but is a sensible and useful instantiation.

Significance
High. The work addresses a core challenge—how to inject motion information into deep models—providing a simple recipe that advances performance and is likely to be adopted and extended (e.g., alternative flow estimators, end-to-end motion learning, trajectory-aware pooling). The ablation insights are impactful for practitioners.

Suggestions for improvement
- Report computational cost: flow precomputation time and storage vs. inference time for each stream; compare against IDT pipeline cost.
- Evaluate explicit camera motion compensation (e.g., via homography) versus mean subtraction, and quantify gains/losses.
- Provide class-wise analysis showing when each stream helps; include typical failure cases.
- Detail multi-task learning schedule: task loss weighting, sampling ratios, and sensitivity analyses.
- Explore lightweight fusion beyond linear SVM (e.g., learned gating, calibration), or explain why overfitting prevents deeper fusion.
- Discuss sensitivity to optical flow algorithm/parameters and the impact of JPEG quantization on flow.

Pros
- Strong, well-supported empirical improvements; clear ablations.
- Simple, general architecture with broad applicability.
- Good clarity and reproducibility.

Cons
- Heavy reliance on precomputed optical flow; limited end-to-end story.
- Limited analysis of camera motion and underperforming variants.
- Missing runtime/resource comparisons.

Recommendation: Accept (strong)
Confidence: 4/5
