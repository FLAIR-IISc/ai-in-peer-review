

This paper proposes a two-stream convolutional network architecture for action recognition in videos, addressing a key limitation in previous deep learning approaches to video classification. The authors decompose video recognition into two complementary streams: a spatial stream that processes individual video frames (using a ConvNet pre-trained on ImageNet) and a temporal stream that processes dense optical flow (representing motion information). The streams are combined via late fusion of softmax scores. This work directly addresses the key limitation identified in [14], where stacked RGB frames as input to ConvNets performed significantly worse than hand-crafted shallow representations.

The paper makes several key contributions. First, it demonstrates that optical flow as input to a ConvNet significantly outperforms stacked RGB frames (56.4% vs. 52.3% on UCF101 when trained from scratch). Second, it introduces a multi-task learning framework to effectively combine datasets (UCF-101 and HMDB-51) for training the temporal stream, which is particularly valuable given the small size of video action recognition datasets. Third, it provides a clear and thorough experimental comparison showing that the two streams are complementary, with the combined system achieving competitive results with state-of-the-art hand-crafted methods.

Regarding quality, the paper is technically sound with well-designed experiments. The authors carefully evaluate different input configurations for the temporal stream (optical flow stacking vs. trajectory stacking, bi-directional flow, etc.) and provide ablation studies to support their design choices. The multi-task learning approach is well-motivated and effectively addresses the data scarcity challenge in video recognition. The implementation details are sufficiently specific for reproducibility, including network architecture, training procedures, and optical flow computation methods. The authors acknowledge limitations (e.g., missing trajectory-based pooling, camera motion compensation) and provide clear directions for future work.

Clarity is excellent. The paper is well-organized, with clear figures illustrating the two-stream architecture and input configurations. The writing is precise and accessible to readers familiar with ConvNets, though the optical flow section may require some background knowledge. The experimental protocol is clearly described, including dataset splits, evaluation metrics, and training/testing procedures. The comparison to previous work is thorough and well-contextualized.

Originality is strong. While the two-streams hypothesis in neuroscience [9] provides inspiration, the specific implementation as deep ConvNets with optical flow input is novel. The paper clearly differentiates from previous deep learning approaches to video recognition, particularly [14], which used stacked RGB frames. The use of optical flow as input to a ConvNet represents a significant shift from prior work, and the authors properly contextualize their approach against both shallow hand-crafted methods [20, 21, 26] and other deep learning approaches [12, 14, 16]. The multi-task learning framework for combining datasets is also a novel contribution to the video recognition literature.

Significance is high. The proposed architecture significantly outperforms previous deep learning approaches to video action recognition, achieving results competitive with state-of-the-art hand-crafted methods. The two-stream approach provides a simple yet effective solution that could be widely adopted in the video recognition community. The multi-task learning framework addresses a critical challenge in video recognition (small dataset sizes) and could be applied to other video understanding tasks. The paper demonstrates clear advancement over prior work, with the two-stream ConvNet achieving 70.1% accuracy on HMDB-51 (vs. 54.0% for the best previous deep learning approach [14]) and 89.6% on UCF-101 (vs. 79.5% for [14]).

Strengths: 
- Clear and well-motivated two-stream architecture
- Thorough experimental evaluation with ablation studies
- Effective multi-task learning framework for data-scarce scenarios
- Strong results that outperform previous deep learning approaches
- Good contextualization against both shallow and deep learning approaches

Weaknesses:
- The paper does not fully address the computational cost of optical flow computation, though they note they pre-computed it to avoid bottleneck
- The temporal stream's reliance on optical flow may be problematic for videos where optical flow estimation is inaccurate (e.g., fast motion, low texture)
- The paper acknowledges missing trajectory pooling (a key component in state-of-the-art shallow methods [26]), but does not explore how to integrate this with the ConvNet architecture
- The comparison to state-of-the-art methods [20, 21, 26] is limited to the results reported in those papers, without direct implementation of the latest methods for fair comparison

Pro-acceptance arguments:
1. The paper presents a clear, novel approach to video action recognition that significantly advances the state of the art in deep learning for this task.
2. The two-stream architecture is well-justified, technically sound, and produces strong empirical results.
3. The multi-task learning framework provides a valuable technique for dealing with small video datasets.
4. The paper is well-written, clearly organized, and provides sufficient details for reproducibility.

Con-acceptance arguments:
1. The paper does not directly compare against the very latest hand-crafted methods (e.g., [26] was published in 2014, while the authors cite it as state-of-the-art).
2. The reliance on optical flow as input may limit applicability to certain video types where flow estimation is challenging.
3. The paper could better address how their approach might integrate with trajectory-based pooling, a key component of current state-of-the-art shallow methods.

Overall, this paper makes a significant contribution to the field of video recognition. It addresses a key limitation in previous deep learning approaches to video action recognition, provides a clear and effective solution with strong experimental results, and offers a useful technique (multi-task learning) for dealing with small video datasets. The work is technically sound, well-written, and represents a meaningful advancement over prior deep learning approaches to video recognition. While there are some limitations (particularly regarding optical flow reliability and missing trajectory pooling), these are acknowledged by the authors and do not undermine the paper's core contributions. The paper meets all four criteria for quality, clarity, originality, and significance, making it a strong candidate for acceptance at NIPS.
