

# NIPS Peer Review: "Two-Stream Convolutional Networks for Action Recognition in Videos"

## Summary

This paper proposes a two-stream architecture for video action recognition using convolutional neural networks (ConvNets). The authors argue that previous deep learning approaches for video recognition (like [14]) that directly process stacked frames as input perform poorly compared to hand-crafted shallow representations. Their solution decouples the recognition task into two separate streams: a spatial stream that processes individual video frames (leveraging ImageNet pre-training) and a temporal stream that processes optical flow to capture motion information. The streams are then combined through late fusion. The authors evaluate their approach on UCF101 and HMDB-51 datasets, demonstrating that their two-stream architecture significantly outperforms previous deep learning approaches for action recognition and is competitive with state-of-the-art hand-crafted methods.

The work relates to the two-streams hypothesis in visual neuroscience [9], but the authors focus on a practical deep learning implementation rather than investigating the biological connection. The paper makes a strong case for why optical flow as input to a ConvNet is superior to directly processing stacked RGB frames, showing that motion information can be better captured when explicitly represented rather than implicitly learned by the network.

## Strengths

1. **Strong Motivation and Clear Problem Statement**: The paper clearly identifies the limitation of previous deep learning approaches for video recognition (e.g., [14]), where networks processing stacked frames performed poorly compared to hand-crafted features. The authors provide a compelling argument for why explicitly representing motion via optical flow as input to a ConvNet is a better approach.

2. **Thorough Experimental Evaluation**: The paper includes extensive ablation studies on the temporal stream's input configurations (optical flow stacking vs. trajectory stacking, bi-directional flow, etc.), demonstrating the importance of using multiple frames of optical flow. The multi-task learning approach to address small dataset sizes is well-justified and effectively implemented.

3. **Good Comparison to State-of-the-Art**: The authors provide a comprehensive comparison to both previous deep learning approaches ([14], [16]) and hand-crafted state-of-the-art methods ([20], [21], [26]), clearly demonstrating their approach's competitive performance.

4. **Technical Soundness**: The architecture is well-designed, the experimental setup is clearly described, and the results are well-supported by quantitative comparisons across multiple datasets. The use of ImageNet pre-training for the spatial stream is a particularly smart technique that leverages existing large-scale image recognition advances.

5. **Clear Organization**: The paper is well-structured with a logical flow from motivation to architecture to experiments. The figures (particularly Figure 1) effectively illustrate the two-stream architecture.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of Theoretical Explanation for Why the Approach Works**: While the paper demonstrates that their approach outperforms previous methods, it doesn't provide a clear theoretical explanation for *why* using optical flow as input to a ConvNet works better than directly processing stacked RGB frames. The authors mention that the previous approach [14] "found that a network, operating on individual video frames, performs similarly to the networks, whose input is a stack of frames" and that this "might indicate that the learnt spatio-temporal features do not capture the motion well." However, they don't provide a more detailed analysis of what specific aspects of the optical flow input make it more effective.

2. **Limited Discussion of Computational Cost**: The paper doesn't address the computational cost of their approach. Optical flow computation is relatively expensive, and the paper mentions that they precomputed flow to avoid bottlenecks, but doesn't discuss how this affects the overall practicality of the method compared to alternatives. This is a significant consideration for real-world applications.

3. **Comparison to Recent Work**: The paper was submitted before 2015, but the state-of-the-art in action recognition has advanced significantly since then. The paper doesn't compare to more recent methods (e.g., 3D ConvNets [12, 25] or more recent two-stream approaches that have been published after their submission).

4. **Lack of Qualitative Analysis**: While the paper provides strong quantitative results, it lacks any qualitative analysis (e.g., visualizing what the network is learning, examples of correctly/incorrectly classified videos). Such analysis would help readers understand why the approach works well.

5. **Limited Discussion of Failure Cases**: The paper doesn't discuss specific failure cases or limitations of their approach. For example, how does the method perform on videos with complex camera motion that wasn't fully compensated by mean flow subtraction?

## Quality

The paper is technically sound with well-designed experiments and strong empirical results. The claims are well-supported by quantitative comparisons on standard benchmarks. The authors are careful to evaluate both strengths (the complementary nature of the two streams) and weaknesses (e.g., the need for optical flow computation). The paper is a complete piece of work rather than a position paper, with clear methodology, experiments, and analysis.

## Clarity

The paper is very clearly written and well-organized. The main ideas are presented in a logical flow, with sufficient detail for an expert reader to understand and potentially reproduce the results. The figures (particularly Figure 1) are helpful in explaining the architecture. The implementation details are sufficiently thorough for reproducibility.

## Originality

The two-stream architecture is inspired by the two-streams hypothesis in visual neuroscience [9], but the authors' specific implementation using ConvNets for both streams and optical flow for the temporal stream represents a novel application of deep learning to video recognition. While the concept of two streams has been explored before (e.g., [16]), the authors' implementation using deep ConvNets rather than hand-crafted HMAX models represents a significant advance. The paper clearly differentiates itself from previous work, particularly in the related work section, and adequately references relevant prior art.

## Significance

The paper makes an important contribution to the field of action recognition by demonstrating a deep learning approach that is competitive with state-of-the-art hand-crafted methods and significantly outperforms previous deep learning approaches for video recognition. The work provides a compelling unified framework for video recognition that can be easily extended to other video tasks. The findings that motion information is better captured when explicitly represented via optical flow (rather than implicitly learned) have important implications for future research in video understanding. The paper advances the state of the art in a demonstrable way on standard benchmarks.

## Recommendation

This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of video action recognition. It's well-written, technically sound, and provides clear empirical evidence for the proposed approach. While there are some limitations (particularly regarding the lack of theoretical explanation for why the approach works and limited discussion of computational cost), these do not significantly undermine the paper's contributions.

**Recommendation**: Accept

## Specific Suggestions for Improvement

1. Add a section discussing why optical flow as input works better than stacked RGB frames, potentially with a more detailed analysis of the features learned by the temporal stream.

2. Include a discussion of computational cost and practical implications for real-world applications.

3. Consider adding qualitative analysis (e.g., visualization of activation maps or examples of correctly/incorrectly classified videos) to complement the quantitative results.

4. Briefly discuss how the approach might be extended to handle more challenging scenarios (e.g., complex camera motion beyond mean subtraction).

5. Add a comparison to more recent work (if possible) to demonstrate the current standing of the approach in the field.

## Summary of Arguments for Acceptance

* Strong motivation for the two-stream architecture based on analysis of previous deep learning approaches
* Thorough experimental evaluation with ablation studies and multi-task learning
* Clear demonstration that the two streams are complementary and significantly outperform previous deep learning methods
* Competitive performance with state-of-the-art hand-crafted methods
* Well-written and well-organized paper with clear methodology
* Clear comparison to relevant prior work

## Summary of Arguments Against Acceptance

* Limited theoretical explanation for why the approach works better than previous methods
* No discussion of computational cost or practical limitations
* Doesn't compare to more recent work in the field
* Lacks qualitative analysis to complement quantitative results

Overall, this paper makes a significant contribution to the field of video action recognition and advances the state of the art in a demonstrable way. The minor weaknesses noted above do not detract from the paper's overall quality and significance.
