This paper proposes a two-stream convolutional neural network architecture for action recognition in videos. The core idea is to decouple spatial and temporal information into separate streams, leveraging the strengths of CNNs for still image recognition (spatial stream) and explicitly modeling motion via optical flow (temporal stream). The streams are then fused for final classification. This approach builds upon earlier work exploring two-stream architectures [16] and the “two-stream hypothesis” from neuroscience [9], but differentiates itself through the use of deep convolutional networks for both streams and a thorough investigation of different input configurations for the temporal stream.

The paper is well-structured and clearly written. The introduction effectively motivates the work and positions it within the context of existing research. The related work section provides a comprehensive overview of both shallow and deep approaches to action recognition, establishing a solid foundation for the proposed method. The descriptions of the two-stream architecture, optical flow ConvNets, and multi-task learning are detailed and easy to follow. The experimental setup and results are presented clearly, and the comparisons to state-of-the-art methods are convincing.

**Strengths:**

* **Strong Baseline & Improvement:** The paper demonstrates a significant improvement over previous deep learning approaches for action recognition [14] and achieves competitive results compared to hand-crafted features [20, 21, 26].
* **Effective Decoupling:** The separation of spatial and temporal streams proves to be a successful strategy, allowing the network to learn complementary representations.
* **Thorough Investigation:** The authors explore various configurations for the temporal stream, including different input representations (optical flow stacking, trajectory stacking, bi-directional flow) and mean flow subtraction.
* **Multi-task Learning:** The incorporation of multi-task learning is a clever way to address the limited amount of labeled video data.
* **Clear and Well-Written:** The paper is exceptionally well-written, making it easy to understand the proposed method and the experimental results.
* **Strong Experimental Validation:** The authors evaluate their method on two standard benchmarks (UCF101 and HMDB51) and provide a comprehensive comparison to other approaches.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Optical Flow Dependency:** The reliance on pre-computed optical flow introduces a dependency on the accuracy and computational cost of optical flow estimation. While acknowledged, the impact of optical flow errors isn’t deeply explored. The authors mention recent work [30] on learning-based motion estimation but don’t integrate or compare against it.
* **Limited Exploration of Fusion:** While SVM-based fusion is shown to be slightly better than averaging, the exploration of different fusion strategies feels limited. More sophisticated fusion mechanisms, such as attention mechanisms or more complex network architectures for combining the streams, could potentially yield further improvements.
* **Computational Cost:** The two-stream architecture, combined with optical flow computation, significantly increases the computational cost compared to single-stream approaches. While this is mentioned, a more detailed analysis of the computational complexity would be valuable.
* **Trajectory Stacking Performance:** The trajectory stacking approach doesn't show a substantial benefit over standard optical flow stacking. The authors note this but don’t delve into potential reasons, such as the quality of the trajectory estimation or the limitations of the network architecture for capturing trajectory information.
* **Limited Ablation Study on Multi-task Learning:** While multi-task learning is effective, the ablation study could be more detailed. For example, analyzing the impact of different weighting schemes for the two tasks or exploring different datasets for multi-task learning.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and effective approach to action recognition that significantly improves upon existing methods.
* The experimental results are compelling and demonstrate the superiority of the proposed method on standard benchmarks.
* The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to understand.
* The authors provide a thorough analysis of their method and identify potential areas for future work.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

* The reliance on pre-computed optical flow could limit the applicability of the method in scenarios where accurate optical flow estimation is challenging.
* The exploration of fusion strategies and the analysis of the computational cost could be more comprehensive.
* The limited performance gains from trajectory stacking suggest that this approach may not be fully exploited.

**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a significant contribution to the field of action recognition. The proposed two-stream architecture is a clever and effective way to leverage the complementary strengths of spatial and temporal information. While there are some minor weaknesses, the strengths of the paper outweigh them.  

**Recommendation:**

**Accept with Minor Revisions.**

Specifically, I recommend the authors:

1.  **Discuss the limitations of relying on pre-computed optical flow more explicitly** and consider briefly exploring the potential benefits of integrating learning-based motion estimation techniques.
2.  **Expand the analysis of fusion strategies** beyond SVM-based fusion.
3.  **Provide a more detailed analysis of the computational cost** of the proposed method.
4.  **Investigate the reasons for the limited performance gains of trajectory stacking.** Perhaps a different network architecture or a more robust trajectory estimation method could improve its effectiveness.
5.  **Expand the ablation study on multi-task learning** to explore different weighting schemes and datasets.
