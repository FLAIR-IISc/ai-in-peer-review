- Proposes a two-stream convolutional architecture that decouples appearance (RGB spatial ConvNet) and motion (temporal ConvNet on stacked optical flow).  
- Temporal stream uses stacked dense optical flow across L frames with variants: flow vs trajectory stacking, uni- vs bi-directional, and mean flow subtraction.  
- Streams are fused by late fusion (average or linear SVM over softmax scores).  
- Uses multi-task learning to share convolutional features while training separate classification heads for UCF-101 and HMDB-51.  
- Implemented in Caffe with multi-GPU training; experiments on UCF-101 and HMDB-51 show temporal and spatial streams are complementary.  
- Temporal ConvNet substantially outperforms RGB-only ConvNet and prior deep baselines; fused model is competitive with improved dense trajectories + Fisher vectors.  
- Builds on prior deep video models and hand-crafted motion pipelines, but key novelty is feeding stacked optical flow directly into a ConvNet.  
- Argues temporal ConvNet generalizes classic motion descriptors (HOF/MBH, kinematic features) as special cases of early filters and non-linearities.  
- Methodology is sound with well-specified inputs, clear training/testing, and thorough ablation studies.  
- Ablations show stacking multiple flow fields (L≈10) and mean subtraction are important; bidirectional/trajectory stacking give limited extra gains.  
- Multi-task learning effectively leverages multiple datasets without manual class filtering.  
- Implementation and preprocessing details (data augmentation, learning rates, optical flow handling) are sufficient for reproduction.  
- Main limitation: reliance on an external optical flow algorithm prevents end-to-end joint training of motion and recognition.  
- Temporal modeling is relatively short-range (L=10) with no explicit long-term temporal aggregation beyond score averaging.  
- Camera motion compensation is rudimentary (mean subtraction) and not as principled as homography-based stabilization used in competing pipelines.  
- Sensitivity analysis is limited to one flow method (Brox); no study of alternative flow algorithms/parameters.  
- Fusion is only at the score level; earlier or learned joint fusion was avoided due to overfitting concerns.  
- Evaluation is limited to UCF-101 and HMDB-51; no experiments on larger datasets or cross-dataset generalization.  
- Paper is clearly written, well organized, and reproducible with minor clarifications suggested (runtime/storage, sensitivity to flow quality).  
- The two-stream with optical-flow input is an original, compelling design that treats motion as an explicit input rather than emergent feature.  
- Work is significant: narrows the gap to hand-crafted methods and provides a practical recipe likely to influence follow-up research.  
- Strengths: clear decomposition, strong ablations, competitive performance, practical multi-task training, and reproducible implementation details.  
- Weaknesses: external flow dependency (compute/storage), short-range temporal focus, basic camera-motion handling, limited flow-algorithm sensitivity analysis, and restricted dataset scope.  
- Questions for authors include sensitivity to flow method/parameters, end-to-end throughput including flow computation, alternative flow encodings, scalability of L, and impact of simple homography stabilization.  
- Pros for acceptance: strong empirical gains, clean architecture, thorough ablations, and high community relevance.  
- Cons for acceptance: non–end-to-end pipeline with heavy precomputation, limited long-range temporal and camera-motion exploration, and dataset scope constraints.  
- Overall recommendation: Accept (high confidence).
