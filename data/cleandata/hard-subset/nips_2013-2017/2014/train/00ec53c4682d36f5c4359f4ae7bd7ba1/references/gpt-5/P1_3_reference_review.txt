Summary
This paper proposes a two-stream convolutional architecture for video action recognition that explicitly separates appearance and motion pathways. The spatial stream is a standard image ConvNet trained on RGB frames (pre-trained on ImageNet and fine-tuned), while the temporal stream operates on stacked dense optical flow fields computed between consecutive frames. Several motion input configurations are compared: stacking flow at fixed spatial locations vs. along tracked trajectories, uni- vs. bi-directional flow, and mean flow subtraction to mitigate camera motion. The two streams are combined by late fusion (averaging or linear SVM over softmax scores). To address limited video supervision, the authors introduce a simple multi-task learning setup that shares all layers but uses separate softmax losses for UCF-101 and HMDB-51.

Extensive experiments show: (i) temporal ConvNets trained on stacked optical flow significantly outperform ConvNets trained on raw RGB stacks (slow fusion) when trained on UCF-101/HMDB-51; (ii) spatial and temporal streams are complementary, with late fusion providing substantial gains; (iii) multi-task learning improves performance, especially on HMDB-51. The method matches or approaches state-of-the-art trajectory-based hand-crafted pipelines and clearly surpasses prior deep models that learn motion implicitly from RGB stacks (e.g., Karpathy et al.).

Strengths
- Clear, well-motivated architectural decomposition with strong connections to neuroscience (two streams) and to prior computer vision pipelines (explicit motion features).
- Thorough and careful experimental study: ablations over number of stacked flows L, trajectory vs. spatial stacking, mean subtraction, bi-directional flow, fusion strategies, and comparison to slow-fusion RGB stacks.
- Practical multi-task learning recipe that effectively leverages multiple datasets without label harmonization.
- Competitive to state-of-the-art handcrafted methods on UCF-101/HMDB-51; large-margin improvement over prior deep baselines.
- Implementation details are reasonably documented (training schedule, augmentation, multi-GPU training, data processing), and many design choices are justified.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Reliance on precomputed optical flow: computation/storage overhead is high (mitigated by JPEG compression), and performance may be sensitive to the choice and parameters of the flow algorithm; this sensitivity is not studied.
- Not end-to-end: motion estimation is decoupled and non-learned; the model cannot adapt flow estimation to the downstream task or benefit from joint training.
- Evaluation scope is limited to UCF-101 and HMDB-51; no results on larger-scale datasets (Sports-1M is mentioned as future work) or cross-dataset generalization.
- Fusion is limited to late score fusion; joint fusion at feature level was attempted but dismissed due to overfitting—alternative regularization strategies are not explored.
- Camera motion handling is simplistic (mean flow subtraction) compared to explicit compensation used in trajectory-based SOTA; no empirical comparison to stronger compensation schemes.
- Runtime/throughput at inference is not reported (both flow computation and ConvNet forward pass), which matters for practical adoption.
- The comparison to 3D ConvNets and other RGB-stack configurations could be richer (one slow-fusion baseline is included, but hyperparameters/architectures may not be fully optimized).

Quality
The methodology is sound and well-executed. The ablations convincingly support key design decisions: stacking multiple flows (L≈10) helps; mean subtraction helps; trajectory stacking and bidirectional flow add little. Training procedures and evaluation protocols are standard and appropriate. Claims are supported by quantitative results and by qualitative reasoning linking learned filters to classical motion features (HOF/MBH). Some aspects (sensitivity to optical flow algorithm/parameters; runtime; stronger camera motion compensation) are underexplored but do not undermine the main claims.

Clarity
The paper is clearly written and well-organized. The relation to prior work is thorough, and the architectural details are sufficient for an expert to reproduce results, given the referenced CNN-M-2048 configuration. Minor clarifications that would improve reproducibility: exact optical flow parameters, precise per-layer filter sizes/strides for both streams, and details of the linear SVM fusion (regularization, cross-validation). A brief runtime/memory analysis would also help practitioners.

Originality
The separation into spatial and temporal streams is not new conceptually (e.g., two-stream HMAX), but the contribution of using deep ConvNets with explicit optical-flow inputs, coupled with a careful design and empirical validation, is novel and impactful. The multi-task learning formulation is straightforward but effective. The work is a strong and timely synthesis rather than a radically new theoretical contribution.

Significance
High. The approach advances deep video recognition by showing that explicit motion inputs are crucial when training data is limited, and that appearance and motion cues are complementary. Given the simplicity and practicality of the recipe, it is likely to be adopted and extended by the community. The results are competitive with strong hand-crafted pipelines and substantially better than previous deep baselines, which is important for the field.

Pros for acceptance
- Strong empirical gains over prior deep models; competitive with SOTA handcrafted methods.
- Clear, simple, and well-justified architecture with thorough ablations.
- Practical multi-task training to leverage multiple datasets.
- Likely to influence subsequent work in deep video understanding.

Cons for acceptance
- Not end-to-end; heavy reliance on precomputed optical flow; compute/storage costs not quantified.
- Limited dataset scope; no large-scale or cross-dataset validation.
- Limited exploration of alternative fusion strategies and camera motion compensation.
- Sensitivity to the choice of optical flow not studied.

Suggestions for improvement
- Report inference-time cost (with and without precomputed flow) and analyze trade-offs between L and accuracy.
- Evaluate robustness across different optical flow methods/parameters and consider learning flow estimation jointly (e.g., adding a differentiable warping/flow module).
- Explore stronger camera motion compensation (e.g., homography/egomotion estimation) and/or motion stabilization baselines.
- Investigate regularized feature-level fusion (e.g., low-rank or gated fusion, dropout on fusion layers) to mitigate overfitting.
- Provide per-class analysis and failure cases to better understand the complementarity of streams.
- Consider cross-dataset transfer and domain shift experiments.

Minor comments
- Ensure reference formatting is consistent; some entries appear incomplete or have typos (e.g., “D convolutional neural networks” should likely be “3D”).
- Clarify whether RGB jittering is used at test time (assumed no).
- Briefly state the exact SVM C used for fusion and how it was tuned.

Overall recommendation: Accept. The paper makes a clear, well-supported, and practically significant contribution to deep action recognition with a clean idea and strong evidence.
