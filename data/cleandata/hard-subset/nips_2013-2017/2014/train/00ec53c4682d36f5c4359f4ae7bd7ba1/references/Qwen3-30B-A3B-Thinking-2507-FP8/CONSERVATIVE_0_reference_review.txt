

# Review of "Two-Stream Convolutional Networks for Action Recognition in Videos"

This paper proposes a two-stream ConvNet architecture for video action recognition, with one stream processing spatial information from individual frames (pre-trained on ImageNet) and the other processing optical flow to capture motion information. The authors claim their approach significantly outperforms previous deep learning methods and is competitive with state-of-the-art shallow hand-crafted representations.

**Strengths:**
The two-stream architecture is conceptually sound and builds on the well-established two-stream hypothesis in visual neuroscience. The authors provide thorough experimental validation across two challenging datasets (UCF-101 and HMDB-51), and their multi-task learning approach for handling limited video datasets is principled and well-justified.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Insufficient comparison to strong baselines**: The paper claims to outperform [14] by a large margin, but the comparison is not sufficiently rigorous. The authors state that [14] "found that a network, operating on individual video frames, performs similarly to the networks, whose input is a stack of frames" and that "the learnt representation, finetuned on the UCF-101 dataset, turned out to be 20% less accurate than hand-crafted state-of-the-art trajectory-based representation [20, 27]." However, they do not provide a direct comparison using the same training protocol and hyperparameters. Without this, the claimed improvements are questionable.

2. **Lack of fair comparison with optical flow approach**: The paper states that their temporal stream achieves 71.4% accuracy (Table 1b) while their "slow fusion" architecture (essentially [14]'s approach) achieves 56.4%. However, they do not report the accuracy of [14]'s method using the same optical flow input, making it impossible to determine if their improvement is due to the two-stream architecture or simply better optical flow processing.

3. **Overstated novelty**: The paper claims "Our proposed architecture is related to the two-streams hypothesis [9]" but notes they "do not investigate this connection any further." The two-stream concept is well-established in [16] (which used HMAX models), and the authors do not provide evidence that their ConvNet-based approach significantly outperforms [16]'s HMAX-based approach. The claimed "learnable generalization" of HMAX is not sufficiently demonstrated.

4. **Lack of thorough ablation studies**: While the paper tests different optical flow configurations (Table 1b), it does not provide a comprehensive ablation study of key architectural decisions. For instance, they do not test how performance changes with different network depths or when using a single stream versus two streams with different configurations.

5. **Insufficient discussion of limitations**: The paper claims competitiveness with shallow hand-crafted methods but fails to adequately address why their approach is limited compared to these methods. In the conclusions, they note that "there still remain some essential ingredients of the state-of-the-art shallow representation [26]... which are missed in our current architecture," yet this limitation is not reflected in their experimental evaluation.

**Significance Assessment:**
While the paper presents a reasonable extension of established approaches, it does not make a significant advance over existing work. The two-stream architecture is conceptually established, the use of optical flow is well-known in the field, and the claimed improvements over previous deep learning approaches are not sufficiently validated. The paper does not provide evidence that their approach represents a meaningful contribution to the field, particularly when compared to the recent state-of-the-art shallow representations [20, 21, 26].

**Recommendation:**
Reject

**Arguments for rejection:**
- Insufficient validation of claims against strong baselines
- Overstated novelty without clear contribution to the field
- Lack of ablation studies to isolate key contributions
- Inadequate comparison to state-of-the-art shallow methods

**Arguments against rejection:**
- The two-stream architecture is conceptually sound
- The multi-task learning approach is well-justified
- The paper provides thorough experimental evaluation across multiple datasets

However, the arguments against rejection are outweighed by the significant weaknesses in validation and novelty. The paper fails to provide sufficient evidence to support its claims of significant improvement over previous work, which is a critical requirement for NIPS publication. The authors should first conduct a more rigorous comparison with the strongest baselines before considering publication at NIPS.
