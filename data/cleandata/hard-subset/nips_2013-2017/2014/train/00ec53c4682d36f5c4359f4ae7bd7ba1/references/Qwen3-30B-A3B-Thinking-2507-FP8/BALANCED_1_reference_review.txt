

# Review of "Two-Stream Convolutional Networks for Action Recognition in Videos"

This paper introduces a two-stream convolutional network architecture for action recognition in videos, with a spatial stream processing individual frames and a temporal stream processing optical flow to capture motion information. The authors argue that decoupling spatial and temporal processing, rather than using stacked frames as input, leads to better performance. They pre-train the spatial stream on ImageNet and use multi-task learning to combine datasets for training the temporal stream. The approach significantly outperforms prior deep learning methods for video action recognition and achieves results competitive with state-of-the-art shallow representations.

## Strengths

The paper makes a compelling contribution to video action recognition by demonstrating that a deep learning approach can match or exceed shallow hand-crafted methods. The two-stream architecture is well-motivated and provides a clear separation of spatial and temporal processing, which the authors convincingly demonstrate is complementary. Their experimental evaluation is thorough, with detailed ablation studies on the optical flow input configurations (stacking depth, trajectory vs. optical flow stacking, mean subtraction), and multi-task learning for dataset combination.

The implementation details are sufficiently detailed for reproducibility, and the authors provide valuable insights into practical considerations (e.g., optical flow precomputation to avoid bottleneck). The comparison with prior work is comprehensive, particularly with respect to the [14] work, which is directly addressed. The paper makes a significant contribution by showing that deep learning can be effective for video action recognition when motion is explicitly represented via optical flow rather than implicitly learned from stacked frames.

## Weaknesses

While the paper is well-executed, it has some notable weaknesses. The theoretical justification for why the two-stream architecture works better than single-stream approaches is limited. The authors observe that the temporal stream outperforms the spatial stream (Table 1b), but they don't provide sufficient analysis of *why* this is the case or how the streams complement each other beyond the qualitative observation that they are complementary.

The paper's claim of "competitive with the state of the art" requires more careful contextualization. The results in Table 4 show the two-stream model achieves 70.2% on UCF-101 and 56.6% on HMDB-51, which are indeed competitive with shallow methods like [20, 21, 26]. However, the paper doesn't sufficiently address how these results compare to more recent deep learning approaches that have emerged after the submission window (e.g., [32-34] published in 2015-2016). This is particularly important since the paper cites [14] as the most relevant prior deep learning work, but newer deep learning approaches may have since surpassed both [14] and the authors' approach.

The paper acknowledges limitations in Section 7, particularly the missing trajectory-based pooling from state-of-the-art shallow methods [26], but doesn't explore how these limitations could be addressed in future work. A more detailed discussion of how to integrate these missing components would strengthen the paper's contribution.

## Overall Assessment

The paper presents a strong, well-executed approach to video action recognition with clear experimental validation. The two-stream architecture is a valuable contribution to the field, and the results demonstrate that deep learning can be effective for this task when motion is explicitly represented. The paper meets the quality standard with sound technical execution and thorough experimental evaluation.

However, the paper's significance could be strengthened by more comprehensive comparison to recent deep learning approaches and by providing deeper theoretical insights into why the two-stream architecture works better than single-stream alternatives. The paper's claim of "competitive with state of the art" would be more convincing if it directly compared against the most recent deep learning methods for video action recognition.

## Recommendation

I recommend acceptance of this paper. It makes a significant contribution to video action recognition by demonstrating that deep learning can be effective when motion is explicitly represented via optical flow. The two-stream architecture is well-designed and the results are compelling. While the paper has some limitations in comparison to the most recent work and theoretical justification, the core contribution is strong and well-executed. The authors should address the comparison with more recent deep learning approaches in the final version.

## Specific Suggestions for Improvement

1. Add a brief comparison with recent deep learning approaches for video action recognition (e.g., [32-34]) to better contextualize the results.

2. Provide more theoretical discussion of why the two-stream architecture works better than single-stream alternatives. A simple analysis of the feature representations learned by each stream could be valuable.

3. In Section 7, expand on how the missing trajectory-based pooling could be integrated into the current framework, perhaps with a brief discussion of potential challenges.

4. Clarify the relationship between the "slow fusion" architecture mentioned in Section 6 and the two-stream approach. The paper states that slow fusion (stacked frames) is inferior to the optical flow approach, but it would be helpful to explain why this is the case from a theoretical perspective.

5. Consider adding a brief discussion of computational efficiency and scalability, particularly as the authors mention plans to train on larger datasets like Sports-1M.
