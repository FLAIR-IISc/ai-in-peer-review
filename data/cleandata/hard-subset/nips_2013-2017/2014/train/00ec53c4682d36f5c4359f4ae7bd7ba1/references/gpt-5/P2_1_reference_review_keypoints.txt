- Proposes a two-stream ConvNet for action recognition: an ImageNet-pretrained spatial (RGB) stream and a temporal stream operating on stacked dense optical flow, fused at score level.
- Justifies decoupling by human two-stream hypothesis and failures of prior ConvNets to learn motion from stacked RGB.
- Introduces multiple temporal input configurations: optical flow stacking, trajectory stacking, bi-directional flow, and mean-flow subtraction.
- Proposes multi-task learning to jointly exploit multiple video datasets and reduce overfitting.
- Provides thorough training/testing protocol with data augmentation, SGD schedules, and multi-GPU training for reproducibility.
- On UCF101 and HMDB-51, the temporal stream outperforms RGB-only ConvNets and the fused model competes with hand-crafted trajectory methods.
- Builds on deep image ConvNets, prior stacked-frame video models, and hand-crafted motion descriptors, connecting first-layer flow filters to classical features.
- Differentiates itself by explicitly feeding optical flow to ConvNets and by a practical multi-dataset training scheme.
- Technical soundness is strong with well-motivated architecture choices and extensive ablation studies on temporal inputs and training regimes.
- Experimental rigor is good: standard protocols, averaged splits, multiple testing crops, and convincing ablations showing where gains come from.
- Main limitation: reliance on precomputed Brox optical flow (not learned end-to-end), causing significant preprocessing cost and storage.
- Other limitations: limited temporal receptive field (L=10), no sensitivity study to flow algorithm, simple score-level fusion, and lack of detailed runtime/latency reporting.
- Paper is clearly written, well-organized, and includes precise temporal input definitions and detailed implementation details aiding reproducibility.
- Originality lies in applying stacked dense optical flow as ConvNet input paired with an RGB stream and the pragmatic multi-task learning across datasets.
- High significance: simple, effective recipe that narrows gap to improved trajectories and provides actionable ablation insights for practitioners.
- Strengths include clear motivation, excellent empirical validation, strong benchmark performance, thorough implementation details, and conceptual ties to classical motion features.
- Weaknesses include non-end-to-end flow dependence, limited long-range temporal modeling, superficial fusion exploration, and missing deployment metrics.
- Key questions for authors: sensitivity to flow algorithm, end-to-end inference time including flow, benefits of longer temporal windows, and potential for joint/regularized stream training.
- Suggestions: report throughput/memory costs, explore regularized/feature-level fusion or parameter sharing, investigate longer-term temporal aggregation, analyze per-class gains and camera-motion robustness, and test noisier/faster flow methods.
- Arguments for acceptance: well-motivated, simple architecture with strong empirical gains, practical impact, and careful, reproducible ablations.
- Arguments against acceptance: not end-to-end, heavy external flow dependence, and limited exploration of long-range temporal modeling and fusion.
- Overall assessment: high-quality, impactful paper with solid evidence and significance despite reliance on precomputed flow; recommendation to accept.
