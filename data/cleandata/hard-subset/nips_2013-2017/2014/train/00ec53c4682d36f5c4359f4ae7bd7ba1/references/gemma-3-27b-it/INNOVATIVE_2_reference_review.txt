This paper presents a two-stream convolutional network architecture for action recognition in videos, decoupling spatial and temporal information processing into separate streams. The core idea – leveraging dedicated CNNs for still frames and optical flow – is a conceptually clean and promising approach, particularly given the recent struggles of directly extending CNNs to spatio-temporal data (as highlighted by the cited work of Karpathy et al.). The connection to the ventral/dorsal stream hypothesis, though not explored deeply, provides a nice biological motivation.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The two-stream architecture is a conceptually novel way to address video action recognition with deep learning. Decoupling spatial and temporal streams, and using optical flow explicitly in a dedicated network, is a strong departure from previous attempts relying on stacking frames. This feels like a genuinely new direction.
*   **Performance:** The results demonstrate a significant improvement over the baseline of directly applying CNNs to stacked frames ([14]), and achieve competitive performance with state-of-the-art hand-crafted features. The ablation studies (Table 1) are well-executed and clearly show the benefit of each component.
*   **Pre-training:** Effectively leveraging pre-training on ImageNet for the spatial stream is a smart move and demonstrates an understanding of practical deep learning techniques.
*   **Multi-task Learning:** The introduction of multi-task learning to leverage larger datasets is a sensible addition, particularly given the limited size of the UCF101 and HMDB51 datasets.
*   **Clear Writing:** The paper is well-written and relatively easy to follow, considering the technical details.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Optical Flow Dependency:** The reliance on pre-computed dense optical flow introduces a potential bottleneck and dependence on a separate, potentially error-prone, processing step. The paper acknowledges this but doesn't explore ways to integrate optical flow computation directly into the network (though this may be a significant undertaking).
*   **Limited Exploration of Fusion:** While SVM-based fusion performs better than averaging, the fusion mechanism itself feels somewhat ad-hoc. Exploring more sophisticated fusion techniques (e.g., attention mechanisms) might yield further improvements.
*   **Superficial Biological Connection:** The allusion to the ventral/dorsal stream hypothesis is weak and doesn't contribute significantly to the technical contribution. It feels more like a justification than a guiding principle.
*   **Limited Analysis of Learned Features:** While the paper mentions visualizing convolutional filters, a more in-depth analysis of *what* the temporal ConvNet is learning from the optical flow would be valuable.
*   **Comparison to Newer Methods:** The comparison to state-of-the-art is done as of the time of writing. It would be valuable to compare to more recent works appearing after the submission deadline.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The core idea is genuinely novel and represents a promising new direction for video action recognition.
*   The experimental results are strong and demonstrate a clear improvement over previous approaches.
*   The paper is well-written and clearly presents its contributions.
*   The limitations are acknowledged and, while not fully addressed, don't invalidate the core contribution.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   The reliance on pre-computed optical flow introduces a dependency.
*   The fusion mechanism is somewhat simplistic.
*   The biological motivation is weak.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with a novel and promising approach to video action recognition. While not perfect, the originality and positive results outweigh the weaknesses. The paper introduces a significant new concept – the decoupling of spatial and temporal streams – that is likely to influence future research in this area. I lean strongly towards acceptance, even with the minor flaws. The boldness of the approach and the clear demonstration of improved performance are highly commendable.

**Recommendation:** Accept. I would encourage the authors to consider investigating end-to-end trainable optical flow estimation or more sophisticated fusion techniques in future work. A deeper analysis of the learned temporal features would also be beneficial.
