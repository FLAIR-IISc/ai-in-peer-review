Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes a two-stream convolutional architecture for action recognition in videos, decomposing video understanding into complementary spatial and temporal components. The spatial stream is a ConvNet operating on individual RGB frames; it leverages pretraining on ImageNet and fine-tuning or linear classification for action recognition. The temporal stream is a ConvNet operating on stacked dense optical flow fields computed between consecutive frames. Several temporal input configurations are explored: stacking flow at fixed pixel locations, stacking along motion trajectories, using bi-directional flow, and subtracting the mean flow to mitigate camera motion. The two streams are fused at the score level (averaging or a linear SVM). To reduce overfitting on small video datasets, the authors introduce a multitask learning setup with two softmax heads sharing the ConvNet body, jointly training on UCF-101 and HMDB-51.

The work is positioned against prior shallow pipelines based on dense trajectories and hand-crafted descriptors (HOF/MBH, Fisher vectors, improved trajectories), and against deep models that either use stacked frames and expect early layers to learn motion implicitly, or hand-crafted HMAX-style two-stream models. The paper argues that using flow as input makes motion explicit and easier to learn, and that the temporal stream generalizes classical descriptors (HOF/MBH/kinematic features) as special cases of early convolutional processing. Experiments on UCF-101 and HMDB-51 show that: (i) temporal ConvNets trained on flow outperform spatial ConvNets trained on frames; (ii) stacking multiple flows (L≈10) and mean-flow subtraction improve performance; (iii) two-stream fusion substantially improves over either stream alone; (iv) multitask learning improves the temporal stream, especially for HMDB-51. The method is competitive with state-of-the-art shallow representations while significantly exceeding earlier deep baselines trained on RGB stacks.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The architecture and training procedures are standard and sound. The temporal input design is systematically studied with ablations on number of stacked flows, trajectory vs fixed-location stacking, mean-flow subtraction, and bi-directionality. The multitask formulation using shared representation with separate softmax heads is correct and appropriate for handling overlapping label spaces.
- Empirical support: Evaluations follow the standard three-split protocol on UCF-101 and HMDB-51 with clear train/test separation. Comparisons include relevant deep baselines (e.g., stacked RGB “slow fusion”) and strong shallow methods are discussed. The ablation studies convincingly support the key claims (flow stacking and mean subtraction matter; temporal > spatial; fusion helps; multitask helps).
- Completeness and care: Implementation details (network architecture, data augmentation, learning schedule, multi-GPU, optical flow computation and storage) are sufficiently described to enable reproduction. One limitation is reliance on precomputed optical flow (Brox et al.), which decouples motion estimation from learning and introduces a computational bottleneck; runtime and resource requirements are discussed but quantitative speed benchmarks are limited. Statistical variability across runs is not reported.

Clarity
The paper is clearly written and well organized. The motivation for the two-stream decomposition is clear, and the relation to prior work is carefully articulated. The temporal input formulations are precisely defined. Suggestions to improve clarity:
- Specify the exact optical flow parameters used (pyramid scales, iterations, smoothness weights), since performance can be sensitive to them.
- Clarify how horizontal flipping affects flow channels (i.e., sign inversion of the horizontal component).
- Report the exact value range and clipping used when rescaling and JPEG-compressing flow, and whether values are clipped before/after mean subtraction.
- Include per-split accuracies and standard deviations across multiple trainings to assess stability.
- Provide brief runtime estimates for training and inference per video, including flow computation time, to contextualize practicality.

Originality
The main novel contributions are: (i) the two-stream ConvNet with an explicit temporal stream operating on stacked optical flow; (ii) systematic design choices for flow-based ConvNet inputs (including mean-flow subtraction and trajectory vs fixed-location stacking); and (iii) a multitask training strategy to leverage multiple datasets with overlapping classes. Score-level late fusion is standard; however, the combination with flow-based temporal ConvNets and the thorough ablations are new at the time. The conceptual connection to hand-crafted descriptors and showing how they can be subsumed by learned convolutional filters is insightful.

Significance
This work offers a simple, effective, and reproducible approach that substantially narrows or closes the gap between deep learning methods and strong shallow baselines on action recognition benchmarks of the time. The findings that (a) explicit motion (optical flow) is crucial and (b) two-stream fusion yields complementary gains are impactful and likely to influence subsequent architectures. The multitask framework is broadly useful for video learning under data scarcity. The primary limitation is the dependence on precomputed optical flow, which adds computational cost and precludes end-to-end optimization of motion estimation; nonetheless, the empirical gains argue for its practical value.

Pros
- Clear, well-motivated two-stream design that decomposes appearance and motion.
- Strong empirical evidence with relevant ablations demonstrating key design choices.
- Temporal stream meaningfully outperforms RGB-based deep baselines; fusion gives sizable gains.
- Multitask learning provides a principled way to use multiple datasets and improves generalization.
- Good discussion connecting learned filters to classical motion descriptors.

Cons
- Requires precomputing dense optical flow, adding significant computational and storage overhead; no end-to-end training of motion estimation.
- Limited evaluation of efficiency (training/inference time) and robustness to flow quality.
- Fusion limited to late score fusion; attempts at joint fully connected fusion were abandoned due to overfitting—more analysis could be informative.
- No analysis of per-class gains or failure modes (e.g., actions dominated by camera motion).
- Results focus on two datasets; large-scale datasets (e.g., Sports-1M) are discussed but not evaluated.

Questions and suggestions for authors
- Please report exact Brox flow parameters and quantify sensitivity to these settings.
- Provide inference speed per video, with and without precomputed flow, and storage footprint after compression.
- Clarify flipping and normalization details for flow channels.
- Could lightweight camera motion compensation beyond mean subtraction (e.g., global homography) further help?
- Explore modest mid-level fusion (e.g., 1x1 fusion of penultimate features with strong regularization) to test whether earlier fusion is feasible.
- Include per-class accuracy analysis to show where temporal vs spatial streams contribute most.

Overall recommendation
The paper makes a clear, technically sound, and practically significant contribution to video action recognition by introducing an effective two-stream ConvNet with explicit motion modeling and by demonstrating strong results with thorough ablations. The reliance on precomputed flow is a trade-off, but justified by performance. The work is suitable for NeurIPS. Arguments favor acceptance outweigh the concerns.
