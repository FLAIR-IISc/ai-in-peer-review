Summary
The paper proposes a two-stream convolutional architecture for action recognition that decouples appearance (spatial stream) and motion (temporal stream). The spatial stream is a standard image ConvNet trained on RGB frames and pre-trained on ImageNet. The temporal stream is a ConvNet trained on stacked dense optical flow fields, with several input variants: stacking at fixed locations vs along trajectories, forward vs bi-directional flow, and mean flow subtraction to mitigate camera motion. The two streams are fused at the score level (averaging or a linear SVM). To combat limited video training data, the authors use a simple yet effective multi-task learning setup with two softmax heads (UCF-101 and HMDB-51). On UCF-101 and HMDB-51, the temporal stream alone substantially outperforms RGB-only ConvNets trained on stacked frames, and fusion of the two streams is competitive with state-of-the-art hand-crafted trajectory-based pipelines. Extensive ablations support design choices (number of flow stacks, mean subtraction, fusion).

Relation to prior work
The approach builds on:
- Shallow video representations with dense trajectories, HOF/MBH, Fisher vectors, and camera motion compensation.
- Deep models with stacked RGB frames (e.g., Karpathy et al.’s early/late/slow fusion) and HMAX-style two-pathway systems.
Key novelty here is to explicitly feed multi-frame dense optical flow volumes to a deep ConvNet (rather than expecting 2D/3D conv layers on RGB to infer motion), while retaining a separate RGB stream and combining by late fusion. The paper also connects the temporal ConvNet’s first-layer operations to classical motion descriptors (HOF/MBH/kinematic features), clarifying how the network generalizes them.

Strengths
- Clear and well-motivated decomposition into spatial and temporal streams with thorough empirical validation.
- Strong performance gains over prior deep approaches, competitive with improved trajectories, despite modest dataset sizes.
- Careful ablations: L-frame stacking, trajectory vs fixed-location stacking, bi-directional flow, mean subtraction, and fusion method.
- Practical multi-task formulation that demonstrably improves the temporal stream on small datasets.
- Reproducibility: detailed training schedules, architectures, data augmentation, and implementation notes (GPU, Caffe, flow precomputation).

Weaknesses and limitations
- Not end-to-end: reliance on a fixed optical flow algorithm is a strong prior and adds a heavy precomputation/storage bottleneck. It also complicates deployment and obscures where errors originate.
- Limited analysis of sensitivity to the chosen flow method/quality, resolution, or flow quantization (8-bit JPEG compression may affect performance).
- Fusion is limited to softmax score-level averaging or linear SVM; more integrated joint training or mid-level fusion is not explored due to overfitting.
- Camera motion handling is reduced to mean flow subtraction; no explicit estimation/compensation is attempted, despite known gains in shallow pipelines.
- Evaluation restricted to trimmed classification on UCF-101/HMDB-51; no analysis on untrimmed videos, robustness to background bias, or per-class breakdowns.
- Missing computational benchmarks (training/testing throughput, storage, and latency), which matter for practicality.
- Some writing/typo issues in references and a few missing specifics (e.g., effect of random seed variability).

Quality
Technically sound with solid empirical evidence. The ablation studies, clear baselines (single-frame RGB, stacked RGB, temporal-only), and multi-task evaluation support the claims. The method choices are justified and connected to prior descriptors. However, lack of evaluation on multiple flow algorithms and absence of runtime/memory analysis leave important practical questions open. Statistical variance across runs is not reported.

Clarity
The paper is well-organized and generally clear. The descriptions of the temporal input construction (fixed-location vs trajectory stacking), bi-directional flow, and mean subtraction are precise. Implementation details (learning schedule, data augmentation, GPU setup) aid reproducibility. Minor reference formatting errors exist.

Originality
The two-stream ConvNet with explicit optical flow inputs is a novel and influential design at the time, distinct from stacked-RGB ConvNets and shallow trajectory methods. The multi-task setup is straightforward but effective. The mean-flow subtraction is a simple, pragmatic contribution.

Significance
High. The results advance deep video recognition to be competitive with strong shallow baselines and demonstrate that explicit motion input is crucial. The two-stream paradigm is likely to influence subsequent work (e.g., better fusion, learnable motion, larger-scale training) and be adopted by practitioners.

Suggestions for improvement
- Explore more integrated fusion: shared mid-level layers or light cross-stream interactions with strong regularization to mitigate overfitting.
- Report compute/storage metrics: flow precompute time, per-video inference time, model sizes.
- Analyze sensitivity to the optical flow algorithm, resolution, and compression; compare at least one alternative flow to establish robustness.
- Incorporate stronger camera motion compensation or learnable global motion modules.
- Provide per-class error analysis and qualitative failure cases to better understand stream complementarity.
- Evaluate combination with improved trajectories to test complementarity with hand-crafted pipelines.
- Discuss generalization to untrimmed videos and longer temporal contexts (e.g., stride, L-sensitivity beyond 10).

Arguments for acceptance
- Clear, conceptually well-grounded architecture with strong empirical gains over prior deep methods.
- Thorough ablations that illuminate what matters for deep video recognition.
- Simple, effective multi-task learning addressing data scarcity.
- Likely to have significant impact and be widely adopted.

Arguments against acceptance
- Heavy dependency on precomputed optical flow; not end-to-end and computationally intensive.
- Limited exploration of fusion strategies and motion compensation compared to shallow SOTA.
- Missing robustness analyses (flow choice, compute cost) and broader evaluation scenarios.

Recommendation
Accept. The paper presents a compelling, well-validated approach that advances deep action recognition by explicitly modeling motion with a temporal ConvNet and demonstrates strong complementarity with an appearance stream. Despite practical limitations around flow precomputation and fusion, the contribution is significant, clear, and likely to influence future research.
