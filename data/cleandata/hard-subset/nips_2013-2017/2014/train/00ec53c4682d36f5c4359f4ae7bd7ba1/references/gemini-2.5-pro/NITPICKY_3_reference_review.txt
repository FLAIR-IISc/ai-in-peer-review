**Summary:**

This paper proposes a two-stream deep convolutional network architecture for action recognition in videos. The core idea is to decouple the learning of spatial appearance features from temporal motion features. The "spatial stream" is a standard ConvNet that operates on individual video frames to recognize actions from static cues. The "temporal stream" is a ConvNet trained on stacked dense optical flow fields computed between consecutive frames, designed to capture motion information. The outputs of these two streams are combined via late fusion (averaging or SVM). The authors demonstrate that this architecture, particularly when the spatial stream is pre-trained on ImageNet, achieves results on the UCF-101 and HMDB-51 datasets that are competitive with state-of-the-art methods based on hand-crafted features, and significantly outperform prior deep learning approaches like that of Karpathy et al. [14]. A multi-task learning framework is also introduced to leverage multiple smaller datasets for training the temporal network.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The central idea of separating spatial and temporal processing into two dedicated ConvNet streams is novel and well-motivated. It directly addresses a key failing of prior deep learning work [14], which struggled to learn effective motion features from raw pixel values. The demonstration that explicitly providing motion information in the form of optical flow leads to a massive performance gain is a significant contribution.
2.  **Strong Empirical Results:** The proposed method achieves very strong performance, challenging the dominance of traditional trajectory-based methods [20, 26] on standard benchmarks. The empirical evidence for the complementarity of the two streams is convincing, with fusion consistently outperforming either stream alone.
3.  **Thoroughness in Temporal Input Evaluation:** The authors provide a useful ablation study (Table 1b) on different configurations for the temporal stream's input, such as the number of stacked flows, trajectory stacking, and mean subtraction.

**Weaknesses:**

Despite the promising core idea and strong results, the paper is marred by a substantial number of minor, yet collectively significant, flaws in its methodology, analysis, and presentation.

1.  **Lack of Technical Detail and Rigor:**
    *   **Optical Flow Generation:** The quality of the optical flow is paramount to the temporal stream's success, yet it is treated as a black box. The paper simply states it used an "off-the-shelf GPU implementation of [2] from the OpenCV toolbox." No details on the version of OpenCV, the specific function called, or the parameters used are provided. This makes precise reproduction of the work impossible.
    *   **Optical Flow Compression:** The authors compress optical flow fields with JPEG to save space. JPEG is a lossy compression algorithm designed for RGB images, not displacement fields. The potential for introducing significant artifacts is high. The paper fails to provide any analysis on how this lossy compression affects the final recognition accuracy. A simple comparison between performance with and without compression is a necessary, but absent, ablation.
    *   **Arbitrary Architectural Choices:** The removal of the second normalization layer in the temporal ConvNet is justified only "to reduce memory consumption." This is an engineering decision, not a principled one. Was its impact on performance measured? Without this analysis, it appears to be an arbitrary deviation from a proven architecture.
    *   **Fusion Details:** The SVM fusion method is not sufficiently detailed. The kernel type (presumably linear, but not stated), regularization parameters, and the method for setting them (e.g., cross-validation) are not described.

2.  **Superficial Analysis and Inconsistencies:**
    *   **Bi-directional Flow:** In Table 1b, bi-directional flow is shown to be slightly better than uni-directional flow (81.2% vs. 81.0%). However, in Table 3, the authors conclude that "using bi-directional flow is not beneficial in the case of ConvNet fusion" (86.9% vs. 87.0%). This minor inconsistency is not discussed or explained, leaving the reader to question the stability of these findings.
    *   **Pre-training Improvement:** The authors "believe that the main reason" for their improved ILSVRC performance over [31] is their data augmentation strategy. This is presented as conjecture rather than an empirically verified claim.
    *   **Multi-task Learning:** The multi-task learning improvement on UCF-101 is only 0.5% (81.5% vs 81.0%). While positive, this is a very small gain and its significance should be discussed with more nuance.

3.  **Poor Presentation and Formatting:**
    *   **Reference List:** The reference list is highly unprofessional and incomplete. Many entries are missing crucial information like conference/journal name, year, and page numbers (e.g., [1], [3], [4], [5], [11], [14], [15], etc.). Reference [13] is just a URL. This level of carelessness is unacceptable for a top-tier publication.
    *   **Inconsistent Referencing:** The paper is inconsistent in how it refers to its own sections. In the introduction, it states "In Sect. 1.1 we review..." which is part of the current section. Later it uses "Sect. 2" and "Sect. 3", but then "A mult-task learning framework is developed in Sect. 4...". This demonstrates a lack of careful proofreading.
    *   **Typos:** There are minor but noticeable typos, such as "mult-task" in the introduction's overview.

**Recommendation:**

While the paper presents an important and influential idea for action recognition, the execution and manuscript preparation fall short of the standards expected for NIPS. The work is undermined by a pattern of methodological shortcuts (JPEG compression without analysis, off-the-shelf flow without details), insufficient analysis of key results, and extremely sloppy presentation (especially the reference list). These issues collectively cast doubt on the meticulousness of the research process and hinder the paper's primary goal of enabling other researchers to reproduce and build upon its results. Therefore, despite the strong headline numbers, I recommend **rejection**. The authors should be encouraged to conduct the missing ablation studies, provide the necessary implementation details, and thoroughly revise the manuscript to address the numerous presentation flaws before resubmitting to a future conference.
