**Summary of the paper:**
This paper proposes a two-stream convolutional network architecture for action recognition in videos. The core idea is to handle spatial and temporal information separately. A spatial stream ConvNet is trained on individual video frames to recognize actions from static appearance, leveraging pre-training on the large-scale ImageNet dataset. A second, temporal stream ConvNet is trained on stacked dense optical flow fields computed between consecutive frames, designed explicitly to learn motion patterns. The predictions from these two independent streams are combined at the end using a late fusion scheme (averaging or an SVM). The authors evaluate their method on the UCF-101 and HMDB-51 datasets, showing that their approach significantly outperforms previous deep learning models and achieves performance comparable to state-of-the-art methods based on hand-crafted features like improved dense trajectories (iDT).

**Strengths:**
1.  **Methodological Clarity:** The paper presents a clear and intuitive architecture. The motivation to decouple appearance and motion processing is well-argued and straightforward to understand. The overall pipeline is described in sufficient detail for reproducibility.
2.  **Strong Empirical Results for a Deep Model:** The proposed model substantially closes the performance gap between deep learning methods and traditional, shallow feature-based approaches. It demonstrates a significant improvement over the recent work of Karpathy et al. [14], which reported disappointing results when applying ConvNets to video. The extensive ablation studies (Table 1) effectively justify the key design choices, such as stacking multiple optical flow fields and using ImageNet pre-training.
3.  **Thoroughness:** The experimental evaluation is comprehensive. The authors systematically investigate various input configurations for the temporal stream, the benefits of pre-training, and the effectiveness of a multi-task learning framework for combining smaller datasets. This rigorous analysis adds credibility to their findings.

**Weaknesses:**
1.  **Reliance on Hand-Crafted Features:** A primary weakness of this work is its heavy reliance on pre-computed optical flow as input to the temporal stream. While effective, this design choice moves away from the end-to-end learning philosophy that is a central promise of deep learning. The network does not learn to extract motion from raw pixel values; rather, it learns to classify patterns from a pre-processed, traditional computer vision feature. The performance of the entire temporal stream is therefore fundamentally dependent on, and upper-bounded by, the quality of the chosen optical flow algorithm [2]. This feels less like a novel learning architecture and more like using a ConvNet as a powerful feature encoder on top of a well-established motion feature.
2.  **Marginal Performance Gain over Established Baselines:** While the results are strong for a deep architecture, they are only "comparable to" the state-of-the-art shallow methods, such as iDT with Fisher Vectors [26]. The paper does not demonstrate a clear superiority over these highly-tuned, established baselines. Given the substantial computational resources, training time, and complexity involved in training two separate deep networks, achieving parity is not a sufficiently compelling reason to abandon existing, well-understood methods. A new, more complex approach should ideally offer a significant performance advantage.
3.  **Limited Novelty in Architecture:** The concept of separate processing streams for form and motion is not entirely new (e.g., [16]). The spatial stream is a standard image classification ConvNet, and the temporal stream applies the same architecture to a different input modality. The main contribution lies in demonstrating that this particular combination, especially using optical flow as input, is an effective recipe for action recognition. However, the architectural and conceptual novelty is limited. The fusion mechanism (averaging or SVM on softmax scores) is also a standard late-fusion technique, rather than a more integrated, learnable component.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   The paper provides a simple and effective framework that dramatically improves the performance of deep learning on standard action recognition benchmarks.
*   It serves as a very strong baseline for future research in deep learning for video analysis.
*   The ablation studies are thorough and provide valuable insights into what is required to make deep models work for this task.

**Arguments for Rejection:**
*   The approach does not convincingly outperform the established state-of-the-art (iDT), making its practical significance questionable given its high complexity.
*   The core idea relies on using a ConvNet to process hand-crafted optical flow features, which can be seen as an incremental step rather than a fundamental contribution to learning spatio-temporal representations.
*   The novelty is more in the successful engineering and combination of existing components than in proposing a new, fundamental machine learning concept.

**Overall Assessment:**
This is a solid, well-executed paper that presents a valuable empirical contribution. It clearly demonstrates how a deep learning approach can be made to work effectively for action recognition, something prior work has struggled with. However, my enthusiasm is tempered by the method's reliance on external, pre-computed optical flow and its failure to decisively beat the best-performing shallow methods. It feels like a robust piece of engineering that finds a successful recipe, but it lacks the fundamental novelty one might hope for in a NIPS paper. It pushes the state-of-the-art for deep models forward, but it only just catches up to the overall state-of-the-art. While I am skeptical of its long-term significance as a "learning" method, its empirical success is undeniable and provides a much-needed strong baseline. Therefore, I lean towards acceptance, albeit with reservations.
