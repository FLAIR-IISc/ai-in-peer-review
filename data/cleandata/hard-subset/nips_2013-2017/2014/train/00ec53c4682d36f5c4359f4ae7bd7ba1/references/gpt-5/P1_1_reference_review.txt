Summary
This paper proposes a two-stream convolutional network (ConvNet) architecture for video action recognition that explicitly decouples spatial appearance and temporal motion. The spatial stream is a standard image ConvNet applied to individual frames and pre-trained on ImageNet. The temporal stream is a ConvNet fed with stacked dense optical flow fields computed between consecutive frames; several input variants are examined (flow stacking vs trajectory stacking, forward vs bidirectional flow, mean flow subtraction). The two streams are fused by late score-level fusion (averaging or a linear SVM). To mitigate limited video data, the authors introduce a simple multi-task learning setup with two softmax heads to jointly train on UCF-101 and HMDB-51. Extensive experiments and ablations on UCF-101 and HMDB-51 show: (i) the temporal stream trained on optical flow substantially outperforms a frame-only ConvNet and a raw-RGB multi-frame “slow fusion” baseline; (ii) spatial and temporal streams are complementary, yielding strong gains when fused; (iii) multi-task learning improves generalization, particularly for HMDB-51. The method achieves performance comparable to state-of-the-art hand-crafted trajectory-based pipelines while using deep learning.

Relation to prior work: The paper situates itself against shallow trajectory-based methods (e.g., HOF/MBH with Fisher vectors; improved dense trajectories with camera motion compensation) and earlier deep video models that either used stacked RGB frames (e.g., Karpathy et al.) or shallow HMAX-like two-stream systems. The key distinction is explicit motion input via optical flow to a deep ConvNet, generalizing hand-crafted flow-based descriptors. The authors also connect their approach to the two-stream hypothesis in visual neuroscience.

Quality
- Technical soundness: Strong. The architectural choices are well-motivated; the temporal input formulation is clear; the multi-task setup is straightforward and appropriate. The ablations are thorough: number of stacked flows (L), trajectory vs flow stacking, bidirectional flow, mean subtraction, training regimes for spatial net (from scratch vs pretrain/fine-tune vs last-layer), fusion method. The comparison to a stacked-RGB baseline is valuable and supports the central claim that explicit motion representation matters.
- Empirical support: Solid. Evaluations follow standard UCF-101 and HMDB-51 protocols with three-way split reporting for state-of-the-art comparison. Training/testing details (data augmentation, learning rate schedules, dropout, sampling strategy) are sufficiently described to replicate. Multi-GPU and optical-flow preprocessing details are practical and reproducible.
- Caveats: Runtime/storage overhead of optical flow is acknowledged but not deeply quantified; the SVM-fusion training protocol could be described more explicitly (trained on training data only? any validation for hyperparameters?).

Clarity
- The paper is well written and organized. The temporal input formulations are formalized with clear notation; the relation to prior hand-crafted features is insightful and helps readers understand what the network can learn. Implementation details are unusually detailed for a deep learning paper (a plus for reproducibility).
- Minor clarity suggestions: Specify clearly the data split used for training the fusion SVM and how hyperparameters are tuned; report per-clip and per-video runtimes for flow computation, inference, and overall pipeline to contextualize practicality.

Originality
- The key novelty is the explicit two-stream deep architecture with a temporal stream operating on stacked optical flow, plus empirical demonstration that this representation greatly improves over stacked RGB inputs. While optical flow and two-stream ideas existed in shallow pipelines, combining them with deep ConvNets, systematic input design (flow stacking, mean subtraction, bi-directional flow), and late fusion in this way is new and influential. The multi-task training is not novel per se, but its application to small video datasets is appropriate and useful.

Significance
- High. The paper advances the state of the art on two standard video benchmarks and, more importantly, introduces a simple, general recipe that many practitioners can adopt. It provides a compelling bridge between hand-engineered flow-based features and deep representations, likely to influence subsequent architectures. The thorough ablations make it a strong reference point for the community.

Strengths
- Clear conceptual decomposition into spatial and temporal streams with simple, effective late fusion.
- Well-justified temporal input design with extensive ablations (L, stacking variants, mean subtraction, bi-directional flow).
- Strong empirical gains over stacked RGB models; competitive with trajectory-based SOTA.
- Practical multi-task training to leverage multiple small datasets.
- Solid implementation and training details; attention to data augmentation and pretraining.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Dependence on precomputed optical flow increases computational cost and storage (though mitigated by compression). Suggest reporting end-to-end training/inference throughput, storage requirements, and wall-clock comparisons to trajectory-based pipelines to inform practicality.
- The approach is not end-to-end trainable with respect to motion estimation. While this is a design choice, a discussion or preliminary experiment on sensitivity to the specific flow algorithm (e.g., Brox vs TV-L1) would strengthen the case and inform reproducibility.
- Camera motion compensation is only approximated via mean flow subtraction. A homography-based compensation baseline (as used in improved trajectories) could test whether more explicit compensation further helps.
- Fusion is limited to score-level due to overfitting when stacking higher layers. Consider regularization strategies (e.g., weight sharing between streams in early layers, stronger dropout, batch normalization) or lightweight gating/attention for fusion; even a small negative result would be informative.
- Limited error analysis. Including class-wise performance or confusion patterns (appearance-dominant vs motion-dominant classes) would provide insight into complementarity.
- Clarify SVM-fusion training protocol to ensure no leakage from test to train and specify regularization.

Pros for acceptance
- Strong, simple idea with compelling empirical validation and clear impact on the field.
- Thorough ablations and careful comparisons against relevant baselines.
- Good clarity and reproducibility; practical training recipe others can adopt.
- Advances understanding of how to incorporate motion in deep video models.

Cons for acceptance
- Not end-to-end with respect to motion; heavy preprocessing requirement.
- Limited analysis of sensitivity to optical flow choice and camera motion handling.
- Fusion exploration is somewhat limited; no intermediate feature fusion due to overfitting.

Minor comments
- Please include exact L used in final models in the main text summary of results (appears to be L=10).
- Add per-dataset training curves or signs of overfitting to support the multi-task regularization claim quantitatively.
- Fix minor typos/formatting (e.g., spaces around punctuation, “bi-directional” vs “bidirectional” consistency).

Overall recommendation
This is a high-quality, clearly presented, and impactful contribution that convincingly demonstrates the value of explicit motion via optical flow in deep video recognition and provides a practical two-stream architecture. I recommend acceptance.
