## Review of "Two-Stream Convolutional Networks for Action Recognition in Videos"

**Overall Assessment:** This paper presents a significant and well-executed contribution to video action recognition. It convincingly demonstrates the benefits of decoupling spatial and temporal information into separate convolutional streams and achieves state-of-the-art or competitive results on standard benchmarks. The work is well-written, clearly explained, and builds logically upon prior work. I recommend acceptance.

**Summary:** The paper addresses the challenge of video action recognition using deep learning.  Previous attempts to directly apply CNNs to video (e.g., stacking frames) have yielded disappointing results. This work proposes a two-stream architecture: a spatial stream processing individual frames, and a temporal stream processing stacked optical flow. Both streams employ CNNs, and their outputs are fused for final classification.  The authors leverage pre-training on ImageNet for the spatial stream and explore various configurations for the temporal stream, including trajectory stacking and bidirectional flow.  They also introduce a multi-task learning framework to improve training on limited video data.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty & Insight:** The two-stream architecture is a clever and effective way to decouple spatial and temporal information, a crucial insight for video understanding.  This contrasts with previous approaches attempting to learn spatio-temporal features implicitly from stacked frames.
* **Strong Empirical Results:** The paper demonstrates significant improvements over previous deep learning methods for action recognition ([14]) and achieves competitive performance with state-of-the-art hand-crafted features ([20, 26]). The results are presented clearly and systematically.
* **Thorough Ablation Studies:** The authors perform a comprehensive series of experiments, evaluating different input configurations for the temporal stream (optical flow stacking, trajectory stacking, bidirectional flow, mean flow subtraction) and fusion methods (averaging, SVM). This provides valuable insights into the effectiveness of each component.
* **Multi-Task Learning:** The introduction of multi-task learning is a significant contribution, addressing the data scarcity problem in video action recognition.
* **Clear Writing and Organization:** The paper is well-written, clearly organized, and easy to follow.  The explanations of the architecture and experiments are detailed and concise.
* **Strong Connection to Neuroscientific Principles:** The paper appropriately draws parallels to the ventral and dorsal streams in the human visual cortex, providing a biologically inspired justification for the architecture.  Although the authors rightly state they do not pursue this connection further, it adds contextual grounding.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Optical Flow Dependency:** The temporal stream's performance is inherently limited by the quality of the optical flow computation. While the authors acknowledge this, the paper doesn’t extensively discuss robustness to inaccurate flow or explore alternative motion representations beyond optical flow.  Considering recent advancements in learned motion representations (e.g., using differentiable rendering), this is a potential area for future work.
* **Limited Discussion of Computational Cost:** While the paper mentions GPU training times, it lacks a detailed analysis of the computational cost associated with both optical flow computation and the two-stream architecture.  Comparing the computational demands to other methods (especially hand-crafted feature approaches) would be beneficial.
* **Fusion Method Limitations:** The authors primarily focus on averaging and SVM-based fusion.  More sophisticated fusion techniques, such as attention mechanisms or learned fusion weights, could potentially further improve performance.
* **Scalability to Larger Datasets:** While multi-task learning mitigates data scarcity, the paper's evaluation is still largely confined to UCF101 and HMDB51.  A discussion of the scalability of the approach to larger and more complex datasets (like Sports-1M, mentioned in the introduction) would strengthen the work.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The two-stream architecture represents a significant architectural innovation for video action recognition.
* The empirical results are strong and demonstrate a clear improvement over existing methods.
* The ablation studies are thorough and provide valuable insights.
* The paper is well-written and clearly explains a complex topic.
* The multi-task learning framework addresses a practical challenge in video action recognition.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The reliance on optical flow introduces a dependency on the accuracy of flow computation.
* The computational cost of the approach could be a limiting factor. (Though, this is common in deep learning.)
* The fusion methods explored are relatively simple.




**Specific Comments/Suggestions:**

* **Section 3.1:** Clarify the choice of 224x224 input size for the ConvNets. Is this based on computational constraints, or is there a specific reason for this resolution?
* **Section 4:** The explanation of the multi-task learning loss function could be more explicit. Provide the equation for the combined loss.
* **Table 4:**  Specify the exact versions of the state-of-the-art methods being compared against (e.g., the specific parameters used in [26]).
* **Future Work:**  Expand the discussion of future work to include learned motion representations and more sophisticated fusion techniques.




**Overall, this is a high-quality paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of video action recognition.  I believe it is suitable for acceptance to NIPS.**
