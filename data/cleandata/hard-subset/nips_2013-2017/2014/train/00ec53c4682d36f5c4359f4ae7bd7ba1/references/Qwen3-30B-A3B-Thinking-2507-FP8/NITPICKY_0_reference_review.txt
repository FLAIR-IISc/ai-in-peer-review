

# Review of "Two-Stream Convolutional Networks for Action Recognition in Videos"

This paper proposes a two-stream convolutional network architecture for video action recognition, combining a spatial stream (processing individual frames) and a temporal stream (processing optical flow between frames). The authors claim their approach significantly outperforms previous deep learning methods for video recognition and is competitive with state-of-the-art shallow representations. The work builds on the two-stream hypothesis from neuroscience but does not explore this connection beyond a brief mention.

## Strengths
The paper presents a well-structured methodology with clear ablation studies comparing different input configurations for the temporal stream. The multi-task learning approach to combine UCF-101 and HMDB-51 datasets is a thoughtful solution to the small dataset problem. The experimental results are comprehensive, and the two-stream fusion approach is convincingly shown to be complementary.

## Weaknesses
Despite the promising results, several technical and presentation issues undermine the paper's credibility:

1. **Technical inaccuracies**: The paper mischaracterizes the dorsal stream in neuroscience literature. Reference [9] (Goodale & Milner, "Separate visual pathways for perception and action") states the dorsal stream is involved in visuomotor control, not "motion recognition" as claimed. This fundamental error casts doubt on the authors' understanding of their own theoretical foundation.

2. **Formatting inconsistencies**: The paper contains numerous unprofessional formatting errors in equations, references, and text. For example:
   - Equation (1) has inconsistent spacing: "Iτ (u, v, 2k − 1) = dxτ+k−1(u, v), (1)"
   - The reference list has incomplete entries (e.g., "A. Berg, J. Deng, L. Fei-Fei, , , ")
   - The phrase "d y τ+k−1" should be "d_y τ+k−1" (missing underscore in subscript)

3. **Misrepresentation of prior work**: The paper claims "unlike [15], the sub-image is sampled from the whole frame, not just its 256×256 center." This is factually incorrect. Krizhevsky et al. (2012) used random cropping, not just center cropping, as standard practice. This misrepresentation damages the authors' credibility.

4. **Missing methodological details**: The learning rate schedule is described as "kept the same for all training sets" but no specific schedule is provided for the multi-task learning case. The paper states "the learning rate is initially set to 10−2, and then decreased according to a fixed schedule" but fails to specify the exact schedule.

5. **Statistical rigor**: The paper reports small performance improvements (e.g., 1-2% on UCF-101) without providing statistical significance tests. Given the small dataset sizes (UCF-101 has only 9.5K training videos per split), these differences might not be statistically meaningful.

6. **Inconsistent claims**: The paper states "the temporal ConvNet, trained using multi-task learning, performs the best both alone and when fused with a spatial net." While Table 3 shows the temporal net alone (81.5%) is better than the spatial net alone (74.3%), the two-stream fusion (84.1%) is better than both. The claim is technically true but misleadingly phrased.

7. **Missing explanation**: The paper doesn't explain why multi-task learning helps the temporal stream. Is it due to more data or shared representations? This is a critical gap in the methodology.

8. **Unverified claim**: The paper states "We believe that the main reason for the improvement is sampling of ConvNet inputs from the whole image, rather than just its center." But they provide no ablation study comparing center-crop versus random crop on the same network architecture.

## Overall Assessment
While the paper presents a reasonable approach to video action recognition with promising results, the cumulative effect of these minor but significant technical and presentation flaws is substantial. The misrepresentation of prior work, neuroscience concepts, and formatting errors demonstrate a lack of attention to detail that is unacceptable for a NIPS submission. The paper's claims about the superiority of their approach over [14] are supported by the results, but the paper fails to meet the standards of technical rigor expected at NIPS.

The authors have made a contribution to the field, but the paper as written does not adequately document the methodology, misrepresents prior work, and contains numerous avoidable errors. These issues collectively undermine the paper's credibility and prevent it from meeting the quality bar for NIPS.

## Recommendation
Reject. While the core idea has merit, the paper's technical shortcomings, misrepresentations, and lack of attention to detail make it unsuitable for publication at NIPS. The authors should address these issues thoroughly before resubmitting.
