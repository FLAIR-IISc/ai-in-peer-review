Summary
The paper proposes a two-stream convolutional architecture for video action recognition that explicitly decouples appearance and motion. The spatial stream is a ConvNet applied to individual RGB frames, pre-trained on ImageNet and fine-tuned on the target video dataset. The temporal stream is a ConvNet that takes as input stacked dense optical flow fields computed between consecutive frames (forward or bi-directional), optionally along motion trajectories, with a simple mean-flow subtraction to reduce global motion. The authors study several design choices (number of stacked flows L, trajectory vs. fixed-location stacking, mean subtraction, bi-directional flow) and fuse the two streams at the score level (averaging or linear SVM). To mitigate small video datasets, they introduce a multi-task learning setup that shares the representation while training separate softmax heads for UCF-101 and HMDB-51. Experiments on UCF-101 and HMDB-51 show large gains over prior deep baselines (e.g., stacked RGB frames) and competitiveness with state-of-the-art hand-crafted pipelines (improved trajectories + Fisher vectors). The work relates to prior dorsal/ventral two-stream theories and shows that the temporal ConvNet generalizes hand-crafted motion descriptors (HOF/MBH) within a learnable framework.

Quality
- Technical soundness: Strong. The architecture is straightforward but well-motivated. The temporal stream’s formulation using stacked flow fields is clear, and the ablations (L, trajectory vs. optical flow stacking, mean subtraction, bi-directional flow) support design choices. The use of multi-task learning to regularize on small datasets is appropriate and empirically validated.
- Empirical support: Thorough for the chosen datasets. Clear training/testing protocols (25 frames per video, 10 crops per frame, CNN-M-2048 config, dropout, learning rate schedules). Meaningful baselines include single-frame spatial net, “slow fusion” on stacked RGB, and different temporal encodings. The claim that explicit motion input (optical flow) is superior to learning motion implicitly from RGB stacks is convincingly demonstrated.
- Limitations: The approach depends on a non-learned preprocessing step (Brox optical flow) that dominates both computation and storage (necessitating precomputation and JPEG-compressed quantization). There is no sensitivity analysis to flow algorithm choice or to the JPEG quantization; these could affect accuracy and reproducibility. Camera motion handling is limited to mean subtraction; comparisons to stronger compensation (e.g., homography) are not provided. Statistical significance (e.g., variance across runs) is not reported.

Clarity
- The paper is well-organized, readable, and provides enough detail to reproduce most aspects (architecture, training schedule, sampling, fusion). The relation to prior hand-crafted features is explained, including how HOF/MBH-like computations can be realized by early conv layers.
- Minor suggestions: Report runtime and storage trade-offs more explicitly (per video inference time with/without flow, training time with precomputation), and provide per-class error analysis to illustrate where each stream helps. Clarify whether code/models will be released.

Originality
- Moderate-to-high. The individual ingredients (optical flow, ConvNets, two-stream hypothesis, multi-task learning) are known, but their combination into a simple two-stream deep architecture with careful design and analysis is novel and impactful at the time. The explicit optical-flow-as-input choice is a key departure from prior deep baselines that tried to learn motion from RGB stacks. The multi-task setup is standard but effectively applied to video recognition.

Significance
- High. The work advances the state of deep learning for video by showing a clear and practical path to competitive performance, narrowing the gap to hand-crafted pipelines and outperforming prior deep models on UCF-101/HMDB-51. The two-stream design and the optical-flow input have strong potential to become a standard baseline and a foundation for future extensions (e.g., end-to-end learned motion, better camera-motion handling, trajectory-aware pooling).
- Practitioners can directly use the method; researchers can build on the architecture and findings. While not end-to-end, the demonstrated gains are substantial and likely to influence subsequent work.

Strengths
- Clear, simple, and effective two-stream design with complementary cues.
- Strong empirical gains over deep baselines; competitive with state-of-the-art hand-crafted approaches.
- Careful ablations substantiate design choices (L=10 helps; mean subtraction helps; optical flow stacking > trajectory stacking; bi-directional offers marginal gains).
- Practical multi-task learning to combat data scarcity; improves both HMDB-51 and UCF-101.
- Solid experimental protocol and implementation details (multi-GPU training, sampling strategy).

Weaknesses
- Dependence on precomputed optical flow (computationally heavy; not end-to-end; possible sensitivity to algorithm/parameters and to JPEG quantization).
- Limited treatment of camera motion (mean subtraction only); no comparison to stronger compensation.
- No analysis of sensitivity to flow choice (Brox vs. alternatives) or to L beyond 10.
- Limited error analysis and runtime reporting; no results on larger-scale Sports-1M (acknowledged as future work).
- Fusion restricted to late score-level; failed attempt at joint fully connected fusion suggests overfitting—could explore better regularization or intermediate fusion.

Questions for authors
- How sensitive is performance to the optical flow algorithm and to the JPEG quantization step?
- What are the runtime/storage costs per video at test time? Could a lighter flow (e.g., TV-L1) preserve accuracy?
- Does stronger camera-motion compensation (e.g., homography) further help beyond mean subtraction?
- How does performance vary with the temporal window L and frame sampling density?
- Any plans or preliminary results on end-to-end learning of motion (e.g., learned flow or warping modules)?

Pros for acceptance
- Strong empirical contribution with clear, generalizable insights.
- Meaningful advance over prior deep baselines; competitive with best hand-crafted methods.
- Well written; ablations and multi-task learning add value.
- Likely to be influential and widely adopted.

Cons for acceptance
- Not end-to-end; heavy reliance on precomputed flow and associated costs.
- Some design choices remain empirical without deeper analysis.
- Limited exploration of camera motion and fusion strategies.

Recommendation
- Accept. The paper is technically sound, clear, original in its effective combination of ideas, and significant for the community.

Reviewer confidence
- High: familiar with deep video recognition and motion representations.
