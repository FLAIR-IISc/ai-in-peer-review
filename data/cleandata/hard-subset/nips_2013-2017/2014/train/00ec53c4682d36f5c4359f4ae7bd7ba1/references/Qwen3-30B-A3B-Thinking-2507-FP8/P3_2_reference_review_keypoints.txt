- Proposes a two-stream CNN architecture separating spatial (RGB frames, ImageNet-pretrained) and temporal (optical flow) processing for video action recognition.
- Temporal stream uses stacked optical flow as CNN input to explicitly model motion instead of learning it from raw frames.
- Multi-task learning is used to combine training data from different datasets to mitigate video data scarcity.
- Builds on neuroscience two-stream hypothesis and extends previous single-stream/deep and hand-crafted feature approaches.
- Demonstrates substantial empirical improvement over prior deep learning methods and competitiveness with top hand-crafted features (14–20% absolute gains).
- Provides thorough ablation studies (flow stacking vs trajectory stacking, bi-directional flow, mean subtraction, multi-task learning) validating component contributions.
- Methodology and implementation details are sufficiently detailed for reproducibility.
- Authors acknowledge practical limitations such as optical flow precomputation bottleneck and suboptimal handling of camera motion.
- Lacks a theoretical explanation for why decoupling spatial and temporal streams outperforms single-stream approaches.
- Paper is clearly written, well-organized, and figures effectively illustrate concepts.
- Multi-task learning section needs clearer explanation of the two softmax layers and backprop across tasks.
- Original contribution is significant: first effective application of separate CNNs to spatial and temporal streams in action recognition.
- Temporal stream alone outperforms spatial stream, and their combination yields the best results.
- High significance: simple, effective approach that sets a strong baseline and suggests directions for future work (scaling, camera motion handling).
- Strengths: novel architecture, comprehensive evaluation, useful multi-tasking approach, explicit motion modeling, strong baseline for future work.
- Weaknesses: no theoretical justification, added preprocessing complexity from optical flow, issues with fast motion/occlusions, limited discussion of computational complexity and scalability to longer videos.
- Arguments for acceptance: clear empirical advance, well-justified approach, strong experiments, and potential influence on video understanding research.
- Arguments against acceptance: lack of theory, reliance on precomputed optical flow, incomplete handling of camera motion, unclear scalability to larger/more complex datasets.
- Overall recommendation: accept — significant, well-executed contribution despite limitations.
- Additional suggestions: clarify whether Sports-1M training was attempted, discuss explicit camera-motion handling beyond mean flow subtraction, and provide computational/inference cost analysis.
