

# Review of "Two-Stream Convolutional Networks for Action Recognition in Videos"

This paper proposes a two-stream convolutional network architecture for video action recognition, which separates spatial and temporal processing streams. The spatial stream processes individual video frames (using a ConvNet pre-trained on ImageNet), while the temporal stream processes optical flow between frames. The authors argue that this separation allows the model to better leverage the complementary information in static appearance and motion, and they introduce a multi-task learning framework to combine training data from multiple datasets (UCF-101 and HMDB-51). The approach significantly outperforms previous deep learning methods for video action recognition and achieves competitive results with state-of-the-art shallow representations.

**Strengths:**

1. **Originality and Novelty:** The two-stream architecture represents a meaningful advancement over previous deep learning approaches for video recognition (e.g., [14]), which attempted to learn spatio-temporal features directly from stacked frames. By explicitly separating spatial and temporal processing and using optical flow as input to the temporal stream, the authors create a more effective architecture that better leverages the complementary information in video data. This aligns with the two-streams hypothesis in neuroscience while providing a practical computational implementation.

2. **Technical Quality:** The paper demonstrates strong technical execution with comprehensive ablation studies on input configurations for the temporal stream (optical flow stacking, trajectory stacking, mean subtraction, bidirectional flow). The multi-task learning approach to combine datasets is well-motivated and effectively addresses the challenge of limited training data for video action recognition. The experimental evaluation on UCF-101 and HMDB-51 is thorough and follows standard protocols.

3. **Significance:** The results are significant - the proposed architecture achieves performance competitive with state-of-the-art shallow methods (e.g., [20, 21, 26]) despite being trained on relatively small datasets. The authors demonstrate that the two streams are complementary, with temporal stream alone outperforming spatial stream by 6%, and the combined model outperforming both by 14%. This represents a meaningful advancement over previous deep learning approaches for video recognition.

4. **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and well-organized, with clear figures illustrating the architecture and input configurations. The relationship to prior work is well-explained, and the connections to the two-streams hypothesis in neuroscience provide useful context.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Comparison with Recent Work:** While the paper compares favorably to prior deep learning approaches (e.g., [14]), it does not sufficiently compare with the most recent state-of-the-art methods (e.g., the "Action Recognition with Improved Trajectories" [26] or "Action Recognition by Dense Trajectories" [29] methods mentioned in the related work). The paper's claim that it's "competitive with the state of the art" could be more convincingly demonstrated with direct comparisons to the latest methods.

2. **Optical Flow Limitations:** The paper relies on a specific optical flow computation method ([2]), but doesn't thoroughly explore how the quality of optical flow affects performance. The authors mention that "mean displacement subtraction" helps reduce the effect of global motion, but they don't provide a detailed analysis of how their approach handles challenging cases like complex camera motion or occlusions, which are known to be problematic for optical flow-based methods.

3. **Computational Cost:** While the paper mentions pre-computing optical flow to avoid a bottleneck, it doesn't sufficiently address the computational cost of this preprocessing step, which is substantial (1.5TB for UCF-101 reduced to 27GB with compression). This represents a significant practical limitation for real-world applications.

4. **Theoretical Justification:** The paper lacks a deeper theoretical justification for why the two-stream architecture works better than end-to-end spatio-temporal learning. While the authors reference the two-streams hypothesis in neuroscience, they don't provide a more rigorous theoretical foundation for their approach.

**Pro and Con Acceptance Arguments:**

*Pro:*
- Novel two-stream architecture effectively separates spatial and temporal processing
- Comprehensive experimental evaluation with strong ablation studies
- Multi-task learning framework provides a principled way to combine limited video datasets
- Results significantly outperform previous deep learning approaches for video action recognition
- Clear connection to prior work and well-organized presentation

*Con:*
- Limited comparison with the most recent state-of-the-art shallow methods
- Insufficient discussion of optical flow limitations and computational cost
- Lack of theoretical justification for the two-stream approach
- The paper's claim of "competitive with state of the art" could be strengthened with more direct comparisons

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a high-quality paper that makes a significant contribution to video action recognition. The two-stream architecture is well-motivated, technically sound, and achieves strong results. While the paper has some limitations in comparing with the very latest methods and addressing optical flow limitations, these are relatively minor compared to the substantial contribution of the work.

The paper clearly meets NIPS standards for quality, clarity, and significance. The technical execution is strong, the methodology is well-justified, and the results are compelling. The authors have demonstrated that a carefully designed deep learning approach can achieve competitive performance with state-of-the-art shallow methods, which is an important contribution to the field.

I recommend acceptance of this paper. The minor weaknesses noted above could be addressed in a camera-ready version, but they do not detract from the overall quality and significance of the work.
