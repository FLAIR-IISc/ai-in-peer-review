Summary
The paper proposes a two-stream ConvNet architecture for video action recognition: a spatial stream operating on RGB frames (pretrained on ImageNet) and a temporal stream operating on stacked dense optical flow fields. The two streams are fused by late score fusion (averaging or SVM). The temporal ConvNet generalizes popular hand-crafted motion features (HOF, MBH, trajectory descriptors) by taking optical flow as input and learning the rest end-to-end. To mitigate data scarcity for the temporal stream, the authors introduce a simple multi-task setup that shares all layers and uses dataset-specific softmax heads for UCF-101 and HMDB-51. Extensive experiments analyze design choices (number of stacked flows, trajectory vs. spatial stacking, mean flow subtraction, bi-directional flow) and show that the temporal stream substantially outperforms single-frame baselines and that fusion yields further gains, competitive with state-of-the-art shallow systems.

Relation to prior work
The approach contrasts with prior deep models that take stacked RGB frames and attempt to learn motion implicitly (e.g., 3D CNNs and slow fusion). It is inspired by the dorsal/ventral two-pathway hypothesis and by trajectory-based features, but implements both pathways with modern ConvNets and an explicit motion representation. Compared to HMAX-style two-stream systems, this is deeper, trained end-to-end, and shows stronger performance. The connection to hand-crafted descriptors is well articulated, including how first-layer conv filters can reproduce HOF/MBH-like computations.

Strengths
- Novelty and conceptual clarity: Decoupling spatial and temporal processing into separate ConvNets with explicit optical flow is a clean, bold departure from prior “stacked RGB” models and provides a compelling abstraction consistent with human vision theories.
- Strong empirical evidence: Thorough ablations on UCF-101/HMDB-51 demonstrate the value of optical flow stacking, mean subtraction, and late fusion. The temporal stream clearly dominates single-frame models, and multi-task learning provides a practical regularizer for small video datasets.
- Practicality: Leverages ImageNet pretraining for the spatial stream; introduces a simple yet effective multi-task formulation; describes multi-GPU training and efficient flow storage.
- Positioning: Nicely situates the model relative to hand-crafted pipelines and shows how the learned model subsumes popular flow-based features.

Weaknesses and limitations
- Not fully end-to-end: The pipeline relies on precomputed optical flow (Brox). This improves performance but moves learning outside the ConvNet and introduces substantial preprocessing/storage overhead. The paper stops short of learning motion estimation or warping jointly with recognition.
- Camera motion handling is rudimentary: Mean flow subtraction helps but is a coarse approximation. Modern trajectory pipelines benefit from explicit camera motion compensation; incorporating homography/stabilization could further improve results.
- Dataset scale: The evaluation is limited to UCF-101 and HMDB-51; Sports-1M is cited but not used. Given the claim that data is the limiting factor, a stronger large-scale evaluation would bolster significance.
- Fusion capacity/overfitting: Attempts at deeper fusion failed due to overfitting; while honest, this suggests the current approach may not fully exploit cross-stream interactions.
- Efficiency: Precomputing flow is a bottleneck and complicates deployment; a discussion of test-time throughput vs. frame stacks would strengthen practical relevance.

Quality
The paper is technically sound and carefully executed. Experimental methodology is clear, ablations are meaningful, and conclusions are supported by data. The comparison to prior deep models is fair, and the rationale for design decisions (e.g., flow stacking length, late fusion) is justified. Minor caveats include lack of runtime analysis for end-to-end inference and limited exploration of earlier fusion mechanisms.

Clarity
The paper is well written and well organized. The descriptions of input configurations (optical flow vs. trajectory stacking, bi-directional flow) are precise. Implementation details (training schedules, augmentation, multi-GPU setup) are sufficient for reproduction. Figures effectively illustrate the approach.

Originality
High. While optical flow and two-stream neuroscience analogies are not new, using explicit stacked flow as ConvNet input, combined with a two-stream architecture and multi-task learning to address data scarcity, is a novel and influential formulation at the time. The argument that the temporal ConvNet generalizes HOF/MBH/trajectory features is insightful.

Significance
High. The approach advances deep video recognition beyond stacked-RGB baselines and offers a principled path to leverage motion. The results are competitive with strong hand-crafted pipelines and substantially better than prior deep models. This work is likely to spur follow-ups (e.g., differentiable flow, early/mid-level fusion, trajectory-aware pooling), and practitioners can adopt it immediately.

Questions and suggestions
- Could the authors report inference speed (fps) with precomputed flow and overall memory footprint? How does accuracy vary with cheaper/faster flow?
- Have the authors tried earlier or mid-level fusion (e.g., shared mid-level layers, cross-stream residuals) with stronger regularization to mitigate overfitting?
- Could homography-based camera motion compensation be integrated before flow stacking?
- Would training with noisy, fast flow (e.g., TV-L1) and test-time ensembling across flow methods improve robustness?

Pros for acceptance
- Conceptually new and impactful decomposition of video recognition into explicit spatial/temporal ConvNets.
- Strong empirical gains with careful ablations; competitive with SOTA shallow methods, clearly better than prior deep approaches.
- Practical multi-task strategy for data scarcity; reproducible implementation details.

Cons for acceptance
- Not end-to-end; reliance on heavy precomputed flow.
- Limited large-scale validation; fusion beyond late score combination underexplored.

Recommendation
Accept. The paper introduces a significant and influential concept with solid evidence. Despite incomplete end-to-end integration and some engineering trade-offs, the novelty and clarity outweigh the limitations.

Confidence
High.
