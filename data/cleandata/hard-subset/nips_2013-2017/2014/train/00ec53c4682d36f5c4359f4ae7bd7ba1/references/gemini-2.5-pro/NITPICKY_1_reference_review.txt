**Summary**
This paper proposes a two-stream deep convolutional network architecture for action recognition in videos. The core idea is to decouple the learning of spatial appearance features and temporal motion features into two separate streams. The "spatial stream" is a standard ConvNet that operates on individual RGB video frames to recognize actions from static cues. The "temporal stream" is a ConvNet that operates on stacked dense optical flow fields computed between consecutive frames, explicitly learning from motion. The outputs of these two streams are combined via late fusion (averaging or SVM on softmax scores). The authors evaluate their approach on the UCF-101 and HMDB-51 datasets, demonstrating that the two streams are complementary and that their combined model significantly outperforms previous deep learning approaches and is competitive with state-of-the-art hand-crafted feature-based methods.

**Strengths**
1.  **Novel and Effective Architecture:** The central idea of separating spatial and temporal processing is conceptually simple, well-motivated by the nature of video data (and loosely by neuroscience), and proven to be highly effective. It directly addresses a key weakness of prior deep learning models (e.g., Karpathy et al. [14]) that struggled to effectively learn motion features from raw pixel data.
2.  **Strong Empirical Results:** The paper achieves state-of-the-art or near state-of-the-art results on two challenging benchmarks. The performance leap over previous deep models is substantial, establishing this as a new baseline for deep learning-based action recognition.
3.  **Thorough Ablation Studies:** The authors provide a comprehensive set of experiments that meticulously validate their design choices. The tables clearly show the benefits of ImageNet pre-training for the spatial stream, the effectiveness of stacking multiple optical flow fields, the utility of mean flow subtraction, and the gains from multi-task learning. This experimental rigor adds significant value to the paper.

**Weaknesses**
Despite the promising results and a strong core idea, the paper is undermined by a significant number of minor flaws, technical omissions, and presentational issues that, in aggregate, call into question its readiness for publication at a premier venue like NIPS.

1.  **Lack of Methodological Rigor and Justification:**
    *   **Optical Flow Computation and Pre-processing:** The choice of the optical flow method [2] is justified only by its popularity. There is no discussion of how sensitive the model's performance is to the quality or type of optical flow. Furthermore, the decision to compress the flow fields using JPEG is a significant implementation detail with potential for introducing artifacts. The impact of this lossy compression is not measured or discussed. A proper evaluation would require a comparison against uncompressed flow or other compression schemes.
    *   **Mean Flow Subtraction:** This is presented as a "simpler approach" to camera motion compensation. While pragmatic, it is a crude approximation. It does not distinguish between camera motion and dominant object motion. The paper fails to analyze cases where this heuristic might fail (e.g., a static camera with a large moving object) or compare it to more principled techniques for camera motion estimation cited in the paper itself (e.g., [26]).
    *   **Fusion Method:** The authors state that training a joint stack of layers was "not feasible... due to over-fitting" but provide no supporting evidence. This is a strong claim that requires empirical backing. Simply resorting to late fusion without exploring other, potentially more powerful, fusion strategies (e.g., mid-level feature concatenation with appropriate regularization) feels like an incomplete investigation.

2.  **Clarity and Presentation Issues:**
    *   **Formatting and Typos:** The paper contains several distracting formatting issues and typographical errors. The abstract contains "mult-task". The section numbering in the introduction ("Sect. 1.1... Sect. 2") is unconventional and reads poorly. The mathematical notation for indices, such as `u = [1;w]`, is non-standard.
    *   **Imprecise Language:** The text refers to "full6 or full7 layers" without specifying which architecture this terminology is from. Given that the paper adopts the "CNN-M-2048" architecture from [3], it should use precise layer names corresponding to that model for clarity and reproducibility.
    *   **Superficial Connections:** The link to the "two-streams hypothesis" [9] is mentioned in the introduction and then immediately dismissed ("we do not investigate this connection any further here"). This comes across as a superficial attempt to add gravitas without contributing substantive scientific insight.
    *   **Incomplete References:** The reference list is unacceptably sloppy. Reference [1] is incomplete. Reference [13] is just a URL. The formatting is inconsistent across entries (e.g., superfluous commas, missing conference details). This lack of attention to detail is concerning.

3.  **Originality in Context:**
    *   While the two-stream *combination* is novel for deep action recognition, the constituent parts are not. The spatial stream is a standard image classification pipeline. The temporal stream uses a standard ConvNet on a pre-processed input (optical flow). The novelty lies in the engineering and empirical validation of this specific combination rather than a fundamental algorithmic or theoretical contribution.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   The paper presents a simple, powerful, and highly influential idea that fundamentally shifted the direction of deep learning for action recognition.
*   The results are very strong and convincingly demonstrate the value of explicitly modeling motion via optical flow.
*   The ablation studies are thorough and provide valuable lessons for the community.

**Arguments for Rejection:**
*   The work suffers from a "death by a thousand cuts." The sheer number of minor flaws—typos, formatting errors, imprecise language, sloppy referencing, and unjustified methodological choices—collectively detract from the paper's scientific quality.
*   Key implementation details that could significantly affect performance (e.g., the impact of lossy optical flow compression) are not evaluated, harming reproducibility and trust in the results.
*   Justifications for important design choices (e.g., why late fusion was the only option, why a specific crude camera motion compensation was used) are weak or absent.

**Recommendation:** Reject

While the paper's core contribution is significant and has since proven to be influential, the manuscript in its current form does not meet the high standards for technical soundness, clarity, and polish expected at NIPS. The numerous minor flaws and lack of rigorous justification for key methodological details overshadow the strong results. I would encourage the authors to undertake a major revision to address the points listed above, particularly by providing a more thorough analysis of the optical flow pre-processing and a more robust justification for the fusion method, before resubmitting to a future conference.
