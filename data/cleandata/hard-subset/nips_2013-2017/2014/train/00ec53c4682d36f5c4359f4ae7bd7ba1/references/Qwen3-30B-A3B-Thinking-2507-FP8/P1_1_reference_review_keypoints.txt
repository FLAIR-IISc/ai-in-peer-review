- Proposes a novel two-stream ConvNet architecture that decouples spatial (RGB frames) and temporal (optical flow) processing for video action recognition.  
- Spatial stream uses ImageNet-pretrained ConvNet on single frames; temporal stream uses stacked dense optical flow to capture motion.  
- Streams are fused via late fusion (averaging or SVM) to improve performance.  
- Introduces multi-task learning to combine training data from multiple datasets (UCF-101, HMDB-51).  
- Explores temporal stream input configurations (optical flow stacking, trajectory stacking) and shows L=10 stacked flows and mean flow subtraction are important.  
- Builds on two-stream neuroscience hypothesis and prior deep/shallow video recognition work while positioning itself as a learnable generalization of shallow representations.  
- Experiments are thorough and technically sound, with comprehensive ablation studies and comparisons to deep and hand-crafted baselines.  
- Provides sufficient implementation details (network configs, training, optical flow) to aid reproducibility.  
- Paper is exceptionally well written, organized, and clearly explained with helpful figures and mathematical descriptions.  
- The use of explicit optical flow input is an original and effective departure from stacked-RGB approaches.  
- Achieves strong empirical results competitive with state-of-the-art hand-crafted methods and significantly better than prior deep methods.  
- Demonstrates that explicit motion representation via optical flow outperforms implicit motion learning from RGB frames.  
- Highlights potential for adoption in future video analysis and applicability of multi-task learning to other tasks.  
- Strength: clear, well-motivated architecture that decouples spatial and temporal information.  
- Strength: thorough ablation studies providing insights into key design choices.  
- Strength: effective multi-task learning to combine overlapping-class datasets.  
- Strength: strong empirical gains (≈20% over prior deep method) and competitiveness with hand-crafted methods.  
- Strength: good related-work comparison and clear explanations.  
- Strength: well-written with supportive figures.  
- Weakness: lacks analysis of computational efficiency, inference time, and memory requirements.  
- Weakness: no discussion or preliminary results for scaling to large datasets like Sports-1M.  
- Weakness: optical flow computation may be a bottleneck and scalability issue is not addressed.  
- Weakness: no comparisons to other (possibly more efficient/accurate) optical flow methods.  
- Weakness: does not propose concrete ways to integrate missing elements of state-of-the-art shallow representations (e.g., local pooling over spatio-temporal tubes).  
- Suggestion: add discussion of computational efficiency, real-time performance, and resource requirements.  
- Suggestion: compare their optical flow method with recent alternatives to justify choice.  
- Suggestion: expand discussion on integrating local feature pooling and other shallow-representation elements.  
- Suggestion: include a table comparing computational/training resource trade-offs with prior deep methods.  
- Suggestion: discuss scalability and practical challenges/solutions for training on Sports-1M.  
- Recommendation: strongly recommend acceptance — original, well-motivated, technically sound, and significant contribution.  
- Pro summary: novel two-stream design, thorough ablations, multi-task learning, strong empirical gains, clear comparisons, reproducible and well-written.  
- Con summary: limited computational/scalability analysis, missing optical flow comparisons, and incomplete integration with some shallow-representation components.
