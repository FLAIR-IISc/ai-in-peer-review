- Proposes a two-stream ConvNet: spatial stream on RGB (ImageNet pre-trained) and temporal stream on stacked dense optical flow.
- Evaluates temporal input variants: optical-flow stacking vs. trajectory stacking; uni- vs. bi-directional flow; mean-flow subtraction.
- Fuses streams at score level (average or linear SVM) and uses multi-task learning with shared backbone and separate softmax heads for UCF101/HMDB51.
- Positioned against prior RGB-stacking deep models and classical dense-trajectory + Fisher vector pipelines; argues explicit flow simplifies motion learning.
- Methodology is clear and sensible, with appropriate ablation studies and standard training/testing protocols.
- Temporal stream significantly outperforms RGB-only deep models; two-stream fusion is competitive with improved trajectories.
- Provides useful implementation details (data augmentation, learning schedules, GPU setup) aiding reproducibility.
- Relies on precomputed Brox optical flow, so not end-to-end and inherits flow algorithm biases; no sensitivity analysis to flow method/quality.
- Runtime and inference cost (flow computation + 25×10 crops per stream) are not quantitatively reported.
- Fusion limited to late score fusion; alternative integrated fusion approaches and stronger regularization are not explored.
- Limited qualitative/error analysis and limited discussion of robustness to camera motion beyond mean-flow subtraction.
- Paper is clearly written, well organized, and precisely specifies temporal input formulations and ablation narrative.
- Minor clarity suggestions: provide quantitative test-time compute estimates and clarify class-imbalance/label-overlap handling in multi-task setup.
- Originality: explicit optical-flow input to ConvNet is novel and impactful; temporal input designs are thoughtful and grounded in classical descriptors.
- Significance: decoupling spatial/temporal processing yields substantial gains and offers an influential, practical blueprint for action recognition.
- Strengths: principled architecture targeting motion, thorough ablations, solid experimental protocol, practical multi-task strategy, and implementation transparency.
- Weaknesses: not end-to-end, unquantified computational cost, simple late fusion, and limited robustness/failure-mode analysis.
- Key questions for authors: sensitivity to flow choice/parameters; end-to-end inference throughput including flow; attempts at learned fusion with stronger regularization; evidence of negative transfer in multi-task training and batch balancing.
- Arguments for acceptance: significant advance in deep video recognition with clear, reproducible gains and insightful ablations.
- Arguments against acceptance: dependence on precomputed flow, unclear runtime/practicality, missing sensitivity/failure analyses, and limited fusion exploration.
- Recommendation: Strong accept.
