Summary
This paper proposes a two-stream convolutional neural network architecture for video action recognition, with decoupled spatial and temporal streams fused at the score level. The spatial stream performs per-frame recognition using an image-classification ConvNet (pre-trained on ImageNet), while the temporal stream takes stacked dense optical flow fields as input to explicitly encode motion across multiple frames. The authors explore several design choices for the temporal input (optical flow stacking vs. trajectory stacking, uni- vs bi-directional flow, mean-flow subtraction) and mitigate limited video training data via multi-task learning across UCF101 and HMDB-51. Extensive experiments show that (i) temporal ConvNets trained on optical flow substantially outperform single-frame RGB nets trained on UCF101/HMDB; (ii) fusing spatial and temporal scores yields complementary gains; and (iii) the method is competitive with state-of-the-art hand-crafted pipelines based on improved dense trajectories (IDT) and Fisher vectors. The paper contrasts its approach with prior deep video models that use stacked RGB frames or hand-crafted HMAX-style pipelines and argues that explicit optical flow input simplifies learning motion-sensitive features.

Quality
- Soundness: The architecture is well-motivated and tested through careful ablations (number of stacked flows L, trajectory vs. location stacking, mean subtraction, bi-directional flow, fusion schemes). The multi-task learning setup is reasonable and improves generalization on small datasets. Claims are supported by empirical evidence on UCF101 and HMDB-51, with comparisons to relevant baselines (single-frame CNN, “slow fusion” RGB stacks, prior deep video models).
- Experimental rigor: Strong overall. Implementation details (training schedule, data augmentation, multi-GPU, test-time sampling) are clear. The analysis of design choices in the temporal stream is particularly informative.
- Limitations: (i) Dependence on precomputed optical flow (Brox et al.) introduces substantial computational and storage overhead not included in runtime comparisons; (ii) no analysis of sensitivity to flow quality (method, parameters, or compression artifacts); (iii) fusion is limited to late score-level fusion—no exploration of joint training or intermediate feature fusion due to overfitting; (iv) evaluation is restricted to two datasets and trimmed clips; (v) camera motion compensation is only via mean subtraction; (vi) statistical significance and variance across runs are not reported.

Clarity
The paper is generally well-written and organized. The relation to prior work is clear, and the mapping from optical flow inputs to what classical descriptors compute (HOF/MBH/kinematics) helps position the contribution. Architectural and training details are sufficient for reproduction. Minor clarity issues: figures are referenced but not all are shown here; a concise summary of computational cost (flow precomputation time and total inference time per video) would aid practitioners.

Originality
The core novelty is to decouple spatial and temporal streams and to drive the temporal stream with stacked optical flow fields. While two-pathway ideas exist (e.g., HMAX-based two-stream models) and many hand-crafted methods use flow, learning a deep temporal representation directly on dense flow with thorough ablations and combining it with a pre-trained spatial network is a substantive and timely contribution. The multi-task training on multiple action datasets is a practical and useful addition.

Significance
The results are strong for the time: the temporal stream markedly outperforms single-frame baselines and prior deep architectures trained on RGB stacks, and the fused two-stream model reaches performance competitive with the best hand-crafted IDT-based pipelines on UCF101/HMDB-51. Given the long-standing gap between deep and hand-crafted methods for video at the time, this is a meaningful advance likely to influence subsequent work on motion representations and network design for video.

Pros for acceptance
- Clear, well-motivated architecture with strong empirical gains from explicit motion input.
- Thorough ablation of temporal input designs and fusion strategies.
- Practical multi-task learning that demonstrably improves performance with limited data.
- Competitive performance with the then state of the art on two standard benchmarks.
- Good exposition and reproducible training details.

Cons for acceptance
- Heavy reliance on precomputed optical flow; lack of end-to-end training and unclear runtime/compute cost.
- Limited exploration of camera motion compensation and robustness to flow quality.
- Fusion limited to late score-level averaging/SVM; joint or intermediate feature fusion not explored due to overfitting.
- Evaluation scope limited to UCF101 and HMDB-51; no analysis on untrimmed videos or longer-range temporal dependencies.
- No comparison or combination with state-of-the-art IDT features to quantify complementarity.

Suggestions for improvement
- Quantify computational costs: report flow precomputation time, storage, and end-to-end inference throughput; compare to RGB-only models.
- Evaluate sensitivity to different optical flow algorithms and parameters, and the impact of the JPEG compression of flow.
- Incorporate stronger camera motion compensation (e.g., homography-based stabilization) and assess its effect.
- Explore deeper or modern backbones for the spatial stream and larger temporal receptive fields (e.g., increasing L, dilated temporal convolution).
- Investigate intermediate fusion and/or light joint-training strategies (e.g., shared feature adapters, modality dropout) to avoid overfitting.
- Analyze failure cases and class-wise gains to better understand complementarity with appearance cues.
- Consider combining with IDT or trajectory-aligned pooling modules to test whether learned temporal features can benefit from trajectory guidance.
- Extend evaluation to additional datasets or untrimmed settings; report result variance across seeds/splits.

Recommendation
Accept. This paper delivers a clear and impactful step toward effective deep video recognition by explicitly modeling motion with optical flow in a learnable temporal stream and demonstrating complementary gains from fusing spatial and temporal cues. Despite computational caveats and limited scope in fusion and datasets, the methodological clarity, thorough ablation, and strong empirical results make it a valuable contribution.
