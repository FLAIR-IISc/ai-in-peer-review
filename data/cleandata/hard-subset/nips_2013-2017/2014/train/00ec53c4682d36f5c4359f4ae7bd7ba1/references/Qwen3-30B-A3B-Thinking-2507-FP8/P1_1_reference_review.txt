

# Peer Review: "Two-Stream Convolutional Networks for Action Recognition in Videos"

## Summary

This paper proposes a two-stream convolutional network architecture for video action recognition, addressing a key limitation of prior deep learning approaches for video analysis. The authors argue that existing methods using stacked RGB frames as input (e.g., [14]) fail to effectively capture motion information, and instead propose decoupling spatial and temporal recognition into two separate streams. The spatial stream processes individual video frames using a ConvNet pretrained on ImageNet, while the temporal stream processes dense optical flow to capture motion. The streams are combined via late fusion (averaging or SVM) to achieve superior performance. The paper also introduces a multi-task learning framework to effectively combine training data from multiple video datasets (UCF-101 and HMDB-51) and explores different input configurations for the temporal stream (optical flow stacking, trajectory stacking, etc.).

This work builds upon the two-stream hypothesis from neuroscience [9] and extends prior deep learning approaches for video recognition [11, 14, 16], while also connecting to the rich literature on hand-crafted video features [17, 20, 26]. The authors clearly position their work as a generalization of previous shallow representations through a learnable convolutional framework, rather than a mere adaptation of existing architectures.

## Quality

The paper is technically sound with well-designed experiments that thoroughly evaluate the proposed architecture. The authors conduct comprehensive ablation studies on different temporal stream input configurations, demonstrating the importance of using multiple stacked optical flows (L=10) and mean flow subtraction. The multi-task learning approach is well-justified and properly evaluated, showing significant improvements over alternative data combination strategies. The experimental evaluation on standard benchmarks (UCF-101 and HMDB-51) is thorough, with results compared against both deep learning and hand-crafted state-of-the-art methods.

The authors are careful to acknowledge limitations, particularly regarding the missing elements of state-of-the-art shallow representations (such as local feature pooling over spatio-temporal tubes centered at trajectories). The paper provides sufficient implementation details for reproducibility, including network configurations, training procedures, and optical flow computation methods.

## Clarity

The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The two-stream architecture is clearly explained with helpful figures (Fig. 1 and Fig. 3), and the different input configurations for the temporal stream are well-described with mathematical formulations. The experimental evaluation is structured logically, with each section building upon the previous one. The writing is precise and accessible to researchers in the field, and the organization follows a standard scientific paper format.

## Originality

The paper presents a novel architecture for video action recognition that effectively decouples spatial and temporal processing. While the two-stream hypothesis [9] has been discussed in neuroscience, the authors' implementation as a deep learning architecture with ConvNets is original. The use of optical flow as explicit input for the temporal stream (rather than implicitly learning motion from RGB frames) is a significant departure from prior deep learning approaches [14] and provides a clear advantage in performance.

The paper clearly differentiates its approach from previous work:
- Unlike [14], which used stacked RGB frames and achieved poor results, the authors use optical flow to explicitly represent motion.
- Unlike [16], which used shallow HMAX models for both streams, the authors use deep ConvNets that can be pre-trained on ImageNet.
- The multi-task learning framework for combining datasets is a novel contribution to the video recognition literature.

## Significance

This work makes a significant contribution to the field of video action recognition. The two-stream architecture achieves competitive performance with state-of-the-art hand-crafted methods while using a unified deep learning framework. The results are particularly impressive given that the authors train on relatively small datasets (UCF-101 and HMDB-51) compared to the massive Sports-1M dataset used in [14]. The paper demonstrates that explicitly representing motion (via optical flow) is more effective than implicitly learning it from RGB frames, which is an important insight for future research.

The proposed architecture has clear potential for adoption in future video analysis systems, and the multi-task learning framework could be applied to other video recognition tasks. The work provides a compelling new direction for video recognition research that moves beyond the limitations of prior deep learning approaches.

## Strengths

1. Clear and well-motivated two-stream architecture that effectively decouples spatial and temporal information.
2. Thorough ablation studies on different temporal stream input configurations, providing valuable insights into what makes the architecture work.
3. Effective use of multi-task learning to combine datasets with overlapping classes, which is a significant practical contribution.
4. Strong empirical results showing significant improvements over prior deep learning methods (20% better than [14]) and competitiveness with state-of-the-art hand-crafted methods.
5. Good comparison with related work, clearly explaining how this paper differs from and improves upon prior research.
6. Well-written with clear explanations and helpful figures.

## Weaknesses

1. The paper doesn't provide a detailed analysis of computational efficiency or real-time performance, which is important for practical applications of video recognition systems.
2. While the authors mention they plan to train on Sports-1M in future work, they don't discuss the challenges of handling such a massive dataset (e.g., memory requirements, training time) or provide preliminary results.
3. The optical flow computation is a potential bottleneck, but the paper doesn't discuss how this affects the scalability of their approach or compare their optical flow method to more recent, efficient alternatives.
4. The paper doesn't compare with other optical flow-based approaches that might be more efficient or accurate (e.g., [30]).
5. The authors acknowledge that their architecture misses some elements of state-of-the-art shallow representations (local feature pooling over spatio-temporal tubes), but don't propose a concrete direction for integrating these improvements.

## Specific Suggestions for Improvement

1. Add a discussion of computational efficiency and real-time performance, including inference time and memory requirements, to help readers understand the practical applicability of the approach.

2. Include a brief comparison with recent optical flow methods (e.g., [30]) to demonstrate that their flow computation method is competitive with state-of-the-art.

3. Expand the discussion of limitations to include potential directions for addressing the missing elements of state-of-the-art shallow representations (e.g., how local feature pooling could be integrated into their architecture).

4. Consider adding a table comparing the computational requirements (training time, memory usage) of their approach with prior deep learning methods to help readers understand the trade-offs.

5. Provide a brief discussion of how their approach might scale to the Sports-1M dataset, including potential challenges and solutions.

## Recommendation

I strongly recommend acceptance of this paper. It makes a significant contribution to the field of video action recognition with a well-designed architecture, thorough evaluation, and clear comparison with prior work. The two-stream approach is original, well-motivated, and achieves state-of-the-art results on standard benchmarks. The paper is technically sound, well-written, and provides valuable insights into the effective use of deep learning for video recognition.

The weaknesses identified are minor and could be addressed in a camera-ready version, but do not detract from the paper's overall quality and contribution. The work has clear significance for both the academic community and potential practitioners in the field of video analysis.

## Summary of Arguments for Acceptance

**Pro:**
- Novel two-stream architecture that effectively decouples spatial and temporal processing
- Thorough ablation studies demonstrating the importance of key design choices
- Multi-task learning framework for combining datasets with overlapping classes
- Strong empirical results showing significant improvements over prior deep learning methods
- Clear comparison with state-of-the-art hand-crafted methods
- Well-written and well-organized paper with sufficient detail for reproduction

**Con:**
- Limited discussion of computational efficiency and real-time performance
- No comparison with recent optical flow methods
- Limited discussion of scalability to massive datasets like Sports-1M
- Missing elements of state-of-the-art shallow representations not addressed in depth

The pro arguments significantly outweigh the con arguments, and the paper makes a clear, valuable contribution to the field.
