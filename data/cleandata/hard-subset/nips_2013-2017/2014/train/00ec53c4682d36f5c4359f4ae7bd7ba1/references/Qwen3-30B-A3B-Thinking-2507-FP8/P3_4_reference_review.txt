

# Peer Review: "Two-Stream Convolutional Networks for Action Recognition in Videos"

This paper presents a compelling two-stream convolutional network architecture for video action recognition, addressing a significant limitation in prior deep learning approaches to video analysis. The authors propose separating spatial (appearance) and temporal (motion) processing into two distinct streams, with the spatial stream leveraging pre-training on ImageNet and the temporal stream processing dense optical flow. The work makes several key contributions: (1) demonstrating that explicit motion representation via optical flow significantly outperforms end-to-end spatio-temporal feature learning; (2) introducing multi-task learning to effectively combine limited video datasets; and (3) establishing a strong baseline for deep learning approaches to video action recognition.

**Strengths**

The paper's most significant strength is its clear insight that decoupling spatial and temporal processing is more effective than attempting to learn both from raw video frames. The authors provide compelling evidence through careful ablation studies showing that optical flow-based temporal streams (with 10-frame stacking) outperform single-frame spatial streams by 20% and outperform the previous best deep learning approach [14] by 25%. Their multi-task learning framework for combining UCF-101 and HMDB-51 is particularly elegant, allowing them to boost HMDB-51 performance by 3.5% over single-dataset training.

The experimental design is robust, with thorough evaluation across multiple ablation studies (optical flow stacking, trajectory vs. optical flow stacking, bi-directional flow), and clear comparison to both shallow hand-crafted features and prior deep learning work. The results on UCF-101 (81.5%) and HMDB-51 (57.4%) are competitive with the best shallow methods [20, 21, 26] while demonstrating a more principled deep learning approach.

The paper also makes an important contribution to understanding why prior deep learning approaches for video failed [14]. The authors correctly identify that end-to-end learning of spatio-temporal features is challenging, and that explicit motion representation via optical flow provides a more effective input for ConvNets.

**Weaknesses and Limitations**

While the paper makes significant progress, several limitations should be acknowledged. First, the reliance on optical flow computation as a preprocessing step is a major practical limitation. The authors mention that flow computation for UCF-101 required 1.5TB of storage (reduced to 27GB via compression), which is prohibitive for many applications. The paper doesn't explore whether their approach could be integrated with more efficient motion representations or end-to-end motion learning.

Second, the paper doesn't fully address the camera motion compensation issue, which has been a critical component of state-of-the-art shallow methods [10, 26]. While they mention mean flow subtraction, this is a simple heuristic that doesn't match the sophisticated global motion compensation in leading shallow approaches.

Third, the paper's comparison to the most recent shallow methods [26] could be more comprehensive. The authors claim their approach is "competitive" with state-of-the-art shallow representations, but don't provide a direct comparison to the very recent 70.4% accuracy on UCF-101 reported in [26], which would strengthen their claim.

Finally, the paper doesn't explore how their two-stream architecture could be integrated with more recent deep learning approaches for video, such as 3D convolutional networks [25, 27] or two-stream architectures with more sophisticated fusion mechanisms [28]. This limits the potential for future work to build on their framework.

**Overall Assessment**

This is a high-quality, well-executed paper that makes a meaningful contribution to video action recognition. The two-stream architecture is conceptually clear, technically sound, and experimentally validated. The work represents a significant step forward in applying deep learning to video analysis by recognizing that motion should be explicitly represented rather than implicitly learned.

The paper is well-written, clearly organized, and provides sufficient detail for reproducibility. The authors properly acknowledge limitations and position their work within the broader context of the field.

While the paper doesn't completely surpass the best shallow methods, it establishes a strong deep learning baseline that is both more interpretable and more scalable than prior deep learning approaches. The multi-task learning framework for dataset combination is particularly valuable for the video action recognition community.

**Recommendation**

I recommend acceptance. This paper makes a significant contribution to the field, with a clear insight that decoupling spatial and temporal processing is more effective than end-to-end learning for video action recognition. The experimental results are convincing, and the approach is both principled and practical. While the reliance on optical flow is a limitation, the authors acknowledge this and frame it as a direction for future work. The paper advances the state of the art in a demonstrable way and provides a foundation for future research in video understanding.

The paper addresses a difficult problem (video action recognition) in a better way than previous research, and the results are significant enough that other researchers are likely to build on this work. The authors have clearly distinguished their contribution from prior work, particularly by demonstrating the limitations of end-to-end spatio-temporal learning.

This paper represents a strong contribution to the field of video understanding and is well-suited for publication at NIPS.
