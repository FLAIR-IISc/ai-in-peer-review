Summary
The paper proposes a two-stream convolutional architecture for video action recognition that decouples appearance (spatial stream operating on RGB frames) from motion (temporal stream operating on stacked dense optical flow). The spatial net is pre-trained on ImageNet and fine-tuned or used as a fixed feature extractor; the temporal net is trained from scratch on video datasets using optical flow inputs with several configurations (flow stacking, trajectory stacking, bidirectional flow, mean-flow subtraction). Final predictions are fused by averaging or a linear SVM on L2-normalized softmax scores. A multi-task learning setup jointly trains the temporal net on UCF-101 and HMDB-51 to mitigate overfitting. Experiments show strong gains of the temporal stream over spatial-only, complementarity of the two streams via late fusion, and results competitive with state-of-the-art hand-crafted pipelines.

Relation to prior work
The approach contrasts with prior deep models that stack RGB frames and implicitly learn motion (e.g., Karpathy et al.), and with shallow hand-crafted pipelines using dense trajectories, HOF/MBH, and Fisher vectors. The temporal ConvNet is positioned as a learnable generalization of optical-flow-derived descriptors, with connections to HMAX-style two-stream models. The multi-task formulation follows standard practice in deep multitask learning.

Strengths
- Clear architectural decomposition matching established cognitive “two-streams” hypothesis; conceptually clean and practical.
- Solid empirical evidence that explicitly encoding motion via optical flow is more effective than raw RGB stacks under comparable training regimes.
- Useful ablations on temporal input design: number of stacked flows L, trajectory vs. spatial stacking, mean-flow subtraction, and bidirectional flow.
- Multi-task learning improves temporal stream on small datasets; sensible and broadly applicable.
- Implementation pragmatics (multi-GPU training, precomputed optical flow) are described, enabling performance at scale.

Weaknesses and concerns
Technical/methodological details and reproducibility:
- Optical flow preprocessing is central yet underspecified: parameter settings for [2], pyramid levels, regularization weights, and whether defaults were used are not provided. No sensitivity analysis to flow algorithm choice or parameters.
- Flow post-processing: rescaling to [0,255] and JPEG compression are nontrivial, potentially lossy choices. There is no ablation showing impact on accuracy or discussing per-frame vs. global rescaling, clipping, and quantization bias.
- Trajectory stacking implementation details are missing: interpolation when sampling at subpixel locations, handling points leaving the frame, and padding/termination policy.
- Bidirectional stacking near temporal boundaries is not described (how frames at start/end are handled; padding vs. reduced L).
- Multi-task learning sampling/weighting: unclear how mini-batches are balanced across datasets and losses; without this, the larger dataset could dominate. No analysis of task interference or catastrophic forgetting.
- SVM fusion lacks detail: which multiclass formulation, C regularization, how trained (train vs. validation split), and whether calibration or cross-validation was used to avoid overfitting to test data.
- Important training hyperparameters are omitted (weight decay, initialization, learning rate policy rationale per network, exact filter sizes/strides/paddings per layer, especially for temporal net input with 2L channels).
- No statistical significance or variance across splits for ablations (e.g., small gains like bidirectional vs. forward-only may be within variance).

Fairness and scope of comparisons:
- Comparison to “slow fusion” RGB models is informative but could be confounded by limited UCF-101 training size; stronger 3D or two-stream RGB architectures might perform differently. The paper acknowledges Sports-1M but does not demonstrate results at that scale.
- No combination with improved trajectories or MBH to establish whether deep streams complement the best hand-crafted pipelines (though authors note the state of the art for context).
- Runtime and resource costs are underreported: precomputing flow (0.06 s/pair) and 25×10 test-time crops per stream imply substantial inference cost; end-to-end throughput, FLOPs, and memory are not quantified nor compared to hand-crafted baselines.

Clarity/organization:
- Several references are incomplete or erroneous (e.g., “[12] D convolutional neural networks…” likely 3D CNNs; ref [14] “classication” typo; many entries lack years/pages or venues).
- Inconsistent notation and hyphenation (“bi-directional” vs. “bidirectional”; “mult-task”; “learnt/learned”; “spatio-temporal”).
- Minor but recurring formatting issues (footnote “1” bleeds into text; “full6/full7” are used without prior definition; “Sect. 1” vs. “Sect. 1.1” cross-referencing).
- Figures (e.g., Fig. 1–3) are essential to understand architecture but the text alone is sometimes insufficient without precise layer specs.

Claims vs. evidence:
- The claim that the temporal ConvNet generalizes HOF/MBH is plausible, but evidence is limited to qualitative first-layer filter visualizations (in supplementary) and lacks quantitative probes.
- The statement that mean flow subtraction “helps” is supported by a single split; no robustness analysis across splits.

Quality
Technically solid high-level design and sensible experimental plan. However, key implementation details and ablations are missing for full reproducibility and to support several design choices (flow preprocessing, multitask sampling, SVM fusion). Reporting of computational costs and statistical rigor could be improved.

Clarity
Generally readable and well-structured, but marred by numerous citation and formatting issues, inconsistent terminology, and occasional underspecified procedures.

Originality
Moderately novel at the time: explicit two-stream ConvNets with optical-flow inputs and systematic investigation of flow stacking configurations were not standard. Multi-task regularization is standard.

Significance
High practical impact in video recognition; method is likely to be adopted and extended. That said, extensive reliance on heavy optical flow preprocessing may limit end-to-end learning prospects.

Questions for the authors
- Provide exact optical flow parameters and ablate flow algorithm/parameters and JPEG compression effect on accuracy.
- Specify temporal boundary handling for bidirectional stacking, and interpolation/boundary policy for trajectory stacking.
- Detail multi-task batching and loss weighting; report per-task loss curves.
- Clarify SVM fusion training protocol and hyperparameters; ensure no test leakage.
- Report runtime (train/test) and memory for each stream and fusion; compare to improved trajectories.
- Provide full architecture specs (kernel sizes/strides/padding per layer) for the temporal net.

Typos/formatting
- “mult-task” -> “multi-task”; “classication” -> “classification”; “[12] D convolutional…” -> “3D convolutional…”.
- Inconsistent “bi-directional”/“bidirectional”, “spatio-temporal”, “learnt/learned”.
- References missing years/venues/pages; fix footnote formatting and undefined “full6/full7”.

Overall recommendation
Borderline reject. Despite a strong and useful idea with promising results, the accumulation of underspecified methodological details, incomplete reporting, and numerous formatting/citation issues hampers reproducibility and undermines confidence. With a thorough revision addressing the above points, this could become a strong submission. Confidence: high.
