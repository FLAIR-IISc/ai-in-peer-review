This paper presents a two-stream convolutional network for action recognition in videos. The core idea is to decouple spatial and temporal information into separate convolutional networks, trained on still frames and optical flow respectively, and then fuse the results. The authors leverage pre-training on ImageNet for the spatial stream and explore different input configurations for the temporal stream, including stacking optical flow and trajectories. The paper is well-written and addresses a relevant problem in video understanding.

**Strengths:**

*   **Clear and well-motivated approach:** The two-stream architecture is a logical extension of the understanding of human visual processing and provides a clean way to address the challenges of spatio-temporal feature learning.
*   **Strong baseline and improvements:** The authors significantly outperform previous deep learning approaches on action recognition ([14]) and achieve competitive results with state-of-the-art hand-crafted features ([20, 21, 26]).
*   **Thorough experimentation:** The paper includes extensive experiments exploring different configurations for both streams, including the impact of pre-training, different optical flow stacking methods, and fusion strategies.
*   **Multi-task learning:** The introduction of multi-task learning to leverage data from multiple datasets is a valuable contribution, particularly given the limited size of available action recognition datasets.
*   **Detailed implementation details:** The inclusion of detailed implementation details, including network configuration, training procedure, and optical flow computation, enhances reproducibility.
*   **Good connection to related work:** The paper provides a comprehensive review of existing literature in action recognition, both shallow and deep approaches.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Limited novelty in the core architecture:** While the combination of two-stream CNNs and optical flow is effective, the fundamental architecture is relatively straightforward and builds directly upon existing ideas in computer vision (e.g., CNNs for image classification, optical flow for motion estimation). The primary contribution lies in the thorough exploration and successful application of these techniques to the specific problem of action recognition.
*   **Optical flow computation as a bottleneck:** While the authors pre-compute optical flow, this remains a significant computational cost. The paper could benefit from a discussion of strategies to mitigate this bottleneck, such as exploring more efficient optical flow algorithms or learning optical flow directly within the network (e.g., using differentiable rendering).
*   **Limited analysis of learned features:** The paper mentions visualizing learned convolutional filters but does not provide a detailed analysis of what features the temporal ConvNet learns. Understanding these features could provide further insights into the model's behavior and potential areas for improvement.
*   **Dependence on hand-crafted optical flow:** The reliance on pre-computed optical flow limits the end-to-end learnability of the system.  Exploring methods for jointly learning the optical flow and action recognition would be a valuable extension.
*   **Comparison lacks modern approaches:** The comparison with state-of-the-art mostly relies on older methods ([20, 21, 26]). A comparison with more recent, transformer-based approaches would strengthen the evaluation.



**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a solid and well-executed study that significantly advances the state of the art in action recognition.
*   The authors provide a thorough empirical evaluation and demonstrate the effectiveness of their proposed architecture.
*   The introduction of multi-task learning is a valuable contribution that addresses the challenge of limited training data.
*   The detailed implementation details enhance reproducibility and facilitate future research.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The core architectural innovation is incremental rather than groundbreaking.
*   The reliance on pre-computed optical flow limits the end-to-end learnability of the system.
*   The comparison to recent approaches is lacking.

**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a strong contribution to the field of action recognition. While the core architecture is not radically novel, the authors demonstrate a significant improvement over previous methods through careful experimentation and a thoughtful combination of existing techniques. The multi-task learning approach is particularly valuable.  The weaknesses are not critical and do not detract significantly from the overall quality of the work.

**Recommendation:**

Accept with minor revisions. I recommend the authors address the following points:

*   **Expand the comparison:** Include a comparison to more recent, transformer-based action recognition models.
*   **Discuss optical flow alternatives:** Briefly discuss potential strategies for mitigating the computational bottleneck associated with optical flow computation or exploring learned optical flow.
*   **Feature Visualization/Analysis:** Include more detailed visualizations or analysis of the features learned by the temporal ConvNet.
*   **Clarify the impact of Bi-Directional flow**: Explain why bi-directional optical flow didn't improve performance in the fusion step, given its benefit in the temporal net alone.
