

# Review of "Two-Stream Convolutional Networks for Action Recognition in Videos"

This paper introduces a novel two-stream architecture for video action recognition that separates spatial and temporal processing streams, each implemented as a ConvNet. The spatial stream processes individual video frames (leveraging pre-training on ImageNet), while the temporal stream processes optical flow to capture motion information. This approach directly challenges the prevailing paradigm of using stacked frames as input to a single network, which previous work [14] found to be suboptimal for learning motion features. The authors position their work in relation to the two-stream hypothesis in neuroscience (ventral/dorsal visual pathways), providing a compelling conceptual framework for their approach.

The paper's main novelty lies in recognizing that explicit motion representation (via optical flow) rather than implicit motion learning from stacked frames is more effective for action recognition. This is a bold conceptual shift that addresses a fundamental limitation of prior deep learning approaches to video recognition. The authors demonstrate that their two-stream architecture significantly outperforms [14] and is competitive with state-of-the-art hand-crafted methods [20, 21, 26], despite training on relatively small datasets.

Key strengths of the paper include:
1. **Conceptual novelty**: The two-stream architecture is genuinely new to video recognition, not just a minor variation of existing approaches. The connection to neuroscience provides a strong theoretical foundation.
2. **Clear experimental validation**: The authors conduct thorough experiments on standard benchmarks (UCF101, HMDB-51), demonstrating that the two streams are complementary and that the temporal stream provides significant performance gains.
3. **Practical implementation**: The paper provides clear implementation details (including optical flow computation, multi-task learning for data efficiency) that would allow others to reproduce the results.
4. **Insightful analysis**: The comparison of different optical flow input configurations (stacking vs. trajectory, bi-directional flow, etc.) provides valuable insights into what makes the temporal stream effective.

The paper makes a compelling case that the motion information in videos is better captured when explicitly represented through optical flow rather than learned implicitly from stacked frames. This insight represents a significant conceptual advance over previous work. The authors' claim that their temporal stream "significantly outperforms" the spatial stream (Table 1b) is well-supported by their experiments.

The main limitation of the paper is that it still relies on pre-computed optical flow, which is not end-to-end differentiable. However, as a reviewer who prioritizes novel ideas over methodological perfection, I consider this a minor drawback given the significant conceptual contribution. The paper acknowledges this limitation and suggests future work to address it.

The paper compares well to related work, particularly [14] which the authors build upon. It clearly demonstrates how their approach differs from previous deep learning methods for video recognition. The authors also provide a thoughtful analysis of how their temporal ConvNet generalizes hand-crafted features (HOF, MBH, etc.), showing that their approach is a learnable generalization of previous methods.

In terms of significance, this paper identifies a fundamental limitation in previous deep learning approaches to video recognition and proposes a novel solution that significantly improves performance. It opens up new directions for video recognition research, particularly the idea of explicitly separating spatial and temporal processing. The paper's results are competitive with the state of the art, which is remarkable given that previous deep learning approaches [14] were substantially worse than hand-crafted methods.

While the paper is well-written and technically sound, it could have provided more detailed analysis of why the two-stream architecture works so well. The authors briefly mention the neuroscience connection but don't explore it further. A deeper analysis of why this particular architecture is effective (beyond the evidence provided by the experiments) would have strengthened the paper.

Overall, this paper presents a bold, novel approach to video action recognition that significantly advances the field. The two-stream architecture is a clear conceptual contribution that addresses a fundamental limitation of previous deep learning approaches. The experimental results are thorough and convincing, demonstrating that the approach is not only conceptually sound but also practically effective.

**Recommendation: Accept**

The paper makes a significant contribution to the field by introducing a novel two-stream architecture for video recognition that outperforms previous deep learning approaches and is competitive with state-of-the-art hand-crafted methods. As a reviewer who prioritizes novelty and bold approaches, I find this paper to be a strong candidate for acceptance at NIPS. The conceptual insight is valuable, the experimental validation is thorough, and the paper opens up promising new directions for future research in video recognition. The minor limitations (like the reliance on pre-computed optical flow) are acceptable given the paper's significant conceptual contribution.
