Summary
The paper proposes a two-stream ConvNet architecture for action recognition that decouples spatial appearance (single RGB frames) from temporal motion (stacked dense optical flow). The spatial stream is pre-trained on ImageNet and fine-tuned on target datasets; the temporal stream operates on volumes formed by stacking optical flow fields across L frames, with variants including trajectory stacking, bi-directional flow, and mean flow subtraction. The two streams are fused at the score level (average or linear SVM). To mitigate small video datasets, the authors employ multi-task learning to jointly train on UCF-101 and HMDB-51 with two separate softmax heads. Experiments on UCF-101 and HMDB-51 show that: (i) motion-based temporal ConvNets substantially outperform spatial-only networks; (ii) stacking multiple flows (L≈10) and mean subtraction help; (iii) two-stream fusion improves over either stream alone; (iv) results are competitive with trajectory-based hand-crafted pipelines though not consistently superior.

Relation to prior work: The approach contrasts with prior deep models that feed stacks of RGB frames and implicitly learn motion-sensitive filters (e.g., Ji et al.; Karpathy et al.), and with HMAX-style two-stream architectures (Jhuang et al.). It is positioned as a learnable generalization of hand-crafted motion features (HOF/MBH/kinematics) by feeding explicit optical flow to a ConvNet.

Strengths
- Methodologically sound decomposition into appearance and motion; leverages established convnet practice and established optical flow.
- Thorough ablations on temporal input design (number of stacked flows, trajectory vs. fixed-location stacking, bi-directional flow, mean subtraction) and fusion strategies.
- Sensible use of ImageNet pretraining for the spatial stream and multi-task learning to combat overfitting on small video datasets.
- Clear, reproducible training/testing protocols; strong baselines (single-frame, slow fusion RGB stacks) and comparison to trajectory-based SOTA.
- Implementation details (multi-GPU, data compression) are practical and useful to the community.

Weaknesses
- Limited originality: two streams and late fusion are conceptually straightforward, and multi-task learning is standard. The primary novelty is feeding stacked flow to a CNN and a set of practical design choices.
- Heavy reliance on precomputed optical flow with non-learned priors; the approach is not end-to-end and delegates a key component to an external algorithm. No analysis of sensitivity to the choice/quality of flow (Brox vs. other methods, parameters).
- Computational cost is high (flow precomputation, 25 frames × 10 crops × two nets), with no discussion of runtime/throughput or memory for training/inference, limiting practical applicability.
- Despite strong performance, the method is only competitive with, not clearly superior to, top hand-crafted pipelines (improved dense trajectories + Fisher/stacked Fisher), so the claimed “generalization” over hand-crafted features is not firmly established empirically.
- The claim that temporal ConvNets generalize HOF/MBH/kinematics is largely qualitative; no quantitative analysis linking learned filters to these features beyond a brief mention of visualizations in supplementary material.
- Dataset scope is narrow (UCF-101, HMDB-51); no large-scale evaluation (e.g., Sports-1M) or cross-dataset generalization. Statistical significance/per-class analyses are absent.
- Trajectory stacking underperforms fixed-location stacking; the paper suggests trajectory-aware pooling as future work rather than delivering it.

Quality
The paper is technically sound, with careful experiments and useful ablations. However, several important analyses are missing: robustness to flow algorithm/parameters, computational cost comparisons, and error analyses. Claims about generalization over hand-crafted features are suggestive rather than proven.

Clarity
Generally well written and organized. Implementation and training details are adequate for reproduction. Some aspects could be more explicit: exact flow parameters, compression ranges and their effect, and precise SVM fusion setup (regularization, cross-validation).

Originality
Moderate. The two-stream idea and multi-task learning are not novel per se; the main contribution is a pragmatic, well-executed instantiation using stacked optical flow with ConvNets and late fusion.

Significance
Moderate to high for the action recognition community. The paper demonstrates that explicit motion via optical flow is crucial and that a two-stream design can be competitive with established hand-crafted pipelines, providing a strong baseline. Broader impact is constrained by compute cost and lack of end-to-end learning.

Questions for the authors
- How sensitive is performance to the optical flow algorithm and its parameters? Have you tried DeepFlow or TV-L1, and can you report a comparison?
- What is the total training and inference time per video (including flow) and memory footprint? Any strategies to reduce test-time compute (e.g., fewer crops/frames) with minimal accuracy loss?
- Did you attempt joint training with a learned fusion layer beyond SVM-on-scores while mitigating overfitting (e.g., regularization, dropout, freezing lower layers)?
- How does performance scale with L beyond 10, and what is the optimal temporal coverage in seconds?
- Can you provide per-class results and confusion matrices, particularly where spatial vs. temporal streams dominate?

Pros
- Solid empirical study with meaningful ablations.
- Clear improvement over single-frame and RGB-stack ConvNets.
- Practical multi-task learning to leverage limited data.
- Competitive with strong hand-crafted baselines.

Cons
- Not end-to-end; depends on external optical flow and heavy precomputation.
- Limited novelty; late fusion and multi-task are standard.
- Missing robustness/runtime analyses; restricted dataset scope.
- Does not decisively surpass best trajectory-based methods.

Recommendation
Weak accept. The paper is careful, well-executed, and likely to be influential as a strong baseline, despite limited conceptual novelty and dependence on precomputed flow. Providing additional analyses on flow sensitivity and computational trade-offs would strengthen the contribution.

Confidence
High.
