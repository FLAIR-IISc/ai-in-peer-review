**Summary of the Paper**

This paper proposes a two-stream deep convolutional network architecture for action recognition in videos. The central idea is to decouple the learning of spatial features from appearance and temporal features from motion. The architecture consists of two separate ConvNets: a "spatial stream" that operates on individual, static RGB video frames, and a "temporal stream" that operates on stacked dense optical flow fields computed between consecutive frames. The spatial stream can be pre-trained on large image classification datasets like ImageNet. The temporal stream is trained to explicitly learn motion patterns. The outputs from the two streams are combined at the end using a late fusion strategy (averaging or an SVM). The authors conduct a series of ablation studies on the UCF-101 dataset to evaluate different configurations for the temporal stream input, such as the number of stacked flows, trajectory-based stacking, and mean flow subtraction. They also introduce a multi-task learning framework to mitigate overfitting on smaller video datasets by jointly training on UCF-101 and HMDB-51. The results demonstrate that the two streams are complementary and their fusion achieves performance competitive with state-of-the-art methods based on hand-crafted features, while significantly outperforming prior deep learning approaches.

**Strengths**

1.  **Novel and Intuitive Architecture:** The core concept of separating spatial and temporal processing is well-motivated by both computer vision principles and the two-streams hypothesis in neuroscience. It is an elegant and simple solution to the challenge of learning motion from raw pixels, which previous works [14] had struggled with.
2.  **Effective Use of Pre-training:** Decoupling the streams allows the spatial network to directly leverage powerful models pre-trained on large-scale image datasets (ImageNet), a key factor that was less straightforward in architectures that fused spatial and temporal information at the input layer.
3.  **Strong Empirical Performance:** The proposed model sets a new state of the art for deep learning-based action recognition on standard benchmarks (UCF-101, HMDB-51) at the time of writing. It closes a significant performance gap that previously existed between deep models and methods based on hand-crafted features like improved dense trajectories (iDT).
4.  **Thorough Ablation Studies:** The paper provides a comprehensive analysis of various design choices for the temporal stream (Table 1b), offering valuable insights into what makes the motion representation effective (e.g., the importance of stacking multiple flow fields and mean subtraction).

**Weaknesses and Concerns**

Despite the significant contributions, the paper suffers from a considerable number of minor flaws, technical omissions, and a general lack of polish that collectively undermine its quality and reproducibility.

1.  **Clarity and Presentation:** The manuscript contains several formatting and organizational issues.
    *   The paper structure is unconventional. The introduction in Section 1 is immediately followed by Section 1.1 (Related Work), with no main body text for Section 1. This makes the flow of the introduction awkward.
    *   The references section is exceptionally sloppy. Citation [1] is incomplete, missing the publication venue and year. Citation [13] for Caffe is just a URL. Several other citations appear to be malformed, which suggests a lack of careful proofreading.
    *   There is a typo in the abstract ("mult-task"). The caption for Figure 3 contains redundant text ("input volume channels at point"). The use of MATLAB-style indexing in the equations (`u = [1;w]`) is non-standard for a formal publication.
    *   These presentation issues, while individually minor, collectively give the impression of a hastily prepared manuscript.

2.  **Critical Methodological Details are Underexplored:**
    *   **Optical Flow Compression:** The authors state they compress the optical flow fields using JPEG after rescaling them to a [0, 255] range to save storage space (1.5TB to 27GB). This is a highly questionable technical choice. JPEG is a lossy compression algorithm optimized for the psychovisual properties of natural images, not for dense motion vector fields. The introduction of compression artifacts could significantly distort the motion information that is the very foundation of the temporal stream. The paper provides no analysis whatsoever on how this lossy compression affects accuracy. Is the performance gain partly due to a denoising effect, or is performance degraded? Without this analysis, it is impossible to assess the true performance of the proposed method on raw optical flow. This is a major scientific omission.
    *   **Mean Flow Subtraction:** This is presented as a simple proxy for camera motion compensation. While the results show it is beneficial, it is a crude approximation. It conflates all motion within the frame into a single mean vector, which is insufficient for complex scenes with parallax, multiple moving objects, and non-dominant camera motion. The limitations of this approach are not discussed.

3.  **Insufficient Justification for Design Choices:**
    *   The network architecture is stated to be "CNN-M-2048" from [3]. There is no justification provided for why this specific architecture was chosen over the more standard AlexNet [15] or other configurations.
    *   The temporal net has one normalization layer removed to "reduce memory consumption". The performance impact of this architectural divergence between the two streams is not measured or discussed.
    *   The testing protocol samples a fixed number of 25 frames per video. This seems arbitrary. The performance sensitivity to this hyperparameter is not evaluated. For very short or very long videos, this fixed number could lead to suboptimal sampling.

4.  **Reproducibility Concerns:** The implementation is based on a modified version of the Caffe toolbox with multi-GPU support. Between the custom code, the unevaluated impact of JPEG compression, and sparse details on the multi-task learning implementation (e.g., mini-batch sampling strategy, loss weighting), reproducing the paper's results exactly would be very challenging.

**Evaluation Summary**

*   **Quality:** The core idea is sound, and the experimental results are strong. However, the quality is diminished by the unevaluated impact of a critical implementation detail (JPEG compression of optical flow) and a general lack of rigor in the presentation and justification of certain methodological choices.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is mostly readable, but suffers from poor organization in the introduction, multiple formatting errors, and typos. Key implementation details lack the clarity needed for easy reproduction.
*   **Originality:** The two-stream concept for deep-learning-based action recognition is highly original and represents a significant departure from prior work that attempted to learn spatio-temporal features from raw pixel stacks.
*   **Significance:** The work is highly significant. It demonstrated a viable and effective path for applying deep learning to action recognition, inspiring a vast amount of subsequent research in the field. The results effectively bridged the gap between learned and hand-crafted features.

**Recommendation:** Reject

While the paper presents a novel, significant, and highly influential idea, the execution of the manuscript is unfortunately not up to the standards of NIPS. The combination of sloppy presentation, particularly in the references, and the failure to analyze a critical and non-standard methodological choice (lossy JPEG compression of the input motion fields) constitutes a major flaw. A scientific paper must not only present strong results but also provide a clear and rigorous account of how they were obtained. In its current state, the paper leaves too many open questions about the details and robustness of its implementation. I would encourage the authors to thoroughly revise the manuscript, address the issues of clarity and formatting, and, most importantly, provide an ablation study on the effect of their optical flow compression scheme before resubmission. The core idea is strong, but the scientific reporting is weak.
