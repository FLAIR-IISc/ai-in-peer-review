## Peer Review for "Two-Stream Convolutional Networks for Action Recognition in Videos"

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a compelling and well-executed approach to action recognition in videos by leveraging a two-stream convolutional network architecture. The key idea – decoupling spatial and temporal information into separate streams, with the temporal stream operating on optical flow – is intuitive and demonstrably effective. The paper is clearly written, technically sound, and achieves strong results on standard benchmarks, outperforming previous deep learning approaches and competing favorably with state-of-the-art hand-crafted features. While the core ideas aren’t radically new (building on the two-stream hypothesis and recent advances in CNNs), the implementation and thorough evaluation represent a significant contribution. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions.

**1. Summary & Related Work:**

The paper addresses the challenge of video action recognition, motivated by the observation that existing deep learning approaches fall short of hand-crafted feature methods. The authors propose a two-stream CNN architecture: a spatial stream processing individual frames and a temporal stream processing dense optical flow. Both streams are CNNs, and their outputs are fused for final classification.  The authors also leverage pre-training on ImageNet for the spatial stream.

The related work section is comprehensive, covering both traditional hand-crafted feature methods (HOG, HOF, trajectories, MBH) and recent deep learning attempts.  It correctly identifies the limitations of previous CNN-based approaches that attempt to learn spatio-temporal features directly from stacked frames.  The connection to the two-stream hypothesis from visual neuroscience is appropriately mentioned as inspiration, though not deeply explored.  The references are current and relevant.

**2. Strengths:**

* **Strong Empirical Results:** The paper demonstrates substantial improvements over previous deep learning-based action recognition methods (particularly [14]) on UCF101 and HMDB51. The results are competitive with hand-crafted feature methods, a crucial achievement.
* **Effective Architecture:**  The separation of spatial and temporal streams is a well-motivated design choice.  Focusing the temporal stream on optical flow allows it to effectively capture motion information without the burden of learning it implicitly from raw pixels.
* **Pre-training:** Leveraging pre-training on ImageNet for the spatial stream is a sensible strategy given the limited size of the action recognition datasets.
* **Multi-task Learning:** The introduction of multi-task learning for temporal stream training is a valuable contribution, demonstrating how to effectively combine datasets and improve generalization.
* **Clear Writing & Organization:** The paper is well-structured and easy to follow. The explanations of the architecture and experimental setup are clear and concise.
* **Ablation Studies:**  The various ablation studies (different optical flow stacking configurations, mean subtraction, fusion methods) provide valuable insights into the effectiveness of different components of the proposed approach.

**3. Weaknesses:**

* **Optical Flow Reliance:** The performance heavily relies on the accuracy of the pre-computed optical flow. While the authors acknowledge this, the sensitivity of the system to optical flow errors is not thoroughly investigated. A discussion of robustness to noisy or inaccurate flow would be beneficial.
* **Limited Exploration of Fusion:** While SVM-based fusion outperforms averaging, the exploration of fusion methods feels somewhat limited. More sophisticated fusion techniques (e.g., attention mechanisms) might yield further improvements.
* **Computational Cost:**  Calculating and storing optical flow is computationally expensive. Although addressed partially with JPEG compression, a more detailed discussion of computational cost and scalability would be helpful.
* **Limited Theoretical Analysis:** The paper is largely empirical. While strong experimental results are presented, a more theoretical justification for the effectiveness of the two-stream architecture would strengthen the contribution.
* **Novelty:** While the combination of established techniques (CNNs, optical flow, two-stream idea) is effective, the degree of novelty is incremental rather than groundbreaking.



**4. Qualitative Evaluation:**

* **Quality (8/10):** The paper is technically sound, and claims are well-supported by experimental results. The experimental design is rigorous, with clear ablation studies.
* **Clarity (9/10):** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The explanations are clear and concise.
* **Originality (7/10):** The core ideas are not entirely novel, but the specific implementation and integration of these ideas within a deep learning framework represent a significant contribution.
* **Significance (8/10):** The results are important and demonstrate a substantial advance in action recognition. The proposed architecture is likely to be adopted by other researchers and practitioners.

**5. Arguments for and against acceptance:**

**For Acceptance:**

* Demonstrably strong performance on benchmark datasets.
* Effective and well-motivated architecture.
* Clear and well-written presentation.
* Thorough experimental evaluation with insightful ablation studies.
* Multi-task learning as a helpful technique.

**Against Acceptance:**

* Incremental novelty – builds heavily on existing techniques.
* Reliance on accurate optical flow, with limited discussion of robustness.
* Limited exploration of fusion methods.




**6. Recommendations:**

* **Optical Flow Robustness:** Include a brief discussion on the sensitivity of the approach to optical flow errors. Perhaps demonstrate performance with slightly corrupted or inaccurate flow.
* **Fusion Exploration:** Briefly explore more sophisticated fusion methods or justify the choice of SVM-based fusion more thoroughly.
* **Computational Cost Discussion:**  Provide more details on the computational cost of optical flow calculation and storage, and discuss potential strategies for reducing this cost.
* **Minor Clarifications:** In section 3.2, specify what “generalised” means when stating the temporal ConvNet generalises hand-crafted features.

**Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of video action recognition. I recommend acceptance with the requested minor revisions.**
