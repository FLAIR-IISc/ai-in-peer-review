**Summary of the Paper**

This paper proposes a two-stream deep convolutional network architecture for action recognition in videos. The core idea is to decouple the learning of spatial and temporal features. The "spatial stream" is a standard ConvNet that operates on individual, static video frames to learn appearance cues. This stream can be pre-trained on large image datasets like ImageNet. The "temporal stream" is a ConvNet trained on a stack of dense optical flow fields computed between consecutive frames, designed to explicitly capture motion. The predictions from these two independently trained networks are combined at a late stage using either averaging or an SVM on the softmax scores. The authors conduct a thorough evaluation on the UCF-101 and HMDB-51 datasets, performing extensive ablation studies on the temporal stream's input configuration, the benefits of pre-training, and a multi-task learning framework for combining datasets. The results demonstrate that the two streams are highly complementary and that the combined model achieves performance competitive with the state-of-the-art hand-crafted feature-based methods of the time, while significantly outperforming prior deep learning approaches.

**Strengths**

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The proposed two-stream architecture is a simple, elegant, and highly effective idea. By explicitly separating the learning of appearance and motion, the authors provide a powerful solution to the problem of learning from video data, which had proven challenging for previous end-to-end deep models that tried to learn motion implicitly from raw pixel data (e.g., Karpathy et al., 2014). This work established a dominant paradigm for deep learning-based action recognition for several years and has been highly influential.

2.  **Technical Quality:** The paper is underpinned by a strong set of experiments. The ablation studies are comprehensive and systematically validate the key design choices: the importance of pre-training the spatial stream, the benefit of stacking multiple optical flow fields (`L=10` vs. `L=1`), the utility of mean flow subtraction, and the complementarity of the two streams. The multi-task learning formulation is a principled approach to leveraging multiple small datasets.

3.  **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and easy to follow. The motivation for the two-stream approach is clearly articulated, and the architectural details are sufficiently described. The connection to and generalization of previous hand-crafted features like HOF and MBH is well-argued in Section 3.2.

**Weaknesses**

Despite the significant contributions, the paper suffers from a number of minor flaws and omissions that detract from its overall quality and reproducibility. A perfectionist review identifies the following concerns:

1.  **Undocumented Impact of Lossy Compression:** A critical implementation detail is the pre-computation and storage of optical flow fields. To manage storage (1.5TB -> 27GB), the flow fields were lossily compressed using JPEG. This is a major uncontrolled variable. The authors provide no analysis of how much information is lost in this compression/decompression step or what its impact is on the final accuracy. Without this, it is impossible to know if the reported performance is affected, or by how much. This detail significantly compromises the technical soundness and reproducibility of the work.

2.  **Oversimplified Methodological Choices:**
    *   The "mean flow subtraction" for camera motion compensation is an extremely coarse approximation compared to methods that estimate and warp based on global transformations (e.g., homographies). While acknowledged as a "simpler approach," its limitations are not discussed, and the comparison to more sophisticated compensation techniques [10, 26] is somewhat superficial.
    *   The trajectory stacking input (Eq. 2), inspired by the highly successful improved dense trajectories (iDT) method, performs worse than simple grid-based stacking. The paper offers no analysis or hypothesis for this counter-intuitive result, which is a missed opportunity for deeper insight.

3.  **Lack of Justification for Hyperparameters:** Several key hyperparameters appear arbitrary. The temporal stream input uses `L=10` stacked flows, but the choice is only weakly justified by a marginal improvement over `L=5`. The learning rate schedules are presented without justification for their specific step-down points. The network architecture itself (CNN-M-2048) is adopted from another work without a clear rationale for why it is more suitable than other contemporary models like AlexNet.

4.  **Clarity and Formatting Issues:** The manuscript contains several minor but distracting errors.
    *   There is a typo in the introduction: "mult-task learning".
    *   The mathematical notation for ranges, e.g., `u = [1;w]`, is unconventional (MATLAB-style) for a formal publication.
    *   The reference section is poorly formatted. Reference [1] is incomplete, and many entries contain superfluous trailing commas (e.g., ", , ,"). The formatting is inconsistent across entries.

5.  **Underwhelming Gains from Multi-task Learning:** The multi-task learning approach is presented as a key contribution for training on small datasets. However, when applied to the UCF-101 task, the reported accuracy gain is only 0.5% (81.0% to 81.5%). This improvement is marginal and could be due to random variance, which slightly weakens the claim of its utility for the larger of the two datasets.

6.  **Performance Claims:** The paper claims to be "competitive with" or "comparable to" the state-of-the-art hand-crafted models. While true, it is important to note that it does not decisively outperform the iDT + Fisher Vector method [26] on all metrics (e.g., on HMDB-51), which was arguably the main benchmark to beat.

**Recommendation**

While the core idea of this paper is novel, significant, and has proven to be highly influential, the execution and presentation contain a substantial number of minor flaws. The undocumented effect of lossy JPEG compression on the optical flow input is a particularly serious concern regarding the work's technical soundness. Combined with the numerous smaller issues—oversimplified methodological choices without sufficient analysis, arbitrary hyperparameters, and formatting errors—the manuscript does not currently meet the high standards expected for NIPS.

I am therefore leaning towards rejection. However, the contribution is strong enough that I would be willing to reconsider if the authors were to address the key issues, most importantly by providing a thorough analysis of their optical flow compression scheme and its impact on performance.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Presents a novel and highly influential two-stream architecture for action recognition.
*   Clearly demonstrates the value of explicitly modeling motion via optical flow in a deep learning framework.
*   Thorough empirical evaluation with extensive ablation studies.

**Arguments for Rejection:**
*   Major technical concern regarding the unanalyzed use of lossy JPEG compression for the primary input of the temporal stream.
*   Several methodological choices (e.g., mean flow subtraction) are overly simplistic and lack deep analysis.
*   Numerous minor issues related to hyperparameter justification, lack of analysis for some results, and poor formatting detract from the paper's quality.
