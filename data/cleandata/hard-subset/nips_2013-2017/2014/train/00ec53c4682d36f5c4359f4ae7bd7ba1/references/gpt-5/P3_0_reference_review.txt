Summary
The paper proposes a two-stream convolutional architecture for video action recognition that explicitly decouples appearance and motion. The spatial stream is a standard image ConvNet trained on individual RGB frames (pretrained on ImageNet, then fine-tuned or with a trained final layer), capturing object/scene cues. The temporal stream is a ConvNet trained on stacked dense optical flow fields across L consecutive frames; several input variants are explored, including flow stacking vs trajectory-aligned stacking, uni- vs bi-directional flow, and mean flow subtraction to suppress camera motion. The two streams are fused by late fusion (average or linear SVM over softmax scores). To mitigate limited video data, a multi-task learning setup shares convolutional features while using two separate classification heads for UCF-101 and HMDB-51. The approach is implemented in Caffe with multi-GPU training and evaluated on UCF-101 and HMDB-51, showing that temporal and spatial streams are complementary: the temporal stream substantially outperforms a frame-only ConvNet and prior deep baselines, and fusion is competitive with the best hand-crafted pipelines (improved dense trajectories with Fisher vectors).

Relation to prior work
This study builds on two lines: (i) deep learning for video, where prior attempts either used shallow HMAX-like models or applied ConvNets directly to stacks of RGB frames (e.g., Karpathy et al.), and (ii) hand-crafted motion-centric pipelines (HOF/MBH on dense trajectories with Fisher encoding), which remain very strong. The key novelty here is to use a ConvNet directly on stacked optical flow fields, explicitly injecting motion into the learning rather than relying on the network to discover motion from RGB stacks. The architecture echoes the ventral/dorsal two-stream hypothesis, but here both are learned ConvNets with late fusion. The paper also argues that the temporal ConvNet generalizes classic descriptors (HOF/MBH, kinematic features) as special cases of first-layer filters and simple non-linearities.

Quality
Methodologically, the paper is sound: the input formulations are well specified, the training/test protocols are clear, and ablations are thorough. The experiments convincingly support core claims: stacking multiple flow fields (L≈10) is crucial; mean flow subtraction helps; bidirectional and trajectory stacking confer limited gains; temporal ConvNets outperform RGB-only baselines; and late fusion improves further. The multi-task learning strategy is simple but effective for leveraging both datasets without manual class filtering. Implementation details (data augmentation, learning rates, GPU setup, optical flow preprocessing) are sufficient for reproduction. One limitation is that the approach depends on an external optical flow algorithm; end-to-end training is not attempted, so the full system is not jointly optimized. Some baselines and analyses could be expanded (see Weaknesses).

Clarity
The paper is clearly written and well organized, with a good overview of related work, precise definitions of the flow-based inputs, and explicit training/testing procedures. The rationale for design choices is articulated and supported by ablations. The connection to hand-crafted features is helpful. Minor clarifications would help (e.g., exact runtime/storage at test time, sensitivity to optical flow quality), but overall it is reproducible.

Originality
The two-stream design with an explicit optical-flow ConvNet is a novel and compelling combination that differs meaningfully from prior deep video models relying on RGB stacks or shallow hand-crafted pipelines. The multi-task setup is standard but suitably applied to small video datasets. The architectural choice to treat motion as input rather than an emergent feature is the central contribution.

Significance
This work addresses an important problem and closes much of the gap between deep and hand-crafted methods on UCF-101/HMDB-51. It provides a practical, high-performing recipe likely to be adopted and extended. The explicit motion modeling insight is likely to influence subsequent architectures and hybrid systems.

Strengths
- Clear, well-motivated decomposition into spatial and temporal streams with strong empirical support.
- Key insight of using stacked optical flow as ConvNet input; solid ablations on L, mean subtraction, trajectory vs location sampling, and bi-directionality.
- Competitive performance with state-of-the-art hand-crafted pipelines; large gains over prior deep baselines.
- Practical multi-task training to exploit multiple datasets; thorough implementation details enabling reproduction.
- Sensible, lightweight late fusion that avoids overfitting seen with deeper joint fusion.

Weaknesses and areas for improvement
- Reliance on external optical flow is computationally and storage intensive (precomputing, JPEG-compressing flow); no end-to-end training of motion estimation and recognition.
- Temporal modeling remains relatively short-range (L=10) and local; no explicit long-term temporal aggregation beyond frame sampling and averaging scores.
- Camera motion handling is rudimentary (mean subtraction) compared to homography-based stabilization used in improved trajectories; no quantitative study of more principled compensation.
- Limited sensitivity analysis to the choice of flow algorithm/parameters; results are tied to one method (Brox).
- Fusion is only at the score level; earlier fusion or learned joint representations are not explored due to overfitting—could regularization or additional data mitigate this?
- Evaluation focuses on UCF-101/HMDB-51; no experiments on larger-scale Sports-1M beyond citing prior work, and no cross-dataset generalization tests.

Questions for the authors
- How sensitive is performance to the optical flow method and its parameters? Have you tried faster flow (e.g., TV-L1) and measured accuracy vs speed?
- What is the end-to-end throughput at test time (frames per second) including flow computation? Can the method run in near real-time with approximate flow?
- Did you evaluate alternative flow encodings (e.g., angle/magnitude normalization or clipping) and different normalizations than simple mean subtraction?
- How far can L be increased before gains saturate or overfitting/memory dominate? Any benefit from temporal pyramids or temporal convolutions/pooling?
- Could simple camera motion stabilization (e.g., per-frame homography) yield additional gains for the temporal stream?

Pros for acceptance
- Strong empirical improvements over prior deep baselines and competitive with SOTA shallow pipelines.
- Conceptually clean, well-argued architecture with practical training recipe and ablations.
- High relevance to the community; likely to spur follow-on work.

Cons for acceptance
- Non–end-to-end pipeline with heavy dependency on precomputed optical flow and associated compute/storage costs.
- Limited exploration of longer-range temporal modeling and camera motion compensation.
- Evaluation scope limited to two medium-scale datasets.

Overall recommendation
Accept. The paper presents a clear and impactful architectural advance for video action recognition, with solid experimental support and practical implications. Confidence: high.
