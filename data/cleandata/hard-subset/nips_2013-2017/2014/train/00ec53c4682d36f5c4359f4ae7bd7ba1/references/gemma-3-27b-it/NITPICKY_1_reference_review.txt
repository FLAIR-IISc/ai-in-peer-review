This paper proposes a two-stream convolutional neural network architecture for action recognition in videos, decoupling spatial and temporal information processing. The spatial stream operates on individual frames, leveraging pre-training on ImageNet, while the temporal stream processes dense optical flow. The streams are fused via averaging or a linear SVM. While the core idea of explicitly separating spatial and temporal streams using CNNs is reasonable and builds upon established concepts (the two-streams hypothesis), the paper suffers from a plethora of minor, yet cumulatively significant, issues that detract from its overall quality.

**Strengths:**

*   **Clear Motivation:** The paper clearly articulates the motivation for decoupling spatial and temporal streams, linking it to the two-streams hypothesis in the visual cortex.
*   **Solid Baseline:** The comparison to the stacked frame approach of [14] provides a valuable baseline, demonstrating the benefit of explicitly modeling temporal information.
*   **Comprehensive Experimentation:** The authors explore multiple configurations for the temporal stream, including different stacking methods and the impact of mean subtraction.
*   **Multi-Task Learning:** The use of multi-task learning to improve generalization performance is a sensible and potentially impactful addition.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Minor Technical Inconsistencies:** Equation (1) describes the input volume construction. However, the explanation of indexing (u = [1;w], v = [1;h], k = [1;L]) is unusual and potentially confusing. Standard indexing notation would be more clear.
*   **Lack of Rigorous Ablation Study:** While various configurations are tested, the ablation study lacks statistical significance testing. Differences reported without p-values or confidence intervals are difficult to interpret. For example, the claim of a “marginal” difference between trajectory stacking and optical flow stacking needs quantification.
*   **Insufficient Detail in Implementation:** The implementation details section is surprisingly sparse. Specifics regarding the optimizer (beyond “momentum = 0.9”), learning rate schedule (beyond initial and final values), and batch size are missing. Reproducibility is hampered. The JPEG compression of optical flow is a highly unusual and potentially detrimental optimization; its impact is not investigated.
*   **Overclaiming in Related Work:** The paper asserts that the temporal ConvNet "generalises" hand-crafted features like HOF and MBH. While there's a connection (convolutional filters can *learn* to detect similar patterns), this is overstated. A more nuanced discussion is required. The claim that the method is a “learnable generalisation of HMAX” is also weak; HMAX is a fundamentally different architecture.
*   **Formatting and Typographical Errors:** Numerous minor formatting errors exist throughout the paper (e.g., "mult-task" in Section 4), and the writing style is sometimes awkward (e.g., “Testing is similar to the spatial ConvNet, and is described in detail in Sect. 5.”).
*   **Ambiguous Description of Bi-directional Flow:** The description of bi-directional optical flow lacks clarity. It is unclear whether the forward and backward flows are simply concatenated or processed in a more sophisticated way.
*   **Missing Error Bars in Tables:** The tables presenting results (1, 2, 3, and 4) lack error bars (standard deviations or confidence intervals), making it difficult to assess the statistical significance of the reported improvements.
*   **Inconsistent Language:** The paper states that the architecture is “competitive with the state of the art,” then acknowledges that it doesn’t incorporate all features of the *current* state-of-the-art.
*   **Insufficient Discussion of Limitations:** The paper does not adequately address the computational cost of computing dense optical flow, or the sensitivity of the approach to optical flow errors.

**Arguments for and against acceptance:**

**Pro:**

*   The core idea of decoupling spatial and temporal streams is sound.
*   The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed architecture.
*   The multi-task learning approach improves generalization.

**Con:**

*   The paper is riddled with minor technical flaws and inconsistencies.
*   The lack of rigorous statistical analysis weakens the claims.
*   The implementation details are insufficient for reproducibility.
*   The writing quality is subpar, with numerous formatting and typographical errors.
*   The overclaiming in the related work section is concerning.



**Overall Assessment:**

While the paper presents a reasonable and potentially valuable approach to action recognition, the numerous minor flaws and lack of rigor significantly detract from its quality. The cumulative effect of these issues casts doubt on the reliability and reproducibility of the results. I believe the paper, in its current form, does not meet the high standards expected for NIPS publication. **I recommend rejection.** The authors should address the identified issues through a thorough revision and resubmission. Specifically, they should: 1) improve the clarity and consistency of the technical descriptions; 2) perform a rigorous statistical analysis of the experimental results; 3) provide more detailed implementation details; 4) revise the related work section to avoid overclaiming; and 5) carefully proofread the manuscript to eliminate formatting and typographical errors.
