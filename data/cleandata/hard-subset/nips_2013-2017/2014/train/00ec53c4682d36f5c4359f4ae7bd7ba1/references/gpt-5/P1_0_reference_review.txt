Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper proposes a two-stream ConvNet architecture for action recognition in videos: a spatial stream that performs per-frame classification and a temporal stream that operates on stacked dense optical flow fields. The streams are trained separately and combined by late fusion (averaging or linear SVM). The spatial net benefits from ImageNet pretraining; the temporal net is trained from scratch on video datasets (UCF101, HMDB-51) and is regularized via heavy dropout and multi-task learning across datasets. The authors carefully study several temporal input designs: stacking L consecutive optical flow fields, trajectory-aligned stacking, bi-directional flow, and mean flow subtraction to mitigate camera motion. Across ablations, stacked flow with mean subtraction (L=10) performs best; temporal > spatial; and fusion provides substantial gains. Compared to prior deep approaches that learn from raw RGB stacks or 3D convolutions (e.g., Ji et al.; Karpathy et al.), the key novelty is to feed explicit motion (optical flow) to a ConvNet, inspired by hand-crafted pipelines (HOF/MBH, trajectories) and the two-stream hypothesis (ventral/dorsal). Results outperform prior deep models and are competitive with handcrafted improved dense trajectories (IDT).

Quality:
- Methodology is sound, with clearly defined architectures, training procedures, and ablations isolating the contributions of L, trajectory vs. spatial stacking, mean subtraction, and bi-directionality.
- Multi-task learning is a principled way to leverage small datasets; the formulation (two softmax heads, summed losses) is simple and effective.
- Experimental protocol follows community standards (UCF101/HMDB-51, 3 splits, standard cropping/flipping).
- Comparisons include relevant baselines (single-frame, slow-fusion RGB stacks) and state-of-the-art shallow pipelines; temporal-stream superiority is convincingly demonstrated.
- Some empirical aspects could use more depth: computational cost and throughput of the temporal stream (including optical flow precomputation), sensitivity to the choice of optical flow algorithm/parameters and JPEG compression, and statistical variability (error bars) across splits. The choice of loss weighting and sampling between datasets in multi-task training is not fully specified.

Clarity:
- The paper is well organized and readable. The temporal input configurations and their relations to classic features (HOF/MBH, trajectories, kinematic features) are clearly explained.
- Implementation details (data augmentation, learning rate schedules, multi-GPU training, test-time protocol) are helpful for reproduction.
- Minor places for clarification: exact optical flow parameter settings; how batches mix UCF/HMDB samples and whether losses are weighted; runtime and storage requirements; rationale for dropping the second LRN layer in the temporal net.

Originality:
- The two-stream decomposition itself is conceptually known from neuroscience and has appeared in shallow hand-crafted forms; the main novelty is a discriminatively trained ConvNet that ingests stacked optical flow as a first-class input, together with a thorough treatment of motion input design choices and multi-task training for data-starved video settings.
- While late fusion is simple and not new, demonstrating its effectiveness and the complementary nature of the streams is valuable. The multi-task setup is standard but appropriately adapted.

Significance:
- The work addresses a central challenge—how to inject motion into deep video models—providing a strong, simple baseline that substantially improves over contemporaneous deep RGB baselines and narrows the gap to IDT. The approach is likely to be adopted by practitioners and serves as a foundation for later research (e.g., end-to-end motion learning, longer-term modeling, trajectory-aware pooling).
- The ablation insights (mean flow subtraction, number of stacked flows, limited benefit of trajectory stacking and bidirectional flow under this architecture) are practically useful.

Strengths:
- Clear, well-motivated two-stream design with strong empirical evidence of complementarity.
- Careful ablations on temporal input design; practical regularization (dropout) and data strategies (ImageNet pretraining, multi-task learning).
- Solid improvements over prior deep models; competitive with strong handcrafted baselines.
- Reproducibility aided by detailed training/testing procedures and use of a public framework (Caffe).

Weaknesses and concerns:
- Reliance on precomputed optical flow limits end-to-end learning and introduces a significant computational/storage bottleneck; the paper provides limited analysis of speed, memory, and scalability.
- Fusion is done at the score level; attempts at learning joint fully connected fusion overfit—an intermediate solution (e.g., lightweight gating/attention) is not explored.
- Trajectory-centric pooling is not integrated, despite its known importance in IDT; the paper acknowledges this as future work.
- Limited evaluation of robustness to flow algorithm choice/compression; no analysis of sensitivity to JPEG quantization of flow.
- Temporal horizon is relatively short (L=10) and long-range dependencies are captured only via frame-level averaging; more principled temporal aggregation (e.g., temporal pooling, RNNs) is not investigated.
- Statistical reporting could be strengthened (e.g., confidence intervals, per-class breakdowns).

Suggestions and questions for the authors:
- Report runtime/throughput and storage for optical flow precomputation and inference, and compare end-to-end wall-clock performance with RGB-only models.
- Evaluate different optical flow methods/parameters and the effect of JPEG compression on accuracy; quantify mean flow subtraction vs. explicit camera motion compensation.
- Explore lightweight learned fusion (e.g., shallow gating on fc7 features) with stronger regularization or cross-stream normalization to mitigate overfitting.
- Incorporate trajectory-aware pooling within the ConvNet (tube-aligned pooling) to more fully close the gap to IDT.
- Investigate longer temporal aggregation (temporal pyramids or sequence models) and whether bidirectional flow helps when coupled with longer horizons.
- Clarify multi-task batching and loss weighting; report how much HMDB benefits when UCF is much larger.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Strong, simple idea that advances deep video recognition with clear empirical gains and practical insights.
- Thorough ablations and careful engineering lead to a widely useful baseline.
- Clear writing and reproducible methodology.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Not end-to-end; heavy reliance on optical flow precomputation without thorough computational analysis.
- Limited novelty in some components (late fusion, standard CNN backbone); some missing analyses (flow choice, robustness, timing).

Overall: A high-quality, impactful contribution that materially improves deep action recognition through a well-executed two-stream design. I recommend acceptance.
