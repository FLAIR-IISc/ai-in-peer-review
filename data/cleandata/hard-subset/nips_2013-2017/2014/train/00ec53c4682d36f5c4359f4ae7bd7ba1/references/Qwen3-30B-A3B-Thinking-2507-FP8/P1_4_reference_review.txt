

# Peer Review: "Two-Stream Convolutional Networks for Action Recognition in Videos"

## Summary
This paper proposes a two-stream convolutional network architecture for video action recognition, where one stream processes spatial information (individual video frames) and the other processes temporal information (optical flow between frames). The spatial stream leverages pre-training on ImageNet, while the temporal stream processes optical flow to capture motion. The authors demonstrate that this approach significantly outperforms previous deep learning methods for video action recognition and achieves competitive results with state-of-the-art shallow hand-crafted representations. The paper also introduces a multi-task learning framework to combine datasets (UCF-101 and HMDB-51) and explores various optical flow input configurations. The work builds on the two-stream hypothesis in neuroscience (ventral/dorsal streams) but focuses on practical implementation rather than biological plausibility.

## Strengths

1. **Technical Soundness and Experimental Rigor**: The paper presents thorough experiments on two standard benchmarks (UCF-101 and HMDB-51) with comprehensive ablation studies. The comparison to previous deep learning approaches (particularly [14]) is convincing, showing that the optical flow-based temporal stream significantly outperforms raw frame stacking. The multi-task learning approach for dataset combination is well-motivated and effective.

2. **Clear Motivation and Novelty**: The paper correctly identifies the limitation of previous deep learning approaches that tried to learn motion implicitly from stacked frames ([14]), and the insight that explicitly providing motion information via optical flow is more effective. This represents a meaningful refinement of prior work.

3. **Reproducibility**: The paper provides detailed implementation information including network architecture, training protocols, optical flow computation details, and even data storage optimizations (JPEG compression of flow fields). This level of detail is valuable for the community.

4. **Practical Impact**: The approach demonstrates that large-scale image pre-training (ImageNet) can be effectively leveraged for video recognition, addressing a key challenge in video action recognition where labeled video data is scarce.

## Weaknesses

1. **Insufficient Distinction from Prior Work**: The paper claims to significantly outperform [14], but fails to adequately explain why their approach works better. The paper states that "the network does not need to estimate motion implicitly," but doesn't provide theoretical or empirical evidence supporting this claim. The relationship to [14] needs clearer elaboration.

2. **Comparison to State-of-the-Art**: While the paper claims to be "competitive with the state of the art shallow representations," it doesn't sufficiently compare with the most recent state-of-the-art methods (e.g., [26] which is cited but not directly compared in the main results). The paper would be stronger with a more direct comparison to the absolute state-of-the-art.

3. **Limited Discussion of Limitations**: The paper acknowledges that their approach misses some elements of state-of-the-art shallow representations (like local feature pooling over spatio-temporal tubes), but doesn't explore how these could be incorporated into the two-stream framework. This is a significant limitation that should be addressed.

4. **Computational Considerations**: The paper doesn't discuss the computational cost of optical flow computation, which is a significant bottleneck (0.06s per frame pair). For real-world applications, this could be prohibitive, and the paper doesn't address this trade-off.

## Quality, Clarity, Originality, and Significance Assessment

**Quality**: The paper is technically sound with well-designed experiments. The claims are well-supported by results, and the authors acknowledge limitations. The only minor quality issue is the insufficient theoretical explanation for why optical flow input works better than raw frame stacking.

**Clarity**: The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with helpful figures (Fig. 1, 2, 3) that effectively illustrate the architecture. The writing is accessible to computer vision researchers, though some optical flow details could be expanded for readers unfamiliar with the technique.

**Originality**: The two-stream architecture is inspired by neuroscience, but the main novelty is in implementing both streams as deep convolutional networks with optical flow for the temporal stream. This represents a significant refinement over previous deep learning approaches for video recognition, though the paper could better distinguish its contribution from [14].

**Significance**: The results are significant as they demonstrate that deep learning can achieve competitive performance with hand-crafted features for video action recognition, while being simpler to implement and leveraging large-scale image pre-training. The multi-task learning approach is particularly valuable for dealing with small video datasets.

## Recommendation

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions. The paper makes a valuable contribution to the field of video action recognition by demonstrating a simple yet effective deep learning approach that outperforms previous deep learning methods and achieves competitive results with state-of-the-art shallow representations. The two-stream architecture with optical flow input represents a meaningful improvement over prior work.

## Specific Suggestions for Revision

1. Clarify the relationship between this work and [14], explicitly explaining why their approach (using optical flow) works better than the stacked frames approach in [14].

2. Provide a direct comparison with the most recent state-of-the-art shallow representation (e.g., [26]) in the main results table.

3. Discuss how the missing elements of state-of-the-art shallow representations (like spatio-temporal tube pooling) could potentially be incorporated into the two-stream framework.

4. Address the computational cost of optical flow computation and its implications for real-world applications.

5. Consider adding a brief discussion of how this approach might generalize to other video understanding tasks beyond action recognition.

The paper is well-written, technically sound, and makes a meaningful contribution to the field. With these minor revisions, it would be an excellent addition to NIPS proceedings.
