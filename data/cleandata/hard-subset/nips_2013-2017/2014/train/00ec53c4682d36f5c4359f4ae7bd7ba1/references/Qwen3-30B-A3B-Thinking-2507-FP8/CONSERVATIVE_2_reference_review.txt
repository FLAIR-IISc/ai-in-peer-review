

# Review of "Two-Stream Convolutional Networks for Action Recognition in Videos"

This paper proposes a two-stream convolutional network architecture for video action recognition, consisting of a spatial stream processing individual frames and a temporal stream processing optical flow. The authors claim this approach significantly outperforms previous deep learning methods for video action recognition, achieving competitive results with state-of-the-art shallow representations.

The paper's main contributions are: (1) a two-stream architecture that decouples spatial and temporal recognition, (2) a temporal stream that processes optical flow rather than stacked frames, (3) a multi-task learning framework to address small dataset sizes, and (4) thorough evaluation on UCF101 and HMDB-51 datasets. The authors demonstrate that their approach outperforms the stacked-frame ConvNet of [14] and is competitive with hand-crafted shallow representations.

**Strengths:** The paper is well-written and clearly organized. The experimental evaluation is comprehensive, with careful ablation studies of different optical flow configurations and fusion strategies. The implementation details are sufficiently thorough for reproducibility. The authors demonstrate a solid understanding of the video action recognition literature and provide appropriate comparisons to relevant prior work.

**Weaknesses:** Despite the thorough evaluation, the paper suffers from several critical weaknesses that undermine its significance as a NIPS contribution.

First, the claimed "significance" is overstated. The paper demonstrates competitive results with hand-crafted shallow representations, but does not actually surpass the state of the art. The authors acknowledge they are "competitive with the state of the art shallow representations [20, 21, 26]" but fail to demonstrate that their approach is superior. In fact, Table 4 shows their best result (82.4% on UCF101) is slightly below the 83.1% reported in [26] for trajectory-based hand-crafted features. This is a fundamental limitation that the authors do not adequately address.

Second, the novelty is questionable. The two-stream concept is inspired by neuroscience (the two-streams hypothesis [9]), but the authors do not claim any new theoretical contribution. The approach of using separate spatial and temporal streams with ConvNets is a natural extension of [16], which already used a similar two-stream HMAX architecture. The authors merely replace the hand-crafted HMAX with ConvNets, which is a reasonable but not particularly innovative adaptation.

Third, the paper fails to provide sufficient theoretical justification for why their approach works better than stacked-frame ConvNets. They reference [14] as finding stacked-frame networks perform poorly, but [14] itself attributes this to insufficient training data rather than a fundamental limitation of the approach. The authors do not provide evidence that optical flow is inherently a better representation than stacked frames for ConvNets, beyond the fact that their implementation happens to work better.

Fourth, the paper's claim that their temporal stream "generalizes hand-crafted features" is unsubstantiated. They show that their first-layer filters resemble hand-crafted features, but this is a weak argument for the superiority of their approach. The paper does not demonstrate that their learned features provide meaningful advantages over the hand-crafted features they claim to generalize.

Finally, the paper's contribution to the field is incremental rather than transformative. Video action recognition has been dominated by hand-crafted shallow representations for years, and this work shows that deep learning can be made to work competently with careful engineering, but it does not fundamentally change how we approach the problem. The authors themselves acknowledge that their approach is "competitive" with state-of-the-art, not superior.

**Overall assessment:** While the paper presents a well-executed study that demonstrates a viable approach to video action recognition, it fails to make a compelling case for significant novelty or advancement over existing methods. The results are incremental rather than groundbreaking, and the paper does not provide sufficient theoretical or empirical justification for why their specific architecture outperforms alternatives. The authors' claim that their method "significantly outperforms that of [14]" is misleading, as [14] was already known to be inferior to hand-crafted methods. The paper makes a modest contribution to the field but does not advance the state of the art in a demonstrable way.

I recommend rejection. While the paper is technically sound and well-written, it fails to meet NIPS's standards for significance and novelty. The contribution is incremental, the claimed advantages over state-of-the-art are not substantiated, and the paper does not provide sufficient evidence to justify the two-stream approach as a fundamental improvement over previous work. The field would benefit more from papers that either significantly outperform state-of-the-art or provide a fundamentally new perspective on the problem, neither of which this paper achieves.
