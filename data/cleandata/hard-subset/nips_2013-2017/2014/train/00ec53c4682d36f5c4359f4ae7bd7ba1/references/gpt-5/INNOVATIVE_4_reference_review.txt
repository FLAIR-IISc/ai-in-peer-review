Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes a two-stream deep architecture for video action recognition that explicitly decouples appearance (spatial stream) and motion (temporal stream). The spatial stream is a ConvNet applied to RGB frames and pre-trained on ImageNet; the temporal stream is a ConvNet applied to stacked dense optical flow fields computed between consecutive frames. The two streams are fused by late score fusion (averaging or a linear SVM). The authors explore several motion-input variants (flow stacking vs. trajectory stacking, uni vs. bi-directional flow, mean flow subtraction), and introduce a simple multi-task learning scheme to jointly train on HMDB-51 and UCF101 to mitigate overfitting in small datasets. Extensive experiments show that (i) temporal ConvNets significantly outperform single-frame spatial ConvNets; (ii) the two streams are complementary; and (iii) the approach is competitive with state-of-the-art hand-crafted methods (improved trajectories + Fisher variants) while clearly surpassing prior deep baselines that operated on stacks of RGB frames (e.g., Karpathy et al., 2014).

The work is well positioned against trajectory-based shallow pipelines (HOF/MBH, iDT) and prior deep video models (HMAX variants, 3D CNNs/RBMs/ISA, multi-frame RGB ConvNets). A useful conceptual link is drawn by showing how the temporal ConvNet can subsume handcrafted features (e.g., orientation histograms and flow gradients) via learned convolutional filters.

Strengths
- Novelty and conceptual clarity: The explicit two-stream design (appearance and motion) with optical flow as a first-class input to ConvNets is a clean, bold reframing of video recognition, inspired by the dorsal/ventral hypothesis. This contrasts with earlier “learn motion implicitly from RGB stacks” approaches and is likely to change practice.
- Empirical validation and ablations: Careful, targeted experiments isolate the contributions of flow stacking length, mean flow subtraction, trajectory vs. location sampling, and bi-directional flow. The findings are actionable (e.g., L=10, mean subtraction helps).
- Practical multi-task training: A simple but effective multi-task loss across datasets mitigates overfitting and provides a practical recipe for low-data regimes.
- Strong results: Significant gains over prior deep models and competitiveness with state-of-the-art hand-engineered pipelines on UCF101 and HMDB-51. The streams’ complementarity is convincingly demonstrated.
- Implementation insights: Details on data augmentation, pretraining, multi-GPU training, and optical flow compression are valuable for reproducibility and adoption.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Not end-to-end: Relying on precomputed optical flow carries a heavy computational/storage burden and breaks end-to-end learning. The paper suggests this as future work, but the current pipeline’s cost may limit deployment.
- Dataset scope: Results are limited to UCF101 and HMDB-51. While standard, they are relatively small; no results on larger-scale video data (e.g., Sports-1M) are reported, and generalization is inferred rather than demonstrated.
- Fusion and architecture choices: Fusion is purely late and linear; joint training or mid-level fusion is briefly attempted but abandoned due to overfitting. The paper could benefit from more systematic exploration of regularization/architectural tweaks to enable joint learning.
- Fairness/cost comparisons: There is limited discussion of computational cost versus strong baselines (e.g., improved trajectories). End-to-end runtime including flow computation and storage overheads is not compared, which matters for practical impact.
- Camera motion handling: Mean flow subtraction is a simplistic proxy for camera motion compensation. Given its known importance, a more explicit module or analysis would strengthen the claim.

Quality
Technically sound, with thoughtful ablations and clear training/testing protocols. Claims are supported by experiments. Some design choices (e.g., why optical flow stacking outperforms trajectory stacking) could be further analyzed, but the empirical evidence is solid. The multi-task learning setup is straightforward and well justified.

Clarity
Well written, organized, and easy to follow. The paper provides sufficient implementation detail to reproduce the main results. Figures (e.g., input configurations) are helpful. Minor editorial issues aside, clarity is high.

Originality
High. While optical flow and two-stream ideas exist in the literature separately, this work’s formulation—treating dense flow as the primary input to a deep temporal stream and coupling it with a pre-trained spatial stream—offers a novel and influential direction beyond “RGB stacks.” The argument that the temporal ConvNet generalizes HOF/MBH is insightful.

Significance
High. The approach advances the deep learning state of the art for video and offers a practical, modular recipe that many will adopt. It provides a foundation for subsequent research on learnable motion representations, fusion strategies, and multi-task training across video datasets.

Suggestions for improvement
- Report or discuss end-to-end computational cost vs. iDT pipelines, including flow computation, to contextualize practical trade-offs.
- Analyze sensitivity to the choice of optical flow algorithm and the effect of JPEG compression on performance.
- Explore regularization enabling mid-level fusion (e.g., factorized FC layers, dropout schedules, shared bottlenecks) to push beyond late fusion.
- Provide per-class analyses to understand failure modes (e.g., camera motion-heavy classes).
- Consider simple camera-motion compensation modules (e.g., global homography warping) within the temporal stream.

Pros for acceptance
- Clear, novel architectural idea with strong empirical impact.
- Thorough ablations and practical training guidance.
- Demonstrated complementarity of streams and superior performance to prior deep baselines.

Cons against acceptance
- Dependence on external optical flow and lack of end-to-end training.
- Limited dataset scope and missing cost comparisons.
- Fusion exploration is conservative.

Recommendation
Accept. The paper introduces a compelling and influential direction for deep video recognition with strong evidence and practical value. The novelty and potential impact outweigh the limitations, which are reasonable for a first, bold step.

Confidence
High.
