- Proposes a two-stream ConvNet architecture for video action recognition: a spatial stream (frames, ImageNet pre-training) and a temporal stream (optical flow), fused late.  
- Evaluated on UCF101 and HMDB-51, showing substantial improvement over prior deep-learning approaches and competitive performance with hand-crafted methods.  
- Draws practical inspiration from the two-streams hypothesis in neuroscience, focusing on implementation rather than biological validation.  

Strengths:
- Clearly motivated: identifies limitations of stacked-frame deep models and argues for explicit motion representation via optical flow.  
- Thorough experimental evaluation with ablation studies on temporal stream input choices and multi-task learning for small datasets.  
- Comprehensive comparisons to prior deep and hand-crafted state-of-the-art methods, demonstrating competitive results.  
- Technically sound architecture and experimental setup; smart use of ImageNet pre-training for spatial stream.  
- Well-organized paper with clear figures that illustrate the two-stream design.

Weaknesses:
- Lacks a theoretical explanation for why optical flow input outperforms stacked RGB frames.  
- Limited discussion of computational cost and practicality, especially due to expensive optical flow computation.  
- Does not compare to more recent methods (e.g., 3D ConvNets or later two-stream variants).  
- Lacks qualitative analysis, such as visualizations of learned features or example successes/failures.  
- Limited discussion of failure cases, e.g., handling complex camera motion beyond mean-flow subtraction.

Quality:
- Technically sound with well-supported quantitative results and careful experiments.

Clarity:
- Very clearly written and organized with sufficient implementation detail for reproducibility.

Originality:
- Novel application of deep ConvNets to a two-stream design using optical flow, distinct from prior hand-crafted two-stream or HMAX-based approaches.

Significance:
- Important contribution showing motion is better captured via explicit optical flow input, advancing deep-learning approaches for action recognition.

Recommendation:
- Strong accept: paper is well-written, technically solid, and empirically convincing despite minor limitations.

Specific suggestions:
- Add analysis explaining why optical flow works better than stacked RGB frames (e.g., feature analysis).  
- Discuss computational costs and real-world practicality of precomputing optical flow.  
- Include qualitative visualizations (activation maps, example correct/incorrect classifications).  
- Address extension to challenging scenarios like complex camera motion.  
- If possible, compare to more recent related work to situate current standing.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Strong motivation and evidence for the two-stream approach.  
- Extensive experiments and ablation studies validating design choices.  
- Demonstrated complementarity of streams and improved performance over prior deep methods.  
- Competitive with hand-crafted state-of-the-art on benchmarks.  
- Clear methodology and reproducibility.  
- Proper contextualization with related work.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Missing theoretical insight into why the method works.  
- No discussion of computational/practical limitations.  
- Lacks qualitative analysis to deepen understanding.  
- Does not compare to more recent or concurrent advances in the field.
