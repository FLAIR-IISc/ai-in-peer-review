- Proposes a two-stream CNN for video action recognition: a spatial stream for frames and a temporal stream using optical flow, with ImageNet pre-training and multi-task learning across datasets.  
- Strong experimental evaluation on UCF-101 and HMDB-51 with comprehensive ablations showing temporal optical-flow stream vastly outperforms stacked-frame baselines.  
- Clear motivation and novelty: explicitly supplying motion via optical flow is a meaningful refinement over prior attempts to learn motion implicitly.  
- Reproducible: provides detailed architecture, training protocols, optical flow computation, and practical data storage tips.  
- Practical impact: leverages image pre-training to address limited labeled video data and achieves competitive results with hand-crafted features.  
- Insufficient theoretical/empirical explanation for why optical flow input outperforms stacked-frame approaches like [14].  
- Lacks direct comparison to the absolute state-of-the-art shallow methods (e.g., [26]) in main results.  
- Does not explore incorporating missing elements of top shallow representations (e.g., spatio-temporal tube pooling) into the two-stream framework.  
- Omits discussion of the computational cost and real-world implications of optical flow computation (0.06s per frame pair).  
- Quality: technically sound experiments and supported claims, but minor theoretical explanation gap.  
- Clarity: well-written and organized with helpful figures, though some optical flow details could be expanded.  
- Originality: meaningful implementation advance by using dual CNN streams with optical flow, but relation to [14] needs clearer distinction.  
- Significance: demonstrates deep learning can match hand-crafted features for action recognition and benefits from multi-task learning on small video datasets.  
- Recommendation: accept with minor revisions.  
- Suggested revisions: clarify relation to [14] and why optical flow helps, add direct comparison to top shallow methods, discuss integrating spatio-temporal pooling, address optical flow computational costs, and comment on generalization to other video tasks.
