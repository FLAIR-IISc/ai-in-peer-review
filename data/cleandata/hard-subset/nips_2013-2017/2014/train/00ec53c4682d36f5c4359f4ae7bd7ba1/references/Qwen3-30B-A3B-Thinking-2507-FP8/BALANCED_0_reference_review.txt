

# Review of "Two-Stream Convolutional Networks for Action Recognition in Videos"

This paper proposes a two-stream convolutional network architecture for video action recognition, decoupling spatial and temporal processing streams. The spatial stream processes individual frames (pre-trained on ImageNet), while the temporal stream processes dense optical flow. Both streams are implemented as ConvNets, with their outputs combined via late fusion. The authors demonstrate that this approach outperforms previous deep learning methods for video action recognition (e.g., [14]) and achieves competitive results with state-of-the-art hand-crafted representations [20, 21, 26] on UCF-101 and HMDB-51 benchmarks.

**Strengths:**
1. The paper makes a compelling case for separating spatial and temporal processing in video action recognition, which aligns with the two-stream hypothesis in neuroscience [9]. This conceptual framework is well-motivated and provides a clear rationale for the architecture.
2. The experimental evaluation is thorough, including ablation studies on different optical flow input configurations (stacking, trajectory, bi-directional) and effective use of multi-task learning to address limited video training data.
3. The authors clearly demonstrate that optical flow as input to a ConvNet significantly outperforms stacking RGB frames (as in [14]), addressing a key limitation of prior deep learning approaches.
4. The implementation details are sufficiently comprehensive for reproducibility, including practical considerations like optical flow pre-computation and multi-GPU training.
5. The results are impressive, showing that the two-stream architecture achieves competitive performance with hand-crafted methods while being end-to-end trainable.

**Weaknesses:**
1. The paper doesn't sufficiently differentiate its approach from the two-stream HMAX model [16], which also uses separate spatial and temporal streams. While the authors note that [16] uses shallow HMAX models, they don't clearly articulate how their deep ConvNet approach represents a fundamental advance beyond the HMAX framework.
2. The computational cost of optical flow computation is not adequately addressed. The paper mentions that flow pre-computation is necessary (taking 0.06s per frame pair), but doesn't discuss how this impacts the overall pipeline efficiency or scalability to longer videos.
3. The comparison with [14] is somewhat limited since [14] used the Sports-1M dataset (1.1M videos), while this work uses smaller datasets (UCF-101, HMDB-51). A more direct comparison would be valuable, especially since the authors note they plan to address Sports-1M in future work.
4. The paper identifies that their approach misses certain elements of state-of-the-art hand-crafted representations (e.g., local feature pooling over spatio-temporal tubes), but doesn't explore how to incorporate these elements into their framework.
5. The paper doesn't discuss failure cases or limitations of the optical flow approach (e.g., performance degradation with motion blur, fast motion, or low-contrast scenes).

**Recommendation:**
The paper makes a solid contribution to video action recognition, demonstrating that a two-stream ConvNet architecture is viable and effective. While the results are compelling, the paper could be strengthened by more clearly articulating the novelty relative to [16], providing a more detailed discussion of computational costs, and addressing how to incorporate missing elements from hand-crafted approaches.

**Suggestions for Improvement:**
1. Add a more detailed comparison with [16], explaining exactly how their deep ConvNet approach advances beyond the HMAX-based two-stream model.
2. Discuss the computational trade-offs of using optical flow, including runtime analysis and potential alternatives for real-time applications.
3. Include a comparison with [14] trained on the same datasets (UCF-101/HMDB-51) to provide a fairer benchmark.
4. Explore how to integrate spatio-temporal tube pooling (mentioned as a missing element) into the two-stream framework.
5. Add a section discussing limitations of the optical flow approach, including qualitative examples of failure cases.

This paper makes a valuable contribution to the field of video action recognition. The two-stream architecture is well-motivated, the experimental evaluation is thorough, and the results are impressive. With some additional clarification of the novelty, discussion of computational implications, and exploration of limitations, this work would be an excellent fit for NIPS. The authors should address the weaknesses noted above to strengthen the paper's contribution and position within the field.
