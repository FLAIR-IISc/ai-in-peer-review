Summary
The paper proposes a two-stream convolutional architecture for action recognition in videos: a spatial stream ConvNet operating on RGB frames (pretrained on ImageNet) and a temporal stream ConvNet operating on stacked dense optical flow fields. Several temporal input variants are studied: stacking per-pixel flow over L frames, trajectory stacking (sampling along flow-induced trajectories), bi-directional flow, and mean flow subtraction. A multi-task learning setup is used to jointly train on UCF-101 and HMDB-51 with two task-specific softmax heads to mitigate overfitting. Fusion is done by late score fusion (averaging or linear SVM). Experiments on UCF-101 and HMDB-51 show that the temporal stream substantially outperforms the spatial stream and that fusing both streams further improves accuracy; multi-task learning helps the temporal stream.

Relation to prior work: The approach is a clear response to earlier deep video models that stacked RGB frames (e.g., 3D ConvNets and “slow fusion”), and to trajectory-based shallow pipelines (improved trajectories with HOF/MBH). The temporal ConvNet on optical flow explicitly encodes motion, arguably generalizing gradient/histogram-based features, while avoiding having to learn motion implicitly from raw RGB.

Strengths
- Conceptual clarity: decomposition into spatial vs temporal streams is well-motivated and aligns with both neuroscience and empirical observations.
- Temporal representation: stacked optical flow as ConvNet input is simple, effective, and empirically superior to stacked RGB frames trained from scratch.
- Careful ablations on temporal inputs (L, trajectory vs per-pixel stacking, bi-directional flow, mean subtraction).
- Practical training recipe: ImageNet pretraining for spatial net; multi-task learning to leverage two video datasets; multi-GPU engineering.
- Results are competitive with strong shallow baselines and significantly outperform prior deep models on these datasets.

Weaknesses and technical concerns
Methodological/detail omissions:
- Data augmentation on flow: For horizontal flips, was the horizontal component dx sign-inverted? This is critical and not stated.
- Mean flow subtraction: Is the mean vector computed per-frame, per-stack, or per-crop? Is it computed over the spatial field only? Does it differ for forward/backward stacks?
- Flow scaling/compression: The displacement components are rescaled to [0,255] and JPEG-compressed. What scale/clipping is used (global vs per-video vs per-frame)? JPEG on flow is unusual and may introduce artifacts; an analysis of its effect is missing.
- Bi-directional flow: L/2 forward and L/2 backward implies L is even; edge handling (near start/end of clips) is unspecified.
- Multi-task learning: How are batches constructed across datasets? Are task losses equally weighted? Are class-imbalance and dataset-size differences addressed?
- Regularization: Weight decay values are not specified; only dropout is discussed. The effect of removing the second LRN layer in the temporal net is not quantified.
- Fusion with SVM: Was the SVM trained only on training scores with cross-validation on a validation set? Otherwise, overfitting is a risk. No calibration discussion.
- Statistical reporting: No standard deviations or confidence intervals; final comparisons average across splits but variability is not reported. Many choices (L=10, mean subtraction, bi-directional) appear tuned on split 1; this may bias results.

Scope/analysis gaps:
- End-to-end vs precomputed flow: The approach relies on an external optical flow algorithm; training time, inference time per video, and computational budget are not reported, hindering a fair comparison to RGB-based models. No analysis of different flow algorithms (e.g., TV-L1 vs Brox vs DeepFlow) or their quality/performance trade-offs.
- Camera motion: Only mean subtraction is used. Given the known benefits of motion stabilization/compensation for trajectory pipelines, a stronger baseline (e.g., homography-based stabilization) and comparisons would be appropriate.
- Trajectory stacking performs worse than per-pixel stacking, but the paper does not investigate why (e.g., trajectory drift, aliasing, sampling noise).
- Spatial net pretraining: The improvement is attributed to sampling from the whole image rather than center crops; stronger evidence or controlled ablation would help.
- Reproducibility: The code is a modified Caffe with multi-GPU support; no release is promised here. The many implementation nuances above are pivotal for reproduction.

Clarity and presentation:
- The paper is generally well written, but there are numerous formatting/typo issues in the references (missing years/venues, misspellings such as “classication”, “D convolutional…” instead of 3D), and occasional typos in the text (e.g., “mult-task”, stray punctuation/spaces).
- Figures are referenced but not all are sufficiently described to reproduce the exact input construction choices without seeing the visuals.

Originality
- The two-stream decomposition plus optical-flow-as-input temporal ConvNet is a novel and timely idea relative to prior deep video work that feeds raw frames. Multi-task learning is standard but applied effectively here. The claimed generalization of HOF/MBH features by a first conv layer is plausible but not rigorously demonstrated (filter visualizations are relegated to supplemental).

Significance
- The gains over prior deep baselines are substantial; fusion achieves results comparable to improved trajectories. The approach is likely to influence practitioners. However, reliance on precomputed flow and the lack of runtime analysis limit practical impact claims.

Questions for the authors
- Precisely specify the flip handling for flow (sign inversion), mean subtraction protocol, flow scaling/clipping, and JPEG parameters. Provide an ablation on compression effects.
- Detail the multi-task batching and loss weighting; report whether mixing ratios matter.
- Provide runtime per video for each stream and the fused model; include flow computation time.
- Report standard deviations across splits and ensure that hyperparameters were not selected on test splits.
- Clarify SVM training protocol and calibration.

Minor notes/typos (non-exhaustive)
- Many references incomplete or malformed; please fix venue/year/pages; “3D convolutional…”, “classification”.
- Notation: clarify domain of u,v (1-indexed vs 0), and that L is even for bi-directional flow.

Pros
- Clear, effective idea; strong empirical gains.
- Thorough ablations on temporal input design.
- Multi-task learning improves data efficiency.

Cons
- Many missing implementation details critical for reproduction.
- No runtime/compute analysis; heavy reliance on precomputed flow.
- Weakness in statistical reporting and validation protocol.
- Numerous formatting/typo issues.

Recommendation: Weak reject (borderline). The contribution is promising and likely impactful, but the current manuscript has too many methodological ambiguities and presentation issues for NeurIPS standards. Addressing the detailed concerns above would likely tip this to acceptance.
