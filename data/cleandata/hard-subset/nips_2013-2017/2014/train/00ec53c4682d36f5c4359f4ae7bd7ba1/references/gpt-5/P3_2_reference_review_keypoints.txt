- Proposes a two-stream ConvNet for action recognition: a spatial RGB stream and a temporal stream using stacked dense optical flow.  
- Key ideas: decouple appearance and motion, feed explicit optical flow instead of learning motion from raw frames, fuse softmax scores at test time, and use multi-task learning to reduce temporal-stream overfitting.  
- Systematically studies temporal input variants (flow stacking, trajectory stacking, bi-directional flow, mean-flow subtraction) and finds stacked flows with mean subtraction work best.  
- Reports large gains over single-frame and slow-fusion RGB baselines and competitive accuracy with hand-crafted trajectory methods.  
- Positions work relative to prior methods (HOF/MBH, trajectory stacking, ventral/dorsal inspiration) with appropriate empirical comparisons.  

Strengths:
- Clear, well-motivated architectural decoupling of appearance and motion with a simple, effective training protocol.  
- Strong ablation study supporting design choices (number of flows, stacking methods, mean subtraction, fusion).  
- Practical multi-task learning across UCF-101 and HMDB-51 that consistently improves performance.  
- Both streams individually outperform prior deep baselines; fusion adds 6–14% improvement.  
- Good implementation and training details that aid reproducibility (augmentation, schedules, multi-GPU, flow precomputation/compression).  

Weaknesses:
- Reliance on precomputed Brox optical flow is a major computational bottleneck and prevents end-to-end learning; runtime not quantified.  
- Camera motion is only handled by mean displacement subtraction; lacks comparison to explicit global motion compensation (e.g., homography).  
- Limited analysis explaining why bi-directional flow and trajectory stacking underperform despite intuitive appeal.  
- Sparse analysis of failure modes and per-class contributions of spatial vs. temporal streams.  
- Multi-task learning lacks details on loss weighting, sampling strategy, and sensitivity to class imbalance.  
- Scalability/generalization to larger datasets is discussed but not empirically validated.  

Quality:
- Technically sound with appropriate baselines and ablations; core contributions are well substantiated despite missing runtime and deeper diagnostics.  

Clarity:
- Clearly written and organized with sufficient architectural/training details for reproduction; would benefit from per-class/error analyses.  

Originality:
- Novel and influential two-stream design with explicit optical flow input and late score fusion, distinct from stacked-frame ConvNets and prior pipelines.  

Significance:
- High impact: provides a simple, effective recipe for injecting motion into deep models and offers actionable ablation insights for practitioners.  

Suggestions for improvement:
- Report computational cost: flow precomputation time, storage, and per-stream inference time; compare to IDT pipelines.  
- Evaluate explicit camera motion compensation (e.g., homography) versus mean subtraction.  
- Provide class-wise analysis and qualitative failure cases to clarify stream complementarity.  
- Detail multi-task learning (loss weights, sampling ratios, sensitivity analyses).  
- Explore or justify fusion strategies beyond linear SVM and address overfitting concerns.  
- Discuss sensitivity to optical flow algorithm/parameters and effects of compression (JPEG quantization).  

Pros:
- Strong empirical improvements with clear ablations, simple general architecture, and good reproducibility.  

Cons:
- Heavy reliance on precomputed optical flow, limited camera motion analysis, and missing runtime/resource comparisons.  

Recommendation:
- Accept (strong). Confidence: 4/5.
