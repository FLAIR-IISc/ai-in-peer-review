Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes a two-stream convolutional architecture for video action recognition that decouples spatial appearance and temporal motion. The spatial stream is a ConvNet trained on single RGB frames and pre-trained on ImageNet; the temporal stream is a ConvNet trained on stacked dense optical flow fields across multiple frames (with variants: trajectory stacking, bi-directional flow, and mean-flow subtraction). The streams are fused by late score fusion (averaging or SVM). To mitigate limited video labels, the authors introduce a simple multi-task learning setup with two softmax heads to jointly train on UCF-101 and HMDB-51. Extensive ablations show that optical-flow inputs, longer temporal stacking, mean subtraction, and multi-task training all help; the two-stream fusion significantly improves over either stream alone. Results on UCF-101 and HMDB-51 are substantially better than prior deep models (e.g., stacked RGB frames) and competitive with contemporary hand-crafted trajectory-based methods.

The work is well situated versus prior video recognition: it contrasts with earlier stacks-of-frames ConvNets (Karpathy et al.), connects the optical-flow inputs to HOF/MBH/kinematic features, and discusses trajectory-based representations. It also references related two-pathway ideas (HMAX ventral/dorsal) while emphasizing the deeper, discriminatively trained CNN implementation here.

Evaluation by NeurIPS criteria

Quality
- Technical soundness: The architectural choices are clearly motivated and implemented carefully. The optical-flow input parameterizations are well defined; the multi-task learning head is straightforward and appropriate.
- Empirical support: Strong ablations support the main claims: stacking multiple flows (L=10) > single flow; mean subtraction helps; flow stacking outperforms trajectory stacking; two-stream fusion yields large gains; multi-task training improves temporal nets, especially on HMDB-51. Testing protocol and data splits follow community standards.
- Limitations: The approach relies on precomputed optical flow (Brox) and is not end-to-end; there is no comparison across different flow algorithms or analysis of the effect of JPEG-compressed flow quantization. Score-level fusion is used due to overfitting when attempting deeper fusion; this is a reasonable engineering choice, but limits joint modeling. Camera motion is only crudely handled via mean subtraction; more principled compensation is not explored. Statistical significance or variance across runs is not reported.

Clarity
- The paper is clearly written, with precise definitions of input volumes, clean figures, and explicit training/testing protocols (learning rate schedules, sampling, augmentation). The connection to prior hand-crafted features is insightful and helps readers understand what the first layers may learn. A few additions could further aid reproducibility: exact optical flow parameters, more detail on temporal sampling stride, and an explicit runtime/memory budget at train/test (beyond the optical flow compute/storage notes).

Originality
- The central idea—decoupling spatial and temporal processing with a deep two-stream architecture and feeding stacked optical flow to a ConvNet—is novel relative to prior deep video models that relied on stacks of RGB frames to learn motion implicitly. The multi-task dual-head training for joint UCF/HMDB optimization is a pragmatic and useful contribution. While individual components (optical flow, late fusion, ImageNet pretraining) are known, their combination and the thorough demonstration that explicit motion inputs overcome the deficiencies of stacked RGB models is a meaningful advance.

Significance
- The results are impactful: the temporal stream alone outperforms prior deep baselines; fusing with the spatial stream yields accuracy competitive with state-of-the-art hand-crafted pipelines at the time. The design is simple, computationally feasible, and likely to be adopted by practitioners. The paper also sets a template for separating appearance and motion and for leveraging large-scale image pretraining, which can influence subsequent video recognition research.

Strengths
- Clear, well-motivated architecture with principled decomposition into spatial/temporal streams.
- Strong empirical evidence with sensible ablations and analysis (L, mean subtraction, stacking variants, fusion, multi-task).
- Competitive to SOTA shallow methods and clearly better than previous deep approaches.
- Practical training/testing details (multi-GPU, flow precomputation/compression) that facilitate reproduction.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Optical flow dependency: No study of different flow methods, their accuracy/latency trade-offs, or the effect of flow compression on accuracy. An ablation varying flow algorithm or compression would strengthen claims.
- Limited fusion exploration: Feature-level fusion attempts were abandoned due to overfitting; reporting those results and trying light-weight joint fusion (e.g., low-rank or calibrated stacking) would be informative. Analysis of why SVM fusion beats averaging would help.
- Camera motion handling: Beyond mean subtraction, it would be valuable to assess global-camera-motion compensation (e.g., homography/affine stabilization) within this pipeline to reduce background motion.
- Dataset scope: Experiments are limited to UCF-101 and HMDB-51; acknowledging whether conclusions hold on larger-scale data (e.g., Sports-1M) is useful. Even small-scale tests on subsets would help.
- Diagnostics: Per-class analysis showing which actions benefit from temporal vs. spatial streams; sensitivity to number of sampled frames and temporal stride; computational costs (end-to-end fps) at test time.

Pro-accept arguments
- Conceptually clean and empirically validated insight: explicit motion via optical flow enables effective temporal ConvNets.
- Substantial performance gains over prior deep methods; competitive with the best hand-crafted pipelines.
- Thorough ablations and a practical multi-task training strategy for small datasets.
- Clear writing and strong potential impact on the field.

Con-accept arguments
- Not end-to-end; depends on an external, potentially slow optical flow pre-process.
- Limited analysis of optical flow choices and compression effects.
- Fusion remains at score level due to overfitting; limited exploration of more integrated models.
- Experiments confined to two datasets; no large-scale validation.

Recommendation
Accept. This is a well-executed, insightful contribution that advances deep video recognition by a significant margin and introduces a simple, influential architecture. With minor additions (flow/compression ablations, runtime metrics, more fusion diagnostics), the paper would be even stronger.
