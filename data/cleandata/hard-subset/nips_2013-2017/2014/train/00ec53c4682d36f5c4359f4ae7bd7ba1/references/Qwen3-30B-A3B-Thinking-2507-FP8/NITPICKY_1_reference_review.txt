

# Review of "Two-Stream Convolutional Networks for Action Recognition in Videos"

## Summary and Relation to Previous Work
The paper proposes a two-stream convolutional network architecture for video action recognition, with separate spatial and temporal streams. The spatial stream processes individual video frames, while the temporal stream processes dense optical flow. The authors claim this approach outperforms previous deep learning methods for action recognition and is competitive with state-of-the-art hand-crafted shallow representations. The work builds on the two-stream hypothesis in neuroscience and extends the work of [14] by using optical flow as input to the temporal stream rather than stacked video frames. The paper also introduces multi-task learning to combine training data from UCF-101 and HMDB-51 datasets.

## Strengths
The paper presents a clear, well-structured approach to video action recognition. The two-stream architecture is a natural extension of the neuroscience-inspired two-stream hypothesis. The experimental evaluation is thorough, comparing multiple input configurations for the temporal stream and demonstrating the benefits of multi-task learning. The results on UCF-101 and HMDB-51 are competitive with state-of-the-art methods, showing that the proposed approach is effective.

## Weaknesses

### Technical and Methodological Issues
1. **Inconsistent Terminology**: The paper uses "ConvNet" and "ConvNets" interchangeably without clear distinction, and inconsistently capitalizes "Spatial ConvNet" vs. "spatial ConvNet." This undermines precision.

2. **Formula Errors**: In Section 3.1, Equation (1) contains a typo: "Iτ(u,v,2k-1) = dxτ+k-1(u,v)" should be "Iτ(u,v,2k-1) = dxτ+k-1(u,v)" (missing space between "dx" and "τ"). Similarly, Equation (2) has a typo: "pk = pk-1 +dτ+k-2(pk-1)" should be "pk = pk-1 + dτ+k-2(pk-1)" (missing space between "d" and "τ").

3. **Reference Inconsistency**: The paper cites "the work of [14]" but [14] is actually "Large-scale video classification with convolutional neural networks" by Karpathy et al., not the Sports-1M dataset as referenced in the text. The Sports-1M dataset is mentioned in the text but not properly cited.

4. **Figure Reference Errors**: The paper references "Fig. 1" and "Fig. 3" multiple times but provides no figure captions or descriptions in the text, making it impossible to understand what these figures show.

5. **Missing Ablation Studies**: The paper claims "the main reason for the improvement is sampling of ConvNet inputs from the whole image," but provides no ablation study to support this claim. This weakens their argument.

### Experimental and Reporting Issues
1. **Incomplete Dataset Information**: The paper states "UCF-101 contains 13K videos (180 frames/video on average)" but doesn't clarify whether this is for the entire dataset or the training split. The standard UCF-101 has 13,320 videos total, with 9,537 for training.

2. **Table Inconsistency**: Table 1a reports results for spatial ConvNets, but doesn't specify which specific configuration was used for the final results. The paper claims "training the last layer only" is optimal, but doesn't clearly state which results were used in subsequent experiments.

3. **Reference Omission**: The paper claims "the two streams were implemented as hand-crafted and rather shallow (3-layer) HMAX models" for [16], but [16] is actually "HMDB: A large video database for human motion recognition" by Kuehne et al., not the two-stream HMAX model. The correct reference for the two-stream HMAX model is [11].

4. **Formatting Errors**: The reference list has multiple entries with missing information, such as "Large scale visual recognition challenge (ILSVRC), A. Berg, J. Deng, L. Fei-Fei, , , " which is incomplete.

### Clarity and Writing Issues
1. **Grammatical Errors**: The paper contains multiple grammatical errors, such as "recognise" (British spelling) used inconsistently with "recognize" (American spelling).

2. **Ambiguous Claims**: The paper states "the temporal ConvNet, trained using multi-task learning, performs the best both alone and when fused with a spatial net" but doesn't clearly indicate which specific configuration achieved this in Table 2.

3. **Inconsistent Dataset Descriptions**: The paper describes "HMDB-51" as having "3.7K videos" in the introduction, but Table 2 states "HMDB-51 split contains 3.7K training videos," which is inconsistent with the standard HMDB-51 dataset (which has 6,766 videos total, with approximately 3,700 for training).

## Recommendation
While the paper presents a reasonable approach to video action recognition with competitive results, the numerous technical errors, inconsistencies in terminology, and formatting issues significantly undermine the paper's quality. The paper would benefit from careful proofreading and careful attention to the details of their methodological descriptions. As a reviewer, I find these issues too numerous and substantive to overlook, despite the paper's overall contribution.

The paper's main weakness is that it fails to meet the standards of technical precision expected at NeurIPS. The paper would need significant revision to address these issues before it could be accepted. The minor technical errors and inconsistencies suggest a lack of meticulous attention to detail that is expected in NeurIPS submissions.

I recommend rejection. The paper has a good idea, but the numerous technical inconsistencies and formatting errors make it difficult to evaluate the method's validity and impact. These issues suggest the authors may not have thoroughly checked their work before submission, which is a serious concern for a NeurIPS paper.
