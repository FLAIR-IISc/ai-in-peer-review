

# Peer Review: Two-Stream Convolutional Networks for Action Recognition in Videos

## Summary and Relation to Prior Work

This paper proposes a two-stream convolutional network architecture for video action recognition, where one stream processes spatial information from individual frames (using a CNN pre-trained on ImageNet) while the other processes temporal information from optical flow. The authors build upon the neuroscience-inspired two-stream hypothesis (Goodale & Milner, 1992) and demonstrate that their approach significantly outperforms previous deep learning attempts that used stacked RGB frames as input. This work relates to NIPS 2014's growing interest in video understanding, particularly following the success of Karpathy et al. (2014) on video classification, but addresses a key limitation in that work by explicitly separating spatial and temporal processing.

## Strengths

The paper's strongest contribution is its clear demonstration that motion information should be explicitly represented rather than implicitly learned. The authors provide compelling evidence that using optical flow as input to a CNN (rather than stacking RGB frames) substantially improves performance, addressing a critical limitation in prior work (e.g., [14]). Their ablation studies (Table 1b) thoroughly analyze different optical flow input configurations, showing that stacking multiple frames of optical flow is crucial for performance.

The multi-task learning framework (Section 4) is particularly elegant, effectively addressing the small dataset problem for temporal networks by sharing representations across UCF-101 and HMDB-51. This approach achieves better results than simple data augmentation (adding non-overlapping classes) and avoids manual class selection. The paper also provides excellent implementation details, including the computational efficiency of pre-computing optical flow and the practical considerations for training on multiple GPUs.

## Weaknesses and Critiques

While the paper makes a strong case for the two-stream architecture, it doesn't sufficiently address the computational bottleneck of optical flow computation. Pre-computing flow for large datasets (like Sports-1M mentioned in Section 7) is feasible for small benchmarks, but the paper doesn't discuss how this would scale to larger datasets where flow computation would become prohibitively expensive. This is a significant limitation for practical deployment.

The connection to neuroscience is mentioned but not deeply explored. While the authors note their work is "related to the two-streams hypothesis," they don't discuss how their learnable ConvNet streams differ from the biological pathways, nor do they draw implications for neuroscience from their findings. This connection feels superficial rather than substantive.

The paper claims to outperform "state-of-the-art shallow representations," but the comparison is somewhat limited. The results in Table 4 show competitive performance with [20, 21, 26], but the authors don't provide a direct comparison with the very recent work of Wang et al. (2013) on "Action Recognition with Improved Trajectories," which was published shortly before this paper. This omission weakens the claim of being "competitive with the state of the art."

## Specific Suggestions for Improvement

1. **Address computational limitations:** Discuss how the approach would scale to larger datasets like Sports-1M. Consider adding a brief analysis of the trade-off between optical flow computation cost and performance gains.

2. **Strengthen the neuroscience connection:** Explain how their learnable two-stream architecture advances or challenges the two-stream hypothesis in neuroscience, rather than merely borrowing the concept.

3. **Expand state-of-the-art comparison:** Include a direct comparison with Wang et al. (2013) and other recent trajectory-based methods to better position their contribution.

4. **Clarify the "why" behind optical flow superiority:** While the paper shows optical flow works better, a more detailed analysis of why stacked RGB frames fail (beyond just "it's difficult to learn motion implicitly") would strengthen their argument.

## Overall Assessment

This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to video action recognition. The two-stream architecture is well-motivated, the experimental evaluation is thorough, and the results are compelling. The paper demonstrates that deep learning can successfully address video action recognition when the right architectural choices are made, particularly by explicitly representing motion through optical flow rather than relying on implicit learning.

The paper meets all NIPS quality criteria: it's technically sound with well-supported claims, clearly written, and makes a meaningful contribution to the field. While the work has some limitations in scalability and the neuroscience connection, these are minor compared to the overall quality and impact of the contribution. The significance is high, as the approach provides a new paradigm for video understanding that could influence future work in the field.

**Recommendation: Accept**

This paper represents a solid advance in video action recognition that would be valuable to the NIPS community. The two-stream architecture provides an elegant solution to a persistent challenge in video understanding, and the results demonstrate clear improvements over prior deep learning approaches. The paper's thorough evaluation, practical implementation details, and connection to neuroscience make it a strong contribution to the field.
