

# Review of "Two-Stream Convolutional Networks for Action Recognition in Videos"

This paper proposes a two-stream convolutional network architecture for action recognition in videos, with one stream processing spatial information (individual frames) and the other processing temporal information (optical flow). The authors claim their approach significantly outperforms previous deep learning methods for video action recognition and is competitive with state-of-the-art hand-crafted shallow representations.

**Strengths:**
The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with a thorough literature review that adequately covers related work in both shallow and deep video recognition methods. The ablation studies on different optical flow input configurations (stacking, trajectory, bi-directional, mean subtraction) provide useful insights into the importance of temporal representation. The multi-task learning approach to combine UCF-101 and HMDB-51 datasets is reasonable and well-executed. The experimental evaluation on standard benchmarks (UCF-101 and HMDB-51) is appropriate, and the results demonstrate that their two-stream approach outperforms the previous deep learning baseline [14].

**Weaknesses:**
Despite the promising results, the paper suffers from significant weaknesses that undermine its contribution to the field:

1. **Lack of sufficient novelty:** The core idea of separating spatial and temporal streams is not new. The paper cites [16], which already proposed a two-stream HMAX architecture (spatial and temporal streams) for video recognition. The authors' main contribution appears to be replacing HMAX with ConvNets and using optical flow instead of stacked frames. However, this is a relatively minor variation on existing work, and the paper fails to adequately differentiate their contribution from [16] beyond the model architecture. The claim that "our proposed architecture is related to the two-streams hypothesis" is not sufficiently novel for NIPS.

2. **Insufficient validation against established baselines:** The paper compares against [14] (which used stacked frames) and some shallow representations, but fails to compare against other optical flow-based deep learning approaches. For instance, [30] proposed "DeepFlow" which combines optical flow with deep matching. The paper should have compared against this and other relevant optical flow-based deep learning methods to demonstrate the true novelty of their approach. The claim that their method "significantly outperforms that of [14]" is unconvincing without comparison to more recent optical flow-based deep learning approaches.

3. **Methodological limitations:** The paper does not adequately address the computational cost of optical flow computation, which is a significant drawback for practical applications. The authors mention they pre-computed optical flow (taking 1.5TB for UCF-101), but don't discuss how this impacts scalability or real-world deployment. The paper also fails to explain why their optical flow approach works better than [14]'s stacked frame approach, beyond the superficial observation that [14] found frame-based networks performed similarly to stacked-frame networks.

4. **Overstated claims:** The paper claims their method is "competitive with the state of the art shallow representations," but the results (Table 4) show they fall short of the best shallow methods (e.g., 70.3% vs. 71.8% on HMDB-51 for the best shallow method). The paper should be more precise about the degree of competitiveness rather than making vague claims.

5. **Lack of theoretical justification:** The paper makes the claim that "motion is explicitly represented using the optical flow displacement field, computed based on the assumptions of constancy of the intensity and smoothness of the flow," but provides no theoretical justification for why this representation is superior to other temporal representations. The authors simply assume this is better without demonstrating it.

**Conclusion:**
While the paper presents a reasonably well-executed application of deep learning to video action recognition, the contribution lacks sufficient novelty to justify publication at NIPS. The two-stream architecture is not new, the comparison to relevant baselines is incomplete, and the paper fails to provide strong theoretical justification for its approach. The authors' claim that their method is competitive with the state of the art is somewhat overstated based on the results presented. The work appears to be an incremental improvement rather than a significant advancement in the field.

The paper would benefit from more thorough comparison to other optical flow-based deep learning approaches, a clearer differentiation from [16], and a more precise discussion of the limitations and practical challenges of their approach. Without these improvements, the paper does not meet NIPS' standards for novelty and significance.

**Recommendation:** Reject. The paper presents an incremental approach with insufficient novelty and incomplete validation against relevant baselines. The contribution does not advance the state of the art in a demonstrable way, and the claims are not fully supported by the evidence provided.
