## Review of "Two-Stream Convolutional Networks for Action Recognition in Videos"

**Overall Assessment:** This paper presents a significant contribution to the field of video action recognition by successfully adapting deep convolutional neural networks to the task. The core idea of decoupling spatial and temporal information into separate streams, leveraging pre-training on ImageNet, and utilizing dense optical flow is both intuitive and demonstrably effective. The paper is well-written, clearly organized, and offers a thorough experimental evaluation. While not pushing the absolute state-of-the-art to new heights, it provides a strong baseline and insightful analysis, particularly given the relatively small datasets used. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions.

**1. Summary & Related Work:**

The paper addresses the challenge of video action recognition, finding existing deep learning approaches lacking compared to hand-crafted features. It proposes a two-stream convolutional network architecture: a spatial stream processing individual frames and a temporal stream processing dense optical flow. Both streams employ ConvNets, with the spatial stream benefiting from ImageNet pre-training. The streams are then fused for final classification. 

The related work section is comprehensive, covering both traditional feature engineering approaches (HOG, HOF, trajectories) and early attempts at deep learning for video. The authors accurately position their work relative to [14] (stacking frames) and [16] (shallow two-stream HMAX), highlighting the advantages of their deeper, discriminatively trained architecture and explicit motion modeling. They also appropriately acknowledge the biological inspiration from the ventral and dorsal stream hypothesis.

**2. Strengths:**

* **Effective Architecture:** The two-stream approach is a clever and effective way to leverage both appearance and motion information. Decoupling the streams allows for specialized learning and the exploitation of large image datasets via pre-training.
* **Strong Performance:** The results demonstrate a significant improvement over the stacked frame approach of [14] and achieve competitive performance compared to existing hand-crafted features on UCF101 and HMDB51.
* **Detailed Experimental Evaluation:** The paper includes a thorough investigation of different input configurations for the temporal stream (stacking vs. trajectories, bi-directional flow, mean subtraction) and fusion strategies.  The ablation studies are valuable for understanding the contribution of each component.
* **Clear Writing & Organization:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow. The figures and tables are clear and informative.
* **Practical Considerations:** The authors address important implementation details, such as training procedures, GPU utilization, and optical flow computation.
* **Multi-task Learning:** The introduction of multi-task learning to mitigate overfitting due to limited video data is a smart and effective technique.

**3. Weaknesses:**

* **Optical Flow Dependence:** The performance is heavily reliant on the quality of the dense optical flow computation.  While the authors acknowledge this, a discussion of the limitations of optical flow (e.g., failure in textureless regions or under significant illumination changes) would be beneficial.
* **Limited Dataset Scale:** While the results are good on UCF101 and HMDB51, these datasets are relatively small compared to current large-scale video datasets like Sports-1M. The paper acknowledges this but a more detailed discussion of potential generalization to larger datasets would strengthen the work.
* **Fusion Strategy:**  While SVM-based fusion outperforms averaging, the paper doesn't explore other more sophisticated fusion techniques (e.g., attention mechanisms, learned fusion weights). 
* **Lack of Qualitative Analysis:** Showing some examples of what the network *learns* – perhaps visualizing filters in the temporal stream or misclassified examples – would enhance understanding and provide more insight.
* **Missing Comparison to 3D CNNs:** While focusing on a 2-stream architecture, comparing to a 3D CNN trained end-to-end (even briefly) would help situate the work within the broader landscape of video action recognition.



**4. Originality & Significance:**

The paper is original in its application of deep convolutional networks in a two-stream architecture specifically tailored for video action recognition, combining spatial and temporal streams with pre-training and dense optical flow.  While the individual components (ConvNets, optical flow) are not novel, the specific combination and the detailed exploration of the architecture are a valuable contribution. 

The significance of this work lies in its demonstrated improvement over existing methods and its potential to serve as a strong baseline for future research. It’s a well-engineered solution that advances the state-of-the-art, even if modestly, and provides a solid foundation for further exploration of deep learning for video understanding.

**5. Pro/Con Acceptance:**

**Pro:**

* Strong experimental results demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed architecture.
* Clear and well-written presentation.
* Thorough ablation studies.
* Addresses a challenging problem with a practical and well-motivated solution.
*  Incorporates multi-task learning to address data limitations.

**Con:**

* Dependence on optical flow computation.
* Limited scale of evaluation datasets.
* Relatively basic fusion strategy.




**6.  Specific Suggestions/Questions:**

* **Optical Flow Robustness:** Could you briefly discuss the limitations of optical flow and how they might impact performance?
* **Scalability:** What are the anticipated challenges and potential solutions for scaling this approach to larger datasets like Sports-1M?
* **Fusion Exploration:**  Have you considered more sophisticated fusion strategies beyond averaging and SVM?
* **Qualitative Results:** Could you include some examples of what the network is learning (e.g., learned filters, misclassified examples)?
* **3D CNN Comparison:** A brief discussion or comparison to 3D CNNs would be helpful.




**Overall, I believe this is a solid and valuable contribution to the field of video action recognition. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the points raised above.**
