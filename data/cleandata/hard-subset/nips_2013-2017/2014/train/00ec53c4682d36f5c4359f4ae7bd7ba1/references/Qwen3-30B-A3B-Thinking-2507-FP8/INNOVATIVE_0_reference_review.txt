

# Review of "Two-Stream Convolutional Networks for Action Recognition in Videos"

This paper introduces a novel two-stream architecture for video action recognition, which represents a significant conceptual leap over previous approaches. The authors propose decoupling spatial and temporal information into separate convolutional streams, with the spatial stream processing individual frames (using pre-trained ImageNet models) and the temporal stream processing optical flow to capture motion. This approach directly addresses a key limitation of prior deep learning methods for video action recognition (e.g., [14]), which attempted to learn spatio-temporal features from stacked frames but performed poorly compared to hand-crafted features.

The paper's main contribution is the conceptual novelty of the two-stream architecture, which draws inspiration from the two-streams hypothesis in visual neuroscience (ventral stream for object recognition, dorsal stream for motion). This is not merely a technical refinement but a fundamental rethinking of how to approach video recognition. The authors demonstrate that this architecture significantly outperforms previous deep learning approaches (by 20% on UCF101) and is competitive with state-of-the-art hand-crafted methods, despite being trained on relatively small datasets.

The paper is well-structured and clearly written. The authors effectively contextualize their work within the literature, carefully distinguishing their approach from previous methods like the two-stream HMAX model in [16] (which was hand-crafted and shallow) and the stacked-frame approaches in [14]. The experimental evaluation is thorough, comparing multiple input configurations for the temporal stream and demonstrating the complementary nature of the two streams through fusion. The multi-task learning approach for combining datasets (UCF101 and HMDB-51) is also a thoughtful technical contribution.

What makes this paper particularly compelling for NIPS is its bold conceptual approach. The authors didn't just tweak existing architectures—they proposed a fundamentally different way to decompose video recognition into two complementary streams, which has since become foundational in the field. This is the kind of novel idea that can redirect research in video recognition, rather than making an incremental improvement to an existing paradigm.

The paper's main limitations are relatively minor from a novelty perspective. The reliance on pre-computed optical flow (rather than end-to-end learning of motion) is a practical limitation but not a conceptual flaw. The authors acknowledge this as a limitation and note it as a direction for future work. The fusion strategy (late fusion via SVM or averaging) is simple but effective, and the authors appropriately acknowledge that more sophisticated fusion might yield further gains.

The significance of this work is substantial. The two-stream architecture has since become a standard baseline for video action recognition, with numerous follow-up works building upon this foundation. The paper demonstrates that deep learning can be competitive with hand-crafted features for video recognition, challenging the prevailing view that hand-crafted methods were superior for this task. The authors' insight that motion should be explicitly represented (rather than implicitly learned from stacked frames) was particularly prescient.

In terms of quality, the paper is technically sound with thorough experiments on standard datasets. The claims are well-supported by empirical evidence, and the authors are careful to acknowledge both the strengths of their approach and its limitations.

This paper represents a significant conceptual advance in video action recognition. The two-stream architecture is a bold, novel idea that fundamentally rethinks how to approach the problem, rather than making incremental improvements to existing methods. While the technical execution has some minor limitations (as is common in novel work), the core concept is sufficiently novel and impactful to warrant acceptance.

I recommend acceptance with the following considerations for authors (though not required for acceptance):
1. The paper could briefly acknowledge that their trajectory stacking approach (Section 3.1) is related to the trajectory-based features in [29], though this is already partially covered.
2. The paper might more explicitly discuss how their approach relates to the later work on 3D convolutions (e.g., [10, 12]), though this wasn't available at the time of writing.

Overall, this paper's novelty and conceptual contribution far outweigh the minor methodological limitations, and it represents a significant step forward in video recognition. The two-stream architecture has become foundational in the field, demonstrating the paper's lasting impact.
