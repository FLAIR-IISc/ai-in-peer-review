- Proposes a two-stream ConvNet that decouples spatial (appearance) and temporal (motion via stacked optical flow) information for video action recognition.
- Spatial stream: ConvNet trained/fine-tuned on ImageNet-pretrained still frames.
- Temporal stream: ConvNet trained on stacked dense optical flow fields, with various temporal input designs evaluated.
- Studies design choices: flow stacking vs. trajectory-aligned stacking, uni- vs. bi-directional flow, and mean flow subtraction.
- Fuses streams at score level using averaging or linear SVM; uses multi-task learning with dual softmax heads for UCF-101 and HMDB-51.
- Positions work between hand-crafted pipelines (dense trajectories+FVs) and prior RGB-stack deep models; makes motion explicit to improve learning.
- Argues early conv layers on flow approximate classical motion descriptors (HOF/MBH/kinematic features).
- Methodology is technically sound with well-motivated temporal input design and thorough ablation studies.
- Training details and data augmentation are clear and largely reproducible.
- Temporal ConvNet on flow significantly outperforms spatial-only and RGB-stack baselines on UCF-101 and HMDB-51.
- Two-stream fusion achieves competitive performance relative to hand-crafted methods; multi-task learning helps when data are scarce.
- Experimental protocol follows standard splits and averaging over crops/frames.
- Lacks systematic comparison to strongest shallow baselines (e.g., improved dense trajectories with camera compensation) and per-class/statistical analyses.
- Computational trade-offs (flow computation, storage, inference latency) are discussed qualitatively but not quantified end-to-end.
- Paper is clearly written, well organized, and provides precise temporal input and implementation details.
- Recommends reporting final selected number of stacked flows (L) alongside results and clarifying experiment changes.
- Suggests providing end-to-end runtime (training including flow precomputation, and per-video inference latency).
- Advises summarizing impact of JPEG compression of flow on accuracy.
- Two-stream with explicit optical-flow input is a novel, timely contribution; multi-task setup is effective.
- Paper offers practical impact and is likely to be widely used and extended toward end-to-end motion learning.
- Strengths: clear decomposition, strong ablations, practical multi-tasking, solid engineering, and links to classical motion descriptors.
- Weaknesses: reliance on precomputed flow (not end-to-end) with compute/storage overhead; needs quantified compute budgets.
- Limited gains from bidirectional/trajectory stacking indicate room for improved motion aggregation approaches.
- Fusion limited to late score fusion; recommends exploring feature-level/joint cross-stream training to reduce overfitting.
- Camera motion compensation is simplistic (mean subtraction); suggest stronger compensation (homography/affine) evaluations.
- Calls for per-class confusion/failure mode analysis, especially for camera-dominated actions.
- Open questions: sensitivity to flow accuracy and faster flow methods, effect of JPEG compression on small motions, and end-to-end inference time per video.
- Asks whether intermediate feature fusion beyond score fusion was attempted (e.g., conv5/fc concatenation).
- Reasons to accept: simple, novel, demonstrably effective approach with strong experiments and high impact potential.
- Reasons to reject: not end-to-end, heavy reliance on precomputed flow, limited compute/storage analysis, and incomplete fusion/trajectory modeling exploration.
- Overall recommendation: high-quality, influential contribution; recommends acceptance.
