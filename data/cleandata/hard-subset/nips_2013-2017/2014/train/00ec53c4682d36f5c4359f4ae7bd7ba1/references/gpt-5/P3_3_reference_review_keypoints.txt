- Proposes a two-stream ConvNet: spatial stream (RGB, ImageNet-pretrained) and temporal stream (stacked dense optical flow) for action recognition.  
- Temporal stream uses several input variants (fixed-location vs trajectory stacking, forward vs bi-directional flow) and mean flow subtraction to reduce camera motion.  
- Streams are fused at score level (averaging or linear SVM).  
- Uses multi-task learning with two softmax heads (UCF-101 and HMDB-51) to mitigate limited video data.  
- Temporal stream alone outperforms RGB-only ConvNets; fused two-stream is competitive with trajectory-based pipelines.  
- Extensive ablation studies validate design choices (flow stack length, stacking method, bi-directional flow, mean subtraction, fusion).  
- Builds on dense trajectories and stacked-RGB deep models; key novelty is explicitly feeding multi-frame dense optical flow to a ConvNet and linking first-layer filters to classical motion descriptors.  
- Clear, well-motivated decomposition into spatial and temporal streams with thorough empirical validation.  
- Strong performance gains over prior deep methods and competitiveness with improved trajectories despite small datasets.  
- Practical and effective multi-task training formulation for small datasets.  
- Good reproducibility: detailed training schedules, architectures, augmentation, and implementation notes.  
- Not end-to-end—relies on precomputed optical flow, introducing heavy precomputation/storage and deployment complexity.  
- Limited analysis of sensitivity to flow algorithm, flow quality, resolution, and flow quantization/compression effects.  
- Fusion limited to score-level averaging or linear SVM; no deeper joint or mid-level fusion explored due to overfitting concerns.  
- Camera motion handling reduced to mean flow subtraction; lacks explicit estimation/compensation used in shallow pipelines.  
- Evaluation restricted to trimmed UCF-101/HMDB-51 classification; no untrimmed video tests, per-class breakdowns, or robustness analyses.  
- Missing computational benchmarks (flow precompute time, inference speed, storage, latency) and statistical variance across runs.  
- Writing is generally clear and organized, with precise temporal input descriptions; minor reference/typo issues remain.  
- Two-stream with explicit optical flow is novel and influential; multi-task and mean-flow subtraction are pragmatic secondary contributions.  
- High significance: advances deep video recognition by showing explicit motion input is crucial and likely to influence follow-up work.  
- Suggested improvements: explore mid-level/shared fusion with regularization, report compute/storage metrics, test alternative flow methods, add camera motion modules, provide per-class/failure analyses, and assess untrimmed/generalization.  
- Arguments for acceptance: conceptually sound architecture, strong empirical gains, thorough ablations, and likely high impact.  
- Arguments against acceptance: heavy dependency on precomputed flow, limited fusion/motion compensation exploration, and missing robustness and compute analyses.  
- Recommendation: Accept.
