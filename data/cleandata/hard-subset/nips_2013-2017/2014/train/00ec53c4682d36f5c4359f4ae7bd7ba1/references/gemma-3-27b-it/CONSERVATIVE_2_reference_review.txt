This paper proposes a two-stream convolutional network architecture for action recognition in videos, decoupling spatial and temporal information processing into separate convolutional networks. The spatial stream operates on individual frames, leveraging pre-training on ImageNet, while the temporal stream processes dense optical flow. The outputs of the two streams are then fused for final classification.

The core idea of separating spatial and temporal streams is not entirely novel, drawing parallels to the “two-streams hypothesis” in visual cortex and previous work using HMAX models with separate ventral and dorsal streams [16]. However, the application of *deep* convolutional networks to both streams, combined with the ImageNet pre-training for the spatial stream and the focused use of optical flow for the temporal stream, represents an incremental contribution. The multi-task learning framework is also a sensible approach given the limited size of video datasets.

**Strengths:**

*   **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and organized, making the proposed method relatively easy to understand.
*   **Strong Baselines:** The comparison to the stacked frame CNN of [14] and various hand-crafted features [20, 21, 26] provides a reasonable evaluation context. The reported improvements over [14] are notable.
*   **Logical Architecture:** The decoupling of spatial and temporal information into separate streams is a reasonable design choice, particularly when combined with pre-training on ImageNet.
*   **Multi-task learning:** The proposed multi-task learning framework is a pragmatic solution for addressing limited training data.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Lack of Novelty:** The central idea, while well-executed, isn't particularly groundbreaking. It’s an application of existing techniques (CNNs, optical flow, pre-training) to a specific problem, rather than a fundamentally new approach. The paper acknowledges related work but doesn’t fully articulate *how* this contribution moves beyond it. Simply achieving better performance isn’t sufficient justification for publication at NIPS.
*   **Insufficient Ablation Studies:** While the paper explores some variations (L=5 vs L=10, trajectory stacking, bi-directional flow), the ablation studies feel incomplete. For instance, the effect of different pre-training datasets (beyond ImageNet) or more sophisticated fusion techniques isn't explored.
*   **Limited Analysis of Learned Features:** The paper mentions that the first-layer convolutional filters generalize hand-crafted features but provides no visual examples or quantitative analysis to support this claim. A more detailed investigation of what the temporal stream *learns* from optical flow would strengthen the contribution.
*   **Dependence on Optical Flow:** The method heavily relies on pre-computed optical flow, which introduces its own set of potential errors and computational costs. The paper briefly mentions efforts to compress the flow data but doesn’t address the potential impact of compression artifacts. A discussion of alternative motion representation learning techniques would be valuable.
*   **Overstated Significance:** While competitive, the performance doesn't demonstrably surpass the state-of-the-art hand-crafted methods by a large margin. The claim of being "competitive with the state of the art" feels slightly inflated given the relatively small gains.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Provides a solid, well-implemented baseline for future research in video action recognition using deep learning.
*   Demonstrates the effectiveness of combining pre-training with a dedicated temporal stream for capturing motion information.
*   The multi-task learning framework is a practical and effective technique.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   The core idea lacks significant novelty.
*   The paper doesn't provide sufficient analysis of learned features or a deeper understanding of the limitations of the approach.
*   The performance gains, while present, aren't substantial enough to warrant acceptance at a highly competitive conference like NIPS.
*   The dependence on pre-computed optical flow is a significant practical limitation.




**Overall Assessment:**

I lean towards **reject** for this paper. While technically sound and clearly presented, the lack of substantial novelty and the limited depth of analysis prevent it from meeting the high standards of NIPS. The work represents a solid engineering effort, but doesn’t offer a significant theoretical or methodological advance. More extensive ablation studies, a more in-depth analysis of learned representations, and a discussion of the limitations regarding optical flow computation are necessary to elevate this paper to an acceptable level for NIPS. The authors should consider submitting this work to a more application-focused conference.
