

# Peer Review: "Two-Stream Convolutional Networks for Action Recognition in Videos"

## Summary and Relation to Previous Work

This paper proposes a two-stream convolutional neural network architecture for video action recognition, which separates spatial and temporal information into distinct streams. The spatial stream processes individual video frames using a CNN pre-trained on ImageNet, while the temporal stream processes optical flow data to capture motion information. The authors argue that this approach is superior to previous methods that used stacked video frames as input to a single CNN (as in [14]), and demonstrate that their two-stream architecture outperforms previous deep learning approaches and is competitive with state-of-the-art shallow hand-crafted features.

The paper builds upon several lines of work:
- The two-stream hypothesis from neuroscience ([9]) which describes separate ventral (object recognition) and dorsal (motion recognition) pathways
- Previous deep learning approaches for video recognition ([11, 14, 16])
- Hand-crafted feature representations for action recognition ([17, 20, 21, 26])

Notably, the paper extends the work of [14] by decoupling spatial and temporal processing rather than using a single CNN on stacked frames. It also generalizes the trajectory-based hand-crafted features ([29, 26]) by using convolutional networks to learn representations from optical flow.

## Quality

The paper is technically sound with well-designed experiments. The authors provide thorough ablation studies to demonstrate the contribution of each component (optical flow stacking vs. trajectory stacking, bi-directional flow, mean flow subtraction, multi-task learning). The experimental results are clearly presented and compared against relevant baselines.

The methodology is well-justified and the implementation details are sufficiently detailed for reproducibility. The authors acknowledge limitations (e.g., optical flow precomputation being a bottleneck, not handling camera motion optimally) which demonstrates appropriate scientific honesty.

However, the paper lacks a theoretical explanation for why the two-stream approach outperforms single-stream approaches. While the empirical results are convincing, the authors do not provide insights into why the decoupled spatial-temporal processing is more effective than end-to-end learning of spatio-temporal features.

## Clarity

The paper is clearly written and well-organized. The introduction provides a good context for the problem, and the related work section effectively positions the paper within the field. The methodology is described in sufficient detail, with clear explanations of the optical flow input configurations and multi-task learning approach.

The figures (though not visible here) appear to be well-designed to illustrate the key concepts. The paper's structure follows a logical progression from problem statement to methodology to evaluation.

One area for improvement would be a more detailed explanation of the multi-task learning framework (Section 4), particularly regarding how the two softmax layers are implemented and how the gradients are backpropagated across tasks.

## Originality

The paper makes a significant contribution to the field by demonstrating that a two-stream CNN architecture is superior to single-stream approaches for action recognition. While the two-stream hypothesis is not new (from neuroscience), the application of deep CNNs to each stream is novel in the context of video action recognition.

The key innovations include:
1. The use of optical flow as input to a CNN for the temporal stream (rather than trying to learn motion from raw video frames)
2. The multi-task learning framework for combining training data from different datasets
3. The demonstration that temporal stream alone significantly outperforms spatial stream (and the combination is even better)

The paper properly situates itself within the field and cites relevant prior work. The comparison to previous deep learning approaches ([14, 16]) is particularly valuable, as it demonstrates the superiority of the two-stream approach.

## Significance

This work is highly significant for the field of video action recognition. The results show that the proposed two-stream CNN architecture outperforms previous deep learning methods by a substantial margin (14-20% absolute improvement) and is competitive with the best hand-crafted shallow representations.

The approach is simple yet effective, and the multi-task learning framework provides a principled way to handle small video datasets. The paper demonstrates that optical flow, when properly integrated with CNNs, is a powerful representation for action recognition.

The paper also identifies important directions for future work, including scaling to larger datasets (Sports-1M) and addressing limitations like explicit camera motion handling.

## Strengths

1. Clear, well-structured presentation of a novel architecture that significantly advances the state of the art
2. Comprehensive experimental evaluation with thorough ablation studies
3. Effective use of multi-task learning to address data scarcity in video action recognition
4. Demonstrates the importance of explicitly modeling motion (via optical flow) rather than trying to learn it implicitly
5. Provides a strong baseline for future work in video action recognition

## Weaknesses

1. Lacks theoretical justification for why the two-stream approach works better than single-stream approaches
2. Optical flow computation adds significant pre-processing complexity (though the authors acknowledge this)
3. Does not fully address limitations of optical flow (e.g., performance degradation with fast motion, occlusions)
4. The comparison to the most recent state-of-the-art (2014) might be somewhat dated by the time of NIPS publication
5. Does not discuss computational complexity in detail (beyond multi-GPU training)
6. The paper doesn't address how the method would scale to longer videos or more complex actions

## Arguments for Acceptance

- The paper presents a simple yet effective architecture that significantly advances the state of the art in video action recognition
- The two-stream approach is well-justified and supported by thorough experiments
- The multi-task learning framework is a valuable contribution for small video datasets
- The results are strong and demonstrate clear improvement over previous deep learning methods
- The paper is well-written and clearly organized
- The work has clear potential for influencing future research in video understanding

## Arguments against Acceptance

- The paper doesn't provide a theoretical explanation for the success of the two-stream approach
- The reliance on optical flow (which requires precomputation) adds complexity to the pipeline
- Some limitations of the approach (e.g., handling camera motion) are not fully addressed
- The paper doesn't discuss how the method would scale to larger datasets or more complex actions

## Overall Recommendation

This paper makes a significant contribution to the field of video action recognition. The two-stream architecture is a clear and effective approach that demonstrates substantial improvement over previous deep learning methods. The methodology is sound, the experiments are well-designed, and the results are compelling.

While there are some limitations (particularly regarding the theoretical justification and reliance on optical flow), these do not detract from the overall quality and significance of the work. The paper provides a strong foundation for future research in video action recognition and demonstrates clear practical value.

I recommend acceptance of this paper for NIPS.

## Additional Comments

1. The paper mentions "Very recently, [14] released the Sports-1M dataset" - this suggests the paper was written in 2014, which would be appropriate for NIPS 2014. However, the authors should clarify whether they have since trained on this larger dataset (as they mention planning to do so in future work).

2. The authors should consider discussing how their approach might be extended to handle camera motion more explicitly, rather than just using mean flow subtraction.

3. A brief discussion of the computational complexity (inference time and memory requirements) would be valuable for practitioners.

4. The paper would benefit from a comparison to more recent approaches that might have emerged after the initial submission (though this is a common limitation for conference papers).

This is a strong, well-executed paper that makes a meaningful contribution to the field. It is clear that the authors have put significant effort into developing and evaluating their approach, and the results demonstrate a clear advance over previous work.
