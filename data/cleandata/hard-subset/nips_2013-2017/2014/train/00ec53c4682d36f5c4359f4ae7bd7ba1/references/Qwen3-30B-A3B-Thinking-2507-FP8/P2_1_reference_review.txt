

# Review of "Two-Stream Convolutional Networks for Action Recognition in Videos"

## Summary of Main Ideas

This paper introduces a two-stream convolutional network architecture for video action recognition, inspired by the two-stream hypothesis in neuroscience (ventral/dorsal visual pathways). The authors propose separating video recognition into two streams: a spatial stream that processes individual frames (like standard image classification) and a temporal stream that processes optical flow between frames. The key innovation is using dense optical flow as input to a ConvNet for the temporal stream, rather than stacking raw RGB frames as in prior work. The authors demonstrate that this approach significantly outperforms single-stream architectures (like [14]) and achieves competitive results with state-of-the-art shallow representations [20, 21, 26]. They also introduce a multi-task learning framework to effectively combine training data from multiple action recognition datasets.

This work builds on prior two-stream approaches like [16], which used shallow HMAX models for spatial and temporal streams, but extends them to deep ConvNets. It also addresses limitations of [14], which found that stacked-frame networks performed poorly compared to hand-crafted features. The paper provides a thorough experimental evaluation on UCF101 and HMDB-51 datasets, including ablation studies on different optical flow input configurations.

## Quality Assessment

**Strengths:**
- The paper presents a well-structured experimental evaluation with comprehensive ablation studies on different optical flow input configurations (L=5 vs. L=10, trajectory vs. optical flow stacking, mean subtraction, bi-directional flow).
- The multi-task learning framework for combining datasets (UCF101 and HMDB-51) is well-motivated and demonstrates clear benefits over simpler approaches like class-based dataset combination.
- The implementation details are thorough and reproducible (including GPU training details, flow computation optimization, and augmentation techniques).
- The results are statistically significant and demonstrate clear improvements over relevant baselines (e.g., 81.5% vs. 81.0% on UCF101 with multi-task learning).

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a theoretical justification for why the two-stream approach works better than single-stream architectures. While they observe that the temporal stream significantly outperforms the spatial stream, they don't explain *why* the explicit motion representation via optical flow is more effective than implicit motion learning.
- The comparison to [14] is somewhat limited; the authors don't directly re-implement [14]'s architecture to show the exact performance gap, though they mention the 20% accuracy difference.
- The paper doesn't provide error analysis or qualitative examples showing what actions the model gets right/wrong, which would strengthen the evaluation.

## Clarity Assessment

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-organized with clear sections and logical flow from problem statement to experimental results.
- The figures (Fig. 1, 2, 3) effectively illustrate the architecture and input configurations.
- The optical flow input configurations are described in sufficient detail with equations (1) and (2).
- The multi-task learning framework (Section 4) is clearly explained.

**Weaknesses:**
- The motivation for mean flow subtraction could be better explained. While they mention it helps with global motion compensation, they don't provide evidence of how much global motion affects performance.
- The relation to prior work (Section 3.2) is good but could be more explicit about how their approach differs from [16] (which also used two streams but with shallow HMAX models).
- Some terms like "slow fusion" (Section 6) are introduced without clear explanation for readers unfamiliar with [14].

## Originality Assessment

**Strengths:**
- The specific application of ConvNets to optical flow for video action recognition is novel. While two-stream architectures have been proposed before [16], this is the first to use deep ConvNets for both streams.
- The paper makes a clear contribution by showing that deep learning can be successfully applied to video action recognition with a two-stream approach, addressing a limitation of prior deep learning work [14].
- The multi-task learning framework for combining datasets is a useful technical contribution that could be applied to other video recognition tasks.

**Weaknesses:**
- The core idea of separating spatial and temporal information for video recognition is not entirely new (it's inspired by neuroscience and has been previously attempted with shallow models [16]).
- The paper doesn't fully explore how their approach relates to the "deep matching" approach of [30] which also uses optical flow but in a different manner.
- The paper is somewhat incremental in that it extends existing two-stream ideas to deep learning rather than proposing a fundamentally new architecture.

## Significance Assessment

**Strengths:**
- The results are significant as they demonstrate that deep learning approaches can achieve competitive results with state-of-the-art shallow representations for video action recognition.
- The paper shows that using optical flow as input to a ConvNet (rather than stacked frames) is a key improvement over prior deep learning approaches.
- The multi-task learning framework is valuable for video recognition tasks where datasets are small, and this technique could be broadly applicable.
- The work addresses an important problem in computer vision (video action recognition) with a practical and effective solution.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper doesn't fully address the computational cost of optical flow computation (though they note they pre-compute it).
- The authors mention they plan to train on Sports-1M in future work but don't provide any results or analysis on this larger dataset, which would have been a significant contribution.
- The paper doesn't explore how their approach might scale to more complex actions or longer videos.

## Overall Assessment

This is a strong paper that makes a meaningful contribution to video action recognition. The two-stream architecture with optical flow for the temporal stream is well-motivated, well-executed, and produces competitive results. The multi-task learning framework is a valuable technical contribution. While the paper has some incremental aspects compared to prior two-stream work, it significantly advances the state of the art for deep learning approaches to video action recognition.

The paper is technically sound, well-written, and provides a clear demonstration of the effectiveness of the proposed approach. The results are significant and would be of interest to the NeurIPS community working on video understanding.

## Recommendation

I recommend acceptance of this paper. It makes a clear contribution to the field with a well-executed approach that demonstrates significant improvements over prior deep learning work on video action recognition. The paper is of high quality, well-written, and addresses an important problem in computer vision.

## Specific Comments for Authors

1. Consider adding a brief theoretical discussion of why explicit motion representation via optical flow is better than implicit motion learning in ConvNets.

2. Provide more context on the "mean flow subtraction" technique. How much does it improve performance compared to not using it? What is the typical magnitude of the mean flow vector?

3. Include qualitative examples showing successful and failed predictions to help readers understand the model's strengths and weaknesses.

4. In Section 3.2, more explicitly contrast your approach with [16] (which also used two streams but with shallow HMAX models) to highlight the novelty of using deep ConvNets.

5. Consider adding a brief discussion of how your approach might handle the computational cost of optical flow computation at test time, which could be a limitation for real-world applications.

6. The paper mentions that "the temporal ConvNet, trained using multi-task learning, performs the best both alone and when fused with a spatial net." It would be helpful to include a brief analysis of why multi-task learning is particularly beneficial for the temporal stream (which is trained on smaller datasets).

This is a high-quality paper that makes a meaningful contribution to video recognition. I believe it would be a valuable addition to the NeurIPS proceedings.
