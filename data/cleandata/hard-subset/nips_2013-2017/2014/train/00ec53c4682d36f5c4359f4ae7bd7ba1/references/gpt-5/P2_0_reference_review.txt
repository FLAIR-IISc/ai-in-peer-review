Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes a two-stream convolutional architecture for video action recognition, explicitly separating spatial appearance and temporal motion. The spatial stream is a ConvNet applied to RGB frames and pre-trained on ImageNet; the temporal stream is a ConvNet applied to stacked dense optical flow fields computed between consecutive frames. Several variants of the temporal input are evaluated (optical-flow stacking vs. trajectory stacking; uni- vs. bi-directional flow; mean-flow subtraction). The two streams are fused at the score level (average or linear SVM). To mitigate limited video data, the authors use multi-task learning with two separate softmax heads (UCF101 and HMDB51) sharing all lower layers for the temporal network.

This work is positioned against prior deep video models that operate on stacked RGB frames and often fail to learn strong motion features (e.g., Karpathy et al., “slow fusion”), as well as classical shallow pipelines based on dense trajectories with HOF/MBH and Fisher vectors. The paper argues that explicitly providing motion via optical flow simplifies learning and can subsume hand-crafted flow-derived features.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The methodology is clear and sensible. Using dense optical flow as input to a ConvNet directly targets motion, addressing a known weakness of RGB-only models. The ablation studies are appropriate: effect of L (number of stacked flows), mean-flow subtraction (camera motion), trajectory vs. location stacking, bi-directional flow, and fusion methods. The training/testing protocols are standard (three splits, 25 frames per video, 10 crops). Multi-task training is well defined (shared backbone, task-specific softmax/loss).
- Empirical support: Results on UCF101 and HMDB51 are reported with comparisons to prior deep models and state-of-the-art shallow baselines. The temporal stream substantially outperforms RGB-only deep models; the fused two-stream system is competitive with improved trajectories. The study includes relevant implementation details (data augmentation, learning schedules, GPU setup) aiding reproducibility.
- Limitations: The approach relies on precomputed optical flow (Brox flow), so the system is not end-to-end and inherits flow algorithm biases; there is no analysis of sensitivity to the flow method or to flow quality. Runtime/throughput and practical inference costs (25×10 crops per stream plus flow computation) are not quantitatively reported. The choice of late fusion (vs. joint fusion layers) is justified by overfitting, but alternatives (e.g., stronger regularization, weight sharing, or lightweight fusion heads) are not explored. Error analysis and qualitative failure modes are limited.

Clarity
The paper is clearly written and well organized. The temporal input formulations are precisely specified with equations and good intuition. The ablation narrative is easy to follow. Implementation details (data augmentation, learning rate schedules, dataset splits) are included. Minor suggestions:
- Provide a brief quantitative estimate of test-time computational cost (flow + forward passes).
- Clarify whether any class-imbalance handling was used in multi-task training and whether overlapping semantics across datasets caused conflicts (the separate heads likely avoid label collisions, but a sentence confirming this would help).

Originality
The two-stream ConvNet with an explicit optical flow input is a novel and impactful architectural choice at the time, distinct from prior deep models attempting to learn motion implicitly from RGB stacks. The specific temporal input designs (stacking along time, trajectory stacking, mean-flow subtraction, bi-directional flow) are thoughtful and connect to classical descriptors, with clear analysis of which matter most. Multi-task learning for video ConvNets is not novel in principle, but its application here is appropriate and effective. Score-level SVM fusion is standard.

Significance
Addressing action recognition by decoupling spatial and temporal processing and explicitly feeding flow substantially improves deep baselines and yields results competitive with leading hand-crafted pipelines, thereby providing a practical and influential blueprint. Given the difficulty of learning motion features from RGB alone, this paper is likely to shape subsequent architectures and benchmarks. The empirical insights (e.g., stacking > single flow, mean subtraction helps, optical-flow stacking > trajectory stacking) are useful to practitioners. The approach’s reliance on flow computation is a trade-off but acceptable given the gains.

Strengths
- Clear, principled architecture that directly targets motion with strong empirical benefits.
- Thorough ablation studies isolating design choices in the temporal stream.
- Solid experimental protocol on standard datasets; competitive with state of the art at the time.
- Practical multi-task strategy to leverage limited video labels.
- Good implementation transparency (data augmentation, schedules, GPU training).

Weaknesses
- Not end-to-end; depends on a specific optical flow algorithm without sensitivity analysis.
- Computational cost and latency (flow + two-stream inference with multi-crop) not quantified.
- Fusion limited to late score fusion; no exploration of more integrated fusion with stronger regularization.
- Limited qualitative/error analysis and discussion of robustness (e.g., camera motion beyond mean subtraction).

Questions for the authors
- How sensitive is performance to the choice of optical flow method and its parameters?
- What is the end-to-end inference throughput per video on your hardware (including flow)?
- Did you try joint fine-tuning of a small fusion head over concatenated fc features with stronger regularization (e.g., dropout, weight decay), or bottleneck fusion?
- In multi-task training, did you observe any negative transfer for specific classes? How were batch compositions balanced across tasks?

Arguments for acceptance
- Advances deep video recognition substantially by a simple, effective architectural choice.
- Strong empirical gains with clear, reproducible methodology.
- Insightful ablations that inform future work and practice.
- High likely impact as a new baseline.

Arguments against acceptance
- Heavy reliance on precomputed flow and unclear runtime may limit practicality.
- Lack of sensitivity analysis to the flow method and limited analysis of failure modes.
- Fusion remains relatively simple; potential for further gains not explored.

Recommendation: Accept (strong). The paper makes a clear, well-supported contribution that advances the field and is likely to be widely adopted and extended.
