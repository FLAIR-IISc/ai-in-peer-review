Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes a two-stream convolutional architecture for video action recognition that explicitly decouples appearance and motion. The spatial stream is a ConvNet trained on RGB frames (pre-trained on ImageNet and fine-tuned), while the temporal stream is a ConvNet trained on stacked dense optical flow fields. Several input variants for the temporal stream are explored: stacking forward flow, trajectory-aligned stacking, bidirectional flow, and mean-flow subtraction to mitigate camera motion. A multi-task learning setup is introduced to combine UCF-101 and HMDB-51 without manual class de-duplication by adding dataset-specific softmax heads and summing losses. The two streams are fused at score level (averaging or a linear SVM). Experiments on UCF-101 and HMDB-51 show: (i) temporal > spatial when trained on the same data; (ii) fusion improves substantially; (iii) optical-flow-based temporal ConvNets outperform stacked-RGB ConvNets; (iv) multi-task training boosts performance on small datasets. Results are competitive with trajectory-based shallow pipelines and significantly better than previous deep models using stacked frames (e.g., Karpathy et al.), and the design is connected to the classic two-stream hypothesis in vision.

Quality
The methodology is sound and carefully evaluated. The ablations in the temporal stream convincingly support design choices: stacking multiple flows (L=10) outperforms single-frame flow; mean subtraction helps; optical-flow stacking slightly outperforms trajectory stacking; bidirectional flow offers only marginal gains. The multi-task learning formulation is simple and effective, improving HMDB and giving a small gain on UCF. Fusion via a linear SVM is consistently better than simple averaging. Training and testing protocols, data augmentation, schedules, and architectures are specified in enough detail to reproduce. The comparison baselines include strong prior deep methods and standard shallow pipelines. Limitations are acknowledged (e.g., not end-to-end; missing trajectory-aligned pooling; limited camera motion handling). One area where the evaluation could be strengthened is runtime/compute analysis at inference, given the need to precompute optical flow and process 25 frames × 10 crops per video; still, the paper provides practical details (Brox flow, preprocessing, compression, multi-GPU training).

Clarity
The paper is clearly written and well organized. The temporal input configurations are precisely defined with equations and intuitive figures. Training/testing protocols and implementation choices are described concretely (network variant, augmentation, learning-rate schedules, multi-GPU, precomputation of flow). The relation to prior work is comprehensive and accurate, and the authors explain how their temporal ConvNet can subsume hand-crafted HOF/MBH features. Minor suggestions: report exact optical flow parameters used (if not defaults), specify SVM regularization settings for fusion, and summarize results inline when tables are referenced.

Originality
The core novelty lies in: (i) a clean two-stream ConvNet architecture that explicitly separates appearance and motion; (ii) feeding stacked dense optical flow directly to a ConvNet as the motion representation; (iii) a simple yet effective multi-task setup for combining heterogeneous video datasets. While optical flow and two-stream ideas have prior art (e.g., HMAX two-stream), and stacking frames in ConvNets is known, integrating flow stacking into a discriminatively trained deep net with careful ablation and demonstrating clear gains over stacked RGB is new and impactful. The mean-flow subtraction is a pragmatic twist aligning with prior camera-motion compensation but adapted to the ConvNet input. Overall, the contribution is a novel and influential combination rather than a brand-new primitive.

Significance
Action recognition is important and challenging; prior deep approaches lagged behind trajectory-based shallow pipelines. This work narrows that gap and, with fusion, reaches competitive performance while advancing deep architectures for video. The approach is likely to be adopted by practitioners because it is conceptually simple, leverages standard ConvNets, and delivers strong gains. The multi-task strategy is broadly useful for small video datasets. The explicit finding that optical-flow inputs outperform stacked RGB for motion modeling is a clear, actionable insight. Despite not being end-to-end, the contribution advances the state of the art and sets a strong baseline that subsequent work can build on (e.g., learning flow, trajectory-aware pooling, larger-scale training).

Strengths
- Clear, well-justified architecture that aligns with known vision pathways and practices.
- Thorough ablations isolating what matters in temporal inputs and fusion.
- Strong empirical gains over prior deep baselines; competitive with best shallow methods.
- Practical multi-task learning to exploit multiple datasets without manual label curation.
- Reproducible implementation details and pragmatic engineering (multi-GPU, flow compression).
- Insightful analysis showing why stacked RGB often fails to capture motion well.

Weaknesses and questions
- Not end-to-end: dependence on precomputed optical flow is a computational bottleneck at scale; no analysis of test-time throughput.
- Limited camera motion handling (mean subtraction only); stronger compensation might help further.
- The trajectory-aligned pooling concept from shallow pipelines is not integrated; authors note this.
- Evaluation remains on small datasets; plans for large-scale Sports-1M are future work.
- Missing specifics: exact Brox flow parameters and SVM fusion hyperparameters; no error bars or statistical significance tests.
- Theoretical understanding of why bidirectional flow provides little benefit is not analyzed.

Pro/Con arguments for acceptance
Pros:
- Significant empirical advance for deep video recognition with strong, reproducible evidence.
- Novel and simple design that others can readily use and extend.
- Careful ablation and comprehensive related work situating the contribution.
- Multi-task strategy addresses a real practical constraint (small datasets).

Cons:
- Relies on precomputed optical flow; not end-to-end and potentially expensive.
- Some components are incremental (fusion, architecture), and novelty rests on the combination.
- Limited analysis of computational cost and scalability at inference.

Overall rating: Accept (high)
Justification: The paper is technically solid, clearly written, and offers a simple, well-validated idea that materially advances deep action recognition. The experimental evidence is strong and the insights are likely to influence subsequent research and practice. The reliance on optical flow is a pragmatic trade-off rather than a flaw, and the authors are transparent about limitations and future directions.
