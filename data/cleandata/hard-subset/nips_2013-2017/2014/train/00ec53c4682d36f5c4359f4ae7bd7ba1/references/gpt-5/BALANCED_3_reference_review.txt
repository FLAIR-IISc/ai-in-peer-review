Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes a two-stream convolutional architecture for video action recognition that explicitly decouples appearance and motion. A spatial stream ConvNet classifies individual RGB frames (pre-trained on ImageNet and fine-tuned), while a temporal stream ConvNet operates on stacked dense optical flow fields computed between adjacent frames. The two streams are fused by late score fusion (averaging or a linear SVM). The paper systematically studies different temporal inputs (flow stacking length, trajectory-aligned stacking, bi-directional flow, mean displacement subtraction) and introduces a simple multi-task learning setup to jointly train on UCF101 and HMDB-51 to mitigate data scarcity. On both datasets, the temporal stream substantially outperforms RGB-only ConvNets, and fusing the streams yields performance comparable to state-of-the-art hand-crafted pipelines (e.g., Improved Dense Trajectories with Fisher vectors), and significantly better than previous deep models that consume raw frames or short frame stacks (e.g., Karpathy et al.; Ji et al.). Conceptually, the approach echoes the two-stream hypothesis in human vision and relates to earlier two-pathway HMAX work, but uses deeper, discriminatively trained ConvNets. The temporal input design generalizes motion features like HOF/MBH and trajectory descriptors, and the authors provide architectural and training details (Caffe-based, multi-GPU) and thorough ablations.

Strengths
- Clear, principled decoupling of spatial and temporal cues, aligning with neuroscientific two-stream hypotheses but operationalized in a modern ConvNet framework.
- Strong empirical gains over frame-based ConvNets and raw multi-frame inputs; temporal stream with stacked optical flow is convincingly more effective.
- Thorough ablations: effect of flow stacking length (L), trajectory vs. fixed-location stacking, bi-directional flow, mean subtraction; and of pre-training and fine-tuning for the spatial stream.
- Practical multi-task learning strategy to exploit multiple datasets without manual de-duplication of classes; clear improvements on HMDB-51.
- Competitive results vs. the best hand-crafted pipelines, narrowing the gap between deep and engineered features at the time.
- Reproducibility-oriented details: network architecture, training schedules, sampling/augmentation, multi-GPU speedups, and handling/storage of optical flow.

Weaknesses
- Reliance on external, pre-computed optical flow breaks end-to-end learning and makes the system computationally expensive and potentially brittle to flow errors; latency and resource costs are not fully quantified for deployment.
- The temporal architecture is relatively shallow (CNN-M) and does not explore architectures tailored for temporal modeling beyond stacking; no temporal pooling beyond standard ConvNet layers, no recurrent/temporal aggregation beyond uniform sampling and averaging.
- Trajectory-aware pooling is acknowledged as missing; experiments suggest trajectory stacking underperforms fixed-location stacking, but the paper does not try trajectory-aligned pooling in the network.
- Camera motion compensation is reduced to mean flow subtraction; more principled compensation (as in IDT) is not integrated.
- Fusion is limited to softmax averaging/SVM; comparisons to feature-level fusion or joint fully-connected layers are dismissed due to overfitting without strong regularization attempts.
- Evaluation is limited to UCF101 and HMDB-51; no experiments on larger-scale datasets (e.g., Sports-1M) or untrimmed videos; no analysis of per-class failures or robustness to viewpoint/camera motion.

Quality
Overall technically sound with well-justified design choices. Experiments are careful, include strong baselines, and provide ablations that support the claims. Some methodological choices (e.g., not attempting stronger regularization to enable joint fusion; optical flow parameter settings and their effect) could be elaborated. The multi-task formulation is straightforward but effective.

Clarity
Well written and organized. The description of temporal inputs and relation to prior hand-crafted features is clear. Minor clarity gaps: exact optical flow parameters (pyramid levels, smoothness weights) and their sensitivity; details of SVM fusion (regularization, calibration); precise frame sampling rate; computational cost at test time.

Originality
Moderately high. The central novelty is the explicit two-stream ConvNet with stacked optical flow as input, which is a simple but impactful shift from learning motion implicitly. Multi-task learning per se is standard, but its application here is appropriate. The work thoughtfully connects and generalizes several prior ideas (HOF/MBH, trajectories, two-stream vision).

Significance
High. The approach demonstrably advances deep learning for video action recognition, providing a practical recipe that others can adopt and build on. It bridges a substantial performance gap to hand-crafted pipelines and is likely to influence subsequent work on motion representations and two-stream architectures.

Suggestions for improvement
- Explore stronger regularization to enable joint fusion beyond score-level (e.g., dropout, batch normalization, weight decay, data augmentation) or use a modest bottleneck to fuse fc features without overfitting.
- Quantify computational cost: training/inference time per video, storage and latency of flow computation, and compare to IDT pipelines.
- Provide sensitivity analysis to optical flow algorithm/parameters, and evaluate robustness to flow noise; consider approximate/learnable flow or warping layers to move toward end-to-end training.
- Investigate trajectory-aligned pooling within the ConvNet (e.g., pooling along estimated trajectories) and more principled camera motion compensation.
- Evaluate on an additional dataset or untrimmed settings, or provide cross-dataset generalization results.
- Release code/models and pre-processing scripts to aid reproducibility.

Questions for authors
- What exact optical flow parameters were used, and how sensitive are results to them?
- How long (wall-clock) does inference take per test video including flow, and what is the GPU/CPU footprint?
- Did you try feature-level fusion with stronger regularization (e.g., higher dropout, L2/L1 penalties) or smaller fusion heads?
- How does performance vary with different frame sampling strategies and temporal stride?

Arguments for acceptance
- Clear, simple, and effective idea with strong empirical gains over prior deep baselines.
- Thorough ablations and analysis that illuminate why it works.
- Competitive with state-of-the-art non-deep methods; likely to be influential.

Arguments against acceptance
- Heavy reliance on pre-computed flow; not end-to-end and computationally costly.
- Limited exploration of temporal modeling and fusion alternatives beyond stacking and score averaging.
- Evaluation scope limited to two standard datasets.

Recommendation
Accept. Despite some limitations, the paper makes a compelling, well-evaluated contribution that advances deep video recognition and will be of broad interest to the NeurIPS community.
