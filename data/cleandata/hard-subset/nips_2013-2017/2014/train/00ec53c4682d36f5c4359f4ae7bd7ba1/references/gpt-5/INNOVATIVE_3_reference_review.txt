Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper proposes a two-stream ConvNet architecture for action recognition that decouples spatial (appearance) and temporal (motion) processing. The spatial stream is a standard image ConvNet pre-trained on ImageNet; the temporal stream takes stacked dense optical flow fields as input, with several variants (flow stacking, trajectory stacking, bi-directional flow, mean-flow subtraction). The two streams are fused by averaging or an SVM on softmax scores. Because video datasets are small, the paper further introduces a simple multi-task training setup that shares the network across UCF-101 and HMDB-51 via dual softmax heads.

This directly contrasts with prior deep video models that operate on stacks of RGB frames (e.g., 3D ConvNets or “slow fusion”), and it connects to the two-stream hypothesis in neuroscience as well as to handcrafted pipelines that leverage optical flow, HOF/MBH, and dense trajectories. The temporal stream is argued to subsume classical motion descriptors through learned convolutions over explicit flow inputs. Empirically, on UCF-101 and HMDB-51, the temporal net outperforms spatial-only models; the fused model is competitive with trajectory-based state-of-the-art shallow methods, and clearly surpasses prior deep approaches.

Strengths:
- Clear, bold architectural idea: explicitly ingest optical flow in a dedicated temporal stream rather than hoping spatiotemporal filters over RGB implicitly learn motion. This inductive bias is simple, powerful, and well-motivated.
- Solid empirical evidence: thorough ablations show the value of stacking L>1 flows, mean subtraction, and the relative (small) effects of trajectory vs flow stacking and bi-directional flow. Two-stream fusion adds substantial gains (≈6% over temporal alone).
- Practical training insights: spatial pretraining on ImageNet; multi-task learning to exploit multiple small video datasets; implementation details (sampling, cropping, normalization, multi-GPU).
- Good positioning vs prior work: relates to HMAX two-stream, 3D ConvNets, trajectory-based pipelines, and provides a coherent argument that learned ConvNets generalize handcrafted motion features.

Weaknesses and questions:
- The approach depends on precomputed optical flow outside the network, breaking end-to-end learning and introducing a substantial computational/storage bottleneck. Sensitivity to the flow algorithm and its parameters (e.g., Brox vs TV-L1 or DeepFlow) is not explored.
- The evaluation is limited to UCF-101/HMDB-51; while standard, they are small. Results on larger, noisier datasets (e.g., Sports-1M) are deferred; some evidence on scalability/generalization would strengthen claims.
- Fusion is by score-level averaging/SVM; attempts at joint fusion over shared FC layers failed due to overfitting. Can the authors regularize or parameterize fusion better (e.g., learnable gating/attention, feature normalization, or low-rank adapters) to enable end-to-end joint training?
- Camera motion compensation is approximated by mean flow subtraction; no analysis of failure modes (e.g., strong panning/zoom) or explicit camera-motion modeling.
- Limited comparison to the strongest handcrafted pipeline when combined with ConvNet scores. A hybrid IDT-FV + two-stream fusion is a natural baseline and likely to be state-of-the-art.
- Some experimental details could be clarified: how was L=10 selected beyond the L=5 vs 10 comparison? How robust is performance to the JPEG quantization of flow? How are the per-task losses weighted in multi-task training (equal, or dataset-size-normalized)?

Quality:
- Technically sound with careful empirical study and ablations that support the main claims. While the optical-flow precomputation is a practical compromise, it is clearly documented. Missing analyses (flow algorithm sensitivity, hybrid fusion with IDT) are notable but not fatal.

Clarity:
- The paper is clearly written and well organized. The motivation, architectural choices, and training/testing protocols are easy to follow. The relation to prior work is thorough and fair. Figures are useful.

Originality:
- High. The two-stream ConvNet with explicit optical-flow inputs is a conceptually clean and novel departure from prevalent RGB-stacking approaches. The multi-task strategy for small video datasets is a practical and useful twist.

Significance:
- High. The idea is likely to influence how the community treats motion in deep models (explicit vs implicit), and provides a strong baseline that narrows the gap with handcrafted pipelines. The approach is simple enough to be widely adopted.

Pros for acceptance:
- Introduces a new and effective architectural decomposition (two streams) and a pragmatic way to inject motion via stacked flow.
- Strong empirical improvements over prior deep baselines and competitive with SOTA shallow methods.
- Thorough ablations; practical training recipe (pretraining + multi-task) with clear impact.
- Conceptual clarity and potential to spur follow-up (end-to-end flow, better fusion, larger-scale training).

Cons for acceptance:
- Not end-to-end; heavy dependence on an external, potentially brittle optical flow module and large storage.
- Evaluation scope limited to two small datasets; no results on larger-scale, noisy data.
- Limited exploration of stronger fusion strategies and of combining with IDT-FV.
- Sensitivity to flow algorithm/parameters and camera motion not analyzed.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Explore robustness to different flow algorithms and parameter settings; report compute/storage trade-offs and accuracy sensitivity.
- Attempt feature-level fusion with stronger regularization (e.g., dropout schedules, weight decay, bottleneck layers) or attention-based fusion.
- Combine two-stream scores with state-of-the-art IDT-FV to establish an upper-bound hybrid baseline.
- Provide per-class analyses, especially for actions where appearance dominates vs where motion is decisive.
- If possible, add experiments on a subset of a larger dataset to assess scalability/generalization.
- Consider light-weight camera motion compensation beyond mean subtraction (e.g., global homography/affine estimation).
- Release code and pre-processing scripts to maximize reproducibility and impact.

Recommendation: Accept. The paper introduces a compelling, novel direction—explicit motion via a temporal ConvNet and a two-stream fusion—that delivers clear gains and is likely to shape subsequent work. Despite some missing analyses and reliance on precomputed flow, the contribution is bold, timely, and significant.
