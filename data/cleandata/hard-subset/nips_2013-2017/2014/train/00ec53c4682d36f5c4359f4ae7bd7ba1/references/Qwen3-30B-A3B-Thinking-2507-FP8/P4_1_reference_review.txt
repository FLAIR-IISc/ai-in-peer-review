

# Review of "Two-Stream Convolutional Networks for Action Recognition in Videos"

This paper proposes a two-stream convolutional network architecture for video action recognition, addressing a significant challenge in computer vision. The authors decompose video recognition into spatial (appearance-based) and temporal (motion-based) streams, each implemented as a deep ConvNet. The spatial stream processes individual video frames (with pre-training on ImageNet), while the temporal stream processes dense optical flow. The authors demonstrate that these streams are complementary, and their fusion significantly outperforms previous deep learning approaches for video recognition. The paper relates to prior work on shallow video representations [17, 20, 26] and earlier deep learning attempts [14, 16], noting that [14] found stacked-frame ConvNets underperformed hand-crafted methods, while [16] used shallow HMAX models for two streams. The authors' key innovation is using deep ConvNets for both streams with optical flow as input to the temporal stream, rather than stacked RGB frames.

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound with comprehensive experimental evaluation. The authors provide clear ablation studies (Table 1) showing the impact of different optical flow configurations, demonstrating that multi-frame optical flow stacking (L=10) outperforms single-frame flow (L=1) and trajectory stacking. The multi-task learning approach (Section 4) is well-justified and effective for combining datasets with class overlap, as evidenced by the improved results in Table 2. The implementation details (Section 5) are sufficiently thorough for reproducibility, including training procedures, data augmentation, and optical flow computation. The paper appropriately acknowledges limitations, such as the need for future work on handling camera motion and trajectory pooling.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-organized and clearly written. The two-stream architecture is effectively illustrated (Fig. 1) and explained in Section 2. The optical flow input configurations (Section 3.1) are precisely defined with mathematical formulations (equations 1-2), supported by visual examples (Fig. 3). The comparison to prior work (Section 3.2) is thorough, showing how the temporal ConvNet generalizes hand-crafted features like HOF and MBH. The experimental section (Section 6) is logically structured, with clear results presented in tables and appropriate statistical comparisons.

**Originality:** The paper makes a clear contribution by applying deep ConvNets to both spatial and temporal streams, with optical flow as the temporal input rather than stacked RGB frames. This addresses a key limitation identified in [14] where stacked-frame networks underperformed. While the two-stream concept is inspired by neuroscience [9], the authors' implementation using deep networks is novel in the video recognition context. The paper distinguishes itself from prior deep learning work [12, 14, 16] by demonstrating that explicit motion representation (via optical flow) significantly outperforms implicit motion learning from stacked frames. The multi-task learning approach for dataset combination is a practical contribution, though not the primary novelty.

**Significance:** The results are highly significant, demonstrating that the two-stream architecture achieves competitive performance with state-of-the-art shallow methods [20, 21, 26] while using deep learning. The paper's approach has become foundational in video action recognition, influencing numerous subsequent works. The authors successfully address a critical problem in the field—how to effectively incorporate temporal information in deep learning frameworks for video. The paper provides a strong baseline that has been widely adopted and extended, advancing the state of the art in video recognition.

**Strengths:**
- Clear, well-structured presentation of the two-stream architecture
- Comprehensive experimental evaluation with ablation studies
- Effective use of multi-task learning to address dataset size limitations
- Strong empirical results showing significant improvement over prior deep learning approaches
- Good theoretical grounding in relating to neuroscience and prior computer vision work

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper doesn't fully explore why the temporal stream outperforms the spatial stream so dramatically (beyond stating motion is important)
- Limited discussion of the optical flow computation method compared to alternatives
- The connection between the two-stream architecture and the neuroscience hypothesis [9] is mentioned but not deeply explored

**Pro and Con Arguments for Acceptance:**

Pro:
- The two-stream architecture with deep ConvNets for both streams is a significant contribution to video action recognition
- The paper provides a clear, reproducible methodology with strong empirical validation
- Results demonstrate substantial improvement over previous deep learning approaches for video recognition
- The work has had lasting impact on the field, with the two-stream approach becoming standard

Con:
- The paper doesn't sufficiently explore why the two-stream approach works better than single-stream alternatives
- Some details about the optical flow computation could be more thoroughly addressed
- The multi-task learning approach is standard, so not particularly novel

Overall, this paper makes a substantial contribution to video action recognition by introducing a novel two-stream architecture that significantly outperforms prior deep learning approaches. The work is technically sound, well-written, and has had lasting impact on the field. The results are clearly presented with appropriate comparisons to relevant prior work. The paper meets all four NIPS criteria with excellence and should be accepted.
