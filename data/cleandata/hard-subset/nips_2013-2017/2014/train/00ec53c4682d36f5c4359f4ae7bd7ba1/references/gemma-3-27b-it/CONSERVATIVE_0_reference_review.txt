This paper proposes a two-stream convolutional network architecture for action recognition in videos, decoupling spatial and temporal processing into separate convolutional networks and fusing their outputs. While the idea of separating spatial and temporal streams is not entirely new (citing related work in both shallow and deep learning contexts), the authors aim to leverage the power of deep convolutional networks specifically for this decomposition and benefit from pre-training on large image datasets.

The paper is generally well-written and clearly structured. The related work section provides a reasonable overview of existing approaches, acknowledging both hand-crafted feature methods and earlier deep learning attempts. The technical details are presented adequately, though some aspects (e.g., the exact configurations of the convolutional layers) are relegated to figure captions, which isn’t ideal. The experimental setup is described sufficiently, and the results are presented in a relatively clear manner.

However, despite the clarity, I have significant reservations about the paper's originality and, more importantly, the strength of the evidence presented to support its claims. The core idea – separate streams for spatial and temporal processing – has precedent, as acknowledged by the authors (citing Goodale & Milner, and previous two-stream HMAX architectures). The novelty appears to reside in the application of *deep* convolutional networks to both streams and the use of optical flow as direct input to the temporal stream, coupled with pre-training. While applying deep ConvNets to this problem is a reasonable step, the gains demonstrated don't immediately justify the paper’s prominence at NIPS.

The performance improvements, while statistically significant within the reported experiments, appear incremental compared to existing hand-crafted methods. The paper claims to be “competitive with the state of the art shallow representations,” but achieving similar results to well-established methods like those from Wang et al. (2013, 2014) doesn’t constitute a substantial advancement, particularly given the computational cost associated with deep learning. The authors downplay the gap by citing the struggles of earlier deep learning approaches ([14] being significantly worse), but this framing feels selective. 

More concerning is the limited ablation study. While different input configurations for the temporal stream are explored, the impact of various architectural choices within the ConvNets themselves isn’t thoroughly investigated. The network configuration is largely borrowed from [3], with minimal justification for its suitability to this specific task. Furthermore, the multi-task learning component feels tacked on, with insufficient analysis of its contribution beyond simply demonstrating it’s “beneficial.” The benefits of multi-task learning could be significantly expanded if the authors explored more detailed effects.

The paper also suffers from a lack of robustness analysis. The results reported on the UCF-101 and HMDB-51 datasets are standard, but there's no discussion of performance variations across different splits or potential sensitivities to hyperparameters. 

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Clear presentation of a reasonable approach to action recognition.
*   Demonstrates that deep convolutional networks can be effectively applied to both spatial and temporal streams.
*   Pre-training provides a substantial benefit, highlighting the usefulness of leveraging large image datasets.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Limited originality - the core idea of separate streams is not new.
*   Incremental performance improvements compared to established methods.
*   Insufficient ablation study to justify architectural choices.
*   Lack of robustness analysis and sensitivity to hyperparameters.
*   Multi-task learning is presented without a thorough analysis.



**Overall Assessment:**

I lean towards **rejecting** this paper. While it represents a solid engineering effort and a well-written contribution, it lacks the significant novelty and compelling evidence required for acceptance at NIPS. The incremental improvements and lack of in-depth analysis do not warrant a presentation at a conference focused on groundbreaking research. The authors should consider expanding the ablation study, conducting a more thorough robustness analysis, and exploring more innovative architectural choices before resubmitting. A more detailed comparison to recent state-of-the-art methods, including a discussion of computational cost, is also crucial.
