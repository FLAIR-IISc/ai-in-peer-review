This paper presents a two-stream convolutional neural network architecture for action recognition in videos, separating spatial and temporal processing streams. While the core idea of decoupling spatial and temporal information is sensible and aligns with neurobiological inspiration, the paper suffers from a remarkable number of minor, yet distracting, flaws and inconsistencies in presentation and implementation details. Despite presenting a reasonable approach and achieving competitive results, the meticulous attention required to navigate these issues leads me to lean towards rejection.

**Summary:** The authors propose a two-stream CNN architecture – a spatial stream processing individual frames and a temporal stream processing optical flow – with late fusion for action recognition. They leverage pre-training on ImageNet for the spatial stream and demonstrate performance comparable to state-of-the-art hand-crafted features on UCF101 and HMDB51.

**Strengths:**

*   **Sound Core Idea:** Decoupling spatial and temporal processing is a reasonable approach for video understanding.
*   **Competitive Performance:** The reported results are competitive with existing methods, particularly given the relatively small datasets used for training.
*   **Clear Architecture:** The overall architecture is well-defined and easy to understand.
*   **Multi-Task Learning:** The introduction of multi-task learning is a sensible technique to address the limited size of video datasets.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Overabundance of Minor Details & Inconsistencies:** The paper is riddled with seemingly small issues that collectively detract from its impact. For instance, the section numbering is inconsistent (Sect. 1.1 vs. Sect. 2). The phrase "mult-task" is misspelled throughout the introduction. There are numerous instances where a more concise or precise phrasing would significantly improve clarity.
*   **Insufficient Justification for Design Choices:** While the architecture is presented, the rationale for specific design decisions (e.g., the choice of layers in the ConvNets, the size of the input volumes) is often lacking. Why, for example, was the second normalization layer removed from the temporal ConvNet?
*   **Ambiguous Input Configurations:** The descriptions of the "ConvNet input configurations" (Section 3.1) are sometimes unclear and rely heavily on equations without sufficient textual explanation. The distinction between "optical flow stacking" and "trajectory stacking" is not adequately motivated. Figure 3, while attempting to illustrate this, is confusing.
*   **Lack of Ablation Study Depth:** While ablation studies are presented, they feel superficial. The impact of individual components (e.g., bi-directional flow) is assessed, but not in a systematic way.  The effect of different fusion methods beyond SVM vs. averaging deserves more exploration.
*   **Pre-training Details:** The pre-training procedure on ImageNet is briefly mentioned, but crucial details (e.g., the precise network configuration used for pre-training, learning rate schedule) are glossed over.
*   **Optical Flow Implementation Specifics:** The choice of optical flow method ([2]) is noted, but there's no discussion of the limitations or potential impact of this specific implementation.
*   **Implementation Section Overload:** Section 5 is excessively detailed, bordering on implementation notes. This section would benefit from a more focused discussion of key implementation choices and their impact on performance.  The JPEG compression of optical flow seems like an unusual and potentially detrimental optimization; its impact should be quantified.
*    **Redundancy in Results:** Results are reported for individual components (spatial, temporal) *and* for the combined system across multiple splits and datasets, leading to a somewhat overwhelming amount of tables and analysis.

**Specific Examples:**

*   “mult-task” is consistently misspelled throughout.
*   The phrase "effortless combination" in relation to multi-task learning is imprecise and should be replaced with a more scientific description.
*   The discussion of the relationship to the two-streams hypothesis is weak and ultimately serves as a mere acknowledgement without further investigation.
*   The explanation of why mean flow subtraction is beneficial is vague (“reduces the effect of global motion”). More specifics would be appreciated.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a logically sound architecture.
*   The experimental results are competitive with existing methods.
*   The multi-task learning framework is a valuable contribution.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

*   The sheer number of minor flaws and inconsistencies is distracting and undermines the paper's credibility.
*   The lack of detailed justification for design choices and the superficial ablation studies limit the paper's impact.
*   The excessive detail in the implementation section detracts from the core scientific contribution.
*   The writing quality, while generally clear, is marred by numerous minor errors and imprecise phrasing.



**Overall Assessment:** The paper describes a reasonable and competitive approach to action recognition. However, the numerous minor flaws and lack of deeper analysis prevent it from reaching the high standards expected for NIPS publication. While the core idea is solid, the execution and presentation leave much to be desired. I recommend rejection, with encouragement to address the identified issues in a revised version for potential consideration in a different venue.
