Summary and contributions
The paper proposes a two-stream convolutional architecture for video action recognition that decouples appearance and motion. The spatial stream is a ConvNet applied to RGB frames and is pre-trained on ImageNet. The temporal stream is a ConvNet applied to stacked dense optical flow fields, with several design variants: stacking L consecutive forward flows, trajectory-based stacking along flow-defined paths, bi-directional flow, and mean flow subtraction to compensate for global motion. The two streams are fused by late score fusion (averaging or linear SVM). To mitigate limited video data, the authors introduce a simple multi-task learning setup with separate softmax heads for UCF-101 and HMDB-51. Extensive experiments on UCF-101 and HMDB-51 show that: (i) temporal ConvNets on stacked flow substantially outperform both spatial ConvNets and RGB multi-frame ConvNets trained from scratch, (ii) spatial and temporal streams are complementary, and (iii) multi-task training improves generalization, especially on HMDB-51. The best two-stream system is competitive with state-of-the-art hand-crafted pipelines (improved trajectories + Fisher vectors) while using relatively modest training sets.

Relation to prior work
The paper is well-situated relative to: hand-crafted trajectory-based pipelines (HOF/MBH, improved trajectories with Fisher vectors), earlier deep video models (3D convolutions, ConvRBM, ISA), and recent large-scale supervised CNNs on RGB frames (Karpathy et al., Sports-1M), as well as dorsal/ventral two-stream conceptual inspiration (HMAX). The main novelty over prior deep methods is the explicit optical-flow input to a temporal ConvNet, generalizing HOF/MBH and trajectory descriptors via learnable filters, and the late fusion with an ImageNet-pretrained spatial stream. Compared to Karpathy et al., the paper shows that giving motion explicitly as optical flow to a ConvNet is far more effective than asking a CNN to infer motion from stacks of RGB frames.

Quality
The methodology is technically sound and well-motivated. The paper includes thoughtful ablations: number of stacked flows (L), trajectory vs standard stacking, mean flow subtraction, forward vs bi-directional flow, and comparisons to RGB multi-frame ConvNets. The multi-task setup is simple and effective. Implementation details (architecture, data augmentation, training schedules, test-time sampling) appear sufficient for reproduction. Results are reported on the standard three-split protocols for UCF-101 and HMDB-51 with meaningful comparisons to recent deep and strong shallow baselines. However, some aspects could be strengthened:
- Flow choice and parameters: only Brox flow is used; the impact of flow quality, parameter choices, and compression quantization (JPEG) is not analyzed.
- Limited exploration of architecture depth; the temporal net is CNN-M-like rather than deeper variants, and no analysis of capacity vs overfitting beyond dropout is provided.
- The trajectory-stacking variant underperforms, but the exploration is limited (e.g., trajectory length, stride, normalization).
- No timing/throughput reporting beyond flow precomputation; an explicit accuracy–compute trade-off analysis would help.

Clarity
The paper is clearly written and well-organized. The optical flow stacking formulations are precise; training/testing protocols are detailed. A few clarifications would help: exact Brox flow parameters; how shot changes are handled; whether validation data was used to tune L and fusion; more details on the bi-directional stacking ordering and normalization.

Originality
The decoupled two-stream design with a temporal ConvNet operating on stacked optical flow, plus mean flow subtraction and multi-task training across datasets, is a novel and impactful combination at the time. While components are individually known (optical flow, ConvNets, late fusion, multi-task), the composition and empirical demonstration that explicit flow is crucial for deep models in video is original and influential.

Significance
The approach substantially closes the gap between deep and hand-crafted pipelines on standard benchmarks and provides a clear path for future improvements. The two-stream decomposition has already become a canonical baseline in video understanding. Practical implications are strong, although dependency on precomputed optical flow imposes computational cost and complicates end-to-end learning.

Strengths
- Clear, simple architecture that is empirically validated and easy to reproduce.
- Strong ablations demonstrating why temporal flow input matters and how to construct it.
- Effective use of ImageNet pretraining for spatial and multi-task learning for temporal stream.
- Competitive results versus strong hand-crafted baselines; clear improvement over prior deep models.
- Careful implementation details (multi-GPU, test-time sampling) and reporting.

Weaknesses and questions
- Reliance on precomputed optical flow: not end-to-end, computationally heavy, and flow errors may limit robustness. Could the model learn to be robust to poorer/faster flow? Any results with faster flow (e.g., TV-L1) or different parameters?
- Underexplored architectural choices: deeper temporal nets, different receptive fields, temporal strides, or 3D convolutions combined with flow.
- Limited fusion strategies: only averaging/SVM over softmax; mid-level feature fusion or attention across streams is not explored due to overfitting—could stronger regularization or shared layers help?
- Trajectory-aware pooling is not integrated; the discussion acknowledges this as future work.
- No error analysis by class type (e.g., camera motion vs object-centric actions), and no robustness analysis to frame-rate variations or flow noise.
- JPEG-compression of flow may introduce artifacts; any measured impact versus lossless storage?
- Bidirectional flow brings marginal gains; is this due to architecture symmetry or redundancy? Could temporal ordering-sensitive layers exploit it better?

Suggestions for improvement
- Evaluate alternative flow algorithms and faster approximations; report accuracy–compute trade-offs and sensitivity to flow noise/parameters.
- Explore deeper temporal architectures and regularization (e.g., stronger data augmentation, temporal jittering, longer L, or dilated temporal kernels).
- Investigate mid-level or gated fusion between streams and/or weak feature sharing to capture appearance–motion interactions.
- Incorporate trajectory-aware pooling or tubelet-aligned aggregation within the ConvNet.
- Analyze failure modes and per-class improvements to guide future design (e.g., camera motion-heavy classes).
- Consider end-to-end learning of flow-like representations (e.g., differentiable warping, learned motion estimation) to reduce the reliance on precomputed flow.

Recommendation: Accept (strong)
This is a well-executed, insightful paper with clear novelty and strong empirical evidence. It advances the state of the art and provides a blueprint that is likely to influence subsequent work.

Confidence: High
