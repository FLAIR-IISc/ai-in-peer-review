

# NIPS Peer Review: "Two-Stream Convolutional Networks for Action Recognition in Videos"

## Summary
This paper proposes a two-stream convolutional network architecture for video action recognition, inspired by the human visual cortex's ventral (object recognition) and dorsal (motion recognition) pathways. The spatial stream processes individual video frames using a pre-trained ImageNet ConvNet, while the temporal stream processes dense optical flow for motion recognition. The authors demonstrate that combining these streams via late fusion significantly outperforms previous deep learning approaches and is competitive with state-of-the-art shallow representations. The paper also introduces a multi-task learning framework to effectively combine training data from UCF-101 and HMDB-51 datasets.

The work builds on and significantly improves upon previous deep learning approaches to video recognition (e.g., [14]), which struggled to effectively learn spatio-temporal features from stacked frames. The authors clearly distinguish their approach from both shallow feature-based methods [20, 26] and previous deep learning attempts [14, 16] by explicitly separating spatial and temporal processing streams.

## Strengths

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound with well-designed experiments. The authors provide comprehensive ablation studies on the temporal stream architecture (optical flow stacking, trajectory stacking, bi-directional flow, etc.) and thoroughly evaluate the two-stream fusion approach. The implementation details are sufficiently detailed for reproducibility, including network architecture, training procedures, and optical flow computation. The multi-task learning approach to combine datasets is well-motivated and effectively implemented.

**Originality:** While the two-stream concept has biological inspiration [9], the authors' implementation using deep ConvNets for both streams represents a significant departure from previous work. The paper clearly articulates how their approach differs from prior deep learning methods (which typically used stacked RGB frames) and how it generalizes hand-crafted features like HOF and MBH. The temporal stream's use of optical flow as explicit input rather than trying to learn motion features from raw frames is a key innovation.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The figures (particularly Figure 1 showing the two-stream architecture and Figure 3 illustrating the optical flow input configurations) effectively support the text. The experimental setup, results, and comparisons are presented in a logical and easy-to-follow manner. The paper strikes an excellent balance between technical detail and accessibility.

**Significance:** The results are significant - the proposed approach substantially outperforms previous deep learning methods for video action recognition and achieves competitive results with state-of-the-art shallow methods. This demonstrates that deep learning can be successfully applied to video action recognition with appropriate architectural choices, potentially opening new avenues for research in the field.

## Weaknesses

**Limited Comparison with Very Recent Methods:** While the paper compares with [20, 21, 26], it doesn't include the most recent state-of-the-art methods like the 2014 work by Wang et al. ("Action Recognition with Improved Trajectories") or the 2015 work by Feichtenhofer et al. ("Convolutional Two-Stream Network for Action Recognition"). Including these would strengthen the significance claim.

**Insufficient Explanation of Why Optical Flow Works Better:** The paper demonstrates that optical flow input significantly outperforms stacked RGB frames, but doesn't provide a thorough analysis of *why*. A brief discussion of how the optical flow representation aligns with the network's architecture or how it addresses the limitations of stacked frames would strengthen the paper.

**Limited Analysis of Failure Cases:** The paper doesn't provide any analysis of cases where the two-stream approach fails, which would help identify the remaining limitations of the method and guide future work.

**Optical Flow Limitations:** The paper acknowledges the computational cost of optical flow, but doesn't explore how this might limit practical deployment or how the method would perform with less accurate optical flow estimation (e.g., on lower quality videos).

## Recommendation

I recommend accepting this paper for NIPS. It presents a well-executed, significant contribution to the field of video action recognition that is clearly distinguished from previous work. The two-stream architecture with late fusion of spatial and temporal streams represents a simple yet effective solution that advances the state of the art for deep learning approaches to video recognition.

The paper is of high technical quality, well-written, and makes a strong contribution to the field. The minor weaknesses noted above are not sufficient to warrant rejection, and the authors could easily address them in a camera-ready version.

## Pro/Con Acceptance Summary

**Pros for Acceptance:**
- Clear, well-executed approach that significantly improves upon previous deep learning methods
- Thorough experimental evaluation with comprehensive ablation studies
- Well-written and well-organized paper
- Competitive results with state-of-the-art shallow methods
- Practical implementation details that enable reproducibility

**Cons for Acceptance:**
- Limited comparison with the absolute most recent state-of-the-art methods
- Insufficient explanation of why optical flow input works better than stacked RGB frames
- Minimal analysis of failure cases

Overall, this is a strong contribution to the field with clear technical merit, significance, and originality. The minor weaknesses do not detract from the paper's overall quality and contribution.
