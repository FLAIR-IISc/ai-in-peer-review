Summary
The paper proposes a two-stream convolutional architecture for video action recognition that explicitly decouples spatial (appearance) and temporal (motion) cues. The spatial stream is a ConvNet applied to individual RGB frames and is pre-trained on ImageNet. The temporal stream is a ConvNet trained on stacked dense optical flow fields between consecutive frames, with several input variants: (i) stacking flow vectors over L frames, (ii) trajectory stacking (sampling along motion trajectories), (iii) bi-directional flow, and (iv) mean flow subtraction to mitigate camera motion. The two streams are fused by late score fusion (average or linear SVM). To address limited video data, the authors introduce a simple multi-task learning setup that jointly trains on UCF-101 and HMDB-51 with task-specific softmax heads and summed losses.

Empirically, the temporal stream markedly outperforms a single-frame spatial ConvNet and multi-frame RGB “slow fusion” baselines trained from scratch. The two streams are complementary, and late fusion yields further gains, outperforming prior deep approaches by a large margin and approaching the best hand-crafted trajectory-based systems. Ablations show benefits of longer flow stacks (L=10), mean flow subtraction, and multi-task learning; trajectory stacking and bi-directional flows offer marginal additional benefit.

Relation to prior work
The approach contrasts with earlier deep models that attempt to learn spatio-temporal features from stacked RGB frames and with shallow pipelines based on HOG/HOF/MBH and dense trajectories. The authors position the temporal ConvNet as a learnable generalization of these hand-crafted motion descriptors, and the two-stream design is thematically related to the ventral/dorsal pathways in biological vision and to earlier two-stream HMAX models. Compared to Karpathy et al.’s Sports-1M architectures, this work is bolder in directly injecting motion via optical flow, which seems to resolve the difficulty of emergent motion filters in early conv layers.

Strengths
- Conceptual clarity and novelty: explicit two-stream decomposition and optical-flow-as-input are simple, bold, and effective, with a compelling connection to ventral/dorsal pathways.
- Strong empirical evidence: substantial improvements over prior deep baselines; complementary streams; thorough ablations (stack length, trajectory vs. flow stacking, mean subtraction, fusion).
- Practical contribution: multi-task learning across datasets is straightforward and yields meaningful gains, addressing data scarcity without manual class curation.
- Reproducibility and details: training schedules, augmentation, GPU setup, and preprocessing choices are well described.

Weaknesses
- Not end-to-end: reliance on precomputed optical flow makes the system computationally heavy and complicates deployment; no learning of motion estimation.
- Limited dataset scope: evaluations are on UCF-101/HMDB-51; Sports-1M is discussed but not used. Risk of overfitting persists despite multi-task learning.
- Fusion is shallow: only softmax score fusion is feasible (deeper joint heads overfit). More integrated fusion or shared early layers are unexplored.
- Sensitivity to flow and preprocessing: only one optical flow method is used (Brox). No analysis of alternative flows, parameter sensitivity, or the impact of JPEG compression of flow fields.
- Trajectory-aware pooling: the architecture does not incorporate trajectory-centric pooling, a key component of the best shallow methods (acknowledged by the authors).

Quality
The methodology is sound and carefully evaluated. The ablations support the main claims, and the training/inference protocols are clear. A few experimental omissions remain (flow algorithm comparisons, computational cost/runtime analysis, and calibration of SVM vs average fusion), but they do not undermine the core result.

Clarity
The paper is clearly written and well organized. The motivation for the two-stream split and the design choices for the temporal input are explained crisply. Implementation details are unusually thorough. Minor suggestions: report storage/compute cost trade-offs more explicitly; clarify the dynamic range rescaling of flow and its effect on performance; provide variance across splits.

Originality
High. While built on known components (ConvNets, optical flow, late fusion), the synthesis—explicit temporal stream fed with stacked flow and multi-task training across datasets—marks a distinct and influential direction. The arguments that the temporal ConvNet generalizes HOF/MBH are insightful and well grounded.

Significance
High. The approach advances the state of deep video recognition and offers a blueprint that others can build on. It is likely to influence both practical systems (due to its simplicity and effectiveness) and future research (e.g., learning motion end-to-end, better fusion).

Pros for acceptance
- Clear, novel, and compelling idea with strong empirical support.
- Practical multi-task solution to data scarcity.
- Thorough ablations and strong gains over prior deep baselines.
- Well written and likely to have substantial impact.

Cons against acceptance
- Heavy reliance on precomputed optical flow; no end-to-end training of motion.
- Limited dataset scope; lack of analysis of flow variants and computational trade-offs.
- Fusion remains late and shallow due to overfitting concerns.

Suggestions for improvement
- Explore end-to-end learning of motion (e.g., differentiable warping/correlation or learned flow proxies).
- Compare multiple flow methods and quantify sensitivity to flow quality and quantization.
- Investigate lightweight or joint fusion mechanisms (shared lower layers, attention) with stronger regularization.
- Incorporate trajectory-aware pooling or tubelets into the network.
- Report runtime/storage and discuss deployment.

Recommendation
Accept. The paper introduces a simple but powerful concept that materially advances deep video recognition and opens a promising line of work, even if some components are not yet end-to-end.
