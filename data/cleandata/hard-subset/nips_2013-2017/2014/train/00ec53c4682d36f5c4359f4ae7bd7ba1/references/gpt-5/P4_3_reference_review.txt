Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes a two-stream convolutional architecture for video action recognition that separates spatial (appearance) and temporal (motion) information. The spatial stream is a ConvNet operating on individual RGB frames and benefits from ImageNet pretraining. The temporal stream is a ConvNet whose input is a stack of dense optical flow fields between consecutive frames, yielding explicit motion cues. Several input variants are explored: (i) stacking flow components across L frames, (ii) trajectory-based stacking along motion paths, (iii) forward vs. bi-directional flow, and (iv) mean-flow subtraction to reduce camera-motion bias. The two streams are fused by averaging or by a linear SVM over softmax scores. To mitigate limited video data, the authors use multi-task learning to jointly train the temporal net on UCF101 and HMDB-51, with dataset-specific softmax heads.

This approach is positioned against: (1) shallow hand-crafted pipelines using dense trajectories and HOF/MBH with Fisher encoding ([20, 21, 26, 29]), (2) earlier deep models that learn spatiotemporal filters from stacked RGB frames or small 3D convolutions ([11, 12, 14, 18, 25]), and (3) dorsal/ventral two-stream HMAX models ([16]). The paper argues that optical-flow-as-input provides explicit motion representation, allowing ConvNets to generalize traditional descriptors (HOF, MBH, kinematic features) via learned filters, while the two-stream decomposition leverages large-scale image pretraining for the spatial pathway.

Empirically, the temporal stream significantly outperforms RGB-only ConvNets trained on video frames; the fused model is complementary and improves further. On UCF101 and HMDB-51, the two-stream approach is markedly better than prior deep baselines ([14, 16]) and competitive with state-of-the-art hand-crafted trajectory-based methods, despite training on relatively small datasets. Ablations show benefits of stacking more flow fields (up to L=10), mean-flow subtraction, and late fusion; trajectory stacking and bi-directional flow provide marginal gains.

Strengths
- Clear, well-motivated decomposition into spatial and temporal streams, with a simple, effective temporal input based on stacked optical flow.
- Strong empirical results that substantially exceed earlier deep approaches on UCF101/HMDB-51 and approach the best shallow representations.
- Thorough ablation of temporal input design (stack length, trajectory vs. regular stacking, mean subtraction, bi-directional flow) and fusion methods.
- Sensible exploitation of ImageNet pretraining for spatial stream and multi-task learning to leverage multiple video datasets for the temporal stream.
- Good implementation detail: architecture, training schedule, data augmentation, and a practical flow precomputation/compression pipeline.
- Relates the proposed temporal representation to classical motion descriptors, clarifying how ConvNets can subsume HOF/MBH/kinematic features.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Not end-to-end: performance depends on precomputed optical flow quality, introducing a computational bottleneck and external algorithmic dependency. No analysis of different flow methods or their runtime/accuracy tradeoffs.
- Temporal modeling is limited to a fixed short window (L≈10); no mechanisms for longer-term dependencies or temporal invariances beyond late score averaging.
- Fusion is shallow (late fusion at softmax or SVM); joint training of the streams was attempted but abandoned due to overfitting. Regularized joint fusion could be explored.
- Limited camera motion handling (mean-flow subtraction only) and no explicit trajectory-aligned pooling in the ConvNet, which remain advantages of improved trajectories.
- Evaluation scope is restricted to UCF101/HMDB-51; generalization to larger, noisier datasets (e.g., Sports-1M) is discussed but not demonstrated.
- Minimal analysis of computational cost at inference (flow + ConvNets), class-wise performance, or typical failure modes.

Quality
- Technically sound with appropriate baselines, clear ablations, and standard protocols across three splits. The arguments for optical-flow input and two-stream late fusion are supported by experiments.
- Some design choices (flow algorithm, flow compression) are fixed without sensitivity analysis; end-to-end learning of motion is not attempted.

Clarity
- Well-written and well-organized. Network configurations, training/testing protocols, and multi-task setup are described sufficiently to support reproduction. The relation to prior descriptors is clearly articulated.

Originality
- Moderate to high. Using stacked optical flow as ConvNet input for a learned temporal stream, combined with a separate pre-trained spatial stream and late fusion, is a novel and impactful combination relative to deep models that learn motion implicitly from RGB stacks. The multi-task application is incremental but practical.

Significance
- High. The method delivers strong improvements over prior deep baselines and closes much of the gap to hand-crafted state-of-the-art, offering a simple and general recipe that is likely to be adopted and extended.

Suggestions for improvement
- Evaluate additional optical flow methods and quantify speed/accuracy tradeoffs; report end-to-end inference cost.
- Explore longer temporal context (e.g., larger L, temporal pyramids, or recurrent/temporal convolution modules) and stronger regularization to enable deeper fusion layers.
- Incorporate trajectory-aligned or tube-based pooling within the ConvNet to better capture motion patterns robust to camera motion.
- Investigate joint learning of motion estimation and recognition (e.g., learnable flow or feature warping) to reduce reliance on external flow.
- Provide per-class analyses, error breakdowns, and sensitivity to the number of sampled frames.
- If possible, include results on a larger dataset (e.g., Sports-1M) or cross-dataset transfer to test robustness.

Arguments for acceptance
- Conceptually simple, well-justified architecture that advances deep video recognition.
- Strong empirical gains vs. prior deep methods; competitive with trajectory-based SOTA.
- Thorough ablations and clear presentation; practical training strategy via pretraining and multi-task learning.

Arguments against acceptance
- Reliance on precomputed optical flow and limited analysis of its impact.
- Restricted temporal modeling and shallow fusion; no end-to-end motion learning.
- Evaluation confined to smaller datasets; scalability and robustness to large-scale, noisy data not demonstrated.

Overall, the paper meets NeurIPS criteria on quality, clarity, originality, and significance, and is in scope for ML for vision and deep learning.
