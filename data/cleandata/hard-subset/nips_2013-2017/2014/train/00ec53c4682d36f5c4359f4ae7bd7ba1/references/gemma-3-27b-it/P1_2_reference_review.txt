## Peer Review for "Two-Stream Convolutional Networks for Action Recognition in Videos"

**Overall Assessment:** This paper presents a significant and well-executed contribution to the field of video action recognition. The authors propose a compelling two-stream CNN architecture that effectively decouples spatial and temporal information, demonstrating substantial improvements over previous deep learning approaches and achieving competitive performance with state-of-the-art hand-crafted features. The paper is well-written, clearly organized, and provides a thorough experimental evaluation. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions.

**1. Summary & Related Work:**

The paper addresses the challenge of video action recognition, arguing that simply stacking frames for a CNN input doesn’t effectively capture temporal information. The authors propose a two-stream CNN architecture – a spatial stream processing individual frames and a temporal stream processing dense optical flow – with late fusion of their outputs. This is inspired by the two-stream hypothesis in visual processing, though the authors do not delve into neurological interpretations. The related work section is comprehensive, covering both traditional hand-crafted feature approaches (HOG, MBH, trajectories) and earlier attempts at deep learning for video (HMAX, stacked frames). The authors accurately position their work within the existing landscape, highlighting the limitations of prior deep learning efforts and the novelty of their decoupled stream approach.

**2. Strengths:**

* **Novelty & Insight:** The two-stream architecture is a conceptually sound and demonstrably effective approach. Decoupling spatial and temporal processing allows for dedicated optimization of each modality and leverages the strengths of CNNs for image (spatial) and motion (temporal) information.
* **Strong Empirical Results:** The paper consistently shows improvements over previous deep learning baselines ([14]) and achieves competitive results with established hand-crafted feature methods on standard benchmarks (UCF101 and HMDB51).
* **Effective Use of Optical Flow:**  Leveraging dense optical flow as input to the temporal stream explicitly provides motion information to the network, circumventing the challenges of learning spatio-temporal features implicitly from stacked frames. The exploration of different optical flow configurations (stacking, trajectories, bi-directional) is insightful.
* **Pre-training & Multi-task Learning:** The authors strategically utilize pre-training on ImageNet for the spatial stream and multi-task learning to improve generalization, particularly crucial given the relatively small size of video action datasets.
* **Clear & Thorough Evaluation:** The experimental setup is well-defined, and the results are presented clearly with detailed analysis.  The ablation studies (different input configurations, fusion methods) are valuable.
* **Well-Written & Organized:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow, with a logical flow and clear explanations of the proposed methods.

**3. Weaknesses:**

* **Computational Cost:** While the paper acknowledges the optical flow computation as a potential bottleneck, it doesn't fully explore strategies to mitigate this cost beyond pre-computation and compression. Exploring techniques like sparse optical flow or more efficient flow estimation methods could be beneficial.
* **Limited Discussion of Failure Cases:** The paper primarily focuses on overall accuracy. A more in-depth analysis of *where* the method struggles – which action classes are frequently misclassified, and *why* – would provide valuable insights.
* **Lack of Comparison with 3D CNNs:** The paper focuses on comparing against 2D CNNs and hand-crafted features. While 3D CNNs were less prevalent at the time of submission, a brief discussion of their strengths and weaknesses in relation to the two-stream approach would strengthen the analysis.
* **Minimal Neurological Connection:**  The paper mentions the two-stream hypothesis but doesn't explore this connection beyond a brief acknowledgement.  Expanding on potential parallels between the proposed architecture and brain processing could add theoretical depth.



**4. Arguments For and Against Acceptance:**

**For:**

* Significant improvement over previous deep learning approaches for action recognition.
* Novel and insightful architecture.
* Thorough experimental validation and ablation studies.
* Clear and well-written presentation.
* Competitive performance with state-of-the-art methods.

**Against:**

* Computational cost associated with optical flow.
* Limited analysis of failure cases.
* Lack of comparison with emerging 3D CNN approaches.




**5. Specific Suggestions for Improvement:**

* **Computational Cost Mitigation:** Briefly discuss potential strategies for reducing the computational burden of optical flow estimation.
* **Error Analysis:** Include a table or discussion of common misclassifications and potential reasons for errors.
* **3D CNN Discussion:** Add a paragraph comparing and contrasting the two-stream approach with 3D CNNs.
* **Clarify Multi-Task Learning Details:** Provide more details on how the class weighting/balancing was handled in the multi-task learning setup.
* **Minor Typo:** Line 50: “mult-task” should be “multi-task”.



**6. Relevance to NIPS:**

This paper is highly relevant to NIPS. It presents a novel and theoretically sound deep learning architecture for a challenging problem (video action recognition), supported by rigorous experimental evaluation.  The contributions are significant and align with NIPS’s focus on innovative machine learning research. The work advances the state-of-the-art and has the potential to influence future research in this area.

**Final Recommendation:**

**Accept** with minor revisions addressing the points mentioned above. This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of video action recognition.
