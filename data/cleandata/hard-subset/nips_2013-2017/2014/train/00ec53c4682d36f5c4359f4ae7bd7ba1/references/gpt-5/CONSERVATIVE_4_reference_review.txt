Summary
The paper proposes a two-stream ConvNet architecture for action recognition in videos: a spatial stream operating on individual RGB frames (pretrained on ImageNet) and a temporal stream operating on stacked dense optical flow fields between adjacent frames. The temporal stream explores several input variants (flow stacking vs. trajectory stacking, uni- vs. bi-directional flow, mean flow subtraction) and the authors fuse the two streams by late score fusion (averaging or linear SVM). They also use a multi-task learning setup to leverage both UCF101 and HMDB-51 for training the temporal stream. On UCF101 and HMDB-51, the temporal stream outperforms single-frame ConvNets and prior deep baselines trained on stacked frames, and fusion of the two streams improves further, reaching performance comparable to state-of-the-art hand-crafted trajectory-based methods.

Strengths
- Clear decomposition into spatial and temporal cues, aligning with established practice in video recognition, and leveraging strong image pretraining for the spatial stream.
- Sensible use of explicit optical flow as input to simplify learning motion features, leading to large gains over stacked RGB baselines.
- Thorough ablative analysis of temporal input design choices (L, mean flow subtraction, bi-directional flow, trajectory vs. spatial stacking).
- Practical training details that improve reproducibility (architecture, data augmentation, learning rate schedules, GPU setup, precomputation and compression of flow).
- Multi-task learning to regularize training on small datasets yields measurable and consistent improvements.
- Standard evaluation protocol on UCF101 and HMDB-51 with clear test-time aggregation.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Not end-to-end: the pipeline depends critically on an external, relatively heavy optical flow algorithm. This introduces a substantial computational and storage overhead and a potential performance bottleneck not captured by accuracy alone. No runtime/throughput/latency measurements are reported for inference with flow precomputation.
- Limited scope of evaluation: only UCF101 and HMDB-51 are used. No evidence on large-scale (e.g., Sports-1M) or cross-dataset generalization beyond the two benchmarks.
- The method does not clearly surpass the strongest hand-crafted baseline (improved dense trajectories with Fisher vectors); results are “comparable,” not better. There is no evaluation of combining the proposed features with iDT, which is common practice to establish a new SOTA.
- The “two-stream” novelty is modest: the idea is well known in the vision literature (including prior two-stream HMAX). Using optical flow as input to a ConvNet is a straightforward adaptation of established motion features. Multi-task learning is also standard.
- Fusion is done by late score combination; attempts at joint training reportedly overfit but no systematic regularization strategies (e.g., weight sharing, bottlenecks, auxiliary losses) are explored. This suggests the approach may be fragile without careful tuning.
- No statistical significance or variance across runs; no per-class or error analysis to substantiate claims (e.g., which actions benefit from motion vs. appearance).
- Camera motion compensation is addressed only by mean flow subtraction; more principled compensation (as in iDT) is not integrated, despite being cited as impactful.

Quality
Technically sound overall, with appropriate baselines against prior deep models and careful ablations of the temporal input. However, the empirical validation stops short of demonstrating superiority over the best hand-crafted pipelines, and there is no analysis of computational cost, which is critical for video models relying on optical flow. Claims that the ConvNet “generalizes” hand-crafted features are plausible but remain largely qualitative (filter visualizations) rather than quantitatively established.

Clarity
The paper is clearly written and well organized. Implementation details are unusually thorough for this domain and time, which is commendable. A few details that would improve clarity: report exact flow algorithm parameters, provide inference-time cost (with and without flow precomputation), and quantify storage/compute trade-offs more explicitly.

Originality
Moderate. The contribution is a careful combination of known components: ConvNets, optical-flow inputs, late fusion, and multi-task learning. The novelty lies in demonstrating that explicit flow stacking as ConvNet input is markedly better than stacked RGB, plus incremental design choices (mean subtraction, bi-directional flow). The link to the two-stream hypothesis is conceptual rather than technical.

Significance
The approach substantially improves over prior deep baselines and produces competitive performance with hand-crafted methods on standard datasets, likely to be of practical interest. However, without surpassing the strongest established baselines or demonstrating broader generality, the significance is moderate.

Questions and requested experiments
- Provide inference-time throughput and end-to-end latency, including optical flow computation, and compare to stacked-RGB models. How does accuracy degrade if a faster flow (e.g., TV-L1) is used?
- Compare to improved dense trajectories with Fisher vectors under identical train/test protocols; and evaluate fusion of the proposed model with iDT to establish an SOTA combination.
- Report variability across multiple training runs and per-class gains to support claims about motion benefits.
- Explore more robust joint fusion (e.g., regularized joint FC layers, shared early layers, or gating) rather than only late fusion.
- Evaluate sensitivity to L (number of flow fields) and stride beyond L={5,10}, and clarify the chosen L in final results.

Pros for acceptance
- Strong empirical gains over prior deep baselines with a simple, well-motivated design.
- Thorough ablation of temporal input choices; good engineering and reproducibility details.
- Practical contribution likely to be adopted by the community.

Cons for acceptance
- Dependence on external optical flow with unreported computational costs and potential scalability issues.
- Does not surpass the best established hand-crafted baselines; no combination with them to demonstrate clear SOTA.
- Limited originality; largely a careful integration of established components.
- Limited evaluation breadth and lack of statistical analysis.

Recommendation
Weak accept. Despite modest novelty and reliance on external flow, the paper offers a solid, carefully validated architecture that clearly advances the performance of ConvNet-based video recognition and is likely to influence practice. Adding runtime analysis and stronger comparisons to iDT (and/or their combination) would strengthen the case.

Confidence
High.
