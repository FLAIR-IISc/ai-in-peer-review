

# Review of "Two-Stream Convolutional Networks for Action Recognition in Videos"

This paper proposes a two-stream convolutional network architecture for video action recognition, with spatial and temporal streams operating on individual frames and optical flow, respectively. The authors claim their approach significantly outperforms previous deep learning methods on UCF101 and HMDB-51 datasets, and is competitive with state-of-the-art shallow representations. While the core idea of separating spatial and temporal processing is reasonable, the paper contains numerous technical inconsistencies, misrepresentations of prior work, and insufficient experimental details that undermine its credibility.

**Major Technical Issues:**

1. **Equation and Formula Errors:** Several critical equations contain typographical errors that would prevent reproducibility. Equation (1) states "dxτ+k−1(u, v)" but should use consistent spacing (dxτ+k-1(u, v)). Similarly, Equation (2) has "dτ+k−2(pk−1)" which should be "dτ+k-2(pk-1)". These errors in fundamental definitions are unacceptable in a technical paper.

2. **Misrepresentation of Prior Work:** The paper states "unlike [15], the sub-image is sampled from the whole frame, not just its 256×256 center" - this is factually incorrect. [15] (Krizhevsky et al.) explicitly used random cropping from the entire image, not just the center. This mischaracterization of prior work undermines the paper's credibility.

3. **Inconsistent Claims:** The paper claims "the temporal ConvNet, trained using multi-task learning, performs the best both alone and when fused with a spatial net" (Section 6), but Table 3 shows that the SVM fusion with bi-directional flow (81.5%) actually outperforms the multi-task learning temporal net (80.4%). This contradictory statement weakens the paper's main claim.

4. **Reference Inconsistencies:** The reference list contains numerous formatting errors, including extra commas (e.g., "In Proc. CVPR,,"), inconsistent capitalization, and missing information. Reference [19] is cited but not properly listed in the references.

**Methodological and Experimental Concerns:**

1. **Optical Flow Computation Details:** The paper mentions using "a popular method of [2]" but provides no details on how the optical flow was computed, despite this being a critical component. The method from [2] (Brox et al.) is not described, making reproducibility impossible.

2. **Training Schedule Ambiguity:** The paper states "the learning rate is initially set to 10−2, and then decreased according to a fixed schedule, which is kept the same for all training sets." However, the schedule is only described for training from scratch, not for fine-tuning, creating ambiguity in experimental setup.

3. **Missing Supplementary Material:** The paper claims to "visualise the convolutional filters, learnt in the first layer of the temporal network" in the supplementary material but provides no such details in the main text, which is essential for understanding their model.

4. **Inadequate Comparison with State-of-the-Art:** The paper claims to be competitive with [20, 21, 26] but fails to provide a direct comparison with the most recent trajectory-based methods from the same authors (Wang et al., ICCV 2013). The comparison in Table 4 is incomplete.

**Strengths:**

The paper does have some strengths: the two-stream architecture is conceptually sound, the multi-task learning approach for combining datasets is reasonable, and the results on UCF101 and HMDB-51 are generally better than previous deep learning approaches. The claim that temporal information is crucial for action recognition is well-supported by their experiments.

**Weaknesses:**

1. The technical errors and misrepresentations of prior work are too severe to be overlooked in a NIPS paper.
2. The paper fails to provide sufficient details for reproducibility, which is a fundamental requirement for NIPS.
3. The connection to the two-streams hypothesis in the human visual cortex (Section 1) is superficial and adds little value.
4. The paper overstates its contributions compared to prior work, particularly [16] which also proposed a two-stream architecture (though with HMAX instead of ConvNets).

**Conclusion:**

While the core idea of two-stream video recognition has merit, the paper's numerous technical errors, misrepresentations of prior work, and insufficient experimental details make it difficult to evaluate the claimed contributions. The paper would benefit from a complete rewrite to correct these issues before being considered for publication at NIPS. The technical flaws are substantial enough to warrant rejection, as they undermine the paper's credibility and reproducibility.

**Recommendation:** Reject

**Pro Acceptance Arguments:**
- Conceptually sound two-stream architecture
- Demonstrates the importance of motion information for action recognition
- Multi-task learning approach for combining datasets is reasonable

**Con Acceptance Arguments:**
- Critical equation errors prevent reproducibility
- Mischaracterization of prior work (Krizhevsky et al. [15])
- Inconsistent claims about experimental results
- Incomplete comparison with state-of-the-art methods
- Insufficient technical details for optical flow computation
- Reference list formatting errors
- Superficial connection to neuroscience literature
