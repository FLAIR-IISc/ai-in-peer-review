Summary
The paper proposes a two-stream convolutional network architecture for action recognition in videos: a spatial stream operating on RGB frames and a temporal stream operating on stacked dense optical flow. Motion is made explicit by feeding displacement fields (dx, dy) over L consecutive frame pairs as 2L input channels. The authors explore variants of temporal inputs: optical-flow stacking vs trajectory stacking, uni- vs bi-directional flow, and mean flow subtraction to mitigate camera motion. The spatial stream is pre-trained on ImageNet; the temporal stream is trained from scratch on UCF101/HMDB51, with a multi-task learning setup (two heads sharing the backbone) to leverage both datasets and reduce overfitting. Scores from the two streams are fused by averaging or a linear SVM. Comprehensive implementation details (training schedules, data augmentation, multi-GPU, flow precomputation and compression) and ablations are provided. The approach significantly outperforms prior deep baselines that learn motion implicitly from stacked RGB frames (e.g., Karpathy et al.) and is competitive with contemporary hand-crafted trajectory-based methods.

Relation to prior work
- The architecture operationalizes the two-stream hypothesis (ventral/dorsal) with modern ConvNets. Unlike HMAX-based two-stream systems, both streams are learned discriminatively and are deeper.
- The temporal stream generalizes classical motion descriptors (HOF, MBH, kinematic features) by learning filters on the raw displacement field; the paper correctly connects these via simple conv+nonlinearity arguments.
- Compared to 3D CNNs/slow fusion models that expect the network to infer motion implicitly, the explicit optical flow input yields stronger performance on small datasets.
- The multi-task training across UCF101 and HMDB51 is a pragmatic and principled way to expand effective supervision without class curation.

Strengths
- Clear, well-motivated decomposition of spatial and temporal cues with a simple, effective late-fusion strategy.
- Strong empirical evidence: ablations on L, mean subtraction, stacking strategies, and comparison to stacked RGB 3D/slow fusion baselines. Temporal stream convincingly outperforms single-frame spatial, and two-stream fusion yields sizable gains (≈+6% over temporal, +14% over spatial on UCF split 1).
- Practical contributions: training/testing protocols, multi-GPU implementation, and a scalable pipeline for optical flow precomputation and storage.
- Multi-task learning improves HMDB51 notably and yields small but consistent gains on UCF101.

Weaknesses and limitations
- Dependence on precomputed optical flow breaks end-to-end learning, increases engineering complexity, and makes the method’s accuracy/latency contingent on the chosen flow algorithm. The paper precomputes Brox flow and compresses it with JPEG; no analysis of how flow quality/compression affects accuracy is provided.
- Camera motion handling is limited to mean flow subtraction; stronger global motion compensation (e.g., homography) might help, especially for handheld footage. The authors acknowledge this as future work.
- Ablations are primarily on UCF101 split 1; generalization of design choices (e.g., optimal L, bi-directional flow, trajectory stacking) across splits/datasets is not fully quantified.
- The “trajectory stacking” variant underperforms basic stacking despite the success of trajectory-pooled descriptors; the paper does not analyze why (e.g., error accumulation along noisy trajectories, misalignment with conv receptive fields).
- Fusion is restricted to late softmax score fusion; attempts at joint fully connected fusion overfit. More systematic exploration of regularized joint fusion (e.g., weight sharing, dropout, or low-rank adapters) is absent.
- Limited discussion of computational cost at inference (end-to-end video throughput including flow computation), which is important for practical deployment.

Quality
Technically sound and carefully evaluated given dataset scale constraints. The design is coherent and each component’s utility is supported by experiments. Some methodological choices (flow algorithm, compression) would benefit from sensitivity analysis. Reporting across all three splits for key ablations would strengthen claims.

Clarity
The paper is clearly written and well organized. The construction of temporal inputs (stacking vs trajectory stacking, bi-directional variants) is precise. Implementation details (architectures, training schedules, augmentation, test-time protocol) are sufficient for reproduction. Minor typos in references and figure callouts aside, presentation is strong.

Originality
Moderate-to-high. The key novelty is the explicit optical-flow-based temporal ConvNet and its integration into a two-stream architecture with learned fusion, plus a simple but effective multi-task training scheme for small video datasets. While constituent ideas (optical flow, ConvNets, two streams, multi-task learning) are known, their combination and the demonstration that explicit motion input outperforms RGB-stacking deep baselines are impactful and nontrivial.

Significance
High. The paper advances deep learning for video by showing a practical, competitive alternative to hand-crafted pipelines and illuminates that explicit motion input is crucial when data is limited. The approach is likely to be adopted as a baseline and extended (e.g., with better motion compensation, learned flow, or end-to-end training), influencing subsequent research.

Suggestions and questions
- Provide accuracy vs. L beyond 10 and across datasets; analyze temporal stride sensitivity and clip length effects.
- Report end-to-end inference cost (ms/frame) including flow, and the accuracy/latency trade-off with faster flow (e.g., TV-L1) and/or lower resolution.
- Quantify the impact of flow JPEG compression on performance.
- Analyze why trajectory stacking underperforms (e.g., trajectory length distribution, flow noise, visualization of first-layer filters for both inputs).
- Explore lightweight joint fusion with stronger regularization or calibration-aware fusion (e.g., temperature scaling).
- Evaluate stronger camera motion compensation (e.g., 2D homography) vs mean subtraction.
- Discuss robustness to flow errors (occlusions, motion blur) and per-class breakdown to see which actions benefit most from the temporal stream.

Pros
- Simple, well-motivated architecture with strong empirical gains over prior deep baselines.
- Thorough implementation and training details; reproducible.
- Useful ablations and multi-task learning to leverage small datasets.
- Competitive with state-of-the-art hand-crafted methods.

Cons
- Not end-to-end; heavy reliance on precomputed flow without sensitivity analysis.
- Limited exploration of fusion and camera motion compensation.
- Some ablations limited to a single split; limited analysis of failure modes.

Recommendation
Accept. The paper offers a clear, effective contribution to deep video recognition, with solid experiments, good clarity, and high anticipated impact.
