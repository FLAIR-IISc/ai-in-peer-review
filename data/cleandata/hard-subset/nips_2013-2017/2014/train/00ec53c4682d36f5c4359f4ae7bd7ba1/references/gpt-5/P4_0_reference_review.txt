Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes a two-stream convolutional architecture for video action recognition, decomposing video into a spatial stream (appearance from individual RGB frames) and a temporal stream (motion from stacked dense optical flow). The spatial network is a standard ConvNet pre-trained on ImageNet and fine-tuned (or with only the last layer trained) on the target video dataset. The temporal network takes as input a volume formed by stacking optical flow fields over L frames (including options for forward/backward flow, trajectory-based stacking, and mean flow subtraction), and is trained from scratch. The two streams are fused by averaging or by a linear SVM on concatenated softmax outputs.

The work builds on and contrasts with: (i) hand-crafted shallow pipelines using dense trajectories, HOG/HOF/MBH and Fisher vectors (e.g., Wang and Schmid 2013; Peng et al. 2014), (ii) deep video models that learn spatiotemporal features from raw frames via 3D convolutions or stacked-frame inputs (Ji et al. 2013; Karpathy et al. 2014), and (iii) earlier two-stream HMAX-style systems (Jhuang et al. 2007; Kuehne et al. 2011). It argues for explicit motion input (optical flow) rather than requiring the ConvNet to infer motion implicitly from RGB stacks. The authors also introduce a simple multi-task learning setup to jointly train the temporal stream on UCF-101 and HMDB-51 with separate classification heads.

Experiments on UCF-101 and HMDB-51 show: (1) temporal ConvNets on optical flow substantially outperform RGB-only ConvNets trained on video frames, (2) stacking multiple flow frames (L up to 10) and mean flow subtraction help, while trajectory stacking and bidirectional flow offer marginal gains, (3) two-stream fusion improves over either stream alone, and (4) multi-task training improves temporal stream performance on small datasets. The combined model is competitive with state-of-the-art hand-crafted representations.

Quality
Technical soundness: The architecture and training procedures are standard and well-motivated. Using stacked optical flow as input is a clear, testable hypothesis; alternatives (trajectory stacking, bidirectional flow) are ablated. The multi-task learning formulation (two task-specific softmax heads with summed losses) is straightforward and correct. Implementation details, data augmentation, learning schedules, and GPU setup are provided. The evaluation follows accepted protocols (three splits, mean accuracy). Comparisons include strong baselines (RGB-only ConvNets, stacked-frame “slow fusion” per Karpathy et al.) and state-of-the-art shallow methods.

Empirical support: Gains from motion stacking, mean subtraction, two-stream fusion, and multi-task training are demonstrated. The model is competitive with improved dense trajectories and stacked Fisher vectors. However, some analyses could be deeper: per-class breakdowns, sensitivity to L beyond 10, effect of flow quality, and computational trade-offs (flow precomputation, storage) at test time.

Reproducibility: Many details are reported (network configuration, data preprocessing, crop strategy, learning-rate schedules). Optical flow algorithm and parameters are specified, and flow compression is described. The paper builds on Caffe with custom multi-GPU code; releasing scripts/configs would further aid reproducibility.

Clarity
The paper is clearly written and well organized. The motivation for separating spatial and temporal streams is clear, and the relation to prior art is explicit. The description of alternative temporal inputs (flow stacking vs trajectory stacking, mean subtraction, bidirectional flow) is clear and formalized. Suggestions to improve clarity:
- Specify the sampling strategy between datasets in multi-task training (e.g., proportions of UCF vs HMDB within mini-batches).
- Provide exact flow rescaling ranges used for JPEG compression and any clipping.
- Include computational costs for training and inference per video, including flow precomputation time and storage, to contextualize practicality.

Originality
The key novelty is the explicit two-stream ConvNet where the temporal stream ingests stacked optical flow rather than raw RGB sequences, combined with simple late fusion. While two-stream ideas and HMAX-based dorsal/ventral separation exist, and optical-flow features are standard in shallow pipelines, feeding motion fields to ConvNets and demonstrating its empirical advantage over stacked RGB frames is a meaningful and nontrivial contribution. The multi-task training across video datasets is a modest but useful addition.

Significance
The results show that ConvNets with explicit motion input can narrow the gap with, and in some settings match, top hand-crafted methods, providing a compelling path forward for deep video recognition. The approach is simple, strong, and likely to be adopted, influencing follow-on work in video CNNs and fusion strategies. The practical requirement to precompute optical flow is a limitation, but common in the field.

Strengths
- Clear decomposition into spatial and temporal streams with principled fusion.
- Strong empirical evidence that optical-flow input outperforms RGB frame stacks for motion modeling.
- Careful ablations on temporal input variants (L, mean subtraction, trajectory vs grid sampling, bidirectional flow).
- Multi-task training effectively leverages multiple datasets for the temporal stream.
- Competitive results on UCF-101 and HMDB-51 with solid baselines.

Weaknesses
- Dependence on precomputed optical flow adds computational and storage overhead; limited discussion of test-time efficiency.
- Limited exploration beyond L=10 and minimal analysis of failure cases or per-class effects.
- Trajectory-aware pooling is acknowledged as missing; experiments do not test fusing with improved dense trajectories to assess complementarity.
- Multi-task training details (dataset sampling ratios, effect of class imbalance) are sparse.
- No statistical significance analysis or confidence intervals.

Suggestions for improvement
- Report inference-time cost and memory footprint with and without flow precomputation; discuss feasibility for real-time or large-scale use.
- Provide per-class accuracy analyses to illustrate where motion vs appearance helps most, and qualitative visualizations of learned temporal filters (mentioned as supplemental; include key examples).
- Explore larger L and variable temporal strides; analyze sensitivity to optical flow quality (e.g., alternative flow algorithms).
- Consider fusing with improved dense trajectories to quantify complementarity with hand-crafted pipelines.
- Clarify multi-task batching/sampling; study the effect of different loss weightings between datasets.
- If possible, release training configurations and scripts.

Arguments for acceptance
- Well-motivated, simple, and effective architecture with clear contributions and strong empirical support.
- Advances understanding of how to incorporate motion into deep models for video.
- Competitive results and thorough ablations make this a valuable contribution to the community.

Arguments against acceptance
- Practicality concerns due to reliance on precomputed optical flow are not fully addressed.
- Some analyses are shallow (limited exploration of temporal depth, lack of error analysis and statistical tests).
- Incremental in some components; fusion and multi-task frameworks are standard.

Overall, the paper is clear, technically sound, and makes a meaningful, impactful contribution to deep video recognition by explicitly modeling motion via optical flow in a two-stream ConvNet.
