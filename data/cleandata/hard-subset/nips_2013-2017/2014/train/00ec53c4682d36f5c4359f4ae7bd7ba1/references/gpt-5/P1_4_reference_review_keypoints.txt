- Proposes a two-stream ConvNet for action recognition: a spatial RGB ConvNet and a temporal ConvNet fed with stacked dense optical flow.
- Fuses spatial and temporal scores (averaging or linear SVM) and introduces multi-task learning sharing the trunk with dataset-specific softmax heads.
- Explicit optical-flow input outperforms approaches that try to learn motion from raw stacked RGB frames.
- Connects to prior two-stream and hand-crafted features, showing temporal ConvNet generalizes HOF/MBH/dense trajectories.
- Multi-task training adapts Collobert & Weston ideas to video and helps reduce overfitting on small datasets.
- Paper is technically sound with careful experimental design and important ablation studies (stack length, stacking method, flow direction, mean subtraction).
- Comparison to raw multi-frame RGB baselines favors the optical-flow-based temporal stream.
- Fusion strategies and multi-task benefits are empirically evaluated; implementation details are sufficient for reproduction.
- Evaluated on UCF-101 and HMDB-51 following standard protocols, achieving competitive results and improving over prior deep baselines.
- Limitations: experiments limited to two datasets, no large-scale pretraining (e.g., Sports-1M).
- Major practical limitation: reliance on precomputed optical flow, creating runtime/storage and non-end-to-end issues.
- Missing analyses: runtime/inference speed, computational cost breakdown, failure-mode and class-wise performance analysis.
- End-to-end motion learning (joint motion estimation + recognition) is not attempted.
- Paper is well written; optical-flow inputs and training protocols are clearly and precisely described.
- Minor clarity suggestions: include key table numbers in text, report inference speed and memory footprint, and add qualitative failure cases.
- Original contributions: explicit spatial/temporal separation, mean-flow subtraction trick, and multi-task temporal ConvNet training.
- Novel combination of existing ideas (flow, two-stream, multi-task) yields an impactful, systematic study of flow-based ConvNets.
- Significance: advances video action recognition, provides a strong baseline, and likely to influence subsequent work.
- Strengths: principled decomposition, convincing ablations, practical implementation tricks, effective multi-tasking, and reproducibility.
- Weaknesses/suggested improvements: explore end-to-end learned motion, large-scale pretraining, better feature-level fusion and temporal models, trajectory-aligned pooling, and improved camera-motion compensation.
- Key questions for authors: test-time throughput including flow, sensitivity to choice/parameters of optical-flow algorithm, and use of temporal augmentation during training.
- Pros for acceptance: strong empirical gains, elegant architecture, thorough ablations, and practical reproducibility.
- Cons for acceptance: not end-to-end, limited large-scale exploration, and missing runtime/failure analyses.
- Overall verdict: high-quality, significant paper that convincingly shows explicit motion input is crucial; recommend acceptance.
