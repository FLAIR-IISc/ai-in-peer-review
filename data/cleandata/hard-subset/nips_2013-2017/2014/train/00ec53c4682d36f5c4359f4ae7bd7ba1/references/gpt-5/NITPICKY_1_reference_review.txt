Summary
The paper proposes a two-stream ConvNet architecture for video action recognition: a spatial stream that operates on RGB frames (pretrained on ImageNet) and a temporal stream that operates on stacked dense optical flow fields. Several variants of temporal input are explored (optical-flow stacking, trajectory stacking, bi-directional flow, mean flow subtraction). The two streams are fused at the score level (average or linear SVM). To mitigate limited video data, the authors employ multi-task learning to jointly train on UCF-101 and HMDB-51. Experiments show the temporal stream substantially outperforms RGB-only baselines; fusion yields further gains and is competitive with trajectory-based shallow methods while outperforming prior deep models.

Relation to prior work
The approach is positioned against Karpathy et al.’s stacked RGB architectures (finding that explicit flow is more effective) and trajectory-based pipelines (HOF/MBH). The authors argue the temporal ConvNet can subsume handcrafted descriptors (e.g., MBH via conv filters on flow gradients). The two-stream idea is loosely motivated by the ventral/dorsal hypothesis and compared to earlier two-stream HMAX-style systems.

Strengths
- Clear decomposition of appearance and motion; explicit optical flow input is empirically strong and conceptually clean.
- Thorough exploration of temporal input design (L frames, trajectory vs. flow stacking, bi-directional flow, mean subtraction).
- Practical multi-task training to leverage multiple datasets with minimal engineering.
- Strong empirical performance: large gains over prior deep baselines; competitive with improved dense trajectories.
- Implementation contributions (multi-GPU training; practical flow precomputation) that enable reproducibility in principle.

Weaknesses and methodological concerns
- Optical flow encoding/compression is underspecified and potentially problematic:
  - “Linearly rescaled to [0,255] and JPEG-compressed,” then “rescaled back to original range.” What is the per-field scale factor stored and how is it recovered? If not stored, this is not invertible. JPEG introduces non-linear quantization and blocking; no ablation on accuracy impact vs. lossless storage.
  - Clarify whether scaling/clipping is global, per-dataset, per-video, or per-frame; handling of outliers; preservation of sign.
- Mean flow subtraction: define precisely (per-frame global mean vector across all pixels? per-channel? before or after cropping/quantization?). No ablation on variants (e.g., robust mean, RANSAC camera motion).
- Trajectory stacking details missing:
  - How are subpixel samples taken along pk (bilinear interpolation?) and what happens near image boundaries?
  - Is the accumulation of pk numerically stable given quantized/compressed flows?
- Bi-directional flow construction: exact temporal window relative to τ, how boundary conditions are handled, and whether forward/backward are normalized identically.
- Training protocol clarity and fairness:
  - For SVM fusion, is the SVM trained on training-set softmax scores and evaluated on test? Any validation split to avoid overfitting calibration? Hyperparameters for SVM?
  - Same learning-rate schedule used across very different dataset sizes without tuning; could bias comparisons.
  - Dropout 0.9: which layers exactly? Is dropout used in both FC layers? Any weight decay values?
  - Sampling: one frame per video per iteration; with L=10, how are temporal start indices sampled to ensure diverse motion? At test-time, 25 frames × 10 crops per video: clarify stride, alignment of flow stacks with chosen frames.
- Pretraining and baselines:
  - ImageNet pretraining improvement (13.5% vs 16.0% top-5) is attributed to sampling from the whole image, but evaluation/augmentation protocols differ from the cited baseline; not an apples-to-apples comparison.
  - The “slow fusion” baseline might not match Karpathy et al. exactly (architecture depth, temporal span, data scale). Reported number (56.4%) is hard to interpret without architectural specifics and tuning parity.
- Statistical rigor:
  - No reporting of variance or confidence intervals over splits; several ablations presented only on split 1 of UCF-101.
  - No significance tests for fusion vs. single-stream improvements.
- Multi-task learning protocol ambiguity:
  - When training multi-task models, do you ever include test splits of the evaluated dataset in training via task mixing? Wording about “full (all splits combined)” for the auxiliary task raises concerns; please ensure no leakage within each dataset’s evaluation protocol and state it explicitly.
- Computational aspects:
  - Inference-time cost (25×10 crops per stream) and memory use are not quantified; missing FPS and FLOPs numbers. The practical deployment cost relative to improved trajectories is unclear.
- Claims about generalizing handcrafted features are mostly qualitative; a more direct probe (e.g., linear separability of HOF/MBH on conv1 activations, or visualization beyond first-layer filters) would strengthen the claim.

Clarity and presentation issues
- Figures/tables are crucial but not fully described in the text excerpt; ensure all figures (e.g., Fig. 1–3) and all tables (1–4) are present, legible, and self-contained with units and settings.
- Several typos and consistency issues:
  - “mult-task” → “multi-task”; “bi-directional” vs “bidirectional” inconsistent.
  - “D convolutional neural networks” likely “3D convolutional…”.
  - “classication” and other minor misspellings.
- References are inconsistent/incomplete: many missing years, venues, page numbers; author lists truncated oddly; some titles inaccurate (e.g., Taylor et al. 2010). Ensure uniform BibTeX formatting and correct metadata.
- Some sections overclaim without citations (e.g., generalization to kinematic features) and lack precise definitions of operations.

Reproducibility checklist items missing
- Exact optical flow parameters for Brox in OpenCV (alpha, gamma, scales, iterations).
- Value ranges/clipping for flow before scaling; dataset mean/std normalization; per-channel normalization.
- Weight decay, initialization details, and learning-rate decay schedule rationale.
- Code/model release plan is not stated.

Pros for acceptance
- Simple, effective decomposition of spatial/motion information with strong empirical gains.
- Clear evidence that explicit flow inputs are better than raw stacked RGB for current ConvNets.
- Multi-task approach is practical and likely to be adopted.

Cons for acceptance
- Numerous missing or ambiguous details impede reproducibility and fair comparison.
- Calibration/fusion methodology and data usage across splits/datasets are not rigorously controlled.
- Presentation and references need significant polishing.

Recommendation
Weak reject. The core idea and results are strong and promising, but the paper in its current form has too many methodological ambiguities, missing ablations (notably around flow compression and calibration), and presentation/reference issues for NeurIPS standards. With clarified protocols, tighter baselines, and improved reproducibility, this work could be a strong submission.

Confidence
High.
