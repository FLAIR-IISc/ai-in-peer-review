- Proposes a two-stream ConvNet separating spatial (RGB) and temporal (stacked optical flow) streams for video action recognition.  
- Temporal stream inputs stacked dense optical flow (forward/bi-directional), optionally along trajectories, with mean-flow subtraction to reduce global motion.  
- Studies design choices: number of stacked flows L, trajectory vs. fixed-location stacking, mean subtraction, and bi-directional flow.  
- Fuses streams at score level (averaging or linear SVM) and uses multi-task learning with shared representation and separate softmax heads for UCF-101 and HMDB-51.  
- Experiments on UCF-101 and HMDB-51 show large gains over deep baselines and competitiveness with state-of-the-art hand-crafted pipelines.  
- Technical soundness: architecture is straightforward and well-motivated; ablations support design choices; multi-task learning is appropriate and validated.  
- Empirical support: thorough protocols, meaningful baselines, and convincing evidence that explicit optical-flow input outperforms learning motion from RGB stacks.  
- Main limitation: reliance on precomputed Brox optical flow which dominates computation and storage and requires JPEG quantization.  
- Lacks sensitivity analysis to flow algorithm choice and JPEG quantization, which could affect accuracy and reproducibility.  
- Camera-motion handling is limited (mean subtraction only); no comparisons to stronger compensation like homography.  
- No reporting of statistical significance or variance across runs.  
- Paper is clear, well-organized, and provides sufficient detail for reproduction; links to hand-crafted features are well explained.  
- Minor suggestions: report runtime/storage trade-offs, per-class error analysis, and clarify code/model release plans.  
- Originality: moderate-to-high—combines known ingredients in a novel, impactful way, with optical-flow-as-input a key departure from prior deep baselines.  
- Significance: high—provides a practical, likely influential baseline that narrows the gap to hand-crafted methods and is useful for practitioners and researchers.  
- Key strengths: simple effective design, strong empirical gains, careful ablations, practical multi-tasking, and solid implementation details.  
- Key weaknesses: heavy dependence on precomputed flow, limited camera-motion treatment, limited sensitivity and runtime analysis, and fusion restricted to late score-level.  
- Questions posed: sensitivity to flow algorithm and JPEG quantization; per-video runtime/storage; impact of stronger camera-motion compensation; variation with temporal window L and sampling density; plans for end-to-end learned motion.  
- Pros for acceptance: strong empirical contribution, well written, generalizable insights, and likely to be influential.  
- Cons for acceptance: not end-to-end, some empirical choices lack deeper analysis, limited exploration of camera motion and fusion alternatives.  
- Recommendation: Accept.  
- Reviewer confidence: High.
