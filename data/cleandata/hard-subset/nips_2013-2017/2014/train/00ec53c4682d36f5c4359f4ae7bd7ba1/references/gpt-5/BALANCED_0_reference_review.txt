Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes a two-stream convolutional architecture for action recognition in videos, with a spatial stream operating on RGB frames and a temporal stream operating on stacked dense optical flow. The streams are trained separately and fused at the score level (averaging or linear SVM). The temporal stream explores several input encodings (optical flow stacking, trajectory stacking, bi-directional flow, mean subtraction), and the authors introduce a simple multi-task learning setup to leverage multiple datasets (UCF-101 and HMDB-51) to mitigate overfitting. Experiments show that (i) temporal ConvNets trained on optical flow outperform ConvNets trained on stacked RGB frames; (ii) fusing spatial and temporal streams provides complementary gains; and (iii) multi-task learning improves robustness on small datasets. The method achieves results competitive with state-of-the-art handcrafted features (e.g., improved dense trajectories with Fisher vectors) and substantially better than prior deep approaches that used raw frames or stacks of frames (e.g., Ji et al., Karpathy et al.).

The work is well positioned with respect to prior shallow methods based on HOG/HOF/MBH, dense trajectories, Fisher vectors, and camera motion compensation; and to prior deep approaches including 3D ConvNets and HMAX-based two-stream systems. A key conceptual contribution is to decouple spatial and temporal processing and to feed motion explicitly via optical flow, addressing the difficulty prior end-to-end models had in learning motion-sensitive filters from RGB alone.

Strengths
- Clear, well-motivated architecture leveraging the ventral/dorsal intuition; decoupling spatial and temporal processing is simple and effective.
- Strong empirical evaluation with informative ablations: number of stacked flows (L), trajectory vs. location stacking, mean-flow subtraction, bi-directional flow, and fusion strategies.
- Multi-task learning across datasets is a practical contribution that demonstrably reduces overfitting and improves accuracy, especially for HMDB-51.
- Good use of pretraining for the spatial stream and careful training protocol details (augmentation, schedules, GPU training).
- Results are competitive with state-of-the-art handcrafted pipelines and substantially exceed previous deep baselines on UCF-101 and HMDB-51.
- Solid connections drawn between the proposed temporal representation and classic motion descriptors (HOF/MBH/kinematics), explaining why the network can subsume them.

Weaknesses and suggestions
Quality
- Optical flow is precomputed with a fixed method (Brox) and compressed; the dependence on the chosen flow algorithm and parameters is not analyzed. Suggest adding sensitivity experiments comparing TV-L1/DeepFlow or different flow qualities/speeds, and reporting performance vs. runtime/storage trade-offs.
- The multi-task learning formulation is minimal (two task-specific softmax heads, summed losses). Details on sampling strategy across datasets, loss weighting, and class-imbalance handling are sparse, and there is no analysis of negative transfer. Suggest reporting: how mini-batches are composed across tasks, whether loss weights were tuned, and per-task accuracy changes as the other task’s data scale varies.
- No statistical confidence intervals or significance tests across the three official splits; given small absolute differences between some variants (e.g., bi-directional vs. uni-directional flow), error bars would help substantiate claims.
- The failed attempt at joint fully connected fusion is informative; however, additional regularization strategies (weight sharing across streams, low-rank bottlenecks, dropout schedules) or parameter-efficient fusion (e.g., gating, feature-wise linear modulation) could be explored to strengthen the case for late fusion as the preferred choice.

Clarity
- Generally very clear. A few implementation clarifications would help replication: exact normalization of flow after JPEG compression (scaling ranges), any clipping of large displacements, and whether mean subtraction is per-frame, per-sample, or dataset-level.
- Training uses one randomly selected frame per video per iteration. It would be useful to describe the temporal sampling scheme in more detail (e.g., total frames seen per epoch, temporal jitter range), as temporal diversity is key.

Originality
- While built from established components, the explicit two-stream design with learned ConvNets on optical flow is a novel and influential combination at the time of writing. The link to classic motion descriptors is insightful.
- Multi-task learning application is straightforward but effective; novelty is incremental here.

Significance
- Strong practical impact: a simple, scalable baseline that clearly advances deep video recognition on standard benchmarks and offers a foundation for future extensions (e.g., end-to-end motion).
- The reliance on precomputed flow imposes computational/storage costs, limiting immediate deployment at scale; addressing this in future work (e.g., learnable flow or motion estimation via differentiable modules) would further increase significance.
- The method is complementary to trajectory-based pipelines; an empirical study fusing two-stream ConvNets with improved dense trajectories could demonstrate whether the approaches capture orthogonal cues.

Additional constructive feedback
- Provide per-class performance and error analysis (e.g., classes benefiting most from temporal stream or bi-directional flow; failure modes with strong camera motion) to guide future architectural changes (trajectory-aware pooling, explicit camera motion compensation beyond mean subtraction).
- Consider temporal modeling beyond stacked flows (e.g., long-term aggregation via temporal pooling, recurrent units, or temporal convolutions) to capture actions with extended dynamics.
- Report computational throughput (frames/second end-to-end including precomputed flow I/O) and training/inference costs, to complement accuracy comparisons.

Arguments for acceptance
- Clear, simple idea that addresses a known limitation of prior deep video models.
- Thorough ablations and strong improvements over deep baselines; competitive with SOTA handcrafted methods.
- Practical multi-task training that improves generalization on small datasets.
- Well written and reproducible, with many implementation details.

Arguments against acceptance
- Dependence on precomputed optical flow hinders end-to-end learning and scalability; limited analysis of flow choice/cost.
- Limited exploration of joint fusion/representation sharing between streams.
- Multi-task learning section could be deeper in analysis and methodology.

Recommendation: Accept
This paper offers a compelling and well-substantiated architectural advance for video action recognition, with clear empirical gains, thoughtful analysis, and high potential impact. Addressing the noted limitations would further strengthen the contribution.
