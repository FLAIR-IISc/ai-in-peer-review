- Proposes two-stream ConvNet for action recognition separating spatial (RGB) and temporal (stacked optical flow) pathways.  
- Temporal stream uses stacked dense optical flow; multiple motion input variants compared (spatial vs trajectory stacking, uni-/bi-directional, mean subtraction).  
- Streams combined via late fusion (averaging or linear SVM on softmax scores).  
- Multi-task learning shares layers but uses separate softmax losses for UCF-101 and HMDB-51 to leverage limited video supervision.  
- Temporal ConvNets on stacked flow outperform ConvNets trained on raw RGB stacks.  
- Spatial and temporal streams are complementary; late fusion yields substantial gains.  
- Multi-task learning improves performance, notably on HMDB-51.  
- Method matches or approaches state-of-the-art handcrafted trajectory pipelines and surpasses prior deep RGB-only baselines.  
- Well-motivated architecture with links to neuroscience and classical motion features.  
- Thorough experimental ablations (flow stack length, stacking method, mean subtraction, bidirectional flow, fusion strategies).  
- Practical multi-task recipe and reasonably documented implementation details.  
- Reliance on precomputed optical flow introduces high computation/storage overhead and possible sensitivity to flow algorithm/parameters (not studied).  
- Not end-to-end: flow estimation is decoupled and non-learned, preventing joint adaptation.  
- Limited evaluation scope: experiments restricted to UCF-101 and HMDB-51, no large-scale or cross-dataset validation.  
- Fusion limited to late score-level methods; feature-level fusion was dismissed due to overfitting and alternatives not explored.  
- Camera motion handling is simplistic (mean flow subtraction) and not compared to stronger compensation schemes.  
- Runtime and inference throughput (flow computation + ConvNet) are not reported.  
- Comparison to 3D ConvNets and optimized RGB-stack baselines could be richer.  
- Methodology and ablations are sound; results support main claims though some sensitivity/runtime issues are underexplored.  
- Paper is clear and reproducible overall; minor missing details (exact flow params, per-layer filter sizes, SVM tuning, runtime/memory analysis).  
- Contribution is novel in combining explicit optical-flow inputs with deep ConvNets and strong empirical validation, though the two-stream idea is not entirely new.  
- High significance: shows explicit motion inputs are crucial with limited data and likely to influence future work.  
- Pros for acceptance: strong empirical gains, clear architecture, thorough ablations, practical multi-task training.  
- Cons for acceptance: not end-to-end, heavy reliance on precomputed flow, limited datasets, limited fusion/camera-motion exploration, unstudied sensitivity to flow choice.  
- Suggested improvements: report inference cost, study robustness to different flow methods/parameters, explore learned flow/joint training, stronger camera-motion compensation, regularized feature-level fusion, per-class/failure analysis, cross-dataset transfer.  
- Minor fixes: reference formatting/typos, clarify RGB jittering at test time, state exact SVM C and tuning procedure.  
- Overall recommendation: Accept.
