

# Review of "Two-Stream Convolutional Networks for Action Recognition in Videos"

## Summary
This paper proposes a two-stream convolutional network architecture for video action recognition, with separate spatial and temporal streams. The spatial stream processes individual video frames (using a pre-trained ImageNet ConvNet), while the temporal stream processes optical flow data. The streams are combined via late fusion. The authors claim their method outperforms previous deep learning approaches and is competitive with state-of-the-art shallow representations.

## Strengths
The paper presents a well-structured approach to video action recognition that effectively leverages the complementary nature of spatial and temporal information. The methodology is clearly presented, and the experimental results are thorough, with comparisons across multiple datasets and configurations. The use of multi-task learning to address data scarcity is particularly insightful.

## Weaknesses

### Formatting and Technical Inconsistencies
1. **Inconsistent terminology**: The paper uses both "UCF101" and "UCF-101" without standardization. This appears in the title, abstract, and throughout the text.

2. **Figure reference issues**: The paper repeatedly references "Fig. 1" without clear context for where this figure appears in the manuscript. The description of the two-stream architecture (Section 2) mentions "as shown in Fig. 1" but no figure is provided in the excerpt.

3. **Equation indexing errors**: Equation (1) uses k = [1;L] but the indexing in the description is inconsistent with the equation's implementation. Similarly, Equation (2) has a recurrence relation that appears to be off by one (p1 = (u,v); pk = pk-1 + dτ+k-2(pk-1), k > 1).

4. **Cropping inconsistency**: The paper states "the smallest side of the frame equals 256" (Section 5), but then describes "a 224×224 sub-image" (Section 5). This creates confusion about the preprocessing pipeline.

### Methodological Issues
5. **Optical flow reference**: The paper cites [2] as "a popular method" for optical flow computation, but [2] is specifically "High accuracy optical flow estimation based on a theory for warping" by Brox et al. The paper should clarify they're using this specific method, not a general optical flow approach.

6. **Multi-task learning description**: The description states "each of the layers is equipped with its own loss function, which operates only on the videos, coming from the respective dataset." This is technically incorrect - loss functions operate on predictions, not on videos themselves.

7. **Missing statistical significance**: The paper claims "the temporal ConvNet significantly outperforms the spatial ConvNet" (Section 6) but provides no statistical tests to support this claim. The 3.5% improvement on UCF-101 (70.2% vs 66.7%) needs proper statistical validation.

8. **Incomplete comparison**: The paper states "the spatial ConvNet alone is fairly competitive on its own" but doesn't compare to other single-stream approaches (e.g., frame-based vs. optical flow-based) under identical conditions.

9. **Inconsistent citation**: The reference to [14] is used to describe both "HMAX architecture" and "Sports-1M dataset," creating confusion about which work is being cited.

### Presentation and Clarity
10. **Unexplained references**: The paper mentions "the use of the Fisher vector encoding [22]" but doesn't explain how this relates to their method, leaving readers confused about its relevance.

11. **Unclear figure description**: The paper references "Fig. 3" but doesn't provide a clear explanation of the figure's content in the text, making it difficult to understand the optical flow stacking vs. trajectory stacking comparison.

12. **Inconsistent reference formatting**: The reference list has inconsistent journal abbreviations (e.g., "IEEE PAMI" vs. "IEEE Transactions on Pattern Analysis and Machine Intelligence") and missing publication details.

## Recommendation
While the paper presents a reasonable approach to video action recognition with some interesting methodological insights, the cumulative effect of these minor but pervasive technical and formatting inconsistencies significantly undermines the paper's credibility. The inconsistent terminology, equation errors, and methodological misstatements create unnecessary confusion and suggest a lack of careful proofreading that is unacceptable for a NIPS submission.

The paper's contribution is not sufficiently novel compared to prior work, and the experimental results are not presented with sufficient rigor to support the claimed improvements. The minor flaws listed above are not merely typographical but represent genuine technical inconsistencies that would frustrate readers attempting to reproduce or build upon this work.

Given the stringent standards of NIPS, I recommend rejection due to these cumulative technical and presentation issues. The paper's core ideas have merit, but the execution lacks the precision and thoroughness expected at this venue.
