Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes a two-stream convolutional architecture for action recognition in videos that decouples spatial (appearance) and temporal (motion) information. The spatial stream is a ConvNet trained on still frames; it benefits from ImageNet pretraining and fine-tuning. The temporal stream is a ConvNet trained on stacked dense optical flow fields computed between adjacent frames. The authors study multiple design choices for temporal input (flow stacking vs. trajectory-aligned stacking, uni- vs. bi-directional flow, and mean flow subtraction) and fuse the two streams at the score level (average or linear SVM). To mitigate limited video labels, they adopt a simple multi-task learning setup with dual softmax heads for UCF-101 and HMDB-51.

This work situates itself between shallow, hand-crafted pipelines (dense trajectories with HOF/MBH and Fisher vectors) and prior deep models that attempted to learn spatiotemporal features from stacks of RGB frames (e.g., Karpathy et al., 2014; Ji et al., 2013), and HMAX-style two-stream systems. The key idea is to make motion explicit via precomputed optical flow, allowing the ConvNet to learn discriminative motion representations more effectively than implicit motion learning from RGB stacks. The authors also relate their temporal ConvNet to classic motion descriptors by noting that HOF/MBH/kinematic features can be approximated by early conv layers applied to flow fields.

Quality
- Methodology: The paper is technically sound. The design of the temporal input is well motivated, and the ablation studies are thorough: number of stacked flows (L), trajectory stacking, mean subtraction, and bidirectionality. Fusion strategies and multi-task learning are evaluated. The training details and data augmentation are clear and largely reproducible.
- Empirical evidence: On UCF-101 and HMDB-51, a temporal ConvNet trained on flow significantly outperforms spatial-only and RGB-stack “slow fusion” baselines, and the two-stream fusion closes the gap to state-of-the-art hand-crafted methods. Multi-task learning improves generalization when data are scarce. The experimental protocol follows standard splits and averaging over crops/frames.
- Limitations in evaluation: While comparisons to deep baselines are strong, more systematic comparison to the strongest shallow baselines (e.g., improved dense trajectories with camera motion compensation and Fisher vectors) would further contextualize performance; the paper states being competitive but provides limited side-by-side analysis. Statistical significance and per-class analyses are not provided. Computational trade-offs (flow computation cost, storage, inference latency) are discussed qualitatively but not quantified end-to-end.

Clarity
The paper is clearly written and well organized. The temporal input formulations are precisely defined, and implementation details (learning schedule, augmentation, multi-GPU) are useful. Minor suggestions:
- Report the final selected L explicitly alongside final results and clarify any changes across experiments.
- Provide end-to-end runtime: training time (including flow precomputation), and per-video inference latency for each stream and the fused system.
- Briefly summarize the impact of JPEG compression of flow on accuracy (the choice is practical but could introduce artifacts).

Originality
The two-stream ConvNet with explicit optical-flow input is a notable and timely contribution. While the idea of separate spatial/temporal streams echoes the dorsal/ventral hypothesis and prior HMAX-based systems, learning deep temporal features from stacked flow fields is novel relative to contemporaneous RGB-stack ConvNets. The multi-task setup is straightforward but effective in this domain. The analysis drawing connections between learned filters and classical motion descriptors is insightful.

Significance
Action recognition is an important problem; this paper provides a pragmatic and impactful step that bridges hand-crafted motion features and deep learning. The empirical results demonstrate clear gains over RGB-stack deep models and competitive performance relative to hand-crafted pipelines, with a simple fusion that further improves accuracy. Given its practicality and performance, this approach is likely to be widely used and extended (e.g., toward end-to-end motion learning or trajectory-aware pooling).

Strengths
- Clear, well-motivated two-stream decomposition with complementary gains from fusion.
- Strong empirical evidence and ablations showing why explicit motion via optical flow is effective.
- Practical multi-task learning to leverage multiple small datasets.
- Solid engineering and reproducibility details; sensible data augmentation; multi-GPU training.
- Insightful discussion connecting temporal ConvNet to classical motion descriptors.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Dependence on precomputed optical flow: not end-to-end, adds significant compute/storage overhead; consider reporting complete compute budgets and exploring lighter/faster flow (e.g., TV-L1) and the accuracy-speed trade-off.
- Limited benefit from bidirectional and trajectory stacking suggests room for better motion aggregation (e.g., trajectory-aligned pooling in later layers, or attention over motion tubes).
- Fusion is limited to late score fusion; try joint training with shared or cross-stream interactions (e.g., feature-level fusion with regularization to mitigate overfitting).
- Camera motion compensation is approximated by mean flow subtraction; evaluate stronger compensation (global homography/affine) to test whether gains carry over.
- Report per-class confusions and failure modes (e.g., actions dominated by camera motion).

Questions for the authors
- How sensitive are results to the accuracy of flow? What is the performance drop when using faster, less accurate flow methods?
- Does JPEG compression of flow harm small motions? Did you test lossless compression or quantization schemes?
- What is the end-to-end inference time per video for each stream and for the fused model on a single GPU?
- Did you attempt any intermediate feature fusion (e.g., concatenating conv5 or fc layers with dropout/weight decay) beyond score fusion?

Reasons to accept
- Conceptually simple and novel approach that makes motion explicit and demonstrably improves over prior deep baselines.
- Strong, carefully executed experiments with informative ablations and practical training strategies.
- High potential impact; likely to become a baseline for video action recognition.

Reasons to reject
- Not end-to-end; heavy reliance on precomputed flow may limit practicality and elegance.
- Limited analysis of compute/storage costs and comparisons to strongest hand-crafted baselines.
- Fusion and trajectory modeling could be more thoroughly explored.

Overall, this is a high-quality, influential contribution that advances deep video understanding. I recommend acceptance.
