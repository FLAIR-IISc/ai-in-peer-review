Summary
The paper proposes a two-stream convolutional architecture for action recognition in videos: a spatial stream operating on RGB frames (pretrained on ImageNet) and a temporal stream operating on stacked dense optical flow. The temporal input is constructed by stacking L optical flow fields (forward and optionally backward), with variants including trajectory stacking and mean flow subtraction. The two streams are fused by late fusion (average or linear SVM). To mitigate limited video data, the authors use a multi-task setup sharing the network trunk and having dataset-specific softmax heads for UCF-101 and HMDB-51. Experiments show: (i) the temporal stream substantially outperforms the spatial stream trained on frames; (ii) stacking multiple flows and mean subtraction help; (iii) bidirectional and trajectory stacking offer marginal gains; (iv) multi-task learning improves especially on HMDB-51; (v) late fusion improves over either stream and is competitive with trajectory-based shallow baselines.

Relation to prior work
The work is positioned against: (a) hand-crafted trajectory-based representations with HOF/MBH and Fisher encodings (Wang & Schmid; Peng et al.), which are still SoTA; (b) earlier deep video models that consume stacks of frames (Karpathy et al.), which underperform; and (c) two-stream ideas in shallow HMAX-style models. The key novelty here is to explicitly feed optical flow into a learned temporal ConvNet and combine with a spatial ConvNet via late fusion, plus a pragmatic multi-task training scheme to leverage multiple datasets.

Strengths
- Clear motivation to decouple spatial appearance and motion; the architecture is simple and pragmatic.
- Good set of ablations on temporal input design (L, mean subtraction, trajectory vs dense stacking, bi-directional flow).
- Sensible training and test protocols; implementation details are provided (mini-batch construction, cropping, learning rate schedules, multi-GPU).
- Multi-task learning is an appropriate and well-justified way to avoid overfitting on small video datasets; demonstrates measurable gains.
- Empirical results are substantially better than prior deep models on UCF-101/HMDB-51 and close to the best hand-crafted pipelines, despite limited training data.
- The paper relates the proposed temporal input to hand-crafted flow-based features and argues how ConvNets can subsume them.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Heavy reliance on precomputed optical flow from a specific algorithm (Brox flow) breaks end-to-end learning and raises questions about generality. There is no evaluation of sensitivity to the optical flow method, its parameters, or the effects of lossy JPEG compression of flow fields.
- Computational burden is significant: precomputing and storing optical flow (27GB compressed for UCF-101) plus large GPU requirements. Runtime/throughput comparisons to strong hand-crafted pipelines (e.g., improved trajectories with Fisher vectors) are missing.
- Comparisons to trajectory-based SoTA are somewhat limited; the method is “competitive” rather than clearly superior. Given the emphasis on rigorous validation, it would be useful to show stronger head-to-head, including late fusion with improved trajectories or at least a combined system.
- Limited architectural exploration. Only late fusion of softmax scores is considered; attempts at joint FC fusion are dismissed due to overfitting without exploring standard regularizers (weight sharing, bottlenecks, dropout schedules, L2/L1 penalties) or more calibrated score fusion.
- Potential data leakage risks are not discussed: multi-task training shares a trunk across datasets; are there duplicate or near-duplicate videos across UCF-101 and HMDB-51 (e.g., YouTube sources)? How are such overlaps handled?
- Evaluation scope is narrow: only two benchmark datasets, no analysis of robustness to camera motion beyond mean subtraction, no per-class error analysis, no statistical significance testing, and no cross-dataset generalization results.
- Some implementation choices merit deeper justification: choice of L=10, mean subtraction vs explicit camera motion compensation, bidirectional flow’s limited benefit. Hyperparameter selection seems guided by a single split; risk of overfitting to split 1 is not addressed.

Quality
Generally sound and careful empirically, with useful ablations. However, the study stops short of the level of rigor expected to displace established baselines: lack of sensitivity analyses to the flow algorithm/parameters, missing runtime/resource comparisons, and limited exploration of fusion alternatives. Claims about generalizing hand-crafted features are plausible but remain speculative without stronger evidence (e.g., visualizations help, but measuring performance without flow magnitude/orientation would be informative).

Clarity
The paper is clearly written and well organized. The construction of temporal inputs is precisely specified. Some figures/tables are referenced but not fully visible here; assuming they are clear. Minor reference formatting issues (e.g., “D convolutional neural networks” presumably 3D; several incomplete citations) should be fixed.

Originality
The two-stream idea is not entirely new, but its instantiation with a learned ConvNet on stacked flow is a meaningful and nontrivial contribution. The multi-task training setup is standard but appropriate. Overall, the contribution is more of a solid, practical combination and ablation rather than a fundamentally new model.

Significance
The work addresses an important problem and provides a strong baseline that improves over prior deep approaches and approaches the best hand-crafted methods. Its dependence on external flow and computational cost may limit adoption in some settings. Nonetheless, it is likely to influence subsequent architectures and practice.

Questions for the authors
- How sensitive are results to the choice of optical flow algorithm (e.g., TV-L1 vs Brox) and parameters? Does the JPEG compression introduce measurable degradation?
- What is the training/inference time per video for each stream and the fused system, compared to improved trajectories + Fisher vectors?
- Did you check for duplicate videos across UCF-101 and HMDB-51 when doing multi-task training?
- Can you provide per-class results and failure mode analysis, especially for classes where temporal vs spatial helps?
- Did you attempt more regularized joint fusion (e.g., shared bottleneck layers, calibrated temperature scaling) before concluding overfitting?

Suggestions for improvement
- Add sensitivity analyses for optical flow choice/quality and the effects of compression.
- Provide runtime and memory comparisons to strong baselines, and report FLOPs.
- Explore stronger fusion strategies with regularization, and/or fuse with hand-crafted trajectory features for a true SoTA.
- Analyze robustness to camera motion and compare mean subtraction with explicit motion compensation.
- Report cross-dataset generalization and statistical significance across splits.

Recommendation
Overall rating: 6 (weak accept). The paper offers a clear, well-executed, and empirically validated approach that substantially improves over prior deep baselines and is competitive with established hand-crafted methods. While I remain cautious about the reliance on precomputed flow and the limited comparisons, the contributions are practical and likely to be useful to the community. Addressing the concerns above would strengthen the case.
