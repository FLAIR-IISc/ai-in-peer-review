Summary
This paper proposes a two-stream ConvNet architecture for video action recognition: a spatial stream operating on RGB frames (pre-trained on ImageNet and fine-tuned) and a temporal stream operating on stacked dense optical flow fields. The temporal input variants include optical-flow stacking, trajectory stacking, and bi-directional flow; mean-flow subtraction is introduced to reduce camera-motion bias. The two streams are fused by averaging or by a linear SVM over softmax scores. To mitigate small dataset size, a simple multi-task training setup shares all layers and uses two task-specific softmax heads for UCF-101 and HMDB-51. Experiments show: (i) temporal networks significantly outperform single-frame spatial ones, (ii) stacking multiple flows (L≈10) and mean subtraction help, (iii) two-stream fusion improves over each stream, attaining results comparable to strong shallow baselines (e.g., improved dense trajectories + FV).

Relation to prior work
The approach is positioned against two main lines: hand-crafted trajectory-based pipelines (HOF/MBH, IDT+FV) and prior deep models that operate on stacks of RGB frames (e.g., [14]) or hand-crafted HMAX two-streams [16]. The key difference is explicit motion input via optical flow (computed externally), which aims to generalize hand-crafted flow-derived descriptors within a ConvNet framework.

Quality
- Empirical support: The paper provides ablations for the temporal input (L, stacking vs trajectory stacking, bi-directionality, mean subtraction) and evaluates on UCF-101 and HMDB-51 using the standard protocol. Multi-task learning is demonstrated to help on HMDB-51 and slightly on UCF-101.
- Methodological rigor: The design choices (flow stacking, mean subtraction, L=10) are motivated and tested, though hyperparameter search appears limited. There is no statistical analysis across multiple runs; reported improvements (especially small gains from fusion method or bi-directionality) may be within run-to-run variance.
- Baselines: Comparisons to prior deep models are favorable. However, comparisons to the most competitive shallow pipelines (IDT+FV with camera-motion compensation) rely on published numbers only; a careful, side-by-side reimplementation or standardized code would strengthen the claims. Also missing are comparisons to improved IDT variants with deep Fisher networks [21] under identical evaluation settings.
- Practicality and efficiency: Optical flow is precomputed (Brox et al.) and JPEG-compressed; although timings are mentioned (0.06 s per frame pair), the cumulative per-video cost and total test-time throughput (including 25×10 crops) are not fully quantified relative to IDT. The approach is not end-to-end and hinges on a heavy pre-processing step; sensitivity to the chosen flow algorithm is not explored.
- Fairness and controls: Camera motion is handled only by mean subtraction, whereas top shallow methods use explicit camera-motion compensation. A controlled comparison (with/without compensation in both pipelines) would clarify fairness. The fusion SVM is trained on softmax outputs; details about cross-validation and prevention of overfitting in SVM training are light.

Clarity
The paper is generally clear and well organized. Implementation details are substantial (architecture, training schedules, data augmentation, multi-GPU training). A few clarifications would help:
- Explicit optical flow parameters (pyramid levels, smoothness weights), and whether they were tuned per dataset.
- Details on SVM fusion training (validation split, regularization).
- Exact cost of inference per video for both streams vs IDT+FV.

Originality
Moderate. The two-stream idea itself is known (e.g., two-pathway HMAX), and multi-task learning is standard. The main novelty is the specific and effective integration of stacked dense optical flow as ConvNet input with mean subtraction and late fusion. The argument that the temporal ConvNet generalizes HOF/MBH/kinematics is reasonable but incremental.

Significance
The paper shows a clear performance lift over prior deep models that rely on RGB stacks and achieves competitive results with strong hand-crafted baselines on standard datasets using relatively modest training data. While not surpassing the best shallow pipelines decisively, the work provides a plausible and pragmatic path for deep architectures to close the gap by leveraging explicit motion input. Given the community’s interest in deep learning for video, this is likely to be influential and widely used.

Pros
- Clear evidence that explicit optical flow input substantially improves over RGB-stack ConvNets.
- Thorough ablation on temporal input configurations; simple mean-flow subtraction helps.
- Complementarity between spatial and temporal streams demonstrated; fusion is effective.
- Practical multi-task setup to leverage multiple datasets; boosts performance on smaller HMDB-51.
- Good engineering detail; likely reproducible.

Cons
- Heavy reliance on precomputed optical flow; not end-to-end, with unquantified overall compute at test time relative to IDT.
- Limited analysis of sensitivity to the optical flow algorithm or its parameters.
- No statistical significance or multiple-run variance; some gains are small.
- Camera-motion handling is rudimentary compared to IDT+CMC; fairness of comparisons is unclear.
- Novelty is incremental; several components (two streams, multi-task learning, late fusion) are established ideas.

Questions for the authors
1. How sensitive are results to the choice of optical flow algorithm and parameters? Have you tried DeepFlow or TV-L1?
2. What is the end-to-end test-time cost per video (including flow and ConvNet passes) for each stream and the fused system? How does this compare to IDT+FV?
3. Could you report variance over multiple training runs and significance for key deltas (e.g., bi-directional vs forward, SVM vs averaging)?
4. Have you tried explicit camera-motion compensation in the flow before stacking?
5. How is the SVM fusion trained/validated to avoid overfitting on the test splits?

Recommendation
Weak accept. The approach is conservative and leverages established components (optical flow, ConvNets, late fusion, multi-task learning) in a coherent way, with solid empirical gains over prior deep baselines and competitive results vs strong shallow methods. While not methodologically groundbreaking and with some missing analyses (compute, sensitivity, variance), the paper is carefully executed and likely to have practical impact. Strengthening the evaluation along the noted points would make the contribution more compelling.
