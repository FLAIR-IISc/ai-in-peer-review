This paper proposes a two-stream convolutional network architecture for action recognition in videos, decoupling spatial and temporal information processing into separate streams. While the core idea of separating spatial and temporal processing isn’t entirely novel (building on the “two-streams hypothesis”), the application of deep convolutional networks to this approach, combined with pre-training on ImageNet and the exploration of various optical flow input configurations, represents a potentially valuable contribution. However, the paper suffers from a multitude of minor, yet persistent, flaws that collectively detract from its overall quality and impact.

**Summary & Related Work:** The introduction adequately frames the problem and situates the work within the context of existing action recognition methods. The review of related work is reasonably comprehensive, covering both shallow, hand-crafted feature approaches and prior deep learning attempts. However, the citations are occasionally inconsistent – for example, [14] is referenced multiple times but the specific contributions of each citation aren’t always clearly delineated. The connection to the “two-streams hypothesis” is mentioned but dismissed quickly, representing a missed opportunity for a deeper discussion.

**Strengths:**
*   **Clear Architecture:** The two-stream architecture is presented clearly and logically.
*   **Pre-training:** Leveraging ImageNet pre-training for the spatial stream is a sensible approach.
*   **Optical Flow Exploration:** The investigation of different optical flow input configurations (stacking, trajectories, bi-directional flow) is a positive aspect.
*   **Multi-task Learning:** The inclusion of multi-task learning to address data scarcity is a pragmatic contribution.
*   **Competitive Performance:** The results demonstrate competitive performance on standard benchmarks, outperforming [14] and approaching the state-of-the-art shallow methods.

**Weaknesses:**
*   **Formatting and Typos:** The manuscript is riddled with minor formatting inconsistencies and typos. "mult-task" appears repeatedly instead of "multi-task," there's an inconsistency in section numbering (Sect. 1.1 vs. Sect. 2), and figure captions are sometimes incomplete. These errors suggest a lack of meticulous attention to detail.
*   **Insufficient Ablation Studies:** While various optical flow configurations are explored, the ablation studies aren’t rigorous enough. For example, the benefit of bi-directional flow is minimal and the explanation lacks depth.
*   **Limited Hyperparameter Exploration:** The paper doesn’t provide sufficient detail on the hyperparameter tuning process. The specific learning rate schedule, dropout rates, and other critical parameters are mentioned but lack justification. A sensitivity analysis would strengthen the findings.
*   **Lack of Statistical Significance:** The performance comparisons lack statistical significance testing. Reporting only mean accuracies without standard deviations or p-values weakens the conclusions.
*   **Missing Baseline:** A direct comparison with a single-stream CNN trained on raw video frames (without optical flow) isn’t provided. This would establish a clearer baseline for assessing the benefit of the temporal stream.
*   **Flow Computation Details:** While [2] is cited for optical flow computation, the impact of parameter choices within that method (e.g., scale selection) isn't discussed.
*   **Implementation Details:** The discussion of multi-GPU training is somewhat superficial. Details about data partitioning and synchronization strategies would be valuable.
*   **Figure Quality:** Figures 2 and 3 are difficult to interpret. The color schemes are unclear, and the diagrams lack sufficient labels. Figure 1 is too small and difficult to read.
*   **Overly Assertive Language:** Claims like "significant improvement" or "state-of-the-art" need to be substantiated with stronger statistical evidence.

**Pro/Con Acceptance:**

**Pro:**
*   Addresses an important problem in video understanding.
*   Presents a novel combination of techniques (deep ConvNets + two-stream architecture).
*   Achieves competitive results on standard benchmarks.
*   Multi-task learning is a practical contribution.

**Con:**
*   Numerous minor flaws (formatting, typos, insufficient detail) demonstrate a lack of thoroughness.
*   Ablation studies are limited and lack statistical rigor.
*   Missing baselines and insufficient hyperparameter exploration weaken the conclusions.
*   The paper feels incomplete and rushed, despite presenting reasonable results.



**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a reasonable approach to action recognition, but its quality is significantly hampered by a pervasive lack of attention to detail. While the core ideas are sound and the results are promising, the numerous minor flaws erode confidence in the overall validity of the work. The paper falls short of the standards expected for NIPS acceptance.

**Recommendation:** **Reject**. 

I recommend rejection due to the large number of minor, but consistent, issues. Addressing these flaws – particularly the formatting errors, the lack of statistical rigor, and the insufficient detail in the experimental setup – would significantly strengthen the paper. The authors should resubmit to a different venue after a thorough revision.
