This paper proposes a two-stream convolutional network architecture for action recognition in videos, decoupling spatial and temporal information into separate CNN streams, pre-training the spatial stream on ImageNet, and combining outputs via late fusion. While the idea of separating spatial and temporal streams is not entirely new (referencing the “two-streams hypothesis” and related work in [16]), the application to deep convolutional networks and the specific implementation details presented are the core contributions. The authors demonstrate improved performance over a direct stacking of frames approach [14] and achieve competitive results compared to established hand-crafted feature methods [20, 21, 26] on UCF101 and HMDB51.

The paper is generally well-written and organized, clearly articulating the motivation and methodology. However, the presentation feels somewhat incremental. The core idea of leveraging separate streams is not groundbreaking, and the benefits demonstrated, while positive, aren't a dramatic leap forward. The work builds heavily on existing methodologies – established CNN architectures, optical flow computation, and late fusion techniques – and the innovation lies more in the combination of these rather than a truly novel algorithmic contribution. 

A significant weakness is the limited scope of novelty and the relatively modest gains. The paper doesn't convincingly demonstrate *why* this two-stream approach is superior beyond empirically observing the improvement. The discussion of the two-streams hypothesis feels superficial and doesn’t justify its inclusion beyond a historical reference. The results, while competitive, don’t significantly outperform established methods, and the improvements are particularly small given the computational cost associated with computing optical flow. The authors state the method is "competitive with the state of the art shallow representations," but that qualification feels necessary, and the reader is left wondering why a *deep* architecture isn't achieving more substantial gains.

Furthermore, the ablation studies, while present, are not exhaustive enough. The effect of different fusion methods (averaging vs. SVM) is explored, but more investigation into the optimal network configurations for each stream, alternative optical flow implementations, and the impact of different pre-training datasets would strengthen the analysis. The performance improvement from multi-task learning, while notable, isn’t thoroughly explored or justified beyond observing an increase in accuracy. The benefits of bi-directional flow are not realised in practice. 

The experimental setup appears sound, utilizing standard datasets and evaluation protocols. However, the reliance on pre-computed optical flow is a potential limitation. While practical, it introduces an external dependency and doesn’t allow for end-to-end training.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Demonstrated improvement over a direct frame stacking approach [14].
*   Competitive performance on standard action recognition benchmarks.
*   Clear and well-organized presentation of the methodology.
*   Successful application of pre-training on ImageNet to the spatial stream.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Incremental novelty – the core idea of separate streams isn't new.
*   Modest performance gains compared to established methods.
*   Limited ablation studies and exploration of design choices.
*   Reliance on pre-computed optical flow.
*   Superficial connection to the “two-streams hypothesis.”
*   Lack of detailed theoretical analysis justifying the architectural choices.



**Overall Assessment:**

This paper is a solid contribution, but ultimately falls short of being a standout submission to NIPS. It presents a reasonable approach to action recognition using deep learning, but lacks the groundbreaking innovation or substantial performance gains that would warrant strong acceptance. While the authors demonstrate a clear understanding of the field and implement a sound methodology, the work feels more like an engineering effort to apply existing techniques effectively rather than a significant scientific advance. I would lean towards **reject**, with the encouragement to further explore the theoretical underpinnings of the two-stream architecture and conduct more comprehensive ablation studies to better understand the factors driving performance. The authors should also consider end-to-end training approaches to remove the dependency on pre-computed optical flow.
