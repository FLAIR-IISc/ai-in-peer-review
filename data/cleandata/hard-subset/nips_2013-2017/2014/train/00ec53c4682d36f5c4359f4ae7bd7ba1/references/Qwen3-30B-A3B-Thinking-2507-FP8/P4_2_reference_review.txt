

# Review: Two-Stream Convolutional Networks for Action Recognition in Videos

This paper proposes a two-stream Convolutional Network architecture for action recognition in videos, building upon the two-streams hypothesis from neuroscience. The spatial stream processes individual video frames using a pre-trained ConvNet (on ImageNet), while the temporal stream processes optical flow to capture motion information. The streams are trained separately and combined via late fusion. The authors demonstrate that their approach significantly outperforms previous deep learning methods on UCF101 and HMDB-51 benchmarks, achieving results competitive with state-of-the-art hand-crafted feature methods.

The work relates to several key NIPS contributions. It extends the two-stream hypothesis (Goodale & Milner, 1992) to deep learning, building upon previous video recognition work including HMAX-based approaches [16] and stacked-frame ConvNets [14]. The paper properly cites these works, acknowledging that [16] previously proposed a two-stream architecture but used shallow HMAX models rather than deep ConvNets. The temporal stream's use of optical flow represents a departure from [14], which found stacked-frame ConvNets to be inferior to hand-crafted features.

**Strengths:**
1. **Technical Soundness:** The paper provides a thorough experimental evaluation with ablation studies on input configurations for the temporal stream (optical flow stacking, trajectory stacking, bi-directional flow, etc.). The multi-task learning approach for combining UCF101 and HMDB-51 datasets is well-justified and effectively addresses the small dataset challenge. Results are consistently reproducible across multiple benchmarks.

2. **Clarity:** The architecture is clearly described with proper figures and mathematical notation. The implementation details are sufficiently thorough for reproducibility. The paper effectively explains why optical flow-based temporal streams outperform stacked-frame approaches.

3. **Significance:** The results demonstrate a substantial improvement over previous deep learning methods for video recognition, closing the gap with state-of-the-art hand-crafted features. The approach provides a strong baseline for future work in video action recognition.

**Weaknesses:**
1. **Novelty:** While the two-stream concept is not entirely novel (as [16] previously proposed a similar architecture), the application to deep ConvNets with optical flow for the temporal stream is a meaningful contribution. However, the paper does not sufficiently differentiate its approach from [16] beyond the use of ConvNets rather than HMAX. The connection to neuroscience is mentioned but not meaningfully developed.

2. **Comparison to Recent Work:** The paper compares to prior deep learning work but doesn't adequately address the most recent state-of-the-art methods published after [14], such as those using 3D convolutions. The paper was submitted before some very recent advances, but this should be acknowledged.

3. **Limitations Analysis:** The paper doesn't thoroughly discuss the limitations of their approach, particularly regarding failure cases or scenarios where the method doesn't perform well. The authors note that their current architecture misses some key components of state-of-the-art shallow representations (like trajectory-based pooling), but don't explore this in depth.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
- Demonstrates significant improvement over previous deep learning methods for video action recognition
- Provides a clear, well-structured architecture that effectively combines spatial and temporal information
- Includes thorough ablation studies and implementation details
- Achieves results competitive with state-of-the-art hand-crafted methods
- Multi-task learning approach is well-justified and effective

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**
- Novelty is incremental rather than groundbreaking (builds on [16] with ConvNets)
- Doesn't sufficiently differentiate from prior work on two-stream architectures
- Limited analysis of failure cases and limitations
- Doesn't fully address the most recent state-of-the-art methods

**Overall Assessment:**
This paper presents a technically sound, well-executed approach to video action recognition that significantly improves upon previous deep learning methods. The two-stream architecture with optical flow for the temporal stream is a meaningful contribution that advances the state of the art for deep video recognition. While the novelty is somewhat incremental (building on [16] with ConvNets), the paper provides sufficient justification for the architectural choices and demonstrates clear improvements over prior work. The quality of the experiments, clarity of presentation, and significance of results make this a strong contribution to the field of video recognition.

The paper meets NIPS standards for technical soundness, clarity, and significance. The novelty is sufficient for acceptance as a solid contribution to the field, though not revolutionary. The authors have adequately addressed the challenges of small video datasets through multi-task learning and have provided a thorough evaluation of their approach. The paper is well-written and would be valuable to the NIPS community as a reference for video recognition with deep learning.

The paper is within the scope of NIPS, as it addresses a fundamental problem in computer vision using deep learning techniques, which is squarely within the conference's focus. The work represents a significant advancement over previous deep learning approaches to video recognition, making it worthy of acceptance.
