Summary
The paper proposes a two-stream architecture for action recognition in videos: a spatial ConvNet operating on RGB frames (pre-trained on ImageNet) and a temporal ConvNet operating on stacked dense optical flow fields. The two streams are fused by late score-level averaging or a linear SVM. The authors explore several temporal input configurations (optical-flow stacking, trajectory stacking, bi-directional flow, mean flow subtraction) and adopt a multi-task learning setup to mitigate small data by sharing a backbone while training separate heads for UCF-101 and HMDB-51. Experiments show substantial gains over prior deep models trained on raw frames and performance competitive with contemporary hand-crafted pipelines (improved dense trajectories with Fisher vectors), with careful ablations and implementation details.

Relation to prior work
The design builds on established pipelines: ConvNets for images (Krizhevsky et al.), dense optical flow (Brox et al.), and trajectory-based features (HOF/MBH, IDT). Unlike earlier deep approaches that stack RGB frames (Karpathy et al.), the temporal stream uses optical flow explicitly. The two-stream idea echoes the ventral/dorsal hypothesis and prior two-branch HMAX work (Jhuang et al.), but here both branches are trainable ConvNets. The paper claims the temporal ConvNet generalizes HOF/MBH via learnable filters, which is plausible and partially illustrated.

Strengths
- Sensible architecture grounded in well-established components (optical flow + ConvNets), with clear motivation for decoupling spatial and temporal cues.
- Solid empirical study: ablations on number of flow frames (L), trajectory vs. regular stacking, bi-directional flow, mean subtraction, fusion method, and multi-task learning.
- Strong improvements over prior deep baselines; the temporal stream alone substantially outperforms single-frame ConvNets trained on RGB stacks.
- Clear and reproducible implementation details (training schedules, augmentation, multi-GPU, test-time sampling), including practical engineering for precomputing/compressing flow.
- Multi-task learning is a reasonable way to leverage multiple small datasets without manual curation of non-overlapping classes.

Weaknesses
- Not end-to-end: the approach relies on a heavy, hand-crafted optical-flow front end (Brox flow). This introduces a strong, non-learned prior and substantial compute/storage overhead. The paper does not quantify sensitivity to the choice of flow algorithm or its parameters. It is difficult to attribute gains between flow quality and ConvNet learning.
- Comparison to the strongest hand-crafted baselines is incomplete. The paper claims competitiveness with improved dense trajectories + Fisher vectors (with camera-motion compensation), but a rigorous, protocol-matched comparison is needed, ideally with statistical significance and exact numbers per split. Fusion with IDT features is not explored to demonstrate complementarity to the de facto standard.
- Dataset scope is limited (UCF-101, HMDB-51); no results on larger-scale datasets (e.g., Sports-1M) despite discussing them. Overfitting remains a risk; the failure of deeper fusion (joint FC layers) is attributed to overfitting, but alternative regularization (weight decay schedules, batch norm, stronger data augmentation, label smoothing) is not investigated.
- Camera motion handling is rudimentary (mean flow subtraction). No comparison to standard homography-based compensation used in IDT, which is known to be important.
- Multi-task learning details could be tighter: the sampling scheme across datasets, balance between tasks in mini-batches, and potential bias from combining all splits of one dataset when evaluating on a split of the other are not fully specified. Reported gains are modest; error bars are absent.
- Computational practicality is under-discussed: precomputing flow (TB-scale uncompressed) and runtime per video at test time are not quantified end-to-end, which matters for applicability.

Quality
Technically sound and carefully engineered; ablations are useful. However, key baselines (IDT+FV with camera-motion compensation) should be compared in a stricter, statistically supported manner. Sensitivity to optical-flow choice and compute/latency trade-offs should be quantified. The multi-task formulation is standard and works, but analysis is limited.

Clarity
Well written and organized. The architectural and training details are sufficiently specified to reproduce. Some aspects (dataset sampling in multi-task training, exact split usage when combining datasets) require clarification.

Originality
Moderate. The components are established; the contribution is a well-executed combination: explicit flow as input to a temporal ConvNet and a simple late-fusion two-stream design. The connection to hand-crafted descriptors is insightful but not deeply analyzed.

Significance
Moderate to high. The method offers a practical recipe that clearly outperforms prior deep baselines and approaches the best hand-crafted pipelines on standard benchmarks. If validated against stronger baselines and with clearer compute/performance trade-offs, it can influence practice.

Questions for the authors
- How sensitive is performance to the optical flow algorithm? Please report results with a faster, lower-quality flow (e.g., TV-L1 or Farnebäck) and with different flow parameters.
- Can you compare to and/or fuse with IDT+FV (with camera compensation) on the same splits? What is the combined performance?
- What is the end-to-end test-time throughput per video (including flow), and memory footprint without precomputation? Is real-time feasible?
- In multi-task training, how are batches balanced across datasets and classes? Do you observe negative transfer for overlapping semantics?
- Have you tried stronger regularization or architectural tweaks to enable joint fusion beyond softmax scores (e.g., weight sharing, bottleneck layers)?

Pros
- Clear, well-motivated architecture; strong empirical gains over prior deep baselines
- Thorough ablations and reproducible details
- Practical multi-task strategy for small datasets

Cons
- Heavy dependence on non-learned optical flow; limited analysis of this dependency
- Incomplete comparisons to strongest hand-crafted methods and lack of statistical rigor
- Limited dataset scope; compute/latency considerations underreported
- Camera-motion handling is simplistic relative to established practice

Recommendation
Weak accept. The approach is conservative and leverages established components effectively, yielding tangible improvements and a clear baseline for future work. However, I would like to see stronger head-to-head comparisons with IDT+FV (with motion compensation), sensitivity to the flow choice, and clearer computational analysis. If these can be addressed in the camera-ready, this would be a solid contribution. Confidence: medium.
