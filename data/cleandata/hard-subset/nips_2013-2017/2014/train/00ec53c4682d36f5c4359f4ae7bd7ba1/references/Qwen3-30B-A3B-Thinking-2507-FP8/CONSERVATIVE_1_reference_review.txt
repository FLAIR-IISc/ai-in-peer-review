

# Review of "Two-Stream Convolutional Networks for Action Recognition in Videos"

This paper proposes a two-stream convolutional network architecture for video action recognition, separating spatial (frame-based) and temporal (optical flow-based) processing streams. The authors claim their approach significantly outperforms previous deep learning methods for video recognition while being competitive with state-of-the-art shallow representations. The spatial stream is pre-trained on ImageNet, while the temporal stream processes optical flow, with fusion performed via late fusion of softmax scores.

**Strengths:**
The paper presents a well-structured, technically sound approach that builds upon established techniques (ConvNets, optical flow) rather than proposing entirely novel concepts. The experimental evaluation on standard benchmarks (UCF101 and HMDB-51) is comprehensive, with appropriate ablation studies on input configurations and fusion methods. The multi-task learning approach to combine datasets is well-motivated and implemented thoughtfully. The paper's writing is clear, and the methodology is sufficiently detailed for reproducibility.

**Weaknesses:**
Despite its solid execution, the paper suffers from significant limitations in originality and validation. The two-stream architecture is inspired by the two-streams hypothesis in neuroscience, but this connection is superficially mentioned without meaningful exploration. The core innovation—using optical flow as input to a ConvNet—is not novel; the paper references [30] which uses a similar approach, and the method is a straightforward extension of existing work. The authors claim their approach "significantly outperforms" [14] and is "competitive with the state of the art," but the actual performance gains are marginal (e.g., 81.5% vs 81.0% on UCF101 with multi-task learning).

Most critically, the paper fails to provide sufficient evidence to support its claims. The comparison to "state-of-the-art shallow representations" [20, 21, 26] is inadequate—the paper doesn't show how their method compares to the absolute best-performing methods, nor does it provide a thorough analysis of why their approach works better than previous deep learning attempts. The paper acknowledges that their method is still behind the best hand-crafted representations, yet presents this as a strength rather than a limitation.

The paper also lacks crucial justification for key design choices. For instance, the authors claim that using optical flow "makes the recognition easier, as the network does not need to estimate motion implicitly," but provide no evidence to support this assertion. They don't compare their optical flow approach to other motion representations (e.g., trajectories, which they mention as a related technique but don't thoroughly evaluate).

The paper's claim of "significant" improvement over [14] is misleading—the performance gain is modest (e.g., 81.5% vs 77.7% on UCF101), and the paper doesn't sufficiently address why previous deep learning approaches failed. The authors note that "the learnt representation, fine-tuned on the UCF-101 dataset, turned out to be 20% less accurate than hand-crafted state-of-the-art trajectory-based representation [20, 27]," but don't provide a convincing explanation for this gap.

**Originality and Significance:**
The paper's main contribution is a practical engineering approach rather than a fundamental methodological innovation. While the two-stream architecture is inspired by neuroscience, the implementation is a straightforward application of existing deep learning techniques. The paper doesn't demonstrate significant advances over prior work in terms of either methodology or performance. The authors acknowledge their method is "competitive with the state of the art shallow representations," but doesn't surpass them—this is a limitation, not a strength.

**Recommendation:**
This paper presents a well-executed, technically sound approach that builds upon established techniques, but it lacks sufficient novelty and fails to provide convincing evidence for its claims. The performance improvements over previous deep learning methods are marginal, and the paper doesn't adequately justify why their approach works better than alternatives. As a skeptical reviewer who prefers established methods with thorough validation, I find the evidence insufficient to support the paper's claims of significant advancement.

The paper would be significantly stronger if it:
1) Provided a more comprehensive comparison to the absolute state-of-the-art (not just representative papers)
2) Offered deeper analysis of why optical flow input works better than raw frames
3) More thoroughly addressed limitations and how they might be overcome
4) Provided stronger theoretical justification for key design choices

Given these limitations, I recommend rejection. While the work is technically sound and well-executed, it does not represent a meaningful advance in the field that warrants publication at NIPS. The paper makes incremental progress rather than presenting a novel, well-validated contribution that would advance the state of the art. The modest performance gains over previous deep learning approaches don't justify the claim of "significant" improvement, and the paper doesn't sufficiently differentiate itself from prior work.
