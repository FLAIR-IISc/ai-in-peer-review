Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes a two-stream convolutional architecture for action recognition in videos, explicitly decoupling spatial (appearance) and temporal (motion) processing. The spatial stream is a ConvNet trained on RGB frames (pre-trained on ImageNet and fine-tuned), while the temporal stream is a ConvNet trained on stacked dense optical flow fields between consecutive frames. The authors explore different motion input configurations (flow stacking vs. trajectory stacking, uni- vs. bi-directional flow, mean flow subtraction) and combine streams via late score fusion (averaging or SVM). They further introduce a simple multi-task learning setup to leverage multiple small video datasets (UCF-101 and HMDB-51) by sharing all network weights and using two task-specific softmax heads.

The work is well positioned in the literature. It contrasts with prior deep video models that input stacked RGB frames and learn motion implicitly (e.g., Ji et al. 3D ConvNets; Karpathy et al. with early/late/slow fusion), with results previously trailing state-of-the-art hand-crafted pipelines. It bridges to shallow methods using optical flow and trajectories (HOF/MBH, improved dense trajectories), arguing the temporal ConvNet generalizes their motion cues. The two-stream idea is related to ventral/dorsal pathways and to earlier two-stream HMAX systems, but here realized with deeper, discriminatively trained ConvNets and explicit optical flow input. The multi-task formulation follows established practice (e.g., Collobert & Weston) and is applied here to video classification where datasets are small.

Strengths
- Clear, modular architecture that isolates appearance and motion, enabling effective ImageNet pre-training for the spatial stream and targeted learning for motion.
- Strong empirical evidence that explicit optical-flow inputs substantially outperform stacked RGB frames for temporal modeling, with thorough ablations (L=1 vs L>1, trajectory vs. flow stacking, bi-directional flow, mean subtraction).
- Demonstrated complementarity of streams; simple late fusion provides sizable gains.
- Sensible and effective multi-task training that improves performance on small datasets without manual class curation.
- Competitive results on UCF-101 and HMDB-51, surpassing prior deep baselines and approaching/improving on contemporary hand-crafted methods.
- Good discussion linking learned features to classical motion descriptors (HOF/MBH, kinematic features), suggesting the ConvNet generalizes these computations.

Weaknesses and limitations
- Dependence on precomputed optical flow makes the pipeline non end-to-end, computationally heavy, and sensitive to flow quality; only simple mean subtraction is used for camera motion, which is limited compared to explicit global motion compensation.
- Temporal modeling remains shallow: inputs cover short windows (e.g., L=10) and final prediction averages per-frame crops; no explicit long-range temporal aggregation (e.g., sequence models, temporal pooling over trajectories).
- Fusion is only at the score level; mid-level or joint feature fusion is not explored due to overfitting, but could be impactful with regularization.
- Evaluation is limited to UCF-101 and HMDB-51; although ablations are careful, broader validation (e.g., Sports-1M) would strengthen claims about generality and scalability.
- Limited analysis of failure cases, class-wise effects, robustness to camera motion, and compute/throughput trade-offs. The storage/compression of flow is described, but a more complete cost/accuracy analysis would be useful.

Assessment by review criteria
- Quality: Technically sound with careful ablations and reasonable training/test protocols. The comparisons to RGB-stacked ConvNets are fair, and the multi-task setup is well executed. Some methodological choices (e.g., flow parameters, impact of JPEG compression of flow, compute budget) could be analyzed more deeply.
- Clarity: Generally clear and well organized. The derivation of input volumes for temporal nets is precise, and implementation details (sampling, data augmentation, learning schedule, multi-GPU) are helpful. A brief discussion of figure/table contents suffices, though including more explicit hyperparameters for flow and per-dataset training curves would aid reproducibility.
- Originality: The two-stream ConvNet with explicit optical flow input and late fusion is a novel and impactful combination at the time. The multi-task application is not novel per se but is an appropriate contribution to the setting of small video datasets.
- Significance: High. The approach substantially improves deep learning baselines in action recognition and provides a blueprint that has influenced subsequent video models. The modularity (pre-training spatial, training temporal) is practical and likely to be adopted.

Arguments for acceptance
- Conceptually simple, well-motivated architecture with strong empirical gains and clear ablations.
- Bridges the gap between deep and hand-crafted video representations by leveraging optical flow explicitly.
- Practical training recipe (pre-training + multi-task) for small video datasets.

Arguments against acceptance
- Not end-to-end; heavy reliance on external flow estimation and associated computational cost.
- Limited temporal modeling beyond short windows and averaging; no exploration of stronger temporal aggregations.
- Evaluation scope restricted to two datasets; no large-scale validation.

Suggestions for improvement
- Explore end-to-end training with differentiable motion estimation (e.g., learnable flow or warping modules) or fine-tuning flow parameters for the task.
- Incorporate explicit camera motion compensation (e.g., global homography/affine stabilization) and analyze its effect vis-à-vis mean-flow subtraction.
- Investigate mid-level fusion (e.g., feature concatenation with shared normalization, gated fusion) with stronger regularization (dropout, stochastic depth, batch norm) to mitigate overfitting.
- Add longer-range temporal aggregation: temporal pyramids, attention over time, sequence models (RNN/Temporal ConvNets), or trajectory-aligned pooling on learned features.
- Provide compute/throughput metrics (training time, FLOPs, inference latency with/without precomputed flow) and a sensitivity study of flow quality and compression.
- Broaden evaluation to larger, noisier datasets (e.g., Sports-1M) and report robustness to domain shift.

Recommendation: Accept. This is a well-executed, influential contribution that advances deep architectures for video by explicitly modeling motion and demonstrates clear empirical benefits with solid analysis.
