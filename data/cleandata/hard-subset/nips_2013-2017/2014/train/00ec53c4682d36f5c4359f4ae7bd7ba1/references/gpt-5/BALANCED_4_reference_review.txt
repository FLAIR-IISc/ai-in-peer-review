Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes a two-stream convolutional network architecture for video action recognition that decouples spatial appearance and temporal motion. The spatial stream operates on RGB frames and is pre-trained on ImageNet, while the temporal stream operates on stacked dense optical flow fields computed between consecutive frames. The authors explore several motion encodings (optical-flow stacking, trajectory-aligned stacking, bi-directional flow) and a simple but effective mean-flow subtraction to reduce camera motion. They fuse the two streams by late fusion (averaging or a linear SVM on softmax scores). To mitigate limited video data, they introduce a multi-task learning setup that shares the representation while using dataset-specific softmax heads (UCF-101 and HMDB-51). Extensive ablations show that stacked optical flow with mean subtraction is crucial, and that the two streams are complementary. On UCF-101 and HMDB-51, the approach substantially outperforms prior deep models trained on raw frames (e.g., Karpathy et al. 2014’s “slow fusion”) and is competitive with the then state-of-the-art handcrafted trajectory-based methods (e.g., Improved Dense Trajectories, MBH, Fisher vectors).

The work is well positioned relative to: (i) shallow spatio-temporal pipelines using HOG/HOF/MBH and dense trajectories [Wang & Schmid 2013], including global motion compensation; (ii) prior deep video models that attempt to learn motion implicitly from raw frames [Ji et al.; Karpathy et al.], which underperform; and (iii) two-stream HMAX-like pipelines [Jhuang et al.], which are shallower and hand-crafted. The authors also argue that their temporal ConvNet generalizes classic motion descriptors (HOF/MBH) via learnable filters.

Evaluation against review criteria
Quality: The methodology is technically sound. The temporal input encodings are clearly defined; training/testing protocols, data augmentation, and network configurations are specified. Ablations cover key design choices (number of flow frames L, stacking vs trajectory stacking, bi-directionality, mean subtraction, pretraining, dropout), and the multi-task setup is reasonable and helpful. However, the paper lacks a thorough analysis of computational efficiency (both training and inference), sensitivity to optical flow accuracy, and per-class failure modes. Fusion is only at the score level; joint training across streams was attempted but abandoned due to overfitting—this could be discussed more deeply. Statistical significance is not reported.

Clarity: The paper is clearly written and well organized. Architectural and training details are generally sufficient for reproduction (with Caffe), including learning rate schedules, augmentation, and test-time sampling. A few implementation details could be expanded (e.g., precise optical flow parameters, effect of flow compression on accuracy), and a more explicit description of the multi-task training schedule would help.

Originality: The two-stream ConvNet with explicit optical flow input is a novel and influential design at the time. The motion encodings and mean subtraction are simple but insightful. The multi-task formulation is standard but well applied to video recognition. The positioning of temporal ConvNets as learnable generalizations of HOF/MBH is interesting.

Significance: Results are strong on two standard benchmarks, outperforming prior deep models by a large margin and approaching or matching handcrafted SOTA, despite limited training data. The architecture is practical and likely to be adopted and extended. The decoupling of appearance from motion and leveraging image pretraining are impactful ideas.

Strengths
- Clear, principled decomposition into spatial and temporal streams with strong empirical complementarity.
- Explicit motion representation via stacked optical flow substantially improves over raw-frame inputs.
- Thorough ablations that illuminate what matters (L, mean subtraction, fusion).
- Effective use of pretraining and multi-task learning to combat data scarcity.
- Competitive results with handcrafted pipelines; large gains over previous deep methods.

Weaknesses
- Reliance on precomputed optical flow breaks end-to-end learning; no analysis of sensitivity to flow method/quality or runtime/storage trade-offs.
- Computational footprint at test time (25 frames × 10 crops, plus flow) is likely high; no throughput/latency reporting or comparison.
- Limited exploration of fusion strategies beyond score-level averaging/SVM; no mid-level or joint training due to overfitting without concrete mitigation.
- Camera motion handling is minimal (mean subtraction) compared to homography-based compensation in IDT.
- Generalization to untrimmed videos, temporal localization, and robustness to domain shift not addressed.
- No per-class analysis or error diagnosis; limited discussion of negative transfer in multi-task learning.

Questions for the authors
- How sensitive is performance to the choice and quality of optical flow (e.g., Brox vs faster methods), the compression scheme, and the number of stacked frames beyond L=10?
- What are the training and inference costs (GPU hours, FLOPs, throughput per video) for each stream and for the fused model?
- Did you try mid-level fusion (e.g., concatenating conv5 or fc6 features) with stronger regularization (dropout, weight decay, data augmentation) to mitigate overfitting?
- How does mean-flow subtraction compare to an explicit global motion model (e.g., homography estimation) in your setup?

Suggestions for improvement
- Move toward end-to-end motion learning: integrate learnable flow or correlation layers, or jointly fine-tune a flow estimator with the temporal stream; study robustness to flow errors.
- Explore camera motion compensation beyond mean subtraction (e.g., homography-based stabilization) and quantify gains.
- Investigate trajectory-aware pooling/tubelet pooling to better align spatial aggregation with motion paths, bringing a key strength of IDT into the ConvNet.
- Evaluate mid-level fusion and/or joint training with stronger regularization (batch norm, data-mixing, weight sharing across streams).
- Provide comprehensive efficiency metrics and consider lighter test-time protocols (fewer crops/frames) with minimal accuracy loss.
- Expand evaluation to untrimmed videos and temporal localization, and analyze failure cases and per-class behavior.
- Report effects of the flow compression scheme on accuracy vs storage.

Pros for acceptance
- Strong empirical performance with clear, interpretable design.
- Novel and impactful architectural decomposition; thorough ablations.
- Practical recipe leveraging image pretraining and multi-task learning.

Cons for acceptance
- Not end-to-end; heavy reliance on precomputed flow with unreported compute/storage costs.
- Limited fusion strategies and analysis of overfitting in joint models.
- Camera motion and efficiency aspects underexplored.

Recommendation: Accept. The paper presents a simple, well-justified, and effective architecture that advances deep video recognition and is likely to influence subsequent work. While the reliance on optical flow and limited fusion are notable limitations, the significance and clarity of the contributions outweigh them.
