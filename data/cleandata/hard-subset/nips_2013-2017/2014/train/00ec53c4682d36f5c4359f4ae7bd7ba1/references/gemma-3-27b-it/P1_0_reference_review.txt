## Review of "Two-Stream Convolutional Networks for Action Recognition in Videos"

**Summary:**

This paper presents a two-stream convolutional neural network architecture for video action recognition. The key idea is to decouple spatial and temporal information by employing two separate CNNs: a spatial stream processing individual frames and a temporal stream processing stacked optical flow. The outputs of these streams are then fused for final classification. The authors demonstrate that this approach significantly outperforms previous deep learning methods for action recognition and achieves competitive results compared to state-of-the-art hand-crafted features, particularly when leveraging pre-training on ImageNet for the spatial stream.

**Relation to Prior Work:**

The work builds upon the growing trend of applying deep learning to video understanding. It directly addresses the limitations identified in [14], which found that directly stacking frames into a CNN did not yield strong results. The authors position their work within the “two-stream hypothesis” of visual processing, drawing parallels to the ventral and dorsal streams in the human visual cortex. They differentiate themselves from prior deep learning approaches ([11, 16]) by utilizing deeper, discriminatively-trained CNNs rather than hand-crafted features or shallow models.  The paper also carefully contextualizes its temporal stream within the trajectory-based methods ([20, 26]), demonstrating how their CNN learns representations comparable to hand-engineered features like HOF and MBH.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novel Architecture:** The two-stream approach is a simple yet effective way to explicitly model spatial and temporal aspects of video, leading to significantly improved performance.
*   **Strong Empirical Results:** The paper presents compelling experimental results on UCF101 and HMDB51, demonstrating a clear advantage over previous deep learning approaches and competitive performance with hand-crafted features.
*   **Effective Use of Pre-training:** Leveraging ImageNet pre-training for the spatial stream demonstrates a practical way to improve performance, particularly with limited video training data.
*   **Thorough Ablation Studies:** The paper includes detailed ablation studies (Table 1a, 1b) exploring the impact of different input configurations (optical flow stacking, trajectory stacking, bi-directional flow) and training techniques (mean subtraction, multi-task learning).
*   **Clear and Well-Organized:** The paper is generally well-written and logically organized. The motivation, architecture, and experimental setup are clearly explained.
*   **Connections to Neuroscience:** The mention of the ventral/dorsal stream hypothesis provides a biologically plausible rationale for the approach, even if not deeply explored.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Optical Flow Dependency:** The temporal stream's performance relies heavily on the quality of the optical flow computation. While standard techniques are used, potential issues with noisy or inaccurate flow could limit performance. The computational cost of optical flow computation isn't extensively discussed.
*   **Limited Analysis of Learned Features:** While the paper claims the learned features generalize hand-crafted ones, it lacks visualisations of the learned filters to explicitly demonstrate this connection beyond referencing [30].
*   **Fusion Method:** While the SVM-based fusion is shown to perform better, the paper lacks a detailed discussion of *why* this is the case.  Is it simply a more powerful classifier, or does it learn a more effective weighting of the spatial and temporal streams?
*   **Limited Discussion of Computational Complexity:** The paper briefly mentions GPU training time but doesn’t provide a detailed analysis of the computational complexity of the entire system, especially considering the optical flow computation.
*   **Future Work is fairly conservative:** The proposed directions for improvement (training on larger datasets, incorporating trajectory-based pooling) are logical but don't suggest a significant departure from the current approach.



**Quality:** The paper is technically sound, with well-supported claims based on thorough experimentation. The ablation studies demonstrate a careful investigation of different design choices. It is a complete piece of work, presenting a clear architecture, training procedure, and evaluation results.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-written and easy to follow. The notation is clear, and the diagrams are helpful.

**Originality:** The two-stream architecture represents a novel and effective approach to video action recognition. While building on existing techniques (CNNs, optical flow), the combination and decoupling of spatial and temporal information are original.

**Significance:** The results are significant, demonstrating a substantial improvement over previous deep learning methods and competitive performance with state-of-the-art hand-crafted features. This work is likely to be influential in the field of video understanding and provides a strong baseline for future research.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

*   Significant performance improvement over prior deep learning methods.
*   Competitive results with state-of-the-art hand-crafted features.
*   Well-designed and thoroughly evaluated architecture.
*   Clear and well-written paper.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   Reliance on accurate optical flow computation.
*   Limited analysis of learned features.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of video action recognition. The two-stream convolutional network architecture is a clever and effective approach that yields compelling results. While there are some minor weaknesses, they do not detract significantly from the overall quality of the work. I **strongly recommend acceptance** to NIPS.

**Confidence:** 9/10
