Summary
The paper proposes a two-stream convolutional architecture for video action recognition: a spatial stream that operates on RGB frames (pre-trained on ImageNet) and a temporal stream that operates on stacked dense optical flow fields. Scores from the two streams are fused (averaging or linear SVM) to yield the final prediction. The authors justify this decoupling by the two-stream hypothesis in human vision and by empirical shortcomings of prior ConvNets that attempted to learn motion directly from stacked RGB frames. They further propose: (i) several input configurations for the temporal stream (optical flow stacking, trajectory stacking, bi-directional flow, mean-flow subtraction), (ii) multi-task learning to exploit multiple video datasets and combat overfitting, and (iii) a thorough training/testing protocol with data augmentation and multi-GPU training. On UCF101 and HMDB-51, the temporal stream substantially outperforms RGB-only ConvNets, and the fused two-stream model is competitive with hand-crafted trajectory-based methods.

Relation to prior work
The work builds on:
- Deep image ConvNets (Krizhevsky et al.; Zeiler & Fergus; Chatfield et al.) for the spatial stream via pre-training and fine-tuning.
- Prior deep video models that use stacked frames (Ji et al.; Karpathy et al.), which the authors argue fail to learn motion features reliably at small scale.
- Hand-crafted video representations (HOG/HOF/MBH, improved trajectories, Fisher vectors), which the authors explicitly connect to their temporal ConvNet by showing that first-layer filters on optical flow generalize these features.
- Earlier two-path models (HMAX-based spatial/temporal streams) but with significantly deeper, learned ConvNets.
The paper clearly differentiates itself by explicitly feeding optical flow to ConvNets and by introducing a practical multi-task training scheme across datasets.

Quality
- Technical soundness: Strong. The architectural choices are reasonable and well-motivated. The paper performs extensive ablations of temporal input design (flow/trajectory stacking, L, bi-directional flow, mean subtraction), fusion strategies, and training regimes (pre-training vs from-scratch; dropout), with careful controls. Claims about the value of motion cues and the benefits of pre-training and multi-task learning are backed by clear empirical evidence.
- Experimental rigor: Good. Evaluations follow standard UCF101/HMDB-51 protocols across splits with averaged performance. Temporal testing samples multiple frames and crops; training uses strong augmentation. The ablations convincingly show why optical flow input helps and how much each component contributes.
- Limitations: The temporal stream relies on precomputed optical flow (Brox), which is not learned end-to-end and adds substantial preprocessing cost and storage (they note 27GB after compression for UCF101). There is no study of sensitivity to the optical flow algorithm or its parameters. The temporal receptive field is limited (L=10), and longer-range temporal modeling is not explored. Fusion at score level is simple; attempts at deeper fusion reportedly overfit, but more controlled regularization or parameter sharing experiments are not provided. Runtime and inference latency for the full two-stream pipeline are not reported quantitatively beyond flow timing.

Clarity
- The paper is clearly written and well-organized. The temporal input formulations are precisely defined, including equations and intuitive illustrations. Implementation details (SGD schedule, data augmentation, GPU setup) are unusually thorough and should enable reproduction. The relation to prior work is fair and informative. Minor editorial issues exist (some reference formatting), but nothing that impedes comprehension.

Originality
- The key novelty is the explicit use of stacked dense optical flow as ConvNet input paired with an RGB stream, trained separately and fused late. While the two-stream idea existed in hand-crafted HMAX models, applying it with deep ConvNets and showing that optical flow input materially outperforms stacked RGB frames at this scale is original and impactful. The multi-task learning setup to combine datasets is a practical, useful contribution for small video datasets. The paper also provides a useful conceptual bridge, showing how hand-crafted motion descriptors can be seen as special cases of ConvNet filters on flow.

Significance
- High. The paper demonstrates a simple, effective recipe that closes much of the gap to hand-crafted improved trajectories and clearly beats prior deep video baselines. Given the persistent difficulty of learning motion from raw RGB at the time, this is a compelling and likely widely adopted approach. The ablation insights (longer flow stacks, mean-flow subtraction, limited gains from trajectory or bi-directional stacking, benefits of multi-tasking) are actionable for practitioners. The work is likely to influence subsequent architectures (e.g., end-to-end flow learning, better fusion, longer temporal modeling).

Strengths
- Clear motivation and architecture; excellent empirical validation and ablations.
- Strong performance on standard benchmarks; complementary streams with substantial fusion gains.
- Thorough implementation and training details aiding reproducibility.
- Practical multi-task learning strategy to leverage multiple small datasets.
- Conceptual unification with classical motion features.

Weaknesses
- Dependence on precomputed optical flow; lack of end-to-end training and sensitivity analysis to the flow method.
- Limited modeling of long-range temporal structure beyond short stacks.
- Fusion explored only at score level; deeper fusion dismissed due to overfitting without extensive alternatives.
- No detailed runtime/throughput analysis for deployment; storage/compute overhead is nontrivial.

Questions for authors
- How sensitive is performance to the choice of optical flow algorithm and its accuracy/speed trade-offs?
- What is the end-to-end inference time per video (including flow) for the reported testing protocol?
- Can longer temporal windows or temporal pooling beyond L=10 further improve accuracy?
- Could lightweight joint training of the last layers across streams with strong regularization outperform SVM fusion?

Suggestions
- Report detailed throughput and memory/storage costs for practical deployment.
- Explore regularized or parameter-sharing fusion layers, or feature-level fusion with strong dropout/BN.
- Investigate longer-term temporal aggregation (e.g., temporal pyramids, RNN/temporal convolutions) on top of flow stacks.
- Analyze per-class gains and failure modes, especially under strong camera motion; consider explicit camera-motion compensation beyond mean subtraction.
- Evaluate robustness to noisier, faster flow methods.

Arguments for acceptance
- Well-motivated, simple architecture with strong empirical gains and clear insights.
- High practical impact; likely to become a standard baseline.
- Careful ablations and reproducibility.

Arguments against acceptance
- Not end-to-end; heavy dependence on external flow with computational overhead.
- Limited exploration of longer temporal modeling and fusion strategies.

Overall assessment
This is a high-quality, clearly written, and impactful paper that advances the state of the art with solid evidence. Despite reliance on precomputed flow, the contribution is substantive and timely, with strong significance for the community. I recommend acceptance.
