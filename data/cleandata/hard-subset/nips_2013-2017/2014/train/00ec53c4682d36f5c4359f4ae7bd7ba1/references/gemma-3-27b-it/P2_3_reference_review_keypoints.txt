- Paper proposes a two-stream CNN architecture separating spatial (frames) and temporal (optical flow) processing for action recognition in videos.  
- Streams are fused (averaging or linear SVM) and the spatial stream benefits from ImageNet pre-training.  
- Strong empirical results: outperforms prior deep-learning methods and is competitive with handcrafted features on UCF101 and HMDB51.  
- Novelty: decoupling spatial/temporal processing and using stacked optical flow as CNN input is a clear contribution.  
- Clear writing: architecture, experiments, and design rationales (e.g., flow stacking, mean subtraction) are well explained.  
- Multi-task learning: combining datasets via multi-task setup is a practical addition for limited video data.  
- Thorough evaluation: various input representations, fusion methods, and ablation studies are provided.  
- Limited discussion of the biological two-stream inspiration; a deeper neuroscience connection could strengthen motivation.  
- Heavy reliance on accurate optical flow; robustness to flow errors is not thoroughly explored.  
- Increased complexity: two-stream approach doubles parameters versus single-stream; computational cost and compression are not discussed.  
- Minor clarity/flow issues between sections, e.g., motivation for input configurations in Section 3.1.  
- Quality: method is technically sound with convincing experiments and acknowledged limitations.  
- Clarity: mostly clear and organized, but could improve transitions and explanations in places.  
- Originality: two-stream design and CNNs on optical flow stacks are novel and well-cited.  
- Significance: represents a notable advance and provides a strong baseline for future work.  
- Pros for acceptance: significant performance gains, novel architecture, thorough experiments, generally well-written.  
- Cons for acceptance: dependence on accurate optical flow, higher model complexity, limited biological discussion.  
- Overall recommendation: Accept with minor revisions to address clarity, robustness to flow errors, and computational cost.
