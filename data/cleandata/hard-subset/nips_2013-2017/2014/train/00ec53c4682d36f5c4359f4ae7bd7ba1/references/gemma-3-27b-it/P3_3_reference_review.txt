## Review of "Two-Stream Convolutional Networks for Action Recognition in Videos"

**Overall Assessment:** This paper presents a significant contribution to the field of video action recognition by proposing a two-stream convolutional network architecture that effectively decouples spatial and temporal information. The approach demonstrably outperforms previous deep learning-based methods and achieves competitive results compared to state-of-the-art hand-crafted features. The paper is well-written and clearly articulates the motivation, methodology, and results. While not groundbreaking in the sense of introducing entirely new concepts, the effective application and combination of existing techniques (deep CNNs, optical flow) represents a strong advance. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions.

**1. Summary & Related Work:**

The paper tackles the challenging problem of video action recognition. It argues that simply stacking frames into a CNN (as in [14]) doesn't effectively capture motion information. Instead, it proposes a two-stream architecture: a spatial stream processing individual frames and a temporal stream processing dense optical flow. These streams are implemented as CNNs and their outputs are fused. The paper correctly positions this work within the context of both traditional hand-crafted feature approaches (trajectory-based methods, HOG/HOF) and more recent deep learning attempts. The connection to the two-stream hypothesis of visual processing is also noted, though not deeply explored. The related work section provides a comprehensive overview of the field, accurately citing relevant publications.

**2. Strengths:**

* **Effective Architecture:** The two-stream approach is a clever and effective way to leverage both spatial and temporal cues in video. Decoupling these allows for dedicated optimization of each stream.
* **Strong Performance:** The results presented demonstrate a significant improvement over [14] and competitive performance against state-of-the-art hand-crafted features on UCF101 and HMDB51. This is a substantial achievement.
* **Leveraging Pre-training:** Utilizing ImageNet pre-training for the spatial stream is a smart strategy, particularly given the limited size of video datasets.
* **Thorough Evaluation:** The paper performs a comprehensive evaluation of different input configurations for the temporal stream (stacking, trajectories, bi-directional flow) and fusion methods (averaging, SVM).
* **Multi-task Learning:** The introduction of multi-task learning is a valuable addition, demonstrating a way to mitigate overfitting and improve generalization.
* **Clear Writing & Organization:** The paper is very well-written, logically organized, and easy to understand. The figures are helpful in illustrating the architecture and input pipelines.

**3. Weaknesses:**

* **Optical Flow Dependency:** The reliance on dense optical flow is a potential bottleneck. While the paper acknowledges its computational cost, it doesn’t explore alternative motion representations that might be more efficient (e.g., sparse optical flow, motion boundaries).  The quality of optical flow directly impacts performance.
* **Limited Exploration of Fusion:** While SVM fusion outperforms averaging, the exploration of more sophisticated fusion techniques (e.g., attention mechanisms, learned fusion weights) is limited.
* **Lack of Ablation Study on Multi-task Learning:** The benefits of multi-task learning are demonstrated, but a more detailed ablation study examining the contribution of each dataset (UCF101 vs. HMDB51) would be beneficial.
* **Camera Motion Compensation:** While mean flow subtraction is used, more robust camera motion compensation techniques (as referenced in the related work) might further improve performance, particularly for videos with significant camera movement.
* **Computational Cost:**  The paper doesn't provide detailed information about the computational cost of training and inference, making it difficult to assess the practicality of this approach for large-scale applications.



**4. Originality & Significance:**

While the individual components (CNNs, optical flow) are not novel, the combination and application to action recognition in this specific two-stream architecture is original and significant. The demonstrated performance improvement over existing methods suggests that this is a promising direction for future research. The paper addresses a core problem in video understanding and provides a strong baseline for future work.  The multi-task learning approach also adds to the originality.

**5. Detailed Questions/Suggestions for Revision:**

* **Optical Flow Alternatives:** Discuss the trade-offs of using dense optical flow and briefly mention potential alternatives for improved efficiency.
* **Fusion Techniques:**  Mention potential avenues for more sophisticated fusion techniques beyond averaging and SVMs.
* **Multi-task Ablation:** Provide a more detailed ablation study demonstrating the relative contribution of each dataset in the multi-task learning setup.
* **Computational Cost:** Include a section discussing the training and inference time for different configurations.
* **Error Analysis:** Provide some qualitative examples of failure cases and discuss potential reasons for these errors.




**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* Significant performance improvement over previous deep learning approaches.
* Competitive results with state-of-the-art hand-crafted features.
* Well-designed architecture and thorough evaluation.
* Clear and well-written paper.
* Addresses a key problem in video understanding.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* Reliance on computationally expensive dense optical flow.
* Limited exploration of more advanced fusion techniques.
* Lack of detailed ablation study on multi-task learning.




**Overall, I strongly recommend accepting this paper. The work is technically sound, well-written, and makes a valuable contribution to the field of video action recognition. Addressing the minor suggestions for revision would further strengthen the paper.**
