- Proposes a two-stream architecture separating appearance (ImageNet-pretrained spatial ConvNet) and motion (temporal ConvNet on stacked optical flow) with late fusion.  
- Evaluates temporal input encodings (stack length, trajectory vs fixed stacking, uni/bi-directional flow, mean subtraction) and uses multi-task learning across UCF-101 and HMDB-51.  
- Demonstrates stacked optical flow is far superior to raw frame stacks for learning motion features.  
- Ablation studies are comprehensive and empirically support design choices.  
- Multi-task learning effectively increases training signal and consistently improves temporal-stream performance.  
- Strong engineering practices and reproducibility: multi-GPU training, careful augmentation, and explicit protocols/schedules.  
- Achieves competitive results with trajectory-based methods and substantially outperforms previous deep video models.  
- Heavy reliance on precomputed optical flow prevents end-to-end learning and incurs significant compute/storage costs that are not quantified.  
- Fusion strategy is limited to late score fusion; learned joint fusion over fully connected layers overfits and needs further regularization analysis.  
- Camera motion is handled only via mean flow subtraction; lacks comparison to explicit camera-compensation methods used in trajectory pipelines.  
- Spatial-stream gains may stem from ImageNet pretraining and crop/augmentation differences; lacks ablation isolating these factors for fair comparison.  
- Limited exploration of spatial frame sampling strategies and of long-term temporal modeling beyond the short (≈10-frame) flow window.  
- Evaluation is on trimmed clips only; scalability to untrimmed videos and temporal localization is unclear.  
- Technical soundness is high; multi-task objective and other methods are well-justified and correctly implemented.  
- Empirical support is strong, with extensive experiments and fair baseline comparisons on two benchmarks.  
- Paper is clearly written and well organized, with sufficient implementation details for reproduction, though some clarifications remain.  
- Originality: novel application of temporal ConvNet on stacked flow and systematic input-encoding study, despite conceptual two-stream origins.  
- Significance: high — narrows the gap between ConvNet-based and trajectory-based approaches and is likely to influence follow-up work.  
- Suggested improvements: isolate spatial-stream ablations, report flow compute/storage/time, test alternative frame sampling, compare flow algorithms, explore stronger regularization/mid-level fusion, add per-class/failure analyses, and discuss untrimmed/video-localization applicability.  
- Pros: simple, well-motivated design, thorough ablations, effective multi-task learning, clear writing, reproducible details.  
- Cons: dependence on precomputed flow, limited fusion exploration, modest camera-motion analysis, and evaluation confined to trimmed datasets.  
- Overall assessment: technically sound, significant empirical contribution with clear presentation — recommend acceptance.
