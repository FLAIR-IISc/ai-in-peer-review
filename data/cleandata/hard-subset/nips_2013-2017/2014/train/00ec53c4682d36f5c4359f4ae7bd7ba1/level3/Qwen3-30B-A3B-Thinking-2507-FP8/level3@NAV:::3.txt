

This paper introduces a two-stream ConvNet architecture for video action recognition, combining spatial (frame-based) and temporal (optical flow-based) streams. The authors demonstrate that their approach significantly outperforms prior deep learning methods for video classification, achieving competitive results with state-of-the-art shallow representations while using relatively small datasets. The methodology is well-documented, with clear descriptions of the optical flow ConvNet input configurations, multi-task learning framework, and implementation details. 

The paper's strengths are substantial: the two-stream architecture is technically sound and well-supported by comprehensive experiments on UCF-101 and HMDB-51 benchmarks, showing impressive improvements over previous deep learning approaches. The temporal stream's effectiveness (particularly with optical flow input) is convincingly demonstrated, and the multi-task learning component provides a principled way to leverage limited video datasets. The writing is clear, and the paper presents a fair case for its contributions.

However, the paper suffers from a lack of cohesion across multiple contributions rather than focusing on a single, unified theme. The introduction and abstract would benefit from better emphasizing the significance of the two-stream approach. Crucially, the authors overlook important prior work, notably missing citations to the CVPR 2011 paper "Learning Hierarchical Spatio-temporal Features..." and the BMVC 2010 "Evaluation of local spatio-temporal features..." papers, which directly relate to their approach.

Quality: Strong technical execution with thorough experiments.  
Clarity: Excellent organization and writing, though minor improvements needed in focus.  
Originality: Novel two-stream architecture that effectively generalizes hand-crafted features.  
Significance: Demonstrates meaningful progress in video action recognition, bridging deep learning and motion representation.

Arguments for acceptance: Strong methodology, clear results, and significant improvements over prior work.  
Arguments against acceptance: Lack of cohesion and missing key citations require addressing.

The paper makes a valuable contribution to video recognition, and with minor revisions to refocus the narrative and include critical citations, it would be an excellent fit for NIPS. I recommend acceptance conditional on these improvements.
