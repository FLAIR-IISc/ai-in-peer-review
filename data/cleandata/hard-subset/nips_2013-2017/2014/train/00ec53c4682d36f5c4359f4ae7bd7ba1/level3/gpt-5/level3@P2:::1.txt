Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes a two-stream convolutional architecture for video action recognition that separates appearance and motion processing. The spatial stream is a ConvNet applied to individual RGB frames (pretrained on ImageNet and fine-tuned for actions), while the temporal stream is a ConvNet applied to stacked dense optical flow fields computed over short windows (e.g., L=10). The two streams are trained separately and fused at test time by averaging or a linear SVM over softmax scores. The authors also introduce a simple multi-task learning setup to train a shared network on both UCF-101 and HMDB-51 simultaneously to mitigate limited data.

Relative to prior work, this addresses the consistent gap observed between deep nets trained on raw frames and hand-crafted trajectory-based pipelines (e.g., HOF/MBH with Fisher vectors and improved trajectories). Instead of expecting early conv layers to learn motion implicitly from stacks of RGB frames as in [14], the paper feeds motion explicitly via dense optical flow, and explores input configurations (optical-flow stacking vs. trajectory stacking, forward vs. bi-directional flow, mean-flow subtraction). The approach is conceptually related to two-stream models in neuroscience and to earlier shallow two-stream recognition systems (e.g., HMAX), and it can be viewed as a learnable generalization of classical flow-based descriptors (HOF, MBH).

Strengths
- Clear, compelling architectural decomposition: decoupling appearance and motion simplifies learning and enables effective ImageNet pretraining for appearance.
- Strong empirical support: temporal ConvNet on stacked flow clearly outperforms RGB-only deep baselines and, when fused with spatial, achieves performance competitive with state-of-the-art hand-crafted pipelines on UCF-101 and HMDB-51.
- Insightful analysis of motion input design: ablations over L, trajectory vs. location stacking, bi-directional flow, and mean subtraction provide useful guidance to the community.
- Practical multi-task training across datasets that yields tangible gains without manual class curation.
- Good engineering details (training schedules, multi-GPU, test-time sampling) and reasonable reproducibility via Caffe.

Weaknesses
- Temporal modeling is limited to short fixed windows (L=10); there is no explicit mechanism for long-range temporal structure or sequence modeling at the video level.
- Limited error analysis: no per-class or per-category breakdown, little discussion of failure modes (e.g., strong camera motion, background bias, small/fast actions).
- Dependence on precomputed optical flow introduces a computational bottleneck and departs from “end-to-end” training; the impact of different flow algorithms/qualities is not studied.
- Fusion is shallow (score-level) due to overfitting concerns; joint feature-level fusion is not explored beyond stating difficulties.
- Although comparisons to [14] are thorough, a more detailed head-to-head against state-of-the-art trajectory Fisher-vector systems on identical splits and protocols (including computational cost comparisons) would strengthen claims.

Quality
The paper is technically sound and thorough in key ablations (flow stacking choices, L, mean subtraction, bidirectional). The training methodology (dropout, pretraining, schedules) is standard and well justified. The evaluation follows standard protocols on UCF-101 and HMDB-51 with three-split averages, and the results substantiate the main claims. The lack of deeper error analysis and of experiments on truly long-range temporal reasoning are the primary limitations. The multi-task learning setup is straightforward and effective.

Clarity
The paper is very clearly written and well organized. The relation of the temporal stream to classical descriptors is explained convincingly, and implementation details are sufficient for reproduction. Figures (e.g., flow stacking vs. trajectory stacking) are helpful. Minor clarity suggestions: include per-class confusion matrices and qualitative success/failure examples; report run-time/compute for flow and inference to contextualize practical trade-offs.

Originality
The main novelty lies in the explicit two-stream deep architecture with a motion stream fed by stacked dense optical flow and trained discriminatively, plus a simple but effective multi-task formulation. While the components (ConvNets, optical flow, late fusion) are known, their combination and the demonstration that flow-as-input is key to strong deep performance on actions represent a meaningful and original contribution beyond prior deep video work that used raw RGB stacks.

Significance
The approach addresses a central challenge in video understanding and provides a viable deep alternative to hand-crafted trajectory pipelines. The demonstrated gains over RGB-only deep baselines and competitiveness with state-of-the-art shallow methods suggest high potential impact. The paper also provides practical guidance (e.g., mean-flow subtraction, depth of stacking) likely to influence subsequent work.

Detailed suggestions and questions
- Provide per-class accuracies and confusion matrices to highlight where motion vs. appearance helps or fails.
- Analyze the effect of window length L beyond 10, or employ simple temporal pooling/aggregation across multiple windows to approximate long-range reasoning.
- Include a brief study of different optical flow methods/parameters and their impact on accuracy and speed; discuss end-to-end alternatives (e.g., learning flow-like features).
- Explore modest feature-level fusion (e.g., concatenating penultimate-layer features with regularization) or sharing earlier layers across streams to reduce overfitting.
- Discuss robustness to camera motion more deeply; compare mean subtraction to explicit global motion compensation.

Arguments for acceptance
- Clear, well-motivated architecture that convincingly demonstrates the value of optical flow as ConvNet input.
- Strong empirical results with careful ablations; competitive with the state of the art and substantially better than prior deep baselines.
- Practical training strategy (pretraining, multi-task) and reproducible details.
- Likely to influence subsequent research on video action recognition.

Arguments against acceptance
- Limited modeling of long-range temporal structure; performance may plateau without sequence-level mechanisms.
- Reliance on precomputed optical flow (computational overhead, not end-to-end); limited analysis of this dependency.
- Insufficient error-mode analysis and qualitative diagnostics.
- Fusion remains at score level; feature-level integration is not demonstrated.

Overall assessment
This is a solid, well-executed paper that makes a clear and impactful contribution: showing that a two-stream ConvNet with explicit optical-flow inputs bridges much of the gap between deep and hand-crafted methods for action recognition. Despite limitations regarding long-range temporal modeling and analysis depth, the work satisfies the NIPS criteria on quality, clarity, originality, and significance. I recommend acceptance.
