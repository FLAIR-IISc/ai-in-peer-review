This paper proposes a two-stream ConvNet architecture for video action recognition that explicitly separates appearance and motion: a spatial stream processes RGB frames (leveraging ImageNet pretraining), while a temporal stream operates on stacked dense optical flow. The authors study several flow-based input configurations (flow stacking vs. trajectory stacking; forward vs. bidirectional flow; mean flow subtraction), and show that stacking multiple flow fields with mean subtraction is especially effective. Scores from the two streams are fused by late fusion (averaging or an SVM trained on softmax scores). To mitigate limited video labels, the paper further introduces a simple multi-task learning setup with dataset-specific softmax heads to jointly train on UCF-101 and HMDB-51. Extensive ablations and implementation details are provided (data augmentation, training schedules, multi-GPU, precomputation and compression of flow). On UCF-101 and HMDB-51, the temporal stream alone substantially outperforms prior deep models trained on raw frames, and the fused two-stream model is competitive with state-of-the-art trajectory-based shallow pipelines.

Relation to prior work: The approach is positioned between shallow hand-crafted pipelines (STIP/HOG/HOF/MBH, improved dense trajectories, Fisher vectors) and earlier deep video architectures. In contrast to 3D CNNs and multi-frame RGB models (e.g., Ji et al., 2013; Karpathy et al., 2014), which must learn motion implicitly, this work injects motion explicitly via optical flow, akin to the dorsal/ventral two-stream hypothesis and earlier two-stream HMAX (Jhuang et al.). The authors argue (and ablations support) that this explicit motion representation better captures discriminative temporal cues on small-to-medium datasets. The multi-task learning setup follows standard practice (e.g., Collobert & Weston) but is fruitfully applied to video datasets.

Strengths
- Clear conceptual contribution: decoupling appearance and motion into two discriminatively trained streams with simple, effective late fusion.
- Strong empirical evidence with careful ablations: number of flow frames, trajectory vs. spatial stacking, mean subtraction, bidirectional flow, and fusion strategies.
- Practical training strategy: ImageNet pretraining for spatial net; multi-task learning to expand temporal net data; thorough implementation details enabling reproducibility.
- Significant performance gains over prior deep models on UCF-101/HMDB-51; competitive with improved dense trajectories while using a learned representation.
- Method complements existing deep frameworks and is likely to be widely useful.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Fragmented narrative: the paper reads as multiple contributions (two-stream design, flow engineering, multi-task learning, fusion) rather than a single unified story. Suggest refocusing abstract/introduction on the central insight: explicit motion via optical flow in a two-stream ConvNet yields most of the gains; multi-task and fusion are supporting elements.
- Not end-to-end from pixels: reliance on precomputed optical flow is a nontrivial preprocessing step and a computational/storage bottleneck. The paper would benefit from a more quantitative discussion of runtime and storage trade-offs vs. RGB-only models and trajectory pipelines.
- Limited handling of camera motion: mean flow subtraction is simple; comparisons to trajectory pipelines with explicit camera motion compensation would clarify gaps.
- Limited analysis of failure cases and invariances (e.g., viewpoint, background bias, motion magnitude).
- Scope of related work: while many references are included, please ensure proper positioning of (i) Learning hierarchical spatio-temporal features with ISA (Le et al., CVPR 2011) and (ii) Evaluation of local spatio-temporal features (Wang et al., BMVC 2010) when discussing baselines and design choices. Also clarify the distinctions to 3D CNNs (Ji et al., 2013) and to Taylor et al., 2010.

Quality
- Technically sound with appropriate training protocols, ablations, and comparisons. Claims are supported by results on standard splits and by diagnostic experiments. The multi-task setup is straightforward and well-motivated.
- A limitation is the absence of large-scale evaluation (e.g., Sports-1M) or robustness/stats tests; however, for NIPS it is acceptable given the depth of analysis on UCF-101/HMDB-51.

Clarity
- Generally very clear, well organized, and sufficiently detailed to reproduce results. Figures describing input configurations are helpful. Manuscript could be tightened to emphasize the core contribution and reduce dispersion across subcomponents.

Originality
- The architectural decomposition and explicit optical-flow input in a discriminatively trained ConvNet are novel relative to prior deep video works relying on raw frames. The multi-task training and score-level fusion are not new per se, but their empirical integration here is effective. The connection to hand-crafted motion features is well articulated.

Significance
- High. The approach substantially improves over prior deep baselines and offers a simple, general recipe that many will adopt. It bridges hand-crafted motion cues and deep learning, likely influencing subsequent video recognition architectures.

Pros for acceptance
- Strong performance and thorough ablations; clear, practical contribution.
- Timely and impactful for the community transitioning from hand-crafted to learned video features.
- Clear writing and reproducible methodology.

Cons for acceptance
- Heavy dependence on precomputed optical flow; not fully end-to-end.
- Somewhat fragmented narrative; limited large-scale validation and failure analysis.
- Camera motion handling is rudimentary compared to trajectory pipelines.

Actionable suggestions
- Reframe abstract/introduction to foreground the two-stream optical-flow insight.
- Add or more explicitly integrate citations: Le et al., CVPR 2011; Wang et al., BMVC 2010.
- Report computational costs (training/inference time per video; storage for flow) and discuss trade-offs versus raw-RGB ConvNets and IDT pipelines.
- Provide qualitative analyses (e.g., typical failures; class-wise gains; sensitivity to L and flow quality).
- Discuss avenues for tighter integration of motion estimation and recognition (e.g., learned flow or end-to-end differentiable motion).

Recommendation: Accept (NIPS 2014), conditional on addressing the presentation and related-work clarifications above.
