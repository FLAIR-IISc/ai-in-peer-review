- Introduces a two-stream ConvNet architecture separating spatial (single frames) and temporal (multi-frame dense optical flow) processing for video action recognition.
- Combines separate stream predictions to leverage both appearance and explicit motion cues.
- Demonstrates strong, competitive results on UCF-101 and HMDB-51, rivaling hand-crafted feature methods.
- Uses well-established CNN architectures, focusing novelty on stream combination and input modality rather than new network design.
- Shows ConvNets trained on stacked optical flow are effective at modeling motion, unlike prior attempts to learn motion implicitly from frames.
- Explores multi-task learning across datasets to mitigate data scarcity and improve generalization.
- Ablation studies (flow stacking, mean subtraction, etc.) effectively isolate component contributions.
- Experiments follow standard protocols and are well-designed and reproducible.
- Paper is exceptionally clear, well-organized, and easy to follow.
- Connects conceptually to the two-stream hypothesis from visual neuroscience, providing helpful context.
- Main limitation: reliance on short, fixed-length temporal windows (L=10) limits modeling of long-range dependencies.
- Struggles to capture long-term temporal structure, reducing effectiveness on actions unfolding over extended periods.
- Experimental evaluation lacks deeper error analysis and per-class performance breakdowns.
- Missing detailed investigation of which action types or videos are most challenging and common failure modes.
- Novelty lies in applying ConvNets to optical flow and demonstrating a decoupled spatial-temporal approach.
- Significance: provides a strong foundation for future work and could shift video analysis toward deep motion-focused methods.
- Suggests clear future directions: integrating mechanisms for long-term modeling (RNNs, attention) to overcome temporal limits.
- Arguments for acceptance: strong results, novel effective architecture, demonstrates ConvNets on optical flow, and clear presentation.
- Arguments against acceptance: limited long-range modeling, insufficient error analysis, and short temporal window.
