This paper proposes a novel approach to action recognition in videos using a two-stream convolutional network architecture. The authors present a clear and well-written description of their methodology, implementation, and procedures, making it easy to follow and understand their approach. The paper is strong in its results, showing impressive improvements over previous work [14] and competitiveness with state-of-the-art methods.

One of the key contributions of this paper is the two-stream formulation, which incorporates optical flow to capture motion information. This approach is particularly interesting, as it provides a new way of thinking about video classification. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of this approach through extensive experiments on two challenging datasets, UCF-101 and HMDB-51.

Another significant contribution is the use of multi-task learning to improve the performance of the temporal ConvNet. By training the network on multiple datasets, the authors are able to increase the effective training set size and reduce overfitting. This approach is novel and complements existing deep learning frameworks.

The paper is well-organized, and the authors provide a thorough review of related work in the field. However, the paper feels slightly split across multiple contributions, rather than having a single cohesive theme. The authors could benefit from refocusing the abstract and introduction to highlight the significance of the two-stream and optical flow contributions.

In terms of quality, the paper is technically sound, and the authors provide a thorough evaluation of their approach. The claims are well-supported by theoretical analysis and experimental results. The authors are careful and honest about evaluating both the strengths and weaknesses of their work.

The clarity of the paper is excellent, with clear and concise writing. The organization is logical, and the figures and tables are well-placed and easy to understand. The authors provide enough information for an expert reader to reproduce their results.

In terms of originality, the paper presents a novel approach to video classification, and the two-stream formulation is a significant contribution to the field. The use of multi-task learning is also a new and interesting approach. The authors provide adequate references to related work and clearly explain how their approach differs from previous contributions.

The significance of the paper is high, as it presents a new and effective approach to action recognition in videos. The results are impressive, and the approach has the potential to be widely adopted in the field. The paper addresses a difficult problem in a better way than previous research and advances the state of the art in a demonstrable way.

In conclusion, this paper is a strong contribution to the field of action recognition in videos. The authors present a novel approach, provide thorough evaluation and analysis, and demonstrate impressive results. With some minor revisions to refocus the abstract and introduction, this paper is ready for publication at NIPS 2014.

Arguments for acceptance:

* Novel approach to video classification using a two-stream convolutional network architecture
* Impressive results on two challenging datasets, UCF-101 and HMDB-51
* Effective use of multi-task learning to improve the performance of the temporal ConvNet
* Clear and well-written description of methodology, implementation, and procedures
* Strong evaluation and analysis of the approach

Arguments against acceptance:

* Paper feels slightly split across multiple contributions, rather than having a single cohesive theme
* Missing citations to related work (e.g., CVPR 2011 "Learning Hierarchical Spatio-temporal Features for Action Recognition with Independent Subspace Analysis" and BMVC 2010 "Evaluation of local spatio-temporal features for action recognition")

Recommendation: Accept at NIPS 2014 conditional on addressing suggested improvements.
