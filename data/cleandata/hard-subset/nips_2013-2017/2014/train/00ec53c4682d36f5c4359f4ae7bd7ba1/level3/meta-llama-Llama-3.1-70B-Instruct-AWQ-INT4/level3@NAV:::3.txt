This paper, "Two-Stream Convolutional Networks for Action Recognition in Videos," proposes a novel approach to video classification using a two-stream formulation and multi-task learning. The authors introduce a two-stream ConvNet architecture that incorporates spatial and temporal networks, which are then combined by late fusion. The spatial stream performs action recognition from still video frames, while the temporal stream is trained to recognize action from motion in the form of dense optical flow.

The paper is well-written and clearly presents a fair case for the methods with strong results. The authors provide a thorough description of the methodology, implementation, and procedures, making it easy to follow and reproduce their work. The paper is well-organized, and the authors adequately inform the reader about the context and related work in the field.

The proposed methods are novel and complement existing deep learning frameworks. The two-stream framework is particularly interesting, as incorporating optical flow yields significant improvements in action recognition. The authors demonstrate that their approach outperforms previous attempts to use deep nets for video classification and is competitive with state-of-the-art shallow representations.

However, the paper feels split across multiple contributions rather than having a single cohesive theme. The authors propose two main contributions: the two-stream architecture and multi-task learning. While both contributions are interesting, they seem to be presented as separate entities rather than being tightly integrated.

The results show impressive improvements over previous work and are competitive with state-of-the-art. The authors provide a thorough evaluation of their approach on two challenging datasets, UCF-101 and HMDB-51. The results demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach in action recognition.

In terms of quality, the paper is technically sound, and the claims are well-supported by theoretical analysis or experimental results. The authors are careful and honest about evaluating both the strengths and weaknesses of their work.

One potential improvement is to refocus the abstract and introduction on the two-stream/optical-flow significance, as this is the core contribution of the paper. Additionally, the authors are missing citations to CVPR 2011 "Learning Hierarchical Spatio-temporal Features for Action Recognition with Independent Subspace Analysis" and BMVC 2010 "Evaluation of local spatio-temporal features for action recognition."

Overall, I recommend accepting this paper at NIPS 2014, conditional on addressing the suggested improvements. The paper makes a significant contribution to the field of video classification and has the potential to impact the development of deep learning frameworks for video analysis.

Arguments for acceptance:

* Novel approach to video classification using a two-stream formulation and multi-task learning
* Strong results that outperform previous attempts to use deep nets for video classification
* Competitive with state-of-the-art shallow representations
* Well-written and clearly presented paper

Arguments against acceptance:

* Paper feels split across multiple contributions rather than having a single cohesive theme
* Missing citations to relevant work
* Could benefit from a more focused abstract and introduction.
