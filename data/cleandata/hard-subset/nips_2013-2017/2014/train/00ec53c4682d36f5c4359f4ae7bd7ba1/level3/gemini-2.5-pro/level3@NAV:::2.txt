This paper presents a two-stream convolutional network architecture for action recognition in videos, addressing the challenge of effectively capturing both static appearance and temporal motion information. The proposed model consists of a spatial stream ConvNet, which processes individual video frames, and a temporal stream ConvNet, which operates on short sequences of dense optical flow. This approach builds upon the success of ConvNets in image classification but diverges from prior video-based deep learning work, such as Karpathy et al. [14], by explicitly modeling motion rather than attempting to learn it from raw pixel data. The final classification is achieved by late fusion of the two streams’ predictions.

The paper’s primary strength is its strong empirical performance. The authors conduct a thorough set of experiments demonstrating that their two-stream architecture significantly outperforms other ConvNet-based methods and matches the state-of-the-art on the challenging UCF-101 and HMDB-51 datasets. The contribution is clear and impactful: applying a standard image ConvNet pipeline to dense optical flow and fusing it with a single-frame network yields impressive results. The ablation studies effectively evaluate various ways to train ConvNets on optical flow, convincingly showing the benefits of this input modality. The work is technically sound and the results are well-supported.

The paper has only minor weaknesses. One point that could be clarified is why the spatial network alone achieves better performance than the similar architectures reported in [13]. While the authors suggest this is due to their data augmentation strategy, a more direct comparison or analysis would strengthen this claim. Additionally, while the temporal spacing of frames is fixed, the evaluation could be enhanced by exploring different frame-sampling schemes for the spatial stream, which might offer further performance gains.

Overall, this is a high-quality, well-executed paper that makes a significant contribution to the video classification community. The proposed architecture is elegant, effective, and demonstrates a clear path forward for applying deep learning to action recognition. Its findings are important and will likely influence subsequent research in this area. I strongly recommend acceptance.
