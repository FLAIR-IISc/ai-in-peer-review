This paper proposes a novel architecture for video classification, introducing a two-stream formulation and multi-task learning framework. The authors investigate architectures of discriminatively trained deep Convolutional Networks (ConvNets) for action recognition in video, aiming to capture complementary information on appearance from still frames and motion between frames.

The paper is well-written and clear, presenting a fair case for the methods with strong results. The authors provide a thorough description of their methodology, implementation, and procedures, allowing for easy reproduction of their results. The proposed two-stream architecture is particularly interesting, as it incorporates optical flow and yields significant improvements in action recognition.

The authors' contribution is three-fold: they propose a two-stream ConvNet architecture, demonstrate the effectiveness of a ConvNet trained on multi-frame dense optical flow, and show that multi-task learning can increase the amount of training data and improve performance on both tasks. The architecture is trained and evaluated on the standard video actions benchmarks of UCF-101 and HMDB-51, where it is competitive with the state of the art.

One of the strengths of the paper is its ability to generalize hand-crafted features, such as Histogram of Oriented Gradients (HOG) and Histogram of Optical Flow (HOF), using a single convolutional layer. The authors also provide a thorough analysis of their results, discussing the importance of motion information for action recognition and the benefits of using optical flow as input.

However, the paper feels split across multiple contributions rather than having a single cohesive theme. The authors could improve the paper by refocusing the abstract and introduction on the two-stream and optical flow significance. Additionally, the authors are missing citations to relevant work, such as "Learning Hierarchical Spatio-temporal Features for Action Recognition with Independent Subspace Analysis" (CVPR 2011) and "Evaluation of local spatio-temporal features for action recognition" (BMVC 2010).

In terms of quality, the paper is technically sound, and the authors provide a thorough evaluation of their methods. The claims are well-supported by theoretical analysis and experimental results. The paper is a complete piece of work, and the authors are careful and honest about evaluating both the strengths and weaknesses of their work.

The paper's originality lies in its novel combination of familiar techniques. The two-stream architecture and multi-task learning framework are new approaches to video classification, and the authors provide a clear explanation of how their work differs from previous contributions. The related work is adequately referenced, and the authors provide a thorough analysis of their results in the context of existing research.

The significance of the paper lies in its potential to advance the state of the art in video classification. The authors' results show impressive improvements over previous attempts to use deep nets for video classification, and their methods are competitive with state-of-the-art shallow representations. The paper provides a unique data-driven learning framework for video classification, which could be used by other researchers and practitioners in the field.

In conclusion, I recommend accepting this paper at NIPS 2014, conditional on addressing the suggested improvements. The paper's strengths in terms of quality, originality, and significance make it a valuable contribution to the field of computer vision.
