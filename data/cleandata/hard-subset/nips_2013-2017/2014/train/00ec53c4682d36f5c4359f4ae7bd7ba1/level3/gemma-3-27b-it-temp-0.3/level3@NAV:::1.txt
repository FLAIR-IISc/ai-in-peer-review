## Review of "Two-Stream Convolutional Networks for Action Recognition in Videos"

This paper presents a compelling approach to video action recognition using two separate convolutional neural networks: one processing individual frames for spatial information and the other processing stacks of dense optical flow for temporal motion cues. The key innovation lies in the simple yet effective decoupling of these two streams and their subsequent fusion, achieving performance comparable to state-of-the-art methods relying on heavily engineered hand-crafted features. This work builds upon the established idea of two visual pathways (ventral and dorsal streams) and demonstrates the power of applying deep learning directly to optical flow data, a significant contribution given previous difficulties in effectively learning spatio-temporal features.

The strengths of this paper are numerous. The proposed model is conceptually straightforward and easy to understand, yet demonstrably effective. The authors clearly articulate the motivation for their approach and position it well within the existing literature on action recognition, referencing both traditional feature engineering techniques and prior attempts at deep learning-based solutions. The experimental evaluation on UCF-101 and HMDB-51 is well-designed, and the results are thoroughly presented. The demonstration of the benefit of multi-task learning for improving generalization is also a valuable contribution. 

However, the model’s limitation to short, fixed-length temporal windows (L=10) is a significant weakness. This restricts the network’s ability to capture long-range temporal dependencies crucial for understanding complex actions. While the paper acknowledges this, a discussion of potential solutions (e.g., recurrent connections, 3D convolutions) would strengthen the work. Furthermore, the experiments lack a detailed error analysis. Understanding *which* actions the model struggles with, or the types of videos that cause errors, would provide valuable insights for future improvements. While the results are competitive, a deeper dive into failure cases is needed.

In terms of quality, the paper is technically sound and the claims are well-supported by the experimental results. The clarity of writing is excellent, making the paper accessible and easy to follow. The originality lies in the novel application of convolutional nets to optical flow features in this specific two-stream architecture. The significance is high, as this approach could significantly impact video and motion research, offering a promising alternative to complex hand-crafted features. 

**Arguments for acceptance:** Strong performance, novel application of CNNs to optical flow, clear writing, and potential for significant impact.
**Arguments against acceptance:** Limited temporal modeling capability, lack of error analysis.

**Overall:** I recommend acceptance. While the limitation regarding long-range temporal dependencies is a concern, the strengths of the paper – particularly the strong performance and novel approach – outweigh this weakness. Addressing the error analysis in a future version would further strengthen the contribution.
