This paper proposes a two-stream ConvNet architecture for action recognition in videos, which incorporates spatial and temporal networks. The spatial network operates on single frames, while the temporal network operates on short dense optical flow sequences. The authors demonstrate that their approach significantly outperforms other ConvNet-based approaches and matches the state-of-the-art on two standard video datasets, UCF-101 and HMDB-51.

The strengths of the paper include:

* The authors provide a thorough evaluation of their approach, including an analysis of the effects of different input configurations, such as stacking multiple optical flows, trajectory stacking, and mean displacement subtraction.
* The paper demonstrates the benefits of using optical flow as input to the temporal network, which is a key contribution of the work.
* The authors also explore the use of multi-task learning to improve the performance of the temporal network, which is a valuable contribution to the field.

The weaknesses of the paper include:

* The authors do not provide a clear explanation of why the spatial network outperforms models from previous work, despite having similar architectures.
* The paper could benefit from a more detailed analysis of the learned features and how they relate to the motion information in the video.

Overall, the paper is well-written and clearly presents the authors' contributions. The experiments are thorough and well-designed, and the results are impressive. The paper is a strong empirical contribution to the field of video classification, and it is likely to be of interest to researchers in the field.

Arguments for acceptance:

* The paper presents a novel and effective approach to action recognition in videos.
* The authors provide a thorough evaluation of their approach, including an analysis of the effects of different input configurations.
* The paper demonstrates the benefits of using optical flow as input to the temporal network.

Arguments against acceptance:

* The paper could benefit from a more detailed analysis of the learned features and how they relate to the motion information in the video.
* The authors do not provide a clear explanation of why the spatial network outperforms models from previous work, despite having similar architectures.

Recommendations for improvement:

* Provide a more detailed analysis of the learned features and how they relate to the motion information in the video.
* Explore different frame-sampling schemes for the spatial stream to improve the performance of the network.
* Consider providing more context and background information on the two-stream hypothesis and how it relates to the authors' approach.
