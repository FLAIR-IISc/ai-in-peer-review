- Proposes a two-stream ConvNet architecture that separates spatial (appearance) and temporal (motion) processing for video action recognition.  
- Trains a ConvNet directly on multi-frame dense optical flow to capture motion cues effectively.  
- Uses multi-task learning to combine datasets and improve generalization.  
- Evaluated on UCF-101 and HMDB-51, achieving competitive results close to state-of-the-art hand-crafted methods.  
- Builds on prior work (HOG/HOF, dense trajectories, MBH) and addresses limitations of previous ConvNet attempts on raw frames.  
- Technical approach is sound with clear architecture, training details, and use of standard deep learning practices (ReLU, pooling, dropout).  
- Detailed analysis of optical flow input configurations (stacking, trajectories, bidirectional) and their performance impact.  
- Paper is exceptionally well-written, organized, and includes comprehensive implementation details for reproducibility.  
- The two-stream design and training on optical flow are original and significant contributions to the field.  
- Demonstrates substantial improvement over previous deep-learning-only approaches and narrows the gap with hand-crafted feature methods.  
- The paper feels somewhat fragmented; the combined contribution (spatial+temporal synergy) could be emphasized more cohesively.  
- Missing relevant citations ([36], [37]) that should be added to complete the related-work overview.  
- Brief mention of the two-stream hypothesis in neuroscience is shallow; a slightly deeper discussion could add useful context.  
- Arguments for acceptance: novel, technically strong, clear writing, strong results, and reproducible implementation.  
- Arguments against acceptance: lack of a unified thematic presentation and omission of some relevant citations.  
- Overall assessment: strong paper with valuable contributions; recommend acceptance contingent on addressing presentation focus and missing citations.  
- Recommended revisions: refocus abstract/introduction on the two-stream and optical-flow contribution, add citations [36,37], and briefly expand the neuroscience connection.
