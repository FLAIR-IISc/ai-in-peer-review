

This paper proposes a two-stream ConvNet architecture for action recognition in videos, combining a spatial stream processing individual frames and a temporal stream processing short dense optical flow sequences. The authors demonstrate that this approach significantly outperforms previous deep learning methods for video classification, matching state-of-the-art results on UCF-101 and HMDB-51 datasets while using a more straightforward architecture than prior work.

The paper makes several important contributions: (1) it effectively decouples spatial and temporal recognition streams, (2) it shows that ConvNets trained on optical flow (rather than raw video frames) achieve superior performance, and (3) it introduces a multi-task learning framework to leverage multiple datasets for training. The authors thoroughly evaluate different optical flow input configurations and demonstrate the clear benefits of using optical flow over stacked RGB frames. Their experiments are comprehensive, with careful ablation studies showing the complementary nature of the two streams.

The paper excels in quality, with well-supported claims backed by extensive experimental results. The architecture is clearly explained and builds logically on prior work, particularly connecting to the two-stream hypothesis in visual processing. The significance is evident as the approach represents a substantial improvement over previous deep learning methods for video classification. The clarity is excellent, with well-organized exposition and sufficient detail for reproducibility.

A minor weakness is the unclear explanation for why the spatial stream outperforms models from [13] (which appears to be the Karpathy et al. paper), despite similar architectural approaches. The authors should clarify this discrepancy in the revision. Additionally, evaluating different frame-sampling schemes for the spatial stream would strengthen the analysis.

Overall, this is a strong empirical contribution that advances the state of the art in video action recognition. The work is technically sound, well-executed, and addresses a significant problem in computer vision. The two-stream approach provides a simple yet effective framework that could inspire future research in video understanding. The paper meets all NIPS quality standards and would be of significant interest to the video classification community. I recommend acceptance.
