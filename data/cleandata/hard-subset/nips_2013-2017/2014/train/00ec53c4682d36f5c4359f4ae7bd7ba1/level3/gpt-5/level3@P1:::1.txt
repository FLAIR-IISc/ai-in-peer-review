This paper proposes a two-stream convolutional architecture for video action recognition that explicitly separates appearance and motion: a spatial ConvNet trained on RGB frames (pre-trained on ImageNet) and a temporal ConvNet trained on stacked dense optical flow between consecutive frames. The two streams are trained independently and fused at the score level (averaging or linear SVM). The temporal stream explores several input configurations (optical-flow stacking over L frames, trajectory stacking, bi-directional flow, and mean-flow subtraction). To mitigate limited video data, the authors also apply multi-task learning to jointly train on UCF-101 and HMDB-51. On both benchmarks, the temporal net substantially outperforms RGB-only models, and the two-stream fusion is competitive with state-of-the-art hand-crafted pipelines based on improved dense trajectories and MBH. The approach improves markedly over previous deep networks trained on raw frame stacks (e.g., Karpathy et al., CVPR’14) and can be viewed as a learned generalization of classical HOF/MBH/trajectory features (e.g., Wang & Schmid, ICCV’13), while conceptually echoing the ventral/dorsal two-stream hypothesis and earlier shallow two-path HMAX systems.

Strengths
- Clear, simple architecture that yields strong performance despite limited training data.
- Key empirical finding: temporally stacked optical flow is an effective ConvNet input, outperforming raw RGB frame stacks for motion modeling.
- Thorough ablations on temporal input design (L, trajectory vs. flow stacking, mean subtraction, bi-directional flow) and practical training details (multi-GPU, data augmentation).
- Multi-task learning is an elegant and effective way to leverage multiple datasets without manual class alignment; improves both UCF-101 and HMDB-51.
- Results are competitive with hand-crafted state-of-the-art pipelines and substantially better than prior deep learning baselines.

Weaknesses
- Limited temporal modeling: fixed short windows (L=10) do not capture long-range structure or action phases over whole videos.
- Dependence on precomputed optical flow (Brox) introduces a computational bottleneck and an external algorithmic choice; sensitivity to flow quality is not analyzed.
- Fusion is restricted to late score-level combination; attempts at joint feature fusion overfit—no exploration of regularization/architecture to learn fused representations.
- Error-mode analysis is missing (per-class breakdowns, failure cases, sensitivity to camera motion or background clutter); only mean-flow subtraction is used for camera motion.
- Generalization to larger, noisier datasets (e.g., Sports-1M) is mentioned but not demonstrated; scalability of precomputing/storing flow is a concern.

Quality
- Technically sound with appropriate ablations and comparisons to relevant baselines (single-frame, slow fusion on RGB stacks, optical-flow variants). Training protocols, data augmentation, and evaluation follow standard practice; multi-task formulation is straightforward and well-justified.
- Claims are supported by experiments on standard splits of UCF-101 and HMDB-51; the observed complementary nature of spatial and temporal streams is convincing.
- Missing analyses: detailed per-class accuracy/confusions, robustness to camera motion, and sensitivity to the choice of optical flow algorithm or parameters. Runtime/storage and deployment considerations would strengthen the empirical evaluation.

Clarity
- Very clearly written and well-organized. The temporal input formulations (flow stacking, trajectory stacking, bi-directional flow, mean subtraction) are precisely defined. Implementation details (learning rates, dropout schedules, sampling, testing) should enable reproduction. Figures effectively convey the two-stream design.

Originality
- Novel and impactful application of ConvNets to dense optical-flow inputs for action recognition, coupled with a two-stream decomposition and multi-task training. While conceptually related to dorsal/ventral and prior HMAX two-stream work, the end-to-end discriminative ConvNet treatment of optical flow and the demonstration of its superiority over raw RGB stacks are original and timely.

Significance
- Likely to influence subsequent video recognition research by establishing optical-flow ConvNets and two-stream fusion as strong baselines and by showing a principled path from hand-crafted motion features to learned representations. The results rival strong hand-crafted systems on widely used benchmarks and substantially improve over previous deep models, underscoring practical impact.

Suggestions for improvement
- Incorporate longer-range temporal modeling (e.g., temporal pyramids, dilated temporal convolutions, or sequence models) to capture action dynamics beyond L=10 frames.
- Explore stronger fusion strategies that avoid overfitting (e.g., shared early layers, factorized lateral connections, feature-level fusion with regularization or dropout, or attention over streams).
- Provide error analysis: per-class confusion, categories benefiting most from motion vs. appearance, and typical failure cases (e.g., camera motion, small/fast objects).
- Evaluate robustness to different optical flow methods (e.g., TV-L1, DeepFlow) and report computational costs for flow computation, storage, and inference to assess scalability.
- Consider explicit camera motion compensation (e.g., homography-based stabilization) and/or trajectory-aligned pooling within the ConvNet to better exploit motion boundaries and tubes.

Arguments for acceptance
- Strong empirical performance with a simple, interpretable architecture; clear advance over prior deep video models.
- Convincing evidence that learned models can generalize and subsume key hand-crafted motion features.
- Cleanly written, reproducible, and practically relevant; multi-task training is a useful contribution to video recognition practice.

Arguments against acceptance
- Limited temporal scope; no modeling of long-range dependencies or sequence structure.
- Heavy reliance on precomputed optical flow with no robustness/efficiency analysis.
- Lack of detailed error analysis and deeper insights into when/why the model fails.

Recommendation: Accept. The paper makes a clear and significant contribution by demonstrating that two-stream ConvNets with stacked optical flow are a powerful and practical approach to action recognition, bridging the gap between hand-crafted motion features and learned deep representations.
