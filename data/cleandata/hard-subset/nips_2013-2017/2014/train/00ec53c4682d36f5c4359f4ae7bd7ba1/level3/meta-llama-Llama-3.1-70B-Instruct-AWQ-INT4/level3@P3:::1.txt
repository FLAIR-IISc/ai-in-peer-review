This paper proposes a novel two-stream convolutional network architecture for action recognition in videos. The model consists of two separate recognition streams, one for spatial information from individual frames and another for temporal information from optical flow. Each stream is implemented as a ConvNet, and the predictions are combined using late fusion. The authors demonstrate that this architecture achieves results comparable to state-of-the-art methods using heavy hand-crafted features on two challenging datasets, UCF-101 and HMDB-51.

The proposed model is a significant improvement over previous attempts to use deep nets for video classification. The authors' decision to decouple spatial and temporal recognition streams and use ConvNets for both streams is a key innovation. The use of optical flow as input to the temporal stream is also a novel and important contribution, as it allows the network to explicitly represent motion.

The experiments are well-designed, and the results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed architecture. The authors also investigate various input configurations for the temporal stream, including stacking multiple optical flows, trajectory stacking, and mean displacement subtraction. They find that stacking multiple flows and mean subtraction are beneficial, while the difference between stacking techniques is marginal.

The paper also explores multi-task learning as a way to increase the effective training set size. The authors demonstrate that multi-task learning can improve performance on both UCF-101 and HMDB-51 datasets.

One of the strengths of the paper is its clarity. The authors provide a clear and concise explanation of the proposed architecture and the experiments. The model is well-explained, and the results are easy to understand.

However, there are some weaknesses in the paper. One of the main limitations is that the model only captures short fixed-length temporal windows and does not model the whole-video sequence structure. This is a significant limitation, as it means that the model may not be able to capture long-term dependencies in the video.

Another weakness is the lack of error-mode analysis. The authors do not provide a detailed analysis of the types of errors made by the model, which would be useful in understanding its strengths and weaknesses.

In terms of originality, the paper makes a significant contribution to the field of action recognition in videos. The use of two-stream convolutional networks and optical flow as input to the temporal stream is a novel and important innovation.

The significance of the paper is also high. The proposed architecture has the potential to significantly impact video and motion research, and the results demonstrate its effectiveness on two challenging datasets.

In conclusion, this paper proposes a novel two-stream convolutional network architecture for action recognition in videos. The model achieves results comparable to state-of-the-art methods using heavy hand-crafted features on two challenging datasets. The paper is well-written, and the experiments are well-designed. However, there are some limitations, including the model's inability to capture long-term dependencies and the lack of error-mode analysis.

Arguments for acceptance:

* The paper proposes a novel and effective architecture for action recognition in videos.
* The experiments are well-designed, and the results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed architecture.

Arguments against acceptance:
