- Proposes a two-stream ConvNet combining a spatial (ImageNet-pretrained single-frame) and a temporal (dense optical flow) stream, fused for video action recognition.
- Main contributions: two-stream architecture, demonstration that temporal ConvNets on dense optical flow work well with limited video data, and a multi-task learning framework across datasets.
- Thorough evaluation of multiple optical flow input configurations, showing clear benefits of optical-flow-based temporal modeling.
- Multi-task learning effectively mitigates limited video training data and improves performance.
- Strong, comprehensive experiments on UCF-101 and HMDB-51 that outperform prior deep methods and match state-of-the-art hand-crafted features.
- Technically sound with well-designed experiments and reproducible implementation details.
- Exceptionally clear writing and organization, with helpful figures and explanations.
- Novelty in successfully implementing the two-stream concept with discriminatively trained deep ConvNets for video recognition.
- Shows temporal ConvNet can generalize and subsume hand-crafted motion features within a learned framework.
- Significant impact: establishes a strong baseline and influenced subsequent video recognition research.
- Strengths include exhaustive optical flow experiments, clear advantage over stacked RGB, effective fusion, and strong benchmark results.
- Weaknesses: lacks analysis explaining why the spatial stream outperforms Karpathy et al.'s spatial network.
- Weaknesses: does not evaluate different frame-sampling schemes for the spatial stream.
- Weaknesses: insufficient explanation for why multi-frame RGB approaches fail to capture motion as effectively.
- Overall: strong empirical paper that bridges deep image and video recognition; minor comparative analysis gaps do not undermine acceptance recommendation.
