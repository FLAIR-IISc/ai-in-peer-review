This paper proposes a two-stream ConvNet architecture for action recognition in videos, which incorporates spatial and temporal networks. The spatial network operates on single frames, while the temporal network is trained on short dense optical flow sequences. The authors demonstrate that their approach significantly outperforms other ConvNet-based methods and matches state-of-the-art performance on two standard video datasets, UCF-101 and HMDB-51.

The proposed architecture is closely related to the two-streams hypothesis in human visual cortex, which contains two pathways for object recognition and motion recognition. The authors, however, do not investigate this connection further. Instead, they focus on applying standard image ConvNet pipelines to dense optical flow and fusing it with a single-frame ConvNet. The experiments are thorough, and the results are impressive.

One of the key contributions of this paper is the evaluation of several ways to train ConvNets on optical flow, showing clear benefits of optical-flow input. The authors also demonstrate that multi-task learning can be used to increase the amount of training data and improve performance on both UCF-101 and HMDB-51 datasets.

The paper is well-written, and the authors provide a clear explanation of their approach, related work, and experimental results. The architecture is well-motivated, and the experiments are carefully designed to evaluate the performance of the proposed approach.

In terms of quality, the paper is technically sound, and the claims are well-supported by theoretical analysis and experimental results. The authors are careful and honest about evaluating both the strengths and weaknesses of their work. The paper is a complete piece of work, and the authors provide sufficient details for the expert reader to reproduce their results.

The clarity of the paper is also excellent. The writing is clear and concise, and the organization is logical and easy to follow. The authors provide sufficient background information and related work to make the paper accessible to non-experts.

In terms of originality, the paper proposes a novel combination of familiar techniques. The two-stream architecture is not new, but the application of standard image ConvNet pipelines to dense optical flow is a new contribution. The paper also provides a new perspective on how to generalize hand-crafted features within a data-driven learning framework.

The significance of the paper is high. The results are important, and other researchers are likely to use these ideas or build on them. The paper addresses a difficult problem in a better way than previous research and advances the state of the art in a demonstrable way.

One minor concern is that it is unclear why the spatial network beats models from [13] given similar architectures. The authors should provide more discussion on this point to clarify the differences between their approach and previous work.

Another suggestion for future work is to evaluate different frame-sampling schemes for the spatial stream. The authors currently sample a fixed number of frames with equal temporal spacing between them, but other sampling schemes may also be effective.

Overall, this is a strong empirical paper of interest to the video-classification community. The authors provide a thorough evaluation of their approach, and the results are impressive. The paper is well-written, and the authors provide sufficient details for the expert reader to reproduce their results.

* The paper proposes a novel combination of familiar techniques, which is a significant contribution to the field.
