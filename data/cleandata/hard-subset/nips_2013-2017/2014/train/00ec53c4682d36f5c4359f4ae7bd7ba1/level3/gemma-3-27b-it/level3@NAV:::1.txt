## Review of "Two-Stream Convolutional Networks for Action Recognition in Videos"

This paper presents a two-stream convolutional network architecture for action recognition, decoupling spatial appearance and temporal motion information. The authors train separate convolutional nets – one on individual frames and the other on stacked dense optical flow – and average their predictions. This is a conceptually simple yet effective approach, demonstrating the utility of optical flow features within a deep learning framework, and achieving results competitive with state-of-the-art methods relying on hand-crafted features on the UCF-101 and HMDB-51 datasets. 

The work builds on prior attempts to extend CNNs to video, notably [14] which found limited success with stacking frames. This paper distinguishes itself by explicitly modeling temporal information via optical flow, effectively generalizing previous trajectory-based representations [20, 26] within a data-driven learning paradigm. The use of multi-task learning to leverage data from both UCF-101 and HMDB-51 is a clever addition, further enhancing performance.

A key strength of this work is its simplicity and strong performance. The architecture is straightforward to understand and implement, yet achieves impressive results. Demonstrating that a ConvNet trained on optical flow can reach state-of-the-art performance is a significant contribution. Furthermore, the clear writing and well-organized presentation enhance the paper's readability. 

However, the model is limited to short, fixed-length temporal windows (L=10), which prevents it from capturing long-range temporal dependencies within the videos. This is a notable weakness, as many actions unfold over extended periods. The experimental evaluation, while thorough, lacks a detailed error-mode analysis. Investigating per-class failures or analyzing the types of videos causing errors (e.g., complex interactions, subtle actions) would provide valuable insights for future improvements.

**Quality:** The experiments are well-designed and results are clearly presented. The lack of error analysis is a minor shortcoming.
**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally clear and well-written.
**Originality:** The application of convolutional nets to optical flow features, while not entirely novel, is a significant and impactful contribution.
**Significance:** The approach holds substantial promise for video and motion research, and the strong results suggest a viable path towards more robust and accurate action recognition systems.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
* Strong performance competitive with state-of-the-art.
* Novel demonstration of the effectiveness of optical flow within a CNN framework.
* Clear and well-written presentation.
* Potentially impactful for the field.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**
* Limited temporal modeling capability (fixed window size).
* Lack of error-mode analysis.



**Overall:** This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of action recognition. While the limitations regarding long-term temporal modeling and lack of error analysis exist, the strengths of the work outweigh these weaknesses. I recommend acceptance.
