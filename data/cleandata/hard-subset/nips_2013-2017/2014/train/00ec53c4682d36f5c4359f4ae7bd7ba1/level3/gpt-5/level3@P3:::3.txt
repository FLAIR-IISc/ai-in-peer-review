Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes a two-stream ConvNet architecture for video action recognition, explicitly decoupling appearance (spatial stream) from motion (temporal stream) and fusing their predictions at the end. The spatial stream is a standard image ConvNet pretrained on ImageNet, while the temporal stream operates on stacked dense optical flow fields, with several carefully designed variants (optical-flow vs. trajectory stacking, uni- vs. bi-directional flow, mean-flow subtraction, number of stacked frames). To mitigate limited video data, the authors introduce multi-task learning (MTL) to jointly train on UCF-101 and HMDB-51 with task-specific softmax heads.

The work is well positioned relative to prior deep approaches that used raw stacked RGB frames (e.g., Karpathy et al., “slow fusion”), and to earlier two-stream HMAX-style models (Jhuang et al.), as well as hand-crafted methods based on improved dense trajectories with HOF/MBH. The paper shows that providing motion explicitly via optical flow enables a ConvNet to generalize and subsume classic flow-derived features, and that combining spatial and temporal cues yields strong performance.

Strengths
- Clear, well written, and detailed. The methodology, training, and testing procedures (input construction, augmentation, schedules, multi-GPU setup, precomputation and compression of flow) are described thoroughly enough to reproduce results.
- Novel and complementary contributions: (i) a discriminatively trained deep two-stream formulation; (ii) an effective temporal ConvNet that ingests stacked optical flow; (iii) multi-task learning to leverage multiple datasets. The temporal-stream design nicely bridges classic flow-derived descriptors (HOF/MBH) with modern ConvNets.
- Strong empirical results and careful ablations: stacking 10 flows, mean-flow subtraction, and late fusion (SVM over softmax) matter; temporal stream substantially outperforms spatial; two-stream fusion adds another sizable gain. Results are competitive with the trajectory-based state of the art and improve markedly over previous deep video ConvNets (e.g., Karpathy et al.), while far outperforming single-frame baselines.
- Particularly interesting insight: explicitly modeling motion with optical flow dramatically improves over implicit learning from raw frames, indicating that motion inference is difficult to learn end-to-end at present dataset scales.

Weaknesses
- The paper feels split across multiple contributions (two-stream design, multiple flow-input variants, and multi-task learning), which somewhat dilutes a single cohesive narrative.
- Limited analysis of the sensitivity to optical flow choice and computational tradeoffs. Flow precomputation dominates the pipeline in storage and time, yet there is no accuracy-cost comparison across flow methods (e.g., TV-L1 vs. Brox vs. DeepFlow) or analysis of inference latency vs. trajectories-based pipelines.
- Fusion design is restricted to late fusion due to overfitting; more discussion on why joint fusion failed (e.g., parameter counts, regularization, data scale) would be useful, as would exploring lighter joint heads or shared normalization/calibration of softmax scores.
- Minor related-work gaps and attributions in the introduction; see suggested citations below.

Quality
Technically sound and carefully executed. The ablations are informative, the training setup is standard and robust, and the evaluation follows accepted UCF-101/HMDB-51 protocols across splits. Claims are well supported by experiments. A few additional analyses (flow algorithm comparison, runtime/storage breakdown, and per-class gains where motion dominates) would further strengthen the empirical case.

Clarity
Very clear and well organized, with enough implementation detail to reproduce. Figures and equations for input construction are helpful. To improve, consider refocusing the abstract/introduction to emphasize the central insight: the two-stream design with optical flow is the key driver of the gains, with MTL as a practical accessory to combat data scarcity.

Originality
High. While two-stream ideas exist in vision neuroscience and in shallow HMAX-like systems, this work’s deep, discriminatively trained two-stream ConvNet with explicit flow input is novel and influential. The MTL setup is a judicious, if standard, application in this context. The paper clearly differentiates itself from prior deep video ConvNets that relied on raw frames.

Significance
High. The approach significantly advances deep learning for video recognition at the time, provides a practical recipe that others can adopt, and competes with the best hand-crafted pipelines. The two-stream paradigm is likely to be widely used and extended.

Suggestions for improvement
- Narrative focus: Reframe the abstract and introduction to center the two-stream/optical-flow idea as the main contribution, with MTL presented as a complementary technique to address data scarcity.
- Flow analysis: Report accuracy vs. compute/storage for different flow algorithms (e.g., Brox, TV-L1, DeepFlow), and discuss deployment implications. Include a brief runtime comparison to improved trajectories.
- Fusion: Elaborate on failed attempts at joint fusion; try lighter joint heads, stronger regularization, or calibration of softmax scores before SVM. Consider early/mid-level cross-stream interactions with parameter sharing.
- Camera motion: Explore stronger camera-motion compensation beyond mean-flow subtraction (e.g., homography-based stabilization), and/or per-clip normalization strategies.
- Trajectory pooling: Given the success of trajectory-based pooling in shallow pipelines, assess trajectory-aware pooling within the ConvNet.
- Reproducibility: If possible, release scripts for flow precomputation, compression parameters, and trained models.

Missing citations to add (if not already included)
- Le et al., CVPR 2011: Learning Hierarchical Spatio-Temporal Features for Action Recognition with Independent Subspace Analysis.
- Wang et al., BMVC 2010: Evaluation of local spatio-temporal features for action recognition.

Pros for acceptance
- Strong, novel, and influential two-stream design with clear empirical gains.
- Thorough methodology and ablations; reproducible.
- Competitive with state-of-the-art and markedly better than prior deep models.
- Complements and advances existing deep learning frameworks for video.

Cons against acceptance
- Somewhat diffuse presentation across multiple contributions.
- Limited analysis of computational tradeoffs and flow algorithm sensitivity.
- No successful joint fusion; only late fusion reported.

Overall recommendation
Accept (NIPS 2014), conditional on minor revisions: refocus the abstract/introduction on the two-stream/optical-flow significance, add the suggested related-work citations, and include brief discussion (or supplemental results) on flow choice/compute tradeoffs and fusion design rationale.
