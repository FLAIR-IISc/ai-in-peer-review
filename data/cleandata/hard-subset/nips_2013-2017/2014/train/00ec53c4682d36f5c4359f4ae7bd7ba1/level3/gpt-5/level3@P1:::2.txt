Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes a two-stream ConvNet architecture for video action recognition that explicitly decouples appearance and motion: a spatial stream processes single RGB frames (pre-trained on ImageNet), and a temporal stream consumes stacked dense optical flow fields over short windows. The two softmax outputs are fused by averaging or a linear SVM. This design is directly motivated by the ventral/dorsal pathways hypothesis and addresses the difficulty observed in prior works where end-to-end ConvNets on stacks of raw frames failed to learn sufficiently motion-sensitive features.

Relative to prior work, the temporal stream generalizes well-known hand-crafted motion representations such as HOF/MBH and trajectory-based features [8, 20, 26, 29] by learning from motion fields directly. Unlike earlier deep approaches that operate on raw frame stacks [12, 14] or use shallow, hand-crafted HMAX pipelines [11, 16], the authors show that using optical flow as input yields substantial gains, and that a clean late-fusion with a strong image ConvNet produces state-of-the-art performance on UCF-101 and HMDB-51. They also introduce a simple but effective multi-task learning setup to leverage multiple datasets and mitigate overfitting in the temporal stream.

Strengths
- Clear architectural insight: separating spatial and temporal streams and feeding the temporal net with optical flow solves a key failure mode in prior video ConvNets.
- Thorough empirical study: ablations over flow stacking length, trajectory vs location stacking, bi-directional flow, mean-flow subtraction, and fusion strategies.
- Strong results: large improvements over deep baselines [14, 16]; competitive with state-of-the-art handcrafted pipelines [20, 21, 26] despite modest training data.
- Practical training recipe: pre-training spatial stream on ImageNet and multi-task learning for temporal stream are simple, reproducible, and effective.
- Good engineering: multi-GPU training, careful data augmentation, and a sensible test-time protocol.

Weaknesses
- Dependence on precomputed optical flow introduces a computational/storage bottleneck and prevents end-to-end learning; flow choice and parameters are only briefly explored.
- Limited temporal modeling: temporal receptive field is short (L up to 10), with long-range aggregation handled by simple score averaging across sparsely sampled frames.
- Camera motion handling is minimal (mean displacement subtraction) compared to explicit compensation in trajectory pipelines.
- The spatial stream’s ImageNet pretraining outperforms prior reports for similar architectures; the paper’s explanation (larger sampling area) is plausible but not rigorously controlled.
- Fusion at score level is effective but shallow; the paper reports overfitting when attempting deeper joint fusion without exploring regularization alternatives.

Quality
The methodology is sound and the ablations are informative. Claims are supported with consistent evaluations on standard splits and metrics. The connection to hand-crafted features is well argued (and supported by first-layer filter visualizations). Some experimental aspects could be strengthened: (i) sensitivity to different optical flow algorithms and parameters; (ii) effect of the multi-task weighting between datasets; (iii) controlled comparison isolating the spatial stream’s augmentation versus architecture.

Clarity
The paper is clearly written and well organized. The temporal input configurations are precisely defined, and implementation/training details are sufficient for reproduction. A small clarification would help: provide a controlled ImageNet pretraining comparison to reconcile spatial stream gains with [31] and similar architectures.

Originality
The two-stream formulation with learned ConvNets and explicit optical-flow input is a clean, novel synthesis that bridges deep learning and the best insights from trajectory-based methods. The multi-task learning application to action recognition is straightforward but appropriate and useful.

Significance
This work is likely to influence subsequent video recognition research by establishing a strong, simple baseline and demonstrating that explicit motion input is critical. The approach achieves competitive or superior performance to prior deep models and approaches the hand-crafted state of the art, making it impactful for both researchers and practitioners.

Questions for the authors
- How sensitive are results to the choice of optical flow algorithm and its parameters (e.g., Brox vs. TV-L1)? Is the mean subtraction still beneficial across algorithms?
- What weighting of task losses is used in multi-task learning? Did you observe negative transfer for overlapping classes?
- Did you try denser temporal sampling at test time or longer flow stacks (L > 10)? Where are the diminishing returns?

Suggestions for improvement
- Evaluate alternative frame-sampling schemes for the spatial stream (e.g., denser sampling, motion-adaptive sampling) and report their impact.
- Provide a controlled experiment isolating the spatial stream’s augmentation strategy from architecture to explain the ImageNet gap with prior reports.
- Explore stronger regularization to enable learned joint fusion (e.g., dropout, weight decay, batch norm, bottleneck layers).
- Analyze long-range temporal aggregation: compare average pooling with simple temporal convolutions or recurrent layers on top of per-frame/-clip features.
- Report compute/storage costs for flow precomputation and runtime throughput; discuss trade-offs versus raw-frame models.

Pros for acceptance
- Strong empirical gains and clear insight into why prior deep video models underperform.
- Simple, general architecture likely to be widely adopted.
- Thorough ablations and reproducible training details.
- Demonstrated utility of multi-task learning for small video datasets.

Cons for acceptance
- Not end-to-end with respect to motion; reliance on precomputed flow.
- Limited exploration of long-range temporal structure and camera motion compensation.
- Some open questions about spatial stream gains vs. closely related ImageNet models.

Recommendation
Accept. This is a strong empirical paper with a clear contribution, careful analysis, and results that will be influential for video action recognition.

Reviewer confidence
High.
