This paper presents a novel deep learning architecture for action recognition in videos. The core idea is to decompose the task into two streams, handled by separate Convolutional Networks (ConvNets). The "spatial stream" operates on individual video frames to capture appearance information, while the "temporal stream" operates on stacked dense optical flow fields to capture motion. The authors demonstrate that pre-training the spatial stream on a large image dataset (ImageNet) is highly effective. For the temporal stream, which must be trained on smaller video datasets, they show that using stacked optical flow as input is significantly more effective than training on raw stacked frames, as explored in prior work (e.g., Karpathy et al., CVPR 2014). The outputs of the two streams are combined via late fusion (averaging or SVM). As a secondary contribution, the paper introduces a multi-task learning framework to effectively combine different video datasets (UCF-101 and HMDB-51) for training, which helps to regularize the model and improve performance. Experiments on UCF-101 and HMDB-51 show that this two-stream approach significantly outperforms previous deep learning methods and achieves performance competitive with state-of-the-art hand-crafted feature-based methods.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound and presents a complete piece of work. The claims are well-supported by extensive experiments on standard benchmarks. The authors perform a thorough ablation study on the temporal stream, investigating the impact of the number of stacked flow frames, mean subtraction, and different stacking strategies. The implementation and training details are described clearly, which is valuable for reproducibility. The evaluation is fair and follows standard protocols.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-written and easy to follow. The motivation for the two-stream approach is clearly articulated, and the architecture is explained well, aided by helpful diagrams. The experimental sections are well-organized, logically progressing from evaluating individual components to the final combined system. One minor point on clarity is that the paper feels slightly split between two major contributions: the two-stream architecture and the multi-task learning framework. While both are valuable, the narrative could be sharpened to present the two-stream concept as the primary contribution, with multi-task learning as a supporting technique to achieve the best results.

**Originality:** The proposed methods are novel and represent a significant conceptual advance for video understanding with deep learning. The key original contribution is the explicit separation of appearance and motion modeling into two deep networks, and specifically, the design of a ConvNet that effectively learns from dense optical flow. While the idea of two processing streams is inspired by neuroscience and has appeared in earlier, shallower models (e.g., Jhuang et al. [11]), this paper is the first to successfully implement it in a modern, end-to-end deep learning framework with such strong results. The use of multi-task learning to combine standard action recognition datasets is also a novel and practical contribution to address data scarcity.

**Significance:** The results presented in this paper are highly significant. At the time of this work, deep learning methods for video action recognition lagged considerably behind traditional methods based on hand-crafted features like improved Dense Trajectories (iDT). This paper closes that gap, showing that a well-designed deep architecture can be competitive with the state-of-the-art. The insight that ConvNets can very effectively learn from pre-computed optical flow, rather than struggling to learn motion from raw pixels, was a breakthrough for the field and has influenced a great deal of subsequent research. The strong performance, coupled with the elegant and intuitive architecture, makes this work a landmark contribution that other researchers are very likely to build upon.

### Arguments for Acceptance:
1.  **Novel and Effective Architecture:** The two-stream architecture, particularly the temporal ConvNet operating on optical flow, is a novel and highly effective approach for action recognition.
2.  **State-of-the-Art Performance:** The method achieves results that are competitive with the best-performing hand-crafted feature methods, representing a major step forward for deep learning in this domain.
3.  **Strong Empirical Validation:** The paper includes a comprehensive set of experiments and ablation studies that rigorously validate the design choices and demonstrate the complementary nature of the two streams.
4.  **High Impact:** The work addresses a difficult and important problem. The core ideas are insightful and have strong potential to influence future research in video understanding.

### Arguments Against Acceptance:
1.  **Slightly Unfocused Narrative:** The paper presents two distinct contributions (two-stream architecture and multi-task learning). While both are valuable, the main story could be tightened by framing the multi-task learning more as a component that helps optimize the primary two-stream model, rather than a standalone contribution of equal weight.

### Detailed Comments and Suggestions:
-   To improve the focus of the paper, I would recommend rewriting parts of the abstract and introduction to more strongly emphasize that the central contribution is the two-stream architecture and the novel use of a ConvNet on optical flow. This is the most significant and impactful idea, and the paper would be stronger if the narrative was built more cohesively around it.
-   The related work section provides a good overview. To further strengthen it, the authors could add a more direct comparison of their learned temporal features to those from prior work. For example, while the paper cites Le et al. (CVPR 2011) [18] and Wang et al. (BMVC 2010) [28], a more explicit discussion contrasting the end-to-end learned filters in the temporal stream against the unsupervised features of [18] or the hand-crafted HOF/MBH features evaluated in [28] would better highlight the advantages of their approach.

Overall, this is a very strong paper with a novel and significant contribution to the field of action recognition. The methodology is sound, the experiments are thorough, and the results are impressive. I recommend acceptance, conditional on the authors considering the minor suggestions for improving the narrative focus.
