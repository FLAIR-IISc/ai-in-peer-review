This paper proposes a two-stream ConvNet architecture for video action recognition that separates appearance (spatial stream on single RGB frames, pre-trained on ImageNet) from motion (temporal stream on stacked dense optical flow). The streams are fused at the score level (averaging or linear SVM). The authors conduct thorough ablations on optical-flow inputs (stack length, trajectory vs. location stacking, mean subtraction, bi-directional flow) and introduce multi-task learning across UCF-101 and HMDB-51 to mitigate limited data. The model substantially outperforms prior ConvNet-based approaches (e.g., single-frame and slow-fusion models) and is competitive with state-of-the-art hand-crafted pipelines based on improved dense trajectories and HOF/MBH. The work is well situated with respect to earlier deep video models (e.g., Karpathy et al.) and the two-stream hypothesis, and it shows how optical-flow inputs generalize classic motion descriptors within a learnable CNN.

Quality: The methodology is technically sound, with extensive, careful experimentation and clear ablations that support the claims. The benefits of optical-flow input and two-stream fusion are convincingly demonstrated. A minor gap is the lack of analysis for why the spatial stream outperforms comparable single-frame CNN baselines (e.g., [13]/related architectures), given ostensibly similar networks and training.

Clarity: The paper is clearly written and well organized. Implementation details (architecture, training schedules, data augmentation, multi-GPU training, optical flow precomputation) are sufficient for reproduction. It would help to clarify the frame-sampling policy for the spatial stream and to discuss runtime/storage implications of precomputing and storing flow.

Originality: While each component (ConvNets, optical flow, late fusion, multi-task learning) is known, their combination into a simple, effective two-stream framework, plus the systematic exploration of optical-flow training strategies, is novel and insightful.

Significance: The approach advances the state of the art for deep video recognition, narrows the gap to trajectory-based methods, and is likely to influence subsequent work on video modeling and multimodal fusion.

Suggestions: (i) Provide a controlled comparison explaining why the spatial stream surpasses similar baselines; (ii) evaluate different frame-sampling schemes for the spatial stream (uniform, random, motion-guided); (iii) report compute/storage costs and throughput; (iv) discuss prospects for end-to-end learning of motion (e.g., learned flow).

Pros (for acceptance):
- Strong empirical gains; thorough ablations and clear insights
- Simple, general approach; complementary streams with effective fusion
- Careful evaluation on two standard benchmarks; multi-task learning benefits

Cons (against acceptance):
- Limited analysis of why the spatial stream outperforms similar CNNs
- Dependence on precomputed optical flow and associated compute/storage overhead
- Missing exploration of spatial frame-sampling strategies
