

This paper introduces a two-stream convolutional network architecture for video action recognition that effectively combines spatial and temporal information. The authors address a key challenge in video action recognition: capturing complementary information from static appearance (spatial stream) and motion (temporal stream). Their approach is particularly notable as it represents a significant improvement over prior attempts to apply deep networks to video classification, which had struggled to match the performance of hand-crafted shallow features.

The paper builds upon the two-stream hypothesis from neuroscience (ventral stream for object recognition, dorsal stream for motion) but implements it using discriminatively trained deep ConvNets rather than hand-crafted features. The spatial stream processes individual video frames using a standard image classification ConvNet architecture (pre-trained on ImageNet), while the temporal stream processes dense optical flow between frames, effectively generalizing previous trajectory-based hand-crafted features. The authors demonstrate that training on optical flow provides substantially better performance than training on stacked RGB frames, and that combining the two streams through late fusion yields state-of-the-art results on UCF-101 and HMDB-51.

The work's quality is strong, with thorough experimental evaluation across multiple input configurations for the temporal stream (optical flow stacking, trajectory stacking, bi-directional flow, mean subtraction). The authors convincingly demonstrate that stacking multiple optical flow frames (L=10) provides the most discriminative motion information, and that mean flow subtraction helps mitigate camera motion effects. The multi-task learning approach for combining UCF-101 and HMDB-51 datasets is well-justified and significantly improves performance on the smaller HMDB-51 dataset. The paper's technical execution is sound, with clear implementation details and appropriate baselines for comparison.

The paper's originality lies in its systematic exploration of how to effectively apply deep learning to video action recognition, specifically by decoupling spatial and temporal processing streams. While related to the two-stream hypothesis in neuroscience, the authors don't overstate this connection, focusing instead on the practical implementation. The work clearly differentiates itself from prior deep learning approaches to video action recognition (notably [14]), which had struggled to match hand-crafted features. The paper also properly situates itself within the broader literature on action recognition, comparing against both shallow and deep approaches.

The significance of this work is substantial. The authors demonstrate that their approach achieves results competitive with the state-of-the-art hand-crafted methods on standard benchmarks, while using a more unified and data-driven framework. The paper provides a clear blueprint for applying deep learning to video recognition that has been widely adopted in subsequent work. The authors also identify important directions for future work, including training on larger datasets and incorporating more sophisticated trajectory-based features.

Clarity is generally good, with the paper well-organized and the technical details sufficiently explained. The figures effectively illustrate the input configurations and network architecture. However, the explanation of why the spatial stream (trained on ImageNet) outperforms the single-frame ConvNet approach in [14] is somewhat unclear. The authors should clarify whether the difference stems from architectural variations, training procedures, or dataset differences. Additionally, the paper could benefit from exploring different frame-sampling strategies for the spatial stream, as the current approach (sampling 25 frames with equal temporal spacing) may not be optimal for all action types.

The paper has a few minor weaknesses. The evaluation of the temporal stream's performance on the HMDB-51 dataset could have included more detailed ablation studies on the multi-task learning component. The discussion of how their approach relates to the trajectory-based hand-crafted features could be expanded to more explicitly show how their learned filters correspond to these features. The paper also doesn't fully explore the computational cost of their approach, which could be important for practical applications.

Overall, this is a strong empirical paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of video action recognition. The two-stream architecture provides a simple yet effective framework that significantly advances the state of the art for deep learning approaches to video classification. The thorough experimental evaluation and clear presentation of results make this paper highly valuable to the video classification community. The authors have successfully demonstrated that deep learning can effectively capture both spatial and temporal information for action recognition, overcoming previous limitations of end-to-end video ConvNets.

The paper's main contribution is not merely incremental—it provides a compelling new approach that outperforms prior deep learning methods by a substantial margin while matching the best hand-crafted features. The work has clearly been well-executed, with careful attention to the details of implementation and evaluation. Given the quality of the experimental results, the clear connection to previous work, and the significance of the problem being addressed, I recommend acceptance. The paper represents an important step forward in applying deep learning to video analysis and has already influenced subsequent work in the field.
