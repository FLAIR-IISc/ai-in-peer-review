Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes a two-stream deep architecture for video action recognition that decouples appearance and motion. The spatial stream is a ConvNet applied to single frames (pretrained on ImageNet), while the temporal stream is a ConvNet applied to stacked dense optical flow fields over short temporal windows. Late fusion (averaging or SVM on softmax scores) combines the streams. The authors evaluate multiple design choices for the temporal input (number of stacked flows, trajectory vs. fixed-location stacking, uni- vs. bi-directional flow, mean-flow subtraction) and introduce a simple multi-task learning setup to exploit both UCF-101 and HMDB-51 for training the temporal net.

This approach directly addresses shortcomings of prior ConvNet-based video models that fed stacks of RGB frames to a single network (e.g., Karpathy et al. 2014), which struggled to learn motion features and lagged trajectory-based hand-crafted pipelines (e.g., Improved Dense Trajectories: Wang & Schmid 2013). The temporal net builds on classical optical flow energy formulations (e.g., Brox et al.) and connects to popular hand-crafted motion descriptors (HOF/MBH, kinematic features) by showing how such cues can be captured within early layers of a ConvNet when flow inputs are used. The two-stream design is also conceptually linked to the ventral/dorsal pathways literature.

Overall, the paper demonstrates large gains over prior deep approaches and competitive results with trajectory-based methods on UCF-101 and HMDB-51, with a thorough ablation.

Strengths
- Clear, well-motivated architecture that cleanly separates appearance and motion; late fusion is simple and effective.
- Strong empirical evidence that stacked optical flow is a far better input to ConvNets than raw frame stacks for motion understanding; ablations are comprehensive (stack length, bi-directional flow, trajectory stacking, mean subtraction).
- Practical and effective multi-task learning setup increases training signal for the temporal stream and yields consistent gains on both datasets.
- Solid engineering: multi-GPU training, careful data augmentation, explicit description of sampling/testing protocols, and reproducible training schedules.
- Competitive to state-of-the-art shallow pipelines and substantially better than previous deep models, with well-reasoned analysis of why.

Weaknesses and questions
- Dependence on precomputed optical flow limits end-to-end learning and adds substantial compute and storage overhead; the paper acknowledges this but does not quantify the trade-offs (e.g., throughput, wall-clock for training/testing).
- Fusion is restricted to late score fusion; attempts at learned joint fusion over fully connected layers overfit. More analysis of regularization strategies or mid-level fusion would strengthen the case.
- Camera motion handling is limited to mean flow subtraction. It would be useful to quantify how much this recovers relative to explicit camera motion compensation methods used in trajectory pipelines.
- The spatial stream’s ImageNet pretraining improves top-5 validation error (13.5% vs. 16% in related architectures). The manuscript attributes this to sampling crops from the full image rather than center; an ablation isolating augmentation and hyperparameter differences would clarify fairness and resolve concerns about why similar architectures outperform prior baselines (e.g., Caffe/Zeiler-Fergus setups).
- Limited exploration of frame sampling for the spatial stream (temporal spacing, uniform vs. random vs. motion-based keyframes) and of long-term temporal modeling beyond the 10-frame flow window.
- Evaluation is on trimmed clips; it remains unclear how the approach scales to untrimmed, long videos and temporal localization.

Quality
- Technical soundness: High. The design choices are sensible and justified; ablations support claims. The multi-task objective is straightforward and implemented correctly.
- Empirical support: Strong. Extensive experiments and ablations on two benchmarks; comparisons to relevant baselines and prior art are fair and informative.
- Limitations are acknowledged (flow computation, camera motion, overfitting in early fusion).

Clarity
- The paper is clearly written and well organized. Implementation details (architectures, training schedules, testing protocol) are sufficiently specific to enable reproduction. A few clarifications would help (see suggestions below).

Originality
- The two-stream decomposition is conceptually known in neuroscience and has been instantiated with shallow models (HMAX), but the specific temporal ConvNet with stacked optical flow, systematic evaluation of input encodings, and multi-task learning for video action recognition are novel and impactful in the deep learning context of the time.

Significance
- High. The approach substantially closes the gap between ConvNet-based and trajectory-based methods and provides a practical path forward for deep video recognition. The ideas are likely to be picked up and extended by the community (e.g., improved flow, better fusion, larger datasets).

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide an ablation isolating the spatial stream improvements relative to prior ImageNet-trained baselines: crop strategy (corner vs. center), learning rate schedule, color jitter, and dropout.
- Report compute/storage costs for the temporal stream: average flow runtime, precomputation time per dataset, storage per video with/without JPEG compression, end-to-end training/inference throughput.
- Evaluate alternative frame sampling for the spatial stream (e.g., random vs. uniform, motion-salient frames), and assess sensitivity to the number of test frames/crops.
- Compare different optical flow methods (e.g., TV-L1 vs. Brox) and flow parameter settings to understand robustness.
- Explore regularization to enable learned fusion (e.g., stronger dropout, batch norm, weight decay) or mid-level fusion (e.g., convolutional feature concatenation) to potentially surpass score-level fusion.
- Add per-class analysis and failure cases, especially where the temporal stream helps most or fails due to camera motion.
- Discuss applicability to untrimmed videos and/or temporal localization (even if out of scope experimentally).

Pro/con arguments for acceptance
Pros:
- Simple, well-motivated architecture with strong empirical gains.
- Thorough ablation demonstrating why and when optical-flow inputs help.
- Multi-task learning that effectively increases training data and improves performance.
- Clear writing and reproducible details.

Cons:
- Heavy reliance on precomputed optical flow; not end-to-end and computationally costly.
- Limited exploration of fusion strategies beyond late fusion due to overfitting.
- Modest analysis of camera motion and spatial-stream sampling choices.
- Results confined to trimmed datasets; generalization to untrimmed scenarios not demonstrated.

Overall assessment
A strong empirical paper that advances deep video action recognition with a conceptually clean and practically effective two-stream design. Despite the reliance on precomputed flow and some missed opportunities for deeper fusion and camera motion handling, the work is technically sound, clearly presented, and significant for the NIPS community. I recommend acceptance.
