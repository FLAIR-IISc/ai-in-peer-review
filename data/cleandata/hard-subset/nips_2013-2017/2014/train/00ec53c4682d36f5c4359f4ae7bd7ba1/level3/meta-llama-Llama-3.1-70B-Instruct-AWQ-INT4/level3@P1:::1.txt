The paper "Two-Stream Convolutional Networks for Action Recognition in Videos" proposes a novel deep learning architecture for action recognition in videos. The main idea is to use two separate convolutional neural networks (ConvNets), one for spatial information (appearance) and one for temporal information (motion), which are then combined to make predictions. The spatial network takes individual video frames as input, while the temporal network takes a stack of dense optical-flow features as input.

The authors evaluate their approach on two popular action recognition datasets, UCF-101 and HMDB-51, and achieve results comparable to state-of-the-art methods that rely on heavy hand-crafted features. The strength of the proposed model lies in its simplicity and its ability to obtain strong performance using a straightforward application of ConvNets. Additionally, the paper provides a novel and important demonstration that optical-flow inputs are useful for action recognition.

One of the weaknesses of the proposed model is that it only models short fixed-length temporal windows (L=10) and does not capture the whole-video sequence structure. This limitation could potentially be addressed by incorporating recurrent neural networks (RNNs) or other sequence modeling techniques into the architecture.

The quality of the experiments is good, but there is room for improvement. For example, the authors do not provide an error-mode analysis, which would help to understand the types of videos or actions that are causing errors. Additionally, it would be interesting to see how the proposed model performs on other action recognition datasets.

The paper is clearly written, and the model is well explained. The authors provide a thorough review of related work and provide sufficient details about their implementation and experimental setup.

The originality of the paper lies in its novel application of ConvNets to optical flow features. While ConvNets have been widely used for image classification tasks, their application to optical flow features is a new and interesting direction.

The significance of the paper is high, as it shows promising results for action recognition in videos using a deep learning approach. The proposed model could potentially have a significant impact on video/motion research and could be applied to a wide range of applications, such as human-computer interaction, surveillance, and robotics.

Arguments for acceptance:

* The paper proposes a novel and interesting deep learning architecture for action recognition in videos.
* The authors achieve state-of-the-art results on two popular action recognition datasets using a straightforward application of ConvNets.
* The paper provides a thorough review of related work and provides sufficient details about the implementation and experimental setup.
* The proposed model is simple and easy to understand, making it a good contribution to the field.

Arguments against acceptance:

* The paper only models short fixed-length temporal windows and does not capture the whole-video sequence structure.
* The authors do not provide an error-mode analysis, which would help to understand the types of videos or actions that are causing errors.
* The paper does not provide a comparison with other deep learning approaches, such as RNNs or long short-term memory (LSTM) networks.

Overall, I believe that the paper makes a significant contribution to the field of action recognition in videos and should be accepted for publication.
