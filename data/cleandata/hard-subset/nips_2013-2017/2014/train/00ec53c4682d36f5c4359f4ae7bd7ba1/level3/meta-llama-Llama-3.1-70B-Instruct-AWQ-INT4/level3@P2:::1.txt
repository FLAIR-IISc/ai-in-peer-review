This paper proposes a two-stream convolutional network architecture for action recognition in videos, which incorporates spatial and temporal networks. The main idea is to capture the complementary information on appearance from still frames and motion between frames. The authors demonstrate that a ConvNet trained on multi-frame dense optical flow is able to achieve very good performance in spite of limited training data. They also show that multi-task learning, applied to two different action classification datasets, can be used to increase the amount of training data and improve the performance on both.

The authors provide a thorough review of related work on action recognition using both shallow and deep architectures. They discuss the challenges of video recognition and the importance of capturing motion information. The proposed two-stream architecture is well-motivated, and the authors provide a clear explanation of the spatial and temporal ConvNets.

The paper is well-organized, and the authors provide a detailed description of the implementation details, including the ConvNet configuration, training procedure, and testing protocol. The evaluation is performed on two challenging datasets, UCF-101 and HMDB-51, and the authors report competitive results with the state of the art.

The strengths of the paper include the simple application of convolutional nets that obtains strong performance, the novel and important demonstration that optical-flow inputs are useful for action recognition, and the well-designed experiments. The authors also provide a clear and well-written explanation of the model and the results.

However, there are some weaknesses to the paper. The authors only model short fixed-length temporal windows (L=10) and do not capture whole-video sequence structure. Additionally, the experiments lack error-mode analysis (e.g., per-class failures or types of videos causing errors).

In terms of quality, the paper is technically sound, and the claims are well-supported by experimental results. The authors are careful and honest about evaluating both the strengths and weaknesses of the work. The paper is a complete piece of work, and the authors provide a thorough evaluation of the proposed architecture.

The paper is also clear and well-written, and the authors provide enough information for the expert reader to reproduce the results. The organization is logical, and the authors use proper citations to related work.

The originality of the paper lies in the novel application of convolutional nets to optical flow features. The authors provide a new perspective on action recognition, and the results are promising.

In terms of significance, the approach could significantly impact video/motion research. The authors demonstrate competitive results with the state of the art, and the proposed architecture is simple and efficient. The paper is likely to be of interest to researchers in the field of computer vision and machine learning.

Arguments for acceptance:

* The paper proposes a novel and effective architecture for action recognition in videos.
* The authors provide a thorough evaluation of the proposed architecture on two challenging datasets.
* The results are competitive with the state of the art, and the approach is simple and efficient.
* The paper is well-written, and the authors provide a clear explanation of the model and the results.

Arguments against acceptance:

* The authors only model short fixed-length temporal windows and do not capture whole-video sequence structure.
* The experiments lack error-mode analysis (e.g., per-class failures or types of videos causing errors).

Overall, I recommend accepting the paper. The strengths of the paper outweigh the weaknesses, and the authors provide a significant contribution to the field of computer vision and machine learning.
