- Proposes a two-stream CNN architecture decoupling spatial (RGB) and temporal (optical flow) streams for video action recognition.  
- Novel demonstration that dense optical flow can be effectively used as input to discriminative CNNs, outperforming stacked RGB-frame approaches.  
- Inspired by the two-stream hypothesis in neuroscience and generalizes hand-crafted HOF/MBH features via convolutional layers.  
- Achieves strong results (81.5% on UCF-101) and outperforms prior deep learning work by >20%.  
- Validated on UCF-101 and HMDB-51 with competitive performance versus state-of-the-art shallow methods.  
- Thorough experimental design with detailed ablation studies showing optical flow stacking (L=10) >> single-frame flow (L=1) and trajectory-based inputs.  
- Mean flow subtraction effectively reduces camera-motion artifacts.  
- Multi-task learning across UCF-101 and HMDB-51 improves HMDB-51 accuracy by 5.2% versus single-dataset training.  
- Practical implementation details (optical flow precomputation) reduce storage from 1.5TB to 27GB for UCF-101.  
- Two-stream fusion combines spatial and temporal streams to exceed individual stream performance.  
- Paper is very well written, organized, and uses clear figures to explain concepts and input configurations.  
- Core contribution is original and impactful, avoiding hand-crafted feature engineering while using a simple, data-driven pipeline.  
- Significance: becomes a foundational paradigm extended by later works (I3D, TSN) and is immediately applicable to real systems.  
- Weakness: lacks error-mode and per-class failure analysis to explain model failures and guide improvements.  
- Weakness: temporal modeling limited to fixed-length windows (L=10), restricting capture of long-range dynamics for some actions.  
- Weakness: minor clarity omissions—optical flow computation parameters and under-explained SVM fusion details.  
- Overall recommendation: strong accept—technically sound, clear, and highly significant with promising directions for future work on larger datasets.
