Summary and relation to prior work
This paper introduces a two-stream convolutional architecture for video action recognition that decomposes the problem into a spatial stream operating on individual RGB frames and a temporal stream operating on short stacks of dense optical flow. The design is motivated by both the two-stream hypothesis in human vision and by the empirical separation of appearance and motion cues in classical video pipelines. The temporal stream ingests optical-flow volumes constructed in several ways (stacking, trajectory-stacking, bi-directional flow, mean-flow subtraction), while the spatial stream benefits from ImageNet pretraining. The two streams are fused at the score level (averaging or linear SVM).

Relative to prior deep approaches that operate on stacks of raw RGB frames and aim to learn motion implicitly [14], the paper explicitly feeds motion through optical flow and shows large gains. Relative to hand-crafted pipelines based on dense trajectories and Fisher vectors [20, 26, 29], the temporal ConvNet subsumes many of their primitives within a learnable architecture, and the two-stream fusion achieves accuracy comparable to the best shallow methods of the time. The paper also explores multi-task learning across UCF-101 and HMDB-51 to mitigate small video datasets, further improving temporal-stream accuracy.

Strengths
- Clear, effective decomposition into spatial (appearance) and temporal (motion) streams; simple late fusion works well and highlights their complementarity.
- Strong empirical results: substantially better than previous ConvNet-based methods [14], and competitive with state of the art on UCF-101 and HMDB-51.
- Thorough exploration of temporal-stream inputs: number of flow frames L, mean-flow subtraction, trajectory vs. location stacking, and bi-directional flow; shows the clear benefit of optical-flow input and of longer motion contexts.
- Practical multi-task learning across datasets improves generalization without manual class curation.
- Careful and reproducible implementation details (training schedules, data augmentation, pretraining, multi-GPU training, and flow precomputation/compression).

Weaknesses and concerns
- Spatial stream advantage over closely related image ConvNet baselines is not fully explained. The paper notes better ILSVRC top-5 error (13.5% vs 16% reported for a similar network), attributing this to sampling inputs from the full image rather than center crops, but a controlled ablation would strengthen the claim. Given the use of Caffe [13] and CNN-M-like architectures, why the sizable gap?
- Reliance on a specific optical flow (Brox et al. [2]) is a strong design choice; sensitivity to the flow algorithm, parameters, and potential error modes (e.g., fast motion, motion blur) is not analyzed.
- Camera motion handling is limited to mean-flow subtraction; stronger compensation (e.g., global motion estimation/homography) may matter and could be evaluated.
- Fusion explores only late-score fusion; intermediate or parameter-sharing fusion variants are mentioned but not pursued due to overfitting—some lightweight alternatives (e.g., gated or calibration layers) could be attempted.
- Minor scope gap: the spatial stream samples a fixed number of frames uniformly at test time; different frame-sampling schemes (importance sampling, motion-adaptive sampling, denser sampling for short clips) are not evaluated.

Quality
- Technical soundness: High. The architecture and training are standard but correctly adapted; experiments are extensive and well-controlled within the chosen design space.
- Empirical support: Strong. Ablations on temporal input configurations, comparison to RGB-frame stacks [14], and multi-task learning analyses support the core claims.
- Limitations are acknowledged (dataset size, lack of trajectory-aware pooling, camera motion).
- A few additional ablations (flow algorithm sensitivity, spatial-stream training details) would improve rigor.

Clarity
- Very clearly written and well-organized. Architectural decisions, training/testing pipelines, and implementation details are easy to follow, and the evaluation protocol aligns with community standards.
- Sufficient detail is provided for reproduction, including data augmentation, optimization schedule, and flow preprocessing.

Originality
- The two-stream conceptual split is known in neuroscience and has proto-forms in shallow vision models, but its instantiation as two discriminatively trained ConvNets with optical-flow input is a novel and impactful contribution for deep video recognition.
- The multi-task application to combine video datasets is straightforward but effective.
- The work is best characterized as a well-executed and insightful re-formulation and integration of known components that yields a new, high-performing deep baseline.

Significance
- High. The results materially advance deep learning for video, narrowing the gap to the best hand-engineered pipelines and providing a simple, scalable recipe that others can build upon.
- The two-stream paradigm and optical-flow input for ConvNets are likely to influence subsequent research and practice.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide controlled ablations explaining why the spatial net exceeds similar architectures (e.g., isolate effects of crop strategy, data augmentation, learning rate schedule, and last-layer-only training vs. fine-tuning).
- Evaluate different spatial-stream frame-sampling schemes: uniform vs. motion-adaptive or entropy-based selection; more/less frames; impact of clip length; training-time sampling beyond 1 frame per video per iteration.
- Analyze sensitivity to the choice of optical flow (e.g., TV-L1, EpicFlow, FlowNet variants), and to flow quality/noise.
- Explore lightweight fusion beyond averaging/SVM (temperature scaling, per-class weighted fusion, shallow learned calibrators) to reduce overfitting while leveraging complementary cues.
- Consider explicit camera-motion compensation and/or trajectory-aware pooling in later layers.

Arguments for acceptance
- Strong empirical gains over prior ConvNet approaches and competitive with SOTA shallow methods.
- Thorough, careful experimentation demonstrating the value of optical flow input and two-stream fusion.
- Clear, practical contribution that is likely to shape follow-up work in video recognition.

Arguments against acceptance
- Limited novelty in components; strength lies in integration and empirical validation.
- Some missing analyses (spatial-stream advantage rationale; flow method sensitivity; camera motion).
- Fusion design space is only partially explored due to overfitting concerns.

Overall assessment
This is a strong empirical paper that convincingly demonstrates a simple and effective two-stream ConvNet for action recognition, with careful ablations and competitive state-of-the-art results on UCF-101 and HMDB-51. Despite some missing analyses and modest architectural novelty, the contribution is clear and impactful. I recommend acceptance.
