Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes a discriminatively trained, two-stream ConvNet architecture for video action recognition that explicitly separates spatial appearance (single RGB frames) and temporal motion (stacked dense optical flow). The spatial stream leverages ImageNet pre-training, while the temporal stream is trained from scratch on multi-frame optical flow volumes, with several input variants (optical-flow stacking, trajectory stacking, bi-directional flow, mean-flow subtraction). The authors also introduce a simple yet effective multi-task learning setup to jointly train on UCF-101 and HMDB-51, alleviating data scarcity and improving generalization.

Relative to prior deep approaches that feed stacks of raw frames into a single network, this paper shows that explicitly providing optical flow substantially improves action recognition. The approach connects to the two-stream hypothesis from neuroscience (ventral/dorsal) and to classical shallow pipelines (HOG/HOF/MBH, dense trajectories, Fisher vectors), effectively generalizing them within a learned ConvNet framework. Compared to earlier deep video architectures trained on Sports-1M, the proposed temporal stream notably closes the gap to trajectory-based state of the art and the fused two-stream model attains competitive results on UCF-101 and HMDB-51.

Strengths
- Clear, well-executed idea: decouple appearance and motion with two specialized ConvNets and fuse their predictions.
- Strong empirical evidence: substantial gains of temporal stream over raw-frame ConvNets; complementary improvements from late fusion; competitive with top shallow baselines.
- Thoughtful design and ablations: comparisons of flow stacking length, trajectory vs. grid stacking, (bi-)directional flow, and mean-flow subtraction; detailed training/testing protocols.
- Practical contribution: multi-task learning effectively increases training data and consistently helps, especially on HMDB-51.
- Solid connections to prior shallow features; the paper argues and demonstrates that ConvNets can subsume HOF/MBH-like computations.

Weaknesses
- The paper feels split across multiple contributions (two-stream design, optical-flow encoding choices, and multi-task learning) without a single unifying storyline; the narrative could be tightened to center on “explicit motion via optical flow in a two-stream ConvNet.”
- Limited discussion of computational costs and deployment: optical flow precomputation is a significant dependency (storage, time), undermining end-to-end training and practical throughput.
- Limited datasets and scale: results on UCF-101 and HMDB-51 are convincing but relatively small; Sports-1M experiments are discussed only as future work.
- Fusion is limited to late score fusion; joint learning/fusion of streams was attempted but overfit—more analysis of why it overfits (and potential remedies) would strengthen the paper.
- Handling of camera motion is reduced to mean-flow subtraction; comparison to more principled compensation (e.g., homography/egomotion) would be informative.

Quality
- Technically sound and carefully evaluated. Claims are supported by ablations and comparisons. Implementation details (architectures, training schedules, cropping, augmentation, GPU setup) are thorough and should support reproducibility.
- The reliance on precomputed flow is a trade-off: strong performance but not end-to-end. The paper acknowledges this and suggests future directions.

Clarity
- Well written and well organized; figures and step-by-step input definitions (flow stacking, trajectory stacking) are clear. The relation to prior handcrafted descriptors and to prior deep models is helpful.
- Suggestion: refocus the abstract/introduction to emphasize the centrality of the two-stream design with explicit optical flow, then present multi-task learning as a practical enhancer rather than a primary contribution.

Originality
- The two-stream ConvNet with explicit optical flow is a novel and well-motivated architectural decision that differs from prior deep video work that relied on learning motion implicitly from RGB stacks.
- Multi-task learning is not novel per se, but its application to video action recognition and its demonstrated impact here are valuable.
- The paper appropriately references prior shallow and deep work; please ensure inclusion and discussion of:
  - CVPR 2011: “Learning Hierarchical Spatio-temporal Features for Action Recognition with Independent Subspace Analysis.”
  - BMVC 2010: “Evaluation of local spatio-temporal features for action recognition.”
  If already cited, strengthen the related-work discussion to clarify distinctions.

Significance
- Advances the state of practice in deep video recognition, showing that explicit motion input is key and competitive with strong hand-crafted pipelines.
- Likely to influence subsequent architectures (indeed, separating appearance and motion has become a lasting design theme).
- The multi-task strategy is practically useful for the community given current dataset constraints.

Suggestions for improvement
- Emphasize the main take-home message in the abstract/intro: explicit optical flow in a two-stream ConvNet is the core driver of gains; multi-task learning is an effective regularizer.
- Provide computational profiles: flow runtime, storage (pre/post compression), training/inference throughput for each stream and combined.
- Analyze failure cases, sensitivity to flow quality, and the effect of different flow algorithms.
- Explore more robust camera-motion compensation and/or spatiotemporal pooling guided by trajectories, as noted in the conclusion.
- Discuss strategies to mitigate overfitting in joint fusion (e.g., shared mid-level features, regularization, dropout schedules, data augmentation).
- Consider experiments on a larger dataset or at least partial Sports-1M experiments to support scalability claims.

Pros (for acceptance)
- Conceptually simple, well-motivated architecture with strong empirical gains.
- Clear evidence that explicit motion (optical flow) is crucial for deep video recognition.
- Complementary fusion yields competitive results to top shallow methods.
- Thorough experimental protocol and useful practical insights (e.g., mean-flow subtraction, flow stacking length).
- Multi-task learning demonstrably improves performance on small datasets.

Cons (against acceptance)
- Multiple contributions dilute the central narrative; fusion beyond late score averaging overfits without deeper analysis.
- Dependence on precomputed flow limits end-to-end learning and raises compute/storage barriers.
- Evaluation limited to two benchmarks; scalability claims remain prospective.
- Camera motion handling and deployment considerations could be stronger.

Recommendation
Accept (conditional). This is a timely, solid contribution that will interest the NIPS community and likely have lasting impact. Conditional on: clarifying the main narrative (two-stream with optical flow as the core contribution), strengthening related-work positioning (including the suggested citations and clearer distinctions), and adding a brief discussion of computational trade-offs and why joint fusion overfits.
