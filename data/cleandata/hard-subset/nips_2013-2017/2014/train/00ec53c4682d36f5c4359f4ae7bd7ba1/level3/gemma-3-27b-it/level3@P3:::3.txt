This paper proposes a novel two-stream convolutional network architecture for video action recognition, leveraging both spatial and temporal information. The authors demonstrate strong performance on UCF-101 and HMDB-51, exceeding previous deep learning approaches and achieving competitive results with state-of-the-art hand-crafted features. The methodology, implementation details, and experimental setup are described thoroughly, making the paper readily reproducible. Overall, this is a strong contribution with clear writing and compelling results.

The core idea – decoupling spatial and temporal processing into separate streams – is a significant advancement. The use of dense optical flow in the temporal stream is particularly noteworthy, as it demonstrably improves performance and provides a data-driven approach to capturing motion cues. The inclusion of multi-task learning to address limited training data is a sensible and effective addition. The paper builds effectively on prior work in both still image classification (leveraging pre-trained spatial networks) and video action recognition, acknowledging and addressing limitations of previous deep learning attempts (like [14]).

However, the paper feels somewhat disjointed. While all three contributions (two-stream architecture, optical flow networks, multi-task learning) are valuable, they aren't woven together into a single, cohesive narrative. The abstract and introduction could be significantly strengthened by emphasizing the significance of the two-stream/optical-flow component as the central innovation, rather than presenting all three as equally important. The connection to the “two-streams hypothesis” [9] is mentioned but not explored, potentially diminishing its impact.

Regarding related work, the authors should include citations to “Learning Hierarchical Spatio-temporal Features for Action Recognition with Independent Subspace Analysis” (CVPR 2011) by Le et al., which is relevant to the discussion of spatio-temporal feature learning, and "Evaluation of local spatio-temporal features for action recognition" (BMVC 2010) by Wang et al., given its focus on evaluating local features which forms the basis for the methods they are surpassing.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The two-stream architecture and the effective use of optical flow within a deep learning framework are novel and promising.
*   **Performance:** The reported results are impressive, demonstrating substantial improvements over [14] and competitiveness with state-of-the-art methods.
*   **Clarity & Completeness:** The paper is exceptionally well-written, clearly explaining the methodology, implementation details, and experimental results.
*   **Reproducibility:**  The thorough description of the approach, including network architecture and training procedures, greatly enhances reproducibility.
*   **Complementary Contribution:** The methods complement existing deep learning frameworks and provide a viable alternative to purely frame-based approaches.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Lack of Cohesion:** The paper feels fragmented across multiple contributions rather than a unified theme.
*   **Abstract/Introduction Focus:** The abstract and introduction do not adequately emphasize the primary contribution of the two-stream/optical-flow approach.
*   **Missing Citations:** Omission of relevant works (Le et al., Wang et al.) hinders a complete contextualization.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Strong technical contribution with novel methods.
*   Excellent experimental results demonstrating significant improvements.
*   Clear, well-written, and reproducible research.
*   Addresses a challenging problem in a promising manner.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The paper lacks a strong central narrative, appearing as a collection of contributions.
*   The abstract and introduction could be more focused.
*   Missing citations limit the completeness of the related work discussion.

**Overall Recommendation:**

Accept at NIPS 2014, conditional on addressing the suggested improvements. Specifically, the authors should: 1) Refocus the abstract and introduction to emphasize the significance of the two-stream/optical-flow contribution. 2) Include citations to Le et al. (CVPR 2011) and Wang et al. (BMVC 2010). Addressing these points will strengthen the paper's narrative and ensure a more complete contextualization of the work.
