This paper proposes a two-stream ConvNet architecture for video action recognition: a spatial network operating on single RGB frames (appearance) and a temporal network operating on stacks of dense optical flow fields (motion). The streams are trained separately and fused by averaging (or an SVM on softmax scores). The temporal net uses L=10 flow frames with mean-flow subtraction; variants such as trajectory stacking and bi-directional flow are analyzed. The authors also employ multi-task learning across UCF-101 and HMDB-51 to mitigate data scarcity. On both datasets, the approach is competitive with state-of-the-art hand-crafted pipelines (e.g., improved dense trajectories with HOF/MBH and Fisher vectors) and clearly outperforms prior deep models trained on stacked RGB frames (e.g., Karpathy et al.) and shallow HMAX-style systems. The central conceptual contribution is a clear, empirical demonstration that explicitly feeding optical flow to ConvNets is highly effective for action recognition.

Quality: The experimental design is careful, with sensible ablations (flow length, stacking variants, mean subtraction, fusion, multi-task). Results substantiate the claims. However, the work models only short, fixed windows (L=10) and does not capture sequence-level structure. There is little error-mode analysis (per-class confusions, typical failure modes, sensitivity to camera motion or scene type). The method is not fully end-to-end as optical flow is precomputed; runtime/storage tradeoffs vs. hand-crafted baselines are not fully discussed, nor is sensitivity to the choice/quality of flow.

Clarity: The paper is clearly written, well organized, and sufficiently detailed to enable reproduction (architectural and training details, sampling, augmentation). Adding confusion matrices and more diagnostic analysis would further inform readers.

Originality: While two-stream ideas have precedent in vision neuroscience and shallow models, the application of ConvNets to stacked optical flow and the demonstration of its efficacy are novel and timely relative to prior deep video work on stacked RGB or 3D convolutions.

Significance: The approach is simple, strong, and likely to shape subsequent video recognition research. It offers a practical recipe that narrows the gap with hand-crafted SOTA and suggests fruitful hybrids.

Suggestions: Provide per-class/attribute error analysis; evaluate longer temporal contexts (temporal pyramids, attention, or sequence models); compare different flow algorithms; report computational cost; explore end-to-end learnable motion (e.g., differentiable flow).

Arguments for acceptance:
- Strong empirical results with a simple, interpretable design
- Clear evidence that optical-flow inputs to ConvNets are highly effective
- Solid ablations and multi-task learning benefits
- Likely high impact on video recognition practice

Arguments against acceptance:
- Limited temporal modeling beyond short windows
- Missing error-mode and computational analyses
- Dependence on precomputed flow and storage overhead
