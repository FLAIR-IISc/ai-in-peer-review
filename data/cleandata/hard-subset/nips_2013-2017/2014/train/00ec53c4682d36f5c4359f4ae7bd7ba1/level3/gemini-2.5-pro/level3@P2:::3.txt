**Summary of the Paper**

This paper introduces a novel deep learning architecture for action recognition in videos, termed the "two-stream" convolutional network (ConvNet). The core idea is to explicitly model the complementary nature of appearance and motion information by dedicating a separate ConvNet to each modality. The "spatial stream" operates on individual video frames to learn features from static appearance, leveraging a standard image classification architecture pre-trained on ImageNet. The more novel "temporal stream" is designed to capture motion dynamics by taking a stack of dense optical flow fields as input. The outputs of these two streams are combined via late fusion (averaging or an SVM) to produce the final classification. This approach is contrasted with previous work, such as Karpathy et al. [14], which attempted to learn spatio-temporal features directly from raw stacked frames with limited success. The authors demonstrate that training a ConvNet on optical flow is a highly effective way to learn motion representations. Additionally, the paper proposes using multi-task learning to combine training data from different video datasets (UCF-101 and HMDB-51), mitigating overfitting on these relatively small datasets, particularly for the temporal stream which cannot be pre-trained on large image corpora.

**Strengths and Weaknesses**

**Quality:**
The paper is of high technical quality. The proposed methodology is described in extensive detail, covering the network architecture, the novel optical flow input configurations, and the training/testing procedures. This level of detail provides a clear path for reproducibility. The claims are strongly supported by a comprehensive set of experiments on the standard UCF-101 and HMDB-51 benchmarks. The authors include valuable ablation studies that systematically evaluate the contributions of key components: the number of stacked optical flow frames, mean flow subtraction, pre-training strategies for the spatial stream, and the benefits of multi-task learning. The work is presented as a complete and well-executed piece of research.

**Clarity:**
The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The motivation for a two-stream approach is clearly articulated, and the architecture itself is intuitive and well-illustrated. The experimental results are presented in clear tables and are easy to interpret. One minor point of critique is that the paper presents several distinct contributions—the two-stream framework, the optical flow ConvNet, and the multi-task learning formulation. While all are valuable, the main narrative could be sharpened to more strongly emphasize the two-stream architecture and the significance of using optical flow as the primary contribution, with multi-task learning framed as a supporting technique to improve its training. For example, the abstract and introduction could be refocused to better highlight this central theme.

**Originality:**
The work is highly original. While the concept of separate pathways for appearance and motion has precedents in neuroscience and earlier computer vision models ([16]), its application within a modern, high-performing deep learning framework is novel and impactful. The central original idea is to train a ConvNet directly on dense optical flow fields. This is a clever and effective method for injecting strong prior knowledge about motion into a data-driven learning system, and it provides a clear solution to the difficulties previous deep models [14] faced in learning motion from raw pixels. The combination of established techniques (ConvNets, optical flow, multi-task learning) is done in a novel way that yields a system significantly greater than the sum of its parts.

**Significance:**
The results presented are very significant and have had a substantial impact on the field. The two-stream network dramatically improved upon previous deep learning methods for action recognition and achieved performance competitive with the state-of-the-art hand-crafted methods of its time (e.g., improved dense trajectories [26]). This work was instrumental in re-establishing ConvNets as a leading paradigm for video understanding. The core insight—that explicitly feeding pre-computed motion information into a deep network is highly effective—is a crucial finding that many subsequent works are likely to build upon. The simplicity and effectiveness of the two-stream idea make it a powerful and accessible baseline for researchers in this area.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
1.  Presents a novel and conceptually elegant two-stream architecture that effectively decomposes video recognition into appearance and motion streams.
2.  Introduces a highly effective method for learning motion representations by training a ConvNet on optical flow, a key insight that addresses shortcomings of prior deep models.
3.  Achieves results that are competitive with the state-of-the-art and significantly advance the performance of deep learning methods on standard action recognition benchmarks.
4.  The paper is technically sound, clearly written, and supported by thorough and convincing experiments.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
1.  The approach relies on a pre-computation step for optical flow, making it not fully end-to-end from raw pixels to labels. However, the authors are transparent about this, and the exceptional performance justifies the design choice.
2.  The paper's focus feels somewhat split across its multiple contributions. A slightly tighter narrative focusing on the two-stream/optical-flow aspect would strengthen it further.

**Detailed Comments:**
The authors correctly cite prior work such as Wang et al. (BMVC 2010) [28] and Le et al. (CVPR 2011) [18]. To further strengthen the paper, the discussion in Sections 1.1 and 3.2 could more directly contrast the proposed learned temporal features with the specific hand-crafted (MBH, HOF) or unsupervised (ISA) features from these works. This would help to better situate the paper's contribution and more explicitly highlight the advantages of the discriminatively learned representation.

**Recommendation:**
This is an excellent paper presenting a novel, effective, and significant contribution to action recognition. It is well-written, thoroughly evaluated, and the results are compelling. I strongly recommend acceptance at NIPS 2014. The minor suggestions for improving the narrative focus are intended to further strengthen an already outstanding submission.
