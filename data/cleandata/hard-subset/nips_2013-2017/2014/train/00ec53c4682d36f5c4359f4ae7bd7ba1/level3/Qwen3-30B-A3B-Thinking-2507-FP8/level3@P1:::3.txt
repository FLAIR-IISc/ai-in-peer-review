

This paper introduces a two-stream Convolutional Network architecture for action recognition in videos, addressing the challenge of effectively capturing both spatial appearance information from individual frames and temporal motion information from optical flow. The authors make three key contributions: a two-stream architecture combining spatial and temporal recognition streams, a demonstration that ConvNets trained on dense optical flow achieve strong performance despite limited training data, and a multi-task learning framework to combine datasets and improve performance on both. The work builds upon the two-stream hypothesis from neuroscience [9], but implements it with deep learning rather than hand-crafted features.

The paper is technically sound with thorough experimental evaluation on UCF-101 and HMDB-51 datasets. The authors demonstrate that their temporal stream (using optical flow as input) significantly outperforms previous deep learning approaches [14] by 20% absolute accuracy, and the two-stream fusion achieves competitive results with state-of-the-art hand-crafted methods [20, 21, 26]. The multi-task learning approach for combining datasets is particularly valuable for addressing the small dataset problem in video action recognition, with the HMDB-51 results improving from 42.1% to 45.3% using the multi-task framework.

The methodology is well-described with sufficient detail for reproducibility. The implementation of the temporal stream using optical flow stacking (rather than raw video frames) is clever and effectively leverages prior work on optical flow computation [2]. The paper's clarity is excellent - it's well-organized with clear sections, good figures, and accessible writing that effectively communicates the technical contributions.

The originality is significant. While previous deep learning approaches [14, 16] used stacked video frames as input, this paper's two-stream architecture with optical flow as temporal input represents a novel approach that better captures motion information. The authors successfully demonstrate that their temporal stream generalizes hand-crafted features like HOF and MBH through their convolutional network architecture, as evidenced by visualizations of the learned filters.

However, the paper could better emphasize the key insight of using optical flow for the temporal stream as the central contribution rather than presenting it as one of multiple contributions. The abstract and introduction would benefit from refocusing to highlight this core innovation. The paper also misses important citations to related work, specifically "Learning Hierarchical Spatio-temporal Features for Action Recognition with Independent Subspace Analysis" (CVPR 2011) and "Evaluation of local spatio-temporal features for action recognition" (BMVC 2010), which would better contextualize their work within the broader literature.

The significance of this work is high. Video action recognition has important applications in surveillance, human-computer interaction, and video search. The authors demonstrate that their deep learning approach achieves competitive results with state-of-the-art shallow methods while using a more principled data-driven framework. The multi-task learning approach also opens up possibilities for leveraging larger datasets, which is particularly valuable given the scarcity of labeled video data.

The paper is well-structured but feels somewhat split across multiple contributions rather than having a single cohesive theme. The authors could better integrate the multi-task learning contribution as a natural extension of the two-stream framework rather than presenting it as a separate contribution.

Overall, this is a strong contribution to the field of video action recognition. The two-stream architecture is elegant, the experiments are thorough, and the results are compelling. The paper makes a convincing case for the effectiveness of deep learning with appropriate architectural choices for video classification. I recommend acceptance at NIPS 2014 with minor revisions: refocus the abstract and introduction to emphasize the two-stream/optical-flow significance, add the missing citations to CVPR 2011 and BMVC 2010, and clarify how the work differs from the HMAX two-stream architecture [16]. The paper's technical soundness, clarity, and significant results outweigh its minor organizational issues, making it a valuable contribution to the field.
