- Proposes a two-stream ConvNet for action recognition: a spatial stream (ImageNet-pretrained) and a temporal stream operating on stacked dense optical flow, fused at the end.  
- Introduces temporal-stream variants (flow vs. trajectory stacking, uni-/bi-directional, mean-flow subtraction, number of stacked frames) and multi-task learning to jointly train on UCF-101 and HMDB-51.  
- Positions the work relative to prior deep raw-frame models and hand-crafted trajectory-based methods, arguing explicit flow enables ConvNets to subsume classic flow-derived features.  
- Clear, well-written, and detailed methodology with reproducible training/testing procedures and implementation details.  
- Novel contributions: discriminatively trained deep two-stream formulation, effective optical-flow-based temporal ConvNet, and multi-task learning to leverage multiple datasets.  
- Strong empirical results and careful ablations showing stacking 10 flows, mean-flow subtraction, and late fusion improve performance; temporal stream outperforms spatial, and fusion gives additional gains.  
- Key insight: explicitly modeling motion with optical flow substantially improves over learning motion implicitly from raw frames at current dataset scales.  
- Paper feels split across multiple contributions, diluting a single cohesive narrative.  
- Limited analysis of sensitivity to choice of optical flow algorithm and of computational/storage/training tradeoffs for flow precomputation.  
- Fusion design limited to late fusion due to overfitting; lacks analysis of why joint fusion failed or exploration of lighter joint fusion strategies.  
- Minor related-work gaps and small attribution issues in the introduction.  
- Technically sound and robust experimental protocol; claims are well supported by experiments.  
- Very clear organization and figures; suggest refocusing abstract/introduction to center the two-stream/optical-flow contribution.  
- High originality for applying deep, discriminatively trained two-stream ConvNets with explicit flow input; MTL is a sensible practical addition.  
- High significance: advances deep video recognition and offers a practical, influential recipe competitive with hand-crafted pipelines.  
- Suggest reframing narrative to emphasize two-stream/optical-flow as primary contribution, presenting MTL as complementary.  
- Recommend reporting accuracy vs. compute/storage for different flow algorithms and a runtime/storage breakdown, plus comparison to improved trajectories.  
- Advise elaborating on failed joint fusion attempts, trying lighter heads/regularization or score calibration, and exploring cross-stream interactions.  
- Suggest stronger camera-motion compensation (beyond mean-flow subtraction) and per-clip normalization strategies.  
- Recommend assessing trajectory-aware pooling inside the ConvNet given success of trajectory pooling in prior work.  
- Encourage releasing scripts for flow precomputation, compression parameters, and trained models to improve reproducibility.  
- Missing suggested citations: Le et al. (CVPR 2011) and Wang et al. (BMVC 2010).  
- Pros for acceptance: novel, influential two-stream design with strong empirical gains, thorough ablations, and reproducibility.  
- Cons against acceptance: diffuse presentation across contributions, limited computational/flow-analysis, and absence of successful joint fusion.  
- Overall recommendation: Accept with minor revisions to refocus narrative, add related-work citations, and include discussion or supplemental results on flow choice/compute tradeoffs and fusion rationale.
