This paper proposes a novel two-stream ConvNet architecture for action recognition in videos, which combines a spatial network operating on individual video frames and a temporal network operating on short dense optical flow sequences. The authors demonstrate that this approach significantly outperforms other ConvNet-based approaches and matches state-of-the-art performance on two standard video datasets, UCF-101 and HMDB-51.

The main strength of the paper is its thorough evaluation of several ways to train ConvNets on optical flow, which shows clear benefits of using optical flow as input. The authors also investigate the effect of different input configurations, such as stacking multiple optical flows, trajectory stacking, and mean displacement subtraction. The results demonstrate that the proposed two-stream architecture is able to capture complementary information from spatial and temporal cues, leading to improved performance.

The paper is well-written and easy to follow, with a clear explanation of the proposed architecture and the experimental setup. The authors also provide a thorough review of related work, which helps to situate their contribution in the context of existing research.

One minor concern is that it is unclear why the spatial network beats models from [13] given similar architectures. The authors do not provide a detailed analysis of the differences between their spatial network and the models from [13], which makes it difficult to understand the reasons behind this improvement.

Another potential area of improvement is the evaluation of different frame-sampling schemes for the spatial stream. The authors use a fixed frame-sampling scheme, where 25 frames are sampled with equal temporal spacing between them. However, it is possible that other frame-sampling schemes, such as sampling frames at a higher or lower rate, could lead to improved performance.

Overall, this is a strong empirical paper that is of interest to the video-classification community. The proposed two-stream architecture is a significant contribution to the field, and the thorough evaluation of the approach provides valuable insights into the benefits and limitations of using optical flow as input for action recognition.
