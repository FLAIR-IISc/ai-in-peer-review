**Summary**

This paper introduces a novel deep learning architecture for video-based action recognition. The central contribution is a "two-stream" framework that processes spatial and temporal information separately before fusing their predictions. The spatial stream consists of a standard Convolutional Network (ConvNet) trained on individual video frames to capture appearance cues. The temporal stream is a ConvNet trained on stacks of dense optical flow fields computed between consecutive frames, explicitly modeling motion. The paper demonstrates that this temporal stream, despite being trained on relatively small datasets (UCF-101, HMDB-51), achieves excellent performance. The authors also propose a multi-task learning framework to effectively combine datasets and combat overfitting. This work builds upon the success of ConvNets in static image recognition [15] but provides a more effective way to incorporate motion than prior deep learning approaches [14], which struggled to learn motion features from raw pixel values. The proposed method achieves results that are highly competitive with state-of-the-art hand-crafted feature methods [26].

**Strengths (Pros)**

*   **Novel and Effective Architecture:** The two-stream design is a simple yet powerful idea. Decoupling appearance and motion processing allows each stream to be optimized for its specific task. In particular, the concept of training a ConvNet directly on pre-computed optical flow is a significant and novel contribution. This approach provides the network with a strong, explicit motion signal, which the experiments show leads to a substantial performance improvement over networks trained on raw frames.
*   **Strong Empirical Results:** The paper presents a thorough experimental evaluation. The results convincingly demonstrate the value of each component. The two streams are shown to be highly complementary, with their fusion yielding a significant boost in accuracy. The final architecture dramatically outperforms previous deep learning attempts for video classification [14] by a large margin and achieves performance competitive with the best contemporary hand-crafted feature-based systems [20, 26].
*   **Clarity and Reproducibility:** The paper is very well-written, clear, and easy to follow. The authors provide substantial detail on their methodology, implementation, and training procedures (e.g., pre-training, data augmentation, optical flow compression, multi-GPU setup). This level of detail is commendable and provides a clear path for others in the community to reproduce and build upon this work.
*   **Practical Contributions:** The multi-task learning approach for combining datasets is a useful and principled method for addressing the common problem of limited annotated video data, which is a key bottleneck for training deep temporal models.

**Weaknesses (Cons)**

*   **Lack of a Single Cohesive Theme:** While the paper presents several strong contributions (the two-stream architecture, the optical flow ConvNet, and multi-task learning), it can feel somewhat split. The narrative could be strengthened by focusing more sharply on the most impactful finding: the success of the two-stream architecture driven by the optical flow input. The multi-task learning component, while useful, feels slightly secondary to this main theme.
*   **Missing Citations:** The paper overlooks some relevant prior work in spatio-temporal feature learning. The authors should acknowledge and discuss the following papers to better position their contribution within the existing literature:
    *   Le et al., "Learning Hierarchical Spatio-temporal Features for Action Recognition with Independent Subspace Analysis" (CVPR 2011). This work is another relevant example of unsupervised deep learning for spatio-temporal features.
    *   Wang et al., "Evaluation of local spatio-temporal features for action recognition" (BMVC 2010). This paper provides an important baseline and context for the hand-crafted feature literature discussed in the related work section.

**Detailed Comments and Suggestions**

The paper's primary strength is the demonstration that explicitly providing motion information via optical flow allows a ConvNet to learn powerful temporal representations. This is a crucial insight. I would recommend that the authors revise the abstract and introduction to emphasize this point more strongly. Currently, the contributions are listed somewhat equally, but the significance of the two-stream/optical-flow approach arguably outweighs the others and should be the central focus of the paper's narrative.

The experimental section is thorough, but the comparison between "optical flow stacking" and "trajectory stacking" in Table 1b is particularly interesting. The fact that the simpler stacking method works as well as, or better than, the trajectory-inspired one is a noteworthy result that could be briefly commented on in the main text.

**Qualitative Evaluation**

*   **Quality:** The paper is technically sound. Claims are well-supported by extensive experiments on standard benchmarks. The work is complete and the authors are honest about potential areas for improvement (e.g., camera motion handling, spatio-temporal pooling).
*   **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally clear and well-organized. The methodology is explained in sufficient detail for reproducibility.
*   **Originality:** The two-stream architecture, and particularly the use of a ConvNet trained on dense optical flow as the temporal stream, is a novel and original approach that effectively bridges the gap between hand-crafted motion features and deep learning.
*   **Significance:** The results are highly significant. They demonstrate a clear path forward for deep learning in video analysis and have had a major influence on subsequent research. The paper advances the state-of-the-art for deep learning-based action recognition substantially and provides ideas that are broadly applicable.

**Recommendation:**

I strongly recommend that this paper be **accepted** at NIPS 2014. It is a high-quality paper with a novel and effective method, backed by strong empirical results. My recommendation is conditional on the authors addressing the minor weaknesses noted above, specifically by sharpening the focus of the introduction and including the suggested citations to relevant prior work.
