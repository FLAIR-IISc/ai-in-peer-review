

# Review of "Two-Stream Convolutional Networks for Action Recognition in Videos"

This paper presents a novel two-stream Convolutional Network architecture for video action recognition, addressing the challenge of effectively capturing both appearance and motion information in videos. The authors propose a spatial stream that processes single frames (leveraging pre-training on ImageNet) and a temporal stream that processes dense optical flow features, with the two streams combined through late fusion. The work significantly advances the application of deep learning to video recognition by demonstrating that optical flow features, when fed into a ConvNet, can outperform previous deep learning approaches that used stacked RGB frames as input.

The paper relates well to previous work at NIPS and elsewhere. It builds upon the two-stream hypothesis from neuroscience [9] and extends the work of [14] which attempted to use stacked frames for deep video recognition but achieved inferior results to hand-crafted features. The authors make a strong connection between their temporal ConvNet and previous hand-crafted motion features (HOF, MBH), showing that their architecture generalizes these features through a learnable convolutional representation. This is a significant contribution that bridges the gap between traditional computer vision features and deep learning approaches.

The key strengths of this paper include the simplicity and effectiveness of the proposed architecture. The two-stream approach is elegantly simple yet achieves strong performance, demonstrating that optical flow features are indeed highly useful for action recognition when processed by deep networks. The authors convincingly show that their temporal stream outperforms both spatial stream alone and previous deep learning approaches. The paper is clearly written with well-explained model architecture and experiments, making it accessible to the NIPS community.

The paper also demonstrates a novel application of multi-task learning to combine training data across UCF-101 and HMDB-51 datasets, which significantly improves performance on the smaller HMDB-51 dataset. This is an important contribution to the field of video recognition where labeled data is often scarce.

However, the paper has some notable limitations. The most significant weakness is that the model only processes short fixed-length temporal windows (L=10), which prevents it from capturing the full structure of longer videos. This limitation is particularly relevant for action recognition where some actions span many frames. Additionally, the evaluation lacks error-mode analysis—there is no exploration of per-class failures or specific video types that cause errors, which would provide valuable insights into the model's limitations.

The quality of the experiments is good overall, with well-designed comparisons to state-of-the-art methods, but the absence of error analysis limits the depth of the evaluation. The paper is technically sound with proper ablation studies on different input configurations for the temporal stream.

The significance of this work is substantial. It demonstrates that deep learning can effectively process optical flow features for video recognition, which was previously thought to be difficult for ConvNets. The approach has clear potential to impact video/motion research and has been highly influential in subsequent work (as evidenced by the paper's high citation count). The authors' claim that their architecture "exceeds by a large margin previous attempts to use deep nets for video classification" is well-supported by the results.

In terms of originality, the paper presents a novel application of convolutional networks to optical flow features, which generalizes previous hand-crafted representations. While the two-stream concept has been explored before (e.g., [16]), this work is the first to implement it with deep ConvNets and demonstrate its effectiveness for action recognition.

The paper is clearly written with a logical structure that effectively communicates the contributions. The implementation details are thorough, and the experimental results are well-presented.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Novel two-stream architecture that successfully combines appearance and motion for video recognition
- Strong empirical results showing superiority over previous deep learning approaches
- Clear demonstration that optical flow features are valuable for action recognition
- Well-designed experiments with appropriate comparisons
- Significant potential impact on video recognition research

Arguments against acceptance:
- Limited temporal modeling (fixed-length windows of L=10)
- Lack of error analysis to understand failure modes
- Does not address camera motion beyond simple mean subtraction

Overall, this paper makes a significant contribution to video action recognition by demonstrating the effectiveness of deep networks with optical flow features. The simplicity of the approach combined with strong results makes it a valuable addition to the NIPS literature. While the temporal window limitation is a valid concern, the paper's strong empirical results and clear contribution to the field outweigh this limitation. The paper is technically sound, well-written, and addresses an important problem in video understanding with a novel and effective solution. I recommend acceptance.
