

# Review for "Two-Stream Convolutional Networks for Action Recognition in Videos"

This paper proposes a two-stream Convolutional Network architecture for action recognition in videos, addressing a key challenge in video understanding: effectively capturing both appearance (spatial) and motion (temporal) information. The authors build upon the two-stream hypothesis from neuroscience (ventral/dorsal visual pathways) by implementing two separate ConvNets: one processing individual video frames for spatial information, and another processing short sequences of dense optical flow for temporal information. This work extends previous attempts at applying deep learning to video action recognition, notably addressing the limitations of [14] who found that stacked-frame ConvNets performed poorly compared to hand-crafted features.

The paper makes three key contributions: (1) a two-stream ConvNet architecture that decouples spatial and temporal processing, (2) demonstration that ConvNets trained on dense optical flow achieve strong performance despite limited training data, and (3) a multi-task learning framework that combines training data across UCF-101 and HMDB-51 datasets to improve performance. The authors thoroughly evaluate different input configurations for the temporal stream (optical flow stacking, trajectory stacking, bi-directional flow) and demonstrate that optical flow input significantly outperforms stacked raw frames. Their approach achieves competitive results with state-of-the-art shallow representations on standard benchmarks while being fully data-driven.

The paper demonstrates strong quality with technically sound methodology. The experiments are comprehensive, including ablation studies on different input configurations and fusion methods, and the results clearly support the claims. The authors carefully address limitations of previous work (notably [14]) and provide appropriate comparisons to the state of the art. The implementation details are sufficiently detailed for reproducibility, including specific network architectures, training procedures, and evaluation protocols.

The clarity of the paper is excellent. The two-stream architecture is well-motivated and clearly explained with helpful figures (Fig. 1-3). The paper logically progresses from the problem statement to the proposed solution, related work, implementation details, and thorough experimental evaluation. The writing is precise and accessible to the NIPS audience.

The originality of the work is strong. While previous work has explored two-stream architectures (e.g., [16] using HMAX), this paper presents the first deep learning implementation of the two-stream hypothesis with ConvNets. The use of dense optical flow as input to a ConvNet represents a novel approach that generalizes previous hand-crafted features (HOF, MBH) within a data-driven framework. The multi-task learning framework for combining datasets is also a useful contribution that could be applied to other video tasks.

The significance of the work is substantial. Video action recognition is an important problem with applications in surveillance, human-computer interaction, and video search. The authors demonstrate that their approach significantly outperforms previous deep learning attempts and achieves competitive performance with state-of-the-art shallow methods. This represents a meaningful advancement in the field, showing that deep learning can be successfully applied to video action recognition when properly designed. The paper also opens new directions for future work, such as applying the approach to larger datasets and incorporating additional features from state-of-the-art shallow representations.

One minor weakness is the unclear explanation of why the spatial stream alone (trained on ImageNet) outperforms similar architectures from previous work (e.g., [13]), despite using similar network configurations. The authors mention that their spatial stream achieves good performance (Table 1a), but don't fully explain why it's better than comparable approaches. Additionally, the paper could have evaluated different frame-sampling schemes for the spatial stream, which might further improve performance.

Overall, this is a strong empirical paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of video action recognition. The two-stream architecture is well-motivated, the experiments are thorough, and the results are impressive. The paper clearly demonstrates that careful design of the input to deep learning models can lead to significant performance gains in video understanding tasks.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Strong empirical results showing significant improvement over previous deep learning approaches
- Thorough experimental evaluation with ablation studies
- Clear explanation of the two-stream architecture and its benefits
- Competitive results with state-of-the-art methods
- Novel application of deep learning to video action recognition with a well-designed input representation

Arguments against acceptance:
- Minor explanation gap regarding why spatial stream outperforms similar architectures
- Limited exploration of frame-sampling strategies for the spatial stream

The paper is clearly within the scope of NIPS, as it makes a significant contribution to machine learning for video understanding, a topic of growing interest in the field. It represents a substantial technical contribution that advances the state of the art in video recognition while being well-grounded in the literature. I recommend acceptance.
