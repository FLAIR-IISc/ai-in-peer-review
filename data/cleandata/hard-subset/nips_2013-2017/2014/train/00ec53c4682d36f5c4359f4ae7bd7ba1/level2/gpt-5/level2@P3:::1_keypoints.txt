- Proposes a two-stream deep ConvNet: spatial stream on RGB (ImageNet pretrained) and temporal stream on stacked dense optical flow, fused by late score fusion.
- Introduces multi-task learning to jointly train on UCF-101 and HMDB-51 to reduce overfitting on limited video data.
- Motivated by prior failures of RGB-stack ConvNets and the need to explicitly encode motion; temporal stream encodes motion via stacked flow (L up to 10) and variants (trajectory stacking, bi-directional flow, mean-flow subtraction).
- Shows stacked optical flow substantially outperforms RGB-only networks and slow-fusion approaches.
- Demonstrates spatial and temporal streams are complementary; combined model is competitive with state-of-the-art hand-crafted pipelines.
- Multi-task learning improves performance, notably on HMDB-51.
- Clear architectural decomposition aligning with the problem: explicit motion input markedly improves recognition over RGB stacks.
- Thorough and well-controlled ablations on temporal input design (stack size, stacking method, bi-directional flow, mean subtraction).
- Careful training protocol with strong regularization, ImageNet pretraining for spatial stream, and multi-GPU implementation aiding reproducibility.
- Simple, principled multi-task formulation with separate heads and summed losses that outperforms ad hoc alternatives.
- Comprehensive evaluation on standard splits with comparisons to deep baselines and hand-crafted systems; linear SVM late fusion improves over averaging.
- Practical implementation details addressed: precomputing optical flow, storage compression, and training time considerations.
- Reliance on precomputed optical flow limits end-to-end learning and creates a deployment computational bottleneck without systematic speed/accuracy analysis.
- Camera-motion compensation is limited to mean-flow subtraction; stronger global motion methods are not evaluated.
- Feature-level fusion or joint cross-stream training is dismissed due to overfitting; stronger regularization or smaller fusion heads are not explored.
- Architecture does not exploit trajectory-aligned pooling, a key component of improved trajectories, acknowledged as future work.
- Comparisons focus on accuracy, lacking computational cost and model size/test-time fps comparisons important for practitioners.
- Generalization to larger-scale datasets (e.g., Sports-1M) is not evaluated; cross-dataset generalization beyond the two datasets is limited.
- Methodology is sound and claims are supported by extensive experiments and convincing analyses of temporal design choices.
- Paper is clearly written with sufficient implementation and training details for reproduction; relation to prior representations is well articulated.
- The two-stream + multi-task combination is novel and impactful relative to contemporaneous deep video models.
- Results significantly advance deep action recognition, closing the gap with hand-crafted methods and providing design principles likely to influence future work.
- Essential suggested improvements: provide deployment-oriented throughput/storage analysis and evaluate stronger camera-motion compensation.
- Nice-to-have suggestions: explore lightweight feature fusion with strong regularization, report per-class gains, test sensitivity to flow algorithms, add trajectory-aligned pooling, and report memory/parameter counts.
- Arguments for acceptance: strong empirical gains, clear intuitive architecture, solid engineering, and competitiveness with hand-crafted pipelines.
- Arguments against acceptance: heavy reliance on precomputed flow and limited exploration of advanced motion compensation and trajectory-centric pooling.
