## Review of "Two-Stream Convolutional Networks for Action Recognition in Videos"

This paper investigates the use of two-stream convolutional neural networks (ConvNets) for action recognition in videos, aiming to effectively capture both spatial (appearance) and temporal (motion) information. The core contribution lies in proposing this two-stream architecture, demonstrating the effectiveness of ConvNets trained on dense optical flow, and showing that multi-task learning across datasets can improve performance. The work is situated within the broader context of action recognition, contrasting with previous approaches using hand-crafted features and earlier attempts at deep learning for video classification.

The paper is technically sound and well-motivated. The two-stream approach is a logical extension of the “two-streams hypothesis” in visual processing and addresses the limitations of previous deep learning approaches that struggled to capture motion effectively. The experimental results on UCF-101 and HMDB-51 are compelling, demonstrating significant improvements over prior deep learning methods and achieving competitive performance with state-of-the-art hand-crafted features. The ablation studies, particularly those exploring different input configurations for the temporal stream and the benefits of multi-task learning, are well-conducted and insightful. The detailed implementation section, including discussion of training procedures and optical flow computation, enhances reproducibility.

A strength of this work is its clear articulation of the problem and its solution. The authors effectively build upon existing knowledge in both image and video processing, and the paper is well-written and organized. The acknowledgment of limitations, such as the relatively small dataset sizes and the potential for further improvement through more sophisticated motion modeling, is also commendable. 

However, the paper could benefit from a more thorough discussion of computational cost. While the implementation details are provided, the overhead of computing and processing optical flow is not fully addressed. Additionally, while the comparison to state-of-the-art is good, a more detailed analysis of the specific advantages of the proposed method over those alternatives would be valuable. 

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel and effective architecture for video action recognition.
*   Strong experimental results competitive with state-of-the-art.
*   Clear writing and thorough experimental evaluation.
*   Addresses a key limitation of previous deep learning approaches for video.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Limited discussion of computational cost.
*   Could benefit from a more in-depth comparison to existing methods.

**Overall:** This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of video action recognition. Despite minor weaknesses, the novelty, technical soundness, and compelling results warrant acceptance. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the points raised above.
