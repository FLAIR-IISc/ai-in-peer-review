Summary and relation to prior work:
This paper proposes a two-stream ConvNet architecture for video action recognition that explicitly decouples appearance (spatial stream operating on RGB frames) and motion (temporal stream operating on stacked dense optical flow). The spatial net is pre-trained on ImageNet and fine-tuned (or last layer trained) on UCF-101/HMDB-51; the temporal net is trained from scratch on multi-frame optical flow volumes with several design choices evaluated: flow stacking vs. trajectory stacking, uni- vs. bi-directional flow, and mean flow subtraction. The two streams are fused by late score fusion (averaging or linear SVM). To mitigate limited video training data, the authors introduce a simple multi-task learning setup with two separate softmax heads (UCF-101 and HMDB-51) sharing the backbone, which yields gains on both datasets. Results show large improvements over prior deep models trained on RGB frame stacks (e.g., Karpathy et al. 2014) and competitiveness with state-of-the-art hand-crafted pipelines based on dense trajectories and Fisher vectors (e.g., Wang & Schmid 2013/2014; Peng et al. 2014). The approach is conceptually related to the (hand-crafted, shallow) dual-path HMAX-style models (Jhuang et al. 2007/2011), but here both streams are discriminatively trained ConvNets. The temporal input design connects to classical descriptors (HOF/MBH, kinematic features), which the authors argue are subsumed by learned conv filters over the flow fields.

Strengths:
- Clear and compelling architectural idea that aligns with both computer vision practice (appearance vs. motion) and cognitive inspiration (two-stream hypothesis), implemented with modern ConvNets.
- Strong empirical gains over prior deep approaches to video (notably over stacked-RGB models), with ablations that convincingly support design choices: stacking multiple flows (L=10) helps; mean flow subtraction mitigates camera motion; optical flow stacking slightly outperforms trajectory stacking.
- Sensible use of pretraining for the spatial stream and a practically effective multi-task formulation that increases effective supervision for the temporal stream; demonstrated improvements on both HMDB-51 and UCF-101.
- Thorough training/testing details and a fair evaluation protocol on standard splits; competitive or better than deep baselines, and approaching hand-crafted SOTA.
- Good engineering considerations (multi-GPU training, compressed flow storage) that make the method feasible.

Weaknesses and concerns:
- Dependence on externally computed optical flow breaks end-to-end learning and introduces a heavy precomputation step and a significant storage/computation footprint. The paper shows little analysis of sensitivity to the chosen flow method/parameters or runtime comparisons vs. improved dense trajectories (IDT).
- The claim that the temporal ConvNet “generalises” hand-crafted motion descriptors is plausible but only qualitatively supported (first-layer visualizations). It would be stronger to quantify this (e.g., linear probes on conv1 features approximating HOF/MBH histograms, or showing that learned filters capture kinematic invariants).
- Fusion is limited to late score fusion; attempts at deeper joint fusion reportedly overfit, but no quantitative evidence or alternative regularized fusion (e.g., low-rank bilinear pooling, feature normalization/weight sharing, or shallow bottlenecks) are explored.
- Camera motion compensation is handled only by mean flow subtraction. Compared to IDT with explicit homography-based stabilization, this is simplistic; an ablation with more explicit camera motion compensation would clarify the performance gap.
- Limited analysis of temporal modeling granularity. The method aggregates short-term flow stacks with uniform frame sampling and averages scores over 25 sampled frames, but does not examine longer temporal contexts, variable frame rates, or robustness to temporal misalignment.
- Multi-task learning details are somewhat sparse (e.g., task sampling ratio, loss weighting, inter-dataset class/name overlap handling), and it is unclear how sensitive gains are to these choices.

Quality:
Overall technically sound with appropriate ablations and careful training/evaluation. The experimental evidence supports the main claims: (i) explicit motion via optical flow substantially improves over stacked RGB; (ii) spatial and temporal streams are complementary; (iii) multi-task learning provides regularization and data scaling benefits. The main methodological limitation is reliance on external flow and relatively shallow fusion.

Clarity:
The paper is clearly written, well-organized, and provides sufficient implementation details (architectures, training schedule, data augmentation, test-time protocol). Figures illustrating input configurations are helpful. Including exact numbers for all ablations and final results (across all splits) is good; adding runtime/throughput measurements would further improve clarity for practitioners.

Originality:
The two-stream ConvNet with an explicitly learned temporal stream on stacked dense flow is a notable and impactful contribution. While the notion of separate spatial/temporal pathways and the use of flow for motion are not new, their integration with modern ConvNets, the specific flow stacking designs, and the simple yet effective multi-task setup are original relative to prior deep video models circa 2014.

Significance:
High. The paper advances deep learning for video by showing a practical, high-performing alternative to hand-crafted pipelines and by clarifying that explicit motion input is key. The approach is likely to be adopted and extended (e.g., improved fusion, better motion compensation, end-to-end flow learning), and provides a strong baseline for future research.

Constructive suggestions:
- Analyze computational trade-offs: report training/inference time and memory, flow precomputation cost, and compare to IDT pipelines; explore lighter/slower flow methods to assess sensitivity.
- Strengthen the “generalizes hand-crafted features” claim with quantitative probes, and include per-class analyses and failure cases to elucidate where temporal vs. spatial streams help.
- Explore more regularized joint fusion (e.g., feature-level fusion with a small bottleneck, batch/layer normalization, or correlation/compact bilinear pooling) to capture inter-stream interactions without overfitting.
- Evaluate explicit camera stabilization (e.g., homography-based compensation) in the flow preprocessing, or use MBH-like gradient of flow as additional channels.
- Study robustness to temporal sampling: different L, strides, and non-uniform sampling; examine longer contexts via temporal pyramids or simple recurrent pooling.
- Assess the impact of different optical flow algorithms and parameters; consider learning a light-weight flow proxy network to reduce precomputation.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Strong empirical improvements over prior deep methods with a simple, well-motivated architecture.
- Clear evidence that explicit motion input is crucial; complementary gains from two streams.
- Practical multi-task learning strategy to leverage multiple datasets.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Heavy reliance on external optical flow and precomputation; limited analysis of computational cost and sensitivity.
- Fusion limited to late score combination; missed opportunity to model spatiotemporal interactions more richly.
- Some claims (feature generalization) are not quantitatively validated.

Overall recommendation: Accept (strong). This is a solid, influential contribution that will likely shape subsequent work in deep video recognition.

Reviewer confidence: High.
