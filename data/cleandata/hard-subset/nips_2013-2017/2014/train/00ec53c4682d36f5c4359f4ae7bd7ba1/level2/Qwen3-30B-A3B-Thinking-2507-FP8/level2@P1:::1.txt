

This paper introduces a two-stream Convolutional Network architecture for video action recognition, addressing the challenge of integrating spatial appearance and temporal motion information. The authors propose a spatial stream (trained on frames, leveraging ImageNet pretraining) and a temporal stream (trained on dense optical flow), with late fusion of their outputs. Key contributions include demonstrating that optical flow-based temporal streams significantly outperform stacked RGB approaches, enabling multi-task learning across UCF-101 and HMDB-51 to overcome small dataset limitations, and showing that the two streams are complementary, yielding state-of-the-art results on standard benchmarks.

The paper is well-motivated and builds effectively on prior work. It correctly situates its two-stream approach within the neuroscience-inspired "two-streams hypothesis" [9] and contrasts with prior deep learning attempts like [14] (which used stacked RGB inputs) and shallow hand-crafted methods [20, 26]. The temporal stream's design explicitly generalizes hand-crafted features (HOF, MBH, trajectory descriptors) into a learnable framework, as demonstrated by visualizing the first-layer filters (supplementary material). The multi-task learning approach for dataset combination is novel and well-executed, providing a practical solution to limited training data.

**Strengths:**  
1. **Technical Soundness & Quality:** Experiments are thorough, with clear ablation studies (Tables 1-2) demonstrating the benefits of optical flow stacking (L=10), mean flow subtraction, and multi-task learning. The results are compelling: the temporal stream alone outperforms [14] by 20%, and the two-stream fusion achieves 81.5% on UCF-101 (vs. 62.5% for [14]), rivaling state-of-the-art shallow methods [20, 26]. The implementation details (flow compression, multi-GPU training) show practical rigor.  
2. **Clarity:** The architecture is clearly described with helpful figures (Figs. 1-3) and precise input configuration definitions (Section 3.1). The paper is logically organized, with related work (Section 1.1) and comparisons to prior deep learning methods well-articulated.  
3. **Originality & Significance:** The two-stream deep network design is novel for video action recognition (though inspired by neuroscience). It fundamentally shifts the paradigm from end-to-end stacked-frame learning to explicit motion encoding, significantly advancing the field. The multi-task learning framework for video datasets is also impactful, enabling better use of limited labeled data. This work became foundational for subsequent video recognition research (e.g., I3D).

**Weaknesses (constructive):**  
1. The paper does not deeply analyze *why* optical flow input outperforms stacked RGB (beyond stating it "explicitly describes motion"). A brief discussion of how flow's structure aligns with ConvNet inductive biases (e.g., sparsity, directional sensitivity) would strengthen the contribution.  
2. While Table 4 shows strong results, the comparison to shallow methods [20, 26] lacks analysis of *why* the two-stream deep model is competitive despite smaller datasets. A short discussion on scalability (e.g., "Our model requires less data than hand-crafted features due to feature learning") would be valuable.  
3. The temporal stream's trajectory stacking (Section 3.1) is evaluated but not deeply justified; the results show it underperforms optical flow stacking, but the paper could more explicitly link this to the effectiveness of the spatial pooling in the ConvNet.

**Overall:** This is a high-quality, impactful contribution that significantly advances video action recognition. The two-stream architecture is technically sound, well-evaluated, and addresses a critical bottleneck (motion representation) in prior deep learning approaches. The multi-task learning innovation is particularly valuable for the field. The paper is clear, well-written, and makes a demonstrable step toward the state of the art. While minor gaps exist in the theoretical justification of flow superiority, the experimental evidence is robust and convincing. The work is highly relevant to NIPS' focus on deep learning for structured data and has clear significance for both practitioners and researchers. **Recommendation: Accept.**
