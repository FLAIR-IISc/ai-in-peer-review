This paper proposes a two-stream convolutional network architecture for action recognition in videos, separating spatial and temporal information processing into distinct streams. The key contributions are the two-stream architecture itself, demonstrating the efficacy of ConvNets on optical flow, and utilizing multi-task learning to enhance performance through increased training data. The work is well-motivated, addressing the challenge of effectively incorporating temporal information into deep learning models for action recognition.

The paper builds upon prior work in both hand-crafted feature representations (e.g., trajectory-based methods [26, 29]) and earlier attempts at deep learning for video classification [14]. The two-stream approach draws inspiration from the ventral/dorsal stream hypothesis in visual cortex [9], although this connection isn't deeply explored. The spatial stream leverages pre-training on ImageNet [15], a common and effective technique for improving generalization. The temporal stream utilizes dense optical flow, a well-established method for capturing motion. The multi-task learning component [5] is a sensible strategy for improving performance with limited video data.

A significant strength of the work is the clear and methodical experimental evaluation. The authors thoroughly investigate different configurations for both the spatial and temporal streams, including various input representations for the temporal stream (stacking, trajectories, bi-directional flow) and regularization techniques. The comparison to state-of-the-art methods [14, 20, 21, 26] on UCF-101 and HMDB-51 demonstrates competitive performance. The ablation studies provide valuable insights into the contributions of different components of the proposed architecture. The inclusion of detailed implementation information (network configuration, training procedure, optical flow computation) enhances reproducibility.

However, there are areas where the paper could be improved. While the connection to the ventral/dorsal stream hypothesis is mentioned, it's not further investigated. A more detailed discussion of the biological plausibility of the two-stream architecture, even if speculative, could strengthen the motivation.  The discussion of the limitations of the work could be expanded.  For example, the computational cost of optical flow computation is significant. Exploring methods for reducing this cost, such as sparse optical flow or learned optical flow estimation, could be beneficial. Furthermore, the paper could benefit from a more detailed analysis of the learned filters in the temporal stream – specifically, how they relate to known motion patterns or hand-crafted features (beyond the statement that they generalize those features).

Regarding clarity, the paper is generally well-written and organized. However, some sections (e.g., Sect. 3.1, describing input configurations) could be more concise. The equations, while correct, could benefit from more descriptive variable names. The flow of the argument in Section 6 could be smoother, potentially by integrating the results of different experiments more directly into the text rather than relying heavily on tables.

Originality is good. While the individual components (ConvNets, optical flow, multi-task learning) are not novel, the combination of these components in a two-stream architecture specifically designed for action recognition represents a significant contribution. The demonstration of strong performance with this architecture, exceeding previous deep learning approaches [14], further highlights the novelty of the work. The multi-task learning aspect also provides a valuable contribution.

Significance is high. Action recognition is a challenging and important problem with applications in areas such as surveillance, human-computer interaction, and robotics. The proposed architecture achieves competitive performance with state-of-the-art methods, and the insights gained from the ablation studies are valuable for future research. The work advances the state-of-the-art and provides a solid foundation for further exploration of deep learning models for video understanding.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Strong empirical results on standard benchmarks.
*   Clear and thorough experimental evaluation.
*   Novel combination of established techniques.
*   Addresses a significant problem in computer vision.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Limited exploration of the biological motivations of the two-stream architecture.
*   Could benefit from a more detailed analysis of the learned filters.
*   Discussion of limitations could be expanded.



Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of action recognition. The strengths of the work outweigh the weaknesses, and the paper is well-deserving of acceptance to NIPS. The suggested revisions are relatively minor and would further enhance the quality of the work.
