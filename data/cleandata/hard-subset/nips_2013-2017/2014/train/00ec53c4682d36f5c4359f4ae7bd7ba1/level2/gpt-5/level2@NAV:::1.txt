This paper proposes a two-stream ConvNet architecture for video action recognition that separates appearance (spatial stream, pre-trained on ImageNet) and motion (temporal stream, trained on stacked dense optical flow), and fuses their predictions at the score level. The main claims are: (1) explicit motion modeling via multi-frame optical-flow input enables a ConvNet to perform strongly despite limited video data; (2) the spatial and temporal streams are complementary, and late fusion improves accuracy; (3) multi-task learning across UCF-101 and HMDB-51 increases effective training data and boosts performance on both. The work is well positioned with respect to prior deep approaches using stacked RGB frames (e.g., Karpathy et al.) and shallow hand-crafted pipelines (improved trajectories with HOF/MBH), and it argues convincingly that the proposed temporal stream can be seen as a learnable generalization of HOF/MBH.

Quality: The empirical support is solid on standard splits of UCF-101 and HMDB-51, with ablations on flow stacking length, trajectory vs. spatial stacking, mean flow subtraction, and bi-directional flow. Comparisons to “slow fusion” RGB models and to single-frame baselines are appropriate. Implementation details (augmentation, schedules, multi-GPU) are sufficient for reproduction, though statistical significance and runtime/throughput comparisons are missing. A notable limitation is reliance on pre-computed optical flow, preventing end-to-end training and adding non-trivial compute/storage overhead.

Clarity: The paper is clearly written and well organized. The architecture and training protocols are described in adequate detail; relating learned filters to traditional motion features is insightful.

Originality: Separating spatial and temporal pathways with explicit optical-flow inputs and leveraging multi-task learning is a clean and novel combination compared to prior deep models on video. The link to classical descriptors and the two-stream hypothesis is well articulated.

Significance: The approach closes a substantial gap between deep and hand-crafted methods on widely used benchmarks and is likely to influence subsequent work on video architectures.

Constructive suggestions:
- Report computational costs end-to-end (flow time, training/inference throughput) and storage; evaluate sensitivity to the flow algorithm.
- Provide per-class analyses and statistical tests across splits.
- Explore camera-motion compensation beyond mean subtraction and trajectory-aware pooling within the network.
- Investigate longer-range temporal modeling (e.g., temporal convolutions/LSTMs) and tighter integration of motion estimation for end-to-end training.
- Consider evaluation on larger datasets (e.g., Sports-1M) to assess scalability.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Strong empirical gains over prior deep baselines; competitive with state of the art.
- Clear, well-motivated architecture with thorough ablations.
- Practical multi-task strategy to leverage multiple datasets.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Heavy dependence on pre-computed optical flow; not end-to-end.
- Limited analysis of computational efficiency and statistical significance.
