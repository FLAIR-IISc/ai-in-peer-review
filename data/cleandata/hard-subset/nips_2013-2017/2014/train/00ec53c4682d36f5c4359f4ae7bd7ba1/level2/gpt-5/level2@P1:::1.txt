Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes a two-stream convolutional architecture for video action recognition that explicitly separates appearance (spatial stream) and motion (temporal stream). The spatial stream is a standard image ConvNet operating on RGB frames and is pre-trained on ImageNet. The temporal stream takes as input stacked dense optical flow fields over multiple frames (typically L=10), with options including forward or bi-directional flow, “optical-flow stacking” vs “trajectory stacking,” and a simple mean flow subtraction to reduce camera motion effects. The two streams are fused at test time by averaging softmax scores or training a linear SVM on concatenated scores. To mitigate limited data, the authors also propose a multitask training setup that shares the ConvNet while using dataset-specific classification heads for UCF-101 and HMDB-51.

This work relates to: (1) shallow hand-crafted approaches, especially dense trajectories with HOG/HOF/MBH and Fisher-vector encodings (e.g., Wang & Schmid, 2013), which remain state of the art; (2) earlier deep models that operate on stacks of raw frames (e.g., Karpathy et al., 2014), which underperform on UCF-101 and HMDB-51; and (3) biologically inspired two-stream HMAX-like models (Jhuang et al.). The proposed temporal ConvNet can be seen as a learnable generalization of HOF/MBH, given that early conv filters can compute oriented flow gradients and pooling can aggregate these features. The empirical study shows strong gains over single-frame or slow-fusion RGB models and competitive results with trajectory-based methods despite using modest training data.

Quality
The methodology is sound, with thorough ablations: number of stacked flow fields, mean subtraction, trajectory vs optical-flow stacking, bi-directional flow, and comparison to multi-frame RGB ConvNets. The multitask setup is simple and appropriate, and the authors provide substantial training detail (architectures, learning rates, augmentations, GPU setup). Results on UCF-101 and HMDB-51 follow the standard protocol and report averages over three splits for state-of-the-art comparison. The approach is not fully end-to-end due to precomputed flow, but the rationale and impact are clearly argued.

Clarity
The paper is well organized and easy to follow. The architectural choices are clearly motivated. Implementation specifics and testing protocols are detailed enough to enable reproduction. The relation to prior shallow features is insightful and helps position the contribution.

Originality
The explicit two-stream decomposition with a dedicated optical-flow ConvNet is a clear and impactful idea relative to prior deep baselines using raw frame stacks. The particular input encodings for the temporal stream (stacked flow, mean subtraction) are simple yet effective. Multitask learning is a known technique but its application here is well justified and empirically useful. Overall originality is solid in the 2014 context.

Significance
The work addresses a central challenge in video recognition—capturing motion—by a principled architectural design that closes much of the gap with trajectory-based methods and substantially outperforms prior deep baselines. Given the strong empirical gains and the simplicity of adoption, this is likely to influence follow-up research and practice. The insights about motion representation (explicit flow vs raw frames) are particularly significant for the community.

Pros
- Clear, simple two-stream architecture with strong empirical performance; large margin over prior deep models on UCF-101/HMDB-51.
- Well-executed ablations demonstrating the value of stacked optical flow and mean subtraction; informative comparisons to multi-frame RGB models.
- Multitask learning effectively leverages multiple datasets to improve generalization under limited data.
- Comprehensive training details (architecture, schedules, augmentation, test-time crops) enhance reproducibility.
- Convincing connection between the temporal ConvNet and generalizing classical motion descriptors.

Cons
- Dependence on precomputed optical flow breaks end-to-end training and introduces computational/storage overhead; sensitivity to the choice/quality of flow is not evaluated.
- Fusion is limited to late score fusion; joint learning of higher-level fusion is reported to overfit—additional regularization strategies are not explored.
- Camera motion compensation is rudimentary (mean flow subtraction); stronger stabilization (e.g., homography-based) might further help.
- Limited analysis of temporal scale: fixed L=10 and dense sampling; little exploration of longer-range or multi-scale temporal modeling.
- Multitask training details could more explicitly address the handling of overlapping classes and potential leakage when combining splits.

Questions and suggestions
- How sensitive is performance to the optical flow algorithm and its parameters? Have you tried alternatives (e.g., TV-L1) or coarser/faster flow to trade accuracy for speed?
- What is the effect of flow quantization (JPEG compression to 0–255) on accuracy? A small study could guide practitioners.
- Can stronger camera motion compensation (e.g., global motion estimation) further boost the temporal stream?
- For late fusion with a linear SVM, how do you avoid overfitting when training on scores from the same data used to train the streams? Please clarify the validation protocol.
- Could additional regularization (e.g., stronger dropout, batch norm, weight decay schedules) enable training a small joint fusion MLP without overfitting?
- Please provide per-class gains from temporal vs spatial streams and an error analysis to illuminate failure modes and class dependencies.
- In multitask learning, how are batches balanced between datasets and how are overlapping classes handled? Clarify the exact data used per split to ensure no test leakage.

Overall assessment
This is a strong, timely contribution with clear ideas, careful experiments, and significant impact. While reliance on precomputed flow limits end-to-end training, the demonstrated effectiveness and clarity of presentation outweigh this drawback. I recommend acceptance.
