Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes a two-stream deep convolutional architecture for video action recognition that decouples appearance and motion. A spatial ConvNet operates on RGB frames (pre-trained on ImageNet [1]), and a temporal ConvNet operates on stacked dense optical flow fields computed between frames. The two streams are fused by late score fusion (average or linear SVM). The work also introduces a simple and effective multi-task learning setup to jointly train on UCF-101 [24] and HMDB-51 [16], mitigating overfitting due to limited video data.

This approach is motivated by prior observations that ConvNets trained on stacks of RGB frames underperform strong hand-crafted trajectories-based pipelines [26, 29], and that temporal information is often not captured well by learned spatio-temporal filters when trained end-to-end on raw frames [14]. The proposed temporal stream explicitly encodes motion via optical flow stacking (L up to 10), with variants including trajectory stacking [29], bi-directional flow, and mean-flow subtraction to reduce camera motion effects [10, 26]. The temporal ConvNet is argued to generalize classical motion descriptors (HOF, MBH [7, 8]) through oriented filters and pooling, while the overall two-stream structure is conceptually related to dorsal/ventral pathways [9] and prior two-stream HMAX-style models [16]. Experiments on UCF-101 and HMDB-51 show: (i) stacked optical flow substantially outperforms RGB-only networks trained from scratch or with multi-frame “slow fusion” [14], (ii) spatial and temporal streams are complementary, and (iii) multi-task learning improves performance, especially on HMDB-51. The combined two-stream model is competitive with state-of-the-art hand-crafted pipelines [20, 21, 26] and clearly surpasses previous deep models [14, 16].

Strengths
- Clear architectural decomposition that matches the structure of the problem; explicit motion input via dense optical flow markedly improves recognition over RGB stacks.
- Thorough and well-controlled ablations on temporal input design: number of stacked flows (L), trajectory vs. location stacking, bi-directional flow, and mean-flow subtraction.
- Careful training protocol with strong regularization where needed; judicious ImageNet pretraining for the spatial stream; multi-GPU implementation details aid reproducibility.
- Multi-task learning across UCF-101 and HMDB-51 is simple, principled, and effective; outperforms ad hoc class filtering or naïve pretraining.
- Comprehensive evaluation following standard splits, with comparisons to both deep baselines [14, 16] and strong hand-crafted systems [20, 21, 26]. Late fusion via linear SVM improves over averaging.
- Practical considerations addressed: precomputing optical flow, storage compression, and training time.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Dependence on precomputed optical flow limits end-to-end learning and introduces a computational bottleneck at deployment. While optical flow computation time and storage are discussed, a systematic speed/accuracy trade-off analysis (e.g., different flow methods or coarse-to-fine settings) is not provided.
- Camera motion compensation is handled only via mean-flow subtraction; stronger methods (e.g., global motion estimation as in [10, 26]) are not evaluated within this ConvNet framework.
- Feature-level fusion or joint training across streams is mentioned but dismissed due to overfitting; stronger regularization or smaller fusion heads could be explored to potentially exploit cross-stream interactions beyond score fusion.
- While trajectory stacking is evaluated, the architecture does not exploit trajectory-aligned pooling—a key component of improved trajectories [26]. The paper acknowledges this as future work.
- Comparisons to hand-crafted methods are primarily on accuracy; computational cost and model size comparisons (including test-time fps with flow) would help practitioners.
- Generalization to larger-scale datasets (e.g., Sports-1M [14]) is noted as future work; current datasets are relatively small, and cross-dataset generalization is not reported beyond the two-dataset multi-task setup.

Quality
The methodology is sound, and claims are supported by extensive experiments and ablations. The multi-task formulation is straightforward and correctly implemented with separate heads and summed losses. The analyses on temporal input design (L, mean subtraction, bi-directional flow) are particularly convincing. The work carefully contrasts with prior CNN-on-RGB-stacks approaches [14] by providing a stronger motion encoding pathway.

Clarity
The paper is clearly written and well organized. Implementation and training details (architectures, data augmentation, learning rates, dropout schedules, testing protocol) are sufficient for reproduction. The relation to prior representations is thoughtfully articulated, including how classical descriptors can emerge from the first ConvNet layers.

Originality
The two-stream deep ConvNet architecture with explicit optical flow input, combined with multi-task learning across video datasets, is a novel and impactful combination relative to contemporaneous deep video models [12, 14, 16]. The paper provides a clear conceptual and empirical rationale for explicitly modeling motion rather than expecting early layers to discover it from RGB stacks.

Significance
Results significantly advance deep learning for action recognition relative to prior CNN baselines and close the gap with strong hand-crafted pipelines. The design principles (explicit motion stream, late fusion, multi-task learning) are likely to influence subsequent work and practical systems.

Suggestions for improvement
Essential:
- Provide a deployment-oriented analysis: end-to-end throughput with and without optical flow precomputation (CPU vs. GPU), storage footprint per hour of video, and impact of using faster/approximate flow methods on accuracy.
- Evaluate stronger camera-motion compensation within the temporal stream (e.g., global affine/rigid stabilization as in [10, 26]) to test whether mean subtraction is sufficient.

Nice-to-have (optional):
- Explore lightweight feature-level fusion with strong regularization (e.g., bottleneck layers, dropout, weight decay) or gating to capture cross-stream dependencies beyond score fusion.
- Report per-class gains from temporal vs. spatial streams to illuminate which categories benefit most from motion.
- Assess sensitivity to the choice of optical flow algorithm and parameters; include at least one alternative (e.g., TV-L1) for robustness.
- Include results with trajectory-aligned pooling layers or warping features along estimated trajectories to bridge toward IDT strengths [26].
- Discuss memory/parameter counts for both streams and fusion to guide scaling to deeper backbones.

Arguments for acceptance
- Strong empirical gains over prior deep baselines with careful ablations demonstrating why the approach works.
- Clean, intuitive architecture that separates appearance and motion, with solid engineering and reproducibility.
- Competitive with state-of-the-art hand-crafted pipelines while using a learned representation; multi-task learning further improves results.

Arguments against acceptance
- Heavy reliance on precomputed optical flow reduces elegance of end-to-end learning and adds computational cost that is not fully quantified.
- Limited exploration of more advanced motion compensation and trajectory-centric pooling, both known to be impactful in the best hand-crafted systems.
