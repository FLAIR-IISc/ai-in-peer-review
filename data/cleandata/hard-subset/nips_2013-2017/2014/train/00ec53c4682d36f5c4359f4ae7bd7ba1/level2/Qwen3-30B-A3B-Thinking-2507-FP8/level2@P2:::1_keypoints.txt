- Two-stream ConvNet combining an ImageNet-pretrained spatial stream and a temporal stream using multi-frame dense optical flow with late fusion achieves strong results on UCF-101 and HMDB-51.  
- Technical soundness: rigorous experiments and ablations demonstrate the importance of temporal context and motion representation; multi-task learning boosts HMDB-51 by 6.5%; results are statistically significant (UCF-101 89.0 vs 79.8; HMDB-51 63.6 vs 57.7).  
- Clarity: architecture, experiments, figures, and implementation details (optical flow precomputation, multi-GPU training) are well-explained and reproducible.  
- Significance: introduces a practical, data-driven decoupling of spatial and temporal learning that became foundational for later video recognition work and shows optical flow is critical for motion modeling.  
- Originality: successfully adapts the two-stream neuroscience-inspired concept to discriminatively trained deep ConvNets with optical flow input, outperforming prior deep and hand-crafted methods.  
- Overstatement of novelty: claim of "exceeding by a large margin" prior deep nets is somewhat overstated given Karpathy et al.’s use of the much larger Sports-1M dataset.  
- Limited baseline comparison: omits direct comparison to Ji et al. (2013), a relevant contemporary ConvNet-for-video work.  
- Optical flow dependency: reliance on precomputed optical flow (fixed method) is a limitation and the paper does not explore end-to-end optical flow learning.  
- Suggestion: clarify that advantages over Karpathy et al. stem from optical flow input and two-stream design, and cite Sports-1M results for context.  
- Suggestion: discuss the computational/storage cost of optical flow precomputation (e.g., 27GB for UCF-101) as a trade-off versus end-to-end approaches.  
- Suggestion: explicitly state in the conclusion that the two-stream design enables effective motion learning where stacked-frame models fail.  
- Overall assessment: high-quality, impactful paper with robust experiments and convincing results; minor issues are addressable — strongly recommend acceptance.
