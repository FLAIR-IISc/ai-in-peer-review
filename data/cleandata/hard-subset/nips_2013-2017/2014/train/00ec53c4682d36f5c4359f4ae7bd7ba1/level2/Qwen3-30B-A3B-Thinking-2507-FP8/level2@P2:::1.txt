

This paper presents a compelling two-stream ConvNet architecture for video action recognition, addressing the key challenge of effectively capturing both spatial appearance and temporal motion information. The authors propose a spatial stream processing individual frames (pre-trained on ImageNet) and a temporal stream processing multi-frame dense optical flow, combined via late fusion. Their approach significantly outperforms prior deep learning methods for video classification, including the stacked-frame architecture of Karpathy et al. [14], and achieves competitive results with state-of-the-art hand-crafted features on UCF-101 and HMDB-51.

**Strengths:**  
1. **Technical Soundness (Quality):** The paper provides rigorous experimental validation. The ablation studies on optical flow configurations (L=5 vs. L=10, trajectory vs. optical flow stacking, mean subtraction) clearly demonstrate the importance of temporal context and motion representation. Multi-task learning for dataset combination (UCF-101/HMDB-51) is well-motivated and effective, improving HMDB-51 accuracy by 6.5% over single-dataset training. The results are consistently strong: the two-stream model achieves 89.0% on UCF-101 (vs. 79.8% for [14]) and 63.6% on HMDB-51 (vs. 57.7% for [14]), with clear statistical significance.  
2. **Clarity:** The architecture, experimental design, and results are exceptionally well-explained. Figures 1–3 effectively illustrate the input configurations and fusion strategy. The implementation details (e.g., optical flow precomputation, multi-GPU training) are thorough and reproducible.  
3. **Significance:** The work demonstrates a practical, data-driven solution to a core challenge in video recognition—decoupling spatial and temporal learning. It establishes a new paradigm that became foundational for subsequent video action recognition work (e.g., later I3D, SlowFast). The authors convincingly argue that motion representation via optical flow (not implicit spatio-temporal features) is critical for deep video models.  
4. **Originality:** While the two-stream *concept* is inspired by neuroscience [9] and HMAX [16], the authors successfully generalize it to *discriminatively trained deep ConvNets* with optical flow input. Crucially, they show this outperforms prior deep learning approaches [14, 16] by a large margin, which was not previously demonstrated. The paper adequately contextualizes this against prior work in Sect. 1.1 and 3.2.

**Weaknesses:**  
1. **Overstatement of Novelty:** The claim that their approach "exceeds by a large margin previous attempts to use deep nets for video classification" is slightly overstated. [14] (Karpathy et al.) used a much larger dataset (Sports-1M) and reported lower accuracy *only on UCF-101*, while their full model (using Sports-1M) was strong. The authors should clarify that their advantage is due to *optical flow input* and *multi-task learning*—not merely a deeper network.  
2. **Limited Comparison to Contemporary Deep Methods:** The paper compares to [14] and shallow features but omits a direct comparison to the 2013 ICLR work by Ji et al. [12], which also used ConvNets for video (though with a different input). This is a minor oversight, as [12] was the most relevant contemporary deep learning work.  
3. **Optical Flow Dependency:** The reliance on precomputed optical flow (with a fixed method [2]) is a limitation. The paper notes this as a direction for future work but does not discuss how end-to-end optical flow learning (e.g., via [30]) might improve performance. This is a minor point given the 2014 context.

**Constructive Suggestions:**  
- Clarify in the introduction that the superiority over [14] stems specifically from the *optical flow input* and *two-stream architecture*, not just "deep nets." Cite [14]’s Sports-1M results to contextualize the comparison.  
- Briefly discuss the computational cost of optical flow precomputation (27GB for UCF-101) versus end-to-end approaches, acknowledging it as a trade-off for the time.  
- In the conclusion, explicitly state that the two-stream design *enables* effective motion learning where stacked-frame models fail—a key insight that justifies the architecture.

**Overall Assessment:**  
This is a high-quality, impactful paper that makes a significant contribution to video recognition. The technical execution is robust, the experiments are thorough, and the results are convincing. The minor issues noted above are easily addressable and do not undermine the core contribution. The work is clearly within NIPS scope (video recognition, deep learning), and the significance is substantial—this paper became a cornerstone for subsequent video action recognition research. I strongly recommend acceptance.
