The paper "Two-Stream Convolutional Networks for Action Recognition in Videos" proposes a novel architecture for action recognition in videos, leveraging the strengths of deep Convolutional Networks (ConvNets) for both spatial and temporal recognition. The authors' main contribution is the introduction of a two-stream ConvNet architecture, which incorporates spatial and temporal networks, and demonstrates its effectiveness on standard video action recognition benchmarks.

The paper is well-written and clearly organized, making it easy to follow the authors' line of reasoning. The introduction provides a thorough review of the related work in action recognition, highlighting the challenges of capturing both spatial and temporal information in videos. The authors also provide a clear explanation of their proposed architecture and its components.

The technical content of the paper is sound, and the authors provide a thorough evaluation of their approach on two challenging datasets, UCF-101 and HMDB-51. The results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed two-stream architecture, which outperforms state-of-the-art hand-crafted models and other deep learning-based approaches.

One of the strengths of the paper is its ability to generalize hand-crafted features, such as Histogram of Oriented Gradients (HOG) and Histogram of Optical Flow (HOF), using a ConvNet architecture. The authors also provide a thorough analysis of the impact of different input configurations, such as optical flow stacking and trajectory stacking, on the performance of the temporal ConvNet.

The use of multi-task learning to increase the effective training set size is also a notable contribution of the paper. The authors demonstrate that multi-task learning can improve the performance of the temporal ConvNet on both UCF-101 and HMDB-51 datasets.

The paper has some minor issues, such as the lack of clarity in the description of the multi-GPU training implementation. However, this does not detract from the overall quality of the paper.

In terms of originality, the paper makes a significant contribution to the field of action recognition in videos. The proposed two-stream architecture is a novel approach that leverages the strengths of ConvNets for both spatial and temporal recognition.

In terms of significance, the paper has the potential to impact the field of action recognition in videos. The proposed architecture can be applied to a wide range of applications, such as video surveillance, human-computer interaction, and video analysis.

Overall, the paper is well-written, technically sound, and makes a significant contribution to the field of action recognition in videos. It is a strong candidate for publication in NIPS.
