## Review of “Two-Stream Convolutional Networks for Action Recognition in Videos”

This paper investigates the application of deep convolutional neural networks (ConvNets) to action recognition in videos, focusing on the complementary information provided by spatial (appearance) and temporal (motion) streams. The authors propose a two-stream ConvNet architecture, demonstrating that a ConvNet trained on multi-frame dense optical flow achieves strong performance even with limited training data, and that multi-task learning across datasets further improves results. The work is evaluated on UCF-101 and HMDB-51, achieving competitive results with the state-of-the-art at the time.

The key strength of this work lies in its clear and effective decoupling of spatial and temporal information. Prior attempts to directly apply ConvNets to video (e.g., stacking frames) struggled to capture motion effectively. By explicitly processing optical flow in a separate stream, the authors circumvent this issue and achieve significantly improved performance. The use of pre-training on ImageNet for the spatial stream and the exploration of multi-task learning are also valuable contributions. The paper is well-written and logically structured, clearly outlining the motivations, methodology, and results. 

However, the work has some limitations. The reliance on dense optical flow, while effective, is computationally expensive. The paper acknowledges this but doesn't extensively explore more efficient motion representations. Furthermore, the architectures presented, while a significant step forward, are relatively simple by today’s standards. The experiments primarily focus on demonstrating the effectiveness of the two-stream approach, and a more in-depth analysis of different network configurations or fusion strategies could have been beneficial. The discussion on the biological plausibility of the two-stream approach (ventral/dorsal streams) feels underdeveloped and could be expanded. 

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Presents a novel and effective architecture for action recognition.
*   Demonstrates strong performance compared to prior deep learning methods.
*   Clearly articulates the benefits of separating spatial and temporal processing.
*   Well-written and clearly explained.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Computational cost of dense optical flow.
*   Relatively simple architecture compared to current state-of-the-art.
*   Limited exploration of alternative fusion techniques.

**Overall:** This is a solid contribution that significantly advanced the field of video action recognition. Despite some limitations, the proposed two-stream architecture is a valuable and influential concept. I recommend acceptance, with encouragement for the authors to explore more efficient motion representations and more sophisticated network designs in future work.
