This paper proposes a novel two-stream Convolutional Network (ConvNet) architecture for action recognition in videos. The architecture combines two separate recognition streams, one for spatial information (still frames) and one for temporal information (motion between frames). The authors demonstrate that this approach significantly outperforms previous attempts to use deep nets for video classification and is competitive with state-of-the-art shallow representations.

The paper is well-written, and the authors provide a thorough review of related work in the field. The proposed architecture is clearly explained, and the experimental results are presented in a clear and concise manner. The authors also provide a detailed analysis of the strengths and weaknesses of their approach.

One of the main strengths of this paper is the use of a two-stream architecture, which allows for the decoupling of spatial and temporal recognition streams. This approach enables the authors to pre-train the spatial net on a large image classification dataset (ImageNet) and fine-tune it on the target video dataset. The temporal net is trained on optical flow displacement fields, which provides a more effective representation of motion than previous approaches.

The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed architecture. The authors evaluate their approach on two challenging datasets (UCF-101 and HMDB-51) and achieve state-of-the-art performance on both datasets. The results also show that the two recognition streams are complementary, and their fusion significantly improves performance.

One potential weakness of this paper is the limited size of the training datasets. The authors acknowledge this limitation and propose a multi-task learning framework to increase the effective training set size. However, this approach may not be effective for very large datasets, and the authors may need to explore other methods to scale their approach.

Another potential area for improvement is the handling of camera motion. The authors use mean displacement subtraction to compensate for global motion, but this approach may not be effective for all types of camera motion. The authors may need to explore more sophisticated methods for handling camera motion, such as explicit modeling of camera motion or the use of more advanced optical flow algorithms.

In terms of originality, this paper proposes a novel two-stream architecture for action recognition in videos. The use of a separate temporal net trained on optical flow displacement fields is a new contribution, and the authors demonstrate its effectiveness in improving performance.

In terms of significance, this paper has the potential to impact the field of action recognition in videos. The proposed architecture is competitive with state-of-the-art shallow representations and has the potential to be used in a wide range of applications, from surveillance to human-computer interaction.

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of action recognition in videos. The authors provide a thorough review of related work, propose a novel two-stream architecture, and demonstrate its effectiveness on challenging datasets.

* The paper proposes a novel two-stream architecture for action recognition in videos.
