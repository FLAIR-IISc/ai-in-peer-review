This paper proposes a two-stream convolutional network architecture for action recognition in videos. The model consists of two separate recognition streams, one for spatial information and one for temporal information, which are then combined by late fusion. The spatial stream operates on individual video frames, effectively performing action recognition from still images, while the temporal stream is trained to recognize action from motion in the form of dense optical flow.

The authors relate their work to previous research in action recognition, highlighting the challenges of capturing the complementary information on appearance from still frames and motion between frames. They also discuss the limitations of existing deep architectures for video recognition, which often rely on stacking consecutive video frames as input, making it difficult for the network to implicitly learn spatio-temporal motion-dependent features.

The paper's main contribution is the proposal of a two-stream ConvNet architecture that incorporates spatial and temporal networks. The authors also demonstrate that a ConvNet trained on multi-frame dense optical flow can achieve good performance despite limited training data. Additionally, they show that multi-task learning can be used to increase the amount of training data and improve performance on both datasets.

The experiments are well-designed, and the results are impressive, matching or coming close to the state-of-the-art methods that use intensively hand-crafted features. The authors evaluate their model on two datasets, UCF-101 and HMDB-51, and report the average accuracy over three splits on both datasets.

Strengths of the paper include:

* The model is a simple application of convolutional nets that gets good results.
* The use of optical flow features as inputs to a conv net is a novel contribution.
* The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach on two challenging datasets.

Weaknesses of the paper include:

* The model does not take into account the overall sequence of actions over the entire video but only models fixed-length adjacent frames.
* The authors do not provide an analysis of the error modes, which would be insightful in understanding the limitations of their approach.

Quality: 8/10
The experiments are well-designed, and the results are impressive. However, the authors could provide more analysis of the error modes to better understand the limitations of their approach.

Clarity: 9/10
The paper is clearly written, and the model is well-explained. However, some sections, such as the related work, could be more concise.

Originality: 8/10
The application of a convolutional net to optical flow features is novel, but the overall architecture is not significantly different from existing approaches.

Significance: 9/10
This approach could have a significant impact on research in using videos and motion for various vision problems. The model is a simple application of convolutional nets to video data and gets very promising results.
