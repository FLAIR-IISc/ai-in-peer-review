This paper proposes a two-stream Convolutional Network (ConvNet) architecture for action recognition in videos, aiming to effectively capture both appearance and motion information. The core idea is to process spatial information (from static frames) and temporal information (from dense optical flow) in two separate ConvNet streams, which are then combined via late fusion. This approach is contrasted with previous work, such as Karpathy et al. (CVPR 2014), which attempted to learn spatio-temporal features directly from stacked raw frames with less success. The proposed temporal stream, trained on pre-computed optical flow, is shown to be a powerful motion representation, effectively generalizing hand-crafted features like HOF and MBH. Additionally, the paper introduces a multi-task learning framework to combine datasets (UCF-101 and HMDB-51) to increase training data and mitigate overfitting, a common problem in video recognition due to relatively small datasets.

**Strengths and Weaknesses**

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The methodology is described in sufficient detail to allow for reproducibility, covering the network architecture, training procedures, and data pre-processing steps like optical flow computation and compression. The claims are well-supported by a comprehensive set of experiments, including ablation studies that systematically evaluate the impact of different input configurations (e.g., optical flow stacking length, mean subtraction) and training strategies (e.g., pre-training, multi-task learning). This thorough evaluation adds credibility to the results.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-written and clearly organized. However, the presentation feels slightly split across multiple contributions (the two-stream concept, the optical flow ConvNet, and multi-task learning) rather than being driven by a single, focused theme. The paper would be strengthened if the abstract and introduction more clearly positioned the two-stream architecture and the effectiveness of the optical flow stream as the central contribution.

**Originality:** The proposed two-stream framework is a novel and interesting contribution to deep learning for video analysis. While the idea of separating appearance and motion processing is not new in computer vision, its implementation with two deep ConvNets, particularly the demonstration that a network trained on optical flow significantly outperforms one trained on raw frames, is a key original insight. This work clearly distinguishes itself from previous deep learning approaches. However, the review of related work appears to be incomplete.

**Significance:** The results presented are highly significant and likely to be impactful. The proposed method achieves results that are competitive with the state-of-the-art based on hand-crafted features, while substantially outperforming previous deep learning models on the UCF-101 and HMDB-51 benchmarks. The finding that explicitly providing motion information in the form of optical flow is so effective is an important result that many researchers in the community will find valuable and are likely to build upon.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   The paper introduces a novel and effective two-stream architecture that successfully separates the learning of spatial and temporal features for action recognition.
*   The demonstration that a ConvNet trained on optical flow is a powerful motion descriptor is a significant finding for the video understanding community.
*   The model achieves impressive results that are competitive with the state-of-the-art, representing a clear advance for deep learning-based video classification.
*   The empirical evaluation is thorough and provides valuable insights into the design choices.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   The paper's narrative could be more focused on its primary contribution.
*   The related work section could be more comprehensive.

**Suggestions for Improvement:**
1.  To improve the paper's focus, I suggest revising the abstract and introduction to emphasize the two-stream architecture and the incorporation of optical flow as the main theme and most significant contribution.
2.  The paper would benefit from including citations to relevant prior work. Specifically, consider adding:
    *   In deep learning: Le et al., "Learning Hierarchical Spatio-temporal Features for Action Recognition with Independent Subspace Analysis," CVPR 2011.
    *   In computer vision: Wang et al., "Evaluation of local spatio-temporal features for action recognition," BMVC 2009.

**Recommendation**
This paper presents significant practical contributions alongside interesting ideas that complement the main trends in deep learning. I recommend accepting this paper, on the condition that the authors incorporate the suggested improvements regarding the paper's focus and the inclusion of missing citations.
