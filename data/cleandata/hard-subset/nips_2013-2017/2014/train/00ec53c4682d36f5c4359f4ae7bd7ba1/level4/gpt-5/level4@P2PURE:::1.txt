Summary:
This paper presents a model for discriminative tasks on video inputs using two convolutional networks. One network processes a single appearance frame, while the other ingests a stack of densely computed optical flow features. Each pathway is trained independently to classify its respective input, and the video-level prediction is obtained by taking a weighted average of the two networks’ predictions. The model is evaluated on two datasets, achieving impressive results that match or closely approach state-of-the-art methods that rely heavily on hand-crafted features.

Strengths:
- A straightforward application of convolutional networks that achieves strong performance.
- Prior deep learning approaches typically avoid optical flow (or other hand-crafted features), aiming instead to learn features directly from raw pixels. This paper demonstrates that, at least for action recognition, optical flow fields provide a valuable input representation—an important contribution.

Weaknesses:
- The model does not capture the full temporal sequence of actions over an entire video; it only models fixed-length (L=10) adjacent frame segments.

Quality:
The experiments are well designed. The paper would benefit from an analysis of error modes—for example, identifying whether certain classes or groups of classes are consistently misclassified. It would also be helpful to characterize the types of videos on which the model performs poorly.

Clarity:
The paper is clearly written, and the model is well explained.

Originality:
Using a convolutional network on optical flow features is novel.

Significance:
This approach could substantially influence research on leveraging video and motion cues for various vision tasks. It is a simple application of convolutional networks to video data that yields very promising results. The use of optical flow features as inputs to a convolutional network is a novel and impactful contribution.
