

Review Summary:  
This work introduces a model for discriminative video tasks using a dual-convolutional network architecture. One network processes appearance frames, while the other ingests a stack of densely computed optical flow features. Each pathway undergoes separate training for classification, with the final video prediction derived from a weighted average of both pathways' outputs. The approach achieves strong results on two datasets, matching or approaching state-of-the-art performance of methods employing hand-crafted features.  

Strengths:  
- The model presents a straightforward application of convolutional networks yielding high accuracy.  
- It challenges the prevailing trend of learning all features directly from pixels by demonstrating optical flow fields as a valuable input for action recognition, marking a significant contribution.  

Weaknesses:  
- The model processes only a fixed-length sequence of adjacent frames (L=10), neglecting broader temporal dynamics across the full video.  

Quality:  
Experiments are well-executed. Additional insight would be gained through error analysis—e.g., identifying specific classes or video types where the model underperforms.  

Clarity:  
The paper is clearly written, with a well-explained model.  

Originality:  
The application of convolutional networks to optical flow features represents a novel approach.  

Significance:  
This simple yet effective method demonstrates promising results for video-based vision tasks. Its novel integration of optical flow with convolutional networks holds potential to influence future research on motion-driven video analysis.
