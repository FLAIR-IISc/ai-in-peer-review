This paper presents a novel neural network architecture for human action recognition in videos. The model leverages two convolutional neural networks: a spatial stream trained on individual video frames and a temporal stream trained on sequences of dense optical flow images. By combining the predictions of these two streams, the authors achieve significant improvements over existing ConvNet-based approaches and achieve competitive performance with the current state-of-the-art on the standard UCF-101 and HMDB-51 video classification benchmarks.

The work effectively extends the established deep Convolutional Neural Network (ConvNet) paradigm for still image classification to the domain of video action recognition. While the core idea of combining spatial and temporal streams builds upon the “two-stream hypothesis” from visual neuroscience [9], the authors demonstrate a robust implementation and thorough empirical evaluation. Specifically, they convincingly show the benefits of training a ConvNet directly on multi-frame dense optical flow, even with limited training data. The experiments are well-designed, comparing different input configurations for the temporal stream (optical flow stacking vs. trajectory stacking, bi-directional flow) and fusion methods (averaging vs. SVM).

A key strength of this work is its clear connection to previous research. The authors meticulously relate their approach to both shallow, hand-crafted feature methods [7, 8, 17, 20, 26, 28, 29] and recent deep learning approaches [4, 11, 12, 14, 16, 18, 25]. This contextualization demonstrates a strong understanding of the field and highlights the novelty of their contribution. Furthermore, the utilization of multi-task learning [5] to effectively increase training data size is a sensible and well-executed strategy.

However, a few points warrant further consideration. The paper does not fully explain the substantial performance gap between the spatial ConvNet and previous models [14], despite their architectural similarity. A deeper investigation into this discrepancy would be valuable. Additionally, while the authors explore various configurations for the temporal stream, a more comprehensive evaluation of different frame sampling strategies for the spatial stream (e.g., different temporal densities, or learned sampling strategies) might provide additional insights.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Strong empirical results:** The proposed architecture achieves state-of-the-art or competitive results on standard benchmarks.
*   **Clear and well-motivated approach:** The two-stream architecture is conceptually sound and builds upon established principles in computer vision.
*   **Thorough experimentation:** The authors conduct a comprehensive set of experiments to evaluate different design choices.
*   **Strong connection to related work:** The paper provides a detailed and insightful discussion of relevant prior research.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The explanation for the superior performance of the spatial network is limited.
*   The exploration of frame sampling for the spatial stream is relatively limited.

Overall, this is a high-quality paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of video action recognition. The technical soundness, clarity, and thorough evaluation justify its acceptance to NIPS. The strengths of the work clearly outweigh the minor weaknesses, and the findings are likely to be impactful for researchers working in this area.
