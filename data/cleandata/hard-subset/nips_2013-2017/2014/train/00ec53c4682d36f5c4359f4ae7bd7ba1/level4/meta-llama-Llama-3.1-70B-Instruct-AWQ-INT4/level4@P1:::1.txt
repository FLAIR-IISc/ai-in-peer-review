This paper proposes a model for solving discriminative tasks with video inputs, consisting of two convolutional nets. The input to one net is an appearance frame, while the input to the second net is a stack of densely computed optical flow features. Each pathway is trained separately to classify its input, and the prediction for a video is obtained by taking a weighted average of the predictions made by each net.

The authors relate their work to previous research in video recognition, highlighting the challenges of capturing the complementary information on appearance from still frames and motion between frames. They also discuss the limitations of existing deep learning models, which have not effectively utilized optical flow or other hand-crafted features for vision tasks.

The proposed two-stream ConvNet architecture is evaluated on two datasets, UCF-101 and HMDB-51, where it achieves competitive performance with state-of-the-art methods that use intensively hand-crafted features. The authors demonstrate the importance of motion information for action recognition and show that their temporal ConvNet outperforms the spatial ConvNet.

Strengths of the paper include its simplicity and effectiveness in applying convolutional nets to video data. The use of optical flow features as inputs to a convolutional net is a novel contribution, and the authors demonstrate its usefulness for action recognition. The paper is clearly written, and the model is well-explained.

However, there are some limitations to the approach. The model does not take into account the overall sequence of actions over the entire video but only models fixed-length (L=10) adjacent frames. Additionally, the authors do not provide an analysis of the error modes, such as classes or groups of classes that the model is unable to classify well.

In terms of quality, the experiments are well-designed, and the authors provide a thorough evaluation of their approach. However, it would be more insightful if they also included some analysis of the error modes. For example, are there some classes or groups of classes that the model is unable to classify well? Is it possible to characterize the kind of videos on which the model does not work well?

The paper is clearly written, and the model is well-explained. The authors provide a good overview of the related work and effectively communicate the contributions of their approach.

The application of a convolutional net to optical flow features is novel, and the authors demonstrate its effectiveness for action recognition. This approach could have a significant impact on research in using videos and motion for various vision problems.

Overall, the paper presents a simple yet effective approach to video recognition using convolutional nets. While there are some limitations to the approach, the authors demonstrate its potential and provide a solid foundation for future research.

Arguments For Acceptance:
- The paper presents a novel application of convolutional nets to video data.
- The use of optical flow features as inputs to a convolutional net is a novel contribution.
- The approach achieves competitive performance with state-of-the-art methods.

Arguments Against Acceptance:
- The model does not take into account the overall sequence of actions over the entire video.
- The authors do not provide an analysis of the error modes.
- The approach may not be effective for videos with complex or variable-length actions.
