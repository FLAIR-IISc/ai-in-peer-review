Summary:
This paper presents a two-stream convolutional network for discriminative tasks on video. One stream processes raw appearance frames, while the other ingests a stack of densely computed optical-flow fields. The two streams are trained independently to classify their respective inputs. Video-level predictions are produced by averaging (optionally with weights) the outputs from the two networks. The approach is evaluated on two datasets and achieves impressive results that match or closely approach state-of-the-art methods relying on heavily hand-engineered features.

Strengths:
- A simple yet effective application of convolutional networks to video that achieves strong performance.
- Prior deep learning approaches largely avoided hand-crafted motion features, preferring end-to-end learning from pixels. This work shows that, at least for action recognition, optical flow is a valuable input representation for deep models—an important contribution.

Weaknesses:
- The model captures only short temporal windows (fixed length L=10) and does not explicitly model long-range temporal dependencies across the full video.

Quality:
The experiments are well designed. The paper would benefit from an analysis of failure modes. For example: Which classes or groups of classes are most frequently confused? Can the authors characterize the types of videos where the method performs poorly (e.g., rapid camera motion, subtle actions, occlusions)? Such analysis would provide insight into limitations and guide future improvements.

Clarity:
The paper is clearly written, and the model is explained thoroughly.

Originality:
Applying convolutional networks directly to optical-flow inputs is novel.

Significance:
This approach could meaningfully influence research on video and motion in computer vision. The model is straightforward yet achieves promising results on standard benchmarks, and the explicit use of optical flow as input to a CNN is a notable and useful contribution.
