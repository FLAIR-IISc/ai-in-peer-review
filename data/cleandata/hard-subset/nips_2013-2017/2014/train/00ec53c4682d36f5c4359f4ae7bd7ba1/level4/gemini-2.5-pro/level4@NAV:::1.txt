**Summary:**
This paper presents a two-stream model designed for video-based classification tasks. The architecture is comprised of two distinct convolutional networks. One network, the spatial stream, processes static appearance information from individual video frames. The other network, the temporal stream, takes a stack of dense optical flow fields as input to capture motion. The two pathways are trained independently to perform classification on their respective inputs. Final predictions for a given video are generated by fusing the scores from both networks, typically through a weighted average.

The proposed method is validated on two benchmark datasets, where it achieves impressive performance that is on par with, or closely approaches, state-of-the-art methods reliant on heavily engineered features.

**Strengths:**
- The model's primary strength lies in its simplicity; it is a straightforward yet effective application of convolutional networks that yields strong empirical results.
- Unlike prior deep learning approaches that have focused exclusively on learning features from raw pixel data, this work demonstrates that incorporating pre-computed optical flow is a highly effective input representation for action recognition. This finding represents a significant contribution to the field.

**Weaknesses:**
- A notable limitation is the model's inability to capture long-range temporal dependencies, as it does not model the complete sequence of actions throughout a video. Instead, its temporal modeling is confined to short snippets of fixed-length (e.g., L=10) consecutive frames.

**Quality:**
The experimental validation is thorough and well-conducted. However, the paper would benefit from a more in-depth analysis of the model's failure cases. For instance, an examination of which action classes (or groups of classes) are frequently confused would provide valuable insights. Furthermore, characterizing the properties of videos on which the model performs poorly would strengthen the experimental section.

**Clarity:**
The manuscript is well-written and easy to follow. The proposed model and its components are explained with sufficient clarity.

**Originality:**
The core novelty of this work lies in applying a convolutional network directly to computed optical flow fields as an input modality.

**Significance:**
This work is poised to have a substantial impact on future research involving motion and video analysis in computer vision. The demonstration that a simple ConvNet-based architecture can achieve such promising results on video data is highly encouraging. The concept of using optical flow as an explicit input to a deep network, rather than relying on the network to learn motion implicitly, is a key and novel contribution.
