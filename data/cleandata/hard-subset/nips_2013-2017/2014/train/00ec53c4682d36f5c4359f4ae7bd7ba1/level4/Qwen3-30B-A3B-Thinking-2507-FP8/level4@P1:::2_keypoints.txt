- Proposes a two-stream ConvNet architecture that decouples spatial (appearance) and temporal (motion) processing for video action recognition.  
- Spatial stream processes single frames and is pretrained on ImageNet; temporal stream processes multi-frame dense optical flow.  
- Training on optical flow rather than stacked RGB frames yields significantly better temporal recognition performance.  
- Uses multi-task learning across UCF-101 and HMDB-51 to mitigate small dataset limitations.  
- Systematic experimental comparison of optical flow input variants (stacking, trajectory-based, bi-/unidirectional).  
- Ablation studies demonstrate complementarity of the two streams; temporal stream alone outperforms spatial (68.7% vs. 58.5% on UCF-101).  
- Fusion of streams (via SVM) achieves 81.5% accuracy, outperforming prior deep-learning methods by ~15–20%.  
- Matches state-of-the-art hand-crafted trajectory-based methods without complex feature engineering.  
- Originality justified by linking to the two-stream neuroscience hypothesis and by learning motion features that generalize HOF/MBH.  
- Multi-task training offers a principled solution to dataset scarcity, avoiding naive class-merging.  
- Paper clarity is generally strong with clear descriptions of architecture, inputs, and training.  
- Missing explicit comparison explaining why their spatial stream vastly outperforms [14]’s single-frame model; ImageNet pretraining noted but more architectural detail would help.  
- Significance: demonstrates deep learning can rival hand-crafted methods and provides a modular, scalable framework.  
- Practical contributions include input configuration analysis, fusion strategies, and multi-task training.  
- Weakness: limited discussion of trajectory-aware pooling and other future extensions.  
- Weakness: optical flow computation (using [2]) is a computational bottleneck; end-to-end flow learning was not explored.  
- Overall recommendation: high-quality, significant contribution—recommend acceptance for NIPS.
