This paper presents a new neural network architecture for classifying videos of human actions, building upon the existing body of work in the field. The authors' contribution is three-fold: they propose a two-stream ConvNet architecture that incorporates spatial and temporal networks; demonstrate that a ConvNet trained on multi-frame dense optical flow can achieve good performance despite limited training data; and show that multitask learning can increase the amount of training data and improve performance on both datasets. The architecture is evaluated on the standard video actions benchmarks of UCF-101 and HMDB-51, where it is competitive with the state of the art.

The paper is well-motivated, drawing connections to previous work on video representations and action recognition. The authors review the related work on action recognition using both shallow and deep architectures, highlighting the challenges of capturing the complementary information on appearance from still frames and motion between frames. They also discuss the limitations of previous attempts to develop deep architectures for video recognition, such as the use of stacked video frames as input to the network.

The proposed two-stream architecture is a key contribution of the paper, as it allows for the decoupling of spatial and temporal recognition streams. The authors demonstrate that the spatial stream ConvNet can be pre-trained on a large image classification dataset, such as ImageNet, and fine-tuned on the target dataset. The temporal stream ConvNet, on the other hand, is trained on multi-frame dense optical flow, which is computed using a popular method based on constancy assumptions for intensity and its gradient, as well as smoothness of the displacement field.

The experimental evaluation is thorough, with the authors comparing different architectures on the UCF-101 and HMDB-51 datasets. They also investigate the effect of various input configurations, such as stacking multiple optical flows, trajectory stacking, and mean displacement subtraction. The results show that the proposed architecture significantly outperforms other ConvNet-based approaches to action recognition and matches the current state-of-the-art on the two standard video classification datasets.

The paper has several strengths, including its thorough experimental evaluation, clear presentation, and well-motivated architecture. The authors also provide a detailed discussion of the related work, highlighting the connections to previous research in the field. However, there are a few minor comments that can be made. Additionally, it would be interesting to see a comparison of different sampling schemes for the frames fed into the spatial stream.
