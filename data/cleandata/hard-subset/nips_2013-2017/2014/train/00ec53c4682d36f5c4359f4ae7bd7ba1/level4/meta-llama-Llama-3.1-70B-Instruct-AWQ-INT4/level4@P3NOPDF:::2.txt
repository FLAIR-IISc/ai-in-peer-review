This paper presents a novel neural network architecture for classifying videos of human actions, achieving state-of-the-art performance on two standard video classification datasets. The proposed model effectively combines the predictions of two convolutional neural networks (CNNs): one trained on individual video frames and the other trained on short sequences of dense optical flow images. This approach significantly outperforms other ConvNet-based methods for action recognition.

Although the paper primarily applies the standard ConvNet image classification pipeline to a new type of data (dense optical flow frames) and combines it with a single-frame ConvNet, the experiments are thorough and the results are impressive. It is particularly noteworthy that the authors explored several different ways of training a ConvNet on optical flow, with the results clearly demonstrating the benefits of using optical flow as input.

The paper raises a few minor points that warrant further discussion. Firstly, it is not entirely clear why the spatial network outperforms the models from [13] given the similarities in their architectures. A more detailed analysis of the differences in their design choices and training procedures would provide valuable insights.

Additionally, it would be interesting to see a comparison of different sampling schemes for the frames fed into the spatial stream. This could help shed light on the optimal strategy for selecting frames that maximize the model's performance.

Overall, this is a well-crafted empirical paper that will undoubtedly be of interest to researchers working on video classification with ConvNets. The thorough experiments and impressive results make a compelling case for the proposed architecture, and the authors' efforts to explore various aspects of the model are commendable.
