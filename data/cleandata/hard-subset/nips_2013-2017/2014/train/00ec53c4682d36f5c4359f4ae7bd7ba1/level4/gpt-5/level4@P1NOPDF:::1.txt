Summary:
This paper proposes a model for discriminative tasks with video inputs composed of two convolutional networks. One network takes an appearance frame as input, and the other takes a stack of densely computed optical flow features. Each pathway is trained separately to classify its input. The final video prediction is obtained by taking a (weighted) average of the predictions from the two networks. The model is evaluated on two datasets and achieves impressive results that match or approach state-of-the-art methods that rely heavily on hand-crafted features.

Strengths:
- The model is a straightforward application of convolutional networks that achieves strong results.
- Previous deep learning models have generally avoided using optical flow (or other hand-crafted features) for vision tasks, preferring to learn features directly from raw pixels. This paper demonstrates that, at least for action recognition, optical flow fields are a valuable input representation. This is an important contribution.

Weaknesses:
- The model does not capture the overall sequence of actions across an entire video; it only models fixed-length (L=10) adjacent frames.

Quality:
The experiments are well designed. It would be more insightful if the authors also included an analysis of error modes—for example, are there specific classes or groups of classes that the model consistently misclassifies? Is it possible to characterize the types of videos on which the model performs poorly?

Clarity:
The paper is clearly written, and the model is well explained.

Originality:
Applying a convolutional network to optical flow features is novel.

Significance:
This approach could significantly influence research on leveraging videos and motion for vision problems. The model is a simple yet effective application of convolutional networks to video data and yields very promising results. Using optical flow features as inputs to a convolutional network is a novel and impactful contribution.
