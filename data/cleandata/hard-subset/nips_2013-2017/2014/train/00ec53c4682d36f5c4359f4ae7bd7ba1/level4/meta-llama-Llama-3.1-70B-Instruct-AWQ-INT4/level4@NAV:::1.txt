Review- Summary:
This paper presents a novel approach to tackling discriminative tasks involving video inputs, leveraging a two-branch convolutional neural network architecture. One branch processes individual appearance frames, while the other operates on a stack of densely computed optical flow features. Each branch is trained independently to classify its respective input, with the final prediction for a video obtained through a weighted average of the outputs from both branches.
The proposed model is evaluated on two benchmark datasets, yielding impressive results that rival or closely match state-of-the-art methods reliant on laboriously hand-crafted features.
Strengths:
- The simplicity of this convolutional neural network-based approach, coupled with its impressive performance, makes it a notable contribution.
- By incorporating optical flow features, this work diverges from previous deep learning models that primarily focus on learning features directly from pixel data. This paper demonstrates the utility of optical flow fields as a valuable input representation, particularly for action recognition tasks.
Weaknesses:
- A key limitation of the proposed model is its inability to account for the overall sequence of actions across the entire video, instead focusing on fixed-length (L=10) adjacent frames.
Quality:
The experimental design is robust, but would benefit from a more in-depth analysis of error modes. Specifically, it would be insightful to investigate whether certain classes or groups of classes are more challenging for the model to classify accurately. Additionally, characterizing the types of videos that the model struggles with would provide valuable insights.
Clarity:
The paper is well-written, with a clear and concise explanation of the proposed model.
Originality:
The application of a convolutional neural network to optical flow features represents a novel and innovative approach.
Significance:
This work has the potential to significantly impact research in video-based vision problems, leveraging motion and optical flow features. The proposed model's simplicity, coupled with its promising results, makes it a valuable contribution to the field.
