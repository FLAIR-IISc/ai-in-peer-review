

Summary:
This paper introduces a model for discriminative video tasks using a dual-branch convolutional network architecture. The first branch processes appearance frames, while the second branch processes a stack of densely computed optical flow fields. Each branch is trained independently to classify its respective input, with the final video prediction derived via a weighted average of the individual branch outputs. The approach achieves strong results on two benchmark datasets, matching or approaching state-of-the-art performance of methods relying on extensively hand-crafted features.

Strengths:
- The model demonstrates that a straightforward application of convolutional networks yields compelling results for video classification.
- Prior deep learning approaches for vision tasks predominantly learned features directly from pixels, avoiding optical flow or other hand-crafted representations. This work establishes that optical flow fields constitute a valuable input modality for action recognition, representing a significant contribution.

Weaknesses:
- The model processes only a fixed-length (L=10) sequence of adjacent frames, neglecting temporal dynamics across the entire video. This limitation restricts its ability to model long-range action sequences.

Quality:
The experimental design is sound. Including an analysis of error patterns—such as identifying specific classes or video types where the model underperforms—would provide deeper insights into its limitations and potential improvements.

Clarity:
The paper is clearly written, with the model architecture and methodology well-explained.

Originality:
The application of convolutional networks to optical flow features represents a novel approach for video-based action recognition.

Significance:
This work has the potential to substantially influence research on video analysis and motion-based vision problems. By demonstrating that optical flow features effectively serve as inputs to convolutional networks, the authors present a simple yet highly promising framework that achieves state-of-the-art results for video classification.
