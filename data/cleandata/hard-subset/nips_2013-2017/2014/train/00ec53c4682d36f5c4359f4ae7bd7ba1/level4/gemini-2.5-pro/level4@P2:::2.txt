**Summary**

This paper proposes a novel two-stream convolutional network architecture for action recognition in videos. The core idea is to decouple the learning of appearance and motion cues. This is achieved by using two separate ConvNets: a "spatial stream" that operates on individual static video frames to capture appearance information, and a "temporal stream" that is trained on stacks of dense optical flow fields to learn motion patterns. The predictions from these two streams are then combined using a late fusion technique (averaging or an SVM). The authors conduct a thorough evaluation on the UCF-101 and HMDB-51 datasets, demonstrating that their approach significantly outperforms previous deep learning methods (e.g., Karpathy et al., 2014) and achieves performance competitive with the state-of-the-art methods based on hand-crafted features like improved dense trajectories (iDT).

**Strengths**

1.  **Effective and Elegant Architecture:** The central contribution—separating the problem into spatial and temporal streams—is a simple yet powerful concept. By feeding pre-computed optical flow to the temporal network, the model is provided with an explicit, high-quality representation of motion, sidestepping the difficult problem of learning motion from raw pixel values that prior deep models struggled with. This design choice is well-motivated by the two-streams hypothesis in neuroscience and is shown to be highly effective.

2.  **State-of-the-Art Performance:** The paper presents impressive results that were, at the time of submission, competitive with the best-performing methods in the field, which were largely based on sophisticated hand-crafted features (e.g., Wang & Schmid, 2013). The fact that this deep learning-based model could match these methods was a significant achievement and demonstrated a promising new direction for video understanding.

3.  **Thorough Experimental Validation:** The authors provide a comprehensive set of ablation studies that convincingly support their design choices. They systematically evaluate:
    *   The benefit of pre-training the spatial stream on ImageNet.
    *   Multiple input configurations for the temporal stream (optical flow stacking vs. trajectory stacking, number of frames, bi-directional flow, mean subtraction).
    *   The effectiveness of multi-task learning for leveraging smaller datasets.
    *   Different fusion methods for combining the two streams.
    This rigorous evaluation adds significant weight to the paper's claims and provides valuable insights for future work.

4.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is very well-written, clearly organized, and easy to follow. The motivation, methodology, and experimental setup are explained in sufficient detail for the work to be understood and likely reproduced.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement**

1.  **Lack of End-to-End Training:** The proposed architecture relies on optical flow that is pre-computed using a classical algorithm (Brox et al., 2004). While effective, this makes the system a two-stage pipeline rather than a fully end-to-end learnable model. This is a practical limitation, as it introduces a computational bottleneck during pre-processing and prevents the motion representation from being optimized directly for the action recognition task. It would be interesting for the authors to discuss the potential for integrating optical flow estimation into the network itself as a direction for future work.

2.  **Unclear Source of Spatial Stream Improvement:** The spatial stream ConvNet achieves significantly better performance (73.0% on UCF-101 split 1) than the single-frame model reported in Karpathy et al. (2014) (63.3%), despite using similar network architectures. The authors attribute this to their data augmentation strategy (sampling crops from the whole frame rather than just the center). While plausible, a more direct comparison or analysis isolating this factor would strengthen the claim and provide a clearer takeaway for the community.

3.  **Limited Exploration of Temporal Sampling:** The spatial stream's input is derived from 25 equally spaced frames from each video. While this is a reasonable heuristic, the performance of the spatial stream might be sensitive to this sampling strategy. An analysis comparing this to other strategies (e.g., denser sampling, random sampling) could provide further insight into the temporal redundancy of appearance cues for action recognition.

**Overall Assessment**

This is a strong empirical paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of action recognition. The proposed two-stream architecture is a novel and highly effective method for leveraging both appearance and motion information in videos using ConvNets. The experiments are thorough, the results are state-of-the-art, and the paper is clearly presented. While the reliance on pre-computed optical flow is a limitation, the work represents a major step forward for deep learning-based video classification and is certain to be influential. I strongly recommend acceptance.
