- Proposes a two-stream ConvNet architecture for video action recognition with separate spatial (frames) and temporal (dense optical flow) streams fused by late fusion.  
- Spatial stream performs recognition from single frames; temporal stream learns motion from multi-frame dense optical flow.  
- Argues that previous deep video approaches (stacking frames) struggle to learn explicit spatio-temporal motion features.  
- Main contribution: demonstrating ConvNets trained on optical flow achieve strong performance despite limited training data.  
- Shows multi-task learning can augment training data and improve performance across datasets.  
- Experiments on UCF-101 and HMDB-51 report average accuracy over three splits and produce state-of-the-art or near state-of-the-art results.  
- Strength: simple ConvNet application yields very good results on challenging video datasets.  
- Strength: novel use of optical-flow inputs to ConvNets for action recognition.  
- Strength: well-designed experiments validating the approach on two benchmark datasets.  
- Weakness: does not model long-range or whole-video action sequences, only fixed-length adjacent frames.  
- Weakness: lacks an analysis of error modes to clarify limitations and failure cases.  
- Quality score: 8/10 — strong experiments and results but needs more error analysis.  
- Clarity score: 9/10 — clearly written and well-explained, though related work could be more concise.  
- Originality score: 8/10 — optical-flow ConvNet is novel, but overall architecture is similar to existing approaches.  
- Significance score: 9/10 — likely significant impact for video/motion-based vision research.  
- Arguments for acceptance: novel approach, impressive results, validated on two challenging datasets.  
- Arguments against acceptance: omission of whole-video sequence modeling and missing error-mode analysis.
