

This paper introduces a novel neural network architecture for video action classification by integrating predictions from two convolutional neural networks (CNNs): one trained on single video frames and another trained on dense optical flow sequences. The proposed model achieves state-of-the-art performance on two standard video classification benchmarks, significantly outperforming existing ConvNet-based approaches. The work effectively adapts the standard CNN pipeline to optical flow data, with thorough empirical validation demonstrating the efficacy of optical flow as a complementary input modality. The authors’ comparative analysis of multiple optical flow training strategies provides valuable insights, clearly illustrating the benefits of incorporating motion information beyond static frames.

The core contribution is methodologically straightforward—leveraging two distinct CNN streams—but the execution is robust. The spatial stream (frame-based) and temporal stream (optical flow-based) are combined via late fusion, a well-established technique, yet the authors provide a compelling case for why this combination is particularly effective for action recognition. Their experimental design is comprehensive: they evaluate various optical flow preprocessing and training protocols, including different flow computation methods and network architectures, and consistently demonstrate improvements over baselines. This thoroughness is a strength, as it moves beyond a simple "pipeline adaptation" to a nuanced exploration of how optical flow data can be optimally integrated into CNN frameworks.

The results are impressive and well-supported. On the UCF-101 and HMDB-51 datasets, the model achieves higher accuracy than prior ConvNet-based methods and matches or exceeds the performance of more complex, non-ConvNet approaches. The ablation studies effectively isolate the contributions of each component, particularly highlighting the temporal stream’s role in capturing motion dynamics. The authors’ choice to include multiple optical flow training variants (e.g., using different flow inputs or augmentation strategies) adds depth to the analysis and addresses a key practical consideration in video action recognition.

That said, two minor points require clarification to strengthen the paper. First, the spatial stream’s performance significantly exceeds that of the method in [13], despite similar architectural designs. The authors should explicitly discuss potential reasons for this gap—such as differences in training data, hyperparameters, or flow preprocessing—which are not evident from the current presentation. This comparison is critical, as [13] represents a direct predecessor in the field, and the performance discrepancy needs justification to avoid confusion. Second, while the paper uses a fixed frame sampling strategy for the spatial stream, a systematic comparison of different sampling schemes (e.g., uniform, random, or motion-aware sampling) would further validate the robustness of their approach. Such an analysis would also provide practical guidance for future work on input data selection.

The paper is well-written and technically sound, with clear explanations of the methodology and results. The authors avoid overclaiming, positioning their work as a practical, high-performing extension of existing paradigms rather than a theoretical breakthrough. This focus on empirical rigor and reproducibility aligns well with the NIPS community’s emphasis on measurable contributions. The code and data availability (if mentioned) would further enhance its impact, though the draft does not specify this.

Overall, this is a strong empirical contribution that advances the state of the art in video action recognition. The simplicity of the architecture, combined with its effectiveness, makes it a compelling baseline for future work. The minor suggestions above are easily addressable and would elevate an already solid paper. The work will undoubtedly be of interest to researchers in video understanding and CNN applications, particularly those seeking efficient, high-performance solutions for action recognition tasks. I recommend acceptance.
