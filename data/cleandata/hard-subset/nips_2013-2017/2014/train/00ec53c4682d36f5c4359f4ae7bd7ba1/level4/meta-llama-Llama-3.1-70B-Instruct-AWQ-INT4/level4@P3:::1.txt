This paper proposes a model for solving discriminative tasks with video inputs, specifically action recognition in videos. The model consists of two convolutional nets: a spatial net and a temporal net. The input to the spatial net is an individual video frame, while the input to the temporal net is a stack of densely computed optical flow features. Each pathway is trained separately to classify its input, and the prediction for a video is obtained by combining the predictions made by each net through late fusion.

The authors relate their work to previous research in action recognition, including shallow and deep architectures. They discuss the limitations of existing deep architectures, which often rely on stacked video frames as input and struggle to capture motion information effectively. In contrast, the proposed two-stream architecture decouples spatial and temporal recognition streams, allowing for more effective exploitation of motion information.

The paper is well-organized, and the model is clearly explained. The authors provide a thorough evaluation of their model on two datasets, UCF-101 and HMDB-51, and demonstrate its competitiveness with state-of-the-art methods that use intensively hand-crafted features.

Strengths:

* The model is a simple yet effective application of convolutional nets to video data, achieving impressive results on action recognition tasks.
* The use of optical flow features as inputs to a convolutional net is a novel contribution, showing that hand-crafted features can be useful in deep learning models.
* The authors demonstrate the importance of motion information for action recognition, highlighting the benefits of decoupling spatial and temporal recognition streams.

Weaknesses:

* The model does not take into account the overall sequence of actions over the entire video, instead focusing on fixed-length adjacent frames.
* The authors could provide more analysis of the error modes, such as characterizing the types of videos or classes that the model struggles to classify.
* The model's performance on larger datasets, such as Sports-1M, is not evaluated, which could provide further insights into its scalability.

Quality:

* The experiments are well-designed, and the authors provide a thorough evaluation of their model on two datasets.
* The use of multiple evaluation metrics, such as accuracy and mean classification accuracy, provides a comprehensive understanding of the model's performance.

Clarity:

* The paper is clearly written, and the model is well-explained.
* The authors provide sufficient background information on action recognition and deep learning architectures, making the paper accessible to a broad audience.

Originality:

* The application of a convolutional net to optical flow features is novel and demonstrates the potential of using hand-crafted features in deep learning models.
* The two-stream architecture is a unique contribution, highlighting the importance of decoupling spatial and temporal recognition streams for effective action recognition.

Significance:

* This approach could have a significant impact on research in using videos and motion for various vision problems.
* The model's simplicity and effectiveness make it a promising starting point for further research in action recognition and video analysis.

Arguments For Acceptance:

* The paper presents a novel and effective approach to action recognition in videos.
* The use of optical flow features and a two-stream architecture is a unique contribution to the field.
* The model's competitiveness with state-of-the-art methods demonstrates its potential for real-world applications.

Arguments Against Acceptance:

* The model's limitations, such as its focus on fixed-length adjacent frames, may restrict its applicability to certain scenarios.
* The lack of analysis on error modes and the model's performance on larger datasets may raise concerns about its robustness and scalability.
