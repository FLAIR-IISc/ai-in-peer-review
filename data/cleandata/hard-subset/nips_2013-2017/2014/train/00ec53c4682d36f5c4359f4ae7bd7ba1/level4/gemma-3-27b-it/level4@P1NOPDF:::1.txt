Summary:
This paper proposes a model for solving discriminative tasks with video inputs. The model employs two convolutional neural networks: one takes an appearance frame as input, while the other processes a stack of densely computed optical flow features. Each network is trained independently to classify its input, and the final video-level prediction is obtained by taking a (weighted) average of the individual network outputs. 

The model is evaluated on two datasets, achieving impressive results that match, or closely approach, the state-of-the-art performance of methods relying on extensively hand-crafted features.

Strengths:
- This model demonstrates that a straightforward application of convolutional neural networks can yield strong results.
- Existing deep learning models have generally favored learning features directly from pixels, largely bypassing the use of optical flow (or other hand-crafted features). This paper convincingly shows that, at least for action recognition, optical flow fields provide a valuable input representation, representing an important contribution to the field. 

Weaknesses:
- The model currently focuses on fixed-length segments of adjacent frames (L=10) and does not explicitly model the overall sequence of actions within the entire video.

Quality:
The experiments are well-designed. Including an analysis of error modes would provide additional insight; for example, identifying whether the model struggles with specific classes or groups of classes. It would also be valuable to characterize the types of videos on which the model performs poorly.

Clarity:
The paper is clearly written and the model is well-explained.

Originality:
The application of a convolutional neural network to optical flow features is novel.

Significance:
This approach has the potential to significantly impact research in utilizing videos and motion for various computer vision problems. The model offers a simple yet effective application of convolutional neural networks to video data, achieving very promising results. The use of optical flow features as input to a convolutional network represents a novel contribution.
