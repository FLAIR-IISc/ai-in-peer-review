This paper introduces a novel neural network architecture for human action recognition in videos. The proposed model leverages the complementary strengths of two convolutional neural networks: one processing individual video frames (“spatial stream”) and the other processing short sequences of dense optical flow images (“temporal stream”). The final prediction is formed by combining the outputs of these two streams. The authors demonstrate that this approach significantly surpasses the performance of existing ConvNet-based methods for action recognition and achieves competitive results – matching the current state-of-the-art – on two widely used video classification datasets.

The core contribution of this work lies in effectively integrating information from both spatial appearance and motion cues within a deep learning framework. While the approach builds upon established ConvNet image classification techniques, extending them to dense optical flow data and then combining them with a single-frame ConvNet, the experimental validation is comprehensive and the performance gains are substantial. The thorough comparison of various training strategies for the ConvNet operating on optical flow is particularly valuable, clearly demonstrating the benefits of incorporating motion information as input. 

Specifically, the ablation studies regarding optical flow input are well-executed and contribute meaningfully to understanding the efficacy of the temporal stream. The significant performance increase achieved by the spatial stream, however, warrants further investigation. Given the architectural similarities to models reported in [13], it is unclear why the presented spatial stream achieves superior results, and an explanation would strengthen the paper. 

One potential avenue for future work, and a point that would further enhance the empirical analysis, would be a comparative evaluation of different frame sampling strategies for the input to the spatial stream. Exploring the impact of various sampling densities or methods could provide valuable insights into optimizing the spatial stream's performance.

Overall, this is a strong empirical paper that will be of significant interest to researchers working in the field of video classification, particularly those employing convolutional neural networks. The clarity of the presentation and the compelling experimental results make this a valuable contribution to the literature.
