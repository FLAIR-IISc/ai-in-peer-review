This paper proposes a model for solving discriminative tasks with video inputs. The model consists of two convolutional networks. One network takes as input a single frame’s appearance, while the second utilizes a stack of densely computed optical flow features. Each pathway is trained separately to classify its input, and the final prediction for a video is obtained by averaging (or via an SVM) the predictions from each network. 

The model is evaluated on two standard datasets, UCF-101 and HMDB-51, achieving impressive results competitive with or approaching the state-of-the-art methods that rely on extensively hand-crafted features.

**Strengths:**

*   This model represents a straightforward application of convolutional networks that achieves strong performance.
*   Previous deep learning approaches for video analysis have largely focused on learning features directly from pixels. This paper demonstrates that, at least for action recognition, optical flow fields provide a valuable input representation – a noteworthy contribution.
*   The multi-task learning framework effectively leverages data from multiple datasets to improve performance and generalization.

**Weaknesses:**

*   The model considers only a fixed-length window of adjacent frames (L=10) and does not explicitly model the long-term temporal dependencies across the entire video sequence.
*   While the results are competitive, the paper lacks detailed analysis of failure cases. Understanding the types of actions or video characteristics where the model struggles would be beneficial.

**Quality:**

The experiments are well-designed and thoroughly evaluated. It would be insightful if the authors included an analysis of error modes – for example, identifying specific classes or video types that the model consistently misclassifies. Further investigation into the limitations of the model would strengthen the paper.

**Clarity:**

The paper is clearly written and well-organized. The proposed model is explained in a straightforward and accessible manner.

**Originality:**

Applying convolutional networks directly to optical flow features is a novel approach. The two-stream network architecture, decoupling spatial and temporal processing, is a valuable contribution.

**Significance:**

This approach has the potential to significantly impact research in video understanding and motion-based vision problems. The model’s simplicity and promising results suggest a potentially powerful direction for future work. The use of optical flow features as input to convolutional networks represents a novel contribution with broad applicability.

**Arguments Pro Acceptance:**

*   Novel and effective application of deep learning to action recognition.
*   Strong experimental results competitive with the state of the art.
*   Clear and well-written paper.
*   Addresses a significant problem in computer vision.

**Arguments Con Acceptance:**

*   Lack of detailed error analysis.
*   Limited modeling of long-term temporal dependencies.
