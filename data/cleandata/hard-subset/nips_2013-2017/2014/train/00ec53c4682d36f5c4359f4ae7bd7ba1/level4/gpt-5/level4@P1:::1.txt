Summary:
This paper proposes a two-stream convolutional network for action recognition in videos. One stream (spatial) operates on individual appearance frames; the other (temporal) operates on a stack of densely computed optical flow fields across adjacent frames. Each stream is trained independently to classify its input, and their predictions are combined by late fusion (e.g., averaging). The model is evaluated on UCF-101 and HMDB-51, where it achieves impressive performance that matches or closely approaches state-of-the-art methods based on heavily hand-crafted features.

Strengths:
- A clean, straightforward application of convolutional networks that yields strong results on standard benchmarks.
- Demonstrates that optical flow fields, when used as input to a ConvNet, provide a highly effective motion representation for action recognition. Prior deep learning work largely attempted to learn spatiotemporal features directly from stacked RGB frames; this paper shows the value of using optical flow as an intermediate representation. This is an important and timely contribution.

Weaknesses:
- The model does not capture the temporal structure of an entire video sequence. It processes only fixed-length windows (e.g., L=10 consecutive frames), limiting its ability to model long-range dynamics or action ordering across the full video.

Quality:
The experiments are well designed and convincingly show the benefits of the temporal stream based on optical flow and the complementarity of the two streams. The paper would be stronger with an analysis of error modes. For example:
- Which classes or groups of classes are consistently misclassified?
- Are there systematic failure modes (e.g., heavy camera motion, small/fast motion, cluttered scenes)?
- Can the authors characterize the types of videos on which the model underperforms?
Such analysis (e.g., per-class accuracies, confusion matrices, and qualitative failure cases) would provide insight into the limitations of the approach and guide future improvements.

Clarity:
The paper is clearly written and well organized. The two-stream architecture and the construction of the temporal input from dense optical flow are explained clearly.

Originality:
Using a ConvNet directly on optical flow features for the temporal stream is novel and contrasts with earlier deep models that relied on stacked RGB frames to implicitly learn motion features. The two-stream decomposition (appearance vs. motion) makes effective use of complementary information and is well motivated by prior work.

Significance:
This approach is likely to have significant impact on video understanding. It shows that a relatively simple ConvNet-based architecture, when paired with an explicit motion representation, can deliver state-of-the-art performance on challenging action recognition benchmarks. The use of optical flow as input to a ConvNet is a notable and practical contribution that others can build on.
