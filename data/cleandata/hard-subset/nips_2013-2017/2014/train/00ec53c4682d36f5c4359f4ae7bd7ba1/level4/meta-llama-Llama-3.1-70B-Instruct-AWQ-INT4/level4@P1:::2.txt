This paper presents a new neural network architecture for classifying videos of human actions. The model combines the predictions of two convolutional neural networks: one trained on single video frames and the other trained on short sequences of dense optical flow images. The model significantly outperforms other ConvNet-based approaches to action recognition and matches the current state-of-the-art on two standard video classification datasets.

The paper provides a comprehensive overview of the existing work on action recognition in videos, highlighting the challenges and limitations of previous approaches. The authors then introduce their two-stream architecture, which incorporates spatial and temporal networks to capture the complementary information on appearance from still frames and motion between frames. This is a key contribution of the paper, as it allows for the generalization of the best performing hand-crafted features within a data-driven learning framework.

The experimental evaluation is thorough, and the results are impressive. The authors demonstrate that their two-stream architecture outperforms other ConvNet-based approaches and is competitive with the state-of-the-art shallow representations. The use of multi-task learning to increase the amount of training data and improve performance on both datasets is also a notable contribution.

One minor comment is that it is not entirely clear why the spatial network performs so much better than the models from [14], given that the architectures are similar. However, this is a minor point, and the paper's contributions and results are substantial.

It would also be interesting to see a comparison of different sampling schemes for the frames fed into the spatial stream. For example, how does the performance change if the frames are sampled at different rates or if different frame selection strategies are used? This could provide further insights into the strengths and weaknesses of the proposed architecture.

Overall, this is a good empirical paper that will be of interest to anyone working on video classification with ConvNets. The authors have demonstrated a clear understanding of the challenges and limitations of previous approaches and have proposed a novel architecture that addresses these issues. The paper is well-written, and the results are clearly presented.

Arguments for acceptance:

* The paper presents a novel architecture for video classification that combines the predictions of two convolutional neural networks.
* The experimental evaluation is thorough, and the results are impressive, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed architecture.
* The use of multi-task learning to increase the amount of training data and improve performance on both datasets is a notable contribution.
* The paper provides a comprehensive overview of the existing work on action recognition in videos, highlighting the challenges and limitations of previous approaches.

Arguments against acceptance:

* The paper's contributions, while substantial, may be seen as incremental rather than revolutionary.
* The comparison with other ConvNet-based approaches could be more comprehensive, including a wider range of architectures and training protocols.
* Some minor points, such as the comparison with [14], could be clarified or expanded upon.
