This paper proposes a model for solving discriminative tasks with video inputs by leveraging a two-stream convolutional neural network architecture. The model takes as input an appearance frame and a stack of densely computed optical flow features, each processed separately by a distinct pathway. The prediction for a video is obtained by computing a weighted average of the predictions made by each pathway.

The model is evaluated on two datasets, yielding impressive results that match or come close to the state-of-the-art methods, which typically rely on intensively hand-crafted features.

The primary strengths of this model are its simplicity and effectiveness. Unlike previous deep learning models that prefer to learn features directly from pixels, this paper demonstrates the utility of optical flow fields as a useful input representation for action recognition tasks, making it an important contribution to the field.

However, a notable weakness of the model is its inability to account for the overall sequence of actions over the entire video, instead focusing on modeling fixed-length (L=10) adjacent frames.

The experiments conducted in the paper are well-designed, providing a solid foundation for evaluating the model's performance. To further enhance the insights gained from these experiments, it would be beneficial for the authors to include an analysis of the error modes. For instance, identifying classes or groups of classes that the model struggles to classify accurately, or characterizing the types of videos on which the model fails to perform well, would provide valuable information.

The paper is clearly written, with the model being well-explained and easy to follow. The application of a convolutional net to optical flow features is a novel contribution, and the significance of this approach lies in its potential to have a substantial impact on research in using videos and motion for various vision problems. Overall, the model's simplicity and promising results make it a valuable addition to the field.
