- Introduces a two-stream ConvNet architecture separating spatial (single-frame appearance) and temporal (stacked optical flow) processing for video action recognition.  
- Spatial stream operates on individual frames to capture appearance, while temporal stream is trained on stacks of dense optical flow to learn motion patterns.  
- Each stream is trained independently and final video predictions are produced by fusing stream scores (simple averaging and SVM fusion explored).  
- Evaluated on UCF-101 and HMDB-51, achieving impressive results that outperform prior deep learning approaches and rival state-of-the-art hand-crafted methods.  
- Strength: elegantly simple and effective design; explicit separation of appearance and motion is intuitive and powerful.  
- Strength: demonstrates the value of using pre-computed optical flow as a mid-level input representation, leveraging domain knowledge when data is limited.  
- Weakness: limited temporal scope — temporal stream uses a fixed-length L=10 frames, hindering modeling of long-range dependencies in complex actions.  
- Experiments are technically sound with comprehensive ablation studies validating key design choices (stack length, mean subtraction, multi-task learning).  
- Suggested improvement: include error/failure analysis to identify which classes or video types (e.g., heavy camera motion, subtle actions) the model struggles with.  
- Clarity: paper is exceptionally well written, clearly organized, and likely reproducible given detailed descriptions of model and training.  
- Originality: novel and impactful application of ConvNets to stacked optical flow and the first successful modern implementation of a two-stream spatial/temporal approach.  
- Significance: provides a simple yet powerful deep learning baseline that narrows the gap with hand-crafted feature methods and is likely to influence future motion-aware video models.
