Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes a two-stream convolutional architecture for action recognition in video that separates appearance and motion cues. The spatial stream is a ConvNet trained on RGB frames (pre-trained on ImageNet and fine-tuned), capturing objects and scenes correlated with actions. The temporal stream is a ConvNet trained on stacked dense optical flow fields, explicitly representing short-term motion. The authors explore several input configurations for the temporal net (optical-flow stacking vs. trajectory stacking, uni- vs. bi-directional flow, and mean-flow subtraction) and combine the two streams by late fusion (score averaging or a linear SVM). To mitigate limited video data, they adopt a multitask learning setup that shares representation while optimizing separate softmax heads for UCF-101 and HMDB-51.

This work is positioned against prior deep approaches that input stacks of RGB frames and rely on the network to learn motion implicitly (e.g., Karpathy et al., CVPR 2014 [14]), as well as earlier two-stream HMAX-style models (Jhuang et al. [11, 16]). It is also related to the dominant hand-crafted pipelines based on dense trajectories and motion descriptors such as HOF/MBH with Fisher encodings [20, 21, 26, 29]. The authors argue, and provide evidence, that their temporal ConvNet generalizes many of these motion features while benefiting from end-to-end supervised learning. Empirically, on UCF-101 and HMDB-51, the two-stream model substantially outperforms prior deep models and is competitive with state-of-the-art shallow representations.

Quality
The paper is technically sound and carefully executed. The ablations on temporal input design (number of stacked flow fields, trajectory vs. fixed-location stacking, mean-flow subtraction, bi-directional flow) are informative and clearly justify the design choices. The authors fairly compare to “slow fusion” baselines on stacked RGB frames and show the clear advantage of optical flow as input. The multitask learning experiments show tangible gains when training data are limited, especially for HMDB-51.

Two aspects could be strengthened:
- Clarify why the spatial stream outperforms prior ConvNet baselines (e.g., [14]). The text suggests better data augmentation (sampling from the whole image) improves ImageNet pre-training, but reporting a controlled comparison on UCF-101/HMDB-51 with matched training protocols would help attribute the gains.
- Provide more detail on computational trade-offs: the cost of precomputing optical flow (time, storage) vs. accuracy benefits, and inference throughput for each stream and the fused model.

Clarity
The paper is generally well written and organized, with precise descriptions of input construction and training/testing pipelines. The relation between the temporal ConvNet and traditional motion features is insightful. Minor suggestions:
- Consolidate the notation in Section 3.1 and give a brief diagram mapping equations (1) and (2) to the visual examples for readability.
- When reporting ablation results, include mean ± std over splits when available, and list the exact L values and flow algorithms in captions to ease reproduction.
- Ensure reference formatting is consistent and complete.

Originality
The work is novel in its explicit two-stream ConvNet formulation with a motion stream trained on stacked dense optical flow, coupled with systematic ablations. The multitask learning application to share supervision across action datasets is a useful and, at the time, relatively underexplored idea in video recognition. While optical flow and late fusion are established components, the overall design and empirical validation provide a clear, well-supported contribution beyond prior deep approaches that used raw frames.

Significance
Significance is high. The approach bridges the gap between hand-crafted motion features and deep learning, offers a practical recipe that improves over prior ConvNet baselines by a large margin, and yields results competitive with the best trajectory-based methods. The two-stream paradigm is likely to be adopted and extended by the community, and the multitask training recipe is directly useful when labeled video data are scarce.

Specific strengths
- Clear architectural decomposition into spatial and temporal streams, grounded in prior neuroscience-inspired two-pathway hypotheses.
- Strong ablations demonstrating why optical flow stacking, mean subtraction, and late fusion matter.
- Pragmatic training strategy: ImageNet pre-training for the spatial stream, multitask learning to leverage multiple action datasets.
- Competitive or superior performance to prior deep baselines on UCF-101 and HMDB-51.

Specific weaknesses and suggestions
- Fairness and attribution: Provide head-to-head comparisons with prior deep models under matched pre-training, augmentation, and fine-tuning settings to isolate which changes drive improvements.
- Sampling policy in the spatial stream: Compare frame sampling schemes (uniform vs. random; using more frames per video; multi-crop strategies) as suggested in the draft—this could increase spatial stream strength with negligible cost.
- Flow sensitivity: Assess robustness to different optical flow algorithms (e.g., Brox [2] vs. TV-L1) and to the chosen L; discuss trade-offs between accuracy and speed/quality of flow.
- Quantization: Since flow is rescaled and JPEG-compressed, report whether this impacts accuracy vs. using raw float flow.
- Efficiency and reproducibility: Report training/inference time per video, GPU memory for each stream, and whether models/flow preprocessing will be released.
- Fusion: Briefly explore earlier fusion (e.g., fc-level joint training with stronger regularization) or lightweight calibration of softmax outputs before SVM.

Pros for acceptance
- Strong empirical results; clear and well-motivated architecture.
- Thorough ablations that provide insight.
- Practical contribution (multitask learning) to address data scarcity.
- Likely to influence subsequent work in video understanding.

Cons for acceptance
- Limited analysis of computational cost and deployment implications due to optical flow precomputation.
- Some ambiguity in attributing gains of the spatial stream relative to prior ConvNet baselines.
- Scope of evaluations restricted to two datasets; no per-class or error analysis.

Overall assessment
This is a solid and timely contribution that advances deep learning for action recognition by explicitly modeling motion with a temporal ConvNet and fusing it with an appearance stream. The methodology is careful, the experiments are convincing, and the results are impactful. I recommend acceptance after addressing the clarifications above.
