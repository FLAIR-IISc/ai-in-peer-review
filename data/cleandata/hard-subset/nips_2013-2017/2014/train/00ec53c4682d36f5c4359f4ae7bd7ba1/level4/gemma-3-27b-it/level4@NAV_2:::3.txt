The paper investigates architectures of discriminatively trained Convolutional Networks (ConvNets) for action recognition in videos, addressing the challenge of capturing complementary appearance and motion information. The authors propose a two-stream ConvNet architecture incorporating spatial and temporal networks, demonstrate the effectiveness of a ConvNet trained on multi-frame dense optical flow, and show that multi-task learning across datasets improves performance. The work is evaluated on UCF-101 and HMDB-51, achieving competitive results with the state of the art and surpassing previous deep learning approaches for video classification.

This paper builds upon the growing body of work applying deep learning to video understanding. The investigation of a two-stream architecture is a key contribution, effectively decoupling spatial and temporal processing and allowing for pre-training on large image datasets. This approach contrasts with previous attempts to directly apply ConvNets to stacked video frames [14], which showed limited success. The use of dense optical flow in the temporal stream is well-motivated and demonstrates the importance of explicit motion modeling. Finally, the application of multi-task learning provides a practical strategy for improving performance in data-limited settings. The methodology is described in detail, and the experimental setup is thorough.

The strengths of this paper lie in its clear presentation, technical soundness, and promising results. The two-stream architecture, coupled with optical flow, represents a significant advancement over previous approaches. The experimental results demonstrate that the proposed method achieves competitive performance on standard benchmarks, exceeding prior deep learning efforts and approaching the accuracy of hand-crafted features. The inclusion of ablation studies (e.g., different input configurations for the temporal stream, multi-task learning) strengthens the analysis and provides valuable insights. The detailed implementation notes, including training procedures and GPU utilization, are also commendable. 

However, the paper could benefit from a more focused narrative. While the three contributions are individually strong, the paper feels somewhat fragmented. A clearer articulation of the overarching theme – perhaps emphasizing the benefits of explicitly modeling motion – would enhance the impact. Additionally, while the related work section is comprehensive, it could benefit from including a few key citations. Specifically, the work on learning hierarchical spatio-temporal features with Independent Subspace Analysis [Le et al., CVPR 2011] and the evaluation of local spatio-temporal features [Wang et al., BMVC 2010] would be relevant additions.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel and well-motivated two-stream architecture.
*   Significant performance improvements over previous deep learning methods for video classification.
*   Competitive results compared to state-of-the-art hand-crafted features.
*   Thorough experimental evaluation and detailed implementation notes.
*   Demonstrates the effectiveness of incorporating optical flow information.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The paper feels somewhat fragmented, lacking a single, unifying theme.
*   A few relevant citations are missing from the related work section.

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of video action recognition. The proposed methods are novel, the results are compelling, and the presentation is clear. I recommend acceptance to NIPS 2014, contingent upon the authors addressing the minor suggestions regarding focus and completeness of the related work.
