

Summary:
This paper introduces a model for discriminative video tasks using a dual-convolutional-network architecture. One network processes appearance frames, while the other processes a stack of densely computed optical flow features. Each network is trained independently for classification, and the final video prediction combines their outputs via a (weighted) average. Evaluated on two datasets, the model achieves results that match or nearly match state-of-the-art methods relying on extensively hand-crafted features.

Strengths:
- The model presents a simple yet effective application of convolutional networks, achieving strong results.
- Previous deep learning approaches for vision tasks have largely avoided optical flow or other hand-crafted features, instead learning directly from pixels. This work demonstrates that optical flow fields provide a valuable input representation for action recognition, representing an important contribution.

Weaknesses:
- The model processes only a fixed window of 10 consecutive frames, failing to capture long-range temporal dynamics or the full sequence of actions within a video.

Quality:
The experimental design is sound. Including an analysis of error modes—such as identifying specific classes or video types where the model underperforms—would provide deeper insights.

Clarity:
The paper is clearly written, with the model well-explained.

Originality:
Applying convolutional networks to optical flow features constitutes a novel approach.

Significance:
This method holds significant potential for advancing research in video-based vision problems. Its simplicity, strong performance on video data, and novel use of optical flow features as network inputs represent a meaningful contribution to the field.
