

This paper introduces a two-stream convolutional network architecture for video action recognition, addressing the challenge of integrating appearance and motion information. The authors propose a spatial stream (processing individual frames) and a temporal stream (processing multi-frame optical flow), demonstrating that this decoupled approach significantly outperforms prior deep learning methods for video classification. Key contributions include: (1) a two-stream ConvNet architecture that leverages complementary spatial and temporal cues, (2) evidence that optical flow-based training achieves strong performance despite limited video data, and (3) a multi-task learning framework to combine datasets (UCF-101/HMDB-51) and boost performance. The work builds on the two-stream hypothesis in vision science [9] and extends recent deep learning efforts like [14] (Karpathy et al., 2014), which used stacked RGB frames but underperformed hand-crafted features.

**Quality**: The technical approach is sound and thoroughly validated. Experiments on UCF-101 and HMDB-51 demonstrate clear improvements over prior deep learning methods (e.g., 81.5% vs. 65.4% on UCF-101 [14]), with ablation studies confirming the necessity of optical flow input and multi-task learning. The authors address limitations (e.g., motion compensation via mean subtraction) and validate claims through controlled comparisons (e.g., trajectory vs. optical flow stacking). The implementation details (e.g., optical flow pre-computation, multi-GPU training) are well-documented, supporting reproducibility.

**Clarity**: The paper is exceptionally clear, with logical organization and precise descriptions of the two-stream architecture, input configurations, and multi-task learning. Figures (e.g., Fig. 3) effectively illustrate the optical flow input derivation. Minor improvements could enhance focus: the abstract and introduction could better unify the narrative around the *core insight* (optical flow enables superior motion modeling for deep networks), rather than treating two-stream and multi-task learning as separate contributions. The discussion of related work [1.1] properly contextualizes the work against shallow methods [17, 20, 26] and prior deep approaches [14, 16].

**Originality**: The two-stream architecture represents a meaningful advance. While [16] proposed a two-stream *hand-crafted* HMAX model, this work demonstrates that *discriminatively trained ConvNets* on optical flow (not RGB stacks) unlock superior performance. The temporal stream generalizes hand-crafted features (e.g., HOF, MBH) via convolutional layers, as shown in the supplementary visualizations. The multi-task learning framework also provides a principled way to leverage cross-dataset data, addressing a key bottleneck in video action recognition.

**Significance**: The results are highly significant for video understanding. The method achieves state-of-the-art performance (competitive with [20, 21, 26]) while using a data-driven approach that avoids heavy hand-crafting. Crucially, it demonstrates that optical flow input—despite being computed via generic assumptions [2]—enables deep networks to effectively model motion, resolving a key limitation in [14]. The multi-task learning framework also offers a practical solution for data-scarce settings, with broad applicability beyond action recognition.

**Strengths**:  
- Clear technical innovation (two-stream ConvNets for video)  
- Rigorous empirical validation across datasets  
- Practical implementation details (e.g., optical flow compression)  
- Strong results (e.g., 6% improvement over temporal stream alone)  

**Weaknesses**:  
- The narrative could better unify the two-stream and multi-task contributions as complementary facets of a single framework.  
- The connection to prior deep learning work (e.g., [18], [27]) could be more explicit in the introduction (the authors cite these papers but do not contrast their approaches with the proposed two-stream method).  

**Recommendation**:  
The paper makes a strong, well-executed contribution to video recognition that advances the state of the art with clear technical and practical value. The two-stream architecture resolves a critical limitation of prior deep learning methods for video, and the multi-task learning framework provides a valuable tool for data efficiency. While the narrative could be tightened, the work is technically robust, well-written, and significant for the field. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions to:  
1. Refine the introduction to emphasize the *unifying theme* of motion modeling via optical flow within a deep learning framework.  
2. Explicitly contrast the two-stream approach with prior deep learning work (e.g., [18], [27]) in the introduction, noting how their methods differ from the proposed end-to-end optical flow ConvNet.  

These adjustments would strengthen the paper’s contribution without altering the core technical merits. The results are compelling, and the work aligns well with NIPS’ focus on high-impact, scientifically rigorous contributions to machine learning.
