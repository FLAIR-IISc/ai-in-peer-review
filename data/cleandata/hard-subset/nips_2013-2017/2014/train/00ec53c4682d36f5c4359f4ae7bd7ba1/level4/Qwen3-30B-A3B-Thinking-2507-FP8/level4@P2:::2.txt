

This paper introduces a two-stream convolutional network architecture for video action recognition, combining a spatial stream trained on individual video frames with a temporal stream trained on dense optical flow. The authors demonstrate that this approach significantly outperforms previous deep learning methods for video classification while achieving competitive results with state-of-the-art hand-crafted features on UCF-101 and HMDB-51 benchmarks.

The work builds upon the two-stream hypothesis in visual processing (Goodale & Milner, 1992) and extends recent advances in deep learning for image recognition (Krizhevsky et al., 2012; Simonyan & Zisserman, 2014) to the video domain. While previous efforts to apply ConvNets to video (e.g., Karpathy et al., 2014; Ji et al., 2013) have struggled to match hand-crafted features, this paper demonstrates that explicitly representing motion through optical flow as input to a ConvNet enables superior performance. The authors also introduce a multi-task learning framework to effectively leverage multiple datasets, addressing the challenge of limited video training data.

The paper excels in Quality. The experimental design is thorough, with careful ablation studies comparing different optical flow input configurations (stacking, trajectory-based, bi-directional), fusion methods (averaging vs. SVM), and multi-task learning approaches. The results consistently demonstrate that the temporal stream provides complementary information to the spatial stream, with the combined model significantly outperforming single-stream approaches and previous deep learning methods. The authors provide detailed implementation specifics, including optical flow computation methods, training protocols, and network configurations, making their work highly reproducible.

The paper demonstrates strong Originality by effectively generalizing hand-crafted motion features through a data-driven learning framework. The temporal stream ConvNet essentially learns to extract motion features that would otherwise require manual design (e.g., HOF, MBH), showing that deep learning can automatically discover effective representations for video action recognition. The authors clearly differentiate their approach from previous two-stream models (e.g., Kuehne et al., 2011), which used shallow HMAX architectures rather than deep ConvNets.

The work shows considerable Significance by establishing a new benchmark for deep learning-based video action recognition. The approach achieves state-of-the-art results on standard benchmarks without requiring complex hand-crafted features, suggesting that this architecture could become the foundation for future work in video understanding. The multi-task learning framework also provides a practical solution to the limited data problem in video recognition.

The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with appropriate visualizations of the network architecture and input configurations. The writing effectively communicates technical details while maintaining accessibility for readers familiar with deep learning for image recognition.

Several minor weaknesses could be addressed to strengthen the paper. First, the authors note that the spatial stream alone achieves competitive performance (around 65% on UCF-101), but it's not clear why their spatial stream outperforms similar models in Karpathy et al. (2014) by such a large margin (15-20% points). A more detailed comparison of implementation differences would be valuable. Second, while the paper compares different optical flow configurations, it doesn't explore alternative motion representations that might further improve performance, such as the trajectory-based features mentioned in the related work.

The paper could also benefit from a more detailed discussion of computational efficiency. The authors note that training on multiple GPUs provides a 3.2x speedup, but it would be helpful to understand the practical constraints of deploying this approach (e.g., inference time, memory requirements).

Overall, this is an outstanding contribution that significantly advances video action recognition. The two-stream architecture provides a simple yet powerful framework for incorporating both appearance and motion information, and the experimental results are compelling. The authors have made a substantial contribution to the field by demonstrating that deep learning can effectively replace hand-crafted motion features while achieving state-of-the-art performance.

Strengths: 
- Thorough experimental evaluation with multiple ablation studies
- Clear demonstration of complementary nature of spatial and temporal streams
- Practical multi-task learning framework to address data limitations
- Reproducible implementation details
- Strong results that outperform previous deep learning approaches by a large margin

Weaknesses:
- Insufficient explanation for superior performance of spatial stream compared to prior work
- Limited exploration of alternative motion representations
- Need for more detailed discussion of computational constraints

Recommendation: Accept. This paper makes a significant contribution to the field with a well-executed approach that achieves state-of-the-art results on standard benchmarks. The two-stream architecture provides a principled way to incorporate both appearance and motion information, and the results demonstrate that deep learning can effectively replace hand-crafted features for video action recognition. The paper is technically sound, well-written, and offers clear directions for future work.
