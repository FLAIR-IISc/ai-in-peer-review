This paper introduces a novel two-stream convolutional neural network (ConvNet) architecture for the task of human action recognition in videos. The model's key innovation is to process spatial and temporal information separately and then combine their predictions. The first stream, the "spatial network," operates on individual video frames to learn features from static scene and object appearance. The second stream, the "temporal network," is trained on sequences of dense optical flow fields, allowing it to explicitly learn patterns of motion between frames. The final classification is achieved by fusing the outputs of these two complementary networks.

The authors demonstrate that this two-stream architecture significantly outperforms previous ConvNet-based approaches and achieves state-of-the-art performance on two standard benchmarks, UCF-101 and HMDB-51.

**Strengths:**

The primary strength of this work lies in its simple, elegant, and highly effective approach to incorporating motion information into a deep learning framework for video analysis. While the individual components—ConvNets for image classification—are not new, the application of this powerful machinery to dense optical flow fields and the subsequent fusion with a spatial network is a significant and well-motivated contribution.

The experimental validation is thorough and compelling. I particularly commend the authors for their detailed ablation studies on the temporal stream, where they systematically compare different methods for training a ConvNet with optical flow. These experiments clearly isolate and quantify the benefits of using stacked optical flow fields as input, providing valuable insights that will benefit the community. The impressive performance of the combined model firmly establishes the two-stream architecture as a formidable new baseline for action recognition.

**Points for Clarification and Minor Suggestions:**

While the paper is strong, a few points could be clarified to further strengthen the contribution:

1.  **Comparison to Prior Work:** The authors report that their spatial network, when trained alone, achieves substantially better results than the single-frame models presented in Karpathy et al. [13], despite the network architectures being similar. Could the authors elaborate on the key factors contributing to this significant performance gap? It would be helpful to understand whether this is due to differences in pre-training, data augmentation, optimization, or other implementation details. This clarification would help readers better contextualize the performance of the spatial stream.

2.  **Frame Sampling Strategy:** The spatial stream operates on individual frames sampled from the video. The paper could be improved by a brief discussion of the sampling strategy used. Furthermore, it would be interesting to see an analysis comparing different temporal sampling schemes for the spatial stream (e.g., a single random frame vs. an average prediction over several uniformly sampled frames). Understanding the sensitivity of the spatial stream's performance to this choice would be a valuable addition.

**Conclusion:**

This is an excellent empirical paper that presents a clean, powerful, and well-executed architecture for video classification. It convincingly demonstrates the value of explicitly modeling motion via optical flow within a ConvNet framework, and the results clearly show the complementary nature of appearance and motion cues. The work is poised to have a significant impact on the field and will be of high interest to researchers working on video analysis and computer vision. I strongly recommend acceptance.
