Summary:
This paper proposes a model for solving discriminative tasks with video inputs. The model comprises two convolutional neural networks. One network receives an appearance frame as input, while the other processes a stack of densely computed optical flow features. Each pathway is trained independently to classify its input, and the final video-level prediction is obtained by taking a (weighted) average of the predictions from each network. The model is evaluated on two datasets, achieving impressive results that match, or closely approach, the performance of state-of-the-art methods relying on extensively hand-crafted features.

Strengths:
- This model represents a straightforward application of convolutional neural networks that achieves strong performance.
- Prior deep learning models for video tasks have largely focused on learning features directly from pixels, often eschewing the use of optical flow or other hand-crafted features. This paper demonstrates that, at least for action recognition, optical flow fields remain a valuable input representation. This is an important contribution to the field.

Weaknesses:
- The model does not explicitly consider the temporal relationships between actions across the entire video sequence; it instead focuses on modeling fixed-length segments of adjacent frames (L=10).

Quality:
The experiments are well-designed. Further insight could be gained by including an analysis of error modes. For example, are there specific classes or groups of classes that the model consistently misclassifies? Is it possible to characterize the types of videos on which the model performs poorly?

Clarity:
The paper is clearly written and the model is well-explained.

Originality:
The application of a convolutional neural network to optical flow features is novel.

Significance:
This approach has the potential to significantly impact research on utilizing videos and motion in various vision problems. The model provides a simple yet effective application of convolutional networks to video data and yields promising results. The use of optical flow features as inputs to a convolutional network represents a novel contribution.
