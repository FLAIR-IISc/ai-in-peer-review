### Review of "Two-Stream Convolutional Networks for Action Recognition in Videos"

**Summary of the Paper**

This paper proposes a novel deep learning architecture for video-based action recognition, termed the "two-stream ConvNet." The core idea is to decouple the learning of spatial and temporal features into two separate, parallel convolutional networks. The spatial stream operates on individual video frames, capturing appearance information, and is pre-trained on the large-scale ImageNet dataset. The temporal stream, the main novelty of this work, is trained to recognize action from motion by taking stacked dense optical flow fields as input. This approach explicitly provides motion information to the network, in contrast to prior deep learning methods (e.g., Karpathy et al., CVPR 2014) that attempted to learn motion implicitly from stacked raw RGB frames with limited success. The outputs of the two streams are combined at the end using a late fusion technique (averaging or an SVM). Additionally, the authors introduce a multi-task learning framework to leverage multiple, smaller video datasets (UCF-101 and HMDB-51) simultaneously, effectively increasing the training data and mitigating overfitting, particularly for the temporal stream which cannot be pre-trained on still image datasets. The authors present a thorough empirical evaluation, demonstrating that their two-stream approach significantly outperforms previous deep learning models and achieves results competitive with the state-of-the-art hand-crafted feature-based methods on the UCF-101 and HMDB-51 benchmarks.

**Strengths (Arguments for Acceptance)**

1.  **Novel and Effective Architecture:** The central contribution—the two-stream architecture—is both simple and highly effective. The insight to treat appearance and motion as separate problems and feed the network with pre-computed optical flow is a significant conceptual advance. It directly addresses a key challenge in video understanding: how to effectively model temporal dynamics within a ConvNet framework, especially with limited data. The results convincingly show that this explicit motion representation is far more effective than trying to learn motion from scratch using raw pixel values.

2.  **Strong Empirical Results and Thorough Analysis:** The paper is supported by a comprehensive set of experiments and ablation studies. The authors methodically validate each component of their proposed system:
    *   They clearly demonstrate the value of pre-training the spatial stream on ImageNet.
    *   They investigate multiple configurations for the temporal stream input (e.g., optical flow stacking vs. trajectory stacking, number of frames, mean subtraction), providing valuable insights into what makes the temporal stream effective.
    *   The experiments on multi-task learning provide a principled solution to the common problem of data scarcity in video action recognition.
    *   Most importantly, the final results are state-of-the-art or near state-of-the-art at the time of submission, substantially advancing the performance of deep learning models on this task.

3.  **High Significance and Impact:** This work is likely to be highly influential. The demonstration that a temporal stream trained on optical flow provides a massive performance boost is a key finding that subsequent research will build upon. The two-stream concept provides a strong and practical baseline for future work in video analysis and has the potential to become a standard approach in the field.

4.  **Clarity and Reproducibility:** The paper is exceptionally well-written, clearly organized, and easy to follow. The authors provide sufficient implementation details (network architecture, training procedures, data handling) that would allow an expert reader to reproduce the main results.

**Weaknesses (Suggestions for Improvement)**

1.  **Framing of Contributions:** The paper presents several valuable contributions: the two-stream architecture, the use of optical flow as a ConvNet input, and the multi-task learning framework. While all are well-executed, the abstract and introduction could be framed to more strongly emphasize the two-stream architecture and the success of the temporal stream as the primary, unifying contribution. The multi-task learning, while important, feels more like a necessary training strategy to make the temporal stream work well on smaller datasets, rather than a standalone core idea. A more focused narrative could strengthen the paper's message.

2.  **Simplicity of Fusion:** The two streams are combined via late fusion (averaging or an SVM on softmax scores), which treats them as almost completely independent until the final prediction. While effective, this is a relatively simple approach. It would be beneficial for the authors to briefly discuss the limitations of this method and speculate on potential future work exploring earlier, more integrated fusion strategies. They correctly note that training a joint model led to overfitting, but this is a valuable point to include in the discussion as a direction for improvement.

3.  **Computational Cost of Optical Flow:** A practical limitation of the proposed method is the need to pre-compute and store dense optical flow for all videos, which can be computationally expensive and require significant storage (though the authors offer a practical compression solution). A short discussion on this practical trade-off in the conclusion would be helpful.

**Recommendation**

Accept.

This is an excellent paper presenting a significant contribution to the field of video understanding. The proposed two-stream architecture is a novel and powerful idea that effectively addresses the limitations of previous deep learning models for action recognition. The claims are substantiated by rigorous and extensive experiments, and the results are competitive with the state-of-the-art. The work is clearly presented and is poised to have a major impact on subsequent research. The identified weaknesses are minor and mainly relate to the framing of the contributions and potential avenues for future work, and do not detract from the overall quality and importance of the paper.
