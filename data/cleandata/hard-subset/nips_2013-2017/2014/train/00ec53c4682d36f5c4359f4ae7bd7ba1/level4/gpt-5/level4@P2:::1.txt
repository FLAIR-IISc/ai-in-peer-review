Summary:
This paper presents a two-stream convolutional architecture for video action recognition. The spatial stream operates on individual RGB frames to capture appearance cues, while the temporal stream operates on stacks of densely computed optical flow fields to capture motion. Each stream is trained independently for classification, and video-level predictions are obtained via late fusion (e.g., averaging). The model is evaluated on UCF-101 and HMDB-51 and achieves results that are competitive with state-of-the-art methods relying on heavily hand-crafted trajectory-based features, while substantially outperforming previous deep models trained directly on raw frames.

Strengths:
- Clear, simple design that leverages ConvNets effectively for video. The separation of spatial and temporal cues and their late fusion is intuitive and practical.
- The paper makes a compelling case that optical flow is a strong, discriminative input representation for temporal modeling in action recognition, contrasting with prior deep approaches that attempted to learn motion features directly from stacks of frames.
- Empirical results are strong across two standard benchmarks and highlight the complementarity of the two streams.

Weaknesses:
- Temporal modeling is limited to short clips of fixed length (e.g., L=10 frames), without any explicit mechanism for capturing longer-range temporal structure or sequence dynamics across entire videos.

Quality:
The experimental design is sound, with sensible training protocols and clear comparisons. The study would be further strengthened by an error analysis examining failure modes: Which classes or class groups are most challenging? Are there characteristic properties (e.g., camera motion, occlusion, subtle motions) that correlate with errors? Such analysis could guide future architectural improvements or data augmentation strategies.

Clarity:
The paper is clearly written and well organized. The two-stream model and temporal input configurations are described in sufficient detail to be understood and reproduced.

Originality:
While ConvNets for video have been explored, applying a ConvNet directly to stacked dense optical flow for temporal modeling, and combining it with a separate spatial ConvNet via late fusion, is a novel and well-motivated contribution.

Significance:
The approach has the potential to influence how motion information is incorporated into deep video recognition systems. The results are promising and suggest that explicitly providing optical flow to a ConvNet can be more effective than learning motion features solely from raw frames, particularly when training data is limited.

Pros for acceptance:
- Strong empirical performance on standard benchmarks with a simple, reproducible architecture.
- Demonstrates the effectiveness of optical flow as a ConvNet input for action recognition.
- Clear evidence of complementarity between spatial and temporal cues.

Cons for acceptance:
- Limited temporal receptive field; no modeling of long-range dependencies across entire videos.
- Lacks deeper analysis of failure cases, which would offer insights for further improvements.
