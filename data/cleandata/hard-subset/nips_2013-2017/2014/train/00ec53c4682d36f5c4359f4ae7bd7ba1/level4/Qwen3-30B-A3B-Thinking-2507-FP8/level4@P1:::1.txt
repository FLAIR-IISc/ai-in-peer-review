

This paper introduces a two-stream convolutional network architecture for action recognition in videos, addressing the challenge of effectively combining appearance and motion information. The authors propose a spatial stream that processes individual video frames (leveraging pre-training on ImageNet) and a temporal stream that processes multi-frame dense optical flow. The streams are trained separately and combined via late fusion, achieving competitive results with state-of-the-art methods on UCF-101 and HMDB-51. This work extends previous deep learning approaches for video classification [14, 16], which either used stacked frames (resulting in inferior performance) or employed hand-crafted shallow representations.

Strengths:
The paper makes a significant contribution by demonstrating that optical flow, a well-established feature in shallow video recognition [20, 21, 26], can be effectively integrated into a deep learning framework. This approach successfully bridges the gap between traditional hand-crafted features and data-driven learning, showing that a ConvNet trained on optical flow outperforms previous deep learning attempts on video data [14]. The multi-task learning framework for combining UCF-101 and HMDB-51 datasets is particularly valuable, as it addresses the challenge of limited training data for video action recognition. The thorough ablation study examining input configurations (optical flow stacking, trajectory stacking, bi-directional flow, etc.) provides important insights into the design of temporal networks.

Weaknesses:
While the two-stream architecture is compelling, the temporal stream processes only a fixed-length sequence of frames (L=10), which may limit its ability to capture long-range temporal dependencies in complex actions. The authors acknowledge this limitation but do not explore architectural modifications to address it (e.g., recurrent networks or temporal modeling beyond fixed-length sequences). Additionally, the paper does not provide detailed analysis of error cases—identifying specific action classes or video types where the model performs poorly would strengthen the evaluation.

Quality:
The experimental design is rigorous and well-executed. The authors compare against relevant baselines, perform thorough ablation studies, and validate on standard benchmarks. The multi-task learning approach is particularly well-justified and effectively demonstrates how to leverage multiple datasets. However, the paper would benefit from a more detailed analysis of error patterns—particularly which action categories the model struggles with and whether these correspond to specific motion characteristics or visual ambiguities.

Clarity:
The paper is exceptionally clear and well-organized. The two-stream architecture is well-motivated, the technical details of the temporal stream (including the different optical flow input configurations) are thoroughly explained, and the experimental results are presented with appropriate detail. The figures effectively illustrate the input configurations for the temporal stream.

Originality:
The core idea of using optical flow as input to a convolutional network for video action recognition is novel in the context of deep learning approaches for video. While optical flow has been used in shallow video recognition [20, 21, 26], this paper demonstrates its effectiveness within a deep learning framework, which represents a significant shift from previous work that attempted to learn spatio-temporal features directly from pixels [14]. The temporal stream's ability to generalize hand-crafted features through a data-driven approach is particularly novel.

Significance:
This work has substantial significance for the field. It demonstrates that deep learning can effectively leverage domain-specific features (optical flow) rather than attempting to learn all features from raw pixels, which may be challenging for video data. The results show that the two-stream architecture significantly outperforms previous deep learning approaches for video classification [14] and achieves competitive performance with the best hand-crafted methods. The multi-task learning framework provides a practical solution to the limited training data problem in video action recognition, which is broadly applicable to other video tasks. This approach could inspire similar integrations of domain knowledge in deep learning for other video understanding tasks.

Overall, this is a strong contribution that effectively bridges deep learning and traditional video recognition techniques. The paper is technically sound, well-written, and addresses an important problem in video understanding. While the limitation of fixed-length frame processing is noteworthy, the proposed approach represents a meaningful advance in the field. I recommend acceptance.
