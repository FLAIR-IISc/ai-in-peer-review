Summary:
This paper proposes a model for solving discriminative tasks with video inputs. The model consists of two convolutional networks: one takes an appearance frame as input, and the other takes a stack of densely computed optical flow features. Each pathway is trained separately to classify its input. The video-level prediction is obtained by taking a (weighted) average of the predictions made by each network. The model is evaluated on two datasets and achieves impressive results that match or come close to state-of-the-art methods relying on intensively hand-crafted features.

Strengths:
- A simple application of convolutional networks that achieves strong results.
- Prior deep learning models have generally avoided using optical flow (or other hand-crafted features), preferring to learn all features directly from pixels. This paper shows that, at least for action recognition, optical flow fields are a valuable input representation. This is an important contribution.

Weaknesses:
- The model does not capture the overall sequence of actions across an entire video; it operates only on fixed-length windows of adjacent frames (L=10).

Quality:
The experiments are well designed. The paper would be more insightful with an analysis of error modes. For example: Are there specific classes or groups of classes that the model struggles to classify? Is it possible to characterize the types of videos on which the model performs poorly?

Clarity:
The paper is clearly written, and the model is well explained.

Originality:
Applying a convolutional network to optical flow features is novel.

Significance:
This approach could have a significant impact on research that uses videos and motion for various vision problems. The model is a straightforward application of convolutional networks to video data and achieves very promising results. The use of optical flow features as inputs to a convolutional network is a novel contribution.
