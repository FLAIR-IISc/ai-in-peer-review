**Summary:**

This paper introduces a two-stream convolutional network model for discriminative tasks on video data. The model consists of two separate convolutional networks (ConvNets). The first network, the spatial stream, takes a single video frame as input to model appearance. The second network, the temporal stream, processes a stack of dense optical flow fields derived from consecutive frames to model motion. Each stream is trained independently for classification. The final prediction for a given video is obtained by fusing the predictions from the two networks, for instance, via a weighted average.

The model is evaluated on two standard action recognition datasets. The results are impressive, achieving performance that is on par with or very close to state-of-the-art methods that rely on extensively hand-crafted features.

**Strengths:**

*   The model represents a straightforward yet highly effective application of convolutional networks to the video domain, achieving excellent results.
*   A key contribution of this work is demonstrating the value of optical flow as an input representation for a deep network. Whereas many previous deep learning approaches focused on learning all features directly from raw pixels, this paper shows that for action recognition, leveraging a well-established motion representation like optical flow is highly beneficial.

**Weaknesses:**

*   A primary limitation is that the model does not capture long-range temporal dynamics. It operates on short, fixed-length snippets of L=10 frames and therefore cannot model the full sequence of an action as it unfolds over an entire video.

**Quality:**

The experiments are well-designed and executed. The paper's analysis would be more insightful if the authors included a discussion of the model's error modes. For example, are there specific classes or groups of classes that the model struggles to classify correctly? Can the types of videos on which the model performs poorly be characterized? Such an analysis would provide valuable insights.

**Clarity:**

The paper is well-written and clear. The proposed model and its architecture are explained thoroughly.

**Originality:**

The application of a convolutional network to dense optical flow fields as a distinct "temporal stream" is a novel contribution.

**Significance:**

This approach is poised to have a significant impact on research involving video and motion analysis for computer vision problems. By demonstrating that a relatively simple architecture can achieve very promising results, this work provides a strong baseline for future video-based deep learning. The successful use of optical flow as an input to a ConvNet highlights a promising direction for combining learned and engineered features.
