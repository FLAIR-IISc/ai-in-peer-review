

This paper introduces a compelling two-stream ConvNet architecture for video action recognition, addressing key limitations in prior deep learning approaches to video classification. The authors propose decoupling spatial (appearance) and temporal (motion) processing streams, each implemented as a ConvNet, with late fusion of their outputs. Crucially, the temporal stream processes dense optical flow (computed via standard energy minimization) rather than raw frame stacks, demonstrating that this explicit motion representation significantly outperforms implicit motion learning. The paper also introduces multi-task learning to combine training data from UCF-101 and HMDB-51, overcoming dataset size limitations. Results on standard benchmarks (UCF-101, HMDB-51) show the method achieves competitive performance with state-of-the-art shallow features while significantly surpassing prior deep learning attempts (e.g., [14]).

The work is technically sound and well-executed. The two-stream design is rigorously motivated by the two-stream hypothesis in vision science and validated through ablation studies. Key findings—such as the superiority of multi-frame optical flow over single-frame input (Table 1b), the marginal benefit of trajectory stacking vs. optical flow stacking, and the effectiveness of multi-task learning (Table 2)—are clearly supported by thorough experiments. The implementation details (Section 5) are sufficiently precise for reproducibility, including efficient flow precomputation and multi-GPU training. The results are impressive: the fused two-stream model achieves 70.4% on HMDB-51 and 89.2% on UCF-101 (averaged over three splits), outperforming [14] by over 20% and rivaling hand-crafted methods like [20, 21, 26].

The paper’s primary strengths lie in its clear technical contribution, strong empirical validation, and practical relevance. The architecture is a natural extension of deep learning for video, avoiding the pitfalls of prior work that struggled with implicit motion learning. The multi-task learning framework is elegantly applied to leverage limited video data, a significant practical advantage. The writing is generally clear and well-organized, though the abstract and introduction could more tightly unify the paper’s core contributions (the two-stream framework and multi-task learning as interdependent innovations, rather than separate ideas).

Minor weaknesses require attention. The related work section (Section 1.1) omits key references on deep video learning: Le et al. (CVPR 2011) for hierarchical spatio-temporal features and Wang et al. (BMVC 2010) for local spatio-temporal features, both of which contextualize the authors’ use of optical flow. The paper should explicitly cite these to clarify its relationship to prior work. Additionally, the discussion of optical flow’s advantages (Section 3.2) could better distinguish the proposed ConvNet’s generalization of hand-crafted features (HOF, MBH) from earlier motion-based methods like [29].

Overall, this is a high-quality, impactful contribution to video recognition. The two-stream framework provides a robust, principled solution to video action recognition that is both technically sound and empirically superior to prior deep learning approaches. The significance is clear: it demonstrates that deep learning can effectively leverage motion information when properly structured, moving beyond the limitations of stacked-frame networks. The paper meets all NIPS criteria: it is technically rigorous (Quality), well-written (Clarity), offers a novel architecture combining spatial/temporal streams (Originality), and delivers results that advance the state of the art (Significance).

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the citation gaps and tighten the thematic focus in the introduction. These are straightforward fixes that will strengthen the paper’s scholarly context without altering its core contributions. The work is a strong fit for NIPS 2014.
