

The paper introduces novel extensions to deep learning methods for video classification, primarily through a two-stream architecture and multi-task learning framework. The methodology is well-motivated, with clear technical descriptions of the proposed approaches, implementation details, and experimental procedures. The writing is concise and accessible, effectively presenting a compelling case for the proposed methods through strong empirical results. The authors demonstrate significant absolute gains over the baseline [13], with results that are competitive with state-of-the-art approaches on standard benchmarks.

The two-stream formulation represents the paper’s most compelling contribution. By integrating optical flow features into the deep learning pipeline, the authors provide clear evidence that this component substantially enhances performance—a finding of broad interest to the video understanding community. This insight is valuable and well-supported by the results. However, the paper’s structure presents a notable weakness: it appears to advance multiple distinct contributions (two-stream, multi-task, and framework refinements) without sufficient thematic cohesion. The introduction and abstract do not establish a unified narrative, making it challenging to discern the core intellectual thread connecting these elements. A more focused presentation centered on the two-stream innovation—particularly its empirical validation—would strengthen the paper’s impact.

The technical contributions are novel in their specific implementation and integration, though they build upon the foundational work in [13]. The authors convincingly show that their enhancements yield meaningful improvements, avoiding the common pitfall of incrementalism. The experimental design is thorough, with ablation studies that isolate the contribution of optical flow and multi-task learning. The results are presented with appropriate statistical rigor, supporting the claim of substantial performance gains.

Two critical improvements are required before acceptance:  
1. **Thematic focus**: The abstract and introduction should be revised to emphasize the two-stream framework as the primary contribution, explicitly framing the multi-task learning and other refinements as complementary extensions to this core idea. The current presentation risks diluting the significance of the optical flow integration.  
2. **Citation completeness**: The paper omits key foundational work in video action recognition. Specifically:  
   - *Learning Hierarchical Spatio-temporal Features for Action Recognition with Independent Subspace Analysis* (CVPR 2011) should be cited to contextualize feature extraction approaches.  
   - *Evaluation of Local Spatio-temporal Features for Action Recognition* (BMVC 2010) is essential for discussing spatio-temporal feature baselines.  
   These omissions weaken the paper’s positioning within the broader literature and should be addressed.

The paper makes a meaningful practical contribution by demonstrating how optical flow integration can be effectively leveraged within deep learning pipelines for video classification—a practical insight with immediate applicability. The proposed methods are well-suited for real-world deployment and complement recent advances in deep learning for video. While the multi-task learning component is less prominently justified, its inclusion adds depth to the framework without undermining the primary two-stream contribution.

In summary, the paper presents a strong empirical case for the efficacy of two-stream deep learning in video classification, supported by clear methodology and robust results. The core insight regarding optical flow’s value is significant and well-documented. With the suggested revisions—particularly the thematic refinement and citation updates—the paper would make a valuable contribution to NIPS 2014. I recommend acceptance pending these minor but necessary improvements. The authors should ensure the narrative centers on the two-stream innovation and fully situates their work within the existing literature on spatio-temporal feature learning.
