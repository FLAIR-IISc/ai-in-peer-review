This paper introduces a novel two-stream convolutional neural network (ConvNet) architecture for human action recognition in videos. The core idea is to leverage complementary information from spatial appearance (single frames) and temporal motion (dense optical flow) using separate, yet combined, ConvNet streams. The authors demonstrate significant performance improvements over existing ConvNet-based methods and achieve competitive results with state-of-the-art approaches on the UCF-101 and HMDB-51 datasets.

The work builds upon recent advances in deep learning for both image and video understanding. Prior research has explored applying ConvNets directly to stacked video frames, but with limited success compared to hand-crafted features. This paper distinguishes itself by decoupling the spatial and temporal aspects of video, training dedicated ConvNets for each, and then fusing their predictions. This approach is motivated by the two-streams hypothesis in visual processing, although the authors do not directly investigate the neurobiological connection. The authors also explore multi-task learning to enhance training data availability and improve generalization.

The strengths of this paper lie in its thorough experimental evaluation and the clarity of its presentation. The ablation studies, comparing different input configurations for the temporal stream (e.g., stacking methods, bi-directional flow) provide valuable insights into the impact of various design choices. The consistent improvements achieved by incorporating optical flow as input, and the subsequent performance gains from the two-stream architecture, strongly support the authors' hypotheses. The comparison against existing methods, including recent ConvNet-based approaches, demonstrates the effectiveness of the proposed method.

However, a few points could benefit from further discussion. The significant performance gap between the spatial network and previous models (as noted in [14]) warrants more in-depth investigation. While the authors acknowledge the similarity in architectures, exploring potential reasons – such as differences in training procedures or data augmentation – would strengthen the analysis. Furthermore, while the results demonstrate the value of dense optical flow, exploring the computational cost implications of its use would be beneficial, especially considering the increasing trend toward real-time applications. Finally, exploring alternative fusion methods beyond averaging and SVMs could potentially yield further improvements. 

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Strong empirical results:** The proposed architecture achieves state-of-the-art or competitive performance on standard benchmarks.
*   **Clear and thorough evaluation:** The ablation studies provide valuable insights into the effectiveness of different design choices.
*   **Novel combination of techniques:** The two-stream architecture offers a compelling approach to leveraging both spatial and temporal information in videos.
*   **Well-written and organized:** The paper is easy to follow and understand.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Limited discussion of architectural differences:** The reason for the superior performance of the spatial network compared to previous work requires further explanation.
*   **No discussion of computational cost:** The computational overhead of using dense optical flow is not addressed.



Overall, this is a well-executed and impactful paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of video action recognition. The clear presentation, thorough evaluation, and strong empirical results make it a valuable addition to NIPS. I recommend acceptance.
