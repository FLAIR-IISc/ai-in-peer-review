- Proposes a two-stream CNN architecture that fuses appearance (RGB) and motion (dense optical flow) for video action recognition.  
- Trains separate convolutional networks for each modality and fuses their predictions.  
- Demonstrates strong, competitive performance on UCF-101 and HMDB-51 benchmarks.  
- Adapts deep image-recognition advances to the temporal domain of video analysis.  
- Relates to the biological “two-stream hypothesis” but does not deeply explore that connection.  
- Strength: simple and effective design that shows ConvNets on optical flow yield strong motion representations.  
- Strength: decoupling spatial and temporal streams enables ImageNet pre-training and efficient use of motion cues.  
- Strength: multi-task learning across datasets is a practical solution to limited video training data.  
- Results exceed prior deep-learning attempts and approach hand-crafted feature methods.  
- Limitation: model only captures short-term temporal relationships (fixed-length L=10) and ignores long-range dependencies.  
- Limitation: lacks explicit modeling of motion context within longer action narratives.  
- Limitation: no detailed error analysis to identify failure modes (e.g., subtle motions, camera motion, occlusion).  
- Quality: experimental setup is sound, using standard benchmarks and appropriate baselines.  
- Clarity: paper is well-written with helpful diagrams explaining the architecture.  
- Originality: novel in applying ConvNets directly to optical flow and combining two-streams with pre-training and multi-task learning.  
- Significance: opens avenues for deep-learning-based motion modeling in action recognition and related video tasks.  
- Arguments for acceptance: strong empirical results, novel optical-flow ConvNet use, clear presentation, practical multi-task approach, and demonstrated benefit of motion cues.  
- Arguments against acceptance: limited long-range temporal modeling, insufficient error analysis, and superficial treatment of the two-stream hypothesis.
