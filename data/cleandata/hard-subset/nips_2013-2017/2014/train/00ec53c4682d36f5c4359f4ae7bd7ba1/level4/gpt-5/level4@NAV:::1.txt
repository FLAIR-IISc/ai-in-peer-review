Review- Summary:
This paper presents a model for discriminative tasks with video inputs. The architecture comprises two convolutional networks: one processes an appearance frame, and the other ingests a stack of densely computed optical-flow maps. Each stream is trained separately to classify its own input. The final video prediction is obtained by taking a (possibly weighted) average of the two streams’ outputs. The model is evaluated on two datasets, achieving impressive results that match or closely approach state-of-the-art methods that rely heavily on hand-crafted features.

Strengths:
- A simple convolutional-network approach that delivers strong performance.
- Prior deep learning work typically avoided optical flow (or other hand-crafted representations), attempting to learn features directly from pixels. This paper shows that, for action recognition, optical-flow fields are an effective input representation—an important contribution.

Weaknesses:
- The method does not capture long-range temporal structure across the entire video, instead operating on fixed-length windows of adjacent frames (L=10).

Quality:
The experiments are well executed. The paper would be more informative with an analysis of failure modes—for example, whether there are specific classes or groups of classes the model struggles with, and a characterization of the types of videos on which the method performs poorly.

Clarity:
The manuscript is clearly written, and the model is explained well.

Originality:
Feeding optical-flow features into a convolutional network is novel.

Significance:
This approach could meaningfully advance research on using video and motion for diverse vision problems. It applies convolutional nets to video in a straightforward way yet yields very promising results, and the use of optical flow as convnet input is a notable contribution.
