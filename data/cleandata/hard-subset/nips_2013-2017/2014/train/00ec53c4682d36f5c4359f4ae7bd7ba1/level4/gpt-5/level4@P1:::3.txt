Summary
This paper proposes a two-stream convolutional architecture for video action recognition that explicitly separates spatial and temporal information. The spatial stream operates on individual RGB frames and benefits from ImageNet pretraining; the temporal stream operates on stacked dense optical flow fields to capture motion cues. The authors study several flow-input configurations (optical-flow stacking vs. trajectory stacking, uni- vs. bi-directional flow, and mean flow subtraction), and combine the two streams by late fusion (average or linear SVM on softmax scores). They also introduce a multi-task learning setup that jointly trains on UCF-101 and HMDB-51 to address data scarcity and improve generalization. Implementation details include a CNN-M-style architecture, aggressive data augmentation, GPU-accelerated training, and precomputation/compression of optical flow.

Relative to prior work, the paper improves on deep approaches that use stacked RGB frames (e.g., Karpathy et al., CVPR’14) by explicitly injecting motion via dense flow, and it frames the two-stream design in the context of the ventral/dorsal pathways hypothesis. It is competitive with state-of-the-art shallow trajectory-based pipelines (e.g., improved dense trajectories with Fisher vectors) while substantially surpassing previous deep baselines on UCF-101 and HMDB-51.

Strengths
- Clear, well-written presentation with solid methodological detail and careful experimental protocols. The ablations on flow stacking length (L), trajectory vs. flow stacking, bi-directional flow, and mean subtraction are particularly informative.
- The two-stream formulation is novel and compelling in this context. The temporal stream trained on multi-frame optical flow delivers strong performance even with limited data, and the late fusion provides consistent complementary gains over either stream alone.
- Multi-task learning across UCF-101 and HMDB-51 is a practical and effective contribution that improves performance without manual class curation.
- Thorough implementation details (e.g., multi-GPU training, data augmentation, pretraining strategy) support reproducibility.
- Empirical results are strong: the method significantly outperforms prior deep architectures and is competitive with hand-crafted trajectory-based state-of-the-art pipelines.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- The paper’s contributions feel somewhat split across multiple threads (two-stream architecture, optical-flow design choices, and multi-task learning). Since most of the significance is driven by the two-stream design and the incorporation of optical flow, the abstract and introduction could be tightened to present a more focused narrative around this central theme.
- Missing citations:
  • Deep learning: Learning Hierarchical Spatio-temporal Features for Action Recognition with Independent Subspace Analysis (Le et al., CVPR 2011).
  • Computer vision: Evaluation of Local Spatio-temporal Features for Action Recognition (Wang et al., BMVC 2009/2010).
  Including and discussing these will better position the work in the literature.

Evaluation by NIPS criteria
- Quality: The work is technically sound, with appropriate baselines and ablations. Claims are supported by experiments on UCF-101 and HMDB-51, and the analysis of temporal inputs and fusion strategies is careful. The multi-task setup is straightforward but effective.
- Clarity: The manuscript is clearly written and well organized. Implementation and training details are sufficient for expert reproduction. A more focused framing in the abstract/introduction would further improve readability.
- Originality: The explicit two-stream ConvNet architecture with an optical-flow temporal stream and the application of multi-task learning to jointly train across action datasets are novel in this combination and context.
- Significance: The results are important and likely to influence practice, especially the finding that an optical-flow ConvNet markedly outperforms stacked-RGB models and complements a spatial network. The approach competes with strong hand-crafted baselines and substantially improves over previous deep methods.

Pros for acceptance
- Strong empirical performance; clear complementary gains from fusing spatial and temporal streams.
- Well-motivated architecture that advances deep video recognition beyond stacked RGB frames.
- Careful ablations and practical contributions (multi-task learning, training and engineering details).

Cons for acceptance
- Narrative could be more tightly focused on the main contribution (two-stream with optical flow).
- Missing citations that would better contextualize the work.

Recommendation
Accept (NIPS 2014), conditional on addressing the suggested improvements—particularly tightening the abstract/introduction around the two-stream/optical-flow contribution and adding the cited related work. The paper offers significant practical and methodological contributions that will interest the community.
