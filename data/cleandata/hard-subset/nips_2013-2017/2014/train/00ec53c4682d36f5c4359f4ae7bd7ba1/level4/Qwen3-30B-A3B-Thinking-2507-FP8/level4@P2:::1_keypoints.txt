- Proposes a two-stream ConvNet for video action recognition: a spatial stream on frames and a temporal stream on dense optical flow, combined via late fusion.  
- Leverages the two-stream neuroscience hypothesis (ventral/dorsal) as motivation for separating appearance and motion processing.  
- Demonstrates that feeding optical flow (a hand-crafted feature) into a ConvNet yields large performance gains over learning motion from raw pixels.  
- Temporal stream outperforms prior deep-learning methods by a large margin (~20% over [14]).  
- Introduces multi-task learning to combine UCF-101 and HMDB-51 without manual class selection, increasing effective training data.  
- Provides careful architectural design, thorough implementation details, and well-executed experiments and ablation studies (flow stacking, mean flow subtraction).  
- Results match or exceed state-of-the-art hand-crafted-feature methods on standard benchmarks.  
- Analysis and empirical evaluation are rigorous and largely reproducible.  
- Paper is exceptionally clear and well-organized with helpful figures and strong situating in related work.  
- Original contribution: novel application of ConvNets on optical flow to bridge deep learning and traditional vision techniques.  
- Significant potential impact: likely to become a baseline for video tasks and influence use of optical flow in other video applications.  
- Main limitation: temporal stream processes fixed-length sequences (L=10), potentially missing long-term motion patterns.  
- Insufficient error analysis: lacks detailed breakdown of failure modes across action classes or video types.  
- The neuroscience two-stream connection is mentioned but not deeply explored, weakening theoretical motivation.
