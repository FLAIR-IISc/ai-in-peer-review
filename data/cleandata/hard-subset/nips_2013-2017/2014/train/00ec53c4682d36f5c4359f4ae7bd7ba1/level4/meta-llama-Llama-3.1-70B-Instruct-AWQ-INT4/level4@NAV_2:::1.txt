This paper proposes a model for solving discriminative tasks with video inputs, consisting of two convolutional nets that incorporate spatial and temporal information. The input to one net is an appearance frame, while the input to the second net is a stack of densely computed optical flow features. Each pathway is trained separately to classify its input, and the prediction for a video is obtained by taking a weighted average of the predictions made by each net.

The authors provide a thorough review of related work on action recognition using both shallow and deep architectures, highlighting the challenges of capturing complementary information on appearance from still frames and motion between frames. They also discuss the benefits of pre-training the spatial net on a large image classification dataset, such as ImageNet, and the use of multi-task learning to combine training data over multiple datasets.

The proposed two-stream ConvNet architecture is evaluated on the UCF-101 and HMDB-51 benchmarks, where it achieves competitive performance with the state of the art. The authors also investigate the effect of different input configurations, such as stacking multiple optical flows, trajectory stacking, and mean displacement subtraction, and find that these variations can improve performance.

The strengths of this paper include its simplicity and effectiveness in applying convolutional nets to optical flow features, which is a novel contribution. The model's ability to match or come close to the state-of-the-art methods that use intensively hand-crafted features is impressive. However, the model has some limitations, such as not taking into account the overall sequence of actions over the entire video, but only modeling fixed-length adjacent frames.

The quality of the experiments is good, but it would be more insightful if the authors also include some analysis of the error modes, such as characterizing the classes or groups of classes that the model is unable to classify well, or identifying the types of videos on which the model does not work well.

The paper is clearly written, and the model is well-explained. The application of a convolutional net to optical flow features is novel, and this approach could have a significant impact on research in using videos and motion for various vision problems. The use of optical flow features as inputs to a conv net is a valuable contribution, and the model's simplicity and effectiveness make it a promising direction for future research.

Arguments for acceptance:

* The paper proposes a novel and effective model for action recognition in videos.
* The use of optical flow features as inputs to a conv net is a valuable contribution.
* The model's simplicity and effectiveness make it a promising direction for future research.
* The paper provides a thorough review of related work and a clear explanation of the model.

Arguments against acceptance:

* The model has some limitations, such as not taking into account the overall sequence of actions over the entire video.
* The paper could benefit from a more in-depth analysis of the error modes and the types of videos on which the model does not work well.
* The model's performance may not generalize well to other datasets or tasks.
