Summary
The paper proposes a two-stream convolutional architecture for video action recognition that separates appearance and motion. The spatial stream is a ConvNet operating on RGB frames and is pre-trained on ImageNet; the temporal stream is a ConvNet trained on stacks of dense optical flow fields computed between adjacent frames. The authors explore several motion encodings (optical flow stacking, trajectory stacking, bi-directional flow, mean-flow subtraction) and fuse the streams by averaging or by a linear SVM over softmax scores. To mitigate limited video data, they apply multi-task learning to jointly train on UCF-101 and HMDB-51. The approach achieves results competitive with state-of-the-art hand-crafted pipelines (e.g., improved dense trajectories) and substantially improves over prior deep models trained on raw frames.

Relation to prior work
The paper situates itself well relative to two lines of work: (i) shallow descriptors with Fisher vector encodings and dense trajectories (Wang & Schmid; Peng et al.), and (ii) deep models operating on stacks of RGB frames or 3D convolutions (e.g., Karpathy et al., Ji et al.). Unlike prior deep approaches that rely on learning motion implicitly from pixels, this work explicitly feeds optical flow to the temporal ConvNet, echoing the dorsal/ventral two-stream hypothesis in vision while remaining purely engineering-driven. The multi-task training across UCF-101 and HMDB-51 is a principled way to expand effective data without manual class-level filtering.

Strengths
- Clear architectural decomposition that exploits complementary cues: appearance (objects/scenes) and motion.
- Strong empirical performance with thorough ablations on temporal input design (number of flow frames, trajectory vs. optical flow stacking, bi-directional flow, mean subtraction).
- Demonstrates that ConvNets trained on optical flow (with limited data) outperform ConvNets trained on stacks of RGB frames, clarifying a key design choice for video CNNs.
- Effective use of multi-task learning to leverage multiple datasets and reduce overfitting, with measurable gains on both.
- The method is straightforward to reproduce with standard toolchains (Caffe), and practical implementation details (multi-GPU, flow compression) are provided.

Weaknesses
- Temporal modeling is local: the temporal stream sees short windows (e.g., L=10) and there is no mechanism to capture long-range dependencies or action structure over the full video.
- The approach depends on pre-computed optical flow, which is computationally heavy and breaks end-to-end learning; only a simple mean-flow subtraction is used for camera motion.
- Fusion is performed at the score level; joint training or deeper fusion is not explored due to overfitting.
- The evaluation is limited to trimmed datasets (UCF-101, HMDB-51); robustness on untrimmed videos and to different flow algorithms is not studied.
- Limited analysis of failure modes or per-class performance to guide future improvements.

Quality
The study is technically sound, with well-designed experiments and ablations supporting the main claims. Training protocols, data augmentation, and hyperparameters are clearly specified. The comparisons are on standard splits and include baselines (e.g., slow fusion on RGB stacks). It would strengthen the paper to (i) quantify training/inference cost including optical flow computation, (ii) analyze sensitivity to the choice of flow algorithm and to the flow compression scheme, and (iii) report more detailed error analysis (confusions, classes most improved by temporal vs. spatial stream).

Clarity
The paper is clearly written and well organized. The two-stream architecture and temporal input variants are explained concisely and with helpful intuition. Implementation details are sufficiently described to enable reproduction. A brief discussion of the loss weighting in multi-task learning and the rationale for L=10 would improve readability.

Originality
Feeding stacked optical flow to a ConvNet as a dedicated temporal stream, and combining it with an ImageNet-pretrained spatial stream, is a novel and effective design choice for 2014. The multi-task training across datasets for action recognition is also a useful contribution. While the two-stream idea has biological inspiration and shallow analogues, the discriminatively trained two-stream ConvNet and its motion encoding present a fresh and impactful approach.

Significance
High. The approach provides a strong and practical baseline for video recognition, narrowing the gap between deep and hand-crafted methods and offering clear guidance on how to inject motion into ConvNets. It is likely to influence subsequent research on video architectures, data regimes, and fusion strategies.

Suggestions for improvement
- Explore longer-term temporal modeling (e.g., temporal pooling over longer horizons, sequence models) to capture action structure beyond 10 frames.
- Move toward end-to-end training of motion features (e.g., learnable warping/cost volumes) or at least study robustness to different flow algorithms and camera motion compensation.
- Provide per-class confusion analysis to illustrate where each stream helps and to identify systematic failures.
- Investigate earlier or mid-level fusion with appropriate regularization to avoid overfitting, and assess whether learned fusion outperforms SVM-on-scores in data-rich settings.
- Report computational cost (including flow) and discuss trade-offs for deployment.

Arguments for acceptance
- Strong empirical gains over prior deep baselines and competitive with state-of-the-art hand-crafted pipelines.
- Clear, well-motivated architecture with thorough ablations that yield actionable insights.
- Demonstrates the value of optical flow as input to ConvNets for action recognition.
- Practical multi-task learning strategy that improves performance under limited data.

Arguments against acceptance
- Limited temporal context and lack of sequence modeling across entire videos.
- Dependence on precomputed optical flow reduces elegance and end-to-end learnability; computational overhead not fully quantified.
- Evaluation confined to trimmed datasets; generalization to more realistic settings is untested.

Overall
A well-executed and influential paper that advances deep video recognition by explicitly incorporating motion via optical flow in a two-stream architecture. I recommend acceptance.
