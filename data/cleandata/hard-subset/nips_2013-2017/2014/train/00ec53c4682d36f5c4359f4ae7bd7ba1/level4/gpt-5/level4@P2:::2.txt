This paper introduces a two-stream convolutional architecture for human action recognition in video. One stream (spatial) operates on individual RGB frames to capture appearance, while the other (temporal) operates on stacks of dense optical flow to capture motion. The two streams are fused at the score level. The authors also explore multi-task learning across datasets to mitigate limited video training data. On UCF-101 and HMDB-51, the method substantially improves over prior ConvNet-based approaches and reaches performance comparable to the state of the art based on hand-crafted features.

Although the approach adapts a standard image ConvNet pipeline to a different input modality (optical flow) and combines it with a single-frame model, the experimental study is careful and compelling. The paper compares several ways to construct ConvNet inputs from optical flow (e.g., stacking length, trajectory stacking, bi-directional flow, mean subtraction) and shows clear gains from using optical flow as input. The results convincingly demonstrate the complementarity of spatial and temporal cues and the value of late fusion. Overall, this is a solid empirical contribution likely to interest researchers working on video classification with ConvNets.

Minor comments:
- The spatial stream’s advantage over prior models (e.g., [13]), despite similar architectures, is not fully explained. A deeper analysis of factors such as data augmentation, input sampling (full-frame vs. center-crop), training schedules, or pretraining/fine-tuning choices would clarify why the spatial network performs so well.
- It would be valuable to compare different frame sampling strategies for the spatial stream (e.g., uniform vs. random temporal sampling, number of frames, and crop policies), as this could affect performance and reproducibility.
