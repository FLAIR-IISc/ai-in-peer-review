Summary:
This paper introduces a discriminative model for video-based recognition composed of two convolutional networks. One network processes raw appearance from individual frames, while the other consumes a stack of densely computed optical flow fields. Each stream is trained independently to perform classification, and video-level decisions are obtained by combining the two streams’ predictions, e.g., via (weighted) averaging. The approach is evaluated on two action benchmarks and attains strong results that match or approach state-of-the-art systems relying heavily on hand-crafted features.

Strengths:
- A straightforward two-stream ConvNet design yields strong performance.
- In contrast to prior deep learning approaches that attempted to learn motion directly from RGB stacks, this work demonstrates that optical flow provides a highly effective input representation for action recognition, which is an important and impactful insight.

Weaknesses:
- The method models only short fixed-length temporal windows (e.g., L = 10 adjacent frames) and does not explicitly capture longer-range sequence dynamics across entire clips.

Quality:
The experimental setup is sound and the comparisons are appropriate. The paper would be strengthened by a deeper error analysis, e.g., identifying specific classes or groups of classes where performance lags, and characterizing the types of videos or motions that cause failures.

Clarity:
The presentation is clear and well-organized, and the model and training procedures are described in sufficient detail.

Originality:
Using a ConvNet trained directly on stacked optical flow fields for supervised action recognition is novel relative to prior deep models that operated primarily on RGB inputs.

Significance:
The approach has the potential to influence future work on video and motion understanding, showing that a simple ConvNet architecture augmented with optical flow inputs can deliver promising results. The choice of optical flow as input to a ConvNet is a noteworthy contribution that could be broadly useful.
