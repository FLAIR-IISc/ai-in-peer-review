This paper presents a two-stream convolutional neural network architecture for action recognition in videos, designed to capture complementary appearance and motion information. The approach leverages separate spatial and temporal networks, with the temporal stream trained on multi-frame dense optical flow. The authors demonstrate strong performance on UCF-101 and HMDB-51, achieving results competitive with the state-of-the-art and significantly exceeding previous deep learning-based methods for video classification. This work builds upon recent advances in deep convolutional networks for still image classification [15] and extends them to the video domain, addressing limitations identified in prior attempts to directly apply ConvNets to video data [14]. The contribution is threefold: a novel two-stream architecture, demonstration of effective ConvNet training on optical flow with limited data, and the use of multi-task learning to improve generalization across datasets.

The paper is well-motivated and tackles an important problem in computer vision. The proposed two-stream architecture is a logical and effective way to incorporate both spatial and temporal information. The thorough experimental evaluation, including ablation studies of different input configurations for the temporal stream (stacking methods, mean subtraction) and fusion techniques, is a significant strength. The results clearly demonstrate the benefits of using optical flow as input and highlight the complementary nature of the spatial and temporal streams. The use of multi-task learning is a practical and effective approach to address the limited size of available video datasets. This work represents a substantial improvement over existing deep learning methods for video action recognition and provides a strong baseline for future research.

I have a few minor suggestions for improvement:
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion of why the spatial network performs comparatively well given its architectural similarities to models presented in [14]. Further analysis of the learned features or a comparison of training procedures might provide valuable insight.
- It would be worthwhile to explore different frame sampling strategies for the spatial stream and analyze their impact on performance. Investigating the sensitivity of the model to the number of frames sampled and the specific sampling method could provide valuable guidance for practical applications.
- While the paper references the two-streams hypothesis [9], a slightly more detailed discussion of the biological plausibility of the architecture, even without a full investigation, might add context.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Strong performance:** The proposed model achieves state-of-the-art or competitive results on standard benchmarks.
*   **Novel architecture:** The two-stream architecture effectively combines spatial and temporal information.
*   **Thorough evaluation:** The paper includes extensive experiments and ablation studies.
*   **Clear writing:** The paper is well-written and easy to understand.
*   **Significant contribution:** The work advances the state-of-the-art in video action recognition.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The core idea (applying ConvNets to optical flow) is not entirely novel, but the specific implementation and thorough evaluation are valuable.
*   The paper does not explore all possible avenues for improvement, such as alternative network architectures or training techniques.

Overall, this is a high-quality paper that makes a significant contribution to the field. I recommend acceptance.
