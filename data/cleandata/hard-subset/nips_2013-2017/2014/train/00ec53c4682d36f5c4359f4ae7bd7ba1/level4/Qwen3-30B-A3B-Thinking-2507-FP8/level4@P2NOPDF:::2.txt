

This paper introduces a novel neural network architecture for human action classification in videos, combining predictions from two convolutional neural networks (CNNs): one trained on single video frames and another on short sequences of dense optical flow. The proposed method achieves state-of-the-art performance on standard video classification benchmarks, significantly outperforming prior ConvNet-based approaches while matching or exceeding existing top results.  

The core contribution adapts established CNN pipelines to a new input modality (dense optical flow) and integrates it with frame-based processing. While the approach does not introduce a fundamentally novel architecture, the experimental rigor and clarity of results are commendable. The authors thoroughly evaluate multiple strategies for training CNNs on optical flow data, with results unambiguously demonstrating the efficacy of optical flow as a complementary input for action recognition. This empirical exploration is a strong point of the work and provides valuable insights for the community.  

Two minor points warrant clarification:  
1. The spatial stream (trained on frames) achieves substantially higher accuracy than the models in [13] despite architectural similarities. The authors should address this discrepancy, as it is critical to understanding the relative strengths of their approach.  
2. A comparative analysis of different frame sampling strategies for the spatial stream would further strengthen the methodology, particularly given the sensitivity of video recognition to temporal resolution.  

Overall, this is a well-executed empirical study that delivers meaningful progress in video action recognition. The work is clearly written, methodically validated, and directly relevant to researchers developing CNN-based solutions for video analysis. It represents a solid advancement in the field and will likely serve as a useful reference for future work.
