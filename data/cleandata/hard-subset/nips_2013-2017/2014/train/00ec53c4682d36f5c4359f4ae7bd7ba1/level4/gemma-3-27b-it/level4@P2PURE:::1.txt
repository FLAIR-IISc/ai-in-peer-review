Summary:
This paper introduces a model for discriminative tasks involving video inputs. The model employs two convolutional neural networks: one processing appearance frames and the other processing a stack of densely computed optical flow features. Each network is trained independently for classification, and the final video-level prediction is obtained by averaging the outputs of the two networks (with learned weights). The model achieves strong performance on two datasets, matching or approaching the state-of-the-art results obtained by methods relying on extensively engineered hand-crafted features.

Strengths:
- This work demonstrates that a straightforward application of convolutional networks can achieve competitive results.
- Unlike many recent deep learning approaches that learn features directly from pixels, this paper successfully integrates optical flow (and by extension, other hand-crafted features) into a deep learning framework. This demonstrates the continued utility of optical flow as an input representation, particularly for action recognition, and represents a significant contribution.

Weaknesses:
- The model currently considers only fixed-length segments of adjacent frames (L=10) and does not explicitly model the temporal relationships and overall sequence of actions across the entire video. 

Quality:
The experimental setup is well-designed. Further insight could be gained by including an analysis of error modes; for instance, identifying classes or groups of classes that the model consistently misclassifies, and characterizing the types of videos that pose the greatest challenge to the model.

Clarity:
The paper is clearly written and the model is well-explained.

Originality:
The application of a convolutional neural network to optical flow features is a novel contribution.

Significance:
This approach has the potential to significantly influence research on video and motion analysis for various computer vision problems. The model offers a simple yet effective application of convolutional networks to video data, yielding promising results. The utilization of optical flow as input to a convolutional network is a noteworthy and original contribution.
