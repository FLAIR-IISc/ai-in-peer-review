This paper introduces a novel model for discriminative tasks involving video data. The approach utilizes two separate convolutional neural networks: one processing appearance information from individual frames, and the other processing densely computed optical flow features. Each network is trained independently for classification, and the final video-level prediction is generated by averaging the outputs of both networks, potentially with learned weights.

The model's performance is evaluated on two datasets, demonstrating compelling results that are competitive with, and in some cases match, state-of-the-art methods relying on extensively engineered, hand-crafted features.

**Strengths:**
- The proposed model achieves strong performance through a straightforward application of convolutional networks.
- Existing deep learning approaches for vision tasks have generally favored end-to-end learning directly from pixel data, often overlooking the potential benefits of established features like optical flow. This work demonstrates the value of incorporating optical flow fields as input, particularly for action recognition, representing a valuable contribution.

**Weaknesses:**
- The model currently operates on fixed-length segments of 10 adjacent frames and does not explicitly model the temporal relationships or overall sequence of actions across the entire video duration.

**Quality:**
The experimental setup is robust and well-executed. Enhancing the analysis with a discussion of error modes would be beneficial. Specifically, investigating whether the model struggles with particular classes or video characteristics could provide valuable insights. 

**Clarity:**
The paper is well-written and clearly articulates the model's architecture and functionality.

**Originality:**
The application of a convolutional neural network directly to optical flow features represents a novel contribution.

**Significance:**
This work has the potential to significantly influence future research in video and motion-based vision applications. The model offers a simple yet effective application of convolutional networks to video data, yielding promising results. The innovative use of optical flow as input to a convolutional network is particularly noteworthy.
