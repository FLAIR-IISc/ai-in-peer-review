This paper proposes a two-stream convolutional architecture for human action recognition in video. One network (the spatial stream) operates on individual frames, while the other (the temporal stream) operates on short sequences of dense optical flow. The outputs are fused at the score level. Across two standard benchmarks, the approach substantially outperforms prior ConvNet-based methods and reaches performance comparable to the state of the art.

The contribution is conceptually straightforward but executed thoroughly. The authors adapt the standard ConvNet image-classification pipeline to dense optical flow inputs and show that this design, combined with a single-frame ConvNet, is highly effective for action recognition. The paper systematically evaluates several ways to construct ConvNet inputs from optical flow, making a clear empirical case for using stacked flow fields. The results convincingly demonstrate that explicitly providing motion via optical flow is more effective than relying on ConvNets to learn motion implicitly from raw RGB stacks.

Strengths
- Clear and effective architectural decomposition into spatial and temporal streams.
- Careful experimental study of optical-flow-based inputs (e.g., stacking length, trajectory vs. location stacking, mean subtraction, and directional flow).
- Strong empirical performance: the method substantially improves over previous ConvNet approaches and matches the best reported results on standard datasets.
- The empirical focus and comparisons make the findings accessible and useful to practitioners.

Weaknesses and minor suggestions
- It is not clear why the spatial network performs substantially better than the models reported in [13], given the apparent architectural similarity. A brief discussion of training details, data augmentation, or other factors that may account for the gap would help.
- It would be interesting to see a comparison of different sampling schemes for selecting frames fed into the spatial stream. This could clarify the impact of temporal sampling on the spatial model’s performance.

Comments on review criteria
- Quality: The work appears technically sound, with well-supported claims and comprehensive experiments. The comparisons across different optical flow configurations are particularly informative.
- Clarity: The paper is clearly written and well organized. The experimental setup and evaluation protocol are described in a way that should enable reproduction.
- Originality: The core idea—applying a standard ConvNet pipeline to stacked optical flow and combining it with a frame-based ConvNet—is not radically novel, but the two-stream formulation and the thorough empirical exploration constitute a solid and useful contribution.
- Significance: High. The approach establishes a strong baseline for ConvNet-based action recognition and is likely to influence subsequent work on leveraging motion information in deep models.

Arguments for acceptance
- Strong empirical gains over previous ConvNet approaches; competitive with the state of the art.
- Thorough, careful experimental design that yields clear insights about motion representations for ConvNets.
- Practical and impactful method for the community working on video classification.

Arguments against acceptance
- Conceptual novelty is limited; the method primarily adapts existing ConvNet practices to optical flow inputs and combines two standard streams.
- Some ablations (e.g., spatial stream frame sampling strategies) are missing, and the performance gap to [13] is not fully explained.

Overall, this is a strong empirical paper with clear takeaways and strong results. It will be of interest to researchers and practitioners working on video classification with ConvNets.
