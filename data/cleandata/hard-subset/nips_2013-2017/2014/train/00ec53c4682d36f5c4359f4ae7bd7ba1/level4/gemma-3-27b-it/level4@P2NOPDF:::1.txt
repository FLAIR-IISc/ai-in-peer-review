This paper presents a novel approach to video classification by combining the strengths of deep learning with traditional motion features. The proposed model employs two separate convolutional neural networks: one processing appearance information from individual frames and the other utilizing densely computed optical flow fields. Each network is trained independently for classification, and the final prediction for a video is generated by averaging the outputs of the two networks, potentially with learned weights.

The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of this model on two datasets, achieving results that are competitive with, and in some cases match, state-of-the-art methods that rely on extensively engineered, hand-crafted features. This performance is particularly noteworthy given the relative simplicity of the proposed architecture.

**Strengths:**

The primary strength of this work lies in its successful application of convolutional networks to both appearance and motion cues in a straightforward manner. While recent deep learning approaches have largely focused on learning features directly from raw pixel data, this paper convincingly demonstrates the continued utility of optical flow as a valuable input representation, particularly for action recognition tasks. This re-validation of hand-crafted features within a deep learning framework is a significant contribution. The model's simplicity is also a notable advantage, making it readily understandable and potentially adaptable to other video-based tasks.

**Weaknesses:**

A key limitation of the current model is its limited temporal scope. The approach operates on fixed-length segments of adjacent frames (L=10) and does not explicitly model long-range temporal dependencies or the overall sequence of actions within a video. Capturing these longer-range relationships could potentially improve performance and robustness.

**Quality:**

The experimental setup is well-defined and provides strong evidence for the model’s effectiveness. To further enhance the evaluation, it would be beneficial to include a detailed analysis of the model's failure cases. Investigating whether the model consistently struggles with specific action classes, or exhibits difficulties with particular video characteristics (e.g., fast motion, cluttered backgrounds), would provide valuable insights for future improvements. Characterizing the types of videos on which the model performs poorly would contribute significantly to understanding its limitations.

**Clarity:**

The paper is exceptionally well-written and clearly explains the proposed model and its implementation. The descriptions are concise and easy to follow, allowing for a quick understanding of the key concepts.

**Originality:**

The application of convolutional neural networks to optical flow features represents a novel and potentially impactful contribution. While convolutional networks have been widely used for image and video analysis, their direct application to densely computed optical flow fields, in conjunction with appearance information, has not been previously explored.

**Significance:**

This work has the potential to significantly influence future research in video understanding and motion analysis. The successful combination of deep learning and traditional motion cues offers a promising pathway for developing more robust and accurate video processing systems. The simplicity and effectiveness of this approach suggest that it could be broadly applicable to a range of vision problems involving temporal data. The demonstrated ability to achieve competitive results with a relatively simple architecture, utilizing optical flow as input to a convolutional network, is a noteworthy advance in the field.
