This paper investigates deep convolutional neural networks for action recognition in videos, focusing on architectures that effectively leverage both spatial (appearance) and temporal (motion) information. The authors propose a two-stream convolutional network, demonstrating the effectiveness of training a network on multi-frame dense optical flow, and utilizing multi-task learning across datasets to improve performance. The methodology is detailed, the paper is clearly written, and the results show substantial improvements over previous deep learning approaches and competitiveness with state-of-the-art hand-crafted feature methods.

The core idea of decoupling spatial and temporal streams is a significant contribution, building upon the “two-streams hypothesis” from visual neuroscience [9]. While recent work [14] explored using stacked frames as input to ConvNets for video, this paper demonstrates a much more effective approach by explicitly modeling motion through optical flow. The demonstrated performance gain from the temporal stream is a key finding, and the exploration of multi-task learning provides a practical method for improving generalization, particularly with limited video data. The architecture builds upon and significantly extends previous efforts like [16] by employing deeper, discriminatively trained ConvNets instead of hand-crafted HMAX models.

**Strengths:**

*   **Strong technical contribution:** The two-stream architecture is a novel and effective approach to video action recognition, explicitly addressing the complementary nature of spatial and temporal information.
*   **Clear and comprehensive methodology:** The paper provides a detailed description of the network architectures, training procedures, and evaluation protocols, facilitating reproducibility.
*   **Significant performance improvements:** The results on UCF-101 and HMDB-51 demonstrate substantial gains over existing deep learning methods [14] and achieve competitive performance with state-of-the-art hand-crafted features [20, 21, 26].
*   **Practical insights:** The multi-task learning framework offers a valuable technique for improving generalization in video action recognition, a common challenge due to limited labeled data.
*   **Thorough Related Work:** The paper provides a comprehensive review of existing approaches in both shallow and deep video representation learning.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Fragmented Focus:** While the individual contributions (two-stream architecture, optical flow, multi-task learning) are strong, the paper feels somewhat disjointed. A stronger unifying narrative emphasizing the core contribution (the two-stream approach) could improve clarity. The abstract and introduction could be more focused.
*   **Missing Citations:** Several relevant papers are not cited. Specifically, [Le et al., 2011] ("Learning Hierarchical Spatio-temporal Features for Action Recognition with Independent Subspace Analysis") and [Wang et al., 2010] ("Evaluation of local spatio-temporal features for action recognition") are important works in the field and should be discussed.
*   **Limited Discussion of Failure Cases:** The paper focuses primarily on overall performance. A discussion of specific actions or scenarios where the method struggles would provide valuable insights and suggest directions for future work.
*   **Computational Cost:** While the paper mentions multi-GPU training, a more detailed discussion of the computational resources required for training and inference would be helpful.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and effective approach to video action recognition with demonstrated improvements over existing methods.
*   The methodology is clearly described and well-supported by experimental results.
*   The findings are likely to be of significant interest to the NIPS community working on computer vision, deep learning, and action recognition.
*   The detailed experimental evaluation and analysis provide strong evidence for the validity of the proposed approach.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The paper lacks a strong unifying narrative and feels somewhat fragmented.
*   The omission of key related work ([Le et al., 2011], [Wang et al., 2010]) weakens the discussion of prior art.
*   The lack of analysis of failure cases limits the insights gained from the experimental results.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of video action recognition. While the paper could benefit from a more focused narrative and inclusion of missing citations, the novel architecture, significant performance gains, and thorough experimental evaluation justify acceptance.

**Recommendation:**

**Accept with minor revisions.** The authors should address the missing citations and consider refining the introduction and abstract to create a more cohesive narrative. A brief discussion of failure cases and the computational cost would also strengthen the paper.
