This paper proposes a novel two-stream convolutional network architecture for action recognition in videos. The core idea is to handle spatial and temporal information separately. The spatial stream is a ConvNet that operates on individual video frames to learn features from appearance, while the temporal stream is a ConvNet trained on dense optical flow fields to learn features from motion. The paper demonstrates that this explicit modeling of motion with optical flow allows the network to achieve excellent performance, even when trained on relatively small datasets. Furthermore, the authors introduce a multi-task learning framework to effectively combine datasets (UCF-101 and HMDB-51) for training. The methodology, implementation, and experimental procedures are described in thorough detail. The proposed model is evaluated on standard benchmarks, showing significant improvements over prior deep learning methods and achieving results that are competitive with the state-of-the-art.

**Quality and Originality**

The paper's main contributions—the two-stream architecture and the application of ConvNets to optical flow fields—are novel and well-motivated. The key finding, that explicitly modeling motion via optical flow significantly boosts performance compared to learning from raw stacked frames, is a valuable contribution to the field. This result is of considerable interest to the research community, as it provides a powerful and practical alternative to end-to-end models that must learn motion representations implicitly. The experimental evaluation is sound, featuring detailed ablation studies that validate the design choices for the temporal stream. The work is technically solid and presents a complete and convincing case for the proposed methods.

**Clarity**

The paper is clearly written and well-organized. The two-stream concept is explained effectively, and the relationship between the proposed temporal ConvNet and previous hand-crafted features like HOF and MBH is thoughtfully discussed. However, the paper presents several distinct contributions (the two-stream architecture, various optical flow configurations, and multi-task learning), which slightly diffuses its focus. The presentation could be strengthened by framing the abstract and introduction more tightly around the central theme of separating spatial and temporal modeling.

**Significance**

The significance of this work is high. The empirical results are impressive, demonstrating a substantial performance gain over the recent deep learning approach of Karpathy et al. [14] while remaining competitive with leading methods based on hand-crafted features. This paper represents a significant advance for deep learning in video analysis and provides a strong baseline that many researchers will likely use and build upon. The demonstration of optical flow's effectiveness as a learned representation for motion is a particularly impactful result.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novel Architecture:** The two-stream framework is an elegant and effective way to combine appearance and motion information for action recognition.
*   **Strong Empirical Results:** The model achieves excellent performance, significantly outperforming previous deep learning models for video and competing with the state-of-the-art.
*   **Key Insight on Motion Representation:** The paper convincingly shows that using pre-computed optical flow as input to a temporal ConvNet is a highly effective strategy for learning motion features.
*   **Thorough Evaluation:** The authors provide a comprehensive set of experiments and ablation studies that validate their architectural choices.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

1.  **Sharpening the Focus:** The paper's primary impact comes from the two-stream architecture and the use of optical flow. The abstract and introduction could be revised to better highlight this as the core contribution, creating a more unified narrative for the paper.

2.  **Missing Citations:** The literature review could be more complete. To better contextualize the work, the authors should consider citing and discussing the following relevant papers:
    *   Le, Q. V., et al. "Learning hierarchical spatio-temporal features for action recognition with independent subspace analysis." CVPR 2011.
    *   Wang, H., et al. "Evaluation of local spatio-temporal features for action recognition." BMVC, 2010.

**Recommendation**

This paper offers significant practical and conceptual contributions to video understanding. The ideas are interesting, complement existing work in deep learning, and are supported by strong empirical evidence. I recommend that this paper be **accepted** at NIPS 2014, conditional upon the authors addressing the minor suggestions for improving the paper's focus and expanding the related work section.
