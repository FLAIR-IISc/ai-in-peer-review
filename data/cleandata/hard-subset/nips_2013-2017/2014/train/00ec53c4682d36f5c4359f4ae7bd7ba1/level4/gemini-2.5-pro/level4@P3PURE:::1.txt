**Summary:**

This paper proposes a two-stream model for discriminative tasks on video data. The model consists of two separate convolutional networks. One network, the spatial stream, processes individual appearance frames. The second network, the temporal stream, takes a stack of densely computed optical flow fields as input. Each network is trained independently to perform classification, and the final prediction for a video is obtained by fusing their scores, for instance via a weighted average.

The model is evaluated on two action recognition datasets, achieving impressive results that match or closely approach the state-of-the-art established by methods relying on intensively hand-crafted features.

**Strengths:**

*   The proposed model is a straightforward yet highly effective application of convolutional networks that achieves strong performance on a challenging task.
*   A key contribution of this work is its demonstration that pre-computed optical flow is a powerful input representation for deep models in action recognition. While many deep learning approaches aim to learn all features directly from raw pixels, this paper shows the significant benefit of incorporating explicit motion information, challenging the prevailing notion that all features must be learned end-to-end. This is an important contribution.

**Weaknesses:**

*   A limitation of the model is its inability to capture long-range temporal dependencies. By operating on short, fixed-length snippets of adjacent frames (L=10), the model does not explicitly reason about the overall temporal structure of the full video sequence.

**Quality:**

The experimental evaluation is thorough and well-designed. The paper would be strengthened, however, by an analysis of the model's error modes. For example, are there specific classes or groups of classes that the model is unable to classify well? An investigation into the characteristics of videos on which the model performs poorly would provide valuable insights into its limitations.

**Clarity:**

The paper is well-written and clear. The proposed model and its components are explained effectively.

**Originality:**

The primary novelty lies in the application of a convolutional network directly to computed optical flow fields as a dedicated motion-processing stream.

**Significance:**

This approach has the potential to significantly impact research in video understanding and motion-based computer vision problems. The model demonstrates that a relatively simple deep learning architecture can achieve highly competitive results on video data. The novel use of optical flow as a direct input to a convolutional network is an important contribution that could inspire further work in multi-modal and hybrid deep learning models.
