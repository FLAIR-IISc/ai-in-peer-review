This paper presents a novel neural network architecture for classifying videos of human actions. The proposed model effectively combines the predictions of two convolutional neural networks (ConvNets): one trained on single video frames (spatial stream) and the other trained on short sequences of dense optical flow images (temporal stream). By leveraging both spatial and temporal information, the model significantly outperforms other ConvNet-based approaches to action recognition and achieves competitive results with the current state-of-the-art on two standard video classification datasets, UCF-101 and HMDB-51.

The authors provide a comprehensive review of related work on action recognition using both shallow and deep architectures. They also clearly explain the motivation behind their proposed two-stream architecture, which is inspired by the two-streams hypothesis in the human visual cortex. The spatial stream ConvNet is pre-trained on the ImageNet challenge dataset, while the temporal stream ConvNet is trained on optical flow images. The authors also investigate the effect of different input configurations, such as stacking multiple optical flows, trajectory stacking, and mean displacement subtraction.

The experimental evaluation is thorough, and the results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed architecture. The authors compare their model with other state-of-the-art approaches and provide a detailed analysis of the results. They also investigate the effect of multi-task learning on the performance of the temporal ConvNet.

The paper is well-written, and the authors provide sufficient details about the proposed architecture and the experimental setup. The results are impressive, and the paper will be of interest to anyone working on video classification with ConvNets.

Strengths:

* The proposed two-stream architecture is novel and effective for action recognition in videos.
* The authors provide a comprehensive review of related work and clearly explain the motivation behind their proposed architecture.
* The experimental evaluation is thorough, and the results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed architecture.
* The authors investigate the effect of different input configurations and multi-task learning on the performance of the temporal ConvNet.

Weaknesses:

* The paper does not provide a clear explanation of why the spatial network outperforms the models from [13] despite having similar architectures.
* The authors do not investigate the effect of different sampling schemes for the frames fed into the spatial stream.

Suggestions for improvement:

* The authors could provide more insights into why the spatial network outperforms the models from [13] despite having similar architectures.
* The authors could investigate the effect of different sampling schemes for the frames fed into the spatial stream and provide a comparison of the results.

Overall, this is a good empirical paper that presents a novel and effective architecture for action recognition in videos. The results are impressive, and the paper will be of interest to anyone working on video classification with ConvNets.
