

This paper introduces a two-stream convolutional network architecture for video action recognition, addressing the challenge of effectively capturing both spatial appearance information from individual frames and temporal motion information from optical flow. The authors propose a novel framework that decouples spatial and temporal processing into separate convolutional networks, which are then combined through late fusion. The paper also demonstrates the effectiveness of multi-task learning for training on limited video datasets by jointly optimizing for multiple action recognition tasks.

The work builds upon the two-stream hypothesis in visual processing (Goodale & Milner, 1992) and extends recent advances in deep learning for image recognition (Krizhevsky et al., 2012) to the video domain. The authors correctly position their work in relation to prior approaches, noting that previous deep learning attempts for video recognition (e.g., Karpathy et al., 2014) suffered from poor performance due to inadequate modeling of motion. Their key insight—that explicitly representing motion through optical flow as input to a convolutional network yields superior results—significantly advances the field.

In terms of quality, the paper is technically sound with well-designed experiments. The authors provide thorough ablation studies on different optical flow input configurations (L=5 vs. L=10, trajectory vs. optical flow stacking, bi-directional flow) and demonstrate that their approach consistently outperforms previous deep learning methods for video action recognition. The multi-task learning framework effectively addresses the challenge of limited video training data, and the results on UCF-101 and HMDB-51 are compelling. The paper acknowledges limitations, such as the need for camera motion compensation and the absence of spatial pooling over trajectories, which demonstrates appropriate scholarly honesty.

The clarity of the paper is excellent. The architecture is clearly explained with well-chosen figures, and the experimental methodology is described in sufficient detail for reproducibility. The writing is precise and well-organized, making complex concepts accessible to readers. The authors' decision to use optical flow as input, rather than relying on networks to implicitly learn motion features, is well-motivated and clearly explained.

Regarding originality, the two-stream architecture represents a significant departure from previous deep learning approaches to video recognition. While some elements (e.g., multi-task learning) are not novel, their application to video recognition with optical flow input is distinctive. The paper clearly differentiates itself from prior work, particularly noting that their approach outperforms the state-of-the-art shallow representations (Wang et al., 2013; Laptev et al., 2008) by a substantial margin. The authors properly cite relevant prior work, though they missed a few important references in the computer vision literature (as noted in the draft review).

The significance of this work is substantial. Video action recognition has numerous applications in surveillance, human-computer interaction, and video understanding. The authors demonstrate that their approach achieves competitive performance with state-of-the-art shallow methods while leveraging the power of deep learning. The paper's insights about the importance of explicitly modeling motion through optical flow rather than relying on networks to implicitly learn motion are particularly valuable for the field. The multi-task learning approach also provides a practical solution to the problem of limited training data for video recognition.

Strengths:
1. Clear, well-organized presentation of a novel and effective architecture
2. Comprehensive experimental evaluation with thorough ablation studies
3. Strong empirical results that significantly outperform previous deep learning approaches for video action recognition
4. Effective use of multi-task learning to address data limitations
5. Clear connection between the proposed architecture and the two-stream hypothesis in visual processing

Weaknesses:
1. The paper could benefit from a more focused narrative that better integrates the two-stream framework and multi-task learning into a single cohesive contribution rather than presenting them as somewhat separate ideas
2. The authors missed citing two important related works in the computer vision literature: "Evaluation of local spatio-temporal features for action recognition" (BMVC 2010) and "Learning Hierarchical Spatio-temporal Features for Action Recognition with Independent Subspace Analysis" (CVPR 2011)
3. While the paper demonstrates impressive results, it does not fully address why the optical flow approach works better than stacked RGB frames (beyond the observation that stacked frames require networks to implicitly learn motion features)

Overall, this is a strong contribution to the field of video action recognition. The two-stream architecture represents a significant advancement over previous deep learning approaches for video recognition, and the results are compelling. The paper is well-written and technically sound, with clear contributions that advance the state of the art. The authors have addressed a key challenge in video recognition—effectively modeling motion—through a simple yet effective architecture that leverages optical flow as input to a convolutional network.

I recommend acceptance at NIPS 2014. The paper makes a significant contribution to the field, with strong empirical results that advance the state of the art in video action recognition. The minor issues noted (particularly the missing citations) are easily addressable in revision and do not detract from the paper's overall quality and impact. The work has clear significance for both researchers and practitioners working on video understanding.
