

This paper proposes a two-stream convolutional network architecture for action recognition in videos, which processes appearance information through a spatial stream (using individual video frames) and motion information through a temporal stream (using stacked dense optical flow). The authors demonstrate that this approach significantly outperforms previous deep learning methods for video action recognition, achieving competitive results with state-of-the-art hand-crafted feature approaches on standard benchmarks (UCF-101 and HMDB-51).

The work builds upon the two-stream hypothesis in human vision (ventral and dorsal pathways) while introducing a data-driven deep learning framework that effectively leverages complementary visual information. The authors address a key limitation in prior deep learning approaches for video recognition (e.g., [14]), which attempted to learn spatio-temporal features directly from stacked RGB frames but failed to capture motion effectively. Instead, they explicitly represent motion using dense optical flow, which the network processes as input. This represents a significant shift from previous deep learning work on video recognition that relied on end-to-end learning from raw pixels.

The paper's strengths are substantial. First, the approach is conceptually simple yet highly effective, demonstrating that carefully chosen input representations (optical flow) can significantly enhance deep learning performance for video tasks. Second, the authors demonstrate how multi-task learning across datasets (UCF-101 and HMDB-51) can effectively address the challenge of limited training data for video action recognition. Third, the empirical evaluation is thorough and well-designed, with comprehensive ablation studies showing the importance of key design choices (e.g., stacking multiple optical flow frames, mean flow subtraction). The results are compelling: the two-stream architecture outperforms the previous state-of-the-art deep learning approach by a large margin (over 20% absolute accuracy gain on UCF-101) while matching the performance of hand-crafted features.

The paper also makes an important methodological contribution by demonstrating how to effectively incorporate optical flow into a deep learning framework. Previous work had largely avoided using optical flow as input to deep networks (e.g., [14] trained on stacked RGB frames), but this paper shows that optical flow provides a natural and effective representation for motion that deep networks can learn from. The authors also provide valuable insights into the relative importance of different input configurations for the temporal stream.

Regarding quality, the experiments are well-designed and appropriately controlled. The authors provide detailed ablation studies on the temporal stream input configurations and demonstrate the benefits of multi-task learning. However, the paper would benefit from additional analysis of error modes to better understand the model's limitations. For instance, it would be valuable to know which specific action classes or video types the model struggles with, and whether these difficulties correlate with particular motion patterns or visual characteristics. This would provide deeper insights into the model's capabilities and limitations.

The paper is exceptionally well-written and clearly organized. The two-stream architecture is explained with sufficient detail, and the experimental setup is thoroughly documented. The figures (particularly Figure 3) effectively illustrate the key input representations. The authors also provide comprehensive implementation details, including training protocols, data augmentation, and computational considerations.

The originality of the work is strong. While the two-stream hypothesis has been previously proposed in vision science [9], this paper is the first to implement it with deep convolutional networks for video action recognition, and to demonstrate its effectiveness. The specific application of deep networks to optical flow representations represents a novel contribution that advances the field beyond previous deep learning approaches for video recognition. The authors also correctly situate their work relative to prior shallow representations [20, 26] and deep learning approaches [14, 16].

The significance of this work is substantial. The two-stream architecture provides a simple yet powerful framework for video recognition that achieves state-of-the-art results while requiring minimal hand-crafted features. This approach has the potential to influence how researchers approach video understanding tasks, demonstrating that carefully selected input representations can greatly enhance deep learning performance for video. The paper's success with optical flow as input challenges the prevailing assumption that deep networks should learn all features directly from raw pixels, opening new avenues for research in video recognition.

The paper's main limitation is its focus on fixed-length (L=10) motion sequences rather than modeling the full temporal dynamics of video. While the authors acknowledge this limitation and note it as a direction for future work, more discussion of how this constraint might impact performance on longer or more complex actions would be valuable. The paper also does not explore the computational cost of optical flow computation relative to other approaches, which could be important for practical applications.

Overall, this is a strong contribution that effectively bridges the gap between hand-crafted feature approaches and deep learning for video action recognition. The paper's technical quality, clarity, and significance meet the high standards of NIPS. The proposed two-stream architecture represents a significant step forward in video recognition, demonstrating that deep learning can effectively leverage complementary visual information when properly represented. I recommend acceptance with confidence.
