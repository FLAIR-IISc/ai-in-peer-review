

This paper introduces a two-stream convolutional neural network architecture for video action recognition, addressing a key limitation in prior deep learning approaches to video classification. The authors propose decoupling spatial (appearance-based) and temporal (motion-based) recognition streams, each implemented as a ConvNet, then fusing their predictions. The spatial stream processes individual video frames (pre-trained on ImageNet), while the temporal stream processes multi-frame dense optical flow. Crucially, the authors demonstrate that training on optical flow—rather than stacked RGB frames—yields significantly better results, and they leverage multi-task learning across UCF-101 and HMDB-51 to overcome small dataset limitations. On standard benchmarks, their approach matches the state-of-the-art hand-crafted methods while outperforming prior deep learning work by a large margin (e.g., +20% over [14] on UCF-101).  

The paper excels in **quality** and **significance**. The experimental design is rigorous: the authors systematically compare optical flow input configurations (stacking vs. trajectory-based, bi-directional vs. unidirectional), validate multi-task learning’s benefits over naive dataset merging, and demonstrate the complementarity of the two streams through ablation studies. Results are compelling—the temporal stream alone outperforms the spatial stream (68.7% vs. 58.5% on UCF-101), and their fusion achieves 81.5% accuracy (surpassing prior deep learning work by 15–20%). Critically, the approach bridges a gap between deep learning and established hand-crafted methods: by using optical flow as explicit input (computed via standard energy minimization), it avoids the need for complex feature engineering while matching the performance of trajectory-based methods [20, 21, 26]. This represents a significant step toward data-driven video recognition.  

**Originality** is well-justified. While prior work (e.g., [14, 16]) used stacked RGB frames or shallow HMAX models for temporal streams, this paper’s two-stream design explicitly separates appearance and motion processing—a concept inspired by the two-stream hypothesis in neuroscience [9]—and leverages discriminatively trained ConvNets to generalize hand-crafted features (e.g., HOF, MBH). The temporal stream’s architecture naturally subsumes these features via convolutional layers (as shown in supplementary visualizations), making it a learnable generalization of prior work. The multi-task learning framework also provides a principled solution to dataset scarcity, avoiding manual class selection required by naive data merging.  

The **clarity** of the paper is generally strong, with clear explanations of the two-stream architecture, optical flow input variants, and multi-task training. However, one aspect could be improved: the paper does not explicitly address why the spatial stream (using single frames) outperforms the single-frame approach in [14] by such a large margin (e.g., 58.5% vs. 42.7% on UCF-101). While the authors note ImageNet pre-training as a factor, a brief comparison of architectural details (e.g., layer depth, data augmentation) between their spatial stream and [14]’s single-frame model would strengthen the argument. This is a minor point, but a more direct comparison would help readers understand the performance gap.  

**Significance** is the paper’s strongest aspect. The work demonstrates that deep learning can rival hand-crafted methods for video action recognition—a long-standing challenge—without requiring domain-specific feature engineering. The two-stream design is modular, scalable (e.g., to larger datasets like Sports-1M [14]), and avoids the pitfalls of implicit motion learning in stacked-frame approaches (e.g., [14]’s 56.4% vs. their 68.7% on temporal stream). The fusion of streams via SVM (not averaging) and multi-task learning for data efficiency are practical contributions that will benefit the community. The results also validate the two-stream hypothesis in a computational context, offering a biologically inspired yet effective framework.  

**Weaknesses**: The paper briefly mentions limitations (e.g., lack of trajectory-aware pooling [26]), but this could be expanded into a dedicated discussion of future work. Additionally, the optical flow computation (using [2]) is noted as a bottleneck, but the authors do not explore whether end-to-end flow learning (e.g., [30]) could further improve performance. These are not critical flaws but opportunities for future extensions.  

**Recommendation**: This is a high-quality, significant contribution to video recognition. It addresses a core challenge in the field with a well-motivated, experimentally validated approach. The results are robust, the methodology is sound, and the work clearly advances the state of the art. The minor clarity issue regarding the spatial stream’s performance gap does not undermine the paper’s strong merits. The paper is **clearly within scope** for NIPS, as it presents a novel deep learning framework for a fundamental computer vision problem with demonstrable impact.  

**Pro**:  
- Strong experimental validation of two-stream design and optical flow input  
- Multi-task learning effectively addresses dataset scarcity  
- Matches SOTA hand-crafted methods without feature engineering  
- Clear technical contributions (input configurations, fusion methods)  

**Con**:  
- Spatial stream performance comparison with [14] could be more explicit  
- Future work on trajectory-aware pooling and end-to-end flow learning is noted but not detailed  

This paper is a valuable addition to NIPS, offering a practical, high-performing solution to video action recognition that will influence both research and applications. I recommend **acceptance**.
