This paper introduces a novel neural network architecture for human action recognition in videos. The core idea is to fuse predictions from two distinct convolutional neural networks: a “spatial” stream processing individual video frames, and a “temporal” stream operating on short sequences of dense optical flow images. The proposed model achieves significant performance gains over existing ConvNet-based approaches and attains state-of-the-art results on two widely used video classification benchmarks.

The work is well-motivated and addresses an important problem in computer vision. While the approach leverages established ConvNet architectures – applying the standard image classification pipeline to both RGB frames and dense optical flow – the effective combination of these two streams, and the thorough investigation of training configurations for the temporal stream, represent a valuable contribution. The experiments are comprehensive, and the reported results are compelling, demonstrating a clear benefit from incorporating optical flow information. In particular, the systematic comparison of different ConvNet training strategies for the optical flow data is insightful.

The paper is generally well-written and easy to follow. However, I have a few minor points that could be addressed to further strengthen the work.

First, the substantial performance advantage of the spatial stream over existing models, specifically those cited as [13], warrants further discussion. Given the reported architectural similarities, a more detailed analysis of potential contributing factors – such as differences in training data, regularization techniques, or optimization procedures – would be helpful. Understanding *why* the spatial stream performs so strongly is crucial for reproducibility and future work.

Second, while the use of dense optical flow is justified and demonstrably effective, it would be interesting to explore the sensitivity of the approach to the frame sampling strategy used for the spatial stream. A comparative analysis of different sampling schemes (e.g., uniform sampling, random sampling, or methods based on shot boundaries) could provide valuable insights into optimizing performance and reducing computational cost.

Despite these minor suggestions, this is a strong empirical paper that will be of significant interest to researchers working in video classification and, more broadly, to those applying ConvNets to spatio-temporal data. The clear presentation of results and the well-defined contributions make this work a valuable addition to the literature. I recommend acceptance.
