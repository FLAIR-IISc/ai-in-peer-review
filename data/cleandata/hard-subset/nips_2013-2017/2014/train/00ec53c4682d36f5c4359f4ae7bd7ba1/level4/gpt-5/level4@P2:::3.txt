Summary and relation to prior work:
This paper proposes a two-stream convolutional architecture for action recognition in videos, combining a spatial ConvNet operating on RGB frames with a temporal ConvNet operating on stacked dense optical flow. The streams are fused at the score level (averaging or linear SVM). The spatial stream benefits from ImageNet pre-training, while the temporal stream exploits multi-frame motion input and is further improved via multi-task learning across UCF-101 and HMDB-51 to mitigate data scarcity. Comprehensive ablations examine optical-flow stacking length, trajectory stacking, bi-directional flow, and mean-flow subtraction. The approach delivers accuracy competitive with state-of-the-art hand-crafted pipelines and substantially better than prior deep models that operate on raw frame stacks.

The method is well-positioned relative to earlier shallow pipelines based on dense trajectories and MBH/HOF features, and to prior deep architectures that either use stacked frames [e.g., work akin to Karpathy et al.] or hand-crafted two-stream HMAX-style models. The explicit separation of spatial and temporal cues with an optical-flow-based temporal stream provides a clear and principled alternative to learning motion implicitly from RGB.

Strengths:
- Clear, well-structured presentation with thorough implementation details (training protocol, data augmentation, multi-GPU optimization, and evaluation protocol).
- Strong empirical evidence: extensive ablations on temporal input design; consistent improvements from multi-task learning; complementary gains from fusing streams.
- Conceptual clarity: the two-stream design directly targets motion vs. appearance, aligning with established insights in vision and with prior two-pathway models.
- Practical impact: shows that explicitly feeding optical flow to a ConvNet is markedly more effective than training on stacked RGB frames when data are limited, and that pre-training/multi-tasking are effective regularizers.

Weaknesses and suggestions:
- The paper reads as multiple contributions (two-stream design, optical-flow stacking, multi-task learning, fusion) rather than a single tightly unified theme. Consider sharpening the abstract and introduction to foreground the two-stream with optical flow as the central idea, with multi-task learning presented as a practical enhancement.
- Related work coverage could be strengthened. Please ensure adequate discussion/citation of:
  • Learning Hierarchical Spatio-temporal Features for Action Recognition with Independent Subspace Analysis (CVPR 2011).
  • Evaluation of Local Spatio-temporal Features for Action Recognition (BMVC 2010).
  Even if already cited, a clearer discussion of how your approach differs and why it performs better would help.
- Limitations could be discussed more explicitly: reliance on precomputed optical flow (compute/storage overhead), sensitivity to flow quality, and scalability to very large web-scale datasets.

Evaluation against NIPS criteria:
- Quality: The methodology appears technically sound, with careful ablations (flow length, trajectory vs. grid sampling, mean subtraction, bi-directional flow) and standard evaluation on UCF-101 and HMDB-51. Claims are supported by experiments; the late-fusion analysis is appropriate. A brief error analysis and discussion of failure modes would further strengthen the empirical section.
- Clarity: The paper is clearly written and reproducible, with helpful implementation details and training schedules. The narrative could be made more cohesive by emphasizing the central contribution early and streamlining the presentation of secondary improvements.
- Originality: The two-stream ConvNet with an explicitly optical-flow-based temporal stream and the application of multi-task learning to action recognition constitute meaningful contributions beyond prior deep models that rely on stacked RGB frames or shallow two-stream HMAX. The work thoughtfully connects to established shallow motion descriptors while generalizing them within a learnable framework.
- Significance: Results are competitive with state-of-the-art hand-crafted approaches and notably stronger than previous deep learning baselines, suggesting the approach will be adopted and extended by the community. The design choices (flow stacking, mean subtraction, multi-task learning) are likely to influence subsequent architectures.

Pros for acceptance:
- Strong empirical gains over prior deep models; competitive with best shallow baselines.
- Clear and compelling architectural idea (two-stream with explicit motion via optical flow).
- Solid ablation studies and practical training insights (pre-training, multi-task learning).
- Good clarity and reproducibility; likely high impact.

Cons against acceptance:
- Somewhat diffuse contribution narrative; multiple threads could be better unified.
- Limited discussion of limitations and computational overhead of optical flow.
- Related work discussion could more thoroughly situate contributions relative to key prior spatio-temporal learning methods.

Recommendation:
Accept (NIPS 2014), conditional on: (1) tightening the abstract/introduction to emphasize the two-stream optical-flow contribution as the main theme; (2) strengthening related work discussion and clarifying distinctions to prior spatio-temporal learning methods; and (3) adding a brief discussion of limitations and computational trade-offs (e.g., precomputing flow, storage/compression, and scalability).
