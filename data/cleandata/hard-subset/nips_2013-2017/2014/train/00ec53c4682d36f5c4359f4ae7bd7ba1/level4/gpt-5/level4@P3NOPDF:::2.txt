This paper proposes a two-stream convolutional architecture for human action recognition in videos. One stream (spatial) operates on individual RGB frames to capture appearance cues; the other (temporal) operates on short sequences of dense optical flow to model motion. The final prediction is obtained by fusing the two streams’ outputs. Despite relying on standard ConvNet components, the approach yields substantial gains over prior ConvNet-based methods and reaches state-of-the-art performance on two widely used action recognition benchmarks. The experimental study is careful and convincing, including multiple ways of constructing and training the temporal stream on optical flow.

Strengths
- Clear, well-motivated architecture: separating appearance and motion is intuitive and effective. The design leverages proven image ConvNet practices while adapting them cleanly to temporal inputs via stacked optical flow.
- Strong empirical performance: the method significantly outperforms other ConvNet-based baselines and is competitive with the best published results on standard datasets.
- Thorough experiments: the paper evaluates several training and input configurations for the temporal stream, providing clear evidence that dense optical flow is a strong supervisory signal for motion modeling.
- Practicality: the approach is straightforward to implement and train, and the fusion strategy is simple yet effective.

Weaknesses and questions
- Novelty is incremental: the main conceptual step is applying the standard image ConvNet pipeline to stacked optical flow and fusing it with a single-frame ConvNet. While the results justify the approach, it would help to more explicitly articulate the architectural or learning insights beyond this adaptation.
- Clarification needed on spatial stream advantage: The spatial network substantially outperforms models from [13] despite apparently similar architectures. Please clarify which differences account for this gap (e.g., pretraining and fine-tuning protocols, data augmentation, sampling density, training schedules, regularization, multi-crop or multi-scale testing).
- Sampling strategies for the spatial stream: It would be valuable to compare different frame sampling schemes (e.g., uniform vs. random vs. motion-aware; single vs. multiple frames per clip at train/test time) and quantify their effect on accuracy and robustness.
- Computational considerations: Precomputing dense optical flow adds storage and time overhead. Reporting training/inference runtimes, flow computation costs, and total resource requirements would help the community assess practicality and fairness in comparisons.
- Sensitivity to flow quality and parameters: Results may depend on the chosen optical flow algorithm, window size, and stack length/stride. An ablation on flow algorithm choice or basic parameter variations would strengthen conclusions about robustness.
- Fusion analysis: The paper uses score-level fusion; a brief comparison with feature-level fusion or learned fusion weights could clarify whether gains are limited by the fusion mechanism.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide a detailed comparison to [13] outlining all training and evaluation differences (dataset splits, pretraining sources, regularization, data augmentation, input resolution, testing protocol). A small ablation narrowing these differences would make the source of improvement more transparent.
- Add a study of spatial-stream frame sampling at train and test time, including the number of frames per video and temporal spacing. Reporting variance across random seeds or sampling draws would illustrate stability.
- Expand temporal-stream ablations: stack length and stride, frame rate effects, and the impact of using forward vs. bidirectional flow stacks, if available. Even a subset of these would clarify what aspects of motion are most beneficial.
- Discuss computational trade-offs: give wall-clock times for flow extraction and ConvNet processing, and memory/storage requirements for cached flow. This will help practitioners plan deployments and future work to reduce overhead.
- Briefly comment on generalization: do the same hyperparameters transfer across datasets? Are there known failure modes (e.g., camera motion, very short actions) where optical-flow inputs struggle?

Overall assessment
This is a strong empirical paper with a simple, well-motivated idea executed carefully. The combination of spatial and temporal streams, particularly the effective use of stacked optical flow, delivers clear gains and advances the state of the art. While the architectural novelty is modest, the experimental validation is thorough and the insights are practically important for the community working on ConvNet-based video understanding. Addressing the questions above—especially clarifying the spatial stream’s advantage over [13] and providing a more systematic look at frame sampling and computational costs—would further strengthen the paper. I recommend acceptance.
