

The paper proposes a two-stream ConvNet architecture for video action recognition, combining spatial stream (still frames) and temporal stream (dense optical flow), along with multi-task learning to leverage multiple datasets. The methodology is technically sound, with thorough ablation studies (optical flow stacking variants, bi-directional flow, mean subtraction) and rigorous evaluation on UCF-101 and HMDB-51. The results demonstrate significant improvements over prior deep learning approaches: the temporal stream alone exceeds Karpathy et al. [14] by 20% on UCF-101, and the fused two-stream model achieves competitive performance with state-of-the-art shallow representations [20, 21, 26] despite limited training data. The implementation details (e.g., optical flow computation, multi-GPU training) are sufficiently clear for reproducibility.

Strengths:  
- **Originality**: The two-stream decoupling (spatial/temporal) provides a novel framework for video recognition, distinct from stacked-frame approaches [14, 16]. It effectively generalizes hand-crafted motion features (HOF, MBH) via convolutional learning, as evidenced by visualized filters in the supplementary material.  
- **Significance**: The work resolves a key limitation of prior deep video networks [14] by showing optical flow input enables robust motion learning without explicit spatio-temporal feature engineering. Multi-task learning further boosts performance on small datasets (HMDB-51), demonstrating practical applicability.  
- **Quality**: Claims are well-supported by experiments (Tables 1–4), including comprehensive comparisons with prior deep learning work and state-of-the-art shallow methods. The ablation studies (e.g., L=10 flow stacking vs. trajectory-based input) rigorously justify design choices.  

Weaknesses:  
- **Clarity**: The narrative could better unify the two core contributions (two-stream architecture and multi-task learning) under a single conceptual thread. The abstract and introduction emphasize optical flow's importance but do not clearly position the two-stream framework as the primary innovation.  
- **Related Work**: The paper overlooks key trajectory-based methods [Le et al., CVPR 2011; Wang et al., BMVC 2010] that predate the optical flow stacking approach, weakening the novelty claim. These should be cited to clarify how the work extends prior art.  

Pro/Con Summary:  
*Pro*: Strong technical execution, clear demonstration of optical flow’s superiority over stacked frames, multi-task learning effectively addresses data scarcity, results exceed prior deep learning work by >20%.  
*Con*: Lack of conceptual cohesion between two-stream and multi-task components; missing citations to foundational trajectory-based methods.  

The paper makes a substantial contribution to video recognition, advancing the field by providing a data-driven alternative to hand-crafted motion features while maintaining strong empirical performance. With minor revisions to clarify the core innovation and update related work, it would be an excellent fit for NIPS. I recommend acceptance.
