This paper presents a novel neural network architecture for classifying videos of human actions. The model combines the predictions of two convolutional neural networks (ConvNets): one trained on single video frames and the other trained on short sequences of dense optical flow images. The proposed two-stream architecture significantly outperforms other ConvNet-based approaches to action recognition and matches the current state-of-the-art on two standard video classification datasets, UCF-101 and HMDB-51.

The paper relates to previous work on video action recognition, which has been largely driven by advances in image recognition methods. The authors discuss how their two-stream architecture extends deep ConvNets, a state-of-the-art still image representation, to action recognition in video data. They also review related work on shallow and deep architectures for video recognition, highlighting the limitations of previous approaches and the benefits of their proposed method.

The paper's main contribution is the introduction of a two-stream ConvNet architecture, which incorporates spatial and temporal networks. The spatial stream performs action recognition from still video frames, while the temporal stream is trained to recognize action from motion in the form of dense optical flow. The authors demonstrate that a ConvNet trained on multi-frame dense optical flow achieves very good performance, even with limited training data. They also show that multitask learning, applied to two different action classification datasets, can be used to increase the amount of training data and improve performance on both datasets.

The authors provide a thorough evaluation of their architecture, comparing different input configurations, training methods, and fusion techniques. They demonstrate that the two recognition streams are complementary and that their fusion significantly improves performance. They also show that temporal ConvNets significantly outperform spatial ConvNets, highlighting the importance of motion information for action recognition.

The paper's strengths include its thorough evaluation, impressive results, and clear presentation. The authors provide a detailed description of their architecture, implementation, and experimental setup, making it easy to follow and reproduce their results.

One potential weakness of the paper is that it does not provide a clear explanation for why the spatial network performs better than similar architectures from previous work. Additionally, it would be interesting to see a comparison of different sampling schemes for the frames fed into the spatial stream.

Overall, this is a good empirical paper that will be of interest to anyone working on video classification with ConvNets. The authors' thorough evaluation and impressive results make a strong case for the effectiveness of their two-stream architecture.

Arguments for acceptance:

* The paper presents a novel and effective architecture for video action recognition.
* The authors provide a thorough evaluation of their architecture, comparing different input configurations, training methods, and fusion techniques.
* The results are impressive, significantly outperforming other ConvNet-based approaches to action recognition.
* The paper is well-written and easy to follow, making it a valuable contribution to the field.

Arguments against acceptance:

* The paper does not provide a clear explanation for why the spatial network performs better than similar architectures from previous work.
* The authors could have explored different sampling schemes for the frames fed into the spatial stream.
