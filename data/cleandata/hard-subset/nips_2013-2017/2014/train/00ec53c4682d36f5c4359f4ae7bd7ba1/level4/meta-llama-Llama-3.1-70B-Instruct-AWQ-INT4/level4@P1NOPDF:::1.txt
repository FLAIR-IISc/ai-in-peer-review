Summary:
This paper proposes a model for solving discriminative tasks with video inputs, consisting of two convolutional neural networks. The input to one network is an appearance frame, while the input to the second network is a stack of densely computed optical flow features. Each pathway is trained separately to classify its input, and the prediction for a video is obtained by taking a weighted average of the predictions made by each network.

The model is evaluated on two datasets, yielding impressive results that match or come close to the state-of-the-art methods that rely heavily on hand-crafted features.

Strengths:
- This model is a simple yet effective application of convolutional neural networks, achieving good results.
- Unlike previous deep learning models that prefer to learn features directly from pixels, this paper demonstrates the usefulness of optical flow (or other hand-crafted features) for vision tasks, particularly action recognition. This is a significant contribution.

Weaknesses:
- The model does not consider the overall sequence of actions throughout the entire video, instead modeling only fixed-length (L=10) adjacent frames.

Quality:
The experiments are well-designed. To provide deeper insights, the authors could include an analysis of error modes, such as identifying classes or groups of classes that the model struggles to classify accurately. It would also be beneficial to characterize the types of videos on which the model fails to perform well.

Clarity:
The paper is clearly written, with the model explained in a well-structured manner.

Originality:
The application of a convolutional neural network to optical flow features is a novel approach.

Significance:
This approach has the potential to significantly impact research on using videos and motion for various vision problems. The model's simplicity, combined with its promising results, makes it a valuable contribution. The use of optical flow features as inputs to a convolutional neural network is a notable innovation.
