Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes a two-stream convolutional architecture for action recognition in videos, with one stream operating on RGB frames (spatial stream) and the other on stacked dense optical flow fields (temporal stream). The key idea is to explicitly decouple appearance and motion, training a ConvNet on each and fusing their softmax outputs (by averaging or a linear SVM). The temporal network takes as input multiple optical flow frames (including variants such as trajectory stacking, bi-directional flow, and mean flow subtraction), while the spatial network is pre-trained on ImageNet and fine-tuned for action classification. To address limited video data, the authors introduce a multi-task learning setup that jointly trains on HMDB-51 and UCF-101 with dataset-specific classification heads.

Relative to prior deep video models that feed stacks of raw frames to a single ConvNet (e.g., Ji et al., PAMI’13; Karpathy et al., CVPR’14), the paper argues and demonstrates that explicitly providing optical flow substantially improves performance and makes learning motion-sensitive features easier. The work also connects to trajectory-based shallow pipelines (e.g., HOF/MBH, improved dense trajectories), showing that the temporal ConvNet can subsume such hand-crafted motion features by learning orientation- and gradient-sensitive filters. Empirically, the approach is competitive with state-of-the-art hand-crafted representations on UCF-101 and HMDB-51 and markedly outperforms prior deep models.

Quality
- The methodology is sound and well-motivated. The authors present a careful set of ablations on the temporal stream (number of stacked flows L, trajectory vs. spatial stacking, mean subtraction, uni-/bi-directional flow), the fusion method, and the benefits of ImageNet pre-training for the spatial net.
- Claims are supported by experiments on standard splits of UCF-101 and HMDB-51, with appropriate evaluation protocols and comparisons to strong baselines (stacked frames, slow fusion).
- The multi-task learning approach is a pragmatic and effective way to leverage both datasets without manual de-duplication of overlapping classes, and it yields consistent gains.
- Implementation details (training schedule, data augmentation, multi-GPU training, precomputation and compression of flow) aid reproducibility.

Clarity
- The paper is clearly written and well organized. The two-stream design and the rationale for explicit motion input are easy to follow.
- The temporal input configurations are described precisely, with helpful formal definitions of optical-flow stacking and trajectory stacking.
- One area for improvement is thematic focus: the narrative at times feels split across multiple contributions (two-stream design, detailed flow input variants, and multi-task learning). A tighter framing in the abstract and introduction that emphasizes two-stream modeling as the central theme, with multi-task learning as a supporting technique, would improve coherence.

Originality
- Decoupling spatial and temporal recognition into two ConvNets and demonstrating the efficacy of an explicitly flow-based temporal stream is a notable and timely contribution.
- The optical flow stacking design and mean flow subtraction are simple but effective; showing that this learned representation generalizes and subsumes classical HOF/MBH features is an insightful connection.
- The multi-task learning application across video datasets is not novel per se but is well executed and valuable in this context.

Significance
- The approach achieves strong performance, outperforming prior deep models by a large margin and approaching state-of-the-art hand-crafted pipelines. The result that optical flow is crucial for deep video recognition is likely to influence subsequent work.
- The two-stream paradigm is simple, scalable, and practical, making it attractive for adoption.

Strengths
- Clear, modular architecture with a compelling motivation.
- Strong empirical gains over prior deep baselines; competitive with hand-crafted SOTA.
- Thorough ablations on temporal inputs and fusion strategies.
- Practical multi-task training to mitigate data scarcity.
- Useful implementation guidance (flow computation, compression, multi-GPU training).

Weaknesses and concerns
- Dependence on precomputed optical flow introduces computational overhead and a non-learned component; discussion of runtime and deployment trade-offs could be expanded.
- While the paper claims to generalize hand-crafted features, more direct quantitative comparisons to HOF/MBH variants within the same pipeline would strengthen the case.
- The discussion of camera motion compensation is limited to mean flow subtraction; a brief analysis of its failure cases or comparison to stronger compensation would be informative.
- The narrative could be more tightly focused around the two-stream thesis.

Suggestions for improvement
- Refocus the abstract and introduction to foreground the two-stream design and the empirical finding that explicit optical flow is essential, with multi-task learning positioned as a secondary contribution.
- Expand the discussion of computational cost (training and inference), including the impact of flow precomputation and the potential for end-to-end learnable motion estimation in future work.
- Clarify the relationship to and differences from prior deep spatiotemporal learning methods (e.g., 3D ConvNets, ISA/unsupervised features) and empirical positioning with respect to improved dense trajectories.
- Ensure adequate citation and discussion of related deep video feature learning and classic spatiotemporal representations (e.g., Le et al., CVPR’11 ISA; Wang et al., BMVC’10 evaluation of local spatiotemporal features), to situate the contribution within both lines of work.

Pro/con list for acceptance
Pros:
- Strong empirical results; clear advance over prior deep approaches.
- Simple, well-motivated architecture with thorough ablations.
- Multi-task strategy addresses dataset limitations.
- Likely to have high impact and be widely adopted.

Cons:
- Reliance on precomputed optical flow (engineering overhead).
- Somewhat diffuse narrative across multiple contributions.
- Limited analysis of camera motion compensation and runtime trade-offs.

Recommendation
Accept. This paper makes a solid, well-validated contribution by establishing a clear and effective two-stream architecture for video action recognition and demonstrating the critical role of explicit optical flow within deep models. With minor revisions to sharpen the narrative and broaden the discussion of related work and computational trade-offs, it would be a strong addition to NIPS 2014.
