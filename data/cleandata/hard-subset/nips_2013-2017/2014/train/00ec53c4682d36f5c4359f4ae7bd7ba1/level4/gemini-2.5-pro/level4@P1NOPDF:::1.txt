**Summary**

This paper proposes a two-stream convolutional network model for discriminative tasks on video data. The model architecture consists of two separate convolutional networks. One stream, the spatial network, processes individual appearance frames. The second stream, the temporal network, takes a stack of densely computed optical flow fields as input. Each network is trained independently for classification. The final prediction for a video is obtained by fusing the outputs of the two streams, for example, through a weighted average of their scores.

The model is evaluated on two standard action recognition datasets. The results are impressive, achieving performance that is on par with or close to state-of-the-art methods that rely on intensively hand-crafted features.

**Strengths**

*   A key strength of the proposed model is its simplicity. It represents a straightforward yet highly effective application of convolutional networks to video analysis, yielding strong empirical results.
*   While many contemporary deep learning models for computer vision aim to learn all features end-to-end from raw pixels, this paper demonstrates the significant benefit of incorporating a well-established, engineered feature like optical flow. By showing that optical flow fields serve as a powerful input representation for action recognition within a deep learning framework, the paper makes an important contribution.

**Weaknesses**

*   A notable limitation is that the model does not account for the long-term temporal structure of a video. It operates only on short, fixed-length clips (L=10 frames) and thus lacks a mechanism for capturing the overall sequence of actions.

**Quality**

The experiments are well-designed and thorough. The analysis would be more insightful, however, if the authors included a discussion of the model's failure cases. For example, are there specific classes or groups of classes that the model struggles with? Characterizing the types of videos on which the model performs poorly would provide valuable insights and directions for future work.

**Clarity**

The paper is well-written and clear. The proposed model and experimental setup are explained effectively.

**Originality**

The primary novel contribution is the application of a convolutional network directly to pre-computed optical flow fields as an input modality for learning motion representations.

**Significance**

This work is poised to have a significant impact on research in video understanding and motion analysis. The model's elegant simplicity and promising results demonstrate a powerful new direction for applying deep learning to video data. The core contribution—using optical flow as a distinct input stream for a convolutional network—is novel and opens up new possibilities for tackling a variety of vision problems involving motion.
