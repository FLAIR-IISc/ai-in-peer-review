This paper introduces a novel neural network architecture for human action classification in videos. The model leverages the complementary strengths of two convolutional neural networks: one trained on individual video frames and the other trained on short sequences of dense optical flow images. The proposed approach substantially outperforms other ConvNet-based methods for action recognition and achieves state-of-the-art performance on two widely used video classification datasets.

While the paper largely applies a standard ConvNet image classification pipeline to dense optical flow frames and integrates this with a single-frame ConvNet, the experiments are comprehensive and the results are compelling. The systematic comparison of various ConvNet training strategies for optical flow is particularly valuable, clearly demonstrating the benefits of incorporating optical flow as an input modality.

I have a few minor comments and questions:

- It remains unclear why the spatial stream performs so significantly better than the models presented in [13], despite their architectural similarities. Further discussion of this discrepancy would be helpful.
- A comparison of different frame sampling schemes for the spatial stream would be a worthwhile addition. 

Overall, this is a strong empirical paper that will be of significant interest to researchers working on video classification using convolutional neural networks.
