**Summary**

This paper proposes a novel model for solving discriminative tasks with video inputs, leveraging the strengths of convolutional neural networks (CNNs). The model consists of two separate CNN pathways: one that takes an appearance frame as input and another that processes a stack of densely computed optical flow features. Each pathway is trained independently to classify its input, and the final prediction for a video is obtained by computing a weighted average of the predictions from both nets. The model's performance is evaluated on two datasets, yielding impressive results that are comparable to, or even surpass, those achieved by state-of-the-art methods that rely heavily on hand-crafted features.

**Strengths**

1. **Simple yet effective**: The proposed model is a straightforward application of CNNs that achieves impressive results, demonstrating the potential of deep learning for video-based tasks.
2. **Novel use of optical flow features**: Unlike previous deep learning models that focus on learning features directly from pixels, this paper explores the utility of optical flow features as a complementary input representation for vision tasks, particularly action recognition. This contribution is significant, as it highlights the value of incorporating domain knowledge into deep learning architectures.

**Weaknesses**

1. **Limited temporal context**: The model only considers a fixed-length sequence of adjacent frames (L=10) and does not account for the overall sequence of actions throughout the entire video. This limitation may impact the model's ability to capture complex, long-range temporal dependencies.

**Quality**

The experiments are well-designed, and the results are convincing. To further strengthen the paper, it would be insightful to include an analysis of the error modes, such as:
* Are there specific classes or groups of classes that the model struggles to classify accurately?
* Can the authors characterize the types of videos on which the model performs poorly?
Such an analysis would provide a more comprehensive understanding of the model's strengths and weaknesses.

**Clarity**

The paper is clearly written, and the proposed model is well-explained. The authors do an excellent job of conveying the key ideas and technical details, making the paper accessible to a broad audience.

**Originality**

The application of a CNN to optical flow features is a novel contribution, and the paper's focus on leveraging domain knowledge to improve deep learning models is a welcome departure from the prevailing trend of learning features solely from pixels.

**Significance**

This approach has the potential to significantly impact research in video-based vision problems, as it demonstrates the effectiveness of a simple yet powerful model that combines the strengths of CNNs with the insights of domain-specific feature engineering. The use of optical flow features as inputs to a CNN is a novel contribution that may inspire further research in this direction.
