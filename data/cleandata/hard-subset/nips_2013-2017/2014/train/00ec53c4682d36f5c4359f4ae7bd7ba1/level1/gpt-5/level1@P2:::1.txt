Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper proposes a two-stream deep architecture for action recognition in videos that explicitly separates appearance and motion: a spatial ConvNet operating on RGB frames (pre-trained on ImageNet) and a temporal ConvNet operating on stacked dense optical flow fields. The two streams are fused at the score level (average or linear SVM). The temporal net explores several optical-flow input designs (stacking L frames, trajectory stacking, forward vs. bidirectional flow, mean flow subtraction). To mitigate limited video supervision, the authors introduce a multi-task learning setup to jointly train on UCF-101 and HMDB-51 with separate softmax heads.

This work is positioned against two main lines of prior art: (1) shallow, handcrafted pipelines based on HOG/HOF/MBH and Improved Dense Trajectories with Fisher Vectors, which were state-of-the-art (e.g., Wang and Schmid, Peng et al.), and (2) deep approaches that take stacks of raw frames and rely on the network to infer motion implicitly (e.g., Ji et al., Karpathy et al.). The paper argues and demonstrates that explicitly supplying optical flow to a ConvNet markedly improves over raw-frame stacks, while approaching the best handcrafted pipelines. The two-stream design is related to the ventral/dorsal hypothesis and earlier two-stream HMAX models, but here both streams are learned, deeper, and discriminative.

Strengths:
- Clear architectural decomposition that leverages ImageNet pretraining for appearance and a well-motivated optical-flow input for motion.
- Thorough and thoughtful ablations on temporal input design (number of flows L, trajectory vs. location stacking, mean subtraction, bidirectionality) and on spatial pretraining/fine-tuning.
- Demonstrates strong performance on UCF-101 and HMDB-51; clearly surpasses prior deep models trained on raw frames and is competitive with (or close to) improved trajectories.
- Practical multi-task training to increase effective data without manual class curation; simple and effective.
- Solid implementation details (multi-GPU training, efficient storage of precomputed flow), making the approach reproducible.

Weaknesses:
- Reliance on precomputed optical flow breaks end-to-end learning and introduces a computational and storage bottleneck; not real-time and may be sensitive to flow quality/hyperparameters.
- Camera motion compensation is handled only by mean flow subtraction; more principled global motion modeling (e.g., homography) is known to help MBH/IDT and could be compared.
- Fusion is performed at the softmax level; attempts at deeper joint fusion reportedly overfit—suggesting limited exploration of regularized/joint training or mid-level fusion.
- Limited analysis of statistical significance and error modes; no per-class breakdown or robustness study to flow errors.
- Comparison to combining deep features with IDT/MBH (a common, strong practice) is missing; this could further contextualize significance.

Quality:
Methodologically sound with well-matched design choices to the problem structure. The ablation studies provide convincing evidence for the key claims: (i) flow-based temporal ConvNets outperform frame-stacking ConvNets trained from scratch; (ii) longer temporal context (L≈10) helps; (iii) mean subtraction reduces camera-motion confounds; (iv) multi-task training regularizes and improves results on small datasets. Training, testing, and data augmentation protocols are carefully described. Some experimental gaps remain (e.g., cost/throughput analysis; stronger camera-motion baselines; combination with IDT), but overall the empirical support is strong and honest about limitations (overfitting when trying joint FC fusion).

Clarity:
Generally very well written and organized. The temporal input configurations are precisely defined, with equations mapping flow fields to input volumes. Implementation details (optical flow computation, data compression, multi-GPU) are explicit. The paper could benefit from a brief discussion on the exact optical flow parameters used and a more explicit runtime/memory table, but clarity is high overall.

Originality:
The two-stream decomposition is not entirely novel conceptually (neuroscience and shallow two-stream HMAX precedents), but the contributions are novel and substantive: a discriminatively trained temporal ConvNet on stacked optical flow, a systematic exploration of optical-flow input design for ConvNets, and a simple multi-task protocol for cross-dataset training. Given that contemporary deep approaches largely relied on raw frame stacks, this explicit motion input is a significant and fresh contribution.

Significance:
High. The paper demonstrates a practical and general recipe for bringing deep learning to video action recognition without requiring massive video datasets, narrowing the gap with the best handcrafted pipelines and surpassing previous deep baselines. The ideas (flow-as-input, two-stream fusion, multi-task training) are likely to be widely adopted and extended (e.g., end-to-end motion estimation, trajectory-aware pooling, better fusion). The work advances the state of the art in a meaningful, reproducible way.

Pros for acceptance:
- Strong empirical gains over prior deep methods; competitive with state-of-the-art handcrafted features.
- Careful ablations isolating what matters in temporal ConvNets.
- Clear, simple, and effective architecture; practical multi-task training for small datasets.
- Good clarity and reproducibility.

Cons for acceptance:
- Not end-to-end; heavy reliance on precomputed optical flow and associated computational/storage costs.
- Limited camera-motion handling and no comparison to more advanced compensation techniques.
- No combination with IDT/MBH to assess complementarity with prevailing handcrafted pipelines.
- Overfitting prevents deeper fusion; limited exploration of alternative fusion/regularization strategies.

Questions/suggestions:
- Provide runtime and memory estimates at test time (per video) for both streams, including flow computation.
- Compare mean subtraction to explicit global motion compensation (e.g., homography) and report impact.
- Explore mid-level fusion (e.g., late-conv/early-FC) with stronger regularization (dropout, weight decay, batch norm) to mitigate overfitting.
- Assess robustness to flow quality by varying the flow algorithm/parameters or injecting noise.
- Report per-class performance and error analysis to illuminate when spatial vs. temporal streams dominate.
- Consider fusing with IDT/MBH features to demonstrate complementarity and push SOTA.

Overall recommendation:
Accept. The paper is technically solid, clear, and impactful, with well-justified design choices and strong results that substantially advance deep video recognition in 2014. Despite the reliance on precomputed flow and some missing comparisons, the contributions are significant and likely to influence future work.
