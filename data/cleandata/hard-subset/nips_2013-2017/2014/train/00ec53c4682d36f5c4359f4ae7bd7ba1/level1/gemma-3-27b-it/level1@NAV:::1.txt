## Review of "Two-Stream Convolutional Networks for Action Recognition in Videos"

This paper presents a two-stream convolutional network architecture for action recognition in videos, separating spatial and temporal information. The core idea is to leverage the strengths of ConvNets on still images (spatial stream) and motion information derived from optical flow (temporal stream). The authors demonstrate that a ConvNet trained on multi-frame dense optical flow achieves strong performance, and that multi-task learning across datasets improves generalization. 

This work builds upon the growing body of research applying deep learning to video understanding. Prior efforts, like those cited from [14, 11], often struggled to capture temporal dynamics effectively. The authors correctly identify this weakness and propose a decoupling strategy, drawing inspiration from the two-stream hypothesis in visual processing [9]. The approach of utilizing pre-trained spatial networks on ImageNet [15] is also a sensible and common practice for leveraging large-scale datasets. The work relates well to existing trajectory-based methods [26, 29] by demonstrating how ConvNets can generalize and learn similar features. 

**Strengths:**

* **Clear Motivation & Problem Definition:** The paper clearly articulates the challenges of video action recognition and the limitations of previous deep learning approaches.
* **Effective Architecture:** The two-stream architecture is a logical and well-motivated approach for separating spatial and temporal information.
* **Strong Empirical Results:** The proposed method achieves competitive results on UCF-101 and HMDB-51, exceeding previous deep learning attempts and approaching the performance of state-of-the-art hand-crafted features.
* **Multi-task Learning:** The inclusion of multi-task learning demonstrates a thoughtful approach to address limited training data.
* **Detailed Implementation:** The authors provide sufficient implementation details, enhancing reproducibility.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Optical Flow Dependence:** The reliance on dense optical flow adds computational cost. While effective, the paper doesn't deeply explore methods for improving the efficiency of flow computation or alternative motion representations.
* **Limited Ablation Study:** While the paper explores different input configurations for the temporal stream, a more thorough ablation study examining the impact of different network depths or fusion strategies would be beneficial.
* **Comparison Scope:** The comparison to existing methods mostly focuses on accuracy. Additional metrics like computational complexity or memory usage would provide a more comprehensive evaluation.
* **Discussion of Biological Plausibility:** While referencing the two-stream hypothesis [9], the paper doesn't further explore the connection to biological vision, which feels like a missed opportunity.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* Novel and effective architecture for video action recognition.
* Strong experimental results demonstrating state-of-the-art or near state-of-the-art performance.
* Clear and well-written presentation. 

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

* Reliance on computationally expensive optical flow computation.
* Relatively limited ablation study.



**Overall:** This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of video action recognition. The proposed two-stream architecture is a well-motivated and effective solution, and the experimental results are compelling. While there are minor weaknesses, they do not detract significantly from the overall quality of the work. I recommend acceptance.
