

# Peer Review: Two-Stream Convolutional Networks for Action Recognition in Videos

This paper proposes a two-stream Convolutional Network architecture for video action recognition, addressing the complementary nature of spatial appearance and temporal motion information. The authors introduce a spatial stream processing individual frames (leveraging ImageNet pre-training) and a temporal stream processing multi-frame dense optical flow. The key contributions include demonstrating the superiority of optical flow over stacked RGB frames for temporal modeling, showing the complementarity of the two streams, and developing a multi-task learning framework to combine datasets (UCF-101 and HMDB-51) for improved training.

The paper relates well to prior work: it builds on the success of deep learning for image classification [15, 19] and addresses the limitations of previous deep video approaches [14], which used stacked frames and underperformed hand-crafted methods. The temporal stream generalizes hand-crafted motion features (HOF, MBH, trajectories) [20, 26, 29] by learning feature hierarchies directly from optical flow, while the two-stream architecture draws inspiration from the human visual system's ventral/dorsal pathways [9]. The authors correctly position their work against related deep learning approaches [12, 16, 18, 25, 28] that failed to effectively model motion.

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound with thorough experimental validation. The authors carefully evaluate different input configurations for the temporal stream (optical flow stacking vs. trajectory stacking, bidirectional flow, mean subtraction), demonstrating that multiple-frame flow stacking with mean subtraction provides the best results. The multi-task learning framework is well-motivated and effectively addresses the data scarcity problem for temporal streams. The evaluation on UCF-101 and HMDB-51 follows standard protocols, and results are clearly presented. A minor concern is the reliance on pre-computed optical flow (using [2]), which doesn't explore how different flow methods might impact performance, though this is a common practice in the field.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The two-stream architecture is clearly explained with good visualizations (Fig. 1, 3), and the methodology sections provide sufficient detail for reproducibility. The experimental results are presented logically, with clear ablation studies on the temporal stream inputs. The writing is concise and avoids unnecessary jargon.

**Originality:** The core contribution—using a two-stream ConvNet with optical flow for the temporal stream—represents a significant advancement over previous deep video approaches. While the two-stream hypothesis [9] and HMAX-based two-stream models [16] exist, this is the first to apply discriminatively trained deep ConvNets to both streams. The paper clearly differentiates from prior work, particularly [14] which used stacked frames and achieved poor results. However, the paper could better address how this approach differs from the recent NIPS 2013 work "Learning to Recognize Human Actions in Video" by Jhuang et al., which also used two streams with hand-crafted features.

**Significance:** The results are highly significant: the proposed architecture substantially outperforms previous deep learning approaches for video classification (by 20%+ over [14]) and achieves competitive results with state-of-the-art hand-crafted methods [20, 21, 26]. The multi-task learning framework effectively addresses data limitations in video action recognition, a critical challenge in the field. The work demonstrates that deep learning can be successfully applied to video action recognition with the right architecture, opening new directions for future research.

**Strengths:**
- Clear and well-motivated two-stream architecture
- Comprehensive evaluation of temporal stream configurations
- Effective use of multi-task learning to overcome data scarcity
- Strong empirical results that significantly advance the state of the art
- Excellent writing and organization

**Weaknesses:**
- Limited exploration of how different optical flow methods might impact results
- Does not provide detailed failure cases or limitations of the approach
- The temporal stream's higher computational requirements (pre-computed flow) are not thoroughly addressed
- Could better explain why their approach outperforms hand-crafted methods

**Overall Assessment:**
This paper makes a strong contribution to the field of video action recognition. The two-stream architecture with optical flow for the temporal stream is well-justified, technically sound, and produces state-of-the-art results for deep learning approaches. The work significantly advances the field beyond previous deep video models [14] and demonstrates that carefully designed deep networks can effectively capture motion information in videos. The multi-task learning approach is particularly valuable for addressing data limitations in video tasks.

The paper is well-suited for NIPS as it addresses a fundamental challenge in computer vision with a technically sound, well-validated solution that advances the state of the art. The results are significant for both practitioners and researchers, with clear implications for future work on video understanding.

**Recommendation:** Accept. This is a high-quality paper that makes a clear contribution to the field. The methodology is sound, the experiments are thorough, and the results are compelling. The authors have clearly differentiated their work from prior art and demonstrated significant improvements over previous deep learning approaches for video action recognition.
