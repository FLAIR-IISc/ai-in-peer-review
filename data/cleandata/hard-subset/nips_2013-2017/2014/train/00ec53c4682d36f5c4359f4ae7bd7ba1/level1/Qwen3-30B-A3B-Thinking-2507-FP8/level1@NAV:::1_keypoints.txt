- Proposes a two-stream ConvNet that separately processes spatial appearance (frames) and temporal motion (dense optical flow) and fuses their outputs.  
- Demonstrates significant gains over prior deep learning methods and competitive results with hand-crafted features on UCF-101 and HMDB-51.  
- Builds on the two-stream neuroscience hypothesis and extends recent deep image-recognition advances to video.  
- Key contributions: (1) decoupled spatial/temporal two-stream architecture, (2) showing optical-flow ConvNets work well with limited video data, (3) multitask learning to combine datasets.  
- Thorough experimental validation including ablation studies (optical flow stacking vs. trajectory stacking) and fusion method comparisons.  
- Comprehensive implementation details that support reproducibility.  
- Clear presentation with well-organized sections and illustrative figures.  
- Originality: principled, data-driven generalization of hand-crafted features, distinct from stacked-RGB approaches.  
- Significance: advances state-of-the-art in video action recognition and provides practical implementation insights.  
- Strengths: clear problem formulation, strong theoretical grounding, extensive experiments, and practical details.  
- Weaknesses: lacks comparison to methods published after submission (2015–2016) and has limited computational cost analysis.  
- Overall recommendation: technically sound, well-executed, strong contribution — recommend acceptance.
