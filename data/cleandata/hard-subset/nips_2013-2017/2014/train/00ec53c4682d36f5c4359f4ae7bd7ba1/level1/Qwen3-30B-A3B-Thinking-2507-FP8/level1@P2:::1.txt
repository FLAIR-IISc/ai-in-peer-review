

# Review of "Two-Stream Convolutional Networks for Action Recognition in Videos"

This paper introduces a two-stream Convolutional Network architecture for video action recognition that decouples spatial (appearance) and temporal (motion) processing streams. The authors build upon the two-stream hypothesis from neuroscience (Goodale & Milner, 1992) and extend deep learning approaches for image recognition to video by training separate spatial and temporal networks. The spatial stream processes individual frames (pre-trained on ImageNet), while the temporal stream processes multi-frame dense optical flow. The authors demonstrate that this approach significantly outperforms previous deep learning methods for video classification and is competitive with state-of-the-art shallow representations.

The paper relates well to previous work at NIPS and elsewhere. It extends the work of Karpathy et al. (2014) who attempted to apply ConvNets to video but found stacked-frame approaches inferior to hand-crafted features. The authors address this limitation by explicitly representing motion through optical flow rather than requiring the network to learn motion implicitly. They also generalize hand-crafted features like HOF and MBH through their convolutional architecture, as noted in their detailed analysis in Section 3.2.

**Quality:** The paper demonstrates strong technical quality. The authors conduct thorough ablation studies on different input configurations for the temporal stream (optical flow stacking vs. trajectory stacking, bi-directional flow, mean subtraction), providing clear evidence for their design choices. The experimental evaluation on standard benchmarks (UCF-101 and HMDB-51) follows established protocols, and the results are well-supported by comprehensive comparisons with previous work. The multitask learning approach effectively addresses the small dataset challenge for video recognition. The authors are careful to acknowledge limitations, such as the reliance on precomputed optical flow rather than end-to-end learning. The only minor quality concern is that the paper doesn't fully explain why stacked-frame approaches (Karpathy et al.) performed poorly, though they provide a reasonable hypothesis about the difficulty of learning motion implicitly.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The two-stream architecture is clearly explained with helpful figures, and the implementation details are sufficiently detailed for reproducibility. The experimental results are presented in a logical progression, with each section building on the previous one. The authors effectively connect their approach to related work, making it easy for readers to understand the novelty. The writing is precise and avoids unnecessary jargon.

**Originality:** The two-stream architecture for video recognition represents a significant original contribution. While the concept of two streams has been discussed in neuroscience, this is the first application to deep learning for video recognition that achieves strong results. The approach of using optical flow as input to a ConvNet generalizes previous hand-crafted features (HOF, MBH) in a data-driven way, which is novel and well-justified. The multitask learning framework for combining datasets is also a practical and novel solution to the small dataset problem in video recognition. Compared to NIPS 2013-2014 proceedings, this work clearly advances the field beyond previous deep learning approaches for video.

**Significance:** The paper addresses a fundamental challenge in computer vision: video action recognition. The proposed method significantly outperforms previous deep learning approaches (by 20% according to the authors) and achieves results competitive with state-of-the-art shallow representations. The two-stream architecture has become foundational for subsequent work in video recognition, as evidenced by its widespread adoption. The authors' demonstration that optical flow is a better input than stacked frames for ConvNets provides a crucial insight that has shaped the field. Their solution to the small dataset problem via multitask learning is also highly practical for the community.

**Strengths:** 
- Clear problem formulation and well-motivated two-stream architecture
- Strong empirical results demonstrating clear advantages over previous approaches
- Thorough ablation studies on key design choices
- Practical solution to the small dataset problem via multitask learning
- Effective connection between the architecture and previous work in both neuroscience and computer vision

**Weaknesses:**
- Limited discussion of why stacked-frame approaches (Karpathy et al.) performed poorly
- Reliance on precomputed optical flow (rather than end-to-end learning) is a limitation
- Some implementation details could be more thoroughly described for full reproducibility

**Recommendation:** This paper makes a significant contribution to video recognition, establishing a new standard for deep learning approaches in the field. It clearly demonstrates that careful architectural design (decoupling spatial and temporal processing) is crucial for video recognition, and the two-stream approach has become foundational for subsequent work. The experimental results are compelling and well-supported. The paper meets all the criteria for a strong NIPS submission, with high quality, clarity, originality, and significance. I recommend acceptance.
