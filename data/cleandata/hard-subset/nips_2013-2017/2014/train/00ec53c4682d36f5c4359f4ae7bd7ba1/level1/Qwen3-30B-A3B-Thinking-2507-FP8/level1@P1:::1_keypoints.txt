- Proposes a two-stream ConvNet architecture separating spatial (appearance) and temporal (optical flow) processing, fused via late fusion.  
- Decomposes video recognition into a spatial stream for frames and a temporal stream for stacked multi-frame optical flow.  
- Key contributions: (1) two-stream architecture, (2) ConvNets trained on optical flow perform well with limited data, (3) multitask learning framework to combine UCF-101 and HMDB-51.  
- Demonstrates state-of-the-art progress for deep learning on video recognition, outperforming prior deep methods and matching competitive hand-crafted approaches.  
- Comprehensive evaluation with ablation studies (flow stacking vs. trajectory stacking, multi-task effects, fusion methods).  
- Provides sufficient implementation and training details for reproducibility.  
- Clearly explains architecture and experiments with helpful figures and logical organization.  
- Carefully situates work relative to prior methods and justifies design choices (e.g., optical flow vs. raw RGB stacking).  
- Acknowledges limitations, including absence of explicit camera motion compensation.  
- Does not incorporate trajectory-based pooling used by top hand-crafted methods, limiting potential gains.  
- Notes computational cost of optical flow computation, only partially mitigated by pre-computation.  
- Lacks deeper analysis on why the temporal stream outperforms the spatial stream beyond empirical results.  
- Originality: extends two-stream hypothesis to deep learning and introduces optical-flow-based ConvNets and dataset multitask learning.  
- Significance: substantial contribution to video action recognition with lasting influence (foundation for later works like I3D).  
- Recommendation: Accept — technically sound, well-executed, significant, and influential.
