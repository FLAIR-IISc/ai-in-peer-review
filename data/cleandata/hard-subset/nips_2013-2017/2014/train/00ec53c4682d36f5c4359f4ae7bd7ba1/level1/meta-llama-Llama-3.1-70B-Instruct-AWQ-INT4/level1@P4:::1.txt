This paper proposes a two-stream Convolutional Network (ConvNet) architecture for action recognition in videos. The main idea is to capture the complementary information on appearance from still frames and motion between frames using two separate recognition streams. The authors also aim to generalize the best performing hand-crafted features within a data-driven learning framework.

The proposed architecture is related to the two-streams hypothesis, which suggests that the human visual cortex contains two pathways: the ventral stream (object recognition) and the dorsal stream (motion recognition). The authors do not investigate this connection further, but instead focus on developing a deep architecture that can learn to recognize actions in videos.

The paper is well-written and clearly organized. The authors provide a thorough review of related work on action recognition using both shallow and deep architectures. They also provide a detailed description of their proposed architecture, including the spatial and temporal ConvNet configurations, and the multi-task learning framework.

The authors evaluate their architecture on the UCF-101 and HMDB-51 datasets, which are among the largest available annotated video datasets. They compare their results to the state of the art and show that their architecture is competitive with the best performing hand-crafted models.

The strengths of the paper are:

* The authors propose a novel architecture that can effectively capture both spatial and temporal information in videos.
* The paper provides a thorough review of related work and clearly explains the proposed architecture.
* The authors evaluate their architecture on large-scale datasets and compare their results to the state of the art.

The weaknesses of the paper are:

* The authors do not provide a detailed analysis of the computational cost of their architecture.
* The paper does not investigate the effect of different hyperparameters on the performance of the architecture.
* The authors do not provide a clear explanation of how their architecture can be applied to other tasks beyond action recognition.
