- Introduces a two-stream ConvNet architecture decoupling spatial (appearance) and temporal (motion) processing for video action recognition.  
- Spatial stream uses ImageNet-pretrained frame-based ConvNet; temporal stream uses multi-frame dense optical flow as input.  
- Builds on two-stream hypothesis from neuroscience and extends deep learning to explicitly model motion rather than learning it implicitly.  
- Demonstrates significantly better performance than prior deep learning methods and competitiveness with state-of-the-art shallow representations on UCF-101 and HMDB-51.  
- Thorough ablation studies (optical flow stacking vs trajectory stacking, bi-directional flow, mean subtraction) justify design choices for the temporal stream.  
- Multitask learning across datasets effectively mitigates the small dataset challenge in video recognition.  
- Relates clearly to prior work (Karpathy et al., HOF/MBH) and generalizes hand-crafted motion features via learned convolutional representations.  
- Strong technical quality with comprehensive experiments and correct evaluation protocols.  
- Exceptionally clear writing, well-organized figures, and sufficient implementation detail for most reproducibility needs.  
- Original contribution: first effective application of a two-stream architecture and optical-flow-based ConvNet for video recognition.  
- Significant impact: establishes a foundational approach widely adopted in subsequent video recognition research.  
- Main limitation: reliance on precomputed optical flow rather than end-to-end motion learning.  
- Minor issues: limited analysis on why stacked-frame ConvNets performed poorly and some implementation details could be expanded.  
- Recommendation: strong accept due to high quality, clarity, originality, and significance.
