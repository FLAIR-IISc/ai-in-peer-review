This paper proposes a two-stream convolutional neural network architecture for action recognition in videos, decoupling spatial and temporal information into separate networks. The key idea is to train a ConvNet on still frames (spatial stream) and another on multi-frame dense optical flow (temporal stream), combining their outputs for improved performance. The authors also explore multi-task learning to leverage data from multiple datasets.

The work builds on prior research in both hand-crafted feature approaches (e.g., trajectory-based methods like MBH [8]) and initial attempts at deep learning for video classification ([14]). Notably, it directly addresses the shortcomings of simply stacking frames as input to a ConvNet [14], which failed to achieve performance comparable to hand-crafted features. The connection to the two-streams hypothesis [9] regarding ventral and dorsal visual pathways is also acknowledged, although not further investigated. Recent work on large-scale video classification with CNNs [14] is appropriately cited and used as a baseline.

**Strengths:**

* **Clear and well-motivated approach:** The paper clearly articulates the problem and the rationale behind the two-stream architecture. Decoupling spatial and temporal information is a sensible strategy.
* **Strong experimental results:** The proposed model achieves competitive results on UCF-101 and HMDB-51, significantly outperforming the approach in [14] and achieving performance comparable to state-of-the-art hand-crafted features.
* **Thorough ablation studies:** The authors conduct a series of ablation studies, examining the impact of different input configurations for the temporal stream (stacking vs. trajectory, bi-directional flow, mean subtraction) and fusion methods.
* **Effective use of multi-task learning:** The multi-task learning framework demonstrably improves performance by effectively increasing the training data.
* **Detailed implementation details:** The paper provides a good level of detail regarding the network architecture, training procedure, and optical flow computation, which aids reproducibility.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited novelty in individual components:** While the combination of two-stream architecture and ConvNets is effective, the individual components (ConvNets, optical flow) are not novel. The core contribution lies in the specific integration and demonstration of its effectiveness for video action recognition.
* **Optical flow computation as a bottleneck:** Although acknowledged, the computational cost of optical flow remains a significant limitation. The authors mitigate this with pre-computation and JPEG compression, but it still represents a potential barrier to scaling the approach to larger datasets.
* **Lack of comparison with more recent deep learning approaches:** While [14] is used as a baseline, several more recent deep learning architectures for video understanding have emerged since the submission date of this paper. A comparison to these more recent methods would strengthen the evaluation.
* **Limited discussion of the learned features:** The paper mentions that the temporal ConvNet generalizes hand-crafted features but lacks a detailed analysis of *which* features are being learned and how they differ from existing approaches. Visualizing the learned convolutional filters beyond the supplementary material would be valuable.



**Arguments for acceptance:**

* The paper presents a technically sound and well-supported approach to video action recognition.
* The experimental results are strong and demonstrate a significant improvement over previous work.
* The thorough ablation studies and detailed implementation details enhance the credibility of the findings.
* The work addresses an important problem in computer vision and provides a valuable contribution to the field.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

* The novelty of the individual components is limited.
* The computational cost of optical flow is a potential drawback.
* A comparison with more recent deep learning approaches would be beneficial.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of video action recognition. The proposed two-stream architecture, combined with the effective use of multi-task learning, achieves state-of-the-art results. While the individual components are not entirely novel, the integration and empirical validation are compelling. I recommend **acceptance** of this paper, with the caveat that a discussion of more recent deep learning methods in the context of this work would further strengthen its impact. The detailed implementation details and thorough ablation studies are particularly commendable.
