This paper proposes a novel two-stream convolutional network architecture for action recognition in videos. The core idea is to decouple the learning of appearance and motion cues into two separate streams: a spatial stream that operates on individual RGB video frames, and a temporal stream that operates on pre-computed dense optical flow. The spatial stream, responsible for capturing scene and object information, is pre-trained on the large-scale ImageNet dataset. The temporal stream is trained from scratch to learn motion patterns from stacked optical flow fields. The final prediction is generated by a late fusion of the two streams' softmax scores. This work directly addresses the shortcomings of prior deep learning approaches (e.g., Karpathy et al., 2014) that struggled to learn effective motion features from raw pixels, and aims to match the performance of state-of-the-art hand-crafted feature methods like Improved Dense Trajectories (Wang & Schmid, 2013).

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Originality and Significance:** The explicit separation of spatial and temporal processing is a simple yet highly effective and novel concept in the context of deep video models. By feeding pre-computed optical flow to the temporal network, the authors provide a powerful and direct motion signal for the model to learn from, bypassing the difficult task of learning motion estimation from scratch. The results are highly significant, as this is one of the first deep learning models to achieve performance competitive with the dominant hand-crafted feature pipelines on standard benchmarks like UCF-101 and HMDB-51. This work sets a strong and influential baseline for future video understanding research.

*   **Quality:** The paper is technically sound and supported by a comprehensive set of experiments. The ablation studies are excellent, clearly demonstrating the contribution of each component: the complementarity of the two streams, the benefit of ImageNet pre-training for the spatial stream, the effectiveness of optical flow over stacked RGB frames for motion, and the gains from multi-task learning.

*   **Clarity:** The manuscript is exceptionally well-written, clearly motivated, and easy to follow. The proposed architecture is explained in detail, and the experimental setup is thorough enough to suggest high reproducibility.

**Arguments against acceptance / Points for improvement:**

*   **Limited End-to-End Learning:** A primary weakness is the reliance on a separate, pre-computed optical flow algorithm. This makes the system a two-stage process rather than a fully end-to-end learned model. The quality of the final recognition is dependent on the performance of this external optical flow method, which may not be optimal for the classification task.

*   **Simple Fusion Method:** The late fusion via averaging or an SVM is straightforward but potentially suboptimal. The authors acknowledge that training a joint network was infeasible due to overfitting, but a discussion of more sophisticated fusion techniques could strengthen the paper.

Overall, the strengths of this paper far outweigh its weaknesses. It presents a clear, powerful, and effective architecture that significantly advances the state of the art for deep learning-based action recognition.
