Summary and relation to prior work:
This paper proposes a two-stream convolutional architecture for video action recognition that decouples appearance and motion. The spatial stream is a ConvNet operating on individual RGB frames (pre-trained on ImageNet and fine-tuned on target video datasets), while the temporal stream is a ConvNet operating on stacked dense optical flow fields across multiple frames. The two streams are fused at the score level (average or linear SVM). The authors further introduce a simple multi-task learning setup with two softmax heads (UCF-101 and HMDB-51), enabling shared feature learning and effective use of limited labeled video data.

The approach is motivated by both neuroscience (ventral/dorsal pathways) and practical limitations of prior deep video models. Compared to (i) earlier hand-crafted pipelines such as improved dense trajectories (iDT) with HOF/MBH and Fisher vectors [Wang & Schmid ICCV’13; Peng et al. ECCV’14], and (ii) deep models operating on raw frame stacks [Ji et al., PAMI’13; Karpathy et al., CVPR’14], the key novelty is to inject motion explicitly via optical flow volumes, which simplifies the learning problem. The authors also relate their temporal stream to classical motion features (HOF/MBH, kinematic features), arguing these can arise from early conv filters, and to prior two-stream HMAX [Jhuang et al. ICCV’07], but with deeper, discriminatively trained ConvNets.

On UCF-101 and HMDB-51, temporal ConvNets on optical flow substantially outperform single-frame or multi-frame RGB ConvNets, and late fusion of spatial and temporal streams achieves accuracy competitive with state-of-the-art hand-crafted pipelines, significantly improving over prior deep baselines (e.g., Karpathy et al.).

Strengths:
- Clear, principled architectural decomposition. Separating appearance and motion is well-justified; temporal stream design (stacked flow with mean subtraction, trajectory vs. fixed-location stacking, forward/bidirectional options) is carefully explored.
- Solid empirical evidence. Extensive ablations on number of stacked flows (L), bi-directional flow, mean flow subtraction, and fusion strategies. Demonstrates strong gains of optical-flow input over stacked RGB.
- Multi-task learning is simple and effective. The two-head loss increases effective data size, improves performance on both datasets, and is easy to reproduce.
- Careful engineering and training protocol. Use of ImageNet pretraining for spatial stream, multi-GPU training, and a rigorous test protocol (multi-crop, multi-frame).
- Meaningful connections to hand-crafted features. The paper positions the temporal ConvNet as a learnable generalization of HOF/MBH and trajectory stacking, and empirically supports this by performance and filter visualization.

Weaknesses and points for improvement:
- Dependence on precomputed optical flow. While effective, it breaks end-to-end learning, adds computational overhead, and introduces a strong dependence on the flow algorithm and its parameters. The paper only uses one flow method (Brox) and does not assess sensitivity to alternative flow or to flow quality at different frame rates. The storage/quantization trick (JPEG-compressed flow scaled to [0,255]) may introduce artifacts; a brief sensitivity analysis would strengthen claims.
- Limited analysis of efficiency. The method requires precomputing and storing large volumes of optical flow and performing two ConvNet passes per frame. Runtime comparisons against iDT and frame-stack ConvNets, and end-to-end throughput (train and test) would help practitioners.
- Scope of fusion. Fusion is limited to late score averaging or a linear SVM; attempts at joint fully connected fusion overfit. Exploring lightweight cross-stream interactions (e.g., gating or feature-level fusion with stronger regularizers, dropout/BN, or partial parameter sharing) could be promising, even as a small-scale experiment.
- Camera motion compensation. Mean flow subtraction helps, but more principled compensation (e.g., global homography/RANSAC as in iDT) might further boost the temporal stream, especially on classes with dominant camera motion. An ablation here is missing.
- Comparison breadth. While the method is competitive with state-of-the-art shallow methods, a combined system (two-stream + iDT) is not evaluated; given complementary cues, such a hybrid would likely set a stronger benchmark. Also, comparisons to 3D convolutional approaches under matched data regimes (e.g., training 3D ConvNets from scratch on UCF/HMDB) are limited.
- Dataset bias and generalization. The spatial stream benefits from strong object/scene priors that may correlate with UCF/HMDB labels. Analysis of failure modes and per-class gains (e.g., motion-dominant vs appearance-dominant classes) would clarify complementarity and robustness.

Quality:
- Technically sound and well-executed. The ablations are thoughtful; training protocols are detailed; and multi-task formulation is appropriate. Claims are supported by experiments on standard benchmarks. Some missing analyses (flow choice sensitivity, efficiency, motion compensation) temper the completeness but do not undermine the main claims.

Clarity:
- The paper is well written and organized. Implementation and training details are sufficient for reproduction. Minor improvements: include explicit runtime and memory footprints; specify exact optical flow normalization ranges and parameters; clarify SVM fusion training (validation split, regularization selection).

Originality:
- The two-stream ConvNet with explicit optical-flow input is a clear and impactful contribution over prior deep approaches on stacked RGB. The multi-task training for video action datasets is a pragmatic and useful addition. Positioning with respect to hand-crafted motion features is insightful.

Significance:
- High. The approach achieves strong accuracy with a clean and scalable design and is likely to influence subsequent work in video understanding. The two-stream idea and optical-flow inputs are practical and broadly adoptable.

Suggestions:
- Evaluate sensitivity to different flow algorithms and to frame rate; include runtime comparisons.
- Explore light cross-stream feature fusion (e.g., 1x1 conv fusion heads) with stronger regularization.
- Incorporate simple camera motion compensation beyond mean subtraction.
- Report class-wise analysis and failure cases to guide future improvements.
- Consider combining two-stream features with iDT to demonstrate complementarity.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Strong, simple idea with clear empirical gains; compelling ablations.
- Competitive with SOTA hand-crafted pipelines; large improvement over prior deep baselines.
- Practical training strategy (multi-task) addressing data scarcity.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Heavy reliance on precomputed optical flow; limited analysis of computational cost and sensitivity to flow.
- Limited exploration of fusion beyond late score fusion; no combination with iDT.
- Some missing analyses (camera motion compensation, generalization to untrimmed videos).

Recommendation: Accept. The paper makes a substantive and influential contribution to deep video recognition with a well-validated and practical architecture.
