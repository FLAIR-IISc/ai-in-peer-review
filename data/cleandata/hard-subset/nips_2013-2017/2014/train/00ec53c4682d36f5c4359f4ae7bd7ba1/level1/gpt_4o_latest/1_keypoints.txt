- Proposes a two-stream ConvNet architecture with separate spatial and temporal streams to capture appearance and motion.
- Uses multi-frame dense optical flow as input to the temporal ConvNet for explicit motion representation.
- Introduces a multi-task learning framework to leverage multiple datasets and improve generalization.
- Technically sound with extensive experiments; dense optical flow input effectively addresses motion capture limitations of stacked frames.
- Novel, well-motivated architecture inspired by the two-stream hypothesis that generalizes handcrafted features in a data-driven way.
- Demonstrates significant improvements over prior deep methods and competitive performance with handcrafted features; multi-tasking helps with small datasets.
- Paper is clearly written with detailed architecture, training procedures, comparisons, and ablation studies.
- Evaluation limited to relatively small datasets (UCF-101, HMDB-51), which may limit generalizability.
- Relies on pre-computed (handcrafted) optical flow, contrasting with end-to-end learning.
- Omits trajectory-based spatio-temporal pooling used by top shallow representations, possibly missing fine-grained motion patterns.
- Computationally expensive due to optical flow precomputation and multi-GPU training, hindering adoption in resource-constrained settings.
- Arguments for acceptance: addresses an impactful problem, introduces a novel architecture, and shows robust empirical results.
- Arguments against acceptance: dependence on pre-computed flow, lack of trajectory pooling, and untested scalability to larger datasets.
- Recommendation: Accept for NeurIPS, with suggested future work on end-to-end motion estimation and scaling to larger datasets.
