- Proposes a two-stream ConvNet separating spatial (appearance from frames) and temporal (stacked dense optical flow) components, fused via late fusion.
- Temporal stream uses stacked dense optical flow as a learnable motion representation; spatial stream benefits from ImageNet pretraining.
- Combines datasets (UCF-101 and HMDB-51) with multi-task learning to regularize training and improve performance on smaller datasets.
- Ablation studies are thorough, evaluating ImageNet pretraining, optical flow input configurations, and multi-task learning effects.
- Achieves strong empirical results, outperforming prior deep learning approaches and matching competitive hand-crafted methods on benchmarks.
- Careful evaluation includes exploration of fine-tuning, fusion methods, and explicit acknowledgment of limitations (no trajectory pooling, camera motion compensation).
- Paper is exceptionally clear and well-organized, with precise architecture descriptions, helpful figures, and reproducible implementation details.
- Novelty lies in effectively using deep ConvNets on precomputed optical flow and explicitly separating appearance and motion modeling.
- Applying multi-task learning to combine partially overlapping action datasets is a sensible, novel solution to data scarcity.
- Significant impact: bridged the gap between deep learning and hand-crafted methods and became an influential baseline in video analysis.
- Demonstrates that ConvNets on optical flow outperform those on raw frames, emphasizing the importance of motion representation.
- Primary weakness is reliance on pre-computed optical flow, making the system non-end-to-end and adding large computational/storage costs (e.g., 27GB for UCF-101).
- Late fusion limits the model's ability to learn feature-level correlations between appearance and motion.
- Does not incorporate trajectory-based pooling to exploit motion-path strengths from dense trajectories.
- Arguments for acceptance: novelty and impact, strong results, thorough experiments, and clarity/reproducibility.
- Arguments against acceptance: heavy computational overhead from optical flow and lack of end-to-end training.
- Conclusion: overall strengths (novelty, significance, empirical validation) outweigh weaknesses; the paper is a major contribution to video understanding.
