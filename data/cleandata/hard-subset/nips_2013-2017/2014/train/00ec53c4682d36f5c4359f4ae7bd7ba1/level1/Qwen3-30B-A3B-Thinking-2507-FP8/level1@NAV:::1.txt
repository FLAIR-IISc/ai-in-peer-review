

This paper proposes a two-stream ConvNet architecture for video action recognition that processes spatial appearance from individual frames and temporal motion from dense optical flow separately before fusing their outputs. The authors demonstrate that this approach significantly outperforms prior deep learning methods for video classification (e.g., [14]) and achieves competitive results with state-of-the-art hand-crafted features on UCF-101 and HMDB-51 benchmarks.

The work builds upon the two-stream hypothesis in neuroscience (Goodale & Milner, 1992) and extends recent deep learning advances in image recognition [15, 31] to video. It makes three key contributions: (1) a two-stream architecture that decouples spatial and temporal processing, (2) demonstration that optical flow-based ConvNets can achieve strong performance despite limited video data, and (3) a multitask learning framework to combine training data across datasets.

The paper excels in quality with thorough experimental validation, including ablation studies on input configurations (optical flow stacking vs. trajectory stacking) and fusion methods. The implementation details are comprehensive, and the authors carefully address limitations (e.g., noting that trajectory stacking underperformed optical flow stacking). The clarity is excellent, with well-organized sections, clear figures, and sufficient technical detail for reproducibility.

Originality is strong as the approach represents a principled generalization of hand-crafted features within a data-driven framework, distinct from prior deep learning work that used stacked RGB frames [14]. The significance is high, as the method provides a robust solution to video action recognition that advances the state of the art while offering practical implementation insights.

Strengths: Clear problem formulation, thorough experiments, strong theoretical grounding, and practical implementation details. Weaknesses: The paper doesn't address how their approach compares to more recent methods published after their submission (e.g., 2015-2016 works), and the computational cost analysis could be more detailed.

Pro: Significant performance gains over previous deep learning approaches, clear technical contributions, well-executed experiments. Con: Limited comparison with very recent work, some technical details could be expanded.

This paper makes a strong contribution to video recognition, advancing the field beyond previous deep learning attempts while providing a practical, effective solution. The work is technically sound, well-executed, and represents a meaningful advancement in the field. I recommend acceptance.
