Summary
The paper proposes a two-stream convolutional network (ConvNet) architecture for video action recognition that explicitly separates appearance (spatial) and motion (temporal) information. The spatial stream processes individual RGB frames and is pre-trained on ImageNet, while the temporal stream operates on stacked dense optical flow fields across multiple frames. The two streams are fused by averaging or by a linear SVM over softmax scores. The authors also introduce a multi-task learning setup to jointly train on UCF-101 and HMDB-51 by sharing the ConvNet trunk and using separate softmax heads, thereby increasing effective training data for the temporal stream. The method is evaluated on UCF-101 and HMDB-51, showing that (1) optical flow–based temporal ConvNets substantially outperform RGB-only models trained from scratch on these datasets, (2) stacking multiple flow fields improves accuracy, (3) mean flow subtraction helps mitigate camera motion, and (4) fusing spatial and temporal streams yields performance competitive with state-of-the-art hand-crafted trajectory-based pipelines (e.g., improved dense trajectories with Fisher vectors). The approach contrasts with prior deep video models that take stacked RGB frames as input (e.g., Karpathy et al., CVPR’14) and often underperform hand-crafted methods. It also generalizes classical optical-flow-based descriptors (HOF/MBH) within a learned convolutional framework and relates conceptually to the two-streams hypothesis and prior two-pathway architectures built with HMAX-like hand-crafted components.

Strengths
- Clear and principled decomposition into spatial and temporal streams; straightforward to implement and reason about.
- Strong empirical results on UCF-101 and HMDB-51, with detailed ablations: number of stacked flows, trajectory vs. fixed-location stacking, bi-directional flow, and mean subtraction.
- Demonstrates that explicitly providing motion via dense optical flow is more effective than expecting the network to infer motion from raw RGB stacks, given limited training data.
- Multi-task learning setup is simple, well-defined, and provides measurable gains for smaller datasets.
- Solid training and testing protocols with reproducible details (optimization schedule, data augmentation, cropping, number of test clips/crops).
- Useful analysis linking learned ConvNet filters to classic motion descriptors, arguing the model subsumes HOF/MBH-like computations.

Weaknesses
- Reliance on precomputed optical flow introduces significant computational and storage overhead (even with compression) and breaks end-to-end learning; no sensitivity analysis to flow quality (e.g., different flow algorithms, noise, compression artifacts).
- Temporal modeling is local (e.g., L=10 frames). There is no explicit long-term temporal aggregation beyond average pooling over clips; actions requiring longer temporal context are not analyzed separately.
- Camera motion compensation is approximated by mean flow subtraction; comparisons to stronger compensation (e.g., homography- or affine-based) are not provided.
- Limited discussion of error modes and per-class improvements/degradations relative to baselines; no robustness analysis to frame rate changes or sampling stride.
- Fusion uses softmax scores; attempts at deeper joint fusion reportedly overfit, but no regularization strategies (e.g., weight decay tuning, stronger data augmentation, or architectural constraints) are explored to enable learnable fusion.
- Comparisons to the best contemporary trajectory-based pipelines could be expanded (e.g., fusion with iDT features), and runtime comparisons are not reported.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The approach is well-motivated and methodologically sound. The temporal input configurations are carefully defined; ablations substantiate design choices (multi-frame stacking, mean subtraction). The multi-task learning objective is straightforward and correctly implemented by summing task-specific cross-entropy losses.
- Empirical support: Experiments cover both datasets with standard protocols across three splits and include relevant ablations and baselines (single-frame, slow fusion RGB). Claims of complementarity between streams and effectiveness of multi-task learning are supported by numbers.
- Reproducibility: Training schedules, architectures, data augmentation, and flow computation details are given. However, end-to-end code availability is not stated; precomputation/encoding of flow may impede easy reproduction.

Clarity
- The paper is clearly written and well organized. Mathematical definitions of optical flow stacking and trajectory stacking are precise, with intuitive figures. Implementation details and evaluation protocols are explicit. Minor suggestions: report computational cost (GPU-hours, test-time throughput) and storage requirements prominently; include per-class accuracy deltas to illuminate where temporal cues help most.

Originality
- Novelty lies in the explicit two-stream deep architecture with a learned temporal stream taking stacked optical flow as input, and in applying a simple multi-task learning formulation to leverage multiple action datasets. While components (optical flow, two-stream neuroscience analogy, HOF/MBH, HMAX two-pathway, multi-task learning) are known, their integration into a discriminatively trained ConvNet framework for video action recognition is original and impactful relative to contemporaneous deep video models relying on RGB stacks.

Significance
- The method advances the state of the art among deep approaches on UCF-101 and HMDB-51 and approaches/tracks hand-crafted trajectory-based methods, indicating a viable deep learning path for video recognition with limited data. The two-stream design and flow-based temporal input are likely to influence subsequent work. Practical limitations (flow computation, short temporal window) suggest clear avenues for future research.

Arguments for acceptance
- Clear, simple, and well-justified architecture with strong empirical evidence.
- Thorough ablations that validate key design decisions.
- Demonstrated complementarity of spatial and temporal streams.
- Effective multi-task learning strategy improving performance on small datasets.
- Competitive results relative to best published methods at the time.

Arguments against acceptance
- Heavy dependence on precomputed optical flow; not end-to-end and computationally expensive.
- Limited modeling of long-range temporal structure; only local stacks and score averaging.
- Insufficient analysis of robustness to flow errors, camera motion, and dataset/domain shifts.
- No runtime/throughput comparison to trajectory-based pipelines; practical trade-offs unclear.

Suggestions for improvement
- Report compute/time/storage costs for training and inference; include latency/throughput.
- Evaluate sensitivity to different optical flow algorithms and to degraded flow (noise/compression).
- Explore stronger camera motion compensation and/or learnable flow front-ends.
- Investigate longer-range temporal aggregation (e.g., temporal pyramids, recurrent units, attention) and learned fusion with stronger regularization.
- Provide per-class analyses and failure case discussion; consider cross-dataset generalization experiments beyond UCF/HMDB splits.

Overall, the paper is clear, technically solid, and makes a significant and original contribution by demonstrating the efficacy of a two-stream ConvNet with optical flow for video action recognition, supported by convincing experiments and ablations.
