- Proposes a novel two-stream ConvNet architecture that separately models spatial (appearance) and temporal (motion) information for video action recognition.
- Spatial stream processes individual frames leveraging ImageNet pre-training; temporal stream processes multi-frame dense optical flow.
- Introduces multi-task learning to jointly train on UCF-101 and HMDB-51, increasing effective training data.
- Three main contributions: two-stream architecture, ConvNets on optical flow demonstrating strong performance, and multi-task dataset combination.
- Thorough experimental evaluation on UCF-101 and HMDB-51 with comprehensive ablation studies.
- Ablation studies show stacking multiple optical flow frames (L=10) significantly outperforms single-frame flow and other flow configurations.
- Demonstrates that ConvNets trained on optical flow achieve strong results despite limited video training data.
- Multi-task learning is well-justified and empirically improves performance over single-dataset training.
- Results significantly outperform previous deep learning approaches and are competitive with state-of-the-art shallow representations.
- Paper clearly acknowledges and discusses limitations and differences from shallow methods.
- Implementation and training details (network configs, optical flow computation) are sufficiently thorough for replication.
- Novelty: first work to explicitly use optical flow as ConvNet input and decouple spatial/temporal processing in this way.
- Significance: advances state of the art in video action recognition and provides a practical method for training deep models on small video datasets.
- Strengths: clear presentation, comprehensive experiments, effective multi-tasking, and good contextualization within prior work.
- Weaknesses: omits some elements of top shallow representations (e.g., local feature pooling over spatio-temporal tubes).
- Weaknesses: temporal stream could benefit from more sophisticated motion handling (e.g., explicit camera motion compensation).
- Suggestion: paper could more explicitly discuss how two-stream ConvNets might complement recent shallow representation methods.
