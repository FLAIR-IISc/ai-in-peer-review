This paper proposes a two-stream ConvNet architecture for video action recognition that explicitly decouples appearance and motion: a spatial stream processes RGB frames (pretrained on ImageNet), and a temporal stream operates on stacked dense optical flow fields. The streams are fused by averaging or an SVM over softmax scores. The authors also introduce a simple multi-task learning setup to jointly train on UCF-101 and HMDB-51, alleviating data scarcity for the temporal net. Extensive ablations show that stacking multiple flow fields, mean displacement subtraction, and multi-task training substantially improve accuracy. The approach outperforms prior deep models that learn from raw frames (e.g., Ji et al., 3D CNN; Karpathy et al., multi-frame “slow fusion”) and is competitive with trajectory-based hand-crafted pipelines (e.g., HOF/MBH and improved dense trajectories by Wang & Schmid). The design connects to the two-stream hypothesis in human vision and demonstrates how ConvNets can generalize traditional motion descriptors within a learned framework.

Quality: The methodology is technically sound with careful experimental design: ablations on flow length, stacking variants (optical vs. trajectory), bi-directional flow, and fusion strategies, plus comparisons to baselines. Training details are thorough (augmentation, optimization, multi-GPU). A limitation is reliance on precomputed optical flow (computational/storage overhead, not end-to-end), and evaluation is confined to two benchmarks without reporting variance/significance or failure analyses.

Clarity: The paper is clearly written and well organized; the optical-flow stacking formulations are precise. Suggestions: clarify the impact of JPEG compression of flow on performance, provide hyperparameters for SVM fusion, and include runtime/memory for the full pipeline (flow+inference).

Originality: While prior work used stacked frames or shallow two-stream HMAX models, the explicit optical-flow temporal ConvNet paired with a pretrained spatial stream and a simple yet effective multi-task scheme is a novel and well-justified combination that differentiates from contemporaneous deep approaches.

Significance: The method sets a strong, reproducible baseline and is likely to be widely adopted, influencing subsequent video recognition architectures that separate appearance and motion.

Pros for acceptance:
- Clear, principled architecture with strong empirical gains and thorough ablations.
- Bridges deep learning with classical motion cues; compelling evidence motion is best injected via flow.
- Practical multi-task learning that improves low-data regimes.

Cons for acceptance:
- Heavy dependence on external flow; no end-to-end learning of motion.
- Limited datasets and missing statistical analyses/runtime reports.
- Fusion at softmax is simple; deeper joint fusion was not feasible due to overfitting.
