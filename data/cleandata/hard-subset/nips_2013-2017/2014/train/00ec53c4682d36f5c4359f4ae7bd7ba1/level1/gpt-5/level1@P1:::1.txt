Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper proposes a two-stream convolutional architecture for action recognition in videos that explicitly decouples appearance (spatial stream) and motion (temporal stream). The spatial stream is a standard image ConvNet applied to single frames and pre-trained on ImageNet. The temporal stream is a ConvNet applied to stacks of dense optical flow fields across multiple consecutive frames (with variants including trajectory stacking, bi-directional flow, and mean flow subtraction). The two streams are fused at the score level (average or linear SVM). To cope with limited video data for training the temporal network, the authors introduce a multi-task learning setup that shares the ConvNet trunk but uses separate softmax heads for UCF-101 and HMDB-51, summing their losses.

This work is well positioned relative to existing literature. Prior high-performing methods are shallow pipelines based on hand-crafted descriptors (HOG/HOF/MBH on dense trajectories, Fisher vectors; e.g., Wang & Schmid, 2013; Peng et al., 2014). Previous deep video models tried to learn spatio-temporal filters from stacks of RGB frames using 3D convs or related architectures (e.g., Ji et al., 2013; Karpathy et al., 2014) but lagged behind hand-crafted pipelines. The authors’ key insight is to present motion explicitly via optical flow to a ConvNet, rather than expect it to be learned implicitly from raw RGB. The two-stream design is also related to the ventral/dorsal hypothesis and to earlier two-stream HMAX (Jhuang et al., 2007), but here both streams are deep, discriminatively trained ConvNets.

Experimental results on UCF-101 and HMDB-51 are thorough: (a) ablations on temporal input design (number of flow fields L, trajectory vs. stacked flow, mean subtraction, bi-directional flow), (b) training from scratch vs. pre-training/fine-tuning for the spatial stream, (c) comparison to RGB multi-frame “slow fusion” baseline, (d) multi-task training effectiveness, and (e) two-stream fusion. The method significantly outperforms prior deep approaches and is competitive with state-of-the-art hand-crafted methods, with clear complementarity between spatial and temporal streams.

Quality:
- Technical soundness: The methodology is straightforward and well-motivated. The design choices are evaluated with relevant ablations (e.g., L=1 vs. 5 vs. 10; mean flow subtraction; bidirectional flow). The multi-task learning is correctly formulated and improves performance where data are scarce. The fusion strategy is simple yet justified by overfitting concerns for deeper fusion.
- Empirical support: Claims are supported by solid experiments on standard splits and with appropriate metrics. The demonstration that optical-flow-based temporal ConvNets outperform ConvNets on stacked RGB frames is compelling.
- Limitations: The approach depends on precomputed optical flow (Brox et al.), which is computationally expensive and not learned end-to-end. The paper does not quantify sensitivity to the choice/quality of the flow algorithm or the impact of lossy flow compression (JPEG). Runtime analysis and memory/computation trade-offs are only briefly discussed.

Clarity:
The paper is clearly written and well-structured. The temporal input formulations are precisely specified, and implementation/training details are sufficient for reproduction (architectures, augmentation, learning rates, GPU setup, number of crops/frames). A few clarifications would help:
- Explicitly define the temporal sampling stride between stacked flows and how τ is chosen at train/test time.
- Provide more details on the temporal data augmentation (e.g., random temporal jitter around τ).
- Report the impact (if any) of JPEG compression on flow and resulting accuracy.
- Describe how class imbalance (if any) is handled in multi-task training and whether task losses are weighted.

Originality:
The two-stream design with a ConvNet trained on multi-frame dense optical flow is novel and differs from prior deep architectures that operate on RGB frames. The mean-flow subtraction and exploration of trajectory stacking are sensible contributions. The application of multi-task learning to jointly train on UCF-101 and HMDB-51 for video is a practical and useful idea. While the use of optical flow itself is not new in action recognition, feeding it directly as input to a deep, discriminatively trained network is a meaningful step beyond hand-crafted descriptors.

Significance:
This work offers a simple and effective recipe that closes much of the gap between deep learning and hand-crafted pipelines for video action recognition at the time of submission. The approach is likely to be influential and adopted by practitioners: it leverages readily available ConvNet components and off-the-shelf optical flow, demonstrates strong results on standard benchmarks, and provides clear ablations and practical training tricks (multi-task sharing, pre-training). The explicit motion representation is an important insight for the field.

Pros:
- Clear, simple architecture with strong empirical performance and comprehensive ablations.
- Convincing evidence that explicit optical flow input enables deep models to capture motion better than stacked RGB.
- Complementarity of spatial and temporal streams is clearly demonstrated.
- Practical contribution: multi-task learning for limited video data, with measurable gains.
- Good discussion relating the temporal ConvNet to classical descriptors (HOF/MBH/trajectories), helping the community understand connections.

Cons:
- Heavy reliance on external optical flow; no analysis of sensitivity to flow methods/parameters or computational cost vs. accuracy.
- Fusion explored only at the score level; deeper fusion discouraged due to overfitting without exploring stronger regularization or parameter sharing.
- No results combining with strong hand-crafted baselines (e.g., improved dense trajectories), which could set a new SOTA and demonstrate complementarity.
- Limited analysis of per-class behavior and failure modes (e.g., actions dominated by scene vs. motion; camera motion effects beyond mean subtraction).
- Minor clarity gaps on temporal sampling and flow compression effects.

Questions and suggestions:
- Please report runtime: preprocessing time per video for flow, and inference time for each stream and fused model. This will aid practicality comparisons to hand-crafted pipelines.
- Evaluate robustness to different flow algorithms (e.g., TV-L1) or parameter settings, and quantify the impact of JPEG compression on flow fields.
- Consider modest architectural or regularization strategies to enable earlier fusion (e.g., joint fc layers with dropout/weight decay, low-rank bilinear pooling, or shared late layers) and compare to score-level fusion.
- Provide per-class results or qualitative analysis to understand where each stream excels; analyze impact of camera motion and whether mean-subtraction suffices.
- Explore combination with improved dense trajectories/Fisher vectors to test complementarity with the best shallow pipelines.
- Clarify multi-task training details: sampling ratio between tasks, whether task losses are balanced, and how you avoid leakage across splits when using “all splits combined” from the auxiliary dataset.

Recommendation:
Strong accept. The paper addresses a central problem with a well-motivated, novel, and practical approach, provides careful empirical validation and ablations, and is likely to have significant impact on deep video recognition. Constructive additions on flow robustness, computational profiling, and fusion/combination with hand-crafted features would further strengthen the paper.
