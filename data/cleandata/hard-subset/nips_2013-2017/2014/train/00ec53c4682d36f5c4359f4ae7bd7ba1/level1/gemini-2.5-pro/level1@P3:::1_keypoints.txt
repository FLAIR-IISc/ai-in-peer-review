- Proposes a novel two-stream ConvNet architecture separating spatial (appearance) and temporal (motion) processing for video action recognition.
- Spatial stream uses a ConvNet pre-trained on ImageNet to process individual frames and capture appearance information.
- Temporal stream uses a ConvNet trained on stacked dense optical flow fields to explicitly model motion between frames.
- The two streams are combined via late fusion (averaging or SVM on softmax scores).
- Introduces multi-task learning to augment limited action recognition datasets (UCF-101, HMDB-51) for training the temporal stream.
- Experiments are extensive and rigorous, with standard evaluation protocols and results averaged over splits.
- Ablation studies thoroughly validate components: ImageNet pre-training benefit, temporal input configurations, mean subtraction, bi-directional flow, and number of frames.
- Demonstrates that using pre-computed dense optical flow as ConvNet input is highly effective and bridges hand-crafted motion features with deep learning.
- Achieves significant performance gains over prior deep learning methods (e.g., 88.0% on UCF-101 vs. 65.4%) and is competitive with iDT-based state-of-the-art.
- Paper is well-written, clearly motivated, and includes helpful diagrams and honest discussion of limitations.
- Key weakness: reliance on external, pre-computed optical flow prevents end-to-end training and adds computational/storage overhead.
- Key weakness: simple late fusion likely suboptimal, preventing earlier complementary feature learning between streams.
- Key weakness: ConvNet architectures within streams are adapted from existing image networks, so architectural novelty is limited.
- Overall assessment: high-quality, original, and significant work that sets a strong baseline for deep video recognition; strong recommendation for acceptance.
