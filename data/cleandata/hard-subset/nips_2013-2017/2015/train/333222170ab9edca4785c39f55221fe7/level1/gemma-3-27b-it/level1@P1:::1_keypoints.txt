- Proposes a novel on-the-job learning framework combining Bayesian decision theory with MCTS to query crowdsourcing while training a model in real time.  
- Addresses a practical deployment problem: balancing latency, cost, and accuracy with limited initial labeled data.  
- Extends prior work and reframes active learning as a stochastic game, offering a principled alternative to traditional methods.  
- Paper is well-structured and clearly explains formulation, model, and algorithm.  
- Strong connection to related work in online learning, active learning, crowdsourcing, and structured prediction.  
- Experiments on NER, sentiment, and image classification show significant cost reductions and improvements over baselines.  
- Notable empirical result: 8% F1 improvement over single-human labeling on NER.  
- Open-source implementation (LENSE) enhances reproducibility and impact.  
- Novelty: unique integration of Bayesian decision theory, MCTS, and on-the-job learning.  
- Practical relevance: useful for deploying systems with minimal labeled data.  
- Clear presentation and organization aid comprehension.  
- Reproducibility is a strong point due to released code.  
- Scalability concerns: MCTS scalability to large state spaces or complex models is not fully addressed.  
- Simplistic crowd model assumption (e.g., 70% accuracy) — sensitivity to crowd quality and richer worker models not thoroughly explored.  
- Limited comparison to advanced active learning methods (uncertainty sampling, query-by-committee) adapted to online settings.  
- Hyperparameter tuning details (UCT constant, utility weights) and sensitivity analysis are lacking.  
- Task specificity: weaker results on Sentiment and Faces than NER, with insufficient analysis of cross-task differences.  
- Arguments for acceptance: novel, theoretically sound approach with strong empirical results and open-source code addressing an important problem.  
- Arguments against acceptance: potential scalability limits, restrictive crowd assumptions, and incomplete comparisons to stronger active learning baselines.  
- Overall recommendation: strong paper, recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address scalability, crowd modeling, advanced baselines, hyperparameter details, and task-specific analysis.
