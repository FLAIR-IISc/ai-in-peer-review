- Introduces “on-the-job learning”: a decision-theoretic framework that queries crowd workers at test time and updates a model online to reduce crowd reliance.
- Formulates the problem as a stochastic game with actions to query, wait, or return, and a Bayesian utility trading off accuracy, latency, and monetary cost.
- Exact optimization is intractable; authors approximate the optimal policy using MCTS with UCT and progressive widening for continuous response times.
- Environment model combines a structured predictor pθ(y|x) (e.g., CRF), a fixed noisy response model pR (70% uniform accuracy), and a response-time model pT.
- Empirical results on NER, sentiment, and image classification show improved accuracy–cost tradeoffs vs human-only and machine-only baselines, with strong NER cost reductions and F1 gains.
- Strength: clear decision-theoretic formulation that yields intuitive strategies (e.g., parallel vs sequential querying).
- Strength: principled planning approximation (MCTS + progressive widening) for asynchronous continuous-time responses.
- Strength: structured prediction setting and partial-label propagation through pθ are well-motivated.
- Strength: favorable empirical results across tasks and public release of code/datasets enhance reproducibility.
- Weakness: simplistic, fixed worker model pR and underspecified pT may misrepresent real worker heterogeneity, correlations, or systematic errors.
- Weakness: mismatch between optimized utility (ExpAcc) and reported evaluation metric (F1), especially for structured outputs, is not discussed.
- Weakness: baselines are limited or not decision-theoretically comparable (online baseline uses gold labels; threshold baseline ignores time); missing myopic VOI or POMDP comparators.
- Weakness: heavy reliance on simulation from a small frozen pool of responses rather than extensive live end-to-end evaluation may understate variance and nonstationarity.
- Weakness: computational cost, number of simulations per decision, and planning latency are not reported, leaving scalability and real-time feasibility unclear.
- Weakness: game restrictions (e.g., launching queries while waiting) are acknowledged but the empirical impact of those restrictions is not evaluated.
- Clarity issues: exact form and estimation of pT, metric used inside ExpAcc for structured outputs, CRF features/training, UCT constant, progressive widening params, and hyperparameter sensitivities are under-specified.
- Originality: novel integration of real-time crowd querying, structured prediction, online model updates, and continuous-time MCTS planning.
- Significance: practically impactful approach to deploy accurate systems without pre-collected training data, but current modeling and evaluation limitations weaken real-world claims.
- Pros for acceptance: principled formulation, solid algorithmic contribution, strong NER results, and open-source release.
- Cons for acceptance: simplistic worker/latency models, simulation-heavy evaluation, weak baselines and metric mismatch, and missing runtime analysis.
- Suggestions: learn or adaptively estimate pR and pT (with sensitivity analyses), align utility with evaluation metric (or justify surrogate), add myopic VOI/POMDP baselines, report runtime/latency overhead, provide live deployment results, and perform ablation studies on key restrictions and hyperparameters.
