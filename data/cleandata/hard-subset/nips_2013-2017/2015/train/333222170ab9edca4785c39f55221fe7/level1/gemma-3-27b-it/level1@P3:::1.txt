This paper presents an interesting and well-motivated approach to on-the-job learning, framing it as a stochastic game and using Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) for decision-making. The core idea – balancing latency, cost, and accuracy by intelligently querying a crowd for information – is a valuable contribution, particularly in scenarios where pre-training data is scarce or unavailable. The experimental results on NER, sentiment classification, and image classification demonstrate substantial cost reductions and performance gains compared to existing methods.

The paper is well-structured and clearly written. The introduction effectively establishes the motivation and context, highlighting the limitations of traditional supervised learning and crowdsourcing approaches. The problem formulation and model description are detailed and mathematically sound. The use of Bayesian decision theory provides a principled framework for handling the trade-offs inherent in the on-the-job learning setting. The experimental setup is comprehensive, including several baselines and a clear description of the crowdsourcing procedure. The inclusion of a publicly available implementation (LENSE) is a significant positive aspect, promoting reproducibility and further research.

However, the paper isn’t without weaknesses. While the MCTS approximation is necessary given the intractability of the optimal policy, the explanation of its implementation (Algorithm 1) feels somewhat dense and lacks sufficient detail to easily reproduce.  Specifically, the progressive widening strategy isn’t described with enough clarity – what constitutes a “visited” state, and how are states “grown” exactly? A more intuitive explanation, perhaps with a small illustrative example, would be beneficial.  

The choice of the utility function (Equation 1) is reasonable, but the weights (wM and wT) are not thoroughly justified. How were these parameters selected, and what is the sensitivity of the results to their values?  A sensitivity analysis would strengthen the claims. Furthermore, the comparison to baselines feels somewhat uneven. While the online learning baseline is appropriate, the “human n-query” baseline, while useful, doesn't truly represent a dynamic learning system.  A stronger comparison would involve active learning methods specifically designed for the online setting, such as those that iteratively query for labels based on uncertainty sampling.  The paper acknowledges Legion:AR [6] but downplays its contribution, stating it doesn’t “explore the machine learning foundations.” This seems inaccurate; Legion:AR certainly tackles similar challenges and introduces learning-based components. 

The experiments also lean heavily towards NER. The improvements on sentiment classification and image classification are less dramatic, and the paper doesn’t delve deeply into why the approach performs better on NER. Is it related to the task structure, the inherent noisiness of the data, or the effectiveness of the CRF model in this domain?

Finally, the discussion of related work, while comprehensive, could be more critical. For example, the paper mentions active learning but doesn’t adequately address the challenges of applying these techniques to the online, continuous-time setting. Similarly, the connection to reinforcement learning, where the system learns a policy through trial and error, is not explicitly drawn, even though the stochastic game formulation lends itself to this interpretation.  

**Arguments for acceptance:**

* **Novelty:** The framing of on-the-job learning as a stochastic game and the application of MCTS are novel contributions.
* **Significance:** The substantial cost reductions and performance gains demonstrated in the experiments are significant, particularly for resource-constrained scenarios.
* **Technical Soundness:** The methodology is theoretically grounded in Bayesian decision theory and supported by empirical evidence.
* **Reproducibility:** The publicly available implementation promotes reproducibility and further research.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

* **Implementation Details:** The lack of clarity in the MCTS implementation hinders reproducibility.
* **Parameter Sensitivity:** The lack of sensitivity analysis regarding the utility function weights weakens the claims.
* **Baseline Comparison:** The baseline comparison is somewhat uneven and doesn't fully capture the state-of-the-art in online active learning.
* **Limited Analysis:**  The discussion of why the approach performs better on some tasks than others is lacking.




**Overall Assessment:**

Despite its weaknesses, this is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of on-the-job learning. The core ideas are innovative, the methodology is sound, and the experimental results are compelling.  I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions, specifically addressing the clarity of the MCTS implementation, providing a sensitivity analysis of the utility function parameters, and strengthening the baseline comparison. A more detailed discussion of the task-specific performance differences would also be beneficial.
