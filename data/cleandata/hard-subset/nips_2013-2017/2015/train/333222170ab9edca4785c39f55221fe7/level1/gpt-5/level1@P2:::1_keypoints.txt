- Contribution: Proposes "on-the-job learning"—a test-time framework that starts with zero labeled data and interleaves model prediction with real-time crowdsourcing to reduce queries over time.
- Core model: Formulates a stochastic game with a CRF predictor, crowd response/noise model, response-time model, and a Bayesian utility trading off accuracy, monetary cost, and latency.
- Planning: Uses Monte Carlo Tree Search (UCT) with progressive widening to approximate intractable planning over continuous response times.
- Experiments: Evaluated on NER, sentiment, and image classification, showing improved accuracy–cost tradeoffs vs human-only, machine-only, and a threshold baseline, with strong NER gains.
- Relation to prior work: Connects to online learning, active learning/feature acquisition, and POMDP crowd control (e.g., TurKontrol) and Legion:AR; novelty is joint structured prediction + real-time crowd + decision-theoretic planning.
- Strength: Well-motivated formal model and utility that naturally trade off cost and latency and yield intuitive querying policies (parallel vs sequential, redundancy).
- Strength: MCTS with progressive widening is a reasonable, scalable planning approximation for continuous-time response dynamics.
- Strength: Broad experimental setup, demonstrable cost reductions while matching/exceeding accuracy, desirable learning behavior over time, and released code/data.
- Weakness: Crowd noise and latency models are simplistic and fixed (constant 70% correctness, uniform confusion), ignoring worker/item heterogeneity and class-dependent confusions.
- Weakness: Learning update for the CRF pθ is underspecified—unclear whether noisy responses are marginalized or treated as hard labels, risking inconsistency with the planner’s noise model.
- Weakness: ExpAcc is based on MAP but "Accuracy" is undefined for structured outputs (token vs sequence vs F1), risking utility–metric misalignment (especially for NER).
- Weakness: Baselines are limited—missing principled myopic VOI, active classification, and POMDP-style comparisons to justify the need for MCTS planning.
- Weakness: Evaluation uses frozen pools of pre-collected responses, which caps redundancy benefits and may distort sequential/asynchronous planning dynamics relative to a live crowd.
- Weakness: No statistical significance tests, no ablations on MCTS hyperparameters (UCT constant, widening), number of simulations, or on utility weights wM and wT.
- Weakness: Computational/planning costs are not reported; need evidence that MCTS overhead is negligible relative to crowd latency and scales acceptably.
- Clarity issues: Paper should clarify pθ training objective with noisy responses, define the accuracy metric per task, describe the response-time model and its estimation, report MCTS budgets, and justify claims about surpassing expert NER labels.
- Originality: Novel combination of decision-theoretic planning, structured prediction, and real-time crowd interaction with continuous-time MCTS planning beyond prior systems.
- Significance: Addresses an important practical problem—deploying accurate systems from day one without labeled data—and shows promising cost savings likely to inspire follow-up work.
- Pros for acceptance: Principled formulation, practical approximation (MCTS), strong empirical improvements, open-source code, and focus on test-time querying for structured outputs.
- Cons for acceptance: Simplistic crowd models, limited baselines and ablations, ecological validity concerns from simulation protocol, unreported computational overhead, and some unclear methodological details.
- Suggestions: Learn/adapt pR and pT online (worker/item heterogeneity), align training with the noise model (e.g., EM/marginalization) and utility with evaluation metrics (expected F1), add stronger baselines and ablations, report planning latency, and validate with a live crowd or richer simulations.
- Recommendation: Lean accept—compelling, well-motivated framework with solid evidence and public code despite notable modeling and evaluation limitations.
