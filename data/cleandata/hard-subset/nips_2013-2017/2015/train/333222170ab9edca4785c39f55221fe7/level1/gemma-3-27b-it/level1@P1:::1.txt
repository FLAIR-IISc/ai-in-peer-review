This paper proposes a novel framework for on-the-job learning, combining Bayesian decision theory with Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) to intelligently query crowdsourcing for labels while simultaneously building a predictive model. The core idea—balancing latency, cost, and accuracy in a real-time, streaming fashion—is compelling and addresses a practical challenge: deploying AI systems with limited initial labeled data. This work extends earlier ideas from the authors ([6]) and offers a principled approach to active learning that differs significantly from traditional methods.

The paper is well-structured and clearly explains the problem formulation, model, and algorithm. It establishes a strong connection to related work in online learning, active learning, crowdsourcing, and structured prediction, appropriately citing relevant literature. The experiments on three diverse tasks (NER, sentiment classification, and image classification) provide convincing evidence of the approach’s effectiveness, demonstrating significant cost reductions compared to full annotation and improvements over baseline methods like online learning and a simple thresholding approach. The availability of an open-source implementation (LENSE) is a valuable contribution.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The integration of Bayesian decision theory, MCTS, and on-the-job learning is a novel combination. The framing as a stochastic game is insightful.
*   **Practical Relevance:** The problem addressed – deploying systems with minimal initial labeled data – is highly relevant and practically important.
*   **Strong Empirical Results:** The experimental results consistently demonstrate the benefits of the proposed approach, both in terms of cost reduction and accuracy improvement. The 8% F1 improvement over single-human labeling on NER is particularly impressive.
*   **Clear Presentation:** The paper is well-written, clearly organized, and easy to follow.
*   **Reproducibility:** The open-source implementation is a significant strength, allowing others to verify and build upon the work.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Scalability:** While MCTS is effective, its scalability to very large state spaces or complex prediction models is not explicitly addressed. The paper mentions progressive widening, but a deeper discussion of its effectiveness in practice, and potential bottlenecks, would be valuable.
*   **Crowd Model Assumptions:** The paper assumes a relatively simple model for crowd responses (70% accuracy). The sensitivity of the approach to variations in crowd quality and the potential for more sophisticated modeling of worker reliability (e.g., using techniques from TurKontrol [26]) are not thoroughly investigated.
*   **Limited Comparison to Advanced Active Learning:** The comparisons to active learning baselines are somewhat limited. A comparison to more sophisticated active learning techniques that incorporate uncertainty sampling or query-by-committee, especially those adapted for online settings, could strengthen the evaluation.
*   **Hyperparameter Tuning:** The details of hyperparameter tuning (e.g., for the UCT constant, the utility weights) are not fully transparent. It would be helpful to understand how sensitive the performance is to these parameters.
*   **Task Specificity:** The results on Sentiment and Faces are less compelling than those on NER. The authors speculate that this is due to the dominance of one class in NER, but a more thorough analysis of the differences in performance across tasks is warranted.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound approach to on-the-job learning.
*   The empirical results are strong and demonstrate a clear benefit over existing methods.
*   The open-source implementation promotes reproducibility and encourages further research.
*   The problem addressed is practically important and the solution has the potential for real-world impact.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   The scalability of the MCTS approach may be a concern for very large problems.
*   The assumptions about crowd worker accuracy could limit the generalizability of the results.
*   A more comprehensive comparison to advanced active learning techniques is desirable.

**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field. The novelty of the approach, the compelling empirical results, and the availability of an open-source implementation outweigh the minor weaknesses. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the points raised above, specifically regarding scalability, crowd model assumptions, and comparison to more advanced active learning methods. The authors should also provide more details on hyperparameter tuning and a more in-depth analysis of the task-specific performance variations.
