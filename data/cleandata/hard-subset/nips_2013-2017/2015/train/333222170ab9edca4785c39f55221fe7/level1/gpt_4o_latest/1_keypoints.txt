- Introduces "on-the-job learning" to deploy high-accuracy systems without pre-existing labeled data by using real-time crowdsourcing that decreases as the model improves.
- Formalizes the setting as a stochastic game grounded in Bayesian decision theory balancing latency, cost, and accuracy.
- Proposes an MCTS-based approximation (with progressive widening) for the intractable optimal policy.
- Evaluated on NER, sentiment, and image classification, showing large cost reductions and accuracy gains (e.g., 8% F1 over single human label, 28% over online learning on NER, cost reduced by an order of magnitude).
- Strength: novel combination of online learning, active learning, and crowdsourcing with an innovative stochastic-game formulation and MCTS policy.
- Strength: practically significant for low-label scenarios (e.g., disaster relief), demonstrating clear cost and accuracy improvements.
- Strength: technically sound Bayesian decision-theoretic framing and reasonable approximation; thorough experiments and analyses.
- Strength: paper is clear, well-organized, and accompanied by open-source code and data for reproducibility.
- Weakness: limited evaluation scope—strong gains mainly on NER, weaker on sentiment and image tasks, needing broader datasets/tasks.
- Weakness: simplified crowd model assumes fixed 70% worker accuracy, not capturing real-world worker variability.
- Weakness: scalability concerns due to reliance on real-time crowdsourcing, especially for high-throughput or latency-sensitive settings.
- Weakness: baselines omit more recent active-learning/human-machine hybrid methods, limiting contextualization of improvements.
- For acceptance: novel, well-executed approach with significant empirical gains and open-source impact.
- Against acceptance: narrow evaluation and simplifying assumptions about crowd behavior and scalability may limit real-world applicability.
- Recommendation: accept with minor revisions to address scalability analysis and broaden evaluation.
