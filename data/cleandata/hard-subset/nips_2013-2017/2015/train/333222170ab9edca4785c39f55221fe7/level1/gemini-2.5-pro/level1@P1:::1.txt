This paper introduces "on-the-job learning," a framework for deploying a high-accuracy prediction system starting with no labeled data. The core idea is to process a stream of inputs, and for each one, decide whether to make a prediction with the current model or to query a real-time crowd for labels on uncertain parts of the input. As the system gathers more labeled data from the crowd, its internal model improves, reducing its reliance on crowd queries over time. This setting is distinct from standard online or active learning, where the goal is typically to build a good model for the future, rather than ensuring high accuracy on the *current* instance before prediction.

The authors formalize this problem as a stochastic game between the system and the crowd. The system's actions include querying a specific part of the input, waiting for a response, or returning a final prediction. The decision-making is governed by Bayesian decision theory, aiming to maximize a utility function that provides a principled trade-off between prediction accuracy, monetary cost (per query), and latency (time spent waiting). The underlying predictive model is a Conditional Random Field (CRF) that is updated online. Since computing the optimal policy for this game is intractable, the authors propose a practical approximation based on Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS), using the UCT algorithm for action selection and progressive widening to handle the continuous nature of time. This approach is related to prior work in active classification (e.g., Gao & Koller, NIPS 2011) and decision-theoretic control of crowdsourcing (e.g., TurKontrol), but novelly integrates the learning of a structured prediction model into a dynamic, test-time querying policy.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Significance and Originality:** The paper addresses a highly significant and practical problem: how to bootstrap a high-quality machine learning system in a "cold start" scenario without a large upfront data collection cost. The "on-the-job learning" paradigm is a compelling and valuable contribution. The formulation as a stochastic game governed by a clear utility function is elegant and provides a solid theoretical foundation for the problem. This combination of ideas from active learning, online learning, and game playing is novel and well-executed.

2.  **Technical Quality:** The proposed solution is technically sound. Modeling the problem as a stochastic game is appropriate, and the choice of MCTS with progressive widening is a state-of-the-art and suitable method for approximating the intractable optimal policy. The experimental evaluation is strong, covering three distinct tasks (NER, sentiment, image classification) and comparing against sensible baselines, including a pure-human approach, a pure-online-learning approach, and a strong heuristic baseline. The results convincingly demonstrate the benefits of the proposed framework, showing substantial cost reductions (over an order of magnitude in the NER task) and accuracy improvements.

3.  **Clarity and Reproducibility:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The problem, model, and algorithm are explained with clarity and precision. The inclusion of a link to a CodaLab worksheet with all code, data, and experiments is a major strength, adhering to the highest standards of reproducibility. The experimental methodology, particularly the use of a pre-collected "frozen pool" of worker responses to ensure fair and controlled comparisons, is commendable.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Model Simplicity:** The model for the crowd (`pR`) is a simple, fixed-accuracy model (70% accurate, uniform errors). In reality, crowd worker quality varies significantly between individuals and can change over time. The system does not model or adapt to individual worker reliability, which is a key aspect of more advanced crowdsourcing systems like TurKontrol. While adding complex worker models would increase the difficulty of the MCTS rollouts, a discussion of this limitation and potential extensions would strengthen the paper.

2.  **Computational Cost:** The decision-making process relies on MCTS, which involves running many simulations to estimate the value of each possible action. The paper does not discuss the computational overhead of this process. For a system that operates in real-time, the time taken to decide *which* query to make (or whether to wait) is a critical factor. Some analysis of the decision-making latency (i.e., the wall-clock time for the `MONTECARLOVALUE` function) and its sensitivity to the number of simulations would be a valuable addition.

3.  **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The utility function in Equation 1 has two crucial weighting parameters, `wM` (cost per query) and `wT` (cost per unit of time). These parameters define the desired trade-off and would presumably be set by the system operator. However, the paper does not mention how these were set in the experiments or how sensitive the system's performance is to their values. An ablation study or discussion on the effect of these hyperparameters would improve the practical applicability of the work.

**Summary of arguments:**

*   **Pro-acceptance:**
    *   Addresses a significant and practical real-world problem.
    *   Novel and principled formulation based on Bayesian decision theory and stochastic games.
    *   Strong technical execution with a state-of-the-art MCTS-based approximation.
    *   Thorough and convincing experimental results across multiple tasks.
    *   Exceptionally clear presentation and fully reproducible research.

*   **Con-acceptance:**
    *   The crowd model is simplistic and ignores known challenges like varying worker reliability.
    *   Potential concerns about the real-time computational feasibility of the MCTS-based decision-making are not addressed.
    *   Lack of discussion on the setting and sensitivity of key hyperparameters in the utility function.

Overall, the strengths of this paper far outweigh its weaknesses. It introduces a valuable new learning paradigm, provides a strong theoretical and algorithmic solution, and demonstrates its effectiveness with a rigorous and reproducible evaluation. This is a complete and high-quality piece of work that is likely to be influential and inspire follow-up research. The weaknesses are primarily limitations that point towards interesting future work rather than fundamental flaws in the current contribution.
