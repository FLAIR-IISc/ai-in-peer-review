

# Peer Review: On-the-Job Learning with Bayesian Decision Theory

This paper proposes a novel "on-the-job learning" framework that enables deploying high-accuracy AI systems from zero training examples by strategically using crowdsourcing to resolve uncertainty in real-time. The authors model the problem as a stochastic game based on Bayesian decision theory, balancing accuracy, cost, and latency objectives. They develop an approximation using Monte Carlo Tree Search with progressive widening, and evaluate their system (LENS) on three tasks: named-entity recognition, sentiment classification, and image classification.

The paper builds on relevant work in active learning (e.g., [8,9,10]), active classification (e.g., [22,23,24]), and crowdsourcing (e.g., [25,26]), but makes a significant contribution by formulating the on-the-job learning setting as a stochastic game where the system both learns from crowd responses and makes predictions in real-time. This differs fundamentally from prior active learning approaches that assume static policies or require full labeled datasets. The authors correctly identify the key distinction from Legion:AR [6], which operates in a similar setting but lacks the formal machine learning foundation this paper establishes.

**Quality:** The paper presents a technically sound framework with appropriate theoretical grounding. The stochastic game formulation is well-motivated, and the utility function (Equation 1) effectively balances the three key objectives. The implementation details are robust, including the environment model (Equation 3) and MCTS approximation. The experimental evaluation is comprehensive, with three different tasks, four baselines (Human n-query, Online learning, Threshold baseline, LENSE), and thorough analysis of cost-accuracy tradeoffs. The results are compelling: on NER, LENSE achieves >10x cost reduction compared to 5-vote human labeling while improving accuracy. However, the paper would benefit from more detail on how the CRF model is updated with crowd responses (beyond "AdaGrad"), and the claim of "70% accurate" crowd workers needs empirical justification beyond the brief mention.

**Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and logically structured. The problem formulation (Section 2) and game model (Section 3) are clear, though the state representation could be more intuitively explained. The figures (2a, 2b, 3, 4) effectively illustrate the key concepts. The experimental section (Section 5) provides sufficient detail for reproducibility, though the "frozen pool of worker responses" methodology could be clarified. The writing is precise and avoids unnecessary jargon.

**Originality:** The paper makes a strong contribution by formalizing on-the-job learning as a stochastic game with Bayesian decision theory. This is a novel approach that extends beyond prior active learning and active classification work. The key innovation lies in the dynamic nature of the system—learning from crowd responses during test time while simultaneously making predictions. While Legion:AR [6] shares a similar application setting, this paper provides the first formal machine learning foundation for the approach, making it a significant extension of prior work. The authors properly contextualize their contribution in Section 6.

**Significance:** The problem addressed is highly relevant to practical AI deployment where labeled data is scarce or expensive. The results demonstrate clear advantages over both pure ML (online learning) and pure crowdsourcing approaches. The >10x cost reduction on NER is particularly compelling, and the system's ability to reduce query costs over time is a major practical advantage. The framework could enable AI deployment in resource-constrained settings (e.g., disaster response, as mentioned in the introduction). However, the paper could better articulate the broader implications for AI deployment in real-world settings.

**Strengths:**
- Novel formalization of on-the-job learning as a stochastic game with Bayesian decision theory
- Strong empirical results across three diverse tasks with significant cost-accuracy improvements
- Comprehensive comparison to multiple relevant baselines
- Open-source implementation (LENS) available for reproducibility
- Clear demonstration of the framework's ability to reduce costs over time

**Weaknesses:**
- Insufficient detail on how the CRF model is updated with crowd responses
- Claim of "70% accurate" crowd workers lacks empirical backing
- Limited discussion of the framework's limitations and failure modes
- Connection to Legion:AR [6] could be more explicitly compared

The paper is well-suited for NIPS as it combines theoretical rigor with practical relevance, addressing a significant challenge in AI deployment. It advances the state of the art by providing a principled framework for balancing multiple objectives in a real-world setting where labeled data is scarce. The results are convincing and the approach has clear potential for real-world impact.

**Recommendation:** Accept. This paper makes a significant contribution to the field by formalizing on-the-job learning as a stochastic game and demonstrating its practical advantages over existing approaches. The technical quality is high, the results are compelling, and the significance is clear. With minor revisions addressing the weaknesses noted above (particularly the crowd accuracy claim and CRF update details), this paper would be an excellent contribution to NIPS.
