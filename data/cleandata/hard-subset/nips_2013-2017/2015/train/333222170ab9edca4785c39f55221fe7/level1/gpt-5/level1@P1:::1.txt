Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes “on-the-job learning,” a decision-theoretic framework that deploys a high-accuracy predictor from zero training data by interleaving real-time crowd queries with model inference and online learning. At test time, the system decides whether to issue additional (possibly redundant) queries, to wait for pending responses, or to output a prediction, trading off accuracy, latency, and monetary cost via a Bayesian expected-utility objective. The environment includes models of the base predictor pθ(y|x) (e.g., a CRF for NER), crowd response noise pR, and response-time pT. Because optimal planning is intractable, the authors approximate with Monte Carlo Tree Search (UCT) and progressive widening to handle continuous time. Experiments on NER, sentiment classification, and image classification (faces) show lower cost for comparable quality to human-only baselines and higher accuracy than machine-only and online learning baselines; an open-source system (LENSE) is provided.

This work sits at the intersection of active learning/active classification, decision-theoretic control of crowdsourcing (e.g., TurKontrol), and structured prediction with partial observations. It extends active classification/value-of-information approaches by (i) planning online per input under latency/cost, (ii) learning during deployment so reliance on the crowd decays, and (iii) handling structured outputs (CRF). It relates to Legion:AR (real-time crowdsourcing for activity recognition) but offers a more formal decision-theoretic formulation and a unified search-based planner. It also connects to “measurements” in structured prediction and test-cost sensitive classification.

Quality
Strengths:
- Clear probabilistic formulation tying together predictive uncertainty, noisy responses, and response-time; explicit utility that trades cost, time, and accuracy.
- A practical planner (MCTS + progressive widening) capable of reasoning about asynchronous responses and parallel vs sequential querying.
- Empirical evidence on three tasks; on NER, substantial cost reductions vs 5-vote crowdsourcing and accuracy gains vs single-vote and online learning. Code and data are released.

Concerns:
- Utility/loss mismatch: the utility uses expected “Accuracy(argmax p(y))”, while evaluation emphasizes F1 for NER. The paper does not explain how ExpAcc is computed for structured F1, nor whether the utility aligns with the reported metric. If the loss used to plan differs from the evaluation loss, this could bias conclusions.
- Noise model pR is fixed at 0.7 (uniform over errors) and not learned or adapted online; no worker heterogeneity. Yet evaluation uses pre-collected real worker responses. This mismatch risks miscalibration of planning under realistic noise rates.
- The predictive model learning details are under-specified: the CRF is trained “based on all actual responses,” but the true labels are never observed. Is the model trained on raw (noisy) responses, aggregates, or via EM/dawid–skene-like inference? How are multiple redundant labels incorporated? How is pθ calibrated to support decision-theoretic planning?
- Planning details and computational costs are not fully reported: UCT parameters (c), rollout policy, horizon, per-decision latency, and sensitivity to these choices are missing. Real-time feasibility is asserted, but wall-clock planning cost vs response-time should be quantified.
- Baselines could be stronger: a myopic value-of-information planner or POMDP-style single-step lookahead would better isolate the benefit of multi-step MCTS; comparisons to TurKontrol-like adaptive redundancy (with learned worker reliabilities) would be informative.
- The “28% F1 improvement over online learning” requires context: online learning baseline receives gold for past examples, which is an optimistic machine-only baseline, but could also be improved with delayed predictions or mini-batching. Clarify fairness.

Clarity
- The game formulation and Figure descriptions are clear, and Algorithm 1 is helpful. However, more detail is needed on:
  - Computing ExpAcc for structured outputs and non-decomposable metrics.
  - How online updates are performed after each example, and how crowd responses enter the training objective.
  - The response-time model pT (parametric form, fit procedure).
- Minor issues: several typos (“one-the-job,” “burgenoning,” “miscellaneos,” “comporable”), and some footnotes are unclear.

Originality
- Novel combination of decision-theoretic planning with real-time crowdsourcing and structured prediction, including explicit modeling of latency. MCTS with progressive widening for this setting is a reasonable and fresh contribution. Prior work on value-of-information, TurKontrol, and Legion:AR addresses related pieces; this paper provides a unified and general framework applicable beyond binary/flat tasks.

Significance
- Practical significance is high: a deployable method to achieve high accuracy from day one while smoothly reducing supervision. The NER gains and cost reductions are promising. However, broader impact depends on robustness to worker heterogeneity, calibration, and metric alignment. With stronger baselines and ablations, the case for state-of-the-art advancement would be more compelling.

Pros
- Principled, general framework that explicitly optimizes accuracy/latency/cost.
- Handles asynchronous responses and structured outputs.
- Open-source system; reproducible experiments on multiple tasks.
- Demonstrates substantial cost savings and accuracy gains on NER.

Cons
- Loss/metric mismatch and unclear computation of ExpAcc for F1.
- Simplistic/noisy response model; no learning of worker reliabilities.
- Incomplete training details for pθ and calibration.
- Limited baseline comparisons and ablations; planning cost not reported.

Suggestions for improvement
- Align planning loss with evaluation metric (e.g., expected token-level F1 or a surrogate calibrated to F1), and detail computation or approximation.
- Learn and adapt the crowd noise model online (e.g., Dawid–Skene or hierarchical Bayes), and analyze sensitivity to worker accuracy and heterogeneity. Consider incorporating per-worker reliabilities into pR and planning.
- Provide full details on online learning: objective, handling of multiple/noisy labels (aggregation or latent-variable training), and calibration techniques (temperature scaling, isotonic regression).
- Report planning overhead (ms per decision), UCT parameters, rollout policy, and sensitivity; include an ablation against a greedy/myopic VOI baseline.
- Evaluate sequential vs parallel querying trade-offs more thoroughly and analyze effect of wM and wT.
- For fairness, include baselines that allow delayed machine predictions or batch updates and an adaptive redundancy crowd baseline (à la TurKontrol).

Overall assessment
A timely, well-motivated paper with a principled formulation and a practical algorithm that shows strong results on NER and reasonable gains on two other tasks. The main concerns are the loss/metric mismatch, simplistic noise modeling, and incomplete methodological details that limit confidence in the generality of the results. With clarifications and the suggested experiments, this could be a strong contribution.

Confidence: moderate.
