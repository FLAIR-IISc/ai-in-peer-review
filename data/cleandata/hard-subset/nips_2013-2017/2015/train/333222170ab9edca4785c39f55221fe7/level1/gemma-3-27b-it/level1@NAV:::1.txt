## Review of "On-the-Job Learning with Bayesian Decision Theory"

This paper presents a compelling framework for on-the-job learning, where a system learns from interactions and crowd-sourced feedback without relying on pre-trained models. The core idea – modeling the learning process as a stochastic game using Bayesian decision theory to balance latency, cost, and accuracy – is a strong contribution. The authors appropriately position their work within the context of online learning, active learning, and crowdsourcing, highlighting how their approach differs by actively improving *during* test time and maintaining high initial accuracy. Related work is comprehensively covered, referencing key papers in each relevant field.

The strengths of this paper lie in its principled approach to a practically important problem. The use of Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) to approximate the optimal policy is a clever solution to the intractability of the full Bayesian decision problem. The experimental results are promising, demonstrating a significant cost reduction in named entity recognition (NER) compared to full annotation, and performance improvements over both single-label and standard online learning baselines. The code release is a huge plus for reproducibility and further research.

However, the paper has some weaknesses. While the experimental validation is solid, the tasks chosen are somewhat limited. Focusing primarily on NER might lead to results not generalizing well to other problem domains. The explanation of the game-theoretic formulation, especially the utility function (Equation 1), could be more intuitive. It's not immediately clear why the chosen coefficients (wM, wT) are appropriate. While the authors acknowledge worker quality variation and attempt to mitigate it, a more detailed analysis of the impact of noisy labels would be valuable. The progressive widening technique used in the MCTS algorithm isn't thoroughly explained and feels somewhat like a black box.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel and principled approach to on-the-job learning.
*   Strong experimental results demonstrating significant cost savings and accuracy improvements.
*   Well-written and comprehensive related work.
*   Code release promotes reproducibility.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Limited scope of experimental evaluation.
*   Utility function formulation could be clarified.
*   Lack of in-depth analysis of worker noise impact.
*   Insufficient explanation of the progressive widening technique.



**Overall:** Despite some minor weaknesses, this is a strong paper with a clear contribution to the field. The novelty of the approach, combined with the promising experimental results and code availability, makes it a worthwhile addition to NIPS. I recommend **acceptance**.
