This paper presents an interesting approach to on-the-job learning, framing it as a stochastic game and utilizing Bayesian decision theory to balance latency, cost, and accuracy. The core idea of strategically querying a crowd to resolve uncertainty in real-time, gradually reducing reliance on human input as the model improves, is compelling and addresses a practical need – deploying AI systems with limited initial labeled data. The connection to existing work in online learning and active learning is well-articulated, and the authors clearly differentiate their approach by emphasizing the simultaneous learning *and* prediction aspect, alongside the dynamic policy refinement. The experimental results are promising, demonstrating significant cost reductions and accuracy improvements compared to baseline methods, particularly on the NER task. The availability of an open-source implementation (LENSE) is a major strength, promoting reproducibility and further research.

**Quality:** The technical foundation appears solid. Modeling the problem as a stochastic game and utilizing Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) is a reasonable approach for tackling the intractability of the optimal policy. The Bayesian decision theory framework provides a principled way to balance competing objectives. However, the details of the MCTS implementation and progressive widening could benefit from further elaboration; the current description feels somewhat high-level. The empirical evaluation is well-conducted, with clear comparisons to relevant baselines. The ablation study (varying features in Figure 3) is helpful for understanding the impact of model capacity. The authors acknowledge limitations in worker quality and address it by freezing a pool of responses, a sensible choice. The paper demonstrates a good level of technical rigor, but a more in-depth analysis of the MCTS search space and its impact on performance would strengthen the work.

**Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The introduction clearly motivates the problem and outlines the proposed solution. The problem formulation and model description are reasonably accessible, though the mathematical notation could be simplified in places. Figure 2 is helpful for visualizing the game tree and the query process. However, Algorithm 1, while conceptually clear, could benefit from more detailed comments explaining each step. The discussion of the experiments is clear, and the results are presented in a concise and informative manner.

**Originality:** The combination of Bayesian decision theory, MCTS, and on-the-job learning in this specific context is novel. While individual components (MCTS, Bayesian optimization, active learning) have been used in related areas, their integration to address the problem of deploying AI systems with zero initial training data is a significant contribution. The authors acknowledge related work appropriately, including Legion:AR, a similar approach, but highlight the differences in their focus on the underlying machine learning foundations.  The formalization of the setting as a stochastic game provides a fresh perspective and a strong theoretical basis for the proposed approach.

**Significance:** This work has the potential to be highly impactful. The ability to deploy accurate AI systems without extensive upfront labeling has broad applications, particularly in scenarios where data acquisition is expensive or time-consuming (e.g., disaster relief, real-time monitoring, emerging markets). The demonstrated cost reductions and accuracy improvements are significant, and the availability of LENSE will likely encourage further research and adoption.  The approach addresses a genuine challenge in practical AI deployment and moves beyond simply improving accuracy on existing benchmarks to tackling the problem of *initial* deployment. The exploration of the interplay between model capacity and the need for human supervision (Figure 3) provides valuable insights for practical system design.



**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel combination of techniques to address a practical problem.
*   Strong experimental results demonstrating significant cost savings and accuracy gains.
*   Clear and well-motivated approach grounded in Bayesian decision theory.
*   Availability of an open-source implementation.
*   Addresses a crucial challenge in real-world AI deployment: the cold-start problem.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The description of the MCTS implementation could be more detailed.
*   A more in-depth analysis of the search space explored by MCTS would be beneficial.
*   Some mathematical notation could be simplified for better readability.



**Overall Assessment:** I believe this paper represents a strong contribution to the field and is worthy of acceptance to NIPS. The novelty of the approach, the solid technical foundation, the compelling experimental results, and the practical significance of the problem addressed all contribute to its overall quality. While there are minor areas for improvement, they do not detract significantly from the paper’s overall merit. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the points mentioned above.
