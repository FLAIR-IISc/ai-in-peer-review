This paper proposes a novel framework called on-the-job learning, which enables the deployment of high-accuracy systems starting with zero training examples. The authors model on-the-job learning as a stochastic game based on Bayesian decision theory, allowing for the principled balancing of latency, cost, and accuracy objectives. The framework is evaluated on three tasks: named-entity recognition, sentiment classification, and image classification, demonstrating significant cost reductions and accuracy improvements over baseline methods.

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound, with a clear and well-supported theoretical analysis. The authors provide a thorough explanation of their framework, including the game tree, utility function, and environment model. The experimental evaluation is also well-designed, with a clear description of the datasets, baselines, and results. However, the paper could benefit from a more detailed analysis of the limitations and potential biases of the framework.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-written and easy to follow, with clear explanations of the technical concepts and a logical structure. The authors provide sufficient background information and context for non-experts, making the paper accessible to a broad audience. However, some of the mathematical notation and technical details may be challenging for non-experts to follow.

**Originality:** The paper presents a novel framework that combines ideas from stochastic games, Bayesian decision theory, and crowdsourcing. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach on three tasks, showing significant improvements over baseline methods. While the individual components of the framework are not entirely new, the combination and application of these ideas to on-the-job learning is innovative and original.

**Significance:** The paper addresses a significant problem in the field of machine learning, namely the deployment of high-accuracy systems with limited training data. The authors demonstrate the potential of their framework to reduce costs and improve accuracy in various applications, making it a valuable contribution to the field. The paper also opens up new research directions, such as the application of on-the-job learning to other tasks and the exploration of new algorithms and techniques for stochastic games and Bayesian decision theory.
