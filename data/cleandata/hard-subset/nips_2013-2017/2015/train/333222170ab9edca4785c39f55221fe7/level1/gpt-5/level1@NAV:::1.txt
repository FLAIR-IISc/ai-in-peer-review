The paper proposes “on-the-job learning,” a deployment-time framework that starts with zero training examples and achieves high accuracy by issuing real-time crowdsourcing queries where the model is uncertain, while continuously updating the model to reduce future reliance on the crowd. The decision problem is formalized as a stochastic game grounded in Bayesian decision theory that trades off accuracy, latency, and cost; the optimal policy is approximated via Monte Carlo Tree Search with progressive widening to handle continuous response times. Instantiated with a CRF for structured prediction, the system (LENSE) is evaluated on NER, sentiment, and image classification, yielding substantial cost reductions vs. majority-vote annotation and accuracy improvements vs. single-human and online learning baselines. This work connects to online learning [7], online active learning [8–10], active classification [11, 22–24], decision-theoretic crowdsourcing (e.g., TurKontrol [26]) and real-time crowd systems (Legion:AR [6]), while leveraging UCT [12] and progressive widening [13].

Quality: The methodology is sound and carefully engineered. The utility is well-motivated and the MCTS approximation is reasonable for the intractable state space. Empirical results are compelling on NER and respectable on sentiment/faces. However, the environment model assumes i.i.d. workers with fixed 70% accuracy and independent token responses, which may bias policy quality; calibration/sensitivity analyses are limited. Comparisons to closely related POMDP-style controllers (e.g., [26]) and adaptive worker modeling are missing. Statistical significance and ablations (e.g., without progressive widening, different utility weights) would strengthen claims.

Clarity: The paper is generally clear and well-organized, with precise game-state definitions and a readable algorithm box. It would help to (i) justify the ExpAcc utility and report how wM, wT are chosen; (ii) clarify the claim “boosting performance relative to expert labels”; and (iii) provide more intuition on policy behavior under parallel vs. sequential querying.

Originality: Formalizing real-time, test-time crowd querying as a stochastic game that jointly optimizes cost/latency/accuracy and improves the model online is novel and meaningfully extends Legion:AR-style systems with a principled decision-theoretic core.

Significance: The approach is practically important for cold-start deployment, with open-source release. The strongest gains are in structured NER; broader task diversity and stronger baselines are needed to cement impact.

Pros for acceptance:
- Principled decision-theoretic formulation with an effective MCTS solution.
- Demonstrated cost savings and accuracy improvements; open-source system.
- Addresses a relevant cold-start deployment problem.

Cons against acceptance:
- Simplistic crowd/error models; lack of worker heterogeneity and adaptation.
- Missing comparisons to decision-theoretic crowdsourcing baselines (e.g., TurKontrol).
- Limited sensitivity analyses and ablations; modest gains on non-NER tasks.
