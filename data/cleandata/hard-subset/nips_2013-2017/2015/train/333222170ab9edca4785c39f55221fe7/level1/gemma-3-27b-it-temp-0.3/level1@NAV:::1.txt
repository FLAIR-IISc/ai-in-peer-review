## Review of "On-the-Job Learning with Bayesian Decision Theory"

This paper presents a compelling framework for on-the-job learning, tackling the challenge of deploying AI systems with zero initial training data. The core idea – strategically querying a crowd to resolve uncertainty during prediction, and gradually reducing reliance on this external resource – is well-motivated and addresses a practical gap between purely supervised learning and full crowdsourcing. The authors cast this as a stochastic game using Bayesian decision theory, providing a principled way to balance latency, cost, and accuracy. The use of Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) to approximate the optimal policy is a reasonable approach given the intractability of the problem. 

The work builds upon existing research in online learning, active learning, and crowdsourcing, but distinguishes itself by focusing on a truly “cold-start” scenario where initial model performance is critical. Unlike traditional active learning, the system *must* make a prediction on every input, and unlike online learning, it actively seeks information *before* predicting. The connection to Legion:AR [6] is acknowledged, but the authors rightly emphasize the theoretical grounding and formalization of their approach.

**Strengths:** The paper is technically sound, with a clear mathematical formulation and a well-defined objective function. The experimental results on three diverse datasets (NER, sentiment classification, image classification) demonstrate significant cost reductions compared to full annotation and performance improvements over online learning and single-label baselines. The availability of an open-source implementation (LENSE) is a major plus. The analysis of the trade-offs between accuracy, latency, and cost is insightful.

**Weaknesses:** While the MCTS approximation is necessary, the computational complexity of the search process isn’t thoroughly discussed. The experiments primarily focus on demonstrating cost reduction; a more detailed analysis of the impact of different query strategies and the convergence behavior of the model would be valuable. The choice of hyperparameters (e.g., the utility function weights) seems somewhat arbitrary and could benefit from a more systematic exploration. The comparison to other active learning methods that *could* be adapted to this online prediction setting is limited. 



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novel and well-motivated problem setting.
*   Principled theoretical framework based on Bayesian decision theory.
*   Strong empirical results demonstrating significant cost savings and accuracy gains.
*   Open-source implementation promotes reproducibility and further research.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   Computational complexity of MCTS is not fully addressed.
*   Limited analysis of query strategy impact and convergence.
*   Hyperparameter selection lacks detailed justification.




**Overall:** This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field. The originality of the problem formulation, the technical soundness of the approach, and the compelling experimental results outweigh the minor weaknesses. I recommend acceptance.
