- Introduces "on-the-job learning": a system that starts with zero training data and queries crowd labels or predicts while updating its model online.  
- Frames the problem as a stochastic game and optimizes query/wait/predict decisions using Bayesian decision theory to trade off accuracy, cost, and latency.  
- Uses Monte Carlo Tree Search with progressive widening as a practical approximation for the intractable optimal policy.  
- Distinguishes itself from standard online learning, active learning, and active classification by actively seeking information before each prediction and updating at test time.  
- Provides formal machine learning foundations beyond prior implementations like Legion:AR.  
- Experimental evaluation is thorough across three tasks (NER, Sentiment, Image Classification) with strong baselines and convincing results.  
- Controls for crowd variance via a pre-collected "frozen pool" of responses, improving experimental reliability.  
- Reports substantial gains: over 10x cost reduction on NER and accuracy exceeding a single human annotator in some settings.  
- Authors release code and data, improving reproducibility.  
- Main technical strength: principled Bayesian formulation and a well-justified MCTS+progressive widening approximation.  
- Main weakness: simplifying assumptions in the environment model, notably uniform worker accuracy (pR=70%) and underspecified response time model (pT).  
- Clarity is high overall, with clear motivation, formulation, and experiments, aided by helpful figures.  
- Presentation weakness: MCTS algorithm details are brief (e.g., UCT constant, node initialization) and may be hard to reproduce from Algorithm 1 alone.  
- Baseline weakness: "Threshold baseline" appears heuristic without detailed justification for chosen parameters (0.3 factor, 0.98 threshold).  
- Originality is strong: formalizing on-the-job learning and using MCTS for the active querying trade-off is a novel, significant contribution.  
- Significance is high: addresses the common cold-start problem and is likely to influence human-in-the-loop and crowdsourcing research.  
- Argument for acceptance: novel problem, sound theory, effective approximation, strong empirical results, clarity, and reproducibility.  
- Argument against acceptance: simplified crowd/response-time models and evaluation based on simulation rather than live deployment, which may miss real-world dynamics.  
- Overall conclusion: excellent paper with minor weaknesses; strongly recommended for acceptance.
