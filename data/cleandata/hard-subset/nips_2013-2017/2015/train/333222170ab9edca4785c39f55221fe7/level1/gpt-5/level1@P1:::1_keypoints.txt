- Proposes “on-the-job learning”: decision-theoretic framework that interleaves real-time crowd queries with model inference and online learning to trade accuracy, latency, and monetary cost.
- Models base predictor pθ, crowd response noise pR, and response-time pT; uses expected-utility objective to decide actions at test time.
- Uses MCTS (UCT) with progressive widening to approximate intractable planning over continuous time and asynchronous responses.
- Empirical results on NER, sentiment, and face classification show cost reductions vs human-only and accuracy gains vs machine-only/online baselines; code (LENSE) released.
- Strength: clear probabilistic formulation tying uncertainty, noisy responses, and response-time into an explicit utility.
- Strength: practical planner capable of reasoning about asynchronous responses and parallel vs sequential querying.
- Strength: empirical evidence across three tasks with substantial NER cost and accuracy improvements; reproducible code/data.
- Concern: utility/loss mismatch — planner optimizes ExpAcc (argmax p(y)) but evaluation uses F1 for NER; computation and alignment with structured F1 unclear.
- Concern: fixed noise model pR (0.7 uniform errors), not learned or adapted, ignores worker heterogeneity despite using real worker responses.
- Concern: training details for pθ under-specified — unclear if model is trained on noisy labels, aggregated labels, or via latent-variable methods (e.g., Dawid–Skene/EM).
- Concern: planning details and computational costs missing — UCT parameters, rollout policy, horizon, per-decision latency, and sensitivity not reported.
- Concern: baselines are weak — missing myopic VOI or POMDP single-step lookahead and TurKontrol-like adaptive redundancy comparisons.
- Concern: reported “28% F1 improvement” needs context — online baseline uses optimistic access to gold for past examples; fairness unclear.
- Clarity needed on computing ExpAcc for structured/non-decomposable metrics and on how online updates incorporate crowd responses.
- Clarity needed on response-time model pT: parametric form and fitting procedure.
- Originality: novel combination of decision-theoretic planning, real-time crowdsourcing, and structured prediction with latency modeling; MCTS+progressive widening is a fresh application.
- Significance: practical and deployable method for high accuracy from day one with decreasing supervision, but broader impact depends on robustness to worker heterogeneity and metric alignment.
- Pros summary: principled general framework, handles asynchronous and structured outputs, open-source, demonstrates cost/accuracy gains.
- Cons summary: metric mismatch, simplistic noise model, incomplete training/calibration details, limited baselines and ablation on planning cost.
- Suggestion: align planning loss with evaluation metric (e.g., expected token-level F1) and detail computation/approximations.
- Suggestion: learn/adapt crowd noise model online (Dawid–Skene or hierarchical Bayes) and model per-worker reliabilities.
- Suggestion: fully specify online learning objective, handling of multiple/noisy labels, and calibration methods (temperature scaling, isotonic).
- Suggestion: report planning overhead (ms per decision), UCT hyperparameters, rollout policy, sensitivity analyses, and ablation against greedy VOI.
- Suggestion: evaluate sequential vs parallel querying trade-offs and sensitivity to cost/time weights.
- Suggestion: include fairer baselines allowing delayed predictions or batch updates and adaptive redundancy crowd baselines.
- Overall: well-motivated, practical paper with promising results but limited by loss/metric mismatch, simplistic noise modeling, and incomplete methodological details; needs clarifications and stronger experiments to be compelling.
