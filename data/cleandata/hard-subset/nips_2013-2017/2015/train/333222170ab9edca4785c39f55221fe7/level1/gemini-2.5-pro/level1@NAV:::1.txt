This paper introduces "on-the-job learning," a framework for deploying machine learning systems that can achieve high accuracy from the outset with no initial training data. The core idea is to integrate real-time crowdsourcing to resolve uncertainty on-demand. As the system processes more inputs and receives crowd feedback, its underlying model improves, gradually reducing its reliance on human experts. The authors formally cast this problem as a stochastic game, using Bayesian decision theory to create a utility function that balances accuracy, latency, and monetary cost. Since computing the optimal query policy is intractable, they propose a practical approximation using Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) with progressive widening. This work connects ideas from online active learning (e.g., Golovin et al., NIPS 2011) and active classification (e.g., Gao & Koller, NIPS 2011), but carves a novel and practical niche by focusing on maintaining high accuracy on every prediction from the very first instance, rather than just efficiently building a good model over time.

**Strengths:**
*   **Significance:** The paper addresses a highly significant and practical problem: the cold-start challenge in deploying ML systems. The proposed framework offers a principled and effective solution that many practitioners could directly benefit from.
*   **Quality:** The technical approach is sound and well-motivated. Formulating the problem within Bayesian decision theory provides a solid foundation, and the use of MCTS is a sensible and well-executed choice for approximating the intractable policy. The experimental evaluation is strong, demonstrating clear benefits over well-chosen baselines across three different tasks. The commitment to reproducibility, with code and data provided, is commendable.
*   **Originality and Clarity:** The formulation of this problem as an integrated learning and real-time querying game is novel. The paper is exceptionally clear, well-written, and easy to follow, successfully explaining a complex model and algorithm.

**Weaknesses:**
*   **Simplified Crowd Model:** The environment model assumes a fixed worker accuracy (e.g., 70%), which is a simplification. Real-world crowd performance is often instance-dependent and varies between workers. While the experimental setup controls for this, a discussion on extending the model to learn worker-specific reliability profiles online would strengthen the paper.
*   **Scalability Concerns:** The MCTS planning step is performed for each incoming instance. The computational overhead of this search could be a bottleneck in high-throughput applications. A brief analysis of the algorithm's runtime complexity and its practical impact would be a useful addition.
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The utility function relies on manually set weights for cost and latency. The paper does not explore the sensitivity of the system's performance to these crucial hyperparameters, which would be important for practical deployment.

Overall, this is an excellent paper that presents a novel framework for a significant real-world problem. The strengths in its formulation, technical quality, and empirical results far outweigh the minor weaknesses. It is a complete and impactful piece of work that will likely inspire further research in human-in-the-loop ML systems. I strongly recommend acceptance.
