

This paper introduces "on-the-job learning," a novel framework for deploying high-accuracy AI systems starting with zero training examples. The authors model the problem as a stochastic game based on Bayesian decision theory, where the system strategically queries crowdsourcing for uncertain predictions while simultaneously improving its model. The approach balances latency, cost, and accuracy objectives through Monte Carlo Tree Search approximation. The authors evaluate on three tasks (NER, sentiment classification, image classification), demonstrating more than an order of magnitude cost reduction compared to full human annotation while achieving 8% F1 improvement over single-human labeling and 28% over online learning.

The work builds upon and clearly distinguishes from relevant prior research. It extends active classification [22-24] by enabling model improvement during test time rather than relying on static policies, and differs from online learning [7] by maintaining high accuracy from the start rather than asymptotically improving. The connection to Legion:AR [6] is acknowledged, though this paper provides deeper machine learning foundations for the on-the-job setting.

Strengths include strong theoretical grounding in Bayesian decision theory, comprehensive experimental validation across multiple domains, and practical implementation with open-source code. The approach is technically sound with well-justified trade-offs between the three key objectives. The paper's clarity is excellent, with clear problem formulation, well-illustrated examples, and thorough experimental design.

Weaknesses include limited discussion of failure modes (e.g., how the system handles systematic crowd errors beyond the 70% accuracy assumption) and insufficient analysis of computational complexity in the MCTS approximation. While the results are impressive, the paper could better explain why the approach works particularly well on NER compared to other tasks.

The significance is high, addressing a real-world deployment challenge where labeled data is scarce but immediate high-quality predictions are needed. The 10x cost reduction with improved accuracy represents a meaningful advance for practical AI deployment. The work is clearly within NIPS scope, addressing core machine learning challenges with principled methods.

Overall, this is a strong contribution that advances both theory and practice in active learning. It successfully bridges the gap between online learning and crowdsourcing while providing a practical, deployable solution. The paper is well-written, technically sound, and makes a significant contribution to the field. I recommend acceptance.
