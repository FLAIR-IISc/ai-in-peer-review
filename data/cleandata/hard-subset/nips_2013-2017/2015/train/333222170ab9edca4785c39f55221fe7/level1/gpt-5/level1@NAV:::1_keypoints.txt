- Proposes "on-the-job learning": a deployment-time framework that starts with zero labels and queries crowds in real time while updating the model to reduce future queries.  
- Formalizes decision as a stochastic game grounded in Bayesian decision theory trading off accuracy, latency, and cost.  
- Approximates the optimal policy using Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) with progressive widening to handle continuous response times.  
- Instantiates the approach with a CRF-based system (LENSE) for structured prediction.  
- Evaluated on NER, sentiment, and image classification, showing substantial cost reductions vs. majority-vote annotation.  
- Shows accuracy improvements over single-human and online learning baselines, with strongest gains on NER.  
- Positions work relative to online/active learning, active classification, decision-theoretic crowdsourcing (e.g., TurKontrol), and real-time crowd systems (Legion:AR).  
- Methodology is sound and engineering is careful; MCTS approximation is reasonable for the intractable state space.  
- Empirical results are compelling for NER and respectable for sentiment/faces, but broader task diversity is limited.  
- Environment model assumes i.i.d. workers with fixed 70% accuracy and independent token responses, risking biased policies.  
- Calibration, sensitivity analyses, statistical significance tests, and ablations (e.g., without progressive widening or varying utility weights) are limited or missing.  
- Missing comparisons to closely related POMDP-style controllers (e.g., TurKontrol) and to adaptive/heterogeneous worker models.  
- Clarity could be improved by justifying the ExpAcc utility and reporting how weights (wM, wT) are chosen.  
- Needs clarification of the claim "boosting performance relative to expert labels" and more intuition on policy behavior for parallel vs. sequential querying.  
- Originality: novel formalization of real-time test-time crowd querying as a stochastic game that jointly optimizes cost/latency/accuracy and updates the model online.  
- Significance: practically important for cold-start deployment and released open-source, but impact would be strengthened by broader tasks and stronger baselines.  
- Pros: principled decision-theoretic formulation, effective MCTS solution, demonstrated cost/accuracy gains, addresses a relevant cold-start problem.  
- Cons: simplistic crowd/error modeling, missing decision-theoretic crowdsourcing baselines, limited sensitivity analyses/ablations, and modest gains outside NER.
