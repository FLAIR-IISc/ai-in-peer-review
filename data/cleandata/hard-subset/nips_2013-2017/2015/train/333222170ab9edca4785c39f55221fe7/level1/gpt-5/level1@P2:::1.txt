Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes “on-the-job learning”: a test-time framework that starts from zero labeled training data and achieves high accuracy by interleaving model prediction with real-time crowdsourcing, gradually reducing human queries as the model improves. The core is a stochastic game formulation with a CRF as the prediction model, a crowd response/noise model, and a response-time model. A Bayesian decision-theoretic utility trades off expected accuracy, monetary cost per query, and latency. Because exact planning is intractable, the paper uses Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) with UCT and progressive widening to handle continuous time. Experiments on NER, sentiment classification, and image classification show improved accuracy-cost tradeoffs over human-only or machine-only baselines, and over a handcrafted threshold baseline.

This work connects to: online learning (improving over time but here maintaining high accuracy from the start), active learning (seeking informative labels, but here querying before predicting on each example), active classification / test-time feature acquisition (value of information, but typically with static policies and full labeled training), and POMDP-based control of crowdsourcing (e.g., TurKontrol) and real-time crowd systems (Legion:AR). The novelty is combining a structured prediction model and a real-time crowd with an explicit decision-theoretic planner that reasons jointly about accuracy, cost, and latency at test time.

Quality
Strengths:
- The formal game-based model is well-motivated, with a clear utility that internalizes cost and latency, yielding intuitive policies (parallel vs sequential queries depending on latency weight; redundancy to combat noise).
- MCTS with progressive widening is a reasonable, scalable approximation for planning with continuous response times.
- The experimental setup covers three tasks with different structures and latencies, and demonstrates substantial cost reduction relative to multiple-vote human baselines while matching or exceeding accuracy; on NER, gains are particularly strong.
- The system shows desirable behavior over time (fewer queries as the model improves) and the code/data are released, aiding reproducibility.

Weaknesses/concerns:
- Crowd noise and latency models are simplistic and mostly fixed. The response model assumes a constant 70% correctness with uniform confusion, independent of instance, worker, or class. This is a strong assumption; many datasets exhibit class-dependent confusions and worker heterogeneity. Not learning pR online (e.g., Dawid–Skene-like estimation, worker calibration) likely leaves performance on the table and may bias planning.
- The learning update for pθ is underspecified. It is stated that the CRF is learned from “actual responses using AdaGrad,” but it is unclear whether the model treats responses as noisy proxies (marginalizing y given pR) or as hard labels (e.g., majority vote). Given the explicit noise model in the planner, the learning objective should ideally be consistent with it.
- The expected accuracy proxy ExpAcc is defined on the MAP prediction under p(y|x, …), but for structured outputs it is not clear what “Accuracy” means (token-level, sequence-level 0/1, or F1 for NER). If evaluation is F1, optimizing expected 0/1 or token accuracy is a mismatch; some discussion of calibration/utility alignment would help.
- Baselines are limited. In addition to the threshold heuristic, more principled myopic value-of-information baselines (e.g., greedy selection by expected risk reduction; QMDP; one-step lookahead) would strengthen the evidence that planning via MCTS is necessary. Comparisons to established test-time feature acquisition methods (active classification) and to POMDP-style crowd control (TurKontrol) would contextualize the advantage.
- Evaluation protocol uses “frozen pools” of 5 pre-collected responses per item to control variance. While practical, this caps redundancy benefits and may distort the dynamics of sequential planning (e.g., dependence between responses is reduced to sampling without replacement). It also blurs realism of asynchronous return times. Reporting results with a live crowd (even for a subset) or with more flexible simulation would bolster claims.
- No statistical significance analysis of reported gains, and no ablations on key planning hyperparameters (UCT constant c, widening schedule, number of simulations), nor on utility weights wM and wT (how chosen/tuned).
- Computational costs are not reported. For a real-time system, end-to-end latency includes planning time; we need evidence that MCTS overhead is negligible relative to crowd latency across tasks and sequence lengths, and how it scales.

Clarity
The paper is generally clear and well-organized. The stochastic game, state representation, and MCTS procedure are explained cleanly. However, several details deserve clarification:
- Specify the learning objective for pθ when responses are noisy (are you maximizing marginal likelihood under pR? using EM? or training on aggregated labels?).
- Define “Accuracy” used in ExpAcc for each task, and discuss utility–metric alignment (especially F1 in NER).
- Describe the response-time model pT (parametric form, per-task fit), and how it is estimated online.
- Provide computational budgets for MCTS per example and their contribution to latency.
- Justify the claim of surpassing expert-provided labels on NER: was the gold noisy? how was this measured?

Originality
The combination of (i) a decision-theoretic planner, (ii) structured prediction, and (iii) real-time crowd interaction at test time is novel and timely. While related to Legion:AR and TurKontrol, this work contributes a general-purpose framework with continuous-time planning via MCTS and demonstrates it beyond activity recognition. The formalization and the practical system are new contributions.

Significance
The problem is important: enabling immediate deployment with high accuracy without upfront data, while amortizing costs as the model learns. The demonstrated cost savings and accuracy gains, especially on NER, suggest practical impact for systems that need reliability from day one. The framework is likely to spur follow-up work on better crowd models, richer utilities, and more efficient planners.

Pros for acceptance
- Clear, principled formulation with a practical approximation (MCTS + progressive widening).
- Strong empirical cost–accuracy improvements; open-source implementation.
- Addresses an important, underexplored operating regime (test-time querying with real-time crowds).
- Extends to structured outputs, not just classification.

Cons for acceptance
- Simplistic and fixed crowd models; mismatch between planning and learning objectives.
- Limited baselines; absence of significance tests and ablations.
- Simulation protocol may limit ecological validity; computational overhead unreported.
- Some unclear details (utility metric alignment, training with noisy labels).

Suggestions for improvement
- Learn and adapt pR and pT online; model worker/item heterogeneity and class-dependent confusions.
- Align training with the noise model (e.g., EM over latent y) and align utility with evaluation metrics (e.g., expected F1 for NER).
- Add stronger baselines (greedy VOI, POMDP approximations), ablations, and report planning latency.
- Provide results with a live crowd subset or richer simulation to validate asynchronous dynamics.

Overall recommendation
Lean accept. The paper presents a compelling and well-motivated framework with solid empirical evidence and public code. Despite modeling and evaluation limitations, it advances the state-of-practice for combining ML with real-time crowds under a principled decision-theoretic approach, fitting well within NIPS.
