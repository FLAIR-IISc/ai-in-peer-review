Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper introduces “on-the-job learning,” a decision-theoretic framework to deploy high-accuracy systems from zero training examples by selectively querying crowd workers in real time at test time, while continuously updating a prediction model so reliance on the crowd decreases over time. The approach formulates the problem as a stochastic game between the system and the crowd with actions to query, wait, or return a prediction. A Bayesian decision-theoretic utility balances accuracy, latency, and monetary cost. Because exact optimization is intractable, the authors approximate the optimal policy using Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) with UCT and progressive widening to handle continuous response times. The environment model comprises a structured predictor pθ(y|x) (e.g., a CRF), a simple noisy response model pR (fixed 70% accuracy), and a response-time model pT. Experiments on NER, sentiment classification, and image classification (faces) show improved accuracy-cost tradeoffs versus human-only and machine-only baselines; notably, on NER the method reduces cost by more than an order of magnitude versus 5-vote majority while improving F1 over expert labels and single-human baselines, and outperforming an online learning baseline.

This work relates to online learning (Cesa-Bianchi & Lugosi), active learning (Settles), active classification (Gao & Koller, Greiner et al.), decision-theoretic control of crowdsourcing (TurKontrol; Dai, Mausam, Weld), and prior real-time crowd-in-the-loop systems (Lasecki et al.’s Legion:AR). It also builds on MCTS planning (Kocsis & Szepesvári) and progressive widening (Coulom), and integrates with structured prediction (CRFs).

Quality:
Strengths
- Clear decision-theoretic formulation with an explicit utility trading off accuracy, time, and monetary cost; the framework naturally yields intuitive strategies (e.g., parallel vs sequential querying).
- A principled planning approximation (MCTS + progressive widening) for a challenging game with asynchronous, continuous-time responses.
- Structured prediction setting is well-motivated; using responses on parts of the structure and propagating information through pθ is appropriate.
- Empirical results are favorable across three tasks; the NER improvements and cost reductions are compelling. Public code and datasets enhance reproducibility.

Weaknesses and questions
- Worker model pR is fixed at 70% with uniform errors; pT is referenced but not specified. This modeling choice is simplistic and may misrepresent real worker heterogeneity, correlations, and adversarial or systematic errors. The paper would be stronger with learned/calibrated pR and pT (possibly online), or sensitivity analyses showing robustness to mis-specification.
- Utility definition vs evaluation metric: ExpAcc uses accuracy of the MAP prediction under the posterior, but experiments report F1 (esp. NER). The mismatch between optimized utility and reported metrics is not discussed; clarity on the task-level accuracy metric embedded in U (sequence vs token accuracy) and any mapping to F1 is needed.
- Baselines: The “online learning” baseline has access to gold labels after prediction—a strong but not decision-theoretically comparable baseline. More relevant alternatives include myopic value-of-information (VOI) policies, POMDP-based policies for crowdsourcing control, or dynamic redundancy strategies that consider time. The threshold baseline seems strawman-like and does not account for time, diminishing its informativeness.
- Real-time evaluation: Although a retainer pool was implemented, the main comparisons rely on simulation from a small frozen set of 5 responses per label to control variance. This may bias results and understate variance and nonstationarity. At least partial end-to-end live evaluations and sensitivity to worker pool changes would strengthen claims.
- Computational aspects: The policy search’s computational cost, number of simulations per decision, and latency overhead due to planning are not reported. Without runtime analysis, it is unclear whether the planner remains feasible at scale or under tight latency constraints.
- Restrictions in the game (e.g., cannot launch a new query while waiting) are acknowledged; empirical assessment of the performance gap induced by this restriction would be useful.

Clarity:
The paper is generally well-organized and readable, with a clear problem statement, game formulation, and algorithm sketch. However, several details are under-specified:
- Exact form and parameters of pT, and how it is estimated.
- The accuracy metric used inside ExpAcc for structured outputs and how it aligns with reported F1.
- CRF features, training schedule, and calibration; the exploration constant c in UCT; progressive widening parameters.
- Hyperparameter choices for wM and wT and sensitivity analyses.
Providing these would improve reproducibility beyond code release and help readers assess when the method will work.

Originality:
The integration of real-time crowd querying, structured prediction, and a decision-theoretic, continuous-time planning approach is novel and timely. Prior work has explored decision-theoretic control of crowdsourcing and active classification, but the on-the-job framing that both queries pre-decision and updates the model online, with MCTS-based planning over asynchronous responses, appears to be a new and substantive combination.

Significance:
The ability to deploy high-accuracy systems immediately without pre-collected training data is practically impactful. The demonstrated accuracy-cost-latency tradeoffs, especially on NER, suggest that practitioners could meaningfully reduce labeling spend while maintaining quality. The framework is general and could spur follow-up work on better worker models, faster planning, and broader domains. However, the current reliance on simplified worker models and simulated evaluation limits the strength of the evidence; stronger live evaluations and more competitive baselines would heighten significance.

Pros for acceptance:
- Principled formulation and solid algorithmic contribution (MCTS + progressive widening) to a relevant problem.
- Strong results on NER and consistent gains across tasks; open-source release aids adoption.
- Clear relation to and extension of multiple active learning and crowdsourcing lines.

Cons for acceptance:
- Simplistic and fixed worker/latency models with limited robustness analysis.
- Evaluation relies heavily on simulation from a small frozen pool; limited live end-to-end evidence.
- Baselines do not include stronger decision-theoretic/myopic VOI comparators; utility–metric mismatch not addressed.
- Missing runtime/latency overhead analysis for the planner.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Learn or adaptively estimate pR and pT online; include sensitivity analyses to model mis-specification.
- Align the utility with the evaluation metric (e.g., expected F1) or justify the surrogate; report both token and sequence metrics for structured tasks.
- Add myopic VOI and POMDP-based baselines; report runtime and planning overhead.
- Provide live deployment results (even small-scale) and ablations on wM, wT, progressive widening parameters, and the “no-new-query-while-waiting” restriction.

Overall: A well-motivated, technically interesting contribution with promising empirical results, particularly on NER, but with evaluation and modeling limitations that should be addressed to maximize confidence in real-world effectiveness.
