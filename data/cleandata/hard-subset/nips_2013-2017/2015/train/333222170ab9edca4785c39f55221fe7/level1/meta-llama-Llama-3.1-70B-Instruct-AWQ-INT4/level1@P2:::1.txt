This paper proposes a novel framework for on-the-job learning, which enables a system to learn from noisy crowds and maintain high accuracy while reducing cost over time. The authors model the on-the-job setting as a stochastic game and use ideas from game playing to approximate the optimal policy. The paper is well-written, and the authors provide a clear explanation of their approach, including the problem formulation, model, and algorithm.

The paper is technically sound, and the authors provide a thorough analysis of their approach. The use of Bayesian decision theory to trade off latency, cost, and accuracy is a key contribution of the paper. The authors also provide a detailed description of their algorithm, which combines ideas from Monte Carlo tree search and progressive widening.

The paper is well-organized, and the authors provide a clear structure for their presentation. The use of figures and tables is effective in illustrating the key concepts and results. The authors also provide a thorough discussion of related work, which helps to situate their approach within the broader context of machine learning and crowdsourcing.

One of the strengths of the paper is its ability to balance theoretical and practical considerations. The authors provide a rigorous analysis of their approach, but they also demonstrate its effectiveness in practice through a series of experiments on three different tasks. The results are impressive, with the authors achieving significant cost reductions and accuracy improvements over baseline methods.

One potential weakness of the paper is its reliance on a specific model of crowd behavior. The authors assume that crowd workers are noisy oracles, which may not always be the case in practice. However, the authors do provide some evidence to support this assumption, and they also discuss the potential limitations of their approach.

The paper is well-written and well-organized, and the authors provide a thorough analysis of their approach.
