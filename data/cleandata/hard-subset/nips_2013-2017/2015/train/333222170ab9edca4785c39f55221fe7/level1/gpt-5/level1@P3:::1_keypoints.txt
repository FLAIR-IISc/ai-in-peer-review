- Proposal: "On-the-job learning" where the system can asynchronously query crowd responses at token/item level and decide when to predict as model improves over time.  
- Main contribution: formal Bayesian decision-theoretic stochastic-game formulation trading accuracy, query cost, and latency.  
- Planning approximation: use Monte Carlo Tree Search (UCT) with progressive widening to handle continuous time and large action spaces.  
- Implementation: instantiated with a CRF for structured NER and simple response/time noise models; evaluated on NER, sentiment, and face classification.  
- Empirical claim: significant cost reductions versus full crowdsourcing and accuracy gains over a single human and an online-learning baseline.  
- Prior work connections: relates to online learning, active learning, test-time querying, POMDP/decision-theoretic crowdsourcing, and real-time crowdsourcing systems.  
- Strength: clear decision-theoretic model that captures asynchronous, partially observable, real-time querying trade-offs.  
- Strength: technically appropriate use of MCTS and progressive widening for continuous-time planning.  
- Strength: experimental setup uses retainer pool timings and tests on structured (NER) and unstructured tasks.  
- Strength: results show intended “use crowd as crutch” behavior with nontrivial cost savings.  
- Weakness: oversimplified response/noise model (fixed pR = 0.7 uniform across workers/items/labels) ignoring worker heterogeneity and item difficulty.  
- Weakness: utility–metric mismatch—planning optimizes expected accuracy while evaluation reports F1 (especially for NER), introducing possible bias.  
- Weakness: weak baselines; missing comparisons to myopic VOI, POMDP controllers with learned reliabilities, and offline active-classification policies.  
- Weakness: evaluation simulates from small pre-collected pools (five responses), sampling without replacement may understate real-world variability and encourage planner exploitation.  
- Weakness: clarity gaps—how ExpAcc is computed for structured outputs, inference used in CRF during rollouts, planning horizon, branching factor, and computational overhead are under-specified.  
- Weakness: training uses noisy crowd labels without jointly estimating true labels and worker reliabilities (no Dawid–Skene or similar), reducing learning and planning robustness.  
- Weakness: action constraints (cannot issue new query while awaiting next response) may hurt optimality in low-latency regimes; impact not quantified.  
- Clarity: paper is generally well written but needs more detail on Accuracy definition, response-time model estimation, planning cost, and task hyperparameters.  
- Originality: novel combination of on-the-job perspective, structured prediction, and UCT with progressive widening for asynchronous crowds, though building on known ingredients.  
- Significance: promising potential for reducing crowd cost over time, especially for NER, but current evaluation and modeling choices limit external validity.  
- Pros summary: principled formulation, appropriate MCTS technique, structured-task demonstration, open-source artifacts.  
- Cons summary: simplistic noise model, utility-evaluation mismatch, missing strong baselines, simulation methodology concerns, and unreported computational/latency costs.  
- Suggestions: learn richer per-worker/item pR (e.g., Dawid–Skene), align planning loss with evaluation metric (or justify choice), add VOI/POMDP/static active baselines, perform sensitivity analyses to pR/pT misspecification and cost weights, and report planning runtime and end-to-end latency.  
- Recommendation: borderline accept conditional on stronger baselines, clearer utility alignment, and more realistic/noise-robust evaluation.
