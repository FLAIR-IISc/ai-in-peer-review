This paper proposes an on-the-job learning framework, where a system lacking pre-training learns by querying a crowd for information as needed during operation. The core idea is to formulate this as a stochastic game using Bayesian decision theory to balance latency, cost, and accuracy, approximated via Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS). The approach is evaluated on NER, sentiment classification, and image classification, demonstrating significant cost reduction and performance improvements over baselines.

The paper builds upon prior work in online learning [7], active learning [8-11], and crowdsourcing [3, 4, 25, 26]. It distinguishes itself from existing approaches by simultaneously addressing the need for continual learning during test time, maintaining accuracy from the start without requiring initial training data, and formally optimizing for a trade-off between cost, latency and accuracy. The connection to Legion:AR [6] is acknowledged, but this work delves deeper into the theoretical foundations and offers a more comprehensive solution. The use of MCTS to approximate the optimal policy in the stochastic game is a key contribution.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The combination of on-the-job learning, Bayesian decision theory, and MCTS for real-time querying is a novel and compelling approach. The formalization of the problem as a stochastic game is well-motivated.
*   **Technical Soundness:** The formulation and algorithm appear theoretically sound. The use of MCTS and progressive widening is appropriate for tackling the intractability of the optimal policy computation.
*   **Empirical Results:** The experimental results on three different datasets are convincing, demonstrating significant cost reduction and performance gains compared to several relevant baselines. The 8% F1 improvement over a single human labeler and 28% over online learning are notable.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The introduction clearly motivates the problem and the proposed solution. The descriptions of the model and algorithm are relatively clear, though some sections are dense.
*   **Reproducibility:** Providing an open-source implementation and datasets on CodaLab is a significant strength, enabling reproducibility and facilitating further research.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Complexity:** The framework is complex, with several interacting components (Bayesian game, MCTS, response/latency models). The detailed explanation of each component can be challenging to follow.
*   **Sensitivity to Parameters:** The performance likely depends on careful tuning of several parameters (e.g., UCT exploration constant, cost/latency weights). The paper does not provide sufficient details on how these parameters were chosen.
*   **Crowd Modeling Assumptions:** The response model (70% accuracy) is a simplification. The performance could be affected by variations in crowd worker quality and biases. The use of a frozen pool of worker responses is a valid choice for controlled experimentation but doesn't fully reflect a live, evolving crowd.
*   **Limited Baseline Comparison:** While the included baselines are relevant, a comparison with more sophisticated active learning strategies that also consider cost and latency could strengthen the evaluation.
* **Potential for Suboptimality:** The MCTS approximation may not always converge to the optimal policy, particularly in complex scenarios. A discussion of the approximation's limitations and potential for improvement would be valuable.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and technically sound approach to on-the-job learning.
*   The empirical results are strong and demonstrate significant improvements over existing methods.
*   The open-source implementation and data availability promote reproducibility.
*   The work addresses a challenging and practically relevant problem.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The framework is complex, and the details can be difficult to grasp.
*   The performance is potentially sensitive to parameter tuning.
*   The crowd modeling assumptions are simplified.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that addresses an important problem with a novel and well-motivated approach. The technical contributions are significant, and the empirical results are compelling. While the complexity of the framework and the potential for parameter sensitivity are concerns, they do not outweigh the strengths of the work. I recommend acceptance.
