

# Peer Review: "On-the-Job Learning with Bayesian Decision Theory"

## Summary of Main Ideas and Relation to Previous Work

This paper introduces "on-the-job learning," a novel framework for deploying AI systems with zero initial training examples. The approach dynamically balances accuracy, latency, and cost by using real-time crowdsourcing to resolve uncertainty during prediction, while simultaneously improving the model over time. The authors formalize this as a stochastic game based on Bayesian decision theory, where the system must decide when to query the crowd, which parts to query, and when to return a prediction. The optimal policy is intractable, so they propose an approximation using Monte Carlo Tree Search with progressive widening. The method is evaluated on three structured prediction tasks (NER, sentiment classification, and image classification), showing significant cost reductions (over an order of magnitude for NER) while improving accuracy compared to both pure crowdsourcing and online learning baselines.

The paper relates well to prior work in online learning [7], active learning [8-10], and active classification [11, 22-24]. It distinguishes itself from these approaches by maintaining high accuracy from the start (unlike online learning, which only achieves good accuracy asymptotically), and by improving the model during test time (unlike static active classification policies). The work also builds on Legion:AR [6], which implemented similar ideas but without the formal machine learning foundation that this paper provides.

## Strengths

**Quality:** The paper presents a technically sound approach with a well-formulated problem and principled solution. The Bayesian decision theory framework is appropriate for the problem, and the MCTS approximation is reasonable given the intractability of the optimal solution. The experimental evaluation is comprehensive, with three distinct tasks, multiple baselines (human n-query, online learning, threshold baseline), and thorough analysis of cost-accuracy tradeoffs. The authors carefully acknowledge limitations, such as the varying performance across tasks and the assumption of a fixed 70% crowd accuracy.

**Originality:** The paper makes a clear contribution by formalizing the on-the-job learning setting with a decision-theoretic framework, which is novel in the literature. While related to active learning and crowdsourcing, it distinguishes itself by the dynamic model improvement during test time and the explicit tradeoff between accuracy, latency, and cost. The authors properly contextualize their work against prior contributions, particularly Legion:AR [6], which implemented similar ideas without the theoretical foundation.

**Significance:** The problem addressed—deploying high-accuracy systems with zero initial training data—is highly relevant to real-world applications where data collection is expensive or impossible. The results demonstrate significant practical value, with cost reductions of over an order of magnitude on NER while improving accuracy over both pure crowdsourcing and online learning. The LENSE system, with its open-source implementation, provides a valuable tool for practitioners.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-written with clear figures (particularly Figures 2 and 4) that effectively illustrate the game-theoretic framework and experimental results. The formalization of the stochastic game is precise, and the algorithm description is understandable. The connection between theory and implementation is well-maintained.

## Weaknesses

**Quality:** The crowd response model assumes a fixed 70% accuracy across all tasks, which may not reflect real-world variability. The paper doesn't explore how different crowd error rates affect performance or how to adapt to changing crowd quality over time. Additionally, the computational complexity of the MCTS approximation isn't thoroughly analyzed—how does it scale with input size or query complexity?

**Significance:** While the results are impressive for NER, the paper doesn't sufficiently explain why performance was much lower on sentiment classification and image classification. The authors speculate about the "dominant class" in NER, but a deeper analysis would strengthen the significance claim. The comparison to online learning as a baseline is problematic because online learning assumes perfect labels for all examples seen so far, which isn't realistic in a real deployment setting.

**Clarity:** The utility function (Equation 1) is well-defined but could be made more accessible by providing an intuitive explanation of how the monetary and time penalties are calibrated in practice.

## Arguments for Acceptance

1. The paper addresses a meaningful real-world problem with significant practical implications.
2. The theoretical foundation (Bayesian decision theory for on-the-job learning) is novel and well-motivated.
3. The experimental results are strong, showing clear advantages over multiple baselines.
4. The open-source implementation (LENSE) enhances reproducibility and practical impact.
5. The paper makes a clear contribution that advances the state of the art beyond prior related work.

## Arguments Against Acceptance

1. The crowd error rate assumption (70%) lacks justification and may not generalize.
2. The comparison to online learning as a baseline is misleading, as it assumes perfect labels for all training data.
3. The paper doesn't sufficiently explain the performance gap across different tasks.
4. The computational complexity of the MCTS approximation isn't thoroughly addressed.

## Overall Assessment

This paper makes a significant contribution to the field by formalizing a novel framework for on-the-job learning that balances accuracy, cost, and latency through Bayesian decision theory. The technical approach is sound, the experimental results are compelling, and the practical implications are clear. While the crowd error rate assumption and baseline comparison could be strengthened, these are minor issues that don't undermine the overall contribution.

The paper meets all NIPS criteria: it is of high quality (sound theory, thorough experiments), clear (well-organized, well-explained), original (novel framework), and significant (practical impact on real-world deployment of AI systems). I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the crowd error rate justification and baseline comparison.
