- Core idea: Proposes "on-the-job learning" — deploy models with zero initial training by querying real-time crowds to trade off accuracy, latency, and cost while improving the model online.  
- Formalism & method: Frames the problem as a Bayesian decision-theoretic stochastic game and approximates the intractable optimal policy via Monte Carlo Tree Search with progressive widening.  
- Empirical results: Evaluated on NER, sentiment, and image classification; shows large cost reductions (over an order of magnitude for NER) and improved accuracy versus pure crowdsourcing and online-learning baselines.  
- Relation to prior work: Related to online/active learning and prior systems like Legion:AR but novel in formalizing dynamic, test-time model improvement and explicit accuracy/cost/latency tradeoffs.  

Strengths:
- Quality: Well-formulated problem and principled Bayesian solution; reasonable MCTS approximation and comprehensive experiments.  
- Originality: Novel formalization of on-the-job learning that distinguishes itself from static active learning and prior engineering systems.  
- Significance: Addresses a practical, real-world deployment problem (zero-data startup) with meaningful cost/accuracy improvements; provides open-source LENSE implementation.  
- Clarity: Paper is well-written with clear figures and precise formalization linking theory to implementation.  

Weaknesses:
- Crowd model assumption: Assumes fixed 70% crowd accuracy across tasks without exploring varying or time-varying crowd quality.  
- Computational analysis: Does not thoroughly analyze MCTS computational complexity or scalability with input/query size.  
- Task variability: Insufficient analysis for why performance drops on sentiment and image tasks compared to NER.  
- Utility interpretability: Utility function penalties (monetary/time calibration) lack intuitive/practical explanation.  

Arguments for acceptance:
- Tackles a meaningful deployment problem with practical implications.  
- Novel, well-motivated Bayesian decision-theoretic foundation.  
- Strong empirical advantages over multiple baselines.  
- Open-source system (LENSE) enhances reproducibility and impact.  
- Advances state of the art beyond prior related work.  

Arguments against acceptance:
- Unjustified fixed crowd error-rate assumption limiting generalization.  
- Online-learning baseline comparison is misleading (assumes perfect labels).  
- Lacks explanation for cross-task performance gaps.  
- Computational cost/scalability of the MCTS approximation is under-addressed.  

Overall assessment:
- Strong, significant, and well-executed contribution deserving acceptance pending minor revisions addressing crowd-model justification, baseline comparisons, task analysis, and computational scaling.
