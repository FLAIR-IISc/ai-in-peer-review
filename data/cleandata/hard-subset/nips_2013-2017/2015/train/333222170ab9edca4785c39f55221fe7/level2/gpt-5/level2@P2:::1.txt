Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes an “on-the-job learning” framework for deploying high-accuracy systems from zero labeled data by interleaving model prediction with real-time crowdsourcing. For each incoming input, the system can launch targeted micro-queries to the crowd, wait for responses arriving asynchronously, and decide when to stop and output a prediction. The decision problem is formalized as a stochastic game whose utility trades off expected accuracy, latency, and monetary cost. Because exact planning is intractable, the authors approximate the optimal policy using Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) with progressive widening to handle continuous response times. The environment model combines a CRF for structured prediction with simple models of worker accuracy and latency. Experiments on NER, sentiment, and face classification suggest substantial cost savings relative to full human annotation and accuracy gains over single-vote crowd labels and a strong “online learning with gold” baseline.

The work connects to real-time crowdsourcing (retainer pools), POMDP-based decision-theoretic control of crowdsourcing (e.g., TurKontrol), online/active learning, and active classification. The main novelty is integrating decision-theoretic, test-time querying with structured outputs and real-time crowd responses, planning with MCTS rather than a static policy, and demonstrating continuous improvement over time.

Strengths
- Clear, principled formulation that directly optimizes a latency–cost–accuracy trade-off; the utility is explicit and interpretable.
- Technically interesting use of MCTS with progressive widening to plan in a continuous-time, partially observed environment.
- Integration with structured prediction (CRF) and demonstration of behavior that matches intuition (redundancy for noisy workers, parallel vs sequential querying).
- Empirical results on three tasks; notably, NER shows large cost reductions vs multi-vote crowds while maintaining or improving accuracy.
- Open-source implementation and attempt at reproducibility via a frozen pool of worker responses and latencies.

Weaknesses
- Environment modeling assumptions are simplistic and potentially brittle: a fixed 70% worker accuracy, independence across workers/positions, and no per-worker reliabilities or adversarial/systematic errors. Sensitivity analyses are missing.
- Baselines do not fully represent strong decision-theoretic alternatives. There is no comparison to myopic value-of-information policies, to POMDP solvers used in prior crowd-workflow control, or to learned stopping policies (e.g., TurKontrol-like redundancy control).
- The “online learning” baseline uses gold labels after each example (an oracle unavailable in deployment) yet cannot query at test time; the comparison is useful but not conclusive.
- Computational aspects of planning (per-example MCTS budget, wall-clock overhead vs latency targets) are not quantified, which is central for “real-time” deployment claims.
- The experimental protocol fixes a pre-collected response pool and samples without replacement; while it controls variance, it may understate real-world variability and strategic effects (worker learning, drift). It also decouples responses from the actual queries’ timing context.
- Ambiguity in some claims: “boosting performance relative to the expert provided labels” requires clarification (how can predictions surpass expert gold?), and statistical significance of improvements is not reported.
- Some practical constraints (cannot issue a new query while waiting; choice of loss/weights wM, wT) are design choices whose impact is not ablated.

Quality
- Soundness: The stochastic game and utility are well defined; the MCTS approximation is reasonable. However, the expected-accuracy objective is computed for the MAP decision; discussion of the induced Bayes risk vs alternative decision rules is thin. The response/latency model is overly simplified.
- Empirics: Results are promising, especially on NER, but would benefit from stronger baselines and robustness checks (worker accuracy priors, mis-specification, noisy-label rates, class imbalance).
- Reproducibility: Code/data availability is commendable. More details on CRF features, hyperparameters, and MCTS budgets would aid reproduction.

Clarity
- The paper is generally well written and organized; the game description is clear and Figure-based narratives help. Some minor typos and formatting issues exist. Clarify ExpAcc, the calibration of pR and pT, and how pθ is updated online (frequency, learning rate, regularization).

Originality
- The combination of decision-theoretic planning with structured prediction and real-time crowd responses, executed via MCTS with continuous time, appears novel relative to Legion:AR and TurKontrol, which either lack structured outputs or use static policies. The framing of “on-the-job learning” that maintains high accuracy from the start is compelling.

Significance
- If robust, the approach can have significant practical impact for rapid-deployment ML systems that bridge model and crowd. The NER results are strong; the sentiment and faces gains are more modest, suggesting benefits depend on task structure and model capacity.

Questions for the authors
- How sensitive is performance to mis-specifying worker accuracy (e.g., true accuracy 60% vs modeled 70%)? Can the system learn per-worker reliabilities online?
- What are the MCTS computational budgets, and how do they affect latency? What is the overall wall-clock latency including planning time?
- How are wM and wT chosen? Can you report Pareto curves and a principled selection procedure?
- Can you compare against a myopic VOI policy and a TurKontrol-style redundancy controller?
- Please clarify “boosting performance relative to expert provided labels.”

Suggestions for improvement
- Add ablations on pR calibration, worker heterogeneity, and response-time modeling; include robustness curves.
- Include decision-theoretic baselines (myopic VOI, POMDP/heuristic controllers) and report statistical significance.
- Report computational costs of MCTS and analyze the impact of the “no mid-wait query” constraint.
- Learn worker reliabilities and incorporate them in planning.
- Clarify the data protocol, CRF/RNN details, and provide full metric breakdowns.

Pros for acceptance
- Principled, novel formulation with a solid approximate planning method.
- Strong empirical gains on a structured task (NER) with meaningful cost reductions.
- Practical relevance; open-source system.

Cons for acceptance
- Simplified and potentially mis-specified environment model; limited robustness analysis.
- Missing comparisons to strong decision-theoretic baselines.
- Unclear computational overhead and some ambiguous claims.

Overall recommendation
A borderline accept leaning to accept. The paper advances a timely and practically important direction with a principled formulation and promising results, especially on structured prediction. Addressing the noted experimental and modeling gaps would strengthen the contribution.
