## Review of "On-the-Job Learning with Bayesian Decision Theory"

This paper proposes a novel framework, “on-the-job learning,” for deploying AI systems without requiring large pre-labeled datasets. The core idea is to strategically query a crowd for labels only when the model is uncertain, balancing accuracy, cost, and latency using Bayesian decision theory and approximating the optimal policy with Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS). The authors convincingly argue this is distinct from online learning and active learning, offering a more dynamic and adaptive approach.

The paper is well-motivated, addressing a practical challenge in real-world AI deployment. The formulation as a stochastic game is a strong technical contribution, providing a principled way to reason about the trade-offs involved. The experimental results on NER, sentiment classification, and image classification are compelling, demonstrating significant cost reductions compared to full annotation and improvements over existing baselines like online learning and a simple thresholding approach. The 8% F1 improvement over single human labeling on NER is particularly noteworthy. The open-source release of the LENSE implementation is also a valuable contribution to the community.

However, the paper could benefit from a more thorough discussion of the limitations. While the authors acknowledge the dependence on worker quality and control for it to some extent, the sensitivity of the approach to noisy crowd responses isn’t fully explored. A more detailed analysis of scenarios where the initial model is severely inaccurate, and the cost of querying the crowd is high, would be beneficial. Additionally, the choice of the CRF model is somewhat arbitrary; exploring other prediction models and their impact on performance would strengthen the work. 

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novel and principled framework for on-the-job learning.
*   Strong experimental results demonstrating significant cost savings and accuracy improvements.
*   Clear and well-written paper with a valuable open-source implementation.
*   Addresses a practically relevant problem in AI deployment.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   Limited discussion of limitations, particularly regarding noisy crowd responses and initial model inaccuracy.
*   The choice of CRF model could be better justified or explored with alternatives.
*   The complexity of the MCTS approximation might hinder wider adoption without further simplification.

**Overall:**

This is a strong paper with a novel and promising approach. While some limitations exist, the technical contributions and experimental results are compelling. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the points raised above, particularly a more thorough discussion of limitations and potential failure modes. The authors should also consider clarifying the computational cost of the MCTS approximation.
