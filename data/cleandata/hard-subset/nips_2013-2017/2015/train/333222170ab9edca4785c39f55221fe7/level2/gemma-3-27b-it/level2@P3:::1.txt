This paper introduces a framework called on-the-job learning, where a system learns from real-time crowdsourcing to resolve uncertainty as inputs arrive, balancing latency, cost, and accuracy. The core idea is to model this as a stochastic game based on Bayesian decision theory, approximated via Monte Carlo Tree Search. The work is well-motivated, addressing a practical scenario where labeled data is scarce. It builds upon previous work in online learning, active learning, and crowdsourcing, but uniquely combines these concepts with a principled game-theoretic formulation and an efficient search algorithm. The presented experimental results on NER, sentiment classification, and image classification demonstrate significant cost reductions compared to full human annotation and improvements over existing baselines, including online learning and a threshold-based querying strategy.

The paper’s strengths lie in its novel combination of techniques and its strong empirical validation. Framing the problem as a stochastic game allows for a principled trade-off between competing objectives (latency, cost, accuracy). The use of MCTS to approximate the optimal policy is well-justified given the intractability of computing it directly. The experiments are comprehensive, covering three diverse tasks and comparing against several reasonable baselines. The significant cost reduction observed in the NER task is particularly compelling, and the open-source implementation (LENSE) enhances reproducibility and potential impact. The discussion of related work is thorough and accurately positions the contribution within the existing literature. The clarity of the presentation is generally high, with clear explanations of the problem formulation, model, and algorithm.

However, several areas could benefit from further development. While the paper demonstrates strong performance, the details of the CRF model used (specifically, feature engineering) for each task are relatively light. A more detailed description of the features used, and an ablation study to assess their impact, would strengthen the results. The paper mentions worker response times being sampled from a frozen pool. This is a pragmatic choice for reproducibility, but it potentially limits the adaptability of the system to changing worker availability or quality over time. Exploring the impact of dynamically updating this pool would be valuable. The utility function incorporates weights for cost and time (wM and wT). The paper does not provide detailed guidance on how these weights were chosen or tuned, and a sensitivity analysis of their influence on performance would be helpful. The discussion of progressive widening is somewhat brief, and a more intuitive explanation of how it addresses the challenge of continuous variables would be beneficial. While the paper states that the experiments aim to simulate a zero-training-example scenario, the use of existing labeled datasets introduces a potential bias. Explicitly acknowledging this limitation and discussing its implications would be appropriate. Finally, while the code is available, a more detailed tutorial or example demonstrating how to integrate LENSE into a real-world application would be valuable for potential users.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel and principled approach to on-the-job learning.
*   Strong empirical results demonstrating cost reductions and performance improvements.
*   Clear and well-organized presentation.
*   Open-source implementation promotes reproducibility and impact.
*   Addresses a relevant and practical problem.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Limited detail regarding feature engineering and weight tuning.
*   Potential limitations related to the frozen worker response pool.
*   Reliance on existing labeled datasets introduces a potential bias.



Overall, this is a strong paper with a significant contribution to the field. The weaknesses identified are relatively minor and addressable through revisions. The originality, clarity, and empirical results justify acceptance to NIPS. Addressing the suggestions above would further strengthen the paper and enhance its impact.
