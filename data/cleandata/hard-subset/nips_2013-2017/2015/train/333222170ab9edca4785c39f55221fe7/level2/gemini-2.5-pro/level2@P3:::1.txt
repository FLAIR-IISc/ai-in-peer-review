**Summary**

This paper introduces "on-the-job learning," a framework for deploying a high-accuracy system from a cold start, with zero initial training examples. The setting involves a stream of inputs where, for each instance, the system can make real-time queries to a crowd to resolve uncertainty before committing to a prediction. The labels obtained from the crowd are used to continuously improve the underlying prediction model, thus reducing the reliance on crowd queries over time. The core of the contribution is a principled formulation of this problem as a stochastic game between the system and the crowd, grounded in Bayesian decision theory. This formulation allows for an explicit trade-off between prediction accuracy, monetary cost, and latency. As the exact optimal policy is intractable to compute, the work proposes an effective approximation using Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) combined with progressive widening to handle the continuous nature of time. This work distinguishes itself from standard online learning and active learning by its ability to actively seek information *before* making a prediction on a given instance, thereby enabling high performance from the very beginning. It also differs from active classification, which typically learns a static query policy from an existing dataset.

**Detailed Review**

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound and presents a complete piece of work. The formulation of the on-the-job learning problem as a stochastic game is rigorous and well-motivated. It provides a solid theoretical foundation for making sequential decisions under uncertainty in this novel setting. The use of MCTS with progressive widening is a suitable and state-of-the-art choice for approximating the intractable value function. The experimental evaluation is strong; it covers three distinct tasks (NER, sentiment analysis, image classification) and uses appropriate baselines to demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method, which is named LENSE. The claims of significant cost reduction and accuracy improvement are well-supported by the experimental results. The methodology for ensuring experimental consistency by pre-collecting and sampling from a fixed pool of crowd responses is a commendable detail that enhances the reliability of the conclusions.

**Clarity:** The paper is very clearly written and well-organized. The motivation for the problem is compelling, and the description of the model and the game-playing algorithm is easy to follow. The inclusion of Figure 2, which illustrates the game tree and the dynamics of the system, is particularly helpful for building intuition. A major strength in terms of clarity and contribution to the community is the release of open-source code and a CodaLab worksheet, which ensures the work is fully reproducible.

**Originality:** The work is highly original. The central contribution is the conceptualization and formalization of the "on-the-job learning" setting. While it draws upon established ideas from active learning, crowdsourcing, online learning, and game theory, the synthesis of these concepts to address the cold-start problem with a high-accuracy-from-the-start constraint is novel. Framing this specific interactive learning problem as a stochastic game and solving it with MCTS is a novel application of these techniques. This approach represents a significant departure from existing paradigms and opens up a new direction for research in interactive machine learning.

**Significance:** The results presented are significant and impactful. The paper tackles a critical and practical challenge in deploying machine learning systems: the need for large, pre-existing labeled datasets. The proposed framework offers a path to build and deploy accurate systems immediately, learning and reducing costs as they operate. The empirical results, particularly the order-of-magnitude cost reduction on the NER task while improving accuracy over expert labels, are impressive and demonstrate the practical viability of the approach. The ideas presented are likely to be influential and could inspire further work on real-time systems that learn interactively from human experts.

**Suggestions for Improvement:**

The work is strong, but the following points could be addressed to further improve it:

*   **Environment Model Assumptions:** The model of the crowd environment makes some simplifying assumptions. The response model `pR(r | yq)` assumes a fixed, uniform error rate (worker accuracy of 70%). In practice, worker accuracy can vary significantly, and some items are inherently more difficult to label than others. A brief discussion on the potential impact of a more sophisticated crowd model (e.g., one that learns worker-specific reliability) would strengthen the paper. Similarly, the response time model `pT` is not fully detailed; specifying the distribution used would add completeness.
*   **Scalability of MCTS:** The proposed system runs MCTS for each new input instance to decide on a sequence of actions. The computational overhead of this search could be a limiting factor, especially for problems with very large action spaces (e.g., long sequences in structured prediction) or in applications with extremely low latency requirements. A discussion regarding the computational complexity of the decision-making process, the typical number of simulations required, and how the runtime scales would be a valuable addition.
*   **Utility Function Hyperparameters:** The utility function in Equation 1 relies on weights `wM` and `wT` to balance monetary cost and latency against accuracy. The selection of these weights is crucial for system behavior but is not discussed. Providing some insight into how these parameters were set for the experiments, or including a sensitivity analysis, would offer important practical guidance for others wishing to apply this framework.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   **Pro:** Presents a novel, well-motivated, and important problem setting ("on-the-job learning").
*   **Pro:** The technical approach is principled, based on a solid foundation of Bayesian decision theory and stochastic games.
*   **Pro:** The experimental results are strong and convincing, demonstrating significant improvements over well-chosen baselines across multiple tasks.
*   **Pro:** The paper is exceptionally clear and includes reproducible code, which is a significant service to the research community.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**
*   **Con (minor):** The environment model contains simplifications that might not hold in all real-world scenarios.
*   **Con (minor):** The practical computational cost and scalability of the MCTS-based decision-making process are not fully analyzed.

Overall, the strengths of this paper far outweigh its minor weaknesses. It introduces a novel and significant problem, proposes a high-quality and principled solution, and validates it with strong empirical results. It represents an excellent contribution to the field.
