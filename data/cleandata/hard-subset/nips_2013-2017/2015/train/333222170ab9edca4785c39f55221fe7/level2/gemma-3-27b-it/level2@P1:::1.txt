This paper proposes an on-the-job learning framework that leverages real-time crowdsourcing to build accurate models without requiring extensive pre-training data. The core idea is to balance the trade-off between querying the crowd for labels, model accuracy, and prediction latency using Bayesian decision theory and a Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) approximation. The approach is evaluated on three tasks – named entity recognition, sentiment classification, and image classification – demonstrating significant cost reductions compared to full human annotation and improvements over online learning baselines.

The paper is well-motivated and tackles a practically relevant problem – deploying accurate systems with limited initial resources. The idea of formalizing the interactive querying process as a stochastic game and employing Bayesian decision theory for optimal policy selection is a strong contribution. The use of MCTS to handle the intractability of the optimal policy is also a sensible choice. The experimental results are compelling, particularly the significant cost reduction achieved on the NER task. The open-source release of the LENSE system is a valuable addition.

However, the paper also has several weaknesses. While the theoretical framework is appealing, the description of the MCTS approximation feels somewhat hand-wavy. Algorithm 1 lacks sufficient detail to fully understand the implementation, especially regarding the progressive widening strategy and how the UCT decision rule is applied in the context of continuous time. The sensitivity analysis of the hyperparameters within the MCTS algorithm (e.g., the exploration constant 'c') is missing. 

The experimental evaluation, while strong overall, could be improved. The choice of baselines feels somewhat limited. For instance, while online learning is included, a more sophisticated active learning baseline that utilizes a static query policy (as the authors acknowledge in the related work section) would provide a stronger comparison point. The discussion of the impact of worker quality is good, but it relies on a frozen pool of worker responses, which might not fully reflect the dynamic nature of crowd worker availability and skill.  The performance discrepancies between the tasks (NER performing significantly better than sentiment and image classification) are not fully explained.  A more detailed error analysis could illuminate the challenges specific to each task and identify potential areas for improvement.

Furthermore, the paper relies heavily on the assumption of independent queries, stating a limitation regarding not allowing queries to be issued mid-wait. The impact of this simplification on performance isn’t thoroughly discussed. The relationship to Legion:AR [6] could be more thoroughly explored; the authors state that it doesn't explore the machine learning foundations, but further comparison of the modeling choices would be beneficial. The description of the utility function (Equation 1) is conceptually clear but lacks specific values for the weights wM and wT. Providing these values and discussing their impact on the querying strategy would enhance understanding. 

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel framework for on-the-job learning combining Bayesian decision theory, MCTS, and crowdsourcing.
*   Strong experimental results demonstrating cost reductions and accuracy improvements.
*   Open-source release of the LENSE system.
*   Addresses a practically relevant problem with a well-defined and theoretically grounded approach.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Lack of detailed explanation of the MCTS approximation and progressive widening strategy.
*   Limited comparison to strong active learning baselines.
*   Insufficient analysis of the impact of hyperparameters and worker quality.
*   Simplification of allowing queries mid-wait and the consequences.
*   Limited discussion of the weights within the utility function.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper is a solid contribution to the field. While the paper has weaknesses, its strengths – the novelty of the approach, the strong experimental results, and the practical relevance of the problem – outweigh them.  I recommend acceptance with minor revisions. Specifically, the authors should provide more details on the MCTS implementation, expand the baseline comparison, and offer a more in-depth discussion of the experimental results. Addressing the points regarding hyperparameters and utility function weights would also significantly strengthen the paper.
