Summary and contributions
The paper proposes “on-the-job learning,” a framework for deploying high-accuracy predictors with zero initial labeled data by interleaving model predictions with real-time crowd queries. The system casts prediction as a stochastic game between the system and the crowd, with actions to issue token-level queries, wait for responses (which arrive asynchronously), or return a prediction. A Bayesian decision-theoretic utility trades off expected accuracy against monetary query cost and latency. Because computing the optimal policy is intractable, the authors approximate it with Monte Carlo Tree Search (UCT) augmented with progressive widening to handle continuous time. The environment model factorizes p(y, r, t | x, q, s) into a CRF pθ(y | x), a simple response-noise model pR, and a response-time model pT. Experiments on NER, sentiment, and image classification suggest improved accuracy-cost-latency trade-offs versus human-only baselines, online learning, and a heuristic thresholding baseline. Code and data are released.

Relation to prior work
The work connects several strands: online learning (learns over time while maintaining high accuracy from the start), active learning and active classification (queries informative information pre-decision), real-time crowdsourcing (retainer pools), and structured prediction (CRFs). Relative to Legion:AR (real-time crowd for activity recognition), this paper provides a principled Bayesian decision-theoretic formulation with a search-based policy. Compared to TurKontrol-style POMDPs for optimizing votes, this work couples querying with structured prediction and explicit time-cost trade-offs, and learns the prediction model online. It leverages UCT (Kocsis & Szepesvári) and progressive widening (Coulom) to plan under uncertainty with continuous delays.

Strengths
- Clear, principled formulation: Casting the problem as a stochastic game with a utility balancing accuracy, cost, and latency is well motivated and yields intuitive behaviors (parallel vs sequential queries, redundancy for noisy workers).
- Practical approximate planning: MCTS + progressive widening is a sensible and novel combination for this setting with asynchronous responses.
- Structured outputs: Extending on-the-job querying to structured prediction (CRFs for NER) is valuable; the model leverages correlations across labels.
- Empirical benefits: On three tasks, the method consistently improves the accuracy-cost curve. Notably, on NER the system achieves an F1 of 88.4% with >10× cost reduction vs 5-vote human annotation, and outperforms single-human and online learning baselines.
- Reproducibility: Open-source code, data, and documented crowdsourcing setup.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Response/noise model simplicity: pR(r | yq) fixes accuracy at 70% uniformly and independent across queries. This ignores per-worker variability, item difficulty, and confusion structure. The system could be sensitive to this misspecification.
- Evaluation realism: To control worker variance, experiments sample from a pre-collected pool of five responses and delays per label without replacement. While this isolates the policy from crowd fluctuations, it likely underestimates real-world variance and may bias planning (e.g., limited diversity of delay distributions).
- Utility-target mismatch: The utility optimizes expected 0-1 accuracy of the MAP solution (ExpAcc), while results are reported in F1 for NER. It is unclear whether the optimized utility aligns with the reported metric and whether optimizing token-level vs sequence-level accuracy affects behavior.
- Limited ablations and sensitivity: There is little analysis of sensitivity to wM and wT, the accuracy of pR and pT, or the contribution of planning vs simpler heuristics beyond the threshold baseline. How much do MCTS depth, rollout policy, and progressive widening parameters matter?
- Scalability and computational budget: The paper does not quantify the computational overhead of MCTS per example or the wall-clock implications when paired with real-time crowdsourcing. Planning under continuous time with multiple in-flight queries may become expensive for longer sequences.
- Partial constraints in action model: Disallowing query launches while waiting simplifies search but could leave performance on the table. Some discussion of the empirical impact would help.
- Dataset and feature details: The NER label set and dataset specifics (e.g., “RESOURCE” and “Twiner”) differ from standard CoNLL; feature sets and CRF configuration are only briefly described, limiting reproducibility for the ML component itself.

Quality
Technically sound overall, with a reasonable environment model and a clear derivation of the game and utility. The approximation is standard and appropriate. The main concerns are the simplifying assumptions for crowd modeling and the controlled evaluation that may not fully capture deployment noise. Empirical results are promising and include multiple baselines, but more diagnostics/ablations would strengthen the evidence.

Clarity
The paper is well organized. The game state, actions, and transitions are clearly specified, and the MCTS algorithm is presented with helpful intuition. Some implementation details are underspecified (e.g., parameterization/estimation of pT, exact CRF features, and training protocol for learning from noisy responses). Clarifying the utility versus reported metrics would improve clarity.

Originality
Moderately high. While components (active querying, crowdsourced labels, POMDP/UCT) are known, their integration for on-the-job learning with structured prediction and explicit latency-cost-accuracy trade-offs, along with continuous-time planning, is novel and timely.

Significance
Practically significant for deploying ML systems without upfront labeled data and for scenarios needing high initial accuracy. The approach could influence real-time, human-in-the-loop ML. Theoretical advances are limited, but the contribution is meaningful for NeurIPS’s applied ML community.

Questions for the authors
- How are pT and its parameters learned and updated over time? Is there adaptation to nonstationary worker response times?
- How sensitive is performance to mis-specification of pR (e.g., true accuracy 60% or 80%, asymmetric confusions)?
- What is the computational cost (planning time per instance), and how does it scale with sequence length and number of in-flight queries?
- Does optimizing ExpAcc correlate with F1 on NER? Have you tried alternative utilities (e.g., token-F1, cost-sensitive per-label utilities)?
- Can the policy launch queries while “waiting” without exploding search (e.g., limited interleaving), and what is the empirical benefit?

Suggestions for improvement
- Learn and adapt a richer crowd model online: per-worker reliabilities, item difficulty, and confusion matrices; incorporate this into pR and the posterior.
- Provide sensitivity and ablation studies: utility weights (wM, wT), MCTS parameters, effect of progressive widening, and analysis of sequential vs parallel querying strategies.
- Report computational overhead and end-to-end latency (planning + crowdsourcing).
- Align utility and evaluation metrics or justify the current choice; consider structured metrics in the utility.
- Expand dataset and feature descriptions; include an ablation showing learning curves with/without richer features and the impact on number of queries.
- If possible, include a live (non-simulated) evaluation to validate robustness to real crowd variability.

Pros
- Principled formulation with practical planning.
- Strong empirical gains on multiple tasks with open-source release.
- Addresses cost/latency explicitly and supports structured outputs.

Cons
- Simplistic and potentially mis-specified crowd model.
- Controlled evaluation may overstate robustness.
- Limited analysis of sensitivity, scalability, and metric alignment.

Overall: A solid, well-motivated contribution with promising results and a clear path to further strengthening. I lean toward acceptance, contingent on addressing modeling assumptions and providing additional analysis.
