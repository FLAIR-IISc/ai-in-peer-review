Summary and contributions
The paper introduces an on-the-job learning framework for deploying high-accuracy systems with zero initial training data by interleaving model predictions with real-time crowdsourcing. Each instance triggers a stochastic game between a system and a crowd, where the system issues token- or item-level queries, can wait for asynchronous responses, and decides when to return a prediction. A Bayesian decision-theoretic utility balances accuracy, latency, and monetary cost. Exact planning is intractable; the work proposes an approximation based on Monte Carlo Tree Search (UCT) with progressive widening to handle continuous response times. Experiments on named-entity recognition (NER), sentiment classification, and face attribute/image classification show improved F1/accuracy at substantially reduced cost compared with full human annotation, a single-vote human baseline, an online learning baseline (oracle labels after prediction, no pre-decision querying), and a hand-crafted threshold policy. An open-source system (LENSE) and CodaLab bundles are provided.

Relation to prior work
The setting connects to online learning [7], active learning [19], and active classification [22–24]. Unlike online (active) learning [8–10], the framework can query before committing a prediction, allowing high accuracy from the outset. Relative to active classification, which typically learns a static test-time policy from fully labeled data, this work both queries at prediction time and updates the model online. The paper is most closely related to real-time crowd-powered systems such as Legion:AR [6] and decision-theoretic control of crowdsourcing (e.g., TurKontrol [26]), but extends them by (a) handling structured outputs with a CRF, (b) formulating a unified game over actions and asynchronous responses, and (c) using MCTS with progressive widening [12, 13] to optimize a joint accuracy/latency/cost utility. The structured-prediction angle resonates with non-local inference for IE [14] and partial observation/measurement frameworks [27, 28].

Quality
- Technical soundness: The game formulation, utility, and MCTS-based planning are well motivated. The decomposition p(y, r, t | x, q, s) = pθ(y | x) ∏ pR(ri | yqi) pT(ti | si) is clear and allows simulation-based value estimation. The progressive widening design is appropriate for continuous time.
- Approximations: The crowd response model pR assumes a fixed per-label accuracy (0.7) with uniform errors, independent of token, instance difficulty, or worker identity. This is a strong simplification; empirical results still improve over baselines, but it limits claims about optimality under realistic crowd heterogeneity. The computation of ExpAcc for sequence F1 is not fully specified; if F1 is used as Accuracy in the utility, the Monte Carlo estimate should be clarified given its non-decomposability.
- Empirical evaluation: The three datasets and baselines are appropriate, and the retainer-based setup is realistic. The reported gains on NER (e.g., >10× cost reduction vs. 5-vote, and F1 improvements over 1-vote and online learning) are convincing. For sentiment and faces, benefits are smaller but consistent, with a reasonable hypothesis about dominant-class structure aiding NER. The strategy to freeze a small pool of crowd responses and latencies provides controlled comparisons across methods, but reduces ecological validity and may understate variance.
- Reproducibility: Code and CodaLab bundles are provided; this is a strong positive.

Clarity
The paper is generally clear and well organized. The game state, actions, and transitions are defined precisely. Algorithm 1 concisely explains the MCTS/progressive-widening scheme. Some areas would benefit from additional detail:
- Exact form and estimation of pT(t | s) (parametric vs. empirical).
- How ExpAcc is computed for structured metrics (token accuracy vs. sequence F1).
- Computational budget/latency of planning: number of simulations per decision, wall-clock overhead relative to worker response times.

Originality
Modeling on-the-job learning with a unified Bayesian decision-theoretic game for structured prediction and solving it via MCTS with progressive widening is novel in this combination. The integration of asynchronous crowd responses, structured dependencies via a CRF, and test-time learning sets the work apart from earlier real-time crowd systems [6] and POMDP-based control [26].

Significance
The approach addresses a practically important regime: immediate high-accuracy deployment without labeled data, with an explicit latency/cost/accuracy trade-off. The improvements over strong human and machine baselines, plus open-source release, suggest potential adoption in systems that require early high precision. The simplifications in the crowd model limit conclusions about robustness across varied crowds, but the framework is sufficiently general to incorporate richer models.

Pros
- Clear, principled formulation using Bayesian decision theory and a well-matched approximate planner (UCT + progressive widening).
- Demonstrated gains in both cost and accuracy across three tasks, with especially strong results on NER.
- Handles structured prediction and asynchronous crowds, which many alternatives do not.
- Open-source implementation and reproducibility artifacts.

Cons
- Simplistic crowd model (fixed 0.7 accuracy, no per-worker or per-item variation), potentially misaligned with real crowdsourcing dynamics.
- Utility/metric mismatch is unclear for non-decomposable measures (e.g., F1); details of ExpAcc approximation are missing.
- Planning overhead and sensitivity to utility weights (wM, wT) are not systematically evaluated.
- Comparisons lack stronger VOI/POMDP baselines (e.g., TurKontrol-style control or knowledge-gradient heuristics) and myopic lookahead policies tailored to the same utility.

Questions/clarifications
- How is ExpAcc computed when the evaluation metric is sequence F1? Is y sampled from p(y | x, q, s, r, t) and F1 averaged, or is a surrogate used?
- What is the exact form of pT? Is it learned empirically per task, and how sensitive are results to its mis-specification?
- What is the computational cost per decision (e.g., simulations per action, average milliseconds) and how does it scale with sequence length and number of pending queries?

Suggestions for improvement
Essential:
- Provide implementation details for ExpAcc under F1 and report sensitivity to the number of Monte Carlo samples used to estimate utility.
- Report planning-time overhead and show that decision latency is negligible relative to worker response times; include the number of MCTS rollouts and stopping criteria.
- Include sensitivity analyses for wM and wT, demonstrating how the policy adapts and how results vary across the cost–latency–accuracy frontier.

Highly recommended:
- Replace the fixed-accuracy pR with a simple but more realistic model (e.g., per-item difficulty or a Beta-Bernoulli reliability prior; or per-class confusion) and show robustness.
- Add a myopic VOI baseline and a POMDP-style control baseline (e.g., TurKontrol-like) that optimizes the same utility, to strengthen comparisons.
- Report results with live, unfrozen crowds to complement the controlled simulations, including variance across different times of day and worker pools.

Nice-to-have:
- Explore limited interleaving (issuing additional queries while “waiting”) to assess the impact of the modeling simplification that forbids mid-wait launches.
- Discuss extension to richer structured models and features (e.g., neural CRFs) and the effect on reliance on the crowd over time.

Overall assessment
A solid and timely contribution that advances decision-theoretic control for human-in-the-loop, on-the-job learning. With clarifications on utility computation, planning overhead, and more realistic crowd modeling/baselines, this work would be a strong fit for NIPS.
