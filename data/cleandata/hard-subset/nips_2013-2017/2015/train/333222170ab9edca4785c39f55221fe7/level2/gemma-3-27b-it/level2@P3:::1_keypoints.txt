- Introduces on-the-job learning: a framework that learns from real-time crowdsourcing to balance latency, cost, and accuracy using a stochastic-game formulation approximated with MCTS.
- Well-motivated for scenarios with scarce labeled data, uniquely combining online learning, active learning, and crowdsourcing in a principled way.
- Uses Bayesian decision theory and MCTS to model and approximate optimal policies, enabling trade-offs among competing objectives.
- Empirical results on NER, sentiment, and image classification show significant cost reductions versus full human annotation and improvements over baselines.
- NER results are particularly compelling, and the open-source LENSE implementation enhances reproducibility and impact.
- Presentation is clear and related work discussion is thorough, accurately positioning the contribution.
- Limited detail on the CRF models and feature engineering for each task; an ablation study is needed to assess feature impact.
- Worker response times are sampled from a frozen pool, which may limit adaptability to changing worker availability and quality.
- Utility function weights (wM and wT) lack guidance on selection/tuning; a sensitivity analysis is missing.
- Progressive widening is only briefly discussed and needs a more intuitive explanation for handling continuous variables.
- Simulating zero-training-examples using existing labeled datasets could introduce bias and should be explicitly acknowledged and discussed.
- Code is available but would benefit from a more detailed tutorial or integration example for real-world use.
- Arguments for acceptance: novel/principled approach, strong empirical validation, clear writing, open-source code, and practical relevance.
- Arguments against acceptance: insufficient feature/weight tuning detail, reliance on frozen worker pool, and potential dataset-simulation bias.
- Overall assessment: strong paper with addressable minor weaknesses; merits acceptance with suggested revisions.
