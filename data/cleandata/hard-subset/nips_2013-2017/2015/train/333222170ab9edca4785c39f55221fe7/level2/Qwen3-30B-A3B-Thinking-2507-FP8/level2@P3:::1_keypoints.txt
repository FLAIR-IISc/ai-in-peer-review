- Proposes "on-the-job learning": deploy high-accuracy systems from zero examples by querying crowds at prediction time and reducing human input as the model improves.
- Formulates the problem as a stochastic game using Bayesian decision theory to balance accuracy, latency, and cost.
- Approximates the optimal policy with Monte Carlo Tree Search and progressive widening.
- Empirical results on NER, sentiment, and image classification show large cost reductions (e.g., >10× vs full human annotation on NER) and accuracy gains (8% F1 over single-human, 28% over online learning).
- Well-motivated for real-world deployment constraints.
- Theoretically grounded formulation that distinguishes the approach from active and online learning.
- Strong empirical performance across diverse tasks.
- Transparent implementation details and open-source release improve reproducibility.
- Comprehensive baseline comparisons that highlight the framework’s advantages.
- Utility function uses fixed cost/latency weights (w_M, w_T) without justification or sensitivity analysis.
- Mapping from monetary payments to utility parameters is unexplained, undermining the claimed principled tradeoff.
- Crowd response model assumes 70% worker accuracy but lacks validation across all tasks (image classification missing error-rate data).
- Related work section fails to adequately distinguish from Dai et al. (2014); the claim about Legion:AR omits its POMDP decision-making.
- Cost-reduction claims need nuance: the "order of magnitude" NER reduction is relative to a 5-vote human baseline, not necessarily the most cost-effective alternative.
- The reported 28% F1 improvement over online learning may be overstated because the online baseline is a naive/perfect-labels setup, not a standard online method.
- Suggested revisions: add ablations on w_M and w_T to validate utility parameters.
- Suggested revisions: empirically validate crowd accuracy (error rates) for each task/dataset.
- Suggested revisions: clarify and explicitly contrast the approach with Dai et al. (2014) and POMDP-based methods.
- Suggested revisions: refine cost-reduction claims and explicitly state comparison baselines.
- Overall: compelling results but require tighter justification and additional experiments to meet NIPS quality standards.
