

This paper introduces "on-the-job learning," a novel framework for deploying high-accuracy AI systems from zero training examples by strategically using real-time crowdsourcing to resolve uncertainty during prediction. The authors model the problem as a stochastic game based on Bayesian decision theory, enabling principled tradeoffs between accuracy, latency, and cost. They develop an approximation using Monte Carlo Tree Search with progressive widening, and evaluate on three tasks (NER, sentiment classification, image classification), demonstrating significant cost reductions while improving accuracy.

The paper's strengths are substantial. The problem formulation is clear and well-motivated, distinguishing the on-the-job learning setting from related work in online learning, active learning, and active classification. The formalization as a stochastic game with Bayesian decision theory is both novel and well-justified, providing a principled way to balance multiple competing objectives. The implementation of LENSE (Learning from Expensive Noisy Slow Experts) is thorough, with detailed experimental setup and reproducibility through code and data on CodaLab. The results are compelling: on NER, the approach achieves more than an order of magnitude cost reduction compared to full human annotation while boosting performance relative to expert labels, with 8% F1 improvement over single-human labeling and 28% over online learning. The analysis of how query patterns evolve over time (Figure 3) provides valuable insights into the system's learning dynamics.

The paper excels in technical quality. The game-theoretic model is carefully constructed with appropriate assumptions about crowd response models and response times. The authors acknowledge limitations (e.g., the fixed 70% crowd accuracy assumption) and provide a clear explanation of how their approach differs from related work. The implementation details, including the use of a frozen pool of worker responses to control for variance, demonstrate careful experimental design.

I have minor suggestions to enhance the paper. The discussion of why the threshold baseline performs worse than LENSE could be strengthened with more specific analysis of the tradeoffs between time and accuracy. While the authors note that LENSE outperforms the threshold baseline, a brief analysis of when each approach might be preferable would add depth. Additionally, the paper could more explicitly address how the framework would scale to larger systems with higher throughput requirements, though this may be beyond the paper's scope.

The significance of this work is high. Many real-world applications (e.g., disaster relief monitoring, real-time information extraction) require high-accuracy systems to be deployed immediately without large labeled datasets. The authors demonstrate that their approach can deliver this while significantly reducing costs over time, making it highly relevant for practical deployment scenarios. The framework's flexibility to balance different objectives (accuracy, latency, cost) is particularly valuable for real-world systems with varying constraints.

In terms of originality, the paper clearly distinguishes its contribution from related work. While active learning and online learning have been studied extensively, the specific setting of maintaining high accuracy from the very first prediction without initial labeled data is novel. The stochastic game formulation with Bayesian decision theory provides a principled foundation that goes beyond the heuristic approaches in existing active classification work.

The paper is well-written and well-organized, with clear figures (Figures 2-4) that effectively illustrate the key concepts. The implementation details are sufficient for reproducibility, and the code availability on CodaLab is a significant plus.

Overall, this is a strong contribution that advances the state of the art in deploying AI systems with minimal initial data. The technical soundness, clear presentation, and compelling empirical results make this work well-suited for NIPS. I recommend acceptance. The paper addresses an important practical problem with a novel theoretical foundation and demonstrates significant practical benefits. The authors have made a valuable contribution to the field of machine learning for real-world deployment scenarios.
