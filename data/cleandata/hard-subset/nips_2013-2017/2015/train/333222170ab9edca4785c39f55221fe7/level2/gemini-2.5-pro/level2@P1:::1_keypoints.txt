- Introduces "on-the-job learning": deploy high-accuracy systems from scratch by using real-time crowdsourcing per input and improving the model over time.  
- Formalizes the problem as a stochastic game and uses Bayesian decision theory to trade off accuracy, query cost, and latency.  
- Exact optimal policy is intractable; proposes an MCTS-based approximation with progressive widening to handle continuous time.  
- Distinct from active/online learning by focusing on immediate high performance from the first example.  
- Implements system LENSE and evaluates on NER, sentiment, and image classification tasks.  
- Empirical results: LENSE reduces crowd cost significantly while achieving higher accuracy than online baselines and single annotators.  
- Technical quality is high: principled formulation, solid Bayesian foundation, and appropriate MCTS approximation.  
- Strong experimental methodology: uses a frozen pool of worker responses/latencies, multiple baselines, and open-sourced code/data for reproducibility.  
- Clarity: paper is well-written and organized; Figure 2 and experiments clearly support claims.  
- Minor clarity suggestions: provide a walkthrough of a game-tree state transition and intuitive explanation of ExpAcc (Eq. 2).  
- Originality: novel synthesis of established techniques applied to a new, practical "on-the-job learning" setting.  
- Significance: addresses real deployment bottleneck, shows large cost reductions (notably on NER), and offers a broadly applicable framework.  
- Strengths: principled problem formulation, elegant BDT+MCTS solution, strong empirical evidence across tasks, and commitment to reproducibility.  
- Weaknesses/suggestions: crowd model is simplistic (e.g., fixed 70% accuracy) and could model worker heterogeneity; lacks discussion of MCTS computational complexity and scalability.
