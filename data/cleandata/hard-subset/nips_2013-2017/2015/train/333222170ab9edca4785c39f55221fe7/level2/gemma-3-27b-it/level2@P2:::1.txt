This paper proposes a novel on-the-job learning framework that combines Bayesian decision theory with Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) to strategically query a crowd for labels, balancing cost, latency, and accuracy. The core idea is to learn a model incrementally while simultaneously utilizing a crowd to resolve uncertainties, gradually reducing reliance on human input. This approach contrasts with traditional supervised learning (requiring large labeled datasets) and pure crowdsourcing, and positions itself between online and active learning paradigms.

The paper is well-motivated, addressing a practical problem of deploying AI systems with limited initial resources. The formulation as a stochastic game is a clever way to formalize the trade-offs between the different objectives. The experimental results on three tasks (NER, sentiment classification, and image classification) are compelling, demonstrating significant cost reductions and performance improvements compared to baselines like full human annotation, single-human labeling, and online learning. The availability of an open-source implementation (LENSE) is also a significant positive. 

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The combination of Bayesian decision theory, MCTS, and on-the-job learning presents a novel approach to active learning in a real-time, resource-constrained setting. The formalization as a stochastic game is a significant contribution.
* **Practical Relevance:** The problem addressed – deploying systems without pre-labeled data – is highly relevant and practical, particularly in rapidly evolving scenarios like disaster response or emerging trends on social media.
* **Strong Empirical Results:** The experimental results are convincing, showing substantial cost savings and accuracy gains across all three tasks, especially the impressive reduction in cost for NER. The comparison to human annotation and single-human labeling is particularly strong.
* **Technical Soundness:** The use of MCTS for approximating the optimal policy appears to be a reasonable and well-justified approach given the intractability of finding the exact solution. The utility function captures the key trade-offs effectively.
* **Reproducibility:** The authors provide code and data, facilitating reproducibility and further research.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Assumptions about Crowd:** The model relies on a pre-characterized distribution of worker response times and accuracy (pR and pT). While they attempt to estimate these from a retainer pool, the performance could be sensitive to the quality and representativeness of this pool.  A more robust approach to adaptively estimate these parameters during operation could be considered.
* **Scalability Concerns:** Although not explicitly discussed, the computational cost of MCTS could become a bottleneck for very large state spaces or complex prediction tasks. Further discussion on the scalability and potential optimizations for MCTS in this context would be valuable.
* **Limited Exploration of Utility Function:** The utility function uses fixed weights (wM and wT) for cost and time. A sensitivity analysis or an adaptive mechanism for adjusting these weights based on application-specific requirements would strengthen the results. The choice of these weights seems rather arbitrary.
* **Comparison to State-of-the-Art Active Learning:** While the paper compares to relevant baselines, a comparison to more sophisticated active learning strategies (e.g., those employing deep learning for uncertainty estimation) would provide a more comprehensive benchmark.
* **Clarity of Algorithm 1:** Algorithm 1, while providing a high-level overview, is somewhat dense and could benefit from more detailed explanation, particularly concerning the progressive widening step and how it interacts with the MCTS exploration.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper addresses a significant and practical problem with a novel and well-motivated approach.
* The experimental results are strong and demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method.
* The technical contribution – formulating the problem as a stochastic game and using MCTS – is substantial.
* The code release promotes reproducibility and further research.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*  The model's performance depends on accurate estimation of crowd characteristics, which could be challenging in real-world scenarios.
*  Scalability concerns with MCTS for complex tasks are not fully addressed.
*  A more thorough comparison to state-of-the-art active learning methods would strengthen the paper.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with a compelling contribution to the field of active learning. The proposed framework is novel, well-motivated, and supported by convincing experimental results. While there are some weaknesses related to crowd modeling, scalability, and comparisons to other active learning methods, these do not fundamentally detract from the significance of the work. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the points raised above, particularly regarding the discussion of scalability and sensitivity analysis of the utility function weights.
