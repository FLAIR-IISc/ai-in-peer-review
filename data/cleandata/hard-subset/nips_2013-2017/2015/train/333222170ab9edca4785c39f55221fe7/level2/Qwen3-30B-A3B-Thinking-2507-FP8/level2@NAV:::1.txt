

This paper introduces "on-the-job learning," a novel framework for deploying high-accuracy AI systems starting with zero training examples by strategically using real-time crowdsourcing to resolve uncertainty. The authors model this as a stochastic game based on Bayesian decision theory, which balances latency, cost, and accuracy objectives. They develop an approximation using Monte Carlo Tree Search with progressive widening to handle the intractable optimal policy computation. The approach is evaluated on three tasks (NER, sentiment classification, image classification), showing significant improvements over baselines: more than an order of magnitude cost reduction on NER compared to full human annotation, 8% F1 improvement over single human labeling, and 28% F1 improvement over online learning.

The paper's main strengths lie in its clear problem formulation, well-motivated Bayesian decision theory framework, and thorough experimental validation across multiple domains. The stochastic game model effectively captures the trade-offs between latency, cost, and accuracy, and the MCTS-based approximation is well-justified. The implementation details and open-source release (LENSE) enhance reproducibility. The comparison with relevant baselines (human n-query, online learning, threshold baseline) is comprehensive, and the results are statistically significant across all three tasks.

However, the paper could strengthen its novelty claim by more explicitly differentiating from related work. While the authors cite Legion:AR as a predecessor, they don't sufficiently address how their Bayesian decision-theoretic foundation fundamentally differs from existing on-the-job learning approaches. The discussion of limitations is somewhat superficial - the paper notes that performance varies across tasks but doesn't deeply analyze why (e.g., whether the dominant "none" class in NER is the primary factor or if task complexity plays a role). Additionally, the paper lacks analysis of the computational overhead of the MCTS approximation, which could be significant for real-time applications.

The work is technically sound, well-written, and addresses a meaningful practical problem. The framework is clearly applicable to real-world scenarios where labeled data is scarce but high accuracy is required from deployment. With more thorough comparison to prior work and deeper analysis of limitations, this would be an even stronger contribution. As it stands, the paper makes a valuable contribution to the field of active learning and crowdsourcing, and I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the novelty and limitations sections.
