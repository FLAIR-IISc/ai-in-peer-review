- Proposes an on-the-job learning framework combining Bayesian decision theory with MCTS to query a crowd, balancing cost, latency, and accuracy.
- Learns a model incrementally while using crowd input to resolve uncertainty, reducing reliance on humans over time.
- Formulated as a stochastic game to formalize trade-offs among cost, time, and accuracy.
- Well-motivated for deploying AI with limited labeled data, especially in fast-changing scenarios.
- Novel combination of Bayesian decision theory, MCTS, and on-the-job learning; stochastic-game formalization is a key contribution.
- Strong practical relevance for settings like disaster response and emerging social-media trends.
- Empirical results on NER, sentiment, and image classification show significant cost reductions and accuracy gains vs. baselines.
- Open-source implementation (LENSE) provided, aiding reproducibility.
- Use of MCTS to approximate the optimal policy is technically sound given intractability of exact solution.
- Utility function effectively captures the main trade-offs among objectives.
- Relies on pre-characterized worker response-time and accuracy distributions (pR, pT), which may not generalize well.
- Computational cost of MCTS could limit scalability for large state spaces or complex tasks; more discussion needed.
- Utility uses fixed weights (wM, wT); lacks sensitivity analysis or adaptive weighting mechanisms.
- Lacks comparison to more advanced active learning methods (e.g., deep-learning uncertainty approaches).
- Algorithm 1 is dense and needs clearer explanation, especially about progressive widening and its MCTS interaction.
- Arguments for acceptance: addresses an important problem, offers a novel, well-motivated approach, strong experiments, and released code.
- Arguments against acceptance: sensitivity to crowd modeling, unresolved MCTS scalability concerns, and incomplete comparisons to SOTA active learning.
- Overall assessment: strong, novel paper with convincing results; recommend acceptance with minor revisions on scalability and sensitivity analysis.
