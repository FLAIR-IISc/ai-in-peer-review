- Summary: Proposes "on-the-job learning" that interleaves model predictions with real-time crowd queries using a stochastic game and a Bayesian utility trading off accuracy, cost, and latency.  
- Method: Approximates the intractable optimal policy with MCTS (UCT) plus progressive widening and models environment via a CRF, simple response-noise pR, and response-time pT.  
- Experiments: Shows improved accuracy–cost–latency trade-offs on NER, sentiment, and image classification versus human-only, online learning, and threshold baselines; code/data released.  
- Relation to prior work: Integrates ideas from online learning, active learning, real-time crowdsourcing, structured prediction, and planning (UCT/progressive widening); extends prior crowd-POMDP work to structured outputs and time-cost trade-offs.  
- Strength: Clear, principled decision-theoretic formulation that yields intuitive behaviors (parallel vs sequential queries, redundancy for noisy workers).  
- Strength: Practical planning via MCTS + progressive widening is sensible and novel for asynchronous responses.  
- Strength: Extends querying to structured prediction (CRFs) and leverages label correlations.  
- Strength: Empirical gains across tasks with notable cost reductions on NER and reproducible release.  
- Weakness: Simplistic response/noise model (fixed 70% accuracy, independent across queries) ignores worker variability, item difficulty, and confusion patterns.  
- Weakness: Evaluation realism limited by sampling from a fixed pool of five responses/delays, which may understate real-world variance and bias planning.  
- Weakness: Utility-target mismatch—utility optimizes expected 0-1 accuracy but results report F1 for NER, raising alignment concerns.  
- Weakness: Limited ablation and sensitivity analysis (weights wM/wT, pR/pT accuracy, MCTS parameters, planning vs heuristics).  
- Weakness: Scalability and computational overhead of MCTS per instance, especially for longer sequences or many in-flight queries, are not quantified.  
- Weakness: Action model restricts launching queries while waiting; empirical impact of this constraint is not analyzed.  
- Weakness: Dataset/feature/CRF configuration details are underspecified, limiting reproducibility of the ML component.  
- Quality: Technically sound overall, but simplifying crowd-model assumptions and controlled evaluation weaken deployment claims.  
- Clarity: Well organized with clear algorithm description, but some implementation and modeling details (pT estimation, CRF features, training from noisy responses) are underspecified; utility vs reported metrics unclear.  
- Originality: Moderately high—novel integration of existing components for continuous-time, structured, on-the-job learning.  
- Significance: Practically significant for deploying models without labeled data and human-in-the-loop systems; limited theoretical novelty.  
- Questions for authors: How are pT parameters learned/updated and adapted to nonstationarity? How sensitive is performance to pR mis-specification? What is planning time per instance and scaling behavior? Does optimizing ExpAcc correlate with F1? Can queries be launched while waiting and with what benefit?  
- Suggestions: Learn richer online crowd models (per-worker reliabilities, difficulty, confusion matrices) and incorporate into pR; provide sensitivity/ablation studies and report computational overhead and end-to-end latency.  
- Suggestions: Align utility with evaluation metrics or justify choice; expand dataset/feature descriptions and include live non-simulated crowd evaluation if possible.  
- Pros: Principled formulation, practical planning, strong empirical gains, open-source release, addresses cost/latency and structured outputs.  
- Cons: Simplistic/mis-specified crowd model, controlled evaluation may overstate robustness, limited sensitivity and scalability analysis.  
- Overall: Solid, well-motivated contribution with promising results; recommendation leans toward acceptance pending stronger modeling and additional analyses.
