This paper introduces "on-the-job learning," a novel and practical framework for deploying a high-accuracy system from zero initial training data. The core idea is to intelligently leverage real-time crowdsourcing to resolve uncertainty for incoming data instances, while simultaneously using the acquired labels to train a model that becomes progressively more self-sufficient. The authors formalize this problem as a stochastic game, using Bayesian decision theory to create a utility function that elegantly balances accuracy, monetary cost, and latency. As the optimal policy is intractable, they propose a well-reasoned approximation based on Monte Carlo Tree Search. This work is well-positioned, clearly distinguishing itself from related areas like online learning, which does not guarantee high initial performance, and active learning, where predictions on every instance are not the primary goal.

The paper's quality and significance are high. The problem formulation is highly relevant for many real-world applications where rapid deployment without a pre-existing labeled dataset is necessary. The proposed solution is technically sound, and the experimental evaluation is strong, with compelling results across three distinct tasks. The order-of-magnitude cost reduction on the NER task, while achieving super-human performance, is a particularly impressive demonstration of the system's value. The clarity of the paper is excellent, and the provision of open-source code and reproducible experiments is commendable.

A minor weakness is the simplicity of the environment model, which assumes a fixed worker accuracy. While effective for the experiments, a more sophisticated model that learns worker reliability and latency over time could make the system more robust in real-world deployments. Additionally, the paper would be strengthened by a sensitivity analysis of the utility function's cost and time weights to better understand their impact on system behavior.

In summary, this is a high-quality paper addressing an important problem with a novel, well-executed approach.

**Arguments for acceptance:**
1.  Novel and significant problem formulation with high practical impact.
2.  Principled, technically sound solution based on decision theory.
3.  Strong empirical results demonstrating substantial improvements over strong baselines.
4.  Clearly written, well-organized, and reproducible.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
1.  The model of the crowd is simplified.

This is a clear accept. The strengths far outweigh the minor limitations, which can be addressed in future work.
