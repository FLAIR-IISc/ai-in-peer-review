- Introduces an on-the-job learning framework that interleaves model predictions with real-time crowdsourcing to achieve high accuracy with zero initial training data.  
- Models each instance as a stochastic game where the system can query tokens/items, wait for asynchronous responses, and decide when to return a prediction.  
- Uses a Bayesian decision-theoretic utility that trades off accuracy, latency, and monetary cost.  
- Exact planning is intractable; proposes an MCTS (UCT) approximation with progressive widening for continuous response times.  
- Evaluated on NER, sentiment, and face/image classification, showing improved F1/accuracy and much lower cost versus full human annotation and several baselines.  
- Provides an open-source system (LENSE) and CodaLab bundles for reproducibility.  
- Connects to online learning, active learning, and active classification, but differs by allowing pre-decision queries and online model updates.  
- Extends real-time crowd systems and decision-theoretic crowd control by handling structured outputs (CRF), asynchronous responses, and MCTS planning.  
- Technical formulation and MCTS-based planning are well motivated and the decomposition for simulation-based value estimation is clear.  
- Progressive widening is appropriate for handling continuous-time response modeling.  
- Crowd response model pR is oversimplified (fixed 0.7 accuracy, uniform errors, no worker/item variability), limiting realism and optimality claims.  
- The computation/estimation of expected accuracy (ExpAcc) for non-decomposable metrics like sequence F1 is not fully specified and needs clarification.  
- Empirical evaluation uses appropriate datasets and baselines; NER shows especially strong gains while sentiment/faces show smaller but consistent benefits.  
- Freezing crowd responses/latencies gives controlled comparisons but reduces ecological validity and may understate variance.  
- Reproducibility is strong due to provided code and bundles.  
- Paper is generally clear and well organized; game state, actions, transitions, and Algorithm 1 are precise.  
- Missing details: exact form/estimation of pT(t|s), ExpAcc computation for structured metrics, and planning computational budget/latency.  
- Novel combination: unified Bayesian game for structured prediction with MCTS + progressive widening and asynchronous crowd integration.  
- Significant practical contribution for immediate high-accuracy deployment without labeled data and explicit cost–latency–accuracy trade-offs.  
- Pros: principled Bayesian formulation, appropriate planner, demonstrated cost/accuracy gains, support for structured prediction and asynchronous crowds, and open-source release.  
- Cons: simplistic crowd model, unclear ExpAcc/utility handling for F1, unreported planning overhead and sensitivity to utility weights, and missing stronger VOI/POMDP baselines.  
- Key questions: how is ExpAcc computed for sequence F1, what is the exact pT form and sensitivity to mis-specification, and what is the per-decision computational cost?  
- Essential fixes suggested: detail ExpAcc under F1 and Monte Carlo sample sensitivity, report planning overhead and MCTS rollout settings, and sensitivity analyses for utility weights.  
- Highly recommended: adopt a more realistic pR (e.g., per-item difficulty or reliability priors), add myopic VOI and POMDP-style baselines, and evaluate with live unfrozen crowds to measure variance.  
- Nice-to-have: explore issuing additional queries while waiting, and discuss extensions to richer structured models (e.g., neural CRFs) and their effect on crowd reliance.  
- Overall assessment: solid, timely contribution advancing decision-theoretic control for human-in-the-loop on-the-job learning, pending clarifications on utility computation, planning overhead, and more realistic crowd modeling/baselines.
