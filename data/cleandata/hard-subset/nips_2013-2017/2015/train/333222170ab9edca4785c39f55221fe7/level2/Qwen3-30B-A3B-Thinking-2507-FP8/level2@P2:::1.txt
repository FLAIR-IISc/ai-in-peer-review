

This paper proposes "on-the-job learning," a framework for deploying accurate AI systems starting with zero training examples by strategically using real-time crowdsourcing to resolve uncertainty during prediction. The authors model the problem as a stochastic game based on Bayesian decision theory, balancing accuracy, latency, and cost. They develop an approximation using Monte Carlo Tree Search with progressive widening to handle the intractable optimal policy computation. The approach is evaluated on three tasks (NER, sentiment classification, image classification), showing significant cost reductions (more than an order of magnitude for NER compared to full human annotation) and accuracy improvements over baselines.

The paper's main strength lies in its clear problem formulation and well-motivated framework. The on-the-job learning setting addresses a genuine practical challenge in deploying AI systems where initial data is unavailable. The Bayesian decision theory formulation provides a principled approach to balancing multiple objectives, and the use of MCTS with progressive widening is a reasonable approximation for the intractable problem. The experimental results are compelling, particularly on the NER task, where the system achieves both high accuracy (88.4% F1) and substantial cost reduction.

However, several critical issues undermine the paper's claims and clarity. First, the paper claims "more than an order of magnitude reduction in cost" compared to "full human annotation," but fails to clearly define what "full human annotation" means in the comparison. The Human n-query baseline with n=5 is mentioned as a comparison point, but the exact cost numbers aren't provided, making the claim difficult to verify. The paper also states they "boost performance relative to the expert provided labels" without clarifying what this baseline entails or how it was implemented.

The claimed 8% F1 improvement over "having a single human label the whole set" is similarly problematic. The paper doesn't specify whether this refers to the Human 1-query baseline or another approach, and doesn't provide absolute F1 scores for comparison. The 28% improvement over online learning is presented without absolute values, making it difficult to assess the practical significance of this claim.

The crowd response model (70% accuracy) is overly simplistic and not thoroughly justified. The paper doesn't explore how sensitive the results are to this assumption or how real-world crowd behavior might differ. This is particularly concerning since the entire approach relies on accurate modeling of crowd responses.

The paper acknowledges Legion:AR as related work but doesn't sufficiently differentiate their theoretical approach from this prior work. The paper's claim that "our system improves itself at test time by learning a stronger model" is somewhat misleading, as Legion:AR also operates in an on-the-job learning setting with similar capabilities.

The paper would benefit from clearer presentation of experimental results, including absolute values for all performance metrics, and more precise definitions of baselines. The discussion of limitations could be strengthened by addressing how their approach might scale to larger datasets or more complex prediction tasks.

Despite these issues, the paper addresses an important problem with a theoretically sound approach. The core idea of on-the-job learning is novel in its specific formulation, and the experimental results on NER are promising. The approach has clear potential for real-world applications where initial data collection is expensive or impractical.

In terms of originality, the paper effectively distinguishes itself from online learning, active learning, and active classification approaches. It correctly identifies the key differences: the need to make predictions from the start, the use of responses to update the model during prediction, and the focus on balancing multiple objectives (accuracy, latency, cost) in a principled way.

The significance of the work is substantial for practitioners who need to deploy AI systems rapidly without large labeled datasets. The demonstrated cost reductions (particularly for NER) are impressive and could have meaningful impact in real-world applications.

Overall, the paper presents a promising approach to an important problem with sound theoretical foundations. However, the presentation of results needs significant improvement to support the claims made. With clearer presentation of baseline comparisons, absolute performance metrics, and more detailed justification of the crowd response model, this work could make a strong contribution to the field.

Strengths: Well-motivated problem, sound Bayesian decision theory framework, compelling NER results, clear experimental setup.

Weaknesses: Unclear baseline comparisons, lack of absolute performance metrics, overly simplified crowd model, insufficient differentiation from Legion:AR, incomplete explanation of key claims.

The paper is promising but requires significant revisions to clarify its claims and provide stronger evidence for its results. I recommend rejection due to the unclear presentation of results that undermines the main claims, but with the understanding that with proper revisions addressing these issues, the paper could be a strong contribution to NIPS.
