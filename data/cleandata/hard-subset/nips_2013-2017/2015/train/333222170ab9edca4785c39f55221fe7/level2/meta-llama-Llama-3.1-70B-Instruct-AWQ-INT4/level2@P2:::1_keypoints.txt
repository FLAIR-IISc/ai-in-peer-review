- Introduces on-the-job learning to deploy high-accuracy systems starting with zero training examples.  
- Proposes a stochastic game formulation based on Bayesian decision theory to trade off latency, cost, and accuracy.  
- Uses Monte Carlo Tree Search approximation to compute the otherwise intractable optimal policy.  
- Evaluated on NER, sentiment classification, and image classification datasets.  

Strengths:
- Novel approach to a challenging on-the-job learning problem.  
- Thorough problem formulation and well-motivated Bayesian decision–theoretic solution.  
- Experiments show significant improvements in cost and accuracy over baselines.  

Weaknesses:
- Assumes crowd workers are reliable and accurate, which may not hold in practice.  
- Lacks a detailed analysis of computational complexity for large-scale applications.  
- Insufficient comparisons with existing active learning and online learning methods.  

Originality:
- Presents a distinct on-the-job learning framework separate from active/online learning.  
- Novel combination of Bayesian decision theory with Monte Carlo Tree Search.  

Significance:
- Addresses an important problem of deploying high-accuracy systems with limited training data.  
- Potentially applicable across NLP, computer vision, and recommender systems.  

Quality:
- Well-written and easy to follow with clear explanations and presentation.  
- Experimental results are thorough and clearly compared to baselines.  

Clarity:
- Well-organized structure with clear headings.  
- Concise explanation of problem formulation and solution.  

Arguments for acceptance:
- Novel, well-motivated approach with strong empirical gains.  
- Broad applicability to multiple domains.  

Arguments against acceptance:
- Reliance on idealized crowd-worker assumptions.  
- Missing computational complexity analysis.  
- Needs more comparisons to related active/online learning methods.  

Recommendation:
- Accept, contingent on addressing computational complexity and comparative evaluation concerns.
