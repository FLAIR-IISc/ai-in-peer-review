## Review of "On-the-Job Learning with Bayesian Decision Theory"

This paper proposes a novel framework, “on-the-job learning,” for deploying AI systems with zero initial training data. The core idea is to strategically query a crowd to resolve uncertainties during runtime, balancing latency, cost, and accuracy using Bayesian decision theory and Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS). The approach is compelling as it directly addresses a practical challenge: building new AI systems without the upfront cost of large labeled datasets.

The paper is well-motivated, clearly articulating the limitations of existing approaches (pure supervised learning vs. pure crowdsourcing). The formulation of the problem as a stochastic game is insightful and provides a principled way to trade off competing objectives. The use of MCTS to approximate the optimal policy is also a reasonable choice given the intractability of the exact solution. The experimental results on NER, sentiment classification, and image classification are promising, demonstrating significant cost reductions compared to full human annotation and improvements over online learning baselines – notably, the >10x cost reduction on NER is particularly impressive. The availability of an open-source implementation (LENSE) is a major strength, facilitating reproducibility and further research.

However, the paper has some weaknesses. While the experimental validation is good, the reliance on pre-existing labeled datasets for evaluation feels slightly contrived given the premise of "zero training examples." A more compelling demonstration would involve evaluating the system’s performance *during* the initial learning phase, showcasing its ability to bootstrap performance from scratch. The crowd modeling, while acknowledging noise, seems relatively simplistic. Exploring more sophisticated models of worker reliability and bias could improve performance. The discussion of related work is thorough but could benefit from a more critical analysis of how this work fundamentally differs from existing active learning approaches beyond simply operating "at test time.” Finally, the presentation could be streamlined; some sections feel dense and could benefit from more illustrative examples.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Addresses a relevant and practical problem.
*   Offers a novel and theoretically sound approach.
*   Demonstrates strong empirical results, particularly on NER.
*   Provides an open-source implementation.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   Evaluation relies on pre-existing labeled data, slightly undermining the “zero-shot” claim.
*   Crowd modeling is relatively simplistic.
*   Could benefit from a more in-depth comparison with and differentiation from active learning literature.

**Overall:**

Despite some minor weaknesses, this paper presents a valuable contribution to the field. The idea of on-the-job learning is promising, and the proposed framework is well-developed and empirically validated. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the points mentioned above, particularly emphasizing the dynamic learning process from scratch and providing a clearer positioning within the active learning landscape.
