Summary: The paper proposes “on-the-job learning,” a decision-theoretic framework that deploys a predictive model with zero initial training data by querying crowd workers in real time only where needed, then learning from these noisy, delayed labels to progressively reduce reliance on the crowd. The process is formalized as a stochastic game with actions issue/wait/return, optimizing a utility that trades off accuracy, latency, and monetary cost. Exact planning is intractable; the authors approximate the optimal policy using Monte Carlo Tree Search with progressive widening to handle continuous time. They instantiate the approach with a CRF for NER and evaluate on NER, sentiment classification, and image classification, showing substantial cost savings over full human annotation and accuracy improvements over single-vote human and online learning baselines. The work relates to active learning, active classification (e.g., Gao & Koller, NIPS), POMDP-based crowd control (TurKontrol), and real-time crowd systems (Legion:AR), and advances them by planning test-time queries jointly with online model improvement in a structured setting.

Quality: The formulation is sound and the MCTS approximation appropriate. The utility and environment models are clearly specified. However, the fixed response model (pR = 0.7 with uniform noise) and limited treatment of worker heterogeneity raise concerns about model misspecification. The evaluation mixes live retainer collection with simulations from a frozen pool of 5 responses/label, which controls variance but may bias results; more end-to-end live trials and sensitivity analyses would strengthen claims. Comparisons omit POMDP/crowd-planning baselines.

Clarity: The paper is well organized; the game, utility, and algorithm are clearly described, with equations and intuition. Some parameters (wM, wT), the response-time model, and ablation details (e.g., effect of progressive widening, parallel vs sequential querying) could be elaborated.

Originality: Novel integration of decision-theoretic planning with structured prediction and real-time crowdsourcing; the use of MCTS for continuous-time query planning in this setting appears new relative to prior NIPS work in active classification and cost-sensitive trees.

Significance: Results on three tasks indicate practical impact—reduced cost at comparable or improved accuracy—useful for rapid deployment when labeled data are scarce.

Pros for acceptance:
- Clear, principled formulation with an effective approximate planner.
- Demonstrated cost/accuracy gains across tasks; open-source release.
- Addresses a timely, practical deployment problem.

Cons against acceptance:
- Simplistic crowd model; no worker-specific reliability.
- Limited live evaluation and sensitivity to pR/pT/wM/wT.
- Missing comparisons to POMDP-based crowd control baselines.

Suggestions: Add experiments with learned worker reliabilities (e.g., Dawid–Skene), sensitivity analyses to misspecified pR/pT and utility weights, ablations of MCTS components, and head-to-head comparisons with TurKontrol-style policies. Overall: weak accept.
