This paper introduces "on-the-job learning," a framework for deploying a high-accuracy system from scratch with no initial training data. The core idea is to process a stream of inputs, and for each input, use real-time crowdsourcing to resolve uncertainty before making a final prediction. As the system observes more labeled data from the crowd, its underlying model improves, and its reliance on crowd queries decreases over time.

The authors formalize this problem as a stochastic game between the system and the crowd. They use Bayesian decision theory to define a utility function that balances three competing objectives: prediction accuracy, monetary cost of queries, and latency. Finding the optimal policy for this game is intractable. Therefore, the authors propose an approximation based on Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS), cleverly adapted with progressive widening to handle the continuous nature of time. This work builds on ideas from online learning, active learning, and crowdsourcing but carves out a distinct and practical niche. Unlike standard online/active learning, the goal here is to maintain high accuracy from the very first example, which is achieved by actively gathering information before committing to a prediction.

The proposed system, LENSE, is evaluated on three diverse tasks: named-entity recognition (NER), sentiment classification, and image classification. The empirical results are strong, showing that LENSE significantly reduces cost compared to a purely crowd-powered approach while achieving higher accuracy than both online learning baselines and a single human annotator.

**Quality:** The paper is of high technical quality. The formulation of the problem as a stochastic game is principled and elegant. The use of Bayesian decision theory provides a solid foundation for trading off accuracy, cost, and latency. The choice to approximate the policy using MCTS is well-justified and appropriate for this type of sequential decision-making problem.

The experimental methodology is sound. A key strength is the use of a "frozen pool" of pre-collected worker responses and latencies. This ensures that different methods are evaluated under identical crowd conditions, removing a significant source of variance and leading to more reliable comparisons. The inclusion of multiple baselines, including a reasonable heuristic-based one, helps to demonstrate the value of the more sophisticated BDT/MCTS approach. The commitment to reproducibility, with all code and data made available, is commendable and significantly increases the value of this contribution.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-written and well-organized. The introduction effectively motivates the problem and clearly situates the work within the existing literature. The core idea of "on-the-job learning" is communicated intuitively. Figure 2 is helpful in illustrating the decision-making process. The experimental results are presented clearly in tables and figures, supporting the main claims of the paper.

A minor point for improvement could be the description of the game tree in Section 3. The formal definition of the state and transitions is quite dense. A slightly more detailed walkthrough of an example state transition could further aid reader comprehension. Similarly, the definition of `ExpAcc` in Equation 2, while formally correct, could benefit from a brief sentence of intuition explaining that it represents the expected accuracy of the MAP prediction under the model's current posterior belief about the true labels.

**Originality:** The paper makes a novel and important contribution. While the components used (CRFs, MCTS, BDT) are established techniques, their application to and synthesis for the "on-the-job learning" problem is new. The authors formalize a problem setting that is highly practical but has received limited formal attention. This work successfully bridges the gap between traditional supervised learning (which requires a large upfront data collection effort) and pure crowdsourcing (which is expensive and does not result in a reusable model). It carves out a unique space distinct from active learning, which typically focuses on building a model for future use rather than ensuring immediate high performance on every instance.

**Significance:** The results presented are significant and address a real-world bottleneck in deploying machine learning systems. The ability to achieve high accuracy from day one while gradually reducing cost is a compelling proposition for many applications. The reported order-of-magnitude cost reduction on the NER task is particularly impressive and highlights the practical impact of this work. The framework is general and could be applied to a wide range of problems beyond the three demonstrated here. The public release of the LENSE system further enhances the paper's significance, as it provides a valuable tool for other researchers and practitioners to build upon. This work is likely to inspire further research in human-in-the-loop systems, interactive learning, and cost-sensitive ML.

**Strengths:**
*   A novel and principled formulation of the important "on-the-job learning" problem.
*   An elegant solution that combines Bayesian decision theory and Monte Carlo Tree Search to balance accuracy, cost, and latency.
*   Strong and convincing experimental results on three different tasks, demonstrating significant cost savings and accuracy improvements.
*   Excellent experimental design and a strong commitment to reproducibility via open-sourced code and data.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions:**
*   The model for the crowd is relatively simple (e.g., a fixed 70% accuracy). The framework could potentially be strengthened by incorporating more sophisticated worker models that adapt to individual worker accuracies or model task-specific confusion matrices.
*   The paper does not discuss the computational complexity or scalability of the MCTS planner. A brief discussion on how the approach would scale to problems with much larger action spaces (e.g., more positions to query) or more complex structured outputs would be beneficial.

In summary, this is an excellent paper that introduces a novel problem setting, proposes a technically sound and well-motivated solution, and validates it with strong empirical results. It is well-written, original, and has high potential for impact.
