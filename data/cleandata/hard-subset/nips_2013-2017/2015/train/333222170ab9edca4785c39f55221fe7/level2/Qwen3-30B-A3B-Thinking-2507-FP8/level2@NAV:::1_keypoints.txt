- Introduces "on-the-job learning": deploy high-accuracy systems from zero examples using real-time crowdsourcing to resolve uncertainty.  
- Models the problem as a stochastic game grounded in Bayesian decision theory that balances latency, cost, and accuracy.  
- Proposes an approximation using Monte Carlo Tree Search with progressive widening to handle intractable optimal policy computation.  
- Evaluated on three tasks (NER, sentiment, image classification) demonstrating cross-domain applicability.  
- Empirical gains: >10x cost reduction on NER vs full human annotation.  
- Empirical gains: +8% F1 over single human labeling.  
- Empirical gains: +28% F1 over online learning baseline.  
- Strengths: clear problem formulation, well-motivated Bayesian framework, and thorough experimental validation.  
- Strengths: stochastic game effectively captures trade-offs; MCTS-based approximation is well-justified.  
- Strengths: implementation details and open-source release (LENSE) improve reproducibility.  
- Strengths: comparisons to relevant baselines are comprehensive and results are statistically significant.  
- Weakness: novelty claim needs clearer differentiation from prior work (e.g., Legion:AR) and stronger argument for the decision-theoretic advance.  
- Weakness: limited analysis of why performance varies across tasks (e.g., role of dominant "none" class vs task complexity).  
- Weakness: lacks evaluation of computational overhead of the MCTS approximation, which may impact real-time use.  
- Overall: technically sound, practically relevant, recommend acceptance with minor revisions to deepen novelty and limitation analyses.
