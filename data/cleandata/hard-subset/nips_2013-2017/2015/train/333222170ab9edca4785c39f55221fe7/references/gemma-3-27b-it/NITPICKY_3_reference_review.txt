This paper proposes an on-the-job learning framework utilizing Bayesian decision theory to intelligently query a crowd for labels during prediction, aiming to reduce cost and maintain accuracy without requiring extensive pre-training. The core idea—balancing exploration (querying for uncertain labels) and exploitation (leveraging learned knowledge)—is appealing and relevant to scenarios with limited labeled data and readily available, though potentially noisy, human input. The authors frame the problem as a stochastic game and employ Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) with progressive widening to approximate the optimal querying policy. They demonstrate the framework on NER, sentiment classification, and image classification tasks, showing significant cost reductions and performance improvements over baseline methods.

However, despite the novelty and potential of the approach, several issues detract from the paper's overall quality, raising concerns about robustness, thoroughness, and clarity. The level of detail provided regarding several critical methodological aspects is insufficient, and minor inconsistencies undermine confidence in the results.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The formalization of on-the-job learning as a stochastic game and the application of Bayesian decision theory to dynamic query selection are novel contributions.
*   **Practical Relevance:** The problem addressed is highly relevant to real-world applications where labeled data is scarce and human input is readily available.
*   **Empirical Results:** The experimental results demonstrate the potential for significant cost reductions and performance gains, particularly on the NER task.
*   **Open-Source Implementation:** The availability of the LENSE implementation promotes reproducibility and further research.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Insufficient Detail in Model Specification:** The description of the CRF model (pθ(y | x)) is remarkably brief. Critical details regarding features used, regularization techniques, and hyperparameter tuning are absent. This lack of detail makes it difficult to assess the suitability of the model for each task and to replicate the results. The statement “learned based on all actual responses” is vague—does this imply online updates *during* the evaluation phase, potentially biasing the results?
*   **Crowdsourcing Methodology:** The crowdsourcing setup is described superficially. The criteria for selecting crowd workers, quality control measures beyond the initial tutorial, and handling of worker disagreements are not discussed. The reliance on a “frozen pool” of worker responses, while intended to control variance, is a questionable design choice. Real-world crowdsourcing environments exhibit dynamic worker populations and response patterns. Simulating these dynamics more accurately would improve the validity of the evaluation. The 70% accuracy claim for workers is a single number without confidence intervals or details about error distribution, making it hard to interpret.
*   **Utility Function Justification:** The utility function (Equation 1) combines accuracy, cost, and latency. While seemingly reasonable, the weighting parameters (wM and wT) are not justified. A sensitivity analysis demonstrating the impact of these parameters on the results is lacking. The definition of "ExpAcc" is awkwardly phrased.
*   **MCTS Implementation Details:** The description of the MCTS algorithm is high-level. The UCT constant 'c' is not specified, and the impact of different values on performance is not explored. The progressive widening strategy is mentioned but lacks a clear explanation of how the set of explored states is expanded.
*   **Baseline Comparison Issues:** The "Online Learning" baseline is inadequately described. It's stated that it has “perfect information about all the data seen so far”, which is unrealistic in a true online setting. Furthermore, it's not clear if the online learning baseline used the *same* feature set as the LENSE model. Without parity in feature representation, the comparison is not fair. The Threshold baseline seems arbitrarily constructed and lacks theoretical justification. Why is 0.3 and 0.98 used?
*   **Minor Inconsistencies & Clarity Issues:** Throughout the paper, there are minor inconsistencies and unclear phrasing. For example, the statement about excluding queries midway through waiting for a response (in Section 3) feels unnecessarily restrictive and doesn’t seem adequately motivated. The notation is sometimes inconsistent. Figure 2(a) labels are difficult to read.
*   **Overstated Claims:** Claims like “more than an order of magnitude reduction in cost” should be quantified precisely, rather than using vague language.

**Arguments for/against acceptance:**

**Against:** The lack of detailed methodological explanation, the questionable crowdsourcing setup, and the minor inconsistencies raise concerns about the reproducibility and robustness of the results. The comparisons to baselines are not entirely fair, weakening the claim of significant improvements. The sensitivity of the results to unspecified parameters (e.g., wM, wT, c) is a concern.

**For:** The core idea is novel and addresses an important problem. The empirical results, despite the aforementioned caveats, suggest that the framework has potential. The open-source implementation is a valuable contribution.



**Overall Assessment:**

While the paper presents a novel and potentially impactful approach to on-the-job learning, the aforementioned weaknesses are substantial. The insufficient detail and inconsistencies detract from the rigor and credibility of the work. I believe that the paper, as currently written, does not meet the standards for acceptance at NIPS. A major revision, addressing the concerns outlined above with significantly more detail, clearer explanations, and more rigorous evaluation, is required.

**Recommendation:** Reject.
