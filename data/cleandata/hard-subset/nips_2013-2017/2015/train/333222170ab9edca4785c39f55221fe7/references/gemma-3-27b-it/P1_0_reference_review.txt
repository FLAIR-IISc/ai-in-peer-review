## Peer Review: On-the-Job Learning with Bayesian Decision Theory

**Summary:**

This paper presents a compelling framework called “on-the-job learning” for tackling prediction tasks when labeled data is scarce and expensive to obtain. The core idea is to strategically query a crowd for labels only when the model is uncertain, gradually improving its self-sufficiency. The authors formulate this as a stochastic game solved using Bayesian decision theory and approximate Monte Carlo Tree Search. They demonstrate strong empirical results across three tasks (NER, Sentiment, Image Classification), significantly reducing costs compared to full annotation while maintaining or improving accuracy. The accompanying open-source implementation, LENSE, is a valuable contribution.

**Relation to Prior Work:**

The paper correctly positions itself within the landscape of online learning, active learning, and crowdsourcing. It clearly differentiates itself from existing online learning methods by actively seeking information *before* prediction, and from traditional active learning by adapting its policy during test time. The connection to Legion:AR is acknowledged, but the authors emphasize their more foundational exploration of the ML aspects of this on-the-job setting.  The referencing of relevant work in Bayesian decision theory, Monte Carlo Tree Search, and structured prediction is thorough.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty & Significance:** The on-the-job learning paradigm is a practical and potentially impactful approach for real-world applications where data acquisition is costly and continuous adaptation is crucial. The combination of Bayesian decision theory with MCTS to handle the complex decision-making process is a clever and novel approach.
* **Strong Empirical Results:** The experimental results are convincing.  The reported cost reductions and accuracy improvements over baselines (full annotation, single human label, online learning) are substantial, particularly for the NER task.  The trend of decreasing query costs over time (Figure 4) is highly encouraging.
* **Clear Formulation:** The formalization of the problem as a stochastic game provides a solid theoretical foundation and allows for principled decision-making. The utility function, incorporating accuracy, cost, and latency, captures the key tradeoffs in the setting.
* **Open-Source Release:** The availability of LENSE is a significant strength, promoting reproducibility and facilitating further research in this area.
* **Detailed Experimental Setup:**  The authors provide a detailed description of their crowdsourcing setup and efforts to control for worker quality, bolstering the reliability of their findings.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Complexity:** The framework is complex, and the mathematical formulation (particularly Section 3) might be challenging for some readers. While the explanations are generally good, simplifying some of the notation or providing more intuitive explanations could improve accessibility.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper doesn’t delve deeply into the sensitivity of the approach to hyperparameters (e.g., the coefficients in the utility function, the MCTS parameters).  Understanding how performance varies with these parameters would be valuable.
* **Limited Generalization Discussion:** While the results are strong on the chosen tasks, the discussion of the generalizability of the approach is somewhat limited.  Are there specific task characteristics that would make on-the-job learning particularly well-suited (or ill-suited)?  
* **Crowd Modeling Assumptions:** The simplification of the response model (p<sub>R</sub>) with a fixed 70% accuracy rate is reasonable, but it's a simplification. Exploring more sophisticated crowd modeling techniques could potentially improve performance.
* **Lack of Comparison to State-of-the-Art:** While comparisons to reasonable baselines are presented, a comparison to more recent and sophisticated active learning methods employing deep learning (e.g., those leveraging uncertainty estimation in neural networks) would strengthen the paper.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper addresses a practically important problem with a novel and well-motivated approach.
* The technical contributions (Bayesian game formulation, MCTS approximation) are significant.
* The empirical results are compelling and demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method.
* The open-source release facilitates further research.
* The paper is generally well-written and clearly presented.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The complexity of the framework may limit its accessibility.
* The limited discussion of hyperparameter sensitivity and generalizability.
* The lack of comparison to the latest active learning techniques.

**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a strong contribution to the field. The on-the-job learning framework is a valuable addition to the toolkit for building AI systems in data-scarce environments. While there are some minor weaknesses, the strengths of the paper – its novelty, theoretical soundness, empirical performance, and open-source release – outweigh them. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions addressing the weaknesses outlined above, specifically a more detailed discussion of hyperparameter sensitivity and generalizability, and a consideration of comparison to more recent active learning approaches.



**Confidence:** 8/10
