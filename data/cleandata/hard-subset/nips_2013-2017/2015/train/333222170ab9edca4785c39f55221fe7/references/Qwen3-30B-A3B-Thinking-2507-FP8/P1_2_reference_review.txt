

# Peer Review: "On-the-Job Learning with Bayesian Decision Theory"

## Summary and Relation to Previous Work

This paper introduces "on-the-job learning," a novel framework for building AI systems that initially rely on crowd-sourced labels to produce high-quality results from the start, while gradually becoming self-sufficient as the model improves. The authors model this as a stochastic game between the system and the crowd, using Bayesian decision theory to balance accuracy, latency, and cost. The key innovation lies in treating on-the-job learning as a sequential decision-making problem where the system strategically queries the crowd for partial labels, then uses the responses to update its model and make predictions. This differs from online learning (which doesn't actively seek information before prediction) and active classification (which uses a static policy rather than improving during test time).

The paper builds on prior work like Legion:AR [6], which implemented similar ideas but lacked a formal machine learning foundation. The authors clearly distinguish their work from active learning [8-10], online learning [7], and active classification [11, 22-24], making a strong case for the novelty of their approach.

## Strengths

1. **Strong Motivation and Practical Relevance**: The problem of building accurate AI systems without large labeled datasets is highly relevant to real-world applications (e.g., disaster relief, public opinion monitoring). The paper convincingly argues that both pure ML and pure crowdsourcing approaches are impractical for resource-constrained scenarios.

2. **Theoretical Foundation**: The stochastic game formulation combined with Bayesian decision theory provides a principled approach to balancing the key tradeoffs (accuracy, latency, cost). The utility function (Equation 1) effectively captures these competing objectives.

3. **Rigorous Experimental Evaluation**: The authors conduct thorough experiments on three distinct tasks (NER, sentiment, image classification) with multiple baselines. The results are impressive, showing more than an order of magnitude cost reduction on NER while improving accuracy over pure crowd approaches.

4. **Reproducibility**: The authors provide an open-source implementation (LENS) and detailed experimental setup, including a CodaLab repository for reproducibility.

5. **Clear Distinction from Related Work**: The paper effectively differentiates its approach from related work in active learning, online learning, and crowdsourcing, making a strong case for its novelty.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited Theoretical Guarantees**: While the game-theoretic formulation is elegant, the paper lacks theoretical guarantees about the performance of the Monte Carlo Tree Search approximation. The authors acknowledge this as "intractable" but don't provide any bounds on the approximation error or convergence properties.

2. **Crowd Model Simplifications**: The paper assumes a specific crowd response model (70% accuracy) and response time distributions. While they acknowledge this as a limitation, they don't explore how sensitive their results are to these assumptions or how to adapt the framework to different crowd characteristics. The claim that "humans we hired were roughly 70% accurate" is too vague without details about the specific tasks or worker qualifications.

3. **Task-Specific Performance Variability**: The paper notes that performance varies across tasks (best on NER, worse on Sentiment and Faces), but only speculates about the cause ("dominant class 'none' in NER"). A more thorough analysis of why the framework performs better on certain tasks would strengthen the paper.

4. **Scalability Concerns**: The paper doesn't address how the system would scale to very large input streams or extremely high-traffic applications. The computational complexity of the Monte Carlo Tree Search approximation could become prohibitive in practice.

5. **Limited Discussion of Limitations**: While the paper acknowledges some limitations, it could better address potential failure modes (e.g., what happens when crowd responses are consistently inaccurate, or when the model cannot learn from the crowd due to insufficient signal).

## Recommendation

This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of human-in-the-loop machine learning. The on-the-job learning framework addresses a real-world problem with a theoretically sound approach that yields impressive practical results. The experimental evaluation is comprehensive and convincingly demonstrates the advantages of the approach.

The paper meets all NIPS quality standards: it's technically sound with well-supported claims, clearly written, and makes a novel contribution that advances the state of the art. While the theoretical guarantees are limited, this is a reasonable tradeoff given the complexity of the problem, and the empirical results strongly support the approach.

The significance of this work is high - it provides a practical framework for building AI systems in resource-constrained environments, which is increasingly important as AI is deployed in real-world applications. The cost reductions (more than an order of magnitude on NER) while maintaining or improving accuracy are particularly compelling for practitioners.

**Pro Acceptance Arguments**:
- Novel framework addressing a practical problem with high real-world relevance
- Strong theoretical foundation with Bayesian decision theory
- Comprehensive experimental evaluation across multiple tasks
- Significant cost reduction (order of magnitude on NER) while improving accuracy
- Open-source implementation for reproducibility

**Con Acceptance Arguments**:
- Limited theoretical guarantees for the approximation algorithm
- Crowd model assumptions could be better justified
- Need for more analysis of task-specific performance differences

Overall, this paper makes a clear and significant contribution to the field. It advances the state of the art in human-in-the-loop machine learning by providing a principled framework for balancing accuracy, cost, and latency during system deployment. The results are impressive, and the approach is likely to be adopted by practitioners in the field.

I recommend acceptance to NIPS.
