- Proposes "on-the-job learning": a deployment-time framework that interleaves prediction with real-time crowd queries to maintain immediate accuracy while learning to reduce crowd dependence.
- Formalizes decision-making as a stochastic game between system and crowd atop a predictive model, optimizing a Bayesian utility trading off expected accuracy, monetary cost, and latency.
- Uses MCTS with UCT and progressive widening to approximate the intractable optimal policy under continuous response times.
- Implements LENSE and evaluates on NER, sentiment classification, and face attribute classification, claiming improved accuracy-cost tradeoffs versus human-only, machine-only, and a threshold baseline.
- Novel integration of planning (MCTS + progressive widening), structured prediction, and continuous-time crowd queries.
- Clear motivation: deploy accurate systems quickly without large upfront labeling or high steady-state crowd costs.
- Principled utility-based formulation with intuitive emergent behaviors (parallelization for latency, redundancy for noise).
- Algorithmic contribution is sound: MCTS + progressive widening makes planning feasible with continuous time and large branching.
- Supports token-level querying and uncertainty propagation for structured prediction, empirically effective on NER.
- Empirical results show substantial cost reductions for comparable accuracy and code/data are released.
- Response model is overly simplistic (fixed 0.7 worker accuracy, uniform confusion), ignoring worker heterogeneity and item difficulty.
- Evaluation protocol is weak: pre-collected 5-response pools sampled without replacement produce small, non-i.i.d. pools and rely largely on replayed simulations, reducing realism.
- Unclear how the CRF is updated from noisy crowd labels — whether responses are treated as gold or marginalized, with missing inference details.
- Utility/metric mismatch: planner optimizes expected MAP accuracy while reporting non-decomposable metrics like F1 (NER), with no analysis of the gap.
- Missing ablations/sensitivity analyses: no study of MCTS budget, UCT constant, progressive widening params, runtime overheads, or sensitivity to utility weights.
- Baselines are weak or incomplete: threshold baseline, no stronger VOI/POMDP-based vote-control methods, and an unusual online-learning baseline that may be unfair.
- Modeling/assumption limitations: no mid-wait query launches allowed, no queueing or batched response effects, and questionable response-time independence assumptions.
- Reporting lacks important experimental details: dataset stats, features, hyperparameters, planning budget, inference approximations, and significance tests.
- Paper is generally well-written but needs explicit derivations for ExpAcc, clear noisy-label learning description, and precise experimental setup including wall-clock decision latency.
- Contribution is a thoughtful synthesis rather than a pure theoretical advance but is novel and nontrivial in integration.
- Problem is significant and practical; strongest empirical gains are on NER but broader robustness is needed for wider impact.
- Suggested improvements: learn per-worker/noise models, clarify noisy-label training (EM/posterior regularization), provide runtime/MCTS budget and latency validations, add stronger baselines, and run sensitivity/ablation studies.
- Recommend replacing frozen 5-response simulation with larger-scale i.i.d. sampling or live experiments and report statistical significance.
- Arguments for acceptance: well-motivated problem, novel integration of methods, promising empirical tradeoffs, open-source release.
- Arguments against acceptance: simplistic response model and evaluation protocol, missing methodological/runtime details, weak baselines, and utility/metric mismatch.
- Reviewer recommendation: Weak accept, contingent on clarifying methodology and strengthening evaluation.
