This paper introduces a novel framework called on-the-job learning, designed to build accurate AI systems without extensive pre-training data or large crowdsourcing efforts. The core idea is to strategically query a crowd for labels only when the system is uncertain, iteratively improving its prediction model during deployment. This is formulated as a stochastic game using Bayesian decision theory, balancing latency, cost, and accuracy. The authors approximate the optimal policy with Monte Carlo Tree Search and progressive widening. They demonstrate the approach on NER, sentiment classification, and image classification tasks, achieving significant cost reductions and performance improvements over baseline methods.

The paper builds upon existing work in online learning [7], active learning [8, 9, 10, 11], and crowdsourcing [3, 4, 25, 26]. Unlike traditional online learning, the system actively seeks information *before* making a prediction.  While similar to active classification, the policy isn’t static but improves during testing. The work extends prior work in on-the-job learning [6] by providing a rigorous theoretical foundation using Bayesian decision theory and a scalable approximation algorithm. Related work in structured prediction [27, 28] acknowledges the importance of considering dependencies in outputs when querying.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The framing of on-the-job learning as a stochastic game with Bayesian decision theory is a novel and principled approach. The combination of MCTS and progressive widening to handle the continuous time aspect is clever.
*   **Technical Soundness:** The mathematical formulation and the proposed algorithm appear well-reasoned and address the key challenges of the problem. The use of established techniques like CRFs and MCTS lends credibility.
*   **Empirical Results:**  The results on three diverse tasks demonstrate the effectiveness of the approach, particularly in reducing costs compared to full annotation and improving upon existing baselines like online learning and single-label human annotation.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the core concepts and methodology. The figures (especially Figure 2) are helpful in illustrating the process.
*   **Reproducibility:** The authors provide code and data, which significantly enhances the reproducibility and potential impact of the work.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Assumptions:** The model relies on accurate estimates of query cost and response time. While they address worker quality, the sensitivity of the algorithm to inaccuracies in these estimates isn't fully explored.
*   **Complexity:**  The algorithm is complex, and the practical implementation details are not fully transparent. It’s difficult to assess the computational overhead beyond the reported running times.
*   **Limited Comparison:** The comparison with other active learning techniques (beyond the stated baselines) could be more comprehensive. A comparison with more sophisticated active learning policies, perhaps within the same on-the-job learning setting, would strengthen the evaluation.
*   **Crowdsourcing Setup:** The use of a "retainer" pool of workers could introduce biases not representative of open crowdsourcing platforms. While the authors acknowledge this, its impact isn't thoroughly analyzed.
*   **Utility Function:** The utility function (Equation 1) combines accuracy, cost, and time with fixed weights (wM and wT). The sensitivity of the results to these weights isn't extensively investigated. A more adaptive weighting scheme might be beneficial.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound framework for on-the-job learning.
*   The empirical results demonstrate significant improvements in cost and accuracy across multiple tasks.
*   The authors provide code and data, promoting reproducibility.
*   The problem addressed is practically relevant and has the potential to impact various applications.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The complexity of the algorithm and the limited discussion of computational overhead might hinder its adoption.
*   The sensitivity of the results to assumptions about query cost and response time is not thoroughly explored.
*   The comparisons with alternative active learning strategies could be more extensive.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that addresses a challenging and practically relevant problem with a novel and well-motivated approach. The technical soundness, empirical validation, and commitment to reproducibility are commendable. While the complexity and assumptions warrant consideration, the potential impact of the work outweighs these concerns. I recommend acceptance.
