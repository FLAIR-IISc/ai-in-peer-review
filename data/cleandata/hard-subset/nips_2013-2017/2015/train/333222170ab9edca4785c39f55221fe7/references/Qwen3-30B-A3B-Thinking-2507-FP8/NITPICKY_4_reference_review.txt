

# Review of "On-the-Job Learning with Bayesian Decision Theory"

This paper proposes "on-the-job learning," a framework for building AI systems that maintain high accuracy from the start by strategically querying crowds while learning from the responses. The authors model this as a stochastic game between the system and the crowd, using Bayesian decision theory to optimize the trade-off between accuracy, cost, and latency. They implement an approximation using Monte Carlo tree search with progressive widening, and evaluate on three tasks showing significant cost reductions while maintaining or improving accuracy.

**Strengths:** The paper addresses a meaningful problem in practical AI deployment where labeled data is scarce. The framework provides a principled approach to balancing crowd queries with model learning. The empirical results on NER (88.4% F1 with >10x cost reduction) are compelling, and the open-source implementation (LENSE) is a nice contribution. The connection to Bayesian decision theory and stochastic games is theoretically grounded.

**Weaknesses:** Despite the promising framework, I identify multiple substantial methodological flaws that undermine the technical validity of the paper:

1. **Critical error in utility definition:** Equation (1) defines utility as U(σ) = ExpAcc(p(y|x,q,s,r,t)) - (nQwM + tnowwT), where ExpAcc(p) = Ep(y)[Accuracy(argmax y' p(y'))]. This is fundamentally incorrect. Accuracy is a function of a specific prediction, not a distribution. The correct formulation should be Accuracy(argmax y p(y)), not an expectation over y. This error invalidates the entire Bayesian decision theory framework.

2. **Inconsistent model specification:** In equation (3), the authors define p(y,r,t|x,q,s) = pθ(y|x) * ∏pR(ri|yqi)pT(ti|si), but pθ(y|x) is the prediction model *before* crowd responses are incorporated. The model should be updated with crowd responses, yet the equation treats it as a fixed prior. This creates a logical inconsistency in their environment model.

3. **Confusing response model:** The text states "r′j* ∼ p(r′j* | x, r)" but r is the current response vector (which doesn't include the new response being sampled). The correct conditional should be p(r′j* | x, y), but y is unobserved. The authors don't explain how they handle this fundamental uncertainty.

4. **Implementation flaws:** The claim "We collected 5 worker responses and their delays on each label ahead of time" and "sample the worker responses and delays without replacement from this frozen pool" means they're not simulating true crowd randomness. This creates an artificial bias toward their specific workers and overestimates performance.

5. **Formatting and presentation issues:**
   - "the the same position" (Section 2, line 6)
   - "pR(r | yq) = 0.7 iff r = yq" should be "if r = yq" (typo)
   - "comparable quality result" (Section 5, Table 2) is misspelled as "comporable"
   - The paper references Figures 2a, 2b, and "Figure 2 (left)" but doesn't include these figures
   - Inconsistent state notation: σ = (tnow,q,s,r,t) vs. later q = (q1,...,qk)

6. **Experimental clarity issues:**
   - "28% F1 improvement over online learning" - is this absolute or relative? The paper doesn't specify.
   - "more than an order of magnitude reduction" on NER - without specifying the baseline cost, this is meaningless.
   - The "5-vote approach" is mentioned but its F1 score isn't provided for comparison.

**Technical concerns:** The paper claims to "improve the model during test time based on observed data" (Introduction), but the model pθ(y|x) is "learned based on all actual responses (not simulated ones) using AdaGrad" (Section 3). However, in the experiments, they simulate crowd responses from a fixed pool, meaning the model isn't actually trained on new data in the simulation. This creates a disconnect between the claimed capability and the experimental setup.

**Related work:** The paper doesn't adequately distinguish itself from active classification [22-24], which also queries at test time. The authors claim their method "improves itself at test time," but active classification algorithms can also adapt based on observed responses. The paper needs to more clearly articulate this distinction.

**Conclusion:** While the paper addresses an important practical problem and presents a theoretically motivated framework, the fundamental errors in the utility formulation, inconsistent model specification, and flawed experimental setup significantly undermine the technical contribution. The paper would require substantial revisions to address these issues before it could be considered for publication at NIPS. The methodological flaws are too severe to overlook, despite the promising empirical results.

**Recommendation:** Reject. The paper contains critical technical errors that invalidate the core theoretical framework, and the experimental results cannot be properly interpreted due to the flawed simulation setup.
