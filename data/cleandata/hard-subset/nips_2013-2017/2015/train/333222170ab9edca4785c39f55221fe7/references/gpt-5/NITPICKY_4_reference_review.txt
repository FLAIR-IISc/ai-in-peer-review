Summary
The paper proposes “on-the-job learning,” a decision-theoretic framework that, at test time, adaptively queries crowd workers for uncertain parts of the input while simultaneously updating a structured prediction model. The setting is formalized as a stochastic game between the system and the crowd, with utility trading off expected predictive accuracy, monetary cost per query, and latency. Exact planning is intractable; the authors approximate with Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) and progressive widening to handle continuous response times. The prediction model is a CRF; the environment model assumes a fixed worker accuracy (pR = 0.7) and a response-time model pT. Experiments on NER, sentiment, and image classification indicate reduced cost and improved quality vs. human-only baselines (majority vote) and an online-learning baseline. The open-source system LENSE is released.

Relation to prior work: The work sits at the intersection of active learning, test-time feature acquisition/active classification, crowdsourcing/POMDP control, and structured prediction. The formalization as a game and the use of MCTS for policy search echoes TurKontrol-like decision-theoretic control but is adapted to structured outputs and continuous time. The “Legion:AR” line is acknowledged as an implementation progenitor; here the authors aim to provide a principled Bayesian framework.

Strengths
- Clear and timely problem motivation: maintaining high accuracy from the start under scarce training data while amortizing supervision cost over time.
- Decision-theoretic modeling that explicitly trades off accuracy, latency, and monetary cost; allows emergent strategies (redundancy, parallelization vs. adaptivity).
- Structured outputs handled in a unified framework; inference naturally incorporates multiple responses as likelihood factors.
- Algorithmic innovation in combining MCTS with progressive widening to handle continuous-time responses.
- Practical system with real-time crowdsourcing infrastructure and open-source release; multiple tasks evaluated.

Weaknesses and detailed concerns
Technical/modeling
- Response/noise model: pR(r|yq)=0.7 if r=yq, uniform otherwise, fixed across tasks, items, classes, and workers. This is unrealistic and discards known heterogeneity (worker reliabilities, item difficulty, class confusion). Learning pR (e.g., Dawid–Skene) or at least per-task/calibrated reliabilities seems essential. The manuscript acknowledges measured “~70%” accuracy, but does not connect this to learned or validated pR.
- Response-time model pT(t|s) is never specified (parametric family, estimation, nonstationarity), yet it is central to the continuous-time planning and utility.
- Training the CRF: “learned based on all actual responses using AdaGrad” is underspecified. Are responses treated as gold, or are they incorporated via the pR likelihood (latent y)? If the former, the model is being trained on noisy labels without accounting for noise—contradicting the environment model.
- Utility definition and metric mismatch: U uses ExpAcc(p)=E_p[Accuracy(argmax p)], but experiments report F1 (NER). If Accuracy is token accuracy or sequence accuracy, this does not align with F1, a non-decomposable metric. How is utility computed in practice (exactly/approximately)? If F1 is the actual objective, the expectation of F1 over the posterior with a MAP decision is nontrivial; no details provided.
- The ExpAcc choice is unusual: for 0–1 sequence accuracy, ExpAcc reduces to the posterior mass on the MAP solution. This may not reflect expected per-token accuracy or F1, potentially leading to suboptimal query policies for the reported metrics.
- Planning restriction disallowing issuing queries while waiting can materially harm latency-accuracy trade-offs in real-time settings; no ablation quantifies this modeling simplification.

Algorithmic details and reproducibility
- MCTS specifics are missing: rollouts/simulations per instance, UCT constant c, computational budget/latency per decision, stopping criteria. Given real-time constraints, these details are crucial.
- Progressive widening rule (|C| ≤ max(1, sqrt N)) is asserted but not justified; sensitivity analysis is absent.
- Inference cost for each node (recomputing CRF marginals/posteriors under multiple partial responses) is nontrivial; no discussion of caching, factor updates, or overall per-instance runtime.
- Utility weights wM, wT are central but not specified; are they tuned per task? How chosen to produce the reported trade-offs?

Experimental design and baselines
- Many experiments appear to be simulation-based using a “frozen pool” of 5 responses per label sampled without replacement. This reduces variance but risks bias and dependence across methods; also, five responses per label is a very small empirical basis to emulate crowd variability and latency distributions.
- End-to-end cost accounting is incomplete: retainer costs ($1 to join) and idle time wages are not amortized in the reported cost figures; reporting only per-query costs understates true monetary cost of real-time crowdsourcing.
- Baselines are weak/incompletely specified:
  - “Online learning” assumes gold labels of past examples (a strong oracle) but is denied the ability to query at test time; however, more competitive baselines (e.g., offline active learning under equivalent budget, adaptive majority voting/TurKontrol-like policies, cost-sensitive test-time feature acquisition) are absent.
  - “Human n-query” majority vote is a strawman if utility includes latency/cost but not same decision policy. No dynamic redundancy/adaptive stopping baseline is included.
  - The “threshold baseline” is tuned with a hand-coded 0.3 uncertainty reduction per query and ignores time; unclear how its parameters are set/tuned.
- Statistical rigor: No confidence intervals, statistical tests, or multiple random seeds are reported. The claim of “order of magnitude cost reduction” (NER) depends on an n=5 vote baseline; unclear whether comparable accuracy vs. a tuned adaptive human baseline holds.
- Dataset/feature details are thin: NER dataset composition (tweets?), label set differs from standard CoNLL; sentiment uses UNIGRAMS vs. RNN features but architecture/training unspecified; faces rely on external embeddings with minimal description. This impedes reproducibility.
- Some claims are surprising and need evidence (e.g., “boosting performance relative to expert provided labels”).

Clarity and presentation
- Several typos and formatting issues throughout: “one-the-job,” “burgenoning,” “miscellaneos,” “comporable,” “Retreival,” dangling footnote markers “1”, “2,” missing punctuation, inconsistent capitalization, and incomplete references (years/venues missing). Figures/tables are referenced but not present in the submission text, hindering verification.
- Notation inconsistencies (e.g., omitting t in state in some places, mixing primes, undefined σ termination condition specifics).

Questions for authors
1. Specify pT(t|s): form, estimation procedure, and validation. Provide summary stats per task.
2. How exactly is the CRF trained with noisy responses? Are y latent with pR incorporated, or are responses treated as ground truth?
3. What is the “Accuracy” used inside ExpAcc in your implementation for NER—token accuracy, sequence accuracy, or F1 surrogate? How is ExpAcc computed efficiently?
4. Provide details on MCTS: rollout budget, UCT constant, wall-clock planning time per instance, and ablations on progressive widening.
5. How are wM and wT chosen? Report sensitivity of results to these weights.
6. Report end-to-end monetary costs including retainer overhead. How does performance change on live runs vs. simulation?

Pros
- Well-motivated problem and principled decision-theoretic framing.
- Handles structured outputs; general approach potentially impactful.
- Open-source release; practical real-time system elements.

Cons
- Key modeling assumptions (fixed pR, unspecified pT) and training details are insufficient/possibly inconsistent.
- Utility/metric mismatch and lack of statistical rigor.
- Baselines are limited; cost accounting incomplete.
- Many presentation/formatting issues.

Recommendation: Weak reject. The idea is promising and relevant, but the current paper has too many methodological ambiguities, unrealistic assumptions about the crowd, incomplete baselines, and presentation issues to meet NeurIPS standards. With clearer modeling/training details, stronger/equitable baselines, full cost accounting, and polished writing, this work could be much stronger.

Confidence: 0.75 (familiar with decision-theoretic control/crowdsourcing and structured prediction; limited by missing figures/tables).
