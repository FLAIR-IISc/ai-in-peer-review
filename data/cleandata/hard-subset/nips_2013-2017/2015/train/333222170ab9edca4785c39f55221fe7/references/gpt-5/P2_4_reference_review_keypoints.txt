- Problem & contribution: Formulates "on-the-job learning" for structured prediction that queries crowd workers at test time to trade off accuracy, latency, and monetary cost.
- Decision framework: Casts the decision process as a stochastic game with a Bayesian utility balancing expected prediction accuracy, time, and query cost.
- Modeling choices: Uses a CRF for prediction, assumes a fixed worker correctness pR=0.7, and models response times to handle asynchrony.
- Planning algorithm: Uses Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) with progressive widening to plan under uncertainty and continuous time.
- Empirical results: Evaluated on NER (tweets), sentiment, and image classification; LENSE reportedly outperforms pure-human and pure-machine baselines and reduces human cost over time.
- Reproducibility: Code and data are released.
- Relation to prior work: Integrates ideas from active/online learning, active test-time classification, crowdsourcing POMDPs, and structured prediction; novelty is the combined focus on asynchronous, test-time querying for structured outputs.
- Strength — formulation: Clear decision-theoretic utility that yields intuitive behavior (parallel queries, redundancy).
- Strength — planning: Appropriate use of MCTS and progressive widening for continuous-time decision-making.
- Strength — evaluation: Multi-task empirical evaluation and analysis of evolving costs.
- Main modeling concern: Strong simplifications—fixed pR for all workers/items, independent response noise, and exogenous response-time model—that may mis-specify real environments.
- Utility mismatch: Planning optimizes expected MAP accuracy whereas reported NER metric is token-level F1, potentially biasing decisions.
- Weak baselines: Comparisons use heuristic baselines; lacks stronger greedy VOI or POMDP-like baselines adapted to structured outputs.
- Evaluation realism: Uses pre-collected pools sampled without replacement, which may not reflect real asynchronous, dynamic worker pools and could bias latency/quality estimates.
- Limited ablations: No direct comparisons of MCTS vs greedy policies, no analysis of progressive widening impact, and little sensitivity to cost weights or pR misspecification.
- Missing computational analysis: Decision/planning latency and computational overhead of MCTS are not reported, important for real-time deployment.
- Clarity issues: Paper should specify how wM and wT are chosen, define accuracy (sequence vs token), give MCTS hyperparameters and rollout budget, and explain CRF training with noisy labels.
- Originality assessment: Novel combination of ideas (test-time querying for structured outputs + asynchronous crowdsourcing + MCTS), but crowd/worker modeling is simpler than state-of-the-art.
- Significance: Potentially impactful for deployable systems under latency/cost constraints, but claims are limited by simplified noise models, weak baselines, and evaluation design.
- Pros summary: Principled formulation, handles structured outputs and asynchrony, practical planning with MCTS, empirical gains, and open-source code.
- Cons summary: Simplistic worker/noise models, metric/utility mismatch, weak baselines, lack of sensitivity analyses, and simulated rather than live crowd evaluation.
- Key recommendations: Learn pR/pT and per-worker/item difficulty; align utility with evaluation metric (e.g., expected F1); add stronger baselines and ablations; report decision latency and scalability; validate with live crowds.
- Overall recommendation: Weak accept — promising and principled but needs stronger empirical validation and more realistic crowd modeling to be a solid NeurIPS contribution.
