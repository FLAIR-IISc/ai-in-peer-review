Summary
The paper proposes on-the-job learning: a deployed system that, for each incoming input, interleaves prediction with real-time, asynchronous crowd queries, using the crowd as a temporary crutch while the model improves online. The core technical contribution is a decision-theoretic formulation of test-time querying as a stochastic game over a structured predictor (CRF), with a utility that trades off accuracy, latency, and monetary cost. The environment includes a response model and a response-time model, enabling the system to decide when to issue redundant or parallel queries versus wait or return. Because exact planning is intractable, the paper uses Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) with UCT and progressive widening to handle continuous time. Experiments on NER, sentiment, and image classification show that the proposed system (LENSE) can achieve high accuracy immediately and reduce cost over time, outperforming simple human-only and machine-only baselines.

Relation to prior work
This sits at the intersection of online learning, active learning, active classification, and crowdsourcing. Unlike standard online/active learning, it seeks information before making each prediction to maintain high early accuracy. Relative to Legion:AR (real-time crowd labeling) and decision-theoretic control of crowdsourcing workflows (e.g., TurKontrol/POMDP approaches), this work contributes a structured-prediction, continuous-time, planning-based formulation that updates the model online during deployment. Compared to active classification/value-of-information methods, policies here adapt at test time with an improving model and never observe true labels.

Strengths
- Novel, bold problem framing: test-time, continuous-time decision-theoretic control of crowd queries for structured prediction, with online model improvement.
- Principled utility balancing accuracy, latency, and cost, yielding intuitive behavior (redundant labeling for robustness, parallelization for latency).
- Algorithmic contribution: MCTS with progressive widening to reason about asynchronous responses and continuous time.
- Demonstrated across three tasks; the system maintains high accuracy from the start and reduces cost over time. Open-source release and reproducibility materials.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Simplified crowd model: fixed accuracy pR(r|y)=0.7 with uniform errors and no worker heterogeneity or online calibration. This is a major modeling gap given the centrality of crowd quality.
- Evaluation methodology: pre-collecting 5 responses per label and replaying them reduces variance but may bias or limit realism; it does not reflect dynamic worker pools or nonstationarity.
- Baselines are limited. Missing comparisons to established decision-theoretic workflow control (e.g., POMDP-based TurKontrol), to myopic value-of-information baselines, adaptive majority-vote policies, or worker-modeling approaches (e.g., Dawid–Skene/GLAD). The threshold heuristic is a strawman.
- Utility/metric mismatch and clarity: ExpAcc is defined on accuracy of the MAP label; evaluation uses F1 for NER. It’s unclear how sequence-level utility aligns with token/F1 metrics. How are wM and wT chosen or tuned? Sensitivity is not reported.
- Learning with noisy partial labels is under-specified. The paper says CRF pθ is learned from “actual responses,” but does not detail how partial/noisy supervision is incorporated (e.g., marginalization, EM, posterior regularization, or majority aggregation). This matters for stability and bias.
- Planning constraints and overhead: the restriction on issuing queries only between waits simplifies search but may sacrifice optimality; compute/latency overhead of MCTS itself is not reported end-to-end, which is essential for real-time deployment claims.
- Some claims are unclear or surprising (e.g., “boosting performance relative to the expert provided labels”): please clarify experimental setup and what is considered “expert.”

Quality
The modeling and algorithmic ideas are sound at a high level; the MCTS/progressive widening approach to continuous-time planning is well-motivated. However, the simplifications in the crowd model, missing details on learning from noisy partial labels, and the limited baselines weaken the empirical support. More ablations (e.g., myopic VOI, removing progressive widening, different response models) and sensitivity analyses would strengthen the case.

Clarity
The paper is generally clear and well-organized. The game formulation is described precisely. Missing details include: training procedure under partial/noisy labels; computation of ExpAcc for structured outputs; selection/tuning of wM and wT; computational budget and wall-clock planning overhead. Clarifying these would make the work more reproducible and persuasive.

Originality
High. The continuous-time stochastic game formulation for test-time, structured, crowd-in-the-loop learning, coupled with MCTS planning and online model improvement, is a fresh and ambitious direction beyond prior active learning or crowdsourced workflow control.

Significance
Potentially high. If refined, the approach could influence how practitioners bootstrap systems that must be accurate from day one. The decision-theoretic framing is general and could catalyze follow-on work on better crowd models, worker selection, and structured VOI. Current empirical support is promising but would benefit from stronger baselines and more realistic crowd modeling.

Suggestions for improvement
- Learn and adapt pR and pT online; incorporate worker heterogeneity and uncertainty (e.g., Dawid–Skene-style modeling), and report sensitivity to miscalibration.
- Provide stronger baselines: POMDP-based workflow control, myopic VOI policy under the same model, adaptive redundancy, and worker modeling baselines.
- Clarify and evaluate the learning procedure under partial/noisy labels; consider EM/marginalized training for the CRF.
- Align utility with evaluation metrics (e.g., token/F1) or justify the choice; report sensitivity to wM, wT.
- Report end-to-end latency including planning overhead and analyze scalability to longer sequences/bigger label spaces.
- Ablate key components (progressive widening, parallel vs sequential querying constraint).
- Validate on a live, non-replayed crowd to demonstrate robustness to real-world variability.

Pro/con summary
Pros:
- Novel and principled formulation; bold and timely direction.
- Elegant integration of continuous-time planning with structured prediction.
- Demonstrated immediate high accuracy with decreasing cost; open-source.

Cons:
- Simplistic crowd model and replay-based evaluation.
- Missing strong baselines and key ablations.
- Incomplete clarity on training with noisy partial labels and utility-metric alignment.

Recommendation
Lean accept. Despite modeling and evaluation gaps, the conceptual contribution and the decision-theoretic continuous-time planning approach for on-the-job learning are innovative and likely to spur further research. With stronger baselines and clearer methodology, this could be a standout paper.
