### Summary

This paper introduces "on-the-job learning," a framework for building high-accuracy prediction systems from scratch by intelligently leveraging crowd workers at prediction time. The core idea is to treat the decision of when and what to query as a sequential decision-making problem. The authors formalize this as a stochastic game between the system and the crowd, using Bayesian decision theory to define a utility function that explicitly trades off prediction accuracy, monetary cost, and latency. As querying the optimal policy is intractable, the paper proposes a practical algorithm based on Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) with progressive widening to approximate the optimal action at each step. The work is positioned as a middle ground between traditional supervised learning (which requires large upfront datasets) and pure crowdsourcing (which can be expensive). It differs from standard online or active learning by seeking information *before* making a prediction on a new instance to ensure high accuracy from the outset. The proposed system, LENSE, is evaluated on NER, sentiment analysis, and image classification, demonstrating significant cost reductions compared to pure crowdsourcing and accuracy gains over a purely online learning approach.

### Strengths

1.  **Novel and Important Problem Formulation:** The "on-the-job learning" setting is a practical and highly relevant problem that has been underexplored. Many real-world applications require deploying a system immediately without a large, pre-existing training set. This paper provides a principled and elegant formalization of this problem, moving beyond the ad-hoc heuristics of prior work like Legion:AR [6]. Framing the problem as a stochastic game and using a decision-theoretic utility function is a strong and novel contribution.

2.  **Technically Sound Approach:** The choice of MCTS to solve the sequential decision problem is well-justified and appropriate, given its success in similar large-state-space planning problems. The integration of progressive widening to handle the continuous nature of crowd response times is a sophisticated and necessary adaptation. The overall model, which combines a predictive model (CRF), a crowd response model, and a latency model, is comprehensive and well-thought-out.

3.  **Thorough and Convincing Experiments:** The evaluation is a major strength. The authors test their system on three diverse tasks, demonstrating its general applicability. The choice of baselines is excellent, providing comparisons against pure-human (Human n-query), pure-machine (Online learning), and a strong heuristic (Threshold baseline) approaches. The results clearly show that LENSE achieves a superior trade-off, significantly reducing cost while maintaining high, and sometimes even super-human, accuracy. The learning curve analysis in Figure 4, showing cost reduction over time, effectively validates the core premise of the framework.

4.  **Clarity and Reproducibility:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow. The problem, model, and algorithm are described with clarity and appropriate detail. The inclusion of an open-source implementation and datasets on CodaLab is commendable and significantly increases the impact and credibility of the work.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Simplifying Assumptions in the Crowd Model:** The environment model assumes a fixed, known noise level for crowd workers (`pR(r | yq) = 0.7`). This is a significant simplification. In practice, crowd worker quality is heterogeneous and can vary over time and tasks. The system's performance might be sensitive to a mismatch between the assumed and actual worker accuracy. For instance, if the model assumes high-quality workers but gets low-quality ones, it might under-query and fail to meet its accuracy goals. A sensitivity analysis or at least a discussion of this limitation would strengthen the paper. While the related work mentions TurKontrol [26] for modeling worker reliability, incorporating such dynamic models into the MCTS planning phase presents non-trivial challenges that are not addressed.

2.  **Scalability of the Planning Algorithm:** The MCTS planner is run for each new input instance. The state space of the game grows with the length of the input sequence `n` and the number of potential queries. While the experiments are convincing for the chosen tasks (e.g., short tweets), the computational feasibility for longer sequences (e.g., document-level NER) is unclear. A discussion of the computational complexity per prediction and how it scales with `n` would be valuable. It's possible that for complex, long-form tasks, the planning time could exceed the benefit gained in latency.

3.  **Parameter Tuning for the Utility Function:** The utility function `U(σ)` depends on weights `wM` and `wT` that balance monetary and time costs against accuracy. These parameters essentially define the operational goals of the system. The paper does not discuss how these weights were set in the experiments or how sensitive the system's behavior is to their values. An ablation study showing the trade-off curves as these weights are varied would provide deeper insight and practical guidance for deploying such a system.

### Overall Assessment

This is an excellent paper that introduces a novel, important, and well-formalized problem setting. The proposed solution is technically sound, principled, and supported by a strong set of experiments. The work successfully bridges the gap between machine learning and human computation, offering a practical path for deploying accurate systems in data-scarce environments. The weaknesses identified are primarily limitations or avenues for future work rather than fundamental flaws in the proposed approach. The clarity of the writing and the commitment to reproducibility further elevate the quality of the submission. This paper is a significant contribution that is likely to inspire follow-up work in interactive and human-in-the-loop machine learning.

**Recommendation:** Accept.
