**Summary of the Paper**

This paper introduces a framework called "on-the-job learning," designed for scenarios where a predictive model must be deployed without a pre-existing labeled dataset. The core idea is to achieve high accuracy from the outset by dynamically querying a human crowd for labels on uncertain parts of an input *before* making a final prediction. The system learns from these crowd responses in an online fashion, gradually reducing its reliance on human input over time. The authors formalize this problem as a stochastic game between the system and the crowd. They employ Bayesian decision theory to create a utility function that balances prediction accuracy against monetary cost and latency. As solving for the optimal policy is intractable, they propose an approximation using Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) with progressive widening. The proposed system, LENSE, is evaluated on NER, sentiment analysis, and image classification tasks, where it is shown to reduce costs compared to a pure crowdsourcing approach and improve accuracy over a standard online learning baseline.

**Strengths**

1.  **Problem Formulation:** The paper addresses a practical and well-motivated problem: deploying accurate ML systems when large-scale upfront data collection is infeasible. The proposed "on-the-job" setting is a compelling middle ground between pure machine learning and pure crowdsourcing.
2.  **Principled Approach:** The use of Bayesian decision theory to frame the trade-off between accuracy, cost, and latency is methodologically sound and elegant. Modeling the problem as a stochastic game provides a formal and coherent foundation, which is preferable to a collection of ad-hoc heuristics.
3.  **Thorough Empirical Evaluation:** The authors evaluate their system on three distinct tasks, including structured prediction (NER), which demonstrates the generality of the framework. The inclusion of a strong "Online learning" baseline (which sees gold labels) and a simpler heuristic "Threshold baseline" provides valuable context for the results.
4.  **Reproducibility:** The public release of code and data is commendable and strengthens the contribution.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Complexity of the Proposed Solution:** The primary weakness is the significant complexity of the proposed MCTS-based solution. While framing the problem as a game is elegant, resorting to a computationally intensive planner like MCTS for each instance seems excessive. The paper does not provide a compelling justification for this complexity over simpler, more established methods. The "Threshold baseline" is a step in this direction, but it is a single, relatively simple heuristic. A more thorough comparison to standard active learning query strategies (e.g., uncertainty sampling, query-by-committee) adapted to this pre-prediction query setting would be necessary to demonstrate that the overhead of MCTS is justified.
2.  **Unrealistic Environment Model Assumptions:** The model of the crowd and its response times (Section 3) relies on strong simplifying assumptions. The response model `pR` assumes a fixed, uniform error rate (70% accuracy) for all workers and all items. This is known to be inaccurate; real-world crowd performance is highly variable across workers and dependent on task difficulty. Similarly, the latency model `pT` is not detailed. The performance of a decision-theoretic planner is critically dependent on the fidelity of its world model. The paper lacks an ablation study or sensitivity analysis to show how robust the system is to misspecifications in these environmental models. Without this, the reported gains might be an artifact of the simulation environment aligning perfectly with the planner's assumptions.
3.  **Lack of Discussion on Computational Overhead:** The paper completely omits any discussion of the computational cost of the decision-making process itself. MCTS requires running many simulations to estimate the value of actions. For a system intended to operate in real-time on a stream of inputs, the time required to run `MONTECARLOVALUE` before issuing queries could be a significant, or even dominant, component of latency. The authors do not report how many simulations are performed, what the wall-clock time for planning is, or how this scales with the length of the input sequence `n`. This is a critical practical detail that undermines the claims regarding latency management.
4.  **Limited Scope of Baselines:** While the online learning baseline is strong, it's not a direct competitor as it cannot query for information before prediction. The paper distinguishes itself from online active learning in the introduction but fails to include any such methods in the empirical comparison. It would be more convincing to compare against an established online active learning algorithm adapted to query for partial labels to reach a confidence threshold before predicting.

**Recommendation**

Weak Accept

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   The paper introduces and formalizes a novel and practically relevant learning setting.
*   The decision-theoretic framework is principled and well-conceived.
*   The empirical results, within the confines of the experimental setup, are strong and demonstrate the potential of the approach.
*   The work is a complete piece, including a system implementation and reproducible experiments.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   The complexity of the MCTS solution is not sufficiently justified against simpler, established heuristic approaches from the active learning literature.
*   The environment model relies on simplistic assumptions about crowd behavior, and the system's robustness to these assumptions is not tested.
*   The practical issue of the computational cost of the planner is not addressed, which is a major omission for a system concerned with latency.
*   The set of baselines could have been stronger by including adaptations of online active learning methods.

In summary, the paper presents a valuable contribution in its formulation of the "on-the-job learning" problem. The application of Bayesian decision theory and game-playing is novel in this context. However, my enthusiasm is tempered by the choice of a highly complex MCTS planner without adequate justification over simpler methods and by the use of an idealized crowd model. The lack of discussion on the computational feasibility of the planner is a significant concern. While the paper has clear flaws, the novelty of the problem setting and the principled nature of the framework warrant its acceptance. The work is likely to inspire follow-up research addressing these limitations.
