**Summary**

This paper introduces "on-the-job learning," a framework for building accurate prediction systems from scratch without a large, pre-existing training set. The core idea is to create a hybrid system that combines a machine learning model with the ability to query a crowd of human experts in real-time. For each new input instance, the system uses Bayesian decision theory to decide which parts of the input to query, whether to wait for responses, and when to make a final prediction, explicitly trading off accuracy, monetary cost, and latency. The authors model this decision-making process as a stochastic game between the system and the crowd. Since finding the optimal policy is intractable, they propose an approximation based on Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) with progressive widening. The proposed system, LENSE, is evaluated on three tasks (NER, sentiment analysis, image classification) and demonstrates significant cost reductions compared to a purely human-powered approach and substantial accuracy gains over a standard online learning baseline.

**Quality**

The paper is of high technical quality. The claims are well-supported by a solid theoretical framework and a thorough empirical evaluation.

*   **Technical Soundness:** The formulation of the problem as a stochastic game is both elegant and appropriate. The use of Bayesian decision theory provides a principled way to unify the competing objectives of accuracy, cost, and latency. The choice of MCTS with progressive widening as a solver is a state-of-the-art approach for this class of planning problems, particularly those involving continuous variables like time. The experimental design is strong, especially the use of a "frozen pool" of pre-collected worker responses, which is an excellent methodological choice to ensure fair and reproducible comparisons between different strategies.

*   **Strengths and Weaknesses:** The authors are generally careful in their evaluation. They include a strong set of baselines, including a non-trivial heuristic ("Threshold baseline"), which helps to justify the complexity of their MCTS-based approach. The analysis of how performance changes over time and with different feature sets (Fig. 3 & 4) adds depth to the results. However, there are some simplifying assumptions in the model that could be discussed more. The worker response model `pR(r | yq)` assumes a uniform, fixed accuracy of 70% for all workers and all items. In reality, worker quality varies significantly, and some items are inherently harder than others. A discussion on the sensitivity of the system to this assumption, or how one might extend the model to learn worker-specific accuracies online (as in [26]), would strengthen the paper. Furthermore, the computational cost of the decision-making process itself is not discussed. MCTS can be computationally intensive, and the time taken to run the simulations before issuing queries adds to the total latency. Quantifying this overhead is important for understanding the practical deployment limits of the system.

**Clarity**

The paper is very well-written and organized. The motivation is established clearly in the introduction, and the problem is formalized precisely in Section 2. The core model in Section 3, including the game tree and utility function, is explained effectively, with Figure 2 providing a helpful illustration. The description of the MCTS algorithm is concise but contains enough detail for an expert to understand the approach. The experimental setup and results are presented clearly. The reproducibility is a major strength, with code and data made publicly available on CodaLab.

One minor point for improvement would be to clarify the `ExpAcc` term in Equation 2. While standard in decision theory, it could be stated more explicitly as the expected accuracy where the expectation is taken over the posterior distribution of the true labels `p(y | x, evidence)`.

**Originality**

The paper makes several original contributions.

1.  **Problem Formulation:** While the idea of combining ML with crowdsourcing is not new, the "on-the-job learning" setting—which demands high accuracy on *every* instance from the very beginning—is a novel and important formalization. The paper does a good job of differentiating this setting from standard online active learning (which focuses on building a good model for the future) and active classification (which typically learns a static policy from a pre-existing dataset).
2.  **Methodology:** Modeling this problem as a stochastic game and applying MCTS is a novel approach in this context. While MCTS is a known technique, its application to dynamically manage crowd queries, considering asynchronous responses and continuous time, is a significant contribution. This work provides a formal machine learning foundation for systems like Legion:AR [6], as the authors claim.
3.  **Synthesis:** The paper successfully synthesizes ideas from online learning, active learning, decision theory, and game playing into a single, coherent framework for a practical problem.

**Significance**

The work is highly significant. It addresses a real-world bottleneck in deploying ML systems: the cold-start problem. The proposed framework offers a practical path to deploying effective systems immediately, which can then gracefully reduce their reliance on expensive human labor as they accumulate data. This has broad implications for applications requiring rapid deployment, such as disaster response, social media monitoring, or bespoke enterprise solutions where large initial datasets are unavailable.

Researchers are likely to build on these ideas by exploring more sophisticated models for the crowd and latency, developing more efficient planning algorithms, and applying the framework to a wider range of structured prediction tasks. Practitioners could directly adopt the open-source LENSE system to bootstrap new classification and extraction services. The work substantially advances the state of the art in human-in-the-loop machine learning.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Addresses a critical and practical problem (the cold-start problem) with a novel and well-motivated framework.
*   The technical approach is principled, combining Bayesian decision theory with modern planning algorithms (MCTS).
*   The empirical evaluation is thorough, convincing, and reproducible across three distinct tasks.
*   The paper is well-written, clearly positioning itself with respect to related work.
*   The contribution is significant and likely to inspire follow-up research and practical applications.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   The crowd model makes simplifying assumptions about worker accuracy, which might not hold in practice.
*   The computational overhead of the MCTS-based decision-making process is not evaluated, leaving an open question about its real-world latency.

**Recommendation:**
The arguments for acceptance far outweigh the weaknesses, which are primarily avenues for future work rather than fundamental flaws. This is a strong paper presenting a novel, well-executed, and significant piece of research. I strongly recommend acceptance.
