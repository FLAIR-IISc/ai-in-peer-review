## Review of "On-the-Job Learning with Bayesian Decision Theory"

**Overall Assessment:** This paper presents a compelling and well-motivated framework for on-the-job learning, tackling a significant problem in deploying AI systems with limited initial resources. The authors effectively formalize the problem as a stochastic game and leverage Bayesian decision theory to balance accuracy, cost, and latency. The empirical results are promising, demonstrating significant cost reductions and performance improvements over existing approaches. While the technical details are complex, the paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the core ideas. I believe this work represents a strong contribution to the field and recommend acceptance, albeit with minor revisions.

**1. Summary and Relation to Prior Work:**

The paper addresses the challenge of building accurate AI systems without large labeled datasets or extensive crowdsourcing upfront. They propose "on-the-job learning," where the system strategically queries a crowd for labels on uncertain parts of the input *during* prediction. This differs from traditional supervised learning and pure crowdsourcing. The key innovation lies in modeling this as a stochastic game and using Bayesian decision theory to optimize query strategies, balancing competing objectives like accuracy, latency, and cost. 

The authors correctly position their work within the broader landscape of online learning, active learning, and active classification. They clearly articulate how their approach differs from these related areas: online and active learning lack the ability to maintain high accuracy from the start, while active classification relies on a static policy learned offline. The connection to Legion:AR [6] is acknowledged, but the authors rightfully emphasize the theoretical foundations and general applicability of their framework, something Legion:AR lacked.  The paper also draws connections to structured prediction and crowdsourcing literature, providing a solid context for their work.

**2. Strengths:**

* **Novelty and Problem Formulation:** The formalization of on-the-job learning as a stochastic game is a significant contribution. It provides a principled framework for tackling the trade-offs inherent in this setting.
* **Technical Soundness:**  The use of Bayesian decision theory and Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) with progressive widening is well-justified and appears technically sound. The algorithm is a clever adaptation of existing techniques to address the challenges of continuous time and large state spaces.
* **Empirical Results:** The experiments on NER, sentiment classification, and image classification demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed approach. The reported cost reductions and accuracy improvements are substantial. The comparison to human n-query, online learning, and a threshold baseline provide convincing evidence of the benefits of LENSE.
* **Clarity and Reproducibility:** The paper is generally well-written and the core ideas are presented clearly.  The release of an open-source implementation (LENSE) and the availability of code and data on CodaLab are commendable and greatly enhance the reproducibility of the work.
* **Practical Relevance:** The scenario addressed (deploying AI systems with limited resources) is highly practical and relevant to many real-world applications.

**3. Weaknesses:**

* **Complexity:** The technical details of the stochastic game formulation and the MCTS implementation are complex and may be challenging for readers unfamiliar with these techniques to fully grasp. While the authors do a good job explaining the concepts, a more intuitive explanation of the algorithm's workings could be beneficial.
* **Crowd Modeling:** The response model (assuming 70% accuracy) seems somewhat simplistic. While justified by their experimental setup, exploring more sophisticated models of crowd worker reliability and potential biases could further improve the system’s performance.
* **Scalability:** The computational cost of MCTS could be a limitation for very large-scale problems. While progressive widening mitigates this to some extent, a discussion of the scalability of the algorithm and potential optimizations would be valuable.
* **Limited Analysis of Time-Cost Tradeoff:** While the paper mentions the ability to trade off latency and accuracy, a more detailed analysis of this tradeoff, perhaps through ablation studies or sensitivity analyses, would be insightful.



**4. Arguments For and Against Acceptance:**

**For Acceptance:**

* The paper addresses an important and practically relevant problem.
* The proposed framework is novel and technically sound.
* The empirical results are compelling and demonstrate significant improvements over existing approaches.
* The open-source release and reproducibility materials are a significant strength.

**Against Acceptance:**

* The technical complexity may be a barrier to entry for some readers.
* The crowd modeling is somewhat simplistic.
* Scalability could be a concern for large-scale applications.

**5. Final Recommendation:**

I recommend **acceptance** of this paper. The strengths significantly outweigh the weaknesses. The contribution is novel, the technical approach is sound, and the empirical results are promising. Addressing the minor weaknesses outlined above (e.g., providing a more intuitive explanation of the algorithm, discussing the scalability limitations) would further strengthen the paper.



**Specific suggestions for improvement:**

* **Clarify the intuition behind the MCTS algorithm.** Perhaps include a simplified example illustrating how the search tree is explored and how the UCT rule guides the search.
* **Discuss the potential impact of different crowd response models.** How sensitive is the system's performance to the assumed accuracy of crowd workers?
* **Include a discussion of the computational cost of MCTS and potential optimizations.** 
* **Expand the analysis of the time-cost tradeoff.** How does the system's behavior change as the cost of queries or the importance of latency increases?
* **Consider adding a visual representation of the state space/game tree to enhance understanding.**
