

# Review of "On-the-Job Learning with Bayesian Decision Theory"

This paper proposes "on-the-job learning," a framework for AI systems that begin with crowd-sourced input and gradually become self-sufficient by strategically querying the crowd about uncertain parts of input. The authors model the problem as a stochastic game using Bayesian decision theory, with an approximation algorithm based on Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) and progressive widening. They claim significant cost reductions (more than an order of magnitude for NER) and accuracy improvements over baselines.

The paper is well-organized and clearly motivated by a genuine practical problem: the high cost of both traditional supervised learning (requiring large labeled datasets) and pure crowdsourcing. The formalization of the problem as a stochastic game is a reasonable approach to model the tradeoffs between accuracy, cost, and latency. The connection to active learning and online learning is well-articulated, and the authors correctly identify the key differences between their approach and prior work.

**Strengths:**
- The problem is clearly articulated and relevant to real-world deployment challenges.
- The framework is theoretically grounded in Bayesian decision theory, which is a strong foundation.
- The experimental results on three different tasks (NER, sentiment, image classification) provide some evidence of the approach's potential.
- The paper makes a reasonable effort to compare against relevant baselines (human n-query, online learning, threshold baseline).

**Weaknesses:**
The paper falls short on several critical dimensions required for NIPS acceptance, particularly in terms of methodological rigor and thorough validation.

First, the claimed "more than an order of magnitude reduction in cost" lacks sufficient context. The paper compares to "full human annotation" but doesn't specify the exact baseline (e.g., 5-vote majority) or provide the absolute cost numbers. Without knowing the baseline cost, the claim of "more than an order of magnitude" is meaningless. For example, if the baseline cost was $10 per example, a "more than order of magnitude" reduction would mean costs below $1 per example, but the paper doesn't provide these numbers.

Second, the experimental validation is insufficiently thorough. The authors use a "frozen pool" of worker responses to control for worker quality, but this approach artificially limits the variability that would be present in real-world deployment. The paper doesn't address how the system would handle worker quality fluctuations over time, which is a critical issue for any crowd-sourcing approach. The 70% accuracy claim for crowd workers is based on limited experiments (as noted in the paper: "We found the humans we hired were roughly 70% accurate in our experiments"), but this is not rigorously validated across different tasks or worker pools.

Third, the comparison to baselines is problematic. The threshold baseline is a simple heuristic that doesn't incorporate Bayesian decision theory, making the comparison somewhat unfair. The paper claims "8% F1 improvement over having a single human label the whole set," but this comparison is misleading because it's not clear how the single human label would be implemented in practice. A more meaningful comparison would be against a system that uses a single human label per example with the same cost structure.

Fourth, the methodological foundation is questionable. The authors claim to use "Bayesian decision theory" but rely heavily on MCTS as an approximation, without providing any theoretical analysis of how close this approximation is to the optimal Bayesian solution. The paper states that "computing the optimal policy is intractable" but doesn't provide any analysis of the approximation error or how it affects the final results. This is a significant gap in the methodological rigor.

Fifth, the paper doesn't adequately address scalability. With the proposed approach, the computational complexity grows with the number of queries and the size of the input, but the paper provides no analysis of how this would scale to larger tasks or real-world applications with high-throughput requirements.

**Overall Assessment:**
While the problem addressed is important and the framework is theoretically grounded, the paper fails to provide sufficient evidence to support its claims. The experimental validation is limited, the baselines are not fairly compared, and the methodological foundation lacks the rigor required for a NIPS paper. The claimed improvements are not convincingly demonstrated, and the paper doesn't adequately address the practical challenges of deploying such a system.

The paper represents an incremental extension of existing active learning approaches rather than a significant methodological advance. The use of MCTS for this specific problem is not novel, and the paper doesn't provide sufficient evidence that this particular application of MCTS offers meaningful advantages over simpler heuristics.

**Recommendation:**
Reject. The paper makes an interesting proposal but fails to provide the rigorous validation and thorough experimental analysis required to support its claims. The methodological foundation is insufficiently justified, and the results are not presented with enough detail to assess their significance. The paper would benefit from a more comprehensive evaluation against a wider range of baselines, a clearer presentation of the cost metrics, and a more rigorous analysis of the approximation error in the MCTS approach.
