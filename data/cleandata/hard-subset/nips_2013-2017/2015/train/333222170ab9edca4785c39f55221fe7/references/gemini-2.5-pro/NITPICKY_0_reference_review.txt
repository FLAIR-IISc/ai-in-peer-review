**Summary**
This paper introduces "on-the-job learning," a framework for building accurate prediction systems from the start without a pre-existing labeled dataset. The system receives a stream of inputs and, for each input, can choose to query a crowd for labels on specific parts of the input before making a final prediction. The responses are used both for the immediate prediction and to update the underlying model (a CRF), allowing the system to become more self-sufficient over time. The core of the contribution is to model this sequential decision-making process as a stochastic game, using Bayesian decision theory to principledly trade off prediction accuracy, monetary cost, and latency. As the optimal policy is intractable to compute, the authors propose an approximate solution using Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) with progressive widening. The approach, implemented in a system called LENSE, is evaluated on NER, sentiment analysis, and image classification, showing significant cost reductions compared to a pure-crowdsourcing approach and accuracy improvements over a pure online learning approach.

**Strengths**
1.  **Novel and Well-Motivated Problem:** The "on-the-job learning" setting addresses a very practical and important problem that lies at the intersection of machine learning and crowdsourcing. It provides a path to deploying high-accuracy systems in scenarios where large upfront data collection is infeasible.
2.  **Principled Framework:** Framing the problem using Bayesian decision theory and stochastic games is elegant and provides a solid theoretical foundation for trading off the competing objectives of accuracy, cost, and latency.
3.  **Strong Empirical Results:** The experiments are conducted on three distinct tasks and demonstrate impressive performance. The reported order-of-magnitude cost reduction on the NER task is particularly significant and clearly shows the value of the proposed approach over strong baselines.
4.  **Reproducibility:** The authors provide open-source code and experimental data, which is commendable and crucial for verifying the work and building upon it.

**Weaknesses**
Despite the promising direction, the paper suffers from a considerable number of flaws in clarity, methodological detail, and experimental reporting that undermine the contribution.

1.  **Clarity and Presentation:** The manuscript is difficult to follow in several key places.
    *   The notation in the game tree formulation (Section 3) is dense and cumbersome. The distinction between `q`, `s`, `r`, `t` as historical logs and how they are updated with a new action `qk` could be made much clearer.
    *   The pseudocode in Algorithm 1 appears to contain a significant error. Line 5, the UCT update rule, is written as `{ V (σ′) N(σ′) + c √ logN(σ) N(σ′) }`. The first term should almost certainly be an average, i.e., `V(σ′)/N(σ′)`. This is a critical mistake in the description of the core algorithm.
    *   There are numerous minor typos and grammatical errors (e.g., "In the both timelines...", "miscellaneos", `argmax y′` instead of `argmax_{y'}`). The reference section is poorly formatted, with inconsistent styles and stray commas (e.g., `[1] In Computer Vision and Pattern Recognition (CVPR),, , ,`).

2.  **Oversimplified Model Assumptions:** The principled framework is built upon several simplifying assumptions that may not hold in practice, and these are not adequately justified or analyzed.
    *   The crowd response model `pR(r | yq)` assumes a single, fixed accuracy of 70% for all workers, all tasks, and all labels. This is a gross oversimplification. Worker quality varies dramatically, and some labels are inherently more difficult than others. The model does not account for this heterogeneity. The casual justification in footnote 2 ("We found the humans we hired were roughly 70% accurate") is insufficient for a rigorous scientific paper.
    *   The model for response time, `pT(ti | si)`, is mentioned but never defined. Its characteristics are crucial for a model that trades off latency, yet it is treated as a black box.
    *   Footnote 1 reveals a significant constraint: the system cannot issue new queries while waiting for a response. This simplifies the search space but is an artificial limitation for a system intended to be "real-time" and might lead to suboptimal, bursty querying behavior.

3.  **Insufficient Experimental Details and Analysis:** The experimental section lacks key details required for a full assessment and reproducibility.
    *   **Hyperparameters:** The utility function (Eq. 1) depends on weights `wM` and `wT`. The MCTS algorithm has an exploration constant `c`. The threshold baseline has its own parameters (0.98, 0.3). The paper provides no information on how these crucial hyperparameters were selected or tuned, nor any sensitivity analysis.
    *   **Computational Cost:** The paper focuses on monetary cost and latency from the crowd's perspective but completely ignores the computational cost of the decision-making process itself. MCTS can be very computationally intensive. What is the wall-clock time required for LENSE to choose an action? Is it truly "real-time"? This is a critical omission for a system intended for online, on-the-job deployment.
    *   **Task Simplification:** For the NER task, the authors removed the "miscellaneous" tag (Footnote 4), simplifying the task. While the reason is understandable, this makes direct F1 score comparisons to standard CoNLL-2003 results potentially misleading. This should be stated more prominently.
    *   **Simulation vs. Live System:** The experiments were run on a "frozen pool" of pre-collected worker responses. While this is a valid methodology for controlling variance, the paper's framing of a live, real-time system is somewhat at odds with the simulated nature of the evaluation. This distinction should be made clearer.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   The paper introduces a novel, well-motivated, and important problem formulation ("on-the-job learning").
*   The core idea of using Bayesian decision theory and game-playing algorithms is a principled and powerful approach.
*   The empirical results show a substantial improvement over baselines in practical terms (cost and accuracy).

**Arguments for Rejection:**
*   The manuscript contains significant clarity issues, including what appears to be a critical error in the core algorithm's description (Algorithm 1).
*   The underlying model of the environment (the crowd) is overly simplistic, casting doubt on how well the results would generalize to real-world settings with heterogeneous workers.
*   The experimental evaluation is missing crucial details regarding hyperparameter selection and, most importantly, the computational cost of the proposed method, which is vital for an online system.
*   The cumulative effect of numerous small flaws (typos, formatting, minor ambiguities) detracts from the paper's overall quality and suggests a lack of careful preparation.

**Recommendation: Reject**

While the paper addresses an interesting problem with a promising approach, the current manuscript is not ready for publication at NIPS. The combination of a significant error in the algorithm's description, overly simplistic assumptions in the core model, and a lack of critical experimental details (especially computational cost) prevents a confident assessment of the work's technical soundness and practical viability. The numerous minor errors throughout the text further detract from its quality. I would encourage the authors to thoroughly revise the paper to address these significant issues and resubmit to a future conference.
