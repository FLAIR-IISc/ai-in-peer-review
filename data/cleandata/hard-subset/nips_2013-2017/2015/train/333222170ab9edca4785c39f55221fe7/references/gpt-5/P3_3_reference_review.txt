Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes “on-the-job learning,” a decision-theoretic framework that interleaves prediction and querying of a real-time crowd to maintain high accuracy from the outset, while reducing dependence on the crowd as the model improves. The system formalizes test-time interaction as a stochastic game between the learner and the crowd. Given a CRF-based predictive model pθ(y|x), an environment model for worker responses pR and response times pT, and a utility that trades off expected accuracy, latency, and monetary cost, the agent decides when and what to query, when to wait, and when to return a prediction. Because exact planning is intractable, the paper uses Monte Carlo Tree Search with progressive widening to handle continuous time. Experiments on NER, sentiment, and image classification show improved cost–accuracy trade-offs relative to human-only and machine-only baselines. An open-source implementation (LENSE) is provided.

The work draws on: online learning (continual improvement), active learning (information-seeking), active classification (test-time acquisition), crowdsourcing/POMDP control (e.g., TurKontrol), and structured prediction (CRFs). Relative to Legion:AR and active classification, the novelty is a unified Bayesian decision-theoretic treatment that simultaneously reasons about label noise, structure, time, and money during test-time interaction, while updating the model over time.

Strengths
- Clear problem formulation that unifies accuracy, latency, and monetary cost in a principled utility.
- Stochastic-game view with an explicit environment model p(y,r,t|x,q,s) is elegant and extensible.
- Use of MCTS with progressive widening to handle asynchronous, continuous-time responses is appropriate and practically motivated.
- Structured prediction setting (CRF) and empirical work on real-time crowdsourcing make the contribution practical, not just conceptual.
- Demonstrated improvements in cost–accuracy trade-offs vs. majority-vote and a myopic threshold baseline; evidence that the system reduces crowd dependence over time when model capacity is higher.
- Open-source code and reproducibility artifacts.

Weaknesses and detailed comments

Quality
- Worker/noise modeling is simplistic and fixed: pR(r|yq)=0.7 for the correct label and uniform otherwise. This ignores per-worker heterogeneity, temporal drift, and instance difficulty. Established approaches (Dawid–Skene; Raykar et al.) and decision-theoretic systems like TurKontrol model worker reliabilities and would likely improve both inference and decision quality. At minimum, learn pR and its calibration from data; ideally, maintain per-worker parameters conditioned on joining the retainer.
- Utility/metric mismatch: the optimized utility uses ExpAcc of the MAP under a token-level accuracy notion, but the main NER evaluation is F1. The paper should either (a) align the utility with task metrics (e.g., expected F1 or sequence-level exact match) or (b) justify why the proxy is sufficient, and quantify any loss from the mismatch.
- Inference and learning details are under-specified. It is not clear how p(y|x,q,s,r,t) is computed efficiently for CRFs when incorporating soft/noisy responses: are worker reports treated as probabilistic factors derived from pR? How is calibration ensured so that the MCTS relies on well-calibrated posteriors? How many simulations per decision are used, and what is the wall-clock planning budget?
- Experimental design concerns:
  - The “online learning” baseline has access to gold labels for all past examples (an oracle advantage) but cannot query at test time; conversely, LENSE can query per test instance. A stronger comparison would include a myopic value-of-information baseline that reasons about time and budget using the same pR/pT model, and POMDP/planning baselines from prior work on crowdsourcing control (e.g., TurKontrol-style policies).
  - Pre-collecting 5 worker responses per label and sampling without replacement reduces variance but poorly reflects the true, heavy-tailed variability of the crowd. Five is likely too small; please report sensitivity to this choice and also provide results with truly live workers.
  - No statistical significance tests or confidence intervals are reported for the F1 and accuracy differences.
- The restriction that the agent cannot launch new queries while waiting for the next response is acknowledged as simplifying but may materially affect latency–cost tradeoffs; an ablation showing the impact of this restriction would be helpful.

Clarity
- Generally clear and well-organized, but several implementation details are missing:
  - How are wM and wT chosen/tuned? Are they task-specific? Is there any calibration procedure to match these to user preferences?
  - Exact specification of pT: empirical mixture, parametric distribution, conditioning on time-of-day or worker? Show fit diagnostics and cross-task portability.
  - MCTS parameters: exploration constant c, progressive widening schedule, rollout policy, termination criteria, and computational overhead per decision.
  - Training under noisy labels: is pθ trained with responses as hard labels, or is pR incorporated (e.g., via EM) to account for noise?
- Some references are incomplete; related work on adaptive submodularity and budgeted information acquisition is cited but could be discussed more concretely in relation to the proposed planner.

Originality
- The formulation as a stochastic game and the integration of time, cost, and accuracy for structured outputs at test time is a valuable synthesis. While elements exist in prior work (e.g., POMDP control for crowdsourcing, active classification, Legion:AR), the combination with structured models and MCTS for continuous-time crowds is novel. That said, the worker/noise modeling and training aspects are comparatively standard or underdeveloped.

Significance
- The problem is important and timely; many deployed systems face cold-start constraints and need reliable test-time supervision. The framework and open-source system may spur further work. However, the impact would be stronger with a more realistic worker model, stronger baselines, and clearer evidence that the decision-theoretic planning (vs. simple heuristics) drives the gains.

Suggestions for improvement
- Learn pR and pT from data; incorporate per-worker reliabilities and possibly contextual difficulty (instance-dependent noise).
- Align the utility with task metrics (e.g., expected F1) or provide a principled surrogate with analysis.
- Provide stronger baselines: greedy myopic EVPI, adaptive submodular policies, and POMDP-based crowd control methods; include ablations isolating contributions of time reasoning, sequential vs. parallel querying, and learning updates.
- Report calibration metrics for pθ, significance tests, and sensitivity to MCTS budget and to the number of precollected worker responses.
- Consider allowing mid-wait query launches or quantify the effect of the current restriction.

Arguments for acceptance
- Addresses a practically important setting with a principled, general framework.
- Novel integration of structured prediction, time/cost-aware decision-making, and real-time crowdsourcing.
- Empirical gains on multiple tasks; open-source release.

Arguments against acceptance
- Worker/noise modeling is unrealistic and fixed; no learning of pR or per-worker reliabilities.
- Utility–metric mismatch and under-specified inference/training details limit reproducibility of the core decision-theoretic claims.
- Baselines are not sufficiently strong; limited statistical analysis; precollection protocol may bias results.

Overall: promising and well-motivated, but I recommend significant revisions to the modeling of crowd noise, experimental baselines, and evaluation rigor to reach NeurIPS standards.
