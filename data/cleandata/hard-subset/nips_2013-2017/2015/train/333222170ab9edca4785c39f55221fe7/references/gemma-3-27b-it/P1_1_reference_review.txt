## Peer Review: On-the-Job Learning with Bayesian Decision Theory

**Summary:**

This paper presents a novel framework for “on-the-job learning,” addressing the challenge of building AI systems with limited labeled data and resources. The core idea is to strategically query a crowd for labels only when the model is uncertain, incrementally improving its performance while minimizing cost and latency. The authors model this as a stochastic game and leverage Bayesian decision theory, approximated via Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS), to optimize query strategies. They demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach (LENSE) on three tasks – NER, sentiment classification, and image classification – showcasing significant cost reductions and accuracy improvements compared to baselines.

**Relation to Previous Work:**

The authors accurately position their work within the broader context of online learning, active learning, and crowdsourcing. They distinguish their approach from traditional online learning by actively seeking information *before* prediction, unlike methods focusing solely on learning from observed data.  Compared to active learning, LENSE adopts a dynamic policy adapted during test time, rather than a static pre-defined one. The connection to Legion:AR [6] is noted, however the authors emphasize their contribution lies in providing a solid machine learning foundation to the on-the-job learning setting. The paper also appropriately references related work in structured prediction, decision-theoretic control, and MCTS.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty and Significance:** The on-the-job learning paradigm is a practical and compelling approach, particularly for real-world scenarios where obtaining large labeled datasets is expensive or impossible. The Bayesian decision-theoretic framework provides a principled way to balance competing objectives (accuracy, cost, latency).
* **Technical Soundness:** The formulation as a stochastic game and the application of MCTS with progressive widening are well-motivated and technically sound. The authors demonstrate a strong understanding of the underlying concepts.
* **Empirical Validation:** The experiments on three diverse tasks convincingly demonstrate the effectiveness of LENSE. The quantitative results (significant cost reduction and accuracy improvements) are compelling.  The ablation study highlighting the benefit of the Bayesian approach is also valuable.
* **Implementation & Reproducibility:** The publicly available code (LENSE) and experimental setup on CodaLab are a major strength, facilitating reproducibility and further research.
* **Clarity of Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The use of Figure 2 to illustrate the game tree and the workflow is particularly helpful.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Complexity:** The framework, while technically sound, is complex. The number of parameters and design choices within the MCTS implementation and utility function could make it difficult to tune and generalize to new tasks. A more detailed discussion of sensitivity to these parameters would be beneficial.
* **Crowd Modeling:** The response model (assuming 70% accuracy) feels somewhat simplistic. Real-world crowd workers exhibit more varied levels of expertise and biases. Investigating more sophisticated crowd modeling techniques could further improve performance.
* **Task Specificity:** While the experiments cover three tasks, they are all relatively well-defined prediction problems. It’s unclear how well the approach would generalize to more complex, open-ended tasks.
* **Limited Comparison to State-of-the-Art:** The comparisons to baselines (Online Learning, Threshold Baseline) are valuable, but a comparison to more recent and sophisticated active learning algorithms would strengthen the paper.



**Detailed Comments:**

* **Section 3 (Model):** The explanation of the game tree and transitions is thorough, but could benefit from a more intuitive explanation of how the progressive widening technique tackles the continuous time dimension.
* **Section 4 (Game Playing):** Algorithm 1 provides a high-level overview of the MCTS process. Providing more details about the UCT implementation (e.g., exploration constant) and the selection criteria for successor states would be beneficial.
* **Section 5 (Experiments):** It would be helpful to see a breakdown of the cost savings for each task individually.  The discussion regarding worker quality is good.
* **Related Work:** The related work section is comprehensive, but could benefit from mentioning recent advances in reinforcement learning for sequential decision-making, which shares similarities with the stochastic game formulation.



**Arguments for/against acceptance:**

**Pro Acceptance:**

* The paper addresses a significant practical problem (building AI systems with limited resources).
* The proposed framework is novel, technically sound, and supported by compelling experimental results.
* The code and experimental setup are publicly available, fostering reproducibility.
* The paper is well-written and clearly explains a complex idea.

**Con Acceptance:**

* The framework is complex and may be difficult to generalize.
* The crowd modeling is somewhat simplistic.
* The comparison to state-of-the-art active learning algorithms is limited.

**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of machine learning. The on-the-job learning framework is a novel and practical approach, and the authors have demonstrated its effectiveness through rigorous experimentation. While there are some weaknesses, they are outweighed by the strengths of the work. 

**Recommendation:**

**Accept** with minor revisions. I recommend the authors address the weaknesses outlined above by providing more details on the MCTS implementation, exploring more sophisticated crowd modeling techniques, and including a comparison to more recent active learning algorithms. Addressing these points will further strengthen the paper and solidify its contribution to the field.
