Summary and contributions
The paper introduces “on-the-job learning,” a decision-theoretic framework that interleaves test-time prediction with real-time crowd queries to maintain high accuracy from the start while gradually reducing human dependence as the model improves. Formally, the problem is cast as a stochastic game between the system and the crowd. The system issues token-level queries, waits for asynchronous responses, or returns a prediction; the crowd responds with noisy labels and stochastic delays. The utility trades off expected prediction accuracy against time and monetary cost. Because exact planning is intractable, the policy is approximated via Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) with UCT and progressive widening to handle continuous time.

The prediction model is a CRF pθ(y|x), updated online from crowdsourced responses. The environment model factorizes into pθ(y|x), a simple crowd response model pR with 70% correctness, and a response-time model pT. The policy combines querying, waiting, and returning actions to maximize expected utility. Experiments on NER (sequence labeling), sentiment classification, and face attribute classification demonstrate cost reductions vs. pure crowdsourcing and accuracy gains vs. pure ML baselines. The system (LENSE) and data are released.

Relation to prior work
The work is positioned against online learning (which does not allow pre-decision querying), active learning (which targets training labels, not per-instance test-time decisions), and active classification/test-time feature acquisition (which typically learns static policies offline and assumes fixed features). The most similar prior art is decision-theoretic control of crowdsourcing (e.g., TurKontrol/POMDP) and real-time crowd-powered systems (Legion:AR). Compared to those, this paper emphasizes structured prediction, continuous-time stochastic control with MCTS, and simultaneous test-time querying and model updating. It also relates to budgeted prediction and value-of-information at test time for ML systems.

Strengths
- Problem significance and timeliness: maintaining high accuracy from the first instance without pretraining is important for rapid deployment scenarios.
- Principled formulation: a clear Bayesian decision-theoretic objective trading off accuracy, time, and money; explicit modeling of asynchronous, noisy crowd responses.
- Technical novelty: the use of MCTS+progressive widening to plan over mixed discrete/continuous action-outcome spaces for this application is original and practically useful.
- Structured outputs: handling sequence labeling with CRFs and showing how crowd feedback propagates through structure is compelling and differentiates from purely i.i.d. tasks.
- Empirical demonstration: multiple tasks, real retainer-based crowdsourcing setup, and open-source code; consistent benefits in cost/accuracy.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Environment model realism: The crowd response model pR assumes a fixed 70% accuracy and uniform error, independent of token, context, or worker. This is a strong and likely unrealistic assumption; it can distort policy learning and value estimates, especially when heterogeneity, item difficulty, or confusion patterns matter. pR and pT appear not learned online from observed responses/delays.
- Utility/metric mismatch: The utility uses expected accuracy of the MAP sequence under 0-1 loss, while NER evaluation reports F1. Optimizing for expected 0-1 on sequences is not aligned with F1; the Bayes decision for F1 differs. The paper should justify this surrogate or approximate the expected task metric.
- Baseline adequacy/fairness:
  - The “online learning” baseline has access to gold labels for past instances but cannot query at test time; for early timesteps it is expectedly weak. Missing are stronger budgeted/test-time acquisition baselines (e.g., myopic value-of-information, learned static policies), and POMDP-based crowd control (TurKontrol) adapted to per-token labels.
  - Human baselines and cost accounting: It is unclear whether retainer costs are included in LENSE’s monetary cost, whereas per-query costs are. Excluding retainer overhead can make LENSE appear cheaper than it would be in practice.
- Algorithmic details and reproducibility gaps: Important MCTS hyperparameters (UCT constant c, rollout policy, search budget), the parameterization/fit of pT, and training details for pθ with noisy labels are underspecified. There is no analysis of computational overhead/latency of planning itself, which is critical for real-time use.
- Sensitivity and robustness: No sensitivity analysis to worker accuracy, delay distributions, or the trade-off weights (wM, wT). It is unclear how robust the learned policy is to model misspecification or changing crowd quality. The restriction that one cannot launch new queries while “waiting” simplifies search but may degrade latency-accuracy trade-offs; some discussion/ablation would help.
- Data and features: The “no initial data” premise is weakened by using richer features (e.g., pre-trained RNN or image embeddings). This is reasonable, but the paper should quantify how much of the gain stems from external features versus the decision-theoretic policy.
- Evaluation clarity: Dataset specifics for NER (tweets vs. CoNLL), label sets, and exact metrics are not fully clear from the text. Reporting F1 improvements against a “single human label” baseline needs careful definition for sequence tagging (token vs. entity-level).
- Theoretical guarantees: While MCTS is a reasonable heuristic, there are no guarantees on performance relative to an optimal policy; some empirical ablations or diagnostic analyses would strengthen the case.

Suggestions for improvement
- Learn pR and pT online from data, modeling worker/item heterogeneity (e.g., Dawid–Skene/GLAD for reliability; context-dependent confusion; hazard models for delays). Show the impact on policy and outcomes.
- Align the utility with the evaluation metric (token accuracy, entity F1, or expected Hamming/F1 approximations) and/or justify the surrogate with empirical evidence.
- Include stronger baselines: (i) myopic expected value-of-information greedy policies; (ii) budgeted test-time feature acquisition methods; (iii) TurKontrol-like POMDP policies; (iv) EM-aggregated crowd labels with adaptive stopping.
- Report computational costs (planning latency) and sensitivity to wM, wT, crowd accuracy, and delay distributions; include ablations on concurrency constraints (issue-while-waiting).
- Clarify cost accounting and include retainer costs; alternatively, report both “per-query only” and “all-in” costs.
- Provide more implementation detail (UCT c, rollout policy, search budget, pT fit) and how noisy labels are handled in CRF training (e.g., marginalization vs. hard labels).
- Discuss applicability to longer sequences and more complex structures; analyze scaling with n.

Pros for acceptance
- Addresses an important, practical problem; principled formulation and novel use of MCTS for real-time querying.
- Demonstrates consistent empirical benefits on multiple tasks; structured prediction focus is valuable.
- Open-source release and real-time crowd integration increase impact.

Cons for acceptance
- Simplifying assumptions on crowd models and metric mismatch; limited analysis of robustness/sensitivity.
- Missing stronger baselines and incomplete cost accounting; some clarity/reproducibility gaps.
- Limited theoretical or diagnostic evidence explaining when/why the policy excels or fails.

Recommendation
Borderline accept. The problem is important and the decision-theoretic/MCTS formulation with structured outputs is compelling and practically relevant. However, the current version would benefit from stronger empirical baselines, clearer cost/metric alignment, and more realistic/learned environment models. Addressing these issues would significantly strengthen the paper. Confidence: high.
