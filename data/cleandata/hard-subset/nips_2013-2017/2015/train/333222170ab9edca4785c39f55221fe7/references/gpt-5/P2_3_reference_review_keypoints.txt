- Proposes "on-the-job learning": a decision-theoretic stochastic-game framework that queries crowd workers at test time while updating a structured prediction model.  
- Uses a CRF for structured outputs, a simple worker response model, and a latency model; actions are query, wait, or return to maximize expected utility (accuracy–cost–latency).  
- Approximates intractable planning with Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) and progressive widening to handle continuous time.  
- Evaluated on NER, sentiment, and image classification; shows cost reductions and accuracy gains versus machine-only and human-only baselines; code (LENSE) released.  
- Distinct from active/online learning by seeking information before predicting each instance; closest to crowd-control/value-of-information methods but extended to structured, asynchronous, parallel settings.  
- Formulation and use of MCTS with UCT/progressive widening are principled and appropriate for the problem.  
- Major modeling limitation: response model assumes fixed 0.7 accuracy with uniform noise, ignoring per-worker reliability, correlations, and instance difficulty.  
- Latency model pT is referenced but not fully specified or learned; calibration is critical for parallel vs sequential query decisions.  
- Unclear how ExpAcc is computed for structured outputs given partial responses (exact vs approximate inference).  
- MCTS description omits rollout policy, termination/depth criteria, and per-decision computational cost; no theoretical guarantees provided.  
- Experimental setup uses a frozen pool of pre-collected worker responses and delays sampled without replacement, which may understate noise and misrepresent online dynamics.  
- Unclear whether retainer overhead costs are included; omission could make cost comparisons optimistic.  
- Baselines are weak or mismatched: online-learning baseline assumes gold labels post-prediction, threshold heuristic is simple, and there's no comparison to POMDP/TurKontrol or per-worker-reliability models.  
- Paper is generally readable and well-organized but misses important implementation/training details and has minor notational slips.  
- Originality: novel framing and combination of decision-theory + MCTS for real-time, structured crowd–ML, though constituent ideas are known.  
- Significance: practically useful for immediate high-quality predictions with demonstrated gains, but broader impact depends on improving realism and stronger baselines.  
- Strengths: principled problem formulation, elegant integration of decision theory and MCTS for asynchronous/parallel queries, demonstrated empirical gains, open-source release.  
- Weaknesses: simplistic response model, underspecified latency and ExpAcc computations, reliance on simulated frozen responses, missing stronger baselines and ablations, limited adaptivity (no mid-wait query launches/cancellations).  
- Key questions for authors: how are pT and pR estimated/updated and how sensitive is performance to misspecification; how is ExpAcc computed for structured y; per-instance MCTS compute time and scaling; do costs include retainer overhead; can you compare to POMDP/TurKontrol with learned worker reliabilities?  
- Recommendation: Weak accept — novel and useful but needs stronger empirical evaluation, more realistic modeling, and sensitivity analyses to substantiate claims.
