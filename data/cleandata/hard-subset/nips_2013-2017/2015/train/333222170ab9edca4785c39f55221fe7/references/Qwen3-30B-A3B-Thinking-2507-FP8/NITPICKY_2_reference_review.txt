

# Review of "On-the-Job Learning with Bayesian Decision Theory"

This paper proposes a framework called "on-the-job learning" that combines crowdsourcing with machine learning to build accurate prediction systems without requiring large labeled datasets upfront. The authors model the problem as a stochastic game between the system and the crowd, using Bayesian decision theory to balance accuracy, latency, and cost. They implement their approach (called LENSE) on three tasks (NER, sentiment, and image classification) and report significant cost reductions compared to full human annotation.

## Strengths
The paper addresses an important practical problem in deploying AI systems with limited labeled data. The framework is novel in its integration of Bayesian decision theory with crowdsourcing, and the empirical results are promising. The implementation details are largely thorough, and the open-source release is a positive contribution.

## Weaknesses and Minor Flaws

1. **Inconsistent Notation and Terminology**: The paper exhibits multiple notational inconsistencies that undermine technical precision. For example:
   - In Equation (1), the utility function uses "nQwM" without space, while earlier in the text it's written as "nQ wM".
   - The response model is defined as "pR(r | yq) = 0.7 iff r = yq" but should specify "pR(r | y_q) = 0.7 if r = y_q" with proper subscript notation.
   - The paper uses both "on-the-job" and "on-the-job" (with inconsistent hyphenation) throughout.

2. **Ambiguous Description of Query Behavior**: The problem formulation states "we make zero or more queries q1, q2, . . . on the crowd to obtain labels (potentially more than once) for any positions in x." This is confusing because "any positions" suggests different positions, but the example (Figure 2) shows multiple queries to the same position (q2=3 and q3=3). The paper should clarify whether queries can be repeated on the same position.

3. **Contradictory Payment Details**: The implementation section states "we paid $0.01 per query (for NER, since response times were much faster, we paid $0.005 per query)." This is contradictory - if response times were faster for NER, why would they pay more per query ($0.01) rather than less?

4. **Unjustified Model Parameters**: The paper claims "We found the humans we hired were roughly 70% accurate in our experiments" but doesn't provide evidence for this claim. The 0.7 accuracy rate is directly used in the response model (pR(r | yq) = 0.7), yet there's no explanation of how this was measured or if it was derived from empirical data.

5. **Typos and Grammar**: Multiple typos undermine the paper's professionalism, including "comporable" (should be "comparable") in the abstract and "the the same position" in the game tree description.

6. **Inconsistent Implementation Details**: The paper states they "sampled worker responses and delays without replacement from this frozen pool," but doesn't explain why this approach was chosen over random sampling. This could bias results if the frozen pool wasn't representative.

7. **Misattribution in Related Work**: The paper cites "Legion:AR [6]" as a notable exception to active classification but fails to explain how it differs from their work beyond stating it "operates in on-the-job learning setting." A more detailed comparison would strengthen the contribution.

8. **Lack of Theoretical Justification**: The paper claims to use Bayesian decision theory but provides no justification for their specific utility function (Equation 1). Why are the weights wM and wT chosen as they are? The paper should explain how these parameters were set or how they could be calibrated.

9. **Ambiguous Performance Claims**: The paper states "We also achieve a 8% F1 improvement over having a single human label the whole set" but doesn't specify the baseline F1 value, making the improvement hard to interpret.

10. **Algorithmic Implementation Issues**: In Algorithm 1, line 5 uses "σ′ ← arg maxσ′" which is confusing - the right-hand side should use a different symbol (e.g., σ*). The notation "V (σ′) N(σ′)" should be "V(σ')/N(σ')" for clarity.

## Conclusion

While the paper presents a promising framework with practical applications, the technical execution is marred by numerous minor but significant flaws in notation, description, and implementation details. These inconsistencies collectively undermine the paper's technical rigor and make it difficult to assess the true novelty and contribution of the work. The paper would benefit from careful proofreading to address these issues and provide more precise technical descriptions.

The paper's significance is diminished by these technical inconsistencies, and the experimental claims are less convincing without proper justification for key parameters and clearer reporting. While the approach has potential value, the current presentation fails to meet NIPS' standards for technical precision and clarity.

I recommend rejection due to the substantial number of minor but critical technical flaws that collectively weaken the paper's contribution.
