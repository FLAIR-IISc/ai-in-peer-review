This paper proposes a novel framework, On-the-Job Learning (OTJ), for building AI systems without extensive pre-training data. The core idea is to strategically query a crowd for labels only when the system is uncertain, balancing accuracy, latency, and cost using Bayesian decision theory and a stochastic game formulation. The system iteratively improves its prediction model as it receives responses, becoming more self-sufficient over time. This builds on prior work in active learning and crowdsourcing, but distinguishes itself by operating directly during deployment (hence, "on-the-job") and by formalizing the trade-offs using a game-theoretic approach. The paper provides a solid experimental evaluation on NER, sentiment classification, and image classification, demonstrating significant cost reductions compared to full human annotation and improvements over online learning and a simple thresholding baseline. An open-source implementation is also provided.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty and Significance:** The OTJ learning framework presents a compelling alternative to traditional supervised learning and pure crowdsourcing, particularly in scenarios with limited resources. The problem setting is well-motivated and addresses a practical need.
*   **Theoretical Foundation:**  The formulation of OTJ learning as a stochastic game and the application of Bayesian decision theory provide a principled way to balance competing objectives. The use of Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) and progressive widening is a clever approach to address the computational intractability.
*   **Strong Experimental Results:** The paper demonstrates substantial cost savings and accuracy improvements on three diverse tasks. The comparison to strong baselines (full human annotation, online learning, thresholding) strengthens the claims. The inclusion of cost as a metric alongside accuracy is a significant advantage.
*   **Practical Implementation & Reproducibility:** The open-source implementation (LENSE) and the detailed experimental setup (including a frozen pool of worker responses) promote reproducibility and encourage further research.
*   **Clear Writing:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the core concepts, the mathematical formulation, and the experimental setup.  Figure 2 is particularly helpful in illustrating the system's operation.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Complexity:** The mathematical formulation, while rigorous, is dense and could be more accessible. Simplifying the notation or providing more intuitive explanations of key equations would be beneficial.
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The performance of MCTS and progressive widening likely depends on careful tuning of hyperparameters. The paper doesn't provide extensive discussion of hyperparameter selection or sensitivity analysis.
*   **Crowdsourcing Assumptions:** The experiments rely on a retainer model with pre-qualified workers. This setup may not be representative of more open crowdsourcing platforms (e.g., Amazon Mechanical Turk without retainers), where worker quality can be more variable. The response time model also appears to be learned from a limited set of data, which might not generalize to different tasks or settings.
*   **Limited Scalability Discussion:** The computational cost of MCTS is a potential concern for very large state spaces or complex prediction tasks. The paper could benefit from a discussion of scalability limitations and potential mitigation strategies.
*   **Comparison to Legion:AR:** The paper mentions Legion:AR [6] but the comparison feels cursory. A more detailed discussion of the differences and similarities between the two systems, especially in terms of their theoretical foundations and practical performance, would be valuable.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper addresses a practically relevant problem with a novel and theoretically sound approach.
*   The experimental results are compelling and demonstrate significant improvements over strong baselines.
*   The open-source implementation and detailed experimental setup promote reproducibility.
*   The paper is well-written and clearly explains the core concepts.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

*   The mathematical complexity might be a barrier to understanding for some readers.
*   The reliance on a specific crowdsourcing setup and the limited discussion of scalability could limit the generalizability of the results.
*   The comparison to related work, particularly Legion:AR, could be more thorough.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a significant contribution to the field of active learning and crowdsourcing. The OTJ learning framework is a clever and well-motivated approach to building AI systems in resource-constrained settings. The strong experimental results and the open-source implementation are major strengths. While the mathematical complexity and potential scalability limitations are concerns, they do not outweigh the overall value of the work.

**Recommendation:** **Accept**

**Suggestions for Improvement:**

*   Simplify the mathematical notation and provide more intuitive explanations of key equations.
*   Conduct a more thorough hyperparameter sensitivity analysis.
*   Discuss the potential impact of worker quality variation on the results and consider evaluating the system on a more open crowdsourcing platform.
*   Address the scalability limitations of MCTS and explore potential mitigation strategies.
*   Provide a more detailed comparison to Legion:AR, highlighting the key differences and similarities.
*   Consider adding a discussion of potential applications beyond the three tasks considered in the experiments.
