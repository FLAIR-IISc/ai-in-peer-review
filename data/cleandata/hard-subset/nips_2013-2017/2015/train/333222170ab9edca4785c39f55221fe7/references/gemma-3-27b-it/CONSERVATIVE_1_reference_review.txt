This paper proposes an “on-the-job learning” framework for structured prediction, aiming to reduce the cost of both fully supervised learning and pure crowdsourcing. The core idea is to strategically query a crowd for labels when the model is uncertain, iteratively improving predictions while minimizing cost and latency. The approach casts this as a stochastic game solved approximately using Monte Carlo Tree Search. While the motivation is reasonable—addressing the resource constraints for deploying new AI systems—the paper suffers from a lack of compelling evidence and overstates the novelty of its contributions.

The primary weakness lies in the incremental nature of the work and the insufficient comparison to well-established baselines beyond simple online learning. The authors position their method against active learning, but primarily focus on differentiating it from *static policy* active learning. However, a substantial body of work exists on *online* active learning strategies, which directly address the dynamic, sequential nature of their proposed approach. A thorough comparison against these methods, particularly those employing bandit-based exploration strategies, is missing. The claim of outperforming a single human labeler by 8% F1 is interesting, but a direct comparison to an ensemble of human labelers (e.g., 3-5 voters) would be far more informative, given the cost of crowdsourcing is a central concern. Moreover, the reported gains on NER are promising but require careful consideration of the task characteristics; the authors acknowledge the dominance of a “none” class, which likely simplifies the learning problem and influences the performance gains.

The modeling as a stochastic game, while potentially providing a principled framework, introduces significant complexity. The reliance on Monte Carlo Tree Search for approximation raises concerns about scalability and computational cost, aspects not adequately addressed in the experimental evaluation. The progressive widening technique is mentioned but lacks detailed explanation of its practical impact. The utility function, while incorporating cost, latency, and accuracy, appears somewhat arbitrary and its weighting parameters are not thoroughly justified. 

The experimental setup, while utilizing Amazon Mechanical Turk, includes a rather ad-hoc approach to controlling worker quality – freezing a pool of responses. While this attempts to mitigate variance, it doesn’t necessarily address inherent biases or inconsistencies within the crowd. The claim of a more than order-of-magnitude cost reduction in NER needs stronger justification. What is the baseline cost *with* the chosen crowd sourcing strategy *without* the active querying?

The related work section is adequate but could be more critical. While acknowledging online learning and active learning, it downplays the connections to existing online active learning algorithms. The mention of Legion:AR is brief and doesn’t fully capture the similarities and differences, especially concerning the learning framework. 

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Addresses a practical problem – deploying AI systems with limited resources.
*   Presents a principled framework (stochastic game) for on-the-job learning.
*   Demonstrates improvements over a simple online learning baseline.
*   Code release promotes reproducibility.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Incremental novelty – builds on existing ideas in active/online learning.
*   Insufficient comparison to strong, relevant baselines (e.g., online active learning).
*   Complexity of the proposed solution (MCTS, progressive widening) not fully justified by the reported gains.
*   Experimental evaluation lacks depth, particularly regarding scalability and cost analysis.
*   Claims of cost reduction require more precise baseline comparison.



**Overall:** I lean towards rejection. While the paper addresses a relevant problem and presents a reasonable approach, the lack of compelling evidence, insufficient comparison to existing work, and the complexity of the proposed solution outweigh its strengths. The paper feels like an early-stage exploration of an idea rather than a significant contribution to the field. A major revision addressing the shortcomings mentioned above – particularly a thorough comparison to state-of-the-art online active learning methods and a more rigorous evaluation of the computational cost – would be necessary for reconsideration.
