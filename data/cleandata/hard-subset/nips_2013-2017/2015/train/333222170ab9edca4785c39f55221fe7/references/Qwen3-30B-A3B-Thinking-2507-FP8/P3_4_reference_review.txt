

# Peer Review: "On-the-Job Learning with Bayesian Decision Theory"

## Summary and Context

This paper introduces "on-the-job learning," a novel framework that combines crowdsourcing with online learning to maintain high accuracy from the start while progressively reducing costs. The authors model the problem as a stochastic game between the system and the crowd, using Bayesian decision theory to optimize the trade-off between accuracy, latency, and cost. They propose an MCTS-based algorithm with progressive widening to approximate the optimal policy and evaluate on three tasks (NER, sentiment, image classification), demonstrating significant cost reductions (over an order of magnitude for NER) while improving accuracy over baselines.

The paper makes a clear contribution to the intersection of active learning, online learning, and crowdsourcing, building on but distinct from prior work in active classification [11, 22-24] and online active learning [8-10]. It addresses a practical problem in deploying AI systems with limited resources, which is increasingly relevant as AI moves toward real-world applications.

## Strengths

1. **Problem Formulation and Motivation**: The paper clearly articulates the limitations of existing approaches (pure supervised learning requiring large datasets, pure crowdsourcing being costly) and presents a compelling motivation for on-the-job learning. The distinction from related work (especially active classification and online learning) is well-explained.

2. **Technical Contribution**: The stochastic game formulation with Bayesian decision theory provides a principled framework for optimizing the trade-offs between accuracy, cost, and latency. The utility function (Equation 1) is well-justified and captures the key dimensions of the problem.

3. **Experimental Results**: The evaluation is thorough across three diverse tasks, showing consistent improvements over multiple baselines. The cost reductions (over 10x for NER) while improving accuracy are compelling. The analysis of how query costs decrease over time (Figure 4) effectively demonstrates the core benefit of the approach.

4. **Implementation and Reproducibility**: The open-source implementation (LENSE) and detailed experimental setup (including worker quality control) significantly enhance the paper's credibility and reproducibility.

## Weaknesses

1. **Scalability Concerns**: While the MCTS approach with progressive widening is clever, the paper doesn't adequately address the scalability of this method for larger problems. For example, with longer sequences in NER or higher-dimensional image classification, the state space could become prohibitively large. The paper should discuss computational complexity and potential limitations for larger-scale applications.

2. **Crowd Response Model Simplification**: The 70% accuracy assumption for crowd responses (Section 3.3) is overly simplistic. In practice, crowd accuracy varies significantly by task, worker expertise, and question difficulty. The paper should discuss how their framework would handle varying crowd quality (e.g., through worker reliability modeling as in [26]) and how this would affect performance.

3. **Threshold Baseline Evaluation**: The threshold baseline (Section 5) is somewhat superficially evaluated. The paper claims LENSE outperforms it, but doesn't explore *why* the Bayesian approach is superior. A more detailed analysis of the threshold baseline's limitations would strengthen the argument for the authors' approach.

4. **Connection to Recent NIPS Work**: While the paper cites relevant work, it doesn't sufficiently connect to recent NIPS papers on active learning with Bayesian methods (e.g., [27, 28] on Bayesian active learning). A stronger discussion of how this work advances the state of the art in this specific subfield would enhance the paper's significance.

5. **Theoretical Justification**: The paper demonstrates empirical success but lacks theoretical analysis of why their approach works better than alternatives. A brief analysis of the regret bounds or convergence properties would strengthen the theoretical foundation.

## Overall Assessment

This is a strong paper that addresses a practical and important problem in AI deployment. The technical approach is sound, the experimental results are compelling, and the framework has clear practical value. The paper makes a meaningful contribution to the intersection of active learning, online learning, and crowdsourcing.

The weaknesses identified are important but not fatal to the paper's contribution. The scalability concerns could be addressed with a brief discussion in the limitations section. The crowd response model simplification is common in this domain but could be acknowledged as a limitation. The connection to recent NIPS work could be strengthened with a few additional citations and discussion points.

## Recommendation

**Accept**. This paper makes a clear, significant contribution to a practical problem in AI deployment. The technical approach is novel and well-justified, the experiments are thorough and convincing, and the results demonstrate substantial improvements over baselines. While there are some limitations to address (primarily regarding scalability and crowd model simplification), these do not undermine the core contribution. The paper is well-written, clearly organized, and makes a strong case for its significance.

The work is highly relevant to NIPS' focus on machine learning theory and applications, particularly in the areas of active learning, online learning, and human-AI interaction. It advances the state of the art in a demonstrable way, addressing a real-world problem with significant practical implications.

## Specific Suggestions for Revision

1. Add a brief discussion of computational complexity and scalability for larger inputs.
2. Expand the discussion of crowd response models to acknowledge the simplification and discuss potential extensions.
3. Strengthen the connection to recent NIPS work on Bayesian active learning.
4. Provide more detailed analysis of why the Bayesian approach outperforms the threshold baseline.
5. Include a brief theoretical discussion of the convergence properties or regret bounds.

These revisions would further strengthen an already strong paper and make it even more compelling for the NIPS audience.
