This paper introduces a novel framework, On-the-Job Learning (OTJ), for performing structured prediction when labeled data is scarce. It formalizes the process of strategically querying a crowd to obtain labels for uncertain predictions, balancing accuracy, cost, and latency. The core idea is to model this as a stochastic game and utilize Bayesian decision theory to optimize query strategies. The authors implement and evaluate their system, LENSE, on NER, sentiment classification, and image classification, demonstrating significant cost reductions compared to full human annotation and performance improvements over online learning.

The paper builds upon existing work in online learning [7], active learning [8, 9, 10, 11], and crowdsourcing [3, 4, 25, 26].  It distinguishes itself by combining these areas in a setting where predictions *must* be made continuously, unlike traditional active learning which focuses on example selection for model training.  The approach also differs from active classification [11] by dynamically improving the model during test time. A closely related work, Legion:AR [6], is acknowledged, but this work extends it by providing a more comprehensive machine learning foundation. 

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The OTJ learning framework, specifically modeling the prediction process as a stochastic game with Bayesian decision theory, is a novel contribution. This allows for a principled approach to balancing accuracy, cost, and latency in a continuous prediction scenario.
* **Technical Soundness:** The formulation is mathematically well-defined, and the use of Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) and progressive widening to approximate the optimal policy appears reasonable given the intractability of the exact solution.  The environment model, with its components for crowd response and latency, is also well-motivated.
* **Empirical Results:** The results presented are compelling, showing significant cost reductions on the NER task and performance improvements over baselines on all tasks. The inclusion of a cost comparison to full human annotation is particularly valuable.
* **Implementation and Reproducibility:** The open-source implementation of LENSE and availability of data/experiments on CodaLab greatly enhance the reproducibility and impact of the work.
* **Clarity:**  The paper is generally well-written and organized.  The problem formulation and model description are clear, and the figures help illustrate the key concepts.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Crowdsourcing Setup Specificity:** The experimental setup relies on a "retainer" model on Amazon Mechanical Turk. The results might not generalize to other crowdsourcing platforms or alternative settings with different worker demographics or compensation structures. While acknowledged, the limited exploration of this sensitivity is a weakness.
* **Model Complexity:** The stochastic game formulation, while theoretically appealing, introduces significant complexity.  The MCTS approximation, though necessary, adds another layer of complexity.  The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion of the computational cost of the MCTS algorithm, particularly as the problem size increases.
* **Limited Exploration of Utility Function:** The utility function (Equation 1) uses fixed weights (wM and wT) for cost and time. A sensitivity analysis exploring different weightings, or even learning these weights adaptively, could strengthen the results.
* **Response Model Simplification:** While the response model is reasonable (70% accuracy), it’s a simplification of real-world crowd worker behavior. Exploring more sophisticated response models, perhaps incorporating worker reliability estimates, could further improve performance.
* **Comparison to Stronger Baselines:** While the baselines are appropriate, a comparison against more recent and sophisticated online learning algorithms, beyond the simple MLE baseline, would be valuable.  Specifically, comparing against algorithms incorporating cost-sensitive learning or active learning principles would strengthen the evaluation.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* This paper presents a novel and technically sound framework for on-the-job learning that addresses a practical problem in deploying AI systems with limited labeled data.
* The empirical results are compelling and demonstrate significant benefits in terms of cost and accuracy.
* The open-source implementation and reproducibility materials are a major strength.
* The work is likely to be of interest to researchers and practitioners in machine learning, crowdsourcing, and human-computer interaction.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The reliance on a specific crowdsourcing platform and the limited exploration of its impact raise concerns about generalizability.
* The complexity of the model and the MCTS approximation could limit its scalability to larger problems.
* A more thorough comparison against stronger baselines would provide greater confidence in the results.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that presents a novel and valuable contribution to the field. The technical soundness, empirical results, and commitment to reproducibility are commendable. While some weaknesses exist, they do not detract significantly from the overall quality of the work. I believe this paper is a good fit for NIPS and recommend acceptance. I suggest the authors address the reviewer concerns related to generalizability and computational cost in a revised version.
