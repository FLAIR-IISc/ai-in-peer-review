Summary
The paper proposes “on-the-job learning,” a decision-theoretic framework for making accurate predictions from the outset by interleaving model inference with real-time, asynchronous queries to the crowd. The system models the process as a stochastic game with actions “query,” “wait,” and “return,” and uses a CRF pθ(y|x), a noisy response model pR, and a response-time model pT to compute expected utility that trades off accuracy, latency, and monetary cost. Because optimal planning is intractable, the authors approximate the policy with Monte Carlo Tree Search (UCT) and progressive widening to handle continuous time. The approach, instantiated as LENSE, is evaluated on NER, sentiment, and face classification. Compared to human-only and machine-only baselines, LENSE reduces cost (e.g., >10x vs 5-vote NER) while maintaining or improving accuracy (e.g., +8 F1 over 1-vote human, +28 F1 over online learning). The system is open-sourced and deploys a retainer model on AMT to enable low-latency responses.

Relation to prior work
The work draws on and differentiates from online learning (improves with time, but here the system actively queries pre-decision to maintain high accuracy), active learning/online active learning (labels typically observed post-decision; cannot guarantee early accuracy), and active classification (value-of-information at test-time but with static policies learned from full labels). It explicitly builds on crowdsourcing frameworks for decision-theoretic control (e.g., TurKontrol) and real-time crowd systems (retainer pools), extends Legion:AR by formalizing the ML foundations for structured outputs, and uses MCTS/UCT (Kocsis & Szepesvári, NIPS) with progressive widening for planning in continuous time. The structured setting (CRF) relates to work on measurements in structured models and distant supervision.

Strengths
- Clear, principled decision-theoretic formulation integrating accuracy, cost, and latency, with intuitive behavior (parallel queries for low latency, redundancy for noisy labels).
- Addresses a practically important regime (bootstrapping systems with no initial training data) and supports asynchronous real-time crowdsourcing.
- Extends value-of-information planning to structured prediction (CRF) and continuous-time actions via MCTS + progressive widening.
- Demonstrates consistent cost reductions while maintaining or improving accuracy across three tasks; shows decreasing reliance on crowd with richer features.
- Open-source implementation and attempt at reproducibility.

Weaknesses
- Simplistic and fixed crowd response model pR (assumes 70% accuracy, uniform confusion) and response-time model; no learning or calibration of these from data, no per-worker variability, and no nonstationarity handling.
- Expected utility uses ExpAcc of the MAP prediction; this surrogate can be brittle under miscalibration and does not reflect full posterior risk over structured outputs.
- Evaluation design freezes worker responses and latencies (sampled without replacement), which reduces variance but undermines adaptivity and could bias timing policies; excludes real-world dynamics like worker learning/fatigue.
- Baselines are weak or mismatched: the “threshold” heuristic is strawman; no comparison to decision-theoretic POMDP baselines (e.g., TurKontrol-like) or learned policies; no ablation of MCTS vs. simpler myopic querying.
- Reporting lacks sensitivity analyses for wM, wT, pR mis-specification, and computational budget of MCTS; unclear how often planning is re-run and at what cost.
- CRF training “based on all actual responses” is under-specified: do responses become noisy labels, are they treated as soft constraints/posteriors, and how is label noise handled during learning?
- Economic accounting omits retainer pool costs and overhead; per-query payments are very low for some tasks, raising questions about generality and ethics.
- Datasets and features are not fully described in the text; some claims (e.g., “boosting performance relative to expert labels”) need careful substantiation.

Evaluation against review criteria
- Quality: The modeling and planning framework is sound and well-motivated, but the environment assumptions (pR, pT) are crude and not validated; several evaluation choices weaken the strength of claims (frozen responses, limited baselines, missing sensitivity analyses). No theoretical guarantees are provided.
- Clarity: Generally well-written; the game, utility, and algorithm are described crisply. However, key details need clarification: how ExpAcc is computed efficiently; training of pθ with noisy responses; selection/tuning of wM and wT; MCTS computational budget and rollout policies; details of datasets/features.
- Originality: The integration of decision-theoretic planning with structured outputs and asynchronous crowds is novel relative to prior active classification and crowdsourcing control. Using MCTS with progressive widening in this setting is also a creative adaptation. The conceptual idea overlaps with Legion:AR and TurKontrol but is meaningfully extended.
- Significance: Potentially impactful for rapid-deployment ML systems and hybrid crowd–machine workflows. Current empirical evidence is promising but limited by modeling assumptions and evaluation design; broader significance would be strengthened by stronger baselines, ablations, and robustness studies.

Questions and suggestions for improvement
- Learn pR and pT online (e.g., Dawid–Skene or hierarchical Bayes per worker; time models conditioned on item/task) and analyze robustness to mis-specified pR.
- Provide ablations: (i) MCTS vs myopic/greedy policy; (ii) with/without progressive widening; (iii) sequential vs parallel query strategies; (iv) MAP vs marginal-decision objectives.
- Compare against a POMDP baseline akin to TurKontrol adapted to structured outputs, and to a supervised active feature acquisition policy learned offline when possible.
- Detail CRF training with noisy, partial labels: objective, use of constraints/marginals, and strategies to mitigate noise (e.g., EM, posterior regularization).
- Report sensitivity to wM and wT, and provide a principled way to set them (e.g., Pareto frontier). Include full economic accounting (retainer costs).
- Run a live evaluation (no frozen pool), or at least simulate richer nonstationary worker models; analyze latency–accuracy tradeoffs as the worker pool fluctuates.
- Clarify computational overhead per example and provide throughput/latency guarantees under the planning algorithm.

Pros for acceptance
- Principled and timely problem formulation with clear practical relevance.
- Novel integration of decision-theoretic planning, structured prediction, and real-time crowdsourcing.
- Solid empirical gains and qualitative insights; open-source system.

Cons for acceptance
- Environment modeling and evaluation choices limit credibility and generality of results.
- Missing strong baselines and ablations; unclear sensitivity and computational costs.
- Several methodological details under-specified.

Recommendation: Weak reject
The paper is promising and could be impactful, but the current version relies on oversimplified crowd models and lacks rigorous baselines, ablations, and sensitivity analyses needed for NeurIPS standards. Addressing the above issues would substantially strengthen the contribution.

Confidence: 0.7
