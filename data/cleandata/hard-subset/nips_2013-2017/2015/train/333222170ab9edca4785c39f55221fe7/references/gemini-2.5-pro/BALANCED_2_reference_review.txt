### Summary
This paper introduces and formalizes the "on-the-job learning" setting, where a machine learning system must make high-quality predictions on a stream of inputs from the very beginning, without a pre-existing labeled dataset. To achieve this, the system can adaptively query a crowd of human workers for labels on parts of an input before making its final prediction. The goal is to balance accuracy, monetary cost, and latency. The authors frame this problem as a stochastic game between the system and the crowd. They propose a principled solution based on Bayesian decision theory to determine the optimal querying policy. Since computing the exact optimal policy is intractable, they develop an approximation using Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) combined with progressive widening to handle the continuous nature of time. The proposed system, LENSE, is evaluated on three diverse tasks (NER, sentiment analysis, and image classification), demonstrating significant cost reductions compared to a purely human-based approach and substantial accuracy gains over a standard online learning baseline.

The work is well-situated within existing literature, clearly distinguishing itself from related areas. Unlike standard online learning, it actively seeks information before prediction to maintain high initial accuracy. Unlike active learning, its goal is to make an accurate prediction on the current instance, not just to build a better model for the future. And unlike most active classification methods, it does not rely on a pre-trained model or a static querying policy, but instead learns both the prediction model and the querying strategy concurrently at test time.

### Quality
The paper is technically sound and presents a complete piece of work. The formulation of the problem as a stochastic game is elegant and appropriate. The use of Bayesian decision theory to define a utility function that explicitly trades off accuracy, cost, and latency is a major strength, providing a principled foundation for the system's decision-making process. The choice of MCTS with progressive widening is a well-justified and state-of-the-art method for approximating policies in such large state spaces.

The experimental evaluation is thorough and convincing. The authors test their approach on three different tasks, demonstrating its generality. The chosen baselines are sensible and serve to highlight the contributions of the proposed framework. The results, particularly the order-of-magnitude cost reduction on the NER task, are impressive. The inclusion of an analysis showing how the number of queries decreases as the model learns (Figure 3) effectively illustrates the "learning" aspect of the system. The commitment to reproducibility, with code and data provided on CodaLab, is commendable and significantly increases the value of the contribution.

A few minor points could be clarified to further strengthen the paper:
-   The crowd model is relatively simple, assuming a fixed 70% accuracy for workers. In practice, annotator quality varies, and some instances are inherently harder than others. A brief discussion on how the framework might be extended to incorporate more sophisticated, adaptive models of annotator noise (e.g., learning per-worker confusion matrices) would be valuable.
-   The computational cost of the MCTS planner is not discussed. For a real-time system, the time spent "thinking" about which queries to issue is a component of latency. How does the planning time scale with the complexity of the problem (e.g., sequence length in NER)?
-   The utility function weights `wM` and `wT` are key hyperparameters. The paper does not specify how they were set for the experiments or provide guidance on how a practitioner should choose them. A sensitivity analysis or discussion on this point would be helpful.

### Clarity
The paper is very well-written and organized. The motivation for "on-the-job learning" is clearly articulated in the introduction. The problem formulation in Section 2 is precise, and the model description in Section 3, while dense, is logical and well-explained. The use of a running example and figures (e.g., Figure 2) greatly aids in understanding the dynamics of the system. The algorithm is presented clearly, and the experimental setup and results are easy to follow.

### Originality
The paper's primary contribution is the formalization of the "on-the-job learning" setting, which, while having conceptual predecessors like Legion:AR [6], is given a rigorous machine learning foundation here for the first time. The combination of ideas—online learning with active information gathering before prediction, framed as a stochastic game, and solved with MCTS—is novel and powerful. While the individual components (CRFs, MCTS, crowdsourcing) are not new, their synthesis into a coherent, decision-theoretic framework for this specific, practical problem is highly original. This work carves out a new and interesting problem space that lies at the intersection of several established fields.

### Significance
The significance of this work is high. It addresses a critical and practical bottleneck in the deployment of AI systems: the need for large, upfront, labeled datasets. The proposed framework provides a path to deploying systems that are effective from day one and become more cost-efficient over time. This could have a substantial impact on how ML systems are built and deployed in domains where data is scarce or expensive to label, such as disaster response, medical imaging, or specialized information extraction tasks. Researchers are likely to build upon this framework, for example by exploring more sophisticated planning algorithms, richer crowd models, or applications to different structured prediction tasks. The open-source implementation further enhances the potential for impact and follow-on research.

### Arguments for Acceptance:
1.  **Novel and Important Problem:** Introduces and formalizes "on-the-job learning," a practical and significant problem setting that addresses the cold-start problem in deploying ML systems.
2.  **Principled Framework:** The use of Bayesian decision theory and a stochastic game formulation provides a solid, well-founded approach to a complex problem involving multiple trade-offs.
3.  **Strong Empirical Results:** The system is shown to be effective across three different tasks, achieving significant improvements in cost and accuracy over well-chosen baselines.
4.  **High Quality and Reproducibility:** The paper is technically sound, clearly written, and includes open-source code and data, which is a major plus.

### Arguments for Rejection:
1.  **Simplified Crowd Model:** The model for crowd worker noise and latency is a simplification of reality, which may limit performance in more complex scenarios.
2.  **Unaddressed Computational Cost:** The paper does not analyze the computational overhead of the MCTS planner, which is an important practical consideration for a real-time system.

### Overall Recommendation:
The strengths of this paper far outweigh its weaknesses. It presents a novel, well-motivated problem and a principled, technically sound, and empirically validated solution. This is a high-quality contribution that is likely to be of significant interest to the NIPS community and to inspire future work. I strongly recommend acceptance.
