- Proposes "on-the-job learning": query crowd at prediction time when uncertain to iteratively improve accuracy while minimizing cost and latency.
- Frames the problem as a stochastic game solved with Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) and progressive widening to trade off accuracy, cost, and time.
- Demonstrates the LENSE system on NER, sentiment, and image classification, showing large cost reductions and accuracy gains over baselines.
- Positions work relative to online learning, active learning, and crowdsourcing, highlighting dynamic test-time querying and links to Legion:AR.
- Builds on CRFs, MCTS, and Bayesian decision theory, adapting established techniques to on-the-job learning.
- Novel formulation and stochastic-game formalization are compelling and practically relevant.
- Uses Bayesian decision theory to provide a principled method for balancing accuracy, cost, and latency.
- Strong empirical results (e.g., +8% F1 over single human label, +28% over online learning on NER).
- Provides an open-source implementation and detailed crowdsourcing setup, aiding reproducibility.
- Paper is well-written, organized, and supported by helpful figures and equations.
- Thorough evaluation against strong baselines and useful analysis of query reduction over time.
- Framework complexity (stochastic game, MCTS, progressive widening) may limit accessibility and has dense notation.
- Assumes a retainer-style pool of consistent crowd workers, which may not generalize; fixed worker responses in simulation could bias results.
- Lacks extensive discussion of scalability of MCTS to high-dimensional or more complex tasks despite progressive widening.
- Performance may be sensitive to the initial model pθ(y|x); poor initialization could cause inefficient querying.
- Response model uses a simplistic fixed 70% accuracy assumption, ignoring real-world worker reliability variability.
- Arguments for acceptance: important problem, novel and principled framework, strong experiments, open-source release, clear writing.
- Arguments against acceptance: framework complexity, reliance on specific crowdsourcing setup, insufficient scalability discussion.
- Overall assessment: strong contribution to active learning and crowdsourcing; merits acceptance with minor revisions.
- Suggested improvements: discuss computational cost of MCTS and scalability strategies, analyze sensitivity to initial model, address generalizability to other crowdsourcing setups, clarify notation with a simple example, and sensitivity-analyze utility weighting parameters.
