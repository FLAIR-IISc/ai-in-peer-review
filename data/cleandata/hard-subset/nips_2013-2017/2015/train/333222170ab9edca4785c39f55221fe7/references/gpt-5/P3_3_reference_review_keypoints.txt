- Paper proposes "on-the-job learning": a decision-theoretic framework that interleaves model prediction and realtime crowd queries to trade off accuracy, latency, and monetary cost.
- Formalizes test-time interaction as a stochastic game using a CRF predictor pθ, environment models pR/pT, and a utility; uses MCTS with progressive widening for planning.
- Empirical evaluation on NER, sentiment, and image classification shows improved cost–accuracy trade-offs vs. human-only and machine-only baselines; code released.

Strengths:
- Clear, principled utility that unifies accuracy, latency, and cost.
- Elegant stochastic-game formulation with an explicit environment model p(y,r,t|x,q,s).
- Appropriate use of MCTS with progressive widening for asynchronous continuous-time planning.
- Practical contribution by applying to structured prediction (CRF) and real-time crowdsourcing experiments.
- Demonstrated reduction in crowd dependence over time for higher-capacity models.
- Open-source implementation and reproducibility artifacts provided.

Weaknesses — Quality:
- Worker/noise model is simplistic and fixed (pR=0.7 correct, uniform otherwise), ignoring per-worker heterogeneity, drift, and instance difficulty.
- Utility/metric mismatch: optimized utility uses expected MAP token accuracy while main evaluations use F1, risking misalignment.
- Inference and learning details are under-specified: unclear how CRF posteriors incorporate noisy responses, calibration of posteriors, MCTS simulation budget, and wall-clock planning cost.
- Experimental baseline issues: online-learning baseline has oracle access to gold labels; missing stronger baselines like myopic VOI, adaptive submodularity, and POMDP crowd-control policies.
- Pre-collecting only five worker responses and sampling without replacement may understate crowd variability; live-worker results and sensitivity analyses missing.
- No statistical significance tests or confidence intervals reported for evaluation metrics.
- Restriction preventing launching new queries while waiting for responses may materially affect latency–cost trade-offs and lacks ablation.

Weaknesses — Clarity:
- Missing implementation/tuning details: how wM and wT are chosen, calibration procedures, and task-specific tuning.
- pT (response-time) model unspecified: parametric form, conditioning, fit diagnostics, and cross-task portability not provided.
- MCTS hyperparameters (exploration constant, widening schedule, rollout policy, termination criteria) and computational overhead per decision are not reported.
- Training under noisy labels unclear: whether responses used as hard labels or pR incorporated (e.g., via EM).

Weaknesses — Originality & Significance:
- Novel synthesis of structured prediction, time/cost-aware decision-making, and MCTS for continuous-time crowdsourcing, but some components (worker modeling, learning) are standard or underdeveloped.
- Important, timely problem with potential impact, but significance hampered by unrealistic worker model, weak baselines, and limited evidence that planning drives gains.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Learn pR and pT from data, model per-worker reliabilities and instance difficulty, and condition pR on retainer specifics.
- Align utility with task metrics (expected F1 or sequence-level measures) or justify and quantify surrogate loss.
- Add stronger baselines (myopic EVPI, adaptive submodular policies, POMDP controllers) and perform ablations for time reasoning and parallel querying.
- Report calibration metrics for pθ, statistical significance, sensitivity to MCTS budget, and to number of precollected responses.
- Allow or evaluate launching queries mid-wait and quantify the impact of the current restriction.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Addresses a practical cold-start setting with a principled, general framework and open-source release.
- Novel integration of structured models, time/cost-aware planning, and real-time crowdsourcing with empirical gains.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Unrealistic fixed worker/noise model and lack of learned per-worker reliabilities.
- Utility–metric mismatch and under-specified inference/training limit reproducibility and the strength of decision-theoretic claims.
- Insufficiently strong baselines, limited statistical analysis, and potentially biased precollection protocol.

Overall recommendation:
- Promising and well-motivated work, but requires significant revisions on crowd-noise modeling, experimental baselines, and evaluation rigor to meet top-tier standards.
