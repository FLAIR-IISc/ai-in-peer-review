This paper proposes an on-the-job learning framework leveraging Bayesian decision theory to strategically query a crowd for labels, aiming to balance accuracy, latency, and cost. While the core idea of actively seeking information during prediction is appealing and addresses a practical need—building systems with limited labeled data—the paper suffers from a multitude of minor but cumulatively significant flaws in presentation, technical detail, and experimental rigor.

The introduction adequately motivates the problem, but the comparison to existing work is superficial. The distinctions between online learning, online active learning, and active classification are presented somewhat inaccurately. Online active learning *can* seek information prior to prediction (e.g., by querying a pool of unlabeled data), and the claim that it “cannot maintain high accuracy independent of the number of data instances seen so far” is a generalization. The authors fail to adequately discuss the connection to exploration-exploitation trade-offs common in reinforcement learning, given the stochastic game framing.

The problem formulation is clear, but the notation is inconsistent. For example, `q` is used to represent both a single query position and a sequence of queries.  The remark that the system must make a prediction on each input, unlike active learning, is a defining characteristic of *online* active learning. The description lacks sufficient detail about the CRF model – what features are used? What regularization is applied? These details are crucial for reproducibility.

The model section is the most problematic. While framing on-the-job learning as a stochastic game is conceptually interesting, the complexity introduced seems disproportionate to the benefits. The game tree description is verbose and difficult to follow. The “reasonable limitation” of not allowing queries mid-wait seems arbitrary and potentially restrictive. The choice of utility function, while borrowing from Bayesian decision theory, lacks justification for the specific weighting parameters (`wM` and `wT`).  The environment model's response model, setting `pR(r | yq) = 0.7`, feels ad-hoc and not grounded in any analysis of expected crowd worker accuracy. This hardcoding significantly impacts the downstream results. The brief discussion of expected behavior is insufficient to convey the model’s dynamics.

The game playing section briefly introduces MCTS and progressive widening but lacks sufficient detail. Algorithm 1 is poorly explained. What does "visits" refer to in line 3? The UCT decision rule in line 6 is missing its exploration constant (often denoted as 'c'). The progressive widening implementation seems overly simplistic; restricting the search space based solely on the number of visits is a potentially suboptimal strategy. The rationale for the chosen parameters in the MCTS algorithm is entirely absent.

The experiments section suffers from several issues. The baseline choices are somewhat weak. Comparing against a single human label is reasonable, but the "threshold baseline" lacks a clear justification and appears poorly tuned. The crowdsourcing setup is described, but the selection criteria for workers aren’t detailed. Crucially, freezing the pool of worker responses, while intended to control for variance, introduces a significant bias. The system is not learning from a *dynamic* crowd but from a static snapshot.  The results themselves are presented with insufficient statistical significance testing. The improvement over online learning (28% F1) is impressive, but without error bars or p-values, it's difficult to assess its robustness. The claim of an "order of magnitude reduction in cost" on NER is overstated; the precise cost reduction should be quantified. The discussion of UNIGRAMS vs. RNN features is insightful, but the conclusion regarding model capacity feels speculative. The link to the CodaLab repository is appreciated.

**Arguments for rejection:**

*   **Lack of Rigor:** Numerous ad-hoc choices (response model, utility function weights, MCTS parameters) lack justification.
*   **Technical Incompleteness:** Insufficient detail regarding model implementation (CRF features, regularization) and algorithm parameters.
*   **Experimental Flaws:** Biased experimental setup (frozen worker responses), lack of statistical significance testing, overstated claims.
*   **Clarity Issues:** Verbose descriptions, inconsistent notation, and a poorly explained algorithm.
*   **Incremental Contribution:** While the framework is interesting, the improvements over simpler baselines are not substantial enough to warrant acceptance given the complexity.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** The on-the-job learning framework and its formulation as a stochastic game are relatively novel.
*   **Practical Relevance:** The problem of building systems with limited labeled data is important.
*   **Reproducibility:** The authors provide code, although its utility is limited by the aforementioned issues.



Overall, this paper presents a promising idea but falls short of NIPS standards due to a lack of technical rigor, clarity, and experimental validation. I recommend **rejection**. The authors should significantly revise the paper, addressing the points raised above, before resubmitting to a different venue.
