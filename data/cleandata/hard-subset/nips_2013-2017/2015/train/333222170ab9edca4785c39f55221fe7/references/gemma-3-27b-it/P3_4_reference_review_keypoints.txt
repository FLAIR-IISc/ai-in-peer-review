- Proposes "on-the-job learning": querying crowd during prediction to balance accuracy, cost, and latency via Bayesian decision theory.
- Frames learning as a stochastic game solved approximately with Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS).
- Demonstrates LENSE on NER, sentiment, and image classification with improved cost and accuracy vs. baselines.
- Clearly situates work relative to online/active learning, active classification, and crowdsourcing literature.
- Novel and significant approach for scenarios with limited upfront labeled data.
- Methodologically sound: principled Bayesian formulation, MCTS with progressive widening, and response time modeling.
- Evaluation is comprehensive across three diverse tasks and multiple baselines.
- Implementation and CodaLab worksheet are publicly released, aiding reproducibility.
- Paper is well-written with helpful figures and clear explanations.
- Experimental crowdsourcing uses a frozen pool of responses, limiting realism and crowd dynamics.
- Utility function includes weights (wM, wT) whose chosen values may strongly affect outcomes; sensitivity analysis is needed.
- Scalability concerns: MCTS complexity may not scale to very large or complex structured prediction problems.
- Strongest gains on NER may leverage a dominant "NONE" class; class-wise generalization is unclear.
- Uses a CRF model but claims model-agnostic framework; experiments with other models (e.g., neural nets) are absent.
- Arguments for acceptance: practical problem, principled framework, strong empirical gains, reproducibility, clear writing.
- Arguments against acceptance: sensitivity to utility weights, scalability issues, reliance on a specific crowdsourcing setup.
- Overall assessment: strong paper with minor weaknesses that do not outweigh contributions.
- Recommendation: Accept with minor revisions addressing sensitivity analysis, scalability discussion, class-wise results, and frozen-pool limitations.
