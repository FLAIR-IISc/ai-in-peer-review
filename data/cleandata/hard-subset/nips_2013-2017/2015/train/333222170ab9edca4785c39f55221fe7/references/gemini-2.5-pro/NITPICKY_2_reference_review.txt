### Summary of the Paper

This paper introduces a framework called "on-the-job learning" for structured prediction tasks. The goal is to build a system that achieves high accuracy from the very first input, without requiring a large pre-existing training set. The core idea is that for each new input, the system can dynamically and asynchronously query a crowd of human workers for labels on specific parts of the input it is uncertain about. These responses are used to make an immediate, high-quality prediction. Over time, the system's underlying model (a CRF) is updated with these responses, allowing it to become more self-sufficient and reduce its reliance on the crowd.

The authors model this decision-making process as a stochastic game between the system and the crowd. They use Bayesian decision theory to define a utility function that formally trades off prediction accuracy, monetary cost (per query), and latency (time to prediction). Since computing the optimal query policy is intractable, they propose an approximation based on Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) combined with progressive widening to handle the continuous nature of time. The approach is evaluated on three tasks (NER, sentiment analysis, image classification), demonstrating significant cost reductions compared to a pure crowdsourcing approach and substantial accuracy gains over a standard online learning baseline.

The work relates to several areas. It differs from online/active learning, which typically does not query for information before making a prediction on a new instance. It is conceptually similar to active classification, but extends it by learning from noisy labels (not querying features) and updating the model at test time, rather than using a static policy.

### Strengths

1.  **Novel and Significant Problem Formulation:** The "on-the-job learning" setting is well-motivated and addresses a critical real-world problem: deploying accurate ML systems in scenarios where data is scarce and initial performance is paramount. The framing of this problem is a valuable contribution.
2.  **Principled Approach:** Modeling the problem as a stochastic game and using Bayesian decision theory to manage the accuracy-cost-latency tradeoff is an elegant and powerful formulation. This provides a solid theoretical foundation for the proposed system, LENSE.
3.  **Strong Empirical Evaluation:** The experiments are comprehensive, covering three distinct tasks. The results are impressive, showing an order of magnitude cost reduction on NER and significant F1 improvements over relevant baselines. The inclusion of an analysis showing how query rates decrease over time (Fig. 3) effectively demonstrates the "learning" aspect of the system.
4.  **Commitment to Reproducibility:** The authors provide code and data via CodaLab, which is commendable and substantially increases the value of the work.

### Weaknesses

Despite the strong core ideas, the paper suffers from a substantial number of flaws in its details, methodology, and presentation that detract significantly from its overall quality.

**Major Issues:**

1.  **Critical Omissions and Lack of Clarity:**
    *   Figure 2, which is central to understanding the game tree and the system's behavior, is referenced multiple times but is not present in the provided text. This makes Sections 2 and 3 very difficult to follow.
    *   The model for crowd response times, `pT(ti | si)`, is a key component of the environment model and utility function, yet its functional form is never specified. Is it an exponential, log-normal, or empirical distribution? This is a critical missing detail for both understanding and reproducibility.
    *   The mechanism for updating the CRF model `pθ(y | x)` is glossed over. The paper states it is learned "based on all actual responses," but it is unclear how the noisy, partial labels from the crowd are incorporated. Are they treated as ground truth? Is there a model for worker reliability? Training directly on noisy labels can lead the model to learn crowd biases rather than the true signal. This is a crucial, under-explained aspect of the system.
    *   There is a mismatch between the utility function and the evaluation metric. The utility in Eq. (1) uses a generic "Accuracy" term, while the experiments report F1 score for NER and accuracy for the other tasks. For structured prediction, these are not the same, and the choice of loss function directly impacts the optimal policy. The `ExpAcc` definition is also non-standard and could benefit from further justification.

**Minor Methodological and Technical Concerns:**

1.  **Oversimplified Crowd Model:** The response model `pR(r | yq)` assumes a constant 70% accuracy and a uniform distribution over incorrect labels. This is unrealistic. Annotator errors are typically structured (e.g., a confusion matrix), and worker quality varies. While a simplification, this assumption weakens the connection to real-world crowd behavior. The 70% figure is stated in a footnote in Section 3, which feels out of place; this empirical detail belongs in the experiments section.
2.  **Unjustified Baseline:** The "Threshold baseline" relies on magic numbers (0.3 and 0.98) that are not explained or justified. It is unclear if these were tuned, making it difficult to assess whether this is a strong heuristic or a strawman.
3.  **Scalability Concerns:** The paper does not discuss the computational complexity of the MCTS planner. For a "real-time" system, the time spent planning the next action is critical. An analysis of how planning time scales with sentence length or the number of queries would strengthen the paper.

**Presentation and Formatting:**

1.  **Formatting Inconsistencies:** The bibliography is poorly formatted, with many entries missing essential information like conference name, year, or page numbers (e.g., [1], [2], [7], [13]). This suggests a lack of care in the paper's preparation.
2.  **Typos and Unprofessional Language:** There are several typos (e.g., "comporable" instead of "comparable," "miscellaneos" instead of "miscellaneous"). The use of informal phrasing like "we feel like this is a reasonable limitation" (Footnote 1) is not appropriate for a scientific publication.
3.  **Confusing Notation:** In Section 3, the symbol `q` is used to denote both the sequence of past actions and a new action `qk`. Using clearer notation (e.g., list concatenation) would improve readability.

### Recommendation: Reject

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   The paper introduces a novel and important problem setting ("on-the-job learning").
*   The core technical approach of modeling the problem as a stochastic game solved with MCTS is sound and principled.
*   The empirical results are strong and demonstrate the practical utility of the proposed method.
*   The work is reproducible, with code and data provided.

**Arguments for Rejection:**
*   The paper is undermined by a large number of minor flaws and several major omissions that collectively question its technical completeness and polish.
*   A critical figure (Figure 2) is missing, severely hindering the reader's ability to understand the core model.
*   Key methodological details are absent (the latency model `pT`, the model update procedure), making the work difficult to scrutinize and reproduce from the text alone.
*   The simplified crowd model and the poorly justified baseline cast some doubt on the robustness of the conclusions.
*   The overall presentation, including numerous formatting errors and typos, falls below the standards expected for NIPS.

While the central ideas are promising, the execution is rushed and lacks the necessary rigor and attention to detail. The paper reads like a strong concept that has not yet been fully developed into a complete and carefully documented piece of scientific work. It requires a major revision to address the omissions and clarify the methodological details before it is ready for publication.
