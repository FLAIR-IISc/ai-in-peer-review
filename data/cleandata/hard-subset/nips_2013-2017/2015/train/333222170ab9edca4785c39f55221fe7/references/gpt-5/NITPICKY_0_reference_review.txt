Summary
The paper proposes “on-the-job learning,” a decision-theoretic framework that, at test time, adaptively queries crowd workers for uncertain parts of an input, while updating a structured prediction model (e.g., a CRF) online. The system trades off accuracy, latency, and monetary cost via a Bayesian expected-utility objective that uses the expected accuracy of the current MAP prediction. Since optimal planning is intractable, the authors approximate the policy with Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) and progressive widening to handle continuous response times. Experiments on NER, sentiment, and face classification suggest substantial cost reductions versus crowd-only baselines and accuracy gains over machine-only baselines; an implementation (LENSE) and a CodaLab worksheet are cited.

Strengths
- Timely, practically relevant problem: real-time, budget-aware prediction with crowd queries.
- Clean formalization as a stochastic game with explicit control of latency and cost.
- Intuitive qualitative behaviors (parallel vs. sequential queries, redundancy against noise) arise naturally from the utility.
- Use of MCTS with progressive widening is a reasonable, principled choice to handle continuous time and large trees.
- Structured prediction setting (CRF) is more realistic than independent labels and increases the paper’s scope.
- Open-sourcing and CodaLab worksheet (if complete) support reproducibility.

Weaknesses and detailed concerns
Technical/modeling
1) Response model oversimplified and mismatched to experiments. pR(r|yq)=0.7 if r=yq and uniform otherwise ignores:
   - Worker heterogeneity, item difficulty, class-dependent confusions, and correlations across repeated queries.
   - The simulation protocol samples from a frozen pool of 5 collected worker responses per label without replacement, which violates the i.i.d. assumptions of Eq. (3) and does not model per-worker reliabilities (cf. Dawid–Skene, TurKontrol).
2) Prediction utility mismatch to evaluation. The utility uses ExpAcc of the MAP; experiments report F1 for NER. It is unclear how sequence-level or span-level F1 is reflected in Eq. (2). If Accuracy is 0/1 sequence accuracy, it poorly correlates with F1; if token accuracy, it still mismatches entity F1. Please define Accuracy precisely and justify the surrogate.
3) Incomplete environment model. pT(t|s) is not specified (parametric form, parameters, estimation procedure). wM and wT are not reported, nor how they are calibrated to actual dollars and target latency/accuracy. The link between the measured latency/cost and the utility weights is thus opaque.
4) Algorithmic details insufficient for reproduction:
   - UCT needs initialization when N(σ′)=0 to avoid division by zero/undefined values.
   - No rollout policy, horizon, or termination conditions are described for MCTS; no simulation budget, c parameter, or convergence criteria.
   - Progressive widening line 11 “sampled from C(σ) based on (3)” is ambiguous: how are probabilities assigned to previously sampled successors?
   - Complexity and wall-clock planning times are not reported; real-time constraints are central to the setting.
5) Online learning specification is vague. The CRF training via AdaGrad lacks hyperparameters, feature templates, regularization, and update schedule. How are crowd responses incorporated during online updates (e.g., soft labels, marginalization)?
6) Multiple queries to the same position assume independent pR draws; in practice, workers may see previous labels or be correlated; no modeling of redundancy aggregation is described beyond majority/MAP.
7) The paper forbids issuing a new query while waiting for the next response; this restriction could be significant for throughput; its impact is not quantified.

Experimental methodology
8) Baseline definitions and thresholds appear incorrect. The “Threshold baseline” inequality (1−pθ(yi|x))×0.3^m ≥ 0.98 is counterintuitive (residual uncertainty increases with the RHS); likely a sign error (should be ≤ 0.02). This needs correction; otherwise the baseline is handicapped.
9) Claims vs. evidence:
   - Tables/Figures (1–4) are referenced but not included here; exact numbers, variances, and statistical tests are missing.
   - The text states “boosting performance relative to the expert provided labels,” which is nonsensical if the expert labels are the gold reference.
   - “28% F1 improvement over online learning”: online learning sees gold labels of all past examples; without early-time curves and significance, this is hard to believe.
10) Data/task inconsistencies:
   - NER: The label set includes RESOURCE and NONE and mentions removing MISC from CoNLL 2003; CoNLL does not have RESOURCE and is not Twitter. The text also references a Twitter NER example. Please clarify datasets, domains, and labeling schema.
   - Sentiment features (UNIGRAMS vs. RNN): the RNN feature extraction is not specified; pretrained models? training data? source.
   - Faces: what is the exact dataset split, label space, and embedding source (acknowledged but not described)?
11) Reliance on pre-collected 5 responses/delays per label to “control worker quality” may cap the attainable redundancy and distort latency distributions; it also contradicts the online-learning narrative of using “actual responses.” Clarify whether training and evaluation both use the frozen pool and justify the ecological validity.

Clarity and presentation
12) Numerous typos and formatting issues reduce clarity: “burgenoning,” “comporable,” “Retreival,” “miscellaneos,” “one-the-job,” inconsistent footnote markers, missing/misaligned equations (e.g., Eq. (2) formatting), inconsistent indices (k−1 vs. k).
13) References are incomplete (years/pages/venues missing) and in places outdated nomenclature (“NIPS” vs. current “NeurIPS”).
14) Informal tone in places (“we feel like”) and non-rigorous claims (e.g., “roughly 70% accurate”) without confidence intervals.

Related work
15) While some relevant work is cited (TurKontrol, active classification), the paper does not sufficiently engage with per-worker reliability models (Dawid–Skene; Whitehill et al.), VOI-based test-time feature acquisition and budgeted prediction beyond the three classics cited, or structured VOI for sequence tagging.

Pros for acceptance
- Interesting and practical problem; neat formalization and use of MCTS/progressive widening.
- Structured prediction focus is valuable; potential impact if robustly demonstrated.
- Promising empirical narrative (cost savings with accuracy maintenance).

Cons for acceptance
- Insufficient technical detail and methodological rigor to reproduce results.
- Several modeling and experimental inconsistencies (utility–metric mismatch, baseline error, dataset mismatch, response model).
- Ambiguous and, at times, self-contradictory claims; missing quantitative evidence in the provided text.
- Presentation quality (typos, references) below NeurIPS standards.

Questions for authors
- Precisely define Accuracy in Eq. (2) for structured outputs; justify its relation to reported F1.
- Specify pT, wM, wT, MCTS hyperparameters, rollout policy, and compute budgets.
- Correct and detail the Threshold baseline inequality and parameters.
- Clarify datasets, label spaces, and feature extraction (especially for RNN and faces).
- Explain how online CRF training incorporates crowd responses; provide hyperparameters.
- Provide ablations: effect of progressive widening, utility weights, redundancy, and per-worker modeling.
- Report run-time/latency of planning and end-to-end decision making.

Recommendation: Reject
While the paper addresses an important problem with a principled approach, the current submission contains too many technical, methodological, and presentation issues to meet NeurIPS standards. With clarified datasets, corrected baselines, rigorous experimental reporting, and stronger modeling of crowd noise and utility, this work could become a solid contribution.
