- Introduces "on-the-job learning": a decision-theoretic stochastic game that interleaves model inference with test-time crowd queries using a CRF and a Bayesian utility trading off accuracy, cost, and latency.
- Uses MCTS with progressive widening to approximate the intractable optimal policy over asynchronous, continuous-time crowd responses.
- Empirically (NER, sentiment, faces) shows cost reductions vs multi-vote crowdsourcing and accuracy gains vs machine-only and heuristic baselines; code released.
- Connects to online/active learning, active classification, and crowdsourcing control but differs by querying pre-prediction, learning at test time, and handling structured outputs.
- Novel combination: decision-theoretic stochastic game + MCTS for on-the-job learning in structured prediction tasks.
- Clear, principled formulation with explicit modeling of response noise and response times and a Bayesian utility objective.
- Algorithmic contribution in planning over continuous-time asynchronous responses and balancing parallel vs sequential queries.
- High practical relevance for bootstrapping systems without large labeled datasets while maintaining immediate quality.
- Reproducible work with provided code and datasets.
- Main weakness: utility optimizes expected MAP accuracy while evaluation for NER uses F1, creating an objective/metric mismatch that can bias decisions.
- Simplified crowd model (fixed 70% pR, uniform noise, no per-worker or per-item difficulty) risks misspecification and distorted redundancy/timing choices.
- Evaluation uses a frozen pool of five worker responses to simulate consistency, reducing variance but limiting ecological validity and sensitivity to worker variance.
- Baselines are weakly chosen: online baseline disadvantaged early and no comparisons to stronger decision-theoretic (POMDP/TurKontrol), active-classification, or learned static-policy baselines.
- Missing quantification of computational cost and planning latency: MCTS budget, branching limits, and real-time feasibility not fully reported.
- Modeling assumptions (independent pR across queries/positions, independent response times, no mid-wait query launches) simplify search but may hurt performance; impacts not deeply discussed.
- Quality assessment: sound core formulation and reasonable algorithms, but experiments need stronger sensitivity analyses, ablations, and baseline comparisons.
- Clarity assessment: generally well written, but needs sharper definitions for ExpAcc computation, pT estimation, MCTS hyperparameters, online CRF updates, and utility-to-latency mapping.
- Originality assessment: novel synthesis of stochastic-game decision-theoretic framing with MCTS for structured on-the-job learning.
- Significance assessment: addresses an important human-in-the-loop problem with promising practical benefits, but impact would increase with stronger evaluation and crowd modeling.
- Key suggestions: align utility with F1 or justify proxy, run sensitivity analyses (wM, wT, worker accuracy, latency shifts), compare to POMDP/TurKontrol and learned baselines, and report end-to-end computation/latency.
- Additional suggestions: learn/update per-worker reliabilities, explore interleaved query launching, and study impacts of independence assumptions.
- Pros for acceptance: principled novel framework, practical relevance, reproducible implementation, and promising multi-task results.
- Cons for acceptance: evaluation gaps, simplified/mis-specified crowd model, and limited discussion of computational scalability and real-time feasibility.
- Recommendation: lean accept (weak accept) with the expectation that stronger evaluation and crowd modeling would strengthen the contribution.
