- Introduces “on-the-job learning”: querying a crowd during prediction to balance accuracy, cost, and latency using Bayesian decision theory.
- Models the problem as a stochastic game and uses MCTS with progressive widening to approximate policies.
- Demonstrates LENSE across NER, sentiment, and image tasks with substantial cost reductions and accuracy gains.
- Situates work well within online/active learning and crowdsourcing literature, clearly distinguishing its contribution.
- Novel and significant paradigm addressing practical deployment with limited labeled data.
- Theoretical foundation is sound; stochastic game formulation and MCTS approach are well-motivated.
- Empirical results are convincing (e.g., 8x cost reduction in NER) and code is publicly released.
- Paper is generally clear and well-organized, with effective figures and explanations.
- Experiments use a “frozen pool” of worker responses, limiting realism and generalizability to dynamic crowds.
- Computational cost of MCTS remains a concern; scalability and deployment resource needs are underdiscussed.
- Utility function depends on weight choices (wM, wT) but lacks systematic sensitivity analysis.
- Response model is simplistic (fixed error rate); more nuanced worker-specific or context-aware models were not explored.
- Discussion of broader limitations and unsuitable task types for the approach is limited.
- Arguments for acceptance: novel, theoretically grounded, strong empirical gains, and reproducible code.
- Arguments against acceptance: frozen worker pool, MCTS scalability concerns, and utility parameter sensitivity.
- Overall assessment: strong paper with significant impact and minor weaknesses that don’t outweigh contributions.
- Recommendation: Accept.
- Reviewer confidence: 8/10.
