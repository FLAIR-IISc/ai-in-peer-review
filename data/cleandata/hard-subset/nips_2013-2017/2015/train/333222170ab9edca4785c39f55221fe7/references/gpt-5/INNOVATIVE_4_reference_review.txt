Summary
The paper proposes “on-the-job learning,” a setting where a system starts with no trained model, maintains high accuracy from the start by querying crowd workers during prediction, and gradually reduces reliance on the crowd as it learns. The problem is formalized as a stochastic game between the system and the crowd. A Bayesian decision-theoretic utility balances expected accuracy (via a CRF posterior), monetary cost per query, and latency. Because exact planning is intractable, the authors use Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) with UCT and progressive widening to handle the continuous time dimension and asynchronous responses. The system (LENSE) is evaluated on NER, sentiment, and face classification, showing large cost reductions relative to majority-vote crowdsourcing and accuracy improvements over an “online learning” baseline. Code and datasets are released.

Relation to prior work
The work sits at the intersection of online learning, active learning/classification, crowdsourcing control (e.g., TurKontrol), and structured prediction. Unlike standard online or active learning, the system can acquire information before committing to a prediction. Unlike active classification with static policies, the policy adapts at test time as the model learns. Legion:AR explored real-time human-in-the-loop classification, but without a formal decision-theoretic treatment. The novelty here is the principled formulation as a stochastic game with an explicit time model and approximate planning over structured outputs.

Strengths
- Novel and timely problem formulation: a compelling middle ground between pure ML and pure crowdsourcing with a clean decision-theoretic backbone.
- Principled utility that jointly trades off accuracy, cost, and latency; the framework yields intuitive behaviors (parallel vs. sequential queries, redundancy for noise).
- Technically interesting use of MCTS with progressive widening to reason about continuous, asynchronous responses.
- Empirical gains across three tasks, including substantial cost reductions for comparable or better accuracy than human-only or machine-only baselines.
- Open-source release and an implementation that leverages a retainer pool to enable low-latency human responses.

Weaknesses
- Environment modeling is simplistic: pR is fixed at 0.7 with uniform errors and independence across queries/workers; no per-worker reliabilities or item difficulty, which are standard in crowdsourcing models and may materially affect policies.
- A substantial portion of the evaluation uses pre-collected worker responses and delays “frozen” per label to reduce variance. While practical for controlled comparisons, it weakens claims about live, adaptive behavior under real worker dynamics.
- Utility calibration is underspecified: choice/sensitivity of wM and wT; why ExpAcc of the MAP rather than full Bayes decision over y? No analysis of calibration or robustness to mis-specified accuracies and latency distributions.
- Limited ablations: no comparison against strong decision-theoretic controllers (e.g., POMDP-style policies like TurKontrol) or simpler greedy/query-by-uncertainty schedulers. No ablation of MCTS vs. myopic or batch policies, or of progressive widening’s role.
- Some design choices (e.g., forbidding launching a query while waiting) are justified by tractability but may be suboptimal; empirical impact is not quantified.
- Scalability and compute: repeated CRF inference under MCTS rollouts could be expensive; the paper doesn’t characterize runtime, memory, or scaling to larger structures.

Quality
Overall technically sound and coherent. The modeling/algorithmic pieces are reasonable; however, stronger experimental validation and sensitivity analyses are needed to fully support the claims, especially around robustness to model mis-specification and live deployment.

Clarity
Generally clear and well-structured. The game formulation and utility are easy to follow. Some details are terse or missing (parametrization of pT, MCTS hyperparameters, exact inference approximations for ExpAcc, dataset sizes/splits). A few typos/formatting issues.

Originality
High. Framing on-the-job learning as a stochastic game with Bayesian decision-making over accuracy/cost/time, plus an MCTS planner handling asynchronous crowd signals in structured prediction, is a fresh and bold direction relative to prior active learning/active classification/crowd-control works.

Significance
High. If adopted, this framework could materially change how practitioners bootstrap new systems under resource constraints, offering a principled path from “crowd-as-crutch” to autonomy. The structured setting further increases practical relevance.

Suggestions and questions for authors
- Please justify and/or relax the pR=0.7 uniform-error model. Can you incorporate per-worker reliabilities (e.g., Dawid–Skene, item-response/beta-binomial) and show the effect on policy and outcomes?
- Provide sensitivity analyses for wM, wT, assumed worker accuracy, and latency distribution. How robust are policies to mis-specification?
- Report live (non-simulated) end-to-end experiments with the retainer pool, including overhead costs and worker churn, to substantiate real-time claims.
- Compare against stronger decision-theoretic baselines (e.g., TurKontrol-style POMDP, myopic VOI with per-token uncertainty) and include ablations (no MCTS, no progressive widening, batch-parallel-only, etc.).
- Clarify computation: rollout budget, wall-clock per instance, CRF inference costs, and scaling to longer sequences or richer models (e.g., neural CRF).
- Explain the ExpAcc choice; would a Bayes-optimal decision over y under 0-1 loss differ materially?
- Consider allowing interleaved query issuance during waits and quantify the trade-off.
- Minor: detail datasets, splits, and hyperparameters sufficiently to enable reproduction from scratch.

Pros
- Bold, well-motivated new setting with a principled formulation.
- Innovative combination of decision theory and MCTS for asynchronous crowd inputs.
- Solid empirical improvements and cost reductions; open-source release.

Cons
- Simplified and partially simulated evaluation environment.
- Limited robustness and ablation studies; missing comparisons to strong decision-theoretic baselines.
- Under-specified components (worker/time models, utility calibration, scalability).

Recommendation
Accept. The conceptual novelty and potential impact outweigh the limitations in modeling realism and experimental depth. With additional robustness and live evaluations, this line of work could open a valuable direction for human-in-the-loop, cost-/latency-aware learning at deployment.

Confidence
Moderate.
