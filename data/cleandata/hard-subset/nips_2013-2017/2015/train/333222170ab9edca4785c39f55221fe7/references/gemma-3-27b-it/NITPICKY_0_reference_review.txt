This paper proposes an “on-the-job learning” framework, modeling interaction with a crowd as a stochastic game and leveraging Bayesian decision theory for query selection. The core idea – dynamically balancing accuracy, latency, and cost by strategically querying a crowd during prediction – is a reasonable and potentially impactful approach, particularly in resource-constrained scenarios. The authors present results on NER, sentiment classification, and image classification, demonstrating cost reductions and accuracy improvements over several baselines. However, the paper suffers from a concerning number of minor flaws, methodological imprecisions, and formatting inconsistencies that detract from its overall quality and impact.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty of Formulation:** Framing the problem as a stochastic game and applying Bayesian decision theory is a novel and theoretically sound approach.
*   **Practical Relevance:** The on-the-job learning setting directly addresses a common real-world challenge: building AI systems with limited labeled data and/or computational resources.
*   **Positive Empirical Results:** The reported improvements in cost and accuracy, particularly on the NER task, are encouraging. The open-source implementation (LENSE) is a commendable contribution.
*   **Clear Motivation:** The introduction effectively motivates the problem and contrasts it with existing approaches (supervised learning, crowdsourcing, online learning, active learning).

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Lack of Rigorous Baselines:** The “Online learning” baseline is vaguely defined. It states it uses “the best possible offline system”, but lacks details on the specific algorithm employed. It’s crucial to compare against a strong, well-defined online learner (e.g., a passive-aggressive algorithm or a state-of-the-art online CRF). Comparing against a single human label *is* a valid baseline, but does not represent a strong machine learning approach.
*   **Implementation Details & Reproducibility Concerns:** The description of the crowdsourcing setup is insufficient for full reproducibility.  Specifically, the authors mention a "pool" of workers and freezing worker responses/delays, but fail to clearly articulate how this pool was initially established. Were workers pre-qualified? How were initial response/latency distributions obtained? The code repository is essential, but a detailed methodological description *within* the paper is crucial. Furthermore, the payment scheme for NER ($0.005 per query) seems unusually low and potentially impacts worker quality.
*   **Utility Function Critique:** The utility function (Equation 1) feels somewhat ad-hoc. The weights *wM* and *wT* are not justified or analyzed. A sensitivity analysis exploring the impact of these weights on performance is absent. The formulation `ExpAcc(p(y | x,q, s, r, t))` also needs clarification – what specifically constitutes the ‘accuracy’ in the case of structured prediction (e.g., token-level F1, sequence-level F1)?
*   **Game Tree Simplifications:** The justification for excluding queries during wait actions ("this rules out the possibility of launching a query midway through waiting…reasonable simplification") is weak. While it might simplify the search space, it introduces a potentially suboptimal policy. This limitation needs more thorough discussion.
*   **Response Model:** The response model `pR(r | yq) = 0.7` is overly simplistic. A more nuanced model, potentially incorporating worker-specific reliability estimates, would be more realistic. The statement “we found the humans we hired were roughly 70% accurate” is presented as justification, but isn't integrated into the model itself.
*   **Formatting & Clarity Issues:** Numerous minor formatting inconsistencies exist (e.g., inconsistent use of italics, spacing issues around equations).  The writing is occasionally verbose and repetitive. For instance, the benefits of online learning are re-stated multiple times. Equation (2) is unnecessary.
*   **Figure 2 Description:** Figure 2 is crucial for understanding the system, yet the description is lacking. A more detailed walkthrough of a specific example within the figure would improve comprehension. The progressive widening concept is described but not visually represented, hindering understanding.
*   **Related Work Coverage:** While the related work section is reasonably broad, it lacks depth. For example, the discussion of active classification feels cursory.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The core idea is novel and addresses an important problem.
*   The empirical results are promising, especially on the NER task.
*   The open-source implementation is a valuable contribution.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

*   The paper suffers from a significant number of minor flaws (methodological imprecisions, formatting issues).
*   The lack of strong baselines and thorough experimental analysis weakens the conclusions.
*   The implementation details and reproducibility concerns are problematic.
*   The ad-hoc utility function and response model lack theoretical justification.



**Overall Assessment:**

While the paper presents a potentially valuable contribution, its numerous shortcomings prevent me from recommending acceptance in its current form. The core ideas are sound, but the execution is lacking in rigor and detail. I believe a major revision addressing the weaknesses outlined above is necessary to elevate this paper to NIPS standards. Specifically, a more thorough experimental evaluation with stronger baselines, a detailed description of the crowdsourcing setup, a justification of the utility function parameters, and improvements to the clarity and formatting are crucial.  The paper currently feels like a promising proof-of-concept rather than a fully polished scientific contribution.



**Recommendation:** Reject (with encouragement for major revision).
