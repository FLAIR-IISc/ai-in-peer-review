Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes “on-the-job learning,” a framework for deploying a prediction system with high accuracy from day one by interleaving real-time crowd queries with model inference and online learning. The system formalizes decision making as a stochastic game between the system and the crowd. A Bayesian decision-theoretic utility trades off expected accuracy, monetary cost, and latency. Because exact planning is intractable, the authors approximate the optimal policy using Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) with progressive widening to handle continuous response times. The prediction backbone is a CRF whose parameters are updated online using crowd responses. The environment model includes a simple noise model for crowd responses and a latency model based on measured response-time distributions. Experiments on NER, sentiment, and face classification show that the approach, implemented as LENSE, reduces cost substantially versus human-only baselines and improves accuracy versus machine-only baselines.

The work sits at the intersection of online learning, active learning, active classification, and crowdsourcing. Unlike online/active learning, which cannot guarantee immediate high accuracy, the system queries the crowd pre-decision. Unlike active classification, which typically learns a static test-time policy from labeled data, LENSE adapts its policy and model while deployed. Compared to prior crowd-POMDP work (e.g., TurKontrol) and real-time crowd systems (e.g., Legion:AR), this paper emphasizes structured prediction, explicit latency-cost-accuracy trade-offs, and continuous-time planning via MCTS.

Strengths
- Novel formulation: Casting deploy-time, real-time querying with ongoing learning as a stochastic game and optimizing a Bayesian utility that jointly considers accuracy, cost, and latency is an impactful and fresh perspective.
- Planning contribution: Using MCTS with progressive widening to reason about asynchronous, continuous-time crowd responses is a clever and appropriate algorithmic choice that could generalize beyond these tasks.
- Practical significance: The setting is highly relevant for rapidly deploying systems without labeled data. The retainer model implementation and open-source release increase the likelihood of impact.
- Structured outputs: Handling sequence labeling with CRFs highlights benefits of querying correlated variables versus independent labels.
- Empirical results: Demonstrated cost reductions (order-of-magnitude on NER against 5-vote) and accuracy gains over single-pass human labeling and online learning baselines support the claim that principled decision-making matters beyond heuristics.

Weaknesses and questions
- Simplistic response model: The fixed pR(r|yq)=0.7 with uniform off-diagonal noise ignores worker/item/task heterogeneity, temporal drift, and per-worker reliability. This could bias the planner’s value estimates and query redundancy. At minimum, a learned Dawid–Skene-style or hierarchical reliability model would strengthen the framework and likely improve decisions.
- Latency modeling details: pT(t|s) is critical, but the paper provides limited detail on how it is estimated and updated online. Is it stationary? Does the planner adapt when the retainer pool changes?
- Evaluation protocol: Simulating by pre-collecting 5 responses/delays per label and sampling without replacement controls variance but risks overfitting the policy to a tiny, fixed pool and underestimates real-world uncertainty. Please clarify: (i) were retainer costs amortized into the monetary cost metric? (ii) How sensitive are results to pool size/quality variance? (iii) Do conclusions hold with live end-to-end trials?
- Baselines: In addition to the heuristic threshold baseline and majority voting, stronger adaptive crowd baselines exist (e.g., POMDP/dynamic stopping for redundancy allocation such as TurKontrol-like policies, quality-aware worker models). Including one would better isolate the contribution of the decision-theoretic planner.
- Utility and objective mismatch: The utility uses ExpAcc of the MAP solution under the posterior. This is a surrogate for expected accuracy that can be suboptimal for structured outputs. Some discussion or ablation (e.g., token-level expected accuracy vs MAP accuracy) would help.
- Computational overhead: Planning with MCTS at test time can be expensive. Please report per-example planning time, number of rollouts, UCT parameters, and effects on end-to-end latency relative to human response times.
- Clarity/organization: Some notation/formatting issues (footnotes in text, missing figure references, minor typos). More explicit pseudo-code for the system’s action set (parallel vs sequential queries) and constraints would help reproducibility.

Quality
Technically sound overall, with a reasonable approximation strategy and solid engineering. However, the environment modeling (noise and latency) is simplistic, and the evaluation could better control for confounders and include stronger adaptive baselines. The claims are generally supported, but the simulation protocol and cost accounting need clearer justification.

Clarity
Readable and well-organized, but could use more detail on pR/pT estimation, the ExpAcc choice, and MCTS hyperparameters and runtime. Some presentation glitches as noted.

Originality
High. The on-the-job learning formulation with continuous-time MCTS planning for structured prediction and explicit latency-cost-accuracy trade-offs is a distinctive and bold contribution relative to prior work on online/active learning and crowd-POMDPs.

Significance
High. If adopted, this line of work could change how practitioners bootstrap systems without labeled data, enabling immediate deployment with quality guarantees while learning from the crowd. The open-source release is a plus.

Pros for acceptance
- Clear, novel problem formulation with practical relevance
- Principled Bayesian decision-theoretic approach and nontrivial planning algorithm
- Strong empirical gains, especially on NER, with transparent cost/latency considerations
- Open-source system likely to catalyze follow-up work

Cons for acceptance
- Simplistic crowd error/latency models; lack of worker/item heterogeneity
- Evaluation relies on small frozen pools; limited discussion of retainer costs and robustness
- Missing comparisons to stronger adaptive crowd baselines
- Limited analysis of computational overhead

Suggestions
- Incorporate and learn worker/item reliability and time-varying latency models online.
- Add an adaptive redundancy baseline (e.g., TurKontrol-style) and report end-to-end live experiments including retainer costs.
- Report planning runtime and sensitivity to MCTS parameters.
- Discuss alternative utility formulations and calibration of posterior confidence.

Recommendation
Accept. Despite some evaluation and modeling limitations, the paper introduces a compelling new direction with a principled and practical solution. The novelty and potential impact outweigh the shortcomings, and the work will stimulate further research in deployable learning systems. Confidence: 4/5.
