Summary
The paper proposes “on-the-job learning,” a framework for deploying a model that achieves high accuracy immediately by dynamically querying a crowd at test time and gradually relying less on the crowd as the model improves. The problem is formalized as a stochastic game between the system and the crowd over a structured predictor (e.g., CRF). A Bayesian decision-theoretic utility trades off accuracy, latency, and monetary cost. Because exact planning is intractable, the paper uses Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) with progressive widening to handle continuous response times. Experiments on NER, sentiment, and image classification using a retainer pool demonstrate substantial cost reductions versus pure crowdsourcing and accuracy gains over pure ML baselines. The open-source system is LENSE.

Relation to prior work
The work is positioned against online learning (which does not obtain information at test time), active learning (which delays label acquisition until after prediction), and active classification (static policies learned offline, often with full labels). It is related to crowdsourcing decision-theoretic control (e.g., TurKontrol) and real-time crowd-powered systems (e.g., Legion:AR), but differs by (i) structured prediction over sequences, (ii) explicit planning over parallel/sequential queries with continuous time, and (iii) continual model improvement during deployment. It also connects to structured prediction and to VOI/POMDP-style control, though the paper emphasizes an MCTS planner rather than exact POMDP solutions.

Evaluation by criteria

Originality
- High. The on-the-job setting, cast as a stochastic game with a Bayesian utility over accuracy/time/cost and solved via MCTS+progressive widening, is a compelling and fresh unification of crowdsourcing control with structured prediction and online improvement. While related to TurKontrol/Legion:AR and active classification, the proposed formulation and its planning for asynchronous, parallel crowd queries in structured outputs is notably novel.

Significance
- Strong potential impact. Many practitioners face a cold-start deployment problem with limited labels and real-time needs; a principled framework that fuses immediate crowd supervision with learning is valuable. The idea of explicitly trading off latency, cost, and accuracy at prediction time is broadly applicable and could influence future work on human-in-the-loop ML and test-time acquisition.

Quality
- Technically sound in high-level formulation and planning approach, but some modeling and evaluation choices are simplified:
  - Response model assumes a fixed 70% accuracy and uniform confusion, with no worker heterogeneity or item difficulty; this underutilizes rich crowdsourcing models (Dawid–Skene/GLAD) and could bias the planner.
  - Learning of pθ uses “actual responses” directly (appears to treat them as labels) rather than marginalizing latent ground truth via the response model; this weakens the Bayesian story and may mis-handle noise.
  - The decision utility uses ExpAcc of the MAP; the choice of token-level vs sequence-level accuracy and Bayes-optimality of this surrogate should be clarified.
  - MCTS/progressive widening is reasonable for continuous times, but there are no guarantees or regret bounds; empirical ablations of search depth/budget are limited.
  - Baselines are fairly weak on decision-theoretic control; missing comparisons to myopic VOI/entropy-based sequential policies, TurKontrol-like reliability modeling, and structured expected error reduction.
  - Evaluation reuses a precollected pool of 5 worker responses per label sampled without replacement; this reduces variance but moves away from a realistic online setting and may overfit to the assumed error model. Live A/B runs or sensitivity analyses to response accuracy/time distributions would strengthen claims.
  - Hyperparameters wM and wT and response-time models are not fully specified; sensitivity to these is important.

Clarity
- Generally well-written and organized, with intuitive figures and a clear description of the game, utility, and planner. Some details are underspecified (worker model, training objective, response-time distribution). A few footnote fragments and minor notation issues appear in the excerpt (likely formatting artifacts).

Pros (arguments for acceptance)
- Introduces a timely, conceptually bold framework for immediate deployment with principled human-in-the-loop decisions.
- Elegant formalization as a stochastic game with a clean Bayesian utility balancing accuracy/latency/cost.
- Practical, scalable planning via MCTS+progressive widening for continuous time and asynchronous responses.
- Demonstrates that planning parallel vs sequential queries emerges naturally from the utility.
- Open-source implementation; results show substantial cost reductions and accuracy gains across tasks.

Cons (arguments against acceptance)
- Simplistic crowd/label noise model; no per-worker reliability or item difficulty.
- Learning under noise not fully Bayesian; training apparently treats responses as ground truth.
- Baseline set omits strong VOI or POMDP/myopic policies; threshold baseline is weak.
- Evaluation uses pre-sampled worker responses; limited live-system validation and sensitivity studies.
- Utility surrogate and metric choices (MAP ExpAcc) need clearer justification for structured outputs.

Questions/clarifications for authors
- How exactly is pθ updated online given noisy responses? Are true labels ever inferred as latent variables, or are crowd responses used as targets?
- What is the accuracy metric inside ExpAcc for sequences (token vs sequence), and why is MAP-based ExpAcc preferred over expected 0/1 loss?
- What is the form/fit of pT(t|s)? Any heteroskedasticity across items/tasks? Sensitivity to wM, wT?
- How does MCTS budget (simulations/time) affect performance/latency? Any rollout policy details?
- Could you compare to a myopic expected error reduction policy that accounts for time/cost?

Suggestions to improve the paper
- Incorporate or at least analyze per-worker reliability and item difficulty (e.g., Dawid–Skene/GLAD) and propagate this through the planner.
- Learn pθ with a latent-label approach that marginalizes response noise, aligning the training with the environment model.
- Add stronger decision-theoretic baselines (myopic VOI with parallel/sequential options), and ablate parallel vs sequential issuing under different wT.
- Provide sensitivity analyses over response accuracy/time distributions and costs, including live-worker experiments where feasible.
- Clarify utility/metric choices for structured outputs and report run-time/compute overhead of planning.

Recommendation
- Accept (weak accept leaning accept). The paper introduces a significant, novel direction—principled test-time human-in-the-loop learning with explicit latency/cost/accuracy trade-offs—supported by a plausible planner and promising empirical results. While some modeling/evaluation choices are simplified, they are addressable and do not overshadow the conceptual contribution. This work is likely to spur follow-up research and practical systems. Confidence: medium-high.
