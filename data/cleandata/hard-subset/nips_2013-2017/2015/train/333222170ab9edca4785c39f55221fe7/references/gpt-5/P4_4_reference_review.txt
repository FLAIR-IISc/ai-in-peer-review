Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes “on-the-job learning,” a decision-theoretic framework that delivers high-quality predictions from the outset by interleaving model inference with real-time crowdsourced queries at test time. For each input, the system decides which parts to query, whether to wait for responses, or when to return a prediction, explicitly trading off accuracy, latency, and cost under a Bayesian utility. The environment (crowd) is modeled via: (i) a prediction model pθ(y|x), instantiated as a CRF for structured outputs; (ii) a response model pR capturing noisy worker labels; and (iii) a response-time model pT. The control problem is cast as a stochastic game and approximately solved using Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) with UCT and progressive widening to handle continuous time. The system, LENSE, is evaluated on NER, sentiment, and image classification, showing improved accuracy-cost tradeoffs versus human-only (n-vote), online learning, and a threshold heuristic baseline. The work extends ideas from Legion:AR (real-time crowdsourcing for activity recognition) by providing a formal decision-theoretic formulation and structured prediction with online model updates, and relates to decision-theoretic control of crowdsourcing (e.g., TurKontrol) and active classification, but differs by (a) querying at test time for each instance, (b) updating the model online, and (c) planning over asynchronous responses and time.

Quality (technical soundness)
- Formulation: Modeling the problem as a stochastic game with an explicit utility that jointly accounts for expected accuracy, monetary cost, and latency is principled. Using MCTS with progressive widening is a reasonable approximation for the otherwise intractable planning problem with continuous time.
- Environment model: The response model assumes a fixed, instance-independent accuracy (0.7) and independence across queries. This is a strong simplification; it ignores per-worker heterogeneity, item difficulty, and correlation between errors, which are known to matter in crowd labeling.
- Utility and metric alignment: Utility uses ExpAcc of the MAP prediction. However, evaluation uses F1 for NER; the paper does not clarify how ExpAcc is computed for structured outputs or aligned with F1 (sequence-level vs token-level accuracy). This mismatch and the computational tractability of ExpAcc for structured outputs should be addressed.
- Learning with noise: The CRF is updated online using the crowd responses, but the training procedure does not appear to model label noise explicitly (e.g., via latent true labels or noise-aware likelihoods). Learning directly from noisy labels can bias the model.
- Approximation details: The MCTS setup (rollout policy, budget, branching, computational latency) is not fully quantified. Since the system targets real-time decision making, additional detail on planning time and its contribution to end-to-end latency is important.
- Evaluation: The use of a retainer pool and pre-collected worker responses/timings provides control but effectively simulates the crowd. This reduces ecological validity (e.g., no dynamic pool changes, learning/forgetting, context drift). Costs reported may not include retainer overhead; this should be clarified. Baselines include human n-vote, online learning, and a threshold heuristic; stronger decision-theoretic baselines (e.g., myopic VOI, simple POMDP heuristics, DS-style reliability modeling) would strengthen claims.

Clarity
The paper is generally well organized and readable. The game, state, and action definitions are clear. However, several key details need clarification: (i) how ExpAcc is computed for structured outputs and which accuracy metric is used; (ii) how wM and wT are selected and sensitivity to them; (iii) computational budget and latency of MCTS; (iv) exact treatment of noisy labels during learning; and (v) whether retainer pool costs are included in reported costs.

Originality
The combination of on-the-job test-time querying, structured prediction, an explicit Bayesian utility over accuracy/cost/latency, and MCTS with progressive widening is a novel synthesis. Prior work in real-time crowdsourcing (Legion:AR) and POMDP control of crowd workflows (TurKontrol) is related but does not address structured outputs with online learning and continuous-time planning in this way. The decision-theoretic framing and analysis of sequential vs parallel querying strategies are insightful.

Significance
The problem is important for rapidly deployable systems where labeled data is scarce and quality must be high from the outset. Results showing substantial cost reductions (especially on NER) and accuracy gains over human-only and machine-only baselines suggest practical impact. That said, stronger baselines, clearer accounting of costs (including retainer), and additional ablations would solidify the significance.

Strengths
- Clear decision-theoretic formulation of test-time querying with explicit latency and cost modeling.
- Structured prediction setting with online learning from crowd responses.
- Approximate planning via MCTS/progressive widening to handle asynchronous, continuous-time responses.
- Empirical gains and code/data release for reproducibility.

Weaknesses
- Simplified and fixed response model (uniform 0.7 accuracy) without per-worker/item heterogeneity.
- Utility/evaluation metric mismatch and unclear computation of ExpAcc for structured outputs.
- Learning from noisy labels without an explicit noise model.
- Evaluation relies on pre-collected responses (simulation) and may omit retainer overhead; ecological validity and cost accounting are unclear.
- Baselines are relatively weak; lack of comparisons to VOI/POMDP heuristics and noise-aware learning.
- Limited sensitivity analyses (wM, wT, worker accuracy, latency distributions).

Suggestions for improvement
- Align the utility metric with the evaluation metric (e.g., expected F1) or justify the proxy and provide ablations to show robustness.
- Incorporate worker/item reliability modeling (e.g., Dawid–Skene variants) into pR and/or learning, and evaluate its impact.
- Provide end-to-end runtime/latency including planning time, and sensitivity to MCTS budget.
- Report whether retainer costs are included; if not, provide a realistic amortized estimate.
- Add stronger baselines: myopic VOI policies, POMDP heuristics (e.g., TurKontrol-style), and sequential uncertainty sampling.
- Conduct sensitivity analyses over wM, wT, worker accuracy, and latency distributions.
- Clarify the noise-aware learning approach or extend to latent-true-label training.

Arguments for acceptance
- Principled, well-motivated framework addressing a relevant and underexplored setting.
- Novel integration of decision theory, structured prediction, and continuous-time planning.
- Promising empirical improvements and open-source release.

Arguments against acceptance
- Simplifying assumptions in the response model and noise handling may limit validity.
- Metric mismatch and missing details on ExpAcc computation for structured outputs.
- Evaluation largely simulation-based with potentially incomplete cost accounting; baselines could be stronger.

Reproducibility
The release of code, data, and experiments is a strong positive. Additional detail on hyperparameters, utility weights, and MCTS budgets would further aid replication.
