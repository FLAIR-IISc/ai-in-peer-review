## Review of "On-the-Job Learning with Bayesian Decision Theory"

**Summary:**

This paper presents a compelling framework called "on-the-job learning" for tackling prediction problems with limited upfront labeled data. The core idea is to strategically query a crowd for labels only when uncertain, balancing accuracy, latency, and cost using Bayesian decision theory and a stochastic game formulation. The authors approximate the optimal query policy using Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) and progressive widening, and demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach – LENSE – on three tasks: NER, sentiment classification, and image classification, showing significant cost reductions and accuracy improvements compared to existing methods.

**Relation to Previous Work:**

The paper correctly positions itself within a landscape of related work, drawing connections to online learning, active learning, crowdsourcing, and structured prediction. It effectively differentiates itself from standard active learning by emphasizing learning *during* test time and not solely focusing on data selection for model building. The connection to Legion:AR is well-noted, highlighting how this work provides a more formal, machine learning-grounded treatment of the on-the-job learning setting.  The authors do a good job of demonstrating awareness of relevant literature and highlighting their contribution as a unified and principled approach.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty and Problem Setting:** The on-the-job learning setting is a practically relevant and underexplored area. Many real-world applications require quick deployment with limited resources, making this approach highly appealing.
* **Principled Framework:** The formulation of the problem as a stochastic game with a Bayesian decision-theoretic objective is a major strength. It provides a strong theoretical foundation for balancing competing concerns.
* **Technical Contribution:**  The combination of MCTS and progressive widening to address the intractability of the optimal policy is a clever and effective technical contribution. This enables scaling to practical problem sizes.
* **Strong Empirical Results:** The experimental results are convincing, showing consistent improvements in cost-effectiveness and accuracy across different tasks. The comparison against a strong baseline (full human annotation) and the cost reduction figures are particularly impressive.  The ablation study in Figure 4 demonstrating the cost reduction over time is insightful.
* **Reproducibility:** The release of an open-source implementation (LENSE) and the availability of code and data on CodaLab is excellent and greatly enhances the paper’s impact.
* **Clarity of Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and organized.  The diagrams (Figures 2 and 3) are helpful in visualizing the framework and experimental results.



**Weaknesses:**

* **Crowdsourcing Setup Specifics:** While the authors mention using Amazon Mechanical Turk, details about worker qualification, quality control beyond the initial pool gathering, and potential biases are limited. It’s unclear how robust the system is to variations in crowd worker quality *after* the initial snapshot.
* **Response Model Simplification:** The fixed 70% accuracy assumption for crowd responses is a significant simplification. In reality, worker accuracy can vary considerably based on task complexity, individual skill, and fatigue. While acknowledging this is a limitation, exploring more sophisticated response models (e.g., modeling worker expertise) could strengthen the framework.
* **Computational Cost of MCTS:**  Although the MCTS approximation addresses the intractability of the optimal policy, the computational overhead of running MCTS during inference could be a limiting factor in real-time applications, especially for complex models or large state spaces.  A discussion of the runtime performance of the algorithm, and potential optimizations, would be beneficial.
* **Limited Exploration of Utility Function:** The utility function’s weights (wM and wT) are not explored extensively.  A sensitivity analysis demonstrating how performance varies with different weightings would be valuable.  
* **Task Selection:** While the three tasks demonstrate generality, they are relatively standard NLP and computer vision problems. Applying LENSE to more complex or real-world tasks with more nuanced uncertainties would further demonstrate its robustness and scalability.




**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper addresses a practically important and relatively unexplored problem.
* The proposed framework is technically sound, well-motivated, and based on strong theoretical foundations.
* The experimental results are convincing and demonstrate significant improvements over existing methods.
* The open-source release and reproducibility efforts are commendable.
* The paper’s clarity and organization are excellent.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The simplifying assumptions regarding crowd worker accuracy and the computational cost of MCTS could limit the framework’s applicability in certain scenarios.
* While the paper acknowledges these limitations, further exploration and discussion of these issues would strengthen the work.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of online learning and crowdsourcing. The on-the-job learning framework is novel, principled, and empirically validated. While there are some limitations, they are appropriately acknowledged and do not detract significantly from the overall quality of the work.  

**Recommendation:**

**Accept with Minor Revisions.**  I recommend accepting this paper, contingent on the authors addressing the following minor revisions:

1.  Provide more details about the crowdsourcing setup, including worker qualification and quality control mechanisms beyond the initial data collection.
2.  Discuss the computational complexity of the MCTS algorithm and potential optimizations for real-time applications.
3.  Include a brief sensitivity analysis of the utility function’s weights.
4.  (Optional) Briefly discuss potential extensions to incorporate more sophisticated response models.
