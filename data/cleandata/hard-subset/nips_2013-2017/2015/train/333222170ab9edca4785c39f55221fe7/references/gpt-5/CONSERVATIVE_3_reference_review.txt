Summary
The paper proposes “on-the-job learning,” a test-time framework in which a model with no pretraining can query crowd workers for labels on parts of the input before committing a prediction. The approach formalizes decision making as a stochastic game between the system and the crowd. A CRF provides pθ(y|x); crowd responses are modeled with a simple noise model pR and a response-time model pT. The system trades off expected accuracy, monetary cost per query, and latency via a Bayesian decision-theoretic utility. Because optimal planning is intractable, the authors approximate with Monte Carlo Tree Search (UCT) plus progressive widening for continuous time. Experiments on NER, sentiment, and image classification claim improved accuracy-cost trade-offs versus “human n-vote,” a threshold heuristic, and an “online learning” baseline, using a real-time crowd retainer setup.

Relation to prior work
The setup touches online learning, active learning, active classification/test-cost sensitive prediction, and decision-theoretic control of crowdsourcing (e.g., TurKontrol). Relative to active classification, the policy adapts at test time and updates the model online. Relative to POMDP-style crowd control, this paper focuses on structured outputs and explicit time-cost trade-offs with an MCTS planner.

Strengths
- Tackles a practically relevant setting: high initial accuracy without pretraining by leveraging a crowd, then reducing dependence on the crowd over time.
- Principled decision-theoretic formulation with a clear utility trading accuracy, latency, and cost; explicit modeling of asynchronous responses and parallel vs sequential querying.
- The combination of MCTS with progressive widening for continuous-time planning is reasonable and leads to intuitive behavior (parallelism for latency vs redundancy for accuracy).
- Empirical evaluation on three tasks, including structured NER, with an implementation of a real-time retainer; open-source code.

Weaknesses
- Evaluation methodology risks bias: policies are “simulated” using a frozen pool of exactly five pre-collected worker responses and delays per label, sampled without replacement. This limits stochasticity, ignores nonstationarity and correlation across items/workers, and can advantage policies tuned to this environment. End-to-end live evaluation (with actual on-policy interaction) is not reported.
- Crowd model is oversimplified and mismatched: planning assumes a fixed pR with 70% accuracy and uniform noise, independent across queries; the environment used for evaluation comes from the 5-response pool. Worker heterogeneity, instance difficulty, and correlation are known to matter and are standard in the crowdsourcing literature (e.g., Dawid–Skene-type models, TurKontrol). The paper neither models nor ablates these factors; robustness to miscalibrated pR is unclear.
- Baselines are weak or incomplete:
  - No comparison to decision-theoretic crowd control/dynamic stopping methods (e.g., TurKontrol-like POMDPs optimizing vote allocation) or simple adaptive myopic VOI policies that launch additional queries based on current posterior uncertainty, including time-aware variants.
  - The “online learning” baseline is peculiar: it trains on gold after each instance but cannot query at test time; more relevant would be (i) a strong pre-trained CRF/RNN with labeled data of varying size; (ii) a calibrated, uncertainty-thresholding dynamic redundancy policy; and (iii) test-cost-sensitive baselines.
  - No ablation to show MCTS planning advantage over simple greedy/sequential or parallel heuristics under identical utility.
- Utility/objective mismatch: the utility optimizes expected accuracy of the MAP, but evaluation uses F1 for NER. It is unclear what “Accuracy” is in ExpAcc for structured outputs; misalignment may bias decisions. Choices of wM and wT are under-specified; no sensitivity analysis or Pareto front is presented.
- Scalability and computational cost are not reported: per-instance planning time, depth/branching of MCTS, and the effect of progressive widening parameters are missing.
- Cost accounting appears incomplete: the fixed cost of maintaining the retainer ($1 per worker) is not clearly amortized into the reported “cost per example.” Claims of “order of magnitude” savings could change with different amortization assumptions or pool utilization.
- Dataset and statistical rigor: dataset sizes, class distributions, and significance testing are not clearly presented; results are reported largely as point estimates without uncertainty.
- Clarity gaps: details about pT, CRF features/training with noisy labels, and how crowd responses are incorporated into learning online are sparse. Some footnotes and figure references are incomplete.

Questions for authors
1. Provide true online, on-policy results (including end-to-end latency distributions) rather than simulations from a frozen response pool. How do results change with real-time nonstationarity?
2. How are wM and wT chosen? Please report sensitivity analyses and Pareto curves for accuracy/cost/latency.
3. Clarify what “Accuracy” means inside ExpAcc for structured outputs and reconcile with F1 used in evaluation.
4. How is pR calibrated? Evaluate robustness to misspecification (vary per-item and per-worker accuracies, introduce adversarial workers).
5. Add baselines: (i) dynamic stopping/dynamic redundancy (e.g., TurKontrol-like); (ii) myopic VOI/entropy-based adaptive querying; (iii) strong pre-trained models with varying labeled budgets; (iv) Dawid–Skene aggregation for crowd labels.
6. Report computational overhead of MCTS per instance and compare to a greedy policy to justify the planner’s complexity.
7. Amortize retainer costs and report total cost per example under different traffic rates.
8. For learning pθ from noisy crowd labels, what loss/estimator is used? Are posteriors over labels used directly or via majority vote?

Minor comments
- Several references/footnotes appear truncated; ensure all figures/tables are legible and units are stated.
- Please specify dataset sizes, train/test splits, and whether tweets are preprocessed (URLs, hashtags).
- Consider citing worker-heterogeneity models (e.g., Dawid–Skene) and adaptive stopping for crowdsourced classification.

Significance and originality
The problem is important and the formulation is interesting, especially the explicit time-cost-accuracy trade-off in a structured setting. However, many pieces build on known ideas (decision-theoretic control, active classification, MCTS planning). The main novelty is the particular integration and real-time crowd interface. Without stronger empirical validation against established decision-theoretic crowd baselines and more rigorous evaluation, the contribution feels premature.

Recommendation
Reject. While the direction is promising, the current evaluation and baseline coverage are insufficient for a strong NeurIPS contribution. A revised version with on-policy experiments, stronger baselines, robust modeling of crowd heterogeneity, and clearer cost/latency accounting could be much more compelling.

Confidence
High.
