Summary
The paper proposes “on-the-job learning” (LENSE), a framework to produce high-quality predictions from the outset by interleaving model inference with real-time crowd queries at test time. The core idea is to formalize the interaction as a stochastic game between the system and the crowd, with actions to issue token-level queries, wait for asynchronous responses, or return a prediction. A Bayesian decision-theoretic utility trades off expected prediction accuracy against monetary and latency costs. Because computing the optimal policy is intractable, the authors approximate it using Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) with progressive widening to handle continuous response times. The environment model comprises a CRF pθ(y|x), a simple noise model for responses pR, and a latency model pT. Experiments on NER, sentiment, and face classification show that LENSE reduces cost versus pure crowdsourcing and improves accuracy over pure ML baselines, with open-source code and a retainer-based real-time crowd setup.

Relation to prior work
- Compared to online learning and online active learning, the system can acquire information before committing to a prediction, allowing high accuracy early on.
- Compared to active classification/test-time feature acquisition, LENSE learns and adapts at test time and explicitly incorporates crowds as noisy, slow oracles with costs and delays.
- LENSE is closest to Legion:AR’s real-time crowd-machine loop but provides a principled Bayesian decision-theoretic formulation with structured prediction and an MCTS planner.
- The POMDP-based optimization of crowdsourcing workflows (e.g., TurKontrol) and crowd aggregation/reliability modeling (e.g., Dawid–Skene; GLAD) are related; here, the novelty is the structured prediction setting and explicit joint optimization of accuracy–cost–latency per instance.

Quality
Strengths:
- Clear, principled formulation linking expected decision utility to accuracy, time, and money.
- Reasonable environment model with CRF-based structure, capturing the value of partial observations and redundancy.
- A pragmatic planning algorithm (MCTS + progressive widening) for a challenging continuous-time game.
- Solid experimental setup with three tasks, retainer-based real-time queries, and code/data release; results show substantial cost reduction and accuracy gains over reasonable baselines.

Weaknesses/concerns:
- Utility–metric mismatch: the utility uses ExpAcc of the MAP label, but results are reported in F1 (especially for NER). The paper should justify or calibrate the surrogate, or evaluate policies under the same metric used during planning.
- The response noise model pR is simplistic (70% correct; uniform otherwise), independent of class/token, context, and worker; this may bias the planner. Established worker/noise models (e.g., Dawid–Skene, GLAD) or per-class confusion would likely improve robustness, especially for non-dominant classes.
- Evaluation protocol simulates worker responses from a pre-collected pool (sampled without replacement). While this controls variance across methods, it can distort the adaptivity benefits and doesn’t model feedback loops (e.g., workers improving with context). Clarify how the protocol handles more than five repeated queries and whether saturation effects occur.
- Missing details on computational performance: planning time per decision, number of simulations/rollouts, and how this scales with sequence length and number of outstanding queries. Real-time viability depends critically on these.
- Hyperparameter selection for wM and wT and latency model pT are not fully specified. Since policy behavior hinges on these, a sensitivity analysis is needed.
- No ablations to isolate contributions (e.g., sequential vs. parallel querying, effect of progressive widening, effect of structured model vs. independent labels).

Clarity
Overall well written and organized, with helpful intuitive examples. Suggestions:
- Precisely define the “Accuracy” function used in ExpAcc, and reconcile with F1 used in reporting. Consider optimizing a differentiable surrogate for F1 or calibrating probabilities to F1 utility.
- Detail how wM and wT are chosen (grid search? user-specified target budgets/SLAs?) and provide sensitivity curves.
- Provide algorithmic details: number of simulations, branching controls, rollout policies, and wall-clock overhead.
- Clarify the response-time model pT and whether it adapts online to observed latencies.

Originality
The combination of a structured prediction environment, real-time crowd querying, and Bayesian decision-theoretic planning via MCTS appears novel and nontrivial. Prior components exist, but the integration and focus on latency–cost–accuracy tradeoffs for per-instance decisions at deployment time are original.

Significance
This is a timely and impactful direction for deployable ML systems under scarce labels and latency constraints. The results suggest practical gains, and the code release enhances reproducibility and likely follow-on work. Extensions to richer worker models and broader tasks could further increase impact.

Pros
- Principled decision-theoretic framework for on-the-job learning with explicit cost/latency.
- Handles structured outputs and partial labeling.
- Practical planner with demonstrated improvements on multiple tasks.
- Open-source implementation and reproducible setup.

Cons
- Simplified and potentially miscalibrated response/utility models.
- Simulation-based evaluation may not fully capture real interactive behavior.
- Missing runtime/scalability characterization and ablations.
- Utility–metric mismatch (accuracy vs. F1) not addressed.

Questions for the authors
1. How are wM and wT chosen in practice, and how sensitive are results to these values?
2. What are the planning compute times per decision across the three tasks? How many simulations per step?
3. How does performance change if pR is learned (e.g., per-class confusion, worker reliability) instead of fixed at 0.7?
4. Can you report results when optimizing a utility aligned with F1 (or a calibrated approximation)?
5. In the simulation protocol, what happens if more than five redundant queries are issued for a label?

Suggestions for improvement
- Learn and adapt pR online with worker and class-specific reliability; compare to fixed 0.7.
- Calibrate model probabilities and align the planning utility with the evaluation metric (F1), or show that ExpAcc is a good proxy empirically.
- Add ablations (no structuring, no progressive widening, batch vs. sequential queries) and detailed runtime stats.
- Provide sensitivity analyses over wM, wT, and latency distributions.
- Include a small-scale exact or near-exact planner on toy problems as a sanity check.

Recommendation: Weak Accept. The paper offers a principled, practical framework with encouraging results and clear potential for impact, though some modeling and evaluation aspects should be strengthened.
