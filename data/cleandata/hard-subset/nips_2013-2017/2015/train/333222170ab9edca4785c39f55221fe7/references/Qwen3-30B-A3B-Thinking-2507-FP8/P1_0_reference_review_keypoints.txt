- Introduces "on-the-job learning": a framework that maintains high accuracy from first prediction by querying crowd for uncertain input parts and learning online.  
- Models the process as a stochastic game and applies Bayesian decision theory to trade off accuracy, latency, and cost.  
- Proposes an approximate solution using Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) with progressive widening to handle continuous-time responses.  
- Distinguishes clearly from online learning and active classification; positions contribution relative to Legion:AR.  

- Strength: technically sound, well-motivated problem formulation, and principled Bayesian/game-theoretic approach.  
- Strength: strong experimental evaluation across NER, sentiment, and image tasks with multiple baselines.  
- Strength: significant empirical results — e.g., 88.4% F1 on NER with >10x cost reduction versus 5-vote human baseline.  
- Strength: clear writing, good organization, and effective figures.  
- Strength: reproducibility — implementation, crowdsourcing setup, code and data released.  

- Weakness: no theoretical guarantees or analysis of the MCTS approximation’s performance or optimality gaps.  
- Weakness: limited discussion of practical limitations (handling consistently wrong crowd responses, scaling to large inputs, and vague claims about reduced supervision).  
- Weakness: insufficient comparison to related work on active learning with noisy labels (e.g., Golovin et al. 2010).  
- Weakness: some implementation details unclear, particularly how progressive widening handles continuous-time beyond sampling response times.  

- Overall: strong, novel, and practically significant contribution with excellent experiments and clarity but lacking theoretical analysis of the approximation.  
- Recommendation: accept, with suggestion to add discussion of theoretical properties and limitations in the camera-ready version.
