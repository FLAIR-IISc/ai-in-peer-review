Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper introduces an on-the-job learning framework that deploys a model without pretraining and achieves high accuracy from the outset by querying a crowd selectively and asynchronously at test time. The system models decision making as a stochastic game between the system and the crowd. Given a structured prediction model pθ(y|x) (e.g., a CRF), the system chooses actions: issue label queries for specific positions, wait for outstanding responses (with stochastic response times), or return a prediction. A Bayesian decision-theoretic utility trades off expected accuracy against monetary cost per query and latency cost. Because exact planning is intractable, the paper uses Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) with UCT and progressive widening to handle the continuous time dimension.

The environment model factorizes p(y, r, t | x, q, s) into the model posterior pθ(y|x), a response model pR(r|yq) (assumed 70% worker accuracy with uniform noise otherwise), and a response-time model pT(t|s). After receiving crowd responses, the system updates its posterior over labels and can issue additional queries adaptively. The model parameters are updated online using observed crowd responses as (noisy) supervision.

Experiments on NER, sentiment, and face attribute classification compare LENSE against: majority-vote n-human baselines, an “online learning” baseline (using gold labels of past examples, but no test-time queries), and a heuristic threshold baseline. Using an AMT retainer pool for responsiveness, the authors pre-collect five responses and delays per label to control worker-quality variance during evaluation. Results show substantial cost reductions at comparable quality relative to pure human annotation, and higher accuracy than pure ML baselines early on. On NER, the paper reports ~88% F1 with >10× cost reduction vs. five-vote and improvements over single-vote and online learning. The paper argues that Bayesian decision theory and explicit reasoning about time (parallel vs. sequential queries) are key to LENSE’s gains. Code and data are released.

This work connects to online learning (Cesa-Bianchi & Lugosi), active learning (Settles; Chu et al.), test-time cost-sensitive active classification (Greiner et al.; Gao & Koller), crowdsourcing control (TurKontrol; Dai et al.), real-time crowd systems (retainer model; Lasecki et al.), and structured prediction with partial observations (Liang et al.). The most similar system-level prior is Legion:AR’s real-time labeling for activity recognition; the paper aims to provide a more general decision-theoretic foundation and handle structured outputs.

Strengths:
- Clear problem formulation that unifies test-time querying, crowdsourcing latency, and online model improvement under a decision-theoretic framework.
- Novel combination: stochastic game view + continuous-time MCTS with progressive widening for planning queries under asynchronous responses.
- Practicality: supports parallel/sequential query trade-offs; allows cold-start deployment with high accuracy; online updating reduces supervision over time, especially with richer features.
- Empirical benefits with code/data release; consistent gains over human-only and machine-only baselines; structured prediction setting (NER) shows strong advantage.

Weaknesses:
- Response model pR is simplistic (fixed 70% accuracy, uniform noise), ignoring per-worker variability, item difficulty, and context. This can bias both the planner and posterior updates; existing models (e.g., Dawid–Skene; TurKontrol) could improve realism and decisions.
- The utility definition and accuracy metric are underspecified. ExpAcc is defined as Ep(y)[Accuracy(argmaxy′ p(y′))], but the paper does not clarify whether accuracy is token-wise (Hamming) or sequence-level, and how ExpAcc is computed efficiently for structured outputs. For 0-1 loss, ExpAcc reduces to maxy p(y); for Hamming, it becomes sum over max marginals. Clarification is needed.
- Experimental protocol uses pre-collected five responses and delays per label, sampled without replacement. While it controls variance across methods, it restricts adaptivity and may not reflect real-world stochasticity (especially heavy-tailed delays or long-run worker drift). Live ablations (without frozen pools) and sensitivity to response-time distributions would strengthen claims about latency-aware planning.
- Baselines could be stronger: comparisons to decision-theoretic/myopic value-of-information policies, POMDP-based controllers (e.g., extensions of TurKontrol to structured outputs), or static policies learned for active classification would contextualize the benefit of MCTS. The “threshold baseline” seems tuned to the 0.7 noise assumption and does not reason about time.
- Limited detail on hyperparameters (wM, wT, UCT constant, planning budget), computational cost per decision, and sensitivity analyses. Since planning must be real-time, per-example wall-clock planning overhead is important.
- Claims such as “boosting performance relative to the expert provided labels” need clarification: if evaluation is against expert gold, the system cannot surpass it unless the “expert provided labels” are different from the evaluation gold or multiple annotation layers exist.

Quality:
- Conceptually sound formulation; the planning approximation is standard and appropriate. However, the assumptions behind pR and ExpAcc, and the experimental protocol’s reliance on frozen responses, limit the strength of the empirical evidence. More robust baselines and ablations, plus clearer evaluation metrics and cost–latency settings, would improve rigor.

Clarity:
- Generally well written and organized. Missing details include accuracy metric used in utility, parameter choices (wM, wT, UCT/pw parameters), computational budget/latency for planning, and learning details for pθ (features, training with noisy labels). Figures/tables referenced but not fully described in the text provided.

Originality:
- Moderate to good. The core idea—decision-theoretic test-time querying with asynchronous crowd responses combined with online learning—has elements present in Legion:AR, TurKontrol, and active classification. The specific stochastic game instantiation with continuous-time MCTS for structured prediction appears novel.

Significance:
- Potentially useful for rapid-deployment ML systems that must deliver quality immediately without training data, especially in structured prediction tasks. If generalized worker/latency models and stronger baselines are addressed, impact could be substantive for human-in-the-loop ML.

Questions and suggestions:
- Specify the accuracy metric used in the utility and how ExpAcc is computed for structured outputs. Provide ablations comparing exact-match vs. token-wise metrics.
- Report sensitivity to wM and wT and to the assumed pR; explore learned/worker-specific pR (e.g., Dawid–Skene or Bayesian hierarchical models) and adaptive calibration.
- Include baselines using myopic VOI/expected error reduction and POMDP controllers; compare policies with/without explicit time modeling.
- Provide real-time computational overhead and decision latency; include live (non-frozen) crowd experiments and sensitivity to response-time distributions.
- Analyze per-task learning curves and feature effects more systematically; clarify the “expert labels” statement.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Clear, pragmatic framework with a solid decision-theoretic core.
- Novel planning approach for asynchronous test-time querying; structured outputs handled cleanly.
- Empirical gains and released code/data.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Simplistic and fixed noise model; unclear accuracy/utility computation; limited baseline comparisons.
- Evaluation protocol with frozen responses may not fully validate time-aware planning advantages.
- Insufficient details on hyperparameters and planning costs; some claims need clarification.
