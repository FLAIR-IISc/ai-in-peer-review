This paper introduces a framework for on-the-job learning, where a system learns from crowd-sourced labels while simultaneously making predictions. It formalizes this as a stochastic game using Bayesian decision theory to balance latency, cost, and accuracy. The core idea builds upon prior work in active learning and online learning, but distinguishes itself by operating during test time and dynamically adapting its querying strategy based on observed data. The authors demonstrate the approach on NER, sentiment classification, and image classification tasks, achieving significant cost reductions compared to full human annotation.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The combination of Bayesian decision theory, stochastic game formulation, and dynamic adaptation during test time presents a novel approach to learning with limited labeled data. It explicitly addresses the trade-offs inherent in querying a crowd in a real-time setting.
*   **Technical Soundness:** The modeling of on-the-job learning as a stochastic game seems well-reasoned, and the use of Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) and progressive widening provides a practical approach to approximating the optimal policy. The utility function effectively captures the competing objectives.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the problem setting, proposed solution, and experimental setup. The figures (particularly Figure 2) are helpful for visualizing the process.
*   **Empirical Results:** The experimental results demonstrate significant improvements in cost-effectiveness and accuracy compared to several baselines, including full human annotation and online learning. The ablation study (Figure 4) is insightful.
*   **Reproducibility:** The authors provide open-source code and data, which is a major strength.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Limited Theoretical Analysis:** While the model is theoretically grounded in Bayesian decision theory, the paper lacks a deeper analysis of the approximation used for solving the stochastic game. The performance guarantees (or lack thereof) of the MCTS-based approximation are not discussed.
*   **Crowdsourcing Setup Details:** The paper mentions using a "retainer model" on Amazon Mechanical Turk, but lacks sufficient details about the worker selection process, quality control measures, and potential biases in the crowd-sourced labels.  A more detailed discussion of how these factors were addressed would strengthen the evaluation.
*   **Task Complexity & Generalization:** The performance gains are most pronounced on NER. The results on sentiment classification and image classification are less dramatic, suggesting the approach may be more effective for certain types of tasks. Further exploration of task-specific factors would be beneficial.
*   **Comparison with Legion:AR:**  The paper mentions Legion:AR [6] as related work, but the comparison feels superficial.  A more in-depth discussion of the differences and similarities between the two systems would be valuable.
*   **Assumptions about Response Times:** The use of pre-collected worker response times simplifies the problem but could limit the model's ability to adapt to changing crowd conditions.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper tackles a practically important problem – learning with limited labeled data in real-time.
*   The proposed approach is novel, well-motivated, and technically sound.
*   The empirical results are compelling and demonstrate significant improvements over existing methods.
*   The code and data are publicly available, facilitating reproducibility.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The theoretical analysis is limited, and the approximation used for solving the stochastic game is not thoroughly investigated.
*   The crowdsourcing setup lacks sufficient detail, raising concerns about potential biases.
*   The generalization of the approach to different tasks is unclear.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that presents a novel and promising approach to on-the-job learning. The technical contributions are solid, the empirical results are compelling, and the code is publicly available. While there are some minor weaknesses, they do not detract significantly from the overall quality of the work. I believe this paper would be a valuable addition to NIPS.

**Recommendation:** Accept.
