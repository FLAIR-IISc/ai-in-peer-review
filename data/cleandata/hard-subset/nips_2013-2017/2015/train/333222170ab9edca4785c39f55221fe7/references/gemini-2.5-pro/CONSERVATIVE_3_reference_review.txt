**Summary**
This paper introduces a framework termed "on-the-job learning," designed to build accurate prediction systems from the outset without a large pre-existing labeled dataset. The core idea is to dynamically and strategically query a human crowd for labels on uncertain parts of an input *before* making a final prediction. The system learns online from these crowd responses, gradually reducing its reliance on human input over time. The authors formalize this problem as a stochastic game between the system and the crowd. The decision-making process—whether to query, wait for a response, or return a prediction—is governed by Bayesian decision theory, aiming to optimize a utility function that balances accuracy, monetary cost, and latency. Due to the intractability of finding the optimal policy, the authors propose an approximate solution using Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) with progressive widening. The proposed system, LENSE, is evaluated on three tasks (NER, sentiment analysis, and image classification), demonstrating significant cost reduction compared to pure crowdsourcing and accuracy improvements over a standard online learning baseline.

**Strengths**
1.  **Problem Formulation:** The paper addresses a practical and important problem: deploying a reliable ML system in scenarios where labeled data is scarce. The formulation of this problem as a sequential decision-making process under uncertainty is principled and compelling. The use of Bayesian decision theory to explicitly model the trade-offs between accuracy, cost, and latency provides a solid theoretical foundation.

2.  **Technical Approach:** The application of MCTS to navigate the complex decision space is a novel and appropriate choice. This is a non-trivial adaptation of a technique from game playing to a human-in-the-loop machine learning setting. Combining it with progressive widening to handle the continuous nature of time is a technically sound contribution.

3.  **Experimental Rigor:** The evaluation is conducted across three distinct tasks, demonstrating the general applicability of the framework. The authors' efforts to ensure reproducibility by open-sourcing their code and data are commendable. The use of a "frozen pool" of crowd responses is a reasonable methodological choice to control for the high variance in live crowdworker performance, enabling more direct comparison between methods.

**Weaknesses**
1.  **Baseline Comparisons:** While the paper includes several baselines, their strength and relevance are questionable. The "Online learning" baseline, which receives the gold label only after making a prediction, is not a fair point of comparison. The core premise of the proposed method is to gather information *before* predicting. A much stronger and more relevant baseline would be an established online *active learning* algorithm, which also selects instances (or in this case, parts of instances) to be labeled. For example, a strategy that queries for the full label of an instance based on model uncertainty would be a more standard and challenging competitor. The "Threshold baseline" is a simple heuristic of the proposed method itself; outperforming it primarily validates the sophistication of the MCTS planner but does not sufficiently benchmark the overall "on-the-job learning" paradigm against existing approaches.

2.  **Simplifications in the Environment Model:** The model of the crowd is overly simplistic and may not reflect real-world complexities, potentially making the decision problem easier than it is in practice.
    *   The assumption of a fixed, known worker accuracy (pR(r|yq) = 0.7) is a significant simplification. In practice, worker quality is heterogeneous and often unknown a priori. More established work in crowdsourcing (e.g., TurKontrol, which the authors cite) employs more sophisticated models to estimate individual worker reliability online. The optimal policy derived by the MCTS planner is likely highly dependent on this known noise parameter, and it is unclear how the method would perform with unknown or time-varying worker quality.
    *   The paper does not detail how the prediction model `pθ` is updated online and how the MCTS planner accounts for the fact that the model will improve in the future. The current formulation seems to plan actions for a single instance `x` with a static model `pθ`, use the responses to update `pθ`, and then repeat the process on the next instance. This myopic planning horizon ignores the long-term benefit of querying for exploration (i.e., to improve the model for future instances), which is a key aspect of active learning.

3.  **Positioning and Novelty:** The paper frames "on-the-job learning" as a new framework. However, it bears strong resemblance to active classification, where feature costs are considered at test time. The main distinctions cited are that labels are never observed (only noisy responses) and the model improves at test time. While valid, the conceptual leap is perhaps not as large as presented. The contribution lies more in the specific formulation as a stochastic game and the MCTS-based solution method rather than in the invention of a wholly new learning paradigm.

4.  **Sensitivity and Ablation:** The utility function `U(σ)` is central to the method, yet there is no analysis of the system's sensitivity to the weights `wM` (monetary cost) and `wT` (time cost). The behavior of the system could change dramatically with different cost structures, and an exploration of this parameter space would have significantly strengthened the paper's conclusions about the practicality of the approach.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Addresses a well-motivated, practical problem.
*   Presents a principled framework based on Bayesian decision theory.
*   The technical contribution of applying MCTS with progressive widening to this human-in-the-loop setting is novel and sound.
*   The empirical results, despite the weak baselines, are promising and demonstrate the potential of the approach.
*   The work is reproducible, with code and data provided.

**Arguments for Rejection:**
*   The baselines used for comparison are not the most competitive or appropriate for the problem, particularly the lack of a strong online active learning baseline. This makes it difficult to gauge the true advance over the state of the art.
*   The crowd model is overly simplified (e.g., fixed, known accuracy), which may limit the applicability of the findings to real-world, dynamic crowdsourcing environments.
*   The paper could be strengthened by a more thorough comparison to the active classification literature and a sensitivity analysis of the key utility function parameters.

**Conclusion:**
This paper presents a well-engineered and principled solution to an interesting and practical problem. The core technical idea of using MCTS to optimize the query policy is solid. However, the evaluation is let down by a choice of baselines that do not represent the strongest alternative approaches, making the claimed improvements difficult to situate within the broader literature of active and online learning. Furthermore, the simplified assumptions about the crowd environment raise questions about the robustness of the method in more realistic settings. While the work is of good quality, the weaknesses in the experimental validation prevent me from giving it a strong endorsement. I am currently borderline on this paper, leaning towards rejection, but would be open to changing my mind if the authors could provide a more compelling justification for their choice of baselines or include comparisons against more established active learning methods.
