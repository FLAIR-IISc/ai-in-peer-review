Summary
The paper proposes “on-the-job learning,” a decision-theoretic framework that, at test time, issues targeted crowd queries to reduce uncertainty before predicting, while updating a CRF model online so reliance on the crowd diminishes over time. The problem is cast as a stochastic game between the system and the crowd, with utility trading off expected predictive accuracy, monetary query cost, and latency. Because exact planning is intractable, the authors approximate the optimal policy using Monte Carlo Tree Search with progressive widening to handle continuous response times. Experiments on NER, sentiment, and image classification compare the method (LENSE) to human n-vote, an “online learning” oracle that sees gold labels post hoc, and a heuristic threshold baseline. The authors report better quality-cost trade-offs, substantial cost reductions relative to multi-vote annotation, and decreasing query rates over time.

Relation to prior work
The paper intersects online learning, active learning, test-time feature acquisition/budgeted prediction, and crowd-POMDP control. Decision-theoretic control of crowd workflows (e.g., TurKontrol/POMDP-based approaches) and real-time crowd systems (e.g., Legion:AR) are close. Test-cost-sensitive and budgeted prediction (e.g., Greiner et al.; Chai et al.; structured prediction cascades) and myopic value-of-information policies are also relevant. The contribution here is the integration of (i) structured prediction (CRFs), (ii) asynchronous crowd responses with latency modeling, and (iii) MCTS for planning.

Strengths
- Clear problem formulation and principled objective trading off accuracy, time, and cost.
- Incorporates structured prediction; queries at token-level and reasons about correlations.
- Accounts for asynchronous response times and parallel vs sequential querying choices.
- Practical planning approach (MCTS + progressive widening) with code released.
- Empirical results suggest better cost-quality trade-offs than simple human voting and a heuristic baseline, and decreasing crowd dependence over time.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Environment model is simplistic and largely unlearned: a fixed, class-independent 70% accuracy for all workers and labels, with uniform error, is unrealistic. No worker- or class-specific reliabilities, no confusion matrices, and no online calibration. This undermines the Bayes-optimality claims and may bias policies.
- Utility/evaluation mismatch: the policy optimizes expected accuracy of the MAP labeling, while the main reported sequence-level metric for NER is F1. There is no demonstration that optimizing the chosen utility correlates with F1, nor any attempt to optimize for F1-like utilities.
- Baselines are weak. Missing:
  - Decision-theoretic/myopic VOI policies (e.g., one-step lookahead) and POMDP baselines used in crowd control literature (e.g., TurKontrol variants).
  - Strong aggregation baselines for crowds (Dawid–Skene, GLAD) and dynamic stopping with redundancy.
  - Budgeted/test-time feature acquisition methods and structured prediction cascades.
  - Stronger task models (e.g., modern NER architectures) for a realistic comparison of the “pure ML” path.
- Experimental protocol raises validity questions:
  - The “frozen pool” of only 5 pre-collected responses and delays per label is extremely small and may cause overfitting of policies to idiosyncratic samples; it also removes real-world variability and strategic behavior of workers.
  - No statistical significance, confidence intervals, or sensitivity analyses; claims like “order of magnitude cost reduction” are relative to simplistic baselines (e.g., 5-vote) and may not hold against optimized dynamic stopping/aggregation.
  - Utility weights wM and wT are not clearly specified or tuned; no ablation or sensitivity to these crucial parameters.
  - Response-time model pT is not described in sufficient detail (parametric form? fitted how?), and the tractability/accuracy trade-offs introduced by progressive widening are not analyzed.
- Planning approximation: MCTS + progressive widening is reasonable, but there is no analysis of convergence, no ablation vs simpler heuristics (e.g., greedy entropy reduction, Thompson sampling variants), and limited insight into when parallel vs sequential querying emerges from the utility rather than being hand-induced.
- Modeling limitations: cannot issue a new query while “waiting,” which is a material constraint in real-time systems; the authors acknowledge it simplifies search but do not quantify the impact.
- Some claims (e.g., outperforming expert labels) need careful qualification; the paper should clarify what “expert” means, how the ground truth is defined, and ensure no circularity.

Quality
Technically coherent at a high level, but the empirical support is not rigorous enough for a new decision-theoretic framework. The simplified noise model, weak baselines, small frozen response pools, and lack of statistical analysis limit confidence in the reported gains.

Clarity
Generally well written, with a clear definition of the game and utility. However, important details are missing: how ExpAcc is computed for structured outputs; precise definitions of Accuracy vs F1; specification/estimation of pT; values/tuning of wM, wT; dataset sizes/splits; and significance testing. A more thorough description of the MCTS configuration and computational budget is needed.

Originality
Moderately novel combination: structured prediction + asynchronous crowds + MCTS planning. However, much of the framing overlaps with existing decision-theoretic crowd control and budgeted prediction literature. The paper would benefit from clearer differentiation and stronger empirical evidence.

Significance
Potentially useful in applications requiring immediate high accuracy with limited training data. As presented, however, the methodological choices and experimental design do not convincingly establish superiority over stronger, established baselines, limiting practical impact.

Questions for the authors
- How are wM and wT chosen per task? Do results change materially under different trade-offs?
- How is pT parameterized and estimated? Do you adapt pT online?
- Why fix pR at 0.7 uniformly? Can you learn worker- and class-specific reliabilities online? Sensitivity to this assumption?
- How is ExpAcc computed for sequences, and why optimize accuracy while reporting F1?
- Can you report CIs and significance tests, and include stronger baselines (DS/GLAD, myopic VOI, dynamic stopping)?

Suggestions for improvement
- Learn a richer noise model (per-worker, per-class confusion) and update it online.
- Align utility with evaluation metrics (e.g., token- or entity-level F1) or provide evidence that the chosen utility correlates with F1.
- Add strong baselines from POMDP-based crowd control, myopic VOI, and budgeted prediction/structured cascades; include dynamic stopping with advanced aggregation.
- Replace the frozen 5-response setup with larger, more realistic simulations and/or real-time experiments; report variance and statistical tests.
- Provide sensitivity analyses for wM, wT, pR, and pT; ablate MCTS vs greedy/myopic strategies.

Pros
- Principled formulation; handles structured outputs and latency.
- Practical planning approximation; code released.
- Promising cost-quality trade-offs in initial results.

Cons
- Simplistic and unlearned noise/latency models; utility/evaluation mismatch.
- Weak baselines and limited experimental rigor; small, artificial simulation pool.
- Missing sensitivity analyses and statistical significance; constrained action model.

Recommendation: Weak reject
Interesting direction with a principled framing, but the current empirical validation and modeling assumptions fall short of the rigor needed to endorse a new decision-theoretic framework over established alternatives.
