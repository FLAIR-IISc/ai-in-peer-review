Summary
This paper proposes “on-the-job learning,” a decision-theoretic framework that interleaves prediction with targeted, real-time crowdsourcing to maintain high accuracy from the outset and reduce crowd dependence over time as the model improves. The setting is formalized as a stochastic game between the system and a crowd “environment” with asynchronous, noisy responses and response-time uncertainty. The system plans which micro-queries to issue (e.g., token labels in a CRF), when to wait, and when to commit, to optimize a utility that trades off expected accuracy, monetary cost, and latency. Optimal planning is intractable; the authors approximate it with Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) and progressive widening to handle continuous time. They implement LENSE and evaluate on NER, sentiment, and image classification, reporting substantial accuracy gains over online learning and cost reductions over pure crowdsourcing. The code and crowdsourcing setup are released.

Relation to prior work: The work connects to online learning (Cesa-Bianchi & Lugosi), active learning (Settles), active classification/value-of-information (Gao & Koller), decision-theoretic control of crowdsourcing (TurKontrol; Dai, Mausam & Weld), and real-time crowd systems (retainer model; Bernstein et al.; Legion:AR by Lasecki et al.). The novelty lies in combining a Bayesian decision-theoretic controller with structured prediction, explicit latency modeling, and on-the-job model improvement, using MCTS to plan over asynchronous responses.

Strengths
- Timely, well-motivated problem: how to deploy accurate systems immediately when labeled data are scarce, leveraging the crowd adaptively at test time.
- Principled formulation: explicit utility trades off accuracy, time, and cost; stochastic game captures uncertainty in responses and latency; planning via MCTS is a reasonable approximation.
- Handles structured outputs: integrates a CRF posterior to decide which positions to query, exploiting label correlations.
- Empirical benefits: On NER, substantial F1 improvement and order-of-magnitude cost reduction compared to multi-vote crowds; sensible qualitative behavior (redundant querying for noisy items, parallel vs sequential strategies).
- Practicality: real-time retainer pool implementation; code, data, and experiments released.

Weaknesses and points needing clarification
Quality
- Response and latency models are overly simplistic: pR assumes a fixed 70% accuracy independent of worker, item difficulty, or class, and uniform noise otherwise. No learning of worker/item reliabilities (e.g., Dawid–Skene) or adaptation over time. pT is referenced but not specified or validated.
- Utility/metric mismatch: the utility uses ExpAcc of the MAP, but the main evaluation uses sequence-level F1 for NER. It is unclear how ExpAcc is computed for structured outputs and whether the optimized utility aligns with F1. Please define the accuracy function used in Eq. (2) and discuss calibration.
- Experimental protocol: To control worker variance, the system samples from five pre-collected responses per label without replacement. This is a very small pool and risks bias/overfitting to a narrow quality/latency regime; it also weakens claims about real-time robustness. Live A/B trials or larger pools would strengthen conclusions.
- Baselines are limited. Missing are:
  - Decision-theoretic controllers for crowdsourcing (e.g., TurKontrol-style POMDPs or myopic VOI policies).
  - Uncertainty/entropy-based sequential query policies that reason about label noise (beyond the provided static threshold baseline).
  - Aggregation methods (Dawid–Skene) under the same budget.
  - Budget-equivalent pretraining baselines (e.g., spend the same budget upfront to label a seed set and then run a purely ML system).
- No ablations or sensitivity analyses:
  - MCTS/planning depth vs. a greedy myopic policy.
  - Effect of progressive widening and continuous-time modeling.
  - Sensitivity to mis-specified pR and pT, and to the cost/time weights wM, wT (which are not reported).
- Computational aspects are underreported: runtime per instance, scalability with sequence length and branching factor, and the overhead of planning under latency uncertainty.

Clarity
- The paper is generally well written. However:
  - Specify the exact form of ExpAcc and how it is computed for the CRF (token accuracy? sequence accuracy? surrogate for F1?).
  - Detail pT (distributional form, parameter estimation) and how it conditions on si and remaining time.
  - Clarify how multiple noisy responses for the same position are integrated in the posterior and in training (are responses treated as soft observations, as features, or as hard labels?).
  - Explain how θ is updated online given noisy labels; discuss calibration of pθ and whether any debiasing/label-denoising is used.

Originality
- The combination of on-the-job learning, structured prediction, explicit latency modeling, and planning via MCTS is novel and interesting. Related lines (Legion:AR; TurKontrol; active classification) are cited, and this work advances the ML formulation and structured setting.

Significance
- The framework has the potential to influence human-in-the-loop ML where immediate high accuracy is needed. The NER gains are compelling. Broader significance would be bolstered by stronger baselines, live evaluations, and sensitivity/ablation studies.

Pros for acceptance
- Clear, principled formulation of a practically important problem.
- Novel planning approach for asynchronous, noisy crowds with continuous time.
- Demonstrated improvements and cost savings; open-source implementation.

Cons for acceptance
- Simplified environment model and limited empirical validation of its assumptions.
- Missing strong baselines and ablations; unclear alignment between optimized utility and reported metrics.
- Limited evaluation of latency trade-offs and computational scalability.
- Experimental protocol relies on a very small pre-collected response pool.

Suggestions for improvement
- Incorporate and/or learn richer worker and item models (e.g., Dawid–Skene, per-worker reliabilities, item difficulty), and study sensitivity to model misspecification.
- Align the utility with evaluation metrics (e.g., expected F1) or justify the surrogate; report calibration of pθ and the impact on ExpAcc.
- Add baselines: myopic VOI with uncertainty sampling, TurKontrol-like POMDP controller, DS aggregation under the same budget, and budget-equivalent pretraining.
- Provide ablations comparing MCTS to greedy policies; vary planning depth/branching; show the effect of progressive widening and continuous-time modeling.
- Report runtime and scalability; include latency–cost–accuracy curves by varying wT and wM, and show the parallel vs sequential querying regimes.
- Evaluate with larger, truly live retainer pools to validate robustness; expand beyond five stored responses per label.
- Clarify training with noisy labels and how multiple responses are fused during learning and inference.

Overall recommendation: Weak accept. The paper tackles an important problem with a principled, novel approach and promising results, but would benefit from stronger baselines, clearer utility–metric alignment, richer environment modeling, and more thorough empirical analysis.
