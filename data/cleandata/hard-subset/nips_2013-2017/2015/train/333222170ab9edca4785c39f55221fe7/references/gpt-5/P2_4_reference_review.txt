Summary
The paper formulates “on-the-job learning” for structured prediction: when a new input arrives, the system can asynchronously query crowd workers for selected parts of the output, trading off accuracy, latency, and monetary cost. The decision process is cast as a stochastic game between the system and the crowd, with a Bayesian decision-theoretic utility that balances expected prediction accuracy against time and query costs. The prediction model is a CRF; a simple noise model assumes crowd responses are correct with probability 0.7; response times are modeled to account for asynchrony. Because exact planning is intractable, the authors use Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) with progressive widening to handle continuous time. Experiments on NER (tweets), sentiment classification, and image classification suggest the approach (LENSE) can achieve higher accuracy and lower cost than purely human or purely machine baselines, and that costs decrease over time as the model improves. Code and data are released.

Relation to prior work
The work sits at the intersection of active learning, online learning, active classification (value of information at test time), crowdsourcing under uncertainty (e.g., POMDP-based TurKontrol), and structured prediction. Compared to online/active learning, the novelty is soliciting information before committing to a prediction to maintain high early accuracy. Compared to active classification, the policy adapts during test time and learns from noisy labels; unlike TurKontrol, the focus is on structured outputs, real-time asynchrony, and test-time learning rather than only vote aggregation. Legion:AR shares the “on-the-job” deployment aim but lacks the ML/game-theoretic formalization. The integration of structured prediction, asynchronous crowdsourcing, and MCTS-based planning is a useful advance, though many modeling choices are simplified.

Quality
Strengths:
- Clear decision-theoretic formulation, with an explicit utility function that captures accuracy, cost, and latency; the framework yields intuitive behaviors (parallel queries for latency, redundancy for noise).
- Sound use of MCTS and progressive widening for planning under uncertainty with continuous time.
- Empirical evaluation on three tasks and analysis of evolving costs over time; open-source implementation enhances reproducibility.

Weaknesses/concerns:
- Modeling assumptions are strong and potentially mis-specified: a fixed pR with 0.7 correctness for all items/workers ignores worker heterogeneity and instance difficulty; response noise is independent across queries; response time distribution is exogenous and not conditioned on workload or worker. These simplifications could materially affect the optimal policy and reported gains.
- Utility mismatch: the planning objective uses expected accuracy of the MAP prediction, while the main NER metric reported is F1; this could bias query decisions and conflates sequence vs token-level measures.
- Baselines could be stronger. The threshold baseline is heuristic; a greedy value-of-information baseline using calibrated posteriors, or a POMDP policy akin to TurKontrol adapted to structured outputs, would better contextualize the contribution. The “online learning” baseline uses gold labels for previous examples but cannot query at test time, which is not an apples-to-apples operational comparison.
- Evaluation protocol uses pre-collected pools of 5 worker responses/latencies per label and samples without replacement to control variance. This reduces realism of the asynchronous, dynamic pool (and may dampen variance benefits of redundancy), and may bias latency/quality distributions.
- Limited ablations: there is no study disentangling the impact of MCTS vs a simple greedy policy, or the impact of progressive widening; little sensitivity analysis on the cost/latency weights (wM, wT), or on mis-specification of the 0.7 accuracy assumption.
- Computational overhead is not reported; given real-time constraints and MCTS rollouts, it is important to quantify decision latency.

Clarity
Overall clearly written and organized, with a precise game definition and algorithm description. Some details should be added for reproducibility and understanding:
- Specify how wM and wT are set and provide sensitivity curves.
- Clarify whether “accuracy” in the utility is sequence-level, token-level, or approximated, and how that relates to F1 reporting.
- Provide hyperparameters and rollout budgets for MCTS, and report planning time per decision.
- Explain how CRF training handles noisy labels; are crowd labels treated as gold, and is any calibration/EM used?

Originality
The core ideas—decision-theoretic test-time querying for structured outputs with asynchronous responses, and using MCTS with progressive widening—compose a novel and interesting combination. Prior work has elements of each piece, but the integration and focus on immediate high accuracy without pretraining is fresh. That said, aspects of the environment/worker modeling are simpler than the state of the art in crowdsourcing (e.g., worker/item difficulty models).

Significance
If validated more thoroughly, the framework could influence how practitioners build deployable systems under data scarcity and tight latency/cost constraints. The demonstration on three tasks and the open-source release add practical impact. However, the strength of the claims is limited by simplified noise modeling, limited baselines, and evaluation design choices; stronger empirical comparisons and robustness analyses would bolster significance.

Pros
- Clear, principled formulation; intuitive and practical decision policies emerge.
- Handles structured prediction and asynchrony; practical planning with MCTS.
- Empirical gains and decreasing human cost over time; code available.

Cons
- Simplistic and potentially mis-specified worker/noise models; no learning of pR or worker/item difficulty.
- Utility/metric mismatch (accuracy vs F1); limited sensitivity analyses.
- Baselines are weak; lack of comparison to stronger VOI/POMDP methods.
- Real-time computational cost not reported; evaluation relies on simulated pools.

Questions and suggestions
- Learn pR and pT from data, including per-worker reliability and item difficulty; analyze robustness to mis-specification.
- Align the utility with the evaluation metric (e.g., expected F1 or a surrogate) and report sensitivity to wM and wT.
- Add stronger baselines (greedy VOI, adapted TurKontrol-like policies) and ablations (greedy vs MCTS; without progressive widening).
- Report decision latency and rollout budgets; discuss scalability.
- Test with live crowd pools in addition to the controlled simulation to validate asynchrony and variability.

Recommendation
Weak accept. The paper presents a timely, well-motivated, and principled framework with encouraging results and open-source code. With stronger baselines, learned crowd models, and more robust evaluation, it could be a solid contribution to NeurIPS.
