### **Summary**

This paper introduces "on-the-job learning," a framework for building high-accuracy AI systems from scratch by intelligently combining machine learning with real-time crowdsourcing. The core problem it addresses is the cold-start issue, where one lacks the labeled data to train an effective model but still needs to make accurate predictions from the very first input. The proposed solution is a system that, upon receiving a new input, can strategically query a crowd of human annotators for labels on specific, uncertain parts of the input before making a final prediction. Over time, the system learns from the crowd's responses, gradually reducing its reliance on human input.

The authors formalize this process as a stochastic game between the system and the crowd. The system's actions include querying, waiting for responses, or returning a final prediction. Bayesian decision theory is used to define a utility function that trades off prediction accuracy, monetary cost (per query), and latency (time spent waiting). Since computing the optimal policy for this game is intractable, the authors propose an approximation based on Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) combined with progressive widening to handle the continuous nature of time. The framework is evaluated on NER, sentiment analysis, and image classification tasks, demonstrating significant reductions in cost and improvements in accuracy compared to pure-ML (online learning) and pure-crowd baselines.

This work relates to several areas discussed at NIPS, including active learning, online learning, and decision-theoretic planning. It differs from online/active learning by actively seeking information *before* making a prediction to guarantee high initial accuracy. It differs from active classification by allowing the decision-making policy to improve at test time as the underlying model learns.

### **Strengths and Weaknesses**

**Strengths:**

1.  **Significance and Novelty of the Problem Formulation:** The "on-the-job learning" setting is highly practical and addresses a common and important real-world challenge. The idea of using a crowd as a "crutch" that a system can lean on less and less over time is compelling. This paper provides a formal, principled foundation for this intuitive concept, moving beyond the heuristic approach of prior work like Legion:AR [6].

2.  **Technical Quality:** The decision-theoretic formulation is elegant and appropriate for the problem. Modeling the interaction as a stochastic game and using a utility function to balance accuracy, cost, and latency is a powerful and principled approach. The use of MCTS with progressive widening is a sound and well-justified method for approximating the intractable optimal policy.

3.  **Experimental Rigor and Reproducibility:** The empirical evaluation is strong. The authors test their system (LENSE) on three diverse tasks and compare it against well-chosen baselines, including a pure-human approach, a strong online learning oracle, and a clever heuristic baseline. The methodology is sound, particularly the use of a pre-collected, "frozen" pool of worker responses to ensure fair and reproducible comparisons across methods. The provision of all code, data, and experiments on CodaLab is a major plus and sets a high standard for reproducibility.

4.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is exceptionally well-written. The motivation is clear, the model is described in sufficient detail, and the experimental results are presented effectively. The figures and tables are informative and support the paper's claims well.

5.  **Impressive Results:** The results strongly support the claims. An order-of-magnitude cost reduction on the NER task for comparable accuracy is a significant achievement. The learning curves in Figure 3, which show how the need for supervision decreases faster with a more powerful model (RNN vs. unigrams), provide excellent insight into the system's dynamics.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Simplicity of Environment Models:** The models for the crowd are quite simple. The response model `p_R` assumes a uniform 70% accuracy for all workers and all labels, which is unlikely to hold in practice. Worker quality varies, and some labels are inherently more confusable than others. Similarly, the response time model `p_T` is not detailed. While these simplifications are understandable for tractability, the paper would be strengthened by a discussion of the framework's sensitivity to a mis-specified environment model. For example, how would the policy adapt if it learned worker-specific or label-pair-specific confusion models online?

2.  **Computational Cost of the Planner:** The MCTS planner is run to determine each action (or batch of actions). For complex structured prediction tasks with a large number of potential queries, this could be computationally intensive, potentially introducing its own latency that is not accounted for in the utility function. A discussion of the computational overhead of the decision-making process itself and its scalability would be beneficial.

3.  **Setting the Utility Function Parameters:** The utility function depends on weights `w_M` (cost per query) and `w_T` (cost per second). The paper does not discuss how these crucial hyper-parameters are set or how sensitive the system's performance is to their values. In a practical deployment, a user would need guidance on how to set these parameters to reflect their specific budget and latency requirements. A sensitivity analysis would have been a valuable addition.

### **Arguments For and Against Acceptance**

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Addresses a significant, practical, and under-explored problem in machine learning.
*   The proposed framework is novel, principled (based on Bayesian decision theory), and technically sound.
*   The experimental evaluation is comprehensive, rigorous, and shows state-of-the-art results.
*   The paper is extremely clear and well-written.
*   The commitment to reproducibility via CodaLab is exemplary.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   The models of the crowd (noise and latency) are simplified, and the impact of this simplification is not fully explored.
*   The computational feasibility of the MCTS planner for real-time decision-making is not discussed.
*   The paper lacks a sensitivity analysis for the key parameters in the utility function.

### **Detailed Comments and Questions for the Authors**

Overall, I think this is an excellent paper and I am strongly recommending acceptance. The weaknesses are minor and mostly represent opportunities for future work rather than fundamental flaws.

*   Regarding the crowd model: Could your framework be extended to learn the parameters of `p_R` and `p_T` online? For instance, by modeling individual worker reliabilities as done in works like TurKontrol [26]? This seems like a natural and important extension.
*   In your experiments, how many simulations were run within the MCTS algorithm at each decision step? What was the typical wall-clock time required to make a decision? This would help in assessing the practical deployment feasibility.
*   Figure 4 shows LENSE taking more time than the 1-vote baseline on NER but using fewer queries. This is an interesting outcome of the utility-based trade-off. It highlights the importance of setting `w_T` correctly. Could you comment on the value used in the experiment and how a different value might change this behavior? For example, could LENSE be configured to be faster than 1-vote at the expense of more queries (and thus higher cost)?

This work is a strong contribution that elegantly combines ideas from machine learning, crowdsourcing, and AI planning to create a system that is both practical and theoretically grounded. It is likely to inspire follow-up research and could have a significant impact on how interactive ML systems are built.
