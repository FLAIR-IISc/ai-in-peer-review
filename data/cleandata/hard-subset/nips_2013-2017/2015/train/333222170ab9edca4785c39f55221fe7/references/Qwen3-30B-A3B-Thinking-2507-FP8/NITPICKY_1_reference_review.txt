

# Review of "On-the-Job Learning with Bayesian Decision Theory"

This paper proposes a novel framework called "on-the-job learning" that combines active learning with crowdsourcing to build AI systems without requiring large labeled datasets upfront. The authors model the process as a stochastic game using Bayesian decision theory, balancing accuracy, cost, and latency through a utility function. They implement a system called LENSE that uses Monte Carlo tree search with progressive widening to approximate the optimal policy, and evaluate it on three tasks showing significant cost reductions compared to full human annotation.

The paper addresses a practical problem in deploying AI systems with limited resources, and the framework is well-motivated. The authors make a reasonable claim that their approach differs from existing work in online learning, active learning, and active classification by maintaining high accuracy from the start while gradually becoming self-sufficient. The technical core of modeling the problem as a stochastic game is interesting, and the implementation details are generally clear.

However, the paper contains several significant technical inconsistencies and minor but critical flaws that undermine the validity of the claims and the reproducibility of the results.

First, the paper contains a factual error in the example: "the crowd correctly responds that 'Soup' is a resource." In standard NER tasks, "Soup" would typically be labeled as NONE (not a resource), making this example misleading and confusing.

Second, there is a critical inconsistency in the crowd response model. The paper states "pR(r | yq) = 0.7 iff r = yq" but later claims "We found the humans we hired were roughly 70% accurate in our experiments." This is confusing - are they using a fixed 70% accuracy model, or is the model calibrated to their experimental results? The paper doesn't clarify whether the model's 70% accuracy assumption matches their empirical observations.

Third, the paper claims "more than an order of magnitude reduction in cost compared to full human annotation" but fails to specify what "full human annotation" means. The baseline they compare to (5-vote approach) is not clearly defined in terms of cost, making it impossible to verify this claim. The paper should explicitly state the cost of the baseline approach.

Fourth, there is a contradiction in the query mechanism: the paper states "the system can make as many queries in a row (i.e. simultaneously) as it wants" but then claims "This rules out the possibility of launching a query midway through waiting for the next response." If queries are truly simultaneous, there should be no "midway" point to launch a query. This needs clarification.

Fifth, the paper has several formatting inconsistencies: inconsistent spacing in equations (e.g., "k∏ i=1" should be "\prod_{i=1}^k"), incorrect punctuation ("e.g. query" should be "e.g., query"), and a placeholder URL for the CodaLab repository instead of a working link.

Sixth, the experimental setup contains a logical inconsistency: "we paid $0.01 per query (for NER, since response times were much faster, we paid $0.005 per query)." If response times were faster for NER, the payment should be lower, but they paid more for NER, which contradicts their stated rationale.

Seventh, the paper claims "the system can make as many queries in a row (i.e. simultaneously)" but the algorithm description suggests sequential querying with waiting periods, creating confusion about the actual implementation.

Eighth, the utility function has notational inconsistencies: "U(σ) , ExpAcc(p(y | x,q, s, r, t))− (nQwM + tnowwT)" should use a colon or equals sign, and "argmax y′ p(y′)" should be "argmax_{y'} p(y')".

Ninth, the paper cites Legion:AR [6] as a similar approach but fails to adequately distinguish their work from it. The paper states "they do not explore the machine learning foundations associated with operating in this setting, which is the aim of this paper" without clearly articulating what those foundations are.

Tenth, the paper claims "we achieve a 8% F1 improvement over having a single human label the whole set" but doesn't specify whether this is compared to a single human labeling the entire dataset (which would be unrealistic) or a single human per example (which would be more reasonable).

The paper would benefit from:
1. Clarifying the cost baseline for the "order of magnitude reduction" claim
2. Fixing the factual error in the NER example
3. Resolving the inconsistency between simultaneous and sequential query mechanisms
4. Providing a working CodaLab URL instead of a placeholder
5. Explicitly defining all terms (e.g., "full human annotation")

While the core idea of on-the-job learning is interesting and potentially valuable for practical applications, these technical inconsistencies and unclear claims significantly undermine the paper's credibility. The minor methodological details matter greatly in a NIPS paper, and the author's failure to address these issues makes it difficult to assess the validity of their claims. The paper would be much stronger with these issues resolved.

The paper is technically sound in its approach but lacks the precision and attention to detail required for NIPS. The minor flaws accumulate to the point where the experimental results cannot be properly evaluated, and the methodology cannot be reliably reproduced. Given the stringent standards of NIPS, I recommend rejection.
