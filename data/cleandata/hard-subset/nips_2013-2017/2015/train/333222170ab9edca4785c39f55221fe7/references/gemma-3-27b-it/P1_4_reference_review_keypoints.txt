- Proposes "on-the-job learning": query crowd when uncertain, formalized as a stochastic game solved with MCTS and progressive widening under Bayesian decision theory to trade off accuracy, latency, and cost.  
- Implements LENSE and demonstrates effectiveness on NER, sentiment, and image classification with significant cost and accuracy gains.  
- Positions work relative to online learning, active learning, Legion:AR, and connects to game theory, decision theory, and structured prediction.  
- Novel framing of the problem as a stochastic game that elegantly captures accuracy/latency/cost trade-offs.  
- Provides a practical implementation using Amazon Mechanical Turk and releases code/data for reproducibility.  
- Strong empirical results: >10x cost reduction in NER and improved F1 over baselines; analyses of cost reduction over time.  
- Paper is well-written and organized with clear problem formulation, helpful figures, and detailed model description.  
- Reproducibility is supported by CodaLab code and data release.  
- Model complexity (MCTS and progressive widening) may hinder understanding and adoption; needs more intuitive explanation or examples.  
- Reliance on AMT raises concerns about worker quality, bias, and the arbitrary choice of per-query pricing that require deeper discussion.  
- Limited exploration of parameter sensitivity (e.g., utility weights wM and wT) weakens confidence in robustness.  
- Strongest gains on NER raise questions about generalizability to tasks without similar class structure.  
- Lacks systematic comparison to task-specific state-of-the-art models (e.g., SOTA NER).  
- Overall quality: technically sound and experimentally supported.  
- Clarity: generally good but explanations of MCTS/progressive widening should be simplified.  
- Originality: high—novel combination of Bayesian decision theory, stochastic game formulation, and MCTS.  
- Significance: good—addresses building accurate systems with limited labeled data and offers practical cost/accuracy benefits.  
- Arguments for acceptance: novelty, strong empirical results, practical implementation, reproducibility, and clear writing.  
- Arguments against acceptance: model complexity, crowdsourcing biases, limited parameter sensitivity and generalizability analysis.  
- Overall recommendation: Accept with minor revisions to clarify MCTS, discuss crowdsourcing limitations, and explore parameter sensitivity.
