

# Review of "On-the-Job Learning with Bayesian Decision Theory"

## Summary
The paper proposes "on-the-job learning," a framework where a system incrementally queries the crowd for uncertain parts of inputs while learning a model, gradually reducing reliance on crowdsourcing. The authors model this as a stochastic game between the system and crowd, using Bayesian decision theory to balance accuracy, latency, and cost. They implement their approach (LENSE) on NER, sentiment, and image classification tasks, reporting significant cost reductions and accuracy improvements over baselines.

## Strengths
The paper addresses an important practical problem in deploying AI systems with limited labeled data. The conceptual framework of on-the-job learning is novel and well-motivated, with clear distinctions from online learning and active learning. The implementation on three diverse tasks demonstrates practical utility, and the open-source release is commendable. The Bayesian decision theory perspective provides a principled foundation for query strategies.

## Weaknesses
Despite these strengths, the paper contains numerous technical and presentation flaws that undermine its scientific rigor and clarity, warranting rejection.

**Technical flaws:**
1. **Fundamental utility function error:** Equation (1) defines utility as "U(σ) , ExpAcc(p(y | x,q, s, r, t))− (nQwM + tnowwT)". The critical flaw is in Equation (2): "ExpAcc(p) = Ep(y)[Accuracy(argmax y′ p(y′))]." Accuracy is a scalar measure, not a random variable, so taking the expectation over y is mathematically incoherent. The authors should instead define expected accuracy as E[Accuracy(p)] where the expectation is over data distribution, not over labels.

2. **Contradictory query handling:** The paper states "the system can make as many queries in a row (i.e. simultaneously) as it wants" (Section 3), but then rules out "launching a query midway through waiting for the next response" (Section 3, footnote 1). This contradiction is not resolved and undermines the entire framework. The algorithm (Algorithm 1) only handles one query at a time, making the "simultaneous" claim incorrect.

3. **Environment model typo:** In Section 3, "pR(r | yq) = 0.7 iff r = yq ,2" contains an unexplained ",2" that appears to be a typo.

4. **Inconsistent notation:** The utility uses "tnowwT" (Equation 1) without multiplication symbol, while Equation (2) uses "Ep(y)" without proper formatting. The game actions use inconsistent notation: "q = ∅W" vs. "wait action (q = ∅W)".

**Presentation flaws:**
1. **Multiple grammatical errors:** "In the both timelines" (Section 3), "a 8% F1 improvement" (Section 1), "burgenoning" (Section 6), "we feel like" (Section 3), and "70% accurate" without specification (Section 5) undermine professionalism.

2. **Inconsistent terminology:** The paper switches between "Faces" (Section 5, Figure 3) and "face classification" (Section 1), and between "image classification" and "Faces" in the abstract and experiments.

3. **Unclear claims:** "28% F1 improvement" (Section 1) is problematic since F1 is a proportion. It should specify "28 percentage points" or "28% relative improvement."

4. **Algorithm notation:** In Algorithm 1, "C(σ)← C(σ) ∪ {[σ′]}" unnecessarily brackets σ', creating confusion.

5. **Formatting inconsistencies:** References have double commas (e.g., "CVPR),, , ,"), inconsistent capitalization of "LENSE" vs. "Lense," and missing spaces in mathematical expressions (e.g., "k∏ i=1" should be "∏_{i=1}^k").

**Methodological concerns:**
1. The claim that "the system can make as many queries in a row (i.e. simultaneously) as it wants" is contradicted by their algorithm, which processes queries sequentially. The paper never explains how simultaneous queries would work within their framework.

2. The "70% accurate" crowd workers claim (Section 5) lacks methodological detail: What was the evaluation procedure? On which dataset? How was accuracy defined?

3. The threshold baseline (Section 5) uses "0.3m" without defining what "m" represents, creating confusion for readers.

## Recommendation
While the core idea of on-the-job learning is promising, the paper's technical flaws are severe enough to undermine its scientific contribution. The fundamental error in the utility function definition is particularly concerning, as it affects the entire theoretical foundation. The numerous grammatical errors, inconsistent terminology, and contradictory claims further diminish the paper's credibility.

This paper would benefit from significant revision to address these technical and presentation issues before submission to NIPS. As it stands, the flaws are too numerous and severe to recommend acceptance, despite the paper's interesting premise. The authors should carefully rework the theoretical foundation, resolve the contradictions in their framework, and meticulously proofread the manuscript.

I recommend rejection.
