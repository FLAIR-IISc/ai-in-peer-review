**Summary**

This paper introduces a framework called "on-the-job learning," designed for scenarios where an AI system must achieve high accuracy from the outset without a pre-existing labeled dataset. The core idea is to combine machine learning with real-time crowdsourcing. For each new input instance, the system strategically decides which parts of the input to query from a crowd of human workers. It uses these potentially noisy and delayed responses to update its model and make a final prediction. This approach is distinct from traditional online or active learning, which typically do not query for information on the current test instance before making a prediction, and from active classification, which usually relies on a static policy learned from a pre-existing dataset.

The authors formalize this problem as a stochastic game between the system and the crowd. The system's objective is to maximize a utility function that provides a principled trade-off between prediction accuracy, monetary cost of queries, and latency. Since computing the optimal policy in this game is intractable, the paper proposes an approximation using Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) combined with progressive widening to handle the continuous nature of time. The framework is evaluated on three structured prediction and classification tasks (NER, sentiment analysis, image classification), demonstrating significant cost reductions compared to a pure crowdsourcing approach and substantial accuracy gains over a standard online learning baseline.

**Strengths**

1.  **Significance and Originality:** The paper addresses a highly practical and significant problem that lies at the intersection of machine learning and human-computer interaction. The "on-the-job" setting, where a system must perform well immediately while learning over time, is a realistic scenario for many applications but is not well-addressed by existing paradigms like standard active or online learning. The formulation of this problem as a stochastic game governed by Bayesian decision theory is both novel and elegant. It provides a principled way to reason about the complex trade-offs between accuracy, cost, and latency, which is a clear contribution over prior heuristic-based approaches.

2.  **Technical Quality:** The technical approach is sound. Modeling the decision-making process as a stochastic game is a powerful abstraction. The use of MCTS with progressive widening is a well-suited and state-of-the-art technique for approximating solutions to such large-scale planning problems. The decomposition of the environment model into a prediction model (CRF), a crowd response model, and a response-time model is logical and modular.

3.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow. The motivation is clearly articulated in the introduction, the problem formulation and model are described with sufficient detail, and the connection between the theoretical model and the algorithmic solution is clear. The inclusion of Figure 2, which illustrates the decision process and game tree, is particularly effective in building intuition.

4.  **Empirical Evaluation and Reproducibility:** The experiments are thorough, covering three distinct tasks with varying characteristics. The choice of baselines—including pure human annotation, online learning, and a strong heuristic—allows for a comprehensive assessment of the proposed method's benefits. The results convincingly demonstrate that the system (LENSE) achieves its stated goals: reducing cost while maintaining or improving accuracy. A major strength is the commitment to reproducibility, with all code, data, and experiments made available on CodaLab. This sets a high standard and is a significant service to the research community.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Model Simplifications:** The environment model, while functional, relies on some strong simplifying assumptions. The crowd response model (`pR`) assumes a fixed, uniform accuracy (70%) for all workers and all items. In practice, worker quality and item difficulty vary significantly. More sophisticated models that learn individual worker reliability (e.g., Dawid & Skene) could potentially improve performance by allowing the system to, for example, re-query an item if the initial response came from a worker with low estimated reliability. Similarly, the details of the response-time model (`pT`) are not specified.

2.  **Computational Overhead:** The paper does not discuss the computational complexity or practical runtime of the MCTS-based decision-making process. For each instance, the system must run a significant number of MCTS simulations to determine the optimal action sequence. For a "real-time" system, this planning time is a critical component of the overall latency. An analysis of how planning time scales with the size of the input (e.g., sentence length in NER) and the MCTS budget would strengthen the paper's practical claims.

3.  **Clarity of Utility Function:** There appears to be a minor notational ambiguity in the definition of Expected Accuracy in Equation (2): `ExpAcc(p) = Ep(y)[Accuracy(argmax_y' p(y'))]`. As written, `argmax_y' p(y')` is a constant with respect to the expectation, so the expectation is trivial. The likely intended meaning is the expected accuracy of the MAP prediction under the posterior `p`, which would be `sum_y p(y) * Accuracy(y, argmax_y' p(y'))`. Clarifying this would improve the precision of the formulation.

4.  **Baselines:** The "Online learning" baseline, which trains on gold labels of past examples but cannot query on the current one, effectively demonstrates the benefit of on-the-job querying. However, a comparison to an online active learning method (e.g., one that queries for the full label of an instance based on model uncertainty) might provide another interesting point of comparison, although it would not directly address the goal of reducing per-instance cost through partial queries.

**Conclusion**

This is a high-quality paper that introduces a novel and important problem setting and presents a principled, effective solution. The strengths—a significant problem, a sound technical formulation, strong empirical results, and full reproducibility—far outweigh the weaknesses, which are primarily related to simplifying assumptions and missing details about computational cost. The work represents a complete and well-executed contribution that successfully bridges ideas from machine learning, decision theory, and crowdsourcing. It is likely to inspire follow-up research in building more adaptive and resource-efficient intelligent systems.
