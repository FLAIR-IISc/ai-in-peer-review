Summary
The paper proposes “on-the-job learning,” a framework that, at test time and before committing a prediction, adaptively queries crowd workers on uncertain parts of the input to trade off accuracy, latency, and monetary cost. The setting is formalized as a stochastic game over a structured predictor (a CRF), with actions to query, wait, or return. A Bayesian decision-theoretic utility balances expected prediction accuracy against cost and time. Because exact planning is intractable, the authors approximate the optimal policy with Monte Carlo Tree Search (UCT) and progressive widening to handle continuous response times. Experiments on NER, sentiment, and face classification suggest that the method (LENSE) achieves better accuracy-cost tradeoffs than a human n-vote baseline, a simple threshold heuristic, and an online-learning baseline. An implementation and data are released.

Strengths
- Problem motivation is clear and practically relevant: ensuring high accuracy from the start in low-data deployments by adaptively querying humans.
- Coherent decision-theoretic formulation that unifies accuracy, latency, and cost within a single utility.
- The combination of asynchronous querying, structured prediction, and planning is interesting and yields intuitive behaviors (sequential vs parallel queries, redundancy to handle noise).
- Open-source implementation and some effort toward reproducibility.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Environment model is oversimplified. The response model assumes a fixed 70% accuracy with uniform errors across labels and workers. This ignores worker heterogeneity, item difficulty, and confusion structure, all of which are well-studied (e.g., Dawid–Skene style models, TurKontrol/POMDP-based control of crowds). The paper neither models nor measures robustness to misspecification of pR.
- Learning with noisy supervision is unclear. The CRF is said to be “learned based on all actual responses using AdaGrad,” but the true labels are never observed. Do the authors perform EM or marginalize over y using pR? How are gradients computed? This is a critical methodological gap.
- Approximate planning details are under-specified. There are no ablation studies on UCT/progressive widening parameters, rollout policies, or convergence behavior. It’s unclear how many simulations are needed, how runtime scales with sequence length, or how sensitive performance is to wM and wT. No computational or latency results for the planner are reported.
- Baselines are weak or mismatched. The “threshold” baseline is a strawman that ignores time and uses a very coarse uncertainty reduction heuristic. The “online learning” baseline is not the right comparator for test-time decision making. Missing are:
  - Crowd-control baselines (e.g., TurKontrol-like POMDP policies with learned worker reliabilities).
  - Active classification/measurement selection methods trained on available labeled data to induce test-time querying policies.
  - Structured prediction methods for budgeted acquisition and adaptive submodular designs.
  - Strong machine-only models trained on existing corpora or pretraining (even if the target scenario has no labels, the evaluation uses standard datasets with labels).
- Evaluation design raises validity questions. To “control for variance,” the authors pre-collect 5 worker answers and delays per label and then sample without replacement during simulation. This effectively fixes a tiny set of outcomes and may under-represent real-world variability and temporal drift; it also induces dependence across methods and trials. It is unclear whether the $1 retainer costs and pool maintenance are included in cost accounting; if not, the claimed order-of-magnitude cost reductions are overstated. Statistical significance and variability are not reported.
- Metrics and utility calibration. The paper uses ExpAcc of the MAP prediction as the reward, but practical utility is per-instance F1 (for NER) and accuracy for others. It is unclear how the utility parameters (wM, wT) were chosen, how calibration of pθ affects decisions, and whether the utility aligns with evaluation metrics.
- Limited analysis. No sensitivity to worker accuracy, label imbalance, or model miscalibration; no analysis of when/why LENSE fails; no comparison of sequential vs parallel querying under different latency regimes beyond anecdotes.

Quality
The decision-theoretic framing is sound, but key modeling and learning choices are under-specified, and the empirical validation does not convincingly support the strong claims. The evaluation protocol and baselines do not meet the rigor expected for a new decision-making framework.

Clarity
The paper is generally readable, but several important components need clearer exposition: the learning objective and procedure under noisy responses; the exact computation/approximation of ExpAcc; parameter settings and sensitivity; the response-time model; and cost accounting. Reporting of dataset splits, runtime, and statistical uncertainty should be improved.

Originality
The work combines known components (CRFs for structured prediction, UCT/Monte Carlo planning, progressive widening) in the context of crowdsourced test-time querying. The formalization as a stochastic game and joint latency-cost-accuracy utility is a reasonable synthesis, but the novelty is incremental relative to prior POMDP-based control of crowd workflows and active measurement selection.

Significance
The problem is important, but due to oversimplified worker modeling, missing rigorous baselines, and evaluation issues, it is difficult to conclude that the method advances the state of the art or would be adopted by practitioners.

Suggestions for improvement
- Use a learned worker model (e.g., Dawid–Skene or Bayesian confusion matrices with per-worker reliabilities and per-item difficulty), and analyze robustness to misspecification.
- Clearly describe and implement learning under noisy supervision (e.g., EM/variational training of the CRF), and report ablations.
- Compare against stronger baselines: POMDP crowd controllers (TurKontrol-like), active classification/measurement selection policies, and strong machine-only models (including pretrained).
- Report planner runtime/latency, number of simulations, and scalability with sequence length; provide sensitivity to wM, wT, and calibration of pθ.
- Evaluate with live crowd experiments or at least a larger, more realistic simulation of worker reliabilities and delays; include full cost accounting (retainer and overhead); report confidence intervals.
- Provide ablations isolating the contributions of decision-theoretic planning vs heuristic policies.

Pros
- Clear and timely problem; principled decision-theoretic framing.
- Integration with structured prediction and asynchronous querying.
- Open-source release.

Cons
- Oversimplified and possibly misspecified worker model; unclear learning.
- Weak/mismatched baselines; limited and potentially biased evaluation setup.
- Missing runtime/scalability and sensitivity analyses.
- Important methodological details are ambiguous.

Recommendation: Reject
Confidence: High
