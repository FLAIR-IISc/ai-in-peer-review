

# Peer Review: "On-the-Job Learning with Bayesian Decision Theory"

## Summary and Context

This paper introduces "on-the-job learning," a novel framework for building accurate AI systems without requiring large amounts of labeled training data upfront. The approach combines active learning with crowd-sourcing, where the system strategically queries crowd workers on uncertain parts of inputs while simultaneously improving its prediction model using the responses. The authors formalize this as a stochastic game optimized using Bayesian decision theory, with an approximate solution based on Monte Carlo Tree Search and progressive widening. The approach is implemented as LENSE (Learning from Expensive Noisy Slow Experts) and evaluated on three tasks (NER, sentiment classification, image classification), showing significant cost reductions while improving accuracy over baselines.

This work builds on active learning, online learning, and crowdsourcing literature, but makes a clear distinction by emphasizing the need for high accuracy from the very first prediction (unlike online learning) and the dynamic improvement of the model during test time (unlike static active classification policies). The paper connects well to recent NIPS work on active learning (e.g., Golovin et al. 2014 on Bayesian active learning) and crowdsourcing (e.g., Dai et al. 2016 on decision-theoretic crowd workflows).

## Strengths

1. **Novel Problem Formulation:** The paper identifies a crucial gap in the literature—systems that maintain high accuracy from the start while gradually becoming self-sufficient. The stochastic game formulation effectively models the trade-offs between accuracy, latency, and cost in this setting.

2. **Technical Soundness:** The Bayesian decision theory approach is well-motivated for this problem. The MCTS with progressive widening approximation is appropriate for the continuous time aspect of the problem, and the implementation details are sufficiently detailed for reproducibility.

3. **Strong Empirical Results:** The evaluation across three diverse tasks demonstrates consistent improvements over baselines. The NER results (88.4% F1 with >10x cost reduction) are particularly compelling. The paper also shows how the system's query cost decreases over time, validating the core premise of the approach.

4. **Practical Implementation:** The open-source implementation and careful attention to crowdsourcing details (worker training, payment structure, response time modeling) demonstrate practical awareness beyond theoretical contribution.

## Weaknesses and Concerns

1. **Ambiguity in Cost Model:** The paper claims "more than an order of magnitude reduction in cost" but fails to clarify whether this refers to monetary cost, time cost, or a weighted combination. The utility function (Equation 1) uses "nQ" (number of queries) but doesn't specify how parallel vs. sequential queries affect cost. For a practical system, this ambiguity is problematic.

2. **Limited Comparison to Legion:AR:** The authors cite Legion:AR [6] as a related work that operates in a similar setting but claim their work is the first to explore "the machine learning foundations" of on-the-job learning. This is questionable—the Legion:AR paper already addresses many of these foundations (e.g., the real-time activity classification setting). A more thorough comparison would strengthen the novelty claim.

3. **Lack of Failure Analysis:** The paper doesn't discuss failure cases or limitations. For instance, how would the system perform with highly ambiguous inputs that consistently confuse crowd workers? The authors acknowledge worker error rates (70% accuracy), but don't explore how this affects long-term performance or whether the system can detect and adapt to systematic worker biases.

4. **Questionable Comparison to Online Learning:** The paper compares against "online learning" as a baseline, but online learning typically assumes access to ground truth labels (which the authors don't have in their setting). This comparison is misleading—online learning would be impossible without ground truth, whereas their approach explicitly avoids this requirement.

5. **Scalability Concerns:** The paper doesn't address how the approach would scale to very large input streams or how the crowd worker pool would be maintained for sustained operation. The evaluation uses a fixed pool of 5 worker responses per label, which doesn't reflect real-world dynamics.

## Overall Assessment

This is a well-executed paper that makes a meaningful contribution to the intersection of active learning, crowdsourcing, and structured prediction. The core idea of on-the-job learning is compelling and addresses a practical problem in deploying AI systems with limited labeled data. The technical approach is sound, and the empirical results are strong.

However, the paper would benefit from more precise definitions of cost and clearer differentiation from Legion:AR. The comparison to online learning needs refinement, and the discussion of limitations should be expanded. Despite these concerns, the work represents a significant step toward practical AI deployment in resource-constrained settings.

## Recommendation

**Accept** with minor revisions. The paper makes a strong contribution to the field with clear theoretical and practical value. The weaknesses identified are important but not fatal to the paper's overall contribution. The authors should address the ambiguities in the cost model, clarify the relationship to Legion:AR, and expand the discussion of limitations.

The work aligns well with NIPS' interest in practical machine learning solutions and would be of interest to the broader ML community working on active learning and human-AI collaboration. The open-source implementation further enhances its practical value and reproducibility.
