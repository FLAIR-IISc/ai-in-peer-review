Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes “on-the-job learning,” a decision-theoretic framework that delivers high-quality predictions from the outset by interleaving model inference with real-time, asynchronous queries to crowd workers. The key idea is to treat test-time querying as a stochastic game between the system and the crowd, optimizing an expected utility that trades off accuracy, latency, and monetary cost. The environment comprises a structured prediction model pθ(y|x) (e.g., a CRF), a response model pR for noisy crowd labels, and a response-time model pT. Because computing the optimal policy is intractable, the authors approximate it with Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) using UCT and progressive widening to handle continuous time. Experiments on NER, sentiment, and image classification show cost reductions versus pure crowdsourcing (with comparable accuracy) and accuracy gains versus purely automated baselines. An implementation (LENSE) is released.

This work connects to online learning (improving with experience), active learning (acquiring labels strategically), active classification/test-time feature acquisition (value-of-information at prediction time), crowd-powered interfaces and decision-theoretic control of crowds (e.g., TurKontrol; Dai et al.), and structured prediction. The main differentiators claimed are (i) maintaining high accuracy from the beginning by querying before committing a prediction, (ii) learning the model online to reduce future query needs, and (iii) reasoning about latency via asynchronous queries and continuous-time planning. Relative to Legion:AR (real-time crowd-labeling), the contribution is a formal decision-theoretic formulation with planning. Relative to POMDP-based approaches to crowdsourcing control, the novelty is in structured outputs and continuous-time MCTS for real-time querying.

Strengths
- Problem relevance: Addresses a real deployment bottleneck—how to deliver high accuracy without large up-front training sets—by integrating crowd and model at test time.
- Principled formulation: Casts the problem as Bayesian decision-making with an explicit utility that trades off accuracy, cost, and latency; supports intuitive behaviors (parallel vs sequential querying, redundancy for noise).
- Handling continuous time: Progressive widening within MCTS to manage asynchronous latencies is technically interesting and practically important.
- Empirical signal: On three tasks, LENSE reduces human cost substantially (order-of-magnitude on NER) while achieving higher F1 than single-worker and pure online-learning baselines; cost per example decreases over time as the model improves.
- Reproducibility: Code, data, and implementation details of the retainer model are provided.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Modeling simplifications of the crowd: The response model pR fixes accuracy to 0.7 with uniform errors across labels and without per-worker reliability. This is a strong assumption; TurKontrol-like worker modeling or context-dependent noise would be more realistic. The paper notes measured 70% accuracy but does not fit pR from data nor test sensitivity to noise profiles.
- Mismatch between optimized utility and reported metric: The utility optimizes expected accuracy of the MAP sequence, but evaluation emphasizes token-level F1 for NER (and accuracy for other tasks). This misalignment may bias planning. At minimum, discuss implications or adapt utility to token-level costs or F1 proxies.
- Experimental methodology relies heavily on simulation: To control variance, the authors pre-collect 5 responses per label and sample without replacement. While fair across methods, this constrains the policy space (e.g., diminishing returns to redundancy are artifacts of the five cached labels) and does not fully validate real-time performance, worker dynamics, or nonstationarity.
- Baseline coverage: The threshold heuristic is weak. A stronger and standard competitor is a myopic value-of-information (one-step lookahead) policy that chooses the next query maximizing expected utility gain per unit cost/latency. Also, comparisons to POMDP-based control of crowd workflows (e.g., Dai et al.) are conceptual rather than empirical. For structured prediction, a greedy entropy/expected error reduction query policy would be informative.
- Incomplete details: Important implementation specifics are missing or brief: parameterization/learning of pT, calibration of pθ posteriors, values and sensitivity for wM and wT, MCTS budget (simulations per decision), runtime and scalability with sequence length, and ablations (e.g., with/without progressive widening, parallel vs sequential query choices).
- Cost accounting: Retainer costs ($1 to join) are not amortized into the reported cost figures, and platform overheads are unclear. Given that retainer models can dominate costs in practice, a fair accounting or sensitivity analysis is needed.
- Scope of generality: The strongest gains appear in NER (with a dominant “NONE” class), which can favor early model gains and reduced querying. The approach’s benefits on balanced or more difficult tasks are less clear; additional analysis would help.

Evaluation by NeurIPS criteria
- Quality: Moderate. The formulation is sound and the planning approximation is reasonable, but several assumptions (crowd noise model, utility/metric mismatch) and the reliance on simulated workers weaken the empirical rigor. More thorough baselines and sensitivity analyses are needed.
- Clarity: Generally well written and organized. However, key methodological details (utility alignment, hyperparameters, computational budgets, pR/pT estimation) should be expanded to enable full reproducibility and to interpret results.
- Originality: Moderate to high. Integrating continuous-time MCTS planning with structured prediction and test-time crowdsourcing is novel in combination, though conceptually adjacent to POMDP-based crowd control and active classification.
- Significance: Moderate. The problem is important and the approach could influence hybrid human-AI systems. Demonstrated cost reductions are promising, but stronger baselines, broader validation, and real-time trials would increase impact.

Constructive suggestions
- Learn and report pR from data (possibly per-worker) and evaluate sensitivity to noise rates and confusions; consider online estimation of worker reliabilities.
- Align the utility to the evaluation metric (e.g., token-level losses, span-F1 proxies) or justify the current choice with analysis.
- Add a myopic VoI baseline and, if possible, a POMDP-based policy baseline; provide ablations on planning components and query parallelism.
- Report MCTS budgets, wall-clock decision times, and scalability; include Pareto curves for latency-accuracy-cost under varying wM, wT.
- Include results from live deployments (even small-scale) and amortize retainer costs; discuss robustness to worker nonstationarity.
- Clarify how pT is estimated; provide calibration diagnostics for pθ.

Pros for acceptance
- Addresses a timely and practically important setting.
- Principled decision-theoretic framework with an interesting continuous-time planning component.
- Demonstrated cost savings and accuracy improvements; open-source release.

Cons for acceptance
- Simplified and fixed crowd noise model; limited realism.
- Utility/metric mismatch and missing sensitivity analyses.
- Heavy reliance on simulated responses; limited real-time validation.
- Missing stronger baselines and ablations; incomplete cost accounting.

Recommendation: Weak accept
The paper presents a compelling and principled approach to an important problem with promising empirical results and useful open-source artifacts. While some modeling and experimental choices limit the strength of the evidence, the contribution is substantive and likely to spur follow-up work in hybrid human-AI decision-making. Strengthening the empirical rigor as suggested would make the case even more convincing.
