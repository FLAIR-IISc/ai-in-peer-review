Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes “on-the-job learning,” a framework to deliver high-quality predictions from day one by interleaving machine predictions with real-time crowd queries at test time. For each input, the system can issue token-level queries (e.g., for NER), wait for asynchronous responses from a retainer pool, and decide when to stop and predict. The decision problem is modeled as a stochastic game between the system and the crowd. A CRF provides pθ(y|x); the crowd response model pR and latency model pT together define the environment. The utility function trades off expected accuracy of the MAP prediction under the posterior given received responses against monetary and latency costs. Because optimal policies are intractable, the authors approximate with Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) using UCT and progressive widening to handle continuous time. They evaluate on NER, sentiment, and image (face) classification and report improved accuracy-cost tradeoffs over purely human or purely machine baselines. An open-source implementation (LENSE) is provided.

This work sits at the intersection of online learning, active learning/classification, crowdsourcing control (e.g., TurKontrol; Dai, Mausam, Weld), and structured prediction. The key differences emphasized are: (i) the ability to query pre-decision to maintain accuracy from the start (unlike classic online learning), (ii) learning during deployment without fully labeled training sets (unlike active classification with static policies), and (iii) structured outputs with correlated labels.

Strengths
- Clear problem motivation and practical relevance: rapidly deployable systems with quality guarantees despite scarce labeled data.
- Principled decision-theoretic formulation that explicitly trades off accuracy, latency, and monetary cost; handling asynchronous responses is important in practice.
- Algorithmic contribution: adopting MCTS with progressive widening to reason over large, mixed discrete–continuous decision spaces; intuitive behavior (parallel vs sequential querying) emerges from the utility.
- Empirical breadth: three tasks, structured and unstructured outputs, with a real-time retainer setup; substantial reported cost savings (order-of-magnitude on NER) for comparable or better accuracy.
- Open-source code and data; attention to reproducibility via frozen response pools for controlled comparisons.

Weaknesses and detailed comments

Quality
- Environment modeling is simplistic and potentially miscalibrated: pR uses a fixed 0.7 accuracy uniformly across items, classes, and workers. This ignores per-worker reliabilities, item difficulty, and class-dependent confusions, all known to matter in crowd labeling. The paper mentions observed ~70% accuracy but does not estimate/learn pR or show robustness to misspecification. Similarly, pT is not specified (parametric form, fitted parameters).
- The CRF training with noisy labels is underspecified. The paper says pθ is learned “based on all actual responses using AdaGrad,” but does not explain how multiple noisy responses per token are handled (e.g., marginalization, EM, weighted supervision, or simple majority vote). This is central to “learning on the job.”
- Utility definition and computation: ExpAcc is defined as Ep(y)[Accuracy(argmaxy′ p(y′))]. How is this expectation computed efficiently over structured y? Is there calibration to ensure expected accuracy correlates with empirical accuracy? This choice differs from expected 0–1 loss under the posterior and may bias decisions.
- Real-time feasibility is not evaluated: no runtime/compute budget for MCTS, number of simulations per decision, or latency overhead introduced by planning itself. Since the method targets quick deployment, the planning cost vs. gains should be quantified.
- Evaluation details are thin for statistical validity. No confidence intervals/significance tests, limited dataset details (size, splits), and no sensitivity analyses for wM/wT, pR, or pT. Latency, a key dimension in the utility, is not reported as an outcome metric (only cost/accuracy).
- Simulation protocol: freezing 5 responses and latencies per label and sampling without replacement controls variance but may distort real-world correlations, nonstationarity, and adaptation benefits. It also enforces a small, deterministic response pool; results may not generalize to live crowds.
- The restriction “cannot launch a new query while waiting” is a design choice to simplify search; its impact is not quantified via ablation.

Clarity
- Overall readable, but several important modeling/implementation choices lack detail (learning from noisy labels, calibration of pR/pT, hyperparameters c in UCT, rollout policies).
- Some typos and formatting issues; several references are incomplete.
- Figures/tables referenced in the text (Tables 2–3, Figures 3–4) are crucial to interpret trade-offs; ensure they include latency, error bars, and axis units.

Originality
- Combining a structured prediction model with a real-time crowd querying policy optimized via MCTS in a stochastic game framing is a novel and useful synthesis for this setting. Handling asynchronous responses is a distinguishing feature.
- Prior decision-theoretic control of crowd workflows (e.g., TurKontrol; Dai et al.) and Legion:AR are acknowledged; however, there is limited empirical or analytical comparison to POMDP/myopic VOI baselines that explicitly model noisy oracles.

Significance
- If robust, the framework is impactful for practitioners who need immediate quality without pretraining and for research on integrated crowd+ML systems with structured outputs.
- The demonstrated cost savings on NER are promising. However, the reliance on simplified crowd models and the absence of stronger baselines/ablations dampen the strength of the claim.

Suggestions for improvement
- Learn richer environment models: per-worker reliabilities (e.g., Dawid–Skene/HMM confusion), class-dependent noise, item difficulty; fit pT from data. Provide sensitivity to misspecification.
- Clarify and justify the learning procedure for pθ with noisy, partial, and repeated labels; compare alternatives (majority vote, EM, posterior regularization).
- Report latency and planning overhead; include ablations on sequential vs parallel issuing (vary wT), effect of MCTS rollout budget, and impact of the “no new queries while waiting” restriction.
- Add stronger baselines: myopic VOI, heuristic POMDP controllers (e.g., TurKontrol), and active classification with learned policies; for structured outputs, compare to per-token VOI.
- Provide trade-off curves (accuracy vs cost vs latency) with error bars; include statistical tests.
- Improve clarity and references; fix typos.

Pros for acceptance
- Addresses an important, underexplored setting (test-time querying with asynchronous crowd responses) with a principled formulation.
- Elegant integration of decision theory, structured prediction, and MCTS; open-source release.
- Promising empirical gains across multiple tasks.

Cons for acceptance
- Environment and learning models are oversimplified; key details missing.
- Limited evaluation rigor: missing ablations, baselines, latency and runtime reporting, and statistical analysis.
- Some design choices (utility, constraints) are not validated.

Overall recommendation: Weak accept. The idea and formulation are compelling and practically relevant, and the initial results are encouraging. With the suggested clarifications and additional experiments, the paper would make a solid NIPS contribution.
