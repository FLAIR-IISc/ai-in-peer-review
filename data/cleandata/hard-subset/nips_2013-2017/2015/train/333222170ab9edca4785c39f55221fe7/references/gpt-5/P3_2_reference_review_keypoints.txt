- Introduces "on-the-job learning": a decision-theoretic framework that interleaves test-time prediction with real-time crowd queries to maintain high initial accuracy while reducing human dependence over time.  
- Formalizes the problem as a stochastic game between the system and crowd with actions to query tokens, wait for asynchronous responses, or return a prediction.  
- Defines utility that trades off expected prediction accuracy against time and monetary cost.  
- Uses MCTS with UCT and progressive widening to approximate the intractable optimal policy over mixed discrete/continuous outcomes.  
- Prediction model is a CRF pθ(y|x), updated online from crowdsourced responses.  
- Environment model factorizes into pθ(y|x), a simple crowd response model pR (assumed 70% correct), and a response-time model pT.  
- Policy decides when to query, wait, or return to maximize expected utility.  
- Empirical evaluation on NER, sentiment, and face attribute tasks shows cost reductions vs. pure crowdsourcing and accuracy gains vs. pure ML baselines; code and data released.  
- Positions work relative to online learning, active learning, active classification, and prior crowd-control systems (e.g., TurKontrol) and Legion:AR.  
- Strength: addresses an important practical problem of high early accuracy for rapid deployment.  
- Strength: principled Bayesian decision-theoretic formulation modeling asynchronous, noisy crowd responses.  
- Strength: technical novelty in applying MCTS+progressive widening to mixed discrete/continuous planning for this domain.  
- Strength: handles structured outputs (CRFs) so crowd feedback propagates through structure.  
- Strength: solid empirical demonstration with real retainer-based crowdsourcing and open-source release.  
- Weakness: unrealistic crowd model pR assumes fixed 70% uniform accuracy, ignoring worker/item heterogeneity and confusion patterns; pR and pT appear not learned online.  
- Weakness: utility optimizes expected MAP sequence under 0-1 loss, which is misaligned with reported NER F1 evaluation.  
- Weakness: baselines are insufficient—missing myopic VOI, budgeted acquisition, POMDP-based crowd control, and stronger aggregation/adaptive stopping baselines.  
- Weakness: unclear cost accounting for retainer overheads; excluding retainer costs may bias cost comparisons.  
- Weakness: key algorithmic/reproducibility details (UCT c, rollout policy, search budget, pT fit, noisy-label handling in CRF) and planning latency are underspecified.  
- Weakness: no sensitivity analyses to worker accuracy, delay distributions, trade-off weights, or concurrency constraints (cannot query while waiting).  
- Weakness: claim of "no initial data" is weakened by use of rich external features; contribution of features vs. policy not quantified.  
- Weakness: dataset/metric reporting lacks clarity (NER dataset specifics, token vs. entity-level definitions).  
- Weakness: no theoretical guarantees or empirical diagnostics showing closeness to optimal policy.  
- Suggestion: learn pR and pT online with worker/item heterogeneity (e.g., Dawid–Skene/GLAD, hazard models) and show impact.  
- Suggestion: align utility with evaluation metric (token accuracy, entity F1, or approximations) or justify surrogate empirically.  
- Suggestion: add stronger baselines (myopic VOI, budgeted feature acquisition, TurKontrol-like POMDPs, EM-aggregated adaptive stopping).  
- Suggestion: report planning latency, computational costs, and sensitivity to wM/wT, crowd accuracy, and delays; ablate concurrency constraints.  
- Suggestion: clarify cost accounting (include retainer vs. per-query) and provide implementation hyperparameters and noisy-label training details.  
- Suggestion: analyze scalability to longer sequences and more complex structures; report how performance scales with n.  
- Pros for acceptance: important practical problem, principled formulation, novel MCTS application, structured-output focus, open-source release.  
- Cons for acceptance: simplifying crowd/metric assumptions, missing stronger baselines, incomplete cost/accounting and reproducibility analyses.  
- Recommendation: borderline accept pending stronger empirical baselines, metric alignment, learned environment models, and more robustness/reproducibility analyses.
