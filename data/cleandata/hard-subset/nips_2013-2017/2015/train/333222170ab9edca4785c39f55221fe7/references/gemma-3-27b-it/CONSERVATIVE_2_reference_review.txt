This paper proposes an “on-the-job learning” framework, formalizing and extending previous work (Legion:AR), wherein a system strategically queries a crowd to obtain labels for inputs, aiming to balance accuracy, cost, and latency. The approach models this as a stochastic game and utilizes Bayesian decision theory coupled with Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) for policy approximation. While the idea of interleaving prediction with active querying is intuitively appealing, and addresses a practical need – building systems with limited initial resources – the paper suffers from a lack of compelling novelty and insufficient validation against well-established baselines.

The core contribution hinges on a sophisticated game-theoretic formulation. However, the description of the game and the utility function feels overly complex given the demonstrated gains. The authors acknowledge that computing the optimal policy is intractable, relying on MCTS, which introduces its own set of challenges related to hyperparameter tuning and convergence guarantees – issues not adequately addressed. The progressive widening technique, while sensible, is a standard MCTS component and doesn't represent a major advancement. 

The experimental results, while positive, are not entirely convincing. The chosen baselines – “Human n-query”, “Online learning”, and a simple “Threshold baseline” – feel weak. Comparing against a strong, pre-trained supervised learning model (even if trained on a separate dataset) would have provided a more meaningful benchmark. Similarly, a comparison with standard active learning strategies (e.g., uncertainty sampling, query-by-committee) would be valuable to demonstrate the benefits of the proposed on-the-job learning approach. The claim of “more than an order of magnitude reduction in cost” compared to full human annotation is interesting, but lacks context; the absolute cost figures aren't provided, making it difficult to assess the practical significance. The 8% and 28% F1 improvements over single-human labeling and online learning, respectively, are also modest. The analysis of the impact of feature richness (UNIGRAMS vs RNN) on query reduction is a positive step, but remains somewhat superficial.

The paper's clarity is acceptable, but the notation is dense and can be difficult to follow. The description of the game tree and the stochastic game framework could benefit from more illustrative examples. While the authors provide code, reproducibility would be enhanced by including more details regarding the crowd-sourcing setup (e.g., qualification criteria for workers, example instructions).

The originality is limited. The core ideas of interleaving prediction with querying, modeling uncertainty, and using MCTS are all well-established. The main novelty lies in the specific combination of these techniques within the on-the-job learning framework, but this combination doesn't appear groundbreaking. The related work section is reasonably comprehensive, but there's a lack of critical discussion of how this work differs from or builds upon existing approaches.

The significance of the work is questionable. While the problem addressed is relevant, the gains demonstrated are not substantial enough to warrant a strong endorsement. The practical utility of this approach seems limited to scenarios where obtaining even a small amount of labeled data is prohibitively expensive. 

**Arguments for acceptance:**

* Addresses a practical problem – learning with limited initial resources.
* Presents a principled framework based on Bayesian decision theory.
* Demonstrates some performance gains over simple baselines.
* Provides a publicly available implementation.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

* Lacks compelling novelty.
* Insufficiently rigorous experimental evaluation – weak baselines.
* Overly complex formulation without clear justification for the complexity.
* Modest performance gains.
* Limited discussion of limitations and potential failure modes.



**Overall assessment:** Reject. The paper presents a reasonable idea, but the contribution is incremental, and the experimental validation is not strong enough to justify acceptance to NIPS. The authors need to significantly strengthen the experimental evaluation by comparing against more relevant and competitive baselines and demonstrating more substantial performance gains. Addressing the concerns regarding the complexity of the formulation and providing a more thorough discussion of the limitations would also improve the paper.
