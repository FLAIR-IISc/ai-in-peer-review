Summary
The paper introduces “on-the-job learning,” a decision-theoretic framework for making high-accuracy predictions from the outset by interleaving model inference with test-time queries to human workers. The system models the process as a stochastic game between the system and the crowd, using a CRF (for structured prediction) as the base model and a Bayesian utility that trades off expected accuracy, monetary cost, and latency. Because computing the optimal policy is intractable, the authors use Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) with progressive widening to handle continuous response times. Empirically, the method (LENSE) is demonstrated on NER, sentiment, and an image (faces) task, showing substantial cost reductions versus pure crowdsourcing and accuracy improvements versus pure ML and heuristic baselines, with open-source code provided.

Relation to prior work
The work connects to online learning (improve over time), active learning (querying), active classification (test-time feature acquisition), and crowdsourcing control (e.g., TurKontrol). It differs from standard online/active learning by querying pre-prediction to maintain high accuracy from the start; from active classification by learning at test time and handling structured outputs; and from prior real-time crowd systems (e.g., Legion:AR) by providing a general decision-theoretic formulation with a formal game model and MCTS-based policy search. The structured prediction angle sets it apart from much of the crowdsourcing control literature, which typically assumes i.i.d. labels/features.

Strengths
- Clear and principled formulation: a coherent stochastic game with a Bayesian utility balancing accuracy, cost, and latency; explicit modeling of response noise and response times.
- Algorithmic contribution: combining MCTS with progressive widening to plan over asynchronous, continuous-time responses; the method naturally captures parallel vs sequential querying trade-offs.
- Practical relevance: addresses a real deployment pain point—bootstrapping systems without large labeled datasets while still ensuring immediate quality.
- Empirical utility: on three tasks, the approach shows sizable cost savings over multi-vote crowdsourcing and better accuracy than machine-only and heuristic policies; the trend of reduced querying over time is especially compelling.
- Reproducibility: code and datasets are provided.

Weaknesses
- Utility-objective mismatch: the utility optimizes expected accuracy (of MAP) while NER is evaluated by F1. For structured outputs, this mismatch can bias decisions; it would be stronger to optimize an approximation to expected F1.
- Simplified crowd model: a fixed pR with 70% accuracy and uniform residual noise, and no per-worker or per-item difficulty modeling. This can be mis-specified and may distort optimal redundancy and timing decisions.
- Evaluation protocol: using a frozen pool of five worker responses per label to simulate consistency reduces variance but can bias conclusions and limits ecological validity; empirical sensitivity to worker variance and model misspecification is not reported.
- Baselines: the “online learning” baseline is intentionally disadvantaged in the early stream and cannot query; stronger decision-theoretic baselines exist (e.g., POMDP-based control like TurKontrol for redundancy/time-cost tradeoffs, active classification policies, or value-of-information policies on structured outputs). A learned static policy baseline would sharpen the comparison.
- Missing details on computational cost and latency-overhead of planning: MCTS settings (simulation budget, branching limits, time per decision) and real-time feasibility for longer sequences are not fully quantified.
- Assumptions: independence in pR across repeated queries to the same position and across positions, independent response times, and the inability to launch new queries mid-wait simplify the search but may leave performance on the table; discussion of impact is limited.

Assessment by NIPS criteria
- Quality: The core formulation is sound and the algorithmic choices are reasonable. Claims are broadly supported by experiments, but evaluation could be stronger: (i) objective mismatch for structured metrics, (ii) limited sensitivity analyses (wM, wT, worker accuracy, latency distributions), (iii) limited ablations (e.g., no MCTS vs heuristic policy, parallel vs sequential policies, progressive widening effects), and (iv) lack of comparisons to stronger decision-theoretic baselines.
- Clarity: The paper is generally well written and organized. The game model and algorithm are described clearly. Some definitions could be sharpened: how ExpAcc is computed/approximated for structures; details of pT estimation; MCTS hyperparameters and computational budget; how CRF posteriors are updated online; and the mapping from utility to actual decision latency constraints.
- Originality: The combination of a decision-theoretic stochastic game with MCTS for on-the-job learning in structured prediction is novel. While related ideas exist (real-time crowds, POMDP control, active classification), this synthesis and the focus on learning at test time with structured outputs is original.
- Significance: The problem is important and timely, and the approach is likely to influence future work on human-in-the-loop, cost/latency-aware prediction. The results suggest practical benefits. Strengthening the evaluation and crowd modeling would further increase impact.

Questions and suggestions for authors
- Can you align the utility with F1 (e.g., structured surrogate of expected F1) or provide evidence that optimizing expected accuracy is a good proxy?
- Provide sensitivity analyses to wM and wT, worker accuracy misspecification, and response-time distribution shifts.
- Compare to a POMDP-based redundancy controller (e.g., TurKontrol-like) and to a learned static policy baseline trained on logged decisions.
- Report computational overhead and end-to-end latency, including MCTS budget and decision times for typical inputs.
- Consider learning per-worker reliabilities online and updating pR; show whether modeling heterogeneity reduces cost/latency.
- Discuss the effect of allowing interleaved query launching during waits (or approximate variants), and the impact of independence assumptions across queries.

Pros for acceptance
- Principled framework with clear novelty.
- Practical, relevant problem with convincing qualitative behavior.
- Open-source implementation; reproducibility.
- Promising empirical gains across multiple tasks.

Cons for acceptance
- Evaluation gaps (objective mismatch, limited baselines, sensitivity/ablation).
- Simplified and possibly mis-specified crowd model.
- Limited discussion of computational scalability and real-time feasibility.

Recommendation
Lean accept (weak accept). The paper offers a well-motivated and original decision-theoretic approach with promising results and a clear path for follow-up work. Strengthening the evaluation and crowd modeling would make the contribution even more compelling.
