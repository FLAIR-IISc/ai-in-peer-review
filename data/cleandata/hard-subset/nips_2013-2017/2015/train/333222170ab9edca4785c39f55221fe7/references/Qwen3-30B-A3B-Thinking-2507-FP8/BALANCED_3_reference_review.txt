

# Review of "On-the-Job Learning with Bayesian Decision Theory"

This paper introduces "on-the-job learning," a novel framework for building AI systems that learn from crowd-sourced labels while making predictions on new inputs, without requiring large amounts of pre-labeled data. The authors model the process as a stochastic game between the system and the crowd, using Bayesian decision theory to balance accuracy, latency, and cost. The framework is implemented as LENSE (Learning from Expensive Noisy Slow Experts), which uses Monte Carlo Tree Search with progressive widening to approximate optimal query strategies. The system is evaluated on three structured prediction tasks (NER, sentiment classification, and image classification), showing significant cost reduction (more than an order of magnitude on NER) while improving accuracy over both pure crowd-based and pure ML approaches.

## Strengths

**Originality and Significance (Strong):** The paper addresses a crucial practical problem in deploying AI systems: the high cost of labeling data upfront. The on-the-job learning framework is genuinely novel, distinguishing itself from active learning (which focuses on building models, not making predictions) and active classification (which uses a static policy). The stochastic game formulation with Bayesian decision theory provides a principled foundation for balancing the key trade-offs, which is not present in prior work. The significance is clear: the approach enables deploying accurate AI systems with minimal upfront data collection costs, which is valuable for real-world applications like disaster response. The results on NER (88.4% F1 at >10x cost reduction) are compelling and demonstrate the practical impact of the work.

**Quality (Strong):** The paper is technically sound with a well-structured theoretical foundation. The game-theoretic formulation is rigorous, and the authors carefully model the crowd response distribution (70% accuracy with uniform noise) and response times. The experimental evaluation is comprehensive, with multiple tasks, baselines (human n-query, online learning, threshold baseline), and thorough analysis of cost-accuracy trade-offs over time. The ablation study showing LENSE outperforming the threshold baseline demonstrates the value of the Bayesian decision theory approach. The open-source implementation enhances reproducibility.

**Clarity (Good):** The paper is well-written and logically organized. The problem statement, game formalization, and experimental results are clearly presented. The running example (NER for "Soup on George str.") effectively illustrates the approach. The pseudocode for the MCTS algorithm is clear. Some sections (e.g., the game tree formalization) could benefit from additional intuitive explanations for readers less familiar with game theory.

## Weaknesses

**Quality (Moderate):** The paper makes some assumptions that could be better justified. The 70% crowd accuracy is stated without justification (beyond a brief mention of "human workers we hired"), which is critical to the model's performance. A more detailed analysis of how crowd accuracy varies across tasks or worker pools would strengthen the work. The scalability of the MCTS approach is not adequately addressed—how does the algorithm scale with more complex tasks or longer sequences? The paper mentions that the crowd response time model uses "response times were generally in the range of 0.5–2 seconds," but doesn't discuss how this would scale to larger systems or different crowd platforms.

**Originality (Moderate):** While the framework is novel, the paper could better distinguish itself from related work. The connection to "Legion:AR" (cited as [6]) is mentioned, but the paper could more explicitly state how it differs in terms of theoretical foundations (the authors state Legion doesn't explore "machine learning foundations," but this needs clarification). The paper also doesn't sufficiently connect to recent work in active learning with noisy labels (e.g., [20, 21]), which shares similar goals but differs in the query mechanism.

**Clarity (Moderate):** The section on the "Environment model" (Section 3.3) could be clearer about how the CRF model pθ(y|x) is updated during test time. The paper states that "pθ(y|x) is learned based on all actual responses (not simulated ones) using AdaGrad," but it's unclear how the model updates affect the query strategy in real-time. A more detailed explanation of the online learning loop would improve clarity.

## Suggestions for Improvement

1. **Justify crowd accuracy assumptions:** Provide more details on how the 70% accuracy was measured and how it varies across tasks (e.g., NER vs. sentiment classification). Consider adding a sensitivity analysis to show how performance changes with different crowd accuracies.

2. **Address scalability:** Discuss the computational complexity of the MCTS approach and how it would scale to larger tasks or longer sequences. Consider comparing with simpler heuristics for larger-scale applications.

3. **Strengthen related work:** More explicitly contrast with recent active learning with noisy labels (e.g., [20, 21]) and clarify the theoretical differences with Legion:AR [6].

4. **Clarify online model updates:** Provide a more detailed explanation of how the CRF model is updated during test time and how this affects the query strategy.

## Conclusion

This is a strong paper that addresses a meaningful problem with a novel framework grounded in sound theory. The results are impressive and demonstrate significant practical impact. While the paper could be strengthened by better justifying assumptions and addressing scalability, the core contribution is valuable and well-executed. The work represents a significant step toward making AI systems more deployable in resource-constrained settings.

**Recommendation:** Accept. The paper makes a compelling contribution to the field, with clear significance, strong technical content, and convincing empirical results. The minor weaknesses identified are addressable in a revision and do not detract from the paper's overall quality.
