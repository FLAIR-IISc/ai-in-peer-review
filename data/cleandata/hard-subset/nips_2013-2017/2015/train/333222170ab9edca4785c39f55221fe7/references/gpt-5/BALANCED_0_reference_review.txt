Summary
This paper proposes “on-the-job learning,” a framework to maintain high prediction accuracy from the very beginning of deployment by interleaving model inference with real-time, selective queries to a crowd. The core formalism is a stochastic game between the system and the crowd over actions {query a token, wait, return}, with utility derived from Bayesian decision theory that trades off expected accuracy against query cost and latency. The environment model factors p(y, r, t | x, q, s) into a prediction model pθ(y | x) (e.g., CRF), a response model pR(r | yq), and a response-time model pT(t | s). Because exact planning is intractable, the paper uses Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) with UCT and progressive widening to handle continuous time. Experiments on NER, sentiment, and image classification report substantial cost reductions versus majority-vote crowdsourcing at comparable or better accuracy, and gains over “machine-only” online learning. The implementation uses a retainer pool for near-real-time responses, and code/data are released.

Relation to prior work: The setting is adjacent to online learning (Cesa-Bianchi & Lugosi), active learning (Settles), active classification/value-of-information at test time (e.g., Gao & Koller, Greiner et al.), and decision-theoretic control of crowds (e.g., TurKontrol; Dai, Mausam, Weld). The framing as a stochastic game with continuous-time modeling and planning via MCTS, combined with structured outputs (CRF), is a novel combination relative to Legion:AR’s real-time system and prior POMDP-based control of votes, which typically use static policies or do not update the prediction model online.

Strengths
- Clear, practical problem setting: maintaining high quality from the outset without pretraining by judicious, real-time, per-instance crowd queries.
- Principled utility that explicitly trades off accuracy, monetary cost, and latency; continuous-time modeling and the wait/return/query action set yield interpretable behaviors (redundancy, parallelization vs adaptivity).
- Methodological contribution: MCTS with progressive widening to plan in a continuous-time, partially observed, structured-prediction setting.
- Structured outputs: integration with CRF makes the case for structured test-time querying more compelling than independent-label settings.
- Empirical coverage across three tasks, including real-time crowdsourcing infrastructure; open-source code and reproducible setup are strong positives.
- Reported gains in cost at comparable/better accuracy over human-only and machine-only baselines; evidence that richer features reduce supervision over time.

Weaknesses and detailed comments
Quality
- Response/noise model pR is overly simplistic (fixed 70% accuracy, uniform confusions), with no per-worker reliability, task difficulty, or context dependence; this undermines optimality and robustness of the planning. Many crowd models (Dawid–Skene, GLAD) could be incorporated and updated online.
- The “ExpAcc” term and the “Accuracy” function are underspecified. For sequence labeling, expected F1 of a MAP prediction is nontrivial; how is it computed/approximated efficiently during planning? If token-level (Hamming) accuracy is used, clarify and align with reported metrics.
- Calibration matters for decision-theoretic control; the paper does not report calibration of pθ or any calibration method (e.g., temperature scaling), nor sensitivity to miscalibration.
- Evaluation protocol uses pre-collected pools of 5 responses/delays per label, sampled without replacement. This limits variability, may bias results, and is not a full live setting; the impact on reported variance and statistical significance is unclear. No confidence intervals or significance tests are reported.
- Missing computational analysis: per-example planning time/latency overhead from MCTS, depth/rollouts, and scalability with sequence length. The action space grows with n; what are typical rollouts and wall-clock overhead relative to human response times?
- Some claims need qualification (e.g., “boosting performance relative to expert labels”); please explain the reference standard and how F1 can exceed expert labels.

Clarity
- Several implementation details are missing or ambiguous: how pT is estimated; features for CRF; when/how pθ is updated online; UCT constants; termination criteria; and the exact accuracy metric used in the utility.
- Dataset descriptions are inconsistent (e.g., NER on tweets vs CoNLL 2003; nonstandard label set including RESOURCE). Please reconcile and provide splits/sizes, and ensure the label schema is clear.
- The restriction that no new queries can be issued while waiting simplifies search; an analysis of its impact on utility (or an ablation) would help.

Originality
- The combination of structured prediction, continuous-time crowd responses, and MCTS-based planning in an on-the-job setting is novel in this form. That said, elements draw from established lines (POMDP control of crowds, value-of-information, active classification). The contribution is a cohesive synthesis tailored to real-time, structured tasks.

Significance
- The problem is practically important; a principled controller that trades off accuracy, latency, and cost at deployment is valuable. Reported gains and open-source release enhance impact. However, stronger and broader baselines (e.g., POMDP vote control like TurKontrol, myopic VOI, learned policies, dynamic feature acquisition adapted to crowd queries) and more rigorous analysis would solidify the significance.

Arguments for acceptance
- Addresses an important, underexplored deployment setting with a principled framework.
- Novel planning approach for continuous-time, structured, real-time crowd querying.
- Demonstrated cost-accuracy-latency trade-offs across tasks; open-source and reproducibility.

Arguments against acceptance
- Simplistic and fixed response model; lack of calibration and sensitivity analyses.
- Incomplete specification of utility/accuracy computation; unclear alignment with F1 metrics.
- Evaluation protocol and baselines are limited; no statistical rigor or scalability analysis.
- Clarity issues and dataset inconsistencies.

Suggestions for improvement
- Model and learn worker reliabilities and task difficulties online (e.g., Dawid–Skene/GLAD variants), and incorporate them into pR and planning.
- Calibrate pθ and report calibration metrics; analyze sensitivity to miscalibration and to misspecified pR/pT.
- Precisely define the utility’s accuracy metric; if using F1, provide an estimator or a justified surrogate; ablate choices.
- Add stronger baselines: TurKontrol-like POMDP for vote control, myopic VOI policies, dynamic feature acquisition baselines, and learned policies via imitation/RL.
- Provide computational/latency overheads and scalability analysis; ablate MCTS depth/rollouts and the “no-launch-while-waiting” constraint.
- Clarify datasets, label sets, splits, and training details; report statistical significance.

Recommendation: Weak accept. The paper tackles a timely, practically relevant problem with a principled and generally novel formulation and shows promising results with open-source code. Addressing the modeling and evaluation gaps above would make the contribution much stronger. Confidence: medium.
