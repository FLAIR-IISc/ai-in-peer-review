## Peer Review: On-the-Job Learning with Bayesian Decision Theory

**Summary:**

This paper presents a novel framework, "on-the-job learning," for tackling prediction tasks with limited upfront labeled data. The core idea is to strategically query a crowd for labels only when the model is uncertain, iteratively improving prediction accuracy while minimizing cost and latency. The approach casts this as a stochastic game solved via Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) and progressive widening, allowing for principled tradeoffs between accuracy, cost, and time. The authors demonstrate the efficacy of their "LENSE" system on NER, sentiment classification, and image classification, achieving significant cost reductions and accuracy improvements compared to baselines like full annotation, single-label baselines, and online learning.

**Relation to Prior Work:**

The paper appropriately positions itself within the landscape of online learning, active learning, and crowdsourcing. It distinguishes itself from online learning by actively seeking information *before* prediction. Unlike traditional active learning, which often relies on static query policies, this work proposes a dynamic policy learned during test time.  The connection to Legion:AR [6] is acknowledged, but the authors rightly emphasize their deeper investigation of the underlying machine learning foundations.  The work also builds upon established techniques like CRFs, MCTS, and Bayesian decision theory, adapting them to this specific on-the-job learning setting.  The related work section is comprehensive and well-integrated, highlighting relevant contributions.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty & Problem Formulation:** The on-the-job learning framework is a compelling approach to a practical problem - deploying systems when labeled data is scarce and expensive. The formalization as a stochastic game is a clever and insightful move.
* **Principled Approach:** Leveraging Bayesian decision theory to balance competing objectives (accuracy, cost, latency) provides a solid theoretical foundation and intuitive strategies.
* **Strong Empirical Results:** The experiments demonstrate significant improvements in both cost and accuracy across diverse tasks. The 8% F1 improvement over a single human label and 28% over online learning on NER are particularly noteworthy.
* **Practical Implementation & Open-Source Release:** The availability of the LENSE implementation on GitHub promotes reproducibility and encourages further research.  The detailed implementation and crowdsourcing setup description are commendable.
* **Clear Writing & Organization:** The paper is generally well-written and logically organized. The figures and equations are helpful in understanding the framework.
* **Thorough Evaluation:** The comparison against relevant baselines, including a strong “human n-query” baseline, provides a convincing assessment of the method's effectiveness. The analysis of query reduction over time is also valuable.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Complexity:** The framework is complex, involving a stochastic game, MCTS, and progressive widening.  While well-explained, it demands a significant understanding of these techniques from the reader.  The notation can be dense at times.
* **Crowdsourcing Specifics:** The assumption of a "pool" of readily available, consistent crowd workers (maintained through a retainer model) might not generalize to all crowdsourcing platforms or scenarios. The fixed worker responses used in simulation while controlling for variance is a pragmatic decision, but introduces a potential bias.
* **Limited Scalability Discussion:** The paper doesn't extensively discuss the scalability of the MCTS approach to very high-dimensional state spaces or complex prediction tasks. While progressive widening helps, its limitations should be addressed.
* **Sensitivity to Prior:** The performance heavily relies on the initial prediction model (pθ(y|x)).  A poor initial model could lead to inefficient querying. Investigating the impact of different initialization strategies would be valuable.
* **Response Model Simplification:** The fixed 70% accuracy response model seems simplistic.  Real-world crowd worker reliability varies considerably. Exploring more sophisticated response models could further improve performance.



**Arguments for/against acceptance:**

**For Acceptance:**

* The paper addresses an important and practically relevant problem.
* The proposed framework is novel and theoretically well-founded.
* The experimental results are compelling and demonstrate significant improvements over strong baselines.
* The open-source release promotes reproducibility and further research.
* The paper is generally well-written and clear.

**Against Acceptance:**

* The complexity of the framework may limit its accessibility.
* The reliance on a specific crowdsourcing setup might restrict generalizability.
* A more detailed discussion of scalability concerns would be beneficial.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of active learning and crowdsourcing. The on-the-job learning framework is a compelling and practical approach to building systems with limited labeled data. While the complexity of the approach and the reliance on specific crowdsourcing assumptions are potential limitations, the strong experimental results and the availability of the code outweigh these concerns.

**Recommendation:** **Accept** (with minor revisions).

**Suggestions for improvement:**

*  Briefly discuss the computational cost of MCTS and potential strategies for improving scalability.
*  Explore the sensitivity of the approach to the initial model (pθ(y|x)).
*  Consider adding a discussion of the generalizability to different crowdsourcing platforms and worker reliability distributions.
*  Clarify the notation and potentially provide a simplified example to illustrate the core concepts.
* Consider a sensitivity analysis of the utility function weighting parameters (wM, wT).
