## Review of "On-the-Job Learning with Bayesian Decision Theory"

**Summary:**

This paper introduces a framework called "on-the-job learning" for performing structured prediction when labeled data is scarce. The core idea is to strategically query a crowd for labels only when the system is uncertain, using Bayesian decision theory to balance accuracy, latency, and cost. The authors model this as a stochastic game and employ Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) with progressive widening to approximate the optimal query policy. They demonstrate the efficacy of their approach (LENSE) on three tasks (NER, sentiment classification, image classification) showing significant cost reductions compared to full annotation and performance improvements over baselines.

**Relation to Previous Work:**

The paper correctly positions itself within the landscape of online learning, active learning, and crowdsourcing. It highlights the differences between its approach and existing techniques, particularly emphasizing the dynamic policy adaptation during test time, unlike static policies in traditional active learning. The connection to Legion:AR is acknowledged, but the authors argue their work provides a more thorough machine learning foundation.  The related work section is comprehensive and demonstrates a solid understanding of the relevant literature.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The formulation of on-the-job learning as a stochastic game with a Bayesian decision-theoretic approach is a novel and elegant way to tackle the problem of limited labeled data.
* **Practicality:** The presented framework addresses a real-world scenario where acquiring large labeled datasets or relying solely on crowdsourcing is infeasible. The significant cost reductions demonstrated in the experiments are compelling.
* **Strong Empirical Results:** The experiments across three diverse tasks consistently demonstrate the superiority of LENSE compared to the baselines, both in terms of accuracy and cost. The 88.4% F1 on NER with significant cost reduction is particularly impressive.
* **Technical Soundness:** The use of MCTS and progressive widening is a reasonable approach to tackle the intractability of the optimal policy computation. The explanations of the game tree and utility function are clear.
* **Reproducibility:** The authors provide open-source code and data, which is excellent and promotes reproducibility.
* **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and well-organized. The problem formulation and model description are presented in a clear and understandable manner.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Complexity:** While the core idea is relatively straightforward, the overall framework is complex. The detailed descriptions of the stochastic game, utility function, and MCTS algorithm may be challenging for readers unfamiliar with these concepts.
* **Crowd Model Assumptions:** The paper assumes a fixed pool of crowd workers with known response characteristics (70% accuracy). In a real-world deployment, worker quality can vary significantly and change over time. The robustness of the framework to such variations could be further investigated.
* **Limited Generalizability of Worker Response Times:** The assumption of pre-collected worker response times may not hold across different tasks or platforms. Real-time response time estimation could be incorporated for more dynamic query scheduling.
* **Heuristic Nature of MCTS:** While MCTS is a powerful search algorithm, it provides an approximation to the optimal policy. The paper doesn't thoroughly analyze the approximation quality or the sensitivity of the results to MCTS parameters.
* **Emphasis on NER:** While the results across all three tasks are positive, the paper focuses disproportionately on NER. More detailed analysis and comparison across tasks would be beneficial.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound approach to a practical problem.
* The empirical results are compelling and demonstrate significant improvements over existing methods.
* The code and data are publicly available, promoting reproducibility.
* The paper is well-written and clearly explains the core ideas.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The framework is complex and may require significant effort to implement and adapt.
* The assumptions about crowd worker behavior may limit the generalizability of the results.
* The analysis of the MCTS approximation quality is limited.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that addresses an important problem with a novel and well-motivated approach. The technical contributions are solid, and the empirical results are convincing. While the complexity of the framework and the limitations of the crowd model are valid concerns, they do not outweigh the strengths of the work.  The originality and significance of the contribution are sufficient to warrant acceptance.  I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions.

**Recommendations for Improvement:**

* Briefly discuss the computational cost of the MCTS algorithm and potential strategies for scaling it to larger problems.
* Elaborate on the sensitivity analysis of the MCTS parameters.
* Explore the robustness of the framework to variations in crowd worker quality.
* Provide a more detailed comparison of the performance across the three tasks.




**Qualitative Evaluation:**

* **Quality:** Excellent - The paper is technically sound and claims are well supported.
* **Clarity:** Good - The paper is generally clear, although some sections could benefit from further simplification.
* **Originality:** Excellent - The approach is novel and builds upon existing work in a meaningful way.
* **Significance:** Excellent - The results are important and have the potential to impact the field of active learning and crowdsourcing.
