This paper proposes an “on-the-job learning” framework where a system learns to make predictions by strategically querying a crowd for information when uncertain, aiming to balance accuracy, cost, and latency. It casts this as a stochastic game and uses Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) with progressive widening to approximate the optimal policy. The approach is evaluated on NER, sentiment classification, and image classification, demonstrating significant cost reductions compared to full annotation and improvements over online learning.

This is a well-motivated and technically sound paper addressing a practically relevant problem. The idea of learning *while* deploying, instead of requiring a fully trained model upfront, is compelling, particularly in resource-constrained scenarios. The framing as a stochastic game and the application of MCTS are clever and appropriate for handling the sequential decision-making process. The experimental results are convincing, showing significant cost savings on NER and improvements over strong baselines. The code release is a major plus, promoting reproducibility and further research.

However, there are some areas for improvement. The paper could benefit from a more thorough discussion of the relationship to related work, especially in the realm of partially observable Markov decision processes (POMDPs) and reinforcement learning. While the authors mention related areas, a deeper dive into these connections would strengthen the paper. Specifically, the utility function appears closely related to reward functions in RL, and exploring this connection could yield insights. Furthermore, the chosen simplification of preventing queries during wait times, while acknowledged, feels somewhat arbitrary and its impact on the solution could be further explored. The response time modeling is fairly simple and relies on pre-collected data; discussing the robustness of the approach to inaccuracies in this model would be valuable. Finally, the results on sentiment and image classification are less dramatic than on NER. A more in-depth analysis of why the approach performs better on NER—possibly due to the structured nature of the problem—would be beneficial. 

Here's a breakdown of strengths and weaknesses:

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The on-the-job learning framework is a practical and interesting approach to learning with limited resources.
* **Technical Soundness:** The use of Bayesian decision theory, stochastic games, and MCTS is well-justified and appropriately applied.
* **Empirical Results:** The experimental results are strong, demonstrating significant cost savings and accuracy improvements on NER.
* **Reproducibility:** The code release is excellent.
* **Clear Writing:** The paper is generally well-written and easy to follow.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Related Work:** The connection to POMDPs and reinforcement learning could be explored in greater depth.
* **Simplification:** The restriction on querying during wait times feels somewhat arbitrary and its impact should be discussed further.
* **Response Time Model:** The robustness of the approach to inaccuracies in the response time model is not discussed.
* **Task Dependence:** The significantly better performance on NER compared to other tasks warrants further investigation and analysis.
* **Limited discussion of scalability**: The MCTS approach is computationally intensive. A discussion of how this scales with problem size, number of queries and complexity would be beneficial.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and well-motivated framework for on-the-job learning.
* The technical approach is sound and the experimental results are convincing.
* The code release promotes reproducibility.
* The work addresses a practically relevant problem.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

* The connection to related work (POMDPs, RL) is not fully explored.
* The simplification regarding querying during wait times feels arbitrary.
* The results on tasks other than NER are less compelling.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this is a strong paper with significant potential. While there are areas for improvement, the novelty, technical soundness, and empirical results justify acceptance. However, addressing the weaknesses outlined above would significantly strengthen the paper and increase its impact. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions, specifically requesting a more thorough discussion of related work and a deeper analysis of the task dependence of the results.
