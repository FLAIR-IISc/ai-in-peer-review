Summary and relation to prior work:
This paper proposes on-the-job learning: a test-time framework that maintains high accuracy from the start by interleaving model predictions with real-time crowd queries, and gradually reduces dependence on the crowd as the model improves. The core contribution is a decision-theoretic formulation: the system selects which parts of an input to query, whether to wait for outstanding responses, and when to return a prediction by maximizing expected utility that trades off accuracy, latency, and monetary cost. The environment is modeled via a CRF prior over outputs, a response model for noisy workers, and a latency model; decision making is approximated with Monte Carlo Tree Search (UCT) plus progressive widening to handle continuous response times. The system (LENSE) is evaluated on NER, sentiment classification, and image classification, showing better accuracy-cost tradeoffs than human-only (majority vote) and machine-only (online learning) baselines, and outperforming a hand-crafted threshold policy.

This work is related to online learning and online active learning (which do not acquire new information at prediction time), active classification and value-of-information approaches (which typically learn static policies offline), decision-theoretic control of crowdsourcing (e.g., POMDP formulations such as TurKontrol), and real-time crowd-powered systems (e.g., retainer models, Legion:AR). The novelty is to couple decision-theoretic, asynchronous crowd querying at test time with continuous-time planning and structured prediction, while updating the model online from noisy responses.

Strengths:
- Clear and timely problem formulation: maintaining high accuracy from the outset without pretraining by using the crowd adaptively is an important and practical goal.
- Principled decision-theoretic framework that jointly optimizes over accuracy, latency, and cost, with intuitive emergent strategies (redundancy for noisy labels, parallelization for latency).
- Technically interesting use of MCTS with progressive widening for continuous-time stochastic games; the formulation naturally accommodates structured outputs via CRFs.
- Real-time deployment with a retainer pool and asynchronous responses; code and data availability supports reproducibility.
- Empirical gains across three tasks, including large cost reductions at comparable or better accuracy, and evidence that query volume declines over time as the model improves.

Weaknesses:
- Environment modeling is oversimplified: a fixed 0.7 accuracy for all workers and labels misses known heterogeneity across workers, items, and classes; no confusion matrices, difficulty modeling, or calibration are incorporated.
- The simulation protocol samples from a frozen set of 5 responses/delays per label, which may understate real-world variability and temporal drift; it is unclear how robust the results are under different worker pools or error rates.
- The utility function and evaluation metrics may be misaligned (e.g., NER is evaluated in F1, but utility is defined over “accuracy” of MAP; details of the accuracy functional are not specified, and expected F1 is non-decomposable).
- Limited ablations and sensitivity analyses: choice of wM and wT (money/time weights), effect of response model misspecification, and the contribution of MCTS vs. simpler policies are not thoroughly explored.
- Important learning details are under-specified: how pθ is updated online from noisy responses (e.g., posterior over y vs. treating responses as noisy labels), how marginalization is computed for ExpAcc in structured outputs, and the exact latency model pT calibration.
- Baselines could be stronger: majority vote and a simple threshold policy are reasonable, but comparisons to decision-theoretic or POMDP-style VOI baselines, or to methods modeling worker reliability (e.g., Dawid–Skene-type aggregation), seem appropriate. An offline CRF trained on a small seed plus test-time selective querying would also be informative.

Quality:
The formulation is sound and the planning approximation is appropriate for the problem class. However, several modeling and experimental choices are simplified: constant worker accuracy, limited worker pool, and simulation protocol may bias results. The empirical results are promising, but more robust evaluation (e.g., different worker quality regimes, ablations, sensitivity to cost/latency weights, and stronger baselines) would strengthen the claims. Details on training from noisy labels and on computing the expected utility for structured outputs should be clarified.

Clarity:
The paper is generally well written and organized. The stochastic game and MCTS/PW algorithm are clearly described. However, several details are missing or ambiguous: (i) precise definition of Accuracy used in utility (token vs. sequence vs. F1 proxy), (ii) training procedure for pθ with noisy, partial responses, (iii) specification and calibration of pT, and (iv) dataset sizes/splits and feature configurations beyond brief mentions (UNIGRAMS vs. RNN). Including these would improve reproducibility.

Originality:
The combination of real-time, asynchronous crowd querying, decision-theoretic planning over structured outputs, and continuous-time MCTS is novel and interesting. The high-level idea relates to prior POMDP/VOI works in crowdsourcing and active classification, but the proposed on-the-job learning setup (learning while guaranteeing accuracy from the start) and the specific technical instantiation represent a meaningful contribution.

Significance:
The problem is practically significant and the approach could be impactful for deployable ML systems under tight data and latency constraints. If borne out under more rigorous evaluation and stronger baselines, the framework could influence future work on interactive ML and crowd-machine hybrids. The open-source release increases potential impact.

Questions and suggestions for authors:
- How is Accuracy defined in Eq. (2) for structured outputs, and how do you approximate ExpAcc for F1? If you use token-wise accuracy, discuss the mismatch with F1 at evaluation.
- Provide details on updating pθ online: do you marginalize over y given r (EM/soft labels), or treat r as noisy labels? How do you prevent confirmation bias?
- How are wM and wT selected? Please include sensitivity curves and Pareto frontiers over cost/latency/accuracy.
- Can you model worker/item heterogeneity (e.g., per-class confusion, difficulty) and show the effect on policy and performance? Even simple Dawid–Skene-style reliability could help.
- Report experiments with a larger, non-frozen worker pool and varying accuracy regimes (e.g., 60–85%) to test robustness.
- Add stronger baselines (e.g., VOI policies; offline CRF with small seed; reliability-aware aggregation) and ablations (no waiting vs. parallel-only; no redundancy).
- Clarify the latency model pT and how it is learned; do you condition on time of day or queue size?

Pros:
- Principled decision-theoretic formulation with continuous-time planning.
- Real-time, asynchronous system with open-source implementation.
- Structured prediction setting handled cleanly; intuitive policies emerge.
- Empirical gains and cost reductions across multiple tasks.

Cons:
- Simplified and potentially misspecified response model; limited simulation protocol.
- Metric mismatch and missing training/utility details.
- Limited sensitivity analyses and ablations; baselines could be stronger.

Recommendation:
Borderline accept. The problem and approach are compelling, the technical framing is well motivated, and the early results are encouraging. Strengthening the empirical evaluation and clarifying modeling/training details would make this a clear accept.
