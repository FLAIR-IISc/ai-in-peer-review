- Proposes "on-the-job learning": interleave model predictions with real-time asynchronous crowd queries at test time, trading accuracy, cost, and latency via a stochastic game.  
- Uses a CRF backbone, a simple noise and response-time model for crowd answers, and approximates optimal policies with MCTS (UCT) plus progressive widening.  
- Empirical results show large cost reductions vs all-human approaches and accuracy gains over pure ML baselines and a threshold heuristic; code (LENSE) released.  
- Novelty: unified decision-theoretic, test-time querying framework that updates online, handles structured outputs, and reasons jointly about cost and latency.  
- Strength: well-motivated problem with a clear Bayesian decision-theory formalization and a practical approximate solver.  
- Strength: structured prediction focus demonstrates label correlations reduce crowd effort.  
- Strength: promising empirical gains and open-source release increase reproducibility and impact.  
- Key weakness: utility/metric mismatch — planner optimizes expected MAP accuracy but evaluation uses token-level F1 (non-decomposable), creating misalignment.  
- Key weakness: simplistic response model (fixed 70% accuracy, uniform confusion) ignores worker heterogeneity and item difficulty, potentially skewing policies.  
- Key weakness: under-specified online learning from noisy crowd responses — unclear whether responses are treated as gold, soft-updated, or handled via EM.  
- Key weakness: planner/runtime details missing — MCTS parameters, rollout policy, simulation count, UCT constant, and wall-clock planning overhead are not reported.  
- Key weakness: baselines are weak — missing comparisons to myopic VOI, POMDP controllers, and learned active-classification policies.  
- Key weakness: experimental setup uses a frozen pool of five worker responses per label, which may not reflect real worker variability or drift.  
- Key weakness: limited quantitative analysis of parallel vs sequential querying and the latency–cost–accuracy tradeoff.  
- Clarity issues: inconsistencies (Twitter vs CoNLL), typos, undefined Accuracy in utility, missing pT calibration and wM/wT sensitivity.  
- Quality assessment: technically sound overall but important modeling choices and planner details are under-specified; empirical evaluation needs stronger baselines and ablations.  
- Suggestions: align utility with evaluation (e.g., expected F1), model and learn crowd heterogeneity, specify/ablate online update rules, and report planner runtime and MCTS settings.  
- Suggestions: add stronger baselines (myopic VOI, POMDP), ablations (CRF vs independent labels), sensitivity analyses for wM/wT, and show Pareto frontiers for latency–cost–accuracy.  
- Arguments for acceptance: addresses an important deployment problem, novel integration of structured prediction and real-time crowds, and solid empirical improvements with released code.  
- Arguments against acceptance: metric–utility mismatch, simplistic crowd model, under-specified learning from noisy labels, and missing runtime/latency analysis.  
- Recommendation: Weak accept, contingent on addressing clarity and evaluation gaps.
