- Introduces "on-the-job learning," combining crowd-sourcing with ML to build accurate systems without large labeled datasets upfront.  
- Models interaction as a stochastic game and uses Bayesian decision theory to trade off latency, cost, and accuracy.  
- Implements approach as LENSE and evaluates on NER, sentiment, and image classification.  
- Reports significant cost reductions (over an order of magnitude for NER) and improved accuracy vs. pure crowdsourcing and pure ML.  
- Clearly distinguishes from online learning, active learning, and active classification by maintaining high accuracy from the start.  
- Technically sound with a solid Bayesian theoretical foundation and well-motivated application.  
- Experimental evaluation is thorough: three tasks, four baselines, clear implementation details, and open-source code for reproducibility.  
- Original and meaningful contribution that bridges pure ML and pure crowdsourcing, with a novel Bayesian tradeoff formulation.  
- Highly relevant and significant for real-world deployment where labeled data is scarce and expensive.  
- Well-written and organized, with clear problem formulation, pseudocode, and comparisons to related work.  
- Lacks theoretical guarantees for the approximation algorithm and no analysis of approximation error or Monte Carlo tree search performance vs. optimal.  
- Crowd response model assumptions (70% accuracy) are underspecified; sensitivity to varying or lower worker accuracy is not thoroughly explored.  
- Missing comparisons to some recent related methods (e.g., Bayesian active learning with noise) that could affect significance.  
- Does not analyze why performance differs substantially across tasks (NER vs. sentiment/image), limiting guidance on applicability.  
- Recommendation: Accept — strong, practical contribution with minor concerns about theoretical guarantees and crowd model sensitivity.
