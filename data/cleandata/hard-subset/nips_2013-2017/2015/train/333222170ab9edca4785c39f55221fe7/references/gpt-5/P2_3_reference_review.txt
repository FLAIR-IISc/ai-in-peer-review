Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes “on-the-job learning,” a decision-theoretic framework that enables a system to make high-quality predictions from the start by selectively querying crowd workers at test time while continuously updating a model. The problem is cast as a stochastic game between the system and the crowd. A CRF provides the structured prediction model; a simple response model captures worker noise; a latency model captures response times. The system chooses actions (issue a query, wait, or return) to maximize an expected utility that trades off expected accuracy, monetary cost, and latency. Because exact planning is intractable, the policy is approximated via Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) with progressive widening to handle continuous time. Experiments on NER, sentiment classification, and image classification suggest substantial cost reductions and accuracy gains over human-only and machine-only baselines. The system (LENSE) is open-sourced.

This work relates to active learning and online learning but differs in seeking information before predicting on each instance to maintain high accuracy from the start. It is closest to decision-theoretic control of crowdsourcing (e.g., TurKontrol) and active classification/value-of-information methods, but extends them to structured outputs and asynchronous, parallel crowdsourcing with learning at test time. Legion:AR demonstrated real-time crowd-powered classification; this paper provides a formal Bayesian/game-theoretic treatment and general-purpose implementation.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The formulation as a utility-maximizing stochastic game is principled, and the use of MCTS with UCT and progressive widening is appropriate for large, continuous-time decision spaces. The utility correctly encodes the accuracy–cost–latency trade-off. However, several modeling choices limit realism:
  - Response model pR assumes fixed accuracy (0.7) with uniform noise, independent across queries and workers, and independent of instance difficulty. This ignores per-worker reliability, correlations, and context effects; it can bias value estimates and query planning.
  - The latency model pT is referenced but not fully specified or learned; its calibration matters critically for parallel vs sequential querying.
  - The prediction model is updated online, but the paper does not detail calibration or how ExpAcc is computed for structured outputs (exact vs approximate inference over y given partial responses).
- Algorithmic details: The MCTS description is clear at a high level, but rollout policy, depth/termination criteria, and computational cost per decision are not fully specified. No theoretical performance guarantees are given (reasonable for this setting), but sensitivity analyses to wM, wT, pR, and pT are limited.
- Experimental support: The three tasks and the retainer setup demonstrate feasibility and practical gains. However:
  - To “control variance,” the main results use a frozen pool of pre-collected worker responses and delays sampled without replacement. This reduces noise but risks misrepresenting nonstationarity and dependence across workers. It also partially disconnects the evaluation from the online nature of the method.
  - It is unclear whether retainer overhead costs are included. If not, cost comparisons to majority-vote may be optimistic.
  - Baselines are relatively weak: the online learning baseline assumes access to gold labels after prediction (advantageous but mismatched to the setting), the threshold heuristic is simple, and there is no comparison to decision-theoretic crowd control (e.g., POMDP/TurKontrol) or per-worker-reliability models (Dawid–Skene variants).

Clarity
The paper is generally well-organized and readable. The game description, utility, and algorithm are presented clearly. Some notational slips exist (footnotes breaking sentences; minor typos). Important details are missing or could be clearer: exact form/learning of pT, computation of ExpAcc for structured y, training protocol for CRF updates during test time, and computational budget/runtime per instance. The experimental section would benefit from explicit inclusion/exclusion of retainer costs and a more systematic sensitivity analysis.

Originality
The problem framing—maintaining high accuracy from the start by interleaving prediction, selective crowdsourcing, and learning—is timely and distinct from conventional active learning. Casting the decision as a stochastic game with explicit latency and cost and solving with MCTS for structured outputs appears novel relative to prior crowd-control and active classification work. The constituent ideas (Bayesian decision theory, value-of-information, MCTS) are known, but the combination and real-time, structured instantiation are original.

Significance
The practical significance is solid: many applications need immediate high-quality predictions before large labeled datasets exist. The approach provides a coherent way to trade accuracy, cost, and latency and shows notable gains on three tasks, with open-source code. That said, broader impact depends on addressing modeling realism (worker heterogeneity, nonstationary quality, better latency models) and stronger baselines. The framework is likely to stimulate follow-on work in decision-theoretic crowd–ML systems.

Strengths
- Clear, principled formulation of an important real-world problem.
- Elegant integration of Bayesian decision theory with MCTS to handle asynchronous, parallel queries and structured outputs.
- Demonstrated improvements in cost and accuracy across tasks; open-source release.
- Insightful qualitative behaviors (redundancy for accuracy, parallelism for latency).

Weaknesses
- Simplistic and fixed response model; no per-worker reliability or difficulty modeling.
- Latency model and ExpAcc computation details are underspecified; no calibration analysis.
- Main results based on simulated “frozen pool” responses; unclear inclusion of retainer overhead; potential mismatch to live deployment.
- Baselines do not include stronger decision-theoretic or worker-modeling alternatives; no ablations on MCTS components or policy choices.
- Search restrictions (no launching queries mid-wait) and no cancellation may reduce adaptivity; not empirically analyzed.

Questions for authors
- How are pT and pR estimated and updated online? How sensitive is performance to mis-specification?
- How is ExpAcc computed for structured y given partial responses (exact/approximate inference)?
- What is the per-instance compute time for MCTS, and how does it scale with sequence length?
- Do reported costs include retainer overhead? If not, how would including it affect cost savings?
- Can you compare to a POMDP baseline with learned worker reliabilities (e.g., TurKontrol-style, Dawid–Skene)?

Recommendation
Weak accept. The paper addresses a relevant and underexplored setting with a principled formulation and promising empirical results. While some modeling choices and baselines limit the strength of the claims, the contribution is novel enough and practically useful, and the work is likely to spur further research. Strengthening the empirical evaluation (real worker studies, stronger baselines, sensitivity analyses) and enhancing the realism of the environment model would significantly improve the paper.
