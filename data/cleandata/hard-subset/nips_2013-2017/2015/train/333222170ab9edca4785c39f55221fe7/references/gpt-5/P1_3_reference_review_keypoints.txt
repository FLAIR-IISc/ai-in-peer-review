- Proposal: "On-the-job learning" that interleaves model predictions with real-time crowd queries to maintain high initial accuracy and reduce crowd dependence over time.
- Core contribution: decision-theoretic framework that selects which parts to query, whether to wait, and when to return a prediction by maximizing expected utility trading off accuracy, latency, and cost.
- Models: uses a CRF prior for outputs, a noisy worker response model, and a latency model; planning approximated via MCTS (UCT) with progressive widening for continuous response times.
- System (LENSE) evaluated on NER, sentiment, and image classification, showing improved accuracy-cost tradeoffs versus human-only and machine-only baselines and a hand-crafted threshold policy.
- Novelty: couples asynchronous, continuous-time decision-theoretic crowd querying with structured prediction and online model updating.
- Strength: timely and practical problem formulation enabling high accuracy from the start without extensive pretraining.
- Strength: principled utility-based framework jointly optimizes accuracy, latency, and cost, yielding intuitive strategies like redundancy and parallelization.
- Strength: technically interesting use of MCTS + progressive widening and accommodation of structured outputs via CRFs.
- Strength: real-time deployment with retainer pool; code and data released for reproducibility.
- Strength: empirical gains across tasks and observed decline in query volume as the model improves.
- Weakness: oversimplified response model assuming fixed 0.7 worker accuracy, ignoring worker/item/class heterogeneity and confusion patterns.
- Weakness: simulation protocol uses a frozen small set of responses/delays, which may understate real-world variability and temporal drift.
- Weakness: utility function and evaluation may be misaligned (utility over MAP accuracy vs. evaluation by F1 for NER); expected F1 is non-decomposable and unspecified.
- Weakness: limited ablations and sensitivity analyses (weights for money/time, response model misspecification, MCTS vs. simpler policies).
- Weakness: under-specified learning details (how pθ is updated from noisy responses, marginalization for ExpAcc, latency model calibration).
- Weakness: baselines are weak; missing comparisons to VOI/POMDP-style methods, worker-reliability aggregation (e.g., Dawid–Skene), and offline CRF+selective querying.
- Quality: sound formulation and reasonable planning approximation, but modeling/simulation choices may bias empirical results; more robust evaluation needed.
- Clarity: paper is well written but omits key details—precise accuracy definition, online training procedure, pT specification, and dataset/feature details.
- Originality: meaningful and novel combination of real-time asynchronous crowd querying, decision-theoretic planning over structured outputs, and continuous-time MCTS.
- Significance: practically significant and potentially impactful for deployable ML under data/latency constraints, especially with open-source release.
- Main suggestions: clarify accuracy definition and ExpAcc computation for structured outputs, detail online updating of pθ, provide sensitivity analyses and Pareto frontiers for wM/wT.
- Main suggestions: model worker/item heterogeneity (e.g., Dawid–Skene), test with larger/varying worker pools, add stronger baselines and ablations, and clarify latency model learning.
- Pros: principled continuous-time planning, real-time asynchronous system, clean handling of structured prediction, open-source code, and empirical cost/accuracy gains.
- Cons: simplified/misspecified response model, metric mismatch and missing training/utility details, limited sensitivity analyses and weaker baselines.
- Recommendation: borderline accept — compelling and well-motivated but needs stronger empirical validation and clarification of modeling/training details.
