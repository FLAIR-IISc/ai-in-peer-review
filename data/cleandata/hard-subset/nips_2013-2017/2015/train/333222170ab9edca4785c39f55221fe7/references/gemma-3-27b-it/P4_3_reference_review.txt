This paper proposes a novel framework, On-the-Job Learning, for building AI systems without extensive pre-training data, leveraging a Bayesian decision-theoretic approach to strategically query a crowd for labels. The core idea is to balance accuracy, latency, and cost by dynamically deciding when and what to query, using the crowd as a temporary "crutch" that gradually becomes less necessary as the model improves. This builds upon prior work by the authors [6] and distinguishes itself from online learning and active learning by actively seeking information *during* prediction and adapting its querying policy based on observed data. The technical contribution lies in formulating this as a stochastic game and approximating the optimal policy using Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) with progressive widening. The framework, named LENSE, is evaluated on NER, sentiment classification, and image classification, demonstrating substantial cost reductions compared to full annotation and accuracy gains over online learning and single-label baselines.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The on-the-job learning paradigm, formalized as a stochastic game with Bayesian decision theory, is a significant contribution.  While building on the earlier work [6], this paper provides a rigorous theoretical grounding and a scalable approximation algorithm. The dynamic adaptation of the querying policy during test time sets it apart from traditional active learning.
*   **Technical Soundness:** The MCTS-based approximation is well-motivated, and the use of progressive widening addresses the challenge of continuous time. The utility function explicitly balances accuracy, cost, and latency, which aligns with real-world constraints.  The environment model incorporating crowd response and latency is realistic.
*   **Empirical Results:** The experiments on three diverse tasks demonstrate the effectiveness of LENSE. The reported cost reductions (e.g., over an order of magnitude on NER) and accuracy improvements are compelling. The ablation studies (Figure 4) provide valuable insights into the system's behavior over time.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the problem formulation, the model, and the algorithm. The inclusion of Figure 2, illustrating the game tree and timelines, is particularly helpful. The code release further enhances reproducibility.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Complexity:** While the MCTS approach is justified, the overall framework is quite complex.  The numerous components (stochastic game, utility function, response model, MCTS, progressive widening) might make it challenging for readers to fully grasp the details without significant effort.
*   **Sensitivity to Hyperparameters:** The performance of MCTS-based algorithms can be sensitive to hyperparameters. The paper doesn't provide a detailed discussion of how these hyperparameters were tuned or their impact on the results.
*   **Crowdsourcing Setup Specifics:** The paper describes the crowdsourcing setup but lacks detailed information about worker qualifications, quality control measures (beyond the initial tutorial), and potential biases in the worker pool. A deeper analysis of the impact of worker quality on the system's performance would be valuable.
*   **Limited Comparison to State-of-the-Art:**  The comparison to related work, while present, could be more thorough. For instance, the paper mentions related work on active classification [22, 23, 24], but does not directly compare LENSE against these methods on the same datasets. The comparison to “Human n-query” is useful but doesn't reflect realistic scenarios where multiple human labels are usually aggregated with more sophisticated methods.




**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and technically sound approach to on-the-job learning, addressing a practical problem with significant real-world applications.
*   The empirical results are strong and demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed framework.
*   The paper is well-written and clearly explains the complex concepts involved.
*   The code release promotes reproducibility and further research.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

*   The complexity of the framework might hinder its adoption.
*   The sensitivity to hyperparameters and the lack of detailed analysis of the crowdsourcing setup are potential concerns.
*   The comparison to related work could be more comprehensive.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that presents a significant contribution to the field. The novelty of the on-the-job learning paradigm, the technical soundness of the approach, and the compelling empirical results outweigh the weaknesses. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions, specifically addressing the sensitivity to hyperparameters, providing more details about the crowdsourcing setup, and strengthening the comparison to related work.
