Summary
This paper proposes “on-the-job learning”: a deployed system that, for each incoming input, adaptively queries crowd workers on uncertain parts before committing a prediction, while continually improving its model from the gathered supervision. The decision process is cast as a stochastic game between the system and the crowd, with a Bayesian decision-theoretic utility that explicitly trades off accuracy, latency, and monetary cost. The environment model couples a structured predictor pθ(y|x) (e.g., a CRF), a response-time model pT, and a noisy response model pR. Because exact planning is intractable, the authors approximate the optimal policy using Monte Carlo Tree Search (UCT) with progressive widening to handle continuous time. Experiments on NER, sentiment, and image classification show improved accuracy–cost trade-offs relative to pure crowd or pure machine baselines, and code is released. The work relates to online learning, active learning, active classification, real-time crowdsourcing (e.g., retainer pools), POMDP-style crowd control (e.g., TurKontrol), and structured prediction; Legion:AR is the closest conceptual antecedent but lacks the ML/planning formalization offered here.

Strengths
- Novel problem formulation and setting: The focus on delivering high accuracy from the first example by interleaving real-time crowdsourcing and prediction, while learning on-the-fly, is compelling and distinct from standard online/active learning.
- Principled utility: The Bayesian decision-theoretic framework makes the latency–cost–accuracy trade-off explicit and optimizable; the resulting strategies (parallel vs sequential querying, redundancy) are interpretable.
- Algorithmic contribution: Using MCTS with progressive widening for continuous-time query planning in structured prediction is creative and practically motivated.
- Structured outputs: Extending this to CRFs (and showing the benefits of structure-aware querying) increases the scope beyond simple classification.
- Empirical promise: On NER, the system achieves large cost reductions vs. majority-vote while improving accuracy over both pure human and pure ML baselines. The trajectory over time (declining query volume) aligns with the intended on-the-job learning behavior.
- Open-source implementation and reproducibility materials.

Weaknesses
- Simplistic response model: Fixing pR to 70% accuracy with uniform errors ignores worker/task heterogeneity, temporal drift, and learnable reliability. This is a strong assumption and likely biases the planning policy (e.g., the value of redundancy).
- Evaluation methodology: Simulating from a frozen pool of 5 worker responses per label stabilizes comparisons but raises ecological validity concerns; it obscures non-stationarity, learning worker reliabilities, and end-to-end effects of the policy on latency/cost. Live end-to-end runs (beyond precollection) and sensitivity to worker pool quality would strengthen claims.
- Baselines: The comparison set omits decision-theoretic/PO(M)DP-based crowd-control methods (e.g., TurKontrol and descendants) and structured budgeted inference baselines. The provided threshold heuristic is weak; stronger myopic/greedy policies and uncertainty-aware structured policies would be informative. Also missing are ablations of MCTS vs. simpler policies.
- Missing details: Key hyperparameters (UCT constant, rollout policy, search budget per instance), the exact computation/approximation of ExpAcc for structured outputs, and the calibration/learning of pT are under-specified. No runtime/scalability analysis is provided for long sequences or higher fanout. The restriction against launching a query mid-wait simplifies search but may materially hurt latency; its impact is unquantified.
- Clarity/Presentation: There are typos, dangling footnotes, and a few ambiguous definitions (e.g., ExpAcc computation for CRFs; details of learning pθ from “actual responses” vs. gold when datasets are used only for evaluation). Some claims (e.g., “boosting performance relative to expert provided labels”) need precise definitions.

Evaluation by criteria
- Quality: The formulation and approximate planning are sound and coherent, with reasonable empirical evidence on multiple tasks. However, the response/latency models are simplistic and not learned; baselines are limited; and some experimental choices reduce external validity.
- Clarity: Generally well organized with helpful intuition, but missing technical details and minor presentation issues hinder reproducibility of the planning component.
- Originality: High. The integration of real-time crowd querying, structured prediction, explicit latency–cost–accuracy utility, and MCTS-based planning is a novel and bold direction beyond standard online/active learning and prior crowd-control systems.
- Significance: High potential impact for deployable systems needing immediate accuracy with limited data. The framework could spur follow-ups on better environment modeling, richer structures, and stronger planning.

Suggestions for improvement
- Learn pR and pT online; incorporate worker/task heterogeneity (e.g., Dawid–Skene/GLAD-like) and adapt the policy accordingly.
- Add stronger baselines: POMDP-based crowd control, greedy/myopic expected-value-of-information policies, and structured budgeted inference heuristics; provide ablations of MCTS and the utility components.
- Report search budgets, UCT constants, rollout policies, and compute cost; analyze scalability and sensitivity to wM/wT.
- Provide live end-to-end experiments without pre-frozen responses; study robustness to shifts in worker quality and to adversarial/noisy conditions.
- Clarify ExpAcc computation for structured outputs and provide approximations used.

Pros for acceptance
- Clear, novel formulation with a principled utility and a creative planning solution.
- Demonstrated empirical gains and compelling qualitative behavior.
- Addresses a timely, impactful deployment problem; open-source release.

Cons against acceptance
- Over-simplified environment model and limited baselines weaken the empirical case.
- Missing technical details and some clarity issues.
- Evaluation design (pre-frozen responses) may not reflect real-world dynamics.

Recommendation
Accept (leaning). The paper introduces a significant, original direction with a principled formulation and a plausible algorithm, and shows promising results. The novelty and potential outweigh the methodological imperfections, which seem addressable. Confidence: moderate.
