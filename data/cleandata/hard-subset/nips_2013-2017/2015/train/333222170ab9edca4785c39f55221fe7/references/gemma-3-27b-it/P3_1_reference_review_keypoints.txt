- Presents "on-the-job learning": query crowd selectively at test time using Bayesian decision theory to balance accuracy, latency, and cost.
- Approximates optimal query policy with Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) and progressive widening, implemented as LENSE.
- Evaluated on NER, sentiment, and image classification, showing cost reductions and accuracy gains versus baselines.
- Positions the work relative to online/active learning, crowdsourcing, and Legion:AR, highlighting novelty in learning during test time.
- Novel and practically relevant problem setting for rapid deployment with limited labeled data.
- Principled stochastic-game/Bayesian decision-theoretic formulation provides a strong theoretical foundation.
- Technical contribution: combining MCTS and progressive widening to scale policy approximation.
- Strong empirical results with comparisons to full human annotation and an informative ablation study.
- Reproducibility: open-source code and CodaLab data release.
- Clear writing and helpful figures that aid comprehension.
- Limited detail on crowdsourcing setup, worker qualification, and post-collection quality control.
- Simplified response model assuming fixed 70% crowd accuracy; lacks modeling of worker variability or expertise.
- Potential runtime/computational overhead of MCTS during inference not thoroughly discussed; may hinder real-time use.
- Utility function weights (wM, wT) lack sensitivity analysis to show robustness under different trade-offs.
- Tasks are standard benchmarks; applying LENSE to more complex real-world scenarios would strengthen claims.
- For acceptance: addresses an important, well-motivated problem with solid methods and empirical validation.
- Against acceptance: simplifying assumptions about crowd accuracy and MCTS cost could limit applicability without further exploration.
- Overall: strong paper with valuable contributions; recommended for acceptance with minor revisions.
- Requested minor revisions: clarify crowdsourcing details, discuss MCTS complexity and optimizations, include utility-weight sensitivity analysis, and optionally discuss richer response models.
