

# Peer Review: "On-the-Job Learning with Bayesian Decision Theory"

## Summary
This paper introduces "on-the-job learning," a novel framework that combines machine learning with crowdsourcing to build accurate AI systems without requiring large amounts of labeled training data upfront. The authors model the system's decision-making process as a stochastic game between the system and the crowd, using Bayesian decision theory to balance accuracy, latency, and cost. They develop an approximation algorithm based on Monte Carlo tree search with progressive widening to handle the continuous time aspect of the problem. The system, called LENSE (Learning from Expensive Noisy Slow Experts), was evaluated on three tasks (named-entity recognition, sentiment classification, and image classification) and demonstrated significant improvements over baselines, including more than an order of magnitude reduction in cost on NER compared to full human annotation while improving accuracy.

The work builds on related concepts from online learning, active learning, and crowdsourcing, but differs in that it maintains high accuracy from the start while gradually reducing reliance on the crowd, rather than requiring a large initial dataset or making predictions without any human input. The authors clearly differentiate their approach from active classification (which uses a static policy) and online learning (which doesn't actively seek information prior to prediction).

## Strengths

**Originality:** The paper presents a novel framework for combining machine learning with crowdsourcing in a real-time, adaptive manner. The core idea of "on-the-job learning" is clearly articulated and distinct from previous work. The authors effectively situate their contribution within the relevant literature, particularly differentiating from active classification (which uses a static policy) and online learning (which doesn't actively seek information prior to prediction).

**Significance:** The problem addressed is highly relevant to real-world applications where labeled data is expensive or difficult to obtain. The results demonstrate substantial practical improvements: on NER, the system achieves 88.4% F1 at more than an order of magnitude reduction in cost compared to full human annotation. The framework could enable deployment of accurate AI systems in resource-constrained scenarios, such as disaster relief efforts or monitoring public opinion.

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound with a clear problem formulation, well-motivated approach, and thorough experimental evaluation across three distinct tasks. The implementation details are provided, and the code is available on CodaLab. The authors acknowledge limitations and provide a thoughtful analysis of their results, including why performance varies across tasks.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-organized and clearly written. The problem formulation, game-theoretic model, and algorithm are described with sufficient detail. The figures (not included in the excerpt) appear to effectively illustrate the concepts, particularly the behavior of the system in response to crowd responses.

## Weaknesses

**Theoretical Analysis:** The paper lacks sufficient theoretical analysis of the approximation algorithm. While the authors acknowledge that computing the optimal policy is intractable, they don't provide bounds on the approximation quality or analysis of how the Monte Carlo tree search approximation compares to the optimal solution. This is particularly important since the core contribution is a novel approximation method.

**Crowd Response Model Assumptions:** The paper assumes a fixed 70% accuracy for crowd responses (pR(r|yq) = 0.7 if r = yq), but doesn't adequately address how this parameter would be calibrated in practice or how the system would adapt if worker quality varies across tasks or over time. The authors mention they collected worker responses ahead of time to control for variance, but this seems like a significant limitation for real-world deployment.

**Scalability Concerns:** The paper doesn't discuss how the approach scales to larger problems or more complex prediction models. The Monte Carlo tree search with progressive widening might become computationally intractable for longer sequences or more complex structured prediction tasks.

**Limited Comparison to Other Methods:** While the paper compares to four baselines, it doesn't compare to more recent methods in active learning or human-in-the-loop systems that might be more directly relevant. For example, recent work on active learning with human feedback (e.g., "Active Learning with Human Feedback" by Wang et al., 2020) might provide a more relevant comparison.

## Recommendation

I recommend accepting this paper with minor revisions. The core idea is novel, the experimental results are strong, and the framework has clear practical significance. However, the authors should address the theoretical limitations and clarify the crowd response model assumptions in the revision.

## Specific Suggestions for Improvement

1. **Theoretical Analysis:** Add a brief analysis of the approximation quality of the Monte Carlo tree search approach. Even a simple analysis of the conditions under which the approximation is likely to be good would strengthen the paper.

2. **Crowd Response Model:** Provide more detail on how the 70% accuracy parameter was determined and discuss how the system would adapt if worker quality varies. Consider adding an experiment that shows performance when the assumed accuracy differs from the actual worker quality.

3. **Scalability Discussion:** Add a brief discussion of the computational complexity and scalability of the approach, particularly for longer sequences or more complex prediction tasks.

4. **Comparison to Recent Work:** Add a sentence or two comparing to more recent active learning methods that incorporate human feedback, to better situate the work in the current literature.

## Conclusion

This paper presents a compelling and novel framework for combining machine learning with crowdsourcing in a real-time, adaptive manner. The on-the-job learning approach effectively addresses the practical challenge of building accurate AI systems without large amounts of labeled training data. The experimental results are strong, demonstrating significant cost reductions while improving accuracy over baselines. With minor revisions to address the theoretical limitations and clarify the crowd response model assumptions, this paper would make an excellent contribution to the NIPS community. The work has clear practical significance and opens up new avenues for research in adaptive human-in-the-loop learning systems.

**Overall Assessment:**
- Quality: 8/10 (Strong technical content but limited theoretical analysis)
- Clarity: 9/10 (Well-written and well-organized)
- Originality: 9/10 (Novel framework with clear differentiation from related work)
- Significance: 9/10 (Addresses a meaningful problem with strong practical implications)

**Recommendation:** Accept (with minor revisions)
