Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes “on-the-job learning,” a deployment-time framework that interleaves prediction with real-time crowd queries to maintain high accuracy from the start while learning to reduce reliance on the crowd over time. The authors formalize decision-making as a stochastic game between the system and the crowd on top of a predictive model (e.g., a CRF for structured prediction). A Bayesian decision-theoretic utility trades off expected accuracy, monetary cost (per query), and latency (time). Because computing the optimal policy is intractable, the authors approximate it using Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) with UCT and progressive widening to handle continuous response times. They implement LENSE and evaluate on NER, sentiment classification, and face attribute classification, showing improved accuracy-cost tradeoffs vs. human-only and machine-only baselines and a heuristic thresholding baseline.

This work builds on and extends several lines: (i) online/active learning, but here the system actively seeks information before committing to a prediction; (ii) active classification/value-of-information at test time, but with dynamic, instance-specific decisions and continuous-time reasoning; (iii) crowdsourcing POMDP/control (e.g., TurKontrol) and real-time crowdsourcing with retainers (e.g., Bernstein et al., Legion:AR), but now integrated with structured prediction and online model updating. The use of MCTS with progressive widening for this setting appears new.

Strengths
- Clear problem motivation: quickly deploy accurate systems without large upfront labeling or high steady-state crowd cost.
- Principled formulation: a utility-based decision framework explicitly trading off expected accuracy, cost, and latency; intuitive emergent strategies (parallelization for latency, redundancy for noise).
- Sound algorithmic idea: MCTS with progressive widening to make planning feasible under continuous time and large branching factors.
- Structured prediction focus: supports token-level querying and uncertainty propagation; empirically effective on NER.
- Empirical findings: substantial cost reductions for comparable accuracy and improvements vs. single-vote and online learning baselines; demonstration across three tasks; code and data release.

Weaknesses and questions
- Response model simplicity: assuming a fixed 0.7 worker accuracy with uniform confusion ignores worker heterogeneity and item difficulty. This can bias the VOI calculation and the optimal redundancy policy. Prior work (e.g., TurKontrol, DS-style models) suggests modeling worker reliabilities and/or confusion matrices is crucial.
- Evaluation protocol: to “control for worker quality,” the authors pre-collect 5 responses per label and sample without replacement. This creates very small, discrete pools, departs from an i.i.d. setting, and may underestimate uncertainty (and potential long-tail behavior). It also weakens claims about real-time operation since most reported results appear to be simulation with replay.
- Learning from noisy labels: it is unclear whether the CRF is updated by marginalizing over crowd noise via pR or by treating responses as gold. If the latter, learning may be biased; if the former, the method is more principled but requires inference details the paper does not provide.
- Utility/metric mismatch: utility uses expected accuracy of the MAP. For NER, results are reported in F1; F1 is non-decomposable, and the relationship between the utility optimized during planning and the reported metric is unclear. Please justify or analyze the impact.
- Missing ablations and sensitivity: no analysis of MCTS budget, UCT constant, or progressive widening parameters; no runtime overhead numbers (critical for real-time use); no sensitivity to wM/wT; no analysis comparing parallel issuing vs. sequential querying using the same planner.
- Baselines: the “threshold baseline” seems weak, and comparisons lack stronger VOI-based policies (e.g., dynamic stopping rules for majority vote, POMDP-based control of votes), or test-time acquisition methods from budgeted prediction. Online learning baseline is also peculiar (it sees gold labels after predicting), which favors it but does not represent practitioners’ realistic baselines that could include active test-time queries.
- Assumptions/constraints: the restriction that no new queries can be launched mid-wait simplifies planning but may sacrifice latency; no queueing model or batched response effects; response-time model independence assumptions.
- Reporting clarity: important details (dataset statistics, feature sets, hyperparameters, planning budget, inference approximations, statistical significance) are insufficiently described in the provided text.

Quality
The formulation is technically sound and well-motivated. However, some key modeling and experimental details are underspecified (noise-aware learning, inference for ExpAcc, MCTS budget and timing). The response model and evaluation protocol are simplistic, limiting external validity. Nonetheless, the approach is coherent and yields promising empirical results.

Clarity
The paper is generally well-written and organized. The stochastic game and algorithm are clearly described at a high level. It would benefit from:
- Explicit derivation/approximation details for ExpAcc and inference in the structured model.
- Clear description of how learning handles noisy labels.
- Precise experimental setup (datasets, features, hyperparameters, number of simulations per decision, wall-clock decision latency).
- More careful proofreading (minor typos: “comparable,” “miscellaneous,” etc.).

Originality
The combination of a Bayesian decision-theoretic planner with structured prediction, continuous-time modeling, and MCTS/progressive widening for real-time crowd queries is novel to my knowledge. It is a thoughtful synthesis rather than a purely new theoretical contribution, but the integration is nontrivial and interesting.

Significance
The problem is important and practical. If broadly validated, LENSE could influence how practitioners deploy systems under tight data and time budgets. The strongest gains are on NER; broader robustness (more tasks, richer workers models) would enhance impact.

Suggestions for improvement
- Incorporate a learned worker/noise model (per-worker confusion matrices, item difficulty) and show its effect on policy and outcomes.
- Clarify how the model is trained under noisy responses; consider EM or posterior regularization consistent with pR.
- Provide runtime and MCTS budget details; demonstrate decisions can be made within the response-time budget.
- Add stronger baselines (dynamic vote control; VOI/POMDP-based acquisition; budgeted prediction methods).
- Conduct sensitivity analyses over wM and wT, and ablations for parallel vs. sequential querying.
- Align utility with evaluation metric (e.g., token accuracy vs. F1) or analyze the gap.
- Replace the “frozen 5-response” simulation with larger-scale i.i.d. sampling or live experiments; report statistical significance.

Arguments for acceptance
- Well-motivated, timely problem with a principled formulation.
- Novel integration of planning (MCTS + progressive widening) with structured, real-time crowd-ML systems.
- Empirically promising cost-accuracy-latency tradeoffs; open-source release.

Arguments against acceptance
- Simplistic response model and evaluation protocol limit realism and generality.
- Missing key methodological and runtime details; weak/sparse baselines.
- Utility/metric mismatch and unclear handling of noisy labels.

Recommendation: Weak accept, contingent on clarifying methodology and strengthening evaluation.

Reviewer confidence: Medium.
