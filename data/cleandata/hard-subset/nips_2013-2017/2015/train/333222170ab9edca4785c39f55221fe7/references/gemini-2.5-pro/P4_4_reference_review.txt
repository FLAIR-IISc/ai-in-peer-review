This paper introduces a framework called "on-the-job learning," designed to build accurate prediction systems from the start without requiring a large, pre-labeled training set. The core idea is to create a system that, upon receiving a new input, can actively query a crowd of human annotators for labels on specific parts of the input where the model is uncertain. The system then uses these (potentially noisy and delayed) responses to make a high-quality prediction for the current input, while simultaneously updating its internal model to become more self-sufficient over time. The authors frame this problem as a trade-off between accuracy, monetary cost, and latency.

The problem is formally modeled as a stochastic game between the system and the crowd. The system's actions include querying a specific part of the input, waiting for a response, or returning a final prediction. The crowd's "actions" are stochastic, involving providing a noisy response after a random delay. Bayesian decision theory is employed to guide the system's policy, aiming to maximize a utility function that explicitly balances prediction accuracy with the costs of queries and time. The underlying predictive model is a Conditional Random Field (CRF) that is updated online. Since computing the optimal policy is intractable, the authors propose an approximation based on Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS), adapted with progressive widening to handle the continuous nature of response times.

This work situates itself at the intersection of several fields. It differs from standard online learning by actively seeking information before prediction to ensure high initial accuracy, rather than only learning from past errors. Unlike active learning, its primary goal is to accurately label the current instance, not just to select examples to train a future model. It advances upon active classification by learning a dynamic policy that improves at test-time, rather than relying on a static policy learned from a pre-existing dataset. The problem framing is related to decision-theoretic control of crowdsourcing (e.g., Dai et al., 2013), but is applied to a structured prediction setting where the system learns and improves its own model.

**Strengths**

1.  **Significance and Originality:** The paper defines and formalizes the "on-the-job learning" setting, which addresses the significant practical problem of deploying machine learning systems in scenarios with little to no initial training data (the "cold start" problem). This is a well-motivated and important contribution. The synthesis of ideas from online learning, active classification, and crowdsourcing into a single, principled framework is novel and compelling.

2.  **Technical Quality:** The formulation of the problem as a stochastic game governed by Bayesian decision theory is technically sound and elegant. The utility function provides a principled way to manage the trade-offs between accuracy, cost, and latency. The choice of MCTS with progressive widening as an approximation method for solving the intractable game is well-justified and appropriate for the problem structure. The overall technical execution is of high quality.

3.  **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The problem is clearly motivated, the model is described with sufficient detail, and the behavior of the system is intuitively explained. The inclusion of Figure 2, which illustrates the decision-making process and the model's evolving beliefs, is particularly effective. The authors clearly delineate how their work differs from related areas.

4.  **Empirical Evaluation:** The experimental evaluation is thorough and convincing. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their system, LENSE, on three distinct tasks (NER, sentiment analysis, image classification). The chosen baselines are appropriate and highlight the advantages of the proposed approach. The results, which show substantial cost reductions (over an order ofmagnitude in one case) and accuracy improvements over both pure-ML and pure-crowd baselines, are impressive and strongly support the paper's claims. The provision of open-source code and data for reproducibility is a significant strength.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Scalability of the Decision Algorithm:** The paper does not analyze the computational complexity of the MCTS-based decision-making process. For each prediction, the system must run a significant number of Monte Carlo simulations to choose the next action. The computational cost of this step could become a bottleneck, especially for structured prediction tasks with large output spaces (e.g., long sentences for NER) or in scenarios requiring very low-latency predictions. A discussion of how decision time scales with input size would strengthen the paper.

2.  **Simplicity of the Environment Model:** The model of the crowd is based on simplifying assumptions. The response accuracy `pR` is modeled as a fixed constant (70%), independent of the specific worker, the query's difficulty, or other context. Similarly, the response time model `pT` is not detailed. In real-world crowdsourcing platforms, worker quality and response times can vary significantly. While these assumptions are reasonable for an initial investigation, the robustness of the system to more realistic, heterogeneous crowd behavior is an open question.

3.  **Clarity on Utility Calculation:** The utility function in Equation 1 relies on calculating an expected accuracy term, `ExpAcc`. The paper does not specify how this expectation is estimated in practice during the MCTS rollouts. Given that this is a core component of the reward signal, a clearer description of its approximation (e.g., via sampling from the posterior `p(y)`) would improve the clarity and reproducibility of the method.

**Summary of Arguments**

*   **For Acceptance:**
    *   Introduces and formalizes a novel, practical, and significant learning setting.
    *   Presents a technically sound and principled solution based on Bayesian decision theory and MCTS.
    *   The paper is exceptionally clear and well-written.
    *   Provides strong and convincing empirical evidence across multiple tasks, demonstrating clear benefits.
    *   The work is fully reproducible, with code and data provided.

*   **Against Acceptance:**
    *   The scalability of the proposed search algorithm for decision-making is not addressed.
    *   The model of the crowd environment relies on simplifying assumptions that may not hold in practice.

In conclusion, this is a high-quality paper that makes a significant contribution by formalizing the "on-the-job learning" problem and presenting an effective, principled solution. The strengths, particularly the novelty of the problem formulation, the technical soundness of the approach, and the strength of the empirical results, substantially outweigh the weaknesses, which primarily point to avenues for future work. The paper is a strong candidate for acceptance.
