This paper introduces a compelling framework, “on-the-job learning,” for tackling prediction problems with limited initial labeled data by strategically querying a crowd. The core idea – modeling this as a stochastic game with Bayesian decision theory to balance cost, latency, and accuracy – is genuinely novel and represents a significant departure from traditional supervised learning or purely crowdsourced solutions. The authors smartly position this within the landscape of online learning and active learning, highlighting the key distinction of simultaneous model improvement during test time. The practical implementation, LENSE, and its evaluation across three diverse tasks (NER, sentiment, image classification) are commendable. The reported results, particularly the >10x cost reduction in NER, are striking.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The on-the-job learning framework is a fresh perspective, especially the formulation as a stochastic game. This moves beyond incremental improvements and suggests a new research direction.
*   **Theoretical Grounding:** The use of Bayesian decision theory provides a principled approach to query selection. The MCTS approximation, though likely imperfect, demonstrates a commitment to a rigorous solution.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The experiments are well-designed, comparing against relevant baselines. The results consistently favor the proposed approach, demonstrating its practical efficacy. The open-source release is a huge plus.
*   **Practical Impact:** The potential to reduce costs while maintaining accuracy is substantial, making this work relevant to real-world applications where labeled data is expensive or unavailable.
*   **Clear Writing:** The paper is, for the most part, clearly written and logically organized. The problem formulation and model description are reasonably accessible.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **MCTS Approximation:** While the MCTS-based approximation is necessary, the paper lacks a thorough analysis of its effectiveness. How well does it scale? What are its limitations? The approximation’s performance isn’t explicitly quantified.
*   **Crowd Modeling:** The assumption of a fixed pool of crowd worker responses (sampled without replacement) feels somewhat artificial. Real-world crowd workers’ performance fluctuates, and the model doesn't adapt to this. While acknowledged, the fixed 70% accuracy in the response model feels simplistic.
*   **Limited Scalability Discussion:**  The computational complexity of the MCTS approach isn't fully addressed, especially concerning large state spaces or complex prediction models.
*   **Task Diversity:** While three tasks are used, the performance gains are most dramatic in NER. The results on sentiment and image classification are more modest, raising questions about the framework’s generalizability.
*   **Utility Function:** The weighting of cost (wM) and time (wT) in the utility function seems arbitrary. Sensitivity analysis on these parameters would be beneficial.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a genuinely novel approach to a challenging problem.
*   The technical contribution (formulation as a stochastic game) is significant.
*   The empirical results are promising, especially in the NER task.
*   The open-source release fosters reproducibility and further research.
*   The potential impact of this work is substantial.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

*   The MCTS approximation lacks a rigorous evaluation of its performance.
*   The crowd modeling is somewhat simplistic.
*   The scalability of the approach is not fully addressed.
*   The generalizability of the framework beyond NER is uncertain.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that I am inclined to accept despite the minor flaws. The novelty and potential impact of the on-the-job learning framework outweigh the weaknesses. While the MCTS approximation and crowd modeling could be improved, they do not fundamentally invalidate the core contribution. The authors have successfully demonstrated the feasibility and benefits of this approach, opening up a promising new avenue for research in areas where labeled data is scarce.  I believe this work has the potential to significantly influence the field, even if further refinement is needed. 

**Rating:** Accept (with minor revisions – perhaps addressing scalability concerns and providing more analysis of the MCTS approximation).
