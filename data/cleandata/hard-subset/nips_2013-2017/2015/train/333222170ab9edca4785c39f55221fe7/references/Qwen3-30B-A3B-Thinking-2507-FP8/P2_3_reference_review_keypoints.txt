- Introduces "on-the-job learning": a framework that queries a crowd for uncertain input parts and learns during deployment to reduce future crowd reliance.  
- Formalizes the problem as a stochastic game using Bayesian decision theory to trade off accuracy, cost, and latency.  
- Uses Monte Carlo tree search with progressive widening as the algorithmic solution, well-justified for the problem.  
- Empirical results are strong: outperforms four baselines on NER, sentiment, and image tasks (88.4% F1 on NER, >10x cost reduction, 28% F1 improvement over online learning).  
- Problem formulation and model presentation are clear, with precise notation and effective figures (e.g., Figure 2).  
- Reproducibility is supported by open-source code (LENS), CodaLab data, and detailed crowdsourcing setup.  
- Originality: novel integration of Bayesian decision theory with dynamic crowd querying for structured prediction, distinct from online/active learning and active classification.  
- Significance: addresses real-world deployment challenges by enabling immediate high-accuracy systems without large labeled datasets, lowering barriers in resource-constrained settings.  
- Limited task scope: experiments focus on well-studied tasks and lack evaluations on more complex structural dependencies or other input types (e.g., tabular, time series).  
- Crowdsourcing assumptions: assumes a stable ~70% accurate crowd and does not fully address varying worker quality or scenarios without crowd availability.  
- Scalability concerns: paper lacks analysis of computational complexity and scalability for larger inputs or high-throughput deployment.  
- Overall recommendation: technically sound, clear, original, and impactful—recommend Accept for NIPS.
