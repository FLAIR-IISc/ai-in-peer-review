This paper proposes an on-the-job learning framework utilizing Bayesian decision theory to balance latency, cost, and accuracy when querying a crowd for labels. The core idea – strategically querying for labels during prediction rather than relying solely on pre-trained models or purely crowdsourced solutions – is intuitively appealing, particularly in resource-constrained scenarios. The formulation as a stochastic game with Monte Carlo tree search for policy approximation is a reasonable approach, and the experimental results on NER, sentiment classification, and image classification demonstrate potential benefits, particularly in cost reduction on NER.

However, despite the novelty of the overall framework, the paper suffers from a multitude of minor (but cumulatively significant) flaws in presentation, technical detail, and experimental rigor. These issues, while individually minor, create a sense of carelessness and ultimately undermine confidence in the validity of the results and the thoroughness of the analysis.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The on-the-job learning paradigm, formalizing the idea from [6], is a valuable contribution.
*   **Principled Approach:** Using Bayesian decision theory to balance competing objectives is a solid foundation.
*   **Experimental Validation:** Demonstrating cost reduction on NER is a strong result.
*   **Open-Source Implementation:** Providing LENSE is commendable and facilitates reproducibility.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Clarity & Organization:** The writing is often convoluted and repetitive. For instance, the distinction between online learning and active learning is restated several times without significant added clarity. Section 3, describing the stochastic game, is exceptionally dense and difficult to follow. The notation is inconsistent. For example, 'q' is sometimes a single query and sometimes a vector of queries.
*   **Technical Incompleteness:** Several aspects of the implementation are glossed over. The AdaGrad learning rate schedule for the CRF is not described. The specifics of the response model *pR(r|yq)* beyond the 70% accuracy assumption are lacking. How is the 30% of probability distributed? Is it uniform, or based on a more sophisticated model of common errors? What CRF implementation was used?
*   **Experimental Rigor:** The experimental setup raises concerns. Freezing a pool of worker responses is a pragmatic choice for reproducibility, but it introduces a potential bias. Did the authors verify that this frozen pool represents the true distribution of worker responses encountered during a live deployment? Furthermore, the justification for the specific cost/accuracy trade-off weights (wM and wT) is absent. Were these values chosen arbitrarily or tuned based on a sensitivity analysis? The comparison with the "Human n-query" baseline is potentially misleading without a clear description of the human annotator expertise. Are these expert annotators or crowd workers like those used for LENSE?
*   **Minor Errors & Inconsistencies:** The paper contains numerous minor errors. The statement that LENSE achieves an "order of magnitude" cost reduction on NER is not precisely quantified in Table 2, making it difficult to verify. Several citations are missing page numbers. The phrase “retainer model of [18]” is used but the reference [18] is not defined in the bibliography. In section 5, the sentence "While on-the-job learning allows us to deploy quickly and ensure good results, we would like to eventually operate without crowd supervision." feels oddly tacked on and lacks connection to the earlier discussion.
*   **Related Work:** While the related work section is comprehensive, it lacks critical assessment. Many papers are simply listed without a clear explanation of how they relate to or differ from the current work. The citation of [27] and [28] seems somewhat tenuous and doesn’t clearly articulate the connection to the proposed method.

**Pro & Con Acceptance:**

**Pro:**

*   Novelty of the on-the-job learning framework.
*   Potential for significant cost reduction in specific applications (NER).
*   Principled theoretical foundation.

**Con:**

*   Lack of clarity and technical detail.
*   Questionable experimental rigor.
*   Numerous minor errors and inconsistencies.
*   Insufficient justification for design choices (weights, response model).
*   Overall impression of a rushed and insufficiently polished manuscript.



**Overall Assessment:**

While the core idea of this paper is promising and the initial results are encouraging, the numerous flaws in presentation, technical detail, and experimental setup raise significant concerns. The paper feels incomplete and lacks the polish expected of a NIPS submission. The cumulative effect of these issues overshadows the positive contributions. I recommend **rejection** at this time. The authors are encouraged to address the concerns raised in this review and resubmit a revised version with greater attention to detail and clarity. Specifically, they should provide more thorough explanations of their technical choices, strengthen their experimental validation, and carefully proofread the manuscript for errors.
