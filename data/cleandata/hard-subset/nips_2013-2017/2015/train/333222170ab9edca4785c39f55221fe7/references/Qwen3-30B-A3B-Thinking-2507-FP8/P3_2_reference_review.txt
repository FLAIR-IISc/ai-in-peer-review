

# Peer Review: "On-the-Job Learning with Bayesian Decision Theory"

## Summary
This paper introduces "on-the-job learning," a novel framework for building AI systems without large labeled datasets by dynamically querying crowds during prediction time while improving the model through observed responses. The authors model the problem as a stochastic game between the system and the crowd, using Bayesian decision theory to optimize the tradeoff between accuracy, latency, and cost. They propose an approximation algorithm using Monte Carlo Tree Search with progressive widening and evaluate on NER, sentiment, and image classification tasks, demonstrating significant cost reduction (order of magnitude for NER) while improving accuracy over baselines.

## Strengths

**Originality and Technical Contribution:** The core concept of "on-the-job learning" is well-motivated and distinct from existing approaches. The authors clearly differentiate their work from online learning (which doesn't query during prediction), active learning (static policy), and active classification (relies on pre-labeled data). The modeling of the problem as a stochastic game with Bayesian decision theory is elegant and well-executed, providing a principled framework for the tradeoffs between accuracy, latency, and cost. This represents a meaningful contribution to the intersection of active learning, crowdsourcing, and online learning.

**Experimental Evaluation:** The experimental design is strong, with appropriate baselines (human n-query, online learning, threshold baseline) across three diverse tasks. The results are compelling: 88.4% F1 on NER with over 10× cost reduction compared to human 5-vote approach, and 8% F1 improvement over single human labeling. The analysis of how query patterns evolve over time (Figure 3) and the clear demonstration of the cost-accuracy tradeoff (Figure 4) are particularly strong.

**Clarity and Presentation:** The paper is exceptionally well-written, with clear mathematical formulations (Section 3) and logical flow from problem formulation to solution to evaluation. The algorithm description (Algorithm 1) is precise and well-motivated. The open-source implementation (LENSE) significantly enhances the paper's reproducibility and practical value.

## Weaknesses

**Theoretical Limitations:** Despite the strong practical results, the paper lacks theoretical guarantees about the quality of the MCTS approximation. How close is the approximate policy to the optimal one? The authors acknowledge the intractability but provide no bounds or analysis of approximation quality, which is a significant gap for a NIPS submission.

**Crowd Model Simplifications:** The crowd response model assumes a fixed 70% accuracy, which is problematic for generalization. The paper states "we found the humans we hired were roughly 70% accurate," but this doesn't account for variations across different tasks, worker skill levels, or time (e.g., worker fatigue). A more robust model that adapts to worker quality over time would strengthen the approach.

**Scalability Concerns:** The paper doesn't address scalability for larger inputs or more complex prediction models. For instance, how does the MCTS runtime scale with sentence length in NER or image complexity in the face classification task? The authors mention "progressive widening to reason about continuous time," but don't discuss computational complexity or practical limits.

**Comparison with Recent Work:** The related work section could better engage with recent advances in active learning with deep learning (e.g., [29-31]). The paper's threshold baseline is a simple heuristic that doesn't leverage modern active learning techniques, potentially making the comparison less meaningful.

## Overall Assessment

This is a strong paper with clear practical significance and a novel, well-executed technical approach. The on-the-job learning framework addresses a real-world problem (deploying AI systems with scarce labeled data) in a principled way, and the experimental results are compelling. The main weaknesses are the lack of theoretical guarantees and the oversimplified crowd model, which are significant for a top-tier conference like NIPS.

**Recommendation:** Accept. The paper makes a valuable contribution to the field, with strong technical content, clear presentation, and meaningful empirical results. While the theoretical gaps are notable, the practical significance and novelty of the approach outweigh these limitations. The authors could strengthen the paper by adding a brief discussion of the theoretical limitations and potential extensions to the crowd model in the revision.

**Pro:** Novel framework addressing a real-world problem, strong empirical results, clear writing, practical implementation.  
**Con:** Limited theoretical analysis, oversimplified crowd model, scalability concerns not addressed.  

This work represents an important step toward practical AI deployment in resource-constrained settings, and I believe it would be a valuable addition to the NIPS proceedings. The authors have made a significant contribution to the intersection of active learning, crowdsourcing, and online learning that will likely inspire further research in this area.
