This paper introduces a compelling and novel framework – on-the-job learning – for tackling prediction tasks when labeled data is scarce and expensive. The core idea of strategically querying a crowd to reduce uncertainty *during* prediction, coupled with a Bayesian decision-theoretic approach to balance cost, latency, and accuracy, is a significant contribution. The authors convincingly position their work against existing approaches like online learning and active learning, highlighting how their method avoids the limitations of needing a pre-trained model or relying on static querying policies. The experimental results, demonstrating order-of-magnitude cost reductions and F1 score improvements compared to baselines, are impressive. 

The paper's strength lies in its boldness and the clear articulation of a novel paradigm. The framing as a stochastic game and the use of Monte Carlo Tree Search to navigate the decision space are clever and appropriate. The open-source release (LENSE) is a valuable addition and encourages reproducibility. The analysis of how feature richness impacts query needs is insightful.

However, the paper isn’t without its weaknesses, though I'm willing to overlook them given the novelty. The response model (assuming 70% accuracy) is somewhat arbitrarily chosen, though justified by empirical observation. A more thorough exploration of the sensitivity of the results to this parameter would be valuable. While the experiments are well-conducted, the chosen tasks (NER, sentiment, image classification) feel somewhat disconnected. A task with a more complex, hierarchical structure might better showcase the benefits of the Bayesian approach.  The discussion of related work, while extensive, could benefit from a more critical assessment of *why* previous approaches haven’t tackled this problem in the same way. The assumption of a retainer model for crowd workers is practical, but limits the generalizability; results using a more standard, per-query payment scheme would strengthen the conclusions. The claim about the utility function balancing accuracy, cost, and latency could benefit from a more concrete example showing how those three are optimized. 

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **High Novelty:** The on-the-job learning framework is a genuinely new way of approaching prediction with limited labeled data.
*   **Strong Theoretical Foundation:** The formulation as a stochastic game and the use of Bayesian decision theory provide a principled approach to the problem.
*   **Compelling Empirical Results:** The reported cost reductions and accuracy improvements are significant.
*   **Practical Impact:** The open-source implementation makes the work immediately accessible and potentially useful to other researchers.
*   **Bold Approach:** The paper tackles a difficult problem and proposes a non-trivial solution, rather than incremental improvement.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Response Model Sensitivity:** The results may be sensitive to the assumed accuracy of crowd workers.
*   **Task Diversity:** The choice of tasks doesn’t fully explore the potential of the framework.
*   **Limited Generalizability:** Reliance on a retainer model for crowd workers may limit the applicability of the results.
*   **Minor Clarity Issues:** Some aspects of the utility function discussion could be more concrete.



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite these minor flaws, the paper presents a highly original and potentially impactful contribution to the field. The novelty of the on-the-job learning framework and the strong empirical results outweigh the limitations. I believe this work represents a significant step forward in enabling practical AI systems in resource-constrained environments. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions to address the concerns regarding response model sensitivity and task diversity. The authors should also consider expanding the discussion of related work to more clearly articulate the unique contributions of their approach.
