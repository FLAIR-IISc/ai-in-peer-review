Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes “on-the-job learning,” a decision-theoretic framework that integrates real-time crowdsourcing into test-time prediction so that a system can deliver high accuracy from the start without pretraining, while gradually reducing reliance on the crowd as its model improves. The authors formalize the problem as a stochastic game between the system and the crowd, define a utility that trades off accuracy, cost, and latency, and approximate the optimal policy with Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) and progressive widening to handle continuous response times. The environment model combines a structured predictor pθ(y|x) (e.g., a CRF), a simple noisy-response model for workers pR, and a response-time model pT. The system is instantiated as LENSE and evaluated on NER, sentiment, and image classification with a retainer-based crowd.

This work sits at the intersection of online learning, active learning, active classification, and crowd-powered systems. It extends earlier real-time crowd-in-the-loop systems (e.g., Legion:AR) by: (i) modeling structured outputs, (ii) learning the machine model online during deployment, and (iii) optimizing a trade-off between latency, monetary cost, and expected accuracy using Bayesian decision theory. Compared to POMDP-style decision-theoretic approaches in crowdsourcing (e.g., TurKontrol), this work emphasizes structured prediction and asynchronous parallel querying; compared to active classification, it forgoes a static policy and updates the model continuously with noisy labels.

Quality
Strengths:
- Sound problem formulation: casting the interaction as a sequential decision problem with explicit costs and a Bayesian expected-utility objective is principled. The stochastic game formulation with asynchronous responses is well-motivated.
- Algorithmic contribution: combining MCTS with progressive widening to handle continuous response times is appropriate and practically useful; the UCT rule makes the search adaptive to uncertainty and value estimates.
- Empirical results indicate material gains: on NER, LENSE reportedly achieves an order-of-magnitude cost reduction relative to 5-vote majority while improving F1; it also outperforms a heuristic thresholding baseline and a strong online-learning-only baseline.

Weaknesses/concerns:
- Utility/metric mismatch: The utility uses expected accuracy of the MAP sequence, but results are reported in F1 (especially important for NER). The paper should clarify the exact accuracy function used in the utility and discuss the mismatch to F1. If utility is token-accuracy or 0–1 sequence accuracy, how sensitive is the learned policy to this choice?
- Environment model simplicity: The constant worker-accuracy assumption pR(r|y)=0.7 (uniform errors otherwise) ignores worker heterogeneity, item difficulty, and context; those are known to matter and are modeled in TurKontrol and related work. At minimum, the paper should estimate pR from pilot data and report sensitivity analyses to misspecification; ideally, it would adapt pR online and/or model worker reliabilities.
- Experimental protocol: To “control variance,” the authors pre-collect 5 responses per label per item and sample without replacement during simulation. While this reduces noise across methods, it also constrains variability and may bias latency distributions and error correlations, possibly favoring policies that rely on adaptivity. Results from live experiments (with truly asynchronous returns and replacement) would strengthen claims.
- Baselines: The 5-vote and online-learning baselines are useful but limited. It would be informative to include:
  - A POMDP-based or Dawid–Skene-style adaptive voting baseline that models worker reliabilities and adaptive stopping.
  - A stronger active classification baseline that accounts for time cost (static but cost-aware).
  - Ablations of LENSE: MCTS vs. greedy myopic policy; sequential vs. parallel querying; removing progressive widening.
- Hyperparameters and calibration: The utility weights wM and wT critically govern the policy; how are they chosen? Is the model pθ calibrated so that ExpAcc is meaningful? The paper mentions calibration feedback but lacks details. Reporting sensitivity curves would help.
- Theoretical guarantees: No bounds on regret or approximation quality of the MCTS policy are discussed; while not strictly necessary, even qualitative analysis of when progressive widening converges would add rigor.
- Reproducibility details: Code and data are provided, which is excellent, but the paper should still include key dataset sizes/splits, CRF and RNN configurations, training hyperparameters, and how often the model is updated during deployment.

Clarity
Overall clear and well-organized with intuitive examples and figures (as described). Some details are underspecified: the exact accuracy function in utility, the procedure for setting wM/wT, formal definitions for structured F1 vs. utility, and complete model/training details. Tightening these would improve reproducibility and interpretability.

Originality
The combination of: (i) decision-theoretic planning over asynchronous crowd queries, (ii) structured prediction with online learning during deployment, and (iii) MCTS with progressive widening for continuous time is novel relative to prior NIPS/NeurIPS work, despite conceptual links to POMDP-based crowd control and active classification. The setting of “on-the-job” structured prediction is a meaningful extension.

Significance
The problem is important and timely: many applications need immediate high-quality predictions with little upfront data. The approach is generic and could impact practitioners building hybrid systems. The strongest empirical gains are on NER; results on sentiment and faces are positive but more modest. Broader adoption will hinge on robustness to worker variability and the practicality of a retainer pool. Nevertheless, the contribution advances the design of principled human-AI decision policies and should stimulate follow-up work.

Pros
- Principled formulation of real-time human-in-the-loop prediction with explicit latency/cost trade-offs.
- Novel use of MCTS + progressive widening in this setting.
- Empirically strong on NER with meaningful cost reductions; open-source release.
- Clear intuition for sequential vs. parallel querying and adaptivity.

Cons
- Simplistic and fixed worker noise model; no worker/item heterogeneity.
- Utility/metric mismatch and limited discussion of calibration and hyperparameter selection.
- Simulation protocol may under-represent real-world variance; limited live evaluation.
- Missing stronger adaptive baselines and ablations; limited theoretical analysis.

Questions and suggestions
- Clarify the definition of Accuracy in the utility and how it relates to F1 optimization.
- How are wM and wT chosen; can you report sensitivity plots?
- Can pR and pT be learned online from retainer data and adapted per worker?
- Provide ablations: greedy policy, different batch sizes, and effect of progressive widening.
- Include a baseline that adaptively collects votes with a worker-reliability model.
- Report computational overhead of planning and its impact on end-to-end latency.

Recommendation
Weak accept. The paper addresses an important problem with a principled framework and shows promising results, especially on structured prediction. Addressing the concerns on modeling realism, metric alignment, and baselines would further strengthen the contribution.
