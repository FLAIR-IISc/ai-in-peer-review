This paper proposes a novel “on-the-job learning” framework that leverages a crowd to iteratively improve predictions while minimizing cost and latency. The core idea is to model the interaction between the system and the crowd as a stochastic game, using Bayesian decision theory to strategically query for labels only when uncertain. This builds on prior work in active learning and crowdsourcing, but distinguishes itself by operating in a continuous, online fashion without a pre-labeled dataset and emphasizing the trade-off between accuracy, cost, and latency. The authors implement their framework (LENSE) and demonstrate its effectiveness on NER, sentiment classification, and image classification tasks, achieving significant cost reductions and performance gains compared to baselines.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty and Significance:** The on-the-job learning paradigm is a compelling and practical approach, especially in scenarios with limited resources and real-time requirements. It addresses a clear gap between purely supervised learning and crowdsourcing. The formalization as a stochastic game and the use of Bayesian decision theory provide a principled foundation.
* **Technical Soundness:** The proposed model, utilizing a CRF and Monte Carlo Tree Search, appears technically sound. The approximations made for tractability (progressive widening) are well-motivated and appear reasonable. The utility function balancing accuracy, cost, and latency captures the key considerations of this setting.
* **Strong Empirical Results:** The experimental results across three diverse tasks are convincing. The reported cost reductions and performance improvements are substantial, particularly on the NER task. The inclusion of baseline comparisons (human n-query, online learning, threshold baseline) provides a good context for evaluating the benefits of LENSE.
* **Implementation and Reproducibility:**  The authors have made their implementation (LENSE) publicly available, which is a significant contribution to reproducibility and further research. The inclusion of a CodaLab worksheet is excellent.
* **Clear Writing:** The paper is well-written and organized. The introduction clearly motivates the problem, and the technical details are explained in a comprehensive manner.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Exploration of Parameter Sensitivity:** While the results are strong, the paper lacks a detailed analysis of the sensitivity of the approach to key parameters (e.g., the cost weights in the utility function, the parameters controlling the MCTS exploration-exploitation trade-off). How do these choices impact performance and cost?
* **Crowdsourcing Setup Specifics:** The description of the crowdsourcing setup is somewhat brief.  More detail about worker qualification, task instructions, and quality control mechanisms would be helpful. Specifically, stating the number of workers in the retainer pool would be valuable. While the authors froze the worker responses during simulation, this approach might not fully reflect real-world variability in crowd quality.
* **Comparison to Legion:AR:** The paper mentions Legion:AR [6] as related work. However, the comparison feels superficial. A more detailed discussion of the differences and similarities, beyond simply stating that LENSE explores the “machine learning foundations,” would strengthen the paper.  For example, how do the approaches differ in their handling of uncertainty or the integration of feedback?
* **Assumptions in the Environment Model:** The assumption of independent responses (Eq. 3) and the fixed response error rate (pR(r | yq) = 0.7) are simplifications. While reasonable, exploring the impact of relaxing these assumptions could be beneficial. How robust is the approach to correlated errors or varying worker reliability?
* **Scalability Considerations:** The computational complexity of MCTS, even with progressive widening, could become a bottleneck for very large problems. The paper doesn’t explicitly address scalability concerns.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper tackles a practically relevant and challenging problem with a novel and principled approach.
* The technical contributions are significant, offering a strong theoretical foundation and a practical algorithm.
* The experimental results are compelling and demonstrate the benefits of the proposed framework.
* The open-source implementation and detailed documentation promote reproducibility and further research.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

* The lack of detailed parameter sensitivity analysis and a thorough comparison to related work (particularly Legion:AR) weakens the paper.
* The simplifying assumptions in the environment model and the limited discussion of scalability could raise concerns about the robustness and applicability of the approach.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that presents a valuable contribution to the fields of active learning, crowdsourcing, and online learning. The on-the-job learning framework is a compelling concept with the potential for real-world impact. While there are some weaknesses, they are not major and can be addressed in future work. I believe the paper warrants acceptance to NIPS, contingent upon the authors addressing the points raised above, particularly a more in-depth discussion of parameter sensitivity and a stronger comparison to Legion:AR.  Specifically, I would like to see a table showing how varying the cost parameters (wM and wT) affect the performance and cost trade-offs. Additionally, elaborating on the practical limitations and potential scalability issues would be beneficial.
