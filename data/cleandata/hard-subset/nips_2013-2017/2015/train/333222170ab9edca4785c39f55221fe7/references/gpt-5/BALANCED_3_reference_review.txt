Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes “on-the-job learning,” a decision-theoretic framework for making high-quality predictions from the outset by interleaving model inference with adaptive, real-time crowd queries. The system issues queries on uncertain parts of the input (e.g., token-level labels in NER), receives asynchronous, noisy responses, and decides when to wait, ask redundantly, or return a prediction given explicit penalties for latency and monetary cost. The core technical contributions are: (i) a stochastic game formulation with a CRF as the base predictor; (ii) a Bayesian utility trading off expected accuracy of the MAP prediction with time and cost; and (iii) an approximate solver combining Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) and progressive widening to handle continuous response times. The system (LENSE) is evaluated on NER, sentiment, and image classification, with results showing higher accuracy and reduced cost relative to pure crowd and pure ML baselines. Code and data are released.

This work touches several NeurIPS-relevant threads: online and active learning (e.g., Settles’ survey; Chu et al.; Sculley), test-time acquisition/active classification (Greiner et al.; Gao & Koller), crowdsourcing under uncertainty (TurKontrol by Dai et al.), and structured prediction (CRFs). Compared to Legion:AR, which handles real-time crowd-in-the-loop decisions for activity recognition, this paper contributes a formal model and solver for structured prediction with explicit time/cost tradeoffs and continuous-time handling via progressive widening. Relative to TurKontrol and VOI-style/POMDP approaches, this work emphasizes structured outputs and asynchronous, per-component queries at test time with online updating.

Strengths
- Problem importance: Addresses a practical and underexplored setting—maintaining high accuracy from the start by adaptively querying at prediction time while the model improves.
- Principled formulation: Clear decision-theoretic framework with a utility that explicitly encodes accuracy, latency, and monetary cost; the resulting strategies (redundancy vs parallelism vs adaptivity) are interpretable.
- Technical novelty: Integrating MCTS with progressive widening to reason about continuous response times in a structured-prediction setting is interesting and, to my knowledge, novel in this context.
- Structured outputs: Moving beyond i.i.d. classification to CRFs is nontrivial and increases the practical relevance.
- Empirical breadth and openness: Experiments on three tasks, a working retainer-based real-time crowd setup, and released code/data enhance reproducibility.

Weaknesses and detailed evaluation
Quality
- Environment/model mismatch: The response model assumes a fixed 70% accuracy and independence across queries. Real crowds exhibit per-worker heterogeneity, confusion matrices, drift, and time-accuracy trade-offs. The paper notes workers were ~70% accurate, but does not learn pR from data or model worker variability; this can bias the policy learned/evaluated under the assumed model.
- Simulation-heavy evaluation: To “control for variance,” the paper pre-collects responses and times and then simulates. While practical, this risks overfitting to the frozen pool and obscures generalization to live conditions. End-to-end live trials (even smaller-scale) are needed to validate latency/accuracy/cost under realistic dynamics.
- Baselines: Comparisons omit stronger decision-theoretic baselines (e.g., TurKontrol-style POMDP/VOI policies, adaptive submodular/myopic VOI heuristics) and modern test-time feature acquisition policies; the “threshold” heuristic is weak.
- Calibration and training under noise: The utility depends critically on calibrated posteriors. The paper does not describe calibration (e.g., temperature scaling) or robust training under noisy labels (beyond “learn on actual responses”). Without denoising/EM or marginalization during training, pθ risks being miscalibrated, degrading decision quality.
- Computational aspects: MCTS settings (budget, branching, exploration constant), runtime per instance, and overhead relative to crowd latency are not reported. This is important for “real-time” claims.
- Statistical rigor: No confidence intervals or multiple runs are reported, and sensitivity to wM and wT is not analyzed. Claimed gains (e.g., +8% F1 over 1-vote; +28% over online learning) should be accompanied by variance estimates and a clear Pareto analysis over time/cost/accuracy.

Clarity
- Generally clear, but some missing details: exact utility weights, crowd response/latency distributions, and training procedure under noise. A few typos and minor notational inconsistencies exist. The ExpAcc definition is unusual; a brief justification that this corresponds to Bayes decision under 0–1 loss for structured outputs would help.

Originality
- The combination—structured prediction with asynchronous crowd queries, continuous-time modeling, and MCTS/progressive widening—is novel. However, links to and distinctions from POMDP/VOI approaches in crowdsourcing could be deepened, and comparisons included.

Significance
- If validated in truly live settings and against stronger baselines, the approach could be impactful for deployable systems that must trade money and latency for accuracy. The cost reductions for NER are promising. As presented, the significance is tempered by evaluation limitations and the simplified environment model.

Questions for the authors
- How sensitive are results to misspecification of pR and pT? Can you learn per-worker confusion/time models online?
- What is the computational budget per instance (number of MCTS rollouts; wall-clock), and how does it compare to crowd latency?
- How do you calibrate the CRF’s predictive probabilities? Did you try temperature scaling or isotonic regression?
- How were wM and wT chosen, and how robust are the policies across different settings?
- Do you prevent training on instances before they are evaluated to avoid leakage in the reported metrics?

Suggestions for improvement
- Include at least a small live A/B evaluation to validate that gains persist without frozen pools.
- Learn pR and pT from data, with per-worker reliability, and show robustness to misspecification.
- Add stronger baselines: a TurKontrol-like POMDP, a myopic VOI policy, and a learned test-time acquisition policy.
- Provide ablations (no progressive widening; no time modeling; no redundancy) and sensitivity to wM, wT.
- Report computational overhead and present Pareto curves (accuracy vs latency vs cost).
- Consider noise-robust training (EM or marginalization over latent true labels) and probability calibration.

Arguments for acceptance
- Practical, important problem with a principled, interpretable formulation.
- Novel integration of MCTS with continuous-time decision making for structured outputs.
- Open-source system and multi-task evaluation.

Arguments against acceptance
- Heavy reliance on simplified/simulated crowd models; limited live validation.
- Missing strong decision-theoretic baselines; no statistical uncertainty reporting.
- Insufficient detail on calibration, training under noise, and computational costs.

Recommendation
Weak reject. The idea and formulation are promising and likely impactful, but the empirical evaluation and modeling of the crowd environment need strengthening. With stronger baselines, calibration/robustness analyses, and some live validation, this would be a compelling submission.
