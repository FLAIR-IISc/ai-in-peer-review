Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes “on-the-job learning,” a setting in which a system must return high-quality predictions from the outset without a pre-trained model by interleaving prediction with real-time, noisy, and delayed crowd queries. The core formulation is a stochastic game between the system and the crowd built atop a structured prediction model (e.g., CRF). At each input, the system chooses among: issuing token-level queries, waiting for an outstanding response, or returning a prediction. A Bayesian decision-theoretic utility trades off expected prediction accuracy against monetary query cost and latency. Because exact planning is intractable, the authors approximate the optimal policy using Monte Carlo Tree Search (UCT) combined with progressive widening to handle continuous response times.

The environment model factors into a prediction model pθ(y|x), a response model pR(r|yq) with fixed accuracy (0.7, uniform otherwise), and a response-time model pT(t|s). The CRF is updated online from received responses. Experiments on NER, sentiment classification, and image classification use a retainer pool on Mechanical Turk; to stabilize comparisons, the authors pre-collect five responses and latencies per label and simulate from this pool. Results suggest substantial cost reductions at comparable quality vs. crowd-only baselines and improved accuracy vs. pure machine baselines; the method gradually reduces queries over time as the model improves. Open-source code is provided.

This work is related to online learning (e.g., Cesa-Bianchi and Lugosi), active learning (Settles; Golovin and Krause), active classification with test-time feature acquisition (Greiner et al., Gao and Koller), decision-theoretic control of crowdsourcing (e.g., TurKontrol), and structured prediction with partial observations/measurements (Liang et al.). Methodologically, the planning approach leverages MCTS (Kocsis and Szepesvári) and progressive widening for continuous branching (Coulom). The paper distinguishes itself by: (i) operating at test time under strict latency/cost constraints; (ii) handling asynchronous crowdsourced signals; and (iii) learning the model on-the-fly while optimizing queries.

Strengths
- Clear problem motivation: maintaining high accuracy from the start in resource-limited settings where neither large labeled datasets nor full crowd annotation are viable.
- Principled formulation: a Bayesian decision-theoretic utility that explicitly trades off accuracy, cost, and latency; modeling of asynchronous, noisy responses and continuous response times.
- Algorithmic contribution: practical planning using MCTS with progressive widening in a mixed discrete/continuous, partially observed setting; intuitive policy behaviors (parallel/sequential querying) emerge.
- Structured prediction focus: token-level querying exploits label dependencies in sequence models, which is a natural and compelling use case.
- Empirical breadth and practicality: three tasks, real-time retainer setup, and open-source implementation; results suggest meaningful cost savings and improved accuracy over baselines.
- Reproducibility: code/data/experiments are available.

Weaknesses
- Worker/response model is simplistic: pR assumes fixed 0.7 accuracy with uniform error. This ignores per-worker reliability, item difficulty, and correlation across positions; modern crowd models (e.g., Dawid–Skene variants, item-response theory) could materially affect policies and outcomes.
- Utility/metric mismatch: ExpAcc is defined on the accuracy of the MAP predictor under the posterior. For structured outputs, the evaluation uses F1 (at least for NER); the utility should align with the evaluation metric or justify the surrogate.
- Incomplete algorithmic details: MCTS specifics (rollout/leaf evaluation policy, computational budget per decision, handling of partial trajectories) and pT parameterization are not fully described, making it hard to assess stability and real-time feasibility.
- Query timing restriction: forbidding launching a new query while “waiting” simplifies search but may leave performance on the table in high-latency regimes.
- Baselines: The threshold baseline is a weak heuristic; stronger decision-theoretic or POMDP-based crowd-control baselines (e.g., TurKontrol-style) and test-time feature acquisition methods would strengthen claims. For NER, comparison to simple redundancy allocation policies conditioned on uncertainty and to structured “measurement” approaches would be informative.
- Evaluation methodology: Pre-collecting five responses per label and sampling without replacement controls variance but departs from real deployment (e.g., variable worker pools and drift). Statistical significance, confidence intervals, and sensitivity to wM and wT are not reported. It is also unclear whether retainer overheads are amortized consistently across methods.
- Learning from noisy labels: The CRF is trained online from noisy responses, but there is no explicit noise-aware learning (e.g., marginalizing over pR or using EM), which could bias θ and the posterior used in planning.

Quality
The formulation is sound and the search approach is reasonable for an otherwise intractable problem. However, the simplified response model, utility/metric mismatch, and missing details on critical components (pT, MCTS budget/rollouts, noise handling in learning) weaken the technical rigor. Experimental results are promising but would benefit from stronger baselines, statistical analysis, and clearer cost accounting.

Clarity
The paper is generally well organized and readable. The game formulation and Algorithm 1 are easy to follow. Missing details noted above (utility metric for structured outputs, pT form/estimation, MCTS configuration, hyperparameters wM/wT selection) should be added. Clarify whether the utility uses token accuracy vs. sequence accuracy and how that relates to the reported F1.

Originality
The combination of on-the-job decision-theoretic planning with asynchronous crowd signals and structured prediction, implemented via MCTS with progressive widening, is novel in this specific form. The components build on known ideas, but the integrated system and setting are original and practically relevant.

Significance
If robust, this approach could impact real-time, human-in-the-loop ML systems, especially for structured NLP tasks. The cost/latency/accuracy tradeoff and the ability to maintain performance from the outset are valuable. The current empirical evidence is encouraging but would need stronger baselines and analyses to firmly establish state-of-the-art significance.

Pros for acceptance
- Addresses an important and underexplored setting with a principled formulation.
- Practical algorithm that handles asynchronous, continuous-time responses.
- Demonstrates tangible cost savings and accuracy gains on multiple tasks.
- Clear writing and open-source release enhance reproducibility and potential impact.

Cons for acceptance
- Simplistic crowd/response model and lack of noise-aware learning.
- Utility definition may not align with evaluation metrics; limited discussion of calibration.
- Missing experimental details, no significance testing, and limited baseline strength.
- Evaluation setup (pre-collected responses) limits external validity.

Suggestions for improvement
- Incorporate richer worker/item models (e.g., Dawid–Skene, per-worker reliabilities, item difficulty) and learn them online; compare policies with/without these refinements.
- Align the utility with the task metric (e.g., expected F1) or justify the surrogate; report calibration of pθ.
- Provide ablations: effect of progressive widening, search budget, parallel vs. sequential querying, and the “no mid-wait issuance” constraint.
- Include stronger baselines (e.g., POMDP-based crowd control, cost-sensitive active classification with learned policies) and report statistical significance.
- Detail pT estimation and wM/wT selection; provide sensitivity analyses.
- Clarify cost accounting (including retainer overhead) and report real wall-clock decision latency vs. human response times.

Scope
Firmly in scope for NeurIPS: online/active learning, decision theory, structured prediction, and human-in-the-loop systems.
