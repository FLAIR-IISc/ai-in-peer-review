

# Peer Review: "On-the-Job Learning with Bayesian Decision Theory"

## Summary and Relation to Previous Work

This paper introduces "on-the-job learning," a novel framework that enables AI systems to maintain high accuracy from the very first prediction without requiring pre-collected training data. The system strategically queries crowdsourcing for specific parts of uncertain inputs, updates its model based on responses, and gradually reduces its dependence on human input. The authors model this process as a stochastic game between the system and the crowd, using Bayesian decision theory to balance accuracy, latency, and cost. They propose an approximate algorithm using Monte Carlo tree search with progressive widening to handle the continuous time aspects of the problem.

The paper clearly distinguishes itself from related work: it differs from online learning (which doesn't actively seek information before prediction) and from active classification (which uses a static policy rather than learning at test time). The authors cite Legion:AR [6] as a related work that operates in a similar on-the-job setting but note that their paper provides the first machine learning foundation for this approach.

## Strengths

1. **Technical Soundness**: The paper presents a well-motivated problem and a principled solution using Bayesian decision theory. The game-theoretic formulation is elegant and well-suited to the problem. The experimental evaluation is thorough, with three distinct tasks (NER, sentiment, and image classification) and multiple baselines.

2. **Significant Results**: The results are compelling, particularly the NER task where the system achieves 88.4% F1 with more than an order of magnitude reduction in cost compared to a 5-vote human approach. The cost reduction while maintaining or improving accuracy is a substantial practical contribution.

3. **Clear Writing and Organization**: The paper is well-structured and clearly written. The problem formulation, model, and experimental setup are easy to follow. The figures (especially Figure 2) effectively illustrate the core concepts.

4. **Reproducibility**: The authors provide a detailed description of their implementation, crowdsourcing setup, and make their code and data publicly available on CodaLab, which is commendable.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of Theoretical Guarantees**: While the authors develop a principled Bayesian decision-theoretic framework, they provide no theoretical analysis of their approximation algorithm's performance. The MCTS approach is heuristic, and the paper doesn't address how close their solution is to the optimal policy or under what conditions the approximation might fail.

2. **Limited Discussion of Limitations**: The paper doesn't adequately address limitations. For instance, it doesn't discuss how the system would handle extreme cases where crowd responses are consistently wrong, or how it would scale to very large input sizes. The claim that "the model is able to learn from the crowd and the amount of supervision needed reduces over time" is somewhat vague without quantification.

3. **Insufficient Comparison to Related Work**: While the paper distinguishes itself from Legion:AR, it could better compare with recent work on active learning with noisy labels (e.g., the near-optimal Bayesian active learning work by Golovin et al. at NIPS 2010). The related work section could be strengthened to better position this contribution.

4. **Implementation Details**: Some technical details about the progressive widening implementation could be clarified. For instance, how exactly does the algorithm handle the continuous time aspect beyond just sampling from the response-time model?

## Overall Assessment

This paper makes a strong contribution to the field of active learning and crowdsourcing by addressing a practical problem with a theoretically grounded approach. The on-the-job learning framework is novel, well-motivated, and demonstrated to provide significant practical benefits in real-world scenarios.

**Quality**: The paper is technically sound, but lacks theoretical analysis of the approximation algorithm. The experimental evaluation is strong and well-designed.

**Clarity**: The paper is exceptionally clear and well-organized. The writing is accessible to the NIPS audience.

**Originality**: The core concept of on-the-job learning is novel, and the Bayesian decision-theoretic approach to modeling the problem is a fresh perspective.

**Significance**: The results demonstrate a significant practical impact (cost reduction of >10x on NER), making this work highly relevant to practitioners. The framework could be applied to many real-world scenarios where labeled data is expensive to obtain.

## Recommendation

This paper should be accepted. It makes a significant contribution to the field by addressing a practical problem with a novel, principled approach. The results are compelling, the writing is excellent, and the work is well-positioned in the literature. While the lack of theoretical guarantees is a minor weakness, the strong empirical results and practical significance outweigh this concern.

The authors should address the limitations mentioned above in the camera-ready version, particularly by adding more discussion of the algorithm's theoretical properties and limitations. The paper already provides a solid foundation that will likely inspire further research in this area.

The work is clearly within NIPS scope, addressing a significant problem in machine learning with a novel approach that combines active learning, Bayesian decision theory, and online learning. The practical impact of reducing the need for expensive data labeling makes this work highly relevant to both researchers and practitioners.
