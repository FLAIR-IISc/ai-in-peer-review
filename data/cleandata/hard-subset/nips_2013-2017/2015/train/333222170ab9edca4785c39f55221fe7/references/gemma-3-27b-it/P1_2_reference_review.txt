## Peer Review: On-the-Job Learning with Bayesian Decision Theory

**Summary:**

This paper proposes a novel framework, “on-the-job learning,” for building AI systems with limited labeled data or resources. The core idea is to strategically query a crowd for labels during inference, balancing the trade-off between accuracy, latency, and cost. The authors formulate this as a stochastic game solved using Bayesian decision theory and approximate Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS). They demonstrate the effectiveness of their system (LENSE) on three tasks – NER, sentiment classification, and image classification – achieving significant cost reductions and/or accuracy improvements compared to existing approaches.

**Related Work:**

The authors correctly position their work within the broader context of online learning, active learning, and crowdsourcing. They clearly differentiate their approach from traditional active learning by emphasizing the crucial aspect of *learning during test time* and maintaining high initial accuracy, which is not a focus of most active learning methods. The connection to Legion:AR [6] is also appropriately highlighted, while noting the gap in theoretical foundations that this paper addresses. The related work section is comprehensive and well-organized, demonstrating a strong understanding of the field.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The on-the-job learning paradigm itself is a compelling and practical approach, particularly for rapidly deploying systems in resource-constrained settings. The formulation as a stochastic game and the use of Bayesian decision theory to optimize queries are also novel contributions.
* **Technical Soundness:** The mathematical formulation appears rigorous, and the use of MCTS with progressive widening provides a reasonable approximation for solving the intractable decision problem. The experimental setup with frozen worker responses is a clever way to control for worker variability and ensure reproducibility.
* **Strong Empirical Results:** The results across the three tasks are convincing, demonstrating substantial cost reductions and/or accuracy gains compared to strong baselines. The 8% F1 improvement over a single human label and 28% over online learning on NER are particularly impressive.
* **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the core concepts, though some sections (like the game tree details) are dense and require careful reading. The inclusion of Figure 2 is helpful in visualizing the process.
* **Reproducibility:** The open-source implementation of LENSE and the availability of data and experiments on CodaLab are highly commendable and facilitate reproducibility.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Complexity:** The framework is quite complex, involving a stochastic game, Bayesian decision theory, MCTS, and progressive widening. While mathematically sound, the sheer number of components might be a barrier to adoption for researchers not already familiar with these techniques.
* **Scalability:** While the experiments demonstrate effectiveness on relatively small datasets, the computational cost of MCTS could become a bottleneck for very large-scale problems with high-dimensional state spaces. The authors don’t fully address scalability concerns.
* **Crowd Model Assumptions:** The assumed crowd response model (70% accuracy) seems somewhat optimistic, and the impact of varying crowd quality isn't thoroughly explored.  Real-world crowd workers exhibit more heterogeneity and potentially more systematic biases than the model allows for.
* **Limited Ablation Studies:** While the comparison to baselines is thorough, more detailed ablation studies on the individual components of LENSE (e.g., the impact of different utility function weights, the effectiveness of progressive widening) would strengthen the analysis.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* This paper presents a genuinely novel and practical approach to building AI systems with limited resources.
* The technical contributions are sound and well-motivated.
* The empirical results are strong and demonstrate significant benefits over existing methods.
* The open-source release promotes reproducibility and facilitates further research.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The framework’s complexity might limit its wider adoption.
* The scalability of the MCTS algorithm could be a concern for large-scale problems.
* The crowd model assumptions are potentially overly simplistic.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with significant potential impact. The authors tackle a challenging problem and propose a well-motivated and technically sound solution.  While the complexity and scalability are potential concerns, the strong empirical results and the open-source release outweigh these drawbacks. 

**Recommendation:**

**Accept with Minor Revisions.** 

I recommend acceptance, contingent on the authors addressing the following minor points:

* **Scalability Discussion:**  Include a brief discussion of the potential scalability challenges and possible mitigation strategies (e.g., parallelization, approximations).
* **Crowd Sensitivity:**  Investigate the robustness of the framework to variations in crowd accuracy and consider exploring more sophisticated crowd modeling techniques.
* **Ablation Study:**  Add a small ablation study to assess the impact of key components (utility function weights, progressive widening) on performance.
* **Clarify Game Tree:** Revisit the explanation of the game tree in Section 3 to improve clarity and readability.  A simpler visual representation could be helpful.
