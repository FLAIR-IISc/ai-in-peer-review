This paper proposes an “on-the-job learning” framework where a system strategically queries a crowd to label parts of an input, aiming to balance accuracy, latency, and cost. The core idea, building a model while simultaneously utilizing human input, is not entirely novel, drawing connections to online learning, active learning, and prior work like Legion:AR [6]. The authors formulate this as a stochastic game and employ Bayesian decision theory and Monte Carlo tree search for policy optimization.

The paper’s strength lies in its formalization of a practical problem—situations where limited resources preclude extensive pre-training or full crowdsourcing. The experimental evaluation on NER, sentiment, and image classification is commendable, and the reported cost reductions on NER are impressive. The availability of an open-source implementation (LENSE) is a positive aspect for reproducibility. 

However, I have significant reservations. My primary concern is the lack of rigorous comparison to well-established baselines beyond simple online learning. While the authors compare to a 5-vote human approach, and a simple thresholding baseline, a more thorough comparison against robust supervised learning models *trained on even a small amount of labeled data* would be crucial. The problem setup acknowledges the existence of labeled data (“existing labeled datasets” in Section 5), yet the experiments largely focus on a zero-shot learning scenario.  It is not clear if the proposed method offers a substantial advantage over fine-tuning a pre-trained model (e.g., BERT for NER or sentiment) with a limited labeled dataset, which would be a realistic alternative in many scenarios. The argument that the method is designed for situations *without* data feels somewhat contrived given the experimental setup.

Furthermore, the technical details, while present, are dense and the reasoning behind certain design choices is not always clear.  The assumption that queries can be issued in parallel is stated but its practical implications aren’t thoroughly discussed (e.g., managing API limits, worker availability). The utility function, while formally defined, feels somewhat arbitrary – the weighting between accuracy, latency, and cost (wM, wT) isn’t justified and may significantly influence performance. The reliance on a “frozen pool” of worker responses is a peculiar choice; a more realistic evaluation would involve querying workers dynamically and accounting for inherent worker variability and potential biases. The claim of achieving a significant reduction in cost needs to be contextualized; the experiments use Amazon Mechanical Turk, and the cost of crowd workers varies significantly.  

The related work section, while comprehensive, doesn't sufficiently engage with the broader literature on active learning and efficient data acquisition. The dismissal of active learning due to its focus on example selection rather than feature/label selection feels overly critical.  Many active learning methods could be adapted to this on-the-job setting.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* Formalizes a realistic, practical problem.
* Demonstrates promising results on NER in terms of cost reduction.
* Open-source implementation promotes reproducibility.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* Lack of comparison to strong supervised learning baselines (even with limited labeled data).
* Technical details are dense and lack sufficient justification.
* Reliance on a "frozen pool" of worker responses limits generalizability.
* Utility function is somewhat arbitrary.
* Incremental improvement over existing work isn't substantial enough.



**Overall Assessment:**

I lean towards rejection. While the paper presents a technically sound framework, it falls short of demonstrating a significant and practically relevant contribution to the field. The experiments, while thorough, do not convincingly establish the superiority of this approach over simpler, more established methods. The paper lacks the “significance” required for NIPS.  The authors need to address the baseline comparison issue and provide a more compelling justification for the technical choices made.
