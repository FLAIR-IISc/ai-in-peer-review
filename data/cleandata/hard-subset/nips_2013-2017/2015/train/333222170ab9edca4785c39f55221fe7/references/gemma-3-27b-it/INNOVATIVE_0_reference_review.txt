This paper introduces a compelling framework for “on-the-job learning” that intelligently balances querying a crowd for labels with building a self-sufficient model. The core idea—modeling the learning process as a stochastic game and using Bayesian decision theory to optimize query strategies—is genuinely novel and a bold departure from traditional supervised learning and active learning paradigms. The authors smartly address a practical problem: resource constraints hindering initial model training, especially in rapidly evolving domains like disaster response or sentiment monitoring. The connection to existing work is well-defined, and the authors correctly position their approach as distinct from online learning and standard active learning.

The strengths are considerable. The conceptual framework is elegant and the application to three different tasks (NER, sentiment classification, and image classification) demonstrates broad applicability. The reported results are very strong, showcasing a substantial reduction in cost compared to full human annotation and significant F1 score improvements over baselines. The availability of an open-source implementation (LENSE) is a major plus, facilitating reproducibility and further research. The authors acknowledge the limitations of the evaluation using existing datasets and the need for future work in more realistic, streaming environments. The discussion of the environment model and the trade-offs in utility function design is well-articulated. The experiments showing reduced query needs with richer feature sets are particularly insightful.

However, the paper isn't without weaknesses. While the MCTS and progressive widening approximation is necessary for tractability, the details of its implementation and the sensitivity to hyperparameters aren’t thoroughly explored. More discussion of the computational complexity of the search procedure would be beneficial. The response model (assuming 70% accuracy) feels somewhat ad-hoc, particularly as this directly impacts the game’s dynamics. More justification or exploration of different response noise models would strengthen the argument. The experiments, while compelling, are somewhat limited in scope. Exploring scenarios with varying crowd worker cost and accuracy would reveal more about the robustness of the approach. The analysis focuses heavily on NER, and the gains are less pronounced on the other tasks; a more in-depth discussion of why this might be the case is warranted.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **High Novelty:** The on-the-job learning framework and the game-theoretic formulation are genuinely new and represent a significant shift in perspective.
*   **Strong Results:** The empirical results demonstrate substantial cost savings and performance improvements compared to established baselines.
*   **Practical Relevance:** The approach directly addresses a real-world problem – building effective AI systems with limited initial resources.
*   **Open Source:** The availability of LENSE facilitates reproducibility and future research.
*   **Bold Approach:** The authors have taken a risk with a non-traditional approach and have largely succeeded in demonstrating its value.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Approximation Complexity:** The MCTS and progressive widening approximation requires further scrutiny regarding computational cost and hyperparameter sensitivity.
*   **Response Model:** The fixed 70% accuracy response model is simplistic and may not generalize to diverse scenarios.
*   **Limited Scope:** Experiments could be expanded to provide more comprehensive insights into the method’s robustness and generalizability.



**Overall:**

Despite some minor weaknesses, I strongly recommend accepting this paper. The novelty and potential impact of the proposed framework are significant. The authors have presented a well-reasoned, technically sound, and practically relevant contribution to the field. The minor flaws are outweighed by the boldness of the idea and the compelling empirical results. The paper introduces a promising new direction for research, and I believe it will stimulate further investigation into on-the-job learning techniques. While a more detailed analysis of the approximation algorithm and a wider range of experimental conditions would be beneficial, the current submission is already of high quality and merits inclusion in NIPS.
