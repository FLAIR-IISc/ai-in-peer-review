## Review of "On-the-Job Learning with Bayesian Decision Theory"

**Summary:**

This paper introduces a novel framework called “on-the-job learning” for tackling prediction tasks with limited initial resources. Unlike traditional supervised learning or pure crowdsourcing, the authors propose a system that strategically queries a crowd for labels *during* prediction, balancing accuracy, cost, and latency using Bayesian decision theory. The core idea is to treat the learning process as a stochastic game, solved approximately via Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS). The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their system (LENSE) on three tasks – NER, sentiment classification, and image classification – showing significant cost reductions and accuracy improvements compared to baselines.

**Relation to Prior Work:**

The paper correctly positions itself within the landscape of online learning, active learning, and crowdsourcing. It clearly differentiates its approach from online/active learning which typically focus on *building* a model, while LENSE aims to maintain accuracy *during* deployment. The connection to active classification is also well-articulated, highlighting the key distinction of adaptive model improvement during test time, absent in static policy approaches. The reference to Legion:AR is relevant, though the authors appropriately emphasize the broader machine learning foundation they provide. The thorough review of related work across several fields (crowdsourcing, structured prediction) demonstrates a good understanding of the context. 

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty and Significance:** The on-the-job learning framework is a compelling approach, particularly relevant in scenarios where upfront data collection is prohibitive. The idea of framing this as a stochastic game and utilizing Bayesian decision theory provides a principled way to balance competing objectives. The experimental results showcasing significant cost reductions and accuracy improvements are substantial.
* **Technical Soundness:** The methodology is well-defined and appears theoretically sound. The use of MCTS and progressive widening to handle the intractable game search space is a clever and appropriate choice. The inclusion of a response time model in the environment is a realistic and important consideration.
* **Comprehensive Evaluation:**  The experiments on three diverse tasks (NER, sentiment, image classification) provide a good breadth of evaluation. The detailed comparison with multiple baselines (human n-query, online learning, threshold baseline) offers a strong assessment of the LENSE system’s performance.
* **Implementation and Reproducibility:** The authors provide a publicly available implementation (LENSE on GitHub) and a CodaLab worksheet, which is excellent and promotes reproducibility and further research.
* **Clear Writing:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the core concepts and methodology. Figures are helpful in visualizing the process.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Crowdsourcing Setup Specificity:** While acknowledging the worker quality variance, the experimental setup relies on a "frozen pool" of responses. This limits the dynamism of the crowd and might not fully reflect a real-world, continuously evolving crowdsourcing environment. The fixed cost per query also doesn't account for potential price fluctuations or worker availability.
* **Utility Function Sensitivity:** The utility function (Equation 1) includes weights (wM, wT) that directly impact the trade-off between cost, time and accuracy. While justifiable, the specific values chosen could significantly influence the results. A sensitivity analysis of these weights would be valuable to understand the robustness of the findings.
* **Limited Scalability Discussion:**  The computational complexity of MCTS, even with progressive widening, might become a bottleneck for very large-scale problems or complex structured predictions. The paper doesn’t adequately address the scalability limitations of the approach.
* **NER Task Dominance:** The strongest results are on NER, which benefits from a dominant “NONE” class. It's unclear if the improvements generalize equally well to tasks without such a characteristic. More detailed analysis comparing performance across different classes in each task would be beneficial.
* **Assumed CRF Model:** The use of a CRF as the prediction model is reasonable, but the framework is inherently model-agnostic.  Exploring the performance of LENSE with other prediction models (e.g., neural networks) could reveal its adaptability and limitations.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* Addresses a practical and important problem: learning with limited resources.
* Presents a novel and theoretically grounded framework.
* Achieves significant performance improvements over strong baselines.
* Provides a publicly available implementation for reproducibility.
*  The paper is well-written and thoroughly evaluated.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* Potential sensitivity to the utility function weighting.
* Scalability concerns with MCTS for large-scale problems.
* Heavily relies on a specific crowdsourcing setup and limited dynamism.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with a compelling contribution to the field. The on-the-job learning framework is both novel and practical, and the experimental results demonstrate its effectiveness. While there are some minor weaknesses, the strengths of the paper significantly outweigh them. 

**Recommendation:**

**Accept with Minor Revisions.** 

I recommend acceptance contingent on the authors addressing the following minor points:

1.  **Sensitivity Analysis:** Provide a brief sensitivity analysis of the weights in the utility function (wM, wT).
2.  **Scalability Discussion:** Briefly discuss the potential scalability limitations of the MCTS approach and suggest possible strategies for mitigating them.
3.  **Class-Wise Analysis:** Include a brief class-wise performance analysis for each task to demonstrate the generalizability of the improvements beyond scenarios with dominant classes.
4. **Crowdsourcing Setup Discussion:** Briefly discuss the limitations of the frozen pool setup and potential implications for real-world deployments.
