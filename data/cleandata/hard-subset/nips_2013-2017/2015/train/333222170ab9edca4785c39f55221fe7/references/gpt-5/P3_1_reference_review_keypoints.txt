- Proposes "on-the-job learning": decision-theoretic framework mixing model predictions with on-demand crowd queries to trade off accuracy, cost, and latency.  
- Formalizes interaction as a stochastic game with actions: query a sub-label, wait, or return prediction; utility trades off expected accuracy, monetary cost, and time.  
- Environment model decomposes into a CRF pθ(y|x), response noise model pR, and response-time model pT; planning approximated with MCTS (UCT) plus progressive widening.  
- Empirical results on NER, sentiment, and image tasks show cost savings vs majority-vote and accuracy gains vs online/single-vote baselines; code (LENSE) is open-sourced.  

- Strength: Clear, pragmatic problem formulation addressing real deployment needs with explicit time and cost constraints.  
- Strength: Decision-theoretic utility unifies accuracy, latency, and cost and yields intuitive querying strategies (parallel/sequential, redundancy).  
- Strength: Structured prediction (CRF) appropriately leverages label correlations to reduce queries for NER.  
- Strength: MCTS with progressive widening is a reasonable planning approximation and is clearly described.  
- Strength: Empirics indicate meaningful cost reductions and diminishing crowd reliance as the model improves.  
- Strength: Reproducibility is strong due to released code and datasets.  

- Weakness: Response model pR is simplistic—assumes fixed 70% accuracy and independence, ignoring worker heterogeneity, item difficulty, and correlated errors.  
- Weakness: Response-time model pT is unspecified/not learned per task or worker, yet time is central to utility decisions.  
- Weakness: The expected-accuracy surrogate (ExpAcc) is underspecified; unclear which accuracy metric is used and how CRF marginals vs MAP are handled.  
- Weakness: Planning cannot issue new queries while waiting (“no mid-wait launching”), which may reduce latency/utility in variable-time settings.  
- Weakness: Experimental design uses tiny frozen pools and without-replacement sampling, potentially underestimating variance and biasing learned policies.  
- Weakness: Baselines are weak—missing adaptive stopping, VOI/POMDP-like policies, cost-sensitive active classification baselines, and a stronger online+limited-query baseline.  
- Weakness: Claim that system “boosts performance relative to expert labels” is unclear or surprising and needs clarification.  
- Weakness: Missing ablations and sensitivity analyses for weight hyperparameters, UCT/progressive widening settings, planner latency, and robustness to pR/pT violations.  
- Weakness: No theoretical guarantees or discussion on convergence/limitations of the progressive widening MCTS scheme used.  

- Clarity: Generally well-written but missing key details (exact pT form, accuracy metric in utility, CRF features, training schedule) and has minor notation issues.  
- Presentation: Figures are convincing but need more quantitative plots of latency-accuracy-cost trade-offs and planner compute time.  

- Significance: Important, deployment-oriented contribution that unifies several lines of work; structured prediction gains are meaningful for NER but unstructured-task benefits need stronger baselines.  
- Suggestion: Learn pR and pT online (e.g., Dawid–Skene/GLAD, hierarchical time models) and test robustness to misspecification.  
- Suggestion: Clarify ExpAcc, assess model calibration, and consider proper scoring rules in utility.  
- Suggestion: Add stronger adaptive baselines, ablations (weights, UCT params), and report planner latency.  
- Suggestion: Relax or justify the “no mid-wait” constraint and provide per-task breakdowns (class imbalance, structured vs unstructured gains).  

- Recommendation: Weak accept—promising and practical but needs stronger baselines, clearer utility specification, and more realistic crowd/time modeling.
