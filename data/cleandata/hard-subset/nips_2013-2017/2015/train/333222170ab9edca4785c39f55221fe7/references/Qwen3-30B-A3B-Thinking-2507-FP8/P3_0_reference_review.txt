

# Peer Review: "On-the-Job Learning with Bayesian Decision Theory"

## Summary
This paper introduces "on-the-job learning," a framework for building AI systems that initially rely on crowd-sourcing for predictions but gradually reduce this dependency as they learn from the crowd's responses. The authors formalize the problem as a stochastic game between the system and the crowd, using Bayesian decision theory to optimize the trade-off between accuracy, cost, and latency. They approximate the optimal policy using Monte Carlo Tree Search with progressive widening and evaluate their approach on named-entity recognition, sentiment classification, and image classification tasks. The results show significant cost reductions (more than an order of magnitude on NER) compared to full human annotation while achieving higher accuracy than other baselines.

## Quality
The paper presents a technically sound approach with a well-motivated problem statement. The stochastic game formalization provides a principled framework for the decision process, and the use of Bayesian decision theory to balance accuracy, cost, and latency is appropriate. The experimental evaluation is comprehensive, with three distinct tasks and multiple baselines including human n-query, online learning, and a threshold baseline.

However, the paper has several quality concerns. The crowd response model (pR(r|yq)) is stated to be 70% accurate without sufficient justification or validation - the authors should have provided details on how this accuracy was measured and how it was incorporated into the model. Similarly, the response time model (pT(ti|si)) is not properly justified; the paper mentions "response times were generally in the range of 0.5-2 seconds" but provides no statistical model or validation of this claim.

The comparison to online learning is problematic. The paper states that online learning "cannot maintain high accuracy independent of the number of data instances seen so far," but this is misleading. Online learning typically refers to algorithms that update their model after each example, which is exactly what the authors' system does (though with crowd-sourced labels). The comparison needs to be more precise to avoid confusion.

## Clarity
The paper is well-structured and clearly written, with a logical flow from problem formulation to implementation to evaluation. The game theory formalization is particularly well-explained. However, some technical details could be clarified, such as the exact implementation of progressive widening and how the system handles multiple in-flight queries. The connection between the theoretical framework and the practical implementation could also be strengthened.

## Originality
The on-the-job learning framework represents a novel combination of active learning, crowd-sourcing, and Bayesian decision theory for structured prediction. The authors effectively distinguish their work from related areas, particularly active classification, by emphasizing the dynamic model improvement during test time. However, the paper doesn't sufficiently differentiate itself from Legion:AR (cited as [6]), which operates in a similar on-the-job learning setting. The claim that Legion:AR "does not explore the machine learning foundations" needs stronger justification and more detailed comparison.

The paper also doesn't adequately reference other online active learning work (cited as [8,9,10]) that might share similar ideas. While the authors cite these papers, they don't sufficiently explain how their approach differs from these earlier works.

## Significance
The approach has clear practical significance for deploying AI systems in resource-constrained environments where labeled data is scarce. The reported cost reductions (more than an order of magnitude on NER) are impressive and could have substantial real-world impact. The framework's ability to maintain high accuracy from the start is particularly valuable for applications like disaster relief efforts.

However, the paper doesn't fully explore the scalability of the approach to larger-scale deployments or more complex tasks. It also doesn't address how the system would handle concept drift or changes in crowd worker quality over time, which could be significant practical limitations.

## Strengths and Weaknesses

**Strengths:**
- Well-motivated problem with practical relevance
- Novel formalization of the decision process as a stochastic game
- Comprehensive experimental evaluation across three tasks
- Strong theoretical foundation using Bayesian decision theory
- Code and data publicly available

**Weaknesses:**
- Insufficient justification for crowd response and response time models
- Problematic comparison to online learning
- Inadequate differentiation from related work (especially Legion:AR)
- Limited discussion of scalability and long-term deployment challenges
- No analysis of the approximation error in the Monte Carlo Tree Search

## Recommendation
This paper presents an interesting and potentially impactful approach to building AI systems with limited labeled data. While the core idea is novel and well-executed, the paper needs to address the weaknesses in the experimental validation and theoretical justification. The comparison to related work should be strengthened, particularly regarding Legion:AR and online active learning.

I recommend **acceptance with major revisions**. The authors should:
1. Provide a more rigorous justification for the crowd response and response time models
2. Clarify the comparison to online learning
3. Strengthen the differentiation from Legion:AR and other related work
4. Include a discussion of approximation error in the Monte Carlo Tree Search
5. Address scalability and long-term deployment considerations

The paper has significant potential to impact real-world AI deployment, and with these revisions, it would make a strong contribution to NeurIPS.
