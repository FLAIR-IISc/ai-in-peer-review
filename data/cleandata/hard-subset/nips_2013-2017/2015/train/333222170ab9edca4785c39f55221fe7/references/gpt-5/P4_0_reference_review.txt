Summary and relation to prior work
The paper introduces “on-the-job learning,” a framework for deploying an accurate system from the start by interleaving test-time crowdsourced queries with model predictions and continual learning. For each input, the system chooses which components (e.g., token labels in NER) to query, when to wait for responses, and when to return a final prediction. The decision process is formalized as a stochastic game between the system and the crowd, with a utility that trades off expected prediction accuracy, monetary cost per query, and latency. The environment model factors into (i) a CRF prediction model pθ(y|x), (ii) a crowd response model pR(r|yq) (assumed 70% accuracy, uniform otherwise), and (iii) a response-time model pT(t|s). The optimal policy is approximated using Monte Carlo Tree Search (UCT) with progressive widening to handle continuous time.

Empirically, the approach (LENSE) is evaluated on NER, sentiment classification, and a faces/image task using a real-time crowd retainer setup. Compared to human-only (majority vote) and machine-only (online learning without test-time queries) baselines, LENSE reports higher accuracy at lower cost, with especially strong gains on NER. The paper situates itself against online learning, online active learning, active classification (test-time feature acquisition), crowdsourcing controllers such as TurKontrol (POMDP-based vote control), and structured prediction, emphasizing its unique combination of test-time querying, continual model updating, and latency-cost-accuracy tradeoffs.

Quality (technical soundness)
Strengths:
- The formalization as a stochastic game with an explicit utility over accuracy, cost, and latency is principled and flexible. It unifies structured prediction with test-time evidence acquisition and budgeted decision-making.
- The environment model neatly incorporates noisy, asynchronous crowd responses; in structured outputs (CRF), soft crowd evidence naturally enters as local potentials.
- The MCTS + progressive widening choice is appropriate for large branching factors and continuous-time transitions, and the algorithmic description is clear at a high level.

Concerns and questions:
- Utility/loss mismatch: The utility uses ExpAcc(p) = Ep(y)[Accuracy(argmaxy′ p(y′))]. If Accuracy is 0–1 sequence accuracy, ExpAcc reduces to maxy p(y). The experiments report F1 for NER; F1 is not decomposable and does not align with the utility. The paper should clarify the accuracy used in utility (token-level accuracy vs sequence-level) and justify optimizing it when the evaluation metric is F1. If a surrogate is used, provide rationale or empirical evidence that it correlates well with F1.
- Response/noise model: pR(r|yq) is fixed at 0.7 and uniform otherwise, with no worker heterogeneity, item difficulty, or label-dependent confusion structure. This simplification can bias value estimates and querying decisions. Even a light-weight Dawid–Skene-style or task/label-calibrated model, learned online, would improve robustness. TurKontrol-like worker modeling is relevant here.
- Timing model: pT(t|s) is not specified in detail; per-worker variability, load, and queueing effects in retainer pools can be significant. A brief description of how pT is estimated (e.g., nonparametric empirical distributions per task) and sensitivity to pT misspecification would strengthen the claims.
- Evaluation protocol: To “control for worker quality,” responses and delays are pre-collected (5 per label) and sampled without replacement for simulation. While this standardizes comparisons, it induces dependence between methods, caps redundancy realism, and does not fully reflect online noise/latency variation. Reporting complementary results from live end-to-end trials (even on a subset) would increase external validity.
- Baselines: The threshold baseline is a strawman relative to decision-theoretic controllers for stopping/redundancy in crowdsourcing (e.g., POMDP-based schemes such as TurKontrol, adaptive submodularity approaches). Including at least one dynamic, vote-control baseline that reasons about uncertainty and stopping would make the comparison more compelling. For test-time acquisition, budgeted/anytime prediction baselines (active classification) with learned policies would also be informative.
- Computational overhead: Real-time deployment hinges on policy computation being small relative to human latencies. The paper does not report runtime per decision, tree sizes, or MCTS budgets. An ablation on planning depth/budget vs. performance would help.

Clarity
The writing is generally clear and well-organized, and the core ideas are conveyed effectively. Some details are underspecified:
- Exact definition of Accuracy in the utility and how it maps to evaluation metrics (F1 vs accuracy).
- Training objective for pθ when only noisy responses are observed (e.g., maximizing marginal likelihood over y under pR?), and how inference incorporates soft evidence in the CRF.
- Parameter choices (wM, wT) and sensitivity analysis across the latency–cost–accuracy frontier.
Minor typos/formatting issues exist but are not critical.

Originality
The combination of:
- a structured prediction model with soft test-time evidence from a crowd,
- a formal, latency- and cost-aware utility,
- continuous-time decision-making with MCTS/progressive widening, and
- continual improvement via learning from responses
is a novel synthesis relative to online active learning (no pre-prediction queries), active classification (static policies learned offline), and prior real-time crowd systems (e.g., Legion:AR) that lack a unified decision-theoretic control. The connection to POMDP-style controllers in crowdsourcing is acknowledged; the structured-output and continuous-time planning aspects add novelty.

Significance
Practical significance is high for applications that require immediate deployment with quality guarantees under budget/latency constraints. The NER gains and cost reductions are promising. However, broader impact depends on robustness to realistic crowd variability, fair comparisons to stronger controllers, and evidence that planning overhead is manageable. Clearer Pareto frontiers and sensitivity studies would strengthen the case.

Pros for acceptance
- Principled formulation unifying accuracy, cost, and latency with a workable planner.
- Demonstrated improvements over human-only and machine-only strategies, with open-source implementation and data.
- Addresses an important and timely problem at the ML–crowdsourcing interface; in scope for NeurIPS.

Cons for acceptance
- Utility/evaluation mismatch (F1 vs optimized objective) and missing details on the training likelihood under noisy labels.
- Simplified crowd models (fixed accuracy, no heterogeneity) and limited description of timing distributions.
- Baseline comparisons omit stronger decision-theoretic crowd controllers and learned active-classification policies.
- Lack of runtime/per-decision computation analysis; limited live evaluation.

Suggestions for improvement
- Clarify and justify the utility’s Accuracy function; consider aligning it with evaluation metrics or adding a calibrated surrogate. Include an ablation showing that optimizing the chosen utility improves F1.
- Learn pR and pT from data online (e.g., per-worker/per-label reliability, response-time mixtures), or provide sensitivity analyses.
- Add baselines from POMDP-style vote control and budgeted/test-time feature acquisition with learned policies.
- Report computational costs and MCTS budget sensitivity; show Pareto curves for latency–cost–accuracy by varying wM, wT.
- Provide more detail on the training objective for pθ with noisy responses and on how soft evidence is injected into CRF inference.

Overall, the paper presents a clear and promising framework with solid ideas and encouraging results, with room to strengthen empirical rigor and modeling realism.
