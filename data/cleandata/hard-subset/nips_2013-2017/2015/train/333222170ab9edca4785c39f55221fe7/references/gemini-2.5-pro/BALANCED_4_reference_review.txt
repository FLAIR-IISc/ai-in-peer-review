This paper introduces and formalizes the problem of "on-the-job learning," a setting where a system must make accurate predictions from the very first input, without a pre-trained model. To achieve this, the system can dynamically query a crowd of human workers for labels on parts of the input it is uncertain about. The core challenge is to decide which queries to make to optimally balance prediction accuracy, monetary cost, and response latency. The system learns an underlying model from the crowd's responses over time, gradually reducing its reliance on human input.

The authors frame this sequential decision-making problem as a stochastic game against a model of the crowd. They propose a principled solution based on Bayesian decision theory, where the system's policy aims to maximize a utility function that explicitly encodes the trade-offs between accuracy, cost, and time. Since computing the optimal policy is intractable, they develop an approximation using Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) combined with progressive widening to handle the continuous nature of time. This approach is evaluated on three distinct tasks (NER, sentiment analysis, image classification) and shown to significantly reduce costs compared to a purely human-powered approach and dramatically improve accuracy over a standard online learning baseline.

The problem setting is well-motivated and highly relevant, bridging a critical gap between traditional supervised learning (which requires large, upfront labeled datasets) and pure crowdsourcing (which is expensive and does not result in a reusable model). The paper clearly distinguishes this setting from related areas like online learning, active learning, and active classification, highlighting its unique requirement to maintain high accuracy on every test instance from the start.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Novel and Significant Problem Formulation:** The "on-the-job learning" setting is a practical and important contribution. It formalizes a scenario that many real-world applications face: the need to deploy an intelligent system immediately, even with little to no training data. The paper provides a strong conceptual framework for this problem.

2.  **Principled Theoretical Framework:** The application of Bayesian decision theory to this problem is elegant and appropriate. Modeling the interaction as a stochastic game provides a solid foundation for reasoning about the complex trade-offs involved. This is a significant step up from heuristic-based approaches.

3.  **Technically Sound Solution:** The use of MCTS with progressive widening to approximate the optimal policy is a sophisticated and well-justified choice. It demonstrates a strong command of relevant techniques from AI planning and reinforcement learning to solve a challenging machine learning problem.

4.  **Thorough and Convincing Experiments:** The empirical evaluation is a major strength. The authors test their system, LENSE, on three different tasks, demonstrating its versatility. The baselines are well-chosen, including a purely human approach (Human n-query), a purely ML approach (Online learning), and a strong heuristic (Threshold baseline). The results are impressive, particularly the order-of-magnitude cost reduction on the NER task while improving accuracy. The learning curves in Figures 3 and 4 effectively illustrate the system's ability to become more efficient over time.

5.  **Reproducibility:** The authors have made their code, data, and experiments available on CodaLab. This commitment to open and reproducible science is commendable and significantly increases the value of their contribution to the community.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

1.  **Clarity on Computational Overhead:** The MCTS-based planner runs simulations to decide on the next action (query, wait, or return). The paper does not discuss the computational cost of this planning phase itself. For a "real-time" system, this overhead could be a significant component of the overall latency. It would strengthen the paper to include a brief analysis of the planning time and discuss how it compares to the crowd response times. Is it negligible? How does it scale with the number of possible queries (i.e., the length of the input sequence `n`)?

2.  **Simplicity of Crowd Models:** The models for crowd responses (`pR`) and latency (`pT`) are relatively simple (e.g., a fixed 70% accuracy for all workers and tasks). While this is a reasonable starting point for demonstrating the framework, real-world crowd performance is far more complex, varying by worker, task difficulty, and time of day. The paper would be more complete if it discussed how the framework could be extended to incorporate more sophisticated, learned models of the crowd, perhaps even learning individual worker reliabilities online as in TurKontrol [26]. This is likely beyond the scope of the current work but would be a valuable direction to mention for future research.

3.  **Sensitivity to Utility Parameters:** The utility function (Eq. 1) relies on weights `wM` and `wT` to balance the costs of money and time against accuracy. The choice of these parameters is crucial for the system's behavior, but the paper does not discuss how they were set or how sensitive the results are to their values. Including a brief sensitivity analysis or a discussion of the practical considerations for setting these hyperparameters would improve the paper's practical utility.

4.  **Details of the Online Model Update:** The paper states that the underlying CRF model `pθ` is learned using AdaGrad based on all actual responses. For clarity and reproducibility, it would be beneficial to provide more detail. Is the model being incrementally updated with a small number of gradient steps after each new set of responses is received? Or is it periodically retrained on the entire history of observations? The former seems more aligned with the online nature of the problem, and clarifying this point would be helpful.

**Summary and Decision:**

Overall, this is an excellent paper that introduces a novel and important problem, proposes a principled and technically sophisticated solution, and validates it with a thorough empirical study. The strengths—particularly the novelty of the problem setting and the quality of the empirical work—far outweigh the weaknesses, which are primarily related to clarifying details and suggesting future extensions. The work is well-written, clearly situated within the literature, and represents a significant contribution to the fields of machine learning and human-computer interaction. It is very likely that other researchers will build upon these ideas. I strongly recommend acceptance.
