Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes “on-the-job learning,” a decision-theoretic framework to deploy high-quality prediction from the outset by interleaving model predictions with real-time, asynchronous queries to a crowd. For a structured prediction model (e.g., a CRF for NER), the system decides which parts of the input to query, when to wait for responses, and when to return a final prediction. A Bayesian utility trades off expected prediction accuracy, monetary query cost, and latency. Because exact planning is intractable, the paper uses Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) with UCT and progressive widening to handle continuous time. The environment model comprises a prior pθ(y|x), a response model pR(r|yq), and a response-time model pT(t|s). The system updates pθ online using crowd responses. Experiments on NER, sentiment, and face classification indicate improved accuracy-cost-latency trade-offs versus human-only (n-vote), online learning without test-time queries, and a handcrafted threshold baseline. Code and data are released.

This work builds on online learning (improving with data), active learning (selective supervision), active classification/test-time feature acquisition (querying costly features at test time), and decision-theoretic control for crowdsourcing (e.g., POMDP approaches such as TurKontrol) and real-time crowd systems (e.g., retainer models, Legion:AR). The contributions here are a unified, test-time decision process that updates the model online while optimizing accuracy, cost, and time, and handling structured outputs and asynchronous responses via MCTS.

Strengths
- Clear, principled decision-theoretic formulation that jointly optimizes accuracy, latency, and monetary cost, with intuitive emergent strategies (redundancy, parallel vs sequential querying).
- Uses MCTS with progressive widening to address intractability and continuous-time dynamics; practical and general.
- Addresses structured prediction, which is realistic and benefits from correlations across output variables.
- Empirical results demonstrate substantial cost savings at comparable or better accuracy, particularly for NER; open-source implementation and reproducibility resources are provided.
- Real-time crowdsourcing setting (retainer) is realistic and relevant to deployed ML systems.

Weaknesses
- Environment assumptions are simplistic: a global, fixed-accuracy response model pR with uniform error (e.g., 0.7 correct) ignores worker/item variability and class-dependent confusions; many crowdsourcing models (Dawid–Skene, GLAD) offer richer, learnable reliability modeling.
- The learning objective for pθ under noisy, partial responses is under-specified. It is unclear whether the model maximizes marginal likelihood of responses, uses EM, or relies on proxy targets; details on how uncertainty in y from r is incorporated into training are needed.
- Utility definition uses ExpAcc of the MAP estimate but the paper reports F1 for NER; the mismatch between the optimized utility and reported metrics is not discussed. The exact “Accuracy” function used in Eq. (2) is not specified.
- Evaluation methodology partially relies on pre-collected pools of five responses/delays per label sampled without replacement to control variance. This may distort the asynchronous dynamics and redundancy benefits and can limit statistical power and generalizability.
- Baselines are limited. There is no comparison to decision-theoretic or POMDP-based controllers for vote acquisition (e.g., TurKontrol), DS-based adaptive aggregation, or myopic VOI policies. The “threshold baseline” is ad hoc; stronger myopic or greedy VOI baselines would clarify the benefits of MCTS.
- The action space disallows launching new queries while waiting (a practical simplification), but the impact on optimality/latency is not analyzed.
- Limited analysis of sensitivity to wM, wT, and pR; no ablations of MCTS components (e.g., exploration constant, progressive widening schedule) or computational overhead per decision.

Quality
- The formulation is sound and consistent with Bayesian decision theory; the MCTS-based approximation is reasonable. However, important modeling/training details (learning with noisy labels; specific accuracy metric in the utility) are missing or ambiguous. The environment model is overly simplified relative to known alternatives. Empirical evidence is positive but would be stronger with additional baselines and ablations, and with more faithful live evaluations without pre-sampled pools.

Clarity
- Generally well written and organized, with intuitive explanations and a helpful algorithm sketch. Clarity gaps: (i) precise training objective for pθ with noisy/partial supervision; (ii) exact form of “Accuracy” in Eq. (2) and handling of structured metrics like F1; (iii) details on computational budget/rollout policy for MCTS, and stopping criteria; (iv) fuller description of pT and its estimation; (v) clearer reporting of what “boosting performance relative to the expert provided labels” means.

Originality
- The combination of on-the-job test-time querying, online model improvement, structured outputs, and a unified Bayesian utility optimized via MCTS appears novel relative to prior active classification and crowdsourcing controllers. The work extends beyond Legion:AR by providing a formal ML framework and handling structured prediction.

Significance
- Addresses a practical and important problem of deploying high-quality systems with minimal upfront data. The approach is general and could influence future work on real-time human-in-the-loop ML. Demonstrated gains on NER are substantial; results on other tasks are more modest. Broader impact would be strengthened by richer worker models and stronger baselines.

Suggestions for improvement
- Specify and justify the training procedure for pθ under noisy, partial responses; consider EM or posterior regularization; discuss calibration.
- Replace the fixed pR with a learnable worker/item confusion model (e.g., Dawid–Skene/GLAD), and analyze its effect.
- Align the utility metric with evaluation metrics (e.g., expected token-F1) or justify the choice; provide sensitivity analyses to wM, wT, and pR.
- Add stronger baselines: myopic VOI, TurKontrol-style POMDP, DS-based adaptive redundancy, and test-time feature acquisition policies.
- Report computational overhead and decision latency of MCTS; provide ablations on UCT/progressive widening parameters and the “no mid-wait querying” constraint.
- Reduce reliance on pre-sampled response pools and include live end-to-end results.

Arguments for acceptance
- Principled, general framework addressing an important deployment setting.
- Novel integration of decision theory, MCTS, and structured prediction in real-time crowdsourcing.
- Empirical cost/accuracy improvements, strong on NER; open-source release.

Arguments against acceptance
- Simplified crowd/error model and unclear training objective for noisy supervision.
- Limited and potentially unrepresentative evaluation methodology; missing strong baselines.
- Metric mismatch and insufficient ablation/sensitivity analyses.
