This paper presents a compelling framework, “on-the-job learning,” for tackling prediction tasks with limited upfront labeled data by strategically querying a crowd during inference. The core idea – modeling this as a stochastic game and using Bayesian decision theory to balance cost, latency, and accuracy – is both novel and intuitively appealing. The authors effectively position their work relative to online learning and active learning, highlighting how it differs in its ability to improve *during* test time and maintain accuracy from the start. The experimental results, particularly the order of magnitude cost reduction on NER, are impressive.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The framing of sequential prediction with active querying as a stochastic game, coupled with Bayesian decision theory, is a genuinely new perspective. This isn’t merely an incremental improvement; it's a fundamentally different way to approach the cold-start problem.
*   **Practical Relevance:** The scenario addressed – situations where labeled data is scarce and costly – is highly relevant to many real-world applications, as exemplified by the disaster relief use case.
*   **Strong Empirical Results:** The significant cost reduction and performance gains on the NER task are persuasive. The 8% F1 improvement over single human labeling and 28% over online learning are substantial.
*   **Clear Presentation:** The paper is well-written and clearly explains the core concepts, even those relating to the game-theoretic formulation. The figures help to illustrate the process.
*   **Open-Source Release:** The availability of LENSE is a significant positive, facilitating reproducibility and encouraging further research.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Approximation Concerns:** While the authors acknowledge the intractability of the optimal policy and propose a MCTS-based approximation, the details of this approximation feel somewhat light. The progressive widening technique is mentioned but its impact isn’t thoroughly analyzed. It would be beneficial to have a more rigorous discussion of the approximation’s limitations and potential biases.
*   **Limited Scope of Experiments:** While the results on NER are excellent, performance on Sentiment and Image Classification are less dramatic. Further investigation into why the approach is more effective on NER—as the authors themselves hint at—would strengthen the paper. Exploring more diverse tasks would also build confidence in the generality of the approach.
*   **Crowdsourcing Setup Detail:** While the authors state they controlled for worker quality, the description of the freezing worker responses lacks details. Did they freeze responses *per task* or *globally*? What were the implications of this for the variability observed?
*    **Utility Function Sensitivity:** The weighting of accuracy, cost, and latency in the utility function (wM, wT) is not thoroughly justified. A sensitivity analysis demonstrating how changes to these weights impact the learned policy would be valuable.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The core idea is exceptionally novel and opens up a new direction for research in online learning and active learning.
*   The experimental results on NER are highly compelling and demonstrate a significant practical benefit.
*   The paper is well-written, clearly explained, and accompanied by a publicly available implementation.
*   Minor methodological flaws (e.g., the level of detail in the MCTS approximation) can be addressed in future work. The contribution here is largely conceptual and methodological, and the practical results outweigh the theoretical gaps.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The approximation method requires further justification and analysis to ensure its reliability.
*   The experiments, while promising, could benefit from greater diversity and a more thorough analysis of the factors influencing performance.
*   The utility function parameterization could be more rigorously justified.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that introduces a truly novel and potentially impactful framework. The authors address a significant practical problem with a creative and well-motivated approach. While some methodological details could be further refined, the boldness of the idea, the strength of the results on NER, and the availability of code make this paper a valuable contribution to the field. I recommend acceptance, even with the acknowledgment of minor weaknesses, as the potential impact of this work is considerable. I believe the novelty significantly outweighs any methodological imperfections.
