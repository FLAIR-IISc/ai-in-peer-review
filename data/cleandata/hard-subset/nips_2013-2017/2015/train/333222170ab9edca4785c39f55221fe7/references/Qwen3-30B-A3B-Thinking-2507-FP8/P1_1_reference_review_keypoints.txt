- Introduces "on-the-job learning": an adaptive framework combining machine learning with crowdsourcing to build accurate systems without large upfront labeled data.  
- Models decision-making as a stochastic game between system and crowd using Bayesian decision theory to balance accuracy, latency, and cost.  
- Proposes an approximation algorithm based on Monte Carlo tree search with progressive widening to handle continuous time.  
- Empirical evaluation on NER, sentiment, and image classification shows strong gains and large cost reductions (e.g., NER: 88.4% F1 with >10x cost reduction vs full human annotation).  
- Original and clearly differentiated from active classification and online learning.  
- Problem is significant for real-world, data-scarce deployments (e.g., disaster relief, opinion monitoring).  
- Technically sound with clear formulation, thorough experiments, available code, and thoughtful analysis of limitations.  
- Well-written and well-organized; figures effectively illustrate system behavior.  
- Lacks theoretical analysis or bounds on the approximation quality of the MCTS-based algorithm.  
- Assumes a fixed 70% crowd response accuracy without adequate discussion of calibration or adaptation to variable worker quality.  
- Potential scalability issues: MCTS with progressive widening may not scale to longer sequences or more complex structured prediction tasks.  
- Limited comparison to recent active learning / human-in-the-loop methods that might be more directly relevant.  
- Recommendation: accept with minor revisions to address theoretical and crowd-model concerns.  
- Suggestion: add analysis or conditions under which the MCTS approximation is reliable.  
- Suggestion: explain how the 70% crowd accuracy was determined and evaluate robustness when worker quality differs.  
- Suggestion: discuss computational complexity and scalability for larger/complex tasks.  
- Suggestion: compare and situate the method relative to recent active learning with human feedback approaches.  
- Overall assessment: strong, novel, and practically significant work (Quality 8/10, Clarity 9/10, Originality 9/10, Significance 9/10); accept with minor revisions.
