- Introduces "on-the-job learning": combines crowdsourcing with online learning to keep high initial accuracy while reducing cost over time.  
- Models the problem as a stochastic game and uses Bayesian decision theory to trade off accuracy, latency, and cost.  
- Proposes an MCTS-based algorithm with progressive widening to approximate the optimal policy.  
- Evaluates on NER, sentiment, and image classification, showing strong results and large cost reductions (10x+ for NER).  
- Open-source implementation (LENSE) and detailed experimental setup improve reproducibility.  
- Strength: clear motivation and distinction from prior active classification and online learning work.  
- Strength: principled technical formulation and well-justified utility function capturing key trade-offs.  
- Strength: thorough experiments demonstrating consistent improvements and decreasing query costs over time.  
- Strength: credible implementation details and worker quality control.  
- Weakness: scalability concerns—MCTS may not scale to longer sequences or higher-dimensional problems; complexity not discussed.  
- Weakness: simplistic 70% crowd accuracy assumption; real crowd quality varies and needs modeling (e.g., worker reliability).  
- Weakness: threshold baseline evaluation is superficial and lacks analysis of why the Bayesian approach outperforms it.  
- Weakness: insufficient connection to recent Bayesian active learning literature from NIPS.  
- Weakness: lacks theoretical analysis (e.g., regret bounds or convergence properties).  
- Overall assessment: strong, practical contribution—empirically convincing; recommended for acceptance.  
- Suggested revisions: discuss computational complexity/scalability, expand crowd response modeling, strengthen ties to recent Bayesian active learning work, analyze threshold baseline limitations, and add brief theoretical discussion.
