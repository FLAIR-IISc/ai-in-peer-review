Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes “on-the-job learning”: when a new instance arrives, the system can solicit crowd labels for uncertain parts of the input, receive responses asynchronously, and decide when to stop querying and return a prediction. The decision making is cast as a stochastic game with a CRF as the base prediction model and a Bayesian decision-theoretic utility that trades off accuracy, latency, and monetary cost. Since exact planning is intractable, the policy is approximated via Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) with UCT and progressive widening to handle continuous time. Experiments on NER, sentiment, and face classification suggest reduced cost versus pure crowdsourcing and higher accuracy versus pure ML. An implementation (LENSE) is released.

This work sits at the intersection of online learning, active learning, active classification (test-time feature acquisition), and decision-theoretic control of crowdsourcing. It is related to Legion:AR (real-time crowd-in-the-loop classification), POMDP approaches to crowd workflows (e.g., TurKontrol), and budgeted/test-time feature acquisition (e.g., Greiner et al.; Esmeir & Markovitch; Gao & Koller). Compared to these, the novelty is a unified decision-theoretic formulation over structured outputs with explicit modeling of asynchronous response times, and a planning approximation via MCTS/progressive widening.

Strengths
- Clear problem motivation: rapid deployment with high initial accuracy by leveraging the crowd, with decreasing dependence over time.
- Principled objective that jointly considers accuracy, time, and cost; asynchronous query handling is an important practical aspect.
- Use of MCTS with progressive widening for continuous-time responses is a sensible approximation for the large state space.
- Demonstrated across three tasks; open-source implementation.

Weaknesses and detailed comments

Quality/technical soundness
- Crowd response model is overly simplistic and fixed: pR(r|yq)=0.7 if r=yq, uniform otherwise. It ignores worker heterogeneity, task/item difficulty, and context, and it is not learned/updated. This is at odds with the literature (e.g., Dawid & Skene 1979; Whitehill et al. 2009 GLAD; Karger–Oh–Shah 2011; Kim & Ghahramani 2012 IBCC) and may bias both planning and evaluation. No sensitivity analysis to mis-specifying pR.
- Utility/metric mismatch: The utility uses ExpAcc of the MAP under 0–1 sequence accuracy, but experiments report token-level F1. Decisions optimized for 0–1 sequence loss may not align with token F1, potentially distorting the planning policy. Either optimize for the deployed metric or justify the surrogate with analysis/ablation.
- Baseline fairness: “Online learning” uses gold labels for all past examples but cannot query at test time, which confounds cost and supervision assumptions. It is unclear whether the cost of gold labels is accounted for consistently. Additional, stronger baselines are missing: myopic value-of-information policies, entropy/marginal-based querying with learned crowd models, POMDP heuristics, or budgeted feature acquisition methods.
- Evaluation protocol: To “control worker quality,” the experiments pre-collect exactly 5 responses and delays per label and then sample without replacement. This makes the environment almost deterministic and undermines the claim of real-time operation; it also biases against strategies that rely on redundancy or adapt to temporal variation. Results with a live retainer pool and realistic variability should be reported, or at least complemented by stochastic simulations learned from empirical distributions.
- Missing critical details: 
  - How are wM and wT chosen? Are they tuned per dataset? How sensitive are results to them and to the UCT constant c? 
  - Number of MCTS simulations per decision, computational budget, and latency overhead of planning itself.
  - Exact form of p(y|x,q,s,r,t) and inference: how are noisy responses integrated into the CRF (as soft potentials?); how are marginals computed efficiently during search?
  - Online learning details: features, optimization, and update schedule for each task; dataset sizes/splits (Table 1/2/3 are referenced but details are not in text).
- Restrictive action space: The constraint “cannot launch a query while waiting” simplifies search but can be suboptimal in asynchronous settings; no analysis of the regret due to this limitation or experiments showing minimal impact.
- No theoretical guarantees: While not required, some discussion of MCTS convergence or empirical ablations (e.g., with/without progressive widening, rollout policies) would strengthen the planning component.

Clarity/presentation
- Numerous typos and grammatical issues: “the the,” “Retreival,” “burgenoning,” “miscellaneos,” “comporable,” “In the both timelines,” etc. Footnote formatting leaks into the main text. Symbols (τ vs tnow) and indices are occasionally inconsistent. Please proofread carefully.
- References are incomplete and inconsistently formatted (many without year/venue/pages). This is below venue standards.
- Several claims refer to figures/tables not present in the text provided; the narrative lacks exact numbers (e.g., sample sizes, confidence intervals).
- The “threshold baseline” formula (for m queries, uncertainty reduced by factor 0.3) is ad-hoc; the inequality (1−pθ(yi|x))×0.3^m ≥ 0.98 seems dimensionally inconsistent.

Originality
- The high-level idea (crowd-in-the-loop at test time) and decision-theoretic planning have precedents (Legion:AR; TurKontrol; active classification). The main novelty is a particular synthesis: structured prediction + asynchronous crowd responses + MCTS progressive widening. This is incremental but potentially useful if executed rigorously.

Significance
- If validated, the approach is practical and could influence deployments that need immediate accuracy. However, current empirical evidence is limited by the simulation protocol, simplistic crowd modeling, and missing sensitivity analyses. The large gains in NER may be driven by class imbalance; generality remains unclear.

Suggestions for improvement
- Learn and update a richer crowd model (annotator reliabilities, item difficulty), or at minimum perform sensitivity analyses to pR mis-specification; compare to Dawid–Skene/GLAD/IBCC-based integrations.
- Align utility with evaluation metric (token F1 or structured loss), or justify the surrogate; report both.
- Provide full experimental details and ablations: effect of wM/wT, UCT constant, simulation budget, rollout policy, parallel vs sequential querying, and live-vs-simulated crowd.
- Strengthen baselines (myopic VOI, entropy-based, POMDP heuristics) and ensure fair cost accounting for all supervision.
- Clarify CRF integration of noisy labels and online update mechanics; include computational costs of planning.
- Fix typos/formatting; complete and standardize references.

Pros
- Timely problem with clear motivation.
- Principled decision-theoretic framing with asynchronous crowd responses.
- Sensible planning approximation (MCTS + progressive widening).
- Open-source implementation; multi-task evaluation.

Cons
- Oversimplified and fixed crowd noise model; no sensitivity analysis.
- Utility/metric mismatch; unclear fairness and completeness of baselines.
- Evaluation relies on a near-deterministic simulated crowd; limited external validity.
- Missing critical algorithmic and experimental details; no analysis of planning overhead/hyperparameters.
- Numerous writing and reference formatting issues.

Recommendation: Reject. The idea is promising, but the current submission has too many methodological and presentation shortcomings to meet the bar. Addressing the modeling, evaluation, and clarity issues could make this a strong resubmission.
