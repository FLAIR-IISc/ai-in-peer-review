- Introduces "on-the-job learning": make predictions while querying the crowd for uncertain input parts, formalized under Bayesian decision theory.
- Models the process as a stochastic game and approximates solutions using Monte Carlo Tree Search with progressive widening.
- System (LENSE) evaluated on NER, sentiment, and image classification, achieving large cost reductions and improved accuracy over pure crowd and pure ML baselines.
- Paper is technically sound with a clear formalization and a Bayesian framework that balances accuracy, latency, and cost.
- Experimental evaluation is comprehensive with multiple tasks, baselines, and clear metrics (F1, cost).
- Major concern: assumption of 70% crowd accuracy is stated without sufficient justification or validation.
- Crowd response model (pR) is overly simplistic and ignores worker variability and task complexity.
- Evaluation simulates on-the-job learning using labeled datasets, which may not reflect a true "no initial data" deployment.
- Paper is exceptionally well-written and organized, with clear figures and a well-documented algorithm.
- The stochastic game formulation and Bayesian decision-theoretic approach to on-the-job learning are novel and well-motivated.
- Distinguishes itself from online, active, and active classification approaches by maintaining high accuracy from the start while learning at test time.
- Results are significant and practically relevant (e.g., 88.4% F1 on NER at over an order-of-magnitude lower cost than a 5-vote human baseline).
- Open-source implementation (LENSE) enhances reproducibility and practical impact.
- Strengths: novel formalization, rigorous framework, broad evaluation, practical implementation, strong empirical gains.
- Weaknesses: unjustified crowd accuracy assumption, simplistic response model, reliance on existing labeled datasets, limited discussion of failure cases.
- Recommendation: accept — strong contribution with practical significance that should interest both researchers and practitioners.
