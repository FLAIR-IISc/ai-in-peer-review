- Introduces on-the-job learning: query crowd during prediction using Bayesian decision theory to trade off accuracy, latency, and cost.  
- Frames the problem as a stochastic game and approximates optimal policy with MCTS plus progressive widening.  
- Demonstrates LENSE on NER, sentiment, and image classification, achieving cost reductions and accuracy gains over baselines.  
- Distinguishes from prior work: queries before prediction, adapts during test time, and explicitly models cost/benefit via Bayesian decision theory.  
- Strong theoretical foundation through stochastic-game formulation and Bayesian decision-theoretic objective.  
- Uses MCTS with progressive widening as a practical approach to otherwise intractable policy computation.  
- Empirical evaluation is comprehensive and compares against strong baselines (human n-query, online learning, threshold baseline).  
- Open-source release and available code/data on CodaLab improve reproducibility and impact.  
- Paper is generally clear, well-written, and logically organized.  
- Major concern: computational cost of running MCTS at prediction time may limit scalability and real-time applicability.  
- Crowd model assumptions (e.g., fixed 70% accuracy, fixed response pool) are simplistic and may reduce robustness.  
- Utility function is relatively simple; more nuanced cost/error-sensitive utilities could improve performance.  
- Results are dominated by NER; weaker gains on sentiment and image tasks need deeper analysis.  
- Explanation of progressive widening lacks detail on how widening factors are chosen and their performance impact.  
- For acceptance: novelty, solid theory, strong experiments, and reproducibility argue in favor.  
- Against acceptance: MCTS complexity, simplistic crowd model, and uneven task performance argue for caution.  
- Recommendation: accept with minor revisions to address scalability, crowd-model assumptions, and cross-task performance analysis.  
- Reviewer confidence: 8/10.
