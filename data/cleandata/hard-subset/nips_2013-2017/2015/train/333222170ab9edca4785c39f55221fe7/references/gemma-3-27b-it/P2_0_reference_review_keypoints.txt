- Introduces "on-the-job learning" using Bayesian decision theory to query crowd selectively, modeled as a stochastic game solved with MCTS and progressive widening; demonstrates LENSE on NER, sentiment, and image tasks with significant cost reductions.  
- Positions work among online learning, active learning, and crowdsourcing, highlighting dynamic test-time policy adaptation and distinguishing from Legion:AR.  
- Novelty: Formulating on-the-job learning as a stochastic game with a Bayesian decision-theoretic approach is elegant and novel.  
- Practicality: Addresses real-world scarce-label scenarios and achieves substantial annotation cost savings.  
- Strong empirical results: Consistently outperforms baselines across tasks; notable 88.4% F1 on NER with reduced cost.  
- Technical soundness: Use of MCTS and progressive widening is reasonable; game tree and utility are clearly explained.  
- Reproducibility: Authors provide open-source code and data.  
- Clarity: Paper is generally well-written and well-organized.  
- Complexity: Framework and exposition are complex and may be hard for readers unfamiliar with stochastic games or MCTS.  
- Crowd model assumptions: Assumes fixed worker pool with known accuracy, limiting robustness to variable or drifting worker quality.  
- Response time assumptions: Relies on pre-collected worker response times, which may not generalize across tasks or platforms.  
- Heuristic nature of MCTS: MCTS approximates the optimal policy and the paper lacks analysis of approximation quality and parameter sensitivity.  
- Task emphasis imbalance: Paper focuses disproportionately on NER; cross-task analysis could be deeper.  
- Arguments for acceptance: Novel, theoretically sound, strong empirical evidence, reproducible code, and clear exposition.  
- Arguments against acceptance: High implementation complexity, limited generalizability of crowd assumptions, and insufficient MCTS analysis.  
- Overall assessment: Strong, significant contribution; recommend acceptance with minor revisions.  
- Recommendations: Discuss MCTS computational cost and scaling, provide sensitivity analysis for MCTS parameters, evaluate robustness to worker quality variation, and offer more detailed cross-task comparisons.  
- Qualitative ratings: Quality, Originality, and Significance rated excellent; Clarity rated good.
