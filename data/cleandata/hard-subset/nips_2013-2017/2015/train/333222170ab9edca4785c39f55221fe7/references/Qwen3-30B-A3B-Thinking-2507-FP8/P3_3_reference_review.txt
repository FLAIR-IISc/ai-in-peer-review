

# Peer Review: "On-the-Job Learning with Bayesian Decision Theory"

## Summary of Main Ideas

This paper introduces "on-the-job learning," a novel framework that combines active learning with crowdsourcing to build accurate AI systems without requiring large amounts of initial labeled data. The key innovation is modeling the interaction between the system and the crowd as a stochastic game, where the system strategically queries the crowd on specific parts of uncertain inputs, then uses these responses to update its model. The authors formalize this using Bayesian decision theory to balance accuracy, cost, and latency, and develop an approximate solution using Monte Carlo Tree Search with progressive widening. They evaluate their system (LENS) on three tasks: named-entity recognition, sentiment classification, and image classification, showing significant cost reductions (more than an order of magnitude on NER) while improving accuracy over baselines.

## Quality

The paper demonstrates strong technical quality with a well-formulated game-theoretic model. The authors properly distinguish their approach from related work, particularly online learning and active classification, by emphasizing the dynamic model improvement during test time and the need for immediate predictions without waiting for a full dataset.

However, there are significant methodological concerns:
1. The comparison to "full human annotation" is misleading - the paper states "more than an order of magnitude reduction in cost compared to full human annotation" but doesn't clearly define what "full human annotation" means in this context. The baseline they compare to is "human n-query" (majority vote of n crowd workers), which is not the same as "full human annotation" (which would typically mean a single human labeling the entire input).

2. The experimental methodology has limitations: They "collected 5 worker responses and their delays on each label ahead of time" and sample without replacement from this "frozen pool." This approach doesn't capture the full stochastic nature of crowd responses and may artificially limit the diversity of responses they observe.

3. The CRF model details are insufficient for reproducibility - the paper mentions "a standard linear-chain CRF" but doesn't describe the feature set or training procedure adequately.

## Clarity

The paper is generally well-structured and clearly written, with good use of figures to illustrate the concepts. However, some key aspects need clarification:

1. The utility function (Equation 1) is confusingly defined. The term ExpAcc(p) = Ep(y)[Accuracy(argmax y' p(y'))] suggests expected accuracy, but the utility should depend on the system's actions, not directly on model accuracy.

2. The paper claims "a 28% F1 improvement over online learning" on NER but doesn't provide the actual F1 scores for the baselines, making it difficult to assess the significance of this improvement.

3. The distinction between on-the-job learning and active classification needs more concrete explanation beyond citing Legion:AR as a related work.

## Originality

The core idea of using Bayesian decision theory to guide active querying in real-time is novel. The paper makes a clear distinction from previous work on active classification (which relies on a static policy learned from a fully labeled dataset) and online learning (which doesn't actively seek information before prediction).

However, the use of MCTS for approximate policy learning in a similar setting has been explored in other contexts (e.g., [12] for game playing), and the paper doesn't sufficiently differentiate their application of MCTS with progressive widening for continuous time from prior work. The connection to the broader literature on active learning with noisy labels (e.g., [20, 21, 22]) is underdeveloped.

## Significance

The paper addresses a practical and important problem in AI deployment: building accurate systems from the start without requiring massive labeled datasets. The results on NER are promising, with substantial cost reductions and accuracy improvements. However, the results on sentiment classification and image classification are less impressive (only ~2x cost reduction), suggesting the approach may not generalize as well as claimed.

The claim that "the number of queries needed (and thus cost and latency) to decrease over time without compromising accuracy" is compelling but lacks sufficient evidence. Figure 4 shows the trend, but a more detailed analysis of how the query rate decreases over time would strengthen this claim.

## Overall Assessment

**Strengths:**
- Novel framework that addresses a meaningful practical problem in AI deployment
- Well-formulated game-theoretic model with Bayesian decision theory
- Strong experimental results on NER task with significant cost reduction
- Good implementation and open-source release (LENS)

**Weaknesses:**
- Misleading comparisons and unclear definitions (e.g., "full human annotation")
- Insufficient details about the CRF model and training procedure
- Limited analysis of task-specific performance differences
- Methodological limitations in crowd response sampling

## Recommendation

I recommend **acceptance with minor revisions**. The paper makes a meaningful contribution to the field by presenting a principled approach to combining active learning with crowdsourcing in a real-time setting. The technical quality is strong, and the experimental results are promising, particularly for the NER task.

**Specific Revisions Needed:**
1. Clarify the comparison with "full human annotation" and define what this means.
2. Provide actual F1 scores for all baselines to contextualize the improvements.
3. Include more details about the CRF model (feature set, training procedure).
4. Add analysis explaining why the approach works better for NER than for other tasks.
5. Clarify the distinction between on-the-job learning and active classification in practice.

With these revisions, this paper would be an excellent contribution to NIPS, addressing a practical problem with a technically sound and innovative approach. The work has significant potential for real-world impact, particularly in domains where labeled data is scarce but immediate deployment is needed (e.g., disaster response, public opinion monitoring).
