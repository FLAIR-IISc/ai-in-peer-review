Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes “on-the-job learning,” a decision-theoretic framework that, at test time, interleaves model prediction with real-time crowdsourced queries to maintain high accuracy from the outset and reduce cost/latency over time as the model improves. The problem is formalized as a stochastic game between the system and crowd, with a CRF pθ(y|x) for structured prediction, a simple noisy response model pR, and a latency model pT. Actions (query, wait, return) are chosen by maximizing expected utility that trades off predicted accuracy, monetary cost, and latency. Because exact planning is intractable, the authors use Monte Carlo Tree Search (UCT) with progressive widening to handle continuous time. Experiments on NER, sentiment, and face attribute classification claim cost reductions relative to pure crowdsourcing and accuracy gains relative to a pure machine baseline, with an open-source implementation (LENSE).

The work connects to online learning, active learning, and especially decision-theoretic control of crowdsourcing workflows (e.g., TurKontrol) and active classification with test-time acquisition costs. It also relates to modeling labeler noise (Dawid–Skene; Whitehill et al.; Karger et al.) and redundant labeling (Sheng et al.). The structured-output setting (CRF) is a useful extension beyond most prior crowd-control POMDPs, and the explicit modeling of asynchronous response times is welcome.

Strengths
- Clear decision-theoretic framing with explicit tradeoffs among cost, latency, and accuracy; the stochastic game formalization is sensible.
- Incorporates structured prediction, capturing cross-label dependencies in a way many crowd-control systems omit.
- Uses MCTS and progressive widening to handle combinatorial planning and continuous latencies; a reasonable engineering choice.
- Qualitative behaviors (redundant queries for noisy items, parallelization for latency) emerge naturally from the utility.
- Open-source implementation and some empirical gains reported across three tasks.

Weaknesses and detailed comments

Quality
- Worker/noise model is oversimplified and not learned: pR is fixed at 70% correctness with uniform confusion, and workers are assumed exchangeable. This is a strong and often false assumption; worker heterogeneity, class-specific confusions, and instance difficulty are well documented. Without learning/calibrating pR (e.g., Dawid–Skene-style), planning quality may be brittle. No sensitivity analysis is provided.
- Evaluation relies heavily on simulated crowds: the authors pre-collect five responses per label and then “sample without replacement” to simulate the crowd. This induces an unrealistically small and static pool, constrains dependence across items, and weakens claims about real-time decision policies. Although a retainer pool was implemented, there is no rigorous live A/B evaluation demonstrating end-to-end benefits under natural worker variation.
- The utility function and evaluation metric appear mismatched. Utility uses “ExpAcc” of the MAP under p(y|·), whereas NER is evaluated via F1. It is unclear what Accuracy means in the utility for structured outputs. If token accuracy is used for planning but sequence F1 is reported, the planning objective may not align with evaluation; this requires clarification and ideally a utility consistent with the task metric or a justification of the surrogate.
- Baselines are weak or incomplete. The “online learning” baseline assumes gold labels post hoc but forbids test-time acquisition—an odd comparison when the central idea is test-time acquisition. Missing are strong decision-theoretic/myopic VOI baselines (e.g., TurKontrol/POMDP-style policies, greedy value-of-information per token, learned static policies from offline data). The “threshold baseline” is heuristic and ignores time; it is not sufficient to establish the necessity of MCTS planning.
- No ablations. It is unclear how much each component contributes: structure vs. independent tokens, sequential vs. parallel querying, modeling pT, choice of utility weights (wM, wT), and the effect of prohibiting launching new queries while waiting. There is no sensitivity to mis-specified pR/pT, nor calibration analysis for pθ.
- No statistical tests or confidence intervals; reported gains (e.g., “order of magnitude cost reduction,” “28% F1 improvement over online learning”) are not contextualized by variance.

Clarity
- The paper is generally readable, but important details are missing: how pT is estimated; exact form of Accuracy in Eq. (2) for each task; MCTS parameters (exploration constant, rollout policy), computational budget per input, and real-time feasibility; training details for CRF updates (learning rate schedule, regularization, feature sets).
- Some typographical issues and dangling footnotes; a clearer separation of simulation vs. live experiments would help.

Originality
- The formulation is a fairly natural combination of known ideas: decision-theoretic control/POMDPs for crowd workflows, structured prediction, and MCTS planning. The novelty lies in applying these to structured on-the-job learning with asynchronous crowd responses and proposing an implementable system. That said, the technical innovation over prior decision-theoretic crowd control is incremental, and stronger empirical evidence is needed to justify the new planning architecture.

Significance
- The problem is practically relevant, but the current evidence is limited by simulation, simplistic worker modeling, and absent baselines. Without stronger validation against established decision-theoretic baselines and live trials, it is hard to assess impact. The approach may be useful, but the paper does not yet convincingly advance the state of the art.

Suggestions for improvement
- Learn and adapt worker- and class-specific reliabilities online; compare to Dawid–Skene-style models and TurKontrol.
- Provide live, end-to-end experiments with a real retainer pool; report full cost–latency–accuracy frontiers with confidence intervals.
- Align utility with evaluation (e.g., expected F1 or a calibrated surrogate) and study sensitivity to wM, wT.
- Add strong baselines: myopic VOI, POMDP variants, greedy sequential halting, static policies learned offline.
- Ablate: structure vs. independent labels; sequential vs. parallel querying; effect of pT modeling; effect of prohibiting mid-wait queries.
- Report computational overhead, wall-clock per decision, and scalability.
- Conduct robustness tests under mis-specified pR/pT and heterogeneous workers.

Arguments for acceptance
- Clear, principled formulation of a timely problem; structured outputs and latency modeling are appealing.
- Practical system with open-source code; some positive empirical results.

Arguments against acceptance
- Heavy reliance on unrealistic simulation and simplistic worker model; limited external validity.
- Missing strong baselines and ablations; unclear that MCTS/planning is needed.
- Utility–metric mismatch; insufficient detail on implementation and compute.
- Lack of statistical rigor and robustness/sensitivity analyses.

Overall evaluation: promising idea and framing, but the empirical validation is not yet rigorous enough for NeurIPS. I recommend rejection in the current form, with the hope the authors strengthen baselines, modeling, and live evaluation.
