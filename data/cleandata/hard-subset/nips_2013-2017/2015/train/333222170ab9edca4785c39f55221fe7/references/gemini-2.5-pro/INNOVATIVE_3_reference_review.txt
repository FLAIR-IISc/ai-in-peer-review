**Summary:**
This paper introduces and formalizes the concept of "on-the-job learning," a novel framework for deploying machine learning systems that require high accuracy from the very first example, without a pre-existing labeled dataset. The core idea is to create a system that, for each incoming instance, can intelligently and asynchronously query a crowd of human experts to gather information before making a final prediction. The system learns online from these interactions, gradually reducing its reliance on the crowd over time. The authors model this complex decision-making process as a stochastic game between the system and the crowd. They propose a principled solution based on Bayesian decision theory, defining a utility function that explicitly trades off accuracy, monetary cost, and latency. Due to the intractability of finding the optimal policy, they develop an approximate solver using Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) with progressive widening. The proposed system, LENSE, is evaluated on NER, sentiment, and image classification tasks, demonstrating significant cost reductions over a pure-crowdsourcing approach and substantial accuracy improvements over a standard online learning baseline.

This work connects to several established fields but carves out a distinct and important niche. It differs from standard online and active learning, where the primary goal is model improvement and high accuracy is only achieved asymptotically; here, high accuracy on every instance is a primary objective from the start. It also advances beyond active classification by allowing the model and policy to adapt and improve at test-time based on the observed crowd responses. The paper builds upon the initial system concept in Legion:AR [6] by providing the first rigorous machine learning formalization of the problem.

**Strengths:**
1.  **Novelty and Significance of the Problem Setting:** The "on-the-job learning" paradigm is a bold and highly significant contribution. It addresses a critical and practical gap in the current ML landscape: how to deploy accurate systems in novel domains where large-scale data collection is infeasible upfront. This vision of a system that begins with a human "crutch" and learns to become self-sufficient is compelling and could inspire a new direction of research at the intersection of ML, active learning, and HCI.
2.  **Principled Theoretical Framework:** The decision to model the problem as a stochastic game and apply Bayesian decision theory is elegant and powerful. It provides a formal language for reasoning about the complex trade-offs between accuracy, cost, and latency, moving far beyond simple heuristics. This theoretical grounding is a major strength and makes the approach generalizable.
3.  **Strong and Convincing Empirical Validation:** The authors have not only proposed a new framework but have also built and evaluated a complete system. The experiments are well-designed, covering multiple tasks including structured prediction (NER). The results are impressive, with an order-of-magnitude cost reduction on the NER task being a standout achievement. The comparisons to well-chosen baselines (human-only, online learning, a heuristic-based method) clearly demonstrate the value of the proposed decision-theoretic approach. The analysis in Figure 3, showing how the need for supervision decreases with better features, further strengthens the paper's claims.
4.  **Clarity and Completeness:** The paper is very well-written and organized. The problem is clearly motivated, the model is explained in detail, and the connection to related work is thoroughly discussed. The authors are also committed to reproducibility, providing open-source code and data, which is a commendable practice.

**Weaknesses:**
1.  **Simplifying Assumptions in the Environment Model:** The model of the crowd is relatively simple (e.g., a fixed 70% accuracy with uniform errors). Real-world crowd markets exhibit much more complex phenomena, such as varying worker quality, adversarial behavior, and systematic biases. While this simplification is understandable and acceptable for a foundational paper introducing a new framework, it is a limitation. Incorporating more sophisticated worker models (e.g., from the crowdsourcing literature) would be a valuable next step.
2.  **Reliance on a General-Purpose Solver:** The use of MCTS is a reasonable and practical choice for approximating the intractable policy, but the paper does not deeply explore whether this is the optimal choice or if problem-specific structural properties could be exploited for a more efficient solver. However, this is a minor point; the main contribution is the framework itself, not the specific planner. The chosen method is sufficient to demonstrate the framework's potential.

**Recommendation: Accept**

This is an excellent paper that introduces a novel, important, and exciting research direction. The "on-the-job learning" problem is both intellectually deep and practically relevant. The authors' formalization using Bayesian decision theory is principled and elegant, and their empirical results are strong and convincing.

While the work relies on some simplifying assumptions, particularly regarding the crowd model, these do not detract from the core contribution. This is a foundational piece that defines a new problem setting and provides the first rigorous, effective solution. I am willing to overlook these minor modeling limitations in light of the paper's boldness and novelty. This work is highly likely to be influential and to inspire follow-up research. It is a perfect fit for NIPS and represents the kind of creative, forward-looking research the conference should champion.
