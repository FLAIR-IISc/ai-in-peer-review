- Introduces "on-the-job learning": systems query crowd for uncertain inputs while learning to reduce future queries.
- Models the problem as a stochastic game using Bayesian decision theory to trade off accuracy, latency, and cost.
- Implements the approach as LENSE (Learning from Expensive Noisy Slow Experts).
- Approximates optimal policy with Monte Carlo tree search (MCTS) plus progressive widening for continuous-time decisions.
- Evaluates on three tasks (NER, sentiment, image classification) with multiple baselines.
- Shows substantial cost reductions (over 10x for NER) and improved accuracy versus pure ML or crowd-only approaches.
- Experimental design is thorough and reproducible; code and data are publicly available.
- Paper is technically sound with clear state/action/utility modeling and well-motivated approximations.
- Writing and presentation are strong; problem formulation, algorithms, and results are clearly explained.
- Contribution is novel, distinct from online learning, active classification, and prior crowdsourcing work.
- Practical significance is high for real-world deployment where labeled data is scarce but immediate accuracy is needed.
- Minor concern: assumes 70% crowd accuracy without deep analysis of performance under lower-quality crowds.
- Additional weaknesses: limited discussion of scalability, some MCTS implementation details lacking, and missing ethical considerations of crowdsourcing.
- Overall recommendation: strong accept — significant, well-executed contribution to human-AI collaboration.
