### Summary

This paper introduces and formalizes the "on-the-job learning" setting, a framework designed to build high-accuracy AI systems from the start, without requiring a large pre-existing labeled dataset. The core idea is to create a system that can intelligently query a crowd for labels on-the-fly for parts of an input it is uncertain about, make a prediction, and simultaneously learn from these interactions to become more self-sufficient over time. The authors model this problem as a stochastic game between the system and the crowd. They use Bayesian decision theory to define a utility function that explicitly trades off prediction accuracy, monetary cost, and latency. Since finding the optimal policy in this game is intractable, they propose an approximation based on Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) with progressive widening to handle the continuous-time nature of crowd responses.

The proposed system, LENSE, is evaluated on three diverse tasks: named-entity recognition (NER), sentiment classification, and image classification. The experimental results show that LENSE significantly reduces the cost compared to a purely crowdsourced approach while improving accuracy, and substantially outperforms a standard online learning baseline in terms of accuracy. This work connects ideas from online learning, active learning, and crowdsourcing, but carves out a distinct and practical problem setting. It differs from online/active learning by actively seeking information *before* making a prediction on a new instance to ensure high accuracy from day one. It differs from prior work in active classification by learning its policy dynamically at test-time and explicitly modeling a noisy oracle.

### Strengths

1.  **Novel and Significant Problem Formulation:** The "on-the-job learning" setting is well-motivated and addresses a highly practical and important gap between conventional supervised learning (which requires large upfront data collection) and pure crowdsourcing (which is expensive and does not result in a reusable model). The concept of a system that starts with a human "crutch" and learns to perform autonomously is compelling and has broad applicability.
2.  **Principled Theoretical Framework:** The formulation of the problem as a stochastic game and the use of Bayesian decision theory to unify the trade-offs between accuracy, cost, and latency is elegant and theoretically sound. This provides a solid foundation for reasoning about a complex interactive learning problem.
3.  **Strong Technical Contribution:** The adaptation of MCTS combined with progressive widening is a non-trivial and suitable solution for approximating the intractable optimal policy. This demonstrates a sophisticated application of techniques from reinforcement learning and game-playing to a novel machine learning problem.
4.  **Thorough and Convincing Experiments:** The evaluation is comprehensive, covering three different tasks with varying structures (structured prediction, text classification, image classification). The choice of baselines is appropriate, allowing for clear comparisons against human-only, machine-only, and a simpler heuristic approach. The results are impressive, demonstrating an order-of-magnitude cost reduction on NER and clear improvements across all tasks. The learning curve in Figure 4, showing cost decreasing over time, powerfully illustrates the core benefit of the proposed framework.
5.  **Clarity and Reproducibility:** The paper is very well-written, clearly explaining the problem, the model, and the algorithm. The inclusion of an open-source implementation and a CodaLab worksheet for reproducibility is commendable and sets a high standard.

### Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement

1.  **Assumptions in the Environment Model:** The model for the crowd (`p_R` and `p_T`) is static. For instance, worker accuracy is fixed at 70%, independent of the instance, the specific token being queried, or the individual worker. In practice, some queries are harder than others, and worker quality can vary. A more adaptive system could learn a model of worker accuracy or query difficulty online, potentially leading to even better performance. Could the parameters of the response model `p_R` be updated online alongside the prediction model `p_θ`?
2.  **Computational Cost of Decision-Making:** The MCTS planner is run for each new input instance to determine the querying policy. This can be computationally intensive. The paper does not discuss the runtime of this decision-making process itself. For a truly real-time system handling a high-throughput stream of data (e.g., tweets), the overhead of MCTS could be a bottleneck. A brief discussion of the computational complexity and practical wall-clock time required to plan a policy for a single instance would strengthen the paper's claims about real-time applicability.
3.  **Sensitivity to Utility Parameters:** The utility function in Equation 1 relies on weights `w_M` and `w_T` to balance monetary cost and time against accuracy. The paper does not discuss how these parameters were chosen or how sensitive the system's performance is to their values. For practitioners to adopt this framework, guidance on setting these crucial hyperparameters would be invaluable. Including a brief sensitivity analysis would be a useful addition.
4.  **Clarity of Expected Accuracy Calculation:** The definition of `ExpAcc` in Equation 2 seems to be the expected accuracy of the MAP prediction under the posterior `p(y)`. It might be beneficial to spell out the expectation more explicitly, e.g., `sum_y p(y) * Accuracy(y_map, y)`, where `y_map` is the argmax. This is a minor point of clarity but could prevent potential misinterpretation.

### Recommendation: Accept

This is a high-quality paper that introduces a novel, well-motivated problem and addresses it with a principled and technically sophisticated solution. The strengths—a significant problem formulation, a strong theoretical framework, impressive empirical results, and a commitment to reproducibility—far outweigh the weaknesses. The weaknesses identified are primarily avenues for future research and do not detract from the core contribution of this work. The paper is well-written, the results are significant, and it is likely to have a substantial impact on the community, inspiring further research at the intersection of machine learning, active learning, and human-computer interaction. It is a clear accept for NeurIPS.
