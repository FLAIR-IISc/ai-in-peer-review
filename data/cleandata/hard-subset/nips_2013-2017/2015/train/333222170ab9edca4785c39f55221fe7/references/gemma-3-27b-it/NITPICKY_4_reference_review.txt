## Review of "On-the-Job Learning with Bayesian Decision Theory"

This paper proposes a framework, “on-the-job learning,” for tackling structured prediction tasks when labeled data is scarce. The core idea – strategically querying a crowd to resolve uncertainties during prediction, modeled as a stochastic game with Bayesian decision theory – is interesting and potentially valuable. The authors present a Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) approximation for solving the resulting intractable problem and demonstrate empirical improvements over several baselines on NER, sentiment classification, and image classification.

However, despite the novelty of the approach and promising results, I find the paper riddled with a concerning number of methodological inconsistencies, unclear explanations, and superficial experimental analysis that, in my assessment, outweigh its strengths. Therefore, I recommend rejection.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The framing of interactive prediction as a stochastic game and the application of Bayesian decision theory to this setting are genuinely novel.
*   **Practicality:** The LENSE implementation and the reported cost reduction on NER are compelling, suggesting practical applicability.
*   **Clear Motivation:** The problem statement – needing accurate predictions with limited resources – is well-defined and relevant.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Methodological Ambiguity:** The description of the game tree and the actions is confusing. For example, the distinction between `∅W` and `∅R` seems unnecessarily complex and the justification provided ("rules out possibility of launching a query midway…") is weak. The explanation of how the game state `σ` is updated lacks detail and precision, making it difficult to understand the full algorithm flow.
*   **Utility Function Concerns:** The utility function (Equation 1) combines accuracy and cost/latency linearly. The weighting coefficients `wM` and `wT` are not discussed or justified. A sensitivity analysis of these weights is crucial but absent. Moreover, the `ExpAcc` calculation uses a somewhat opaque definition based on maximizing over *all* possible labels, instead of focusing on the predicted label; this seems potentially problematic.
*   **Environment Model Simplifications:** The response model `pR(r | yq)` uses a fixed 70% accuracy. This is a gross oversimplification. The paper acknowledges worker variability yet only uses a single, fixed parameter.  The frozen pool of worker responses is an unusual choice and the reasoning behind this choice isn’t compelling, especially given the reported worker quality variations.
*   **Insufficient Baselines:** The "Online Learning" baseline is unrealistically strong (access to perfect information) and doesn't accurately represent a typical online learning scenario. The "Threshold Baseline" is presented without a clear explanation of how the 0.3 and 0.98 thresholds were chosen.  Why weren't other active learning strategies (e.g., uncertainty sampling) included for comparison?
*   **Experimental Details Lacking Rigor:** The experimental setup feels rushed. The hyperparameters for the CRF model aren't reported.  The ablation study showing the impact of different features (UNIGRAMS vs. RNN) is superficially presented, offering little insight into the benefits of richer feature representations beyond an observation that more complex models need less supervision. The qualitative example given in Figure 2a is trivial and doesn’t demonstrate the sophistication of the approach.
*   **Formatting & Writing:** The paper suffers from numerous minor formatting issues (e.g., inconsistent equation numbering, missing periods). The writing style could be more concise and precise.
*    **Missing Proofs/Justifications:** Critical claims, like the simplification in the game tree regarding not launching queries mid-wait, are stated as "reasonable limitations" without further justification. 

**Specific Points & Questions:**

*   Section 3: The definition of the game state `σ` is ambiguous. What does `tnow` represent?  Is it the time the query was *issued* or the time a response is *received*?
*   Section 4: Algorithm 1 lacks sufficient detail. The UCT decision rule is mentioned but not fully explained. How are the values `V(σ)` initialized?
*   Section 5: The cost calculation in the NER experiment needs clarification. What is the cost of a human-annotated label in the 5-vote approach?
*   The paper consistently uses "queries" and "actions" interchangeably. This should be clarified.

**Arguments Pro Acceptance:**

*   The approach is novel and addresses an important problem.
*   The initial experimental results are encouraging.

**Arguments Con Acceptance:**

*   Significant methodological ambiguities and simplifications.
*   Insufficient experimental rigor and baseline comparisons.
*   Lack of detailed analysis and justification for key design choices.
*   Numerous minor flaws in writing and formatting.



**Overall Assessment:**

While the core idea behind on-the-job learning is promising, this paper falls short of NIPS standards due to a lack of methodological clarity, insufficient experimental validation, and numerous minor flaws. The authors need to address these concerns by providing more detailed explanations, conducting more thorough experiments, and revising the paper for clarity and precision before it could be considered for publication. I believe a major revision is required to address these issues.
