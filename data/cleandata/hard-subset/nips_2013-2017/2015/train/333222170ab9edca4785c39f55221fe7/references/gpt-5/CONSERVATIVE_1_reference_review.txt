Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes “on-the-job learning,” a framework that interleaves prediction with on-demand crowd queries at test time to maintain high accuracy from the outset while gradually reducing reliance on the crowd as the model improves. The problem is cast as a stochastic game between the system and the crowd, with actions to issue label queries, wait, or return. A CRF serves as the base prediction model. A simple crowd response model assumes fixed per-label accuracy and i.i.d. errors; a response-time model allows asynchronous arrivals. A Bayesian utility trades off expected accuracy of the MAP prediction, monetary cost per query, and latency. Because exact planning is intractable, the policy is approximated with MCTS (UCT) and progressive widening to handle continuous time. Experiments on NER, sentiment, and face classification suggest cost savings versus majority-vote crowdsourcing and accuracy improvements over an online-learning baseline. Code/data are released.

This work sits at the intersection of online/active learning, cost-sensitive/active classification at test time, and decision-theoretic control of crowdsourcing. It is closest to Legion:AR for real-time crowd labeling and POMDP-based approaches such as TurKontrol that optimize query allocation under noisy workers, but extends to structured outputs and continuous-time dynamics with a CRF-based environment model and an MCTS planner.

Strengths
- Clear problem motivation: achieving high accuracy immediately without large labeled datasets, by trading off cost, time, and accuracy.
- Decision-theoretic formulation that yields intuitive behaviors (parallel vs sequential queries, redundancy under noise).
- Support for structured prediction, where partial labels can inform others via the CRF.
- Practical planning approach (MCTS + progressive widening) that can, in principle, operate under time constraints.
- Reported cost reductions on NER with comparable or improved accuracy; open-source code and datasets.

Weaknesses and concerns (quality, evaluation rigor)
- Environment model is simplistic and potentially mis-specified:
  - Fixed accuracy pR with uniform error distribution and i.i.d. responses ignores worker heterogeneity, item difficulty, and correlation across repeated queries. Established models (Dawid–Skene/GLAD, worker/item latent traits) are not used, risking overconfidence and suboptimal query allocation.
  - Response-time model pT lacks detail; stationarity and dependence on worker/item are not addressed. Sensitivity to latency miscalibration is not studied.
- Simulation protocol risks biasing results:
  - The main experiments sample from a pre-collected pool of five worker responses per label without replacement. This limits redundancy effects, collapses time variation, and tightly couples test items and workers, reducing ecological validity for “on-the-job” operation.
  - While an AMT retainer is described, most results appear simulated rather than live. No end-to-end latency including system planning time is reported.
- Utility and decision-theoretic choices:
  - The utility uses expected accuracy of the MAP predictor. This is neither a proper scoring rule nor necessarily aligned with expected 0–1 risk under the posterior; calibration issues may distort decisions. No calibration analysis is shown.
  - Hyperparameters (wM, wT, UCT constant c, progressive widening schedule) and their tuning are not detailed; sensitivity analyses are missing. Without this, it is hard to assess robustness or fairness when trading cost vs accuracy.
- Comparisons and baselines:
  - No comparison to POMDP-based crowd control (e.g., TurKontrol) or to worker-modeling baselines that aggregate noisy annotations more effectively than majority vote.
  - The “threshold baseline” is a weak strawman (fixed 0.3 factor, no timing), and the “human n-query” baselines do not reflect modern aggregation with learned reliabilities.
  - The “online learning” baseline trains on gold labels for all prior examples but cannot query at test time; this is not apples-to-apples, since the proposed method uses additional test-time supervision. A stronger machine-only baseline with access to the same total annotation budget (but acquired offline/actively) is needed.
- Reporting and completeness:
  - Missing details on feature sets, CRF training (regularization, learning rate schedules), planning time per decision, and computational overhead. In a real-time setting, planning cost matters as much as human latency.
  - No statistical significance or confidence intervals; effect sizes could be within noise given small worker pools.
  - Some claims (“boosting performance relative to expert provided labels”) require careful explanation and ablation to be credible.

Clarity
The high-level narrative is clear and the formal game description is helpful, but key implementation and modeling details are underspecified (pT, pR learning vs fixing, calibration, hyperparameters), and some typos remain. Reproducibility is aided by code release; nonetheless, clarity would benefit from a more thorough methods section and ablations.

Originality
Framing test-time querying as a stochastic game with continuous time and solving via MCTS/progressive widening is a reasonable combination of known components. The idea of on-the-job learning was explored in Legion:AR; decision-theoretic control of noisy crowd labels via POMDPs is established. The primary novelty is integrating these ideas for structured prediction with an explicit planning layer. Overall originality is moderate.

Significance
If validated more rigorously, the approach could be useful for rapid-deployment systems where steady accuracy is essential. However, given the simplified environment model, limited live evaluation, and lack of strong baselines/ablations, the current evidence is insufficient to conclude a meaningful advance over existing decision-theoretic crowd-control methods.

Questions for the authors
- How sensitive is performance to miscalibration of pθ and to the assumed pR=0.7? Can you learn pR (worker/item reliabilities) online and show improvements?
- What is the end-to-end decision time (planning plus communication) per example, and how does it scale with sequence length?
- Can you provide comparisons to TurKontrol-like POMDP baselines and to worker aggregation models (Dawid–Skene/GLAD)?
- How were wM and wT selected? Please include sensitivity curves and statistical tests.
- Do results change when running fully live on AMT (without precollected responses), especially under variable worker pools?

Suggestions to strengthen the paper
- Incorporate and evaluate learned worker/item reliability models; report calibration.
- Add strong baselines (POMDP controller, EM aggregation, active offline budget-matched learners).
- Provide ablations: MCTS vs greedy; with/without timing; parallel vs sequential issuing; impact of utility choice.
- Report computational overhead and live experiments; include significance/error bars and sensitivity to wM/wT and latency shifts.

Recommendation
Reject. The problem is interesting and the formulation is appealing, but the evaluation relies on oversimplified assumptions about workers and latency, simulated protocols that limit generality, and weak baselines. With stronger empirical validation, learned reliability modeling, and comprehensive ablations, this work could be competitive.

Confidence
High.
