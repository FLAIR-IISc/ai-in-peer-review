Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes an “on-the-job learning” framework (LENSE) for making high-quality predictions from the outset by interleaving model inference with real-time crowd queries. Each input triggers a stochastic game in which the system can: issue multiple label queries (possibly redundant), wait for asynchronous responses, or return a prediction. A Bayesian utility trades off expected accuracy (under the current posterior) against monetary query cost and latency. Exact planning is intractable, so the authors approximate with Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) and progressive widening to handle continuous response times. The prediction backbone is a CRF; crowd responses are modeled with a simple response model pR (fixed ~70% accuracy with uniform errors) and a latency model pT. Experiments on NER, sentiment, and face classification report sizable cost reductions vs. pure crowd voting and accuracy gains vs. a pure online-learning baseline. Code is released.

The work connects to (i) online learning (e.g., Cesa-Bianchi & Lugosi) but differs by acquiring information before committing predictions; (ii) active learning (Settles survey), but here labels are obtained at test-time to maintain accuracy and the model improves over time; (iii) active classification/value-of-information at test time (e.g., Gao & Koller, Greiner et al., Chai et al.)—LENSE is dynamic, reasons about structured outputs, and handles asynchronous costs; (iv) decision-theoretic control of crowds (e.g., TurKontrol by Dai, Mausam, Weld; Legion:AR by Lasecki et al.), but LENSE couples structured prediction with planning over label queries and continuous-time delays. The paper also relates to learning from crowds (e.g., Dawid–Skene; Raykar et al.; Whitehill et al.) though it does not model per-worker reliabilities.

Strengths
- Timely, practical problem formulation: maintaining high accuracy from the start via test-time information acquisition, with cost/latency trade-offs.
- Clear decision-theoretic formalization and utility that unifies accuracy, monetary cost, and time, leading to intuitive behaviors (redundancy for accuracy; parallelization for latency).
- Novel use of MCTS with progressive widening to plan over asynchronous, continuous-time responses.
- Structured prediction setting (CRF) where partial labels are informative across positions; the framework is general enough to apply beyond sequences.
- Empirical results on three tasks, with consistent cost reductions vs. naive crowd voting and accuracy improvements over a machine-only online baseline; open-source implementation and reproducibility materials.

Weaknesses and detailed comments

Quality
- Evaluation relies mostly on “frozen” pre-collected worker responses/delays (five per queried label) to simulate all methods. While this controls for worker variance, it constrains adaptivity (e.g., redundant queries beyond five, timing effects) and may not reflect live end-to-end behavior of a real-time system. Please include a live evaluation or a simulation that samples from parametric models learned from many workers, allowing variable redundancy and timing.
- The response model pR is fixed at 70% with uniform errors; no learning of worker reliabilities, class-dependent confusion, or item difficulty. This limits both the fidelity of planning and the ability to benefit from repeated interactions with workers. Incorporating a DS-style model (Dawid–Skene), or per-worker Bayesian reliability and updating pR online (cf. TurKontrol; Raykar et al.), would strengthen both modeling and results.
- The utility uses ExpAcc under the model pθ as a surrogate for true accuracy. This is sensible in a Bayesian framework but hinges on calibration. There is no calibration analysis. Please report calibration (e.g., ECE/Brier) and show sensitivity of policies to miscalibration; consider temperature scaling or Bayesian CRFs to improve calibration.
- Missing baselines: A myopic value-of-information policy (greedy one-step VOI), rollout with limited lookahead, or a POMDP solver on a simplified abstraction would clarify the incremental benefit of MCTS/progressive widening. Also compare to adaptive submodularity-style greedy querying (Golovin & Krause) where applicable.
- Computational overhead: The paper lacks runtime/latency overhead of planning itself, search depth, UCT constants, number of simulations, and how these interact with human response times. Provide these to assess deployability.
- Learning details are underspecified: How are CRF parameters updated online with noisy responses (loss, weighting, handling conflicting labels)? Are multiple responses aggregated probabilistically or as hard labels? Clarify training objective and regularization.

Clarity
- Overall well-written and organized. However, add a precise description of the posterior p(y|x, q, r, t) computation and how pR enters inference; specify the latency model pT and how it is fit. Clarify the constraint prohibiting issuing new queries during waiting, and quantify its impact in ablations (sequential vs. parallel/overlapped querying).

Originality
- The integration of decision-theoretic planning with structured prediction and asynchronous crowd responses is interesting. MCTS for test-time feature/label acquisition is not entirely novel, but applying progressive widening to continuous response times and coupling to a learning model is a useful contribution. The response/noise modeling is relatively standard and could be strengthened.

Significance
- Results on NER are compelling; sentiment and faces show smaller gains. Broader significance would be clearer with stronger baselines, live evaluations, and more realistic crowd models. The framework is promising for deployable ML systems where immediate performance is needed.

Pros for acceptance
- Addresses an important practical setting with a principled decision-theoretic approach.
- Novel planning instantiation (MCTS + progressive widening) for asynchronous, continuous-time crowds.
- Demonstrated cost/latency/accuracy trade-offs; open-source code.

Cons for acceptance
- Evaluation design (frozen responses) limits claims about real-time adaptivity and planning benefits.
- Simplistic and fixed crowd noise model; no learning of reliabilities or calibration analysis.
- Missing strong decision-theoretic baselines and ablations; computational overhead unreported.
- Some key modeling/training details are under-specified.

Suggestions to improve
- Learn and update pR and pT online (per-worker reliabilities, class confusion, and latency), and show gains.
- Add myopic VOI, rollout, and/or simplified POMDP baselines; ablate MCTS depth and progressive widening.
- Report planning runtime and sensitivity to wM, wT; include calibration analysis and improvements.
- Provide live end-to-end experiments with real crowd interactions and larger worker pools; allow >5 redundant queries.
- Clarify learning from noisy responses (objective, aggregation); explore more expressive base models and structured tasks.

Recommendation: Weak reject. The idea is appealing and practically relevant, and the formalization is sound, but the current empirical methodology and modeling choices make it difficult to assess the true benefits of the planning component and robustness in realistic settings. With stronger baselines, learned crowd models, calibration analysis, and live evaluations, this work could be a strong contribution.
