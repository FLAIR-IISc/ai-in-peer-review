

# Review of "On-the-Job Learning with Bayesian Decision Theory"

## Summary
This paper introduces "on-the-job learning," a framework that combines crowdsourcing with online learning to produce accurate predictions from the start without requiring a pre-trained model. The authors model the process as a stochastic game between the system and the crowd, using Bayesian decision theory to balance accuracy, latency, and cost. They propose an approximate solution using Monte Carlo Tree Search with progressive widening, and evaluate their approach on three tasks (NER, sentiment classification, image classification), reporting significant cost reductions and accuracy improvements over baselines.

## Strengths
The paper presents a novel framework that addresses a practical gap in AI deployment where labeled data is scarce. The formalization as a stochastic game with Bayesian decision theory is conceptually sound. The empirical results on NER show compelling improvements (88.4% F1 with >10x cost reduction), and the open-source implementation (LENS) is a positive contribution. The distinction from related work (online learning, active classification) is generally well-maintained.

## Weaknesses

### Methodological Inconsistencies
1. **Inconsistent notation**: The paper uses inconsistent indexing in the game state definition (e.g., "q = (q₁, ..., qₖ₋₁)" vs. "q' = (q₁, ..., qₖ)"). This makes the formalism difficult to follow and raises questions about the precision of the model.

2. **Ambiguous response model**: The paper states "pR(r | yq) = 0.7 iff r = yq ,2" - the ",2" is clearly a typo. More importantly, the authors claim "we found humans were roughly 70% accurate" but provide no statistical evidence (e.g., standard deviation, confidence intervals) to support this critical parameter. This undermines the credibility of the environment model.

3. **Utility function confusion**: Equation (1) defines U(σ) = ExpAcc(...) - (nQwM + tnowwT), but Equation (2) defines ExpAcc(p) = E_p(y)[Accuracy(argmax_y' p(y'))]. The notation is inconsistent (p(y) vs. p(y|...)), and it's unclear how the expectation over y is computed in practice given the model p(y|x,q,s,r,t).

### Technical Nuances
4. **Missing implementation details**: The paper claims to use "AdaGrad" to learn the CRF model but provides no hyperparameters, feature sets, or training details. Without this, it's impossible to assess whether the model is properly trained or whether the improvements are due to the framework or better training.

5. **Cost comparison ambiguity**: The paper states "more than an order of magnitude reduction in cost compared to full human annotation" but fails to specify what "full human annotation" means (e.g., 5 workers per example? 10 workers?). The threshold baseline (which makes all queries upfront) is described but not properly compared to the full LENSE system in the main results.

6. **Inconsistent terminology**: The system is called "LENS" in the implementation but "lense" in the title and "LENS" in the GitHub URL. The paper also uses "the crowd" and "crowd workers" interchangeably without clarifying if they're distinct concepts.

### Experimental Design Issues
7. **Missing tables and figures**: The paper references "Table 1," "Table 2," "Figure 2," and "Figure 3" multiple times but provides none of them. This is a critical omission for evaluating the claims.

8. **Worker quality control**: The paper states they "collected 5 worker responses and their delays on each label ahead of time" but doesn't explain how they selected these workers or why 5 is sufficient. The phrase "without replacement from this frozen pool" suggests they're not accounting for worker quality variation, which could bias results.

9. **Incomplete baseline comparison**: The paper claims "8% F1 improvement over having a single human label the whole set" but doesn't define what "single human label" means (one worker per example? one worker for all examples?). This ambiguity makes the claim difficult to interpret.

### Formatting and Clarity
10. **Typographical errors**: The utility function has a comma instead of colon ("U(σ) , ExpAcc(...)"), and there are inconsistent tense usage ("We pay workers" vs. "We paid workers").

11. **Citation issues**: Reference [14] is listed in the bibliography but never cited in the text, suggesting an oversight in the proofreading process.

12. **Inconsistent capitalization**: The system is referred to as "LENS" in the GitHub URL but "lense" in the title and implementation description.

## Conclusion
While the paper presents a theoretically interesting framework with promising empirical results on NER, the numerous methodological inconsistencies, technical ambiguities, and formatting errors significantly undermine the paper's credibility. The lack of detail in key components (response model, utility function, implementation) makes it difficult to assess whether the claimed improvements are due to the framework or other factors. The missing tables and figures further prevent proper evaluation of the results.

The paper makes a good conceptual contribution but fails to provide the necessary technical rigor and clarity to support its claims. The minor flaws I've identified are not merely superficial—they directly impact the ability to understand and reproduce the work. As a reviewer, I cannot recommend acceptance given the current state of the manuscript.

## Recommendation
Reject. The paper's methodological and technical inconsistencies outweigh its conceptual novelty and empirical results. The authors should address these issues before considering resubmission.
