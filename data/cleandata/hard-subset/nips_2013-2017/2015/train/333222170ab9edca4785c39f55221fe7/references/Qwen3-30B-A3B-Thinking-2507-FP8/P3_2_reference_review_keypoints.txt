- Paper introduces "on-the-job learning": a Bayesian decision-theoretic framework that queries crowds at prediction time and improves the model from responses.  
- Models the problem as a stochastic game optimizing accuracy, latency, and cost, and approximates the solution with MCTS plus progressive widening.  
- Empirical evaluation on NER, sentiment, and image classification shows strong results (e.g., 88.4% F1 for NER with >10× cost reduction versus human 5-vote).  
- Originality: well-motivated, distinct from online learning, active learning, and active classification; principled formulation is a meaningful contribution.  
- Experimental design: strong baselines and analyses (query patterns, cost-accuracy tradeoffs), open-source implementation enhances reproducibility.  
- Clarity: clear writing, precise math and algorithm description, logical flow.  
- Theoretical limitation: no guarantees or bounds on the quality of the MCTS approximation relative to the optimal policy.  
- Crowd model simplifications: assumes a fixed ~70% worker accuracy and does not model worker variability, skill, or fatigue.  
- Scalability concerns: lacks analysis of MCTS runtime and scalability to longer inputs or more complex models (e.g., sentence length, image complexity).  
- Comparison gap: related work discussion and baselines do not sufficiently engage recent active learning advances with deep learning.  
- Overall assessment: strong, practical, and novel work with compelling empirical results; recommended for acceptance despite theoretical and modeling gaps.
