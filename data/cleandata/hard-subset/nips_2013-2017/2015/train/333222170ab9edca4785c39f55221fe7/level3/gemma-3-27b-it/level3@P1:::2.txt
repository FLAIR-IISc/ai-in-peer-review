This paper introduces a framework, On-the-Job Learning, for building high-accuracy systems without requiring pre-labeled training data. The core idea is to leverage real-time crowdsourcing to resolve uncertainty during prediction, gradually reducing reliance on human input as the model improves. The problem is formalized as a stochastic game based on Bayesian decision theory, balancing latency, cost, and accuracy, and solved via a Monte Carlo Tree Search approximation. The authors demonstrate significant cost reductions on Named Entity Recognition, Sentiment Classification and Image Classification tasks, outperforming several baselines.

The work builds upon previous research in both online learning and active learning, but distinguishes itself by operating directly during test time and actively seeking information *before* making predictions, unlike traditional online learning. Compared to active learning, the proposed framework dynamically adapts its querying policy based on observed data rather than relying on a static pre-defined strategy. The authors appropriately reference seminal work in these areas [7, 8, 11]. The paper also connects to recent efforts in crowdsourcing [3, 4, 25, 26], emphasizing the need to handle noisy and variable response times, a crucial aspect of their approach.  Furthermore, the authors position their work within the context of structured prediction problems, acknowledging relevant work on measurements and distant supervision [27, 28].

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty and Significance:** The on-the-job learning paradigm is a compelling approach to address a practical challenge: building accurate systems with limited initial resources. The framework's ability to dynamically balance cost, latency and accuracy is a significant contribution.
*   **Strong Empirical Results:** The results across three datasets demonstrate substantial cost reductions and accuracy improvements compared to both full human annotation and standard online learning approaches. The 8% F1 improvement over a single human label and 28% improvement over online learning on NER are particularly impressive.
*   **Technical Soundness:** The formulation of the problem as a stochastic game and the application of Monte Carlo Tree Search provide a principled and theoretically grounded solution. The inclusion of response time modeling and a utility function that explicitly accounts for cost, latency, and accuracy strengthens the technical contribution.
*   **Availability of Code:** Providing an open-source implementation (LENSE) is a commendable practice that promotes reproducibility and facilitates further research.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Clarity & Explanations:**  While the core idea is well-motivated, some explanations are somewhat opaque. For example, the illustration at line 154 lacks concrete details. A specific example demonstrating a query, realistic crowd responses, and corresponding timings would greatly enhance understanding.
*   **Inconsistencies:**  The inconsistent labeling of the baseline method as “entropic” versus “threshold” in Tables 2 and 3 is confusing and should be standardized.
*   **Organization of Related Work:** Positioning related work in Section 5 feels somewhat disjointed and weakens the narrative flow. A more integrated discussion, woven throughout the paper when relevant, would be more effective.
*   **Minor Errors:** Line 86 contains a broken figure reference ("Figure ??").
*   **Comparison to Stronger Baselines:** The comparison to a 3-vote or 5-vote crowd consensus is useful, but it would be valuable to compare LENSE against more sophisticated aggregation methods for noisy labels, like those considering worker reliability [26]. The fact that LENSE underperforms 5-vote, while highlighting a tradeoff, warrants further investigation.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper tackles a challenging and practically relevant problem.
*   The proposed approach is novel and theoretically well-founded.
*   The empirical results are strong and demonstrate significant improvements over relevant baselines.
*   The code availability promotes reproducibility and future research.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

*   The clarity of explanations could be improved, potentially hindering understanding and reproducibility.
*   The organization of related work is suboptimal.
*   The inconsistency in baseline labeling detracts from the presentation.



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite some minor weaknesses in clarity and organization, this is a strong paper that presents a valuable contribution to the field of machine learning. The on-the-job learning framework is a promising approach for building practical AI systems, and the technical details are well-developed and supported by compelling experimental results. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions to address the clarity and consistency issues. Specifically, I suggest providing a concrete example to illustrate the query process, standardizing the baseline labels, and reorganizing the related work section for a more coherent presentation.
