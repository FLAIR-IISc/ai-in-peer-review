

# Review of "On-the-Job Learning with Bayesian Decision Theory"

This paper proposes "on-the-job learning," a framework for deploying high-accuracy AI systems starting with zero training examples by strategically using crowdsourcing to resolve uncertainty during inference. The authors model the problem as a stochastic game between the system (which issues queries to the crowd) and the crowd (which provides noisy responses), using Bayesian decision theory to balance accuracy, latency, and cost. The system gradually reduces its reliance on crowdsourcing as it learns from the data it processes. The authors develop an approximation algorithm using Monte Carlo Tree Search with progressive widening to handle the continuous-time nature of the problem. They evaluate their approach on three tasks (NER, sentiment classification, image classification) against baselines including majority voting (1, 3, 5 votes), online learning, and a threshold-based baseline.

The work builds on and extends prior work in online learning, active learning, and crowdsourcing. It distinguishes itself from online learning by maintaining high accuracy from the beginning (rather than asymptotically improving), and from active learning by not requiring a pre-labeled dataset to learn a static query policy. It also differs from Legion:AR (their prior work) by providing a formal machine learning foundation for on-the-job learning.

**Quality**: The paper presents a technically sound approach with well-motivated theoretical foundations. The experimental evaluation is comprehensive across three distinct tasks, with appropriate baselines. The authors carefully consider tradeoffs between accuracy, latency, and cost. However, the continuous-time extension appears to provide limited practical benefit compared to the simpler threshold baseline, which performs nearly as well (Table 2). The claim that their system "boosts performance relative to the expert provided labels" is misleading for the NER task, as the 5-vote majority baseline (which uses expert labels) outperforms their method (88.4% vs. 87.1% F1). The technical description of the continuous-time model (Section 3) is complex and poorly motivated, making the approach difficult to understand and adopt.

**Clarity**: The paper suffers from significant clarity issues. The introduction of continuous time in Section 3 is abrupt and poorly explained, creating unnecessary confusion. The notation is inconsistent (e.g., equation (5) uses q for a distribution function), and key details about the threshold baseline's implementation are unclear. The paper claims to achieve "more than an order of magnitude reduction in cost" but fails to report the exact cost values for comparison. The threshold baseline is missing from Table 3 and Figure 4 for sentiment classification, making the comparison incomplete. The paper would benefit from presenting the core method without continuous time first, treating the time aspect as an extension.

**Originality**: The core idea of on-the-job learning is novel, as it provides a principled way to combine crowdsourcing with online learning during inference. The stochastic game formulation is a fresh perspective on the problem. The paper adequately cites related work, including Legion:AR (which it extends), active classification, and online learning. The approach is sufficiently different from previous NIPS papers.

**Significance**: The problem addressed is important for real-world deployment scenarios where labeled data is scarce. The results demonstrate significant cost reductions (particularly on NER) while maintaining high accuracy. The framework could be valuable for practitioners needing to deploy systems quickly without large labeled datasets. However, the limited marginal improvement over the threshold baseline (which is simpler to implement) somewhat reduces the significance, especially since the continuous-time extension doesn't clearly justify its added complexity.

**Pro-acceptance arguments**: 
- Addresses an important real-world problem (deploying AI systems without initial labeled data)
- Well-motivated theoretical framework with Bayesian decision theory
- Comprehensive experimental evaluation across three diverse tasks
- Demonstrates significant cost reduction (order of magnitude on NER)
- Provides open-source implementation (LENSE)

**Con-acceptance arguments**: 
- Misleading claims about performance relative to human labels
- Continuous-time extension appears unnecessarily complex with limited benefit
- Significant clarity issues that hinder understanding and adoption
- Missing experimental data (threshold baseline for sentiment classification)
- Overstates the improvement over the threshold baseline

This paper presents an interesting approach to a relevant problem, but the current presentation lacks clarity and the continuous-time extension doesn't provide sufficient justification for its complexity. I recommend major revisions to clarify the core method, remove the continuous-time extension (or better justify it), and correct the misleading performance claims. The work has potential but requires significant improvement before acceptance. With these revisions, it would be a strong contribution to the field.
