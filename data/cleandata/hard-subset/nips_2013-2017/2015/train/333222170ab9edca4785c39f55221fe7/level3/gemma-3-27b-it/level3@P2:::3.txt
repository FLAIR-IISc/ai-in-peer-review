This paper introduces a compelling framework for on-the-job learning, aiming to deploy high-accuracy systems without requiring extensive pre-training data. The core idea is to strategically leverage crowdsourcing to resolve uncertainty, gradually reducing reliance on human input as the model improves. This is formalized as a stochastic game based on Bayesian decision theory, and an approximation algorithm utilizing Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) is developed to handle the intractable optimal policy computation. The experimental evaluation on three tasks – named entity recognition, sentiment classification, and image classification – demonstrates promising results, achieving significant cost reductions and performance improvements compared to several baselines.

The paper builds upon a growing body of work in online learning, active learning, and crowdsourcing, notably extending ideas from Legion:AR [6]. It distinguishes itself from traditional active learning by operating in a purely online setting without a separate training phase, and from existing active classification approaches by dynamically improving the model during test time. The formulation as a stochastic game offers a principled way to balance latency, cost, and accuracy, addressing a practical challenge in many real-world applications. The combination of Bayesian decision theory and MCTS is a novel and technically sound approach to tackle the inherent complexity of the problem.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The framework of on-the-job learning, formalizing the interplay between a learning model and real-time crowdsourcing as a stochastic game, is a significant contribution.
* **Technical Soundness:** The use of Bayesian decision theory and MCTS provides a solid theoretical foundation and a practical approximation algorithm. The authors demonstrate a careful consideration of the trade-offs involved in balancing latency, cost, and accuracy.
* **Significance:** The proposed approach has the potential to enable the rapid deployment of AI systems in resource-constrained scenarios, such as disaster relief or monitoring public opinion, where immediate action is crucial. The demonstrated cost savings and performance improvements are substantial.
* **Clarity (Overall):** The core concepts are reasonably well-explained, and the problem formulation is clearly defined. The implementation details and experimental setup are also well-documented.
* **Reproducibility:** The authors provide a publicly available implementation (LENSE) and code on CodaLab, facilitating reproducibility and further research.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Clarity (Continuous Time):** The introduction of continuous time in Section 3 significantly complicates the exposition. While the motivation is understandable, the added complexity detracts from the clarity of the core method. The benefit of this extension does not appear to justify its complexity, as the threshold baseline performs comparably.
* **Experimental Reporting Errors/Omissions:** The claim of outperforming human performance is inaccurate for the 5-vote majority baseline in NER. Furthermore, the 5-vote results are missing for Face-ID and Sentiment in several tables/figures, hindering a comprehensive comparison.
* **Presentation/Formatting:** The presentation could be improved by consistently bolding best results and relevant latency values. The threshold baseline is inexplicably absent from the sentiment experiments in Table 3 and Figure 4.
* **Minor Technical Issues:** Equation (2) should include 's' in the distribution p(y,r,t|x,q,s). Also, the use of 'q' as a distribution function in equation (5) is confusing and should be replaced with a different variable. There appears to be a potential typo in the description of the threshold baseline, where the inequality condition seems incorrect.
* **t_deadline and Reporting Criteria:** The paper lacks clarity regarding the experimental use of the *t_deadline* parameter and the criteria used for reporting the final answers (entropic threshold vs. time limit).


**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and technically sound approach to on-the-job learning with significant potential impact.
* The experimental results demonstrate substantial cost savings and performance improvements compared to relevant baselines.
* The authors provide a publicly available implementation, fostering reproducibility and further research.
* The stochastic game formulation provides a principled framework for balancing competing objectives.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The continuous-time extension adds significant complexity with limited experimental benefit.
* There are several reporting errors and omissions in the experimental results.
* The presentation and formatting could be improved for better clarity and readability.



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite some clarity issues and reporting errors, this paper presents a strong contribution to the field of active learning and human-in-the-loop systems. The core idea of on-the-job learning, formalized as a stochastic game, is novel and technically sound. The experimental results are promising, demonstrating the potential for significant cost savings and performance improvements. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions to address the clarity issues, correct the reporting errors, and improve the presentation. Specifically, I suggest the authors consider presenting the core method without the continuous-time extension initially and treating the latter as a separate extension. Addressing the missing experimental data and clarifying the experimental setup (especially regarding *t_deadline*) are also crucial.
