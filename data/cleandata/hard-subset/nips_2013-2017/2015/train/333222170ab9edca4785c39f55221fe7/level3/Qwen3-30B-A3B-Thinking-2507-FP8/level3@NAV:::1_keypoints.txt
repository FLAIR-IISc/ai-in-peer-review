- Proposes "on-the-job learning": start with zero training data, use real-time crowdsourcing to resolve uncertainty, and transition to self-prediction.  
- Formulates the problem as a stochastic game using Bayesian decision theory, extending prior work with a more principled approach.  
- Uses Monte Carlo Tree Search to approximate an optimal query policy that trades off latency, cost, and accuracy.  
- Strength: elegant, theoretically grounded formulation of the crowd→model transition.  
- Implementation (LENS) shows promising NER results: over an order-of-magnitude cost reduction versus full human annotation and performance gains over expert labels.  
- Framework can maintain high accuracy at deployment while gradually reducing reliance on crowd queries.  
- Empirical evaluation is underwhelming and comparisons (e.g., threshold-based heuristic) lack justification—the source of thresholds 0.3 and 0.88 is unclear.  
- Presentation omits critical technical details: Eq. 2 should condition on s and does not explain why y disappears from p_R(r_i | y, q_i).  
- Algorithm 1 is incomplete: the term N(s) is not defined.  
- Analysis fails to determine whether improvements arise from human annotation or improved ML models—lacks error analysis and example cases.  
- Needs more rigorous baselines, clearer justification of design choices, and deeper analysis of the crowd→model transition.  
- Contribution is original and theoretically meaningful but does not substantially advance empirical state-of-the-art; merits further investigation.
