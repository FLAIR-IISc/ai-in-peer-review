This paper introduces a novel on-the-job learning framework for deploying high-accuracy systems without requiring substantial pre-labeled data. The core idea is to strategically leverage real-time crowdsourcing to resolve uncertainty, gradually reducing reliance on human input as the model learns. The authors formulate this as a stochastic game based on Bayesian decision theory, balancing latency, cost, and accuracy, and employ Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) to approximate the optimal policy. The paper presents promising results on named entity recognition, sentiment classification, and image classification, demonstrating significant cost reductions compared to full annotation and performance improvements over online learning and simpler baselines.

This work builds upon existing approaches in online learning [7] and active learning [8, 9, 10, 11]. However, it distinguishes itself by operating in a truly online setting where predictions are made continuously, and by explicitly modeling the trade-offs between latency, cost, and accuracy using a game-theoretic framework.  Prior work on active learning typically focuses on selecting the most informative examples for labeling *before* making a prediction, whereas this approach actively seeks information *during* prediction.  Related work in crowdsourcing [3, 4, 25, 26] often focuses on aggregating multiple labels for a single instance, while this work dynamically queries the crowd only when necessary.  The authors also acknowledge Legion:AR [6] as a related work, but highlight their more comprehensive investigation of the underlying machine learning foundations.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The formulation of on-the-job learning as a stochastic game, coupled with the use of MCTS for policy approximation, is a novel and potentially impactful contribution.
*   **Practicality:** The proposed framework addresses a real-world challenge – deploying accurate systems with limited initial resources – and demonstrates significant cost savings in the experiments.
*   **Strong Empirical Results:** The experimental results on three diverse datasets consistently show improvements over strong baselines, particularly in terms of cost reduction. The 8% F1 improvement over a single human label and 28% improvement over online learning on NER are noteworthy.
*   **Clear Motivation and Problem Definition:** The introduction clearly articulates the problem and motivates the need for a new approach.
*   **Open-Source Implementation:**  The availability of LENSE on GitHub promotes reproducibility and encourages further research.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Clarity:** The paper suffers from clarity issues, particularly concerning the introduction of continuous time.  The interplay between query send times (s) and response times (t), and its impact on the optimization process, is difficult to follow. This significantly complicates the exposition and algorithmic details, potentially hindering adoption.
*   **Limited Benefit of Continuous Time:** While technically sophisticated, the continuous-time extension doesn't appear to yield substantial experimental advantages over the simpler threshold baseline. The added complexity may not be fully justified.
*   **Experimental Errors and Omissions:**  The claim of "beating human performance" is inaccurate for the 5-vote majority baseline on NER. Additionally, 5-vote results are missing for Face-ID and Sentiment in certain tables/figures. Presentation inconsistencies, such as missing bolding of best results and a missing threshold baseline in Table 3 and Figure 4, further detract from the clarity of the results.
*   **Notational Issues:**  Equation (2) should explicitly include *s* in the distribution p(y,r,t|x,q,s). Furthermore, the use of *q* as a distribution function in equation (5) is confusing and should be changed.
*   **Potential Typo:** The inequality in the description of the threshold baseline seems suspect and requires verification.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and technically sound approach to a challenging problem.
*   The experimental results are generally strong and demonstrate the practical benefits of the proposed framework.
*   The open-source implementation will facilitate further research and adoption.
*   The work addresses a significant need in the machine learning community.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The clarity issues, particularly surrounding the continuous-time extension, may make the paper difficult for some readers to fully understand.
*   The experimental inconsistencies and omissions raise concerns about the thoroughness of the evaluation.
*   The limited experimental benefit of the continuous-time extension weakens the justification for its added complexity.



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite the clarity and presentation issues, this paper presents a significant contribution to the field of online learning and active learning. The proposed framework is novel, well-motivated, and supported by promising empirical results.  I recommend **acceptance** with the caveat that the authors address the identified clarity issues and correct the experimental inconsistencies. Specifically, I recommend the authors consider presenting the core method *without* the continuous-time extension first, and framing the extension as a separate contribution to improve clarity. Addressing the notational issues and typo is also critical. Revising the experiments to accurately reflect the results (e.g., including the 5-vote baseline consistently and correcting the statement about outperforming humans) will further strengthen the paper.
