## Review of “On-the-Job Learning with Bayesian Decision Theory”

This paper presents a novel framework for on-the-job learning, casting it as a stochastic game and employing Bayesian decision theory to balance cost, latency, and accuracy when querying a crowd for labels. The core idea of proactively seeking information *during* prediction, rather than relying solely on pre-trained models or static active learning policies, is intriguing and addresses a practically relevant scenario – deploying systems with limited initial labeled data. The authors demonstrate promising results on three tasks (NER, sentiment classification, and image classification), showcasing significant cost reductions compared to full annotation and improvements over online learning.

However, the application to face recognition remains unclear. The paper states improvement but doesn't specify whether the entire instance (image) was queried, or only specific regions, and how this querying strategy impacts performance. Furthermore, the claimed strength of the approach – adaptability in non-structured settings – appears undermined by the fact that the proposed utility function doesn’t inherently propagate information *across* examples. The model learns with each instance, but doesn't leverage previous query resolutions to inform subsequent queries on different inputs. This limits its effectiveness in truly dynamic environments.

The threshold baseline utilizes two parameters (0.3 and 0.98) seemingly chosen arbitrarily. The origin of these values, and importantly, a sensitivity analysis demonstrating their impact on results, are absent. A critical omission is the lack of comparison to an uncertainty-sampling baseline, a standard technique in active learning. This makes it difficult to assess whether the gains achieved are due to the sophisticated Bayesian game-theoretic framework, or simply a smart choice of query strategy. 

The distinction from active online learning is also weak. The authors argue their approach improves the model during test time; however, active learning *could* be adapted to this scenario by treating the input sequence as the active set and updating the model with each response, effectively mimicking the on-the-job learning process.

Overall, while the framework demonstrates novelty and potential, the lack of clarity in certain experimental details, limited comparison to established baselines, and questions regarding the generalizability of the utility function raise concerns. 

**Arguments for acceptance:**

* Novel approach to combining Bayesian decision theory with on-the-job learning.
* Promising empirical results, particularly in cost reduction for NER.
* Clear presentation and available code.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

* Unclear application to face recognition.
* Limited generalizability due to lack of inter-example information propagation.
* Arbitrary parameters in baseline without sensitivity analysis.
* Missing uncertainty-sampling baseline.
* Weak distinction from adaptable active online learning techniques.



I recommend a **weak reject** with encouragement to address the points raised, particularly the inclusion of a more thorough baseline comparison and clarification regarding the applicability and limitations of the proposed framework.
