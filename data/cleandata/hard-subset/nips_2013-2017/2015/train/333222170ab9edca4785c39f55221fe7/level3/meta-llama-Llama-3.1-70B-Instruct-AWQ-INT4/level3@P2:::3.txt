This paper proposes a novel framework for on-the-job learning, which combines the benefits of crowdsourcing and online learning to achieve high accuracy with minimal cost. The authors model the on-the-job learning setting as a stochastic game based on Bayesian decision theory, which allows them to balance latency, cost, and accuracy objectives in a principled way. The paper provides a thorough description of the problem formulation, model, and algorithm, and presents experimental results on three tasks: named-entity recognition, sentiment classification, and image classification.

The main ideas of the paper are well-motivated and clearly related to previous work at NIPS and elsewhere. The authors provide a comprehensive review of related work in online learning, active learning, active classification, crowdsourcing, and structured prediction. The paper's contributions are significant, as it introduces a new framework that learns from noisy crowds on-the-job to maintain high accuracy and reduce cost over time.

The strengths of the paper include its originality, significance, and potential impact on the field. The authors provide a thorough evaluation of their approach on three tasks, and the results show that their method outperforms most baselines. The paper also provides a detailed description of the algorithm and its components, which makes it easy to follow and understand.

However, there are some weaknesses and areas for improvement. The paper's clarity is sometimes compromised by the introduction of complex concepts and notation. For example, the mid-Section-3 introduction of continuous time complicates the exposition and algorithmic details. Additionally, the continuous-time extension yields limited experimental benefit, which may not justify its added complexity. The reviewer recommends presenting the core method without continuous time first and treating continuous time as an extension to improve clarity and adoption.

There are also some minor errors and omissions in the paper. For example, the claim of "beats human performance" is incorrect for 5-vote NER, and 5-vote results are missing for Face-ID and Sentiment in several tables and figures. Additionally, the presentation and formatting of the paper could be improved, with best results and certain latency values not bolded, and the threshold baseline missing from the sentiment experiments in Table 3 and Figure 4.

In terms of the review criteria, the paper scores high on originality, significance, and impact. The authors provide a novel framework that combines the benefits of crowdsourcing and online learning, and the results show that their method outperforms most baselines. However, the paper could be improved in terms of clarity, with a more gradual introduction of complex concepts and notation. The reviewer recommends acceptance, but with revisions to address the minor errors and omissions, and to improve the presentation and formatting of the paper.

Arguments for acceptance:

* The paper proposes a novel framework that combines the benefits of crowdsourcing and online learning.
* The authors provide a thorough evaluation of their approach on three tasks, and the results show that their method outperforms most baselines.
* The paper is original, significant, and likely to spur discussion and adoption.

* The continuous-time extension yields limited experimental benefit, which may not justify its added complexity.

Recommendations for improvement:

* Address the minor errors and omissions in the paper, such as the incorrect claim of "beats human performance" and missing 5-vote results.
* Improve the presentation and formatting of the paper, with best results and certain latency values bolded, and the threshold baseline included in the sentiment experiments in Table 3 and Figure 4.
