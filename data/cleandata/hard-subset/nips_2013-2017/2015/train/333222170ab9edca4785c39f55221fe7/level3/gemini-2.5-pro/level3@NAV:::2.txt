This paper introduces "on-the-job learning," a framework for deploying high-accuracy classifiers with zero initial labeled data. The core idea is to use real-time crowdsourcing to resolve uncertainty for incoming instances, balancing accuracy, latency, and cost in a principled way. The authors model this setting as a stochastic game based on Bayesian decision theory, developing an approximate policy using Monte Carlo Tree Search. This work builds upon ideas from online active learning and active classification but distinguishes itself by actively seeking information *prior* to making a prediction to maintain high accuracy from the outset, a notable departure from traditional approaches that achieve high performance only asymptotically.

The paper's primary strength is its novel and significant problem formulation. The LENSE system addresses the practical challenge of cold-starting a classifier by intelligently managing noisy, slow, and expensive human experts. The integration of latency and cost optimization into the learning framework is a compelling contribution. The experimental results are strong, demonstrating an order of magnitude cost reduction on the NER task compared to a 5-vote human baseline, while simultaneously boosting performance. The comparison of classifier accuracy against multiple human-vote baselines provides a thorough evaluation.

However, the paper's main weakness is a lack of clarity that makes the contribution somewhat opaque. The behavioral example in Section 3 (around Line 154) is too abstract; a concrete example with a specific text query, realistic crowd responses, and timings would significantly improve comprehension. There are also several minor but distracting inconsistencies. For example, the threshold baseline is labeled "entropic" in Table 2 but "threshold" in Table 3, causing confusion. The paper also contains missing references (e.g., "Figure ??" on Line 086), which should be corrected. Furthermore, placing the Related Work section at the end of the paper (Section 6) feels like an afterthought and hinders the reader’s ability to contextualize the work early on.

In summary, the paper presents a compelling approach to a significant real-world problem. The technical framework is sound and the results are promising, particularly the novel handling of latency trade-offs. While the LENSE system underperforms a 5-vote human baseline in some cases, this highlights an interesting trade-off between the model's sophisticated de-noising and simply acquiring more human consensus. The paper's impact is currently limited by its opaque explanations. I would recommend acceptance, contingent on major revisions to improve clarity, particularly through more concrete examples and better manuscript organization.
