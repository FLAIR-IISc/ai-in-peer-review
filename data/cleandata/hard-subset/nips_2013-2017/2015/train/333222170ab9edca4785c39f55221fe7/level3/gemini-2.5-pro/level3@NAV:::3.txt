This paper introduces "on-the-job learning," a framework for deploying a high-accuracy system from scratch by intelligently combining real-time crowdsourcing with online model training. The authors formalize the problem as a stochastic game where the system and the crowd are players. Using Bayesian decision theory, the system develops a policy to query the crowd, balancing accuracy, cost, and latency. A key contribution is the incorporation of continuous time for query and response events, which is optimized within the utility function. As the exact policy is intractable, the authors propose an approximation using Monte Carlo Tree Search. This work builds upon and formalizes ideas from active learning, online learning, and real-time crowdsourcing, offering a unified, principled approach to a practical problem.

**Strengths (Originality & Significance):**
The problem formulation is novel, timely, and significant. The concept of a system that learns "on the job," initially relying on a human "crutch" and becoming more autonomous, addresses a critical need in deploying ML systems rapidly. Framing this within a principled decision-theoretic framework is elegant and is likely to inspire future work in hybrid human-AI systems. The work is a solid contribution that should spur discussion at the conference.

**Weaknesses (Clarity & Quality):**
The paper suffers from significant clarity issues that hinder its impact. The core ideas are obscured by a complex presentation, particularly the introduction of continuous time in Section 3, which complicates the game formulation and the MCTS algorithm, making the method difficult to follow.

Furthermore, the experimental results do not fully justify the model's complexity. The full model (LENSE) offers limited improvement over a much simpler "threshold" baseline that ignores temporal reasoning, and it does not outperform a 5-vote majority baseline on NER. This raises questions about the practical utility of the complex continuous-time optimization. The paper also contains several reporting errors that undermine its quality, including an unsubstantiated claim of "beating human performance," missing 5-vote and threshold baseline results in several tables/figures, formatting inconsistencies (e.g., lack of bolding), and notational errors (e.g., in Equation 2 and the threshold baseline description).

**Recommendation for Authors:**
I recommend acceptance, as the core idea is strong. However, a major revision is necessary. I strongly suggest restructuring the paper to first present the model *without* continuous time, which would clarify the core contribution. The continuous-time aspect can then be introduced as an extension. Please also meticulously correct the experimental reporting omissions, notational errors, and formatting inconsistencies to improve the paper's quality and reproducibility.
