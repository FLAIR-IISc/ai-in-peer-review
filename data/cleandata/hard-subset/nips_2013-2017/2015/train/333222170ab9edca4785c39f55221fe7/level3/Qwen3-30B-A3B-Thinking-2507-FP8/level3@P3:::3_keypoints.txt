- Proposes an on-the-job learning framework that combines crowdsourcing and machine learning to achieve high accuracy without large labeled datasets.  
- Models the interaction as a stochastic game between the system and the crowd, using Bayesian decision theory to optimize a utility trading off accuracy, latency, and cost.  
- Core methodological novelty: Monte Carlo Tree Search with progressive widening to approximate optimal query strategies in continuous time.  
- Contributions: a principled approach that reduces crowd dependency over time, a continuous-time latency model, and empirical validation across NER, sentiment, and face-ID tasks.  
- Empirical wins: LENSE achieves large cost reductions on NER (order-of-magnitude) and improves performance relative to expert labels; implementation is open-sourced.  
- Positions and extends prior work in active learning, online learning, and crowdsourcing, and is distinguished from Legion:AR by a theoretical foundation.  
- Strengths: clear problem formulation, elegant Bayesian/game-theoretic integration, and thorough experimental evaluation.  
- Major clarity issues, notably the confusing continuous-time exposition in Section 3 that obscures algorithmic details.  
- Continuous-time extension appears to yield limited empirical benefit; threshold baseline often performs nearly as well or with lower latency.  
- Reporting errors undermine claims: incorrect “beating human performance” for 5-vote NER and missing 5-vote results for Face-ID and Sentiment.  
- Experimental omissions: threshold baseline is missing from sentiment experiments in key tables/figures.  
- Notation and equation inconsistencies (e.g., equation (2) missing s in p(y,r,t|x,q,s); equation (5) misuses q).  
- Recommendation: accept with major revisions—improve clarity, present core method without continuous time first, treat continuous time as an extension, and correct reporting/notation errors.  
- Pros summarized: novel stochastic-game formulation, strong empirical results, open-source code, and practical significance for real-world deployment.  
- Cons summarized: major clarity and reporting issues, limited demonstrated benefit from continuous-time complexity, and inconsistent notation/algorithmic details.
