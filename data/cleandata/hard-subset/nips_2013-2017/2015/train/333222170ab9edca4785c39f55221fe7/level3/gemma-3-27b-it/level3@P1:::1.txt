This paper introduces a framework for “on-the-job learning,” where a system learns to perform a task without initial training data by strategically querying a crowd for assistance and gradually becoming self-sufficient. This builds upon prior work by the authors [6] and casts the problem as a stochastic game using Bayesian decision theory, balancing cost, latency, and accuracy. The intractable optimal policy is approximated using Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) with progressive widening. The approach is evaluated on named-entity recognition, sentiment classification, and image classification, demonstrating significant cost reductions compared to full human annotation and performance improvements over online learning and a single-human labeling baseline.

The core idea of learning from the crowd while simultaneously performing the task is a compelling one, especially in scenarios with limited initial resources. This work builds on a growing body of research in online learning [7] and active learning [8, 9, 10], but distinguishes itself by focusing on continuous learning *during* test time, unlike traditional active learning which aims to improve a model before deployment. The formulation as a stochastic game and the use of Bayesian decision theory provides a principled framework for trading off different objectives. While Legion:AR [6] explored a similar setting, this paper dives deeper into the theoretical foundations and proposes a more sophisticated solution using MCTS. The comparison to related work is generally thorough, covering areas like crowdsourcing [3, 4], structured prediction [14, 28], and cost-sensitive learning [23, 24].

The strengths of this paper lie in its principled approach and the potential for practical impact. The experimental results, particularly on the NER task, are impressive, demonstrating a substantial reduction in cost while achieving competitive performance. The availability of an open-source implementation (LENSE) is a significant benefit for reproducibility and further research. The authors also acknowledge the limitations of their approach and clearly articulate the trade-offs involved. The explicit modeling of crowd response times and the ability to reason about latency are valuable contributions.

However, the paper also has several weaknesses. While the high-level idea is clear, some technical details are missing, hindering a complete understanding of the implementation. Specifically, the conditioning in Equation 2 is unclear – why does the expectation over accuracy not depend on the state *s*?  The definition of N(σ) in Algorithm 1 should be clarified. The justification for the threshold values (0.3 and 0.88) used in the threshold baseline is absent and requires explanation. The comparison to the threshold baseline seems somewhat underwhelming, and the authors should better highlight the advantages of their MCTS-based approach over this simpler heuristic.  

Furthermore, the authors claim performance improvements over "online learning" but do not fully characterize the online learning baseline. It is unclear if this baseline represents the best possible online machine learning approach for each task. A more in-depth error analysis, including illustrative examples of cases where LENSE outperforms the online baseline, would strengthen the claims. Given the reliance on crowd-sourced annotations, it would also be beneficial to investigate the impact of worker quality and potential biases in the results. The discussion on worker accuracy (70%) feels somewhat tacked on at the end and could be integrated more effectively into the experimental analysis.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel and principled framework for on-the-job learning.
*   Impressive experimental results, especially on the NER task, demonstrating significant cost savings.
*   Open-source implementation facilitates reproducibility and further research.
*   Clear articulation of the problem and its potential applications.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Lack of technical detail in certain areas (Equation 2, Algorithm 1).
*   Unclear justification for baseline parameters.
*   Insufficient analysis of the online learning baseline and error cases.
*   Limited discussion of worker quality and potential biases.


**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of active learning and human-computer interaction. The theoretical formulation of on-the-job learning is insightful, and the experimental results demonstrate the practical potential of the approach. While there are some areas for improvement, the strengths of the paper outweigh its weaknesses. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the technical clarifications and provide a more thorough analysis of the experimental results. The authors should focus on providing more detail regarding the missing components, justifying the baseline parameters, and providing a deeper understanding of the performance gains over alternative approaches.
