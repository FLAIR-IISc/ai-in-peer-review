This paper proposes “on-the-job learning,” a decision-theoretic framework that deploys a high-accuracy system with zero initial training data by interleaving real-time crowd queries with model inference and online updates. The problem is cast as a stochastic game between the system and the crowd. A utility trades off expected accuracy of the MAP prediction against monetary and latency costs, and the action policy is approximated via Monte Carlo Tree Search with progressive widening to handle continuous time. The prediction component is a CRF for structured outputs; worker responses are modeled with a fixed confusion model and exogenous response-time model. Experiments on NER, sentiment, and face classification (using a retainer on AMT) show large cost reductions vs. pure crowd labeling and accuracy gains vs. pure machine learning and a heuristic threshold baseline, with the strongest improvements on NER. The approach connects to online learning, active learning, active classification, and crowdsourcing control (e.g., TurKontrol), but emphasizes pre-prediction querying, asynchronous responses, and latency-aware decision-making.

Strengths
- Novel formulation: Casting pre-prediction crowd querying as a stochastic game and optimizing a utility over accuracy, latency, and monetary cost is elegant and principled. Using MCTS with progressive widening for continuous-time decision-making is technically interesting.
- Structured outputs: Integrating a CRF highlights how evidence can propagate within an instance, enabling adaptive, sequential queries that are more efficient than fixed, parallel querying.
- Real-time deployment angle: The retainer-based setup and explicit modeling of response times make the approach practical for settings requiring immediate high accuracy.
- Empirical gains: On NER, the method achieves an order-of-magnitude cost reduction relative to 5-vote crowdsourcing while improving F1, and outperforms both a 1-vote baseline and an online-learning baseline. The code release further enhances reproducibility.

Weaknesses and points needing clarification
- Applicability and clarity for image/face experiments: It is unclear what was actually queried. Was each query an image-level class label, attributes, or regions (e.g., inspired by attribute-based verification)? How (if at all) does structure enter the face task? Without explicit structure, within-instance evidence propagation is limited, which undercuts the main advantage of the framework.
- Utility scope and myopia: The utility optimizes per-instance expected accuracy minus time and cost, but does not value information for improving future instances. In unstructured settings (e.g., sentiment, faces), queries within an instance primarily reduce immediate uncertainty and have minimal within-instance propagation; thus the policy may be shortsighted about learning. This weakens the “learning on the job” claim. Consider explicitly accounting for the value of information across the stream (even approximately via a horizon-limited or batched lookahead).
- Crowd response model: Assuming a fixed 70% accuracy with uniform confusion is unrealistic; no per-worker or per-item difficulty modeling, and no calibration/ablation is reported. Methods like Dawid–Skene or the decision-theoretic control in TurKontrol could provide stronger baselines and better calibrated uncertainty. Sensitivity to pR and pT (and to calibration of pθ) is missing.
- Baselines: The “threshold” baseline uses seemingly arbitrary parameters (0.3 and 0.98) with no derivation or sensitivity analysis; results may hinge on these choices. A more compelling set of baselines is needed:
  - Uncertainty sampling (active learning) that sequentially queries the most uncertain token(s)/instances until a margin/entropy threshold or budget is reached before predicting.
  - An online active learning variant that allows a small pre-prediction budget per input (with or without latency modeling) to highlight the incremental benefit of the authors’ planning and continuous-time reasoning.
  - A simple greedy value-of-information heuristic that estimates expected accuracy gain per unit time/cost.
- Relation to active online learning: The distinction rests largely on pre-prediction queries and latency awareness. However, active learning can be adapted to the per-instance setting (treat each instance as an active pool, query before predicting, and update online). The paper should empirically isolate what the decision-theoretic game and continuous-time MCTS contribute over such baselines (e.g., ablate latency penalties, parallel vs. sequential querying, or replace MCTS with a greedy policy).
- Evaluation details: The practice of pre-collecting worker responses and simulating during evaluation controls variance but couples tightly to the simplistic response model. Please report runtime and decision latency per instance (overheads of MCTS), confidence intervals or statistical significance, and Pareto curves illustrating the accuracy–time–cost trade-offs. Also report sensitivity to wM and wT, and to progressive widening/UCT parameters.

Quality
- Technically sound overall, but the response/noise modeling is simplistic and some design choices (threshold baseline parameters, utility specification around MAP/ExpAcc) lack sensitivity analyses. The per-instance myopia relative to learning value is a conceptual limitation.

Clarity
- Generally well written, but the face task setup and querying granularity need clearer description. Add algorithmic details on MCTS hyperparameters and computational cost. Define how and when pθ is updated during an instance vs. across instances.

Originality
- The continuous-time, utility-optimized querying policy via MCTS is a novel combination. However, the conceptual gap to online active/active classification is narrower than suggested; the paper should better differentiate and validate the added value.

Significance
- Potentially impactful for rapid deployment in structured prediction with real-time crowds. The strongest evidence is on NER; applicability to unstructured classification appears limited without stronger modeling of cross-instance learning value and crowd noise.

Suggestions
- Clarify querying granularity and structure in the faces task.
- Add uncertainty-sampling and online active learning baselines; report sensitivity of the threshold baseline.
- Incorporate or compare to a worker/difficulty model (e.g., Dawid–Skene; TurKontrol-style POMDP) or at least perform sensitivity analyses on pR/pT and calibration of pθ.
- Report runtime/latency overheads and provide Pareto trade-off curves.
- Discuss extensions to value future learning, e.g., horizon-limited planning or bandit-style exploration bonuses.

Arguments for acceptance
- Principled, latency-aware decision-theoretic framework with a novel continuous-time MCTS approximation.
- Strong empirical gains and cost savings on a real structured task (NER).
- Practical real-time crowdsourcing implementation and open-source release.

Arguments against acceptance
- Ambiguities and limited demonstrated benefit on unstructured tasks; unclear face experiment setup.
- Simplistic crowd/noise modeling and lack of sensitivity analyses.
- Missing, stronger baselines (uncertainty sampling; online active learning) and limited ablations to attribute gains to the proposed planning.
- Per-instance myopia undermines the “learning on the job” claim.

Recommendation: Weak reject. The framework is promising and could become a solid contribution with clearer experimental setups, stronger baselines, and analyses that isolate the benefits of the decision-theoretic planning and continuous-time reasoning.
