

This paper proposes "on-the-job learning," a hybrid system that strategically balances crowdsourced labels and online model training to maintain high accuracy while reducing costs over time. The authors frame the problem as a stochastic game between the system and crowd, modeled as a CRF-based Bayesian decision process with continuous time considerations for query timing. They develop an approximate solution using Monte Carlo Tree Search with progressive widening (Algorithm 1) to optimize expected utility, which trades off accuracy, latency, and cost. The system, named LENSE, is evaluated on NER, sentiment, and face-ID tasks against baselines including majority voting (1,3,5 votes), online learning, and a threshold baseline.

The work demonstrates significant originality by extending active classification to an on-the-job setting where the system improves during deployment without requiring initial labeled data. The core idea of gradually reducing crowd reliance as model confidence increases is well-motivated and addresses a practical deployment challenge. The theoretical framing as a stochastic game with utility optimization is a notable contribution to the active learning literature. The experimental results show promising cost reductions (over an order of magnitude for NER) and accuracy improvements over baselines, with the system demonstrating decreasing reliance on crowds over time.

However, the paper suffers from significant clarity issues that undermine its presentation. The continuous-time extension (Section 3) is introduced abruptly and complicates the core method without clear justification, as the threshold baseline achieves comparable performance with simpler implementation. The paper contains multiple reporting errors: it incorrectly claims to "beat human performance" for 5-vote NER (where 5-vote actually outperforms LENSE), omits Face-ID and Sentiment results for 5-vote in several tables, and misstates the threshold baseline's performance. Notation inconsistencies (equation 2 missing s, equation 5 using q for distribution) further reduce readability.

While the core idea is compelling and the work is technically sound, the added complexity of continuous time appears unjustified by the marginal experimental benefits. The recommended approach of presenting the core method first (without continuous time) would strengthen the paper's impact and adoption potential. The authors should address the reporting errors and clarify the relationship between the threshold baseline and their approach.

The paper makes a significant contribution to on-the-job learning, but clarity and presentation issues prevent it from reaching its full potential. I recommend acceptance with major revisions to improve exposition and correct the experimental reporting.
