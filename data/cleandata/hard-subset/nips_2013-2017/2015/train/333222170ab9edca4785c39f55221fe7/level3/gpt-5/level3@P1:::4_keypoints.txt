- Paper proposes "on-the-job learning": a decision-theoretic framework that interleaves real-time crowd queries with model inference and online updates to start with zero training data.
- Problem cast as a stochastic game; utility trades off expected MAP accuracy against monetary and latency costs.
- Action policy approximated via Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) with progressive widening to handle continuous time.
- Prediction uses a CRF for structured outputs; worker responses modeled with fixed confusion and response-time models.
- Experiments on NER, sentiment, and face classification using an AMT retainer show cost reductions vs. pure crowd and accuracy gains vs. machine-only and a threshold baseline, strongest on NER.

Strengths:
- Novel, principled formulation of pre-prediction crowd querying as a stochastic game optimizing accuracy/time/cost.
- Technically interesting use of continuous-time MCTS with progressive widening for decision-making.
- Integration with structured CRFs enables within-instance evidence propagation and adaptive sequential queries.
- Real-time deployment focus (retainer setup and response-time modeling) increases practical relevance.
- Empirical gains on NER and public code release improve reproducibility.

Weaknesses / Clarification needed:
- Face/image experiments unclear: unspecified query granularity (image label, attributes, regions) and limited evidence of structure used.
- Utility is per-instance myopic and does not value information that improves future instances, weakening the "learning on the job" claim.
- Crowd response model is simplistic (fixed 70% uniform confusion); lacks per-worker or difficulty modeling and calibration analyses.
- Threshold baseline parameters appear arbitrary (0.3 and 0.98) with no sensitivity study; stronger baselines missing.
- Missing baselines: uncertainty sampling, online active learning with small pre-prediction budgets, and greedy value-of-information heuristics.
- Relation to active/online active learning is not empirically isolated; need ablations to show MCTS/continuous-time gains over greedy policies.
- Evaluation relies on pre-collected responses which ties results to the simplistic response model; runtime, decision latency, confidence intervals, and Pareto curves are missing.
- Sensitivity analyses to parameters (wM, wT, pR, pT, progressive widening/UCT) are absent.

Quality:
- Technically sound overall but limited by simplistic response/noise modeling and insufficient sensitivity analyses around key design choices.

Clarity:
- Generally well written but needs clearer description of the face task querying granularity, MCTS hyperparameters, computational costs, and pθ update timing.

Originality:
- Continuous-time, utility-optimized querying via MCTS is a novel combination, but distinction from active/online active learning is smaller than claimed.

Significance:
- Potentially impactful for rapid deployment in structured prediction with real-time crowdsourcing; strongest evidence limited to NER.

Suggestions:
- Clarify face task querying granularity and how structure is used.
- Add baselines: uncertainty sampling, online active learning, and greedy VOI heuristics; perform sensitivity analyses on threshold baseline.
- Incorporate or compare to worker/difficulty models (e.g., Dawid–Skene, TurKontrol-style POMDP) and test sensitivity to pR/pT and pθ calibration.
- Report runtime/decision latency, confidence intervals, Pareto trade-off curves, and sensitivity to MCTS parameters.
- Consider extensions to value future learning (horizon-limited planning or exploration bonuses).

Arguments for acceptance:
- Principled, latency-aware decision-theoretic framework with novel continuous-time MCTS approximation.
- Strong empirical cost and accuracy gains on a real structured task (NER).
- Practical real-time crowdsourcing implementation and open-source code.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Ambiguous face experiment setup and limited demonstrated benefit on unstructured tasks.
- Simplistic crowd/noise modeling and lack of sensitivity analyses.
- Missing stronger baselines and ablations to attribute gains to planning.
- Per-instance myopia undermines the "learning on the job" claim.

Recommendation:
- Weak reject; promising framework but needs clearer experiments, stronger baselines, and analyses to isolate the benefits of decision-theoretic planning and continuous-time reasoning.
