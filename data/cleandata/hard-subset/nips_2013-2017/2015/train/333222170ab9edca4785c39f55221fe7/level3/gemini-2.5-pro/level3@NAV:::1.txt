This paper addresses the practical problem of deploying a high-accuracy prediction system with zero initial training data. The authors propose an "on-the-job learning" framework that begins by relying on real-time crowdsourcing and gradually transitions to a self-sufficient machine learning model. This work provides a more theoretically grounded formulation for the system described by Lasecki et al. [6]. The core contribution is to model the decision of when and what to query as a stochastic game, using Bayesian decision theory to balance accuracy, latency, and cost. As computing the optimal policy is intractable, the authors propose an approximation based on Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS). The approach is evaluated on NER, sentiment analysis, and image classification tasks.

**Strengths:**
The primary strength of this paper is its novel and principled formulation of the on-the-job learning problem. Framing the dynamic trade-off between crowd-sourcing and model prediction as a stochastic game is an elegant and significant contribution that moves beyond the heuristics of previous systems. The problem itself is highly relevant and addresses a key challenge in deploying ML systems for new tasks. The paper is generally well-written, and the motivation is clear.

**Weaknesses:**
While the theoretical framing is strong, the paper is weakened by a lack of clarity in key technical areas and an underwhelming empirical evaluation.
1.  **Clarity and Reproducibility:** Several important details are missing. In Algorithm 1, N(σ) is used but never explicitly defined. In the model description, the formulation of expected accuracy in Eq. 2 should likely be conditioned on the current state. Furthermore, the response model `pR(ri | yqi)` in Eq. 3 drops the full output `y` from its conditioning set without justification.
2.  **Experimental Rigor:** The empirical results are not fully convincing. The performance improvement over the threshold-based heuristic is modest, which raises questions about the added value of the complex MCTS machinery. The justification for the baseline’s threshold values (0.3, 0.98) is also missing. Critically, the paper does not establish if the underlying "online learning" model is a strong baseline for these tasks. If it is not, the reported gains may simply reflect the benefit of adding human annotation rather than the efficacy of the proposed decision-making framework. The evaluation would be significantly improved by an error analysis with concrete examples illustrating the policy's behavior.

**Overall Assessment:**
This paper presents a reasonable and interesting approach to an important problem. The theoretical framing is a valuable contribution. However, the impact is currently limited by the missing technical details and a lack of depth in the experimental analysis. The work certainly merits further investigation, but the claims need to be better substantiated to warrant acceptance at a top conference.
