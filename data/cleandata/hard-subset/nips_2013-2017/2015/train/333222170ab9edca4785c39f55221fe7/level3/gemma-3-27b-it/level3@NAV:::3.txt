## Review of "On-the-Job Learning with Bayesian Decision Theory"

This paper presents a compelling framework, LENSE, for on-the-job learning, tackling the problem of deploying accurate AI systems with limited initial labeled data. The core idea – a hybrid approach leveraging crowdsourcing initially and gradually transitioning to a self-improving model – is well-motivated and addresses a practical challenge. Framing this as a stochastic game with a clear utility function incorporating latency, cost, and accuracy is a strong theoretical contribution, and the use of MCTS + TD learning to approximate the optimal policy is a sensible approach given the intractability of exact optimization. 

The experimental evaluation across NER, sentiment, and face-ID tasks demonstrates the potential of the approach, particularly in reducing costs compared to full annotation. The observation that the model requires fewer crowd labels over time as it gains confidence is a key result, and the improvements over online learning are significant. The availability of an open-source implementation further enhances the value of this work. This is a novel and significant contribution that will likely stimulate further research in this area.

However, the paper suffers from clarity issues, particularly regarding the introduction of continuous time. While the authors argue for its necessity, the added complexity feels largely unjustified given the strong performance of the simpler threshold baseline. The continuous-time extension doesn't yield commensurate experimental benefits, and its presentation muddies the core method.  I strongly recommend presenting the foundational method *without* continuous time first, treating the extension as secondary. This would substantially improve accessibility and encourage wider adoption. 

Several specific issues need addressing. The claim of "beating human performance" on NER (5-vote) is incorrect, and 5-vote results are missing from several tables/figures for Face-ID and Sentiment. The sentiment experiments lack the threshold baseline in Table 3 and Figure 4. Notationally, equation (2) should include *s* in the distribution, and equation (5) uses *q* for a distribution function, creating confusion. The description of the threshold baseline contains a likely typo in its uncertainty expression.  Finally, further clarification is needed on the practical use of *t_deadline* and the criteria used for reporting answers (e.g., entropic threshold vs. time-based).

**Arguments for Acceptance:** Novel approach, strong theoretical grounding, promising experimental results, open-source implementation, potential for significant impact.
**Arguments against Acceptance:** Clarity issues, limited benefit from continuous-time extension, reporting errors/omissions, notational inconsistencies. 

Despite these flaws, the originality and potential impact of this work outweigh its shortcomings. I recommend acceptance with a strong request for the authors to address the clarity and presentation issues outlined above.
