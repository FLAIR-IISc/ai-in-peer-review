- Introduces a novel on-the-job learning framework using Bayesian decision theory to trade off latency, cost, and accuracy.
- Models the on-the-job setting as a stochastic game and uses Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) to approximate the optimal policy.
- Evaluated on three datasets: named-entity recognition, sentiment classification, and image classification.
- Deploys a high-accuracy system from zero training examples by relying on real-time crowdsourcing where uncertain, reducing queries as the model improves.
- Useful for scenarios with scarce labeled data or limited resources to pre-label large datasets.
- Paper is well-written, with clear explanations, helpful figures/tables, and a technically sound presentation.
- Empirical results show significant cost reductions versus pure crowd approaches and accuracy gains versus pure ML approaches.
- Application to face recognition is unclear—paper does not specify whether full instances or parts were queried.
- The threshold baseline uses two seemingly arbitrary parameters; their origin and sensitivity analysis are not reported.
- Distinction from active online learning is weak; active learning could be adapted to this setting by updating on responses.
- Applicability appears limited and the framework’s broader potential and use cases need further exploration.
- Arguments for acceptance: principled Bayesian formulation, use of MCTS, and empirical effectiveness on multiple datasets.
- Arguments against acceptance: unclear face-recognition details, poorly justified threshold baseline parameters, weak novelty relative to active learning, and limited applicability.
