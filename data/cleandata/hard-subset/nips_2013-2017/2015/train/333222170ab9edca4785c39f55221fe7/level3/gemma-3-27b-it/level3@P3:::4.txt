This paper introduces a novel framework, “on-the-job learning,” for deploying AI systems without requiring pre-labeled training data. The core idea is to interleave prediction with querying a crowd for labels on uncertain parts of the input, gradually reducing reliance on human input as the model improves. This is formalized as a stochastic game solved approximately using Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS). The approach is evaluated on NER, sentiment classification, and image classification, demonstrating significant cost reductions compared to full annotation and improvements over online learning and a simple threshold baseline.

The paper builds upon existing work in online learning (Cesa-Bianchi & Lugosi, 2006), active learning (Settles, 2010), and crowdsourcing (Bernstein et al., 2010). It differentiates itself by combining these elements in a dynamic, test-time learning setting, unlike static policy active learning or purely offline approaches. Related work in real-time crowd labeling (Lasecki et al., 2013) and active classification (Gao & Koller, 2009) is also discussed, highlighting the novelty of the proposed framework's adaptive, game-theoretic approach.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The on-the-job learning framework, framing the problem as a stochastic game, and the use of MCTS for policy approximation are genuinely novel contributions. The idea of interleaving learning and querying in a principled Bayesian manner is compelling.
* **Strong Empirical Results:** The paper demonstrates significant cost reductions and performance improvements on the NER task, which is a substantial practical benefit. The results on sentiment classification and image classification, while not as dramatic, still show positive results.
* **Clear Problem Formulation:** The authors clearly define the on-the-job learning setting and the tradeoffs between latency, cost, and accuracy. The utility function provides a sound theoretical foundation for the approach.
* **Open-Source Implementation:** Providing an open-source implementation (LENSE) is commendable and facilitates reproducibility and further research.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Application to Face Recognition Unclear:** The description of the face classification experiment is brief. It’s unclear if the entire image was queried, or specific regions. Understanding the granularity of queries is crucial for evaluating the effectiveness of the method in this context.
* **Utility Function Appropriateness:** In non-structured prediction settings (e.g., image classification), where examples are independent, the model's inability to propagate information across examples might render the proposed utility function less effective. The benefits of querying one image don't directly inform future predictions. This limitation should be discussed.
* **Threshold Baseline Arbitrariness:** The threshold baseline relies on two parameters (0.3 and 0.98) whose origin and sensitivity are not clearly explained.  A sensitivity analysis showing how the results change with different parameter values would strengthen the evaluation.
* **Missing Uncertainty-Sampling Baseline:** A more direct comparison to active learning is needed. An uncertainty-sampling baseline, where the model queries the labels it's least confident about, would be a strong point of comparison.  This is a standard baseline in the active learning literature.
* **Weak Distinction from Active Online Learning:** The distinction between on-the-job learning and active online learning is subtle. It’s argued that active learning could be adapted by treating the input sequence as the "active set" and updating the model after each response. The authors should provide a more compelling argument for why their approach is fundamentally different.
* **Limited Applicability & Clarity of Details:** While the framework is novel, several implementation details are glossed over (e.g. the specifics of progressive widening). The paper acknowledges limited applicability, but a more thorough discussion of the types of problems where this approach would be most suitable would be helpful. The explanation of the environment model could also benefit from increased clarity.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and well-motivated framework for on-the-job learning.
* The empirical results on NER are compelling and demonstrate the practical benefits of the approach.
* The open-source implementation enhances the reproducibility and impact of the work.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The lack of clarity regarding certain implementation details and the limited discussion of applicability could hinder broader adoption.
* The comparison to existing active learning techniques is incomplete, particularly the absence of an uncertainty-sampling baseline.
* The utility function's effectiveness in non-structured settings is questionable and requires further discussion.

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents an interesting and innovative approach to building AI systems with limited initial training data. While some details require clarification and the evaluation could be strengthened with additional baselines, the novelty of the framework and the promising results on the NER task suggest that this work has merit. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions, specifically addressing the points raised regarding the face recognition experiment, utility function discussion, baseline comparisons, and clarity of implementation details. A more thorough discussion of the limitations of the approach and its potential applicability would also be beneficial.
