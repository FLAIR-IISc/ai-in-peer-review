This paper formalizes “on-the-job learning” as a Bayesian decision-theoretic stochastic game that trades off accuracy, latency, and monetary cost, approximated with MCTS/UCT and progressive widening. The system begins by querying a real-time crowd and transitions toward self-prediction as its CRF improves, deciding which labels to query and when to stop. The framing builds on value-of-information ideas from active classification (e.g., Gao & Koller, NIPS’11) and connects to POMDP-style control for crowdsourcing (e.g., TurKontrol), while extending Legion:AR’s real-time crowd-machine loop with a more principled objective and search-based policy.

Quality: The modeling choices are sensible and the MCTS-based approximation is reasonable for this large state/action space. However, several technical details are missing or unclear: the noise model assumes uniform 70% worker accuracy and independence (omits heterogeneity and calibration), the latency model and its priors are under-specified, and key hyperparameters (UCT c, widening schedule, wM/wT scaling) are not reported or analyzed. Empirically, LENSE’s gains over a threshold heuristic are modest; stronger baselines (myopic VOI, greedy expected utility with exact marginals, POMDP approximations, and stronger offline/online learners) are needed to support the claims.

Clarity: The paper is generally well written, but please address:
- Eq. (2): clarify conditioning; ExpAcc should be computed under p(y | x, q, s, r, t). As written, the LHS appears unconditional.
- Eq. (3): earlier text uses pR(ri | y, qi) but later reduces to pR(ri | yqi). Explain why dependence on full y and qi disappears (locality and stationarity assumptions).
- Algorithm 1: define N(·) consistently (N(σ)? N(s)?); specify c for UCT and the progressive widening rule explicitly.
- Baseline thresholds: justify the 0.3 uncertainty-reduction factor and the 0.88/0.98 target-confidence thresholds. Were these tuned on a dev set or derived from the response model?

Originality: The decision-theoretic game formulation and MCTS planning for crowd→model transition is a nice synthesis; while components are known, the combination for this setting is novel and timely.

Significance: Potentially impactful for rapid deployment with limited data. That said, conclusions are limited by evaluation design. Are the “online” baselines the best available ML systems for these tasks? If so, improvements may largely come from access to human annotations; provide an error analysis (where does MCTS help vs. heuristic; representative examples) and ablations (remove progressive widening; vary pR accuracy; worker heterogeneity).

Pros for acceptance:
- Clear, principled formulation of an important setting.
- Plausible algorithmic approach; open-source implementation.

Cons for acceptance:
- Empirical comparisons underwhelming vs. weak baselines.
- Missing technical details and sensitivity analyses.

Overall: A reasonable and promising approach; merits further investigation, but requires clarifications and stronger empirical support.
