- Proposes a novel Bayesian decision-theoretic framework for on-the-job learning to start with zero training examples and balance latency, cost, and accuracy.  
- Models the problem as a stochastic game and uses an MCTS-based approximation with progressive widening to handle continuous time.  
- Builds on prior work in online learning, active learning, and structured prediction but claims distinct advantages in maintaining high initial accuracy and adapting over time.  
- Empirically evaluated on three tasks, showing improvements in both quality and cost versus machine-only and human-only baselines.  
- Strength: principled multi-objective tradeoff via Bayesian decision theory.  
- Strength: technically sound presentation with theoretical analysis and organized exposition.  
- Weakness: unclear applicability to non-structured settings where information does not propagate across examples.  
- Weakness: threshold baseline uses seemingly arbitrary parameters without reporting origin or sensitivity analyses.  
- Weakness: insufficient clarity on distinction from active online learning; active learning might be adaptable to this setting.  
- Weakness: some framework details unclear—more information needed on model calibration and parameter sensitivity.  
- Clarity: well-written and organized, but some technical parts may be hard for non-experts without more background.  
- Originality: novel application of Bayesian decision theory to on-the-job learning and a distinct multi-objective approach.  
- Significance: potential for substantial cost reductions and accuracy gains, especially with limited labeled data, but broader applications and limitations need more discussion.  
- Arguments for acceptance: novel framework, clear differentiation from related work, and strong empirical gains.  
- Arguments against acceptance: unclear generality to non-structured tasks, opaque baseline calibration, and ambiguous relation to active learning.
