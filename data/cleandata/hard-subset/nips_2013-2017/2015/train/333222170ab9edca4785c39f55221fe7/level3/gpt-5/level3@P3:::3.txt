Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes on-the-job learning: a hybrid system that uses real-time crowdsourcing to resolve uncertainty for each incoming instance and gradually relies more on an online-trained model as confidence increases. The decision problem is framed as a stochastic game built on a CRF: the system and crowd take turns issuing/answering queries, and the system decides when to stop and return a prediction to maximize a Bayesian expected utility that trades off accuracy (via ExpAcc), monetary cost, and latency. The model explicitly incorporates continuous time by modeling query send times s and response times t; the environment model P(y,r,t|x,q,s) combines the CRF, a noisy response model pR, and a latency model pT. As exact optimization is intractable (especially with continuous time), the authors approximate with Monte Carlo Tree Search (UCT) and progressive widening (Algorithm 1) to estimate action values. Experiments on NER, sentiment, and face-ID compare against majority-vote baselines (1/3/5), an online-learning oracle (no queries), and a non-time “threshold” heuristic. The approach, implemented as LENSE, reduces dependence on the crowd over time and maintains high initial accuracy by invoking the crowd when uncertainty is high.

The work connects to online learning (improving over time), active learning/classification (selective querying at test time), POMDP-style control of crowdsourcing (e.g., TurKontrol), and structured prediction with partial measurements. Distinctively, it allows pre-prediction queries on each instance to ensure quality from the start and learns online at test time.

Quality
- Formulation: The stochastic game and Bayesian utility are principled and well-motivated. The environment model factorization is reasonable, though assuming constant worker accuracy (e.g., 0.7) is simplistic.
- Algorithms: The MCTS with UCT and progressive widening is appropriate for continuous-time branching; however, the benefit of continuous time over a simpler discrete-time or batch model is not isolated empirically.
- Empirics: LENSE generally outperforms 1- and 3-vote baselines and the online-learning oracle; however, it does not surpass the 5-vote majority on quality. The threshold baseline is surprisingly competitive and sometimes offers lower latency, raising questions about the practical added value of the full continuous-time planning.
- Reporting issues affect credibility (see Specific corrections).

Clarity
The paper has substantial clarity issues centered on the mid-Section-3 introduction of continuous time. The notation and state definition (σ with tnow, q, s, r, t) complicate exposition and obscure the core idea (query, wait, return). Algorithm 1’s description is terse, and some variables/notations are inconsistent or overloaded.

Recommendation for improving clarity and adoption:
- Present the core method first in a discrete-time or event-driven setting without continuous time; then add continuous time as an extension with a focused ablation demonstrating its benefit.
- Provide a clear description of when the system chooses to return (q=∅R): is it driven by an entropy/confidence threshold, an explicit deadline t_deadline, or purely the utility with wT? Clarify how t_deadline (mentioned in text/experiments) is set and used, and how timing and rate of queries are controlled in practice.

Originality
Framing on-the-job learning as a stochastic game over a CRF with a continuous-time environment model and planning via MCTS is novel. The integration of accuracy-cost-latency trade-offs in a deployable hybrid system is original relative to standard online/active learning and prior crowd-POMDP work.

Significance
The problem is important and practical. The approach can spur discussion and adoption of hybrid human-ML systems that deliver high initial accuracy without upfront training data. Open-source code adds value. However, the limited gains over a strong threshold heuristic and the failure to beat 5-vote majority on all tasks temper the empirical impact; demonstrating when/why the full planner substantially outperforms simpler heuristics would increase significance.

Pros for acceptance
- Timely, well-motivated problem with a principled decision-theoretic formulation.
- Novel game-theoretic treatment and continuous-time environment modeling.
- Solid, multi-domain evaluation and open-source release.
- Demonstrates decreasing crowd usage over time while preserving quality.

Cons against acceptance
- Clarity issues, especially around continuous time and algorithmic details.
- Empirical gains over a simple threshold baseline are modest; 5-vote majority often remains strongest.
- Reporting errors/omissions and notation problems weaken credibility.
- Limited analysis of when continuous time materially helps.

Specific corrections and requests
- Claims: The paper’s “beats human performance” claim is incorrect for 5-vote NER; please correct. Include 5-vote results consistently for Face-ID and Sentiment wherever applicable.
- Baselines: The threshold baseline is missing from the Sentiment experiments in Table 3 and Figure 4; please add. Bold the best results and latency values consistently.
- Clarify return policy: Specify whether answers are reported based on a confidence/entropy threshold, an explicit t_deadline, or purely optimizing U with wT. Define and justify t_deadline and how it controls query timing/rate.
- Algorithm details: Algorithm 1 implements UCT MCTS with progressive widening; there is no TD learning. Please avoid referring to TD unless actually used, and provide pseudocode for ExpAcc estimation and the rollout policy.
- Notation fixes:
  - Equation (2): p(y,r,t|x,q, s) should explicitly include s (send times) in the conditioning set.
  - Equation (5): Do not reuse q both as an action index and as a distribution/function; choose a different letter to avoid clash.
- Threshold baseline description: The uncertainty reduction inequality appears reversed. It should capture decreasing uncertainty with increasing m (e.g., multiplicative reduction by 0.3^m); please correct and justify the 0.3 factor.
- Worker modeling: Discuss sensitivity to pR and the assumption of uniform worker accuracy; consider comparing to learned worker reliabilities (e.g., TurKontrol) or adding a brief analysis.
- Continuous-time benefit: Provide an ablation comparing the discrete-time/planned-without-latency version to the continuous-time planner, and quantify latency/quality gains attributable to continuous time.

Minor presentation issues
- Ensure all best-performing entries and key latency figures are bolded; fix inconsistent formatting across tables/figures.
- Clearly state monetary/time weights (wM, wT), query costs by task, and any deadlines.
- Describe how many parallel queries are allowed and any concurrency limits.

Overall recommendation
Accept. The paper is original and significant, with a principled formulation and a practical system likely to influence hybrid human-ML deployments. Addressing the clarity and reporting issues—especially simplifying the presentation and correcting errors—will materially improve the paper and facilitate adoption.
