Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes an “on-the-job learning” framework in which a model maintains high prediction quality from the start by issuing real-time crowd queries for uncertain parts of an input and gradually reduces reliance on the crowd as it improves online. The setting is formalized as a stochastic game grounded in Bayesian decision theory, with actions to query, wait, or return, and a utility that trades off expected accuracy, latency, and monetary cost. Because optimal planning is intractable, the paper uses Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) with progressive widening to handle continuous time. Experiments on NER (structured), sentiment, and face classification (unstructured) show improved F1 and reduced cost vs. human-only and machine-only baselines. Code and data are released.

The work sits at the intersection of online learning, active learning, active classification, POMDP-based crowd control (e.g., TurKontrol), and structured prediction. Unlike standard online or active learning (which typically acquires labels post-prediction and improves accuracy over time), the proposed system queries pre-prediction to maintain high accuracy immediately, and accounts for asynchronous returns and timing. Compared to prior active classification/value-of-information methods that learn static policies from labeled data, this framework adapts at test time and updates the predictive model continually. The use of MCTS/UCT and progressive widening follows standard planning heuristics.

Strengths
- Clear problem motivation: rapidly deploy a high-accuracy system without upfront training data, with an explicit cost–latency–accuracy tradeoff.
- Principled modeling: a coherent game-theoretic/Bayesian decision formulation with a transparent utility and explicit environment model (response noise and latency).
- Algorithmic contribution: adaptation of MCTS with progressive widening to handle asynchronous, continuous-time crowd responses.
- Empirical promise: on NER, large cost reductions compared to human majority vote and accuracy gains over single-vote and an online learning baseline; cost decreases over time as the model learns.
- Reproducibility: code and data provided; real-time crowdsourcing implemented via a retainer model.

Weaknesses and points needing clarification
- Unclear application on faces: What exactly is queried? Is the entire instance labeled as a single class, are attributes queried, or is there any structured decomposition? Please specify the query granularity, interface, and how pR was estimated for this task.
- Non-structured settings and utility: For single-label tasks (sentiment, faces), the proposed utility operates per-instance and the model does not propagate information across examples at decision time. This weakens the benefit of pre-prediction queries relative to simply training and then predicting. Please discuss whether the same utility is appropriate for unstructured problems and consider a variant that explicitly values information mainly through its effect on future examples (e.g., an exploration bonus or multi-step lookahead that includes future-instance utility).
- Response/noise model: pR is fixed at 0.7 with uniform off-diagonal mass, while actual worker accuracy varies by task and even over time. This mismatch can bias planning. Consider learning worker- and task-specific reliabilities (e.g., Dawid–Skene/GLAD) and report sensitivity to pR.
- Time model and progressive widening: The latency model pT(t|s) is central to the utility but not sufficiently specified (parametric form, estimation, per-task variability). Similarly, progressive widening parameters are not analyzed. Please add details and sensitivity.
- Threshold baseline: The baseline uses two seemingly arbitrary parameters (0.3 reduction factor; 0.98 confidence target) and assumes all queries are launched up front. The origin, tuning protocol, and sensitivity are not reported. As currently defined, this baseline is weak and may exaggerate improvements.
- Missing uncertainty-sampling baseline: Please add a standard active learning heuristic adapted to the on-the-job setting, e.g., sequential uncertainty sampling with batch size control, issuing queries until the posterior margin exceeds a threshold, and updating the model online on received labels.
- Comparison to active online learning: The distinction is currently weak. One can view the input stream as the active pool and query a subset of labels before committing a prediction, then update on received responses—very close to your setup. Please provide a stronger conceptual and empirical comparison (e.g., an active online baseline that queries pre-prediction but uses a simpler greedy policy).
- Evaluation protocol: The “frozen pool” of five pre-collected worker responses per label may distort dependence between policies and responses, limit the benefit of redundancy, and introduce ceiling effects. Please justify this choice and, if possible, report results with live workers or larger pools.
- Modeling/algorithm ablations: It would be helpful to ablate (i) MCTS vs. a greedy myopic policy, (ii) parallel vs. sequential query issuance, and (iii) the effect of prohibiting interleaved “issue-while-waiting” actions (your simplifying restriction).

Quality
The formulation is sound and the algorithmic choices are reasonable. However, several modeling details (pR/pT estimation, faces query design), baselines (uncertainty sampling, stronger active online), and sensitivity analyses (wM, wT, threshold baseline parameters, progressive widening) are missing, which limits the strength of the empirical evidence.

Clarity
The paper is generally well written and organized, with intuitive figures. Some details are underspecified (face task queries, latency/noise model estimation, parameter settings). Adding a succinct description of the sentiment/faces feature sets and the exact MCTS hyperparameters would improve reproducibility.

Originality
Formulating on-the-job learning as a stochastic game with explicit time and using MCTS to plan crowd queries is a novel and interesting combination, especially for structured prediction. The relation to POMDP-based crowd control and active classification is acknowledged, but the distinction from active online learning should be sharpened.

Significance
The setup is practically relevant, and the NER results suggest meaningful impact. The benefits on unstructured tasks appear modest, and applicability depends on availability of retainer-based real-time crowds, which may limit deployment. With stronger baselines and sensitivity analyses, the contribution would be more compelling.

Suggestions and specific requests
- Clarify the face classification task’s query granularity and interface; report pR and pT estimation per task.
- Add an uncertainty-sampling baseline and a stronger active online baseline that queries pre-prediction and updates online.
- Report sensitivity to wM and wT and provide full accuracy–latency–cost curves.
- Analyze sensitivity to the threshold baseline’s parameters and justify their choice.
- Include ablations: greedy vs. MCTS, parallel vs. sequential querying, and allowing issue-while-waiting.
- Consider learning worker reliabilities and testing robustness to mis-specified pR.

Pros for acceptance
- Novel, principled formulation with a practical algorithm.
- Strong empirical gains on NER with significant cost savings.
- Clear motivation and potential for real-time applications.
- Open-source implementation.

Cons for acceptance
- Missing key baselines and sensitivity analyses.
- Unclear details on the faces task; limited gains on unstructured tasks.
- Noise/time models and planning parameters insufficiently specified.
- Evaluation protocol with a frozen worker pool may bias results.

Recommendation
Borderline reject. The framework is promising and potentially impactful, but the paper needs stronger baselines, clearer task/method details (especially for faces), and sensitivity/ablation studies to substantiate the claims across settings.

Reviewer confidence
High on methodology and evaluation concerns; moderate on the application-specific details pending clarification.
