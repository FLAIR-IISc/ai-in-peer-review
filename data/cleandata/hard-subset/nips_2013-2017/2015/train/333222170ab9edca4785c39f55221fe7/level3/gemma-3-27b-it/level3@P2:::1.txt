This paper introduces a framework for “on-the-job learning,” where a system learns to perform a task without pre-training by strategically querying a crowd for assistance and gradually becoming self-sufficient. The core idea builds upon previous work by the authors [6] and formalizes the problem as a stochastic game solvable (approximately) using Bayesian decision theory and Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS). The authors evaluate their system, dubbed LENSE, on three datasets – named-entity recognition (NER), sentiment classification, and image classification – demonstrating significant cost reductions compared to full human annotation and performance improvements over online learning and a threshold-based baseline.

The paper addresses an important problem: deploying accurate AI systems with limited initial resources. Existing approaches typically rely on large labeled datasets or extensive crowdsourcing, both of which can be expensive and time-consuming. The proposed on-the-job learning framework offers a compelling alternative by dynamically balancing the trade-off between querying the crowd and making predictions based on the current model. This relates to existing work in online learning [7], active learning [8, 9, 10], and active classification [11]. However, the authors rightly highlight the differences: unlike online learning, the system maintains high accuracy from the start; unlike active learning, the strategy is not static but adapts during testing; and unlike active classification, the system doesn’t rely on a pre-labeled dataset to define its query policy.  The work also connects to the growing body of research leveraging crowdsourcing for machine learning [3, 4, 25, 26].

**Strengths:**

* **Novel Formulation:** The formulation of on-the-job learning as a stochastic game based on Bayesian decision theory provides a principled framework for balancing latency, cost, and accuracy.
* **Practical Algorithm:** The proposed MCTS-based approximation offers a practical solution for tackling the intractability of the optimal policy.
* **Strong Empirical Results:** The results on the NER task are particularly impressive, showing a substantial reduction in cost while improving performance. The gains on sentiment and image classification, while less dramatic, are still noteworthy.
* **Open-Source Implementation:**  The availability of the LENSE implementation promotes reproducibility and facilitates further research.
* **Clear Motivation and Problem Statement:** The introduction clearly articulates the motivation and outlines the limitations of existing approaches.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Missing Technical Details:**  The description of the utility function (Equation 1) lacks sufficient detail. The conditioning on *s* in Equation 2 is unclear, and the disappearance of *y* from *p<sub>R</sub>(r<sub>i</sub> | y, q<sub>i</sub>)* requires explanation.
* **Baseline Justification:** The choice of the 0.3 and 0.88 thresholds in the threshold baseline is not adequately justified.  A more principled derivation or sensitivity analysis would strengthen the comparison.
* **Online Learning Baseline:** The paper doesn't sufficiently characterize the "online learning" baseline. Are these the best possible ML models for each task?  If so, the gains may largely be attributable to the benefits of human annotation – a more thorough error analysis and illustrative examples would be valuable.
* **Limited Analysis of Error Cases:** Providing a more detailed analysis of the types of errors made by LENSE and the baseline methods would provide deeper insights into the strengths and weaknesses of the approach.
* **Algorithm 1 Clarity:** The definition of N(σ) in Algorithm 1 is missing, hindering full understanding of the MCTS implementation.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound approach to on-the-job learning.
* The experimental results demonstrate the practical effectiveness of the proposed framework, especially on the NER task.
* The open-source implementation facilitates reproducibility and further research.
* The paper addresses a relevant and challenging problem in machine learning.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

* The lack of technical detail in certain areas (utility function, baseline justification) hinders a complete evaluation of the work.
* The analysis of the results is somewhat limited, and a deeper understanding of the error cases would be beneficial.
* The comparison to the threshold-based baseline feels less compelling than it could be due to the arbitrary parameter choices.


**Overall Assessment:**

The paper presents a promising and well-motivated approach to on-the-job learning. While there are some weaknesses related to technical detail and analysis, the novelty of the formulation, the practical algorithm, and the positive empirical results make this a worthwhile contribution. The theoretical framing of the crowd-to-model transition is a valuable insight. However, the empirical and explanatory gaps limit strong conclusions.  I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the clarity and justification issues outlined above. Specifically, the authors should provide more detailed explanations of the technical details, justify the baseline parameters, and perform a more in-depth analysis of the results, including error analysis and illustrative examples.
