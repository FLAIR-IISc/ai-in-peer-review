- Introduces "on-the-job learning": a Bayesian decision-theoretic stochastic-game framework that queries crowdsourcing to build accurate models from zero labeled examples.  
- Implements the approach as LENSE and evaluates on NER, sentiment, and image classification.  
- Key innovation: principled balancing of latency, cost, and accuracy rather than static active-learning policies.  
- Uses MCTS to approximate the optimal decision policy, integrating structured prediction with real-time crowdsourcing.  
- Theoretical foundation is sound and experimentally shows substantial improvements, notably NER 88.4% F1 with >10× cost reduction vs 5-vote labeling.  
- Experimental presentation issues: inconsistent labeling of the threshold baseline ("entropic" vs "threshold") in Tables 2 and 3.  
- Paper lacks a concrete illustration of the abstract example (Line 154) showing realistic crowd responses and timing.  
- Does not adequately explain why LENSE underperforms the 5-vote human baseline while outperforming the 3-vote baseline, leaving tradeoffs unclear.  
- Clarity problems: related work placed late (Section 5), missing figure reference (Line 086), and opaque description of game tree/MCTS and continuous-time handling.  
- Originality: novel combination of prior ideas with a formal ML foundation for on-the-job learning and novel use of Bayesian decision theory + MCTS.  
- Significance: addresses an important real-world problem with compelling cost/accuracy gains and reduced reliance on crowdsourcing, though scalability to larger tasks is not fully addressed.  
- Pro arguments: important problem, principled framework, significant cost reductions, MCTS innovation, open-source implementation and comprehensive experiments.  
- Con arguments: inconsistent baseline labeling, abstract/unclear explanations, poorly integrated related work, missing figure reference, and unclear accuracy vs. crowd-response tradeoffs.  
- Overall: strong paper with minor presentation/clarity issues; recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
