- Paper introduces "on-the-job learning" and LENSE: a system that queries crowd workers at test time to build high-accuracy classifiers from zero labeled examples.  
- Models the process as a stochastic game and optimizes accuracy/latency/cost trade-offs using Bayesian decision theory + MCTS.  
- Differs from online and active learning by maintaining high initial accuracy while improving during test time.  
- Strength: addresses a practical, important problem of deploying AI with limited labeled data.  
- Strength: reports >10x cost reduction on NER compared to full human annotation.  
- Strength: timing/latency optimization (parallel vs sequential queries) is novel and well-motivated.  
- Weakness: significant clarity issues; key example is abstract and hard to follow.  
- Weakness: inconsistent terminology/labels in tables (e.g., "entropic" vs "threshold") and a missing figure reference.  
- Weakness: related work section is unclear and fails to distinguish this work from close active/online learning literature.  
- Weakness: experimental explanation lacking—LENSE beats 3-vote but loses to 5-vote without sufficient analysis.  
- Weakness: cost-reduction claim is not clearly defined and baseline comparisons (threshold heuristic) are inadequately justified, especially regarding time.  
- Quality: Bayesian decision framework and MCTS approximation are sound and well-motivated.  
- Quality limitation: no theoretical analysis of MCTS/approximation error and reliance on a fixed 70% crowd-accuracy model may hurt generalization.  
- Practical limitation: method may depend on a retainer pool of workers, limiting real-world applicability.  
- Recommendation: reject due to opacity, inconsistent presentation, insufficient explanation of experiments, and weak related-work positioning.  
- Arguments for acceptance: important problem, strong cost reduction on NER, novel integration of Bayesian decision theory with crowdsourcing.  
- Arguments against acceptance: poor clarity and consistency, unexplained experimental weaknesses (vs 5-vote), and insufficient theoretical support.
