This paper introduces a novel framework for on-the-job learning, which leverages Bayesian decision theory to balance latency, cost, and accuracy objectives in a principled way. The authors model the on-the-job setting as a stochastic game and use Monte Carlo Tree Search to approximate the optimal policy. The proposed approach is evaluated on three datasets: named-entity recognition, sentiment classification, and image classification.

The main idea of the paper is to deploy a high-accuracy system starting with zero training examples. The system uses real-time crowdsourcing to resolve uncertainty where needed and outputs its prediction when confident. As the model improves over time, the reliance on crowdsourcing queries decreases. This approach is particularly useful in situations where there is a lack of resources to gather a huge amount of labeled training data or to use crowdsourcing to directly perform the task.

The paper is well-written, and the authors provide a clear explanation of their framework and its components. The use of Bayesian decision theory to trade off latency, cost, and accuracy objectives is a significant contribution. The evaluation of the proposed approach on three datasets demonstrates its effectiveness in reducing cost and improving accuracy.

However, there are some limitations and unclear details in the paper. The application of the framework to face recognition is unclear, and it is not specified whether the entire instance was queried or something else. Additionally, the threshold baseline uses two seemingly arbitrary parameters, and their origin and sensitivity are not reported. Furthermore, the distinction from active online learning is weak, and active learning could be adapted by treating the sequence as the active set and updating the model on responses.

In terms of quality, the paper is technically sound, and the authors provide a clear explanation of their framework and its components. The use of Bayesian decision theory is well-justified, and the evaluation of the proposed approach on three datasets demonstrates its effectiveness. However, the paper could benefit from a more detailed analysis of the threshold baseline and its parameters.

In terms of clarity, the paper is well-written, and the authors provide a clear explanation of their framework and its components. The use of figures and tables helps to illustrate the main ideas and results. However, some details, such as the application of the framework to face recognition, are unclear and require further explanation.

In terms of originality, the paper introduces a novel framework for on-the-job learning, which leverages Bayesian decision theory to balance latency, cost, and accuracy objectives in a principled way. The use of Monte Carlo Tree Search to approximate the optimal policy is also a significant contribution. However, the distinction from active online learning is weak, and active learning could be adapted by treating the sequence as the active set and updating the model on responses.

In terms of significance, the paper demonstrates the effectiveness of the proposed approach in reducing cost and improving accuracy. The results on the three datasets show that the proposed approach can achieve significant cost reductions over a pure crowd approach and significant accuracy improvements over a pure ML approach. However, the applicability of the framework appears limited, and further research is needed to explore its potential applications.
