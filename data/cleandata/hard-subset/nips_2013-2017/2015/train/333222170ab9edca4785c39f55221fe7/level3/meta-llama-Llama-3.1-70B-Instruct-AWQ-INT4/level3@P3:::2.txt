This paper proposes a framework for on-the-job learning, where a system learns from noisy crowd responses in real-time to maintain high accuracy while reducing cost over time. The authors model this setting as a stochastic game and use ideas from game playing to approximate the optimal policy. They evaluate their approach on three tasks and demonstrate significant cost reductions over a pure crowd approach and accuracy improvements over a pure machine learning approach.

The main idea of the paper is to deploy a high-accuracy system starting with zero training examples. The system uses real-time crowdsourcing to resolve uncertainty where needed and outputs its prediction when confident. As the model improves over time, the reliance on crowdsourcing queries decreases. The authors cast their setting as a stochastic game based on Bayesian decision theory, which allows them to balance latency, cost, and accuracy objectives in a principled way.

The paper has several strengths. First, the problem addressed is compelling and relevant to many real-world applications. The authors provide a thorough analysis of the problem and propose a novel solution that combines ideas from game playing and Bayesian decision theory. The experimental evaluation is comprehensive and demonstrates the effectiveness of the proposed approach. The authors also provide an open-source implementation of their system, which is a significant contribution to the research community.

However, the paper also has some weaknesses. One of the main limitations is that the explanations are somewhat opaque, and it is not always clear how the different components of the system interact with each other. For example, the relationship between the prediction model, the response model, and the utility function is not clearly explained. Additionally, the paper could benefit from more concrete examples and queries with realistic crowd responses and timings. The example at Line 154 is abstract and hard to follow, and it would be helpful to have more specific examples to illustrate the system's behavior.

Furthermore, there are some inconsistencies in the notation and labeling of the tables. For example, the threshold baseline is labeled as "entropic" in Table 2 but as "threshold" in Table 3. This can be confusing for the reader and should be corrected.

Another issue is the placement of the related work section. While it is common to place related work at the end of the paper, it is not entirely clear why the authors chose to do so in this case. The related work section is quite comprehensive, but it would be more effective to integrate it into the main body of the paper, perhaps in the introduction or the problem formulation section.

In terms of originality, the paper proposes a novel combination of familiar techniques from game playing and Bayesian decision theory. The use of Monte Carlo tree search and progressive widening to approximate the optimal policy is also a significant contribution. However, the paper could benefit from more discussion of how this approach differs from previous contributions in the field.

Overall, the paper is well-written and provides a significant contribution to the field of machine learning. While there are some limitations and areas for improvement, the authors demonstrate a thorough understanding of the problem and propose a novel solution that is effective in practice.
