

This paper introduces "on-the-job learning," a framework that starts with zero training examples and uses real-time crowdsourcing to resolve uncertainty while gradually transitioning to self-prediction. The authors formulate this as a stochastic game based on Bayesian decision theory, extending the work of [22] with a more theoretically grounded approach. They employ Monte Carlo Tree Search to approximate the optimal query selection policy, which dynamically balances latency, cost, and accuracy objectives.

The main strength lies in the elegant theoretical formulation of the crowd→model transition process, which provides a principled foundation for the on-the-job learning paradigm. The implementation (LENS) demonstrates promising results on NER, achieving over an order of magnitude cost reduction compared to full human annotation while boosting performance over expert labels. The framework's ability to maintain high accuracy from deployment while gradually reducing crowd reliance is conceptually valuable.

However, several weaknesses limit the paper's impact. The empirical evaluation is underwhelming, particularly the comparison to a threshold-based heuristic that lacks proper justification (the origin of 0.3 and 0.88 thresholds is unclear). The presentation omits critical technical details: Eq. 2 fails to condition on s and explains why y disappears from p_R(r_i | y, q_i), Algorithm 1 doesn't define N(s), and the analysis doesn't address whether gains stem from human annotation or improved ML systems (requiring error analysis and example cases).

While the theoretical framing is a meaningful contribution to the literature on active classification and online learning, the empirical and explanatory gaps prevent strong conclusions. The paper would benefit from more rigorous baselines, clearer justification of design choices, and deeper analysis of the transition from crowd to model. Despite these limitations, the approach is reasonable and merits further investigation as a promising direction for deploying AI systems with minimal initial data requirements. The work is original in its theoretical grounding of the on-the-job learning concept, though it doesn't significantly advance the state-of-the-art in empirical performance.
