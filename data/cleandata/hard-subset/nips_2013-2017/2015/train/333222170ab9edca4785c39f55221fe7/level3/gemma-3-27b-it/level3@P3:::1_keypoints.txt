- Introduces "on-the-job learning" framework for deploying ML with minimal initial data using real-time crowdsourcing to resolve uncertainty and improve iteratively.
- Casts the problem as a stochastic game using Bayesian decision theory and approximates the optimal querying policy with MCTS to balance latency, cost, and accuracy.
- Addresses the practical "cold start" problem and is relevant to online learning, active learning, and crowdsourcing communities.
- Situates work within prior literature and cites related work, but underplays the overlap with Legion:AR [6].
- Strength: theoretically grounded formulation modeling crowd-to-model transition as a stochastic game.
- Strength: use of Bayesian decision theory and MCTS is principled and appropriate for the intractable policy search.
- Strength: Implementation (LENSE) is open-source, aiding reproducibility and adoption.
- Weakness: Presentation lacks sufficient technical detail for reproduction (e.g., Eq. 2 unclear and conditioning on state s missing).
- Weakness: Response model notation is inconsistent—p_R(r_i | y, q_i) drops y without explanation despite depending on true label.
- Weakness: Algorithm 1 uses N(s) without defining what state s represents (full vs. partial state ambiguity).
- Weakness: Threshold baseline choices (0.3 and 0.88) are unexplained and unjustified.
- Empirical results are underwhelming and comparison to a simple threshold heuristic is insufficient.
- Reported gains (8% F1 over single human, 28% over online learning) are positive but possibly inflated by weak online learning baseline.
- Requires clarification whether the online baseline represents state-of-the-art online methods for each task.
- Need stronger baselines (competitive active learning or supervised models on small strategically selected datasets) to validate efficacy.
- Crucial to provide thorough error analysis with example cases to show where LENSE leverages crowdsourcing beyond annotation benefits.
- Experiments rely on pre-collected worker response times, which may not reflect dynamics of real-time crowdsourcing—needs discussion or mitigation.
- Arguments for acceptance: principled theory, Bayesian/MCTS approach, open-source implementation, potential real-world cost savings.
- Arguments against acceptance: insufficient technical clarity, weak empirical evaluation and baselines, lack of error analysis, limited novelty beyond prior work [6].
- Overall recommendation: promising and worthy of acceptance contingent on minor revisions to improve clarity and a more robust empirical evaluation with stronger baselines and detailed error analysis.
