This paper proposes LENSE, a hybrid “on-the-job” system that starts with zero training data, queries the crowd in real time when uncertain, and progressively shifts reliance to an online-trained CRF as confidence grows. The decision problem is framed as a stochastic game between the system and the crowd with a Bayesian utility that trades off accuracy, latency, and monetary cost. Actions include issuing queries, waiting, or returning a prediction; continuous time (send times s, response times t) is explicitly modeled. Because exact optimization of the expected utility under the dynamics P(y,r,t|x,q,s) is intractable, the authors approximate with MCTS (UCT + progressive widening) and temporal-difference value estimates. Experiments on NER, sentiment, and face ID compare against majority-vote (1/3/5), online learning (oracle), and a non-time “threshold” baseline. LENSE reduces crowd usage over time and maintains high initial accuracy by deferring to the crowd when uncertain.

Relation to prior work is appropriate and timely: the paper bridges online learning and active classification, connects to crowd-POMDP work (e.g., TurKontrol) and real-time crowd systems (Legion:AR), and leverages UCT for planning in large decision spaces.

Quality: The problem formulation and utility are principled; the search approximation is reasonable. However, the continuous-time extension—introduced mid-Section 3—adds substantial complexity with limited empirical benefit; the threshold baseline performs nearly as well and sometimes with lower latency, and 5-vote majority can outperform LENSE on NER. Reporting has issues: “beats human performance” is not correct against 5-vote on NER; 5-vote results are missing for Face-ID and Sentiment in several tables/figures; the threshold baseline is absent for Sentiment in Table 3 and Figure 4; some best scores/latencies are not bolded. Please also clarify the role of t_deadline and the precise return criterion (entropy vs time).

Clarity: Recommend presenting a discrete-time core method first (actions, belief updates, utility), then layering continuous time as an extension; move some algorithmic details to an appendix. Fix notation: include s in p(y,r,t|x,q,s) where referenced; avoid using q for both actions and a distribution in Eq. (5); correct the Threshold baseline inequality so uncertainty decreases with m.

Originality: High—the game-theoretic, continuous-time treatment of hybrid machine–crowd prediction is novel.

Significance: Strong practical relevance; likely to spur discussion and follow-on work, despite mixed wins vs strong baselines.

Pros for acceptance:
- Novel, principled formulation; practical system with code
- Strong cross-domain evaluation; compelling cost–latency–accuracy trade-offs
- Clear evidence of decreasing crowd dependence over time

Cons against acceptance:
- Clarity and notation problems; presentation burden
- Limited incremental benefit from continuous time vs strong simple baseline
- Incomplete/incorrect reporting and missing baseline comparisons

Recommendation: Accept, contingent on addressing clarity, notation, and reporting issues.
