## Review of "On-the-Job Learning with Bayesian Decision Theory"

This paper presents a compelling framework for “on-the-job learning,” where a system bootstraps performance from zero labeled data by strategically querying a crowd for assistance, gradually reducing reliance on human input as it learns. The core contribution lies in formulating this process as a stochastic game based on Bayesian decision theory, offering a principled approach to balancing accuracy, cost, and latency. This builds upon prior work in active learning and crowdsourcing (e.g., [6, 19, 25]) by explicitly modeling the trade-offs inherent in a real-time, iterative learning setting. 

The method leverages Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) to approximate the optimal querying policy, a reasonable approach given the intractability of finding an exact solution. The experimental evaluation on three datasets – NER, sentiment classification, and image classification – demonstrates the potential of the approach, achieving cost reductions compared to full human annotation and improvements over online learning baselines. 

However, the paper suffers from several weaknesses. While the theoretical framing is valuable, the empirical results, particularly the comparison to a threshold-based heuristic, are underwhelming and don’t strongly demonstrate the benefits of the proposed Bayesian approach. The presentation, while generally good, lacks crucial technical details. Specifically, Eq. 2 requires clarification regarding the conditioning on *s* and the disappearance of *y* from *p<sub>R</sub>(r<sub>i</sub> | y, q<sub>i</sub>)*. Algorithm 1 needs a definition of *N(s)*. Furthermore, the choice of 0.3 and 0.88 thresholds in the baseline is not justified.

A critical point for further analysis is whether the observed gains are genuinely attributable to the proposed method or simply reflect the quality of the human annotation used. Are the “online” systems truly the best possible ML systems for these tasks? A detailed error analysis with illustrative examples would be highly beneficial. 

**Arguments for acceptance:** The theoretical formulation of the crowd-to-model transition is a valuable contribution. The approach is reasonable and offers a principled way to address the on-the-job learning problem.

**Arguments against acceptance:** Empirical results are not convincing. Significant technical details are missing, hindering reproducibility and full understanding. The paper lacks a thorough analysis of the source of performance gains.



**Overall Assessment:** This paper presents a promising direction for research in on-the-job learning. While the current work has limitations, the theoretical framing and the overall approach merit further investigation. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the clarity and technical detail issues, and a stronger emphasis on empirical validation and analysis.
