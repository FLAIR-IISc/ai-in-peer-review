Summary and relation to prior work
This paper tackles “on-the-job learning”: deploying a high-accuracy system with no initial training data by interleaving prediction with real-time crowd queries. The core idea is to cast test-time querying and prediction as a stochastic game grounded in Bayesian decision theory that optimizes a utility trading off accuracy, latency, and cost. The system decides when and what to query, whether to wait for outstanding responses, or to return a prediction. Because exact planning is intractable, the authors approximate the optimal policy via Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) with progressive widening to handle continuous time. Experiments on NER, sentiment, and image (faces) classification show cost reductions versus crowd-only strategies and accuracy gains versus machine-only baselines. Code/data are released.

The work aligns with and extends several areas: online learning (improving over time while maintaining high early accuracy), active learning/active classification (acquiring information at test time), decision-theoretic control for crowdsourcing (e.g., TurKontrol, POMDP-style formulations), and structured prediction with partial/measurement-based supervision. Compared to prior “on-the-job” systems such as Legion:AR, this paper offers a more explicit Bayesian decision-theoretic framing and a general-purpose planner (MCTS) that reasons about asynchronous, noisy human responses and time.

Strengths
- Timely problem and practical setting: deploying systems without upfront labeled data while guaranteeing quality.
- Principled formulation: stochastic game + Bayesian utility explicitly balancing accuracy, time, and monetary cost; clean decomposition of prediction, response, and latency models.
- Planning under asynchrony: progressive widening over continuous time and MCTS are natural and well-motivated choices here.
- Structured outputs: modeling interactions across labels (e.g., CRF for NER) is important and exploited.
- Empirical evidence across three diverse tasks; real-time crowd infrastructure; open-source release.

Weaknesses and requested clarifications
- Missing technical details:
  - Eq. (2): Please condition ExpAcc on x and all available information, including the issued queries and their timestamps. As written in the paper text (U(σ) uses p(y | x, q, s, r, t)), but Eq. (2) is ambiguous. Clarify the LHS to ExpAcc(p(y | x, q, s, r, t)) and define Accuracy(·) precisely for structured outputs (token-level vs sequence-level).
  - Response model notation: In Section 3 you define pR(ri | yqi). Later you refer to p(r′ | x, r, q) by marginalizing out y and t from Eq. (3). Please explicitly show this marginalization and explain “where y went” to avoid confusion: p(r′ | x, q, s, r, t) = ∑y ∫t′ p(y, r ∪ r′, t ∪ t′ | x, q, s).
  - Algorithm 1: Define N(σ) clearly (visit counts for state σ). The text mentions N(s) in places—ensure consistent notation. Specify the UCT exploration constant c, terminal condition, rollout policy (if any), and how the system enumerates/limits possible actions (which tokens to query, how many parallel queries).
- Baseline design and justification:
  - Threshold baseline hyperparameters: The factor 0.3 and the 0.98 target confidence are not justified. If 0.3 comes from assuming a 70% accurate worker (i.e., residual uncertainty ≈ 0.3), please show the derivation and provide sensitivity analyses to worker accuracy. Similarly, justify the 0.98 threshold and report sensitivity curves (accuracy/cost vs threshold).
  - “Online learning” baseline: Please clarify whether this baseline corresponds to the best possible ML system in each domain (e.g., strong CRF with modern features for NER; strong RNN/CNN for sentiment; state-of-the-art face classifiers). If not, add stronger non-crowd baselines, and/or explain that the setting prohibits accessing labels before prediction. Otherwise, the reported gains may primarily reflect access to human labels rather than superior modeling/planning.
- Empirical analysis:
  - The comparison to the threshold heuristic is underwhelming without stronger ML baselines and ablations. Please add ablations of the planner (e.g., greedy myopic query selection, no time model, no progressive widening) to establish the contribution of the decision-theoretic planner beyond a heuristic.
  - Provide error analysis and qualitative examples where the system (i) saves queries versus humans, (ii) corrects noisy crowd responses via redundancy, and (iii) outperforms the “online” ML baseline. This will help disentangle gains due to aggregation/redundancy from those due to the learned model.
  - Justify and evaluate the constant-accuracy response model pR (0.7) and the “frozen pool” simulation protocol. Worker heterogeneity and nonstationarity are important; report robustness to response accuracy and latency distributions.
- Reporting/claims:
  - “Boosting performance relative to the expert provided labels” needs clarification. If expert labels are the gold standard, how is performance exceeding them defined? If experts are imperfect and you treat crowd consensus as superior, please explain your evaluation protocol and reconcile with the gold labels.
  - Complexity and runtime: Provide planning latency per example, number of simulations, and how costs/latency scale with sequence length and crowd pool size.

Evaluation by NeurIPS criteria
- Quality: The formulation is sound and the approximations are reasonable, but some modeling choices (constant worker accuracy, fixed thresholds in baselines) and missing ablations weaken the empirical case. More rigorous comparisons and sensitivity analyses are needed.
- Clarity: Generally well-written and organized, but the noted notational inconsistencies and missing definitions hinder reproducibility. Adding complete algorithmic details and derivations would help.
- Originality: The combination of decision-theoretic on-the-job test-time querying with MCTS/progressive widening for structured outputs appears novel and thoughtfully executed relative to prior crowd/active learning work.
- Significance: Potentially impactful for rapid deployment scenarios where quality guarantees matter. Demonstrated across three tasks, but conclusions are limited by baseline strength and analysis depth.

Pros (arguments for acceptance)
- Principled and timely formulation addressing an important deployment gap.
- Handles continuous-time, asynchronous crowd responses in a coherent planner.
- Demonstrates cost–latency–accuracy tradeoffs with public code/data.

Cons (arguments against acceptance)
- Empirical comparisons are not yet compelling; baseline choices and hyperparameters insufficiently justified.
- Missing technical details and ablations; limited analysis of where gains come from.
- Simplified response model and simulated evaluation may overstate robustness.

Suggestions for improvement
- Clarify equations/notation (conditioning in ExpAcc, response-model marginalization) and define all algorithmic quantities (N(σ), c, terminal/rollout policies).
- Strengthen baselines (state-of-the-art ML without crowd; better active classification/AL baselines) and add planner ablations and sensitivity studies (wM, wT, worker accuracy/latency).
- Provide detailed error analysis and qualitative case studies.
- Report runtime/compute budgets and discuss scalability.
- Justify threshold baseline parameters and report sensitivity; consider adaptive thresholds calibrated to model uncertainty.

Overall assessment
A reasonable and promising approach with a useful theoretical framing of the crowd-to-model transition. The contribution merits further investigation, but the current empirical and explanatory gaps limit the strength of the conclusions. With clarifications and stronger experimental evidence, this could be a solid contribution. I lean toward a borderline/weak accept if the authors can address the key clarity and empirical issues.
