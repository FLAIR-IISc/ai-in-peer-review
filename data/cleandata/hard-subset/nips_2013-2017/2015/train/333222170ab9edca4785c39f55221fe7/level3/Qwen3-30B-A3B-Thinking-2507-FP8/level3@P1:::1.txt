

The paper proposes a theoretically grounded framework for "on-the-job learning," extending prior work [6] by modeling the transition from crowd-dependent to self-sufficient prediction as a stochastic game under Bayesian decision theory. The core contribution is formalizing the tradeoff between latency, cost, and accuracy using a CRF-based prediction model and Bayesian utility maximization, with Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) as a practical approximation for query selection. This addresses a gap in existing active learning [8, 9, 10] and active classification [22, 24] approaches, which lack dynamic model updating during test time or fail to balance real-time constraints. The method, implemented as LENSE, is evaluated on named-entity recognition (NER), sentiment classification, and image classification, reporting significant cost reductions (e.g., >10× on NER) and F1 improvements over baselines.

**Strengths:**  
The theoretical framing is a clear advance over prior on-the-job learning work [6], providing a principled foundation for resource allocation. The MCTS-based approximation effectively handles the intractable optimal policy, and the empirical results on NER are compelling: 88.4% F1 with 10× lower cost than 5-vote human labeling, outperforming online learning by 28% F1. The open-source release and rigorous control for worker quality (via frozen response pools) enhance reproducibility. The paper also thoughtfully contextualizes the approach within related work, distinguishing it from online learning (which improves asymptotically) and active classification (which uses static policies).

**Weaknesses:**  
The most significant limitation is the underwhelming empirical comparison to the threshold baseline. The baseline’s 0.3 and 0.88 thresholds (Section 5) lack justification—were these tuned on the test set? If so, the gains of LENSE (8% F1 over the baseline) may merely reflect better human annotation rather than a superior algorithm. The paper claims "8% F1 improvement over having a single human label the whole set," but this conflates human effort (one worker per example) with LENSE’s multi-query strategy. Without error analysis (e.g., *why* LENSE corrects specific mistakes), it’s unclear if the gains stem from better query selection or simply more human input. The "online learning" baseline is also questionable: it uses a classifier trained on *perfect* gold labels, which is unrealistic for the proposed setting where labels are initially unavailable. For sentiment/image tasks, the marginal gains (2× cost reduction) suggest the method’s benefits are task-dependent and less generalizable than claimed.

Technical gaps further weaken the evaluation. Equation (2) defines ExpAcc(p) but omits conditioning on *s* (the sequence of queries), and p_R(r_i | y_{q_i}) in Equation (3) inexplicably drops *y* from its argument in the text (Section 3.3). Algorithm 1’s N(σ) is undefined, and the crowd response model’s 70% accuracy (Section 3.4) is not validated against the dataset’s actual worker performance. The analysis of why LENSE outperforms the threshold baseline (Figure 4) is superficial—without quantifying when sequential vs. parallel queries are optimal, the method’s adaptivity remains unproven.

**Overall Assessment:**  
The theoretical contribution is novel and well-motivated, offering a valuable framework for resource-constrained deployment. However, the empirical claims are overstated due to weak baseline comparisons and insufficient analysis of *why* the method succeeds. The NER results are strong, but the significance for broader tasks is undermined by the modest gains on sentiment/image data and the unresolved baseline issues. The paper would benefit from: (1) justifying threshold values via ablation, (2) comparing against state-of-the-art online learning baselines (e.g., [7, 10]), and (3) providing error analysis to isolate algorithmic vs. human annotation effects. While the approach is reasonable and merits further investigation, current evidence does not fully support the claimed "order-of-magnitude" cost reduction as a generalizable advance. The quality is sound but the significance is limited by empirical gaps. I recommend **weak acceptance** pending minor revisions addressing the baseline justification and technical clarifications.
