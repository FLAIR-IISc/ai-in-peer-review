## Review of "On-the-Job Learning with Bayesian Decision Theory"

This paper proposes a theoretically grounded framework for “on-the-job learning,” where a system starts with no training data and progressively improves by strategically querying a crowd for assistance, ultimately aiming for self-sufficiency. The core contribution lies in formulating this process as a stochastic game based on Bayesian decision theory, allowing for a principled trade-off between latency, cost, and accuracy. The approach utilizes Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) to approximate the optimal query policy.

The paper builds upon prior work in online learning, active learning, and crowdsourcing, notably extending ideas from [6] with a stronger theoretical foundation. It correctly identifies the limitations of existing approaches – either requiring large labeled datasets or relying solely on crowdsourcing – and presents a compelling alternative for scenarios with limited resources. The use of a stochastic game framework is a sensible approach for modeling the sequential decision-making process inherent in on-the-job learning. 

The strengths of the paper include its clear motivation, theoretical justification, and a reasonably well-described methodology. The experimental evaluation on three diverse tasks (NER, sentiment classification, and image classification) demonstrates the potential of the approach. However, the empirical results, particularly the comparison against a threshold-based heuristic, are underwhelming. The improvements reported are not substantial enough to definitively prove the superiority of the proposed MCTS-based approach. 

Several points require clarification. Eq. (2) lacks conditioning on the state *s* on the LHS, and the disappearance of *y* from *p<sub>R</sub>(r<sub>i</sub> | y, q<sub>i</sub>)* is unexplained. In Algorithm 1, the definition of *N(s)* needs to be specified. The justification for the specific threshold values (0.3 and 0.88) used in the baseline is missing. Crucially, the paper does not thoroughly investigate whether the observed gains are attributable to the on-the-job learning framework or simply to the quality of the human annotations used. An error analysis with specific examples would be highly beneficial to understand the scenarios where the system effectively leverages the crowd and where it struggles. Are the "online" systems used for comparison truly the best possible ML systems for these tasks?

Overall, the paper presents a reasonable and potentially valuable approach to on-the-job learning. The theoretical framing of the crowd-to-model transition is a significant contribution. However, the empirical gaps and lack of detailed analysis limit the strength of the conclusions. I recommend acceptance with major revisions, contingent upon addressing the clarification points and strengthening the empirical evaluation with more robust baselines and a thorough error analysis.
