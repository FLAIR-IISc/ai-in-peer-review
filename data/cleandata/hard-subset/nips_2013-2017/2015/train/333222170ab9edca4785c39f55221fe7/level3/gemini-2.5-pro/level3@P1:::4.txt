This paper introduces "on-the-job learning," a framework for deploying a high-accuracy system starting with zero labeled data. The core idea is to use real-time crowdsourcing to resolve uncertainty for incoming data instances before making a prediction. As the system observes more crowd-provided labels, its internal model improves, reducing its reliance on the crowd over time. The authors formalize this problem as a stochastic game between the system and the crowd. They employ Bayesian decision theory to construct a utility function that balances prediction accuracy, monetary cost, and latency. Since computing the optimal policy for this game is intractable, the paper proposes an approximation based on Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) combined with progressive widening. This approach is evaluated on three tasks: named-entity recognition (NER), sentiment classification, and image classification. The proposed system, LENSE, is shown to significantly reduce annotation costs compared to a fully human-labeled approach while improving accuracy over standard online learning baselines. The work relates to online learning, active learning, and active classification, but distinguishes itself by its focus on querying for information to maintain high accuracy on the current instance at test time, rather than solely for model improvement.

### Strengths

1.  **Novel and Principled Formulation:** The paper tackles an important and practical problem: how to bootstrap a high-quality machine learning system in real-time without a pre-existing labeled dataset. Framing this problem as a stochastic game and using Bayesian decision theory to navigate the trade-offs between accuracy, cost, and latency is a novel and elegant approach. This provides a strong theoretical foundation for a problem that has previously been addressed with more heuristic methods.

2.  **Significant Empirical Results:** The experimental results, particularly on the structured prediction task of NER, are very strong. Achieving an order of magnitude reduction in cost while simultaneously boosting F1 performance by 8% over a single human annotator is a compelling demonstration of the framework's potential. The learning curves (Fig. 4) effectively show the system's ability to reduce costs over time as the model improves.

3.  **High-Quality Implementation and Reproducibility:** The authors have built a complete system, LENSE, and made the code, data, and experiments fully available. This commitment to reproducibility is commendable and significantly increases the value of the contribution to the research community.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Limited Applicability to Non-Structured Tasks:** The strength of the proposed method seems heavily tied to structured prediction problems like NER, where resolving uncertainty about one part of the output (e.g., a single token) can propagate information and reduce uncertainty about other parts. For non-structured tasks like sentence-level sentiment or standard image classification, it is less clear how querying a "part" of the input is meaningful, or how information propagates within a single instance. If the only option is to query the label for the entire instance, the rich, sequential decision-making process described seems less applicable. The paper's weaker results on the Sentiment and Faces tasks may be evidence of this, and this limitation should be discussed more explicitly.

2.  **Clarity of Experimental Setup:** Certain experimental details are unclear. For the Faces dataset, the paper does not specify what a "query" entails. Is the system asking for a label for the entire image, or for specific attributes of the face as in the original dataset paper? This detail is crucial for understanding how the method was applied and for interpreting the results.

3.  **Weaknesses in Baselines:**
    *   The "Threshold baseline" is a reasonable heuristic, but it contains two magic numbers (0.3 and 0.98) whose origins are not explained. A sensitivity analysis or justification for these parameter choices is needed to ensure this is a fair and robust baseline.
    *   The paper is missing a comparison to a more standard active learning baseline. For instance, a simple uncertainty sampling approach could be adapted to this setting: if the model's confidence on an example is below a certain threshold, it queries the crowd for the full label and then updates itself. Comparing the sophisticated MCTS approach against such a simpler, widely-understood method would help isolate the benefits of the proposed game-theoretic framework.

4.  **Distinction from Related Work:** The paper argues that it differs from online active learning because it seeks information *prior* to making a prediction on the current instance. While this is true for standard formulations, the distinction feels somewhat brittle. One could easily adapt an online active learning framework to this setting by treating the decision "to query or not" as the active learning step for the current instance. The novelty here seems to be more in the sophisticated, within-instance, sequential querying policy rather than the overall setting. A more nuanced discussion of this relationship would strengthen the paper's positioning.

### Summary of Arguments

**Arguments for acceptance:**
*   Addresses a significant, practical problem with a novel and principled game-theoretic formulation.
*   The proposed MCTS-based solution is technically sound and well-motivated.
*   Demonstrates impressive, high-impact results on a challenging structured prediction task (NER).
*   The work is fully reproducible, with code and data provided.

**Arguments for rejection:**
*   The applicability of the core idea seems limited, primarily benefiting structured prediction tasks, a point that is not adequately addressed.
*   The experimental evaluation could be strengthened with better-motivated baselines (e.g., uncertainty sampling) and more transparent parameter choices for existing ones.
*   Key details about the experimental setup for some tasks are missing, hindering full understanding and reproducibility.

### Detailed Comments

Overall, this is a high-quality paper with a strong core idea and impressive results on the primary NER task. My main concerns relate to the generality of the approach and the strength of the baselines. I lean towards acceptance but would strongly urge the authors to address the following points in a final version:

*   Please clarify the querying mechanism for the Faces and Sentiment classification tasks. What specific information is being requested from the crowd for each query?
*   Please provide justification for the parameters used in the threshold baseline or include a sensitivity analysis.
*   I highly recommend adding a baseline based on a standard active learning heuristic like uncertainty sampling, adapted to the on-the-job setting. This would provide a crucial point of comparison.
*   I would appreciate a more detailed discussion on the limitations of the approach for non-structured prediction tasks, where the benefit of resolving partial uncertainty is less obvious. This would help readers better understand the conditions under which this framework is most likely to be effective.
