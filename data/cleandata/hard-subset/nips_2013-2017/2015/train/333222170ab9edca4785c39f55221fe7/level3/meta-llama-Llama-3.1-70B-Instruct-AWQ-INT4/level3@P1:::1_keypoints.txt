- Proposes a novel on-the-job learning framework combining crowdsourcing with machine learning to achieve high initial accuracy without a pre-trained model.
- Models the on-the-job setting as a stochastic game and applies game-playing ideas to approximate the optimal policy.
- Uses Monte Carlo Tree Search to decide when to query the crowd versus rely on the model, transitioning from crowd→model over time.
- Evaluated empirically on three tasks: named-entity recognition, sentiment classification, and image classification.
- Main contribution is a more theoretically grounded formulation of an "on-the-job learner" building on [22].
- Presentation is generally clear and concise, with a useful framing of the crowd→model transition.
- Empirical results show the approach outperforms machine-only and human-only baselines on both quality and cost.
- Missing technical details: unclear definition and role of the response model pR(r | yq) in the utility function.
- Algorithmic clarity issues: lack of definition for N(s) in Algorithm 1 and unexplained choice of baseline threshold values (0.3 and 0.88).
- Baselines are limited: comparison only to a threshold-based heuristic may be insufficient to establish empirical significance.
- Needs more analysis of the "online" systems in experiments, including error analysis and illustrative example cases to explain gains.
- Overall assessment: technically sound and original, merits further investigation but has empirical and explanatory gaps.
- Reviewer scores: Quality 7/10, Clarity 8/10, Originality 8/10, Significance 7/10.
- Arguments for acceptance: novel framework, original approach, empirical effectiveness across three tasks.
- Arguments against acceptance: missing technical details, limited baseline comparisons, insufficient analysis versus state-of-the-art.
