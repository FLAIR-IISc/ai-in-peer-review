- Proposes LENSE, a hybrid on-the-job system that starts with zero training data, queries the crowd when uncertain, and gradually shifts to an online-trained CRF as confidence grows.  
- Frames decision-making as a stochastic game between system and crowd with a Bayesian utility trading accuracy, latency, and monetary cost.  
- Actions include issuing queries, waiting, or returning a prediction, with continuous time (send times s, response times t) explicitly modeled.  
- Exact optimization under P(y,r,t|x,q,s) is intractable; authors approximate with MCTS (UCT + progressive widening) and temporal-difference value estimates.  
- Experiments on NER, sentiment, and face ID compare LENSE to majority-vote (1/3/5), online oracle, and a non-time “threshold” baseline; LENSE reduces crowd usage over time while maintaining high initial accuracy.  
- Relation to prior work is appropriate, bridging online learning, active classification, crowd-POMDPs (e.g., TurKontrol), and real-time crowd systems.  
- Problem formulation and utility are principled; the search approximation is reasonable.  
- Continuous-time extension adds substantial complexity but shows limited empirical benefit; the threshold baseline often performs nearly as well and sometimes with lower latency.  
- 5-vote majority can outperform LENSE on NER, contradicting the paper’s “beats human performance” claim.  
- Reporting issues: missing 5-vote results for Face-ID and Sentiment, threshold baseline absent for Sentiment in some tables/figures, and some best scores/latencies not bolded.  
- Requests clarification on t_deadline and the precise return criterion (entropy vs time).  
- Clarity suggestions: present a discrete-time core method first, move algorithmic detail to appendix, and fix notation inconsistencies (include s in p(y,r,t|x,q,s), avoid using q for both actions and a distribution, correct Threshold baseline inequality).  
- Originality is high — novel game-theoretic, continuous-time treatment of hybrid machine–crowd prediction.  
- Significance is strong with practical relevance and potential to spur follow-on work despite mixed wins vs strong baselines.  
- Pros: novel principled formulation with code, strong cross-domain evaluation, clear evidence of decreasing crowd dependence.  
- Cons: clarity and notation problems, limited incremental benefit from continuous time vs simple baseline, incomplete/incorrect reporting and missing baseline comparisons.  
- Recommendation: Accept conditional on addressing clarity, notation, and reporting issues.
