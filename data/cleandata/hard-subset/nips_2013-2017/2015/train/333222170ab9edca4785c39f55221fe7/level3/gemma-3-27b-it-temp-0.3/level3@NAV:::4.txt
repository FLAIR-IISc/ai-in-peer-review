## Review of “On-the-Job Learning with Bayesian Decision Theory”

This paper proposes a novel framework, “on-the-job learning,” for deploying AI systems with zero initial training data. The core idea is to strategically query a crowd to resolve uncertainty during prediction, balancing cost, latency, and accuracy using Bayesian decision theory and Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS). The approach is framed as a stochastic game, allowing for adaptive querying as the model improves. The authors demonstrate promising results on named entity recognition, sentiment classification, and image classification, achieving significant cost reductions compared to full annotation and improvements over online learning.

The work builds upon existing research in online learning, active learning, and crowdsourcing, but distinguishes itself by focusing on a setting where *both* model learning and prediction happen concurrently, and by explicitly modeling the trade-offs between cost, latency, and accuracy within a Bayesian game-theoretic framework. The use of MCTS to approximate the optimal querying policy is a clever approach to address the intractability of the problem. The open-source implementation (LENSE) is a valuable contribution.

However, several weaknesses need addressing. The application to face recognition is not clearly explained – was the entire instance queried, or only specific regions? In non-structured settings like sentiment classification and image classification, the model’s ability to propagate information across examples seems limited, potentially undermining the effectiveness of the proposed utility function. The threshold baseline relies on two parameters with unclear origins and sensitivity analyses; their justification is lacking. A comparison against an uncertainty-sampling baseline (common in active learning) would strengthen the evaluation. 

Furthermore, the distinction from active online learning is weak. Active learning could be adapted to this sequential setting by treating the input sequence as the active set and updating the model based on responses. The framework appears novel, but details regarding the implementation and scalability are somewhat opaque. While the results are encouraging, the applicability of this approach seems limited to tasks where selective querying is feasible and cost-effective.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel framework for on-the-job learning with a principled Bayesian approach.
*   Promising experimental results demonstrating cost reduction and accuracy improvements.
*   Open-source implementation available.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Unclear application to certain tasks (e.g., face recognition).
*   Potential limitations in non-structured settings.
*   Weak justification for baseline parameters and lack of uncertainty-sampling baseline.
*   Limited discussion of scalability and broader applicability.



**Overall:** This paper presents an interesting and potentially valuable contribution. However, the authors need to address the weaknesses outlined above to strengthen the technical soundness and demonstrate the broader impact of their work. I recommend a borderline accept, contingent on addressing the reviewer concerns.
