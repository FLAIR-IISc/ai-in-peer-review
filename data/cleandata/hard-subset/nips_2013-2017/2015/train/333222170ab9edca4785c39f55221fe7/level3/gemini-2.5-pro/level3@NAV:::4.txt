This paper introduces "on-the-job learning," a framework for deploying a high-accuracy system that starts with zero training examples. The system uses real-time crowdsourcing to resolve uncertainty for incoming inputs, making predictions only when confident. The model improves over time, gradually reducing its reliance on human queries. The authors formulate this problem as a stochastic game using Bayesian decision theory to balance accuracy, cost, and latency, and use Monte Carlo Tree Search for policy approximation. This work connects ideas from online learning, active learning, and crowdsourcing, but is novel in its focus on maintaining high accuracy from the very first instance rather than just optimizing model learning.

The paper's main strength is its originality and potential significance. The on-the-job learning paradigm addresses the important practical problem of deploying AI systems without large upfront data collection costs. The formulation is principled, and the empirical results are compelling, showing over an order-of-magnitude cost reduction on the NER task. The public release of the code and data is another significant strength that enhances reproducibility.

However, the paper has several weaknesses that temper its contribution.
*   **Clarity:** The application to face recognition is unclear. While the framework is well-described for structured tasks like NER where parts of an instance can be queried, it is not explained how this translates to image classification. This ambiguity hinders the reader's ability to understand and reproduce the results for that task.
*   **Quality:** The experimental evaluation could be strengthened. The threshold baseline uses two seemingly arbitrary parameters whose origin and sensitivity are not reported, weakening it as a point of comparison. Furthermore, a standard uncertainty-sampling baseline from the active learning literature should be included to better contextualize the performance of the proposed method.
*   **Applicability & Originality:** The proposed utility function seems most appropriate for structured settings where information propagates within an example. For non-structured tasks, its benefit is less clear. Additionally, the distinction from online active learning is not as sharp as claimed; one could adapt active learning to this setting by treating each new instance as part of the active set to be queried before prediction.

In conclusion, the paper presents a novel and potentially impactful framework. The core idea is sound and the results are promising. However, the manuscript requires significant revision to clarify experimental details, strengthen its baselines, and better position its contribution relative to existing online active learning paradigms.
