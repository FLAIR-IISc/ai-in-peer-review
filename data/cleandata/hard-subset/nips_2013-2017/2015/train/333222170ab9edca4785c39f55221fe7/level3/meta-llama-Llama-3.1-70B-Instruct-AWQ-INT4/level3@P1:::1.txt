This paper proposes a novel framework for on-the-job learning, which combines crowdsourcing with machine learning to achieve high accuracy from the start without requiring a trained model. The authors model the on-the-job setting as a stochastic game and use ideas from game playing to approximate the optimal policy. The framework is evaluated on three tasks: named-entity recognition, sentiment classification, and image classification.

The main contribution of this paper is a more theoretically grounded formulation of an "on the job learner" from [22]. The authors provide a clear and concise presentation of their approach, which starts by queuing the crowd and gradually transitions to self-prediction, deciding queries via Monte Carlo Tree Search. The empirical results show that the proposed approach outperforms machine and human-only comparisons on both quality and cost.

However, there are some limitations and areas for improvement. The presentation is generally good, but it is missing some important technical details. For example, the authors do not provide a clear explanation of the response model pR(r | yq) and how it is used in the utility function. Additionally, the empirical results are limited to a comparison with a threshold-based heuristic, which may not be the most informative baseline.

The authors also need to provide more clarification on some of the technical details, such as the definition of N(s) in Algorithm 1 and the justification for the 0.3 and 0.88 threshold values in the baseline. Furthermore, it would be useful to provide more analysis on the "online" systems used in the experiments, such as error analysis and example cases, to better understand the gains achieved by the proposed approach.

Overall, the approach is reasonable and merits further investigation. The theoretical framing of the crowd→model transition is useful, but the empirical and explanatory gaps limit the conclusions that can be drawn from this work. With some additional clarification and analysis, this paper has the potential to make a significant contribution to the field of on-the-job learning.
