

# Review for NIPS 2015

This paper introduces "on-the-job learning," a novel framework for building accurate AI systems starting from zero labeled examples. The authors model the problem as a stochastic game based on Bayesian decision theory, where the system strategically queries crowdsourcing for uncertain parts of input while gradually improving its model. The key innovation lies in balancing latency, cost, and accuracy through a principled decision-theoretic approach, rather than relying on static active learning policies. The authors implement their approach as LENSE (Learning from Expensive Noisy Slow Experts) and evaluate it on three tasks: named-entity recognition, sentiment classification, and image classification.

The paper builds upon several strands of previous work. It relates to online learning [7] but differs by maintaining high accuracy from the start rather than asymptotically. It extends active classification [11] by allowing the model to improve during test time rather than using a static policy. It also connects to crowdsourcing work like Flock [25] and TurKontrol [26], but uniquely integrates these with structured prediction and real-time decision-making.

**Quality:** The paper presents a technically sound framework with a clear theoretical foundation in Bayesian decision theory. The experimental evaluation demonstrates significant improvements over relevant baselines, particularly on the NER task (88.4% F1 with more than an order of magnitude cost reduction compared to 5-vote human labeling). However, the paper has some quality issues: the inconsistent labeling of the threshold baseline in Tables 2 and 3 as "entropic" versus "threshold" is confusing and undermines the results. The example at Line 154 is abstract and would benefit from a concrete illustration with realistic crowd responses and timing. Additionally, the paper doesn't adequately explain why LENSE underperforms the 5-vote human baseline (Table 2) while outperforming the 3-vote baseline, which is crucial for understanding the tradeoffs.

**Clarity:** The problem formulation is well-motivated, and the core idea of balancing accuracy, cost, and latency is compelling. However, the paper suffers from several clarity issues. The related work is placed in Section 5 rather than a dedicated section, making it difficult to understand how this work relates to prior contributions. Line 086 references "Figure ??" without a proper figure reference. The explanation of the game tree and MCTS implementation is somewhat opaque, particularly the handling of continuous time with progressive widening. A clearer exposition of the algorithm's behavior would greatly improve understanding.

**Originality:** The paper makes a significant contribution by combining several existing concepts (structured prediction, crowdsourcing, active learning) in a novel way specifically designed for on-the-job deployment. The use of Bayesian decision theory to balance latency, cost, and accuracy, and the application of MCTS to approximate the optimal policy, are particularly novel. While Legion:AR [6] addresses a similar problem, this paper provides the first formal machine learning foundation for on-the-job learning, making it substantially more original than prior work.

**Significance:** The problem addressed—building accurate AI systems with zero labeled examples—is highly significant for real-world deployment scenarios. The reported cost reductions (more than an order of magnitude for NER) and accuracy improvements (8% F1 over single-human labeling, 28% over online learning) are compelling. The framework's ability to gradually reduce reliance on crowdsourcing while maintaining accuracy is particularly valuable for practical applications. However, the significance would be stronger if the paper more clearly explained how the approach scales to larger problems or more complex tasks.

**Pro and Con Acceptance Arguments:**

*Pro:*
- Addresses an important real-world problem (building accurate systems with zero labeled examples)
- Presents a principled theoretical framework based on Bayesian decision theory
- Demonstrates significant cost reductions (more than an order of magnitude) while maintaining accuracy
- Introduces a novel application of MCTS to on-the-job learning
- Provides an open-source implementation (LENSE) and comprehensive experimental results

*Con:*
- Inconsistent labeling of baselines in tables undermines experimental clarity
- Some key explanations (e.g., the Line 154 example) are abstract and hard to follow
- Related work section placement is confusing and poorly integrated
- Doesn't adequately explain the tradeoff between accuracy and number of crowd responses
- Missing figure reference (Line 086) indicates incomplete manuscript

Overall, this is a strong paper with significant potential impact. The core idea is compelling, the theoretical foundation is sound, and the experimental results demonstrate meaningful improvements over baselines. The main weaknesses are primarily in presentation and clarity rather than in the core contribution. With minor revisions to address the clarity issues and inconsistencies, this paper would be an excellent contribution to NIPS.

The paper makes a valuable contribution to the field by providing a principled approach to on-the-job learning that balances multiple objectives in a way that previous approaches cannot. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to improve clarity and presentation.
