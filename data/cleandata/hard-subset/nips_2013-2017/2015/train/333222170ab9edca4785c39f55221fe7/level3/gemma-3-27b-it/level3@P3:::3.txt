This paper proposes LENSE, a novel on-the-job learning framework that dynamically balances querying a crowd for labels and relying on an internally trained model. The core idea – starting with heavy reliance on crowdsourcing and gradually transitioning to self-sufficiency – is compelling and addresses a practical challenge in deploying AI systems with limited initial labeled data. Framing this as a stochastic game with Bayesian decision theory provides a principled approach to balancing accuracy, latency, and cost. The use of MCTS and TD learning to approximate the intractable optimal policy is a reasonable strategy.

The work is original and significant. The problem of deploying systems without upfront training is important, and the proposed approach offers a potentially effective solution. The experimental results on NER, sentiment, and face ID demonstrate the promise of the approach, particularly the cost reduction observed on NER. The open-source release of the code is a valuable contribution. This paper is likely to stimulate further research in on-the-job learning and related areas. I recommend acceptance, but with several revisions detailed below.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The framework of on-the-job learning as a stochastic game is a new and interesting perspective.
*   **Practical Relevance:** Addresses a crucial problem of deploying AI systems in resource-constrained environments.
*   **Sound Methodology:** The use of Bayesian decision theory, MCTS, and TD learning is well-motivated and provides a strong theoretical foundation.
*   **Positive Empirical Results:** Demonstrates cost reductions and performance improvements, especially on NER.
*   **Open Source Release:** Facilitates reproducibility and further research.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Clarity:** The introduction of continuous time in Section 3 significantly hinders readability and understanding. The explanation is dense and the connection to the overall framework isn’t immediately clear.
*   **Limited Benefit of Continuous Time:** The experimental results suggest the continuous-time extension yields limited gains, raising questions about the complexity it adds.
*   **Reporting Errors:** The claim of surpassing human performance on NER (5-vote) is incorrect. 5-vote results are missing for sentiment and face ID in several tables and figures.
*   **Presentation Issues:**  Key results (best performance) and latency values aren’t consistently bolded, hindering quick interpretation. The threshold baseline is missing from sentiment experiments.
*   **Ambiguity in Experimental Setup:** The use of `t_deadline` and the criteria for reporting answers (entropic threshold vs. time-based) are not fully clarified.
*   **Notational Issues:** Equation (2) should include `s` in the distribution `p(y,r,t|x,q,s)`. Equation (5) uses `q` for a distribution function, which is confusing.
*   **Potential Typo:** The uncertainty expression in the threshold baseline description appears incorrect.

**Detailed Comments & Recommendations:**

1.  **Restructure for Clarity:** Present the core method *without* continuous time first.  Treat the continuous-time extension as an addition or future work. This will significantly improve comprehension.  A clearer explanation of the core stochastic game and the UCT/TD learning approximation is essential.

2.  **Re-evaluate Continuous Time:** Justify the inclusion of continuous time more convincingly. If the benefits are marginal, consider removing it to simplify the presentation.

3.  **Correct Reporting Errors:**
    *   Remove the incorrect claim of surpassing human performance in NER.
    *   Add 5-vote majority baseline results to Tables 3 and 4, and corresponding figures for sentiment and face ID.
    *   Include the threshold baseline in the sentiment experiments (Table 3 and Figure 4).

4.  **Clarify Experimental Details:** Explain how `t_deadline` was used in the experiments and the specific criteria used for reporting answers (e.g., was it based on an entropic threshold, or a fixed time limit?).

5.  **Address Notational Issues:**
    *   Correct Equation (2) to include `s`: `p(y,r,t|x,q,s)`.
    *   Replace `q` in Equation (5) with a different variable to avoid confusion.

6.  **Review Threshold Baseline Description:** Double-check the uncertainty expression/inequality in the threshold baseline description to ensure it accurately reflects decreasing uncertainty with increasing `m`.

7.  **Formatting:**  Bold the best results in tables and relevant latency values for improved readability.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper addresses a significant and practical problem.
*   The proposed approach is novel and theoretically sound.
*   The experimental results are promising, demonstrating both cost reduction and accuracy improvements.
*   The open-source release of the code facilitates reproducibility.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The lack of clarity, particularly regarding continuous time, hinders understanding.
*   The limited empirical benefit of continuous time raises concerns about its added complexity.
*   The reporting errors and presentation issues detract from the paper's overall quality.



Addressing these weaknesses would significantly strengthen the paper and enhance its impact on the research community. Despite these issues, the originality and potential significance of the work warrant acceptance, contingent upon satisfactory revisions.
