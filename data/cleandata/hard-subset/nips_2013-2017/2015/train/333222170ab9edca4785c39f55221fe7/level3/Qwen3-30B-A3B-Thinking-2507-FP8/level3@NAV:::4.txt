

This paper proposes "on-the-job learning," a framework for deploying high-accuracy AI systems with zero initial training examples by strategically using real-time crowdsourcing to resolve uncertainty during prediction. The authors model the problem as a stochastic game using Bayesian decision theory, balancing latency, cost, and accuracy, and approximate the optimal policy using Monte Carlo Tree Search. The approach is evaluated on named-entity recognition, sentiment classification, and image classification tasks, showing significant cost reduction compared to full human annotation and performance improvements over baseline methods.

The paper demonstrates several strengths: the problem formulation is well-motivated, addressing a real-world need for systems that require immediate deployment without large labeled datasets. The Bayesian decision theory framework provides a principled approach to balancing competing objectives. The implementation is thorough, with code and data made publicly available, and the empirical results are compelling on the NER task.

However, several critical weaknesses limit the paper's impact. The application to face recognition (image classification) is unclear—was the entire image queried or specific features? The utility function appears designed for structured prediction (NER), but the paper lacks discussion of its applicability to non-structured settings where information propagation across examples is limited. The threshold baseline uses arbitrary parameters (0.3m ≥ 0.98) without justification or sensitivity analysis. The authors omit a standard uncertainty-sampling baseline from active learning for comparison, making it difficult to assess the novelty of their query strategy. The distinction from active online learning is weak; the paper fails to explain why standard active learning approaches cannot be adapted to this setting by treating the input stream as the active set.

While the framework shows promise for structured prediction tasks, the limited applicability, unclear details, and methodological gaps weaken the contribution. The paper's significance is constrained by these issues, despite the interesting problem and strong NER results. The authors should clarify the face recognition implementation, justify baseline parameters, add a standard active learning baseline, and better differentiate their approach from existing active learning methods to strengthen the paper's contribution.
