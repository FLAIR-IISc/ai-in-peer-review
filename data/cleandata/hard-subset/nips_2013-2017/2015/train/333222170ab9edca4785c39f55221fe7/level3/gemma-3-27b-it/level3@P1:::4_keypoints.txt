- Proposal: Introduces On-the-Job Learning (OTJ), combining real-time crowdsourcing with Bayesian decision theory, modeled as a stochastic game and approximated with MCTS to trade off latency, cost, and accuracy.
- Empirical evaluation: Tested on NER, sentiment, and image classification, showing large cost reductions vs full annotation and improvements over online learning and single-label baselines.
- Novelty: Framing on-the-job learning as a stochastic game with a principled decision-theoretic objective is a novel contribution.
- Strong results: Particularly on NER, reports an order-of-magnitude cost reduction and notable performance gains.
- Motivation: Clearly addresses deploying AI under scarce labeled data and resource constraints.
- Reproducibility: Open-source LENSE implementation is provided.
- Limited applicability: Approach may be best suited to structured prediction tasks and less effective on non-structured problems.
- Face experiment ambiguity: Unclear what was queried to the crowd (whole image vs specific features/regions), hindering assessment.
- Utility function concern: The proposed utility may not propagate information across examples in non-structured tasks, reducing Bayesian benefits.
- Threshold baseline sensitivity: Baseline uses arbitrary parameters (0.3, 0.98) without justification or sensitivity analysis.
- Missing baseline: Evaluation omits a standard uncertainty-sampling active learning baseline for direct comparison.
- Distinction from active online learning: The claimed difference is weak; streaming active learning could be adapted similarly, so advantages of the game-theoretic view need clearer justification.
- Clarity issues: Additional details are needed on the MCTS implementation and the crowd response model.
- Arguments for acceptance: Novel principled framework, strong empirical evidence (especially NER), and open-source code.
- Arguments for rejection: Potentially limited applicability, missing key baseline and sensitivity analysis, and an unconvincing distinction from existing methods.
- Overall assessment: Above-average and recommend acceptance conditional on clarifying the face experiment, justifying baseline parameters, adding uncertainty-sampling comparisons, and discussing applicability to non-structured data.
