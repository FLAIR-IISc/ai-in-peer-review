This paper introduces "on-the-job learning," a novel framework for deploying high-accuracy systems from scratch with no initial training data. The system operates by querying a crowd of human workers in real-time to resolve uncertainty for incoming data points. As the system gathers labeled data from these interactions, it trains an underlying model (a CRF in this case) online, gradually reducing its reliance on the crowd.

The core contribution is framing this problem as a stochastic game between the system and the crowd, grounded in Bayesian decision theory. The system's actions (querying, waiting, or returning a prediction) are chosen to maximize a utility function that balances prediction accuracy, monetary cost, and latency. Since computing the optimal policy is intractable due to the large state space and continuous time variables (query and response times), the authors propose an approximation based on Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) combined with progressive widening. This work relates to and differentiates itself from online learning, active learning, and active classification by its ability to actively seek information before making a prediction for each instance and dynamically improve its querying policy at test time.

### Strengths
*   **Originality and Significance:** The paper addresses a highly practical and significant problem: how to build an accurate system when a large, pre-labeled dataset is unavailable. The "on-the-job" paradigm, where a system bootstraps its own performance by intelligently using a human-in-the-loop, is a compelling and valuable contribution. This work is likely to inspire follow-up research and could have a substantial impact on how ML systems are deployed in resource-constrained settings.
*   **Principled Formulation:** The use of Bayesian decision theory to model the trade-offs is elegant and principled. Casting the problem as a stochastic game provides a solid theoretical foundation for making decisions about when and what to query, going beyond simple heuristics.
*   **Strong Empirical Evaluation:** The authors validate their system, LENSE, on three distinct tasks (NER, sentiment analysis, and image classification), demonstrating the generality of the framework. The experiments clearly show that the system learns over time, reducing its query cost while maintaining high accuracy. The inclusion of multiple baselines, including human-only, online learning, and a heuristic-based method, provides a solid context for the results.

### Weaknesses
*   **Clarity and Organization:** The paper's main weakness is its clarity. The core concepts are quite complex, and the immediate introduction of continuous time (query/response delays) makes the model and algorithm difficult to follow. The exposition would be significantly improved by first presenting the fundamental stochastic game model in a simpler, discrete-time setting. The continuous-time aspect, which appears to provide limited experimental benefit, could then be introduced as an extension. This restructuring would make the paper's core ideas more accessible.
*   **Justification for Complexity:** The experimental results do not provide a strong justification for the full complexity of the proposed MCTS-based model (LENSE). The simpler "threshold" baseline performs surprisingly well across tasks, sometimes achieving comparable accuracy with lower latency. The paper should include a more direct discussion of the trade-offs between LENSE and this strong heuristic baseline to better motivate the need for the more complex game-playing machinery.
*   **Inaccuracies in Experimental Reporting:** There are several issues with how the results are reported, which undermines confidence in the conclusions.
    *   The abstract's claim of "boosting performance relative to the expert provided labels" is misleading. Table 2 shows that the 5-vote human majority (F1 91.0%) outperforms LENSE (F1 88.4%) on the NER task. This claim should be revised to be more precise (e.g., outperforming a single human annotator).
    *   Results for the 5-vote baseline appear to be missing for the Sentiment and Faces tasks in Table 3. This is a crucial baseline for comparison, and its absence makes it difficult to fully assess performance.
    *   The threshold baseline is missing from the sentiment analysis results in Table 3 and from the cost-over-time plot in Figure 4.
    *   Minor formatting errors exist, such as not bolding the best F1 score for NER (5-vote) or the lowest latency for Faces (Threshold) in Table 2.

### Detailed Comments and Questions
1.  **Threshold Baseline:** The description of the threshold baseline seems to have a typo. The inequality `(1-p_theta(y_i|x)) * 0.3^m >= 0.98` suggests that uncertainty increases with more queries `m`, which is counter-intuitive. Please clarify this heuristic.
2.  **Termination Condition:** It is unclear how the system decides when to stop querying and return a result. Is there an implicit `t_deadline` that forces a decision, or is it based on a confidence threshold? The utility function includes a latency penalty `t_now * w_T`, but the mechanism for the `return` action is not fully specified in the experimental setup.
3.  **Notational Issues:**
    *   The definition of utility in Equation (1) conditions on `p(y | x,q, s, r, t)`, but the environment model in Equation (3) is `p(y, r, t | x,q, s)`. It seems the conditioning set in Eq (1) should be `x,q,s,r`, as `t` is part of the outcome. Please check for consistency.
    *   In Algorithm 1, `V(sigma)` and `N(sigma)` are defined in line 1, but line 5 uses `V(sigma')/N(sigma')` where `sigma'` has not been visited yet, so `N(sigma')` would be 0. Please clarify the UCT update rule for unvisited nodes. (Typically, they are assigned an initial value of infinity to encourage exploration).

In summary, this paper introduces an original and important problem setting and proposes a well-founded, principled solution. The primary weaknesses are related to clarity of presentation and issues in experimental reporting. I believe the work is a strong contribution to the field and recommend acceptance, with the strong suggestion that the authors revise the paper to address the issues of clarity and reporting accuracy mentioned above. A clearer presentation would significantly increase the paper's impact and adoption.
