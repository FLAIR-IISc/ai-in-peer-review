This paper introduces "on-the-job learning," a framework for deploying a high-accuracy classification system starting with zero labeled training examples. The setting involves a stream of inputs where, for each input, the system can make real-time queries to a crowd of human labelers to resolve uncertainty before committing to a final prediction. As the system observes more crowd responses, its internal model improves, gradually reducing its reliance on the crowd. This paradigm is distinct from standard online or active learning, which typically learn from labels *after* a prediction has been made and thus cannot guarantee high accuracy from the start.

The authors formally model this problem as a stochastic game between the system and the crowd, using Bayesian decision theory to create a utility function that balances accuracy, monetary cost, and latency. Since computing the optimal policy for this game is intractable, they propose an approximation based on Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS), adapted with progressive widening to handle the continuous nature of time. The framework is evaluated on three tasks: named-entity recognition (NER), sentiment classification, and image classification, demonstrating significant cost reductions compared to purely human-powered approaches and significant accuracy gains over standard online learning.

**Quality:**
The paper is technically sound. The formulation of the problem as a stochastic game is a principled and appropriate way to model the sequential decision-making process under uncertainty. The use of MCTS is a well-founded approach for approximating the intractable policy. The experimental setup is thorough, with evaluations on three distinct tasks using real-time crowdsourcing infrastructure. The comparisons against multiple baselines, including purely human-based methods and a standard online learner, are well-chosen and effectively highlight the benefits of the proposed approach.

One point that could be explored further is the trade-off with high-consensus human labeling. In Table 2, the proposed LENSE system outperforms the 3-vote human baseline on the NER task but underperforms the 5-vote baseline in F1 score. While LENSE achieves this with significantly lower cost, this result highlights an interesting trade-off. It suggests that for tasks where extremely high accuracy is paramount and cost is less of a concern, simply increasing human consensus may still be a competitive strategy. A brief discussion on the regimes where each approach (LENSE vs. high-consensus voting) is preferable would strengthen the analysis.

**Clarity:**
While the overall problem is compelling, the explanations are at times opaque and could be improved to make the work more accessible.
- The example described from Line 154 onwards and depicted in Figure 2 is abstract. A more concrete, step-by-step example showing a specific query (e.g., "Is 'George' a LOCATION?"), a realistic crowd response (e.g., "Yes"), the time it took (e.g., 1.2s), and how this single piece of information updates the model's posterior would greatly clarify the system's mechanics.
- The paper has some minor inconsistencies that create confusion. The heuristic baseline is referred to as "entropic baseline" in Table 2 but as "threshold baseline" in the main text and Table 3. These should be harmonized.
- A figure reference appears to be missing on Line 086 ("Figure ??").
- The organization is unusual. Placing the Related Work section (Section 6) after the Experiments section disrupts the logical flow. This section would be more effective if positioned after the introduction to contextualize the work upfront.

**Originality:**
The paper presents a novel and valuable contribution. While components like learning from noisy crowds, active learning, and MCTS are individually well-established, their combination within the proposed "on-the-job" setting is new. The key originality lies in addressing the problem of maintaining high performance from the very first instance by actively seeking information *before* prediction. This distinguishes the work from most online and active learning literature. Furthermore, the explicit modeling and optimization of latency as a first-class citizen in the decision-making process is a novel and important aspect for real-time, human-in-the-loop systems. The work significantly advances concepts from active classification by moving from a static, pre-learned policy to one that is dynamically learned and refined at test time.

**Significance:**
The work is highly significant. It addresses a critical and practical problem in deploying machine learning systems: the cold start problem where no labeled data is available. The proposed framework provides a principled and effective solution that balances the costs and benefits of human intervention. The results, especially the order-of-magnitude cost reduction on the NER task, are impressive and demonstrate clear practical utility. Researchers and practitioners working on interactive systems, human-in-the-loop AI, and crowdsourcing are very likely to build upon these ideas. The release of the open-source LENSE system is a commendable step that will foster reproducibility and encourage adoption.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
1.  **Novel Problem Formulation:** The "on-the-job learning" setting is a novel and practically important paradigm that extends beyond traditional active and online learning.
2.  **Principled Approach:** The use of Bayesian decision theory and MCTS provides a solid theoretical foundation for balancing the complex trade-offs of accuracy, cost, and latency.
3.  **Strong Empirical Results:** The experiments demonstrate significant, real-world benefits, achieving high accuracy with drastically reduced costs compared to sensible baselines.
4.  **High Potential Impact:** The problem is of broad interest, and the proposed solution is general enough to be applicable to a wide range of tasks requiring real-time, human-assisted AI.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
1.  **Clarity Issues:** The paper's clarity could be substantially improved. The core mechanism is difficult to grasp from the provided examples, and the paper's structure is unconventional.
2.  **Limited Discussion of Trade-offs:** The comparison against the 5-vote human baseline raises important questions about the limits of the approach that are not fully discussed.

Overall, the strengths of this paper, particularly its novelty and significance, far outweigh its weaknesses, which are primarily related to presentation and could be addressed in a revision. The paper introduces an important new direction for building interactive, learning-based systems.
