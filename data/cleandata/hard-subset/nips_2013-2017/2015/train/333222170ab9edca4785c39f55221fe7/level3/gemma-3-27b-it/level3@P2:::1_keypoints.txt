- Introduces "on-the-job learning": systems start without pre-training and query a crowd strategically to become self-sufficient.  
- Formalizes the problem as a stochastic game using Bayesian decision theory and approximates the optimal policy with MCTS.  
- Evaluates LENSE on NER, sentiment, and image classification, showing large cost reductions vs full human annotation and improvements over baselines.  
- Addresses a relevant problem: deploying accurate AI with limited initial labeled data or crowdsourcing budget.  
- Differentiates from online learning, active learning, and active classification by maintaining high initial accuracy and adapting test-time query policy.  
- Strength: novel, principled formulation balancing latency, cost, and accuracy.  
- Strength: practical MCTS-based approximation that makes the approach implementable.  
- Strength: strong empirical results, especially substantial cost and performance gains on NER.  
- Strength: open-source LENSE implementation improves reproducibility.  
- Strength: clear motivation and problem statement.  
- Weakness: utility function (Eq.1) and conditioning on s in Eq.2 are under-specified; disappearance of y in p_R(r_i | y, q_i) unexplained.  
- Weakness: threshold baseline choices (0.3, 0.88) lack principled justification or sensitivity analysis.  
- Weakness: online learning baseline is not well characterized—unclear if models are the best for each task, complicating attribution of gains.  
- Weakness: limited analysis of error cases and lack of illustrative examples to explain where LENSE succeeds/fails.  
- Weakness: Algorithm 1 omits definition of N(σ), reducing clarity of the MCTS implementation.  
- Argument for acceptance: novel, theoretically sound approach with effective practical results and open-source code.  
- Argument against acceptance: incomplete technical details and limited empirical analysis hinder full evaluation; baseline comparisons could be stronger.  
- Overall: promising contribution recommending acceptance with minor revisions to clarify technical details, justify baselines, and add error analysis.
