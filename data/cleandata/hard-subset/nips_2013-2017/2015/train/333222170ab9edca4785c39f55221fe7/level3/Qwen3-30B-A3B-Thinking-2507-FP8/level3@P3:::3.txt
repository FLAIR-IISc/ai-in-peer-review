

# Review of "On-the-Job Learning with Bayesian Decision Theory"

This paper proposes a novel framework for on-the-job learning that balances crowdsourcing and machine learning to achieve high accuracy from the start without requiring a large labeled dataset. The authors model the problem as a stochastic game between a system and a crowd, using Bayesian decision theory to optimize a utility function that trades off accuracy, latency, and cost. The core innovation lies in using Monte Carlo Tree Search with progressive widening to approximate the optimal query strategy in a continuous-time setting.

The paper makes several significant contributions. First, it formulates a principled approach to on-the-job learning that gradually reduces crowd dependency as the model improves. Second, it introduces a continuous-time model that captures realistic response latencies, which is a substantial extension over previous work on active learning. Third, it provides thorough empirical validation across three distinct tasks (NER, sentiment, and face-ID), showing consistent improvements over baselines. The framework, named LENSE, achieves an order of magnitude cost reduction compared to full human annotation on NER while boosting performance relative to expert labels.

The work builds upon and extends several key areas: active learning (which typically assumes a static policy), online learning (which doesn't actively seek information prior to prediction), and crowdsourcing (which typically doesn't incorporate feedback into the model). The authors make a strong case for their approach being distinct from Legion:AR [6], which operates in a similar setting but lacks the theoretical foundation.

Strengths include the clear problem formulation, the elegant integration of Bayesian decision theory with game-theoretic reasoning, and the thorough experimental evaluation. The paper demonstrates that the system requires decreasing numbers of crowd labels over time and attains high initial accuracy by leveraging crowd expertise when uncertainty is high. The implementation is open-sourced, enhancing reproducibility.

However, the paper has significant clarity issues that undermine its impact. The introduction of continuous time in Section 3 is particularly confusing, making the algorithmic details difficult to follow. The continuous-time extension appears to yield limited experimental benefit, as the threshold baseline performs nearly as well and sometimes with lower latency. The authors would be better served by presenting the core method without continuous time first and treating continuous time as an extension, which would improve clarity and adoption.

Several reporting errors further weaken the paper. The claim of "beating human performance" for the 5-vote NER is incorrect (the 5-vote majority is superior). Critical results are missing for Face-ID and Sentiment (e.g., 5-vote results in several tables/figures), and the threshold baseline is missing from sentiment experiments in Table 3 and Figure 4. Notation inconsistencies also plague the paper, including equation (2) missing s in the distribution p(y,r,t|x,q,s) and equation (5) using q for a distribution function.

The paper's significance is substantial, as it presents a practical solution to a common problem in deploying AI systems with limited labeled data. The approach could be valuable for real-world applications like disaster response or social media monitoring. The work is original and likely to spur discussion and adoption in the field.

Pro and Con for Acceptance:

**Pro:**
- Novel formulation of on-the-job learning as a stochastic game with Bayesian decision theory
- Strong empirical results across three distinct tasks with meaningful cost reductions
- Open-source implementation enhances reproducibility
- Addresses an important practical problem in real-world AI deployment

**Con:**
- Major clarity issues, especially in the continuous-time treatment
- Continuous-time extension appears to provide limited benefit compared to simpler baseline
- Critical reporting errors and omissions weaken claims
- Inconsistent notation and unclear algorithmic details

While the paper presents an interesting and potentially valuable approach, the clarity issues and reporting errors significantly undermine its impact. The continuous-time extension adds complexity without clear justification given the threshold baseline's strong performance. The authors should address these issues before publication.

I recommend acceptance with major revisions. The work is significant, original, and well-motivated, but requires substantial improvements in clarity, presentation, and reporting accuracy to fully realize its potential. The authors should present the core method without continuous time first and treat the continuous-time extension as a refinement. They should also correct all reporting errors and clarify the relationship between the threshold baseline and the main approach.
