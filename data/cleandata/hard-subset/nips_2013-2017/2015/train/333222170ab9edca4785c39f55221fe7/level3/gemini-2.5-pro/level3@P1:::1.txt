This paper addresses the problem of "on-the-job learning," where a system must make accurate predictions from the very first input, despite having no initial training data. The core idea is to start by relying on real-time crowdsourcing to resolve uncertainty and gradually transition to a self-sufficient machine learning model as more labeled data is acquired. This work formalizes the problem, previously explored in systems like Legion:AR [6], within the principled framework of Bayesian decision theory. The authors model the interaction between the system and the crowd as a stochastic game, where the system's policy aims to maximize a utility function that balances prediction accuracy, monetary cost, and latency. Since computing the optimal policy is intractable, the paper proposes an approximation using Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) combined with progressive widening to handle the continuous nature of time. The approach is evaluated on NER, sentiment analysis, and image classification tasks, demonstrating significant cost savings compared to a pure crowdsourcing approach and accuracy improvements over a standard online learning baseline.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Quality:** The paper presents a technically sound and complete piece of work. The formulation of on-the-job learning as a stochastic game is well-motivated, and the choice of MCTS is a reasonable and established method for approximating policies in such large state spaces. The experiments are conducted on three distinct tasks, providing a good breadth of evaluation. The inclusion of an open-source implementation is a commendable step towards reproducibility.

However, the empirical evaluation has some weaknesses that temper the conclusions. The primary concern is the comparison to the "Threshold baseline." While the proposed LENSE system outperforms this heuristic, the margin of improvement on the Sentiment and Faces tasks is not substantial (e.g., F1 of 69.1 vs 68.3 on Sentiment). This raises questions about the practical necessity of the complex MCTS framework when a much simpler, albeit less principled, heuristic performs nearly as well. A more in-depth analysis of *why* and *when* LENSE makes better decisions would strengthen the paper's claims. Furthermore, the strength of the "Online learning" baseline is not well-established. It is described as the "best possible offline system" because it trains on gold labels, but it is unclear if the underlying models (e.g., CRF for NER) are state-of-the-art for these tasks. If the base ML models are weak, a large portion of the performance gains may simply come from the ability to query humans at test time, rather than from the sophisticated decision-making policy itself.

**Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The problem formulation is introduced clearly, and the motivation is compelling. The high-level description of the model and the MCTS-based algorithm is easy to follow.

However, several important technical details are missing, which hinders a full understanding and reproducibility of the work.
- In Section 3, the utility definition in Eq. (1) and its components could be more precise. For instance, the expectation in `ExpAcc` is over `p(y | x, q, s, r, t)`. It would be clearer to condition the left-hand side on these variables as well.
- In the environment model (Eq. 3), the response model is `pR(ri | yqi)`. The text states this is based on `yq`, the true answer for the query `q`. It should be clarified why the dependency is only on the label at position `qi` and not the full label sequence `y`, as structured prediction correlations might influence worker responses.
- In Algorithm 1, `N(σ)` is used in the UCT rule but is only defined in a comment as "visits." A more formal definition in the main text would be helpful for clarity.
- The description of the threshold baseline lacks justification for its hyperparameters. The choice of 0.3 as an uncertainty reduction factor and the 0.98 confidence threshold seem arbitrary. An explanation of how these values were chosen (e.g., via a validation set) is necessary to assess the fairness of this comparison.

**Originality:** The core concept of on-the-job learning has been explored before (e.g., [6]). The main originality of this paper lies in its rigorous and principled formulation of the problem. Casting the decision-making process as a stochastic game solved with MCTS is a novel approach in this specific context of balancing accuracy, cost, and latency with a live crowd. This provides a more theoretically grounded foundation compared to previous heuristic-based systems. The work effectively synthesizes ideas from active classification, online learning, and crowdsourcing in a new and interesting way.

**Significance:** The problem addressed is of high practical importance. The ability to deploy accurate systems without a large upfront investment in data labeling would be transformative for many applications. The theoretical framework presented is valuable and could inspire further research into principled methods for human-in-the-loop ML systems. However, the significance of the empirical results is somewhat limited by the weaknesses in the evaluation. For the work to have a broader impact, it needs to more convincingly demonstrate that the proposed complex decision-theoretic approach offers substantial benefits over simpler, well-tuned heuristics. A deeper analysis, including qualitative examples of query strategies or an error analysis comparing LENSE to the threshold baseline, would significantly enhance the paper's impact by providing concrete insights into the value of the proposed method.

### Summary and Arguments

In summary, this paper presents a valuable and novel formalization of the "on-the-job learning" problem. The framing as a stochastic game is elegant, and the MCTS-based solution is technically sound. While the idea is promising, the empirical evidence is not as strong as it could be, particularly concerning the modest gains over a simple heuristic baseline and the lack of justification for some experimental choices.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
1.  Provides a novel and principled Bayesian decision-theoretic formulation for the important and practical problem of on-the-job learning.
2.  The approach of modeling the system-crowd interaction as a stochastic game and using MCTS is a strong and original technical contribution.
3.  The problem itself is highly significant, and this work provides a solid foundation for future research in real-time, human-in-the-loop systems.
4.  The system is open-sourced, facilitating reproducibility and follow-up work.

**Arguments for Rejection:**
1.  The empirical advantage of the complex MCTS approach over a simple, tuned heuristic baseline is not overwhelmingly demonstrated across all tasks.
2.  The paper lacks critical details regarding baseline implementations (e.g., hyperparameter justification) and could benefit from stronger ML baselines to better isolate the contribution of the query policy.
3.  The lack of qualitative analysis or error analysis makes it difficult to understand *why* the proposed method is better and under what conditions its complexity is justified.
