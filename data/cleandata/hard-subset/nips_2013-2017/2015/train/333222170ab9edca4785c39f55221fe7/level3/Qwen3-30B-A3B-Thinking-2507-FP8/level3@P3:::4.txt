

# Review of "On-the-Job Learning with Bayesian Decision Theory"

This paper introduces "on-the-job learning," a novel framework for deploying accurate AI systems without requiring pre-existing labeled training data. The authors model the process as a stochastic game between a system and a crowd, where the system can query the crowd for labels on specific parts of an input when uncertain, using Bayesian decision theory to balance latency, cost, and accuracy. The authors propose an approximation using Monte Carlo Tree Search with progressive widening, and evaluate their approach on named-entity recognition, sentiment classification, and image classification tasks. The results show significant cost reduction compared to full human annotation while maintaining or improving accuracy.

The paper makes a reasonable connection to previous work in online learning (e.g., [7]), active learning (e.g., [8-10]), and active classification (e.g., [22-24]). The distinction from active learning is particularly relevant, as the authors correctly note that traditional active learning focuses on building a model rather than maintaining high accuracy during deployment. However, the paper could better clarify how their approach differs from Legion:AR [6], which also operates in an on-the-job learning setting for activity recognition.

**Strengths:**
- The paper presents a clear and principled formulation of the on-the-job learning problem using Bayesian decision theory, which is well-motivated and addresses a genuine practical challenge in deploying AI systems without extensive labeled data.
- The implementation and experimental results are thorough, with the authors providing a detailed evaluation across multiple domains (NER, sentiment, image classification) and demonstrating significant cost reduction (more than an order of magnitude for NER) compared to full human annotation.
- The open-source implementation (LENS) and reproducibility efforts (CodaLab) are commendable.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Clarity of Face Recognition Application:** The paper claims results on image classification (face recognition), but the description is unclear. Were entire images queried (e.g., "Is this a face?") or was the system querying specific parts of the image? The paper states "image classification" but fails to specify how the crowd was queried in this setting, making it difficult to assess the applicability of their approach to non-structured data.

2. **Non-structured Data Limitations:** The authors focus on structured prediction problems (NER, sentiment), but claim applicability to image classification. The model's utility function assumes structured outputs where information propagates across labels (e.g., in NER, "George" being a location affects the label for "str."). In non-structured settings like image classification, the model does not propagate information across examples, making the proposed utility function potentially inappropriate.

3. **Threshold Baseline Parameters:** The threshold baseline uses two seemingly arbitrary parameters (0.3 and 0.98) without justification or sensitivity analysis. It's unclear why these values were chosen, how they were tuned, or how they might affect the baseline's performance across different datasets. This undermines the baseline's credibility as a meaningful comparison.

4. **Missing Baseline:** The authors should include an uncertainty-sampling baseline (common in active learning) for comparison. This would provide a more direct comparison to related work and help establish whether their Bayesian decision-theoretic approach offers significant advantages over simpler active learning strategies.

5. **Weak Distinction from Active Learning:** The paper claims their approach differs from active learning, but it's unclear why active learning cannot be adapted to this setting. If active learning treats the stream as the active set and updates the model on responses, the distinction becomes tenuous. The paper would benefit from a clearer theoretical or practical distinction.

6. **Limited Applicability:** While the framework is novel, the paper's experimental validation is limited to three specific tasks. The claim that the approach "reduces cost significantly over time" appears to be largely dependent on the specific characteristics of the NER dataset (e.g., the presence of a dominant "none" class). The paper does not convincingly demonstrate that the approach generalizes to other structured or non-structured prediction problems.

**Overall Assessment:**

The paper presents an interesting and novel framework for on-the-job learning with clear practical motivation. The technical approach using Bayesian decision theory and MCTS is well-justified for the structured prediction setting. However, the paper's weaknesses significantly undermine its contribution. The unclear application to face recognition, lack of justification for baseline parameters, and weak distinction from active learning are particularly concerning. The claim of significant cost reduction appears largely dependent on the specific characteristics of the NER dataset, without clear evidence of generalizability.

**Recommendation:** Reject. While the paper addresses a genuine problem with a novel framework, the weaknesses in the experimental design, lack of clarity on key implementation details, and insufficient comparison to relevant baselines prevent it from making a strong contribution to the field. The authors should address these issues before considering resubmission.
