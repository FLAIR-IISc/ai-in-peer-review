- Proposes "on-the-job learning": hybrid system that starts with zero training examples, uses real-time crowdsourcing for uncertainty, and learns online to become autonomous.  
- Formalizes the problem as a Bayesian stochastic game where actions are: query a human, wait, or return a prediction, optimizing a utility balancing accuracy, cost, and latency.  
- Implements structured prediction with a CRF and models asynchronous crowd responses by incorporating continuous time.  
- Optimal policy is intractable; authors approximate via Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) with progressive widening to handle continuous time.  
- Connects to online/active learning and crowdsourcing, but aims for high accuracy from the first example and test-time policy improvement.  
- Strength: original, practical, and well-motivated formalization likely to inspire human-in-the-loop ML work.  
- Strength: technically sound approach; MCTS + progressive widening is appropriate and system demonstrably reduces crowd reliance as it learns.  
- Strength: empirical validation across NER, sentiment, and image tasks shows cost reduction and accuracy gains over pure online learning.  
- Weakness: clarity issues—continuous-time formulation complicates exposition and obscures the core game-theoretic contribution.  
- Weakness: questionable necessity of continuous-time complexity—Threshold baseline (non-temporal) performs nearly as well and sometimes with lower latency.  
- Weakness: experimental reporting problems and reproducibility concerns (overstated claims vs Table 2, missing baselines in Tables 2/3, table formatting errors).  
- Comment: possible typo in Threshold baseline formula—term should likely decrease with number of queries (e.g., 0.3^m) rather than increase.  
- Comment: unclear how the "return" action is decided in experiments (confidence threshold, time limit, or emergent from MCTS).  
- Suggestion: present a simpler discrete-time core model first, then introduce continuous-time as an extension to improve readability.  
- Recommendation: accept, but require revisions to improve clarity and to correct and complete experimental reporting.
