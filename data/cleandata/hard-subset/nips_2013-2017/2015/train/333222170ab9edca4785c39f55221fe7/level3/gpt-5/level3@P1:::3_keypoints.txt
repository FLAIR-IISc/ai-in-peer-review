- Proposes an on-the-job learning framework that interleaves real-time crowdsourcing with model predictions to achieve high accuracy from the start without pre-collected training data.
- Casts decision-making as a two-player stochastic game built on a structured predictor (CRF) with a Bayesian utility trading off accuracy, cost, and latency.
- Models the environment with a joint p(y, r, t | x, q, s) over outputs, crowd responses, and response times.
- Uses Monte Carlo Tree Search with progressive widening to approximate the intractable optimal policy and handle continuous response times.
- Novelty: combines structured prediction, test-time crowd interaction, Bayesian utility, and MCTS-based policy for on-the-job learning.
- Strength: principled objective balancing accuracy, cost, and latency with a coherent environment model and scalable solver.
- Strength: solid empirical evaluation across NER, sentiment, and image classification, plus open-source release.
- Weakness: simplistic crowd response model assuming fixed 70% accuracy and conditional independence across responses.
- Suggestion: estimate worker reliabilities online or marginalize a Beta prior to improve robustness and leverage redundancy.
- Weakness: use of ExpAcc over MAP is unusual; recommend using expected accuracy under the full predictive distribution or a proper scoring rule and clarify alignment with goals.
- Weakness: continuous-time extension complicates state/search; need ablation comparing discrete-time or batch rounds to justify added complexity.
- Weakness: threshold baseline is competitive in some settings; include analysis explaining when the decision-theoretic policy outperforms heuristics (parallelization, adaptive stopping, structure).
- Clarity issue: introducing continuous time mid-section hampers readability; present a discrete-time version first then extend to continuous time.
- Clarity issue: decision-to-return criterion and any deadlines (t_deadline) are not clearly specified in methods and experiments.
- Notation issues: Eq.(2) posterior should include s; avoid reusing q for different meanings; threshold baseline inequality appears misstated.
- Presentation issue: highlight best values and latencies consistently, include missing baselines (e.g., threshold on sentiment) and report 5-vote results across tasks.
- Empirical takeaway: LENSE generally outperforms single-vote and online-learning baselines and reduces queries over time, especially with richer features.
- Empirical caveat: threshold heuristic is sometimes close in accuracy/latency; deeper analysis of when planning helps is needed and 5-vote comparisons should be consistent.
- Pros: clear motivation, principled formulation, effective MCTS planning, cost–accuracy trade-offs, decreasing crowd reliance, and open-source impact.
- Cons: clarity and notation problems, simplistic noise model with no online worker calibration, limited ablations on continuous-time value, and reporting inconsistencies.
- Recommendation: Accept, contingent on addressing clarity, strengthening the noise model, and adding ablations on continuous-time planning.
