- Proposes "on-the-job learning": use real-time crowdsourcing to start with zero training data and reduce queries as the model learns.  
- Formalizes the problem as a stochastic game between system and crowd with a utility balancing accuracy, cost, and latency.  
- Uses Bayesian decision theory to define the utility and applies MCTS with progressive widening to approximate the intractable optimal policy.  
- Evaluated on NER, sentiment, and image classification, showing large cost savings versus pure crowdsourcing and accuracy gains over a standard online baseline.  
- Technically sound formulation; modeling sequential querying as a stochastic game is elegant and appropriate.  
- MCTS is a well-motivated approximation for the decision problem.  
- Experimental results, especially on NER, demonstrate strong practical benefits and the authors provide code/data for reproducibility.  
- Weak baseline choices: the "threshold baseline" uses unexplained constants (0.3 and 0.98) without sensitivity analysis or justification.  
- Missing a standard uncertainty-sampling (active learning) baseline to better quantify gains from the MCTS-based policy.  
- The "online learning" baseline has access to gold labels and functions more like an oracle, making comparisons potentially misleading.  
- Unclear what constitutes a "query" in non-structured tasks; if queries are instance-level, the advantage of partial-label querying is diminished.  
- Method appears highly dependent on task structure and label distribution, with reduced gains on non-structured classification datasets.  
- Insight on avoiding queries for dominant "none" class in NER is useful but highlights potential limitations for other tasks.  
- Originality: novel synthesis framing on-the-job learning as a stochastic game and explicitly modeling latency alongside cost and accuracy.  
- Positioning against online active learning is weak; online active learning could be adapted to query before prediction, so distinction is more about principled decision-theoretic framing than a fundamental difference.  
- Significance: addresses an important practical cold-start problem and shows impressive NER results (order-of-magnitude cost reduction).  
- Broader impact may be limited if benefits do not generalize beyond structured prediction tasks.  
- Arguments for acceptance: addresses a practical problem, novel/stable technical approach, strong NER results, reproducible work.  
- Arguments for rejection: weak experimental comparisons (missing uncertainty-sampling baseline, poorly motivated threshold baseline), unclear advantage over adapted active learning, and limited clarity/details for classification experiments.  
- Recommendation: lean towards acceptance but require stronger baselines, clearer experimental details (especially for classification queries), and better positioning against active learning.
