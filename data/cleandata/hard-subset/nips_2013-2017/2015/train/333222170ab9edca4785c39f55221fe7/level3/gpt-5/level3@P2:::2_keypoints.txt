- Proposes “on-the-job learning” that interleaves real-time crowdsourcing with model predictions to deploy high-accuracy systems with zero initial training data.  
- Frames each input as a stochastic game where the system queries crowd parts, waits, or returns a prediction under a Bayesian utility trading off accuracy, latency, and money.  
- Uses Monte Carlo Tree Search with progressive widening to approximate planning for continuous response times.  
- Environment model combines a structured predictor (e.g., CRF), a simple crowd noise model, and a response-time model.  
- Experiments on NER, sentiment, and image classification show substantial cost reduction while maintaining or improving accuracy versus baselines.  
- LENSE often outperforms 3-vote human labels but underperforms 5-vote consensus, highlighting a cost–quality trade-off.  
- Decision-theoretic formulation and MCTS/progressive widening are sound and well-motivated for the large stochastic game.  
- Empirical comparisons to online learning and single-vote crowds are encouraging; multi-vote comparisons are informative.  
- Major limitation: crowd noise model assumes fixed 70% worker accuracy with uniform confusion, ignoring worker heterogeneity and class-specific errors.  
- Evaluation uses a frozen pool of pre-collected responses/delays, which may not reflect live worker variability or feedback loops.  
- Sensitivity to cost/latency weights (wM, wT) and the response-time model is underexplored.  
- Computational overhead and added latency from MCTS planning are not reported, raising practical scalability concerns.  
- Missing ablations (e.g., without progressive widening, without time modeling, learned worker models) weaken causal claims.  
- Clarity issues: abstract example is opaque and needs a fully worked example with timestamps; a figure reference is missing.  
- Terminology inconsistency across tables (e.g., “entropic” vs “threshold”) and confusing related-work placement should be fixed.  
- Notation density could be reduced with a diagram and symbol table to improve readability.  
- Originality: novel framing of on-the-job learning as a Bayesian decision problem jointly optimizing accuracy, latency, and cost for structured prediction.  
- Significance: high practical value for zero-data deployment scenarios and methodological importance of jointly optimizing latency and cost.  
- Pros: clear problem motivation, principled objective including latency, elegant MCTS use, empirical gains, and open-source artifacts.  
- Cons: simplistic worker model, limited sensitivity/ablation analyses, clarity problems, unreported planning cost, and frozen-pool evaluation.  
- Key suggestions: add a worked example, standardize baseline names, fix figure refs, report planning overhead, run sensitivity analyses and ablations, explore richer worker models, and discuss generalization beyond retainer pools.  
- Recommendation: Weak Accept pending revisions; the approach is promising but needs clarity and stronger evaluations.  
- Reviewer confidence: Medium.
