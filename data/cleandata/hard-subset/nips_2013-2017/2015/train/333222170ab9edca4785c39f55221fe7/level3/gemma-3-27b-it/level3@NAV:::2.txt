## Review of "On-the-Job Learning with Bayesian Decision Theory"

This paper presents a novel framework for on-the-job learning, aiming to build accurate systems from zero labeled examples by strategically querying a crowd for information. The core idea – framing the problem as a stochastic game and using Bayesian decision theory to balance latency, cost, and accuracy – is compelling and addresses a practically relevant challenge. The authors clearly position their work against existing approaches in online learning, active learning, and crowdsourcing, highlighting the key distinction of building accuracy *during* test time. The experimental results on three diverse tasks (NER, sentiment classification, and image classification) demonstrate the potential of the approach, particularly in terms of cost reduction compared to full annotation. The publicly available code is a significant plus for reproducibility and further research.

However, the paper suffers from a lack of clarity in several areas. The explanations of the underlying concepts, while theoretically sound, are often opaque. The discussion of the stochastic game and its associated utility function (Section 3) feels dense and could benefit from more intuitive explanations or illustrative examples. The example at Line 154 is particularly abstract and makes it difficult to understand the system’s behavior; a concrete example with realistic crowd responses and timings would be significantly more helpful. 

There are also some inconsistencies and minor issues. The labeling of the threshold baseline in Tables 2 and 3 as both “entropic” and “threshold” is confusing and should be standardized. Furthermore, the placement of related work in Section 5 feels disjointed and obscures the connections to relevant research – a more integrated discussion woven throughout the paper would be preferable. 

Table 2 highlights an interesting trade-off: LENSE outperforms a 3-vote consensus but underperforms a 5-vote approach, suggesting that the additional consensus from more votes can be more effective than the LENSE approach for denoising crowd responses. This observation should be discussed in more detail. A minor issue is the missing figure reference on Line 086 ("Figure ??").

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novel approach to on-the-job learning with a strong theoretical foundation.
*   Promising experimental results demonstrating cost reduction and accuracy improvements.
*   Publicly available code promotes reproducibility.
*   Addresses a compelling and practically relevant problem.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   Lack of clarity in explanations and technical details.
*   Inconsistencies in labeling and presentation.
*   Related work section is oddly placed and insufficiently integrated.



**Overall:** This paper presents a valuable contribution to the field, but requires significant improvements in clarity and organization before it’s ready for publication. I recommend acceptance with major revisions.
