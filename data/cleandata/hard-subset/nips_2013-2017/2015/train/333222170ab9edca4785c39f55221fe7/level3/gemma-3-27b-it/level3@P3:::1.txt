This paper introduces a principled framework, “on-the-job learning,” for deploying machine learning systems with minimal initial training data, leveraging real-time crowdsourcing to resolve uncertainty and iteratively improve performance. The core idea, building on prior work [6], is to cast the problem as a stochastic game using Bayesian decision theory, approximating the optimal querying policy with Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS). This approach aims to balance latency, cost, and accuracy.

The work addresses a practical and important problem: the “cold start” scenario where obtaining large labeled datasets is infeasible. It’s relevant to NIPS given its focus on machine learning foundations and algorithmic development. The problem is situated well within the broader context of online learning, active learning, and crowdsourcing, appropriately citing relevant literature ([7, 8, 19, 25]). However, while the paper acknowledges Legion:AR [6] as a precursor, it perhaps underplays the degree of overlap in the core concept, framing the contribution primarily as providing the “machine learning foundations” lacking in that earlier work.

The paper’s strength lies in its theoretically grounded formulation.  Modeling the process as a stochastic game and employing Bayesian decision theory offer a principled way to navigate the trade-offs inherent in the on-the-job learning setting. The use of MCTS is a reasonable approach to tackling the intractability of finding the optimal policy. The implementation, LENSE, and its open-source availability are also commendable.

However, the paper suffers from some weaknesses. The presentation, while generally good, lacks sufficient technical detail to fully reproduce the results. For instance, Eq. 2 is unclear. The left-hand side (ExpAcc) should be conditioned on the state *s*, and the disappearance of *y* from *p<sub>R</sub>(r<sub>i</sub> | y, q<sub>i</sub>)* is unexplained – the response model should inherently depend on the true label *y*.  Additionally, Alg. 1 uses *N(s)* without defining what *s* represents; is it the full state or a partial state? The choice of threshold values (0.3 and 0.88) in the threshold baseline is also not justified; a clear explanation for their selection is needed.

The empirical results are underwhelming. Comparing against a simple threshold-based heuristic feels insufficient to demonstrate the efficacy of the proposed approach. The 8% F1 improvement over single human labeling and 28% over online learning are positive, but the online learning baseline appears weak. A more competitive baseline—a strong, actively learning model or a state-of-the-art supervised model trained on a small, strategically selected dataset—would provide a more convincing evaluation. 

A critical clarification is needed regarding the nature of the "online" learning baseline. Are these truly state-of-the-art online learning systems for each task? If LENSE demonstrably outperforms the best possible online learning approach, the gains may be attributable to the human annotation incorporated through active querying, rather than the algorithm itself. A thorough error analysis with example cases, illustrating scenarios where LENSE effectively leverages crowdsourcing to improve upon the online learning baseline, is crucial. It should also be noted that the experiments rely on pre-collected worker response times, which might not fully reflect the dynamic nature of real-time crowdsourcing.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Theoretically sound formulation of a practical problem.
*   Principled approach using Bayesian decision theory and MCTS.
*   Open-source implementation available.
*   Potential for significant cost reduction in real-world applications.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Insufficient technical detail and clarity in the presentation.
*   Weak empirical evaluation with inadequate baselines.
*   Lack of error analysis to disentangle algorithmic gains from the benefits of human annotation.
*   Limited novelty beyond the extension of prior work [6].



**Overall Assessment:** The approach presented is reasonable and merits further investigation. The theoretical framing of the crowd-to-model transition is valuable. However, the empirical and explanatory gaps currently limit the conclusions that can be drawn. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the clarity issues and a request for a more robust empirical evaluation with stronger baselines and a detailed error analysis.
