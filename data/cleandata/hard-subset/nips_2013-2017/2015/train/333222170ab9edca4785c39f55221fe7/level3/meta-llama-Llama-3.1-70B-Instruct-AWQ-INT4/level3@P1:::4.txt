The paper "On-the-Job Learning with Bayesian Decision Theory" proposes a novel framework for on-the-job learning, which aims to deploy a high-accuracy system starting with zero training examples. The authors model the on-the-job setting as a stochastic game based on Bayesian decision theory, allowing them to balance latency, cost, and accuracy objectives in a principled way. The paper presents an approximation algorithm based on Monte Carlo Tree Search and progressive widening to reason about continuous time.

The main ideas of the paper are related to previous work at NIPS and elsewhere, particularly in the areas of online learning, active learning, and structured prediction. The authors draw inspiration from these areas to develop their framework, which is distinct from existing approaches in its ability to maintain high accuracy from the beginning and adapt to changing conditions over time.

The paper's strengths include its novel application of Bayesian decision theory to on-the-job learning, its ability to balance multiple objectives in a principled way, and its empirical evaluation on three different tasks. The results show that the proposed approach outperforms machine and human-only comparisons on both quality and cost.

However, there are also some weaknesses and limitations to the paper. One of the main concerns is the applicability of the framework to non-structured settings, where the model does not propagate information across examples. Additionally, the threshold baseline used in the experiments seems to rely on arbitrary parameters, and the authors do not report on their origin and sensitivity. Furthermore, the distinction between the proposed approach and active online learning is not entirely clear, and it is possible that active learning could be adapted to the on-the-job setting by treating the sequence as the active set and updating the model on responses.

In terms of quality, the paper is technically sound, and the authors provide a clear and well-organized presentation of their ideas. The claims are well-supported by theoretical analysis and experimental results. However, some details of the framework are unclear, and the authors could provide more information on the calibration of their models and the sensitivity of their results to different parameters.

In terms of clarity, the paper is well-written, and the authors provide a clear and concise introduction to the problem and their approach. The paper is well-organized, and the authors provide a clear explanation of their framework, algorithm, and experiments. However, some of the technical details may be challenging for non-experts to follow, and the authors could provide more background information or references to help readers understand the context and significance of their work.

In terms of originality, the paper presents a novel framework for on-the-job learning, which is distinct from existing approaches in its ability to balance multiple objectives in a principled way. The application of Bayesian decision theory to on-the-job learning is also novel, and the authors provide a clear explanation of how their approach differs from existing work in online learning and active learning.

In terms of significance, the paper's results show that the proposed approach can achieve significant cost reductions and accuracy improvements over existing approaches. The framework has the potential to be applied to a wide range of tasks, particularly in situations where there is limited labeled data available. However, the authors could provide more discussion on the potential applications and limitations of their approach, as well as its potential impact on the field.
