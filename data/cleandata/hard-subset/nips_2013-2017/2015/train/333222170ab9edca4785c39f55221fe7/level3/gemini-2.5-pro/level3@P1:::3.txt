This paper introduces "on-the-job learning," a framework for deploying a high-accuracy system that starts with zero training examples. The core idea is to create a hybrid system that initially relies on real-time crowdsourcing to handle uncertainty, and then gradually becomes more autonomous as its internal model improves through online learning. The authors formalize this problem as a stochastic game between the system and the crowd, grounded in Bayesian decision theory. The system's goal is to choose a sequence of actions (query a human, wait for a response, or return a prediction) to maximize a utility function that balances accuracy, monetary cost, and latency.

The problem is cast on a Conditional Random Field (CRF) for structured prediction tasks. The model accounts for the asynchronous nature of crowd responses by incorporating continuous time for when queries are sent and when responses are received. Since computing the optimal policy for this game is intractable, the authors propose an approximation based on Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) combined with progressive widening to handle the continuous state space introduced by time. This work relates to several areas, including online learning, active learning, and crowdsourcing. Unlike standard online/active learning, this framework aims for high accuracy from the very first example by actively seeking information before making a prediction. It extends active classification by allowing the policy to improve at test time as the model learns.

**Strengths (Arguments for Acceptance):**

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The paper tackles a highly relevant and practical problem: how to deploy a machine learning system in a new domain without a pre-existing labeled dataset. The "on-the-job learning" paradigm is a compelling and novel solution. The formalization of this problem as a stochastic game using Bayesian decision theory is a strong and principled contribution. This work is likely to be influential and inspire follow-up research in practical, human-in-the-loop ML systems.

2.  **Technical Quality:** The proposed approach is technically sound. Modeling the problem as a game is appropriate, and the choice of MCTS with progressive widening is a well-justified and state-of-the-art technique for approximating solutions in such large, continuous state spaces. The system's ability to gracefully reduce its reliance on the crowd as it gains experience is a key strength, which is demonstrated effectively in the experiments (e.g., Figure 3).

3.  **Experimental Evaluation:** The authors validate their system, LENSE, on three distinct tasks (NER, sentiment analysis, image classification), demonstrating its versatility. The experiments clearly show that the on-the-job learning approach significantly reduces costs compared to a purely human-powered approach (e.g., Human 5-query) while achieving substantial accuracy improvements over a pure online learning baseline.

**Weaknesses (Arguments for Revision):**

1.  **Clarity:** The paper's primary weakness is its clarity, particularly in the model description (Section 3). The introduction of continuous time, while technically interesting, significantly complicates the exposition of the core game-theoretic framework. The state representation becomes more complex, and the algorithm requires modifications like progressive widening. This added complexity makes the central contribution harder to grasp. The paper would be much stronger and more accessible if it first presented the core model in a simpler, discrete-time setting (where actions are taken in turns) and then introduced the continuous-time formulation as a subsequent extension.

2.  **Justification for Complexity:** The experimental results do not provide a strong justification for the added complexity of the continuous-time model. The "Threshold baseline," a much simpler heuristic that does not reason about time, performs nearly as well as the full LENSE system, and in some cases with lower latency (Table 2, NER). This raises the question of whether the sophisticated MCTS machinery for handling time provides a significant practical benefit over a simpler, non-temporal uncertainty-based heuristic.

3.  **Experimental Reporting and Claims:** There are several issues with the reporting of results that undermine the paper's claims and reproducibility:
    *   The abstract claims the system "boost[s] performance relative to the expert provided labels." However, in Table 2 (NER), the Human 5-query baseline achieves an F1 of 91.0%, which is higher than LENSE's 88.4%. This claim should be revised to be more precise.
    *   Results for the Human 5-query baseline are missing for the Sentiment and Faces tasks in Table 3, making it impossible to fully assess LENSE's performance against the best human-only baseline on those tasks.
    *   The Threshold baseline is missing from the Sentiment experiment results in Table 3.
    *   Formatting errors in the tables make them harder to read (e.g., in Table 2, the best F1 score and the lowest latency for the Threshold baseline are not bolded).

**Detailed Comments and Suggestions:**

*   **Threshold Baseline Formulation:** The description of the threshold baseline seems to contain a typo. The condition is given as `(1−pθ(yi | x))×0.3m ≥ 0.98`. As `m` (number of queries) increases, the left-hand side increases, making the condition *easier* to satisfy. It should likely be a term that decreases with `m`, such as `(1-p)*0.3^m`, to reflect decreasing uncertainty. Please clarify this.
*   **Return Action Criteria:** The paper does not specify the exact criteria used in the experiments for the system to choose the "return" action. Is it based on reaching a certain confidence threshold, a time limit, or is this decision purely an emergent property of the MCTS search? Clarifying this is important for understanding the experimental setup.
*   **Presentation Improvement:** As mentioned above, restructuring the paper to present the core idea without continuous time first would significantly improve readability and impact. The continuous-time aspect could be framed as a valuable but non-essential extension for fine-tuning the latency/accuracy trade-off.

In summary, this paper presents a novel, significant, and well-motivated framework for on-the-job learning. The core idea is strong and the technical approach is solid. However, the paper is hampered by significant clarity issues and several gaps in the experimental reporting. I recommend acceptance, but I strongly urge the authors to address the clarity of the model presentation and correct the omissions and inaccuracies in the experimental results for the final version.
