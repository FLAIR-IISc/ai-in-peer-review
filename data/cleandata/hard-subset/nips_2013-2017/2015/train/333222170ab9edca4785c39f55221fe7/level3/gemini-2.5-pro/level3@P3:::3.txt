### **Summary of the Paper**

This paper introduces "on-the-job learning," a framework for deploying a high-accuracy prediction system from scratch, without any initial training data. The core idea is a hybrid system that initially relies heavily on real-time crowdsourcing to label incoming data points and make predictions. As the system collects these labels, it simultaneously trains a model (a CRF in this work) in an online fashion. Over time, as the model's confidence grows, its reliance on the crowd decreases, thereby reducing cost and latency.

The authors formalize this problem as a stochastic game between the system and the crowd. Using Bayesian decision theory, the system chooses actions (querying a specific label, waiting for a response, or returning a final prediction) to maximize an expected utility function that balances prediction accuracy, monetary cost, and time latency. A key modeling contribution is the explicit incorporation of continuous time, modeling the asynchronous nature of query submission and response arrival. As computing the optimal policy is intractable, the authors propose an approximation using Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) combined with progressive widening to handle the continuous-time action space.

This work sits at the intersection of online learning, active learning, and crowdsourcing. Unlike standard online/active learning, it aims to maintain high accuracy on every prediction from the very first instance, rather than just improving a model for future use. It also moves beyond typical crowdsourcing models by integrating an adaptive learning component that becomes more self-sufficient over time.

### **Strengths**

*   **Originality:** The "on-the-job learning" setting is a novel and highly practical formulation of a real-world problem. Framing this as a stochastic game with a utility function that explicitly models accuracy, cost, and continuous-time latency is a creative and principled approach. It formalizes and extends prior work in a sophisticated manner.
*   **Significance:** The paper addresses a significant bottleneck in deploying machine learning systems: the cold start problem. The proposed method provides a path to deploying accurate systems immediately, which could be highly impactful for practitioners in time-sensitive domains (e.g., disaster response) or those without the resources for large-scale upfront data collection. The work is ambitious and likely to spur further research and discussion in the community.
*   **Technical Quality:** The use of Bayesian decision theory provides a solid theoretical foundation. The experimental evaluation is comprehensive, covering three different tasks (NER, sentiment analysis, face identification) and comparing against a reasonable set of baselines. The results compellingly demonstrate that the system successfully learns to reduce its reliance on the crowd over time while maintaining high accuracy, a key goal of the framework.

### **Weaknesses**

*   **Clarity:** The paper's primary weakness is its clarity. The introduction of continuous time in Section 3, while novel, significantly complicates the exposition of the game state, transitions, and the MCTS algorithm. The core idea of the decision-theoretic framework gets somewhat lost in the details of handling asynchronous events. The manuscript would be much easier to understand and more impactful if the core method (the stochastic game and MCTS policy) were presented first in a simpler, discrete-time setting, with the continuous-time component introduced subsequently as an extension.
*   **Justification for Complexity:** The experimental results do not provide a strong justification for the added complexity of the continuous-time model and MCTS search. The much simpler "Threshold" baseline performs remarkably well, achieving accuracy close to the full LENSE model and, in some cases, with lower latency. The paper does not sufficiently analyze why its more sophisticated, computationally intensive approach is superior to this strong heuristic. This raises questions about the practical necessity of the full model, diminishing the perceived significance of the complex game-playing machinery.

### **Detailed Comments and Questions for the Authors**

The work is promising, but several issues in the experimental reporting and presentation should be addressed to strengthen the paper.

**Reporting and Presentation:**
1.  **Unsupported Claim:** The abstract and introduction claim the system "boost[s] performance relative to the expert provided labels" on the NER task. However, Table 2 shows the 5-vote majority baseline achieving an F1 of 89.1%, which is higher than LENSE's 88.4%. This claim should be corrected.
2.  **Missing Baselines:** The 5-vote majority vote is a critical baseline for assessing performance relative to a high-quality human-only system. Its results are missing for the Face-ID and Sentiment tasks in Table 2. Furthermore, the strong "Threshold" baseline is absent from the Sentiment analysis results in Table 3 and is not shown in the learning curve in Figure 4. Including these results is essential for a complete comparison.
3.  **Formatting:** In Table 2, the best F1 score for NER (achieved by 5-vote) and the lowest latency for NER (achieved by 1-vote) are not bolded, which can be misleading. Please ensure all best-performing results in tables are correctly highlighted.

**Clarifications:**
1.  **`t_deadline`:** The utility function (Eq. 1) penalizes time via `t_now`, but it is not clear how the model is optimized to meet a specific latency target or deadline. Was a `t_deadline` used to shape the reward or policy during experiments? If so, how?
2.  **Return Criterion:** What is the precise condition under which the system chooses the "return" action? Is it based purely on the MCTS value estimate, or is there a separate stopping criterion, such as when the model's posterior entropy for the labels falls below a certain threshold?
3.  **Threshold Baseline Typo:** The description of the Threshold baseline states the condition for querying is `(1−pθ(yi | x))×0.3^m ≥ 0.98`. This inequality seems incorrect. As `m` (number of queries) increases, the left-hand side decreases, meaning the system would stop querying when uncertainty is high. Shouldn't the uncertainty term be *less than* a threshold? Please clarify or correct this formula.

**Notation:**
1.  **Equation 3:** In the text, the response model is given as `p(r' | x, r, q)`. To be precise, the distribution over a future response `r'` should also depend on the time `s` at which its corresponding query `q` was issued, as this influences the response time `t` and thus which responses are available.
2.  **Confusing Notation:** The use of `q` to denote a query action and (in other contexts, not shown in the excerpt) a distribution function is potentially confusing. Using a different letter (e.g., `p` or `π`) for the distribution would improve clarity.

### **Recommendation**

I recommend acceptance. The paper introduces a novel and significant problem, "on-the-job learning," and proposes a principled, albeit complex, framework to address it. The core ideas are original and have the potential to be highly influential. The primary weaknesses relate to clarity of presentation and the empirical justification for the model's full complexity. However, these weaknesses do not undermine the paper's core contribution. The work is a solid piece of research that will be of great interest to the NIPS community. I strongly urge the authors to use the revision period to address the clarity and reporting issues outlined above, which would significantly strengthen the final paper.
