Summary and relation to prior work
This paper introduces on-the-job learning: a system that delivers high-accuracy predictions from the first input by dynamically querying crowd workers in real time to resolve uncertainty, while simultaneously updating a prediction model so that reliance on the crowd declines over time. The framework is formalized as a stochastic game grounded in Bayesian decision theory that trades off accuracy, latency, and monetary cost. Because exact planning is intractable, the authors approximate the optimal policy with Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) and progressive widening to handle continuous response times. The system, LENSE, is evaluated on NER, sentiment classification, and face/image classification, and compared to human-majority baselines (1/3/5 votes), a threshold heuristic baseline, and an (oracle) online-learning baseline.

The work builds on and contrasts with online learning and online active learning (which do not query before predicting), with active classification (which typically learns a static test-time policy from labeled data), and with real-time crowdsourcing systems such as Legion:AR. It also connects to crowdsourcing models for label aggregation (e.g., POMDP-based control) and to structured prediction for sequence labeling.

Strengths
- Important problem: deploying high-accuracy classifiers from zero labeled examples while controlling cost/latency is compelling for time-sensitive domains.
- Clear formal objective: the Bayesian decision-theoretic formulation that explicitly trades off cost, time, and accuracy is principled and useful.
- Novel technical angle: combining MCTS with progressive widening to reason over asynchronous, continuous-time crowd responses is interesting and, to my knowledge, new in this setting.
- Practicality: the retainer-pool setup and asynchronous querying address real deployment constraints.
- Empirical gains: LENSE improves over machine-only (online learning) and over single-vote human baselines, and reduces cost vs multi-vote consensus at comparable accuracy. The system includes noisy-workers modeling and latency optimization.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Clarity/opacity in exposition: While the core idea is strong, key parts are hard to follow. For example, the example around line 154 is abstract. Please replace with a concrete, end-to-end micro-trace showing:
  - Input: tweet “Soup on George str.”
  - Initial model beliefs (marginals) on each token and why “George” and “str.” are uncertain.
  - Issued queries with timestamps (e.g., q1=2 at 0.0s, q2=3 at 0.0s), sampled response times (e.g., 0.8s, 1.4s), and responses (including one incorrect worker label).
  - How p(y | x,q,r,s,t) updates after each response, the decision to issue a redundant vote on “George,” and the final return action with utility components (ExpAcc, nQ wM, tnow wT).
  This would concretely illustrate how the policy balances parallelism vs adaptivity and redundancy for de-noising.
- Baseline naming inconsistency: Tables 2 and 3 inconsistently label the same heuristic as “entropic” vs “threshold,” which is confusing. Please standardize the name and ensure legends and text match.
- Missing references/typos: Line 086 has “Figure ??”—please fix. There are a few other small proofreading issues; a careful pass would help.
- Placement of related work: Presenting related work late (Section 5/6 in the current draft) makes it hard to situate contributions. Consider moving a succinct related work section earlier (after the introduction) to better contrast with online active learning, active classification, and POMDP-based crowd control (e.g., TurKontrol, GLAD/MACE/Dawid–Skene).
- Worker/noise modeling: The response model pR(r|yq)=0.7 for the true label is simplistic and constant across tasks/workers. Since crowds exhibit heterogeneity and drift, modeling per-worker reliabilities or at least per-task confusion matrices (possibly learned online) could strengthen both realism and performance. A comparison to standard aggregation methods (Dawid–Skene, GLAD, MACE) would make the human comparison more informative.
- Evaluation protocol: Much of the analysis samples responses and delays from a frozen pool collected in advance. While this controls variance, it undercuts the real-time nature of the approach. Please clarify how many results are from truly live experiments vs simulation, and consider a sensitivity analysis to different latency/noise distributions.
- Utility and calibration: ExpAcc with MAP may overweight overconfident models early on. Discuss model calibration, the choice of wM and wT, and provide sensitivity curves. An ablation comparing LENSE with and without time reasoning (forcing all queries at time 0) would isolate the benefit of latency optimization.
- Human comparisons: Table 2 indicates LENSE outperforms the 3-vote majority but underperforms the 5-vote baseline in accuracy, while being substantially cheaper and faster. This is a useful trade-off, but please make the cost–accuracy frontier explicit (plots) and include confidence intervals to gauge statistical significance.

Quality
- Generally sound and well-motivated. The game formulation, utility, and MCTS+PW approximation are appropriate. Empirical setup is reasonable, but would benefit from more rigorous baselines for crowd aggregation and a deeper sensitivity analysis to worker noise and latency. Some clarity gaps hinder reproducibility (e.g., exact features, hyperparameter settings for wM, wT, and MCTS parameters).

Clarity
- The paper is readable but occasionally opaque: the abstract example (line 154), the missing figure reference, and inconsistent baseline naming hurt clarity. A more walk-through example and a consolidated notation table would help.

Originality
- The problem framing and the integration of Bayesian decision theory with real-time crowdsourcing and MCTS over continuous-time responses are novel. Clear differentiation from Legion:AR and active classification is appreciated.

Significance
- Addresses a relevant and difficult deployment problem. Likely to influence follow-on work in crowd-in-the-loop systems, test-time feature acquisition, and decision-theoretic control of annotation.

Pros for acceptance
- Compelling, timely problem with principled formulation.
- Novel and practical algorithmic contribution (MCTS + progressive widening for real-time, noisy crowd responses).
- Demonstrated cost/latency/accuracy trade-offs vs humans and machine-only baselines.
- Open-source implementation and datasets.

Cons for acceptance
- Exposition issues (opaque example, baseline naming, missing figure ref).
- Simplistic worker noise model; missing comparisons to standard aggregation methods.
- Heavy reliance on pre-collected response/latency pools; limited analysis of real-time variance and sensitivity.
- LENSE underperforms 5-vote in accuracy on Table 2; the trade-off needs clearer presentation.

Questions for authors
- How sensitive is performance to mis-specification of pR and pT? Could you learn these online?
- Can the policy incorporate worker selection (e.g., routing to historically fast/accurate workers)?
- How do wM and wT map to target SLAs (latency/accuracy constraints)? Could you support constrained optimization rather than scalarized utility?

Minor comments
- Standardize “threshold” vs “entropic” naming in all tables/text.
- Fix “Figure ??” (Line 086) and any other placeholder references.
- Consider moving related work earlier to aid framing.

Overall: A strong and promising contribution. If clarity and baseline issues are addressed, I would lean toward acceptance.
