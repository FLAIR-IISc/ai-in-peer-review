Summary
The paper proposes “on-the-job learning,” a framework that delivers high-accuracy predictions from the start by interleaving real-time crowdsourced queries with model inference, while continually updating the model to reduce reliance on the crowd over time. The decision process is cast as a stochastic game between the system and the crowd grounded in Bayesian decision theory. The system’s actions (query, wait, return) are chosen to maximize expected utility that trades off accuracy, monetary cost, and latency. The environment model couples a structured prediction model pθ(y|x) (instantiated as a linear-chain CRF), a crowd-response noise model pR(r|yq), and a response-time model pT(t|s) to reason about asynchronous responses in continuous time. Exact optimization is intractable; the authors use Monte Carlo Tree Search (UCT) with progressive widening to plan over continuous time. Experiments on NER, sentiment classification, and face attributes compare to majority-vote baselines, a strong online-learning oracle, and a non-time-aware “threshold” baseline. The full system (LENSE) achieves strong accuracy at substantially lower cost and decreasing latency over time, especially on NER.

Relation to prior work
The setting differs from online learning and online active learning in that the system actively seeks information before predicting to maintain high initial accuracy. Compared to active classification, the policy is adaptive at test time and the model is updated continuously. The framework also relates to decision-theoretic control in crowdsourcing (e.g., TurKontrol) and to Legion:AR, but contributes a structured prediction formulation with explicit continuous-time reasoning and a game-theoretic planning algorithm. The paper cites relevant work at NIPS and elsewhere and clearly positions itself.

Strengths
- Well-motivated problem: delivering high accuracy from zero labeled data while managing cost and latency is important and timely.
- Principled formulation: a coherent Bayesian decision-theoretic game couples prediction, noisy responses, and response times.
- Effective planning approximation: MCTS with progressive widening is a sensible and practical choice for continuous-time decision-making.
- Empirical gains: clear improvements over single-vote and online-learning baselines; cost reductions relative to full human annotation; decreasing number of crowd queries over time.
- Open-source code and reproducible experiments.

Weaknesses
- Clarity: the introduction of continuous-time modeling mid-Section 3 complicates exposition; key algorithmic details are hard to follow on first read. Several notation issues/typos impede readability.
- Continuous-time benefit vs. complexity: experimental gains attributable specifically to continuous-time reasoning appear limited; the simple threshold baseline performs nearly as well and sometimes with lower latency.
- Reporting inconsistencies: the claim that the method “beats human performance” appears to exclude the 5-vote NER baseline; 5-vote results are missing for sentiment and faces in places; the threshold baseline is not consistently reported (e.g., sentiment in Table 3/Figure 4).
- Experimental realism: simulation reuses a frozen pool of worker responses and latencies; it is unclear how robust conclusions are to shifts in worker accuracy/latency distributions or different priors (pR).

Quality
The methodological core is sound and well-grounded. The utility function, environment model, and planner are appropriate; restrictions (e.g., not issuing new queries while waiting) are reasonable for tractability. However:
- Sensitivity analyses are limited (e.g., worker accuracy 0.7, latency distributions, penalty weights). Robustness to miscalibration of pθ is not assessed.
- Ablations isolating the incremental value of continuous-time planning vs. a discrete-time or batched variant are missing.
- Some evaluation choices (frozen worker pool) may inflate stability and understate variance; this should be discussed more explicitly.

Clarity
Writing is generally good, but several issues should be addressed:
- Present a discrete-time or “no-time” version first to convey the core idea, then add continuous-time as an extension. This would greatly improve accessibility.
- Clarify the decision criterion for returning an answer: is there an entropy/confidence threshold vs. a time/deadline parameter, and how are wT (time penalty) or any t_deadline applied in experiments?
- Explicitly state how many queries are launched in parallel vs. sequentially and how that interacts with wT.
- Fix notation/typos:
  - In Eq. 2/3-related text, when writing p(y|x,q,s,r,t) or p(y,r,t|x,q,s), s should appear consistently in the conditionals.
  - Avoid using q for both query indices and any probability distribution elsewhere (eq. 5); rename the latter.
  - Threshold baseline inequality appears incorrect: the expression should reflect that uncertainty decreases with larger m; please correct and derive.
  - Ensure best numbers and latency values are consistently bolded; ensure all baselines (incl. threshold) appear in Sentiment tables/figures; include 5-vote for all tasks.

Originality
The combination of on-the-job, continuous-time crowd queries with structured prediction and MCTS planning is novel and differentiates from active learning/classification and prior crowd decision-theoretic systems. The game-theoretic formalization for structured outputs is a strong conceptual contribution.

Significance
High practical potential for deployable systems that need immediate accuracy with constrained budgets. NER results are compelling; sentiment/faces show moderate gains, suggesting room for improved modeling or richer features. The work is likely to spur follow-up on adaptive test-time information acquisition under latency/cost constraints.

Questions for the authors
- How sensitive is performance to the assumed worker accuracy (0.7) and to misspecification of pR and pT? Please provide sensitivity plots.
- What measurable benefit does continuous-time planning confer over a discrete-time/batch query policy when holding total queries constant?
- How are wM and wT selected per task? Is there a principled calibration to user-facing SLAs (e.g., max latency) or is it tuned on a dev set?
- Can the system learn worker reliabilities online (heterogeneous pR) and adapt query redundancy accordingly?
- How does the policy behave under bursty arrivals and variable pool sizes?

Pros for acceptance
- Important problem, principled formulation, and open-source implementation.
- Solid empirical improvements with meaningful cost reductions.
- Likely to influence future work on real-time human-in-the-loop learning.

Cons for acceptance
- Clarity issues and notation/formatting problems.
- Limited evidence isolating the incremental value of continuous-time modeling.
- Reporting gaps/inconsistencies for baselines.

Recommendation
Weak accept. The contribution is novel and practically significant; with improved presentation, consistent reporting, and ablations clarifying the role of continuous time, this would be a strong NIPS paper.

Reviewer confidence
High.
