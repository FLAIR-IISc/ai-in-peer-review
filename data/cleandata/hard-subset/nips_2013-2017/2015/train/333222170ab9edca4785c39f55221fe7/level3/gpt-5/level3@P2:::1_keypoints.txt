- Paper tackles on-the-job learning: interleaves predictions with real-time crowd queries using a Bayesian decision-theoretic stochastic game optimizing accuracy, latency, and cost.
- Uses MCTS with progressive widening to approximate intractable planning for asynchronous, continuous-time querying.
- Evaluated on NER, sentiment, and face classification; shows cost reductions vs crowd-only and accuracy gains vs machine-only; code/data released.
- Relates to online learning, active learning/active classification, decision-theoretic crowdsourcing (e.g., TurKontrol), and structured prediction with partial supervision.
- Strength: timely, practical problem (deploy without upfront labels while maintaining quality).
- Strength: principled formulation with explicit utility balancing accuracy, time, and monetary cost and clean model decomposition.
- Strength: planning handles asynchrony naturally via progressive widening and MCTS.
- Strength: supports structured outputs and models label interactions (e.g., CRF for NER).
- Strength: empirical evidence across diverse tasks with real-time crowd infrastructure and open-source release.
- Weakness: ambiguous notation/equations—ExpAcc should be conditioned on x and all available information; define Accuracy precisely for structured outputs.
- Weakness: unclear response-model marginalization—need explicit derivation showing p(r′ | x,q,s,r,t) and where y,t are marginalized.
- Weakness: Algorithm 1 missing precise definitions (N(σ) vs N(s)), UCT constant c, terminal condition, rollout policy, and action enumeration/limits.
- Weakness: threshold-baseline hyperparameters (0.3 factor, 0.98 target confidence) unjustified; need derivation and sensitivity analyses to worker accuracy.
- Weakness: unclear strength of “online learning” baseline—specify whether strong, domain-appropriate models were used and whether labels are disallowed before prediction.
- Weakness: empirical comparisons lack planner ablations (greedy queries, no time model, no progressive widening) to isolate planning contribution.
- Weakness: need error analysis and qualitative examples showing query savings, redundancy corrections, and cases beating ML baseline.
- Weakness: simplistic constant-accuracy response model and “frozen pool” simulation—must evaluate robustness to worker heterogeneity and nonstationarity.
- Weakness: unclear claim about “boosting performance relative to expert labels”—explain evaluation protocol and gold-standard reconciliation.
- Weakness: missing complexity/runtime reporting—planning latency per example, number of simulations, scaling with sequence length and pool size.
- Evaluation (Quality): formulation sound but modeling choices and missing ablations weaken empirical case; more rigorous comparisons needed.
- Evaluation (Clarity): generally clear but hampered by notational inconsistencies and missing algorithmic details affecting reproducibility.
- Evaluation (Originality): novel combination of decision-theoretic on-the-job querying with MCTS/progressive widening for structured outputs.
- Evaluation (Significance): potentially impactful for rapid deployment, but conclusions limited by baseline strength and analysis depth.
- Pros: principled, handles continuous-time asynchronous responses, demonstrates tradeoffs with public artifacts.
- Cons: empirical comparisons not yet compelling; missing technical details and simplified response model may overstate robustness.
- Suggestions: clarify notation/equations, strengthen ML and active-learning baselines, add planner ablations and sensitivity studies, provide error analysis and runtime/scale metrics.
- Overall: promising approach but empirical and explanatory gaps limit conclusions; borderline/weak accept pending major clarifications and stronger experiments.
