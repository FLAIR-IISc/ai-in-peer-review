## Review of "On-the-Job Learning with Bayesian Decision Theory"

This paper presents a compelling framework for on-the-job learning, aiming to build accurate systems from zero labeled examples by strategically querying a crowd for assistance. The core idea of balancing latency, cost, and accuracy using Bayesian decision theory and approximating the optimal policy with Monte Carlo Tree Search is novel and addresses a practically important problem – deploying AI systems in resource-constrained scenarios. The work builds upon ideas from online learning and active learning but distinguishes itself by actively seeking information *during* prediction and adapting the model in real-time. 

The paper is well-motivated, highlighting the limitations of both purely supervised and purely crowdsourced approaches. The experimental results on three datasets (NER, sentiment classification, and image classification) demonstrate significant cost reductions compared to full annotation and improvements over baseline methods like single-labeling and online learning. The availability of an open-source implementation (LENSE) is a strong positive.

However, the paper suffers from some clarity issues. The explanation of the stochastic game formulation, while theoretically sound, is somewhat opaque. The example at Line 154 is particularly abstract and difficult to follow; a concrete query with realistic crowd responses and timings would greatly improve understanding. Furthermore, Tables 2 and 3 inconsistently label the threshold baseline as “entropic” vs “threshold,” creating confusion. The placement of related work in Section 5 feels disjointed and weakens the narrative flow; it would be more natural to integrate relevant work throughout the introduction and discussion. 

A minor point is Line 086, which has a missing figure reference ("Figure ??"). The authors acknowledge worker variability and address it by freezing a pool of responses, which is a reasonable approach, but further discussion of the impact of worker quality on performance would be valuable.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Addresses a compelling and practical problem.
*   Novel combination of Bayesian decision theory, MCTS, and crowdsourcing.
*   Strong experimental results demonstrating cost reduction and accuracy improvements.
*   Open-source implementation available.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Opaque explanations of the technical details.
*   Clarity issues with the example and table labeling.
*   Unconventional placement of related work.



**Overall:** Despite the clarity issues, the paper presents a significant contribution to the field. The core ideas are novel and the results are promising. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to improve clarity and address the identified issues.
