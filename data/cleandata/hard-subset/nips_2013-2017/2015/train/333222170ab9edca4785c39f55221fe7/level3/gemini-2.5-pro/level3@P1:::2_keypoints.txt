- Proposes "on-the-job learning" to achieve high accuracy from zero labeled examples by querying crowd labelers before making predictions.
- Formulates the problem as a stochastic game and defines a Bayesian utility balancing accuracy, monetary cost, and latency.
- Computes policies approximately using Monte Carlo Tree Search with progressive widening to handle continuous time.
- Evaluated on NER, sentiment, and image classification, showing large cost reductions versus human-only methods and accuracy gains over standard online learning.
- Technically sound: principled stochastic-game formulation, appropriate use of MCTS, and thorough experiments with real-time crowdsourcing and multiple baselines.
- Trade-off noted: LENSE beats 3-vote humans on NER but underperforms 5-vote consensus in F1, highlighting cost vs. absolute-accuracy regimes.
- Clarity issues: core example is abstract and needs a concrete step-by-step illustration of queries, responses, timing, and posterior updates.
- Minor presentation problems: inconsistent naming of the heuristic baseline, a missing figure reference, and unconventional placement of Related Work.
- Originality: novel combination of pre-prediction crowd queries, online model improvement, and explicit latency modeling in a single framework.
- Significance: addresses the cold-start problem with practical, reproducible gains (open-source LENSE) likely useful for human-in-the-loop systems.
- Arguments for acceptance: novel problem formulation, principled Bayesian+MCTS approach, strong empirical gains, and broad practical impact.
- Arguments against acceptance: paper clarity needs improvement and the limits/trade-offs with high-consensus human labeling are not fully discussed.
- Overall: substantive, high-impact contribution whose weaknesses are mainly presentation and discussion issues that can be fixed.
