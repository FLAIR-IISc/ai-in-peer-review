This paper proposes a framework called on-the-job learning, which aims to deploy high-accuracy systems starting with zero training examples. The authors model this setting as a stochastic game based on Bayesian decision theory, allowing them to balance latency, cost, and accuracy objectives in a principled way. The paper is related to previous work at NIPS and elsewhere in the areas of online learning, active learning, active classification, crowdsourcing, and structured prediction.

The main idea of the paper is to use real-time crowdsourcing to resolve uncertainty where needed and output predictions when confident. As the model improves over time, the reliance on crowdsourcing queries decreases. The authors develop an approximation based on Monte Carlo Tree Search to compute the optimal policy, which is intractable to compute exactly.

The paper presents experiments on three datasets: named-entity recognition, sentiment classification, and image classification. The results show that the proposed approach, called LENSE, outperforms machine and human-only comparisons on both quality and cost. On the NER task, LENSE achieves an F1 of 88.4% at more than an order of magnitude reduction in cost compared to full human annotation.

The strengths of the paper include:

* The problem is compelling and well-motivated, addressing a real need in many applications.
* The authors provide a clear and well-structured presentation of their framework and experiments.
* The use of Bayesian decision theory to balance latency, cost, and accuracy objectives is novel and interesting.
* The experiments are thorough and well-designed, providing a good evaluation of the proposed approach.

However, there are also some weaknesses:

* The explanations of some concepts, such as the example at Line 154, are abstract and hard to follow.
* The related work section is placed in an unclear and questionable position.
* The paper could benefit from more concrete and realistic examples to illustrate the concepts and ideas.
* There are some minor errors and inconsistencies in the presentation, such as the missing figure reference and the inconsistent labeling of the threshold baseline.
