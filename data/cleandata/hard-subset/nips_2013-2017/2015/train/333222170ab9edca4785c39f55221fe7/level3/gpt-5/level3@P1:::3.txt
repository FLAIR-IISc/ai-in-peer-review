Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes an on-the-job learning framework that aims to deliver high accuracy from the outset without any pre-collected training data by interleaving real-time crowdsourcing with model predictions. At test time, the system decides which parts of an input to query the crowd about, waits for responses as needed, and returns a prediction when expected utility (balancing accuracy, cost, and latency) is maximized. Over time, the model is updated online, reducing reliance on the crowd.

Formally, the decision process is cast as a two-player stochastic game between the system and the crowd, built on a structured predictor (CRF). The utility function is derived from Bayesian decision theory and trades off expected prediction accuracy against monetary cost and time. The environment model specifies a joint over outputs, crowd responses, and response times. Because exact optimization is intractable, the authors approximate the optimal policy using Monte Carlo Tree Search with progressive widening to handle continuous response times.

This connects to several threads. Unlike online learning, which guarantees only asymptotic regret, the method maintains high accuracy immediately. Unlike active learning, it queries before committing to a prediction. Compared to active classification (e.g., value-of-information), the policy adapts over time by improving the model online; it also operates without access to ground-truth labels at test time. It is related to decision-theoretic control of crowd work (e.g., POMDP-based approaches such as TurKontrol) and to Legion:AR’s real-time crowd-in-the-loop classification, but here the formalization for structured prediction and the use of MCTS for policy computation are new.

Quality
Strengths:
- A principled formulation with a clear objective that balances accuracy, cost, and latency.
- A coherent environment model p(y, r, t | x, q, s) combining the CRF, a simple noise model for crowd responses, and a response-time model.
- A scalable approximate solver (MCTS + progressive widening) that naturally captures sequential vs. parallel querying trade-offs.
- Solid empirical evaluation across NER, sentiment, and image classification; open-source release.

Weaknesses and suggestions:
- The response model pR assumes a fixed 70% accuracy and conditional independence across responses. Estimating worker reliabilities online (or at least marginalizing a Beta prior) would strengthen robustness and better leverage redundant labeling.
- The use of ExpAcc over the MAP prediction is somewhat unusual; expected accuracy under the full predictive distribution or a proper scoring rule would be preferable. Clarify why this surrogate aligns with the stated goals.
- The continuous-time extension complicates the state and search substantially; an ablation comparing discrete-time (or fixed batch rounds) versus the full continuous-time model would clarify its marginal benefit.
- The threshold baseline is competitive in some settings. Include analysis explaining where/why the decision-theoretic policy outperforms simple heuristics (e.g., via query parallelization, adaptive stopping, or structured correlations).

Clarity
The core ideas are compelling but several clarity issues impede readability:
- The introduction of continuous time mid-Section 3 (with send times s and response times t) makes the exposition harder to follow. Recommend first presenting a simpler discrete-time version (single-step/batch queries and a wait/return decision), then extending to continuous time.
- Clearly state the decision-to-return criterion in experiments (e.g., entropy/confidence threshold vs. time/deadline). The text mentions trading off latency but the precise stopping rule and any t_deadline need explicit specification in methods and consistent use in experiments.
- Notation issues:
  - Equation (2): the posterior conditioning used in ExpAcc should include s alongside q, r, t, as elsewhere.
  - Avoid using q both as an action and as a distribution symbol later (rename the latter).
  - The Threshold baseline inequality appears reversed or misstated; the expression should show uncertainty decreasing with the number of queries m.
- Presentation and formatting: ensure best values and relevant latencies are clearly highlighted and consistent across tables/figures. Some baselines (e.g., the threshold baseline on sentiment) are missing in places; please include for completeness.

Originality
The combination of a structured predictor, real-time crowd interaction pre-decision, a Bayesian utility balancing accuracy/time/cost, and an MCTS-based approximate policy is novel and timely. Prior work in active learning/classification and crowd decision-theory provides foundations, but the on-the-job, test-time adaptive querying with online model improvement for structured outputs is a distinct contribution.

Significance
The approach addresses a practical and pressing problem: deploying high-quality systems with zero initial training data and strict latency/cost constraints. The results show:
- On NER, substantial cost reductions versus full human annotation while achieving higher F1 than single-vote and online-learning baselines, and strong overall accuracy (though not necessarily surpassing 5-vote majority).
- On sentiment and faces, competitive accuracy at lower cost than human-only strategies, with the number of crowd queries decreasing as the model improves.
The open-source system is likely to be adopted and extended.

Empirical evaluation
- LENSE generally outperforms single-vote and online-learning baselines and reduces queries over time, especially with richer features (e.g., RNN for sentiment).
- The threshold heuristic is sometimes close in accuracy and latency; a deeper analysis of when decision-theoretic planning pays off would strengthen the empirical narrative.
- Please report 5-vote results consistently across all tasks (they appear missing in some tables/figures) and temper claims of “beating human performance” to specify the human baseline (e.g., 1-vote vs. 5-vote).

Pros
- Clear problem motivation; principled formulation.
- Effective approximate planning with MCTS and progressive widening.
- Strong cost–accuracy trade-offs; decreasing crowd reliance over time.
- Structured prediction setting and open-source release increase impact.

Cons
- Clarity issues around continuous time, stopping rules, and notation.
- Simplistic crowd noise model; no online worker calibration.
- Limited ablation on the value of continuous-time planning vs. simpler baselines.
- Incomplete reporting/formatting inconsistencies in the experimental section.

Recommendation
Accept. The paper presents a well-motivated, original, and practically significant framework that advances decision-theoretic, crowd-in-the-loop learning for structured prediction. Addressing the clarity issues, strengthening the noise model, and adding ablations on continuous time would further improve the work.
