- Introduces an on-the-job learning framework that interleaves model inference with real-time per-part crowd queries to give accurate predictions from the first input without labeled data.  
- Formalizes the problem as a Bayesian decision-theoretic stochastic game trading off accuracy, cost, and latency via a utility function.  
- Uses MCTS with progressive widening to approximate optimal policies in a large continuous-time state space.  
- Implements a CRF for structured prior pθ(y|x), a simple fixed-accuracy response model pR, and an empirical response-time model pT to define the environment.  
- Empirically evaluated on NER, sentiment, and face classification, showing substantial cost reductions and improved accuracy over machine-only and single-vote human baselines.  
- Achieves 88.4% F1 on NER with over an order-of-magnitude cost reduction and outscores online learning and single-vote crowd baselines.  
- Strength: principled decision-theoretic formulation integrating accuracy, monetary cost, and time into a clear utility.  
- Strength: practical planning algorithm and deployable retainer-based crowdsourcing implementation.  
- Strength: careful comparisons to multiple baselines and analysis showing reduced crowd reliance with richer features.  
- Weakness: simplistic pR (fixed 0.7 accuracy, uniform noise) ignores worker heterogeneity, class difficulty, and temporal drift, biasing redundancy decisions.  
- Weakness: utility optimizes expected MAP accuracy while evaluation uses F1 for NER, causing a loss-function mismatch for structured outputs.  
- Weakness: limited details on pT; simulations from a frozen small pool may underrepresent real response-time variance and correlations.  
- Weakness: baseline fairness concerns—online learning baseline uses gold labels unavailable in deployment; baselines should be given equivalent crowd budgets.  
- Weakness: missing discussion of computational budget and planner latency (simulations per decision and wall-clock overhead).  
- Clarity issues: some explanations are opaque—need a concrete end-to-end micro-trace and consistent baseline naming in tables.  
- Presentation issues: missing figure reference, unusual placement of related work after experiments, and unspecified utility weights (wM, wT) and ExpAcc estimation.  
- Originality: novel integration of decision-theoretic planning with real-time crowd querying for test-time structured prediction, and apt use of progressive widening.  
- Significance: addresses a practical cold-start deployment problem, shows meaningful cost/accuracy gains, and provides an open-source system, though limitations temper claims.  
- Arguments for acceptance: principled, novel formulation with implemented system, solid empirical benefits, and insightful latency/parallelism analysis.  
- Arguments against acceptance: simplistic crowd/noise model, insufficient planning/timing details, and presentation/baseline issues.  
- Key questions: how many MCTS simulations and what is planner wall-clock overhead; sensitivity to miscalibrated pR/pT and feasibility of online pR learning; aligning utility with task loss; results under equal crowd budgets for baselines; inclusion of retainer costs.  
- Overall assessment: compelling, practically relevant contribution with promising results; leans toward acceptance pending improved crowd/timing modeling and clearer presentation.
