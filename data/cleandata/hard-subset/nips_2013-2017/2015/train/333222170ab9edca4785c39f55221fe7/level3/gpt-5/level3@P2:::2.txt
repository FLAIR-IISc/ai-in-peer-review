Summary
This paper proposes an “on-the-job learning” framework to deploy high-accuracy systems with zero initial training examples by interleaving real-time crowdsourcing with model prediction. Each incoming input triggers a stochastic game: the system chooses which parts to query the crowd for, whether to wait for responses, or to return a prediction. A Bayesian decision-theoretic utility balances accuracy, latency, and monetary cost. Because exact planning is intractable, the authors approximate the optimal policy using Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) with progressive widening to handle continuous response times. The environment model comprises a structured predictor (e.g., CRF), a simple noise model for crowd responses, and a response-time model. Experiments on NER, sentiment, and image classification show that the approach (LENSE) reduces cost substantially while maintaining or improving accuracy versus baselines, including single- and multi-vote human labels and online learning. LENSE often outperforms 3-vote humans but underperforms 5-vote consensus, illustrating a cost–quality trade-off. This work relates to online learning (improving over time), active learning (seeking labels), active classification (test-time information acquisition), and crowdsourcing control (e.g., POMDP-based approaches such as TurKontrol), and extends them by optimizing jointly over cost, latency, and accuracy in a structured prediction setting with continuous time.

Quality
- Soundness: The decision-theoretic formulation and the use of MCTS/progressive widening are appropriate for the large stochastic game. The utility is well-motivated and captures accuracy, time, and monetary cost.
- Empirics: Results on three datasets are encouraging, including reduced costs and accuracy improvements versus online learning and single-vote crowds. The comparison against multi-vote crowd labels is informative: Table 2 indicates LENSE outperforms 3-vote but underperforms 5-vote, consistent with expectations about consensus denoising.
- Limitations:
  - The response model pR assumes a fixed 70% worker accuracy with uniform confusion. This is a strong simplification; worker heterogeneity and class-specific confusions are common and likely affect optimal policies.
  - The evaluation uses a “frozen pool” of pre-collected worker responses and delays, which is helpful for control but may not reflect live variability or feedback loops (e.g., learning effects, fatigue).
  - Sensitivity of performance to cost/latency weights (wM, wT) and to the response-time model is not fully explored.
  - Computational overhead and latency added by MCTS are not characterized; a practical system must ensure planning time is small relative to crowd response times.
  - Ablations (e.g., without progressive widening, without time modeling, or using a learned worker model) would strengthen the causal claims.

Clarity
- The overall narrative is compelling, but several explanations are opaque:
  - The example around line 154 is abstract; a concrete, fully worked example with actual queries, returned labels, and timestamps would help readers internalize the policy behavior (e.g., parallel vs sequential querying).
  - There is a missing figure reference at line 086 (“Figure ??”).
  - Tables 2 and 3 use inconsistent names for the same baseline (“entropic” vs “threshold”); please standardize terminology and describe the baseline once clearly.
  - Consider reorganizing related work; its current placement (noted as Section 5 in one place) is confusing. A conventional placement immediately after the introduction (or clearly labeled Section 6) would aid readers.
  - Minor notation density: the game-state definition and transition dynamics could be streamlined with a small diagram and a table of symbols.

Originality
- The formulation of on-the-job learning as a Bayesian decision problem that explicitly trades off accuracy, latency, and monetary cost, combined with MCTS and progressive widening for continuous-time crowdsourcing, is novel and timely.
- Differences from prior art are clear: unlike standard online/active learning, the system can solicit information before committing to a prediction; unlike prior active classification with static policies, the model improves during test time; unlike prior POMDP-based crowdsourcing work, this paper emphasizes structured prediction and continuous-time planning.

Significance
- Practical significance is high for domains requiring immediate deployment with no labeled data (e.g., crisis informatics, new domains for NER). The demonstrated cost reductions while maintaining accuracy will interest both researchers and practitioners.
- Methodologically, jointly optimizing for latency and cost is important for real-time systems and underexplored in ML literature.
- While LENSE does not always beat a strong 5-vote consensus, the cost–quality trade-offs are attractive and the idea can seed further work on richer worker models and more scalable planners.

Pros
- Clear problem formulation addressing a real need: high accuracy from zero labeled data.
- Principled Bayesian decision-theoretic objective that includes latency.
- Elegant use of MCTS with progressive widening for continuous-time decisions.
- Demonstrated improvements over online learning and single-vote crowds; competitive with multi-vote at lower cost.
- Open-source implementation and shared datasets support reproducibility.

Cons
- Simplistic crowd noise model; no worker heterogeneity or uncertainty calibration.
- Limited sensitivity analyses and ablations; unclear robustness across settings and cost/latency weights.
- Some clarity issues (missing figure ref, inconsistent baseline naming, abstract example).
- Computational cost of planning not reported; scalability concerns.
- Evaluation via frozen response pools may not capture dynamics of real deployments.

Questions and suggestions for the authors
- Provide a fully worked example with concrete queries, response timings, and evolving posteriors to illustrate sequential vs parallel querying and return decisions (addressing the abstractness noted around line 154).
- Standardize baseline naming (“threshold” vs “entropic”) across Tables 2 and 3; ensure consistent descriptions.
- Fix the “Figure ??” reference (line 086).
- Clarify the placement of Related Work and ensure consistent section numbering.
- Report planning overhead (wall-clock per decision) and its impact on end-to-end latency; include a budget on maximum planning time.
- Add sensitivity analyses over wM and wT and over response-time distribution parameters; plot cost–latency–accuracy Pareto frontiers.
- Explore more realistic worker models (e.g., Dawid–Skene/GLAD or per-class confusion), or at least provide an ablation showing the impact of mis-specified pR.
- Include ablations: no time modeling; no progressive widening; heuristic policies; and a POMDP baseline if feasible.
- Discuss generalization beyond retainer pools and implications for worker recruitment latency.

Recommendation
Weak Accept. The problem is important, the formulation is principled and novel, and empirical results are promising, though clarity issues and modeling simplifications should be addressed. With revisions, this could be a strong contribution to decision-theoretic learning with human-in-the-loop.

Confidence
Medium.
