- Proposes a framework for on-the-job learning that starts with zero training examples by modeling the setting as a stochastic game and applying game-playing ideas.  
- Combines real-time crowdsourcing with self-prediction, gradually shifting reliance from crowd to model as it improves.  
- Main contribution is a more theoretically grounded formulation of an "on-the-job learner" building on prior work [22].  
- Presents LENSE, an algorithm based on Monte Carlo Tree Search and progressive widening to handle continuous time.  
- Evaluated on NER, sentiment, and image classification; reports LENSE outperforming machine- and human-only baselines in both quality and cost.  
- Strong empirical result: NER F1 of 88.4% with over an order-of-magnitude reduction in cost compared to full human annotation.  
- Paper is technically sound and well-organized, with clear connections to online learning, active learning, and crowdsourcing.  
- Presentation omits important technical details (e.g., definition of N(s) in Algorithm 1 and justification for baseline thresholds).  
- Empirical comparisons are not fully convincing: the threshold-based heuristic baseline is weak and “online” systems aren’t thoroughly analyzed or confirmed as best choices.  
- Model capacity appears limited; the impact of richer features or models is not explored.  
- Risks and challenges of real-time crowdsourcing (worker quality/reliability) are not discussed.  
- Originality is moderate: a novel combination of familiar techniques but not a major advance over prior work.  
- Recommendation: accept, contingent on addressing technical omissions and providing deeper empirical analysis.  
- Review scores: Quality 8/10, Clarity 8/10, Originality 7/10, Significance 7/10.
