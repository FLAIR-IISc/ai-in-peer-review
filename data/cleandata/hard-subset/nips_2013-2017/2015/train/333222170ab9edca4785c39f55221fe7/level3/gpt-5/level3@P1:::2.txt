This paper introduces an on-the-job learning framework that delivers high-accuracy predictions from the first input without upfront labeled data by interleaving model inference with real-time, per-part crowd queries. The approach is formalized as a stochastic game grounded in Bayesian decision theory, explicitly trading off accuracy, cost, and latency via a utility function. Optimal policies are approximated using Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) with progressive widening to handle continuous time. A CRF (for NER) provides the structured prior pθ(y|x); a simple response model pR (fixed accuracy, independent noise) and an empirical response-time model pT together define the environment. Experiments on NER, sentiment, and face classification demonstrate that the proposed system (LENSE) reduces cost substantially relative to full human annotation and outperforms machine-only and single-vote human baselines; on NER it achieves 88.4% F1 with over an order-of-magnitude cost reduction, and improves F1 by 8% over single-vote crowd and 28% over online learning. Compared with prior work, the paper combines ideas from online/active learning and active classification with decision-theoretic crowdsourcing control (e.g., TurKontrol) and real-time crowd systems (e.g., Legion:AR), but develops a unified, principled planner that reasons jointly about cost, latency, and uncertainty for structured prediction.

Quality
Strengths:
- Sound decision-theoretic formulation with a clear utility that integrates accuracy, monetary cost, and time.
- Practical planning algorithm (MCTS + progressive widening) appropriate for the large, continuous state space; retainer-based crowdsourcing implementation shows deployability.
- Careful comparisons to multiple baselines, including human consensus, a heuristic thresholding policy, and an optimistic online-learning baseline.
- Empirical evidence across three tasks; analysis of how richer features reduce reliance on the crowd over time.

Weaknesses and concerns:
- The crowd response model pR uses a fixed 0.7 accuracy and uniform noise across classes and workers. This ignores worker heterogeneity, class-dependent difficulty, and temporal drift; it likely biases the planner’s redundancy decisions. Modeling worker reliabilities and task/item difficulty (e.g., Dawid–Skene, GLAD) or learning pR on-the-fly would strengthen results.
- The utility uses expected accuracy of the MAP prediction; however, evaluation for NER is by F1. The mismatch may misguide planning for structured outputs. Consider a token-level expected accuracy proxy or a structured loss closer to F1.
- Details on pT are limited; while the retainer setup is described, the timing model used by the planner is not fully specified. Also, simulation from a frozen pool of five responses per label may underrepresent variance and correlations.
- Baseline fairness: “online learning” trains on gold labels after each instance; in the intended deployment setting, such gold labels are not available. A stronger comparison would allow baselines the same budget spent on crowd labels (e.g., batch active learning with the same query budget but without test-time adaptivity).
- Computational budget and latency of planning are not discussed (number of simulations per decision, wall-clock overhead). This is important for real-time operation.

Clarity
- Explanations are sometimes opaque. The example around line 154 is abstract; include a concrete, end-to-end micro-trace with explicit queries, timestamps, received labels (including a noisy response), posterior updates, and the planner’s action choices.
- Tables 2 and 3 inconsistently name the same baseline as “threshold” vs “entropic”; please standardize.
- There is a missing figure reference at line 086 (“Figure ??”).
- The placement of related work after experiments is unusual and makes it harder to situate the contribution early. Consider moving or summarizing key related threads earlier to contrast with online active learning, active classification, and crowd-POMDP control.
- Specify wM and wT and include a sensitivity analysis; briefly state how ExpAcc is estimated in practice for structured outputs.

Originality
- Novel integration of decision-theoretic planning with real-time crowd querying for test-time structured prediction, explicitly optimizing the latency–cost–accuracy frontier. While related to NIPS work on active classification and MCTS planning, the formulation and deployment focus are distinct.
- The use of progressive widening for continuous response times in this setting is a thoughtful adaptation.

Significance
- Addresses a practically important setting—cold-start, high-accuracy deployment without labeled data—where neither pure ML nor pure crowdsourcing is satisfactory.
- Demonstrates meaningful cost reductions and accuracy gains, particularly on NER; provides an open-source system, increasing impact and reproducibility.
- The limitations in the response model and evaluation protocol reduce the strength of the claims but are addressable and do not negate the main contribution.

Arguments for acceptance
- Clear, relevant problem with a principled, novel formulation and an implemented system.
- Solid empirical benefits and insightful analysis of latency/parallelism versus accuracy tradeoffs.
- Likely to influence subsequent work on interactive and crowd-in-the-loop ML.

Arguments against acceptance
- Simplistic and fixed crowd/noise model; lack of worker/task heterogeneity undermines the decision-theoretic claims.
- Limited detail on planning budget and timing overhead; unclear end-to-end real-time feasibility.
- Some clarity and consistency issues in presentation and baselines.

Questions for the authors
- How many MCTS simulations are used per decision, and what is the planner’s wall-clock overhead relative to response times?
- How sensitive is performance to miscalibration of pR and pT? Can pR be learned online, per-worker, and folded into the planner?
- Can the utility be aligned more closely with the task loss (e.g., token-level accuracy, structured F1 approximations)?
- Do results persist if baselines receive the same total crowd budget but cannot interleave queries with test-time inference?
- Are retainer costs (e.g., $1 sign-up) included in utility/cost analysis?

Overall assessment
A compelling and practically relevant contribution with a principled formulation and promising results. With improved modeling of crowd noise/timing and clearer presentation, this work would be strong. I lean toward acceptance.
