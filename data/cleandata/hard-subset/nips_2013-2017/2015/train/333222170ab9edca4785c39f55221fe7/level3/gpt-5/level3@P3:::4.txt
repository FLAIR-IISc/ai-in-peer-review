Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes “on‑the‑job learning,” a test‑time interactive framework that maintains high accuracy from the start by querying a real‑time crowd when uncertainty is high, and gradually reducing dependence on the crowd as a statistical model improves. The decision process is cast as a stochastic game and optimized (approximately) with Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) and progressive widening under a Bayesian decision‑theoretic utility that trades accuracy, latency, and monetary cost. The prediction model is a CRF for structured outputs (NER) and standard classifiers for unstructured outputs (sentiment, faces). The environment includes a fixed noise model for crowd responses and an empirical latency model. Experiments on NER, sentiment, and face classification compare the proposed system (LENSE) to human n‑vote, an “online learning” baseline, and a handcrafted “threshold” baseline.

The work sits at the intersection of online learning, active learning, active classification, crowdsourcing, and structured prediction. It is closest in spirit to decision‑theoretic test‑time acquisition (active classification) and crowd‑powered POMDPs (e.g., TurKontrol), but differs by continually updating the model online while optimizing per‑instance decisions before prediction.

Strengths
- Clear and timely problem: delivering high accuracy with zero pretraining via real‑time crowd interaction is compelling for rapid deployment.
- Principled formulation: a unified utility over accuracy/latency/cost and a coherent stochastic game are well motivated.
- Practical algorithm: MCTS + progressive widening is a reasonable, scalable approximation for continuous‑time stochastic actions.
- Real‑time implementation: the retainer pool and released code/data improve reproducibility and demonstrate feasibility.
- Positive results on NER: substantial cost reductions relative to full human labeling and improvements over single‑vote and the online learning baseline.

Weaknesses and questions
- Scope and utility across examples: The per‑instance utility does not account for the value of information for future instances. In non‑structured tasks (sentiment, faces) where there is no intra‑instance structure to propagate, the only way to amortize cost is via parameter updates. Optimizing a purely myopic per‑instance utility may underinvest in learning early. Please discuss and, if possible, quantify whether policies that spend more early to reduce later costs would be superior.
- Relation to active online learning: The distinction remains weak. One can adapt online active learning to this setting by treating the current instance as the “active pool,” issuing label requests prior to prediction (possibly sequentially), and updating the model with the responses. A strong baseline would be a myopic expected value‑of‑information (EVI) or uncertainty‑sampling policy that queries until a calibrated confidence threshold is met, with online updates after each response.
- Baselines: 
  - The threshold baseline uses two seemingly arbitrary parameters (0.3 error reduction per in‑flight query and a 0.98 confidence target). Their origin and sensitivity are not reported. This undermines comparisons to LENSE.
  - An uncertainty‑sampling/entropy baseline (active learning style), with sequential querying and early stopping upon confidence, is missing and is an important comparator.
  - For structured prediction, a simple structured EVI or lookahead‑1 policy (issue the next query that maximizes expected marginal F1 gain per unit time/cost) would be informative.
- Modeling of the crowd: The fixed pR(r|y)=0.7 uniform‑else model is crude, while the experiments use recorded responses from a small frozen pool. This mismatch may bias results and ignores worker heterogeneity. A Dawid–Skene–style worker confusion model or per‑item difficulty model would be more realistic; at minimum, report sensitivity to the assumed 0.7.
- Application to face recognition: It is unclear what is queried. Is the entire instance label requested from the crowd, or are attributes/regions queried (e.g., following Kumar et al.)? If only whole‑image labels are queried, how does LENSE differ from a standard crowd‑in‑the‑loop classifier; if attributes/parts are queried, please specify the query space and how those responses feed the classifier.
- Metric and decision loss: ExpAcc uses MAP accuracy of argmax(y). For structured outputs and skewed metrics (e.g., F1 in NER), optimizing expected 0‑1 accuracy of the MAP sequence may be misaligned. Consider expected F1 or token‑level utility in the decision.
- Sensitivity and calibration: Results depend on wM (money) and wT (time). Sensitivity analyses, calibration of predictive probabilities, and ablations on MCTS parameters (rollout depth, progressive widening exponent) are not reported.
- Clarifications on updates: The paper states the CRF is learned from “actual responses,” not simulated ones. Are noisy responses used directly as labels without debiasing? How is latency handled during learning updates? How often are parameters updated (per response vs per instance)?

Quality
Technically sound overall, with a reasonable approximation to an intractable decision problem and a working system. However, the evaluation is limited by missing strong baselines (uncertainty sampling/EVI), unclear choices in the threshold baseline, and lack of sensitivity analyses. The crowd noise model and its alignment with the deployment setup could be improved.

Clarity
The main idea is well presented. Important details are missing or ambiguous: the exact query space for faces (and sentiment), how responses are integrated for unstructured tasks, the rationale for threshold‑baseline parameters, and hyperparameters for MCTS/progressive widening. Including pseudocode is helpful, but clearer descriptions of termination criteria, rollout policies, and training updates would aid reproducibility.

Originality
The combination of real‑time crowd querying before prediction, online updating, and a unified decision‑theoretic formulation solved with MCTS is novel and interesting. Related work is cited appropriately, though the distinction from adaptable active online learning could be sharpened with stronger baselines.

Significance
Promising for rapid deployment scenarios, with convincing gains on NER. Applicability to non‑structured tasks appears limited in its current form, both because there is no structural propagation within an instance and because the utility ignores cross‑instance VOI. Strengthening the baselines and clarifying the non‑structured settings would improve the case for broader impact.

Requested additional experiments/analyses
- Add uncertainty‑sampling and myopic EVI baselines (sequential and parallel querying), with online updates after each response.
- Report sensitivity to threshold‑baseline parameters (0.3, 0.98), wM and wT, and the crowd accuracy assumption pR=0.7.
- Evaluate worker‑modeling variants (e.g., Dawid–Skene; per‑worker reliabilities) and show effect on policy and outcomes.
- For faces, specify the query granularity (whole‑image vs attributes/regions) and compare attribute‑level querying to whole‑label querying.
- For structured outputs, compare sequence‑level vs token‑level utility and expected F1 decision rules.
- Provide ablations on MCTS hyperparameters and time budgets; report compute latency to ensure on‑the‑job feasibility.

Pros for acceptance
- Novel, principled, and practical framework with real‑time implementation.
- Significant improvements on NER with reduced cost.
- Clear problem motivation and good potential for real‑world deployment.

Cons against acceptance
- Missing key baselines and sensitivity analyses; threshold baseline appears ad hoc.
- Unclear application and limited gains on non‑structured tasks; no cross‑instance VOI in the utility.
- Weak distinction from adaptable active online learning; could be addressed empirically.
