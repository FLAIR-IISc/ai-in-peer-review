

This paper introduces "on-the-job learning," a framework for building high-accuracy classifiers from zero labeled examples by strategically querying crowdsourced responses during deployment. The authors model the problem as a stochastic game based on Bayesian decision theory, balancing latency, cost, and accuracy. They develop an approximation using Monte Carlo Tree Search to handle the intractable optimal policy computation. The approach is evaluated across three tasks (NER, sentiment, image classification), showing significant cost reductions (over an order of magnitude for NER) compared to full human annotation while improving accuracy over baselines.

The problem is compelling and addresses a meaningful gap between traditional supervised learning and pure crowdsourcing. The timing and latency optimization aspects represent a novel contribution to the field. The authors demonstrate significant improvements over both human-only and online learning baselines, with LENSE achieving 8% F1 improvement over single-human labeling and 28% over online learning on NER.

However, the paper suffers from several clarity issues that hinder understanding. The example at Line 154 (Section 3) is abstract and would benefit from a concrete illustration with realistic crowd responses and timing data. Tables 2 and 3 inconsistently label the threshold baseline as "entropic" versus "threshold," creating confusion. The related work placement in Section 5 is poorly organized and fails to adequately distinguish from prior work like Legion:AR. Additionally, Line 086 references a missing figure, and the explanation of the tradeoffs between LENSE and multi-vote approaches (Table 2) remains somewhat opaque despite the promising results.

The technical quality is strong with well-motivated experiments, but the presentation needs significant improvement. The paper's core innovation—using Bayesian decision theory for on-the-job learning with dynamic crowd querying—is valuable and addresses an important practical challenge. However, the inconsistent labeling, unclear examples, and poor organization of related work weaken the overall presentation.

Pro: Compelling problem statement, novel timing/latency optimization, strong empirical results showing significant cost reduction with improved accuracy.  
Con: Poorly explained examples, inconsistent terminology, unclear related work placement, and missing figure reference undermine the otherwise strong contribution.

The paper makes a meaningful contribution to the literature on active learning and crowdsourcing, but requires substantial revision to clarify its methodology and presentation before acceptance.
