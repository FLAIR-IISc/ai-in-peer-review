- Proposes “on-the-job learning”: decision-theoretic framework that interleaves model predictions with real-time crowd queries to deploy high-accuracy classifiers from zero labels.  
- Formulates test-time querying as a stochastic game optimizing a Bayesian utility that trades accuracy, latency, and monetary cost; uses MCTS with progressive widening to approximate the policy.  
- Evaluates LENSE on NER, sentiment, and face classification against online learning, a threshold baseline, and n-vote human consensus, situating work relative to prior online/active learning and crowd-POMDP systems.  
- Novelty: timing/latency optimization for real-time crowd querying is a compelling and original contribution, especially for structured prediction.  
- Quality: formulation is sound and utility-based policy well motivated; empirical results are informative and include human-consensus comparisons.  
- Need clearer framing: Table 2’s result (LENSE >3-vote but <5-vote) should be explicitly presented as a cost–accuracy (Pareto) tradeoff.  
- Response model (pR ≈ 0.7) requires calibration justification and sensitivity analysis.  
- Frozen worker pool simulation is practical but needs robustness studies across varying worker accuracy and latency distributions.  
- Clarity problems: exposition sometimes opaque and the example (Line 154) is abstract—provide a concrete query sequence with realistic responses and timestamps.  
- Presentation issues: inconsistent baseline naming (“entropic” vs “threshold”), missing figure reference (Line 086), and Related Work placement disrupts flow.  
- Significance: high—important for rapid deployment, shows cost savings vs human-only and accuracy gains vs machine-only.  
- Pros: compelling problem, principled utility formulation, novel latency-aware querying, clear empirical tradeoffs, human baselines, and open-source code.  
- Cons: clarity/consistency issues, limited sensitivity analyses, and lack of framing for the 5-vote underperformance as a Pareto tradeoff.  
- Recommendation: Weak accept contingent on addressing clarity, unifying baseline names, adding a concrete example, and including sensitivity/ablation studies for worker noise, latency, and time reasoning.
