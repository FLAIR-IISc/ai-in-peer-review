## Review of “On-the-Job Learning with Bayesian Decision Theory”

This paper presents a compelling framework, LENSE, for on-the-job learning, tackling the problem of deploying AI systems with limited initial labeled data. The core idea – a hybrid system that intelligently balances querying a crowd with leveraging an online-trained model – is well-motivated and addresses a practical challenge. Framing the problem as a stochastic game with a CRF and optimizing for expected utility is a novel and theoretically sound approach. The use of MCTS + TD learning to approximate the intractable optimization is a reasonable strategy. 

The experimental evaluation on NER, sentiment, and face-ID tasks demonstrates the potential of LENSE, showing cost reductions and accuracy improvements over several baselines. The observation that the model’s reliance on crowd labels decreases over time is a key strength, highlighting its adaptive nature. The authors’ commitment to open-sourcing the implementation is commendable and will facilitate reproducibility and further research.

However, the paper suffers from clarity issues, particularly in the introduction of continuous time in Section 3. This extension, while theoretically interesting, adds significant complexity and its experimental benefit appears limited, as the threshold baseline performs competitively. The authors should strongly consider presenting the core method *without* continuous time first, and then treat the continuous-time extension as a separate, more advanced addition. 

Several specific issues require attention. The claim of “beats human performance” for 5-vote NER is incorrect. Crucially, 5-vote results are missing for Face-ID and Sentiment in several tables/figures, hindering a complete comparison. The experimental setup regarding the use of `t_deadline` and the criteria for reporting answers (entropic threshold vs. time) needs clarification. Notationally, equation (2) should include `s` in the distribution, and equation (5) uses `q` for a distribution function, which is confusing. Finally, the description of the threshold baseline contains a likely typo in the uncertainty expression.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novel and significant approach to on-the-job learning.
*   Strong theoretical foundation based on Bayesian decision theory and stochastic games.
*   Promising experimental results demonstrating cost reduction and accuracy gains.
*   Open-source implementation promotes reproducibility and adoption.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   Clarity issues, particularly regarding the continuous-time extension.
*   Limited experimental benefit from the continuous-time extension.
*   Reporting errors and omissions in experimental results.
*   Minor notation and presentation issues.



Despite these weaknesses, the originality and potential impact of this work are substantial. I recommend acceptance, contingent upon the authors addressing the clarity, reporting, and notation issues outlined above. Prioritizing clarity and streamlining the presentation will significantly enhance the paper’s accessibility and impact.
