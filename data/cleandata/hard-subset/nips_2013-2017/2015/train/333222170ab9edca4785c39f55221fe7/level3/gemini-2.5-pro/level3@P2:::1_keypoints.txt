- Presents "on-the-job learning": start from zero training examples using real-time crowdsourcing and gradually transition to a learned model.
- Formulates the problem as a stochastic game and uses Bayesian decision theory to trade off accuracy, cost, and latency.
- Uses MCTS with progressive widening to approximate the intractable optimal policy.
- Implements system LENSE and evaluates on NER, sentiment, and image classification, showing cost reductions vs. pure-crowd and accuracy gains vs. pure online learning.
- Strength: problem is significant and the on-the-job learning paradigm is practical and original.
- Strength: formalizing the setting as a stochastic game is elegant and a valuable theoretical contribution.
- Strength: combining Bayesian decision theory with MCTS is a sound, well-motivated approach.
- Weakness: missing/unclear technical details hinder reproducibility (e.g., conditional independence assumption in crowd response model pR(ri | yqi) not justified).
- Weakness: notation issues (Algorithm 1 uses N(s) without explicit definition or consistent symbols).
- Weakness: "Threshold baseline" uses unexplained constants (uncertainty reduction 0.3, confidence 0.98), making the baseline seem arbitrary.
- Weakness: experimental baselines are weak or not well-justified—threshold heuristic is non-adaptive and online baseline trained on gold labels may not reflect realistic or state-of-the-art comparisons.
- Weakness: unclear whether gains come from human-in-the-loop rather than the MCTS policy; needs analysis to disentangle effects.
- Weakness: lacks qualitative analysis—should include example query sequences and error analysis to illustrate policy behavior and practical benefits.
- Recommendation: paper is promising and merits acceptance but should address clarity and evaluation gaps in the final version.
