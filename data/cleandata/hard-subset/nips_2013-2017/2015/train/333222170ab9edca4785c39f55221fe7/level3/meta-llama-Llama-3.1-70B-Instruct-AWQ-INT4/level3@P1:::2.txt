This paper proposes a novel framework called on-the-job learning, which enables the deployment of high-accuracy systems starting from zero training examples. The framework leverages real-time crowdsourcing to resolve uncertainty where needed and outputs predictions when confident. The authors model the on-the-job learning setting as a stochastic game based on Bayesian decision theory, which allows for the principled balancing of latency, cost, and accuracy objectives. The paper presents a comprehensive evaluation of the proposed approach on three datasets, demonstrating its effectiveness in reducing cost while maintaining high accuracy.

The paper is well-motivated, and the problem it addresses is compelling. The authors provide a clear and concise introduction to the problem, highlighting the limitations of existing approaches and the need for a new framework. The technical core of the paper, which models the on-the-job setting as a stochastic game, is well-presented and easy to follow. The authors also provide a thorough evaluation of their approach, comparing it to several baselines and demonstrating its effectiveness in reducing cost while maintaining high accuracy.

However, there are some areas where the paper could be improved. For example, the related work section is placed in a somewhat unclear and questionable location. It would be more conventional to place this section immediately after the introduction, as is typical in most research papers. Additionally, some of the explanations in the paper are somewhat opaque, and the authors could benefit from providing more concrete examples to illustrate their points. For instance, the example at Line 154 is abstract and hard to follow, and a more concrete query with realistic crowd responses and timings would be more helpful.

Furthermore, there are some minor errors and inconsistencies in the paper. For example, Tables 2 and 3 inconsistently label the threshold baseline as "entropic" vs "threshold," which is confusing. Additionally, Line 086 is missing a figure reference ("Figure ??"). These errors are minor and do not detract from the overall quality of the paper, but they should be addressed in a revised version.

In terms of the criteria for evaluation, the paper scores well on originality, as it proposes a novel framework for on-the-job learning. The paper also scores well on significance, as it addresses a compelling problem and demonstrates the effectiveness of the proposed approach. The paper is well-written and easy to follow, scoring well on clarity. However, the paper could benefit from more concrete examples and a more conventional placement of the related work section.
