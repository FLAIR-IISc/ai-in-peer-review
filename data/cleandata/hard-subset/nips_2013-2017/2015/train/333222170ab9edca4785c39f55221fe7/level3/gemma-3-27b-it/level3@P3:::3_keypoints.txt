- Proposes LENSE, an on-the-job learning framework that dynamically balances crowd labeling and an internally trained model, transitioning from heavy crowdsourcing to self-sufficiency.  
- Frames the problem as a stochastic game using Bayesian decision theory to balance accuracy, latency, and cost.  
- Uses MCTS and TD learning to approximate the intractable optimal policy.  
- Original and significant contribution addressing deployment without upfront labeled data.  
- Practical relevance for resource-constrained AI system deployment.  
- Methodology is well-motivated and theoretically sound (Bayesian decision theory, MCTS, TD).  
- Empirical results show promise across NER, sentiment, and face ID, with notable cost reduction on NER.  
- Open-source code release improves reproducibility and impact.  
- Reviewer recommends acceptance contingent on revisions.  
- Continuous-time formulation in Section 3 hurts clarity and makes the paper harder to follow.  
- Experimental gains from the continuous-time extension appear limited, questioning its added complexity.  
- Incorrect claim of surpassing human performance on NER (5-vote) and missing 5-vote baselines for sentiment and face ID in tables/figures.  
- Presentation issues: best results and latency values are not consistently bolded; threshold baseline missing from sentiment experiments.  
- Ambiguity in experimental setup: unclear usage of t_deadline and whether answers are reported by entropic threshold or time limit.  
- Notational errors: Equation (2) omits s in p(y,r,t|x,q,s); Equation (5) reuses q confusingly as a distribution.  
- Potential typo in the threshold-baseline uncertainty expression that needs correction.  
- Recommendation to restructure: present core method without continuous time first and treat continuous time as an extension.  
- Recommendation to re-evaluate or remove continuous-time component if benefits are marginal.  
- Required corrections: remove incorrect human-performance claim, add missing 5-vote baselines and threshold baseline for sentiment.  
- Clarify experimental details (t_deadline and answer-reporting criteria) and fix notation and inequality/typo errors.  
- Improve formatting: bold best results and relevant latency values for readability.  
- Acceptance arguments: addresses a significant problem, novel and sound approach, promising results, and open-source code.  
- Rejection arguments: current lack of clarity on continuous time, limited empirical benefit of that component, and reporting/presentation errors detract from quality.
