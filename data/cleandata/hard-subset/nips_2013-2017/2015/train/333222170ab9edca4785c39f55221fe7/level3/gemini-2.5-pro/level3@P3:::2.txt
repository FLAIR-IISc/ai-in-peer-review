This paper introduces "on-the-job learning," a framework for deploying a high-accuracy classifier starting with zero labeled examples. In this setting, as new inputs arrive, the system uses real-time crowdsourcing to resolve uncertainty for parts of the input, making a prediction only when confident. The model improves over time, gradually reducing its reliance on the crowd. The authors frame this problem as a stochastic game, using Bayesian decision theory to balance the competing objectives of accuracy, cost, and latency. Since computing the optimal policy is intractable, they propose an approximation based on Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) with progressive widening. This work builds on ideas from online learning, active learning, and crowdsourcing, but distinguishes itself by actively seeking information *before* making a prediction on a test instance to guarantee high performance from the outset, a key difference from standard online and active learning paradigms.

**Strengths:**

*   **Significance and Originality:** The problem of building accurate systems from a cold start is highly significant and practical. The "on-the-job learning" formulation is a compelling and novel approach to this challenge. The key contribution is the principled integration of latency and cost into the decision-making process, moving beyond the traditional active learning question of *which example* to label to the more nuanced questions of *which part* of an example to query, how many times, and when to stop.
*   **Technical Quality:** The proposed method of modeling the problem as a stochastic game and using MCTS is technically sound. The experiments are well-designed, evaluating the LENSE system on three different tasks (NER, sentiment, and image classification) against reasonable baselines. The use of a "frozen pool" of pre-collected worker responses is a thoughtful detail that enhances the reproducibility and fairness of the experimental comparisons.
*   **Impactful Results:** The results are strong, particularly on the NER task, where LENSE achieves a greater than 10x reduction in cost compared to a 5-vote human baseline while improving accuracy over the expert labels. This demonstrates the system's potential for significant practical impact in real-world applications where both accuracy and resource efficiency are critical.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

*   **Clarity of Explanation:** While the overall idea is compelling, the paper's explanations are at times opaque, which could hinder reader comprehension and adoption. The core concepts could be made more accessible with clearer examples.
    *   The example in the "Behavior" subsection (following Figure 2a, around line 154) is too abstract. It describes timelines where the system queries tokens and receives correct or incorrect labels. This would be much more powerful as a concrete walkthrough. For example: "For the input 'Soup on George str.', the system is uncertain about 'George'. Action: Query(George). The crowd response is 'LOCATION' with a 1.2s delay. The model's posterior for P(y='LOCATION'|x) at that position increases, and its confidence in 'str.' also being a 'LOCATION' is boosted through the CRF structure. Action: Return." A contrasting timeline showing a noisy 'PERSON' response and the system's decision to issue a second query would effectively illustrate the framework's logic.
    *   The results in Tables 2 and 3 are confusing due to inconsistent naming for one of the baselines. In Table 2, it is called the "entropic baseline," while in Table 3, it is the "threshold baseline." This should be standardized.
*   **Discussion of Results:** The results in Table 2 are interesting and merit a deeper discussion. LENSE outperforms the 3-vote baseline but underperforms the 5-vote baseline on the NER and Faces tasks. This highlights a crucial tradeoff between the sophisticated de-noising strategy of LENSE and the brute-force consensus of additional human votes. The paper would be strengthened by a more in-depth analysis of this result. Is this gap due to limitations in the MCTS approximation, the noise model, or an inherent difficulty in modeling complex human error patterns that multiple votes can overcome? Acknowledging and exploring this limitation would provide a more complete picture of the method's capabilities.
*   **Paper Organization:** The placement of the "Related Work" section near the end of the paper (Section 6) is unconventional for a NIPS paper. This section is crucial for contextualizing the work and highlighting its novelty. Moving it to follow the introduction would help readers better situate the contribution within the existing literature from the outset.
*   **Minor Issues:** The manuscript requires careful proofreading. For instance, there appears to be a missing figure reference on Line 086 ("Figure ??").

**Summary and Recommendation:**

This paper presents a novel and significant contribution to the problem of learning with limited labeled data. The "on-the-job learning" framework and its formulation as a stochastic game are elegant and produce impressive empirical results. The primary weaknesses relate to clarity and the depth of the discussion, which are addressable through revision. The work is original, technically solid, and likely to be influential for researchers and practitioners working on interactive machine learning and crowdsourcing systems. I recommend acceptance, with the strong suggestion that the authors revise the paper to improve its clarity and expand the discussion as outlined above.
