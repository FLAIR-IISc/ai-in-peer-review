This paper proposes a novel framework for on-the-job learning, which leverages real-time crowdsourcing to resolve uncertainty and output predictions when confident. The authors model this setting as a stochastic game based on Bayesian decision theory, allowing them to balance latency, cost, and accuracy objectives in a principled way. The paper presents a comprehensive evaluation of the proposed approach on three tasks: named-entity recognition, sentiment classification, and image classification.

The paper's main contribution is the development of a hybrid system that uses crowdsourced labels early and gradually relies on an online-trained model as confidence increases. This approach is framed as a stochastic game, where the system and crowd are players issuing queries and receiving responses with utility U(q,r). The authors incorporate continuous time and optimize expected utility with respect to a deadline that controls query timing and rate. While the exact optimization is intractable, the authors use Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) and Temporal Difference (TD) learning as approximations.

The evaluation of the proposed approach, dubbed LENSE, shows that it outperforms most baselines but not the 5-vote majority. The threshold baseline performs nearly as well and sometimes with lower latency. The results also demonstrate that the model requires decreasing numbers of crowd labels over time and attains high initial accuracy by leveraging crowd expertise when uncertainty is high.

The paper's strengths include its originality, significance, and potential to spur discussion and adoption. The authors provide a thorough evaluation of their approach and demonstrate its effectiveness on multiple tasks. The paper's clarity is also notable, with a well-organized and easy-to-follow presentation.

However, there are some areas for improvement. The introduction of continuous time in Section 3 complicates the exposition and algorithmic details, making it challenging to follow. The authors could consider presenting the core method without continuous time first and treating continuous time as an extension to improve clarity and adoption. Additionally, there are some notation issues, such as the use of q for a distribution function in equation (5), which could be confusing.

The paper also has some minor errors and omissions, such as the claim of "beating human performance" being incorrect for 5-vote NER, and missing 5-vote results for Face-ID and Sentiment in several tables and figures. The authors should ensure that all results are accurately reported and consistent throughout the paper.

In terms of related work, the authors provide a comprehensive overview of the relevant literature, including online learning, active learning, active classification, crowdsourcing, and structured prediction. However, they could further clarify how their approach differs from existing work in these areas.

Overall, this paper presents a novel and effective approach to on-the-job learning, with a thorough evaluation and a clear presentation. While there are some areas for improvement, the paper's strengths make it a valuable contribution to the field.
