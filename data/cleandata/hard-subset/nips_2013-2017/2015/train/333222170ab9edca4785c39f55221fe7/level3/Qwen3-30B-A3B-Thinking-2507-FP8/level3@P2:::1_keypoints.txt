- Introduces "on-the-job learning": start with zero training data and use real-time crowdsourcing during inference to resolve uncertainty.  
- Frames the problem as a stochastic game using Bayesian decision theory to trade off latency, cost, and accuracy.  
- Optimal policy is intractable; authors approximate it with Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) plus progressive widening.  
- Evaluated on NER, sentiment, and image classification, reporting large cost reductions and improvements over baselines.  
- Builds on prior "on-the-job" and active/online learning work but claims a more principled, Bayesian formulation.  
- Technical soundness is generally good, but significant gaps undermine the contribution.  
- Equation (2) for expected accuracy omits conditioning on query history and responses, making it problematic.  
- Transition from p_R(r_i | y) to p_R(r_i | y, q_i) in the environment model is not justified.  
- MCTS implementation details are insufficient: N(σ) undefined and progressive widening over continuous time is unclear.  
- Baseline comparison uses a threshold heuristic lacking theoretical grounding, so gains may reflect better heuristics rather than the framework.  
- Paper is well written and organized, with good figures, but lacks critical technical and implementation details.  
- Core idea and theoretical framing are novel and meaningful; applying MCTS is appropriate but not novel by itself.  
- Results are promising and practically significant (e.g., major cost reductions), but significance is weakened by baseline issues.  
- Strengths: clear problem, stronger theoretical grounding than prior work, cross-domain empirical gains, and open-source code (LENSE).  
- Weaknesses: missing algorithmic details, weak baselines, no error analysis, poor justification for hyperparameters, and limited task-specific analysis.  
- Recommend acceptance only with major revisions to fix technical gaps, strengthen baseline comparisons, and provide deeper empirical analysis.
