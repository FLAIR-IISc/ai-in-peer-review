- Introduces "on-the-job learning": deploy high-accuracy AI from zero training examples by querying a crowd during prediction.  
- Models the problem as a stochastic game and uses Bayesian decision theory to trade off latency, cost, and accuracy.  
- Core innovation: strategic, partial-input crowd queries that decrease as the model improves, extending active/online learning to real-time model updates.  
- Uses Monte Carlo Tree Search to approximate the optimal policy; formulation is technically sound.  
- Experimental evaluation on NER, sentiment, and image classification is thorough and well-structured.  
- Strong empirical results: NER achieves 88.4% F1 with >10x cost reduction vs. 5-vote human labeling, +8% over single-human and +28% over online learning.  
- Cost reduction over time (Figure 4) provides compelling evidence of practical value.  
- Crowd response model (70% accuracy per query) is reasonable and matches experimental outcomes.  
- Related work placement in Section 5 is unusual and confusing for readers.  
- Presentation issues: stochastic game model explanation (Line 154) is abstract and needs a concrete example with responses and timing.  
- Editorial problems: inconsistent baseline labeling ("entropic" vs "threshold") and a missing figure reference (Figure ?? at Line 086).  
- Need clearer explanation of how crowd responses are incorporated (Section 3) and distinction between threshold baseline and LENSE's Bayesian approach.  
- Claim that the system can make unlimited consecutive queries needs more justification and analysis.  
- Would benefit from a deeper analysis of when parallel vs. sequential querying is optimal.  
- Overall significance: addresses a practical deployment gap, novel extension of active/online learning, and with presentation fixes would be a strong NIPS candidate.
