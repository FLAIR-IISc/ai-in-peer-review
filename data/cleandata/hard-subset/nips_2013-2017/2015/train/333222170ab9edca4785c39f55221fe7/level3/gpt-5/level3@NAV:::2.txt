Summary: This paper proposes “on-the-job learning,” a decision-theoretic framework that deploys high-accuracy classifiers from zero labeled examples by interleaving model predictions with real-time crowd queries. The approach formulates test-time querying as a stochastic game, optimizing a Bayesian utility that trades off accuracy, latency, and monetary cost, and uses MCTS with progressive widening to approximate the optimal policy. Experiments on NER, sentiment, and face classification compare LENSE against online learning, a threshold baseline, and n-vote human consensus, thereby situating the work alongside prior NIPS research on online/active learning (e.g., Settles), active classification (Gao & Koller), and crowd-POMDP control (TurKontrol), as well as real-time crowd systems (Legion:AR). The timing/latency optimization dimension is novel and compelling.

Quality: The formulation is sound and the utility-based policy is well motivated. The empirical results are informative, including direct comparisons to human consensus. However, some claims would benefit from clearer support and diagnostics: Table 2 shows LENSE outperforms 3-vote but underperforms 5-vote, which is consistent with a cost–accuracy frontier but should be explicitly framed as such. The response model (pR ≈ 0.7) needs calibration justification/sensitivity analysis. The “frozen worker pool” simulation is practical, yet a robustness study to varying worker accuracy/latency distributions would strengthen confidence.

Clarity: The exposition is sometimes opaque. The example at Line 154 is abstract and hard to follow; please provide a concrete query sequence with realistic worker responses and timestamps. Tables 2 and 3 inconsistently name the same baseline “entropic” vs “threshold,” which is confusing. Line 086 contains a missing figure reference (“Figure ??”). Placing Related Work as Section 5 disrupts flow; moving it earlier would help situate contributions.

Originality: Strong. Framing zero-shot deployment with real-time crowd interaction as a Bayesian decision problem with explicit latency costs is novel, especially for structured prediction.

Significance: High. The problem is important for rapid deployment scenarios, and the demonstrated cost savings vs human-only and accuracy gains vs machine-only are promising. The comparison to human performance is valuable.

Pros:
- Compelling problem; principled utility-based formulation; novel latency-aware querying.
- Clear empirical tradeoffs; human baselines included; open-source code.

Cons:
- Clarity/consistency issues (tables, figures, example).
- Limited sensitivity analyses; underperforming 5-vote at comparable accuracy not framed as Pareto tradeoff.

Recommendation: Weak accept. Address clarity issues, unify baseline naming, add a concrete example, and include sensitivity to worker noise/latency and ablations of time reasoning to solidify the contribution.
