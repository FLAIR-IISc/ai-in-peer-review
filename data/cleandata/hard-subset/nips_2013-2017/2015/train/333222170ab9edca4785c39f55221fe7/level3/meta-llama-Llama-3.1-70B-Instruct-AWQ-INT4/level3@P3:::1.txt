This paper proposes a novel framework for on-the-job learning, which aims to deploy a high-accuracy system starting with zero training examples. The authors model the on-the-job setting as a stochastic game and use ideas from game playing to approximate the optimal policy. The proposed approach combines real-time crowdsourcing with self-prediction, gradually transitioning from relying on the crowd to self-prediction as the model improves.

The main contribution of this paper is a more theoretically grounded formulation of an "on-the-job learner" from [22]. The authors provide a clear and well-organized presentation of their ideas, relating them to previous work in online learning, active learning, and crowdsourcing. The paper is technically sound, with a thorough analysis of the problem and a well-motivated approach.

The proposed algorithm, LENSE, is based on Monte Carlo Tree Search and progressive widening to reason about continuous time. The authors evaluate their approach on three tasks: named-entity recognition, sentiment classification, and image classification. The results show that LENSE outperforms machine and human-only comparisons on both quality and cost. On the NER task, LENSE achieves an F1 of 88.4% at more than an order of magnitude reduction in cost compared to full human annotation.

However, there are some limitations to the paper. The presentation is missing some important technical details, such as the definition of N(s) in Algorithm 1 and the justification for the threshold values in the baseline. Additionally, the empirical results are not entirely convincing, as the comparison to a threshold-based heuristic is underwhelming. Furthermore, the authors do not provide a thorough analysis of the "online" systems, and it is unclear whether they are the best ML systems for these tasks.

The paper raises several interesting questions and avenues for future research. For example, the authors mention that the model capacity is limited, and it would be interesting to explore the impact of using richer features on the performance of LENSE. Additionally, the authors do not discuss the potential risks and challenges of using crowdsourcing in real-time, such as ensuring the quality and reliability of the crowd workers.

In terms of originality, the paper proposes a novel combination of familiar techniques from game playing and crowdsourcing. The approach is well-motivated and well-executed, and the results are promising. However, the paper does not significantly advance the state of the art in a demonstrable way, as the ideas are not entirely new and the results are not groundbreaking.

Overall, I would recommend accepting this paper, as it provides a clear and well-motivated approach to on-the-job learning. However, I would suggest that the authors address the limitations mentioned above and provide a more thorough analysis of their results.
