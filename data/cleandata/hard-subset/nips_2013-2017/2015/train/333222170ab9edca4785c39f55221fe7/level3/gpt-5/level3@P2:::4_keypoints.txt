- Paper proposes an on-the-job learning framework that issues real-time crowd queries for uncertain inputs, formalized as a stochastic game and planned with MCTS + progressive widening to trade off accuracy, latency, and cost.  
- Experiments on NER, sentiment, and face classification show improved F1 and reduced cost vs. human-only and machine-only baselines; code/data released.  

Strengths:
- Clear motivation: rapidly deploy high-accuracy systems without upfront training data, with explicit cost–latency–accuracy tradeoffs.  
- Principled modeling: coherent Bayesian/game-theoretic formulation with transparent utility and explicit noise/latency environment models.  
- Algorithmic contribution: adaptation of MCTS with progressive widening to handle asynchronous continuous-time crowd responses.  
- Empirical promise: large cost reductions on NER and accuracy gains over baselines; cost decreases as model learns.  
- Reproducibility: code, data, and real-time crowdsourcing implementation provided.

Weaknesses / points needing clarification:
- Faces task unclear: query granularity, interface, and pR estimation are unspecified.  
- Utility questionable for unstructured single-label tasks because decision-time model doesn’t propagate information across examples.  
- Response/noise model pR fixed at 0.7 with uniform errors, which likely mis-specifies worker accuracy and biases planning.  
- Time/latency model pT(t|s) and progressive widening parameters are underspecified and unanalyzed.  
- Threshold baseline uses arbitrary parameters and up-front querying assumptions without justification or sensitivity analysis.  
- Missing uncertainty-sampling baseline adapted to on-the-job setting.  
- Distinction from active online learning is weak; need stronger conceptual/empirical comparison to active online baselines.  
- Evaluation uses a frozen pool of five pre-collected worker responses, which may distort policy-response dependence and limit redundancy benefits.  
- Missing modeling/algorithm ablations (MCTS vs greedy, parallel vs sequential querying, effects of forbidding interleaved issue-while-waiting).

Quality:
- Formulation is sound and algorithmic choices reasonable, but missing pR/pT estimation, stronger baselines, and sensitivity analyses limit empirical strength.

Clarity:
- Generally well written, but key experimental/modeling details (faces queries, latency/noise estimation, MCTS hyperparameters, feature sets) are underspecified.

Originality:
- Novel formulation combining stochastic games with explicit time and MCTS planning for crowd queries, especially for structured prediction; relation to POMDP/crowd control acknowledged but distinction from active online learning should be sharpened.

Significance:
- Practically relevant with strong NER impact; benefits on unstructured tasks modest and deployment depends on retainer-based real-time crowdsourcing.

Suggestions:
- Clarify face task query design and report pR/pT estimation per task.  
- Add uncertainty-sampling and stronger active-online baselines that query pre-prediction and update online.  
- Report sensitivity to wM and wT and full accuracy–latency–cost curves.  
- Analyze threshold baseline parameter sensitivity and justify choices.  
- Include ablations: greedy vs MCTS, parallel vs sequential querying, allow/disallow issue-while-waiting.  
- Consider learning worker reliabilities (e.g., Dawid–Skene/GLAD) and test robustness to mis-specified pR.

Pros for acceptance:
- Novel, principled formulation with practical algorithm; strong empirical gains on NER; clear motivation; open-source implementation.

Cons for acceptance:
- Missing key baselines and sensitivity analyses; unclear faces task details and limited gains on unstructured tasks; insufficient specification of noise/time models; frozen worker-pool evaluation may bias results.

Recommendation:
- Borderline reject pending stronger baselines, clearer task/method details, and sensitivity/ablation studies.

Reviewer confidence:
- High on methodology and evaluation concerns; moderate on application-specific details.
