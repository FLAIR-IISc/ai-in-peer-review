- Proposes a theoretically grounded on-the-job learning framework modeling crowd→model transition as a stochastic game under Bayesian decision theory.  
- Uses Monte Carlo Tree Search to approximate an optimal query policy that balances accuracy, latency, and cost.  
- Empirical results on NER, sentiment, and image classification show promising cost reductions (over an order of magnitude for NER).  
- LENSE implementation is well-documented with code and data on CodaLab, aiding reproducibility.  
- Main technical weakness: insufficient detail in presentation (notably Equation (2) should be conditioned on state s and the disappearance of y in p_R(r_i | y, q_i) is unexplained).  
- Algorithm 1 defines N(σ) but fails to explain what N(σ) represents.  
- Baseline threshold values (0.3 and 0.88) are arbitrary and lack justification or data-driven/theoretical derivation.  
- Empirical comparisons are underwhelming and potentially unfair: the threshold heuristic doesn’t account for time, and its thresholds are unexplained.  
- The claimed 8% F1 improvement over a single human label lacks error analysis or examples to show whether gains come from the ML model or human annotation quality.  
- Unclear whether the “online” systems used are the best possible ML baselines; reported gains may reflect better human annotation rather than model improvements.  
- Presentation clarity of the core idea is strong, but missing implementation and baseline details hinder full evaluation and reproducibility.  
- Originality: provides a novel principled formulation extending prior on-the-job learning work, though related to existing active classification literature.  
- Significance: moderate—approach is promising for low-label deployment but empirical evidence does not yet convincingly show superiority over simpler baselines.  
- Overall assessment: valuable theoretical contribution but limited by technical and empirical gaps that need addressing.  
- Recommendation: weak acceptance, contingent on addressing technical clarifications and strengthening empirical comparisons and error analysis.
