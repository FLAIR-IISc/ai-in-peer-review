- Introduces an on-the-job learning framework that uses real-time crowdsourcing to resolve uncertainty and reduce reliance on pre-labeled data.  
- Formulates the problem as a stochastic game balancing latency, cost, and accuracy, approximated via Monte Carlo Tree Search.  
- Operates in a truly online setting (queries during prediction) distinguishing it from prior active learning and crowdsourcing work.  
- Shows strong empirical improvements on NER, sentiment, and image classification with substantial cost reductions and notable F1 gains.  
- Novelty: the stochastic-game + MCTS formulation is a meaningful and original contribution.  
- Practicality: addresses deployment with limited initial resources and provides an open-source implementation (LENSE).  
- Clear motivation and problem definition in the introduction.  
- Continuous-time formulation is unclear; interaction of query send times and response times complicates exposition and optimization.  
- Continuous-time extension provides limited experimental benefit over a simpler threshold baseline, questioning its added complexity.  
- Experimental errors/omissions (incorrect “beating human” claim, missing 5-vote results for some tasks, inconsistent result formatting) weaken the evaluation.  
- Notational issues (missing s in Eq.2, confusing use of q in Eq.5) and a suspected typo in the threshold-baseline inequality need correction.  
- Arguments for acceptance: novel, technically sound approach with strong empirical results and reproducible code addressing an important problem.  
- Arguments against acceptance: clarity problems, experimental inconsistencies, and limited payoff from the complex continuous-time extension.  
- Overall recommendation: accept if authors fix clarity/notation, correct experimental issues, and consider presenting the core method without the continuous-time extension up front.
