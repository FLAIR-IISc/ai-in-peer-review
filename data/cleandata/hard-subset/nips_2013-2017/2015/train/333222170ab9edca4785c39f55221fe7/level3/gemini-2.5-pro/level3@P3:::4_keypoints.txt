- Introduces "on-the-job learning": a system with no training data that queries crowd workers per-instance, improves online, and balances accuracy, cost, and latency via a Bayesian stochastic-game formulation.

- Uses Monte Carlo Tree Search with progressive widening to approximate the intractable optimal policy.

- Positioned between ML-only and crowdsourced systems, focusing on maintaining high per-instance accuracy from the start.

- Achieves strong empirical results on NER, with high F1 and >10x cost reduction versus fully human labeling.

- Evaluated on sentiment and face classification as well; shows accuracy gains over a standard online learning baseline.

- Novel and principled problem formulation using Bayesian decision theory to trade off accuracy, cost, and latency.

- Sophisticated technical approach: MCTS provides a robust method for the complex decision space.

- Clear writing and reproducibility: code and data are released.

- Experimental setup lacks clarity for non-structured tasks (e.g., what constitutes a "query" in face recognition).

- Baselines are weak/poorly motivated: threshold baseline uses unexplained parameters; the online-learning baseline is not a suitable competitor to crowd-querying methods.

- Missing stronger active-learning baselines (e.g., uncertainty sampling that queries full labels) to better demonstrate advantages of partial querying.

- Overstates distinction from active learning: online active learning variants could be adapted to this setting, narrowing the claimed conceptual gap.

- Applicability may be limited: partial querying is most useful for structured tasks (like NER) and less so for single-label tasks (sentiment/image classification).

- Utility is myopic—per-instance reward does not model exploration value of queries for improving future learning, which may slow long-term learning.
