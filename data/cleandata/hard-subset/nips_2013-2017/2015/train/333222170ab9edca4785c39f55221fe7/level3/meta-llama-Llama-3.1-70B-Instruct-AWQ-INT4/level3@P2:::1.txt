This paper presents a novel framework for on-the-job learning, which enables a system to produce high-quality results from the start without requiring a trained model. The authors model this setting as a stochastic game based on Bayesian decision theory, allowing them to balance latency, cost, and accuracy objectives in a principled way. The system uses real-time crowdsourcing to resolve uncertainty where needed and outputs its prediction when confident. As the model improves over time, the reliance on crowdsourcing queries decreases.

The authors' approach is closely related to previous work in online learning, active learning, and crowdsourcing. However, their framework differs from existing approaches in that it maintains high accuracy from the beginning, whereas regret bounds only achieve this asymptotically. The authors also draw inspiration from active classification, which asks what are the most informative features to measure at test time. However, existing active classification algorithms rely on having a fully labeled dataset, which is not the case in the on-the-job learning setting.

The technical core of the authors' approach is the use of Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) to approximate the optimal policy. MCTS is a popular algorithm for solving complex decision-making problems, and the authors adapt it to their stochastic game setting. The algorithm combines ideas from MCTS to systematically explore the state space and progressive widening to deal with the challenge of continuous variables (time).

The authors evaluate their approach on three tasks: named-entity recognition, sentiment classification, and image classification. They compare their system, LENSE, to several baselines, including a threshold-based heuristic and online learning. The results show that LENSE outperforms the baselines in terms of both quality and cost. On the named-entity recognition task, LENSE achieves an F1 score of 88.4% at a cost that is more than an order of magnitude lower than the cost of achieving comparable quality using the 5-vote approach.

The authors' presentation is generally good, but there are some technical details that are missing or unclear. For example, the definition of N(s) in Algorithm 1 is not provided, and the justification for the 0.3 and 0.98 threshold values in the baseline is not explained. Additionally, the authors could provide more analysis of the "online" systems they compare to, including error analysis and example cases.

The authors' approach is reasonable and merits further investigation. The theoretical framing of the crowd→model transition is useful, and the empirical results are promising. However, there are some empirical and explanatory gaps that limit the conclusions that can be drawn from this work. For example, it is unclear whether the "online" systems are the best ML systems for these tasks, and whether the gains achieved by LENSE are due to human annotation rather than the on-the-job learning framework itself.

In terms of quality, the paper is technically sound, and the authors provide a clear and well-organized presentation of their approach. The use of MCTS to approximate the optimal policy is a key contribution, and the evaluation on three tasks provides a good sense of the approach's strengths and weaknesses. However, the paper could benefit from more analysis and explanation of the results, including error analysis and example cases.
