**Summary of Main Ideas**

The paper proposes a novel framework called on-the-job learning, which combines crowdsourced labels with an online-trained model to achieve high accuracy in structured prediction tasks. The framework is formulated as a stochastic game based on Bayesian decision theory, where the system and crowd are players issuing queries and receiving responses. The authors develop an approximation algorithm using Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) and progressive widening to optimize the expected utility of the system. The approach is evaluated on three tasks: named-entity recognition, sentiment classification, and image classification, and shows significant cost reductions and accuracy improvements over baseline methods.

**Strengths and Weaknesses**

Strengths:

* The paper proposes a novel and original framework that combines crowdsourced labels with an online-trained model to achieve high accuracy in structured prediction tasks.
* The framework is well-formulated and theoretically sound, using Bayesian decision theory and stochastic games to model the problem.
* The authors develop an efficient approximation algorithm using MCTS and progressive widening to optimize the expected utility of the system.
* The approach is evaluated on three tasks and shows significant cost reductions and accuracy improvements over baseline methods.

Weaknesses:

* The paper has major clarity problems, especially in the mid-Section-3 introduction of continuous time, which complicates exposition and algorithmic details.
* The continuous-time extension yields limited experimental benefit, and its added complexity may not be justified given the threshold baseline's strong performance.
* There are several reporting errors/omissions, including the claim of "beats human performance" being incorrect for 5-vote NER, and missing results for Face-ID and Sentiment in several tables/figures.
* There are presentation/formatting issues, including the lack of bolding for best results and certain latency values, and the threshold baseline being missing from the sentiment experiments in Table 3 and Figure 4.

**Originality and Significance**

The paper is original and significant, as it proposes a novel framework that combines crowdsourced labels with an online-trained model to achieve high accuracy in structured prediction tasks. The approach is likely to spur discussion and adoption in the field, and the authors' use of Bayesian decision theory and stochastic games to model the problem is a significant contribution.

**Clarity and Presentation**

The paper has major clarity problems, especially in the mid-Section-3 introduction of continuous time, which complicates exposition and algorithmic details. The authors could improve clarity by presenting the core method without continuous time first and treating continuous time as an extension. Additionally, the authors should fix notation issues, such as equation (2) not including s in the distribution p(y,r,t|x,q,s), and improve notation/notation clash, such as equation (5) using q for a distribution function which is confusing.

**Qualitative Evaluation**

The paper is well-written and well-organized, but has several clarity problems and reporting errors/omissions. The authors could improve the paper by addressing these issues and providing more detail on the experimental setup and results.
