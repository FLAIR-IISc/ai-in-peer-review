This paper introduces a novel framework for on-the-job learning, aiming to build high-accuracy systems without initial labeled data by strategically querying crowdsourced labels during inference. The core idea is to model this process as a stochastic game using Bayesian decision theory, balancing latency, cost, and accuracy. The authors approximate the optimal policy using Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) and demonstrate improvements over existing approaches on NER, sentiment classification, and image classification tasks.

The problem addressed is compelling: deploying accurate AI systems with limited initial resources is a common challenge. The approach of actively seeking information during prediction, coupled with a principled Bayesian framework for trading off cost and accuracy, is a valuable contribution. This work builds upon previous research in online learning (Cesa-Bianchi & Lugosi, 2006), active learning (Settles, 2010), and crowdsourcing (Bernstein et al., 2010; Dai et al., 2014), but distinguishes itself by tackling the problem in a real-time, on-the-job setting where the model *improves during* test time, unlike static policy active learning or purely offline approaches. The connection to game theory and the use of MCTS for policy approximation are also novel and potentially impactful.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The combination of Bayesian decision theory, stochastic game modeling, and MCTS for on-the-job learning is a unique and promising approach.
* **Practical Impact:** The demonstrated cost reductions (over an order of magnitude on NER) and accuracy improvements compared to baselines suggest real-world applicability.
* **Thorough Evaluation:** The experiments on three diverse tasks (NER, sentiment, image classification) provide a good initial assessment of the method’s generalizability.
* **Open Source Release:** The availability of the LENSE implementation promotes reproducibility and further research.
* **Clear Motivation:** The paper clearly articulates the challenges of deploying AI systems without pre-labeled data and positions on-the-job learning as a viable solution.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Clarity & Opacity:** While the overall concept is sound, certain explanations are opaque.  For instance, the example provided at Line 154 lacks concrete details (e.g., specific queries, realistic crowd responses, timings) making it difficult to grasp the dynamic interaction between the system and the crowd. A more illustrative example would significantly improve understanding.
* **Inconsistencies & Minor Errors:** There are minor inconsistencies (e.g., inconsistent labeling of the threshold baseline in Tables 2 and 3) and errors (e.g., missing figure reference at Line 086) that detract from the paper's polish.
* **Related Work Placement:** Placing related work in Section 5 feels disjointed and weakens its impact. It would be more appropriate to integrate related work throughout the introduction and problem formulation sections, providing a more cohesive narrative. The section currently reads more as a list of citations than a critical analysis of the field.
* **Baselines & Discussion:** The comparison to a 5-vote human baseline (Table 2) reveals LENSE underperforms. While the authors acknowledge this, a more in-depth discussion of the tradeoffs—why LENSE might choose to query fewer times even at the cost of some accuracy—is warranted. Examining why LENSE doesn't achieve the same level of consensus as simply aggregating multiple human labels is crucial.
* **Crowdsourcing Details:** The description of the crowdsourcing setup is relatively brief.  More detail about worker selection, quality control mechanisms (beyond the initial tutorial), and potential biases in the crowd responses would be valuable.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper tackles a challenging and important problem with a novel and well-motivated approach.
* The experimental results demonstrate significant improvements over existing methods in terms of cost and accuracy.
* The open-source release fosters reproducibility and further research.
* The use of Bayesian decision theory and MCTS provides a principled and flexible framework for on-the-job learning.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

* The explanations are sometimes opaque, hindering understanding of the system's dynamics.
* The inconsistencies and minor errors detract from the paper's polish.
* The lack of a detailed discussion of the tradeoffs with stronger human baselines (e.g., 5-vote) raises questions about the method's limitations.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a solid paper with a compelling idea and promising results. While some aspects of the presentation could be improved, the novelty and potential impact of the work justify acceptance. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the clarity and consistency issues highlighted above, and to provide a more thorough discussion of the limitations and tradeoffs of the proposed approach. Specifically, adding a concrete illustrative example and clarifying the comparison with the 5-vote baseline would significantly strengthen the paper.



**Rating:** Accept (with minor revisions)
