This paper proposes “on-the-job learning,” a decision-theoretic framework that, at test time, interleaves model inference with real-time crowdsourcing to balance accuracy, latency, and monetary cost. The authors cast the problem as a stochastic game, define a Bayesian utility that trades off expected accuracy against time and query cost, and approximate the optimal policy with MCTS and progressive widening. Experiments on NER, sentiment, and faces suggest substantial cost savings vs. full crowd labeling and gains over a pure online learner. The work relates to active learning (Settles 2009), online active learning, test-time feature acquisition/active classification, and crowd-control POMDPs like TurKontrol, as well as prior on-the-job human-in-the-loop systems (e.g., Legion:AR).

Strengths
- Conceptually novel framing that unifies accuracy–latency–cost via Bayesian decision theory; principled search-based policy with MCTS.
- Strongest benefits clearly demonstrated on structured output (NER), where partial queries propagate through a CRF.
- Careful engineering of a retainer setup for real-time crowds; open-source release aids reproducibility.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Clarity: The face recognition application is underspecified. What exactly is queried per instance—entire image label, attributes, regions, or redundant votes? Without this, it is hard to assess how the utility is computed and what alternatives exist.
- Appropriateness for non-structured tasks: For single-label settings, there is no within-instance propagation, so the framework largely reduces to “how many crowd votes before returning.” Please discuss whether the proposed utility (based on CRF marginals) and the MCTS policy offer advantage beyond adaptive majority voting.
- Baselines: The “threshold baseline” uses two ad hoc parameters (0.3 reduction factor; 0.98 threshold) with no origin or sensitivity analysis; results could hinge on these choices. Add (i) an uncertainty-sampling baseline (query until posterior confidence exceeds a target), and (ii) an online active learning adaptation that treats the incoming sequence as the active pool and updates the model on received responses.
- Distinction from active/online learning: The paper argues differences in timing, but an adapted active learner with immediate model updates and budgeted pre-decision queries may approximate the proposed setting. Please position more sharply and compare empirically.
- Crowd/error model: Assuming a fixed 70% accuracy and sampling from a frozen pool may bias results; calibrate pR from pilot data and report sensitivity to worker accuracy/latency.

Evaluation
- Quality: Promising, but baseline coverage and sensitivity analyses are incomplete.
- Clarity: Generally well-written; several experimental details need clarification.
- Originality: High in formulation; related work is cited but differentiation from active online learning needs tightening.
- Significance: Likely impactful for structured prediction with on-demand supervision; applicability to unstructured tasks appears limited.

Pros for acceptance
- Novel, principled framework with meaningful gains on NER.
- Solid integration of planning under uncertainty with real-time crowds.

Cons for acceptance
- Missing key baselines and sensitivity studies.
- Unclear face experiment and limited benefits on non-structured tasks.
- Weak empirical separation from adapted active/online learning.
