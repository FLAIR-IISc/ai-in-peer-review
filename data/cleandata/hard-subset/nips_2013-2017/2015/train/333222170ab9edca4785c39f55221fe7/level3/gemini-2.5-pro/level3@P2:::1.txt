This paper presents "on-the-job learning," a framework for deploying a high-accuracy system from a cold start (i.e., with zero training examples). The core idea is to begin by relying heavily on real-time crowdsourcing to make predictions, and as the system's model is updated with these crowd-sourced labels, it gradually becomes more self-sufficient, reducing its reliance on the crowd. The authors formulate this problem as a stochastic game and use Bayesian decision theory to create a principled trade-off between accuracy, cost, and latency. Since computing the optimal policy is intractable, they propose an approximation based on Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) with progressive widening. This work provides a more theoretically grounded formulation of the "on-the-job" learning setting previously explored in systems like Legion:AR [6]. The proposed system, LENSE, is evaluated on NER, sentiment analysis, and image classification tasks, demonstrating significant cost reductions compared to a pure-crowd approach and accuracy improvements over a pure online learning approach.

### Strengths

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The problem of building a useful system from day one without a large pre-existing training set is highly significant and practical. The "on-the-job" learning paradigm is a compelling solution. The main contribution is the formalization of this setting as a stochastic game, which is an elegant and principled way to model the sequential decision-making process of when to query the crowd, when to wait for responses, and when to predict. This theoretical framing is a valuable contribution to the literature on active and online learning.

2.  **Quality of the Approach:** The use of Bayesian decision theory to unify the objectives of accuracy, latency, and cost is sound. The application of MCTS to find an approximate policy in the resulting large state-space is a reasonable and well-motivated choice, drawing on successful techniques from game-playing AI. The overall approach of starting with human intelligence as a crutch and transitioning to a self-sufficient model is a novel and powerful concept.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Clarity and Missing Details:** While the paper is generally well-written, several important technical details are either missing or insufficiently explained, which hinders a full understanding and reproducibility.
    *   In the environment model (Equation 3), the crowd response model `pR(ri | yqi)` assumes that a worker's response for position `i` depends only on the true label `yqi` at that position. This conditional independence assumption ignores the context of the full label sequence `y`, which might be a strong simplification. This should be explicitly stated and justified.
    *   In Algorithm 1, the variable `N(s)` is used in the UCT rule but is not explicitly defined. While one can infer from the text that `s` represents the state `σ` and `N(σ)` is the visit count for that state, the notation should be consistent and clear.
    *   The "Threshold baseline" is a key point of comparison, but the constants used in its heuristic rule—specifically the uncertainty reduction factor of 0.3 and the confidence threshold of 0.98—are presented without justification. This makes the baseline seem arbitrary and potentially under-tuned, which could inflate the perceived performance of LENSE.

2.  **Quality of Experimental Evaluation:** The empirical results are promising but could be made more convincing.
    *   The comparison to the threshold-based heuristic is somewhat underwhelming. Given that this heuristic is non-adaptive and uses arbitrary constants, a stronger baseline that more intelligently adapts its querying strategy (even if simpler than MCTS) would provide a more compelling comparison.
    *   The "Online learning" baseline is trained on gold standard labels, which is a strong form of supervision. However, it is not clear if the underlying CRF and other models represent the state-of-the-art for these tasks. The significant gains of LENSE over this baseline may simply highlight the benefit of having a human-in-the-loop at test time, rather than the superiority of the MCTS-based decision-making framework itself. The paper would be strengthened by a more detailed analysis that disentangles these two factors.
    *   The evaluation would benefit from more qualitative analysis. Providing a few concrete examples of the query sequences generated by LENSE versus the baselines would offer valuable insight into the behavior of the learned policy. An error analysis showing the types of mistakes that LENSE avoids would also help to better understand the practical benefits of the approach.

### Summary of Arguments

*   **Pro-acceptance:** The paper addresses an important and practical problem with a novel and theoretically sound formulation. The idea of framing on-the-job learning as a stochastic game solved with MCTS is a solid contribution. The approach is reasonable, and the overall vision merits further investigation by the community.
*   **Con-acceptance:** The empirical evaluation is weakened by the choice of baselines and a lack of deeper analysis, making it difficult to fully assess the significance of the results. The paper also suffers from a lack of clarity on key technical details, which affects the rigor and reproducibility of the work.

Overall, this paper introduces a useful theoretical framing for an important problem. However, the explanatory and empirical gaps currently limit the strength of its conclusions. I lean towards acceptance but strongly encourage the authors to address the clarity and evaluation issues raised above in the final version.
