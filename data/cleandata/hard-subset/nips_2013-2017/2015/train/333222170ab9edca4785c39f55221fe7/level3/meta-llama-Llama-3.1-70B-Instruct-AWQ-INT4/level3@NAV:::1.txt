This paper introduces a novel framework for on-the-job learning, which enables a high-accuracy system to be deployed from scratch without requiring a large amount of labeled training data. The authors model the on-the-job learning setting as a stochastic game and use Bayesian decision theory to trade off latency, cost, and accuracy objectives in a principled way. They develop an approximation based on Monte Carlo Tree Search to compute the optimal policy, which is intractable to compute exactly.

The main contribution of this paper is a more theoretically grounded formulation of an "on-the-job learner" from previous work. The approach is reasonable and merits further investigation. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach on three tasks: named-entity recognition, sentiment classification, and image classification, achieving significant cost reductions over a pure crowd approach and significant accuracy improvements over a pure ML approach.

However, there are some limitations and areas for improvement. The presentation is generally good, but some important technical details are missing. The empirical results are underwhelming, particularly in comparison to a threshold-based heuristic. The authors should provide more justification for the choice of threshold values (0.3 and 0.88) in the baseline. Additionally, it is unclear whether the "online" systems are the best ML systems for these tasks, and if so, what gains may be attributed to human annotation. The authors should provide error analysis and example cases to support their claims.

Furthermore, the authors should clarify some technical details, such as the definition of N(s) in Algorithm 1 and the explanation for why y disappears from p_R(r_i | y, q_i) in Equation 2. They should also provide more analysis on the behavior of the model and the impact of different parameters on the results.

Overall, this paper presents a promising approach to on-the-job learning, but some technical details and empirical results need to be improved to make the paper more convincing.
