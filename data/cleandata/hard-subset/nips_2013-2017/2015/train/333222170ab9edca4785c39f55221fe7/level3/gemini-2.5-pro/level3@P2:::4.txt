This paper introduces "on-the-job learning," a framework for deploying a high-accuracy system starting with zero training examples. The core idea is to use real-time crowdsourcing to resolve uncertainty on-demand for incoming data instances. As the system sees more labeled data from the crowd, its underlying model improves, reducing its reliance on crowd queries over time. The authors formalize this problem as a stochastic game between the system and the crowd. Using Bayesian decision theory, they define a utility function that balances prediction accuracy, monetary cost, and latency. Since finding the optimal policy for this game is intractable, they propose an approximation based on Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) with progressive widening. The approach is evaluated on named-entity recognition (NER), sentiment classification, and image classification, showing significant cost savings compared to a pure crowdsourcing approach and accuracy improvements over a standard online learning baseline.

The problem this paper tackles is highly significant and practical, and the proposed framework is novel and principled. However, the evaluation and positioning of the work have some weaknesses that should be addressed.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound. Modeling the sequential decision-making process of querying a crowd as a stochastic game is an elegant formulation. The use of MCTS is a well-motivated and appropriate choice for approximating the intractable policy. The experiments on three distinct tasks demonstrate the potential of the approach, and the commitment to reproducibility by providing code and data is commendable.

However, the experimental evaluation could be strengthened.
1.  The choice of baselines is a concern. The "threshold baseline" relies on two unexplained constants (0.3 and 0.98), and without a sensitivity analysis or justification for these values, it serves as a weak point of comparison. A more standard and stronger baseline would be an uncertainty sampling strategy, common in active learning, where the system queries for a label if the model's confidence is below a certain threshold. Including such a baseline would provide a much clearer picture of the benefits gained from the sophisticated MCTS-based decision-making.
2.  The "online learning" baseline, which is given access to the gold labels of past examples, seems more like an oracle-based upper bound for a passive learner rather than a realistic alternative. This makes the reported 28% F1 improvement on NER impressive but potentially misleading, as a real-world online system would also have to acquire labels somehow.

**Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and the core concepts are motivated clearly. The introduction does a good job of situating the work within the existing literature.

There are, however, some points that need clarification:
1.  It is unclear what a "query" constitutes for the non-structured prediction tasks (sentiment analysis and face classification). In these tasks, there is typically a single label per instance. Does the system query for this single label? If so, the framework's ability to "query on parts of the input it is uncertain about" does not apply. This is a critical detail, as the primary advantage of the proposed utility function seems to stem from propagating information from partial labels within a structured instance (as seen in the NER example). If queries are only at the instance level for classification tasks, the benefit of the framework is significantly reduced to simply deciding *whether* to query, not *what* to query.
2.  The discussion of how the model avoids querying on examples from the dominant "none" class in NER is insightful but also highlights a potential limitation. The reduced gains on the other two datasets suggest that the method's effectiveness might be highly dependent on the task structure and label distribution.

**Originality:** The formulation of the "on-the-job learning" setting as a stochastic game solved with MCTS is a novel contribution. While it draws on ideas from active learning, online learning, and crowdsourcing, the synthesis is new. In particular, framing the problem with a utility that explicitly models latency alongside cost and accuracy is a valuable and original perspective.

The main weakness in originality lies in the paper's positioning against online active learning. The authors claim a key distinction is that active learning observes labels only after classification. However, an online active learning system could easily be adapted to query for labels before committing to a prediction for a given instance, especially in a low-latency crowd setting. The more nuanced, and perhaps more accurate, claim is that this work provides a more principled, decision-theoretic framework for making these choices compared to common heuristics used in active learning.

**Significance:** The work addresses a problem of high practical importance: reducing the cold-start problem for deploying machine learning systems. The results on the NER task are particularly impressive, demonstrating an order-of-magnitude reduction in cost. This suggests the approach could have a significant impact on applications involving structured prediction where annotation is expensive.

The broader significance is somewhat constrained by the method's apparent reliance on structured prediction tasks for its full effect. As mentioned under "Clarity," the benefits for standard classification tasks are less clear from the paper's description. If the framework provides only marginal gains over simpler active learning strategies for these common tasks, its overall impact might be more limited than suggested.

### Arguments for Acceptance:
*   Addresses the important and practical problem of deploying ML systems with no initial training data.
*   The formulation as a stochastic game optimized with MCTS is a novel and principled technical contribution.
*   The empirical results on the NER task are very strong, showing significant real-world benefits in cost and accuracy.
*   The work is reproducible, with code and data publicly available.

### Arguments for Rejection:
*   The experimental comparison is weakened by the lack of a standard uncertainty-sampling baseline and the inclusion of a poorly motivated "threshold" baseline.
*   The distinction from and advantages over a well-designed online active learning system are not convincingly established.
*   The applicability and benefits for non-structured classification tasks are unclear, potentially limiting the work's scope.
*   Missing details about the experimental setup for classification tasks hinder a full assessment of the results.

In summary, this is a strong paper with a novel idea and impressive results on a key task. While I have some reservations about the experimental comparisons and the generality of the approach, the core technical contribution and the significance of the problem are high. I lean towards acceptance but strongly recommend the authors address the points above in the final version.
