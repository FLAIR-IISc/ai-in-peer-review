- Proposes “on-the-job learning”: a decision-theoretic framework that interleaves model inference with real-time crowdsourcing to trade off accuracy, latency, and cost via a stochastic game and Bayesian utility.  
- Uses MCTS with progressive widening to approximate the optimal policy.  
- Experiments (NER, sentiment, faces) show substantial cost savings vs. full crowd labeling and improvements over a pure online learner, with strongest gains on structured tasks (NER).  

Strengths:
- Novel, principled framing unifying accuracy–latency–cost and a principled search-based policy.  
- Clear, strong benefits demonstrated for structured output where partial queries propagate through a CRF.  
- Careful engineering of a real-time retainer crowd setup and open-source release for reproducibility.  

Weaknesses / Suggestions:
- Face recognition experiment underspecified: unclear what is queried (whole image, attributes, regions, or votes), making utility computation unclear.  
- For single-label tasks, framework may reduce to deciding vote counts—need discussion of advantages over adaptive majority voting.  
- Baselines are weak/ad hoc (threshold baseline with unexplained parameters); add uncertainty-sampling and an online active-learning adaptation as baselines and report sensitivity.  
- Need clearer distinction and empirical comparison versus adapted active/online learning that updates models immediately and uses budgeted pre-decision queries.  
- Crowd/error model assumptions (fixed 70% accuracy, frozen pool) may bias results; calibrate pR from pilot data and report sensitivity to worker accuracy and latency.  

Evaluation summary:
- Quality: promising but incomplete baseline coverage and sensitivity analyses.  
- Clarity: generally well-written but several experimental details need clarification.  
- Originality: high formulation novelty, but differentiation from active online learning needs tightening.  
- Significance: impactful for structured prediction with on-demand supervision; limited applicability to unstructured tasks.  

Pros for acceptance:
- Novel, principled framework with meaningful gains on NER.  
- Solid integration of planning under uncertainty with real-time crowds.  

Cons for acceptance:
- Missing key baselines and sensitivity studies.  
- Unclear face experiment and limited benefits on non-structured tasks.  
- Weak empirical separation from adapted active/online learning.
