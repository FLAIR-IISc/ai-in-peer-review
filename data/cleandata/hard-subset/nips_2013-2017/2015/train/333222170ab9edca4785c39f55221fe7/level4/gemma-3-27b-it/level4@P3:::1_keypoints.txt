- Core idea: Formalizes on-the-job learning as a stochastic game solved with Bayesian decision theory and MCTS, transitioning from crowd reliance to a self-sufficient model.
- Motivation: Practical approach for scenarios with scarce or expensive labeled data, emphasizing continuous-time decision making and simultaneous learning/prediction.
- Technical soundness: Strong technical foundation; stochastic-game formulation is well-motivated and MCTS is a reasonable approximation for the intractable optimal policy.
- Theoretical analysis: Provides a principled way to balance latency, cost, and accuracy, though theory is not extensive.
- Experimental evaluation: Tested on NER, sentiment, and image classification, showing general applicability and reported cost/performance improvements.
- Implementation and reproducibility: Open-source implementation (LENSE) indicates careful development, but some algorithmic details are missing for full reproducibility.
- Clarity: Generally well-written and organized; introduction and positioning are good, but Equation (1) and Algorithm 1 need clearer, more intuitive explanations.
- Algorithmic detail gap: Algorithm 1 lacks sufficient detail (e.g., define N(s)), and progressive widening specifics are missing.
- Originality: Novel integration of Bayesian decision theory, MCTS, and on-the-job learning; distinguishes itself from active/online learning by simultaneous prediction and learning.
- Related work comparison: Adequately referenced but needs a deeper comparison to closely related work (e.g., Legion:AR).
- Significance: High potential impact for rapidly changing domains and low-data settings; open-source release increases practical value.
- Strengths summary: Principled Bayesian framework, effective use of MCTS, demonstrated cost/performance gains across tasks, clear motivation, and open-source code.
- Weaknesses summary: Equation (1) unclear, Algorithm 1 under-specified, online baselines comparison and error analysis insufficient, weak threshold baseline justification, and limited discussion of limitations/future work.
- Acceptance arguments: Addresses an important problem with a sound, well-motivated approach and promising empirical results plus open-source code.
- Rejection arguments: Presentation and empirical evaluation need more detail and stronger comparisons to emphasize novelty.
- Requested clarifications: Fix Equation (2)/condition on state and role of y, define N(s) in Algorithm 1, justify threshold values (0.3, 0.88), clarify whether online baselines are state-of-the-art and provide error analysis, and discuss MCTS computational cost vs. performance trade-offs.
