Summary
The paper proposes an on-the-job learning framework that produces high-quality predictions from the outset by interleaving model inference with real-time crowdsourcing. Upon receiving an input, the system selectively queries the crowd for uncertain parts (e.g., token labels in NER), collects noisy, asynchronous responses, and returns a prediction when sufficiently confident. Over time, the model improves and relies less on the crowd, reducing cost and latency.

The approach is formalized as a stochastic game between the system and the crowd built on a CRF prediction model. A Bayesian decision-theoretic utility trades off expected accuracy against monetary cost and latency. Because optimal control is intractable (especially with continuous time), the authors approximate the policy using Monte Carlo Tree Search with UCT and progressive widening to handle continuous response times.

Experiments on named entity recognition, sentiment classification, and face classification compare: (i) majority vote from n crowd workers, (ii) online learning with oracle labels, (iii) a “threshold” baseline that issues a fixed number of queries per variable until uncertainty falls below a threshold (no explicit reasoning about time), and (iv) the full system (LENSE). LENSE achieves strong accuracy at substantially reduced cost versus crowdsourcing alone and outperforms online learning while maintaining high accuracy from the very first examples. It also shows a decreasing reliance on crowd queries as the model learns.

Relation to prior work
The work is well-situated among online learning, active learning, active classification, crowdsourcing, and structured prediction. Unlike online/active learning, which typically updates after committing to predictions, this framework actively solicits information before predicting. Compared to active classification, which learns static test-time policies from labeled data, this system updates itself at test time and treats labels as never directly observed truth. It is also related to real-time crowd-powered systems (e.g., Legion:AR) and decision-theoretic control of crowds (e.g., TurKontrol/POMDPs), but contributes a unified treatment for structured outputs with explicit latency-cost-accuracy tradeoffs and continuous-time decision-making.

Evaluation by review criteria
- Quality: The formulation is sound, and the decision-theoretic objective is appropriate. Approximations via MCTS and progressive widening are sensible given intractability. The empirical protocol (including a retainer model for low-latency workers and a frozen pool of responses to control variability) is thoughtful. However, clarity gaps and a few missing/uneven baselines weaken the presentation of evidence supporting the continuous-time benefits. Some notation inconsistencies and omitted implementation details make it harder to reproduce the exact decision criteria at return time.
- Clarity: The main conceptual arc is clear, but the introduction of continuous time in the game greatly complicates exposition and the algorithmic description. In the current experiments, continuous time appears to yield limited improvements over the simpler threshold baseline, making the added complexity feel under-motivated. Several reporting inconsistencies (missing baselines/rows and bolding) and minor notation issues hinder readability.
- Originality: High. The paper integrates Bayesian decision theory, real-time crowdsourcing, and structured prediction in a coherent stochastic game with an approximate solution method, going beyond heuristic hybrids.
- Significance: High. The framework addresses a practical and important need: building accurate systems with zero initial training data while managing time and cost. The empirical gains and open-source implementation suggest strong potential for adoption and follow-up work.

Pros (arguments for acceptance)
- Principled formulation that jointly optimizes accuracy, cost, and latency.
- Effective approximation via MCTS with progressive widening for continuous time.
- Strong empirical results across three tasks; clear cost reductions versus crowdsourcing alone and accuracy gains versus machine-only baselines.
- Demonstrates decreasing query rates over time; practical system with open-source code and real-time crowd infrastructure.
- Advances the state of practice for hybrid human–machine learning with structured outputs.

Cons (arguments against acceptance)
- The continuous-time component substantially complicates the model and algorithm, yet yields modest empirical benefits compared to the simpler threshold policy in reported settings.
- Inconsistent reporting: missing 5-vote results for some tasks and missing threshold baseline for sentiment; best results not consistently highlighted.
- Some claims (e.g., “beats human performance”) are overstated given that 5-vote crowdsourcing is not always surpassed.
- Notational and editorial issues (ambiguous symbols, equation references) and incomplete details on decision thresholds/deadlines.

Suggestions for improvement
1) Positioning of continuous time: Consider presenting the threshold/no-time variant as the core method and framing continuous time as an extension that brings adaptivity and parallelization when latency constraints matter. Provide targeted experiments isolating scenarios where continuous time is beneficial (e.g., stricter deadlines, heterogeneous response-time distributions, more complex structures).

2) Reporting completeness and consistency:
- Include 5-vote results for sentiment and face classification wherever relevant (tables and figures), and clearly bold the best accuracy and best latency values in tables.
- Include the threshold baseline for sentiment in Table 3 and corresponding figures.
- Calibrate claims to reflect that the system does not surpass 5-vote results in all settings.

3) Decision criteria and hyperparameters:
- Clarify whether predictions are returned upon reaching a confidence/entropy threshold, upon hitting a time/cost budget, or both; specify any t_deadline used in experiments and how it interacts with the utility.
- Provide sensitivity analyses for wM and wT and for the assumed worker accuracy in pR (e.g., 0.7), and discuss robustness to worker heterogeneity and drift.

4) Algorithmic details:
- Give more detail on MCTS rollout policies, depth limits, computational budget per example, and wall-clock overhead. Report the planning overhead relative to savings in queries/latency.
- Add ablations: without progressive widening, without parallel querying, or with fixed query batches.

5) Notation and minor corrections:
- Ensure the joint distribution conditions on s in p(y, r, t | x, q, s).
- Replace ambiguous uses of q in F(q) with a different symbol to avoid confusion with queries.
- In the threshold baseline description, correct the inequality so that uncertainty decreases with increasing m (the expression should reflect that uncertainty is multiplied by 0.3^m).
- Clean up equation references and typos.

6) Broader discussion:
- Discuss limits when model capacity is low (e.g., unigram features) and the system’s behavior as it converges to a stable query rate.
- Comment on generalization to other structured tasks (e.g., coreference, parsing) and the impact of stronger sequence models.

Recommendation
Accept. The paper is original, timely, and practically significant. With improved clarity, more balanced positioning of the continuous-time contribution, and complete reporting of baselines, it can make a strong NIPS contribution and serve as a foundation for future hybrid human–machine systems.
