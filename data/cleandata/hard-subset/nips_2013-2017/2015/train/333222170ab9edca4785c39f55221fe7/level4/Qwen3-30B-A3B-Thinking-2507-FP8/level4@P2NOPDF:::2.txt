

The paper presents a compelling problem: building high-accuracy classifiers from zero labeled examples while managing noisy crowd responses. A key strength is its comparison of classifier accuracy against human performance, particularly revealing in Table 2 that the LENSE system outperforms the 3-vote consensus baseline yet underperforms the 5-vote baseline. This contrast effectively highlights the relative efficiency of denoising crowd responses through additional consensus versus the LENSE approach. The novelty of the paper’s timing and latency optimization factors for crowd-sourced data is well-justified and represents a meaningful contribution beyond existing denoising techniques.

However, several aspects require clarification to enhance readability and rigor. The example in the paragraph starting at Line 154 is overly abstract and difficult to follow. To improve accessibility, the authors should replace this with a concrete, realistic case study—e.g., a specific query with sample crowd responses, associated timestamps, and how LENSE processes them. This would make the methodology tangible for readers.

In Tables 2 and 3, the inconsistent terminology for the threshold baseline is confusing. It is sometimes labeled "entropic" and other times "threshold," creating ambiguity. The authors should standardize this term (e.g., "threshold baseline" or "entropic threshold") and clarify the relationship between these labels in the methodology section to avoid misinterpretation.

The placement of the related work in Section 5 is problematic. Given its role in contextualizing the novelty of the latency-optimization angle, this section should appear earlier—ideally in Section 2 or 3—to frame the problem statement and motivate the proposed approach. As currently positioned, it disrupts the flow of the narrative.

The problem statement in Line 086 is strong: "This paper tackles the problem of creating high-accuracy classifiers beginning with zero labeled examples." The focus on latency as a critical dimension for crowd-sourced data, alongside accuracy, is a significant and underexplored aspect. The claim that "timing and latency optimization factors are novel and interesting" is well-supported by the literature gap identified.

Overall, the paper addresses a relevant challenge with a clear contribution, but the explanations lack sufficient clarity. The core findings in Table 2 are valuable but obscured by vague descriptions and inconsistent terminology. The abstract example at Line 154 and the Section 5 placement further detract from the paper’s impact. With targeted revisions—specific examples, standardized terminology, and restructured related work—the paper would significantly strengthen its contribution and readability. The core idea is promising, but current presentation hinders its evaluation.
