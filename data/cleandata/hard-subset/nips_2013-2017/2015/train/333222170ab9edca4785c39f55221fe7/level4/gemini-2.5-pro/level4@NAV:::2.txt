A strength of this paper is its comparison of the proposed classifier's accuracy against human performance. Table 2 presents a noteworthy result: the LENSE system surpasses the 3-vote baseline in performance, yet does not reach the level of the 5-vote baseline. This highlights a crucial trade-off between the LENSE system's approach to de-noising crowd responses and the alternative of simply increasing consensus with more votes. The illustrative example beginning on Line 154 is difficult to comprehend as it is presented too abstractly. To enhance readability, the authors could substitute this with a concrete example, detailing a specific query with realistic crowd response data and corresponding timings. Furthermore, the inconsistent terminology for the baseline in Tables 2 and 3, referred to as both "entropic" and "threshold," creates confusion. The placement of the related work section late in the paper (Section 5) is unconventional, and its rationale is unclear. In summary, the paper addresses a compelling problem, but its impact is hindered by explanations that are at times opaque.

The authors address the challenge of building high-accuracy classifiers without any initial labeled data. A key sub-problem is the management of noisy responses from crowd workers. While this is a well-studied area, the paper's focus on timing and latency optimization introduces a novel and interesting contribution.
