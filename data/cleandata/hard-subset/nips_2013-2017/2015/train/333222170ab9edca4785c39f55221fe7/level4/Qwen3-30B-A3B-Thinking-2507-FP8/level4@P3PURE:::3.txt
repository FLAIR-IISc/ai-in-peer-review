

This paper presents a hybrid approach for combining crowdsourced labels with an incrementally trained model to address prediction problems. The core idea leverages the crowd heavily during system initialization, learns from acquired labels, and progressively reduces crowd reliance as model confidence increases. This is framed as a stochastic game involving a CRF prediction model where the system and crowd act as players. The system issues queries $q$ for tokens $x$ (with true label $y$), eliciting responses $r$ with utility $U(q,r)$; the system optimizes actions to maximize expected utility. Crucially, queries are issued at times $s$ (with response times $t$), and utility is maximized with respect to a deadline $t_{\text{deadline}}$, determining query volume and rate.

Computing expected utility requires the simulation dynamics model $P(y,r,t \mid x,q,s)$ (Eq. 4). While optimal action selection is theoretically possible, continuous-time dynamics render exact optimization intractable, necessitating an approximation using Monte Carlo Tree Search and TD learning (Algorithm 1). Experiments evaluate the method on simplified named entity recognition (NER), sentiment recognition, and face identification tasks, comparing against: (1) majority vote of $n$ human judges ($n=1,3,5$), (2) online learning (using true labels), (3) a "threshold" baseline (the authors' model without continuous time, sending $m$ queries per step until uncertainty drops below a threshold), and (4) the authors' full model (LENSE). LENSE outperforms all baselines except the 5-vote crowdsourcing on precision/recall/F1, with the threshold baseline performing nearly as well (and achieving lower latency for NER; Table 2). Figure 3 demonstrates LENSE progressively reduces crowdsourced label requests as model confidence grows, while Figure 4 shows LENSE achieves high accuracy from the first example—unlike online learning—by leveraging crowd expertise during high model uncertainty.

This is an interesting paper that merits discussion, further work, and potential adoption. It presents the first complete, principled approach to hybrid learning from crowds, with impressive results against baselines. The work is highly original and significant, warranting acceptance. However, clarity issues risk diminishing its impact, many of which are correctable in camera-ready form.

The primary clarity concern stems from introducing continuous time mid-Section 3. While the motivation (operating within time/cost budgets) is reasonable, the experimental benefits are marginal. Table 2 shows the threshold baseline achieves significantly lower latency on NER (with comparable performance) and slightly better accuracy on face ID (still roughly equivalent). The authors attribute the face ID advantage to the absence of label interactions, yet NER—which exhibits strong label interactions—only marginally benefits from continuous time. For many practical tasks, the threshold baseline suffices as a significant improvement over active/online learning. The paper would better serve its audience if it developed the core method fully (without continuous time, enhancing understandability and implementability) and presented continuous time as a secondary extension.

Additional concerns: 
- The claim that the model "beats human performance" is inaccurate (5-vote NER does not exceed human-level performance). 
- The 5-vote results are missing for Face ID and Sentiment in Tables 2, 3 and Figure 4 (right halves), where they should be included. 
- In Table 2, 5-vote results and the threshold baseline's NER latency should be bolded as they represent top-performing entries; LENSE's Face ID latency should also be bolded. 
- The threshold baseline is absent from the sentiment experiment (Table 3, Figure 4), where it should be included.

Minor technical points: 
- Section 3.1 (l.183–187) mentions $t_{\text{deadline}}$ but does not specify its experimental value or criteria for reporting results (e.g., entropic threshold vs. deadline). 
- Equation (2) likely requires $s$ as an argument: $p(y,r,t \mid x,q,s)$. 
- Equation (5) uses $q$ in $F(q)$, which is confusing (it denotes a nominal distribution function, not a query). A different symbol (e.g., $\phi$) should be used. 
- The threshold baseline description (l.246–252) contains an inequality error: $(1 - p_{\theta}(y_i \mid x)) \cdot 0.3^m \leq (1 - 0.88)$ (not $\geq 0.88$), as increasing $m$ reduces uncertainty.

This work elegantly models crowdsourcing dynamics, including temporal aspects of query issuance and response. It demonstrates impressive results against strong baselines, with the core method (without continuous time) being sufficiently powerful for practical adoption. While the continuous time extension complicates the presentation without clear gains, the paper's core contributions are significant. With clarified exposition—particularly regarding the minimal benefits of continuous time—the paper will have greater impact and facilitate wider adoption. The method is original, novel, and compelling, and merits acceptance.
