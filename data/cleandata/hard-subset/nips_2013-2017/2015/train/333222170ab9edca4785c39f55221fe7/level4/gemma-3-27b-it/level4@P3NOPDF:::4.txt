## Review of NIPS 2015 Submission: [Paper Title – *Please Replace*]

This paper introduces a novel framework for “on-the-job learning,” a setting where a model can query an annotator for information *between* receiving an input and making a prediction. The core idea of strategically requesting labels to improve performance during inference is appealing, and the approach is demonstrated on both structured prediction and face recognition tasks. However, significant clarity is needed regarding the application to the non-structured face recognition problem, and several aspects of the experimental setup warrant further investigation.

The paper clearly articulates the algorithm’s mechanics within the context of structured prediction, where querying focuses on individual elements of the output sequence. However, the adaptation to the face recognition task – an inherently non-structured problem – remains opaque. The description suggests querying the *instance* itself, but it's unclear precisely what information is being requested from the annotator in this scenario. Without a clear explanation of the query mechanism in this setting, the proposed utility function feels ill-defined; the model doesn’t propagate information between examples as it does in the structured case, making the benefit of the utility function questionable. A detailed explanation, perhaps with a concrete example illustrating a typical query and response, is crucial for understanding this application.

The baseline comparison employs a threshold-based querying strategy with two parameters. The choice of these values appears somewhat arbitrary, and the paper lacks justification for their selection. A sensitivity analysis examining the performance impact of varying these parameters would substantially strengthen the evaluation. Understanding how robust the results are to changes in these thresholds is critical for assessing the practicality of the proposed approach.

Furthermore, the inclusion of an uncertainty sampling baseline, commonly used in active learning, would provide valuable context. This would allow for a more direct comparison with existing active learning strategies and demonstrate the benefits of the proposed “on-the-job” learning framework. Given the sequential nature of the problem, considering a baseline that selectively queries based on the model’s uncertainty after receiving each response would be particularly insightful. 

The authors rightly distinguish their setting from traditional active online learning. However, the latter *could* be adapted to this domain by treating the sequence of queries as an evolving active set. For example, the model could be updated incrementally with each received annotation and then used to generate a prediction at the end of the sequence. While this approach wouldn’t fully capture the temporal dynamics of the task, it would allow for leveraging the rich theoretical foundation of active learning for query selection.  The paper would benefit from a discussion of such adaptations and a clear articulation of the advantages of the proposed method over these alternatives.

In summary, this paper presents a potentially novel framework, but its applicability feels somewhat limited due to the lack of clarity surrounding the face recognition application and the insufficient comparison to related work.  The core idea is intriguing, but requires significantly more detailed explanation and a more comprehensive experimental evaluation to convincingly demonstrate its advantages. Addressing the questions raised regarding the query mechanism, baseline choices, and comparison to active learning will be crucial for a stronger contribution.
