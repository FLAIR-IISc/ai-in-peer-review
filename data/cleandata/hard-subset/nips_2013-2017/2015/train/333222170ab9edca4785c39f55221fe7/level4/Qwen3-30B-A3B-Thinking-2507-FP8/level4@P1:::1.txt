

This paper introduces a novel framework called "on-the-job learning" that enables deploying accurate AI systems from zero training examples by strategically incorporating real-time crowdsourcing. The authors formalize the problem as a stochastic game based on Bayesian decision theory, where the system balances latency, cost, and accuracy objectives by determining when to query the crowd and when to make predictions. They propose a Monte Carlo Tree Search-based approximation to handle the intractable optimal policy computation, implemented in a system called LENSE. The approach is evaluated on three tasks: named-entity recognition, sentiment classification, and image classification, demonstrating significant cost reductions (over an order of magnitude for NER) compared to full human annotation while achieving higher accuracy than both pure online learning and single-human-label baselines.

The paper makes several valuable contributions. It successfully formalizes the on-the-job learning concept previously implemented in [6] with rigorous Bayesian decision theory, providing a principled framework for balancing multiple objectives. The authors' theoretical formulation represents a meaningful extension beyond prior active learning and active classification work, which typically assume static policies or cannot maintain high accuracy from the beginning. The experimental evaluation is comprehensive across three distinct tasks and provides compelling evidence of the approach's effectiveness in reducing cost while maintaining accuracy.

However, the paper has several weaknesses that require addressing. First, the presentation lacks sufficient technical detail to fully evaluate the proposed model. The authors should clarify several key points: (1) In Equation (2), the left-hand side should explicitly condition on the query history s, as the accuracy depends on the observed crowd responses; (2) The threshold baseline's parameters (0.3 and 0.88) are not adequately justified—how were these values derived, and why do they work for the specific tasks? (3) The meaning of N(s) in Algorithm 1 is unclear—this appears to be a critical component of the Monte Carlo Tree Search implementation. (4) The paper does not sufficiently justify why the "online learning" baseline represents the best possible machine learning approach for these tasks. If the online learning system is indeed the state-of-the-art for these specific tasks, then the performance gains over it may primarily stem from the additional human annotation rather than the on-the-job learning framework itself.

The empirical results are somewhat mixed. While the NER task shows dramatic improvements (88.4% F1 with an order-of-magnitude cost reduction), the gains on sentiment classification and image classification are more modest (only ~2x cost reduction). The authors should provide error analysis to explain why the approach performs better on NER than on the other tasks—perhaps due to the presence of a dominant "none" class that makes learning more efficient. The comparison to the threshold baseline is valuable but would benefit from more detailed discussion of why the Bayesian approach outperforms this heuristic.

The paper also lacks sufficient discussion of the crowd response model. The authors state that crowd workers were "roughly 70% accurate" but do not provide the full response model pR(ri | yqi) used in the experiments. The current formulation (pR(r | yq) = 0.7 iff r = yq) seems overly simplistic and may not reflect real crowd behavior.

Overall, the paper presents a theoretically grounded approach to on-the-job learning that addresses an important practical problem in deploying AI systems. While the core idea is promising, the current presentation requires substantial clarification of key technical details before it can be fully evaluated. The authors should provide more rigorous justification for their model parameters, clarify the implementation details of their algorithm, and conduct more thorough analysis of the results across different tasks. With these improvements, the paper could make a significant contribution to the field of human-in-the-loop machine learning.

Strengths:
- Strong theoretical foundation using Bayesian decision theory
- Novel formulation of on-the-job learning as a stochastic game
- Practical implementation and evaluation across multiple domains
- Demonstrated significant cost reduction (order of magnitude on NER) while improving accuracy

Weaknesses:
- Insufficient technical detail in key components (Equation 2, Algorithm 1, threshold baseline parameters)
- Lack of justification for crowd response model parameters
- Unclear explanation for varying performance across tasks
- Limited discussion of why online learning baseline represents the best machine learning approach for these tasks
- Overstated claims about "more than an order of magnitude" improvement without sufficient context about baseline cost

The paper addresses a meaningful problem in AI deployment where labeled data is scarce, and the authors make a valuable contribution to the growing literature on human-in-the-loop machine learning. With the suggested clarifications and more thorough analysis, this work could significantly advance the field.
