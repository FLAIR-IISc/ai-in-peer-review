- Introduces a novel "on-the-job learning" framework using Bayesian decision theory modeled as a stochastic game to enable deployment with zero labeled examples.
- Implements LENSE (Learning from Expensive Noisy Slow Experts) and evaluates on named-entity recognition, sentiment classification, and image classification.
- Technical approach combines Bayesian decision theory with Monte Carlo Tree Search for policy approximation; experiments compare against four baselines and code is open-source.
- Strong empirical results: e.g., 88.4% F1 on NER with over an order-of-magnitude cost reduction versus full human annotation, outperforming 3-vote and 5-vote baselines.
- Originality: clearly distinguishes from online learning, active learning, and active classification and formalizes a practically important "on-the-job" setting.
- Practical significance: useful for real-world deployment without initial labeled data (e.g., disaster response), maintaining high accuracy from the first prediction.
- Weakness — Clarity: stochastic game formulation and utility description are abstract and hard to follow; needs concrete examples and consistent terminology.
- Weakness — Methodological detail: lacks sufficient detail on how progressive widening handles continuous time and the link between theory and implementation.
- Weakness — Organization: related work is misplaced in the experiments section and should be moved.
- Recommendation and fixes: strong accept with minor revisions—add a detailed timed query example, standardize "threshold"/"entropic" terminology, and relocate related work.
