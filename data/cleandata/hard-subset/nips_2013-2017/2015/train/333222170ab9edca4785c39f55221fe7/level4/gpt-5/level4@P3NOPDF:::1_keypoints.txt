- Paper formalizes on-the-job learning that starts with crowd annotations and transitions to automated predictions, using MCTS to decide when to query the crowd.
- Strength: Clear motivation to balance accuracy, latency, and monetary cost when human input is available at prediction time.
- Strength: Decision-theoretic framing is appropriate and can subsume ad hoc heuristics if done carefully.
- Strength: MCTS is a reasonable approximate planner when exact dynamic programming is infeasible.
- Strength: Overall presentation is generally readable.
- Weakness: Key technical details are missing or ambiguous, hindering assessment and reproducibility.
- Weakness: Empirical gains over a simple threshold heuristic are modest and stronger baseline comparisons are missing.
- Weakness: Unclear whether the used “online” systems are state-of-the-art, raising questions about practical relevance versus gains due to human input.
- Weakness: Little analysis on when the method helps or fails across noise, latency regimes, or specific error modes.
- Major question 1: Clarify Equation (2)’s conditioning on state s and whether p_R(r_i | y, q_i) should be marginalized over current belief p(y | s).
- Major question 2: Define N(s) in Algorithm 1 precisely, distinguish it from N(s,a), and specify its role in UCT and initialization.
- Major question 3: Explain how threshold baseline values (0.3 and 0.88) were chosen, what confidence they represent, and report sensitivity and budget/latency parity.
- Major question 4: Justify the competitiveness of the “online” systems or, if near SOTA, analyze cases where human queries improve or fail due to worker noise/latency.
- Request: Provide explicit formal model specification (state, actions, transitions, rewards/costs), worker noise model, priors, and latency modeling.
- Request: Report planning details (MCTS simulations, horizon, rollout policy, UCT constant), stopping criteria, and computational overhead including wall-clock comparisons.
- Request: Present comprehensive cost–accuracy–latency curves, Pareto analyses, and statistical significance measures (confidence intervals).
- Request: Compare to stronger baselines (myopic value-of-information, uncertainty sampling with budget, bandit-style query allocation) to isolate MCTS contribution.
- Request: Report calibration metrics (e.g., ECE) and calibration methods used for thresholding.
- Request: Provide ablations for worker accuracy priors, latency distributions, query limits, and analyze failure cases where planning harms performance.
- Request: Improve reproducibility with complete pseudocode, detailed configurations, and preferably released code.
- Overall assessment: Promising, timely formulation with reasonable solver choice, but requires technical clarifications, stronger comparisons, and deeper analysis to be compelling.
