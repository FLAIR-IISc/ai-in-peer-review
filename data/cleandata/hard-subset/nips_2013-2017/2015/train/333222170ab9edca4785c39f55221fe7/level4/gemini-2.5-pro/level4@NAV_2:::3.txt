This paper introduces a framework for "on-the-job learning," a hybrid approach that combines real-time crowdsourcing with an online-trained model to solve prediction tasks from a cold start. The core concept is to rely heavily on crowd input initially and then gradually decrease this reliance as the model gains confidence from the acquired labels. This process is formally modeled as a stochastic game between the system and the crowd, grounded in Bayesian decision theory. The system can issue queries for specific parts of an input, and its actions aim to maximize an expected utility function that balances accuracy, monetary cost, and latency.

Since the introduction of continuous time for query and response events makes computing the optimal policy intractable, the authors propose an approximation based on Monte Carlo Tree Search and progressive widening. The framework is evaluated on named-entity recognition (NER), sentiment classification, and image classification tasks. It is compared against several baselines: human majority vote (n=1,3,5), a standard online learning model with access to true labels, and a simplified "threshold" baseline that uses the same decision-theoretic model but without the complex continuous-time dynamics. The results show that the full model, LENSE, achieves a strong balance of performance and cost, significantly outperforming the online learning baseline in initial accuracy and substantially reducing costs compared to purely human annotation. LENSE's performance is shown to be comparable to the simpler threshold baseline and the 5-vote human baseline.

**Strengths:**

*   **Originality and Significance:** This paper presents a novel and highly significant contribution. It is, to my knowledge, the first work to provide a complete and principled decision-theoretic framework for combining real-time crowdsourcing and online model improvement. Previous approaches have often been more heuristic. The problem formulation is elegant, and the potential for practical impact and stimulating further research is high. The work is a strong candidate for acceptance based on these merits alone.
*   **Quality:** The experimental results are impressive, demonstrating clear advantages over standard online learning and pure crowdsourcing approaches across multiple tasks. The comparison to a strong "threshold" baseline is also commendable.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Clarity:** The paper's primary weakness is its clarity, particularly concerning the introduction and justification of the continuous-time model. While the motivation to incorporate latency is sound, its inclusion significantly complicates the problem formulation, the utility function, and the solution algorithm. This added complexity does not appear to be justified by the empirical results. For example, in Table 2, the simpler "threshold" baseline achieves lower latency on the NER task and slightly better F1 on the Faces task, with only a marginal drop in performance on NER. This disconnect between the complexity of the proposed solution and its marginal benefit over a simpler alternative could hinder the paper's impact and the adoption of its methods.

**Constructive Suggestions:**

The paper's clarity and impact could be greatly improved by reframing the presentation. I suggest the authors first develop the core framework without the time component (which is effectively the "threshold" baseline) and demonstrate its value. The continuous-time model could then be introduced as an extension. This would make the central contribution much easier to understand and implement. In the results discussion, the authors should be more transparent about the strong performance of the "threshold" baseline, even in structured prediction tasks like NER, as this is a significant finding in itself. While I recommend the paper for acceptance as is, these changes would substantially strengthen the final version.

**Detailed Comments:**

*   **Reporting of Results:**
    *   The claim in the introduction that the model "boost[s] performance relative to the expert provided labels" is not supported by the data, as the 5-vote baseline outperforms LENSE on the NER task (Table 2).
    *   The 5-vote human baseline is a critical point of comparison, but its results are missing for the Sentiment and Faces tasks in Tables 2 and 3, and from Figure 4. These should be included for completeness.
    *   In Table 2, the F1 for the 5-vote baseline and the latency for the Threshold baseline should be bolded, as they are the best results for those metrics.
    *   The "Threshold" baseline is a key ablation, but it is missing from the sentiment classification experiments in Table 3 and Figure 4. It should be included.

*   **Clarity of Method:**
    *   The utility function incorporates a time penalty (`wT`), which implies a soft deadline. However, it is unclear what criteria the system used in the experiments to decide when to stop querying and return an answer. Was a hard deadline (`t_deadline`) used, or was termination based on an uncertainty threshold?
    *   In Equation (3), the model `p(y, r, t | x,q, s)` seems correct, but this notation is inconsistent with its usage in Equation (1) which omits the query issue times `s` from the conditioning set.
    *   In the description of the Threshold baseline (lines 246-252), the inequality `(1−pθ(yi | x))×0.3m ≥ 0.98` appears to be reversed. Increasing the number of queries `m` should decrease the uncertainty (the left-hand side), so the condition should likely be `<=`.

In summary, this paper introduces a novel and important framework for on-the-job learning, supported by strong empirical results. Its primary weakness is a lack of clarity arising from a complex continuous-time formulation that yields minimal performance benefits over a much simpler variant. Despite these clarity issues, the paper's originality and significance are high, and it should be accepted.
