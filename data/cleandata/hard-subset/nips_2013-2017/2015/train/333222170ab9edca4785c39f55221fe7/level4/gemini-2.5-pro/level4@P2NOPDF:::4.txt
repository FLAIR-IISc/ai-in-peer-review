The authors introduce the "on-the-job learning" paradigm, where a model can query an annotator after receiving an input but before making a final prediction.

While the algorithm's application to structured prediction tasks is well-explained, its use in the face recognition experiment is unclear. The paper should clarify how queries were made in this context; was the entire instance queried? In such a non-structured setting, it is not obvious how the model would propagate information between adjacent examples, which calls into question the applicability of the proposed utility function.

Regarding the experiments, several points need to be addressed. The threshold-based baseline relies on two parameters whose values appear arbitrary. The authors should provide a justification for these specific choices and include a sensitivity analysis to demonstrate their impact on performance. To better situate the proposed method's contributions, a comparison against a standard uncertainty sampling baseline, a common approach in active learning, would also be a valuable addition.

Finally, the paper makes a concerted effort to distinguish "on-the-job learning" from active online learning. However, this distinction may not be as sharp as presented. A conventional active learning framework could be adapted to this domain by defining the sequence as the active set for querying. For instance, a model could still be updated upon receiving annotation responses before outputting its final prediction. Although this adaptation might not fully capture the temporal aspects of the task, it would allow the work to draw upon the rich theoretical foundation of active learning for selecting the query set. A more detailed discussion of this relationship would strengthen the paper's positioning.

In summary, the paper presents a seemingly novel framework, but its contribution is diminished by a lack of clarity in key areas, particularly regarding its application to non-structured tasks. Furthermore, the experimental validation could be strengthened with more rigorous baselines and a more thorough discussion of the relationship to existing learning paradigms.
