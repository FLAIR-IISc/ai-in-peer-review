## Review of NIPS 2015 Submission

This paper presents a theoretically grounded formulation of an “on-the-job” learning approach, building upon the initial work in [22]. The core idea is to begin with a system heavily reliant on crowd-sourced labels, and gradually transition towards a learned model that selectively queries the crowd only for inputs where its own predictions are uncertain. This selective querying is modeled as a decision problem efficiently solved using Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS). While the approach appears reasonable and has the potential to be valuable, the empirical results, compared to a simple threshold-based baseline, are not particularly compelling. Nevertheless, the work offers a solid theoretical foundation and invites further exploration of this important area – learning to actively query for labels.

The presentation is generally clear, but suffers from a lack of crucial detail in several key areas. Addressing these points would significantly strengthen the paper. 

Specifically, I have the following concerns:

1. **Equation (2):** The current formulation is unclear. The left-hand side should be conditioned on the state *s* to accurately reflect its dependence on the current learning progress. Furthermore, the variable *y* appears to disappear within the probability *p{R}(ri | y, qi)*. A detailed explanation of how *y* is integrated into, or eliminated from, this probability calculation is necessary. 

2. **Algorithm 1: N(s):** The notation *N(s)* within Algorithm 1 is undefined.  Please clarify what *N(s)* represents – is it the number of visits to state *s* during MCTS, or something else entirely? A clear definition is essential for understanding the algorithm's behavior.

3. **Threshold Baseline Parameter Selection:** The values of 0.3 and 0.88 used in the threshold baseline are not justified.  How were these thresholds determined? What methodology was used to find these optimal values (e.g., cross-validation)? Providing this information will allow for a fairer comparison to the proposed MCTS-based approach.

4. **Comparison to State-of-the-Art Online Systems:**  I lack familiarity with the specifics of the tasks employed in the experiments. It’s crucial to establish whether the "online" systems used as baselines represent the current best machine learning performance on these tasks. If they *don’t*, the gains achieved through human annotation are potentially overstated. Conversely, if they *do* represent state-of-the-art performance, any further improvement attributable solely to human input is particularly noteworthy. Providing examples of such cases, and a deeper error analysis showcasing *where* and *why* the proposed system improves upon existing methods, would greatly enhance the paper's impact. For instance, is the proposed system better at handling ambiguous cases or edge scenarios that the fully learned model struggles with? 

In summary, this paper introduces a theoretically sound framework for on-the-job learning with active querying. The MCTS-based approach to optimizing query selection is a promising direction. However, the empirical evaluation doesn't convincingly demonstrate a significant advantage over a simple baseline, and several technical details require clarification. Addressing the points raised above – particularly regarding equation (2), the definition of *N(s)*, baseline parameter justification, and a more comprehensive comparison to strong online learners coupled with error analysis – would substantially improve the paper’s quality and increase its potential impact. Despite its current limitations, this work is a valuable contribution to the discussion around active learning and intelligent crowdsourcing, and deserves further investigation.
