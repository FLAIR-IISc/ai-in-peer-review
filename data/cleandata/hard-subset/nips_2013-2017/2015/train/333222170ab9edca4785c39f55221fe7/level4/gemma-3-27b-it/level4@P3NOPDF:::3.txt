This paper presents a hybrid approach for leveraging both crowdsourced labels and an incrementally (online) trained model to address prediction problems. The core idea is to heavily rely on the crowd during initial stages, learn from the acquired labels, and subsequently reduce dependence on the crowd as the model gains confidence. This is elegantly framed as a stochastic game based on a CRF prediction model, where both the system and the crowd are players. The system issues queries *q* for tokens *x* (with true label *y*), eliciting responses *r*, and aims to maximize expected utility *U(q, r)*. Crucially, queries are issued over time *s* with response times *t*, and utility is maximized subject to a *t<sub>deadline</sub>*—determining the number and rate of queries. 

Calculating this expected utility necessitates using a simulation dynamics model *P(y, r, t | x, q, s)* to compute utilities as in (4). While optimal action selection is intractable due to the continuous time formulation, the authors employ an approximation based on Monte Carlo Tree Search and TD learning (Algorithm 1). Experiments were conducted on simplified named entity recognition (NER), sentiment recognition, and face identification tasks, comparing four methods: majority vote of *n* human judges (1, 3, 5), online learning (using true labels), a “threshold” baseline (the authors’ model without continuous time, issuing *m* queries until uncertainty falls below a threshold), and the authors’ full model (LENSE).  

Results (Table 2) demonstrate that LENSE outperforms all but the 5-vote crowdsourced version in terms of precision/recall/F1. The “threshold” baseline performs nearly as well, and with lower latency for NER. Figure 3 illustrates that LENSE requires progressively fewer crowdsourced labels as the model strengthens. Figure 4 shows that LENSE achieves high accuracy from the first example, leveraging crowd expertise while model uncertainty is high – an advantage over purely online learning.

This is a highly interesting and potentially impactful paper. It’s the first principled proposal I’ve encountered for learning from the crowd while simultaneously training a system, and the results against reasonable baselines are impressive. I believe the work is original, significant, and deserves acceptance. However, the paper’s clarity could be significantly improved, potentially hindering its broader impact. Many of these issues are addressable even in a camera-ready version, perhaps through refining the tone and emphasizing the strengths of the “threshold” baseline.

The primary clarity issue stems from the introduction of continuous time (Section 3). While the motivation – accommodating time and cost budgets – is reasonable, the experimental benefit appears minimal while substantially increasing complexity in formulation, discussion, and optimization.  In fact, Table 2 suggests the “threshold” baseline achieves significantly lower latency on NER with comparable performance, and even outperforms LENSE on face ID. The authors attribute the baseline’s success on face ID to the task’s single-label nature, but NER – with its inherent label interactions – sees only marginal benefit from continuous time. For many practical applications, the "threshold" baseline will likely be sufficiently effective, offering a significant gain as a principled hybrid learning system that outperforms active and online learning. 

I suggest the authors consider presenting the core method *without* continuous time – simplifying understanding and implementation – and framing the addition of continuous time as an extension.  Similarly, in the results discussion, highlighting the strong performance of the “threshold” method, even with sequence dependencies, would be beneficial. While not strictly necessary for acceptance, this clarification would increase the paper’s accessibility and potential for widespread adoption.

Several minor issues require attention regarding results and reporting. First, the claim of “beating human performance” is inaccurate in the 5-vote NER case. Furthermore, the 5-vote results are inexplicably missing for Face-ID and Sentiment in multiple tables and figures (the right half of Tables 2, 3, and Figure 4) and *should* be included.  The 5-vote results in Table 2 should be bolded, as they represent the best performance. Similarly, the latency value for Threshold in NER should be bolded. More importantly, the “Threshold” baseline is absent from the sentiment experiment (Table 3 and Figure 4) – its inclusion is crucial for a complete comparison.

Finally, a few minor method-related points:

*   The paper discusses *t<sub>deadline</sub>* (l.183-187) but doesn’t specify its use in the experiments or the criteria for determining response readiness (e.g., an entropic threshold vs. reaching *t<sub>deadline</sub>*).
*   Equation (2) should include *s* in the distribution: *p(y, r, t | x, q, s)*.
*   The use of *q* in equation (5)’s *F(q)* is confusing; it refers to a nominal distribution function, not a query.  Please use a different letter.
*   In the “Threshold” baseline description (l.246-252), the expression should likely be *(1 - p<sub>θ</sub>(y<sub>i</sub>|x))*0.3<sup>m</sup> <= (1-0.88)*, as increasing *m* reduces uncertainty, not increases it.

In conclusion, this paper elegantly casts a hybrid approach for combining crowdsourcing and online learning as a stochastic game, effectively modeling data, labels, and crowdworker responses including response times. The results are promising, demonstrating competitive performance against strong baselines.  The method is original and interesting, warranting acceptance. However, addressing the clarity concerns, particularly regarding the continuous time extension, would substantially enhance the paper’s impact and facilitate wider understanding and adoption.
