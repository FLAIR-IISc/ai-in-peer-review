This paper presents a novel hybrid approach that leverages both crowdsourced labels and an incrementally trained model to address prediction problems. The core idea is to heavily rely on the crowd during the system's initial stages, learn from the acquired labels, and gradually reduce the crowd's involvement as the model becomes more confident. This approach is elegantly cast as a stochastic game based on a Conditional Random Field (CRF) prediction model, where the system and the crowd are both players.

The system can issue one or more queries for tokens, eliciting responses from the crowd, with each outcome associated with a utility value. The system's goal is to pick actions that maximize the expected utility, taking into account the time at which queries are sent and answers are received. The expected utility is computed using a simulation dynamics model, which requires approximations based on Monte Carlo Tree Search and TD learning.

The authors conduct experiments on three tasks: named entity recognition (NER), sentiment recognition, and face identification. They compare their approach, called LENSE, with four baselines: the majority vote of n human judges (1, 3, and 5), online learning with true labels, a "threshold" baseline using the authors' model without continuous time, and LENSE itself. The results show that LENSE outperforms all baselines except for the 5-vote crowdsourced version, with the "threshold" baseline performing nearly as well, especially in terms of latency for NER.

The authors also demonstrate that their model requires fewer crowdsourced labels over time, given a sufficiently strong model to train, and that it achieves high accuracy from the first example, leveraging crowd expertise while the model uncertainty is still high.

This paper is highly original and significant, presenting a complete and principled proposal for learning from the crowd while training a system. The results against reasonable baselines are impressive, and the approach has the potential to generate a lot of discussion and further work, as well as adoption in practice.

However, the paper's clarity is a major concern. The introduction of continuous time, starting in Section 3, significantly complicates the formulation and discussion, as well as the optimization procedure. While the motivation for continuous time is reasonable, its experimental benefits are minimal, and the "threshold" baseline performs nearly as well, if not better, in some cases.

To improve the paper's clarity and impact, I suggest that the authors develop the core method without introducing continuous time, making it easier to understand and implement. The addition of continuous time can then be presented as an extension. Furthermore, when discussing the results, the authors should emphasize the strong performance of the "threshold" method, even in cases with sequence dependence between tasks.

There are also several minor issues with the results and their reporting. The introduction claims that the model "beats human performance," but this is not true in the 5-vote case for NER. The 5-vote case is missing for the Face-ID and Sentiment tasks in multiple tables and figures, and the "Threshold" baseline is missing for the sentiment experiment. These omissions should be addressed, and the results for the 5-vote case should be bolded in Table 2, as they represent the best performance.

Additionally, there are a few minor comments regarding the method description. The discussion of t_deadline in lines 183-187 is unclear, and it is not specified what t_deadline was used in the experiments or what criteria were used to determine when the answer was ready to report. The distribution in equation (2) should be given the variable s, and the use of q in equation (5) is confusing and should be replaced with a different letter. Finally, the expression in the description of the Threshold baseline should be corrected to (1 - ptheta(yi|x))*0.3^m <= (1-0.88).

In conclusion, while this paper presents a novel and interesting approach, its clarity and presentation need improvement. Addressing the mentioned issues will enhance the paper's impact and make it more accessible to a wider audience.
