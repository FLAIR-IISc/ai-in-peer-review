

The paper introduces "on-the-job learning," a framework for deploying accurate AI systems from zero training examples by strategically using crowdsourcing during prediction time. The authors frame this as a stochastic game based on Bayesian decision theory, allowing principled tradeoffs between latency, cost, and accuracy. They propose an approximation using Monte Carlo Tree Search with progressive widening to handle the intractable optimal policy. The method was evaluated on named-entity recognition (NER), sentiment classification, and image classification, showing significant cost reductions compared to full human annotation while improving accuracy over baselines.

This work relates to online learning (which doesn't maintain high accuracy from the start), active learning (which assumes labels are only observed after classification), and active classification (which relies on a static policy learned from a fully labeled dataset). The closest related work appears to be Legion:AR [6], which operates in a similar on-the-job setting but without the theoretical foundation that this paper provides.

Strengths:
1. The framework addresses a significant real-world challenge: deploying AI systems with minimal initial data, particularly valuable for applications like disaster relief monitoring where labeled data is scarce at deployment time.
2. The theoretical foundation as a stochastic game with Bayesian decision theory provides a principled approach to balancing multiple objectives, which is lacking in related work.
3. The experimental results are compelling, showing more than an order of magnitude cost reduction on NER while improving F1 by 8% over single-human labeling and 28% over online learning.
4. The implementation is well-documented with an open-source release (LENS), enhancing reproducibility.

Weaknesses:
1. The application to the face recognition task is unclear. The paper treats face recognition as a structured prediction problem, but face classification is inherently non-structured (each image is an independent instance). For non-structured tasks, the model wouldn't propagate information between "adjacent positions," making the utility function's rationale questionable. The authors should clarify how the method was applied to face recognition and whether the structured prediction framework was adapted for this purpose.

2. The threshold baseline has two parameters (0.3 and 0.98) that lack justification. The paper states: "For each label, yi, we ask for m queries such that (1−pθ(yi | x))×0.3m ≥ 0.98." The authors should provide more justification for these choices and demonstrate sensitivity analysis showing how results vary with these parameters.

3. The paper claims to distinguish itself from active learning, but this distinction is not fully convincing. A more rigorous comparison is needed: active learning could be adapted to this domain by treating the incoming stream of examples as the active set, updating the model after each response, and outputting a prediction at the end. The authors should address this point more thoroughly and include a baseline using uncertainty sampling (a common active learning strategy) for comparison.

4. The paper's applicability appears limited to structured prediction tasks. For non-structured tasks, the benefits of the framework are unclear, as the model wouldn't propagate information between "adjacent positions" as it does in structured prediction. The authors should discuss the limitations of their approach more explicitly.

Overall assessment:
The paper presents a novel and well-motivated framework with clear theoretical foundations and strong empirical results. The core contribution is significant for real-world applications where labeled data is scarce at deployment time. However, the unclear application to non-structured tasks, lack of justification for key baseline parameters, and incomplete comparison with active learning approaches are notable weaknesses.

Quality: The technical approach is sound, and the experimental results are well-supported. The main issue is the unclear application to non-structured tasks.

Clarity: The paper is generally well-written, but the face recognition application and threshold baseline parameters need clarification.

Originality: The framework is novel compared to previous work on online learning and active classification.

Significance: The method addresses a real-world problem with potential for significant cost reduction in practical applications.

Pro/Con acceptance:
Pro: Novel framework with strong empirical results addressing a real-world problem; clear theoretical foundation; well-documented implementation.
Con: Unclear application to non-structured tasks; arbitrary baseline parameters; incomplete comparison with active learning approaches.

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the concerns above, particularly clarifying the face recognition application, justifying the threshold baseline parameters, and providing a more thorough comparison with active learning approaches.
