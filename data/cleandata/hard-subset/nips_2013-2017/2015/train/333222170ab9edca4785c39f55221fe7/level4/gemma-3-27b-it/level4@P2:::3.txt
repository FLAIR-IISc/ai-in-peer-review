This paper presents a hybrid approach for using both crowdsourced labels and an incrementally (online) trained model to address prediction problems. The core idea is to lean heavily on the crowd as the system is ramping up, learn from the labels thus acquired, and then use the crowd less and less often as the model becomes more confident. This is achieved by framing the problem as a stochastic game based on a CRF prediction model where the system and the crowd are players. The system issues queries *q* for tokens *x* (with true label *y*) to elicit responses *r*, with a utility *U(q,r)* for each outcome, attempting to maximize expected utility. Importantly, queries are not issued all at once, but at times *s* (with response times *t*), allowing for optimization with respect to a time budget. 

Computing this expected utility requires using the simulation dynamics model *P(y,r,t|x,q,s)* to compute utilities, as in (4).  An optimal action is then selected; however, the introduction of continuous time makes optimization intractable, leading to an approximation based on Monte Carlo Tree Search and TD learning (Algorithm 1). Experiments were conducted on named entity recognition (NER), sentiment recognition, and face identification tasks, using four methods: majority voting of crowd workers (1, 3, 5 votes), online learning (with true labels), a “threshold” baseline (the authors’ model without continuous time, where *m* queries are sent until model uncertainty falls below a threshold), and the authors’ full model (LENSE).  LENSE generally outperforms all but the 5-vote crowdsourced version, though the threshold baseline performs nearly as well (and with lower latency on NER; Table 2). The authors demonstrate that the model requires fewer crowdsourced labels over time with sufficient model capacity (Figure 3) and achieves high accuracy from the first example compared to online learning (Figure 4).

This is a compelling and original paper that I expect will generate discussion and adoption.  There have been many heuristic approaches to learning from the crowd while training a system, but this is the first complete and principled proposal I’ve seen. The results against reasonable baselines are impressive, and I believe the work is significant enough for inclusion in the conference. However, the clarity could be improved, potentially impacting its impact.

The primary clarity issue arises with the introduction of continuous time in Section 3. The motivation is reasonable – a time (and cost) budget for each classification may be necessary – but experimentally, it appears to offer minimal benefit while significantly increasing complexity. In fact, in Table 2, the threshold baseline achieves lower latency on NER with comparable performance, and outperforms LENSE on the face ID task. While the authors acknowledge the benefit might depend on task characteristics, the performance difference is noteworthy. The paper would likely have greater impact if the core method was developed fully *without* continuous time, simplifying understanding and implementation, and presenting continuous time as an extension.

When discussing results, the authors should more clearly state that the threshold baseline performs strongly, even with sequence dependencies between labels. This isn’t a critique preventing acceptance, but highlighting this could enhance the paper's impact and encourage wider adoption.

There are several smaller issues with the results presentation. The introduction claims the model "beats human performance," which is inaccurate for NER with 5 votes. The 5-vote case is also missing for Face-ID and Sentiment in multiple tables and figures (right half of Tables 2, 3, and Figure 4) and should be included. The results for the 5-vote case should be bolded to highlight the best performance. Similarly, the latency value for the Threshold baseline in NER should be bolded. Crucially, the Threshold baseline is missing for the sentiment experiment, and thus absent from Table 3 and Figure 4; it should be included.

Minor methodological comments:

*   Regarding *t\_deadline* (l.183-187), it's unclear what deadline was used in the experiments, or the criteria for reporting an answer (e.g., an entropy threshold or reaching a deadline?).
*   Equation (2) requires the variable *s* in the distribution, i.e., *p(y,r,t|x,q,s)*.
*   In equation (5), the use of *q* in *F(q)* is confusing – it seems to refer to a query *q*, but it’s actually a nominal distribution function. A different letter would improve clarity.
*   In the threshold baseline description (l.246-252), the expression should likely be *(1 - pθ(yi|x))*0.3^m <= (1-0.88)*, as increasing *m* reduces uncertainty, not increases it.

In summary, this paper presents a novel and effective approach to on-the-job learning. Despite some issues with clarity, particularly surrounding the introduction of continuous time, and some inconsistencies in the presentation of results, the strong technical contributions and promising experimental results warrant acceptance. Addressing the clarity concerns would further strengthen the paper and increase its potential impact.
