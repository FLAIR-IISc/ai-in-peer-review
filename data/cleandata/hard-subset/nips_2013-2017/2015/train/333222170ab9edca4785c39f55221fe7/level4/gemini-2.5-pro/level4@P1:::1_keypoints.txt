- Paper proposes an "on-the-job learning" framework that starts with real-time crowdsourcing and trains an online model to reduce human dependence.  
- Core contribution: principled Bayesian decision-theoretic formulation modeling the task as a stochastic game (query, wait, or output) to trade off accuracy, latency, and cost.  
- Optimal policy is intractable; authors propose an MCTS-based approximate solution with progressive widening to handle continuous time.  
- Evaluated on three tasks: named-entity recognition, sentiment classification, and image classification.  
- Strength: addresses a practical problem of deploying accurate systems without initial labeled data.  
- Strength: elegant, theoretically sound Bayesian stochastic-game formulation that unifies accuracy, cost, and latency trade-offs.  
- Strength: MCTS-based approximation is a sophisticated, appropriate choice for the large state space.  
- Clarity issue: unclear how p(r' | x, r, q) is computed by marginalizing y and t given that pR(ri | yqi) depends on unknown true labels and posterior integration may be expensive.  
- Clarity issue: Algorithm 1 uses N(σ) without explicit definition—likely visit count but needs to be stated.  
- Baseline question: threshold heuristic uses constants 0.3 and 0.98 with no justification or description of tuning—authors should explain their choice.  
- Experimental concern: empirical gains of full LENSE over the simpler threshold heuristic are modest, raising questions about practical benefit of the complex approach.  
- Baseline concern: the "Online learning" baseline’s strength is not well established; comparison to strong pre-trained offline models and detailed error analysis are needed to clarify contributions.  
- Overall conclusion: theoretically solid and well-motivated work that introduces important ideas, but requires clearer presentation and stronger empirical validation.
