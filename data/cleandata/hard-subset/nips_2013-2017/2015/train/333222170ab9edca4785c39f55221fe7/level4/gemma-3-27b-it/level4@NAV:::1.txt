This paper’s primary contribution lies in a more rigorous theoretical framework for an on-the-job learning algorithm, building upon the work in [22]. The proposed learner begins by relying on crowd-sourced responses, progressively incorporating its own predictions and reducing its dependence on human input. A Monte Carlo Tree Search is employed to determine when to solicit crowd feedback for each new input, framing this as a decision-making process.

The paper is generally well-written, but lacks sufficient detail in certain areas. The empirical evaluation, when compared to a threshold-based heuristic, shows limited improvement. Nevertheless, the proposed approach appears promising and warrants further exploration. 

The authors should address the following points for clarification:

1. Equation (2) requires revision for clarity. The left-hand side should be conditioned on the state *s*. Additionally, the role and disappearance of the variable *y* within the probability *p{R}(ri | y, q_i)* needs explanation.
2. The meaning of *N(s)* in Algorithm 1 is not clearly defined.
3. Please provide details on how the values of 0.3 and 0.88 were determined for the threshold baseline.
4. Given my unfamiliarity with the specific tasks, it's important to understand the performance level of the "online" systems used. Are these state-of-the-art machine learning models for these tasks? If not, what accounts for the performance difference? If they *are* state-of-the-art, then any observed gains from the proposed method are likely solely attributable to the use of human annotation. Providing examples of such instances, alongside a more thorough error analysis, would be beneficial. 

This work offers a valuable theoretical treatment of an on-the-job learner that dynamically shifts from a fully crowd-sourced approach to a model leveraging learned predictions with selective crowd queries. While the empirical findings are not overwhelmingly conclusive, the paper initiates a worthwhile discussion and direction for future research.
