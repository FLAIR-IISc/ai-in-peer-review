- Problem: creating high-accuracy classifiers from zero labeled examples while handling noisy crowd responses and optimizing timing/latency.
- Novel focus: emphasizes timing and latency optimization in de-noising crowd responses.
- Method: formulates the task as a stochastic game using Bayesian decision theory to trade off latency, cost, and accuracy.
- Approximation: optimal policy is intractable, so the authors use Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) as an approximation.
- Evaluation: experiments on NER, sentiment, and image classification show significant cost reductions vs full human annotation and improvements over online learning.
- Implementation: open-source system LENSE provided, increasing reproducibility and impact.
- Baseline comparison: LENSE beats the 3-vote baseline but loses to the 5-vote baseline, illustrating consensus vs model-based trade-offs.
- Clarity issue: the example around line 154 is too abstract and should use a concrete query with realistic responses/timings.
- Terminology inconsistency: the threshold baseline is labeled both “entropic” and “threshold” in Tables 2 and 3, causing confusion.
- Structural issue: related work is placed unusually late (Section 6) and would typically appear earlier.
- Minor error: missing figure reference at line 86 ("Figure ??").
- Quality assessment: technically sound with clear math and experiments, though some explanations need more detail.
- Originality: novel combination of Bayesian decision theory, MCTS, and on-the-job learning.
- Significance: results are practically impactful for low-data settings (e.g., real-time extraction, disaster relief) and amplified by open-source release.
- Arguments for acceptance: principled novel approach, strong results, clear experimental setup, and open-source implementation.
- Arguments against acceptance: dense/difficult explanations, inconsistent terminology, and odd placement of related work.
