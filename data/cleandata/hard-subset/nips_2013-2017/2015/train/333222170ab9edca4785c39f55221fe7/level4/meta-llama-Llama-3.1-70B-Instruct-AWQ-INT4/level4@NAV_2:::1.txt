The main contribution of this paper is to provide a more theoretically sound formulation of an on the job learner proposed in prior work. The learner starts by querying the crowd to produce its output, but gradually starts making its own predictions and querying the crowd infrequently. The choice of whether to query the crowd for any given input is modeled as a decision problem solved via a Monte Carlo Tree Search.

The presentation is mostly good, although lacking in important details. The empirical results vis a vis a threshold-based heuristic are somewhat underwhelming. However, it certainly seems like a reasonable approach and merits further investigation.

The paper is technically sound, with a clear and well-organized structure. The authors provide a thorough description of their model and algorithm, and the empirical evaluation is well-designed. The use of Bayesian decision theory to trade off latency, cost, and accuracy is a notable strength of the paper.

One of the main strengths of the paper is its ability to balance the trade-offs between latency, cost, and accuracy in a principled way. The authors demonstrate that their approach can achieve significant cost reductions while maintaining high accuracy, which is a key contribution of the paper.

However, there are some areas where the paper could be improved. The empirical results are somewhat limited, and it would be helpful to see more detailed analysis of the performance of the system. Additionally, the authors could provide more context about the specific tasks used in the evaluation, and how they relate to other work in the field.
