

The authors introduce "on-the-job learning," a scenario where annotators are queried about examples between receiving input and generating predictions. While the algorithm is clearly explained for structured prediction tasks (e.g., querying elements within a sequence), it remains unclear how this framework applies to the face recognition task. Specifically, was the instance itself the queried element? In non-structured settings, the model cannot propagate information across adjacent examples, rendering the utility function conceptually problematic—without such propagation, how does the model leverage sequential context?  

The threshold baseline introduces two parameters that appear arbitrary. The authors provide no justification for their selection or analysis of sensitivity to these values. Without such details, the baseline lacks credibility as a meaningful comparison.  

A more compelling baseline would incorporate uncertainty sampling, a standard active learning strategy. This would better contextualize the proposed method against established techniques for query selection, strengthening the experimental evaluation.  

The authors emphasize a distinction between their setting and active online learning, yet the latter could be adapted to this domain with minimal modification. Specifically, the active learning framework could operate over a sequence (where the "active set" is the incoming example stream), updating the model incrementally upon receiving annotations and outputting a final prediction. While this adaptation would not fully capture the temporal dynamics of the task, it would leverage substantial existing theory for optimal query selection. Thus, the purported novelty of the framework appears overstated.  

Overall, the core idea presents a plausible adaptation of active learning to sequential annotation, but several critical gaps undermine its contribution. The application to face recognition lacks clarity, the non-structured case is theoretically unsound, and key experimental baselines are missing. The framework also appears restricted in applicability—its utility hinges on the assumption that sequential context is irrelevant or unexplored, which limits its relevance to real-world tasks where temporal dependencies matter. Without addressing these issues, the work’s significance remains unclear.
