### **Summary**

This paper introduces "on-the-job learning," a framework for deploying a high-accuracy system from scratch, without any initial training data. As new inputs arrive, the system decides whether to predict immediately or to query a real-time crowd for labels on uncertain parts of the input. The collected labels are used both for the immediate prediction and to update the system's model, thus reducing reliance on the crowd over time. The authors formalize this problem as a stochastic game between the system and the crowd. They use Bayesian decision theory to define a utility function that balances accuracy, monetary cost, and latency. As finding the optimal policy is intractable, they propose an approximation based on Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) with progressive widening. This work formalizes and extends ideas previously implemented in Legion:AR [6]. It is distinguished from standard online active learning by its ability to query for information before making a prediction on the current instance, and from active classification by its capacity to learn and improve its policy at test time.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Significance and Originality:** The paper addresses a significant and practical problem: how to bootstrap a high-quality ML system in a new domain with minimal upfront cost and time. The "on-the-job learning" formulation is compelling and novel, providing a valuable conceptual framework for systems that must perform well from the very first example.

2.  **Quality of the Approach:** The framing of the problem within Bayesian decision theory provides a principled way to handle the complex trade-offs between accuracy, cost, and latency. The choice of MCTS to approximate the intractable policy is a technically sound and appropriate approach for this sequential decision-making problem.

3.  **Empirical Results:** The experiments demonstrate strong performance, particularly on the NER task. The proposed system (LENSE) achieves a significant (over 10x) cost reduction compared to a 5-vote human annotation baseline while also improving the F1 score over a single expert. This result powerfully illustrates the practical potential of the approach.

4.  **Reproducibility:** The authors have made their code and data publicly available on CodaLab. This is highly commendable and greatly enhances the transparency and reproducibility of their work.

### **Weaknesses**

1.  **Clarity of Technical Details:** While the high-level idea is clear, the paper could be improved by clarifying several key technical details in the model and algorithm descriptions. This makes it difficult for a reader to fully grasp the implementation.

2.  **Comparison to Heuristic Baseline:** The performance gains of the proposed LENSE model over the simpler threshold-based heuristic are modest on the Sentiment and Faces tasks. While LENSE is consistently better, this raises the question of whether the additional complexity of the MCTS-based game-playing approach provides a significant practical benefit in all scenarios. A discussion of this complexity-performance trade-off would strengthen the paper.

3.  **Strength of Baselines:** The choice of the "online learning" baseline could be better justified. It is unclear if the underlying model architecture (e.g., linear-chain CRF) represents the state-of-the-art for these tasks. If a more powerful base model were used, the performance gap between the pure ML system and the on-the-job learner might change. This makes it difficult to assess how much of the performance gain comes from the proposed decision-theoretic framework versus simply adding a human-in-the-loop component to a standard model.

4.  **Lack of Deeper Analysis:** The paper would benefit from a more in-depth error analysis. For instance, on which types of instances does the system still rely heavily on the crowd, even after processing many examples? Understanding these failure modes could provide valuable insights into the limitations of the current model and highlight promising directions for future work.

### **Questions and Suggestions for the Authors**

To improve the paper, I encourage the authors to address the following points:

1.  **Model Clarity:** In Section 3, the paper describes computing p(r' | x, r, q) by marginalizing out y from Equation 3. Could you please clarify the exact formulation of this marginalization? Equation 3 models the joint `p(y, r, t | x, q, s)`, and the dependency of the response model `pR(ri | yqi)` on only a single component `yqi` seems to simplify this step. A more detailed explanation would be helpful.

2.  **Algorithm Notation:** In Algorithm 1, line 5, could you please confirm the notation `V(σ')/N(σ') + c * sqrt(log N(σ) / N(σ'))`? It appears that `N(σ)` is the visit count for the parent state and `N(σ')` is for the child state, which is standard for UCT, but an explicit confirmation would improve clarity.

3.  **Baseline Hyperparameters:** The threshold baseline is an important point of comparison. Could you provide justification for the choice of its hyperparameters (e.g., the uncertainty reduction factor of 0.3 and the confidence threshold of 0.98)? Information on how these values were selected (e.g., tuned on a validation set) is crucial for a fair comparison.

4.  **Baseline Strength and Error Analysis:** It would be valuable to compare the performance of the "online learning" baseline to established results on these datasets to contextualize the improvements achieved by LENSE. Furthermore, could you provide some qualitative error analysis? For example, showing instances where LENSE correctly decides to query the crowd versus instances where the online learner fails would powerfully illustrate the benefits of the proposed framework.

### **Overall Recommendation**

This paper presents a novel, well-motivated, and principled framework for "on-the-job learning." The decision-theoretic formulation is elegant, and the empirical results on the NER task are impressive. While there are some weaknesses regarding the clarity of the model description and the strength of the baselines, the core ideas are significant and address an important real-world problem. The paper makes a solid contribution and, with revisions to address the clarity issues raised, would be a strong addition to the NIPS program.
