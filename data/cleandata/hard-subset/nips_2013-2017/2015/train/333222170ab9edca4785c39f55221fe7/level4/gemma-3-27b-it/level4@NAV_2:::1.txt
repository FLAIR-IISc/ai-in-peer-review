The main contribution of this paper is to provide a more theoretically sound formulation of an on-the-job learner previously proposed in [6]. The learner begins by leveraging crowd-sourced labels, gradually transitioning to a learned model that selectively queries the crowd only for low-confidence inputs. This query selection process is framed as a decision problem and solved using Monte Carlo Tree Search. The work builds on concepts from Bayesian decision theory and stochastic games to balance latency, cost, and accuracy.

The presentation is generally good, though lacking in specific implementation details. The empirical results, when compared to the threshold-based heuristic, are somewhat modest. Nevertheless, the proposed approach appears reasonable and warrants further exploration. The authors provide an open-source implementation, which is valuable for reproducibility and future research.

Please address/clarify the following points:

1. Equation (2) appears incomplete. The left-hand side, ExpAcc(p), should likely be conditioned on the input x and the query history, similar to the prediction model p(y | x, q, s, r, t). Furthermore, the disappearance of 'y' from p<sub>R</sub>(r<sub>i</sub> | y, q<sub>i</sub>) within the expectation is unclear and requires explanation.

2. The meaning of N(σ) in Algorithm 1 requires clarification. Is this the number of times a state has been visited during the search, or does it represent something else?

3. How were the parameters 0.3 and 0.88 determined for the threshold baseline? Providing justification for these values would strengthen the analysis.

4. The paper would benefit from a more detailed comparison to existing state-of-the-art machine learning systems for each task. Are the “online” systems described the highest-performing machine learning models available for these tasks? If not, the gains attributed to the proposed approach may primarily stem from the quality of the human annotations. Error analysis to identify cases where the on-the-job learner excels or struggles compared to these established baselines would be informative.

This paper presents a theoretical formulation of an “on-the-job learner” that begins as a crowdsourcing system and gradually transitions to a learned model, querying the crowd only for uncertain inputs. The empirical results are somewhat mixed, but this work offers a valuable contribution by initiating a discussion about this learning paradigm. 

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** The paper provides a principled, Bayesian decision-theoretic framework for on-the-job learning, which is a compelling and practical setting.
*   **Technical Soundness:** The combination of Monte Carlo Tree Search and progressive widening addresses the challenges of continuous time and intractable policy optimization.
*   **Potential Significance:** The potential for cost reduction and improved accuracy in real-world applications, such as rapid deployment of AI systems with limited labeled data, is significant.
*   **Reproducibility:** The authors provide an open-source implementation of their system.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Modest Empirical Gains:** The improvements over strong baselines, particularly the threshold baseline, are not substantial.
*   **Lack of Detail:** The paper could benefit from more detailed explanations of the implementation and experimental setup.
*   **Limited Error Analysis:** The lack of in-depth error analysis hinders a thorough understanding of the strengths and weaknesses of the approach.
