This paper introduces "on-the-job learning," a framework for deploying a prediction system that starts with no training data. The core idea is a hybrid approach that initially relies on real-time crowdsourcing to make predictions and then gradually learns from these labels, reducing its dependence on the crowd as its internal model improves. The authors formalize this setting by elegantly framing the problem as a stochastic game between the system and the crowd. In this game, the system makes sequential decisions about when to query the crowd, for which parts of the input, and when to return a final prediction, aiming to maximize a utility function that balances accuracy, cost, and latency.

The technical core of the approach models the task using a Conditional Random Field (CRF), where the system's policy for querying is guided by Bayesian decision theory. Because computing the optimal policy is intractable, particularly with the introduction of continuous response times, the authors propose an approximation based on Monte Carlo Tree Search combined with progressive widening. The proposed system, LENSE, is evaluated on named-entity recognition (NER), sentiment classification, and image classification tasks. It is compared against several baselines, including a multi-vote human baseline, a standard online learning system with oracle access to labels, and a simplified "threshold" version of their own model that omits the complex continuous-time reasoning.

**Strengths**

This is a highly interesting and valuable paper that addresses an important practical problem. The proposed framework is, to my knowledge, the first to offer a complete and principled decision-theoretic approach to hybrid crowd-machine learning systems that must perform well from the very first example. This stands in contrast to previous heuristic methods or other learning paradigms like online and active learning, which cannot guarantee high accuracy from the start.

*   **Originality and Significance:** The work is highly original and significant. The formulation of this problem as a stochastic game is novel and powerful. This work could inspire a great deal of further research and see practical adoption in real-world systems.
*   **Quality:** The technical approach is sophisticated, and the experimental results against well-chosen baselines are impressive. The system demonstrates a clear ability to reduce costs over time while maintaining high accuracy.

**Weaknesses**

While the paper is strong in its contribution, its primary weakness is a lack of clarity, which could unfortunately hinder its potential impact.

*   **Clarity and Presentation:** My main concern lies with the introduction and emphasis on the continuous-time model. While motivated by the need to manage latency, this formulation significantly complicates the model description, the game-tree formalization, and the optimization algorithm. The experimental results do not seem to justify this added complexity. As shown in Table 2, the simpler "threshold" baseline (which omits continuous-time reasoning) achieves nearly identical performance on the NER task (with even lower latency) and slightly better performance on the face classification task.
*   **Reporting of Results:** There are several issues with how the experimental results are reported that obscure a clear comparison of the methods:
    1.  The claim in the abstract to "surpass his master" is slightly misleading, as the 5-vote human baseline still achieves the highest F1 score on the NER task. The claims of outperforming human experts should be stated more precisely.
    2.  Crucial baseline results are missing from the evaluation. The 5-vote human baseline is not reported for the Sentiment and Faces tasks, and the Threshold baseline is missing entirely from the Sentiment task evaluation (e.g., in Table 3 and Figure 4). These are important comparisons and should be included.
    3.  For readability, the best-performing result in each column of the tables should be bolded. For example, in Table 2, this would include the F1 for the 5-vote baseline and the latency for the Threshold baseline on the NER task.

**Suggestions for Improvement**

The paper's impact could be greatly improved with some revisions. I would strongly suggest reframing the narrative. The core contribution—a principled, discrete-time framework for balancing crowd queries and model predictions—is already very significant and much easier to understand. The paper would be stronger if it first fully developed this model (akin to the "threshold" baseline) and then presented the continuous-time formulation as a more advanced extension. In the discussion, the authors should also more explicitly acknowledge the strength of the "threshold" baseline, as it represents a practical and powerful alternative to the full LENSE model.

**Detailed Comments and Questions**
*   In the experiments, it is not specified how the system decides when to stop querying and return an answer. Was a fixed `t_deadline` used, or was the decision based on a model-confidence threshold similar to the baseline?
*   In the description of the Threshold baseline (lines 246-252), the update rule `(1 − pθ(yi|x)) × 0.3^m ≥ 0.98` seems incorrect. As uncertainty decreases with more queries (m increases), the left-hand side should fall *below* a small uncertainty threshold, not remain above a large confidence one. Perhaps the condition should be `≤ (1 - 0.98)`?

**Conclusion**

This paper presents an original, significant, and technically sophisticated framework for a challenging and practical problem. The results are strong, and I recommend that the paper be accepted. However, its impact could be significantly enhanced by addressing the clarity issues, primarily by reframing the contribution to better highlight the powerful and more accessible discrete-time version of the model and by providing a more complete reporting of the experimental results.
