- Introduces an on-the-job learning framework that combines crowdsourced labels with an incrementally trained model to reduce human annotation over time.
- Formalizes the task as a stochastic game between system and crowd using Bayesian decision theory and a CRF prediction model.
- The system dynamically queries crowds for uncertain tokens, updates its model with responses, and decides when to predict using a utility function balancing accuracy, cost, and latency.
- Exact optimal policy is intractable due to continuous-time modeling; authors approximate it with Monte Carlo Tree Search and progressive widening.
- Empirical evaluation on NER, sentiment, and image classification shows large cost reductions (over an order of magnitude for NER) and F1 improvements (8% over single-human baselines, 28% over online learning).
- Paper is highly original and significant, sitting at the intersection of active learning, crowdsourcing, and online learning.
- Technical quality is strong with thorough experiments, appropriate baselines, and solid theoretical grounding.
- Clarity suffers from an unnecessarily complex continuous-time treatment with limited justification and marginal empirical benefit.
- Recommendation to present the core method without continuous time first and treat the time-dependent extension as secondary.
- Missing 5-vote crowdsourcing baseline for Face-ID and Sentiment tasks in Tables 2–3 and Figure 4.
- Threshold baseline is absent from the sentiment experiment results despite being a key comparison.
- The claim of “beats human performance” is unqualified; the 5-vote case is the strongest baseline and should be highlighted.
- Possible error in the threshold baseline inequality direction (should be ≤ 0.12 rather than ≥ 0.88).
- t_deadline parameter is referenced but not clearly defined in the experimental setup.
- Overall strengths outweigh clarity issues; recommend acceptance pending minor revisions to address clarity and presentation problems.
