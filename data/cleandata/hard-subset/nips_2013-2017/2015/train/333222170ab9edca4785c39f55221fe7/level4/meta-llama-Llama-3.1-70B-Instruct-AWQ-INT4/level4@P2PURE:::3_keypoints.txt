- Proposes a hybrid system that uses heavy crowdsourcing initially and incrementally trains a model to reduce crowd involvement over time.
- Formulates the problem as a stochastic game between the system and the crowd using a CRF prediction model.
- System issues token queries to the crowd and maximizes expected utility under time and response-time constraints.
- Introduces continuous-time dynamics, making optimization intractable and requiring an approximation with Monte Carlo Tree Search and TD learning.
- Experiments on NER, sentiment, and face identification compare the method (LENSE) to four baselines including a "threshold" baseline and 5-vote crowdsourcing.
- Results: LENSE outperforms all baselines except the 5-vote crowdsourced version; the threshold baseline performs nearly as well and achieves lower latency on NER.
- Shows the model needs fewer crowdsourced labels over time and gets high accuracy from the first example by leveraging crowd expertise.
- Paper is original, significant, and presents a principled approach worthy of inclusion despite issues.
- Major concern: clarity problems, especially the introduction of continuous time from Section 3, which adds complexity but little experimental benefit.
- The threshold baseline may be sufficient for many tasks and is simpler to understand and implement; continuous time could be presented as an extension.
- The authors' explanation for why continuous time helps in face ID (single-label interactions) is unconvincing given marginal NER gains despite label interactions.
- Reporting issues: incorrect claim of "beats human performance" for NER 5-vote case; 5-vote results missing for Face-ID and Sentiment in multiple tables/figures; threshold latency for NER should be bolded; threshold missing from sentiment experiment tables/figures.
- Minor method comments: t_deadline description and used value are unclear; distribution in Eq.(2) should include variable s; rename q in F(q) for clarity; correct threshold expression to (1 - p_theta(yi|x))*0.3^m <= (1-0.88).
- Recommendation: emphasize the strong performance of the threshold method in the discussion to improve clarity, impact, and adoptability.
