- Paper presents a Bayesian decision-theoretic framework for "on-the-job learning" that transitions from crowd-sourcing to autonomous prediction as confidence grows.  
- Formulation uses a stochastic game and Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) to compute policies balancing accuracy, latency, and cost.  
- Evaluated on NER, sentiment, and image classification, showing large cost reductions versus full human annotation and gains over online-learning baselines.  
- Positions work relative to prior literature (online learning, active learning, crowdsourcing, structured prediction) and builds on prior related ideas.  
- Strength: novel, principled formulation providing a strong theoretical foundation for on-the-job learning.  
- Strength: practical empirical benefits, notably significant cost reduction on NER and improvements against baselines.  
- Strength: clearly motivated problem setting with real-world relevance (e.g., disaster relief, real-time monitoring).  
- Strength: authors provide an open-source implementation (LENSE) enhancing reproducibility.  
- Weakness: mathematical notation needs clarification—Equation (2) seems incomplete and potentially misleading.  
- Weakness: comparisons rely on limited baselines; more thorough comparison to state-of-the-art models trained on available data is needed.  
- Weakness: sensitivity to hyperparameters (cost/latency trade-offs) is not sufficiently analyzed.  
- Weakness: lacks deeper error analysis of system and crowd mistakes to elucidate limitations.  
- Specific question: Equation (2) appears to omit conditioning on state s and the disappearance of label y needs explanation.  
- Specific question: Algorithm 1 references N(s) but does not define it—definition is necessary for understanding MCTS.  
- Specific question: justification required for chosen threshold baseline values (0.3 and 0.98).  
- Specific question: clarify whether online baselines are the best possible learners given labeled data and justify baseline choices or provide more detailed error comparisons.  
- Argument for acceptance: theoretically sound, novel approach with promising empirical results and practical impact potential.  
- Argument for acceptance: open-source release supports reproducibility and follow-up work.  
- Argument for rejection: current clarity issues in mathematical presentation and insufficient baseline/evaluation depth weaken the manuscript.  
- Overall recommendation: accept with minor revisions contingent on clarifying notation, expanding baseline justification/comparisons, and adding hyperparameter and error analyses.
