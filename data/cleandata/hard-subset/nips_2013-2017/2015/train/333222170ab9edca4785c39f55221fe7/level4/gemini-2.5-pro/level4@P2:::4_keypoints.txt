- Introduces "on-the-job learning": a system that starts with zero training data, queries humans per example, updates online, and reduces crowd reliance over time.  
- Formalizes the problem as a stochastic game balancing accuracy, latency, and cost, using Bayesian decision theory.  
- Proposes an MCTS-based approximation with progressive widening to make the intractable optimal policy practical.  
- Strength: Novel and significant problem formulation addressing immediate-deployment needs and practical trade-offs.  
- Strength: Strong empirical results on NER showing large cost reductions and improved F1 while decreasing human supervision over time.  
- Strength: Code, data, and experiments are open-sourced, supporting reproducibility.  
- Weakness: Unclear application to non-structured tasks (e.g., face classification); unclear how single-instance queries provide part-level benefit.  
- Weakness: Threshold baseline appears ad-hoc (parameters 0.3 and 0.98) with no justification or sensitivity analysis.  
- Weakness: Lacks a concrete comparison to adapted active learning baselines (e.g., uncertainty sampling) to isolate gains from the game-theoretic approach.  
- Accept argument: Principled framework, strong NER results, and reproducibility make it a valuable contribution.  
- Reject argument: Poor clarity on generality to standard classification and insufficient baseline comparisons weaken the empirical case.  
- Overall: Promising and thought-provoking work that requires clarifications and stronger baseline experiments to be fully convincing.
