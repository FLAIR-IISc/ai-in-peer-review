This paper introduces a hybrid system that combines crowdsourced labels with an incrementally trained online model to solve prediction problems. The core idea is to rely heavily on human input during the initial "ramp-up" phase and gradually shift towards the automated model as it learns from the acquired labels and becomes more confident.

The authors elegantly frame this challenge as a stochastic game between the system and the crowd, built upon a Conditional Random Field (CRF) prediction model. In this game, the system issues queries (q) for data tokens (x) to elicit responses (r) from the crowd, aiming to select actions that maximize its expected utility, U(q,r). A key contribution is the incorporation of continuous time: queries are issued at specific times (s) and receive responses at later times (t), with the system optimizing its strategy to meet a final deadline (t_deadline).

Since optimizing this expected utility in continuous time is intractable, the authors propose an approximation based on Monte Carlo Tree Search and Temporal Difference (TD) learning. The approach is evaluated on three tasks—named entity recognition (NER), sentiment analysis, and face identification—against several baselines: majority vote from n human judges (n=1,3,5), standard online learning with true labels, and a "Threshold" baseline (the authors' model without the continuous-time component).

Experimental results show that the full model (LENSE) outperforms most baselines in terms of precision, recall, and F1-score, though it does not surpass the 5-voter human consensus. The "Threshold" baseline performs nearly as well and, in the case of NER, achieves this with lower latency. The authors successfully demonstrate that their system reduces its reliance on crowdsourced labels over time (Figure 3) and achieves high accuracy from the very first example by leveraging crowd intelligence when model uncertainty is high (Figure 4).

**Strengths and Weaknesses**

This is a highly original and significant paper that proposes a principled and comprehensive framework for a problem often addressed with heuristics. The idea of modeling the interaction between an online learner and a crowd as a stochastic game is novel and powerful. The results are impressive when compared against strong baselines, and the work is likely to inspire considerable discussion and follow-up research. For these reasons, I believe the paper is a strong candidate for inclusion in the conference.

However, the paper's potential impact is diminished by significant clarity issues, which could hinder its adoption. Fortunately, most of these issues are correctable. The central problem lies in the introduction of the continuous-time model, which substantially complicates the formulation and optimization procedure for what appears to be a minimal performance benefit in the experiments.

**Detailed Comments**

The primary source of confusion is the introduction of continuous time in Section 3. While the motivation—operating under a time and cost budget—is sound, the experiments do not convincingly demonstrate its advantage over the simpler "Threshold" baseline.
*   In Table 2, the "Threshold" baseline achieves significantly lower latency on the NER task with only a marginal drop in performance. On the Face ID task, it actually achieves a slightly higher F1-score.
*   The authors argue (l. 314) that the baseline performs well on the Face ID task because it is a single-label prediction problem without significant label interactions. However, the NER task, which is rich with such dependencies, only seems to benefit marginally from the more complex continuous-time model.

Given that the "Threshold" baseline is itself a principled and high-performing hybrid learning system, it represents a significant contribution. The paper would have a greater impact if this core method were developed first, making it easier for readers to understand and implement. The continuous-time model could then be presented as an extension. In the results discussion, the authors should more clearly acknowledge the strength of the "Threshold" method, even in tasks with sequential dependencies. While this is a suggestion, reframing the paper this way would significantly improve its clarity and accessibility.

**Issues with Experimental Reporting**

There are several issues with how the results are presented:
1.  **Unsupported Claims:** The introduction states the model "beats human performance," but this is not true for the 5-vote case on the NER task.
2.  **Missing Results:** Key baseline results are missing. The 5-vote case is absent for the Face ID and Sentiment tasks in Tables 2 and 3 and Figure 4. Similarly, the "Threshold" baseline is entirely missing from the sentiment experiment (Table 3 and Figure 4). These baselines are crucial for a complete comparison and should be included.
3.  **Formatting:** Standard formatting conventions are not followed. In Table 2, the best-performing results for each metric should be bolded. This includes the F1-scores for the 5-vote case and the latency for the Threshold (NER) and LENSE (Face ID) models.

**Minor Methodological Questions**
*   It is unclear what value of `t_deadline` was used in the experiments or what criteria determined when the system produced an answer. Was it a fixed deadline or an entropy-based threshold?
*   In Equation (2), the distribution `p(y,r,t|x,q)` appears to be missing the conditioning variable `s` (the query time), which should be `p(y,r,t|x,q,s)`.
*   In Equation (5), the use of `q` in the function `F(q)` is confusing, as `q` is already used to denote a query. Please use a different letter for the nominal distribution function.
*   In the description of the Threshold baseline (l.246-252), the inequality seems to be reversed. To reduce uncertainty below a threshold, the expression should likely be `(1 - p_theta(yi|x))*0.3^m <= (1-0.88)`, not `>= 0.88`.

**Conclusion**

This paper presents a novel and sophisticated hybrid approach for combining crowdsourcing and online learning. The formulation as a stochastic game is a significant contribution, and the results are compelling. Despite major issues with clarity, primarily stemming from the complex continuous-time formulation that yields little empirical benefit, the paper's originality and significance warrant its acceptance. I strongly encourage the authors to address the clarity and reporting issues to maximize the paper's impact.
