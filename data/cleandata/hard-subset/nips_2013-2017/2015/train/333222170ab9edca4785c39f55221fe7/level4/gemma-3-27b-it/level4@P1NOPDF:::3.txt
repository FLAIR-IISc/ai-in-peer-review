This paper presents a hybrid approach for leveraging both crowdsourced labels and an incrementally (online) trained model to address prediction problems; the core idea is to heavily rely on the crowd during the initial phase, learn from the acquired labels, and then gradually reduce reliance on the crowd as the model gains confidence. This is achieved through a sophisticated formulation of the problem as a stochastic game based on a CRF prediction model, where both the system and the crowd are players. The system can issue one or more queries *q* for tokens *x* (with true label *y*), eliciting responses *r*, with a utility *U(q,r)* associated with each outcome. The system aims to select actions that maximize expected utility. Furthermore, queries are not issued all at once, but at times *s* (with response times *t*); utility is maximized with respect to a *t_deadline* – the time by which an answer must be computed – which determines the number of queries sent and their rate.

Computing this expected utility requires using the simulation dynamics model *P(y,r,t|x,q,s)* (as in equation 4) to calculate the utilities. Once the utility values are known, the optimal action can be chosen; however, the introduction of continuous time renders this optimization intractable, necessitating an approximation based on Monte Carlo Tree Search and TD learning (Algorithm 1). Experiments were conducted on a simplified named entity recognition (NER) task, a sentiment recognition task, and a face identification task, comparing four methods: the majority vote of *n* human judges (1, 3, 5), online learning (using true labels), a “threshold” baseline (the authors’ model without continuous time, which sends *m* queries at each step until the model’s prediction uncertainty falls below a threshold), and the authors’ full model (LENSE). In terms of precision/recall/F1, LENSE outperforms all methods except the 5-vote crowdsourced version, though the “threshold” baseline performs nearly as well (and with lower latency for NER; Table 2). The authors also demonstrate that the model requires progressively fewer crowdsourced labels over time with a sufficiently strong training model (Figure 3), and that, compared to online learning, accuracy is high from the first example (Figure 4), because the system leverages crowd expertise while model uncertainty remains high.

This is a very interesting paper that I expect will stimulate discussion, further research, and practical adoption. While various heuristic approaches exist for learning from the crowd while training a system, this is the first complete and principled proposal I have encountered. The results against reasonable baselines are impressive, and I believe the work is highly original and significant, meriting inclusion in the conference. However, the paper suffers from clarity issues that could diminish its potential impact. These issues are largely correctable, even for the camera-ready version, with sufficient effort and potential adjustments to the tone, especially regarding the comparison between the “threshold” baseline and the full continuous-time version.

The primary clarity issue arises from the introduction of continuous time beginning in Section 3. While the motivation – the need to operate under both time and cost budgets – is reasonable, experimentally it appears to offer minimal benefit while substantially increasing the complexity of the formulation, discussion, and optimization procedure. In fact, Table 2 suggests the “threshold” baseline achieves significantly lower latency on the NER task with roughly equivalent performance (slightly worse), and even outperforms LENSE on the face ID task (though again, performance is roughly equivalent). The authors argue (line 314) that the baseline’s better performance on the latter task is due to its single-label prediction nature and lack of label interaction; however, the NER task – which *does* exhibit significant label interaction – only shows a marginal benefit from continuous time. 

For many practical tasks, the “threshold” baseline will likely suffice and represents a significant improvement as a principled hybrid learning system that outperforms active and online learning. The paper would likely have greater impact if the authors emphasized this point, potentially by developing the core method fully (without continuous time, making it easier to understand and implement) and presenting the addition of continuous time as an extension. Similarly, when discussing the results, they could highlight the strong performance of the “threshold” method, even in tasks with sequence dependencies between labels. This is a suggestion, and the paper is already strong enough for inclusion, but I believe it would improve the paper and facilitate broader understanding and adoption of the technique.

There are also several minor issues with the results and their reporting. First, the introduction states the model "beats human performance," which is inaccurate given the 5-vote case for NER. The 5-vote case is also missing from the Face-ID and Sentiment tasks in multiple tables and figures (the right half of Tables 2, Table 3, and Figure 4) – it should be included. The results for the 5-vote case in Table 2 should be bolded, as they represent the best performance. The latency value for Threshold in NER should also be bolded for the same reason, and the LENSE value for Face ID. Critically, the “Threshold” baseline is missing from the sentiment experiment, and as such does not appear in Table 3 or Figure 4; it should be included.

A few minor comments on the method description:
-While *t_deadline* is discussed (lines 183-187), it is unclear what *t_deadline* was used in the experiments, and what criteria determined when an answer was ready to be reported—was it an entropic threshold as with the baseline, or was it reaching a specific *t_deadline*?
-In equation (2), the distribution should likely include the variable *s* (i.e., *p(y,r,t|x,q,s)*).
-In equation 5, the use of *q* in *F(q)* is confusing; I initially assumed it referred to a query, but it represents a nominal distribution function. Please choose a different letter.
-In the description of the Threshold baseline (lines 246-252), the expression should likely be *(1 - pθ(yi|x))*0.3^m <= (1-0.88)* (note the right-hand side) as opposed to >= 0.88, as increasing *m* reduces uncertainty, not increases it.

This paper presents a hybrid approach for leveraging both crowdsourced labels and an incrementally (online) trained model to address prediction problems, elegantly cast as a stochastic game that models many aspects of the data, true labels, and crowdworker responses, including the time at which queries are sent out and answers received. Impressive results are shown with respect to multiple human labelers, online learning with oracle labels, and a “threshold” baseline using the authors’ model but removing the dependence on continuous time that performs on par. The method is original, novel, and interesting, and as such the paper should be accepted, but there are some issues with clarity, particularly with respect to the introduction of continuous time, which greatly complicates the discussion and algorithmic mechanics yet seems to yield minimal benefits in performance.
