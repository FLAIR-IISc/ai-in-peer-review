This paper presents a compelling framework for on-the-job learning, formalizing and extending prior work [6] by modeling the interaction between a learning system and a crowd as a stochastic game solved via Bayesian decision theory and Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS). The core idea – starting with heavy reliance on crowdsourcing and gradually transitioning to a self-sufficient learned model – is a practical and appealing approach, especially in scenarios where labeled data is scarce or expensive to obtain. The paper relates well to existing work in online learning, active learning, and crowdsourcing, clearly distinguishing itself by its focus on continuous-time decision making and simultaneous learning and prediction.

**Quality:** The paper demonstrates a strong technical foundation. The formulation as a stochastic game is well-motivated, and the use of MCTS for approximating the optimal policy appears reasonable given the intractability of finding the exact solution. The theoretical analysis, while not extensive, provides a principled way to balance latency, cost, and accuracy. The experimental evaluation on three diverse tasks (NER, sentiment classification, and image classification) strengthens the claim of general applicability. However, some details require clarification (see below). The authors acknowledge limitations and present a complete system with an open-source implementation, indicating careful development and honest evaluation.

**Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized, though some sections could benefit from more detailed explanations. The introduction effectively motivates the problem and positions the work within the broader context of machine learning and crowdsourcing. The problem formulation and model description are reasonably clear, but Equation (1) and the surrounding explanation could be more intuitive. Algorithm 1, while providing a high-level overview of the MCTS approach, lacks sufficient detail for full reproducibility. More specifics on the progressive widening strategy would be helpful.

**Originality:** The combination of Bayesian decision theory, MCTS, and on-the-job learning represents a novel approach. While the individual components are not new, their integration to address the specific challenges of real-time learning from a crowd is a significant contribution. The authors clearly differentiate their work from related areas like active learning and online learning by highlighting the simultaneous learning and prediction aspect and the ability to maintain high accuracy from the outset. The paper adequately references relevant work, though a more thorough comparison to Legion:AR [6] beyond a single sentence would be beneficial.

**Significance:** The potential impact of this work is considerable. The ability to deploy accurate systems with minimal initial training data is crucial in many real-world applications, particularly those involving rapidly changing data streams or emerging needs (e.g., disaster relief, social media monitoring). The reported cost reductions and performance improvements on the experimental tasks demonstrate the practical value of the proposed approach. The open-source implementation (LENSE) further enhances the significance by making the technology accessible to a wider audience. 

**Strengths:**
*   Principled framework based on Bayesian decision theory.
*   Effective use of MCTS to handle the complexity of the problem.
*   Demonstrated cost reductions and performance improvements on multiple tasks.
*   Open-source implementation.
*   Clear motivation and positioning within the related work.

**Weaknesses:**
*   Equation (1) and the surrounding explanation require clarification.
*   Algorithm 1 lacks sufficient detail for reproducibility.
*   The comparison to online learning baselines could be strengthened with more detailed error analysis.
*   The threshold baseline comparison is somewhat weak, and the parameter selection process (0.3 and 0.88) is unclear.
*   The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion of the limitations of the approach and potential avenues for future research.




**Arguments for acceptance:**
*   The paper addresses an important and practical problem.
*   The proposed approach is technically sound and well-motivated.
*   The experimental results are promising and demonstrate the potential of the framework.
*   The open-source implementation promotes reproducibility and wider adoption.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
*   Some aspects of the presentation lack sufficient detail.
*   The empirical evaluation, while positive, could be more comprehensive.
*   The novelty, while present, could be further emphasized through a more thorough comparison to closely related work.




**Specific questions/requests for the authors:**

1.  Equation (2) appears to be missing a condition on *s* (state).  Please clarify. Also, the term *y* within *p<sub>R</sub>(r<sub>i</sub> | y, q<sub>i</sub>)* seems to vanish as *y* is the true label, and not a predicted label.
2.  Please define *N(s)* in Algorithm 1.
3.  Please explain the rationale behind the values 0.3 and 0.88 used in the threshold baseline. How were these parameters selected?
4.  Are the "online" systems used as baselines the state-of-the-art machine learning systems for these tasks? If not, the gains achieved by LENSE may be largely attributed to the quality of the baseline. Provide a brief error analysis to understand the types of errors made by LENSE and the online baselines.
5.  Could the authors elaborate on the trade-offs between computational cost of the MCTS search and the potential performance gains?



Overall, this is a strong paper with a significant contribution to the field of on-the-job learning. Addressing the minor weaknesses outlined above would further enhance the clarity and impact of the work. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
