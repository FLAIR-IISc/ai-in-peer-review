The authors introduce “on the job learning,” a scenario where annotators are queried for information between receiving an example and producing a prediction. While the algorithm’s operation is well-defined for structured prediction tasks involving queries about sequence elements, its application to face recognition is unclear – specifically, what constitutes the queried instance in this context? In the non-structured setting, information does not propagate between examples, which casts doubt on the utility function’s validity. 

The threshold baseline employs two parameters that appear arbitrarily chosen. The origin of these values and the model’s sensitivity to them should be discussed. An additional baseline utilizing uncertainty sampling, similar to approaches in active learning, would be a valuable comparison.

Finally, the authors emphasize the distinction between this setting and active online learning; however, active learning could be readily adapted to this domain by treating the sequence as the active set. For instance, the model could be updated upon receiving responses and then produce a final prediction. Though this adaptation might not fully capture the task’s temporal dynamics, it allows leveraging the substantial body of work on query set selection within active learning. This framework appears novel, but lacks clarity in certain details and seems relatively limited in its applicability.
