This paper’s primary contribution lies in a refined theoretical framework for an on-the-job learning approach, building upon the work in [22]. The proposed learner begins by relying on crowdsourced responses, progressively shifting towards self-prediction and reducing reliance on crowd queries. A Monte Carlo Tree Search is employed to strategically determine when to solicit input from the crowd for each new instance.

The paper is generally well-written, though certain details require further elaboration. While the empirical results demonstrate a reasonable approach when compared to a threshold-based heuristic, the gains observed are not substantial. Nevertheless, the methodology appears promising and warrants continued exploration.

The authors should address the following points for clarification:

1. Equation (2) requires revision; the left-hand side should be conditioned on the state *s*. Additionally, the role and disappearance of the variable *y* within the probability *p{R}(ri | y, q_i)* are unclear.
2. Please define *N(s)* as it is used in Algorithm 1.
3. The origin of the values 0.3 and 0.88 used in the threshold baseline should be explained.
4. Given my unfamiliarity with the specific tasks, it is important to establish the performance level of the “online” systems relative to state-of-the-art machine learning models for these tasks. If these systems are not the best available, the observed gains from human annotation need to be contextualized. Conversely, if they *are* the best, any improvements achieved through this approach are likely solely attributable to the incorporation of human feedback. Providing examples of such cases, alongside a detailed error analysis, would significantly strengthen the paper.

In summary, this work provides a valuable theoretical treatment of an on-the-job learner that dynamically balances crowdsourcing and learned prediction, transitioning from a fully crowdsourced system to one that selectively queries the crowd based on prediction confidence. The empirical findings are somewhat inconclusive, but the work has the potential to stimulate further research in this area.
