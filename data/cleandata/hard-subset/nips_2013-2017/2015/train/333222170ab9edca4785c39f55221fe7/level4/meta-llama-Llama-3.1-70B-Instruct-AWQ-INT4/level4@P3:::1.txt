This paper presents a theoretical formulation of an "on the job learner" that starts as a pure crowdsourcing system and gradually transitions to a learned model that only queries the crowd on low confidence inputs. The main contribution of this paper is to provide a more theoretically sound formulation of an on-the-job learner proposed in previous work. The learner starts by querying the crowd to produce its output, but gradually starts making its own predictions and querying the crowd infrequently.

The presentation is mostly good, although lacking in important details. The empirical results vis a vis a threshold-based heuristic are somewhat underwhelming. However, the approach seems reasonable and merits further investigation.

The paper relates to previous work at NIPS and elsewhere in the areas of online learning, active learning, and crowdsourcing. The authors draw on ideas from these areas to formulate their on-the-job learning framework. The paper is well-positioned in the context of NIPS, as it addresses the challenge of learning from noisy and expensive data.

In terms of quality, the paper is technically sound, and the authors provide a clear and well-motivated formulation of their problem. The use of Bayesian decision theory and Monte Carlo Tree Search is well-justified, and the authors provide a thorough explanation of their approach. However, there are some minor issues with the presentation, such as the lack of clarity in Equation (2) and the omission of important details in Algorithm 1.

The paper's clarity is generally good, but there are some areas where the presentation could be improved. For example, the authors could provide more intuition about the behavior of their algorithm and how it relates to the threshold-based heuristic. Additionally, the authors could provide more details about the tasks used in their experiments and how they relate to the state-of-the-art in machine learning.

In terms of originality, the paper presents a novel formulation of the on-the-job learning problem and a new algorithm for solving it. While the authors draw on previous work in online learning and crowdsourcing, their approach is distinct and well-motivated. The use of Bayesian decision theory and Monte Carlo Tree Search is a key contribution of the paper.

The significance of the paper is that it addresses a challenging problem in machine learning, namely, how to learn from noisy and expensive data. The authors' approach has the potential to be widely applicable in a variety of domains, including natural language processing, computer vision, and robotics. The paper's results, while somewhat mixed, suggest that the approach is promising and merits further investigation.

Arguments for acceptance:

* The paper presents a novel and well-motivated formulation of the on-the-job learning problem.
* The authors provide a clear and thorough explanation of their approach.
* The paper addresses a challenging problem in machine learning and has the potential to be widely applicable.

Arguments against acceptance:

* The empirical results are somewhat underwhelming, particularly in comparison to the threshold-based heuristic.
* The paper lacks some important details, such as the derivation of Equation (2) and the explanation of the threshold baseline.
* The authors could provide more intuition about the behavior of their algorithm and how it relates to the state-of-the-art in machine learning.

To address the reviewer's questions:

1. Equation (2) should be conditioned on s, and the y in p{R}(ri | y, q_i) should be clarified. The authors should provide a clear explanation of how they derived this equation and how it relates to the rest of their approach.
2. N(s) in Algorithm 1 should be clearly defined and explained. The authors should provide a detailed description of how this variable is used in their algorithm.
3. The authors should provide a clear explanation of how they derived the 0.3 and 0.88 in the threshold baseline. This should include a detailed description of the heuristic and how it relates to their approach.
4. The authors should provide more information about the tasks used in their experiments and how they relate to the state-of-the-art in machine learning. This should include a detailed description of the tasks, the datasets used, and the performance of the state-of-the-art algorithms on these tasks.
