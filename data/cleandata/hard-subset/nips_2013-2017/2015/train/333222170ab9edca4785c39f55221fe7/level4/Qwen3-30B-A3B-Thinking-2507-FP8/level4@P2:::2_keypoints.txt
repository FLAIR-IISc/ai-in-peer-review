- Introduces "on-the-job learning," a framework for deploying high-accuracy systems with zero initial training examples.  
- Models the problem as a stochastic game using Bayesian decision theory to trade off latency, cost, and accuracy.  
- Uses Monte Carlo Tree Search with progressive widening as an approximate solution for continuous-time decision making.  
- Evaluated on NER, sentiment, and image classification, demonstrating cost reductions versus full human annotation and accuracy gains versus crowd/machine baselines.  
- Provides robust experimental comparisons against multiple baselines and supplies open-source code and datasets for reproducibility.  
- Delivers substantial NER results: over an order-of-magnitude cost reduction and +8% F1 vs single-human, +28% vs online learning.  
- Performance is weaker on Sentiment and Faces tasks; authors attribute this to dataset class distributions (dominant classes aiding NER).  
- Clarity issue: the abstract example (Line 154) is too abstract and needs a concrete walkthrough with queries, responses, and timing.  
- Clarity issue: inconsistent naming of the threshold baseline ("entropic" vs "threshold") causes reader confusion.  
- Clarity/organization issue: related work placement disrupts flow (Section 5 placement problematic).  
- Originality: formalizes on-the-job learning as a stochastic game and uniquely integrates latency and cost into decision-making, distinct from prior work like Legion:AR.  
- Significance: addresses an important real-world deployment problem, with practical applications and an open-source implementation (LENS) to aid adoption.  
- Strengths: well-motivated problem, sound theoretical grounding, practical benefits, thorough evaluation, and reproducibility.  
- Weaknesses: presentation and organization need improvement and the paper should better explain weaker performance on two tasks.  
- Recommendation: accept with minor revisions to improve clarity and fix organizational issues.
