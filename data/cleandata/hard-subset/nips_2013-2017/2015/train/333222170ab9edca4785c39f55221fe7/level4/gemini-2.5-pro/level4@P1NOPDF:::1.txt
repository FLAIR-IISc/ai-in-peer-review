This paper presents a more theoretically grounded formulation of the "on the job learner" model proposed in [22]. The learner begins by querying the crowd to generate its output, but gradually learns to make its own predictions, querying the crowd only infrequently. The decision of whether to query the crowd for any given input is modeled as a sequential decision problem, which the authors solve using a Monte Carlo Tree Search.

The presentation is generally clear, although it lacks some important details. The empirical results are somewhat underwhelming when compared to a simple threshold-based heuristic. Nevertheless, the proposed approach is reasonable and certainly merits further investigation.

Please address the following points:

1.  Equation (2) is unclear. It appears the left-hand side should also be conditioned on *s*. Additionally, please clarify how the *y* term vanishes from p{R}(ri | y, q_i) during the derivation.
2.  What does N(s) represent in Algorithm 1?
3.  How were the values of 0.3 and 0.88 selected for the threshold baseline?
4.  Regarding the experiments, do the "online" systems used for comparison represent the state-of-the-art machine learning models for these tasks? If not, please justify their selection. If they are, then any performance gain from your model is presumably the result of the additional human annotation. In this case, an error analysis would be very beneficial to highlight the specific instances where human input adds the most value.

In summary, this paper introduces a principled theoretical framework for an "on the job learner" that transitions from a crowdsourcing system to a semi-automated one. While the empirical results could be stronger, the paper opens a valuable line of inquiry and a promising direction for future work.
