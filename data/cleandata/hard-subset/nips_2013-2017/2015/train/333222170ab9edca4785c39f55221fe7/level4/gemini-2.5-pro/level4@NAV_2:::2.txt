This paper introduces "on-the-job learning," a framework for deploying a high-accuracy prediction system from scratch, without any initial labeled data. The core idea is that as new inputs arrive, the system can use real-time crowdsourcing to resolve uncertainty where needed before making a prediction. The model is updated online with the collected labels, allowing it to become more self-sufficient over time, thereby reducing latency and cost. The authors formalize this problem as a stochastic game and employ Bayesian decision theory to navigate the trade-offs between accuracy, latency, and monetary cost. Since computing the optimal policy is intractable, the paper proposes an approximation using Monte Carlo Tree Search combined with progressive widening.

This work connects ideas from online learning, active learning, and crowdsourcing. It differs from standard online and active learning by actively seeking information *prior* to making a prediction on a given instance, with the goal of maintaining high accuracy from the outset, rather than only asymptotically. While handling noisy crowd responses has been studied extensively, the novelty here lies in the principled integration of latency and cost into the decision-making process for querying, which is a significant and practical contribution.

**Strengths:**

*   **Significance and Originality:** The paper tackles a compelling and highly practical problem. The "on-the-job learning" setting, which combines real-time querying with online model improvement, is a novel contribution that addresses a clear gap between purely ML-driven and purely crowd-driven systems. The formalization as a stochastic game that explicitly models latency is particularly innovative.
*   **Quality of the Technical Approach:** The formulation based on Bayesian decision theory is principled and provides an elegant framework for balancing the system's competing objectives. The use of Monte Carlo Tree Search is a well-justified and appropriate choice for approximating the intractable optimal policy.
*   **Thorough Empirical Evaluation:** The experiments are comprehensive, covering three distinct tasks (named-entity recognition, sentiment classification, and image classification). The results are strong, demonstrating an order of magnitude cost reduction on the NER task and significant improvements over both online learning and static human-voting baselines. The inclusion and analysis of the human n-vote baselines are particularly insightful; for instance, the fact that LENSE outperforms a 3-vote baseline but not a 5-vote provides a valuable benchmark for the efficiency of intelligent querying versus brute-force consensus.
*   **Reproducibility:** The authors are to be commended for making their code, data, and experiments publicly available, which greatly enhances the value and reproducibility of their work.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Clarity and Exposition:** The primary weakness of the paper lies in its clarity. While the ideas are strong, the explanations are sometimes opaque, which may hinder a full appreciation of the method.
    *   The "Behavior" section (Sec. 3), intended to provide intuition, remains rather abstract. A concrete, step-by-step example with specific queries, simulated crowd responses (e.g., "PERSON"), and example timings (e.g., t=1.5s) would make the system's decision-making process much more tangible and easier to follow.
    *   The organization of the paper could be improved. Placing the Related Work section after the Experiments is unconventional and delays the contextualization of the work within the existing literature. Moving it earlier would improve the paper's flow.
    *   There are minor inconsistencies that detract from readability. For example, the naming of the "Threshold baseline" should be kept consistent throughout the text and tables to avoid confusion. Minor proofreading for issues like the broken figure reference ("Figure ??") mentioned in the draft is also recommended.

In summary, this is a strong paper presenting a novel framework for a significant real-world problem. The technical approach is sound and the experimental results are convincing. The main weakness is in the clarity of the presentation, which could be substantially improved with more concrete examples and better organization.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Addresses a novel, important, and practical problem.
*   Presents a principled and well-motivated technical solution based on Bayesian decision theory.
*   Demonstrates strong empirical results across multiple diverse tasks.
*   The contribution is a valuable synthesis of ideas from online learning, active learning, and crowdsourcing.
*   The work is reproducible thanks to the public release of code and data.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   The clarity of the paper is a significant issue, with key explanations and examples being difficult to follow.
*   The paper's structure could be improved to better situate the work and guide the reader.
