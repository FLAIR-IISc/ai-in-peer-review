The paper’s primary contribution is a more principled, decision-theoretic formulation of an on-the-job learner previously proposed in [22]. The system initially relies on real-time crowd responses to produce outputs, then increasingly makes its own predictions, querying the crowd only for low-confidence components. The decision of whether and what to query is framed as a sequential decision problem and approximately solved using Monte Carlo Tree Search.

The writing is generally clear, but several important details are missing. The empirical improvements over a simple threshold-based heuristic are not particularly strong. Nevertheless, the approach is sensible and worth further exploration.

2. In Algorithm 1, what does N(s) denote?

4. I am not fully familiar with the exact tasks here—are the “online” systems the strongest machine-learned baselines for these tasks? If not, why not? If so, then any gains would be attributable to human annotation; can you provide examples of such cases? Some error analysis would be useful.

Overall, the paper provides a theoretical framing of an “on-the-job learner” that begins as pure crowdsourcing and gradually shifts toward a learned model that queries the crowd only when confidence is low. The empirical results are mixed, but the work should prompt useful discussion in this direction.
