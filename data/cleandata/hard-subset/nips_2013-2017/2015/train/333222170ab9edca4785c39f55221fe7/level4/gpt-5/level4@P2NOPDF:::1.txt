This paper’s main contribution is a more principled, theoretically grounded formulation of the “on-the-job learner” introduced in [22]. The system begins by relying on crowd annotations to produce outputs and, over time, transitions to making its own predictions, querying the crowd only when necessary. The decision of whether to query the crowd for a given input is cast as a sequential decision problem and solved using Monte Carlo Tree Search.

Overall, the presentation is generally clear but omits several important details that are necessary to fully evaluate the approach. Empirically, the method’s performance relative to a simple threshold-based heuristic is somewhat underwhelming. That said, the framework appears sensible and worth further exploration.

Requests for clarification:
1) Equation (2): As written, the left-hand side appears not to be conditioned on s, which is confusing given the surrounding text; should the LHS also condition on s? Also, in p_R(r_i | y, q_i), how does the dependence on y disappear in subsequent steps? Please provide the missing steps or assumptions that justify this transformation.

2) Algorithm 1: What exactly is N(s)? Is it the visit count for state s in MCTS, a rollout budget, or something else? Please define it explicitly and explain how it is initialized and updated.

3) Threshold baseline: How were the thresholds 0.3 and 0.88 determined? Were they tuned on a validation set, selected by grid search, or chosen heuristically? Please report the selection procedure and whether the baseline was fairly tuned to its best performance under the same constraints (e.g., cost/latency).

4) Choice of “online” systems and error analysis: I am not familiar with the exact tasks used here. Are the “online” systems you compare against the strongest available machine-learned models for these tasks? If not, why were they chosen? If they are near state of the art, then any observed improvements likely stem from human annotation. In that case:
   - Please provide examples where the hybrid system improves over the standalone model, including typical error categories it corrects.
   - Conversely, show cases where it fails to help or introduces errors.
   - A brief error analysis would clarify when crowd queries are most beneficial and whether the MCTS policy is meaningfully better than confidence-thresholding.

In summary, the paper offers a clean theoretical framing of an on-the-job learner that starts as a crowdsourcing system and gradually transitions to a learned model that only queries on low-confidence inputs. The empirical results are mixed relative to a threshold baseline, but the approach is promising. Addressing the above issues—especially the missing derivation details, the definition of N(s), the procedure for choosing baseline thresholds, and a focused error analysis—would substantially strengthen the paper.
