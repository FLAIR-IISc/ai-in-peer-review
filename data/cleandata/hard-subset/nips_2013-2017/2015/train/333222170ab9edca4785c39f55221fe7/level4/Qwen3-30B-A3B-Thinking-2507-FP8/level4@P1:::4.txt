

The authors propose a novel "on-the-job learning" framework where the system queries human annotators during prediction to maintain high accuracy without initial training data. While the approach is clearly articulated for structured prediction tasks (e.g., named-entity recognition), the application to face recognition remains unclear. Specifically, in the unstructured face recognition task, it is unclear how the system would query individual image features or pixels, as the utility function relies on propagating uncertainty across adjacent sequence elements—a mechanism absent in non-structured settings. This ambiguity undermines the claimed generality of the method.

The threshold baseline introduces two parameters (e.g., the uncertainty threshold and query multiplier) without justification. The authors do not explain how these values were derived or demonstrate sensitivity analysis. Given that these parameters directly control query frequency and cost, their arbitrary selection weakens the baseline’s credibility as a meaningful comparison.

A more compelling baseline would include uncertainty sampling (e.g., querying the most uncertain instance in the stream), a standard active learning approach. This would better isolate the contribution of the proposed Bayesian framework versus conventional active learning strategies adapted to the streaming context. The authors rightly distinguish their work from online active learning, but their counterargument could be strengthened by showing how standard active learning would fail to maintain high accuracy *from the first prediction*—a core claim of the paper.

The distinction between on-the-job learning and active online learning is valid but not fully persuasive. Active learning could be adapted to this setting by treating each input as a "batch" of potential queries (e.g., querying the most uncertain image region before prediction), with model updates after each response. While this would lack the temporal dynamics of the proposed framework, it would still leverage established active learning theory for query selection. The authors should explicitly address why such an adaptation fails to achieve the same accuracy-latency-cost trade-offs.

Overall, the framework is conceptually interesting and technically sound for structured tasks, with strong empirical results on NER (88.4% F1, >10× cost reduction over human labeling). However, the unclear application to unstructured tasks, ungrounded baseline parameters, and insufficient comparison to adapted active learning approaches limit the paper’s significance. The method’s applicability appears restricted to structured prediction domains where label correlations exist—a limitation the authors should acknowledge. While the stochastic game formulation and Monte Carlo Tree Search approximation are novel contributions, the narrow scope reduces the potential impact for broader NIPS audiences. The paper would benefit from deeper exploration of the face recognition implementation and a more rigorous baseline comparison.
