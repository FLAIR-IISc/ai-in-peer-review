This paper proposes a novel framework for on-the-job learning, where a high-accuracy system is deployed starting with zero training examples. The authors model the on-the-job setting as a stochastic game based on Bayesian decision theory, allowing them to balance latency, cost, and accuracy objectives in a principled way. The paper is well-motivated, and the problem tackled is compelling.

The technical core of the approach is sound, and the authors provide a clear explanation of their model and algorithm. The use of Monte Carlo Tree Search and progressive widening to approximate the optimal policy is a good choice, given the intractability of computing the optimal policy exactly. The experimental evaluation is thorough, and the results are promising, showing that the proposed approach can achieve significant cost reductions and accuracy improvements over baseline methods.

The paper is well-written, and the authors provide a good overview of the related work in the field. However, there are a few areas where the paper could be improved. For example, the example given in the paragraph starting at Line 154 is somewhat abstract and difficult to follow. Using a specific query example with crowd responses and times similar to what they would be in a real case might make the explanation more concrete and easier to understand.

Additionally, the notation and terminology used in the paper could be clarified in some places. For instance, the use of "entropic" and "threshold" to refer to the same baseline method in Tables 2 and 3 is confusing. It would be better to use a consistent terminology throughout the paper.

Overall, the paper presents a novel and interesting approach to on-the-job learning, and the experimental results are promising. With some minor revisions to improve clarity and consistency, the paper has the potential to make a significant contribution to the field.
