This paper presents a more theoretically grounded formulation for an "on-the-job" learning system, an idea previously explored in [22]. The proposed system begins by relying entirely on crowdsourcing for its predictions. Over time, it learns a model from the crowd-labeled data and gradually transitions to making its own predictions, only querying the crowd when its confidence is low. The core of this transition mechanism is a decision-theoretic problem that determines whether to query the crowd for a given input. The authors model this problem and solve it using Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS).

The paper's primary strength lies in its novel and principled approach to balancing automated prediction with human-in-the-loop intervention. Framing the query decision as a planning problem solved with MCTS is an elegant formulation.

However, the paper has some notable weaknesses. First, the experimental results are not entirely convincing. The proposed MCTS-based approach provides only a marginal improvement over a simpler threshold-based heuristic, which raises questions about the practical benefits of the added complexity. Second, the presentation, while generally clear, omits several key details necessary for a full understanding and replication of the work.

Overall, this is a reasonable approach that merits further investigation. The theoretical formulation for an "on-the-job learner" is a valuable contribution, and this work provides a good starting point for discussion along these lines.

To strengthen the paper, I urge the authors to address the following points:

1.  **Clarity of Equation (2):** The derivation in Equation (2) is difficult to follow. It appears the left-hand side (LHS) should also be conditioned on the state `s`. Furthermore, it is unclear how the variable `y` in the term `p{R}(ri | y, q_i)` is marginalized out. Please provide a more detailed derivation or explanation for this step.

2.  **Definition in Algorithm 1:** The variable `N(s)` is used in Algorithm 1 but is not defined in the text. Please provide a clear definition for this term.

3.  **Justification for Baseline Parameters:** For the threshold-based baseline, the paper uses specific confidence values of 0.3 and 0.88. Could the authors please explain how these values were selected? Were they tuned on a validation set, or is there another justification for this choice?

4.  **Experimental Context and Error Analysis:** It would be helpful to provide more context for the "online" systems used as the base learners in the experiments.
    *   Are these systems considered state-of-the-art for the experimental tasks? If not, the observed improvements might stem partially from a weak baseline rather than purely from the effectiveness of the proposed query strategy.
    *   If they are state-of-the-art, then the performance gain is attributable to the selective use of human annotation. In this case, an error analysis would be highly beneficial. What types of examples does the system correctly identify as needing human input? Understanding these cases would significantly strengthen the paper's contribution and provide valuable insights into the model's behavior.
