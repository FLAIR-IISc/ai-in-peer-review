The primary contribution of this work is the development of a principled theoretical framework for an "on the job learner" system, first introduced in [22]. This system initially relies on crowd workers for its outputs but progressively learns to make its own predictions, eventually reducing its reliance on the crowd to infrequent queries. The decision of whether to engage the crowd for a given input is framed as a sequential decision problem, which the authors address using Monte Carlo Tree Search.

The manuscript is generally well-written and clear; however, it omits several key details. The experimental improvements demonstrated over a simple threshold-based heuristic are somewhat modest. Nevertheless, the proposed method is conceptually sound and represents a promising direction for future research.

I would ask the authors to clarify the following points:

1.  Equation (2) is unclear. It appears the left-hand side (LHS) should also be conditioned on the state `s`. Furthermore, please explain how the `y` term is marginalized out from the expression `p{R}(ri | y, q_i)`.
2.  In Algorithm 1, the term `N(s)` is used without a definition. Please specify what it represents.
3.  The paper should clarify the selection process for the 0.3 and 0.88 values used in the thresholding baseline to ensure reproducibility.
4.  Regarding the experimental baselines, are the "online" systems used for comparison considered state-of-the-art for these tasks? If not, the justification for their use should be strengthened. If they are, then any performance improvement is attributable solely to the human-in-the-loop component. In this case, an error analysis would be highly valuable to illustrate the specific scenarios where human annotation provides the most benefit.

In summary, this paper introduces a theoretical formalization for an "on the job learner"—a model that begins by leveraging human computation and transitions toward an automated system that queries the crowd only for low-confidence predictions. Although the empirical results are mixed, the paper should stimulate valuable discussion in this area.
