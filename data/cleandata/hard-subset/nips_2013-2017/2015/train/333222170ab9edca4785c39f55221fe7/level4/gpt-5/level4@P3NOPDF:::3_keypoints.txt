- Paper proposes a principled hybrid human–machine prediction framework that uses crowdsourced labels plus an incrementally trained model, formulated as a stochastic game over a CRF and optimized with MCTS + temporal-difference learning.
- Experiments (NER, sentiment, face ID) show the full model (LENSE) generally outperforms baselines, reduces crowd usage over time, and attains high early accuracy by leveraging crowd input.
- Strength: novel and significant joint learning and crowd-allocation approach that moves beyond heuristics.
- Strength: technically deep framing with explicit response-timing modeling and approximate planning via MCTS + TD.
- Strength: empirical results are competitive against strong baselines and show reduced crowd dependence and good early performance.
- Main clarity issue: introducing continuous-time mid-section complicates presentation, and its empirical benefits over a simpler threshold (discrete-time) variant are modest.
- The threshold (discrete-time) variant often matches or beats LENSE on latency/accuracy in some tasks, suggesting continuous time may be unnecessary in many practical cases.
- Recommendation to restructure: present discrete-time threshold variant as the core method and continuous time as an extension, with clearer guidance on when continuous time helps.
- Experimental reporting problems: overclaiming “beats human performance” (not true vs. 5-vote NER); missing 5-vote baselines for face ID/sentiment and missing threshold baseline for sentiment in some tables/figures.
- Request to bold best-performing entries and report baselines (1/3/5 votes, Threshold, Online oracle) consistently with accuracy, latency, and standard deviations.
- Technical clarifications needed: what deadlines (t_deadline) were used and how stopping was decided; correct conditioning in Equation (2) to include s; rename F(q) argument to avoid name clash; confirm/correct threshold inequality.
- Suggest an ablation isolating the time component, clearer analysis of when continuous time helps (e.g., high latency variability, strict deadlines), and discussion of practical deployment issues (worker model calibration, robustness to timing misspecification, sensitivity to response variance).
- Overall assessment: strong, original, and practically relevant work with compelling empirical strengths, but presentation and experimental completeness should be improved; recommendation: Accept.
