This paper introduces a hybrid approach to prediction problems, intelligently combining crowdsourced labels with an incrementally trained model. The core idea – to heavily rely on crowd input initially, learn from it, and gradually decrease reliance as model confidence grows – is framed as a sophisticated stochastic game based on a CRF prediction model. The system and the crowd are modeled as players, with the system issuing queries *q* for tokens *x* (with true label *y*) to elicit responses *r*, aiming to maximize expected utility *U(q,r)*. Crucially, queries are issued over time *s* with response times *t*, optimizing utility with respect to a *t_deadline* to control query rate and number.

Computing this expected utility requires a simulation dynamics model *P(y,r,t|x,q,s)* to estimate utilities as in (4). While optimal action selection is intractable due to continuous time, the authors employ an approximation based on Monte Carlo Tree Search and TD learning (Algorithm 1). Experiments were conducted on simplified NER, sentiment recognition, and face identification tasks, comparing four methods: majority vote of human judges (1, 3, 5), online learning (using true labels), a "threshold" baseline (the authors’ model without continuous time, querying until model uncertainty falls below a threshold), and the full LENSE model.  

Results (Table 2) demonstrate that the full model outperforms all but the 5-vote crowdsourced version, although the "threshold" baseline achieves nearly comparable performance, and with lower latency for NER. The authors also demonstrate that the model requires progressively fewer crowdsourced labels over time with sufficient training data (Figure 3), and achieves high accuracy from the first example compared to online learning (Figure 4) by leveraging crowd expertise during high model uncertainty.

This is a truly interesting and potentially impactful paper. It represents the first complete and principled proposal for learning from the crowd while simultaneously training a system, distinguishing it from existing heuristic approaches. The results against reasonable baselines are impressive, signifying high originality and significance, and justifying inclusion in the conference. However, the paper suffers from clarity issues that could limit its impact. These are largely correctable, even for the camera-ready version, potentially through adjustments to tone and a more nuanced discussion of the "threshold" baseline versus the full continuous-time version.

The primary clarity issue stems from the introduction of continuous time in Section 3. While the motivation – accommodating both time and cost budgets – is reasonable, the experimental benefit appears minimal, while significantly increasing complexity in formulation, discussion, and optimization. Indeed, Table 2 suggests the "threshold" baseline achieves lower latency on NER with roughly equivalent performance, and even outperforms LENSE on face ID. The authors’ explanation regarding task characteristics doesn’t fully reconcile this, as the NER task, with its inherent label interactions, shows only marginal benefit from continuous time. For many practical applications, the "threshold" baseline will likely suffice, representing a substantial advancement as a principled hybrid learning system.  The paper would benefit from explicitly acknowledging this, potentially by developing the core method fully *without* continuous time, presenting the latter as an extension.

Furthermore, the discussion of results could emphasize the strong performance of the "threshold" method, even in sequence-dependent tasks. While not strictly necessary for acceptance, this would likely improve understanding and adoption.

Several minor issues regarding results reporting require attention. The claim of “beating human performance” is inaccurate for NER with 5 votes. The 5-vote case is also missing from multiple tables and figures (Tables 2, 3, and Figure 4) and should be included. Results for the 5-vote case in Table 2 should be bolded to highlight best performance, as should the latency value for Threshold in NER and the LENSE value for Face ID. Critically, the "Threshold" baseline is missing from the sentiment experiment (Table 3 and Figure 4) and should be included.

Finally, a few minor methodological points: the use of *t_deadline* in the experiments and criteria for determining answer readiness are unclear; equation (2) should include *s* as a variable; the use of *q* in equation (5) is confusing and should be replaced; and the expression in the Threshold baseline description (l.246-252) should likely be (1 - *p<sub>θ</sub>*(y<sub>i</sub>|x))*0.3<sup>m</sup> <= (1-0.88).



In summary, this paper presents a novel and interesting hybrid approach to leveraging both crowdsourced labels and online learning. Despite some clarity issues, particularly surrounding the introduction of continuous time, the work is original, significant, and deserving of acceptance. Addressing the noted points would further enhance the paper’s impact and facilitate wider understanding and adoption of the technique.
