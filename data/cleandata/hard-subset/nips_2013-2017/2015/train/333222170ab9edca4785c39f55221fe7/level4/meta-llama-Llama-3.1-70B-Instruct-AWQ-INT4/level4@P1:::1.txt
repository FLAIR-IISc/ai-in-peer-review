The main contribution of this paper is to provide a more theoretically sound formulation of an on-the-job learner, extending ideas first proposed in [6]. This learner starts by querying the crowd to produce its output, but gradually begins making its own predictions and querying the crowd infrequently. The choice of whether to query the crowd for any given input is modeled as a decision problem solved via a Monte Carlo Tree Search.

The presentation is mostly clear, although lacking in important details. The empirical results, particularly in comparison to a threshold-based heuristic, are somewhat underwhelming. However, the approach seems reasonable and merits further investigation.

To improve the paper, the authors should address and clarify the following points:

1. Equation (2) requires clarification. The left-hand side should also be conditioned on s. Additionally, it is unclear how the y in pR(ri | y, qi) is handled.
2. The definition of N(s) in Algorithm 1 is not provided. It would be helpful to include this detail to ensure reproducibility.
3. The threshold baseline uses the values 0.3 and 0.98, but the authors do not explain how these values were chosen. Providing this information would help readers understand the design decisions behind the baseline.
4. The authors should discuss whether the "online" systems used in the experiments are the best machine-learned systems for these tasks. If not, they should explain why not. If so, any gain achieved by the proposed approach is likely due to human annotation. Some error analysis would be helpful in understanding the strengths and limitations of the proposed method.

Overall, this paper presents a theoretical formulation of an "on-the-job learner" that starts as a pure crowdsourcing system and gradually transitions to a learned model that only queries the crowd on low-confidence inputs. While the empirical results are mixed, the approach is worth exploring further, and addressing the above points will help strengthen the paper.
