This paper introduces "on-the-job learning," a framework for deploying a prediction system that starts with zero training data and improves over time. The core idea is to use a hybrid approach that initially relies heavily on real-time crowdsourcing to make accurate predictions and gradually becomes more autonomous as it learns from the acquired labels. This problem is elegantly formulated as a stochastic game between the system and the crowd, grounded in Bayesian decision theory. This allows the system to make principled decisions about when to query the crowd and when to make a prediction, balancing the trade-offs between accuracy, monetary cost, and latency.

The system's policy determines which parts of an input to query, whether to wait for responses, and when to terminate and return a final prediction. As computing the optimal policy is intractable, the authors propose a sophisticated approximation based on Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) combined with progressive widening to handle the continuous nature of time. The authors evaluate their system, named LENSE, on three tasks: named-entity recognition (NER), sentiment classification, and image classification. It is compared against several baselines, including human majority voting, a standard online learning approach with oracle labels, and a simplified "threshold" version of their own method that does not model time explicitly. The results show that LENSE can achieve accuracy comparable to a 5-person human team at a fraction of the cost and significantly outperforms a standard online learning baseline, especially in the initial stages.

**Strengths**

*   **Originality and Significance:** The paper addresses a highly relevant and practical problem: how to build an accurate system from a cold start without a large, pre-existing labeled dataset. The proposed "on-the-job learning" framework is novel, and the formulation of the problem as a stochastic game is a principled and complete approach that moves beyond the ad-hoc heuristics often used in hybrid human-machine systems. This work has the potential for significant impact, both in guiding the development of practical systems and in inspiring future research.
*   **Technical Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The decision-theoretic formulation is well-motivated, and the use of MCTS to approximate the policy is a suitable and non-trivial technical contribution. The experimental evaluation is comprehensive, covering three different tasks and comparing against strong and appropriate baselines. The results are impressive, demonstrating substantial improvements in the cost-accuracy trade-off. The authors also make their code and data available, which is commendable and enhances the reproducibility of their work.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement**

The primary weakness of the paper lies in its clarity, which could hinder its potential impact. While the work itself is strong, the presentation could be significantly improved.

*   **Clarity and Justification for Complexity:** The introduction of continuous time, while conceptually interesting for modeling latency, substantially complicates the problem formulation, the solution algorithm, and the overall exposition. The experimental results, however, do not provide a compelling justification for this added complexity. In the NER task, the simpler "Threshold" baseline (which does not model time) achieves a significantly lower latency with only a marginal drop in F1-score. On the face identification task, the Threshold baseline performs on par or slightly better.

    This suggests that the core strength of the method lies in the principled, sequential querying strategy, not necessarily the complex temporal reasoning. The paper would be much clearer and likely have greater impact if the core method (without continuous time, akin to the Threshold baseline) was developed first, establishing its effectiveness. The continuous time model could then be presented as an extension. This would make the work easier to understand, implement, and adopt.

*   **Reporting of Results:** The experimental reporting is incomplete in several places, which makes it difficult to fully assess the performance of the proposed method.
    *   The 5-vote human baseline, which serves as a crucial upper-bound on quality, is missing for the Sentiment and Faces tasks in Tables 2, 3, and Figure 4. This baseline should be included for a complete comparison.
    *   The "Threshold" baseline, which is a key ablation and performs very strongly, is missing entirely from the sentiment analysis experiments (Table 3 and Figure 4). Its inclusion is essential for understanding the benefits of the full LENSE model.
    *   In the results tables, the best-performing values are not consistently bolded, making them harder to interpret at a glance (e.g., the 5-vote F1 and Threshold latency in the NER results should be bolded).

**Detailed Comments**

Here are some specific points for the authors to consider:

*   The claim in the introduction that the model "boost[s] performance relative to the expert provided labels" is not fully supported by the NER results in Table 2, where the 5-vote human baseline still achieves the highest F1 score. This claim should be toned down or clarified.
*   It is unclear what `t_deadline` was used in the experiments or how the system determined when it was "confident enough" to return a prediction. Was this based on a fixed time limit or an uncertainty threshold similar to the baseline?
*   In Equation (3), the conditioning set for the probability distribution should likely include `s`, the query issue times: `p(y, r, t | x, q, s)`.
*   The description of the Threshold baseline (lines 249-250) seems to have an error in the inequality. To reduce uncertainty below a threshold (e.g., below 0.02 to get to 0.98 confidence), the expression should likely be `(1-p_theta(y_i|x)) * 0.3^m <= 1 - 0.98`, as increasing `m` should decrease the left-hand side.

In summary, this is a high-quality paper presenting a novel and significant contribution to the field. The proposed framework is principled, and the empirical results are strong. My main reservations concern the clarity of the presentation, particularly the justification for the complexity added by the continuous time model. Despite these issues, which can be addressed in a final version, the paper's originality and significance make it a clear candidate for acceptance.
