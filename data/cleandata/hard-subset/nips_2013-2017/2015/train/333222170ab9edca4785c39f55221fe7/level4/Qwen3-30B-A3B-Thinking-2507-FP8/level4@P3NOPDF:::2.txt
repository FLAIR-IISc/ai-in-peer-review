

The paper presents a compelling approach to building high-accuracy classifiers from zero labeled examples, with a novel focus on optimizing latency while handling noisy crowd responses. The comparison of classifier accuracy against human performance (Table 2) is particularly valuable, as it demonstrates that the LENSE system outperforms the 3-vote consensus baseline but underperforms the 5-vote baseline. This contrast effectively highlights a critical trade-off: the efficiency gains of de-noising via consensus (3-vote) versus the LENSE system’s approach. The finding warrants deeper discussion regarding when each method is preferable, given their distinct latency and accuracy profiles.

However, several aspects of the presentation require clarification. The example provided in the paragraph starting at Line 154 is overly abstract and difficult to follow. To enhance readability, the authors should replace this with a concrete, real-world illustration. For instance, they could present a specific query (e.g., "Is this image a dog?"), show representative crowd responses with timestamps, and demonstrate how LENSE processes these inputs compared to the vote baselines. This would make the method’s operational logic immediately accessible.

Terminology inconsistencies in Tables 2 and 3 also undermine clarity. The "threshold baseline" is occasionally labeled "entropic threshold," creating confusion about whether these refer to the same method or distinct baselines. The authors should standardize the terminology (e.g., consistently using "entropic threshold baseline") and explicitly define it in the methodology section. Without this, readers cannot reliably interpret the comparative results.

The placement of related work in Section 5 is problematic. This section should logically precede the core methodology (Section 3) to contextualize the paper’s contributions against prior work on noisy crowdsourcing and latency optimization. Moving related work to Section 2 (or merging it with the introduction) would improve the paper’s flow and help readers quickly grasp the novelty.

The claim at Line 86 that the paper "tackles the problem of creating high-accuracy classifiers beginning with zero labeled examples" is accurate but slightly misleading. The core contribution is not the zero-shot learning itself but the *latency-aware optimization* of crowd-sourced labeling for such classifiers. The paper should clarify this distinction early to avoid conflating the broader problem with the specific technical innovation.

Overall, the problem addressed is significant and timely. The experimental results (Tables 2–3) provide strong evidence that LENSE reduces latency without sacrificing accuracy relative to baselines, though the 5-vote baseline’s superior accuracy suggests room for improvement. The authors’ focus on real-world latency constraints—often overlooked in similar work—is a key strength. However, the current presentation obscures these contributions through inconsistent terminology, insufficiently concrete examples, and misaligned section structure.

To strengthen the paper, I recommend:  
1. Replacing the abstract Line 154 example with a detailed, realistic case study.  
2. Standardizing "entropic threshold" as the baseline name and defining it unambiguously.  
3. Moving related work to Section 2 (or a dedicated Section 1.2) to establish context before methodology.  
4. Refining the abstract to emphasize *latency-optimized* crowd labeling as the novel contribution, not zero-shot learning per se.  

With these revisions, the paper would clearly communicate its methodological innovation and empirical advantages. As it stands, the core ideas are promising but obscured by presentation issues that hinder their impact. The technical content is sound, but the clarity of exposition must improve to match the quality of the results.
