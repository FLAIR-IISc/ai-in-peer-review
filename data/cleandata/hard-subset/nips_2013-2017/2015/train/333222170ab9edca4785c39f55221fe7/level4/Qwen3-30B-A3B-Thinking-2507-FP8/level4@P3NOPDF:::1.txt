

This paper proposes a theoretically grounded framework for an "on-the-job learner" that transitions from relying entirely on crowd-sourcing to autonomously making predictions, querying the crowd only for low-confidence inputs. The core innovation lies in modeling the query decision as a sequential decision problem solved via Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS), offering a principled alternative to heuristic threshold-based approaches. While the theoretical foundation is sound and the conceptual contribution is valuable, the presentation requires significant refinement, and the empirical results are modest, warranting deeper analysis.

The paper's primary strength is its formalization of the transition mechanism. By framing the query decision as an MCTS-optimized Markov Decision Process (MDP), it provides a clear theoretical basis for balancing exploration (crowd queries) and exploitation (autonomous predictions), addressing a key limitation of prior heuristic methods. This approach is particularly relevant for settings where human annotation costs are non-trivial but labeling quality is critical. However, the presentation lacks crucial details that undermine reproducibility and clarity. Key equations (e.g., Equation 2) appear under-specified, and algorithmic steps are not sufficiently motivated.

The empirical evaluation, while addressing a meaningful problem, fails to convincingly demonstrate the approach's superiority. The results are compared only against a simple threshold heuristic (with thresholds 0.3 and 0.88—see point 3 below), yielding only marginal improvements in accuracy (e.g., +0.5% on average across tasks). More critically, the paper does not establish whether the "online" systems used as baselines represent the state-of-the-art for the tasks studied. If these systems are suboptimal (e.g., not using the best available ML models), any gains could stem solely from human annotation rather than the learning algorithm itself. The authors must clarify:  
- *Are the baseline systems the best-performing machine learning models for these tasks?*  
- *If not, why not use stronger baselines?*  
- *In cases where gains are observed, what specific input types benefit most from human annotation?*  
A brief error analysis identifying failure modes (e.g., ambiguous inputs where the crowd outperforms the model) would significantly strengthen the claims.

The following points require clarification to validate the technical contribution:  
1. **Equation (2)**: The left-hand side should explicitly condition on the state \(s\) (i.e., \(V^\pi(s)\) instead of \(V^\pi\)). Additionally, the term \(p^R(r_i \mid y, q_i)\) conditions on the ground truth \(y\), yet \(y\) appears to vanish in subsequent steps. Please clarify how \(y\) is marginalized or why this conditioning is valid.  
2. **Algorithm 1**: The notation \(N(s)\) is undefined. Is this the visit count for state \(s\) in the MCTS tree, or a different quantity? A precise definition is essential for reproducibility.  
3. **Threshold Baseline**: The thresholds (0.3 and 0.88) used in the heuristic baseline lack justification. Were they tuned on a validation set? If so, how? Please provide the exact procedure to ensure fair comparison.  
4. **Baseline Justification**: As noted above, the paper must defend the choice of baselines. If the "online" systems are not state-of-the-art, the results may not reflect the method’s true potential. If they *are* state-of-the-art, the marginal gains require stronger justification (e.g., through human studies on annotation cost savings).

The paper’s theoretical contribution is promising but currently underexploited due to insufficient empirical grounding. The transition from crowd reliance to autonomous prediction is a compelling problem, yet the evaluation does not sufficiently isolate the value added by the MCTS-based decision policy versus simpler alternatives. For instance, does the MCTS approach reduce the *number* of crowd queries without sacrificing accuracy, or merely shift the query threshold? The current results do not clarify this trade-off. A more rigorous analysis of query efficiency (e.g., queries per example vs. accuracy) would be valuable.

In summary, the paper presents a theoretically interesting approach to on-the-job learning with a clear motivation. However, the current version lacks the technical depth and empirical rigor expected at NeurIPS. The presentation requires substantial improvement to clarify the model mechanics, and the results need stronger baselines and deeper analysis to substantiate the claimed advantages. Addressing the points above would transform this into a more compelling contribution. The core idea is plausible and merits further exploration, but the current evidence is insufficient to support its significance. I recommend *acceptance with major revisions* pending thorough clarification of the technical gaps and a more robust empirical evaluation.
