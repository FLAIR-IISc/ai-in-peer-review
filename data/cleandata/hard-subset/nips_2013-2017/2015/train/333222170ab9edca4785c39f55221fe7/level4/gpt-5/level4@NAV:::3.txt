This paper proposes a hybrid strategy that combines crowdsourced annotations with an incrementally trained (online) model for prediction tasks. The central idea is to rely heavily on the crowd during the system’s initial phase, learn from the collected labels, and progressively reduce crowd involvement as the model’s confidence increases. The formulation casts the problem as a stochastic game built atop a CRF predictor, with the system and the crowd treated as players. The system may issue one or more queries q for tokens x (with true label y), eliciting responses r and yielding a utility U(q,r) for each outcome; the system then aims to choose actions that maximize expected utility. Queries are dispatched over time at points s (with response times t), and utility is optimized with respect to a t_deadline by which an answer must be produced (thereby governing how many queries to send, at what cadence, etc.).

Estimating this expected utility relies on a simulation dynamics model P(y,r,t|x,q,s) to compute utilities as in (4). With these utilities, one could in principle select the optimal action; however, introducing continuous time renders optimization intractable, so the authors employ an approximation using Monte Carlo Tree Search and TD learning (Algorithm 1). Experiments cover a simplified NER task, a sentiment classification task, and a face identification task, comparing four approaches: majority vote from n human judges (1, 3, 5), online learning (with true labels), a “threshold” baseline (the authors’ model without continuous time, issuing m queries at each step until the model’s predictive uncertainty falls below a threshold), and the full model (LENSE). In precision/recall/F1, the full model outperforms all but the 5-vote crowd condition, though the “threshold” baseline is close (and has lower latency on NER; Table 2). The authors also demonstrate that the model increasingly reduces reliance on crowd labels over time given a sufficiently capable learner (Figure 3), and that, compared to online learning, accuracy is high from the first example (Figure 4), since the system can draw more heavily on crowd expertise when model uncertainty is high.

I found this paper very compelling and expect it will spark discussion, spur follow-on work, and see practical use. While many heuristic methods exist for leveraging the crowd during training, this is the first complete and principled treatment I have encountered. The results against reasonable baselines are strong. Consequently, I view the contribution as highly original and impactful, and I believe it merits acceptance. That said, clarity falls short, which may limit impact. Many issues are fixable (even for the camera-ready) with additional effort and a modest adjustment in tone when contrasting the “threshold” baseline with the full continuous-time version.

The main clarity problem arises with the introduction of continuous time midway through Section 3. The motivation is sensible—some settings require both time and cost budgets per decision—but empirically it appears to add limited benefit while substantially increasing the complexity of the formulation, exposition, and optimization. In Table 2, for example, the “threshold” baseline achieves markedly lower latency on NER with roughly comparable performance (slightly worse); on Face ID it actually does better (again roughly comparable). The authors argue (l. 314) that the baseline fares better on the second task because it involves a single label and limited label interaction; however, NER, which has strong label dependencies, only gains marginally from continuous time. For many practical problems, the “threshold” baseline will suffice, and it already represents a substantial advance (a principled crowd–model hybrid that outperforms active and online learning). The paper would likely have more impact if the core method were developed fully without time (making it easier to understand and implement), with continuous time presented as an extension. Along similar lines, the discussion of results could be clearer in acknowledging the strong performance of the “threshold” method, even when sequence dependencies are present. This is a suggestion rather than a requirement—the paper is still strong enough to accept—but it would, in my view, improve clarity and adoption.

There are several smaller issues in the results and their presentation. The introduction’s claim that the model “beats human performance” is not borne out in the 5-vote NER condition; moreover, the 5-vote condition is missing for Face ID and Sentiment in multiple places (right half of Table 2, Table 3, and Figure 4) and should be included. In Table 2, the 5-vote results should be boldfaced as the best-performing entries; the Threshold latency for NER should likewise be bolded (and the LENSE value for Face ID). More importantly, the “Threshold” baseline is absent from the sentiment experiment and therefore missing from Table 3 and Figure 4; it should be included as well.

Minor comments on the method description:
 - Although t_deadline is discussed in l. 183–187, it is unclear what t_deadline (if any) was used in experiments and what criterion determined when to output a prediction—was it an entropy-based threshold as in the baseline, or reaching some t_deadline?
 - In equation (2), the distribution should also be conditioned on s (i.e., p(y,r,t|x,q,s)).
 - In equation (5), using q in F(q) is confusing—I initially read it as a query variable, but it actually denotes a nominal distribution; please choose a different symbol.
 - In the Threshold baseline description (l. 246–252), I believe the expression should be (1 - ptheta(yi|x))*0.3^m <= (1 - 0.88) (note the RHS), rather than >= 0.88, since increasing m reduces uncertainty rather than increasing it.

In sum, the paper introduces a crowd–model hybrid framed as a stochastic game over a CRF, explicitly modeling when queries are issued and responses received. The results are impressive against multiple human voters, online learning with oracle labels, and a “threshold” variant that removes continuous time and performs comparably. The work is novel and compelling and should be accepted, but the introduction of continuous time complicates the method and exposition while offering limited performance gains; clarifying this would strengthen the paper.
