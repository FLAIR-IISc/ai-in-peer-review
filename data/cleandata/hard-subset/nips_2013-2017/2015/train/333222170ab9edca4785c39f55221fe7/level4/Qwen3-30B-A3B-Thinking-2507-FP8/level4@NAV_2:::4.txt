

The authors present "on-the-job learning," a framework for maintaining high accuracy from the very first prediction without requiring initial training data. The system strategically queries crowdsourcing for uncertain input elements during prediction, with the model improving over time as it incorporates crowd responses. The authors formalize this as a stochastic game based on Bayesian decision theory, enabling principled tradeoffs between latency, cost, and accuracy. They propose an approximate solution using Monte Carlo Tree Search with progressive widening and evaluate on three tasks: named-entity recognition, sentiment classification, and image classification. The results demonstrate significant cost reduction (over an order of magnitude for NER) while improving F1 scores over baselines.

The work builds on active learning and online learning literature, distinguishing itself from traditional active learning (which requires building a model before making predictions) and from static active classification approaches (which don't update the model during test time). The authors effectively contextualize their contribution in Section 6, noting similarities to and differences from Legion:AR and other related work.

Strengths:
- The problem is well-motivated with clear real-world applications (e.g., disaster response systems).
- The formalization as a stochastic game with Bayesian decision theory is elegant and principled.
- The experimental results are compelling, showing significant improvements in both cost and accuracy.
- The implementation is open-sourced, enhancing reproducibility.

Weaknesses:
- The application to the face recognition task (image classification) is unclear. The paper states it was used for image classification, but the structured query mechanism (which relies on relationships between input elements) doesn't naturally extend to non-structured image classification. The utility function appears designed for structured data where relationships between elements matter, making its application to image classification questionable.
- The threshold baseline has two parameters that lack justification. The paper states the threshold is set to "ensure (1−pθ(yi | x))×0.3m ≥ 0.98" but doesn't explain how these specific values were chosen or demonstrate sensitivity analysis.
- The comparison to standard active learning approaches is insufficient. The authors claim their approach differs from active online learning, but it's not clear why standard active learning techniques (e.g., uncertainty sampling) couldn't be adapted to this setting by treating the input stream as an active learning pool. A comparison to such a baseline would strengthen the evaluation.

The work demonstrates significant value in the NER task, showing clear improvements over both human-only and machine-only approaches. However, the limited applicability to non-structured tasks and the lack of a comprehensive comparison to standard active learning methods reduce the paper's general significance.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Novel formalization of the on-the-job learning problem as a stochastic game with Bayesian decision theory.
- Strong empirical results showing substantial cost reduction and accuracy improvements in the NER task.
- Clear real-world relevance and well-motivated problem statement.
- Open-source implementation enhancing reproducibility.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Unclear application to non-structured tasks (image classification).
- Unjustified parameters in the threshold baseline.
- Incomplete comparison to standard active learning approaches.
- The claimed distinction from active online learning is not sufficiently supported.
