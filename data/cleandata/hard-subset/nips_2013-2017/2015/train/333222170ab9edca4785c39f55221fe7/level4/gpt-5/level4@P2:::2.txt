Summary
This paper proposes on-the-job learning: a decision-theoretic framework that starts with zero labeled data and maintains high accuracy by querying crowd workers in real time before committing to predictions. The system models the interaction with the crowd as a stochastic game and uses Bayesian decision theory to balance accuracy, latency, and monetary cost. Because exact optimization is intractable, the authors approximate the optimal policy with Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) and progressive widening to handle continuous time. The approach is instantiated with a CRF for structured prediction and evaluated on NER, sentiment classification, and image (face) classification. Compared to human-only annotation and pure machine-learning baselines (including online learning), the system (LENSE) reduces cost substantially while preserving or improving accuracy; for NER, it reduces cost by more than an order of magnitude versus full human annotation and improves F1 over both a single-vote crowd baseline and online learning. This work relates to online learning, online active learning, and active classification; unlike those, it queries before predicting, optimizes time/cost/accuracy jointly, and adapts the model during test time.

Strengths
- Clear problem motivation: deploying accurate systems without upfront labeled data.
- Principled formulation using Bayesian decision theory and a stochastic game.
- Practical search with MCTS and progressive widening to manage continuous-time responses.
- Realistic experimental setup with a real-time crowd retainer; open-source implementation.
- Compelling results: strong cost savings and competitive accuracy; method sometimes surpasses single-annotator and online-learning baselines; structured outputs handled naturally.

Weaknesses and clarity issues
- The paper sometimes underperforms the 5-vote human baseline while outperforming 3-vote, suggesting the trade-off between added consensus and decision-theoretic querying deserves deeper analysis. A more systematic cost–accuracy–latency frontier (e.g., Pareto curves) would help.
- The example starting around line 154 is abstract and hard to follow. A concrete, end-to-end walkthrough (specific tokens, issued queries, arrival times, costs, and decision points) would greatly improve readability.
- In Tables 2 and 3, the “threshold baseline” is referred to inconsistently (as “entropic” in places). Please unify the terminology across text and tables.
- The placement of related work is confusing in the current draft. Consider a dedicated Related Work section clearly positioned and integrated with the introduction to situate the contribution early.
- There is at least one unresolved figure reference: “Figure ??” (e.g., around line 86). Please fix all cross-references.
- The response model assumes a fixed 70% worker accuracy and uses precollected pools of five responses per label during simulation. This may understate variability and limit external validity; modeling worker-specific reliabilities or using larger, live crowds could improve robustness.
- The loss/utility uses ExpAcc of the MAP prediction. A short justification and comparison to alternative decision rules (e.g., expected utility under 0–1 loss, risk-sensitive objectives) would clarify design choices.
- Sensitivity to key hyperparameters (wM, wT, UCT constant, progressive-widening schedule) and to the response-time model pT is not fully explored.
- A design constraint disallows launching new queries while waiting; discussing its impact or allowing limited interleaving would be informative.

Quality
- The formulation is sound and well-motivated. The approximation strategy is reasonable for an otherwise intractable problem, though formal guarantees are limited.
- Experimental results support the main claims, especially on NER. However, additional sensitivity analyses, ablations (e.g., without progressive widening, static policies), and confidence intervals would strengthen the empirical case.

Clarity
- Overall readable, but several sections (e.g., the illustrative example) are opaque. Inconsistent baseline naming and missing figure references impede comprehension. Reorganizing related work and adding a detailed example would help.

Originality
- The on-the-job setting that queries before predicting, framed as a stochastic game with explicit time/cost trade-offs, is novel relative to online learning, online active learning, and most active classification work. The use of MCTS with progressive widening for real-time crowdsourcing decisions is also distinctive.

Significance
- The approach addresses a practical and common need: bootstrapping high-accuracy systems without labeled data. The demonstrated cost reductions and the possibility of decreasing reliance on the crowd over time make it attractive for deployment, particularly for structured tasks like NER.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide a concrete, step-by-step decision episode illustrating queries, response arrivals, model updates, and the return decision.
- Unify baseline names (“threshold” vs. “entropic”) and ensure consistent notation across text and tables.
- Fix all unresolved figure/table references and consider moving related work to a clearly labeled section.
- Report sensitivity to wM and wT and to the response-time and accuracy models; include CIs or error bars.
- Compare against worker-reliability-aware methods (e.g., TurKontrol-like POMDPs) and include ablations on MCTS/progressive widening.
- Discuss scalability to longer sequences and the impact of the no-interleaving constraint during waits.

Pros for acceptance
- Timely problem with clear practical impact.
- Principled formulation and clever approximate inference for continuous-time decisions.
- Strong cost savings with competitive accuracy; open-source code.

Cons for acceptance
- Clarity issues (example abstraction, inconsistent baseline naming, figure references).
- Limited analysis of sensitivity to key assumptions and hyperparameters.
- Underperforms 5-vote baseline in some cases; cost–accuracy trade-off not fully characterized.

Overall
A compelling and original contribution with solid empirical results, particularly on NER. I lean toward acceptance provided the authors address the clarity issues and add analyses that characterize sensitivity and trade-offs more thoroughly.
