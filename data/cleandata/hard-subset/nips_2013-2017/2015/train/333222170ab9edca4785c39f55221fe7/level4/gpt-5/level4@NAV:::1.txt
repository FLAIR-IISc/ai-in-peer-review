The primary contribution of this paper is a more theoretically grounded formulation of the on-the-job learner proposed in [22]. The system initially queues the crowd to generate outputs but progressively shifts to making its own predictions, querying the crowd only occasionally. The decision of whether to consult the crowd for a given input is cast as a decision problem and addressed via a Monte Carlo Tree Search.

The presentation is generally good but omits some important details. The empirical results, relative to a threshold-based heuristic, are somewhat underwhelming. Nonetheless, the approach appears reasonable and deserves further study.

Please address/clarify the following:
1. Equation (2) is unclear. The LHS should also be conditioned on s. Also, how does the y in p_R(r_i | y, q_i) disappear?
2. What does N(s) represent in Algorithm 1?
3. How were the 0.3 and 0.88 values selected for the threshold baseline?
4. I am not familiar with the exact tasks used here. Are the “online” systems the strongest machine-learned systems for these tasks? If not, why not? If so, then any gains would stem purely from human annotation. What are some examples? Some error analysis would be helpful.

This paper presents a theoretical formulation of an on-the-job learner that begins as a pure crowdsourcing system and gradually transitions to a learned model that only queries the crowd on low-confidence inputs. The empirical results are mixed, but the direction is promising and merits further discussion.
