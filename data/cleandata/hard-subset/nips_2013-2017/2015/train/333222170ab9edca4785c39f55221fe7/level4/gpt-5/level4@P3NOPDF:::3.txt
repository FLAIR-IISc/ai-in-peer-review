Summary
The paper presents a principled framework for hybrid human–machine prediction that combines crowdsourced labels with an incrementally trained model. The system starts by relying more heavily on the crowd, learns from the collected labels, and progressively reduces crowd usage as model confidence increases. The problem is formulated as a stochastic game atop a CRF prediction model, where the system issues queries q for tokens x with true labels y, receives responses r at times t, and optimizes expected utility U(q, r) subject to time and cost constraints. Because continuous time makes exact optimization intractable, the paper employs a Monte Carlo Tree Search combined with temporal-difference learning (Algorithm 1) to approximate the optimal policy. Experiments on NER, sentiment classification, and face identification compare majority-vote baselines (1/3/5 workers), online learning with oracle labels, a “threshold” variant that omits continuous time, and the full model (LENSE). The full model generally outperforms all but the 5-vote crowd baseline on precision/recall/F1, reduces crowd usage over time, and achieves high accuracy from the first example by leveraging crowd input while model uncertainty is high.

Strengths
- Originality and significance: This is the most complete and principled approach I have seen for joint learning and crowd allocation, moving beyond heuristic strategies.
- Technical depth: The stochastic game framing, explicit modeling of response timing, and approximate planning with MCTS + TD learning are thoughtful and well motivated.
- Empirical results: The approach is competitive with strong baselines. It reduces crowd dependence over time and delivers high early accuracy versus pure online learning.

Clarity and framing
The main clarity issue arises from the introduction of continuous time midway through Section 3. While operating under time budgets is a valid practical concern, the continuous-time formulation substantially complicates the model and optimization, yet appears to yield modest empirical benefits:
- In Table 2, the “threshold” baseline achieves significantly lower latency on NER with roughly equivalent accuracy, and performs on par or slightly better on face identification (a single-label task).
- The paper argues that continuous time should help more when label dependencies matter (e.g., NER), but the observed gains over the threshold baseline are marginal.

For many practical applications, the threshold variant seems strong enough and already represents a substantial advance over standard online/active learning. The paper would have greater impact if it:
- First developed the core method in discrete steps (no continuous time), which would be easier to understand and reimplement; and
- Then presented continuous time as an extension, together with a clearer analysis of when it yields measurable benefits.

Experimental reporting and claims
- The abstract/intro states the model “beats human performance,” but this is not true for NER with 5-vote aggregation. Please temper this claim or qualify it by task/metric.
- The 5-vote majority baseline is missing for face ID and sentiment in the right half of Table 2, Table 3, and Figure 4. It should be reported consistently across tasks.
- The threshold baseline is missing for sentiment (Table 3 and Figure 4). Including it is important given its competitive performance elsewhere.
- For readability, bold the best-performing entries in Tables 2 and 3: e.g., the 5-vote case for NER in Table 2, the latency winner for NER (Threshold), and the face ID winner (LENSE).

Technical questions and requests for clarification
- Deadline usage: Lines 183–187 discuss a t_deadline, but it is unclear what deadlines were used in the experiments and how the system decided when to stop querying and return an answer. Was stopping governed by an entropy/confidence threshold (as in the threshold baseline), by t_deadline, or a combination? Please specify for each task.
- Simulation model: In Equation (2), the conditional distribution should include s, i.e., p(y, r, t | x, q, s), to align with the text and later usage in Equation (4).
- Notation in Equation (5): The use of q in F(q) is confusing since q already denotes queries. Please rename the argument (e.g., F(·) or F(φ)) to avoid ambiguity.
- Threshold stopping inequality: In the description of the threshold baseline (lines 246–252), it seems the condition should be (1 − pθ(yi | x)) · 0.3^m ≤ (1 − 0.88), reflecting that increasing m reduces uncertainty, not increases it. Please confirm and correct.

Suggestions for strengthening the paper
- Reframe the narrative: Emphasize the discrete-time “threshold” variant as the core algorithm and present continuous time as an optional extension. This makes the contribution more accessible and lowers implementation barriers.
- Provide a clearer analysis of when continuous time helps (e.g., high latency variability, strict deadlines, tight budgets), including an ablation isolating the time component’s contribution to utility and latency.
- Report comprehensive baselines (1/3/5 votes, Threshold, Online with oracle labels) consistently across all tasks, with both accuracy and latency, and standard deviations where feasible.
- Discuss practical deployment considerations: how the system calibrates worker models over time, robustness to misspecified timing distributions, and sensitivity to crowd response variance.

Overall assessment
This is a strong, original, and practically relevant paper that takes a principled approach to combining crowdsourcing with online learning and makes a meaningful step beyond prior heuristic methods. The empirical results are compelling, particularly in early accuracy and reduced long-term reliance on the crowd. However, the clarity suffers from the heavy emphasis on continuous time, whose empirical benefits appear limited relative to the added complexity. With improved presentation, complete baselines, and tempered claims, the paper’s impact would be substantially greater.

Recommendation: Accept.
