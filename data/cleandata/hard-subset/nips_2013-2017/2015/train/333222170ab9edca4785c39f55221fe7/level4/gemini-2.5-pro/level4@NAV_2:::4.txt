This paper introduces "on-the-job learning," a framework for deploying a high-accuracy system that starts with zero training data. The core idea is to treat each incoming example as a sequential decision-making problem: the system can query a crowd for labels on parts of the input, wait for responses, and incorporate them to update its model before making a final prediction. This process is formalized as a stochastic game, leveraging Bayesian decision theory to provide a principled trade-off between prediction accuracy, monetary cost, and latency. As computing the optimal policy is intractable, the authors propose an approximation using Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) with progressive widening. This work relates to online learning and active learning but differs in its ability to actively seek information *prior* to making a prediction on a given instance, aiming to maintain high accuracy from the very first example.

**Strengths:**
*   **Significance:** The paper addresses a highly relevant and practical problem: how to build an effective system in scenarios where large, pre-labeled datasets are unavailable. The goal of achieving high accuracy from the outset while learning over time is ambitious and important.
*   **Quality:** The formulation of the problem as a stochastic game managed by Bayesian decision theory is principled and elegant. It provides a solid theoretical foundation for balancing the competing objectives of accuracy, cost, and time.
*   **Experimental Results:** The empirical evaluation demonstrates strong performance, particularly on the NER task. Achieving an order-of-magnitude reduction in cost compared to full human annotation, while simultaneously boosting F1 score over both human-only and standard online learning baselines, is a significant result.
*   **Reproducibility:** The authors provide open-source code and a CodaLab worksheet, which is commendable and greatly benefits the research community.

**Weaknesses:**
*   **Clarity on Non-structured Tasks:** While the application to structured prediction tasks like NER is clear (querying labels for individual tokens), the paper is less clear on how the framework applies to the image classification (Faces) task. It is not specified what "parts of the input" are queried. If the system only queries for the label of the entire instance, it is unclear how the structured prediction model propagates information and how the utility function for querying different "parts" is calculated.
*   **Baselines:** The threshold baseline, while intuitive, includes two seemingly arbitrary parameters (0.3 and 0.98). The paper would be strengthened by a justification for these values and an analysis of the system's sensitivity to them. Furthermore, a comparison against a more standard active learning baseline, such as uncertainty sampling, would be valuable. One could imagine an adaptation where the system queries for the labels it is most uncertain about, updates its model with the responses, and then makes a prediction. A direct comparison would help solidify the benefits of the more complex MCTS-based game-playing approach.
*   **Positioning vs. Active Learning:** The authors emphasize the distinction from online active learning. However, a strong online active learning strategy could be adapted to this setting. For example, one could use an active learning criterion to select a batch of queries, issue them, wait for responses, update the model, and then predict. While this might not explicitly model the temporal dynamics, its performance relative to the proposed method is not obvious. A more thorough discussion or empirical comparison would better delineate the novel contributions and practical advantages of the proposed framework.

**Arguments for acceptance:**
*   The paper introduces a novel and well-motivated framework for an important, practical problem.
*   The technical approach is sound, using Bayesian decision theory and MCTS in a principled way.
*   The empirical results are strong and demonstrate significant improvements in cost and accuracy over relevant baselines.
*   The work is reproducible, with code and data made publicly available.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
*   Lack of clarity regarding the application of the method to non-structured prediction tasks raises questions about its generality.
*   The experimental comparison could be strengthened with better-justified baseline parameters and the inclusion of a baseline derived from standard active learning techniques (e.g., uncertainty sampling).
*   The claimed novelty over adapted online active learning methods is not fully substantiated through comparison.
