This paper tackles the problem of creating high-accuracy classifiers beginning with zero labeled examples. A key component is handling noisy crowd responses, a topic extensively studied in the literature, but this work uniquely focuses on optimizing timing and latency. The authors cast on-the-job learning as a stochastic game based on Bayesian decision theory, allowing for principled trade-offs between latency, cost, and accuracy, and approximate the optimal policy using Monte Carlo Tree Search. This approach is evaluated on named-entity recognition, sentiment classification, and image classification tasks.

The paper’s strength lies in its novel framing of the problem as a stochastic game and its demonstration of significant cost reductions compared to full human annotation, particularly on the NER task (over an order of magnitude reduction). Achieving an 8% F1 improvement over a single human label and a 28% F1 improvement over online learning is also notable. The publicly available implementation (LENSE) is a valuable contribution. The comparison of classifier accuracy to human performance is insightful; observing that LENSE outperforms the 3-vote baseline but underperforms the 5-vote baseline offers a compelling point of analysis regarding the efficiencies of denoising crowd responses through consensus versus the LENSE system’s approach.

However, the paper has some weaknesses. The illustrative example starting on line 154 is rather abstract and difficult to follow. Using a specific query example with realistic crowd responses and timings would improve readability. There’s inconsistency in the naming of the threshold baseline (referred to as both “entropic” and “threshold” in Tables 2 and 3), which is confusing. The placement of the related work section (Section 6) feels unconventional; it typically precedes the experimental evaluation. While the mathematical formulation is rigorous, it could benefit from increased intuition for readers less familiar with stochastic game theory.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novel application of Bayesian decision theory and Monte Carlo Tree Search to on-the-job learning.
*   Demonstrated significant cost reductions compared to traditional crowdsourcing.
*   Strong empirical results across three diverse datasets.
*   Publicly available implementation promotes reproducibility.
*   Addresses a practically relevant problem with a well-defined framework.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   Some explanations are opaque and require more clarity (e.g., the example on line 154).
*   Inconsistent naming of the threshold baseline.
*   Unconventional placement of the related work section.
*   The mathematical formalism might be inaccessible to some readers.

**Overall:**

This is a compelling paper addressing a challenging problem with a novel and well-motivated approach. While some improvements in clarity and presentation would be beneficial, the significant results and the practical relevance of the work make it a strong candidate for acceptance. The strengths of the paper outweigh its weaknesses. I recommend acceptance, pending minor revisions to address the clarity concerns raised above.
