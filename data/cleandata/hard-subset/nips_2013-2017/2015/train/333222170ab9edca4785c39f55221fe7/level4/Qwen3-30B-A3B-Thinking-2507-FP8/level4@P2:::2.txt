

This paper introduces "on-the-job learning," a novel framework for deploying high-accuracy AI systems with zero initial training examples. The authors model the problem as a stochastic game using Bayesian decision theory to balance latency, cost, and accuracy objectives, with an approximate solution based on Monte Carlo Tree Search. The approach is evaluated across three tasks (named-entity recognition, sentiment classification, and image classification), demonstrating significant cost reductions compared to full human annotation while improving accuracy relative to both crowd-based and purely machine learning approaches.

The paper makes a compelling contribution to the intersection of active learning, crowdsourcing, and online learning. It addresses a real-world problem where deploying an accurate system from scratch is challenging due to resource constraints, and it does so with a principled theoretical framework that extends beyond previous work. The authors clearly distinguish their approach from related work in online learning, active learning, and active classification, particularly noting that their method maintains high accuracy from the beginning rather than asymptotically improving.

**Quality:** The technical approach is sound, with appropriate theoretical grounding in Bayesian decision theory. The implementation of Monte Carlo Tree Search with progressive widening to handle continuous time variables is well-motivated. The experimental results are robust, with clear comparisons against multiple baselines (human n-query, online learning, threshold baseline, and LENSE) across three distinct tasks. The authors provide sufficient detail for reproducibility, including open-source code and dataset availability. A notable strength is the demonstration that the system reduces cost by more than an order of magnitude on NER while improving F1 by 8% over single-human labeling and 28% over online learning. The paper acknowledges limitations, such as the fact that the approach performs less well on Sentiment and Faces tasks compared to NER, and attributes this to the presence of dominant classes in NER.

**Clarity:** While the core ideas are well-conceived, the paper suffers from some clarity issues that hinder understanding. The example in the paragraph starting at Line 154 is overly abstract and would benefit from a concrete illustration with specific query examples, crowd responses, and timing information to demonstrate how the system operates in practice. The inconsistent naming of the threshold baseline (alternating between "entropic" and "threshold" in Tables 2 and 3) creates confusion for readers. The placement of related work in Section 5 rather than a dedicated Section 6 is also problematic, as it disrupts the logical flow of the paper.

**Originality:** The paper's core contribution lies in formalizing the on-the-job learning setting as a stochastic game and developing a Bayesian decision-theoretic framework to optimize query strategies. This extends beyond previous work in active learning and online learning by maintaining high accuracy from the beginning of deployment while simultaneously improving the model through test-time learning. The authors effectively distinguish their approach from related work, particularly noting that while Legion:AR operates in a similar setting, it lacks the theoretical foundation that this paper provides. The integration of latency and cost considerations into the decision-making process represents a novel contribution.

**Significance:** The problem addressed is highly significant for real-world AI deployment scenarios where labeled data is scarce or expensive to obtain. The paper demonstrates tangible benefits across multiple tasks, with substantial cost reductions (more than an order of magnitude on NER) while simultaneously improving accuracy. The open-source implementation (LENS) further enhances the significance by enabling practitioners to adopt and build upon the approach. The framework has clear potential for application in domains like disaster response or public opinion monitoring, where rapid deployment of accurate systems is critical.

**Strengths:** The paper presents a well-motivated problem with a theoretically grounded solution that delivers practical benefits. The experimental evaluation is thorough and demonstrates clear advantages over multiple baselines. The open-source implementation enhances reproducibility and adoption potential. The distinction between on-the-job learning and related approaches is well-articulated.

**Weaknesses:** The paper would benefit from greater clarity in explaining the core mechanism of the system. A concrete example with specific query sequences, crowd responses, and timing would greatly enhance understanding. The inconsistent naming of the threshold baseline and misplaced related work section detract from the paper's quality. While the NER results are impressive, the paper could better explain why the approach performs less well on the other two tasks.

**Recommendation:** This is a strong contribution to the field with clear theoretical and practical significance. The paper makes a compelling case for the on-the-job learning framework as a practical solution to a real-world deployment problem. While the current presentation has some clarity issues that should be addressed, the core ideas and results are robust and significant. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to improve clarity and fix the organizational issues noted above. The paper represents a meaningful advancement in the intersection of active learning, crowdsourcing, and real-time AI deployment, with clear potential for practical impact.
