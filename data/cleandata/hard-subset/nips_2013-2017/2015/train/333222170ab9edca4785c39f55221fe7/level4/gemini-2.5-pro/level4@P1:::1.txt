This paper introduces a framework for "on-the-job learning," designed to deploy a high-accuracy system from scratch with no initial training data. The system starts by relying on real-time crowdsourcing to make predictions. As it accumulates labeled data from the crowd's responses, an underlying model is trained online, gradually reducing the system's dependence on human input. The core contribution is a principled formulation of this problem using Bayesian decision theory. The task is modeled as a stochastic game where the system must decide whether to query the crowd for a label, wait for a response, or output a final prediction. This framework allows for a unified treatment of accuracy, latency, and monetary cost. Since computing the optimal policy is intractable, the authors propose an approximate solution based on Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) combined with progressive widening to handle the continuous nature of time. The approach is evaluated on three tasks: named-entity recognition, sentiment classification, and image classification.

**Strengths:**
*   The paper addresses a significant and practical problem: how to build an accurate system when a large labeled dataset is not available beforehand. The "on-the-job" setting is well-motivated and has many potential applications.
*   The formulation of the problem as a stochastic game governed by Bayesian decision theory is elegant and theoretically sound. It provides a principled way to reason about the complex trade-offs between accuracy, cost, and latency, which is a clear improvement over more heuristic-driven approaches.
*   The proposed MCTS-based solution is a sophisticated and suitable method for approximating the policy in the large state space defined by the game.

**Weaknesses and Questions for Clarification:**
The paper is promising, but its presentation lacks some important details, and the empirical evaluation could be strengthened.

*   **Clarity:** Several key aspects of the model and experimental setup need clarification.
    1.  The paper states that `p(r' | x, r, q)` can be computed by marginalizing `y` and `t` from Equation (3). Could the authors please elaborate on this step? The response model `pR(ri | yqi)` is conditioned on the true (but unknown) label `yqi`. It is not immediately clear how `y` is marginalized out to make decisions, as this seems to require integrating over the posterior `p(y | x, ...)` which could be computationally expensive.
    2.  In Algorithm 1, what does the notation `N(σ)` represent? While it appears to be the visit count for state `σ`, an explicit definition would improve clarity.
    3.  The threshold baseline is governed by the heuristic `(1−pθ(yi | x))×0.3^m ≥ 0.98`. Could the authors provide some intuition or justification for the choice of the constants 0.3 and 0.98? Were these values tuned on a development set?

*   **Experimental Evaluation:**
    1.  The empirical results, particularly the performance gap between the full LENSE system and the simpler threshold-based heuristic, are somewhat underwhelming. This raises questions about the practical benefits of the complex MCTS-based game-playing approach over a well-tuned heuristic.
    2.  The strength of the "Online learning" baseline is not well established. Is this model representative of a state-of-the-art supervised system for these tasks? It would be helpful to understand how the performance compares to a powerful, pre-trained offline model. The improvement over the online baseline is largely due to the selective use of human annotation at prediction time. To better understand this contribution, some error analysis would be highly valuable. For instance, what types of errors made by the online learner are corrected by LENSE's targeted queries?

**Conclusion:**
This paper presents a theoretically sound and well-motivated formulation for on-the-job learning, a practical setting where a system must learn and perform simultaneously. The approach of modeling the problem as a stochastic game is compelling. While the empirical results are a mixed bag and several key details require clarification, the paper introduces important ideas and provides a strong foundation for future work in this area. It would be valuable to see this line of research discussed within the community.
