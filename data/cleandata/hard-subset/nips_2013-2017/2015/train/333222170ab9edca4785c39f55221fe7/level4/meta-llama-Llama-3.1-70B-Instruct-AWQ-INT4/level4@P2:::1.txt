This paper proposes a novel framework for on-the-job learning, which enables a high-accuracy system to be deployed starting with zero training examples. The authors model this setting as a stochastic game based on Bayesian decision theory, allowing them to balance latency, cost, and accuracy objectives in a principled way. The paper's main contribution is the development of an approximation algorithm based on Monte Carlo Tree Search to compute the optimal policy, which is intractable to compute exactly.

The paper is well-motivated, and the authors provide a clear overview of the problem and their approach. The technical core of the paper is sound, and the authors provide a thorough explanation of their model and algorithm. The empirical results are promising, showing that the proposed approach can achieve significant cost reductions and accuracy improvements over baseline methods.

However, there are some areas that require clarification or improvement. Firstly, the presentation could benefit from more details on the experimental setup and the choice of hyperparameters. For instance, the authors mention that they used a threshold-based heuristic as a baseline, but they do not provide a clear explanation of how this heuristic was chosen or how the hyperparameters were set.

Additionally, there are some minor errors and inconsistencies in the notation. For example, in Equation (2), the LHS should be conditioned on s, and it is unclear how the y in p(R)(ri | y, q_i) vanishes. Furthermore, the notation N(s) in Algorithm 1 is not clearly defined.

The empirical results are somewhat underwhelming, particularly in comparison to the threshold-based heuristic. However, the authors provide some interesting insights into the behavior of their system, such as the fact that the cost of the system decreases over time as the model improves.

To further strengthen the paper, the authors could provide more error analysis and comparisons to state-of-the-art machine learning systems on the tasks used in the paper. This would help to establish the effectiveness of their approach and provide a clearer understanding of the benefits and limitations of on-the-job learning.

Overall, the paper presents a promising approach to on-the-job learning, and the authors provide a thorough explanation of their model and algorithm. With some minor revisions to address the issues mentioned above, the paper has the potential to make a significant contribution to the field.
