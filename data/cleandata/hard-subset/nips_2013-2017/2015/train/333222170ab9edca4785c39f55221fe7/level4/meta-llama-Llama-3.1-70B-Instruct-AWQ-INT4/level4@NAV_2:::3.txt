This paper presents a novel approach for deploying high-accuracy systems with zero training examples by leveraging real-time crowdsourcing and Bayesian decision theory. The authors frame on-the-job learning as a stochastic game, allowing them to balance latency, cost, and accuracy objectives in a principled manner. This formulation enables the system to adaptively query the crowd for uncertain inputs and update its prediction model accordingly.

The paper builds upon previous work in online learning, active learning, and crowdsourcing, but introduces a unique twist by incorporating continuous time and a stochastic game framework. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach, dubbed LENSE, on three datasets: named-entity recognition, sentiment classification, and image classification. The results show that LENSE achieves significant cost reductions and accuracy improvements compared to traditional approaches.

The paper's technical contributions are substantial, particularly in the development of the stochastic game framework and the approximation algorithm based on Monte Carlo Tree Search. The authors also provide a thorough evaluation of their approach, including comparisons with multiple baselines and an analysis of the system's performance over time.

However, the paper's clarity and organization could be improved. The introduction of continuous time in Section 3 is not well-motivated, and the benefits of this addition are not clearly demonstrated in the experimental results. In fact, the "threshold" baseline, which removes the dependence on continuous time, performs comparably to LENSE in many cases. The authors could strengthen the paper by providing a clearer justification for the inclusion of continuous time and highlighting the scenarios in which it provides significant benefits.

Additionally, there are some minor issues with the paper's presentation. The notation could be improved, particularly in equations (2) and (5), where the use of variables is not clearly defined. The description of the threshold baseline could also be clarified, and the results for the 5-vote case should be included for all datasets.

Overall, the paper presents a significant contribution to the field of machine learning, and its technical merits are substantial. With some revisions to address the clarity and presentation issues, the paper has the potential to make a major impact in the field.

Strengths:

* Novel approach to on-the-job learning using stochastic games and Bayesian decision theory
* Significant technical contributions, including the development of the stochastic game framework and approximation algorithm
* Thorough evaluation of the approach, including comparisons with multiple baselines and analysis of performance over time

Weaknesses:

* Clarity and organization could be improved, particularly in the introduction of continuous time
* Benefits of continuous time not clearly demonstrated in experimental results
* Minor issues with notation and presentation

Arguments for acceptance:

* Original and novel approach to on-the-job learning
* Significant technical contributions and thorough evaluation
* Potential to make a major impact in the field

Arguments against acceptance:

* Clarity and organization issues may detract from the paper's impact
