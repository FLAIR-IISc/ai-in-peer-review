The primary contribution of this paper is a more rigorous theoretical foundation for the on-the-job learner introduced in [22]. This learner begins by relying on crowdsourced outputs, but progressively develops its own predictive capabilities, reducing its dependence on crowd queries. The decision of when to query the crowd for a given input is formulated as a decision problem and solved using Monte Carlo Tree Search. 

The presentation is generally clear, though it lacks crucial details in places. The empirical results, when compared to a threshold-based heuristic, are not particularly compelling. Nevertheless, the approach appears reasonable and warrants further exploration.

Please address the following points for clarification:

1. Equation (2) is unclear. The left-hand side should be conditioned on *s*. Additionally, the role and disappearance of *y* in *p{R}(ri | y, q_i)* are not readily apparent.
2. What is the definition of *N(s)* in Algorithm 1?
3. How were the values of 0.3 and 0.88 for the threshold baseline determined?
4. I lack specific knowledge of the tasks employed in this study. Are the “online” systems used as baselines the state-of-the-art machine learning systems for these tasks? If not, please explain why not. If they are, then any performance gains achieved are attributable solely to the benefits of human annotation; examples of such cases would be insightful. Error analysis would be particularly helpful in understanding these results. 

This paper offers a theoretical framework for an "on the job learner" that evolves from a purely crowdsourced system to a learned model querying the crowd only for inputs where it lacks confidence. While the empirical results are somewhat mixed, this work has the potential to stimulate valuable discussion in this area.
