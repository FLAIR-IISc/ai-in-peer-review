Summary and relation to prior work
This paper addresses the problem of deploying high-accuracy classifiers starting from zero labeled examples by using “on-the-job learning.” For each incoming input, the system decides which parts to query from the crowd, waits for responses as needed, and returns a prediction when sufficiently confident. Over time, as the model improves, reliance on the crowd decreases. The approach formalizes this setting as a stochastic game grounded in Bayesian decision theory, explicitly trading off accuracy, latency, and monetary cost. Because computing the optimal policy is intractable, the authors use Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) with progressive widening to handle continuous time. They evaluate on three tasks (NER, sentiment, image classification) against human n-vote baselines, an online learning baseline, and a heuristic “threshold” baseline.

Compared with prior work in online learning and active learning, the system differs by actively seeking information before committing to a prediction, and by optimizing query timing and parallelism to manage response noise and latency. Relative to active classification, the policy is not static and the model continues to learn during test time. The emphasis on real-time decision making under latency and cost constraints is novel and interesting.

Strengths
- Problem importance: A compelling and practical setting that many applications face when labeled data is scarce but decisions must be accurate immediately.
- Principled formulation: The Bayesian decision-theoretic framework cleanly balances accuracy, time, and cost; it captures intuitive behaviors (e.g., redundant labels for robustness, parallel queries for latency).
- Algorithmic approach: MCTS with progressive widening is a reasonable approximation for the intractable game tree and continuous-time responses.
- Empirical evaluation: Multiple datasets, comparisons to human majority vote and machine baselines, and a real-time crowdsourcing setup (retainer model) strengthen the case. Reproducibility via open-source code and CodaLab is a plus.
- Comparative insight: It is interesting in Table 2 that LENSE outperforms the 3-vote baseline but underperforms the 5-vote baseline. This highlights the trade-off between de-noising via additional human consensus versus the proposed decision-theoretic approach.

Weaknesses and clarity issues
- Example clarity: The example starting at Line 154 is abstract and hard to follow. For readability, please include a concrete, fully worked query sequence with realistic response labels and timestamps, showing how the posterior evolves, when the system waits versus queries in parallel, and when it stops.
- Baseline naming: In Tables 2 and 3, the “threshold” baseline is sometimes labeled “entropic” and elsewhere “threshold.” Please standardize terminology throughout the paper and captions to avoid confusion.
- Organization of related work: I do not understand why the related work is in Section 5; its placement after experiments disrupts the flow. Consider moving it earlier (e.g., immediately after the introduction or before experiments) so readers can better contextualize the technical contributions.
- Broken reference: Line 086 shows “Figure ??”; please fix this unresolved figure reference.
- Exposition: Some explanations are opaque. Certain modeling choices (e.g., when to batch vs. sequence queries, how the utility’s weights are selected) would benefit from clearer narrative and a running example, as above.

Evaluation by review criteria
- Quality: The formulation is technically sound and the MCTS-based approximation is appropriate for the problem’s complexity. The experiments across three tasks are informative, and the comparisons to both human and machine baselines are useful. However, some presentation choices hinder full transparency (e.g., the abstract example, naming inconsistency).
- Clarity: The paper is generally well-structured, but the example around Line 154 is difficult to parse, the baseline naming is inconsistent, and there is at least one broken figure reference. These issues reduce readability.
- Originality: Modeling on-the-job learning as a stochastic game with continuous-time decision-making and optimizing latency/cost alongside accuracy is novel relative to prior online/active learning and active classification work.
- Significance: The setting is practical and broadly relevant. The empirical results suggest meaningful cost reductions over full human annotation and improvements over single-vote and online learning baselines. The observation that LENSE beats 3-vote but not 5-vote provides actionable insight about when additional consensus may be more effective than adaptive querying.

Specific comments and suggestions
- Line 086: “Figure ??” appears; please correct the reference.
- Line 154: Replace the abstract description with a specific, step-by-step example including actual token labels, returned crowd responses (correct and incorrect), their timestamps, and the resulting system actions and utility.
- Tables 2 and 3: Use a single, consistent name for the heuristic baseline (either “threshold” or “entropic”) in both text and tables, and clarify briefly in the caption how it operates (e.g., the 0.3m factor and lack of time reasoning).
- Related work section: Move it earlier and clearly articulate how the paper differs from online learning, online active learning, active classification, and systems like Legion:AR.

Pros for acceptance
- Addresses an important and realistic problem setting with immediate practical utility.
- Principled Bayesian decision-theoretic formulation that integrates accuracy, cost, and latency.
- Reasonable approximation via MCTS/progressive widening for continuous-time decisions.
- Strong empirical comparisons, including against human consensus baselines; open-source and reproducible.

Cons for acceptance
- Some explanations are opaque; the key example is hard to follow.
- Inconsistent baseline naming and a broken figure reference detract from clarity.
- The observation that LENSE underperforms 5-vote raises questions about when human consensus may be preferable; further discussion would help guide practitioners.

Overall assessment
A compelling problem and a principled approach with solid empirical evidence, but the paper would benefit from clearer exposition, consistent terminology, and corrected references. The timing and latency optimization aspects are novel and interesting.
