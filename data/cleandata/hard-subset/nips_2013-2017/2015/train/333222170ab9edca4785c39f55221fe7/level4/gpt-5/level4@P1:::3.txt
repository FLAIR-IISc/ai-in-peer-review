This paper proposes an on-the-job learning framework that combines real-time crowdsourcing with an incrementally trained model to deliver high-accuracy predictions from the start, even with zero initial training data. The key idea is to rely on the crowd heavily early on, learn from their (noisy) labels, and gradually reduce crowd queries as the model’s confidence grows. The authors formalize this as a stochastic game (based on a CRF for structured prediction) between the system and the crowd, using Bayesian decision theory to balance accuracy, latency, and cost. The system issues token-level queries asynchronously, reasons over response noise and delays, and decides when to wait for more information or return a prediction. Because exact optimization is intractable, they approximate the optimal policy using Monte Carlo Tree Search with progressive widening to handle continuous time. Experiments on NER, sentiment classification, and face identification compare four methods: human majority vote with n workers (n ∈ {1,3,5}), online learning with oracle labels, a “threshold” baseline (the authors’ approach without continuous-time reasoning), and the full method (LENSE). The full method generally matches or exceeds the baselines in accuracy at considerably lower cost, and requires fewer queries over time as the model improves.

Originality and relation to prior work: The work sits at the intersection of online learning, active learning, active classification, crowdsourcing, and structured prediction. Unlike online/active learning, the system actively seeks information before predicting to maintain high accuracy from the outset. Compared to active classification, the policy is not static and improves at test time based on observed responses. Prior systems such as Legion:AR implement related ideas in specific domains; this paper provides a more general, principled formulation and algorithm, and handles structured outputs via a CRF.

Significance: The contribution is timely and practically relevant. It offers a unified decision-theoretic treatment of accuracy/cost/latency trade-offs, and the empirical results against reasonable baselines are compelling. The ability to deploy accurate systems immediately, then taper crowd use as the model learns, is valuable in settings where data collection is expensive or urgent.

Quality: The methodological core is sound and well-motivated. Modeling the environment (noisy responses and response times), defining a principled utility, and approximating the optimal policy via MCTS are appropriate choices. Empirically, LENSE outperforms online learning (which cannot query pre-prediction) and provides strong performance relative to human-majority baselines at substantially reduced cost.

Clarity: This is where the paper is weakest. The introduction of continuous time (midway through Section 3) substantially increases conceptual and algorithmic complexity, but delivers limited measurable benefit in the reported experiments. The “threshold” baseline—essentially the same model without continuous-time reasoning—often performs nearly as well and, in some cases, better in latency. In particular:
- In NER, the threshold baseline achieves significantly lower latency while obtaining roughly equivalent F1.
- In face identification, the threshold baseline performs slightly better (still roughly equivalent overall).
The paper attributes the face-ID result to minimal label interactions (single-label task), but the NER gains from sequence structure appear modest. For many practical applications, the simpler threshold baseline may suffice—and it is already a substantial advance over pure machine- or crowd-only approaches. Presenting the discrete-time method as the core, with continuous time as an extension, would likely improve readability and adoption.

Results reporting and presentation:
- The introduction claims the model “beats human performance,” but this is not true vs. 5-vote for NER. Please qualify the claim.
- The 5-vote baseline should be included consistently (it is missing for Face-ID and Sentiment in parts of the right halves of Tables 2/3 and Figure 4). It should also be bolded where it is best.
- In Table 2, the best latency for Threshold in NER should be bolded; likewise, LENSE’s best latency in Face-ID should be bolded.
- The Threshold baseline is missing for Sentiment (and thus from Table 3 and Figure 4); it should be included.

Specific methodological clarifications and nits:
- The text discusses a time budget t_deadline (lines 183–187), but it is unclear what deadline, if any, was used in experiments. What exact criterion determined when to return a prediction—an entropy/uncertainty threshold as in the baseline, a fixed deadline, or the utility maximization itself?
- When defining the environment model p(y, r, t | x, q, s), ensure all conditioning variables are explicit wherever the distribution is referenced.
- In the notation around equation 5, using q in F(q) is confusing because q also denotes a query. Please rename the distribution to avoid ambiguity.
- Threshold baseline inequality: I assume the intended condition is (1 − pθ(yi | x)) × 0.3^m ≤ (1 − 0.88), not ≥ 0.88, since increasing m should reduce uncertainty. Please clarify the exact threshold (0.98 vs. 0.88 appear in different places) and the derivation.

Pros for acceptance:
- Original, principled framework for integrating crowd queries with model learning in real time.
- Solid empirical results across three tasks; high accuracy from the first examples; decreasing reliance on the crowd as the model improves.
- Clear demonstration that structured prediction benefits from targeted querying.
- Open-source implementation and reproducibility materials.

Cons / concerns:
- Clarity suffers due to the heavy emphasis on continuous time, which adds complexity with modest demonstrated gains over a simpler threshold policy.
- Inconsistent inclusion and highlighting of baselines (notably 5-vote and Threshold for Sentiment).
- Some presentation and notation issues that could be fixed with careful editing.

Recommendation: Accept. The paper is original, significant, and technically sound, with strong empirical support. To maximize impact, I encourage the authors to (i) present the discrete-time core method clearly and position continuous time as an extension; (ii) report and highlight all baselines consistently (including 5-vote and Threshold for Sentiment); and (iii) clarify the decision criterion for returning predictions and fix the noted notation and inequality issues. These revisions would greatly improve clarity and facilitate broader adoption.
