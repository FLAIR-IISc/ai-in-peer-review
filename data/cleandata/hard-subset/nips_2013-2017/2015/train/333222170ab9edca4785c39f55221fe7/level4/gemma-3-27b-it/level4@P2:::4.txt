This paper introduces a framework termed “on-the-job learning” for deploying high-accuracy systems without initial training data. The core idea is to combine model prediction with real-time crowdsourcing, querying human annotators only when uncertain about parts of an input, and progressively reducing reliance on crowdsourcing as the model improves. This is formally modeled as a stochastic game using Bayesian decision theory, with a Monte Carlo Tree Search approximation for policy optimization, balancing latency, cost, and accuracy. The authors demonstrate the approach on named-entity recognition, sentiment classification, and image classification, achieving significant cost reductions and performance gains compared to full annotation and online learning.

This work builds on existing research in online learning [7], active learning [8, 9, 10, 11], and crowdsourcing [3, 4, 25, 26]. Unlike traditional active learning, which focuses on selecting informative examples for labeling a static dataset, this approach actively seeks information *during* prediction. It also differs from pure online learning by proactively querying for labels to maintain high accuracy even with limited initial data. Related work in structured prediction [14, 27, 28] highlights the benefits of modeling dependencies between elements, which is leveraged in their CRF-based implementation. 

**Strengths:**

*   **Novel Framework:** The paper proposes a compelling and potentially impactful framework for bridging the gap between purely supervised learning and purely crowdsourced systems. The formalization as a stochastic game provides a principled approach to balancing competing objectives.
*   **Strong Empirical Results:** The experimental results on NER are particularly impressive, demonstrating an order of magnitude cost reduction while improving performance. Positive results are also shown on sentiment classification and image classification.
*   **Practical Implementation:** The open-source implementation (LENSE) and detailed experimental setup contribute to the reproducibility and potential adoption of the approach.
*   **Clear Motivation:** The introduction clearly articulates the need for this type of system, especially in scenarios where immediate deployment is crucial and labeled data is scarce.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Clarity of Application to Non-Structured Tasks:** The application of the algorithm to the face recognition task (a non-structured prediction problem) is not clearly explained. It’s unclear how the querying mechanism and the utility function, designed for sequential dependencies, apply in this context. Was the entire instance queried at once, effectively resembling a simple active learning setup?
*   **Parameter Sensitivity:** The threshold baseline relies on two parameters whose selection process is not sufficiently justified. A sensitivity analysis demonstrating the robustness of the results to variations in these parameters would strengthen the findings.
*   **Missing Baseline:** A comparison against a standard uncertainty sampling baseline, adapted for online query selection, would be valuable. This would help clarify the advantages of the proposed Bayesian decision-theoretic approach.
*   **Relationship to Active Online Learning:** The authors emphasize the distinction from active online learning, but a simplified adaptation of active learning could potentially achieve competitive results within this framework. A more in-depth discussion acknowledging the connection and highlighting the unique benefits of their approach is needed.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and well-motivated framework for on-the-job learning.
*   The empirical results demonstrate significant practical benefits, particularly in the NER task.
*   The code release promotes reproducibility and further research.
*   The formalization using Bayesian decision theory provides a solid theoretical foundation.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The application to non-structured prediction tasks lacks clarity.
*   The sensitivity of the threshold baseline to parameter choices is not addressed.
*   The absence of a standard uncertainty sampling baseline weakens the comparative analysis.
*   The discussion of related work could more thoroughly address the connection to active online learning.

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a promising new approach to on-the-job learning. While some aspects of the application to non-structured tasks and the sensitivity of baseline parameters could be further clarified, the novelty, strong empirical results, and potential impact of the work suggest that it is a worthwhile contribution to the field. I recommend **acceptance** contingent on the authors addressing the weaknesses outlined above, particularly providing a clearer explanation of the face recognition setup and justifying the parameter choices for the threshold baseline.
