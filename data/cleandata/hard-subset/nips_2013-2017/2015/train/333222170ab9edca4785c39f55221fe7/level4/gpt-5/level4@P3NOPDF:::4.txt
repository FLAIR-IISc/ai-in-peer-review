This paper introduces an “on-the-job learning” framework in which a model can query annotators between receiving an input and producing a prediction. The idea is interesting and potentially impactful, especially for time-constrained or resource-aware prediction settings.

Strengths
- Novelty and motivation: The formulation of interleaving queries with inference is compelling and distinct from standard batch or post-hoc annotation paradigms. The emphasis on temporal decision-making under cost and latency constraints is timely.
- Structured prediction setting: For sequence labeling, the setup is intuitive—querying elements of the sequence and propagating information across positions is natural, and the proposed algorithm is easy to follow in this case.

Concerns and questions
1) Application to non-structured tasks (face recognition):
- It is unclear how the framework is instantiated for face recognition. Was the unit of query simply the entire instance (e.g., “Who is this face?”), or did you decompose the image into parts/attributes analogous to sequence positions?
- In a non-structured setting, the model cannot propagate constraints between adjacent positions as in a CRF or HMM. How, then, is your utility function defined and justified? If utilities rely on interactions across positions, they may not translate directly to independent examples. Please clarify the adaptation and provide a formal description for the unstructured case.

2) Utility and decision policy:
- If the utility relies on downstream propagation (e.g., expected error reduction through structured dependencies), explain how this generalizes to single-instance classification. If instead you use a per-instance uncertainty or value-of-information criterion, please specify the exact form and any approximations.

3) Threshold baseline:
- The threshold baseline uses two parameters that appear ad hoc. How were these values chosen (validation set, heuristic, grid search)? Provide a sensitivity analysis: performance as a function of each threshold, ideally with confidence intervals, to show robustness.
- If the thresholds materially affect outcomes, consider reporting results across a reasonable range or selecting them via a principled criterion (e.g., optimizing F1 under a latency/budget constraint).

4) Additional baselines:
- Please include an uncertainty sampling baseline analogous to active learning: at each step, query the instance (or component) with highest uncertainty (e.g., margin, entropy), possibly with a budget or time constraint. This would help disentangle the benefits of your decision-theoretic formulation from simpler, widely used heuristics.
- If applicable, compare against cost-sensitive or budgeted active learning methods adapted to the streaming/online setting.

5) Relation to (online) active learning:
- The paper emphasizes distinctions from active online learning, yet the gap seems narrower than suggested. One can adapt active learning to the sequential decision setting by treating the active set as the evolving sequence of candidate queries over time, updating the model as responses arrive, and outputting a final prediction at the end of the query budget or deadline.
- While such a baseline would not fully capture your temporal trade-offs (e.g., dynamically balancing expected value of additional queries against remaining time), it would benefit from established theory for query selection and could be a strong comparator. A careful discussion or empirical comparison would sharpen the contribution.

6) Scope and applicability:
- As presented, the approach appears most natural for structured prediction with interpretable local queries. Please clarify the conditions under which it applies effectively to unstructured tasks. Are there assumptions about decomposability of the loss, availability of localized queries, or the ability to simulate partial supervision?
- If the framework requires a factorized representation to compute utilities efficiently, state this explicitly and discuss limitations.

Requested clarifications and improvements
- Provide a precise description of the face recognition instantiation: query space, response format (labels vs attributes), timing model, and how responses update beliefs. Include implementation details for computing utilities and for integrating human responses into the model.
- Add a sensitivity analysis for the threshold baseline parameters and, if feasible, for key hyperparameters in your decision policy (e.g., cost of delay, query cost).
- Include an uncertainty sampling baseline and, if possible, a simple online active learning adaptation with model updates upon receiving responses and a single final prediction. Report results under comparable time/budget constraints.
- Discuss computational complexity and latency: how quickly can the policy evaluate utilities and select a query in real time? Are there approximations or caching strategies?
- Clarify the annotation model: response times, noise assumptions, and how these are learned or estimated. If you assume i.i.d. annotator noise or fixed latency distributions, state and justify these assumptions.

Overall assessment
The paper puts forward a promising and novel framework for interactive, time-aware querying during prediction, with a clear motivation and an elegant instantiation for structured prediction. However, key details for unstructured tasks are missing, making it difficult to assess generality. The choice and tuning of baselines—particularly the threshold baseline—could be more principled, and the absence of standard active learning–style baselines leaves open whether the proposed policy substantially outperforms simpler approaches.

With clearer exposition of the non-structured instantiation, a stronger baseline suite (including uncertainty sampling and an online active learning variant), and sensitivity analyses for thresholding and policy parameters, the contribution would be significantly strengthened. As it stands, the framework appears novel but somewhat restricted in applicability, and several methodological details require clarification before I can fully evaluate its impact.
