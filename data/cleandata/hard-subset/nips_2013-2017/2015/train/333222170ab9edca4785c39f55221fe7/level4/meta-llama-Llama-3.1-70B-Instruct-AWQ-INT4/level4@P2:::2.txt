This paper tackles the problem of creating high-accuracy classifiers beginning with zero labeled examples. A component problem in this paper is handling noisy crowd respondents, and while this topic has been considered from many angles in the literature, the timing and latency optimization factors of this paper are novel and interesting.

The authors propose a framework called on-the-job learning, which uses Bayesian decision theory to balance latency, cost, and accuracy objectives in a principled way. They model on-the-job learning as a stochastic game based on a CRF prediction model and use Monte Carlo Tree Search to approximate the optimal policy. The paper presents an evaluation of the proposed approach on three datasets: named-entity recognition, sentiment classification, and image classification.

The results show that the proposed approach, LENSE, outperforms the baseline methods in terms of accuracy and cost. Specifically, on the NER task, LENSE achieves an F1 score of 88.4% at a cost that is more than an order of magnitude lower than the cost of achieving comparable quality using the 5-vote approach. The paper also presents an analysis of the performance of LENSE over time, showing that the cost of the system steadily reduces as the model improves.

Strengths of the paper include the novelty of the problem setting, the thorough evaluation of the proposed approach, and the presentation of a compelling comparison between the LENSE system and the baseline methods. However, the explanations in the paper can be somewhat opaque, and the example given in the paragraph starting at Line 154 is rather abstract and difficult to follow. It would be helpful to use a specific query example with crowd responses and times similar to what they would be in a real case to illustrate the approach.

Additionally, there are some minor issues with the presentation of the results. For instance, in Tables 2 and 3, the threshold baseline is sometimes referred to as "entropic" and sometimes as "threshold." It would be helpful to use consistent terminology throughout the paper.

In terms of related work, the paper presents a thorough discussion of the relevant literature, including online learning, active learning, active classification, crowdsourcing, and structured prediction. However, it is not entirely clear why the related work is presented in Section 5, rather than in a separate section or as part of the introduction.

Overall, the paper presents a compelling problem and a novel approach to solving it. While there are some minor issues with the presentation, the paper is well-written and easy to follow. The results are thorough and well-presented, and the analysis of the performance of LENSE over time is particularly insightful.

Arguments for acceptance:

* The paper presents a novel and compelling problem setting.
* The proposed approach, LENSE, is thoroughly evaluated and shown to outperform the baseline methods.
* The paper presents a compelling comparison between the LENSE system and the baseline methods.
* The results are thorough and well-presented.

Arguments against acceptance:

* The explanations in the paper can be somewhat opaque.
* The example given in the paragraph starting at Line 154 is rather abstract and difficult to follow.
* There are some minor issues with the presentation of the results.
* The related work is not entirely clear and could be presented in a separate section or as part of the introduction.
