This paper tackles the problem of creating high-accuracy classifiers beginning with zero labeled examples. A component problem in this paper is handling noisy crowd respondents, and while this topic has been considered from many angles in the literature, the timing and latency optimization factors of this paper are novel and interesting.

The authors propose a framework called on-the-job learning, in which a system produces high-quality results from the start without requiring a trained model. When a new input arrives, the system can choose to asynchronously query the crowd on parts of the input it is uncertain about. After collecting enough evidence, the system makes a prediction. The goal is to maintain high accuracy by initially using the crowd as a crutch, but gradually becoming more self-sufficient as the model improves.

The authors model on-the-job learning as a stochastic game based on Bayesian decision theory, which allows them to balance latency, cost, and accuracy objectives in a principled way. They develop an approximation based on Monte Carlo Tree Search to compute the optimal policy, as the exact computation is intractable.

The authors evaluate their approach on three datasets: named-entity recognition, sentiment classification, and image classification. They compare their approach to several baselines, including human performance, online learning, and a threshold baseline. The results show that their approach outperforms the baselines in terms of accuracy and cost.

One of the strengths of this paper is its ability to balance multiple objectives, including latency, cost, and accuracy. The use of Bayesian decision theory and Monte Carlo Tree Search allows the authors to reason about these objectives in a principled way. The evaluation of the approach on multiple datasets also adds to the paper's strength.

However, there are some areas where the paper could be improved. The example given in the paragraph starting at Line 154 is rather abstract and difficult to follow. For easy readability, it might be useful to use a specific query example with crowd responses and times similar to what they would be in a real case. Additionally, the related work section could be improved by providing more context and explanations for the different approaches mentioned.
