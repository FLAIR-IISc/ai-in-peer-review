The paper introduces “on the job learning,” a method where annotators are prompted for information between receiving an example and generating a prediction. While the algorithmic process is well-defined for structured prediction tasks involving sequential queries, its application to face recognition remains unclear. Specifically, it is not evident what constitutes a query in this non-structured context – was the entire instance queried? Given the lack of information propagation between examples in this setting, the proposed utility function’s rationale is questionable.

The threshold baseline employs two parameters whose selection appears arbitrary. The justification for these values, and an analysis of their impact on performance, are missing. Including a baseline based on uncertainty sampling, as commonly used in active learning, would strengthen the evaluation.

The authors emphasize the distinction between their approach and active online learning; however, active learning techniques could be readily adapted to this sequential domain. For instance, the model could be updated incrementally upon receiving responses, outputting a prediction at the sequence’s completion. Though this adaptation wouldn’t fully capture the task’s temporal aspect, it would allow leveraging established theory for query set selection.

Overall, the framework appears novel, but lacks clarity in certain areas and appears relatively limited in its generalizability.
