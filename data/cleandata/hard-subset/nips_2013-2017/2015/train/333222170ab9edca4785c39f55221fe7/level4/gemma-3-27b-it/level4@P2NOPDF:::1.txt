This paper presents a theoretically grounded formulation for an “on-the-job” learner, building upon the concept introduced in [22]. The approach begins by relying on crowdsourced labels for all predictions, and progressively shifts towards self-reliance, querying the crowd only selectively. This decision of when to query is formalized as a sequential decision problem solved using Monte Carlo Tree Search.

The presentation is generally clear, though lacking in specific details in places. While the empirical results demonstrate a reasonable approach compared to a threshold-based heuristic, the gains are not substantial. Nevertheless, the concept is promising and warrants further exploration.

I have several questions and requests for clarification that would strengthen the paper:

1. Equation (2) requires revision. The left-hand side appears to be missing a conditioning on the state *s*. Furthermore, the variable *y* disappears within the probability term *p{R}(ri | y, q_i)*; please explain how this occurs and ensure the equation is mathematically sound.

2. Algorithm 1 utilizes *N(s)*. Please define this function and its purpose within the algorithm.

3. The threshold baseline utilizes values of 0.3 and 0.88. Please detail how these specific thresholds were determined. What methodology was used to select these values – were they tuned on a validation set, or chosen based on prior knowledge?

4. I am not fully conversant with the tasks employed in the evaluation. Are the "online" systems used as a comparison truly state-of-the-art machine learning models for these tasks? If not, the observed gains from incorporating human annotation may be less significant. If they *are* state-of-the-art, then any improvements attributable to human input are particularly noteworthy. Could the authors provide specific examples where human annotation demonstrably improves performance beyond existing machine learning techniques? A more detailed error analysis would be extremely valuable in understanding the benefits of this approach, specifically identifying cases where the learner effectively leverages the crowd and where it does not. 

In summary, this paper offers a valuable theoretical treatment of an on-the-job learner, effectively bridging the gap between pure crowdsourcing and learned models. The empirical results, while modest, provide a foundation for future research. Addressing the questions above would significantly enhance the clarity and impact of this work and encourage further investigation into this promising line of inquiry.
