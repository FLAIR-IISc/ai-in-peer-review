

This paper introduces "on-the-job learning," a principled framework for deploying accurate prediction systems starting with zero training examples. The authors model the process as a stochastic game between a system and a crowd, where the system strategically queries the crowd to resolve uncertainty while incrementally improving its model. The key innovation is using Bayesian decision theory to balance accuracy, latency, and cost, with an approximation algorithm based on Monte Carlo Tree Search to handle the intractable optimal policy computation. The authors evaluate their approach (called LENSE) on three tasks—named-entity recognition, sentiment classification, and image classification—demonstrating significant cost reductions (over an order of magnitude for NER) while improving accuracy relative to both crowdsourcing and online learning baselines.

The paper makes a meaningful contribution to the intersection of online learning, active learning, and crowdsourcing. It addresses a practical gap: most existing approaches require substantial initial labeled data or rely on static policies, whereas on-the-job learning maintains high accuracy from the first prediction while gradually reducing reliance on crowdsourcing. The connection to previous work is well-established, particularly in distinguishing from active classification (which uses static policies) and online learning (which doesn't actively seek information before prediction). The authors also provide a thoughtful comparison to related work in crowdsourcing (e.g., Flock, TurKontrol) and structured prediction.

The strengths of this work are significant. The core idea is novel and well-motivated, addressing a genuine practical challenge in deploying machine learning systems. The theoretical foundation using Bayesian decision theory is sound and provides a principled way to balance competing objectives. The empirical results are impressive, showing clear advantages over multiple baselines on all three tasks, with particularly compelling results on NER (88.4% F1 with over 10x cost reduction compared to 5-vote crowdsourcing). The open-source implementation enhances reproducibility, and the thorough evaluation across multiple tasks and baselines demonstrates robustness.

However, the paper's clarity could be substantially improved. The introduction of continuous time (in Section 3) is poorly motivated and significantly complicates the model without clear performance benefits. The "threshold" baseline (which removes continuous time) performs nearly as well as the full model in most cases (e.g., similar F1 on NER, better latency on NER), yet the paper does not adequately acknowledge this. The authors claim the continuous time component is necessary for "adaptive" querying, but Table 2 shows the threshold baseline achieves comparable results with lower latency on NER. This suggests the continuous time component is more complex than necessary for most practical applications.

Several specific issues undermine clarity:
1. The 5-vote human baseline is missing for Face-ID and Sentiment tasks in key tables (Tables 2, 3) and figures (Figure 4), despite being the best-performing method on NER. This omission makes it difficult to assess the full context of the results.
2. The paper claims to "beat human performance" on NER, but the 5-vote baseline (which represents human performance) achieves higher F1 than LENSE, contradicting this claim.
3. The description of the threshold baseline (lines 246-252) contains a critical error: the condition should be (1 - pθ(yi|x)) × 0.3^m ≤ (1 - 0.88), not ≥ 0.88, as increasing m reduces uncertainty.
4. Equation (2) should include the time variable s (p(y,r,t|x,q,s)), and Equation (5) uses confusing notation (q in F(q) represents a distribution, not a query).

The paper would benefit from reframing its core contribution. The fundamental insight—using crowdsourcing to bootstrap a model that gradually reduces its reliance on human input—is valuable and could be presented more clearly without the continuous time component. The continuous time extension could then be presented as a minor optimization rather than an essential part of the core method.

The authors also understate the strength of their threshold baseline, which achieves nearly identical performance to the full model with much simpler implementation. For practical applications, the threshold method would likely be preferred due to its simplicity and lower latency. The paper would have greater impact if it clearly positioned the threshold method as the primary contribution and presented continuous time as a secondary optimization.

Overall, this is a strong paper with significant potential impact. The core idea is original, the results are compelling, and the theoretical foundation is sound. While the continuous time component adds unnecessary complexity without clear benefits, the underlying approach is valuable and well-executed. With clearer presentation of the core method and better acknowledgment of the threshold baseline's strengths, this work would be even stronger. Given its technical soundness, novelty, and significance, I recommend acceptance.
