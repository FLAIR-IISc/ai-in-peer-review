The authors introduce a setting termed "on-the-job learning," where an algorithm can query an annotator after receiving an input but before producing a prediction.

While the algorithm's application to structured prediction tasks is clear (i.e., querying elements of a sequence), its use in the face recognition task is not well-explained. It is unclear if entire instances were queried. In such a non-structured setting, information would not propagate between adjacent examples, which calls into question the applicability of the proposed utility function.

The threshold baseline relies on two parameters whose values appear arbitrary. The paper provides no justification for their selection, nor does it include a sensitivity analysis. A comparison against an uncertainty sampling baseline, which is common in active learning, would also be informative.

Finally, the authors emphasize the distinction between their setting and active online learning. However, this distinction may be overstated, as active learning could be adapted to this domain by treating the sequence as the active set under consideration. For instance, the model could be updated after receiving query responses and before outputting its final prediction. Although such an adaptation might not fully capture the task's temporal nature, it would allow the authors to leverage the extensive theoretical work from active learning for selecting the query set.

In summary, the paper presents a seemingly novel framework, but its applicability appears restricted, and crucial details regarding its implementation and experimental comparison are unclear.
