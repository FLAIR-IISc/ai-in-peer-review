The authors describe a scenario termed "on the job learning," where a model can query an annotator between receiving an example and making a prediction. While the algorithm's mechanics are clear for structured prediction tasks involving queries on sequence elements, its application to the face recognition task is not. It is unclear what is being queried—is it the entire instance? In a non-structured setting like this, where information does not propagate between adjacent examples, the justification for the proposed utility function is weak.

The experimental evaluation could be improved. The threshold baseline utilizes two parameters whose values appear arbitrary. The authors should justify their choice and provide a sensitivity analysis. Furthermore, including an uncertainty sampling baseline, a common technique in active learning, would provide a stronger point of comparison.

The paper attempts to distinguish this setting from active online learning. However, standard active learning could be readily adapted to this problem by treating the sequence as the active set. For instance, a model could be updated with annotation responses before making a final prediction. Although this would not fully capture the temporal nature of the task, it would allow the rich body of theory from active learning to be leveraged for selecting the query set. A more thorough discussion of this connection is warranted.

In conclusion, while the framework is presented as novel, several details are unclear, and its applicability appears restricted. The paper would be strengthened by clarifying its application to non-structured tasks and providing a more robust experimental comparison.
