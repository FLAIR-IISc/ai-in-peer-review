

This paper introduces "on-the-job learning," a framework for deploying high-accuracy AI systems starting with zero training examples by strategically using real-time crowdsourcing to resolve uncertainty during prediction. The authors model the problem as a stochastic game based on Bayesian decision theory, which allows principled trade-offs between latency, cost, and accuracy. They develop a Monte Carlo Tree Search approximation to handle the intractable optimal policy computation and evaluate their approach on named-entity recognition, sentiment classification, and image classification tasks. The results demonstrate significant cost reductions (over an order of magnitude for NER) compared to full human annotation while improving accuracy over both pure ML and crowdsourcing approaches.

The paper relates well to prior work at NIPS, particularly drawing connections to active learning (Gao & Koller, 2007), online learning (Cesa-Bianchi & Lugosi, 2006), and crowdsourcing (Bernstein et al., 2010). It extends the Legion:AR work (Lasecki et al., 2013) by providing a theoretically grounded framework rather than just an implementation.

Strengths:
- The theoretical foundation using Bayesian decision theory for on-the-job learning is novel and well-motivated, providing a principled approach to balancing multiple objectives that previous work lacked.
- The empirical evaluation across three distinct tasks demonstrates consistent improvements in both cost efficiency and accuracy compared to multiple baselines, with particularly strong results on NER.
- The implementation details and open-source release of LENSE (Learning from Expensive Noisy Slow Experts) enhance reproducibility.
- The paper addresses important practical considerations like worker reliability (70% accuracy) and response time modeling, which are crucial for real-world deployment.

Weaknesses:
- The presentation of the mathematical formulation (Section 3) contains several ambiguities. Equation (2) should explicitly condition the expected accuracy on the query history (s), and the derivation of the response model (p_R(r_i | y_{q_i})) is not fully explained, particularly how the true label y_{q_i} is handled in the marginalization.
- Algorithm 1 references N(s) without defining this notation, which is critical for understanding the progressive widening implementation.
- The threshold baseline (Section 5) uses parameters 0.3 and 0.88 without justification; the paper should clarify how these values were derived from the data or prior work.
- The comparison to "online learning" baselines requires more context; the paper should clarify whether these represent the state-of-the-art machine learning approaches for these tasks, as the improvements could be primarily attributed to human annotation rather than the on-the-job learning framework.

Quality: The technical approach is sound, with appropriate use of Bayesian decision theory and Monte Carlo Tree Search. The experimental design is rigorous, with multiple baselines and controlled crowdsourcing setup. However, the theoretical analysis of the approximation error and the connection to the intractability of the optimal policy could be strengthened.

Clarity: The paper is generally well-organized, but several sections (particularly the mathematical formulation) would benefit from additional detail and clarification. The presentation of the game tree and utility function could be more accessible to readers unfamiliar with stochastic games.

Originality: The paper makes a significant contribution by formalizing on-the-job learning as a stochastic game with Bayesian decision theory, which distinguishes it from prior active learning and online learning work. The connection to the Legion:AR implementation (Lasecki et al., 2013) is acknowledged, but the theoretical foundation here is substantially novel.

Significance: The framework addresses an important practical problem in deploying AI systems with limited initial data. The demonstrated cost reductions (over an order of magnitude for NER) and accuracy improvements (8% F1 over single-human labeling) suggest tangible real-world impact. The approach could be particularly valuable for applications requiring immediate deployment with limited initial data, such as disaster response systems.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Novel theoretical framework that provides principled trade-offs between latency, cost, and accuracy
- Strong empirical results across multiple tasks with significant cost reduction
- Well-motivated problem statement addressing a practical deployment challenge
- Open-source implementation enhances reproducibility

Arguments against acceptance:
- Several mathematical ambiguities that need clarification before the work can be fully appreciated
- Insufficient justification for key parameters in the threshold baseline
- The connection to state-of-the-art machine learning approaches needs more context

The paper makes a meaningful contribution to the field by providing a theoretically grounded approach to on-the-job learning that balances multiple practical objectives. With minor revisions addressing the mathematical clarity and baseline justification, this work would be a strong candidate for presentation at NIPS.
