- Introduces "on-the-job learning": query annotators between receiving an example and making a prediction; demonstrated on structured tasks (NER, sentiment), but unclear applicability to non-structured tasks (e.g., face recognition).

- Models the problem as a stochastic game using Bayesian decision theory to balance latency, cost, and accuracy.

- Uses a Monte Carlo Tree Search approximation to compute the optimal policy.

- Empirical results show on-the-job learning outperforms machine-only and human-only baselines in both quality and cost.

- Paper is generally well-written with a clear explanation of the framework and applications.

- Threshold baseline in experiments has two seemingly arbitrary parameters; authors should justify their choice and test sensitivity.

- Missing comparison to uncertainty sampling (standard active learning) to better establish novelty.

- Unclear why active online learning approaches couldn’t be adapted to this setting; the distinction from active learning needs stronger justification.

- Applicability may be restricted beyond structured prediction tasks; clarification on non-structured task handling is needed.

- Overall technically sound and novel, but some methodological and applicability details require clarification.

- Arguments for acceptance: novel framework and strong empirical gains in quality and cost.

- Arguments against acceptance: unclear applicability to non-structured tasks, weak baseline justification, and insufficient distinction from active online learning.
