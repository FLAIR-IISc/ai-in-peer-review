

The primary contribution of this paper is a theoretically rigorous formulation of an "on-the-job learner" introduced in [22]. This learner begins by relying entirely on crowd-sourced outputs but progressively shifts toward autonomous predictions while querying the crowd infrequently. The decision to query the crowd for a given input is framed as a sequential decision problem solved using Monte Carlo Tree Search.  

The presentation is generally clear but omits key technical details. The empirical results, compared against a threshold-based heuristic, are modest. Nevertheless, the approach is reasonable and warrants further investigation.  

Please address the following points:  
1. Equation (2) requires clarification: the left-hand side should explicitly condition on $s$, and the variable $y$ in $p^{(R)}(r_i \mid y, q_i)$ appears unaccounted for in the probability term.  
2. Define $N(s)$ in Algorithm 1.  
3. Specify how the threshold values 0.3 and 0.88 for the baseline were derived.  
4. Clarify whether the "online" systems used as baselines represent state-of-the-art machine learning approaches for these tasks. If not, explain why alternative baselines were not employed. If they are state-of-the-art, note that any performance gain stems solely from human annotation, and provide concrete examples or error analysis to contextualize this.  

This work offers a theoretically grounded formulation of an on-the-job learner that transitions from crowd reliance to autonomous prediction, with the caveat that empirical gains over existing methods are limited. Resolving the above points would strengthen the paper and stimulate valuable discussion.
