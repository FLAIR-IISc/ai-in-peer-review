- Proposes a theoretically grounded "on-the-job learner" that shifts from crowd-sourcing to autonomous predictions, querying the crowd only on low-confidence inputs.  
- Core innovation: models query decision as a sequential decision problem solved via Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) instead of heuristic thresholds.  
- Theoretical foundation (MDP + MCTS) is sound and addresses exploration–exploitation trade-offs in costly human annotation settings.  
- Presentation is weak: crucial details and motivations for equations and algorithmic steps are under-specified, harming clarity and reproducibility.  
- Equation (2) is incorrect/ambiguous: V^π should condition on state s, and the conditioning on ground truth y (p^R(r_i | y, q_i)) is unexplained/marginalized unclearly.  
- Algorithm 1 lacks definition for N(s) — unclear if it denotes visit counts or another quantity, impeding reproducibility.  
- Baseline comparisons are inadequate: only a simple threshold heuristic (thresholds 0.3 and 0.88) is used, with no justification of threshold selection or tuning procedure.  
- Unclear whether the used "online" baselines are state-of-the-art; without strong baselines, reported gains may be driven by human annotation rather than the algorithm.  
- Empirical gains are marginal (e.g., average +0.5% accuracy) and not convincingly significant.  
- Missing error analysis: no breakdown of which input types benefit most from crowd queries or where the model fails versus the crowd.  
- Need to demonstrate query-efficiency trade-offs (queries per example vs. accuracy) to show MCTS reduces queries without sacrificing performance.  
- Paper underutilizes its theoretical contribution due to insufficient empirical grounding and comparative analysis.  
- Recommendation: accept with major revisions contingent on clarifying technical gaps, defining notation, justifying/tuning baselines, stronger baselines, and deeper empirical analyses.
