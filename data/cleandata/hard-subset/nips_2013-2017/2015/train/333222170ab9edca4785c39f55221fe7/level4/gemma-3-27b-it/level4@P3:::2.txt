This paper addresses the challenging problem of building high-accuracy classifiers without requiring pre-labeled training data, leveraging real-time crowdsourcing to resolve uncertainty during prediction. The authors formulate this “on-the-job learning” setting as a stochastic game based on Bayesian decision theory, balancing latency, cost, and accuracy. They develop an approximation to solve this game using Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) and demonstrate its effectiveness on three datasets: named-entity recognition (NER), sentiment classification, and image classification. The core idea of strategically querying the crowd to reduce uncertainty and iteratively improve the model is compelling, and the results demonstrating cost reductions compared to full human annotation are significant. 

This work builds upon and differentiates itself from several existing lines of research. The authors rightly position their approach as distinct from traditional supervised learning [1], pure crowdsourcing [3, 4], online learning [7], and active learning [8, 9, 10, 11]. Unlike active learning, their system adapts *during* test time, rather than relying on a fixed policy learned from a training set.  Their focus on the interplay between latency, cost, and accuracy, alongside the use of a game-theoretic framework, represents a novel contribution to the field. Related work in crowdsourcing [25, 26] and structured prediction [27, 28] are appropriately acknowledged.

**Strengths:**

* **Novel Formulation:** Modeling on-the-job learning as a stochastic game is a clever and principled approach to balancing competing objectives.
* **Significant Cost Reduction:** The reported >10x cost reduction on the NER task compared to full human annotation is impressive and demonstrates the practical value of the proposed approach.
* **Strong Empirical Results:** The system consistently outperforms several baselines, including online learning and a simple threshold-based approach, across all three datasets.
* **Clear Implementation and Reproducibility:** The authors provide an open-source implementation (LENSE) and make their code and data available on CodaLab, which greatly facilitates reproducibility.
* **Addresses a Practical Problem:**  The scenario of building accurate systems with limited initial resources is highly relevant in many real-world applications.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Clarity of Explanation:**  While the core idea is sound, some explanations are opaque. The example beginning at Line 154, describing the timeline of queries, is difficult to follow. A more concrete example, including specific query content and simulated response times, would improve understanding.
* **Inconsistent Terminology:** The inconsistent use of "threshold baseline" and "entropic baseline" in Tables 2 and 3 is confusing. Maintaining consistent terminology would improve readability.
* **Unconventional Related Work Placement:** Placing the related work section (Section 6) *after* the conclusion is unusual and disrupts the typical flow of a research paper. It would be more logical to present related work earlier, after the problem formulation.
* **Limited Discussion of Worker Quality:** While the authors acknowledge variations in worker quality and employ a method to mitigate this, a more in-depth discussion of the impact of worker reliability on performance would be beneficial.  Specifically, how does performance degrade with lower-quality workers, and how robust is the system to such degradation?

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a solid paper that presents a novel and promising approach to on-the-job learning. The technical contributions are sound, the experimental results are compelling, and the code is publicly available. While some aspects of the presentation could be improved, the strengths of the paper clearly outweigh its weaknesses. 

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper addresses a practical and important problem with a novel and well-motivated approach.
* The empirical results demonstrate significant improvements over existing methods.
* The open-source implementation and readily available data promote reproducibility.
* The formulation as a stochastic game provides a principled framework for balancing competing objectives.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The clarity of some explanations could be improved.
* The placement of the related work section is unconventional.
* The discussion of worker quality could be more thorough.



**Recommendation:**

I recommend **accepting** this paper, with a request for minor revisions to improve clarity and address the aforementioned weaknesses. Specifically, I suggest:

1.  Provide a more concrete example to illustrate the query process and timing dynamics (Line 154).
2.  Maintain consistent terminology throughout the paper (e.g., use "threshold baseline" consistently).
3.  Move the related work section to a more conventional location (after the problem formulation).
4.  Expand the discussion of worker quality and its impact on performance.
