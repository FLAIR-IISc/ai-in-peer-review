### Summary

This paper introduces "on-the-job learning," a framework for deploying a high-accuracy system starting with zero training examples. The system processes a stream of inputs and, when uncertain, queries a real-time crowd to gather labels before making a prediction. As the model is updated online with the collected labels, its reliance on the crowd for cost, latency, and accuracy diminishes over time. The core of the contribution is to frame this problem as a stochastic game based on Bayesian decision theory, which provides a principled way to balance the trade-offs between accuracy, cost, and latency. Since computing the optimal policy is intractable, the authors propose an approximation based on Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) with progressive widening. The approach, implemented in a system called LENSE, is evaluated on named-entity recognition (NER), sentiment classification, and image classification tasks, demonstrating significant cost reductions compared to purely human annotation and accuracy improvements over standard online learning.

### Quality and Significance

The paper addresses a significant and practical problem: how to build and deploy an effective system when a large pre-existing labeled dataset is unavailable. The proposed "on-the-job" setting is a valuable contribution, formalizing an operational paradigm that many practitioners wish to achieve. The technical approach of modeling the problem as a stochastic game and using Bayesian decision theory is sound and well-motivated. The experiments are well-designed, comparing the proposed system against appropriate baselines, including both human-only (n-vote) and machine-only (online learning) approaches.

The results are strong and demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method. A particularly interesting and significant finding is presented in Table 2, where the LENSE system outperforms a 3-vote human baseline but underperforms a 5-vote baseline on the NER task. This result provides a valuable insight into the relative efficiencies of de-noising crowd responses through intelligent, model-based querying versus achieving consensus through redundant labeling. The authors are also commended for making their code and data publicly available, which significantly improves the quality and reproducibility of the work.

### Originality

The problem of learning from noisy crowd labels has been widely studied. However, the paper's main novelty lies in its formulation, which explicitly and dynamically optimizes the trade-offs between accuracy, monetary cost, and *latency*. While active learning and online learning address parts of this problem, they typically do not seek information to improve a prediction on the *current* instance at test time, nor do they model the decision-making process in such a comprehensive manner. The application of MCTS to navigate this complex decision space is a novel combination of techniques for this problem domain.

### Clarity and Suggestions for Improvement

While the paper tackles a compelling problem with a solid technical approach, the clarity of the explanations could be significantly improved, as some sections are opaque. The following points are offered as constructive feedback:

1.  **Concrete Walkthrough Example:** The example in the "Behavior" paragraph (Section 3) describing the system's decision-making process is abstract and difficult to follow. To improve readability and intuition, it would be highly beneficial to provide a concrete, step-by-step example. For instance, walking through the NER example ("Soup on George str.") with specific query times, simulated crowd responses, and response delays would make the dynamics of the MCTS-based policy much clearer to the reader.

2.  **Inconsistent Terminology:** There appear to be minor inconsistencies in the paper that create confusion. For example, in Tables 2 and 3, the "threshold baseline" seems to be referred to by different names (e.g., "entropic" vs. "threshold"). Using consistent terminology throughout the manuscript for baselines and key concepts would improve clarity.

3.  **Paper Organization:** The placement of the "Related Work" section (Section 6) after the "Experiments" section is unconventional for a NIPS paper. Placing this section earlier, perhaps after the Introduction, would help readers better contextualize the paper's contributions with respect to prior art from the outset.

4.  **Minor Corrections:** The manuscript should be carefully proofread for minor errors. For example, there may be missing figure references (e.g., "Figure ??") that should be corrected.

### Overall Assessment

This paper presents a novel and significant contribution to the field of human-in-the-loop machine learning. The problem formulation is compelling, the technical approach is sound, and the experimental results are strong. The primary weakness is in the clarity of the presentation. With revisions to improve the explanations and organization, this could be an excellent paper.

**Arguments for acceptance:**
*   Addresses a practical and important problem with a novel formulation.
*   The use of Bayesian decision theory to balance accuracy, cost, and latency is principled and interesting.
*   Strong experimental results that clearly demonstrate the value of the approach over relevant baselines.
*   The comparison between LENSE and n-vote human consensus provides valuable insights into the efficiency of different de-noising strategies.
*   The work is reproducible, with code and data provided.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
*   The clarity of the paper is a significant issue. Key parts of the model and its behavior are difficult to understand without more concrete examples.
*   The unconventional structure (Related Work at the end) detracts from the paper's flow.
