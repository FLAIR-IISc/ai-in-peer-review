Summary and relation to prior work:
This paper presents a decision-theoretic framework for on-the-job learning that starts with zero training data, initially relies on crowdsourcing to resolve uncertainty, and transitions over time to a learned model that queries the crowd only on low-confidence parts of an input. The system casts query selection as a stochastic game grounded in Bayesian decision theory, balancing latency, cost, and accuracy. Because exact optimization is intractable, the authors use Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) with progressive widening to approximate the optimal policy. The prediction component is instantiated with a CRF. The approach is evaluated on NER, sentiment classification, and image classification. On NER, the authors report more than an order-of-magnitude cost reduction versus full human annotation, an 8% F1 improvement over single-vote human labeling, and a 28% F1 improvement over an online learning baseline.

Conceptually, the work formalizes an “on-the-job learner” previously proposed in [22], providing a more principled treatment of decisions about when to query and when to return a prediction. It is related to, but distinct from, online learning (which improves asymptotically but does not guarantee high initial accuracy), active learning (which typically queries labels after prediction), and active classification (which uses static test-time query policies). The emphasis on structured prediction and real-time crowdsourcing is aligned with recent NIPS work on decision-theoretic control of hybrid human–machine systems.

Strengths:
- A clear, principled decision-theoretic formulation of the on-the-job setting that explicitly trades off accuracy, time, and monetary cost.
- Use of MCTS with progressive widening is a reasonable and well-motivated approximation for a large continuous-state decision problem.
- The framework naturally captures intuitive strategies (parallel vs. sequential queries; redundancy to mitigate noise).
- Experiments across three tasks, with notable gains on NER, and an open-source implementation.

Weaknesses:
- Important modeling and algorithmic details are unclear or missing (see questions below), which makes it hard to fully assess technical soundness or reproduce results from the text alone.
- The empirical improvements over a threshold-based heuristic appear modest in places; the comparison is not fully convincing without more diagnostic analysis.
- The status of “online” baselines relative to task-specific state-of-the-art systems is unclear; without that context, it is difficult to gauge significance beyond the contribution of human annotation.

Quality:
The overall approach is sound and the choices (CRF for structured prediction; MCTS for planning; simple noise and latency models) are reasonable. However, the paper needs to clarify several mathematical details (e.g., conditioning in Eq. (2), how responses are marginalized, and undefined notation in Algorithm 1). The experimental protocol controls for worker variability, which is good, but additional analysis (e.g., when human input truly corrects the model vs. when it merely confirms) would strengthen claims.

Clarity:
The paper is generally well written and organized, but a few definitions are missing or ambiguous. Clarifying the utility expression, the environment model, and the threshold baseline parameters would improve readability and reproducibility.

Originality:
The framing of on-the-job learning as a stochastic game coupled with MCTS is a novel and useful formalization of ideas previously explored informally (e.g., [22]). The combination of real-time crowdsourcing with structured prediction and decision-theoretic planning is a valuable synthesis.

Significance:
The ability to deploy a high-accuracy system without pre-collected training data is practically important. The strongest evidence is on NER, where the method substantially reduces cost while maintaining or improving accuracy. Results on sentiment and faces are more mixed; understanding when the approach yields large benefits would help establish broader impact.

Questions and requests for clarification:
1) Equation (2): The expression is unclear. The left-hand side should also be conditioned on s (the query issue times), consistent with the dependence used elsewhere. In addition, in pR(ri | y, qi), how does the dependence on y disappear in the subsequent derivation? Please explain whether y is marginalized, approximated, or omitted by assumption.

2) Algorithm 1 notation: What is N(s)? It is not defined in the text. If N(σ) is the visit count for state σ, please clarify the role of N(s) and ensure consistent notation.

3) Threshold baseline parameters: How were the constants 0.3 and 0.88 chosen? Is 0.3 derived from the assumed 70% worker accuracy (i.e., a 30% residual uncertainty per query), and if so, why is it applied in that specific functional form? What motivates the 0.88 threshold, and how sensitive are results to these values?

4) Baselines and error analysis: Are the “online” systems you compare against the best machine-learned systems for these tasks (given the same features and supervision)? If not, why not? If they are, then any improvement must come from human input—can you provide examples or an error analysis showing the categories of errors corrected by the crowd versus those corrected by model learning over time?

Pros for acceptance:
- Principled decision-theoretic formulation with a practical approximation scheme.
- Demonstrated cost savings and accuracy gains on NER.
- Timely topic with clear relevance to real-world deployment scenarios.
- Open-source release.

Cons for acceptance:
- Missing or ambiguous technical details (equations, notation, baseline specification).
- Underwhelming advantage over a simple threshold heuristic in some settings.
- Limited analysis of when and why the method helps beyond human votes or standard online learning.

Overall assessment:
This paper presents a thoughtful and theoretically grounded formulation of an “on-the-job learner” that begins as a crowdsourcing system and transitions to a learned model, querying only when necessary. While the empirical results are a mixed bag, the approach is reasonable and merits further investigation. Clarifying the technical details and strengthening the baseline comparisons and error analysis would significantly improve the paper.
