

This paper introduces a novel framework for on-the-job learning that strategically combines crowdsourced labels with an incrementally trained model to achieve high accuracy from deployment while progressively reducing reliance on human annotation. The authors formalize the problem as a stochastic game between the system and the crowd, modeled through Bayesian decision theory with a CRF prediction framework. The system dynamically issues queries to the crowd for uncertain tokens, incorporates responses to update its model, and determines when to make predictions based on a utility function balancing accuracy, cost, and latency. The optimal policy is intractable due to continuous time considerations, so the authors propose an approximation using Monte Carlo Tree Search with progressive widening. Evaluated across three tasks (NER, sentiment classification, and image classification), the approach demonstrates significant cost reductions (over an order of magnitude for NER) compared to full human annotation while improving F1 scores relative to both single-human-label baselines (8% improvement) and online learning (28% improvement).

The paper makes a strong contribution to the intersection of active learning, crowdsourcing, and online learning. It is highly original in its principled formulation of the on-the-job learning problem as a stochastic game with Bayesian decision theory, distinguishing it from previous work on active classification (which relies on static policies) and online learning (which cannot maintain high accuracy from the start). The significance is evident through the substantial cost-performance tradeoffs demonstrated across multiple domains. The technical quality is strong, with thorough experimentation, appropriate baselines, and sound theoretical grounding.

However, the clarity of the paper suffers from an unnecessarily complex treatment of continuous time. The authors introduce continuous time as a critical component (Section 3) but provide minimal justification for why it is essential, and the experimental results (Table 2) show only marginal benefits for the NER task where sequence dependencies exist, while the threshold baseline (which ignores continuous time) achieves comparable performance with lower latency. This makes the continuous time extension seem more like an added complication than a meaningful improvement. The paper would be significantly clearer and more impactful if the core method were first presented without continuous time, with the time-dependent extension discussed as a secondary contribution.

Several specific issues affect the paper's clarity and presentation: The 5-vote crowdsourcing baseline is missing for Face-ID and Sentiment tasks in Tables 2-3 and Figure 4, though it represents the best performance in NER. The threshold baseline is absent from the sentiment experiment results (Table 3 and Figure 4), despite being a key comparison. The paper claims "beats human performance" but does not qualify that the 5-vote case is actually the strongest baseline, which should be highlighted. In the threshold baseline description, the inequality direction in the uncertainty reduction formula appears reversed (should be ≤ 0.12 instead of ≥ 0.88). The t_deadline parameter is mentioned but not clearly defined in the experimental setup.

The paper's strengths in originality, significance, and quality outweigh its clarity weaknesses, and the issues noted are correctable with minor revisions. The core contribution of a principled hybrid learning framework is substantial and merits acceptance. I recommend acceptance with the authors addressing the clarity issues, particularly regarding the continuous time component and the presentation of results.
