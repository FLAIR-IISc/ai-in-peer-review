Summary
The paper’s main contribution is a more theoretically grounded formulation of the “on-the-job learner” proposed in [22]. The system initially relies entirely on crowd responses to produce outputs, and over time transitions to making its own predictions while querying the crowd only on low-confidence inputs. The decision of whether to query the crowd for a given input is framed as a decision problem and approximated via Monte Carlo Tree Search.

Evaluation
- Presentation: Mostly clear, but several important details are missing.
- Empirical results: Relative to a threshold-based heuristic baseline, the improvements are somewhat underwhelming.
- Overall: The approach is reasonable and merits further investigation.

Requests for clarification
1. Equation (2): The left-hand side appears to be missing conditioning on s. Also, how does the y in p_R(r_i | y, q_i) disappear?
2. Algorithm 1: What is N(s)?
3. Threshold baseline: How were the threshold values 0.3 and 0.88 chosen?
4. Tasks and baselines: I am not fully familiar with the specific tasks used. Are the “online” systems the strongest machine-learned systems for these tasks? If not, why not? If they are, then any performance gain should be attributable purely to human annotation—what are some representative cases where this occurs? Some error analysis would be helpful.

Overall assessment
The paper provides a useful theoretical formulation of an on-the-job learner that starts from pure crowdsourcing and gradually becomes a learned system that queries the crowd only on low-confidence instances. The empirical results are mixed, but the direction is promising and worth broader discussion.
