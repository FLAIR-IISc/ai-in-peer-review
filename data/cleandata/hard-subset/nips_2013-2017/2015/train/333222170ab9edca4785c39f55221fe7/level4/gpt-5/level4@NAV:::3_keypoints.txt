- Proposes a hybrid strategy that combines crowdsourced annotations with an incrementally trained online model, reducing crowd involvement as model confidence grows.  
- Formulates the problem as a stochastic game on top of a CRF, treating the system and crowd as players who exchange queries and responses.  
- Models queries q issued over time s with response times t and optimizes expected utility U(q,r) under a deadline t_deadline.  
- Estimates expected utility via a simulation dynamics model P(y,r,t|x,q,s) and uses MCTS + TD-learning (Algorithm 1) because continuous time makes exact optimization intractable.  
- Experiments on NER, sentiment, and face-ID compare majority-vote (1/3/5), online learning (oracle labels), a “threshold” baseline (no continuous time), and the full model (LENSE).  
- LENSE outperforms all baselines except the 5-vote human condition; the threshold baseline is often close and has lower latency on NER and better latency on Face ID.  
- Shows the model reduces reliance on crowd labels over time with a capable learner and achieves high accuracy from the first example by leveraging crowd when uncertain.  
- Reviewer finds the contribution highly original, impactful, and meriting acceptance, likely to spur follow-on work and practical use.  
- Main clarity concern: introduction of continuous time mid-Section 3 increases complexity while offering limited empirical benefit; suggests presenting time-free core method first and continuous time as an extension.  
- Results/presentation issues: overclaim that the model “beats human performance” is not supported vs. 5-vote NER; 5-vote condition missing in several tables/figures and some best values not bolded.  
- Missing inclusion of the Threshold baseline in the sentiment experiment (Table 3 and Figure 4).  
- Minor methodological/notation issues: unclear what t_deadline was used and the stopping criterion; equation (2) should condition on s; F(q) notation is confusing; and a likely sign/error in the Threshold baseline expression.  
- Overall: novel and compelling work with strong results, but clarity and presentation fixes (especially around continuous time and baselines) would strengthen impact.
