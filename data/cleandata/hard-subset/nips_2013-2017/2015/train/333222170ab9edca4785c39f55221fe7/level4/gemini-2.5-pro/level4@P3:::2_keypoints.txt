- Introduces "on-the-job learning": deploy a high-accuracy system with zero labeled data by querying a crowd in real time and updating the model as labels arrive.  
- Formalizes the problem as a stochastic game and uses Bayesian decision theory to optimize a utility balancing accuracy, cost, and latency.  
- Uses Monte Carlo Tree Search with progressive widening as a tractable approximation for the intractable optimal policy.  
- Evaluated on NER, sentiment, and image classification, showing major cost reductions vs. full human consensus and accuracy gains over online baselines.  
- Novelty: actively seeks instance-specific information before prediction, enabling high accuracy from the first example—distinct from standard online/active learning.  
- Technical quality: sound Bayesian framing and well-motivated use of MCTS; experiments include strong baselines (human n-vote and ideal online learner).  
- Reproducibility: code and data are public, strengthening the contribution.  
- Empirical highlight: LENSE achieves >10x cost reduction on NER vs. 5-vote majority and large F1 improvements vs. single annotator and online learner.  
- Clarity concern: exposition is sometimes opaque; key algorithmic mechanics need clearer, step-by-step examples.  
- Request for clearer visuals: the game tree/state transitions section is dense and would benefit from diagrams illustrating state flow.  
- Presentation issues: Related Work is placed late; inconsistent terminology (e.g., "entropic" vs. "threshold"); minor typos and broken references.  
- Analysis gap: needs deeper discussion of trade-offs between LENSE’s decision-making and brute-force consensus voting for achieving target accuracy/cost.  
- Acceptance pros: novel and practical problem, principled Bayesian + MCTS approach, strong empirical results, and reproducible artifacts.  
- Acceptance cons: clarity and presentation problems and insufficient analysis of baseline trade-offs, but these are fixable in revision.
