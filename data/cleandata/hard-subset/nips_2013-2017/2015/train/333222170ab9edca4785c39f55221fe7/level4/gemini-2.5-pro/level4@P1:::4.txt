This paper introduces "on-the-job learning," a framework for deploying a high-accuracy prediction system that starts with zero labeled data. The core idea is to process a stream of inputs and, for each input, use real-time crowdsourcing to query uncertain parts of the output before making a final prediction. As the system observes more labeled data from the crowd, its underlying model improves, gradually reducing its reliance on human annotators over time. The authors formalize this problem as a stochastic game, using Bayesian decision theory to construct a utility function that balances accuracy, monetary cost, and latency. Since computing the optimal policy is intractable, they propose an approximation based on Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) with progressive widening. The approach is evaluated on three tasks—named-entity recognition (NER), sentiment classification, and image classification—and demonstrates significant improvements over human-only, machine-only, and heuristic baselines.

**Strengths**

*   **Novelty and Significance:** The "on-the-job learning" setting is novel and highly relevant. It addresses the practical challenge of deploying machine learning systems in cold-start scenarios where high accuracy is required from the very first example, which is a common and difficult problem.
*   **Technical Quality:** The theoretical formulation is principled and elegant. Casting the problem as a stochastic game with a utility function derived from Bayesian decision theory provides a solid foundation for navigating the trade-offs between accuracy, cost, and latency. The use of MCTS is a sensible and well-justified method for approximating the intractable policy.
*   **Empirical Results:** The experimental results are strong, particularly on the structured prediction (NER) task. The proposed system, LENSE, achieves a substantial reduction in annotation cost while simultaneously improving F1 score over several baselines, including a system that relies on a 5-person majority vote.
*   **Reproducibility:** The commitment to reproducibility is commendable. By providing open-source code, data, and experiments, the authors greatly increase the value of this contribution to the community.

**Weaknesses and Areas for Improvement**

*   **Clarity on Applicability:** The paper's applicability to non-structured prediction tasks is unclear. While the framework is well-motivated for structured tasks like NER, where querying one token can provide information that propagates to adjacent tokens, its application to the face classification task is not explained. It is not specified whether the queries are for the entire instance label. If so, it is difficult to see how the structured prediction model is leveraged or why the proposed utility function would be superior to simpler active learning heuristics in this context. The manuscript would be stronger if this were clarified.
*   **Experimental Baselines:** The experimental comparison could be strengthened with additional, well-motivated baselines.
    1.  The "Threshold baseline" relies on two parameters (0.3 and 0.98) that appear to be set without justification. The paper would be more convincing if it explained how these values were chosen and included a sensitivity analysis to demonstrate the baseline's robustness to these settings.
    2.  Given the connection to active learning, a baseline using a standard uncertainty sampling heuristic would be highly informative. For example, one could query the label(s) with the lowest model confidence until a budget is exhausted or a confidence threshold is met. This would provide a valuable point of comparison.
*   **Positioning Relative to Active Learning:** The authors emphasize the distinction between their setting and online active learning. However, this distinction could be made clearer through empirical comparison. A standard active learning framework could be adapted for this "pre-prediction querying" setting: the system could query a set of labels based on an acquisition function, update its model with the responses, and then make a prediction. While such a baseline would not explicitly model the temporal dynamics, it would draw upon a large body of existing theory for selecting queries and would help to quantify the specific benefits of the proposed game-theoretic approach over simpler, established methods.

**Summary**

Overall, this paper presents a novel and interesting framework for an important practical problem. The technical approach is sound, and the results on the NER task are impressive. However, the paper's clarity suffers from an inadequate explanation of its application to non-structured tasks, and the experimental evaluation would benefit from more rigorous and relevant baselines to better contextualize the work's contribution with respect to the active learning literature. Despite these weaknesses, the novelty of the problem formulation and the strength of the core idea make this a valuable contribution.
