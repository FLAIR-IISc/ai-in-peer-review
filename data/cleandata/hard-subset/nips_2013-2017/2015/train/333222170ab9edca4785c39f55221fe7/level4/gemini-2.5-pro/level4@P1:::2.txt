This paper introduces "on-the-job learning," a framework for deploying a high-accuracy classifier starting with zero labeled examples. The system processes a stream of inputs and, for each one, can query a crowd in real-time to resolve uncertainty before making a prediction. As the model is updated with crowd responses over time, its reliance on these queries decreases. The authors formalize this problem as a stochastic game, using Bayesian decision theory to manage the trade-offs between accuracy, latency, and cost. To find a policy in the intractable state space, they propose an approximation based on Monte Carlo Tree Search. This approach is novel in that, unlike standard online or active learning, it actively seeks information before predicting on a given instance to maintain high accuracy from the outset.

**Strengths:**

*   **Problem Formulation:** The paper tackles a compelling and practical problem. The "on-the-job" setting, where a system must be accurate from day one without a large, pre-existing training set, is highly relevant. The principled formulation of balancing latency and cost against accuracy is a significant contribution.
*   **Novelty:** The combination of online model improvement with pre-prediction active querying is a novel and interesting approach. While handling noisy crowd responses has been studied extensively, the optimization of query timing and latency within a decision-theoretic framework is a strong point of this work.
*   **Experimental Insights:** The experiments provide a valuable comparison between the proposed system's accuracy and that of purely human annotation. It is particularly interesting to see in Table 2 that the LENSE system outperforms the 3-vote baseline but underperforms the 5-vote baseline. This provides an insightful benchmark on the relative efficiencies of de-noising crowd responses via the proposed learning approach versus simple consensus.
*   **Strong Results:** The empirical results are impressive, showing that LENSE can achieve more than an order of magnitude reduction in cost on the NER task compared to obtaining a result of comparable quality via full human annotation, while simultaneously boosting performance.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

*   **Clarity of Explanation:** While the high-level idea is clear, the detailed explanations of the system's behavior are somewhat opaque. The paper would benefit from improved clarity to help the reader fully grasp the mechanics.
*   **Concrete Examples:** The example given in the "Behavior" paragraph (Section 3) is rather abstract and difficult to follow. For better readability, it would be highly beneficial to walk through a specific, concrete example, including realistic query times and crowd responses, to illustrate how the system's beliefs and subsequent actions evolve.
*   **Inconsistent Terminology:** In the experiments section, the baseline method is referred to as "entropic" in the text but "Threshold baseline" in the tables and the method description. Using consistent terminology throughout the paper would avoid potential confusion for the reader.
*   **Paper Organization:** The placement of the "Related Work" section (Section 6, after the experiments) is unconventional. Moving it to either directly after the introduction or just before the conclusion would improve the paper's flow and structure.
*   **Minor Correction:** There appears to be a missing figure reference on Line 86 ("Figure ??").

Overall, this paper presents a novel and well-motivated solution to an important problem. The core ideas are strong, and the results are significant. The main weaknesses lie in the clarity of the presentation, which could be substantially improved with more concrete examples and minor organizational changes.
