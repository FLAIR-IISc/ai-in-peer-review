This paper proposes a hybrid methodology that integrates crowdsourced labels with an incrementally trained online model to solve prediction tasks. The central concept involves initially relying heavily on the crowd while the system is new, learning from the acquired labels, and progressively reducing the reliance on crowd input as the model's confidence increases. This is achieved through an elegant formulation of the problem as a stochastic game, utilizing a Conditional Random Field (CRF) as the prediction model, where both the system and the crowd act as players. The system can issue queries `q` for tokens `x` (with an unknown true label `y`), which generate crowd responses `r`. A utility function `U(q,r)` is defined for each outcome, and the system's objective is to select actions that maximize its expected utility. Moreover, queries are issued at specific times `s` with corresponding response times `t`, and utility is maximized subject to a deadline `t_deadline`, which dictates the number and rate of queries.

To compute the expected utility, the system must employ the simulation dynamics model `P(y,r,t|x,q,s)` to evaluate the utilities as described in Equation (4). While this allows for the selection of an optimal action, the introduction of continuous time renders direct optimization intractable. Consequently, the authors use an approximation based on Monte Carlo Tree Search and Temporal Difference (TD) learning, detailed in Algorithm 1. The approach was evaluated on a simplified Named Entity Recognition (NER) task, a sentiment analysis task, and a face identification task. Four methods were compared: a majority vote baseline using 1, 3, and 5 human annotators; an online learning model using oracle (true) labels; a "threshold" baseline (the authors' model without the continuous time component, which queries until model uncertainty falls below a set threshold); and the authors' complete model, LENSE. In terms of precision, recall, and F1-score, the full LENSE model surpasses all baselines except for the 5-annotator crowdsourced approach. However, the "threshold" baseline achieves nearly identical performance and, notably, has lower latency on the NER task (Table 2). The authors demonstrate that their model's need for crowdsourced labels diminishes over time (Figure 3) and that, unlike standard online learning, it achieves high accuracy from the very first example by leveraging the crowd when model uncertainty is high (Figure 4).

This is a compelling and interesting paper that I anticipate will inspire significant discussion, future research, and practical adoption. While various heuristic methods for learning from crowd-labeled data exist, this work presents one of the first comprehensive and theoretically grounded proposals I have encountered. The results are impressive when compared against a set of strong and reasonable baselines. I therefore consider the work to be highly original and significant, warranting inclusion in the conference. However, the paper's clarity is a significant weakness, which could unfortunately diminish its potential impact. Many of these issues are addressable before the camera-ready version if the authors are willing to invest the effort, possibly by adjusting the narrative emphasis between the "threshold" baseline and the full continuous-time model.

The main clarity problem stems from the introduction of continuous time, which begins midway through Section 3. While the motivation—operating within time and cost budgets for each classification—is sound, the experimental evidence for its benefit appears limited, especially when weighed against the substantial increase in formulation complexity and algorithmic machinery. In fact, Table 2 shows that the simpler "threshold" baseline not only achieves roughly equivalent (though marginally lower) performance on the NER task but does so with significantly lower latency. On the face identification task, the "threshold" baseline actually performs slightly better. The authors suggest (l. 314) that the baseline excels on the latter task due to its single-label nature with minimal label interactions. However, this raises the question of why the more complex NER task, which is rich with such interactions, benefits only marginally from the continuous-time formulation.

For many real-world applications, the "threshold" baseline would be sufficient and already represents a major advancement as a principled hybrid learning system that outperforms traditional active and online learning. The paper's impact could be enhanced by restructuring the presentation. The core method could be developed fully without the time component, making it more accessible and easier to implement, with the continuous-time aspect then introduced as a subsequent extension. In a similar vein, the results section would be more transparent if it more clearly acknowledged the strong performance of the "threshold" method, even on tasks with sequential dependencies. While this is a suggestion for improvement and not a condition for acceptance—as the paper is strong enough as is—I believe it would improve the work's overall clarity and increase the likelihood of its methods being widely understood and adopted.

There are several smaller issues regarding the presentation of the results. First, the claim in the introduction that the model "beats human performance" is contradicted by the 5-vote case for NER. Furthermore, the 5-vote results are notably absent for the Face ID and Sentiment tasks in multiple places (the right side of Table 2, Table 3, and Figure 4) and should be included for a complete comparison. Similarly, the 5-vote results in Table 2 should be bolded as they represent the best performance on that task. The latency for the Threshold baseline in NER and the LENSE result for Face ID should also be bolded, as they are the best in their respective categories. Most importantly, the "Threshold" baseline is missing entirely from the sentiment analysis experiment, including Table 3 and Figure 4; it is a critical baseline and should be included.

A few minor points concerning the method description:
- The discussion of `t_deadline` (l.183-187) is present, but it is not specified what deadline was used in the experiments or what criteria (e.g., an entropy threshold or reaching `t_deadline`) determined when a final answer was reported.
- In Equation (2), the probability distribution `p(y,r,t|x,q)` seems to require `s` (the query time) as a condition as well, i.e., `p(y,r,t|x,q,s)`.
- The use of `q` in `F(q)` in Equation (5) is confusing, as `q` already represents a query. I initially mistook it for a query variable when it appears to be a nominal distribution function. A different letter should be chosen to avoid ambiguity.
- In the description of the Threshold baseline (l.246-252), the uncertainty expression `(1 - ptheta(yi|x))*0.3^m >= 0.88` appears to be incorrect. To reduce uncertainty by increasing `m`, the inequality should likely be `<= (1-0.88)`.

In summary, this paper introduces a novel hybrid approach that combines crowdsourcing and online learning, framing it as a stochastic game that accounts for data, labels, crowd responses, and timing. The method demonstrates impressive performance against several baselines, including human labelers, an oracle-trained online model, and a strong non-temporal version of the proposed model that performs competitively. The work is original, innovative, and interesting, and I recommend its acceptance. However, its clarity is hampered by the complex continuous-time formulation, which adds significant overhead for what appears to be a minimal performance gain.
