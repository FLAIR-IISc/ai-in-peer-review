

This paper presents a hybrid approach for combining crowdsourced labels with incrementally (online) trained models to address prediction problems. The core idea leverages the crowd heavily during system initialization, learns from acquired labels, and progressively reduces crowd reliance as model confidence increases. This is framed as a stochastic game involving a conditional random field (CRF) prediction model, where the system and crowd act as competing players. The system issues queries $q$ for tokens $x$ (with true label $y$), eliciting responses $r$ with associated utility $U(q,r)$; the system selects actions to maximize expected utility. Queries are issued at times $s$ (with response times $t$), and utility maximization considers a deadline $t_{\text{deadline}}$ governing response timing, thereby determining query volume and rate.  

Computing expected utility requires the simulation dynamics model $P(y,r,t|x,q,s)$ (Equation 4). While optimal action selection is theoretically possible, continuous-time dynamics render exact optimization intractable, necessitating an approximation via Monte Carlo Tree Search and TD learning (Algorithm 1). Experiments evaluate the approach on named entity recognition (NER), sentiment recognition, and face identification tasks, comparing four methods: majority vote (1, 3, 5 human judges), online learning (using true labels), a "threshold" baseline (the authors’ model without continuous time, sending $m$ queries per step until uncertainty drops below a threshold), and the full model (LENSE). The full model achieves superior precision/recall/F1 scores compared to all baselines except the 5-vote crowdsourced version, with the threshold baseline performing nearly equivalently (and exhibiting lower latency for NER; Table 2). The model reduces crowdsourced label usage over time with a sufficiently strong training model (Figure 3), and maintains high accuracy from the first example versus online learning (Figure 4), leveraging crowd expertise during high model uncertainty.  

This is an interesting paper with high potential for discussion, adoption, and further work. It represents the first complete, principled proposal for hybrid learning from crowds during model training, with impressive results against reasonable baselines. The work is highly original and significant, warranting acceptance. However, clarity issues—correctable in camera-ready form—risk diminishing its impact. The primary clarity flaw arises from introducing continuous time mid-Section 3. While the time/budget motivation is reasonable, experimental results show minimal performance gains (e.g., threshold baseline achieves significantly lower NER latency with equivalent performance; it even outperforms LENSE on face ID), yet the formulation, discussion, and optimization become substantially more complex. For practical applications, the threshold baseline is already a significant advancement (principled hybrid learning surpassing active/online learning), and the paper would better serve its impact goals by developing the core method without continuous time (simpler to understand/adopt) and presenting continuous time as an optional extension. The authors should also clarify that the threshold method’s performance remains strong even with label interaction (e.g., in NER), not merely in single-label tasks.  

Additional minor issues include:  
- The claim "beats human performance" is inaccurate for NER (5-vote case), and the 5-vote results are omitted for Face ID/Sentiment in Tables 2, 3, and Figure 4—these should be included. The 5-vote results (and threshold latency for NER, LENSE latency for Face ID) should be bolded in Table 2.  
- The threshold baseline is missing for sentiment (Table 3, Figure 4)—it must be added.  
- Section 3.1 (l.183–187) does not specify whether $t_{\text{deadline}}$ was used in experiments or how answer readiness was determined (e.g., entropic threshold vs. $t_{\text{deadline}}$).  
- Equation (2) should include $s$ as a parameter ($p(y,r,t|x,q,s)$).  
- Equation (5) uses confusing notation $q$ in $F(q)$; $q$ is already a query, so a different symbol (e.g., $d$) is needed for the distribution function.  
- The threshold baseline description (l.246–252) likely contains an error: $(1 - p_\theta(y_i|x)) \cdot 0.3^m \leq (1 - 0.88)$ (not $\geq 0.88$), as increasing $m$ reduces uncertainty.  

The paper presents an elegant hybrid approach, casting the problem as a stochastic game that models data, true labels, crowd responses, and temporal dynamics. It demonstrates impressive results against multiple baselines. The method is original, novel, and compelling. While the continuous-time extension complicates the framework without substantial performance gains, the core technique is strong enough for acceptance. Addressing the clarity and reporting issues would significantly enhance the paper’s impact.
