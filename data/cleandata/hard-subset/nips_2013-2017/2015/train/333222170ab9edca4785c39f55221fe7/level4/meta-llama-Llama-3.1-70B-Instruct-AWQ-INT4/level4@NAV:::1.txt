This paper offers a significant theoretical contribution by presenting a more sound formulation of an "on the job learner" initially proposed in [22]. The learner begins by relying on crowdsourcing for output generation but progressively develops its own predictive capabilities, resorting to crowd queries only when necessary. Notably, the decision to query the crowd for a given input is framed as a decision problem, which is solved using Monte Carlo Tree Search.

The presentation is generally clear, although it lacks crucial details in some areas. The empirical evaluation, particularly in comparison to a threshold-based heuristic, yields somewhat underwhelming results. Nevertheless, the approach appears reasonable and warrants further exploration.

To enhance the paper's clarity and validity, the authors are requested to address the following concerns:

1. Equation (2) seems to be incorrect, as the left-hand side should also be conditioned on 's'. Furthermore, the disappearance of 'y' in p{R}(ri | y, q_i) requires explanation.
2. The definition and purpose of N(s) in Algorithm 1 need to be clarified.
3. The authors should provide a detailed explanation for the choice of 0.3 and 0.88 in the threshold baseline.
4. To better understand the experimental setup, it would be helpful to know whether the "online" systems used in this study represent the state-of-the-art machine-learned systems for this task. If not, the authors should justify their choice. If they do represent the state-of-the-art, any observed improvements would likely be attributed to human annotation. Providing error analysis and examples of such cases would be beneficial.

This paper proposes a theoretical formulation of an "on the job learner" that transitions from a crowdsourcing system to a learned model, querying the crowd only for low-confidence inputs. While the empirical results are mixed, the paper has the potential to spark valuable discussions in this area.
