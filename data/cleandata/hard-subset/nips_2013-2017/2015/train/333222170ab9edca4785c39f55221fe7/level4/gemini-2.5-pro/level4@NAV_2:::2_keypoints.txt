- Introduces "on-the-job learning": deploy high-accuracy prediction from scratch using real-time crowdsourcing when uncertain.  
- System updates online with collected labels to become more self-sufficient and reduce latency and cost over time.  
- Formalizes the problem as a stochastic game and uses Bayesian decision theory to trade off accuracy, latency, and monetary cost.  
- Computing the optimal policy is intractable; paper proposes an approximation using Monte Carlo Tree Search with progressive widening.  
- Novelty lies in integrating latency and monetary cost into principled querying decisions, bridging online learning, active learning, and crowdsourcing.  
- Differs from standard online/active learning by querying prior to prediction to maintain high accuracy from deployment rather than only asymptotically.  
- Thorough empirical evaluation on three tasks (NER, sentiment, image classification) demonstrating strong results.  
- Shows an order-of-magnitude cost reduction on the NER task and significant improvements over online learning and static human-voting baselines.  
- Analysis of human n-vote baselines is insightful (e.g., LENSE outperforms 3-vote but not 5-vote), highlighting efficiency of intelligent querying.  
- Authors released code, data, and experiments, enhancing reproducibility.  
- Strength: Significant, original, and practical problem setting with an innovative latency-aware stochastic-game formalization.  
- Strength: Principled Bayesian decision-theoretic framework and appropriate use of MCTS for approximation.  
- Weakness: Clarity and exposition are weak; explanations can be opaque and hard to follow.  
- Weakness: "Behavior" section is abstract and would benefit from a concrete, step-by-step example with timings and sample crowd responses.  
- Weakness: Paper organization could be improved (Related Work placed after Experiments delays context).  
- Weakness: Minor inconsistencies and proofreading issues (e.g., inconsistent baseline naming, broken figure reference).  
- Arguments for acceptance: novel/practical problem, principled solution, strong empirical results, synthesis of prior ideas, and public release of resources.  
- Arguments against acceptance: significant clarity and structural issues that hinder understanding.
