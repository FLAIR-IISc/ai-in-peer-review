- Main contribution: Provides a more theoretically sound, Bayesian decision-theoretic formulation of an on-the-job learner that shifts from crowd queries to model predictions over time.  
- Decision mechanism: Models the query-vs-predict choice as a decision problem solved approximately with Monte Carlo Tree Search.  
- Presentation quality: Generally well presented but missing important details in places.  
- Empirical results: Improvements over a threshold-based heuristic are modest; results mixed but suggestive.  
- Overall verdict: Reasonable approach that merits further investigation.  

Clarification requests:
- Equation (2): The left-hand side should be conditioned on x; it’s unclear how y in p_R(r_i | y, q_i) is eliminated in the derivation.  
- Algorithm 1: The meaning of N(σ) is not specified.  
- Baseline thresholds: How the values 0.3 and 0.88 for the threshold baseline were chosen is not explained.  
- Online baselines and error analysis: Are the “online” systems state-of-the-art for these tasks, and if so, is any gain solely due to human annotation—additional error analysis is needed.  

Arguments for acceptance:
- Novelty: Introduces a principled Bayesian framework for on-the-job learning in a relatively unexplored area.  
- Technical soundness: Using MCTS to approximate the optimal policy is reasonable given intractability of exact solution.  
- Empirical benefit: Demonstrates cost reductions versus full human annotation while maintaining or improving accuracy.  
- Problem clarity: Clearly defines and motivates the on-the-job learning problem.  

Arguments against acceptance:
- Modest gains: Improvements over strong baselines, especially online learning baselines, are not always substantial.  
- Reproducibility concerns: Paper omits details necessary to fully understand or reproduce results.  
- Limited experiments: Evaluation on only three datasets may not generalize to broader on-the-job learning scenarios.
