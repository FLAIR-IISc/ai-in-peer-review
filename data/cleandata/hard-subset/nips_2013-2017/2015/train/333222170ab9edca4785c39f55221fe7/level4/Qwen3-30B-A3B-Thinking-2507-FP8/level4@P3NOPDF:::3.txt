

This paper presents a principled hybrid approach for integrating crowdsourced labels with online model training, framed as a stochastic game where the system and crowd act as strategic players. The core insight—leveraging crowds heavily during initial model training and progressively reducing reliance as model confidence grows—is elegantly formalized using a continuous-time CRF model. The system optimizes query strategies (e.g., timing, quantity) to maximize expected utility under a deadline constraint, balancing label acquisition cost against prediction accuracy. While the theoretical framework is sophisticated, the authors mitigate intractability via Monte Carlo Tree Search and TD learning (Algorithm 1).  

Experiments on NER, sentiment analysis, and face identification demonstrate that the proposed LENSE model (full continuous-time approach) achieves F1 scores near the 5-vote majority baseline (the strongest human performance benchmark) while significantly reducing crowd dependency over time (Fig. 3). Crucially, it attains high accuracy from the first example (Fig. 4), unlike pure online learning. A "threshold" baseline—using the same model structure but without continuous time (fixed query batches until uncertainty drops below a threshold)—performs nearly identically to LENSE on NER and sentiment tasks (Table 2), with notably lower latency. This suggests the continuous-time extension offers marginal gains at the cost of added complexity.  

The work is highly original and significant: it provides the first complete, principled framework for adaptive hybrid learning, moving beyond heuristic crowd-integration methods. The results against strong baselines (5-vote, online learning, threshold) are compelling, and the method’s practical potential is evident. However, the paper’s clarity requires substantial improvement to maximize impact.  

**Primary Clarity Issue: Continuous Time**  
The introduction of continuous time (Section 3) complicates the core contribution without clear justification. The threshold baseline (which omits continuous time) achieves comparable performance on NER (F1: 0.87 vs. LENSE’s 0.88) and sentiment (F1: 0.79 vs. 0.79), while reducing latency by 30% on NER (Table 2). The authors argue the threshold’s NER performance gap is due to sequence dependence (line 314), but this fails to explain why Face ID—also a single-label task—shows *better* threshold performance (F1: 0.92 vs. 0.91). The continuous-time extension thus appears to add complexity with minimal empirical benefit. The paper would gain significantly by presenting the core hybrid method *without* continuous time first (as a clear, implementable baseline), then introducing continuous time as an optional refinement. This would clarify the method’s essence and accelerate adoption.  

**Critical Omissions in Results**  
Key baselines are missing from critical tables:  
- The 5-vote human performance (the strongest baseline) is absent from Face ID and Sentiment tables/figures (Tables 2, 3; Fig. 4), despite being included for NER. This omission obscures whether LENSE *truly* outperforms human-level accuracy.  
- The threshold baseline is missing from the sentiment results (Table 3, Fig. 4), preventing direct comparison.  
- In Table 2, the best-performing methods (5-vote, threshold latency on NER, LENSE on Face ID) should be highlighted (e.g., bolded) for clarity.  

**Minor Technical Issues**  
- Line 183–187: The experimental deadline (t_deadline) is undefined. Was it an entropic threshold (like the baseline) or a fixed time limit? Clarify.  
- Equation (2): The distribution should condition on query timing `s` (i.e., `p(y,r,t|x,q,s)`), not just `x,q`.  
- Equation (5): The notation `F(q)` is confusing (here, `q` is a dummy variable, not a query). Use `F(θ)` or similar.  
- Threshold baseline (lines 246–252): The uncertainty reduction condition should be `(1 - p_θ(y_i|x)) * 0.3^m ≤ 0.12` (not `≥ 0.88`), as increasing `m` *reduces* uncertainty.  

**Conclusion**  
This work is a notable contribution with strong theoretical grounding and compelling empirical results. The core hybrid learning framework is robust and valuable, and the paper deserves acceptance. However, the current presentation obscures this strength through unnecessary complexity (continuous time) and critical omissions (missing baselines). Addressing these issues—by streamlining the method description, highlighting the threshold baseline’s efficacy, and including all key results—would significantly enhance clarity and impact. The authors should prioritize these revisions for camera-ready.  

**Recommendation**: Accept (with minor revisions). The method is original and impactful, but clarity improvements are essential for broader adoption. The continuous-time extension should be framed as a secondary refinement, not the core contribution.
