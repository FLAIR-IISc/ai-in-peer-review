The authors present a framework for “on-the-job learning,” addressing the challenge of deploying accurate systems without initial labeled data. Their approach involves querying crowdsourced annotators for information when the model is uncertain, balancing latency, cost, and accuracy using Bayesian decision theory and approximating the optimal policy with Monte Carlo Tree Search. This work builds upon and formalizes ideas initially implemented in [6], offering a principled approach to integrating human feedback during prediction. Related work in online learning [7], active learning [8, 9, 10, 11], and crowdsourcing [3, 4, 25, 26] is well-contextualized, highlighting how this work differentiates itself by actively seeking information *during* prediction and adapting the model based on observed data, unlike static-policy active learning or purely online methods.

The strengths of this paper lie in its novel framing of a practical problem – deploying models in data-scarce environments – and its rigorous mathematical formulation. Modeling the problem as a stochastic game allows for a clear and principled trade-off between competing objectives. The experimental results on three diverse tasks – named entity recognition, sentiment classification, and image classification – demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed approach, achieving substantial cost reductions compared to full annotation and improvements over online learning and a single-label baseline. The availability of an open-source implementation (LENSE) is also a significant positive.

However, the paper also has some weaknesses. The application of the framework to the image classification task is not fully explained. It is unclear how the querying mechanism translates to a non-structured prediction problem, and the utility function's relevance in this context is questionable. The threshold baseline utilizes parameters chosen somewhat arbitrarily; a justification for their selection and a sensitivity analysis would strengthen the evaluation. Furthermore, while the authors distinguish their approach from active learning, a more direct comparison with an adapted active learning baseline—one that updates its model incrementally after receiving annotations—would be beneficial. Such a baseline, operating on the same sequence of inputs, could reveal the advantages of the proposed temporal modeling. The choice of AdaGrad for learning the CRF model is mentioned but could benefit from brief justification. 

Here’s a pro/con summary for acceptance:

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novel and well-motivated problem setting.
*   Principled mathematical formulation using Bayesian decision theory.
*   Strong experimental results demonstrating cost savings and performance improvements.
*   Open-source implementation promotes reproducibility and further research.
*   Clear connection to and differentiation from relevant prior work.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

*   Lack of clarity regarding the application of the framework to non-structured prediction tasks.
*   Arbitrary parameter selection in the threshold baseline.
*   Missing comparison to a more directly comparable, adapted active learning baseline.

Overall, this paper presents a significant contribution to the field of online learning and human-in-the-loop machine learning. While some clarifications and additional comparisons would be valuable, the novelty, rigor, and promising experimental results justify acceptance. I recommend accepting this paper, contingent on the authors addressing the concerns raised regarding the image classification task and the threshold baseline parameters.
