This paper is commendable for comparing the accuracy of classifiers to human performance, providing a useful benchmark for evaluating the proposed LENSE system. The results in Table 2 are particularly noteworthy, as they indicate that LENSE outperforms the 3-vote baseline but underperforms the 5-vote baseline. This finding has significant implications for understanding the relative efficiencies of de-noising crowd responses via additional consensus versus the LENSE system approach. However, the example provided in the paragraph starting at Line 154 is somewhat abstract and challenging to follow. To enhance readability, it would be beneficial to use a concrete query example with crowd responses and times similar to those encountered in real-world scenarios. Furthermore, the terminology used in Tables 2 and 3 is inconsistent, with the threshold baseline sometimes referred to as "entropic" and other times as "threshold." This inconsistency may cause confusion and should be standardized. Additionally, the placement of the related work in Section 5 is unclear. Overall, the problem tackled by this paper is compelling, but the explanations could be improved for clarity.

The paper addresses the challenging problem of creating high-accuracy classifiers without any labeled examples. One key component of this problem is handling noisy crowd respondents, a topic that has been explored from various angles in the literature. However, the novel aspect of this paper lies in its consideration of timing and latency optimization factors, which adds an interesting dimension to the existing body of work (Line 086: Figure ??).
