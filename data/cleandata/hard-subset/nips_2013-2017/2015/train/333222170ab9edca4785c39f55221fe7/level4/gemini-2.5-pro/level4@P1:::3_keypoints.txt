- Introduces a principled "on-the-job learning" framework combining real-time crowdsourcing with online learning to reduce human reliance over time.
- Formulates the problem as a stochastic game grounded in Bayesian decision theory where the system queries, waits, or predicts to maximize expected utility.
- Models continuous time (queries at s, responses at t) and balances accuracy, cost, and latency in the utility function.
- Uses Monte Carlo Tree Search with progressive widening to approximate the intractable optimal policy in continuous time.
- Presents LENSE and evaluates it on NER, sentiment, and face classification against baselines including 1/3/5-vote humans, an online learner with gold labels, and a "threshold" baseline.
- Results: LENSE achieves high accuracy from the first example, reduces queries over time, outperforms online learner and 1-vote baselines, and approaches 5-vote performance at lower cost.
- Praises originality, significance, and practical impact; recommends acceptance as a strong candidate likely to inspire further work.
- Major clarity concern: continuous-time modeling adds significant complexity while yielding marginal experimental gains over the simpler threshold baseline.
- Suggests restructuring: present the core non-time-aware framework first (the threshold model) and introduce continuous-time as an extension to improve accessibility and adoption.
- Missing baselines: include 5-vote human results for Sentiment and Faces and the Threshold baseline in sentiment experiments/figures for fair comparison.
- Performance claim should be qualified: LENSE outperforms a single expert but not the 5-vote human baseline on NER.
- Presentation tweak: bold best-performing results in each table column (e.g., 5-vote F1 on NER, Threshold latency on NER, LENSE latency on Faces).
- Methodology question: unclear whether a hard deadline t_deadline or other stopping criterion was used to decide when to stop querying.
- Methodology question: clarify the Threshold baseline update inequality, which appears incorrect (inequality direction or threshold value seems mistaken).
- Overall: significant, well-executed work with strong results, but impact would improve with clearer presentation and justification of the continuous-time complexity.
