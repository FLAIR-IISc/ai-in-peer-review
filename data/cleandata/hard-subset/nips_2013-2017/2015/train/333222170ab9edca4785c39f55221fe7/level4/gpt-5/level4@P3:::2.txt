Summary and relation to prior work:
This paper addresses the problem of deploying high-accuracy systems from a cold start by combining real-time crowdsourcing with a learning model that improves on-the-job. The authors formalize decision making (what to query, when to wait, when to return a prediction) as a stochastic game grounded in Bayesian decision theory, trading off accuracy, cost, and latency. Because exact planning is intractable, they approximate the optimal policy with Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) and progressive widening to handle continuous time. The approach is evaluated on NER, sentiment classification, and image classification, with a reported open-source implementation (LENSE).

The work is positioned against online learning and online active learning, emphasizing that unlike those settings, the system can acquire information before predicting. It also relates to active classification (test-time feature acquisition) but differs by learning during test time and never observing true labels. The closest prior system-level effort appears to be Legion:AR (on-the-job crowd-powered activity recognition), while the decision-theoretic control perspective relates to POMDP-style crowd control (e.g., TurKontrol). The use of UCT and progressive widening follows established MCTS literature.

Strengths:
- Problem significance: A practical and important setting for rapid deployment when labeled data is scarce.
- Principled formulation: Clear decision-theoretic objective balancing accuracy, time, and monetary cost; intuitive behavior emerges (e.g., redundancy for noisy responses, parallelization for latency).
- Technical contribution: Modeling the control problem as a stochastic game and applying MCTS with progressive widening for continuous response-time dynamics is appropriate and novel in this context.
- Structured prediction: Extends beyond simple classification to CRF-based NER, where partial queries and label correlations matter.
- Empirical results: Across three tasks, LENSE achieves notable cost reductions; on NER it reportedly improves over single-human labeling and online learning. Open-source code improves reproducibility.

Weaknesses and suggestions:
Quality:
- Evaluation clarity versus claims: In Table 2, LENSE appears to outperform the 3-vote baseline but underperform the 5-vote baseline in accuracy; please reconcile this with the textual claim that it outperforms human-only comparisons “on both quality and cost.” If the main argument is cost-effectiveness at comparable accuracy, make that explicit (e.g., Pareto frontier plots) and quantify cost to match 5-vote quality.
- Utility/metric alignment: The utility uses expected accuracy of a MAP prediction, but the results emphasize F1 (especially for NER). Please discuss the mismatch and whether optimizing ExpAcc approximates F1 or if a structured surrogate was used; consider reporting token/entity-level accuracy alongside F1 or adapting the utility to optimize a closer proxy.
- Worker/error modeling: The response model fixes pR(r=y|y)=0.7 and distributes the remainder uniformly. This seems simplistic given known heterogeneity in worker quality and item difficulty. Comparing to (or incorporating) per-worker reliability or Bayesian aggregation (e.g., Dawid–Skene, TurKontrol) would strengthen the evaluation. At minimum, provide sensitivity analysis to the assumed 0.7 and to non-uniform confusions.
- Simulation protocol: Results are driven by a pre-collected pool of 5 responses and delays per label. While this controls variance, it may understate real-time variability and adaptation effects. Please report complementary results with live crowd interactions or larger sampled pools, and include confidence intervals.
- Hyperparameters and ablations: Report how wM and wT were chosen, and provide sensitivity analyses. Include ablations for (i) MCTS parameters, (ii) progressive widening on/off, and (iii) parallel vs sequential querying policies to validate the decision-theoretic gains over heuristics.

Clarity:
- Walkthrough example: The paragraph starting at line 154 is hard to follow. A concrete, step-by-step example showing queries, response times, posterior updates, and the return decision would greatly aid readability. Using a short NER sentence with realistic timestamps would be ideal.
- Baseline naming: In Tables 2 and 3 and the text, the “threshold” baseline is sometimes called “entropic.” Please standardize the name throughout and describe the heuristic clearly once.
- Section/figure references: There is a “Figure ??” placeholder at line 86; fix broken references. I was also confused about the location/numbering of the Related Work section—ensure consistency and update cross-references.
- Minor copyediting: A few typos (e.g., “Retreival,” “miscellaneos,” “burgenoning,” “one-the-job”) should be corrected.

Originality:
- The on-the-job formulation, decision-theoretic control, and application of MCTS with progressive widening to real-time crowdsourcing are novel in combination and go beyond prior systems such as Legion:AR by formalizing the learning and control problem. The paper also connects to structured prediction with partial, noisy observations. Originality is solid.

Significance:
- If the reported gains hold under more realistic worker models and live settings, this work could meaningfully impact how practitioners deploy ML systems under tight data, time, and cost constraints. The open-source release further increases potential impact.

Questions for the authors:
- How sensitive are results to wM and wT, and how should practitioners set them for a target accuracy/latency budget?
- Can the system adaptively estimate pR (e.g., per-worker reliabilities) online and does that improve performance?
- How often does the “no queries while waiting” constraint materially hurt performance compared to a policy that can interleave launching and waiting?
- For NER, do you optimize entity-level F1 or token-level proxies during decision making?

Pros (arguments for acceptance):
- Addresses an important and practical problem with a principled, general framework.
- Novel integration of Bayesian decision theory with MCTS/progressive widening for real-time crowd interaction.
- Demonstrated across multiple tasks; strong cost-effectiveness on NER; code and data released.

Cons (arguments against acceptance):
- Some evaluation claims appear inconsistent with tables; needs clarification and stronger cost–quality analysis relative to multi-vote baselines and POMDP-based crowd control.
- Simplistic noise model and limited simulation protocol may inflate performance; lacks sensitivity analyses and ablations.
- Clarity issues (example walkthrough, baseline naming, broken references) hinder readability.

Overall assessment:
A compelling and timely contribution with a sound formulation and promising empirical results, particularly for NER. I would lean toward acceptance if the authors (i) reconcile claims with the reported numbers, (ii) strengthen the evaluation with sensitivity analyses and clearer cost–quality tradeoffs, and (iii) improve clarity with a concrete example and consistent baseline/section references.
