

This paper proposes a theoretically grounded formulation for an "on-the-job learner" that transitions from pure crowdsourcing to a learned model querying the crowd only on low-confidence inputs. The core innovation lies in modeling the query decision as a Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) problem, enabling a principled shift from crowd reliance to autonomous prediction. The presentation is generally clear, though it omits critical implementation details. Empirical results against a threshold-based heuristic show modest gains, suggesting the approach is reasonable but not yet transformative; further investigation is warranted.

Key concerns require clarification:

1.  **Equation (2):** The left-hand side (LHS) must be conditioned on the current state $s$ (e.g., $V^\pi(s)$), as the value function depends on the agent's state. Additionally, the term $p^{(R)}(r_i | y, q_i)$ contains $y$, but the observation $y$ should not appear in the reward distribution for the query decision. Clarify the role of $y$ and its disappearance in the expression.

2.  **Algorithm 1, Line 4:** Define $N(s)$ explicitly. Is it the visit count for state $s$ in the MCTS tree? This is standard, but the notation must be specified for reproducibility.

3.  **Threshold Baseline:** Justify the specific values (0.3 and 0.88) used in the threshold heuristic. Were these tuned on a validation set? If so, provide details on the tuning procedure and the dataset used.

4.  **Baseline Comparison & Task Context:** The paper does not establish whether the "online" systems (e.g., the learned model baselines) represent the state-of-the-art for the tasks studied. If they do not, explain why these systems were chosen over stronger baselines. If they are SOTA, then any performance gain is solely attributable to human annotation, not model improvement. Provide specific examples where human annotation significantly boosts performance over the best automated system, and include error analysis to contextualize the gains.

The theoretical contribution is sound and addresses a meaningful gap in adaptive learning systems. However, the empirical evaluation lacks depth in comparing against appropriate baselines and justifying key design choices. Addressing the points above—particularly clarifying the formalism, providing baseline justification, and offering task-specific insights—would significantly strengthen the paper. While the results are not compelling on their own, the core idea merits further exploration, especially with stronger empirical validation.
