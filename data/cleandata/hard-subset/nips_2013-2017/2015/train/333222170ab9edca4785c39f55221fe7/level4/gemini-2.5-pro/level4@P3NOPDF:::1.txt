This paper proposes a principled, decision-theoretic framework for an "on-the-job" learning system. The system begins by relying entirely on crowd-sourced annotations and progressively learns a model to make its own predictions. Over time, it reduces its reliance on the crowd, querying human annotators only when the model's confidence is low. The core contribution is framing the decision of whether to query the crowd for a given input as a sequential decision problem, which is solved using Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS).

The paper's primary strength lies in its novel formulation. Casting the problem of selective crowd-querying into a formal decision-making framework is an elegant and promising direction. The presentation is generally clear. However, the paper currently suffers from a lack of clarity in some key technical details (outlined below) and the empirical evaluation. The performance gains over a simple threshold-based baseline appear modest, which somewhat tempers the excitement for the proposed method's practical impact. Nevertheless, the approach is reasonable and certainly merits further investigation.

**Questions and Suggestions for Clarification:**

The paper would be significantly strengthened if the authors could address the following points:

1.  **Equation (2):** There appears to be a notational issue in Equation (2) that makes it difficult to follow.
    *   Should the left-hand side, P(y | s_i, q_i), also be conditioned on the state s, resulting in P(y | s_i, q_i, s)? The probability of a label *y* for a specific item *i* should depend on the current state of the model.
    *   Could you clarify the marginalization step? It is not immediately clear how *y* is removed from the expression p_R(r_i | y, q_i) during the integration to yield p_R(r_i | s, q_i).

2.  **Algorithm 1:** In Algorithm 1, the notation N(s) is used but does not appear to be defined in the text. Please specify what this term represents.

3.  **Baseline Thresholds:** The threshold-based baseline uses confidence values of 0.3 and 0.88. Could you provide the rationale for selecting these specific values? Were they tuned on a validation set, or do they have some other justification? Understanding how these were chosen is crucial for interpreting the comparative results.

4.  **Baseline Model and Error Analysis:** The experimental evaluation relies on "online" systems as the base learners.
    *   Are these models considered state-of-the-art for the tasks in question? It is important to establish whether the proposed MCTS approach is improving upon a strong, competitive baseline.
    *   If the base learners are indeed state-of-the-art, then any improvement demonstrated is a direct result of a more intelligent querying strategy, which is a significant finding. It would be beneficial to discuss the types of instances where the system correctly decides to query a human, which a simpler baseline would not. A brief error analysis could be very insightful here.
    *   Conversely, if the base learners are not state-of-the-art, the gains might partially stem from the crowd compensating for a weaker underlying model. Clarifying this point would help in accurately assessing the contribution.

**Conclusion:**

In summary, this paper introduces a theoretically sound and interesting approach to managing the trade-off between model prediction and crowd-sourcing in an online learning setting. While the core idea is compelling, the paper would be significantly strengthened by addressing the points of clarification mentioned above, particularly regarding the experimental setup and the justification for the baselines. Despite the modest empirical results, the formulation is novel and warrants further investigation, making it a valuable contribution to the discussion on hybrid human-machine learning systems.
