Summary
This paper addresses the challenge of training high-accuracy classifiers from zero labeled examples by leveraging crowd workers under timing and latency constraints. A central contribution is the LENSE system for denoising and scheduling crowd responses. While noise handling in crowdsourcing has been extensively studied, the paper’s explicit focus on latency-aware decision-making and timing optimizations is novel and interesting.

Strengths
- Human comparison: I appreciate the direct comparison of classifier accuracy to human performance. This grounds the results and makes the practical value of the approach easier to assess.
- Informative baselines: Table 2’s finding that LENSE outperforms a 3-vote consensus but underperforms a 5-vote consensus is useful. It highlights a meaningful trade-off between algorithmic denoising/scheduling and simple consensus escalation.
- Problem significance: Building classifiers from zero labels is compelling, and the combination of crowd noise modeling with latency constraints targets a relevant, underexplored setting.

Weaknesses and points of confusion
- Exposition opacity: Some explanations are hard to follow. In particular, the example beginning at Line 154 is abstract and does not help the reader internalize how LENSE operates in realistic scenarios.
- Inconsistent terminology: In Tables 2 and 3, the same baseline appears to be referred to as both “threshold” and “entropic,” which is confusing. It is unclear whether these are identical, variants of the same method, or distinct baselines.
- Placement of related work: Presenting related work in Section 5 makes it difficult to position the contribution early. Readers would benefit from understanding how the approach differs from prior crowd modeling, aggregation, and active learning work before diving into the method.
- Unresolved reference: Line 086 contains “Figure ??” which suggests a missing or broken reference.
- Interpretation gap: The LENSE vs. 5-vote result invites a cost–accuracy–latency analysis that is not fully developed in the text. The paper could more explicitly contextualize when LENSE is preferable to increasing consensus.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide a concrete, end-to-end example (for Line 154). Walk through a single query that includes:
  - The initial uncertainty state and decision criterion for dispatching to the crowd.
  - A few plausible crowd responses with timestamps and latencies (including conflicting answers).
  - How LENSE updates beliefs and decides whether to solicit additional labels, wait, or stop.
  - The final classification decision and its expected loss/time trade-off.
  Using realistic numbers would make the scheduling and denoising logic tangible.
- Standardize baseline names. If “threshold” and “entropic” refer to the same baseline, pick one name and use it consistently across the paper, tables, and captions. If they are different, clearly define both and ensure the tables and text reflect this distinction.
- Move or summarize related work earlier. A concise related-work subsection in the introduction (with fuller discussion later) would orient readers and clarify contributions relative to:
  - Crowdsourcing aggregation and noise models (e.g., Dawid–Skene and its variants).
  - Active learning with humans in the loop.
  - Real-time/latency-aware crowdsourcing and pricing/scheduling strategies.
  This will help highlight the novelty of the latency-optimized decision process.
- Clarify evaluation framing in Tables 2 and 3. Since LENSE beats 3-vote but trails 5-vote, report or discuss:
  - Relative costs (number of votes solicited) and latencies for each method.
  - Accuracy at matched cost/latency budgets, or Pareto curves showing accuracy vs. time/cost.
  - Scenarios where LENSE achieves comparable accuracy at lower latency or cost.
  This will better justify when LENSE is the right choice versus simply increasing consensus.
- Improve figure and reference hygiene. Resolve “Figure ??” at Line 086 and check for other placeholder references, caption mismatches, or numbering inconsistencies.
- Tighten the narrative. Some portions read as high-level descriptions without sufficient operational detail. A brief algorithm box or pseudocode for the decision policy (when to query, wait, or stop) would aid clarity.

Minor comments
- Define all baselines once, clearly, and reference the definition consistently.
- Ensure table captions are self-contained (dataset, metric, number of tasks, budget constraints).
- If “threshold” refers to an entropy-based stopping rule, define the threshold and how it is chosen or tuned (per-dataset, validation, theoretical guidance).
- Consider adding a brief error analysis to illustrate typical failure modes (e.g., adversarial workers, long-tail classes, or high-ambiguity items).

Overall assessment
The paper tackles an important problem with a thoughtful integration of crowd modeling and latency-aware decision-making. The human-performance comparisons are particularly compelling, and the LENSE vs. consensus analysis is informative. However, several presentation issues—abstract examples, inconsistent baseline naming, late placement of related work, and unresolved references—undermine readability and make it harder to appreciate the contribution’s scope and practical advantages. With clearer exposition, a concrete walkthrough example, standardized terminology, and a cost/latency-aware framing of the empirical results, the paper’s contributions would be much more accessible and convincing.
