

This paper introduces a novel framework called "on-the-job learning" that strategically combines crowdsourcing and incremental model training to achieve high accuracy from the very first prediction without requiring initial labeled data. The authors model the problem as a stochastic game between the system and the crowd, where the system strategically queries specific input components (e.g., tokens in a sentence) to resolve uncertainty, then updates its model based on crowd responses. The framework uses Bayesian decision theory to balance accuracy, latency, and cost, with the optimal policy approximated using Monte Carlo Tree Search with progressive widening.

The paper makes several important contributions to the field. First, it provides a principled framework for a setting that has received little attention in the literature—deploying accurate systems without initial training data while reducing long-term crowdsourcing costs. This contrasts with both traditional supervised learning (which requires extensive labeled data) and pure crowdsourcing (which is expensive and slow). The authors position their work clearly against related approaches: unlike online learning (which only improves asymptotically), online active learning (which cannot maintain high accuracy early on), and active classification (which uses static policies), their framework dynamically improves the model during test time while strategically querying the crowd.

The core technical contribution is the stochastic game formulation with continuous time modeling, which allows for principled tradeoffs between latency, cost, and accuracy. The authors demonstrate the approach on three tasks: named-entity recognition, sentiment classification, and image classification. Their system, LENSE, achieves significant improvements over baselines: more than an order of magnitude reduction in cost compared to full human annotation on NER, 8% F1 improvement over single-human labeling, and 28% F1 improvement over online learning. The results are well-supported by experiments across multiple datasets and baselines.

**Strengths:**
- **Originality:** The paper presents a genuinely novel approach to the problem of deploying accurate systems without initial labeled data, with clear theoretical grounding in Bayesian decision theory and game theory. The stochastic game formulation represents a significant advance beyond previous work on active learning and crowdsourcing.
- **Significance:** The problem addressed is highly relevant for practical applications where labeled data is scarce (e.g., disaster response, real-time monitoring). The demonstrated cost reductions (over an order of magnitude on NER) and accuracy improvements make this work potentially impactful for practitioners.
- **Quality:** The technical approach is sound, with appropriate theoretical grounding and thorough experimental validation. The implementation details (including crowd worker management and response modeling) are well-described, and the results are convincing.
- **Clarity:** While the core idea is well-explained, the presentation of the continuous time component (Section 3) is unnecessarily complex. The authors could have developed the core method without continuous time first, then presented time as an extension. This would make the paper more accessible while preserving the main contribution.

**Weaknesses:**
- The introduction of continuous time (Section 3) significantly increases complexity without clear benefits in performance. The threshold baseline (Section 5) performs nearly as well as LENSE on NER (Table 2) and better on Face ID, with substantially lower latency. This undermines the justification for the complex continuous-time formulation. The paper would be stronger if it presented the core method without continuous time first, then showed time as an optional extension.
- Several important results are missing from the paper: the 5-vote crowdsourcing baseline is not included for Sentiment and Face ID tasks (Tables 2-3, Figure 4), despite being a key baseline for NER. The 5-vote results should be included for all tasks and bolded as the best-performing baseline.
- Minor technical issues: In Equation (2), the distribution should include the time parameter s; the "Threshold" baseline description (lines 246-252) contains a sign error in the uncertainty reduction formula; and the paper claims "beating human performance" without clarifying that the 5-vote baseline is actually better than LENSE on NER.

**Pro/Con for Acceptance:**
- *Pro:* This is a highly original, significant contribution with strong technical quality and compelling experimental results. The problem is well-motivated, the approach is principled, and the results demonstrate clear practical value.
- *Con:* The presentation could be substantially improved by simplifying the continuous time component and including all baseline results for all tasks. These are fixable issues, but they currently detract from the paper's clarity and impact.

Overall, this is a strong paper with significant potential impact. The core idea of on-the-job learning is novel and well-executed, and the results are impressive. The main issue is that the continuous-time component, while theoretically interesting, complicates the paper without providing clear performance benefits. With some refinement to focus on the core contribution and include all baseline results, this paper would be excellent. As it stands, it represents a meaningful contribution to the field that should be accepted, with the understanding that the authors could improve the paper further in camera-ready form by simplifying the continuous time component and including all baseline results.
