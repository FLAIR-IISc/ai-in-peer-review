- Summary: Formalizes "on-the-job learning" where a model starts with no labels, queries crowd at test time, and trades off accuracy, latency, and monetary cost while learning from noisy responses.  
- Method: Casts the decision as a stochastic game solved approximately with MCTS plus progressive widening to handle continuous response times.  
- Tasks/evaluation: Evaluated on NER, sentiment, and image classification against human majority, an online learner, and a threshold heuristic.

- Prior work relation: Connects to online learning, active learning, test-time acquisition/value-of-information, crowd-POMDPs (TurKontrol), and real-time crowd-in-the-loop systems, extending them with a decision-theoretic MCTS formulation.

Strengths:
- Clear, timely problem addressing deployment without upfront labeled data and explicit accuracy–latency–cost tradeoffs.  
- Principled Bayesian decision-theoretic objective over structured outputs with noisy, asynchronous crowd responses.  
- Algorithmic novelty in adapting MCTS with progressive widening for hybrid human–machine continuous-time decisions and parallel/redundancy querying.  
- Practical system implementation with real-time retainer infrastructure and open-source artifacts for reproducibility.  
- Empirical gains on NER showing substantial cost reductions and improvements over baselines.

Weaknesses:
- Simplified response model pR (fixed ~70% accuracy, no worker heterogeneity or task difficulty) that may bias policies and underrepresent real crowd noise.  
- Notational and clarity issues plus limited ablations/sensitivity analyses (utility weights, response-time distributions).  
- Baseline strength concerns: online learner may be weak, so gains might primarily come from human input rather than better decision policies.  
- Evaluation lacks deep error analysis and unclear claims about exceeding expert labels (could be aggregation/schema effects).  
- Underexplored structured prediction aspects—insufficient analysis of how sequence dependencies affect query allocation.

Quality:
- Formulation and utility/game dynamics are technically sound and MCTS is an appropriate approximate solver.  
- Learning currently treats noisy crowd responses as ground truth; a latent-variable/noise-aware approach (e.g., Dawid–Skene/EM) would be more robust.  
- Empirical methodology is thoughtful (retainer, pre-sampled pools) but needs sensitivity studies and ablations (MCTS budget, progressive widening, deterministic vs stochastic times).  
- Threshold baseline appears mis-specified and comparisons would benefit from stronger baselines (myopic VOI, dynamic programming, calibrated confidence thresholds).

Clarity:
- Generally well organized and readable, but several definitions, equations, and algorithmic details need clarification (notation, conditioning on s, Algorithm 1 constants).  
- Reporting should include exact features/models per task, how noisy labels are used in updates, and MCTS inference budget.

Originality:
- Novel combination of Bayesian decision-theoretic game formulation with MCTS for structured on-the-job learning in continuous-time crowdsourcing.

Significance:
- Practically compelling for immediate high-accuracy predictions without labeled data; promising NER results though wider task validation would increase confidence.

Specific requests/questions (need clarification or experiments):
- Clarify ExpAcc notation and consistent conditioning on s in Eqs. (1)–(3).  
- Show marginalization steps for p(r′ | x, r, q, s) and state independence assumptions explicitly.  
- Fix Algorithm 1 notation (N(s) vs N(σ)), and report UCT constant, rollout depth/budget, and stopping criteria.  
- Explain and correct threshold baseline parameters (origin of 0.3, inequality sign, reconcile 0.98 vs 0.88, show calibration).  
- Justify online baseline choices and add stronger machine-only baselines or clarify gains mainly from crowd input.  
- State whether CRF is trained on crowd responses as gold or via latent-variable/noise-aware methods, and discuss bias.  
- Provide sensitivity analyses over wM and wT (Pareto fronts) and response-time model pT.  
- Add error analysis with examples where LENSE helps or fails and clarify the claim of outperforming expert labels.

Pros for acceptance:
- Principled, general framework with practical relevance for label-sparse deployment.  
- Novel integration of decision-theory, MCTS, and continuous-time crowdsourcing.  
- Reproducible implementation and strong NER empirical results demonstrating tradeoffs.

Cons for acceptance:
- Simplified crowd/noise model and limited analysis of its impact on policies.  
- Notational/algorithmic ambiguities and potentially weak baselines.  
- Insufficient sensitivity studies and error analysis; uncertain generality beyond NER.

Overall assessment:
- Solid, promising contribution that advances decision-theoretic crowd-in-the-loop learning; would be stronger with clarifications, noise-aware learning, stronger baselines, and additional analyses—reviewer leans positive.
