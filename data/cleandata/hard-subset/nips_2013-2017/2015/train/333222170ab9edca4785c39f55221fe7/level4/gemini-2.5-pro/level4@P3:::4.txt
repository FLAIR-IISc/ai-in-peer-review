This paper introduces "on-the-job learning," a framework for deploying a high-accuracy system that starts with zero training examples. The core idea is to process a stream of inputs, and for each input, use real-time crowdsourcing to resolve uncertainty before making a final prediction. As the underlying model is updated with the crowd's responses, its reliance on human annotation is expected to decrease over time. The authors formalize this problem as a stochastic game using Bayesian decision theory, which provides a principled way to balance the competing objectives of accuracy, latency, and monetary cost. As computing the optimal policy is intractable, the paper proposes an approximation based on Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) with progressive widening. The approach, implemented in a system called LENSE, is evaluated on three tasks: named-entity recognition (NER), sentiment classification, and image classification. The results demonstrate significant cost reductions compared to a pure crowdsourcing approach and substantial accuracy gains over a standard online learning baseline.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Significance and Originality:** The problem setting is both novel and practically significant. It addresses a common real-world scenario where a high-quality predictive model is needed immediately, without the luxury of a pre-existing labeled dataset. The proposed "on-the-job" framework elegantly bridges the gap between traditional supervised learning (which requires upfront data investment) and pure crowdsourcing (which can be prohibitively expensive). This is a valuable contribution that could inspire further research in human-in-the-loop machine learning systems.

2.  **Technical Quality:** The formulation of the problem as a stochastic game grounded in Bayesian decision theory is principled and compelling. It provides a solid theoretical foundation for the sequential decision-making process of when and what to query. The use of MCTS is a well-motivated and appropriate choice for approximating the intractable optimal policy in this complex state space.

3.  **Experimental Results:** The empirical evaluation is strong. The reported results on the NER task are particularly impressive, showing more than an order of magnitude reduction in cost while simultaneously improving F1 score over both human-only and machine-only baselines. The inclusion of experiments on multiple, diverse tasks strengthens the paper's claims.

4.  **Clarity and Reproducibility:** The paper is generally well-written and easy to follow. The authors have also made their code and data publicly available, which is a commendable contribution to the community and greatly facilitates the verification and extension of their work.

**Weaknesses and Areas for Improvement:**

1.  **Clarity on Non-Structured Tasks:** The paper's formulation is well-motivated for structured prediction tasks like NER, where querying a label for one token can propagate information and reduce uncertainty about adjacent tokens via the CRF model. However, its application to the non-structured image classification task ("Faces") is unclear. In an instance-level classification setting, the outputs for different examples are independent. Does the system simply query for the label of the entire instance? If so, it is not apparent how the sequential decision-making process provides a significant advantage over simpler strategies (e.g., querying multiple times if uncertainty remains high after the first response). The paper would be strengthened by explicitly detailing how the framework is adapted for this setting and justifying why the structured game-playing approach remains beneficial.

2.  **Comparison to Active Learning Baselines:** The authors draw a firm distinction between their setting and online active learning. While the ability to query *before* prediction is a key difference, the distinction feels somewhat overstated. Many principles from active learning could be adapted to this setting to create strong baselines. For example:
    *   An **uncertainty sampling** baseline would be highly relevant. For a given input sequence, the system could rank all potential queries (e.g., token labels) by the uncertainty of the current model (e.g., entropy of the marginal distribution) and query the top-k items in parallel. This would provide a direct comparison against a well-established heuristic and help isolate the contribution of the more complex sequential MCTS-based policy.
    *   It seems possible to adapt an active learning framework to this domain by considering the set of possible queries for an example as the "active set" to choose from. While this would not capture the temporal dynamics of waiting for responses, it could draw upon a rich body of work for selecting an informative query set. A more thorough discussion and comparison would better situate the paper's contribution.

3.  **Details of the Threshold Baseline:** The threshold baseline relies on two constants (0.3 and 0.98) that appear arbitrary. The paper would be more complete with a justification for these specific values. Are they tuned on a validation set? A sensitivity analysis showing how performance varies with these parameters would also add credibility to this baseline comparison.

**Summary of Arguments:**

*   **Pro-acceptance:** The paper addresses a novel, important, and practical problem. The proposed technical approach is sound, principled, and well-executed. The experimental results are strong and demonstrate significant real-world benefits. The work is a solid contribution to the field of human-in-the-loop ML.
*   **Con-acceptance:** The applicability and benefits of the framework for non-structured tasks are not clearly explained. The experimental comparison could be strengthened by including more direct and powerful baselines adapted from the active learning literature, which would help to more precisely delineate the paper's novel contributions.

Overall, this is a strong paper that introduces a valuable framework and demonstrates its effectiveness convincingly. The weaknesses, primarily related to clarity on one of the tasks and the choice of baselines, are addressable and do not undermine the core contribution. I believe the work is of high quality and would be of significant interest to the NIPS community.
