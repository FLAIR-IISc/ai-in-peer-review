

The paper introduces "on-the-job learning," a framework where annotators are queried *during* the prediction process for a sequence of examples, rather than before. While the approach is well-motivated for structured prediction tasks (e.g., querying specific sequence elements), its application to the face recognition task remains unclear. Specifically, it is ambiguous whether the "query" in this non-structured setting involved the entire instance (e.g., asking for a label about the face) or individual features. Crucially, in non-structured settings, the model cannot propagate information between examples (as there are no inherent sequential dependencies), rendering the proposed utility function—designed to leverage temporal context—problematic. Without this propagation, the rationale for querying specific points in the sequence collapses, suggesting a fundamental mismatch between the framework and the face recognition task.

The threshold-based baseline also lacks justification. Its two parameters appear arbitrary, with no explanation of their selection or sensitivity analysis. How were these values determined? How do results vary with small perturbations? A more rigorous treatment of baseline parameters is essential for credibility. Furthermore, the omission of an uncertainty sampling baseline—standard in active learning—weakens the comparison. Such a baseline would provide a stronger reference point, as uncertainty sampling is a natural candidate for query selection in sequential settings.

The authors emphasize a distinction between their work and active online learning, arguing the latter cannot model the "on-the-job" scenario. However, this distinction appears overstated. Active learning could be adapted to this domain by treating the *sequence* as the active set (e.g., querying the model about a specific position in the sequence, updating the model upon receiving the response, and proceeding to the next example). While this adaptation would not capture the *temporal dynamics* of the authors' framework (e.g., how prior queries influence later predictions), it leverages established active learning theory for query selection. The authors’ claim of novelty hinges on this distinction, yet the adaptation is straightforward, suggesting the core idea may not be as novel as presented.

Overall, the framework is conceptually interesting but suffers from implementation ambiguities and limited scope. The unclear handling of non-structured tasks (like face recognition) undermines the generality claim, and the lack of rigorous baseline comparisons weakens the empirical contribution. The paper’s focus on a narrow setting—where queries are interleaved with predictions—also restricts applicability. While the temporal aspect is novel, the method’s viability hinges on resolving the structural inconsistencies in non-structured settings. Without clarification on the face recognition implementation and stronger baselines, the work’s significance remains uncertain. The framework is promising but requires deeper technical justification and broader validation to justify its novelty claim.
