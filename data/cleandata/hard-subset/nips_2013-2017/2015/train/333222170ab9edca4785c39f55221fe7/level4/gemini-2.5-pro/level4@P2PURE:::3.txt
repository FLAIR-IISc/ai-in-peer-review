This paper introduces a hybrid system that combines crowdsourced labels with an incrementally trained online model. The central idea is to rely heavily on human input during the initial stages and progressively decrease this reliance as the model's confidence improves. The authors formalize this problem as a stochastic game between the system and the crowd, built upon a Conditional Random Field (CRF) prediction model. In this game, the system issues queries (q) for tokens (x) and receives responses (r), with an associated utility U(q,r). The system's goal is to select actions that maximize its expected utility. A key contribution is the incorporation of continuous time: queries are issued at time s and receive responses at time t, with the system optimizing its strategy to meet a specified deadline (t_deadline).

Calculating the expected utility requires a simulation dynamics model, P(y,r,t|x,q,s). However, optimizing this utility in continuous time is intractable. The authors therefore propose an approximation based on Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) and Temporal-Difference (TD) learning. The proposed model, LENSE, was evaluated on three tasks: named entity recognition (NER), sentiment analysis, and face identification. It was compared against several baselines: majority vote from n human judges (n=1,3,5), a standard online learning model trained with true labels, and a "threshold" baseline. This "threshold" baseline is a simplified version of the authors' model that omits the continuous-time component, instead issuing a fixed number of queries (m) until the model's uncertainty falls below a predefined threshold. The results show that the full LENSE model outperforms all baselines except for the 5-vote human consensus, although the "threshold" baseline achieves comparable performance (and notably lower latency on the NER task). The authors also demonstrate that their system requires progressively fewer crowd labels as the underlying model improves and that it achieves high accuracy from the very first example by leveraging the crowd when model uncertainty is high.

This is an interesting and significant paper that I recommend for inclusion in the conference. The proposed method represents, to my knowledge, the first principled and comprehensive framework for hybrid crowd-machine learning, moving beyond the more heuristic approaches common in prior work. The empirical results, evaluated against strong baselines, are impressive.

However, the paper's primary weakness is its clarity, which could hinder its potential impact. The main source of confusion is the introduction of continuous time in Section 3. While the motivation of managing time and cost budgets is sound, this addition substantially complicates the formulation and optimization procedure without offering a clear empirical benefit. In fact, the "threshold" baseline, which omits the time component, achieves nearly identical performance on the NER task (while being significantly faster) and slightly better performance on the face identification task. The authors' argument that the benefit is minimal on the single-label face ID task does not fully explain why the benefit is also so marginal on the structured NER task.

Given these results, the "threshold" method appears to be a significant contribution in its own right—a principled hybrid system that already outperforms standard active and online learning. The paper's impact could be enhanced by restructuring the narrative. The authors could first fully develop the simpler, time-agnostic "threshold" model, making it easier to understand and implement. The continuous-time model could then be presented as an extension. Similarly, the results discussion should more clearly acknowledge the strength and practical utility of the "threshold" baseline, even in sequence-labeling tasks. While the paper is strong enough for acceptance as is, these changes would improve its accessibility and encourage wider adoption of the core ideas.

Finally, there are several smaller issues regarding the experimental reporting and method description:
*   The claim in the introduction that the model "beats human performance" is not supported by the NER results, where the 5-vote human consensus performs best.
*   Results for the 5-vote baseline are missing from the Face-ID and Sentiment tasks in several places (e.g., Table 2, Table 3, Figure 4). This baseline should be included for a complete comparison.
*   The "Threshold" baseline is missing entirely from the sentiment analysis experiment (Table 3, Figure 4) and should also be included.
*   In Table 2, boldfacing should be used consistently to indicate the best-performing method for each metric. For example, the 5-vote F1 score for NER and the latency for the Threshold baseline on NER should be bolded.
*   The role of `t_deadline` in the experiments is unclear. Was a deadline used, and if so, how was the final answer triggered (e.g., by reaching the deadline or by an entropy threshold)?
*   In Equation (2), the distribution p(y,r,t|x,q) appears to be missing the conditioning variable `s`.
*   In Equation (5), the notation `F(q)` is confusing, as `q` is already used for queries. A different symbol should be used for the distribution function.
*   The description of the Threshold baseline (l.246-252) likely contains a typo. The inequality should probably be `(1 - p_theta(yi|x))*0.3^m <= (1-0.88)`, as uncertainty should decrease with more queries `m`.

In summary, this paper presents a novel and original hybrid approach for combining crowd and machine intelligence, elegantly formulated as a stochastic game. The model demonstrates impressive empirical results against several relevant baselines. The primary weakness is a lack of clarity stemming from the introduction of a continuous-time formulation, which adds significant complexity for what appears to be a minimal performance gain over a simpler, time-agnostic version of their model. Despite this, the contributions are significant, and I recommend acceptance.
