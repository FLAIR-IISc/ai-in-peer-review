The main contribution of this paper is to provide a more theoretically sound formulation of an on-the-job learner, initially proposed in [22]. This learner commences by relying on the crowd to produce its output but gradually transitions to making its own predictions, only querying the crowd when necessary. The decision to query the crowd for any given input is modeled as a decision problem, solved using Monte Carlo Tree Search.

The presentation is generally clear, although it lacks important details in some areas. The empirical results, particularly in comparison to a threshold-based heuristic, are somewhat underwhelming. Nevertheless, the approach appears reasonable and warrants further investigation.

To strengthen the paper, the authors should address the following points:

1. Equation (2) requires clarification. The left-hand side should also be conditioned on s, and it is unclear how the variable y in p(R|ri, y, qi) is eliminated.
2. The definition of N(s) in Algorithm 1 is not provided and should be explicitly stated.
3. The authors should provide a clear explanation for the choice of 0.3 and 0.88 in the threshold baseline.
4. To better understand the performance of the proposed method, it would be beneficial to know whether the "online" systems used are the best machine-learned systems for this task. If not, the authors should justify their choice. If they are, it would be helpful to discuss cases where the gain is purely due to human annotation. Some error analysis would also be useful in this context.

This paper presents a theoretical formulation of an "on-the-job learner" that evolves from a pure crowdsourcing system to a learned model, querying the crowd only for low-confidence inputs. Although the empirical results are mixed, the paper contributes to an important discussion and merits further exploration.
