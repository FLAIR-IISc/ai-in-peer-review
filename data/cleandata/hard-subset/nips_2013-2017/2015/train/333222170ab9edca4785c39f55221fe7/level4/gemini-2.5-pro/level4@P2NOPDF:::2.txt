This paper addresses the compelling challenge of creating high-accuracy classifiers from the ground up, starting with zero labeled examples and relying on crowdsourcing. A significant component of this work involves managing noisy responses from crowd workers. While this is a well-studied area, the paper's focus on incorporating timing and latency as optimization factors presents a novel and interesting contribution to the field.

A key strength of the paper is its direct comparison of the proposed classifier's accuracy to human performance benchmarks. The results in Table 2 are particularly insightful; the observation that the LENSE system outperforms a 3-vote majority baseline but underperforms a 5-vote baseline is significant. This finding provides a valuable analysis of the relative efficiencies of two different de-noising strategies: the algorithmic approach of LENSE versus the simpler method of improving consensus by collecting additional labels. This trade-off is an important practical consideration that the paper highlights well.

However, the paper's primary weakness lies in its clarity, with several sections being difficult to follow. The overall explanation of the method feels opaque, which detracts from the novel ideas presented. A specific area for improvement is the example given in the paragraph starting at Line 154. The current description is highly abstract, making it difficult to build intuition about the system's mechanics. The authors could greatly improve readability by grounding this example in a concrete scenario. For instance, walking through the process with a specific query, a set of plausible crowd responses, and their corresponding timings would make the method much easier to understand.

Furthermore, there are several points of confusion in the presentation:

*   **Inconsistent Terminology:** In Tables 2 and 3, a baseline method is referred to as "entropic" in some instances and "threshold" in others. This inconsistency creates unnecessary confusion and should be standardized throughout the paper.
*   **Paper Structure:** The placement of the Related Work section (Section 5) is unconventional. Typically, this section appears earlier in a paper (e.g., after the introduction) to properly contextualize the work before the proposed method is detailed. The current structure disrupts the logical flow, and the authors should consider moving this section to a more standard position.
*   **Missing Reference:** There appears to be a missing figure reference on Line 086 ("Figure ??").

In summary, the paper tackles an important problem with a novel approach, particularly concerning the use of latency in modeling crowd responses. However, the potential impact of the work is currently hindered by opaque explanations and presentational issues. Improving the clarity of the core method, especially through the use of a concrete example, and addressing the inconsistencies would significantly strengthen the submission.
