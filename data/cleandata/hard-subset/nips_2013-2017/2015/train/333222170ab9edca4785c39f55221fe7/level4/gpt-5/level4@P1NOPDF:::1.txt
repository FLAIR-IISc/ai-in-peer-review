The main contribution of this paper is a more theoretically grounded formulation of the on-the-job learner proposed in [22]. The learner initially relies on querying the crowd to produce outputs, but over time it transitions to making its own predictions and only queries the crowd for low-confidence inputs. The decision of whether to query the crowd for a given input is cast as a decision problem and solved via Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS).

The presentation is generally good but omits several important details. The empirical results, compared to a simple threshold-based heuristic, are somewhat underwhelming. Nevertheless, the approach is reasonable and merits further investigation.

Please address/clarify the following:
1. Equation (2) is unclear. The left-hand side should also be conditioned on s. Additionally, how does y in p_R(r_i | y, q_i) disappear?
2. What is N(s) in Algorithm 1?
3. How were the 0.3 and 0.88 thresholds for the baseline determined?
4. I am not familiar with the specific tasks used here. Are the “online” systems the best machine-learned systems for these tasks? If not, why not? If they are, then any gains must come purely from human annotation. Please provide examples of such cases and include some error analysis.

Overall, the paper presents a clear theoretical formulation of an on-the-job learner that begins as a pure crowdsourcing system and progressively transitions to a learned model that only queries the crowd on low-confidence inputs. While the empirical results are mixed, the direction is promising and worth further exploration.
