The authors cast the problem as a stochastic game based on Bayesian decision theory, which allows for principled trade-offs between latency, cost, and accuracy. Computing the optimal policy is intractable, so they develop an approximation using Monte Carlo Tree Search. They evaluate their approach on three datasets—named-entity recognition, sentiment classification, and image classification—demonstrating significant cost reductions compared to full human annotation and improvements over existing online learning methods. The open-source implementation, LENSE, is a valuable contribution.

**Quality:** The paper appears technically sound, with a clear mathematical formulation and a well-defined experimental setup. Claims are generally well-supported by experimental results, although some aspects of the explanation could be more detailed. It is a complete piece of work that thoughtfully addresses both the strengths and weaknesses of the proposed approach.

**Clarity:** While the overall structure is logical, the writing could benefit from some polishing for clarity. The explanation of the game-theoretic formulation is dense and could be made more accessible with simpler language and more illustrative examples. The inconsistent terminology for the threshold baseline detracts from readability. 

**Originality:** The combination of Bayesian decision theory, Monte Carlo Tree Search, and on-the-job learning is novel and represents a significant contribution to the field. The authors appropriately reference related work, acknowledging prior efforts in online learning, active learning, and crowdsourcing.

**Significance:** The results are significant, demonstrating substantial cost reductions and accuracy improvements compared to existing methods. This work has the potential to be highly impactful in scenarios where labeled data is scarce or expensive to obtain, such as real-time information extraction and disaster relief efforts. The provision of an open-source implementation further enhances its impact.
