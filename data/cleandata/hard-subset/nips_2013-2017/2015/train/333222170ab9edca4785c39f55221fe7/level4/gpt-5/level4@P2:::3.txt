This paper proposes an on-the-job learning framework that produces high-accuracy predictions from the outset by combining real-time crowdsourcing with a model that improves over time. The system treats prediction as a stochastic game between the learner and the crowd, grounded in Bayesian decision theory, with a utility function that trades off accuracy, latency, and monetary cost. For structured prediction, a CRF provides pθ(y | x); the system can issue token-level queries, receive noisy responses asynchronously, and decide whether to issue more queries, wait, or return a prediction. Exact optimization is intractable, especially with continuous time and asynchronous responses, so the authors approximate the optimal policy using Monte Carlo Tree Search with progressive widening. Experiments on NER, sentiment classification, and face classification compare LENSE against human n-vote baselines, an online-learning oracle, and a non-temporal “threshold” variant of the proposed method. Results show substantial cost savings relative to full human annotation, strong accuracy from the first example (via crowdsourcing when the model is uncertain), and decreasing reliance on the crowd as the model improves.

Relation to prior work: The paper clearly engages with online learning, active learning, active classification, and crowdsourcing. Unlike online/active learning, the system can acquire information before committing to a prediction, allowing high initial accuracy. Unlike most active classification work, it learns at test time and never observes gold labels directly. Compared to prior heuristic systems that mix crowd and machine labeling, this is a principled, decision-theoretic formulation with a concrete planning algorithm.

Evaluation (NIPS criteria)
- Quality: The formulation is sound and the components are well-motivated. The utility properly balances accuracy, cost, and time; MCTS with progressive widening is a reasonable approximation for continuous-time planning. Empirically, LENSE performs strongly vs. baselines and demonstrates meaningful cost–accuracy trade-offs. That said, the clarity issues around the continuous-time component make it hard to assess when and how much it helps empirically; in the current results, the simpler threshold variant is often close and sometimes faster (e.g., NER latency).
- Clarity: The paper is generally readable up to the point where continuous time is introduced; after that, notation and exposition become dense. Several reporting and notation inconsistencies (detailed below) further detract from clarity.
- Originality: High. Casting on-the-job learning as a stochastic game with Bayesian decision-theoretic utility and solving it approximately with MCTS/progressive widening is novel and integrates several ideas in a new way.
- Significance: High. The setting is practically important, the approach is general, and the empirical results suggest real-world impact. The open-source release is a plus.

Strengths
- Principled, end-to-end framework for real-time, cost-aware hybrid human–machine prediction.
- Clear utility trade-offs that produce intuitive strategies (parallel vs. sequential querying, redundancy for noisy workers).
- Strong empirical results showing reduced cost and immediate high accuracy, with decreasing reliance on the crowd over time.
- Open-source implementation and datasets for reproducibility.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Continuous time vs. threshold baseline: While the continuous-time formulation is well motivated, the experimental benefit appears limited and sometimes comes with higher latency or complexity. The threshold baseline—essentially the same model without continuous time—performs nearly as well on NER and better on face ID latency. For many practical deployments, the threshold variant may suffice. A clearer narrative would (i) present the non-temporal version as the core method, easy to implement and already strong, and (ii) introduce continuous time as an extension, with concrete guidance on when it helps (e.g., tight latency budgets, highly variable response times).
- Reporting gaps/inconsistencies:
  - The 5-vote human baseline is omitted for sentiment and face tasks in several tables/figures (e.g., right half of Table 2, Table 3, Figure 4). It should be included consistently.
  - Claims that the model “beats human performance” should be qualified; for NER, the 5-vote baseline remains the top performer by F1.
  - Highlight best numbers appropriately (e.g., bold 5-vote F1 in Table 2; bold Threshold latency for NER and LENSE latency for face ID).
  - The Threshold baseline is missing for sentiment in Table 3 and Figure 4; please include it.
- Exposition and notation:
  - Stopping criterion: The text discusses a time budget (t_deadline) but it is unclear what stopping rule was used in experiments. Was prediction returned at a fixed deadline, when an entropy/accuracy threshold was met, or via the utility policy’s return action? Please specify and state the chosen thresholds/weights.
  - In the probability model, p(y, r, t | x, q, s) depends on s; ensure s is included where appropriate (e.g., in equations analogous to (2)/(3)).
  - In the equation defining F(q), the use of q is ambiguous (query index vs. function name). Use a different symbol for the distribution/function to avoid confusion.
  - Threshold baseline description: The inequality appears reversed. If uncertainty is reduced by a factor of 0.3 per additional query, the condition should reflect decreasing uncertainty with increasing m; please correct the sign and state the target threshold explicitly.

Pros for acceptance
- Novel, principled formulation with a practical and timely application area.
- Demonstrated improvements over machine-only, human-only, and online-learning baselines in accuracy–cost trade-offs.
- Clear demonstration that accuracy can be high from the first example and that crowd usage declines as the model improves.
- Open-source code and datasets support impact and reproducibility.

Cons against acceptance
- Clarity and presentation suffer after introducing continuous time; the added complexity yields modest empirical gains in the current experiments.
- Reporting inconsistencies and missing baselines weaken the empirical story.
- Some notation and stopping-criterion details are underspecified.

Recommendation
Accept. The paper is original and significant, with a sound methodology and strong empirical results. Addressing the clarity issues—especially by clearly delineating the non-temporal core method from the continuous-time extension, including all baselines consistently, and fixing the notation/stopping-criterion details—would substantially strengthen the final version and improve adoption.
