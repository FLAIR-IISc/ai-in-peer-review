This paper presents a hybrid approach that leverages both crowdsourced labels and an incrementally trained model to address prediction problems. The core idea is to heavily rely on the crowd during the system's initial phase, learn from the acquired labels, and gradually decrease the crowd's involvement as the model becomes more confident. This approach is formulated as a stochastic game based on a Conditional Random Field (CRF) prediction model, where the system and the crowd are both players. The system can issue one or more queries for tokens, eliciting responses, with each outcome having a utility value. The system aims to maximize the expected utility, taking into account the time constraints and response times.

The computation of the expected utility relies on the simulation dynamics model to calculate the utilities. However, the introduction of continuous time makes the optimization intractable, and an approximation based on Monte Carlo Tree Search and TD learning is used instead. The authors conducted experiments on three tasks: named entity recognition (NER), sentiment recognition, and face identification. They compared their method to four baselines: the majority vote of human judges, online learning with true labels, a "threshold" baseline without continuous time, and their full model (LENSE). The results show that the full model outperforms all but the 5-vote crowdsourced version, although the "threshold" baseline performs nearly as well, with lower latency for NER.

The authors demonstrate that their model requires fewer crowdsourced labels over time, given a sufficiently strong model to train. They also show that their model achieves high accuracy from the first example, leveraging crowd expertise while the model uncertainty is still high. This paper is highly original and significant, presenting a complete and principled proposal for learning from the crowd while training a system. The results against reasonable baselines are impressive, and the work deserves to be included in the conference.

However, the paper's clarity is a major issue, which could detract from its potential impact. The introduction of continuous time, starting in Section 3, is a primary concern. Although the motivation is reasonable, the experimental results suggest that it has little benefit, while increasing the complexity of the formulation and discussion. In fact, the "threshold" baseline achieves significantly lower latency on the NER task while maintaining roughly equivalent performance. The authors' argument that the baseline performs better on the face ID task due to the single-label prediction task and lack of interaction between labels is not convincing, as the NER task, which has significant interaction, only benefits marginally.

For many practical tasks, the "threshold" baseline may be sufficient, offering a significant gain as a principled hybrid learning system that outperforms active and online learning. The paper would likely have greater impact if the authors developed the core method without introducing continuous time, making it easier to understand and implement. The addition of continuous time could then be presented as an extension.

When discussing the results, the authors should emphasize that the "threshold" method performs strongly, even in cases with sequence dependence between tasks. This suggestion is not strictly necessary, as the paper is strong enough for inclusion, but it would improve the paper and increase the chances of the technique being widely understood and adopted.

There are several smaller issues with the results and their reporting. The introduction claims that the model "beats human performance," but this is not true in the 5-vote case for NER. The 5-vote case is missing for the Face-ID and Sentiment tasks in multiple tables and figures, and it should be included. The latency value for the "Threshold" baseline in NER should be bolded, as it represents the best performance. The "Threshold" baseline is also missing for the sentiment experiment and should be included in Table 3 and Figure 4.

Minor comments on the method description include: the discussion of t_deadline is unclear, and it is not specified what t_deadline was used in the experiments or what criteria were used to determine when the answer was ready to report; the distribution in equation (2) should be given the variable s; the use of q in F(q) is confusing and should be replaced with a different letter; and the expression in the description of the Threshold baseline should be corrected to (1 - ptheta(yi|x))*0.3^m <= (1-0.88).

Overall, this paper presents a novel and interesting hybrid approach that leverages crowdsourced labels and an incrementally trained model. Although it has some clarity issues, particularly with the introduction of continuous time, the method is original and the results are impressive, making it a strong candidate for inclusion in the conference.
