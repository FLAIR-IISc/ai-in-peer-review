

This paper introduces "on-the-job learning," a novel framework for deploying high-accuracy AI systems starting from zero labeled examples. The approach strategically leverages real-time crowdsourcing during deployment to resolve uncertainty, gradually reducing reliance on human input as the model improves. The authors formalize this as a stochastic game using Bayesian decision theory, balancing accuracy, latency, and cost objectives. They propose an approximation via Monte Carlo Tree Search with progressive widening to handle the intractable optimal policy computation. The method is evaluated across three tasks (NER, sentiment, image classification), demonstrating significant cost reductions (over 10× for NER vs. full human annotation) and accuracy gains (8% F1 over single-human labeling, 28% over online learning) compared to baselines. The open-source implementation, LENSE, is available for reproducibility.

**Strengths:**  
The paper addresses a compelling real-world problem—deploying systems without initial labeled data—through a well-motivated, principled framework. The Bayesian decision theory formulation provides a rigorous foundation for trade-offs between accuracy, cost, and latency, which is notably absent in prior work like online learning or active classification. The experimental results are strong and well-supported: the NER cost reduction (10×) and F1 improvements (8% over 1-vote, 28% over online learning) are significant and clearly contextualized against relevant baselines. The system’s ability to reduce query costs over time while maintaining high accuracy (Fig. 4) demonstrates a practical, scalable solution. The open-source release further enhances reproducibility.

**Weaknesses:**  
The primary weakness lies in exposition clarity. The example in Paragraph 3.3 (starting near line 154) is overly abstract, making it difficult to grasp the query-resolution dynamics. A concrete, step-by-step illustration with specific labels, response times, and confidence updates would greatly aid reader comprehension. The terminology around the threshold baseline is inconsistent: it is called "entropic" in Tables 2–3 but "threshold" in Section 5, creating confusion. This should be standardized. The related work discussion (Section 6) is misplaced; it should follow the problem formulation (Section 2) to better contextualize the novelty against prior work on active classification (e.g., Legion:AR) and online learning. The paper also underemphasizes why their Bayesian approach *outperforms* the threshold baseline—while the results show LENSE is better, the technical reason (e.g., adaptive query sequencing vs. static thresholds) warrants clearer explanation.

**Recommendation:**  
This is a high-quality contribution that advances the state of the art in practical AI deployment. The core technical idea—modeling on-the-job learning as a Bayesian stochastic game—is novel, well-justified, and delivers compelling empirical results. The weaknesses are primarily in presentation, not substance. With minor revisions to clarify the methodology example, standardize terminology, and reposition related work, the paper would be excellent. I recommend **acceptance**.  

**Pro/Con Summary:**  
*Pro:* Novel framework for zero-shot deployment; strong empirical results with significant cost/accuracy gains; principled Bayesian decision theory; open-source implementation.  
*Con:* Abstract example in methodology; inconsistent baseline naming; related work section misplaced; insufficient technical explanation of LENSE’s superiority over threshold baseline.
