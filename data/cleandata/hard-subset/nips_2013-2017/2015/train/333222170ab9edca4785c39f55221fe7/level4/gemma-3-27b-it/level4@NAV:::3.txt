This paper introduces a hybrid approach to prediction that leverages both crowdsourced labels and an incrementally trained model. The core idea is to prioritize crowd input during initial stages, learn from these labels, and gradually rely less on the crowd as model confidence increases. This is formalized as a stochastic game based on a CRF prediction model, framing the system and the crowd as interacting players. The system issues queries *q* for tokens *x* (with true label *y*), receiving responses *r* with a utility *U(q,r)*, aiming to maximize expected utility. Importantly, queries are issued over time *s* with response times *t*, optimized with respect to a deadline *t_deadline* which dictates the query rate.

Computing the expected utility requires the simulation dynamics model *P(y,r,t|x,q,s)*, as defined in (4), to evaluate utilities. Optimal action selection is then approximated using Monte Carlo Tree Search and TD learning (Algorithm 1) due to the intractability of optimizing over continuous time. The approach was evaluated on simplified NER, sentiment recognition, and face identification tasks, comparing it against majority vote of human judges (1, 3, and 5 votes), online learning (with true labels), a “threshold” baseline (the authors' model without continuous time, querying until model uncertainty falls below a threshold), and the full LENSE model. Results (Table 2) demonstrate that the full model outperforms all but the 5-vote crowdsourced baseline, while the “threshold” baseline achieves comparable performance with lower latency for NER. The authors also demonstrate a decreasing reliance on crowdsourced labels over time (Figure 3) and faster initial accuracy compared to online learning (Figure 4) due to leveraging crowd expertise during high model uncertainty.

This is a compelling and interesting paper with the potential to stimulate discussion and further research, as well as practical adoption. While various heuristic approaches exist for learning from the crowd during system training, this work presents the first complete and principled proposal I have encountered. The results against reasonable baselines are impressive, making this work highly original and significant, and deserving of inclusion in the conference. However, its clarity could be improved, potentially impacting its broader influence. Many of these issues are correctable, even during the camera-ready stage, with focused effort and a potential adjustment of tone regarding the comparison of the “threshold” baseline and the full continuous-time version.

The primary clarity issue stems from the introduction of continuous time (mid-Section 3). While the motivation – accommodating both time and cost budgets – is understandable, the experimental benefit appears limited while significantly increasing complexity in formulation, discussion, and optimization. In fact, Table 2 suggests the “threshold” baseline achieves superior latency on NER with roughly equivalent performance, and even outperforms LENSE on face ID (though still with equivalent results). The authors attribute the baseline’s success on face ID to the task’s single-label nature, but this advantage isn’t consistently observed on NER, which exhibits substantial label interaction. For many practical applications, the “threshold” baseline may be sufficient, already representing a substantial advancement as a principled hybrid learning system. 

The paper would benefit from explicitly highlighting this point, perhaps by developing the core method fully *without* continuous time for greater clarity and easier implementation, then presenting continuous time as an extension. Similarly, discussing results with greater clarity regarding the strong performance of the “threshold” method, even with sequence dependencies, would be valuable. While not strictly necessary, these refinements would likely enhance the paper's impact and facilitate broader understanding and adoption of the technique.

Finally, several minor issues relate to result reporting. The introduction claims the model “beats human performance,” which is inaccurate for NER with 5 votes.  The 5-vote case is missing from tables and figures for Face-ID and Sentiment (right half of Tables 2, Table 3, and Figure 4) and should be included.  Results for the 5-vote case in Table 2 should be bolded, as should the latency value for Threshold in NER and the LENSE value for Face ID. Importantly, the “Threshold” baseline is absent from the sentiment experiment (Table 3 and Figure 4) and should be included. 

Minor methodological comments include: the criteria for determining response readiness when a *t_deadline* is used is unclear; equation (2) should include *s* (i.e., *p(y,r,t|x,q,s)*); the use of *q* in equation (5) is confusing and should be replaced; and the expression in the Threshold baseline description (l.246-252) should likely be *(1 - pθ(yi|x))*0.3^m <= (1-0.88).* 

Despite these points, this paper presents a hybrid approach for using both crowdsourced labels and an incrementally (online) trained model to address prediction problems, elegantly cast as a stochastic game that models many aspects of the data, true labels, and crowdworker responses, including the time at which queries are sent out and answers received. Impressive results are shown with respect to multiple human labelers, online learning with oracle labels, and a “threshold” baseline using the authors’ model but removing the dependence on continuous time that performs on par. The method is original, novel, and interesting, and as such the paper should be accepted, but there are some issues with clarity, particularly with respect to the introduction of continuous time, which greatly complicates the discussion and algorithmic mechanics yet seems to yield minimal benefits in performance.
