This paper presents a hybrid approach for using both crowdsourced labels and an incrementally (online) trained model to address prediction problems; the core idea is to lean heavily on the crowd as the system is ramping up, learn from the labels thus acquired, and then use the crowd less and less often as the model becomes more confident. This is done via a sophisticated framing of the problem as a stochastic game based on a CRF prediction model in which the system and the crowd are both players. The system can issue one or more queries for tokens to elicit responses, where there is a utility for each outcome; the system thus attempts to pick the actions that will maximize the expected utility. Furthermore, the queries are not issued all at once, but over time; utility is maximized with respect to a deadline by which an answer needs to be computed, determining the number and rate of queries.

Computing this expected utility requires using the simulation dynamics model to compute the utilities. Given the utility values, the optimal action can be chosen; however, the introduction of continuous time makes this intractable to optimize, and as such an approximation is used based on Monte Carlo Tree Search. Experiments were conducted on named entity recognition (NER), sentiment recognition, and face identification tasks, using four methods: majority voting of crowd workers, online learning (using true labels), a “threshold” baseline (the authors’ model without continuous time, where a fixed number of queries are sent until model uncertainty is reduced below a threshold), and the authors’ full model (LENSE). In terms of precision/recall/F1, LENSE often outperforms the baselines, though the 5-vote crowdsourced version frequently achieves the best performance. The authors also show how the model requires fewer crowdsourced labels over time with a sufficiently strong model and that compared to online learning, accuracy is high from the first example, as the system can leverage crowd expertise while model uncertainty is high.

This was a very interesting and potentially impactful paper. There have been heuristic approaches to learning from the crowd while training a system, but this is the first complete and principled proposal I have seen. The results against reasonable baselines are impressive. As such, I believe the work is highly original and significant, and deserves consideration for inclusion in the conference. However, the paper could benefit from improvements in clarity, which could detract from its potential impact. Many of these issues are correctible, even for the camera-ready version, if the authors are willing to refine the presentation and potentially adjust the emphasis on continuous time.

The primary clarity issue arises from the introduction of continuous time, beginning in Section 3. The motivation is reasonable – the need to operate under both time and cost constraints – but experimentally it appears to yield limited benefit while significantly increasing complexity. In fact, Table 2 shows that the “threshold” baseline achieves lower latency on the NER task with comparable performance, and on the face identification task it even outperforms LENSE. The authors argue that the baseline performs better on the face ID task due to the single-label prediction, but the NER task, with its label dependencies, only sees a marginal benefit. 

For many practical tasks, the “threshold” baseline will be sufficient and represents a significant advance as a principled hybrid learning system that outperforms active and online learning. The paper would likely have greater impact if the authors made this clearer in their presentation; the core method could be fully developed without continuous time, making it easier to understand and implement, with the addition of continuous time presented as an extension.

When discussing the results, the authors could also emphasize that the “threshold” method performs strongly, even with sequence dependencies. This is a suggestion, and the paper is strong enough for inclusion as it is, but it would improve understanding and adoption of the technique.

There are also a number of smaller issues with respect to the results and their reporting. First, the introduction states that the model "beats human performance," but this is not true when comparing to the 5-vote case for NER. The 5-vote case is also missing for the Face-ID and Sentiment tasks in multiple tables and figures. It should be included, and the results for the 5-vote case should be bolded as they represent the best performance. Similarly, the latency value for the Threshold baseline in NER should be bolded. More importantly, the "Threshold" baseline is missing for the sentiment experiment and therefore does not appear in Table 3 or Figure 4 – it should be included. 

A few minor comments regarding the method description:
- While the discussion of the deadline is present, it is not clear what deadline, if any, was used in the experiments, or what criteria determined when an answer was ready to report. Was it an entropic threshold, as with the baseline, or reaching some fixed deadline?
- In equation (2), the distribution appears to require the variable s as well (i.e., p(y,r,t|x,q,s)).
- In equation (5), the use of q in F(q) is confusing. I assumed this referred to a query q, but it represents a nominal distribution function. Please choose another letter.
- In the description of the Threshold baseline, the expression should likely be (1 - pθ(yi|x))*0.3^m <= (1-0.88), as increasing m will reduce uncertainty.

In summary, this paper presents a hybrid approach that elegantly casts learning from crowdsourced labels as a stochastic game. Impressive results are shown with respect to multiple baselines. The method is original, novel, and interesting, and as such, the paper should be accepted. However, some issues with clarity, particularly the benefit of continuous time, should be addressed.
