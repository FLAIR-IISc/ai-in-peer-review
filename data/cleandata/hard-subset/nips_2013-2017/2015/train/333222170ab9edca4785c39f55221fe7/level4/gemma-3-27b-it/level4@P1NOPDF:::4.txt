The authors introduce a setting termed “on-the-job learning,” where annotators are queried for information between receiving an example and producing a prediction. While the algorithm’s functionality is clear for structured prediction tasks – specifically, querying individual elements within a sequence – its application to face recognition is less obvious. It’s unclear whether, in this case, the entire instance is simply being queried. Because the model doesn’t propagate information between examples in a non-structured setting, the utility function’s rationale is questionable.

The threshold baseline employs two parameters that appear arbitrary. The origin of these values is not explained, and their sensitivity deserves investigation. An additional baseline utilizing uncertainty sampling, similar to techniques in active learning, would be a valuable comparison. 

Furthermore, the authors emphasize the distinction between this setting and active online learning. However, active learning could be readily adapted to this domain by considering a sequence as the active set. For instance, the model could be updated incrementally upon receiving responses and then output a final prediction. While this approach wouldn't fully capture the temporal aspect of the task, a substantial body of theoretical work exists to guide the selection of queries. 

Overall, the work presents a seemingly novel framework, though some details remain unclear. It also appears relatively restricted in its general applicability.
