This paper introduces "on-the-job learning," a framework for deploying a high-accuracy system from scratch, without any initial training data. The system intelligently queries a crowd of human workers in real-time to resolve uncertainty on incoming data points, making a prediction only when confident. Over time, as the underlying model is updated with the collected labels, its reliance on the crowd decreases, reducing cost and latency.

The authors formalize this problem as a stochastic game between the system and the crowd. They use Bayesian decision theory to create a utility function that balances the competing objectives of accuracy, monetary cost, and latency. Since computing the optimal policy for this game is intractable, they propose an effective approximation using Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) combined with progressive widening to handle the continuous nature of time. This work provides a more rigorous, theoretical foundation for ideas previously implemented in systems like Legion:AR [6] and connects to the related fields of online learning, active learning, and active classification. The proposed system, LENSE, is evaluated on three tasks (NER, sentiment analysis, image classification), demonstrating significant cost reduction compared to a pure crowdsourcing approach and accuracy improvements over a standard online learning baseline.

### Strengths (Arguments for acceptance)

1.  **Novel and Important Problem:** The paper addresses a significant and practical problem. The "on-the-job" setting, where a system must perform well from the very first instance while learning over time, is a realistic scenario that bridges the gap between traditional supervised learning (which requires large, upfront datasets) and pure crowdsourcing (which is expensive and does not learn).
2.  **Principled Formulation:** The primary strength of the paper is its principled and theoretically sound formulation. Modeling the problem as a stochastic game and using Bayesian decision theory to unify the trade-offs between accuracy, cost, and latency is an elegant and powerful approach. This provides a solid foundation for future work in this area.
3.  **Technically Sound Solution:** Given the intractability of finding the optimal policy, the use of Monte Carlo Tree Search is a well-justified and clever choice for approximating the solution. It allows the system to reason effectively about a complex sequence of decisions involving querying, waiting, and predicting.
4.  **Reproducibility:** The authors provide an open-source implementation and the datasets used for the experiments. This is a commendable practice that significantly strengthens the paper and facilitates future research.

### Weaknesses (Arguments against acceptance / Questions for authors)

While the approach is promising, the paper could be significantly strengthened by addressing the following points regarding clarity and the experimental evaluation.

1.  **Clarity of the Model:**
    *   Could the authors provide more detail on the computation of conditional probabilities derived from the joint model in Equation 3? The text states that `p(r' | x, r, q)` is computed by "simply marginalizing out y and t," but this process is central to the model's inference and warrants a more explicit explanation.
    *   In Algorithm 1, the notation `N(σ)` is used. While it is defined in the preceding text as the number of visits to a state, for the algorithm to be self-contained, it would be helpful to define all terms in the algorithm's caption or as comments in the pseudocode.

2.  **Experimental Baselines and Analysis:**
    *   The hyperparameters for the **threshold baseline** (e.g., the uncertainty reduction factor of 0.3 and the confidence threshold of 0.98) appear arbitrary. Could the authors provide a justification for these specific values or include a sensitivity analysis to show how they were chosen? Without this, it is difficult to know if this baseline was tuned to be competitive.
    *   The **"online learning" baseline** needs more characterization. Is this model representative of a strong, state-of-the-art system for these tasks (NER, sentiment, etc.)? The improvement of LENSE over this baseline is attributed to the human queries. However, if the underlying machine learning model is weak, the gains might be overstated. It is crucial to establish the strength of this baseline to properly contextualize the contribution of the on-the-job querying strategy.
    *   A more in-depth **error analysis** would be highly beneficial. What kinds of examples does LENSE correctly handle that the online learning baseline fails on? Understanding which instances benefit most from on-the-job querying would provide valuable insight into the method's strengths and limitations.

3.  **Significance of Empirical Results:**
    *   The performance gains of LENSE over the simpler, heuristic-based threshold baseline are modest on the Sentiment and Faces tasks (Table 2). This raises a question about the practical benefit of the computationally expensive MCTS approach versus a simpler heuristic. A discussion on this complexity-performance trade-off would strengthen the paper's conclusions about the value of the proposed decision-theoretic framework.

### Summary

This paper presents a novel, theoretically-grounded framework for the important problem of on-the-job learning. The formulation as a stochastic game is elegant and the proposed MCTS-based solution is technically sound. The main weaknesses lie in the experimental evaluation, particularly in the characterization of the baselines and the depth of the analysis. Addressing the questions about the baselines and providing a more detailed analysis would substantially improve the paper and make a more convincing case for the significance of the proposed method. Overall, this is a solid contribution that introduces an interesting problem and a reasonable approach, and it merits further discussion in the community.
