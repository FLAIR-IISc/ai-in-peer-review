- Proposes an on-the-job learning framework that starts with zero labels and reduces crowd reliance as the model improves.  
- Models the decision process as a Bayesian decision-theoretic stochastic game balancing latency, cost, and accuracy.  
- Formulates prediction as structured prediction where the system can query crowd responses for uncertain input components.  
- Uses Monte Carlo Tree Search with progressive widening to approximate the intractable optimal policy and handle continuous time.  
- Evaluated on NER, sentiment, and image classification, showing large cost reductions (over an order of magnitude for NER) and accuracy gains over baselines.  
- Builds on prior active learning and online learning work but claims novelty by formalizing on-the-job learning as a stochastic game.  
- Relates to recent crowdsourcing and structured prediction literature but asserts a more principled theoretical foundation.  
- Strength: sound theoretical formulation with a clear utility function balancing key objectives.  
- Strength: MCTS approximation is well-motivated and implementation details are largely reproducible.  
- Strength: Paper is well-organized and figures effectively illustrate the decision process, though some technical parts need clarification.  
- Weakness: Empirical evaluation lacks error analysis explaining varying performance across tasks (strong on NER, weaker on others).  
- Weakness: Threshold baseline parameters (0.3 and 0.88) are unjustified and comparison to a 5-vote human baseline is unexplained.  
- Weakness: “Online learning” baseline is misleading—implemented as an offline system with perfect prior information rather than a comparable online algorithm.  
- Clarity issues: Equation (2) should condition on system state s; environment model notation p_R(r | y_q) and handling of y_q are unclear; N(s) in Algorithm 1 is undefined.  
- Originality concern: should better distinguish contributions from related work (e.g., Legion:AR and recent NIPS crowdsourcing/active learning papers).  
- Significance concern: strong cost reductions for NER are notable, but benefits for other tasks and specific real-world scenarios need clearer discussion.  
- Specific requested clarifications: fix Eq.(2) conditioning and response-model notation; justify threshold baseline parameters; use a fair online learning baseline; clarify what “single human” labeling means.  
- Overall: theoretically sound and practically promising contribution that merits consideration, but needs revisions addressing baseline justification, empirical analysis, and technical clarifications.
