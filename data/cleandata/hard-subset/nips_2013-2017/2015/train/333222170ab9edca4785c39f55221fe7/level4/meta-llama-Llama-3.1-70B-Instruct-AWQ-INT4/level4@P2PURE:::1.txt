This paper presents a more theoretically sound formulation of the on-the-job learner proposed in [22], which begins by relying on the crowd for output and gradually transitions to making its own predictions, querying the crowd only occasionally. The decision to query the crowd for a given input is modeled as a decision problem, solved using Monte Carlo Tree Search.

The presentation is generally clear, although it lacks important details in some areas. The empirical results, which compare the proposed approach to a threshold-based heuristic, are somewhat underwhelming. Nevertheless, the approach appears reasonable and warrants further investigation.

To strengthen the paper, the authors should address the following points:

1. Equation (2) is unclear. The left-hand side should also be conditioned on s, and it is not evident how the y in p(R|y, q_i) is eliminated.
2. The definition of N(s) in Algorithm 1 is not provided.
3. The origin of the 0.3 and 0.88 values in the threshold baseline is not explained.
4. It is unclear whether the "online" systems used in the evaluation are the best machine-learned systems for this task. If not, why were they chosen? If so, any improvement over these systems would be due to human annotation. Providing some examples and error analysis would be helpful in this regard.

Overall, this paper contributes a theoretical formulation of an on-the-job learner that transitions from a pure crowdsourcing system to a learned model that selectively queries the crowd. While the empirical results are mixed, they spark an interesting discussion that merits further exploration.
