This paper presents a novel approach to prediction problems by leveraging both crowdsourced labels and an incrementally trained model. The core idea is to rely heavily on the crowd during the initial stages, learn from the acquired labels, and gradually reduce the crowd's involvement as the model becomes more confident. This is achieved through a sophisticated stochastic game framework based on a Conditional Random Field (CRF) prediction model, where the system and the crowd are both players.

The system issues queries to the crowd, which elicit responses, and each outcome has a utility value. The goal is to maximize the expected utility while considering the time constraints, as queries are not issued simultaneously, but rather at specific times with corresponding response times. The expected utility is computed using a simulation dynamics model, which takes into account the true labels, crowd responses, and time. However, the introduction of continuous time makes the optimization problem intractable, and the authors resort to an approximation based on Monte Carlo Tree Search and TD learning.

The authors conducted experiments on three tasks: named entity recognition (NER), sentiment recognition, and face identification. They compared their approach, LENSE, to four baselines: the majority vote of 1, 3, and 5 human judges, online learning with true labels, and a "threshold" baseline that uses the authors' model without continuous time. The results show that LENSE outperforms all baselines except the 5-vote crowdsourced version, while the "threshold" baseline achieves comparable performance with lower latency in the NER task.

The paper demonstrates the potential of the proposed approach, particularly in leveraging crowd expertise during the initial stages of model training. The authors also show that the model requires fewer crowdsourced labels over time, given a sufficiently strong model to train. However, the introduction of continuous time significantly complicates the discussion and algorithmic mechanics, which may detract from the paper's clarity and impact.

The primary issue with clarity arises from the introduction of continuous time, which seems to have minimal benefits in performance. The "threshold" baseline, which removes the dependence on continuous time, achieves comparable performance in most tasks and even outperforms LENSE in the face identification task. The authors could improve the paper's clarity by developing the core method without continuous time and presenting the addition of continuous time as an extension.

Additionally, there are several minor issues with the results and their presentation. The introduction states that the model "beats human performance," but this is not true in the 5-vote case for NER. The 5-vote case is missing for the Face-ID and Sentiment tasks in multiple tables and figures, and the results for the 5-vote case should be bolded in Table 2. The latency value for the "Threshold" baseline in NER should also be bolded. Furthermore, the "Threshold" baseline is missing for the sentiment experiment and should be included in Table 3 and Figure 4.

Some minor comments on the method description include the need to clarify the t_deadline used in the experiments and the criteria for determining when the answer is ready to report. The distribution in equation (2) should be given the variable s, and the use of q in equation (5) is confusing and should be replaced with a different letter. Finally, the expression in the description of the "Threshold" baseline should be corrected to (1 - ptheta(yi|x))*0.3^m <= (1-0.88).

In conclusion, this paper presents a novel and interesting approach to prediction problems, and the results are impressive. However, the introduction of continuous time complicates the discussion and algorithmic mechanics, and the authors could improve the paper's clarity by developing the core method without continuous time and presenting the addition of continuous time as an extension. With some revisions to address the minor issues, the paper has the potential to make a significant impact in the field.
