This paper presents a hybrid approach for addressing prediction problems by combining crowdsourced labels with an incrementally trained online model. The core idea is to rely heavily on the crowd in the early stages and then query human labellers progressively less as the model's confidence increases. This problem is elegantly framed as a stochastic game between the system and the crowd, based on a Conditional Random Field (CRF) prediction model. The system chooses actions (queries) to maximize its expected utility, considering the time `s` a query is sent and the time `t` a response is received, all within a specified deadline `t_deadline`. Since optimizing the expected utility in continuous time is intractable, the authors employ an approximation based on Monte Carlo Tree Search and TD learning.

Experiments are conducted on named entity recognition (NER), sentiment analysis, and face identification tasks. The proposed full model (LENSE) is compared against several baselines: majority vote from human judges (1, 3, and 5 votes), online learning with true labels, and a "threshold" baseline. The threshold baseline is a simplified version of the authors' model that removes the continuous-time aspect, instead querying the crowd until the model's uncertainty falls below a predefined threshold. The results demonstrate that the full model outperforms most baselines, with the exception of the 5-vote crowdsourcing method. However, the simpler threshold baseline achieves nearly identical performance, and in some cases, with lower latency.

### Strengths and Significance

This is a highly original and significant paper that addresses the important problem of integrating machine learning with crowdsourcing. While many heuristic approaches exist for this problem, this work is the first complete and principled proposal I have seen. The formulation of the problem as a stochastic game is sophisticated and well-motivated. The experimental results, which show strong performance against reasonable baselines, are impressive. The work is likely to generate substantial discussion and further research, and it has clear potential for practical adoption. For these reasons, I believe the paper is a strong candidate for inclusion in the conference.

### Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement

Despite its strengths, the paper suffers from significant clarity issues that could detract from its potential impact. The primary source of confusion is the introduction of continuous time in Section 3. While the motivation to operate within a time budget is sound, this feature adds substantial complexity to the model's formulation, the technical discussion, and the optimization procedure. Crucially, the experimental results suggest that this added complexity yields minimal performance benefits.

The "threshold" baseline, which is the authors' model without the continuous-time component, performs on par with the full LENSE model. For instance, in the NER task (Table 2), the threshold baseline achieves significantly lower latency with only a marginal decrease in performance. On the face identification task, it actually performs slightly better. The authors argue that the full model's benefit should be more pronounced on tasks with strong dependencies between labels (like NER), but the experimental benefit remains small. For many practical applications, the simpler and more transparent threshold model would be sufficient and is, in itself, a major contribution over standard active and online learning methods.

To improve the paper's impact and accessibility, I suggest the authors consider restructuring the presentation. The core method (i.e., the "threshold" model) could be fully developed first, as it is easier to understand and implement. The continuous-time formulation could then be introduced as an extension. In the discussion of the results, the authors should be more transparent about the strong performance of the threshold baseline, even on sequence-dependent tasks. While I believe the paper is acceptable in its current form, these changes would greatly enhance its clarity and increase the likelihood of the technique being widely understood and adopted.

### Minor Issues

There are several smaller issues concerning the reporting of results and the description of the method:

**Results Reporting:**
*   The introduction's claim that the model "beats human performance" is not supported by the results, as the 5-vote baseline outperforms it on the NER task.
*   The 5-vote baseline is strangely missing for the Face-ID and Sentiment tasks in Tables 2 and 3, and Figure 4. It is a crucial point of comparison and should be included.
*   In Table 2, the best-performing result in each column should be bolded. This includes the 5-vote results for performance metrics and the "Threshold" result for latency in the NER task.
*   The "Threshold" baseline is missing entirely from the sentiment analysis experiment (Table 3 and Figure 4). It should be included for a complete comparison.

**Method Description:**
*   While `t_deadline` is discussed (l.183-187), it is unclear what deadline was used in the experiments or what criteria determined when the model should report an answer. Was it a fixed deadline or an entropy threshold like the baseline?
*   In Equation (2), the distribution `p(y,r,t|x,q)` appears to be missing the conditioning variable `s` (the query time), i.e., it should likely be `p(y,r,t|x,q,s)`.
*   In Equation (5), the use of `q` in `F(q)` is confusing, as `q` is already used to denote a query. Please consider using a different letter for the nominal distribution function to avoid ambiguity.
*   In the description of the Threshold baseline (l.246-252), the stopping condition appears to be incorrect. To reduce uncertainty by sending `m` queries, the expression should likely be `(1 - p_theta(y_i|x)) * 0.3^m <= (1-0.88)`, not `>= 0.88`.
