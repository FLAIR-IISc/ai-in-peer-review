This paper proposes a novel hybrid approach that leverages both crowdsourced labels and an incrementally trained model to tackle prediction problems. The core concept revolves around heavily relying on the crowd during the system's initial stages, learning from the acquired labels, and gradually reducing the reliance on the crowd as the model gains confidence. This is achieved by formulating the problem as a stochastic game based on a Conditional Random Field (CRF) prediction model, where the system and the crowd are both players.

The system can issue one or more queries for tokens, eliciting responses that yield a utility for each outcome. The system aims to maximize the expected utility, taking into account the time at which queries are sent and responses are received, as well as the deadline by which an answer must be computed. This requires computing the expected utility using a simulation dynamics model, which is approximated using Monte Carlo Tree Search and TD learning.

Experiments were conducted on three tasks: named entity recognition (NER), sentiment recognition, and face identification. The results show that the proposed method outperforms all baselines except for the 5-vote crowdsourced version, while the "threshold" baseline performs nearly as well, albeit with lower latency for NER. The authors also demonstrate that the model requires fewer crowdsourced labels over time, given a sufficiently strong model to train.

This paper presents a highly original and significant contribution to the field, offering a principled approach to learning from the crowd while training a system. The results against reasonable baselines are impressive, and the work has the potential to generate significant discussion and further research. However, the paper's clarity is compromised by the introduction of continuous time, which adds complexity to the formulation and discussion without yielding substantial benefits.

The primary issue with clarity arises from the introduction of continuous time, which seems to have minimal impact on the results. The "threshold" baseline, which does not rely on continuous time, performs nearly as well as the proposed method, with lower latency for NER. The authors could improve the paper's clarity by developing the core method without continuous time and presenting the addition of continuous time as an extension.

Additionally, the authors could be more explicit in discussing the results, highlighting the strong performance of the "threshold" method, even in cases with sequence dependence between tasks. Minor issues with the results and reporting include the omission of the 5-vote case for Face-ID and Sentiment tasks, the lack of bolding for the best performance in Table 2, and the missing "Threshold" baseline for the sentiment experiment.

Minor comments on the method description include the unclear use of t_deadline in the experiments, the need to specify the distribution in equation (2), the confusing use of q in equation (5), and the incorrect expression in the description of the Threshold baseline.

Overall, this paper presents a novel and original approach to hybrid learning, with impressive results and significant potential for impact. While some issues with clarity and reporting need to be addressed, the paper's strengths make it a strong candidate for acceptance.
