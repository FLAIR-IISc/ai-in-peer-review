This paper introduces a compelling hybrid approach to prediction problems, intelligently combining crowdsourced labels with an incrementally trained model. The core idea—to heavily leverage the crowd initially, learn from the acquired labels, and gradually reduce reliance on crowdsourcing as model confidence grows—is elegantly framed as a stochastic game based on a CRF prediction model. The system and the crowd are modeled as players, with the system issuing queries *q* for tokens *x* (with true label *y*) to elicit responses *r*, aiming to maximize expected utility *U(q,r)*. Critically, queries are issued over time *s* with response times *t*, optimizing utility with respect to a deadline *t_deadline* that governs query rate and number.

Calculating this expected utility requires a simulation dynamics model *P(y,r,t|x,q,s)*, as described in equation (4). While optimal action selection is intractable due to the continuous time formulation, the authors employ an effective approximation based on Monte Carlo Tree Search and TD learning (Algorithm 1). The approach was evaluated on simplified Named Entity Recognition (NER), sentiment recognition, and face identification tasks, comparing it against majority voting by human judges (1, 3, and 5 votes), online learning (with true labels), a “threshold” baseline (the authors’ model without continuous time, querying until prediction uncertainty falls below a threshold), and the full LENSE model. 

Results (Table 2) demonstrate that the full model consistently outperforms all methods except the 5-vote crowdsourced approach. The “threshold” baseline performs nearly as well, and crucially, exhibits lower latency in the NER task. The authors further show that the model's reliance on crowdsourced labels diminishes over time with sufficient training data (Figure 3), and achieves high accuracy from the initial examples compared to online learning (Figure 4), effectively leveraging crowd expertise during periods of high model uncertainty.

This is a truly interesting and potentially impactful paper. It’s the first principled proposal I’ve encountered for learning from the crowd while simultaneously training a system, and the results against reasonable baselines are impressive. I believe the work is highly original, significant, and worthy of inclusion in the conference. However, its clarity could be significantly improved, potentially hindering its impact. Many issues are addressable, even for the camera-ready version, with focused effort and potentially a recalibration of the emphasis given to the continuous time extension.

The primary clarity issue arises from the introduction of continuous time in Section 3. While the motivation—accommodating both time and cost budgets—is reasonable, the experimental benefit appears limited, while substantially increasing the complexity of the formulation, discussion, and optimization process. Indeed, Table 2 suggests the “threshold” baseline achieves superior latency on NER with comparable performance, and even surpasses LENSE on face ID. The authors attribute the baseline’s success on face ID to the task's lack of label dependencies, but the NER task, rich with such dependencies, shows only marginal benefit from the continuous time formulation.

For many applications, the “threshold” baseline appears sufficient, representing a significant advancement as a principled hybrid learning system that surpasses active and online learning approaches. The paper would benefit from explicitly acknowledging this; the core method could be fully developed *without* continuous time, resulting in greater clarity and ease of implementation. Continuous time could then be presented as a natural extension. Similarly, the discussion of results should emphasize the strong performance of the “threshold” method, even in tasks with sequential label dependencies.

Several minor issues related to result presentation require attention. The introduction claims the model “beats human performance,” which is untrue in the NER task for the 5-vote case. Moreover, the 5-vote case is inexplicably omitted from multiple tables and figures (the right half of Tables 2, Table 3, and Figure 4) and should be included. The 5-vote results in Table 2 should be bolded to indicate best performance, as should the latency value for the Threshold baseline in NER and the LENSE value for Face ID. Critically, the “Threshold” baseline is missing from the sentiment experiment, and therefore absent in Table 3 and Figure 4; it should be incorporated.

Finally, minor methodological points: 
- The paper discusses *t_deadline* (l. 183-187) but doesn’t clarify its use in the experiments, or the criteria for determining response readiness (entropic threshold vs. *t_deadline*).
- Equation (2) requires *s* as an argument (i.e., *p(y,r,t|x,q,s)*).
- The use of *q* in equation (5) is confusing; a different variable name would improve clarity.
- In the Threshold baseline description (l. 246-252), the inequality should likely be (1 - *p<sub>θ</sub>(y<sub>i</sub>|x)*)*0.3<sup>m</sup> <= (1-0.88), not >= 0.88, as increasing *m* reduces uncertainty.

In summary, this paper presents a novel and interesting hybrid learning approach, effectively modeling the interplay between a system and a crowd. While the method is promising and the results compelling, improved clarity, particularly regarding the contribution of continuous time, would significantly enhance the paper’s impact and facilitate wider adoption.
