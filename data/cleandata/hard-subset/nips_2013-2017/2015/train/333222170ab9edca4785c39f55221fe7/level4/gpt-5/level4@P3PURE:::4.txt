This paper introduces a setting the authors call “on-the-job learning,” in which the system may query annotators between receiving an example and producing a prediction. The approach is relatively clear for structured prediction tasks, where the model can query elements within a sequence.

However, it is not at all clear how this framework was applied to the face recognition task. Was the queried unit simply the instance itself? In a non-structured setting, the model does not propagate information between adjacent positions (i.e., across examples), so it is unclear how the proposed utility function still makes sense in this case. More detail is needed to explain what is being queried and how information from queries influences subsequent predictions in the non-structured scenario.

The threshold baseline appears to rely on two seemingly arbitrary parameters. Please clarify how these values were chosen and provide an analysis of the sensitivity to these choices.

A further baseline using uncertainty sampling, akin to that employed in active learning, would also be a useful point of comparison.

Although the authors emphasize distinctions from active online learning, active learning could be adapted quite straightforwardly to this domain by treating the active set as a sequence: the model could update upon receiving responses and then produce a final prediction at the end. While this would not capture the temporal aspect of the task, there is substantial theory to leverage for choosing the query set.

Overall, the framework appears novel, but several details remain unclear, and the approach seems fairly restricted in applicability. Clarifying the non-structured setting, strengthening baselines (including parameter sensitivity and uncertainty sampling), and more carefully positioning the work relative to adapted active learning approaches would significantly improve the paper.
