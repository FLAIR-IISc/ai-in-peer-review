This paper addresses the challenging problem of building high-accuracy classifiers without relying on pre-existing labeled data, instead leveraging real-time crowdsourcing to resolve uncertainty as inputs arrive. The core idea—on-the-job learning—is framed as a stochastic game based on Bayesian decision theory, balancing latency, cost, and accuracy. The authors develop a Monte Carlo Tree Search approximation to navigate the intractable optimal policy space. They demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach, dubbed LENSE, on three tasks: named-entity recognition, sentiment classification, and image classification.

The work builds upon existing research in online learning [7], active learning [8, 9, 10], and crowdsourcing [3, 4, 25, 26]. However, it distinguishes itself by explicitly modeling the trade-offs between latency and cost during the prediction process, and by iteratively improving the model *during* test time, unlike static policy approaches in active classification [11]. The formulation as a stochastic game is a novel and interesting contribution.

A key strength of the paper lies in its empirical validation. The results in Table 2 are particularly compelling, showcasing that LENSE outperforms both fully supervised machine learning and majority-vote human labeling in terms of cost-accuracy trade-offs. The comparison to the 3-vote vs. 5-vote baselines is insightful, illuminating the balance between crowd-sourced consensus and the algorithmic de-noising performed by LENSE. The demonstration of cost reduction over time (Figure 4) further strengthens the practical relevance of the work. The open-source release of LENSE is also commendable.

However, the paper suffers from some clarity issues. The illustrative example starting on Line 154 is abstract and difficult to follow. Replacing it with a concrete scenario—detailing specific queries, responses, and associated times—would significantly enhance understanding. Additionally, inconsistencies in terminology (e.g., the “threshold baseline” being referred to as both “entropic” and “threshold” in Tables 2 and 3) create confusion. The placement of related work in Section 5 is unconventional; it would be more standard to integrate it more thoroughly throughout the introduction and methodology sections.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** The formulation of on-the-job learning as a stochastic game and the use of Monte Carlo Tree Search to address the optimization problem are novel contributions.
*   **Significance:** The problem addressed—building accurate classifiers with minimal initial investment—is practically important, particularly in dynamic environments where labeled data is scarce.
*   **Empirical Results:** The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of LENSE, consistently outperforming baselines in terms of cost and accuracy.
*   **Reproducibility:** The availability of the code and data promotes reproducibility and further research.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   **Clarity:** The explanations are occasionally opaque, hindering comprehension. The example on Line 154 and the inconsistent terminology need to be addressed.
*   **Related Work Placement:** The organization of the related work section is unconventional and could be improved through better integration with the main text.



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite the clarity issues, I believe this paper presents a valuable contribution to the field. The technical approach is sound, the empirical results are promising, and the problem addressed is both important and timely. I recommend acceptance, contingent upon the authors addressing the clarity concerns outlined above.
