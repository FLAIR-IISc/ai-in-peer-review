- Paper proposes on-the-job learning: a decision-theoretic framework that queries crowd workers in real time, modeling interaction as a stochastic game and trading off accuracy, latency, and cost.
- Optimization is intractable so authors approximate policies with Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) and progressive widening to handle continuous time.
- Instantiated with a CRF for structured prediction and evaluated on NER, sentiment, and face classification tasks.
- LENSE substantially reduces annotation cost while preserving or improving accuracy, sometimes outperforming single-vote crowd and online-learning baselines.
- Clear motivation: deploying accurate systems without upfront labeled data.
- Principled formulation using Bayesian decision theory and a stochastic-game model.
- Practical search strategy (MCTS + progressive widening) to manage continuous-time responses.
- Realistic experimental setup with a live crowd retainer and open-source implementation.
- Compelling results showing strong cost savings and competitive accuracy, especially on structured outputs like NER.
- Sometimes underperforms the 5-vote human baseline; the trade-off between consensus and querying needs deeper analysis.
- The illustrative example is abstract and hard to follow; a concrete end-to-end walkthrough is needed.
- Inconsistent naming of the “threshold” baseline (also called “entropic”)—terminology should be unified.
- Related work placement is confusing; a dedicated, clearly positioned Related Work section is recommended.
- Several unresolved figure/table cross-references (e.g., “Figure ??”) must be fixed.
- Response-model assumptions (fixed 70% worker accuracy, precollected five-response pools) may understate variability and limit external validity.
- Loss/utility choice (ExpAcc of MAP prediction) needs justification and comparison to alternative decision rules.
- Sensitivity to key hyperparameters (wM, wT, UCT constant, progressive-widening schedule) and to response-time model pT is not fully explored.
- Design constraint forbidding new queries while waiting may hurt performance; its impact or relaxed interleaving should be discussed.
- Formulation is sound and approximation reasonable, but formal guarantees are limited.
- Experimental results support claims (notably NER), but additional sensitivity analyses, ablations, and confidence intervals would strengthen the empirical case.
- Novelty: on-the-job querying before predicting framed as a stochastic game and using MCTS with progressive widening for real-time crowd decisions is distinctive.
- Significance: addresses practical need to bootstrap high-accuracy systems without labels and shows promising cost reductions.
- Suggested improvements: add a step-by-step decision episode, unify baseline names, fix references, report sensitivity analyses and CIs, compare to worker-reliability-aware methods, and include ablations on MCTS/progressive widening.
- Pros for acceptance: timely practical problem, principled formulation, clever approximation, strong cost savings, and open-source code.
- Cons for acceptance: clarity issues, limited sensitivity analysis, and occasional underperformance vs. 5-vote baseline.
- Overall: a compelling, original contribution with solid empirical results (especially on NER); leaning toward acceptance if clarity and analysis issues are addressed.
