- Proposes on-the-job learning that combines real-time crowdsourcing with incremental model training to deliver high accuracy from the start with zero initial training data.  
- Key idea: rely heavily on crowd labels early, learn from noisy responses, and reduce queries as model confidence grows.  
- Formalizes the problem as a stochastic game for structured prediction (CRF) using Bayesian decision theory to trade off accuracy, latency, and cost.  
- Issues token-level queries asynchronously, models response noise and delays, and decides whether to wait or return a prediction.  
- Exact optimization is intractable; policy approximated via Monte Carlo Tree Search with progressive widening for continuous time.  
- Evaluated on NER, sentiment, and face identification against human-majority (n∈{1,3,5}), online learning with oracle labels, a Threshold baseline, and full method (LENSE).  
- Results: LENSE generally matches or outperforms baselines at much lower cost and issues fewer queries as the model learns.  
- Originality: intersects online/active learning, crowdsourcing, and structured prediction; provides a principled, general formulation beyond prior domain-specific systems.  
- Significance: timely, practical framework for immediate deployment and tapering crowd use where data is scarce or urgent.  
- Quality: sound modeling of noisy responses/times, principled utility, and appropriate MCTS approximation; strong empirical performance versus reasonable baselines.  
- Clarity weakness: continuous-time formulation adds complexity mid-paper but shows limited empirical benefit over simpler discrete-time Threshold baseline.  
- Empirical caveat: Threshold baseline often performs nearly as well and sometimes better in latency (notably in NER) and slightly better in face-ID.  
- Results/reporting issues: claim of “beats human performance” is incorrect vs 5-vote for NER and should be qualified.  
- Baseline presentation problems: 5-vote and Threshold baselines are inconsistently included/highlighted (missing for some tasks/figures and mis-bolding in tables).  
- Methodological clarifications needed: unclear use of t_deadline in experiments and exact decision criterion for returning predictions.  
- Notation and exposition fixes: make conditioning variables explicit in p(y,r,t | ·), avoid naming collisions (q in F(q)), and clarify the Threshold inequality and inconsistent thresholds (0.98 vs 0.88).  
- Pros for acceptance: original, principled framework; solid multi-task empirical results; structured prediction benefits; open-source code and reproducibility.  
- Cons: poor clarity due to continuous-time emphasis, inconsistent baseline reporting, and several notation/presentation issues.  
- Recommendation: Accept with revisions to present discrete-time core clearly (continuous time as extension), include/highlight all baselines consistently, and fix notation, inequality, and decision-criterion clarity.
