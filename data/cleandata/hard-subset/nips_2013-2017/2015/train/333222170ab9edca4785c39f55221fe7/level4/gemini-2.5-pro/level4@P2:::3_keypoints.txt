- Introduces "on-the-job learning": a hybrid system that starts with no training data, uses real-time crowdsourcing, and learns from labels to reduce crowd reliance.
- Formalizes the problem as a stochastic game where the system decides when/what to query and when to return a prediction to maximize a utility balancing accuracy, cost, and latency.
- Models tasks with a Conditional Random Field (CRF) and uses Bayesian decision theory to guide the query policy.
- Because optimal policy computation is intractable (esp. with continuous response times), proposes an approximation using Monte Carlo Tree Search with progressive widening.
- Presents LENSE and evaluates on NER, sentiment classification, and image classification against baselines (multi-vote human, online oracle, Threshold baseline).
- Strength: Highly original and significant; first principled decision-theoretic approach for hybrid crowd-machine systems needing strong performance from first example.
- Strength: Technically sophisticated; experiments show LENSE can reduce cost over time while maintaining high accuracy against well-chosen baselines.
- Weakness: Paper clarity is lacking, particularly due to emphasis on a complex continuous-time model that complicates presentation.
- Weakness: Continuous-time formulation appears unjustified by experiments—Threshold baseline achieves nearly identical or better results in some tasks.
- Weakness: Reporting issues obscure comparisons—claim of "surpass his master" is misleading since 5-vote human has highest F1 on NER.
- Weakness: Missing crucial baseline results—5-vote human not reported for Sentiment and Faces; Threshold baseline not reported for Sentiment.
- Weakness: Presentation could use clearer table formatting (e.g., bold best-performing results for readability).
- Suggestion: Reframe narrative to foreground the simpler discrete-time (Threshold-like) model and present continuous-time as an advanced extension.
- Suggestion: Explicitly acknowledge and discuss the strong performance of the Threshold baseline as a practical alternative.
- Question: Experimental stopping criterion unclear—was a fixed t_deadline used or a model-confidence threshold similar to baselines?
- Question: Possible error in the Threshold baseline update rule—inequality direction and threshold interpretation appear incorrect.
- Conclusion: Paper is original, significant, and technically strong and should be accepted, but impact would improve by addressing clarity and more complete/result-transparent reporting.
