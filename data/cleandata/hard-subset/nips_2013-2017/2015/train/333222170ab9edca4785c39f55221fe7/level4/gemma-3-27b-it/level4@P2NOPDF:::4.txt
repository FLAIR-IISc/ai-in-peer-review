This paper introduces a framework for “on-the-job learning,” where a model can request additional information from an annotator between receiving an input and making a prediction. The core idea is sound and well-explained in the context of structured prediction, where queries can focus on specific elements within a sequence. However, the application to the face recognition task is less clear. It’s unclear *what* is being queried in this instance – is it simply the entire instance itself? Without the sequential information propagation present in structured prediction, the proposed utility function appears less meaningful, as there’s no inter-example relationship to leverage.

The presented threshold baseline relies on two parameters whose selection seems arbitrary. The paper would benefit from a discussion of how these values were determined, and an analysis of the method’s sensitivity to changes in these parameters. Furthermore, a comparison to uncertainty sampling – a common strategy in active learning – would strengthen the evaluation. Such a baseline would provide a valuable point of reference for assessing the benefits of the proposed approach.

The authors rightly distinguish their setting from traditional active online learning. However, it's worth noting that active learning techniques *could* be adapted to this sequential context. For instance, the model could be updated iteratively upon receiving annotations, and a final prediction generated after all responses are collected. While this adaptation wouldn’t fully capture the inherent temporal nature of the “on-the-job learning” problem, it would allow the authors to leverage the extensive theoretical foundation available in active learning for query selection.

Overall, the paper presents a potentially novel framework, though certain details regarding its implementation and broad applicability remain unclear. The restricted nature of the approach, particularly its dependence on the specific characteristics of “on-the-job” learning, limits its potential impact. Further clarification regarding the face recognition application, justification for baseline parameters, and a comparison to relevant active learning strategies would significantly improve the paper.
