

This paper introduces "on-the-job learning," a framework for building accurate AI systems from zero training examples by strategically using real-time crowdsourcing to resolve uncertainty during prediction. The authors model the process as a stochastic game based on Bayesian decision theory, balancing latency, cost, and accuracy through a Monte Carlo Tree Search approximation. The approach is evaluated on three tasks: named-entity recognition (NER), sentiment classification, and image classification, with significant cost reductions (over an order of magnitude for NER) and accuracy improvements (8% F1 over single-human labeling, 28% over online learning) compared to baselines.

The paper makes a valuable contribution by formalizing a setting where systems maintain high accuracy from deployment without requiring pre-labeled data. This differs from standard active learning, which focuses on building better models through selective labeling of training examples, and from pure crowdsourcing approaches that lack model improvement over time. The work builds on active classification [22-24] but extends it by enabling model improvement during test time rather than relying on a static policy learned from a pre-labeled dataset. The connection to stochastic games and Bayesian decision theory provides a principled foundation that distinguishes this work from prior approaches like Legion:AR [6], which operates in a similar setting but lacks the formal machine learning foundation.

The paper's strengths are significant. The formalization of on-the-job learning as a stochastic game is elegant and provides a principled framework for balancing multiple objectives (accuracy, latency, cost). The implementation of LENSE (Learning from Expensive Noisy Slow Experts) demonstrates practical utility, with compelling empirical results showing both cost reductions and accuracy improvements. The evaluation design is thoughtful, with careful attention to worker quality control through pre-sampling responses and delays. The open-sourcing of the implementation enhances reproducibility and utility for the community.

However, the paper has several important weaknesses that need addressing. The most significant concern is the unclear application of the method to the face recognition task, which is non-structured. The paper states that "the model wouldn't propagate information between adjacent positions (examples), so it seems like the utility function wouldn't make sense." The authors should clarify how the framework handles non-structured prediction tasks, whether the queries were made at the instance level (e.g., asking about the entire image) or if the framework was adapted for this task. Without this clarification, the applicability of the method to non-structured prediction remains questionable.

The threshold baseline lacks sufficient justification. The paper states the baseline uses "m queries such that (1−pθ(yi | x))×0.3m ≥ 0.98" but doesn't explain how these parameters were chosen or demonstrate sensitivity to them. The authors should provide more details on parameter selection and possibly conduct a sensitivity analysis to show these choices aren't arbitrary.

Additionally, the paper's distinction between on-the-job learning and active online learning requires stronger justification. The authors claim active learning can't maintain high accuracy from the beginning because "labels can only be observed after classification," but this overlooks the possibility of adapting active learning to this setting. A more nuanced comparison with uncertainty sampling (a standard active learning technique) would strengthen the paper's position. The authors should either demonstrate why uncertainty sampling wouldn't work in this setting or directly compare their method to it.

The paper also doesn't fully address how their framework handles cases where the model's predictions are highly uncertain about many positions simultaneously. The utility function assumes that queries can be made sequentially or in parallel, but the paper doesn't discuss how the system would handle situations where multiple tokens are highly uncertain and the model needs to decide which to query first.

Overall, this paper presents a novel and valuable framework for on-the-job learning with compelling empirical results. The main concerns center on the unclear application to non-structured prediction tasks, insufficient justification of the threshold baseline parameters, and an incomplete comparison with active learning approaches. With clarifications to these points, the paper would make a strong contribution to the field.

The authors should address these concerns in revision by:
1. Clarifying the application of the framework to the face recognition task
2. Providing justification for threshold baseline parameters or conducting sensitivity analysis
3. Including a comparison with uncertainty sampling as a baseline
4. Explaining how the system handles multiple uncertain positions

Given the novelty of the framework and the strong empirical results, I recommend acceptance with minor revision. The paper makes a significant contribution to the field by providing a principled approach to on-the-job learning that bridges the gap between online learning and crowdsourcing, with clear practical implications. The identified concerns are important but not fundamental to the paper's core contribution.
