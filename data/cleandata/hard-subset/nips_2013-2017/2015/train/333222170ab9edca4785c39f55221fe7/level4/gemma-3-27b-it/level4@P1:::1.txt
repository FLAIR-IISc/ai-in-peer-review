The main contribution of this paper is to provide a more theoretically sound formulation of an on-the-job learner initially proposed in [6]. The learner begins by querying the crowd to produce its output, but gradually transitions to making its own predictions and querying the crowd less frequently. The decision of whether to query the crowd for any given input is modeled as a decision problem solved via Monte Carlo Tree Search.

The presentation is generally good, although it lacks detail in certain areas. The empirical results, when compared to a threshold-based heuristic, are somewhat modest. However, it appears to be a reasonable approach and merits further investigation.

This paper presents a theoretical formulation of an "on-the-job learner" that begins as a pure crowdsourcing system and gradually transitions to a learned model that only queries the crowd on low-confidence inputs. The empirical results are somewhat mixed, but it would be valuable to encourage further research along these lines.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** The paper presents a principled Bayesian decision-theoretic framework for on-the-job learning, which is a relatively unexplored setting.
*   **Technical Soundness:** The use of Monte Carlo Tree Search to approximate the optimal policy is a reasonable approach given the intractability of finding the exact solution.
*   **Positive Empirical Results:** The results demonstrate that the proposed approach can achieve significant cost reductions compared to full human annotation while maintaining or improving accuracy.
*   **Clear Problem Formulation:** The problem of on-the-job learning is clearly defined and motivated.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Modest Empirical Gains:** The improvements over strong baselines (especially the online learning baseline) are not always substantial.
*   **Lack of Detail:** Some parts of the paper lack sufficient detail, making it difficult to fully understand and reproduce the results. (addressed in questions above)
*   **Limited Scope:** The experiments are limited to three datasets, which may not be representative of all possible on-the-job learning scenarios.
