- Summary: Presents a decision-theoretic on-the-job learning framework that starts with zero training data, uses crowdsourcing initially, transitions to a learned CRF model, and chooses queries via a stochastic-game formulation optimized approximately with MCTS + progressive widening; evaluated on NER, sentiment, and images with strong NER gains.
- Relation to prior work: Formalizes the on-the-job learner from prior informal work, distinct from online/active learning, and aligns with decision-theoretic control of human–machine systems.
- Strength: Principled decision-theoretic formulation that explicitly trades off accuracy, latency, and monetary cost.
- Strength: MCTS with progressive widening is a well-motivated approximation for the large continuous-state planning problem.
- Strength: Framework captures intuitive strategies (parallel vs sequential queries, redundancy for noise) and includes experiments across three tasks plus open-source code.
- Weakness: Key modeling and algorithmic details are unclear or missing, hindering technical assessment and reproducibility.
- Weakness: Empirical improvements over a simple threshold-based heuristic are modest in some settings and not fully diagnosed.
- Weakness: Unclear whether “online” baselines represent task-specific state-of-the-art, making it hard to gauge gains beyond human annotation.
- Quality: Overall approach and component choices are reasonable, but mathematical clarifications (e.g., Eq. (2), response marginalization, Algorithm 1 notation) and deeper experimental analysis are needed.
- Clarity: Generally well written but missing/ambiguous definitions for the utility expression, environment model, and threshold baseline parameters.
- Originality: Novel and useful formalization of on-the-job learning as a stochastic game with MCTS, synthesizing real-time crowdsourcing and structured prediction.
- Significance: Practically important ability to deploy high-accuracy systems without pre-collected data; strongest empirical support on NER, mixed on other tasks.
- Question 1: Clarify Eq. (2) conditioning on s and explain how pR(ri | y, qi) loses dependence on y (marginalization/approximation/assumption).
- Question 2: Define N(s) / N(σ) in Algorithm 1 and ensure consistent notation for visit counts.
- Question 3: Justify threshold baseline constants (0.3 and 0.88), their derivation, and sensitivity to these values.
- Question 4: Specify whether online baselines are best-in-class given the same features/supervision and provide error analysis showing which errors are corrected by crowd input versus model learning.
- Pros for acceptance: Principled formulation with practical approximation, demonstrated NER cost/accuracy gains, timely and relevant topic, and open-source release.
- Cons for acceptance: Ambiguous technical details, limited advantage over simple heuristics in places, and insufficient analysis of when/why the method outperforms human votes or online learning.
- Overall assessment: Thoughtful, theoretically grounded approach that merits further investigation but requires clearer technical details, stronger baseline comparisons, and deeper error analysis to fully validate claims.
