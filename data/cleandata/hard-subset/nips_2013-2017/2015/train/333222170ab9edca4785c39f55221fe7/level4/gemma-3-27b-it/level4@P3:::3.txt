This paper presents a hybrid approach for using both crowdsourced labels and an incrementally (online) trained model to address prediction problems; the core idea is to lean heavily on the crowd as the system is ramping up, learn from the labels thus acquired, and then use the crowd less and less often as the model becomes more confident. This is done via a sophisticated framing of the problem as a stochastic game based on a CRF prediction model in which the system and the crowd are both players. The system can issue one or more queries for tokens, attempting to maximize expected utility given responses and associated costs. The introduction of continuous time allows for modeling latency and time budgets, though its practical benefit is debated. 

The authors evaluate their approach, dubbed LENSE, on named entity recognition (NER), sentiment recognition, and face identification tasks, comparing it to several baselines: majority vote of human judges, online learning with true labels, and a “threshold” baseline that omits continuous time optimization. Results demonstrate that LENSE frequently outperforms online learning and the threshold baseline, though it is often surpassed by the 5-vote crowdsourced approach. The authors also show that LENSE requires fewer crowdsourced labels over time as the model improves, and achieves high accuracy from the beginning by leveraging crowd expertise.

This is a genuinely interesting and well-motivated paper, and I believe it has the potential to stimulate significant discussion and further research. The principled formulation of on-the-job learning as a stochastic game is a novel contribution. The results against reasonable baselines are promising, suggesting the work is both original and significant, and warrants inclusion in the conference. However, the clarity of the presentation could be improved, which may hinder its impact. 

The primary concern is the complexity introduced by continuous time. While the motivation for incorporating time is sound – reflecting real-world constraints – the experimental results do not overwhelmingly support its necessity. The “threshold” baseline performs comparably, and sometimes better, particularly regarding latency (as observed in the NER task). The authors acknowledge this, but a more direct comparison and discussion would strengthen the paper. It would be valuable to clearly articulate scenarios where the added complexity of continuous time optimization is truly essential. A clearer presentation might involve developing the core method *without* continuous time first, then presenting its extension as a separate contribution.

Several issues related to the experimental results and reporting also detract from clarity. The claim of “beating human performance” is inaccurate given the 5-vote crowdsourced result for NER. Crucially, the 5-vote case is missing for the Face-ID and Sentiment tasks in several tables and figures – its inclusion is essential for a fair comparison. Results for the 5-vote case should be bolded to highlight its superior performance. Similarly, latency values for the Threshold baseline (NER) and LENSE (Face ID) should be bolded. The “Threshold” baseline is also missing from the sentiment analysis experiments (Table 3 & Figure 4) and should be included.

Minor points regarding the method description include: the experiments do not clearly state the criteria or t_deadline used for determining prediction readiness; there appears to be a missing variable 's' in Equation (2); the variable 'q' used within F(q) in Algorithm 1 is confusing and should be replaced; and the expression in the Threshold baseline description (lines 246-252) appears to have an incorrect inequality.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** The framing of on-the-job learning as a stochastic game is a novel and principled approach.
*   **Significance:** The potential to reduce reliance on expensive and time-consuming data labeling is significant.
*   **Strong Baselines:** The authors compare against relevant and strong baselines, demonstrating the value of their approach.
*   **Positive Results:** LENSE consistently outperforms online learning and frequently outperforms the threshold baseline.
*   **Open Source:** The availability of an open-source implementation (LENSE) is commendable.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Clarity:** The introduction of continuous time adds significant complexity with limited demonstrated benefit.
*   **Incomplete Results:** Missing data and inconsistent formatting in the tables and figures hinder interpretation.
*   **Overstated Claims:** The claim of “beating human performance” is inaccurate and requires clarification.
*   **Minor Technical Issues:** Several minor technical issues in the method description and equations.

**Overall:**

Despite some clarity and presentation issues, I recommend acceptance. The paper presents a novel and significant contribution to the field. Addressing the points raised above, particularly clarifying the benefits of continuous time optimization and completing the experimental results, would substantially strengthen the paper and increase its impact.
