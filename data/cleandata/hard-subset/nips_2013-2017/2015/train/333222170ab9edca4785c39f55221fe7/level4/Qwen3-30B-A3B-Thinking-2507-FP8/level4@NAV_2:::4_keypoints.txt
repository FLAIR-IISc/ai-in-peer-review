- Proposes "on-the-job learning": maintain high accuracy from first prediction by querying crowd for uncertain input elements and updating the model online.
- Formalizes the problem as a stochastic game using Bayesian decision theory to trade off latency, cost, and accuracy.
- Implements an approximate solution with Monte Carlo Tree Search and progressive widening.
- Evaluates on NER, sentiment, and image classification tasks, showing strong empirical results.
- Demonstrates large cost reductions (over an order of magnitude for NER) and improved F1 over baselines.
- Distinguishes the approach from traditional active learning and static active classification, situating contributions relative to prior work.
- Strength: well-motivated problem with clear real-world applications (e.g., disaster response).
- Strength: elegant, principled formalization via stochastic games and Bayesian decision theory.
- Strength: compelling experimental results and open-sourced implementation for reproducibility.
- Weakness: application to image/face recognition is unclear because structured query mechanisms may not extend to unstructured images.
- Weakness: threshold baseline uses unexplained parameter choices (e.g., 0.3m and 0.98) with no sensitivity analysis.
- Weakness: insufficient comparison to standard active learning baselines (e.g., uncertainty sampling adapted to streaming).
- Weakness: claimed distinction from active online learning is not convincingly justified.
- Arguments for acceptance: novel formalization, strong NER results, practical relevance, and open-source code.
- Arguments against acceptance: limited applicability to non-structured tasks, unjustified baseline parameters, incomplete comparisons, and weak support for claimed novelty.
