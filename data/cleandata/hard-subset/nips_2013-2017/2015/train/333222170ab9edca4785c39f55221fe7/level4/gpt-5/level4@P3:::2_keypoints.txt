- Paper tackles deploying high-accuracy systems from cold start by combining real-time crowdsourcing with an on-the-job learning model using a decision-theoretic stochastic game.  
- Uses Bayesian decision theory to trade off accuracy, cost, and latency, approximating optimal policy via MCTS with progressive widening for continuous time.  
- Evaluated on NER, sentiment, and image classification with an open-source implementation (LENSE).  
- Positioned relative to online/active learning, active classification, Legion:AR, and POMDP-style crowd control; leverages established UCT/progressive widening MCTS literature.  

Strengths:
- Addresses a practical, important setting for rapid deployment with scarce labeled data.  
- Principled decision-theoretic objective that yields intuitive behaviors (redundancy, parallelization).  
- Technical novelty in modeling control as a stochastic game and applying MCTS + progressive widening for response-time dynamics.  
- Extends to structured prediction (CRF-based NER) where partial queries and label correlations matter.  
- Empirical results show cost reductions across tasks and claim improvements on NER; code released for reproducibility.  

Weaknesses / suggestions:
- Reported results seem inconsistent: LENSE beats 3-vote but not 5-vote baseline—clarify claims about outperforming human-only comparisons and present cost-quality tradeoffs (e.g., Pareto plots).  
- Utility/metric mismatch: optimizes expected MAP accuracy but reports F1 for NER; discuss relation or adapt utility and report aligned metrics.  
- Simplistic worker/error model (fixed pR=0.7 with uniform confusion) ignores worker/item heterogeneity; add per-worker models or sensitivity analyses.  
- Simulation uses pre-collected 5-response pools, which may understate real-time variability; include live-crowd or larger-pool experiments and confidence intervals.  
- Missing hyperparameter and ablation analyses (wM, wT, MCTS settings, progressive widening, parallel vs sequential querying).  

Clarity issues:
- Key walkthrough paragraph is hard to follow; include a concrete step-by-step example with timestamps and posterior updates.  
- Inconsistent baseline naming (“threshold” vs “entropic”) and broken figure/section references need fixing.  
- Minor copyediting errors and typos throughout the manuscript.  

Originality:
- Novel combination of on-the-job formulation, decision-theoretic control, and MCTS with progressive widening for real-time crowdsourcing; solid originality claim.  

Significance:
- Potentially high impact for deploying ML under tight data/time/cost constraints if results hold under realistic worker models and live settings; open-source release increases impact.  

Questions for authors:
- Sensitivity of results to wM and wT and guidance for setting them for target budgets?  
- Can the system estimate per-worker reliabilities online and does that help?  
- Does forbidding queries while waiting hurt performance compared to interleaving launches and waits?  
- For NER, is decision-making optimizing entity-level F1 or a token-level proxy?  

Pros (for acceptance):
- Principled, general framework addressing an important problem.  
- Novel integration of Bayesian decision theory with MCTS/progressive widening for crowd interaction.  
- Multi-task demonstration with released code and promising cost-effectiveness on NER.  

Cons (against acceptance):
- Inconsistencies between claims and tables; needs clearer cost–quality analysis versus multi-vote baselines and POMDP methods.  
- Simplistic noise model and limited simulation protocol may overstate performance; lacks sensitivity analyses and ablations.  
- Clarity problems (example, baseline names, references) reduce readability.  

Overall assessment:
- Compelling and timely contribution with promising results, would lean toward acceptance conditional on reconciling reported numbers, strengthening evaluation (sensitivity/ablations/live experiments), and improving clarity.
