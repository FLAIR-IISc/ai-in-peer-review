### **Summary**

This paper proposes a novel hybrid system that intelligently combines crowdsourced labels with an incrementally trained online model for prediction tasks. The central contribution is a sophisticated framing of this problem as a stochastic game, where the system and the crowd are players. The system learns to issue queries to maximize expected utility, factoring in response times and an overall deadline. This is modeled using a Conditional Random Field (CRF), and the intractable optimization is addressed with a combination of Monte Carlo Tree Search and TD learning. Experiments on NER, sentiment analysis, and face identification tasks demonstrate strong performance against several baselines, including majority voting, online learning with true labels, and a simplified version of the proposed model.

### **Strengths**

The paper's primary strengths lie in its originality and significance. While heuristic methods for combining human and machine intelligence exist, this work presents one of the first comprehensive and principled frameworks for this problem. The formulation as a stochastic game is elegant and powerful, providing a solid theoretical foundation for a practical challenge.

The experimental results are impressive. The proposed model, LENSE, is shown to outperform several strong baselines. It effectively bootstraps its performance by relying on the crowd early on and gradually reduces this dependence as the model gains confidence, achieving high accuracy from the very first example. This demonstrates a clear advantage over standard online learning approaches. The work is likely to inspire further research and could see practical adoption in real-world systems.

### **Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement**

Despite the paper's strengths, its potential impact is hampered by a significant clarity issue centered around the introduction of continuous time.

1.  **Clarity vs. Complexity:** While the motivation for modeling continuous time is sound—addressing cost and time budgets—its inclusion substantially complicates the model's formulation, the discussion, and the optimization procedure. This added complexity does not appear to be justified by the empirical results. As shown in Table 2, the simpler "threshold" baseline (which omits the continuous time component) achieves comparable, and in one case better, performance with significantly lower latency on the NER task. The authors' argument that the full model's advantage lies in tasks with label interactions (like NER) is only weakly supported by the marginal gains observed.

2.  **Paper Structure and Positioning:** The paper would be significantly clearer and more impactful if it were restructured. The authors could first fully develop the core hybrid model without the temporal dynamics (i.e., the "threshold" baseline), which is a significant contribution in itself. The continuous time model could then be presented as an extension. This would make the core ideas more accessible and easier for others to understand and implement.

3.  **Acknowledging the Baseline's Strength:** The authors should more explicitly acknowledge the strength of the "threshold" baseline in the results discussion. Given its relative simplicity and strong performance, it is a highly practical method that deserves more prominence. Framing it as a core contribution rather than just a baseline would strengthen the paper's overall message.

While I strongly recommend acceptance, addressing these points would substantially improve the paper and increase its long-term impact.

### **Minor Issues and Questions**

There are several smaller issues regarding the experimental reporting and methodological description that should be addressed:

1.  **Missing Results:** Several key results are missing from the tables and figures, which makes a full comparison difficult. Specifically:
    *   The 5-vote baseline is absent for the Face-ID and Sentiment tasks in Tables 2, 3, and Figure 4.
    *   The "Threshold" baseline is missing from the sentiment experiment (Table 3, Figure 4).
    These should be included for completeness.

2.  **Presentation of Results:**
    *   In Table 2, the best-performing result in each column should be bolded for clarity. This includes the 5-vote results for F1 and the "Threshold" result for latency in the NER task.
    *   The introduction's claim that the model "beats human performance" should be qualified, as it does not hold true against the 5-vote baseline in the NER task.

3.  **Experimental Details:** The role of `t_deadline` in the experiments is unclear. Was a specific deadline used, and if so, how was it determined? What was the stopping criterion for the LENSE model in practice? Was it a fixed deadline or an entropy threshold?

4.  **Equation Clarifications:**
    *   **Equation (2):** The distribution `P(y,r,t|x,q)` appears to be conditioned on the query time `s`. Should it be written as `P(y,r,t|x,q,s)`?
    *   **Equation (5):** The use of `q` in `F(q)` is confusing, as `q` is already used for queries. Please consider using a different symbol for the nominal distribution function to avoid ambiguity.
    *   **Threshold Baseline (lines 246-252):** There appears to be a typo in the inequality. To reduce uncertainty as `m` increases, the expression should likely be `(1 - p_theta(y_i|x)) * 0.3^m <= (1 - 0.88)`, not `>= 0.88`.

### **Overall Recommendation**

This paper presents an original, significant, and elegant solution to the important problem of integrating crowdsourcing with online model training. The results are strong, and the proposed framework is a substantial contribution. I recommend acceptance. However, the paper's clarity and potential for impact would be greatly enhanced if the authors restructured the presentation to separate the core contribution from the more complex continuous-time extension and addressed the minor issues noted above.
