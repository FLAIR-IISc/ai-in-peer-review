The main contribution of this paper is a more theoretically grounded formulation of an on-the-job learner, building upon the work proposed in [22]. This learner initially relies on crowdsourcing for its outputs, but progressively shifts towards making its own predictions and querying the crowd less frequently. The decision of whether to query the crowd for a given input is modeled as a decision problem solved via Monte Carlo Tree Search.

The presentation is generally good, though lacking in certain details. The empirical results, when compared to a threshold-based heuristic, are somewhat underwhelming. Nevertheless, the approach appears reasonable and warrants further investigation.

Please address/clarify the following points:

1. Equation (2) is unclear. The left-hand side should also be conditioned on *s*. Additionally, could you explain how the *y* in *p{R}(r<sub>i</sub> | y, q<sub>i</sub>)* disappears?

2. What is *N(s)* in Algorithm 1?

3. How were the values of 0.3 and 0.88 determined for the threshold baseline?

4. I am unfamiliar with the specific tasks used in this work. Are the "online" systems used for comparison the state-of-the-art machine learning systems for these tasks? If not, why not? If so, then any performance gain achieved on top of these systems is likely attributable solely to the human annotation process. Could you provide examples of such cases? Some error analysis would be helpful in understanding this dynamic. 

In summary, this paper presents a theoretical formulation of an “on-the-job learner” that begins as a purely crowdsourced system and gradually transitions to a learned model, querying the crowd only for inputs where it has low confidence. The empirical results are somewhat mixed, but this work has the potential to stimulate valuable discussion and further research in this area.
