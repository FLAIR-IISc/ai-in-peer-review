The authors present a novel framework for on-the-job learning, which leverages Bayesian decision theory to balance latency, cost, and accuracy objectives in a principled way. The paper is well-organized, and the authors provide a clear explanation of their approach, including the formulation of the problem, the model, and the algorithm.

The authors' use of a stochastic game to model on-the-job learning is a key contribution of the paper. This formulation allows them to capture the complex interactions between the system and the crowd, and to reason about the optimal policy for querying the crowd. The authors' approximation algorithm, which combines Monte Carlo tree search with progressive widening, is also a significant contribution.

The experiments demonstrate the effectiveness of the authors' approach on three different tasks: named-entity recognition, sentiment classification, and image classification. The results show that the authors' approach outperforms several baselines, including human-only and machine-only approaches, in terms of both quality and cost.

However, there are some areas where the paper could be improved. For example, the authors do not provide a clear explanation of how their approach was applied to the face recognition task, which is a non-structured prediction task. It is not clear how the utility function was adapted for this task, or how the model propagated information between adjacent positions.

Additionally, the threshold baseline used in the experiments has two parameters that seem arbitrary. The authors do not provide a clear explanation of how these parameters were chosen, or how sensitive the results are to these parameters. A further baseline of uncertainty sampling, akin to that used in active learning, would be interesting to include.

Finally, while the authors make a distinction between their setting and active online learning, it is not clear why their approach could not be adapted to work in the active online learning setting. For example, the model could be updated in the same fashion on receiving responses, and then output a prediction at the end. There is much theory to draw upon for choosing the query set in this setting.

Overall, the paper presents a novel and well-motivated approach to on-the-job learning. While there are some areas for improvement, the authors' contributions are significant, and their approach has the potential to be widely applicable.

Strengths:

* Novel formulation of on-the-job learning as a stochastic game
* Effective approximation algorithm that combines Monte Carlo tree search with progressive widening
* Strong experimental results that demonstrate the effectiveness of the authors' approach

Weaknesses:

* Lack of clarity on how the approach was applied to the face recognition task
* Arbitrary parameters in the threshold baseline
* Limited comparison to active online learning approaches

Arguments for acceptance:

* The paper presents a novel and well-motivated approach to on-the-job learning.
* The authors' contributions are significant, and their approach has the potential to be widely applicable.
* The experimental results are strong, and demonstrate the effectiveness of the authors' approach.

Arguments against acceptance:

* The paper could benefit from a clearer explanation of how the approach was applied to the face recognition task.
* The authors should provide a more detailed explanation of the threshold baseline, including how the parameters were chosen and how sensitive the results are to these parameters.
* The authors should consider including a further baseline of uncertainty sampling, akin to that used in active learning.
