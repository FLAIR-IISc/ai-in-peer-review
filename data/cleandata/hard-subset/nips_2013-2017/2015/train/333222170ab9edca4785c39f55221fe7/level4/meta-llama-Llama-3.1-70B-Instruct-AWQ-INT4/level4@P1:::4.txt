The authors describe a scenario called "on the job learning", where requests are made to annotators in between receiving an example and outputting a prediction. This setting is novel and has the potential to be useful in situations where there is a need to balance latency, cost, and accuracy.

The authors formalize this setting as a stochastic game based on Bayesian decision theory, which allows them to trade off latency, cost, and accuracy objectives in a principled way. They develop an approximation based on Monte Carlo Tree Search to compute the optimal policy, which is intractable to compute exactly.

The authors evaluate their approach on three datasets: named-entity recognition, sentiment classification, and image classification. They compare their approach to several baselines, including a human-only approach, an online learning approach, and a threshold-based approach. They find that their approach outperforms the baselines in terms of both quality and cost.

One of the strengths of this paper is its ability to balance latency, cost, and accuracy in a principled way. The use of Bayesian decision theory and Monte Carlo Tree Search allows the authors to develop an approach that can adapt to changing circumstances and make decisions in real-time.

However, there are some limitations to this approach. For example, the authors do not provide a clear explanation of how their approach was applied to the face recognition task. It is not clear whether the instance was simply queried, or whether some other method was used. Additionally, the threshold baseline has two parameters that are seemingly arbitrary, and the authors do not provide a clear explanation of where these values came from or how sensitive the results are to these parameters.

A further baseline of uncertainty sampling, akin to that used in active learning, would be interesting to see. This would allow the authors to compare their approach to a more established method for selecting queries.

Finally, the authors are keen to make the distinction between this setting and active online learning, but active learning could be adapted quite simply to work in this domain. For example, the model could be updated in the same fashion on receiving responses, and then output a prediction at the end. Whilst this clearly wouldn't capture the temporal nature of the task, there is much theory to draw upon for choosing the query set.

Overall, this is a well-written and well-organized paper that presents a novel approach to a challenging problem. While there are some limitations to the approach, the authors provide a clear explanation of their method and demonstrate its effectiveness on several datasets.
