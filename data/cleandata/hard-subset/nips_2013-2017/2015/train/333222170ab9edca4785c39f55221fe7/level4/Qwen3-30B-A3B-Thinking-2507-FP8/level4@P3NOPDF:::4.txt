

The paper introduces "on-the-job learning," a framework where annotators are queried during prediction, intervening between example receipt and output. While the structured prediction application (e.g., sequence labeling) is well-motivated, the extension to face recognition remains unclear. Specifically, in non-structured settings like face recognition, the model lacks propagation between adjacent examples, rendering the utility function—designed to leverage sequential context—meaningless. If the face recognition task simply queried the instance itself (e.g., "Is this face correctly labeled?"), the proposed mechanism would reduce to standard prediction with a single annotation, failing to capture the intended "on-the-job" dynamics. The authors should clarify how the framework operationalizes queries in non-structured tasks and justify the utility function’s applicability.

The threshold baseline introduces two parameters without justification. Their values appear arbitrary; the authors provide no rationale for their selection or analysis of sensitivity to these choices. For instance, are these thresholds derived from empirical tuning, theoretical bounds, or heuristic assumptions? A sensitivity analysis would strengthen the baseline’s credibility. Additionally, a baseline based on uncertainty sampling (a standard active learning strategy) would offer a more meaningful comparison. Uncertainty sampling, which selects examples with high prediction entropy, could be adapted to this sequential setting (e.g., querying the most uncertain instance in the current batch) and would better contextualize the proposed method’s advantages over established active learning principles.

The authors emphasize a distinction between on-the-job learning and active online learning. However, active learning frameworks could be straightforwardly adapted to this domain by treating the sequence as the active pool. For example, the model could update incrementally upon receiving annotations and output a final prediction, mirroring standard active learning. While this adaptation might not fully capture the temporal dynamics of the task (e.g., the need for real-time adaptation during a sequence), it leverages a robust theoretical foundation for query selection. The paper’s claim of novelty hinges on this distinction, yet the proposed framework’s core mechanics—querying during prediction—overlap significantly with active learning applied to sequential data. The authors should clarify why existing active learning theory does not adequately address their setting, beyond the temporal aspect.

The paper’s core contribution appears novel in its specific operationalization of mid-prediction queries, but key details remain ambiguous. The face recognition application is particularly underdeveloped: without demonstrating how queries interact with non-structured data (e.g., whether features are shared across examples or queried independently), the approach’s utility in such settings is questionable. Similarly, the lack of parameter justification for the threshold baseline weakens the empirical evaluation. Including uncertainty sampling as a baseline would also better isolate the proposed method’s unique value.

In summary, the work presents an interesting idea for sequential prediction with intermediate annotations, but its applicability is limited by unclear implementation in non-structured tasks and insufficient baseline comparisons. The distinction from active learning requires stronger justification, as the core mechanics align closely with adapted active learning paradigms. To strengthen the submission, the authors should: (1) clarify the face recognition implementation and utility function’s validity in non-structured settings; (2) justify the threshold baseline parameters and conduct sensitivity analysis; (3) include uncertainty sampling as a baseline; and (4) more rigorously differentiate the framework from established active learning approaches. Without these clarifications, the paper’s scope and novelty appear overstated, particularly given the restricted applicability to structured sequential tasks. The method holds potential but currently lacks sufficient detail to demonstrate its broader relevance.
