

The main contribution of this paper is to provide a theoretically sound formulation of an on-the-job learner proposed in [22]. The learner begins by queuing the crowd to produce outputs but gradually transitions to making autonomous predictions while querying the crowd infrequently. The decision to query the crowd for any given input is modeled as a sequential decision problem solved via Monte Carlo Tree Search.

The presentation is generally clear but omits important technical details. The empirical results compared to a threshold-based heuristic are somewhat underwhelming. Nevertheless, the approach is reasonable and merits further investigation.

Please address the following specific concerns:
1. Equation (2) is unclear: the left-hand side should be conditioned on the state \(s\), and it is unclear how the variable \(y\) vanishes in the term \(p^R(r_i \mid y, q_i)\).
2. The meaning of \(N(s)\) in Algorithm 1 is not defined.
3. The derivation of the threshold values 0.3 and 0.88 used in the baseline comparison requires clarification.
4. The tasks employed are not specified in detail. Are the "online" systems used as baselines the state-of-the-art machine learning systems for these tasks? If not, why are they chosen? If yes, any performance gains must stem solely from human annotation; please provide specific examples where this occurs and include error analysis to contextualize the results.

The paper presents a theoretically grounded formulation of an on-the-job learner that transitions from pure crowdsourcing to a learned model querying only on low-confidence inputs. While the empirical results are modest, the work stimulates valuable discussion on this class of adaptive learning systems.
