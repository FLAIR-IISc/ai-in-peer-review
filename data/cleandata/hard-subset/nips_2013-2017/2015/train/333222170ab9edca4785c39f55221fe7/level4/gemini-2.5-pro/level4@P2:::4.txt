This paper introduces "on-the-job learning," a framework for deploying a high-accuracy system that starts with zero training data. The core idea is that for each incoming example, the system can make real-time queries to a human crowd to resolve uncertainty before outputting a prediction. The model is updated online with the responses, allowing its reliance on crowd queries—and thus its cost and latency—to decrease over time while maintaining high accuracy. The authors formalize this setting as a stochastic game, using Bayesian decision theory to provide a principled trade-off between accuracy, latency, and cost. As computing the optimal policy is intractable, they propose an approximation based on Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) and progressive widening. This work relates to online learning and active learning but distinguishes itself by actively seeking information to improve a specific prediction *before* it is made, rather than solely for improving the model for future predictions.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Novel and Significant Problem Formulation:** The "on-the-job" setting addresses a critical and practical challenge: how to build and deploy an accurate system immediately, without a large, pre-existing labeled dataset. The goal of maintaining high performance from the very first example while becoming more efficient over time is highly relevant for many real-world applications (e.g., disaster response, real-time event monitoring). The formalization as a stochastic game is a principled and elegant way to model the complex trade-offs involved.

2.  **Strong Empirical Results:** The experiments, particularly on the named-entity recognition (NER) task, are compelling. The proposed system, LENSE, achieves a significant reduction in cost (over an order of magnitude) compared to a pure crowdsourcing approach while simultaneously improving F1 score over both a single human annotator and a standard online learning baseline. The learning curves in Figure 3 effectively demonstrate the system's ability to reduce its reliance on human supervision over time, especially when using richer features.

3.  **Reproducibility:** The authors have open-sourced their code, data, and experiments, which is a major strength that benefits the community and allows for verification and extension of their work.

**Weaknesses and Questions for the Authors:**

1.  **Clarity on Non-Structured Tasks:** While the application of the method to structured prediction tasks like NER is clear (querying labels for individual tokens), its application to the face classification task is not. Was the query simply for the label of the entire instance? If so, it is not obvious how the model propagates information or why the utility function, which seems to benefit from resolving uncertainty on *parts* of an input, would be effective. In the non-structured setting, a single query resolves all uncertainty for that instance, and there are no adjacent elements within the example to benefit from the new information. The paper would be significantly strengthened by a more detailed explanation of how the framework operates in this non-structured classification scenario.

2.  **Insufficient Justification for Baselines:** The "Threshold baseline" is presented as a key point of comparison, but its design seems ad-hoc. It contains two parameters (0.3 and 0.98) that appear arbitrary. The paper should provide a justification for these specific values, explain how they were selected, and ideally include a sensitivity analysis to demonstrate how performance varies with these choices. Without this information, it is difficult to assess the strength of this baseline.

3.  **Comparison to Active Learning:** The authors draw a sharp distinction between their setting and online active learning. However, this distinction could be made more concrete with a stronger experimental comparison. A baseline using a standard active learning heuristic, such as uncertainty sampling, could be adapted to this setting. For the NER task, such a baseline could identify the most uncertain tokens, query their labels from the crowd, update the model with the responses, and then make a final prediction. While this approach would not explicitly model the temporal dynamics and associated latency costs, its inclusion would help to better isolate and quantify the performance gains that are specifically attributable to the paper's more complex game-theoretic formulation.

**Summary of Arguments:**

*   **Arguments for Acceptance:** The paper addresses a novel and important problem with a principled theoretical framework. The proposed method is shown to be highly effective on a challenging NER task, demonstrating significant improvements in both accuracy and cost-efficiency. The work is well-supported by an open-source implementation, ensuring reproducibility.

*   **Arguments for Rejection:** The clarity of the method's application to standard classification tasks is poor, raising questions about its generality. Furthermore, the experimental evaluation could be strengthened by providing more details on the baselines and by including a more direct comparison to adapted active learning strategies, which would better situate the paper's contribution relative to existing work.

Overall, this is a well-motivated paper presenting a novel framework with strong results on a key task. While there are weaknesses related to clarity and the breadth of the experimental comparison, the core ideas are significant and thought-provoking. With revisions to address the points above, this could be a strong contribution.
