

This paper proposes a novel framework for "on-the-job learning" that begins with zero training examples and gradually reduces reliance on crowdsourcing as the model improves. The authors model the decision process as a stochastic game based on Bayesian decision theory, which allows balancing latency, cost, and accuracy objectives. They formulate the problem as a structured prediction task where the system can query the crowd for specific input components when uncertain, then integrate these responses into the prediction model. The optimal policy is intractable, so they develop a Monte Carlo Tree Search approximation with progressive widening to handle continuous time variables. The approach is evaluated on three tasks (named-entity recognition, sentiment classification, and image classification), showing significant cost reductions (over an order of magnitude for NER) compared to full human annotation while improving accuracy over baseline methods.

The work builds on previous NIPS contributions in active learning (e.g., [22, 23, 24]) and online learning [7], but makes a significant contribution by formulating the on-the-job learning setting as a stochastic game with Bayesian decision theory. This differs from prior active classification approaches that rely on static policies learned from fully labeled datasets, as the authors' system improves during test time by incorporating crowd responses. The paper also relates to recent NIPS work on crowdsourcing [25, 26] and structured prediction [27, 28], but provides a more principled theoretical foundation for the specific on-the-job learning setting.

Strengths:
Quality: The theoretical formulation is sound, and the authors provide a clear utility function that balances accuracy, cost, and latency. The Monte Carlo Tree Search approximation is well-motivated for an intractable problem. The implementation details are generally sufficient for reproducibility.

Clarity: The paper is well-organized and generally clear, with helpful examples illustrating the system's behavior. The figures effectively demonstrate the decision process. However, some technical details (particularly in the utility function and environment model) could be clarified.

Originality: The core contribution is the principled Bayesian decision-theoretic formulation of on-the-job learning, which extends prior work [6] by providing a formal foundation and theoretical analysis. This represents a meaningful advance over previous approaches that lacked such theoretical grounding.

Significance: The framework addresses a practical problem in deploying AI systems with scarce labeled data, with demonstrated cost reductions and accuracy improvements over baselines. The approach could be valuable for real-world applications like disaster response systems where labeled data is unavailable initially.

Weaknesses:
Quality: The empirical evaluation lacks sufficient error analysis to understand why the system performs well on NER but less so on other tasks. The threshold baseline's parameters (0.3 and 0.88) are not justified, and it's unclear why the threshold approach outperforms the 5-vote human baseline. The comparison to "online learning" is potentially misleading since it's the best possible offline system (which sees perfect information about all data seen so far) rather than a comparable online method.

Clarity: The paper contains some confusing technical details. Equation (2) should be conditioned on the system state s (not just the model), and the notation in the environment model (p_R(r | y_q)) needs clarification on how y_q is handled. The definition of N(s) in Algorithm 1 is missing, which is critical for understanding the implementation.

Originality: While the framework is novel, the paper could better distinguish itself from related work. The comparison to Legion:AR [6] is helpful, but the paper should more explicitly address how their Bayesian decision-theoretic foundation differs from other recent NIPS work on crowdsourcing and active learning.

Significance: The cost reductions are impressive for NER (over an order of magnitude), but the significance for other tasks is less clear. The paper would benefit from discussing specific real-world scenarios where these cost reductions would be critical, beyond the general statement about disaster response.

Specific concerns:
1. Equation (2) is ambiguous; the left-hand side should include conditioning on the system state s, and the notation should clarify how the true label y_q is handled in the response model.
2. The threshold baseline uses parameters 0.3 and 0.88 without justification. Please explain how these values were determined and why they are appropriate for the tasks.
3. The "online learning" baseline is not comparable to the on-the-job learning approach since it sees perfect information about all previous examples. It would be more appropriate to compare to a standard online learning algorithm that doesn't have access to the true labels during prediction.
4. The paper claims "8% F1 improvement over having a single human label the whole set" but doesn't clarify what "single human" means (e.g., one human labeling the entire dataset with a single vote per example).

Overall, this paper presents a theoretically sound and potentially valuable approach to on-the-job learning with clear practical benefits. The core contribution of formulating the problem as a Bayesian decision-theoretic stochastic game is significant, though the empirical evaluation could be strengthened with better justification of baselines and more detailed error analysis. The paper would be strengthened by addressing the specific concerns above, particularly regarding the threshold baseline parameters, the comparison to online learning, and the theoretical formulation details. As submitted, the paper makes a reasonable contribution that merits consideration for acceptance, but the authors should address these points in a revision.
