The authors describe a scenario called “on-the-job learning,” where requests are made to annotators in between receiving an example and outputting a prediction. While it is clear how the algorithm works for a structured prediction task, where elements of the sequence are queried, it’s not immediately obvious how this was applied to the face recognition task – was it simply the instance being queried? In the non-structured setting, the model wouldn't propagate information between adjacent positions (examples), so the utility function’s rationale is less clear.

The threshold baseline has two parameters that appear somewhat arbitrary. The paper would benefit from discussing where these values originated and analyzing the sensitivity of performance to changes in these parameters. Furthermore, a baseline employing uncertainty sampling, similar to that used in active learning, would be a valuable addition for comparison.

Finally, the authors emphasize the distinction between this setting and active online learning, but active learning could be readily adapted to operate in this domain, treating the query set as a sequence. For example, the model could be updated upon receiving responses and then output a prediction at the end. While this adaptation wouldn't fully capture the temporal aspects of the task, it could leverage the established theory for query set selection. 

Overall, this presents a seemingly novel framework, although some details require clarification, and the applicability appears somewhat restricted.

**Summary of Strengths and Weaknesses:**

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The on-the-job learning framework addresses a practical problem – deploying accurate systems with limited initial labeled data.
*   **Principled Approach:** The formulation as a stochastic game based on Bayesian decision theory offers a rigorous way to balance competing objectives (latency, cost, accuracy).
*   **Empirical Results:** The experiments demonstrate significant cost reductions and performance improvements compared to full annotation and online learning, particularly on the NER task.
*   **Implementation & Reproducibility:** The publicly available implementation (LENSE) encourages further research and allows for verification of the results.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Clarity of Application to Non-Structured Tasks:** The application of the framework to the face recognition task is not fully explained, and the benefits of the querying strategy in this context are unclear.
*   **Baseline Selection:** The threshold baseline relies on parameters with unclear justification, and a standard uncertainty sampling baseline is missing.
*   **Connection to Active Learning:** The potential for adapting active learning techniques to this setting is downplayed, and a comparison would strengthen the argument for the novelty of the approach.
*   **Limited Generalizability:** While the results on NER are strong, the performance on sentiment classification and face recognition is less compelling, suggesting potential limitations of the approach.


**Arguments For Acceptance:**

*   The paper proposes a novel and theoretically sound framework for on-the-job learning.
*   The empirical results demonstrate significant practical benefits in a challenging setting (NER).
*   The authors provide a publicly available implementation, promoting reproducibility and further research.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The application of the framework to non-structured prediction tasks lacks clarity and justification.
*   The baseline comparison is incomplete, lacking a standard uncertainty sampling baseline.
*   The paper does not fully address the relationship to existing active learning techniques.
