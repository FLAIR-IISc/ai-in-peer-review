

The paper introduces "on-the-job learning," a framework for deploying AI systems that begin with no training data and gradually reduce reliance on crowdsourcing as the model improves. The authors model the problem as a stochastic game based on Bayesian decision theory, where the system must balance accuracy, latency, and cost when deciding whether to query the crowd for additional information. They approximate the optimal policy using Monte Carlo Tree Search with progressive widening, and evaluate their approach on three tasks (NER, sentiment classification, and image classification), reporting significant cost reductions (over an order of magnitude on NER) while improving performance relative to both pure crowd-based and pure ML approaches.

This work builds on several previous directions. It extends the "on-the-job learning" concept from Lasecki et al. (2013) [6], which implemented the idea in a practical system, by providing a more theoretically grounded formulation. The authors also relate their work to active learning [8,9,10], active classification [22,23,24], and crowd-sourced workflows [25,26]. A key distinction from active learning is that on-the-job learning maintains high accuracy from the start (not asymptotically), and from active classification is that the system improves during test time rather than relying on a static policy.

Strengths:
1. Theoretical formulation: The authors provide a principled Bayesian decision-theoretic framework for balancing accuracy, latency, and cost, which is a significant contribution over the more ad-hoc approaches in prior work.
2. Empirical results: The results on NER are compelling, showing an order of magnitude cost reduction while improving F1 by 8% over single-human labeling and 28% over online learning. The steady reduction in queries over time (Figure 3) is particularly convincing.
3. Implementation: The open-source implementation (LENSE) and detailed experimental setup (including worker response times and quality control) enhance reproducibility.

Weaknesses:
1. Clarity issues: Several technical details are unclear. Equation (2) defines ExpAcc(p) but doesn't properly condition on the state (s), which is crucial for the formulation. The notation p_R(r | y_q) appears to have a variable (y) that's not properly defined in context. The authors should clarify how y is eliminated from this equation.
2. Algorithm details: Algorithm 1 refers to N(s) without defining it, which is problematic for reproducibility. The authors should explicitly define this notation.
3. Baseline justification: The threshold baseline uses parameters 0.3 and 0.88 without explanation. The authors should justify how these values were derived (e.g., through hyperparameter tuning or empirical analysis).
4. Comparison to state-of-the-art: The paper doesn't clearly establish whether the "online learning" baseline represents the best possible machine learning approach for these tasks. If the baseline is suboptimal, the gains over it may partly reflect the ML model's limitations rather than the on-the-job learning approach itself. The authors should provide more context about the state-of-the-art for these tasks.

Originality: The paper's main contribution is the theoretical framing of on-the-job learning as a stochastic game. While the practical implementation builds on [6], the theoretical foundation and Bayesian decision-theoretic approach are novel. The authors should more clearly articulate how this differs from previous work, especially [6] and [22], regarding the theoretical foundation.

Significance: The potential to reduce crowdsourcing costs by an order of magnitude while improving accuracy is significant for real-world applications where labeling is expensive. The steady reduction in queries over time (Figure 3) suggests the approach could be particularly valuable for long-running systems. However, the paper's significance would be strengthened by more thorough analysis of why the approach works better on NER than on other tasks.

Recommendation: I recommend acceptance with minor revisions. The paper presents a valuable framework with strong empirical results, but needs to clarify several technical details and better contextualize the results within the state-of-the-art.

Specific revisions needed:
1. Clarify the notation in Equation (2) and the definition of ExpAcc(p).
2. Define all symbols used in Algorithm 1, including N(s).
3. Justify the parameters (0.3 and 0.88) used in the threshold baseline.
4. Provide more context about the state-of-the-art for the tasks used, and clarify whether the online learning baseline represents the best ML approach for these tasks.
5. Elaborate on the differences between this work and [6] and [22], especially regarding the theoretical foundation.
6. Discuss why the approach works better on NER than on other tasks.

The paper makes a strong contribution to the field of interactive machine learning. With the suggested clarifications, it would be an excellent addition to NIPS.
