

The paper's comparison of classifier accuracy to human performance is a valuable contribution. Table 2 reveals that the LENSE system outperforms the 3-vote majority baseline but falls short of the 5-vote majority baseline, highlighting a meaningful trade-off between de-noising efficiency via consensus aggregation versus the LENSE approach. However, the example provided at Line 154 is overly abstract and difficult to follow; supplementing it with a concrete query example—showing actual crowd responses, timing data, and how LENSE processes them—would greatly improve clarity.  

In Tables 2 and 3, the inconsistent terminology ("entropic" versus "threshold") for the baseline creates confusion. The term "entropic" appears in Table 2 but "threshold" in Table 3, and the paper does not clarify whether these refer to the same method. This inconsistency undermines the reader's ability to interpret the results. Additionally, the placement of related work in Section 5 feels misplaced; this section should precede the methodology or problem formulation to contextualize the paper's novelty.  

The paper tackles the compelling problem of building high-accuracy classifiers from zero labeled examples, with a focus on optimizing timing and latency when handling noisy crowd responses. While the core problem is well-motivated, the explanations throughout remain opaque. For instance, the rationale for LENSE’s design choices and its relationship to existing de-noising techniques is not sufficiently developed. The claim that "timing and latency optimization factors are novel" (Line 86) lacks supporting context—how do these factors specifically differentiate the approach from prior work on crowd-sourced labeling?  

The paper’s strengths lie in its empirical comparison to human performance and the clear demonstration that LENSE improves upon simple consensus (3-vote) but not more intensive aggregation (5-vote). Yet, the lack of concrete examples, inconsistent terminology, and structural issues in the related work section detract from the overall impact. To strengthen the submission, the authors should: (1) replace the abstract example with a real-world case study; (2) standardize the baseline terminology across tables; (3) relocate related work to better frame the novelty; and (4) explicitly justify how timing/latency optimization advances beyond existing approaches. The problem is compelling, but the execution requires significant refinement to be convincing.
