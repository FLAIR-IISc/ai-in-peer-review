Summary
This paper formalizes an on-the-job learning system that initially relies on crowd annotations and, over time, transitions to automated predictions while querying the crowd only when necessary. The decision of when and what to query is cast as a sequential decision problem, approximated via Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS). Compared to prior work [22], the paper aims to provide a clearer and more principled formulation.

Strengths
- Clear motivation: balancing accuracy, latency, and monetary cost in settings where human input can be solicited at prediction time.
- Decision-theoretic framing is appropriate and, if done carefully, can subsume many ad hoc heuristics.
- MCTS is a reasonable approximate planner when exact dynamic programming is infeasible.
- The overall presentation is generally readable.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Several key technical details are missing or ambiguous, making it difficult to fully assess correctness and reproducibility.
- Empirical gains over a simple threshold-based heuristic are modest, and the baselines do not exhaust strong alternatives (e.g., one-step myopic value-of-information, well-calibrated cost-sensitive thresholding, or active-learning-style uncertainty sampling with budget constraints).
- It is unclear whether the “online systems” used are competitive state-of-the-art models for the tasks. If not, the practical relevance is diminished; if yes, then the improvements may be largely attributable to human input rather than the proposed planning method.
- Little analysis is provided on when the method helps or fails (e.g., under different noise/latency regimes, or for specific error modes).

Major questions for the authors
1) Equation (2) clarity:
- As written, the left-hand side appears to be missing conditioning on the state s. Please clarify the full conditional structure; the state should summarize all information (e.g., past queries, responses, and current time/budget).
- The term p_R(r_i | y, q_i) seems to drop the dependence on y in subsequent expressions. Is there an implicit marginalization over y with respect to the current belief p(y | s)? If so, please write this explicitly; otherwise, explain why y can be omitted.

2) Algorithm 1: What is N(s)?
- Please define N(s) precisely (e.g., visit count for node s in the search tree) and disambiguate it from any per-action counts N(s, a). If using UCT, indicate which counts appear in the exploration term and how they are initialized.

3) Threshold baseline parameters (0.3 and 0.88):
- How were these values chosen (e.g., tuned on a validation set for a fixed cost/latency constraint, derived from calibration, or set a priori)?
- Are they posterior probability thresholds or some other confidence measure?
- Please report sensitivity to these thresholds and ensure budget/latency parity with your method when comparing.

4) Competitiveness of the “online” systems:
- Are these the best-performing machine learning systems available for these tasks at the time of writing? If not, why were stronger baselines not used?
- If they are near state of the art, then any additional gain likely comes from human annotation. Please analyze representative cases where human queries improve outcomes (and where they do not), including errors due to worker noise or slow responses.

Additional comments and requests for clarification
- Formal model specification: Provide an explicit definition of the state, action, transition, and reward/cost functions. In particular, describe the worker noise model p_R(r | y, q), any priors over worker quality, and how latency is modeled (e.g., as a stochastic delay with a known distribution).
- Planning details: Specify MCTS parameters (number of simulations, depth/horizon, rollout policy, UCT constant), stopping criteria, and computational overhead. Report wall-clock overhead relative to latency savings to justify practicality.
- Budget and latency trade-offs: Present comprehensive cost–accuracy–latency curves and include area-under-curve or Pareto-front analyses. Indicate statistical significance (e.g., with confidence intervals over multiple runs/seeds).
- Baselines: In addition to a fixed threshold, consider:
  - A calibrated myopic value-of-information policy (one-step lookahead),
  - Uncertainty sampling with a fixed budget per instance,
  - A bandit-style approach that adapts query allocation across items/components.
  These would clarify how much MCTS contributes beyond simpler, principled heuristics.
- Calibration: Since thresholding relies on calibrated confidence scores, report calibration metrics (e.g., ECE) and whether temperature scaling or isotonic regression was applied.
- Ablations: Show sensitivity to worker accuracy priors, response latency distributions, and the number of allowable queries. Include an analysis of failures where planning hurts due to noisy or misleading responses.
- Reproducibility: Provide pseudocode with complete definitions, and, if possible, release code or detailed configurations to facilitate verification.

Overall assessment
This is a timely and sensible formulation of on-the-job learning as a sequential decision problem, with MCTS as a practical solver. The approach is appealing and, in principle, can unify several heuristic strategies. However, the current draft lacks critical technical details (notably around Equation (2) and Algorithm 1), and the empirical evidence is not yet compelling relative to stronger baselines. With clarifications, stronger comparisons, and a more thorough analysis of when and why the planner helps, the paper could make a valuable contribution. In its current form, it raises important questions and is promising, but it requires additional technical precision and experimental depth.
