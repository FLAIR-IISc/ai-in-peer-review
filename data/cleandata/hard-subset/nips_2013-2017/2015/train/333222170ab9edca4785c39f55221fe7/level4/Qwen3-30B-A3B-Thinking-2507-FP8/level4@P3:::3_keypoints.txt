- Introduces "on-the-job learning": combines crowd queries and incremental model updates to get high accuracy from first prediction without initial labeled data.  
- Models the problem as a stochastic game between system and crowd, using Bayesian decision theory to trade off accuracy, latency, and cost.  
- Approximates the optimal policy with Monte Carlo Tree Search and progressive widening.  
- Demonstrates method (LENSE) on NER, sentiment, and image classification with strong empirical gains.  
- Reports large practical benefits: >10x cost reduction on NER, +8% F1 over single-human, +28% F1 over online learning.  
- Originality: novel, principled framework distinct from supervised, online learning, and static active classification.  
- Significance: highly relevant for low-label scenarios (e.g., disaster response), with meaningful cost and accuracy improvements.  
- Quality: sound technical grounding, thorough experiments, and well-described implementation and response modeling.  
- Clarity: core idea well-explained, but continuous-time exposition (Section 3) is overly complex and could be deferred as an extension.  
- Major weakness: continuous-time formulation adds complexity without clear performance gains and is undermined by simpler baselines' comparable results.  
- Missing baselines: 5-vote crowdsourcing results omitted for Sentiment and Face ID—these should be included and highlighted across tasks.  
- Minor technical issues: Equation (2) should include time parameter s; sign error in Threshold baseline uncertainty formula; unclear claim of "beating human performance" given 5-vote baseline.  
- Pro for acceptance: highly original, significant, and well-executed with compelling results.  
- Con for acceptance: needs clearer presentation (simplify or reframe continuous-time part) and inclusion of all baseline results; these are fixable in revision.
