This paper presents a hybrid approach to prediction problems that combines crowdsourced labels with an incrementally (online) trained model. The core idea is to rely heavily on the crowd initially, learn from the acquired labels, and then gradually reduce dependence on the crowd as the model's confidence grows. The problem is elegantly framed as a stochastic game, based on a Conditional Random Field (CRF) model, where the system and the crowd are players. The system issues queries `q` for tokens `x` (with true label `y`), eliciting responses `r`. The system's goal is to choose actions that maximize its expected utility, `U(q,r)`.

Crucially, the model incorporates continuous time: queries are issued at specific times `s` and receive responses at times `t`. The system optimizes utility with respect to a deadline `t_deadline`, which determines the number and rate of queries. To compute this expected utility, the system uses a simulation dynamics model, P(y,r,t|x,q,s), as defined in Equation (4). While the optimal action could be chosen given these utility values, the introduction of continuous time makes direct optimization intractable. The authors therefore propose an approximation based on Monte Carlo Tree Search and TD-learning (Algorithm 1).

The authors evaluate their approach on named entity recognition (NER), sentiment analysis, and face identification tasks. They compare their full model (LENSE) against several baselines: majority vote from `n` human judges (`n`=1,3,5), standard online learning with access to true labels, and a "threshold" baseline. This threshold baseline is a simplified version of their model that omits the continuous-time component, instead issuing `m` queries at each step until model uncertainty falls below a certain threshold. In terms of precision, recall, and F1 score, the full LENSE model outperforms all baselines except for the 5-judge majority vote. However, the "threshold" baseline achieves nearly identical performance, and even has lower latency on the NER task (Table 2). The authors also demonstrate that their model's reliance on crowdsourced labels decreases over time as the underlying model improves (Figure 3). Furthermore, unlike standard online learning, the system achieves high accuracy from the very first example by leveraging the crowd when model uncertainty is high (Figure 4).

This is a very interesting paper that I expect will generate significant discussion, inspire further research, and see practical adoption. While many heuristic approaches exist for combining model training with learning from the crowd, this work presents the first complete and principled framework I have encountered. The results, demonstrated against strong and sensible baselines, are impressive. Therefore, I view this work as highly original and significant, and I recommend its acceptance to the conference.

However, the paper's clarity is a significant weakness that could detract from its potential impact. Fortunately, many of these issues are correctable, provided the authors are willing to revise the text and perhaps reconsider the framing of the "threshold" baseline versus the full continuous-time model.

The primary clarity issue stems from the introduction of continuous time in Section 3. The motivation—to operate within a time and cost budget for each prediction—is sound. However, this addition provides little apparent experimental benefit while substantially increasing the complexity of the formulation, discussion, and optimization procedure. According to Table 2, the simpler "threshold" baseline achieves significantly lower latency on the NER task with only a marginal drop in performance. On the face identification task, it actually performs slightly better than the full model. The authors argue (line 314) that the full model's advantage is most pronounced on tasks with significant label interactions, such as NER. However, the experimental results show only a marginal benefit on NER, which does not seem to justify the added complexity.

For many practical applications, the "threshold" baseline is likely sufficient. It represents a significant contribution on its own: a principled hybrid system that outperforms standard active and online learning. The paper's impact could be much greater if it were restructured. The authors could first fully develop the core method (the "threshold" model) without the temporal component, which would be far easier for readers to understand and implement. The continuous-time model could then be introduced as an extension. Similarly, the discussion of the results should more clearly acknowledge the strength of the "threshold" baseline, even on sequence-labeling tasks. While I believe the paper is strong enough for acceptance as is, this change would improve its readability and increase the likelihood of the technique being widely understood and adopted.

There are also a number of smaller issues with the results and their presentation:
*   The introduction's claim that the model "beats human performance" is not supported by the results, as it underperforms the 5-judge majority vote on the NER task.
*   The 5-judge majority vote baseline is conspicuously missing for the Face-ID and Sentiment tasks in several places (the right side of Table 2, Table 3, and Figure 4). This baseline should be included for a complete comparison.
*   In Table 2, the best-performing result in each column should be bolded for clarity.
*   The "Threshold" baseline is missing entirely from the sentiment analysis experiments (Table 3 and Figure 4). This is a key baseline and should be included.

A few minor comments on the method description:
*   The paper discusses `t_deadline` (lines 183-187), but it is unclear what values were used in the experiments. What was the stopping criterion for the full model? Was it a fixed deadline, or an uncertainty threshold similar to the baseline?
*   Equation (2), p(y,r,t|x,q), appears to be missing the conditioning variable `s` (i.e., it should be p(y,r,t|x,q,s)).
*   The use of `q` in `F(q)` in Equation (5) is confusing, as `q` is already used to denote a query. Please use a different variable for the distribution function to avoid ambiguity.
*   In the description of the Threshold baseline (lines 246-252), the inequality appears to be incorrect. To reduce uncertainty by increasing `m`, the expression should likely be `(1 - p_theta(y_i|x)) * 0.3^m <= (1 - 0.88)`, not `>= 0.88`.

This paper presents a hybrid approach for combining crowdsourced labels and online model training, elegantly framed as a stochastic game that models data, labels, crowd responses, and even the timing of queries and answers. The model demonstrates impressive results against several baselines, including multiple human labelers and online learning with an oracle. Notably, a simplified "threshold" version of the model, which omits the continuous-time component, performs nearly as well. The method is original, novel, and significant, and the paper should be accepted. However, its clarity needs improvement. The introduction of continuous time, in particular, adds substantial complexity to the model and exposition for what appears to be minimal performance gain.
