The authors describe a scenario termed “on-the-job learning,” where annotators are queried for information between receiving an example and outputting a prediction. While the algorithm’s operation is clear in the context of structured prediction tasks – where individual elements of a sequence are queried – its application to face recognition is less obvious. Specifically, it is unclear whether the entire instance was simply the subject of the query. In a non-structured setting, the model would not propagate information between adjacent examples, rendering the utility function questionable.

The threshold baseline employs two parameters that appear arbitrary. The origin of these values is not explained, and an analysis of their sensitivity would be beneficial. A further baseline comparing against uncertainty sampling, similar to methods used in active learning, would also be informative. 

Finally, the authors emphasize the distinction between this setting and active online learning. However, active learning could be adapted to this domain relatively easily by treating the sequence as the active set under consideration. For instance, the model could be updated incrementally upon receiving responses and then provide a final prediction. Although this approach might not fully capture the temporal nature of the task, a substantial body of theory exists to guide the selection of the query set. 

Overall, the framework appears novel, although several details remain unclear, and its applicability seems somewhat restricted.
