

The authors introduce a scenario termed "on the job learning," wherein annotator requests occur between receiving an example and generating a prediction. While the algorithm's operation for structured prediction tasks—where sequence elements are queried—is well-defined, its application to face recognition remains ambiguous: was the entire instance queried, or was there a different mechanism? In the non-structured setting, the model lacks propagation of information between adjacent examples, rendering the utility function inapplicable. The threshold baseline employs two parameters that appear arbitrary; their derivation and sensitivity analysis require justification. A baseline incorporating uncertainty sampling, as commonly used in active learning, would provide a more meaningful comparison. Furthermore, the authors emphasize a distinction between this framework and active online learning, yet standard active learning could readily adapt to this domain by treating the active set as a sequence (e.g., updating the model upon each response and predicting sequentially). Although this adaptation would not fully capture the temporal dynamics of the task, it leverages established theoretical foundations for query selection. The proposed framework presents a seemingly novel perspective, but key details remain unclear, and its applicability appears constrained.
