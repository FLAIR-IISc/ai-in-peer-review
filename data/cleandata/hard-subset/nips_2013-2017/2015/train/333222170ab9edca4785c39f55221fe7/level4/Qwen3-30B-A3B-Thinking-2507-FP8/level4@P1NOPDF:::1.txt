

The paper's main contribution lies in providing a theoretically rigorous formulation for an "on-the-job learner" introduced in [22]. This learner begins by relying entirely on crowd-sourced responses for all inputs but progressively shifts toward self-prediction, querying the crowd only for low-confidence instances. The decision to query the crowd for any given input is framed as a sequential decision problem addressed via Monte Carlo Tree Search.  

The presentation is generally clear, though it omits several critical implementation details. Empirically, the results compared to a threshold-based heuristic are modest at best, offering limited evidence of superiority. While the approach is logically sound and merits further exploration, the current evaluation does not strongly establish its practical advantages.  

Please address the following points:  
1. **Equation (2)**: The left-hand side appears to lack conditioning on state \(s\), which is essential for consistency. Additionally, the dependence on \(y\) in \(p^{(R)}(r_i \mid y, q_i)\) seems unexplained—how does \(y\) disappear from the conditional distribution?  
2. **Algorithm 1**: The notation \(N(s)\) is undefined. Please clarify its meaning (e.g., visit count, state frequency).  
3. **Threshold baseline**: The values 0.3 and 0.88 used in the threshold heuristic lack justification. How were these parameters selected?  
4. **Baseline comparison**: The paper compares against "online" systems, but it is unclear whether these represent state-of-the-art machine learning models for the tasks. If not, why were non-competitive baselines chosen? If they are SOTA, any observed gains must stem solely from human annotation—please clarify this and provide specific examples where human input demonstrably improves performance over the best learned model. A brief error analysis would strengthen this point.  

The theoretical framework is promising, but the empirical validation requires significant strengthening. The current results do not convincingly demonstrate that the proposed method outperforms simpler alternatives, particularly given the limited gains over the threshold baseline. To advance this line of work, the authors should rigorously establish that their approach offers tangible benefits over existing methods, especially when deployed with high-quality learned models. The discussion should explicitly address whether the observed improvements are attributable to the learner's adaptation mechanism or merely to the human-in-the-loop setup.
