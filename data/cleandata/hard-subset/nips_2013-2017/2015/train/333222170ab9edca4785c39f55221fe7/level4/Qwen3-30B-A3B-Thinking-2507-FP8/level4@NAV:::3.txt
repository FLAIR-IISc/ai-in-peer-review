

This paper introduces a hybrid framework leveraging crowdsourced labels and incrementally trained models for prediction tasks, where the system initially relies heavily on crowd input during early model training phases, gradually reducing crowd dependence as model confidence increases. The core strategy frames the problem as a stochastic game between the system and crowd, using a CRF-based prediction model. The system issues queries $q$ for tokens $x$ (with true label $y$), receiving responses $r$ with utility $U(q,r)$, and selects actions to maximize expected utility subject to a $t_{\text{deadline}}$ constraint governing response timing. Computing the expected utility requires the simulation dynamics model $P(y,r,t|x,q,s)$ (Equation 4), but continuous time renders exact optimization intractable, necessitating a Monte Carlo Tree Search and TD learning approximation (Algorithm 1). Experiments on NER, sentiment recognition, and face identification tasks compare four methods: majority vote (1, 3, 5 judges), online learning with true labels, a "threshold" baseline (model without continuous time, sending $m$ queries per step until uncertainty drops below a threshold), and the authors' full model (LENSE). LENSE achieves near-best precision/recall/F1, trailing only the 5-vote crowdsourcing approach, while the threshold baseline matches its performance with lower NER latency (Table 2). The model progressively reduces crowd dependency as the model strengthens (Figure 3) and attains high accuracy from the first example (Figure 4), leveraging crowd expertise during high uncertainty.  

This work presents a highly original, principled solution to hybrid learning, significantly advancing beyond heuristic prior approaches. The results against strong baselines are compelling, and the method’s potential for practical adoption and further research is substantial. However, the paper’s clarity is compromised by the unnecessary introduction of continuous time. While theoretically motivated (Section 3), the continuous-time extension substantially complicates the formulation and optimization without clear empirical gains: the threshold baseline achieves equivalent or better performance (e.g., superior latency in NER, better results in face ID) while being simpler to implement (Table 2). The authors should clarify that the core method (without continuous time) is already a robust, principled hybrid system, with continuous time as an optional extension. Emphasizing the threshold baseline’s strong performance—especially given sequence dependencies in NER—would enhance the paper’s impact.  

Additional issues require correction:  
1. The claim that the model "beats human performance" is inaccurate (5-vote NER does not surpass LENSE).  
2. The 5-vote results are omitted for Face ID and Sentiment in Tables 2, 3 and Figure 4; these must be included.  
3. 5-vote and threshold latency values (NER) should be bolded in Table 2.  
4. The threshold baseline is missing from sentiment experiments (Table 3, Figure 4).  
5. Clarify the $t_{\text{deadline}}$ usage in experiments (e.g., entropic threshold vs. fixed deadline; l. 183–187).  
6. Equation (2) requires $s$ as an argument: $P(y,r,t|x,q,s)$.  
7. Equation (5) uses confusing notation $F(q)$; replace $q$ with another symbol.  
8. Threshold baseline calculation (l. 246–252) should use $(1 - p_\theta(y_i|x)) \cdot 0.3^m \leq (1 - 0.88)$ (not $\geq 0.88$).  

The method is original, impactful, and well-supported, but clarity improvements are essential for broader adoption. The paper should be accepted with revisions addressing these points.
