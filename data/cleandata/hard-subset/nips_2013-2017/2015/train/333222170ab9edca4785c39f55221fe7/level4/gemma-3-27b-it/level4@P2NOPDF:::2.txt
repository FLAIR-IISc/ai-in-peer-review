This paper presents an interesting approach to building high-accuracy classifiers from scratch, starting with no labeled data. The core challenge of handling noisy crowd-sourced labels is well-motivated, and the focus on timing and latency optimization represents a novel and valuable contribution within this area. Comparing classifier performance to human-level accuracy is a strong point, and the results in Table 2 are particularly insightful. The observation that LENSE outperforms a 3-vote baseline but underperforms a 5-vote baseline highlights a crucial trade-off between the efficiency of leveraging additional consensus and the benefits of the LENSE system’s denoising approach.

However, several aspects of the presentation could be improved. The example provided in the paragraph beginning at Line 154 is currently quite abstract and difficult to grasp. Concrete illustration with a specific query, accompanied by realistic crowd response data and associated timings, would significantly enhance readability and understanding. 

Furthermore, the terminology used regarding the baseline comparison is inconsistent. In Tables 2 and 3, the baseline is alternately referred to as “entropic” and “threshold,” which creates unnecessary confusion. Maintaining consistent terminology throughout would streamline the reader’s comprehension.

I also question the placement of the related work section (Section 5). Traditionally, related work is presented earlier in a paper to provide context and establish the paper's contribution relative to existing research. Placing it later disrupts the flow and makes it harder to assess the novelty of the work upfront.

Overall, the paper addresses a compelling problem, and the core idea is promising. However, the explanations currently lack clarity and could benefit from more concrete examples and a more standard paper structure. Addressing these points would substantially strengthen the presentation and impact of this work.
