- Proposes "on-the-job learning": deploy high-accuracy system from zero labeled data by querying a real-time crowd for uncertain instances and updating online.

- Frames the problem as a stochastic game using Bayesian decision theory to trade off accuracy, cost, and latency.

- Uses MCTS with progressive widening as an approximate solution since optimal policy is intractable.

- Implements the approach in LENSE and evaluates on NER, sentiment, and image classification tasks.

- Demonstrates significant cost reductions versus human-only labeling and accuracy improvements over standard online learning.

- Notable result: LENSE outperforms a 3-vote human baseline but underperforms a 5-vote baseline on NER, highlighting trade-offs between intelligent querying and redundant labeling.

- Addresses a practical deployment problem lacking large labeled datasets, making the on-the-job setting a valuable formalization.

- Technical approach (stochastic game + Bayesian decision theory) is sound and well-motivated.

- Experimental design and baselines (human n-vote and machine-only online learning) are appropriate and comparisons are strong.

- Novelty lies in dynamically optimizing accuracy, cost, and latency at test time and applying MCTS to this decision space.

- Explanations and some sections are opaque and would benefit from clearer presentation.

- Suggestion: add a concrete, step-by-step walkthrough example (e.g., the NER "Soup on George str." case) with timings and crowd responses.

- Suggestion: fix inconsistent terminology (e.g., "entropic" vs. "threshold") to avoid confusion.

- Suggestion: move Related Work earlier (after Introduction) for better context.

- Suggestion: proofread manuscript to correct minor errors and missing figure references.

- Overall assessment: novel, significant, technically sound, and experimentally strong, but clarity and organization need improvement.

- Acceptance arguments: addresses important problem, principled Bayesian formulation, strong results, insightful comparison to human consensus, and reproducible code/data.

- Rejection arguments: paper clarity is a major issue and unconventional structure harms readability.
