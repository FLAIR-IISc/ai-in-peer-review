- Proposes an on-the-job learning framework combining real-time crowdsourcing with a model that improves over time, framed as a stochastic game with a Bayesian utility trading off accuracy, latency, and cost.  
- Uses a CRF for structured prediction, allowing token-level asynchronous noisy queries and decisions to query, wait, or return a prediction.  
- Exact optimization is intractable; policy is approximated with Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) plus progressive widening to handle continuous time.  
- Evaluated on NER, sentiment, and face classification against human n-vote baselines, an online-learning oracle, and a non-temporal “threshold” variant (LENSE).  
- Results show substantial cost savings vs full human annotation, strong initial accuracy via crowdsourcing, and decreasing reliance on the crowd as the model learns.  
- Unlike prior online/active learning, the system can acquire information before committing and learns at test time without observing gold labels.  
- Formulation quality: sound decision-theoretic setup and reasonable MCTS approximation, but continuous-time benefits are not clearly established empirically.  
- Clarity: paper is readable until continuous-time is introduced, after which notation and exposition become dense and inconsistent.  
- Originality: high — novel casting of on-the-job learning as a stochastic game solved with MCTS/progressive widening.  
- Significance: high — practical, general approach with open-source code and datasets for reproducibility.  
- Strength: principled, end-to-end cost-aware human–machine prediction framework.  
- Strength: utility yields intuitive querying strategies (parallel vs sequential, redundancy for noisy workers).  
- Strength: empirical evidence of reduced cost, immediate high accuracy, and declining crowd use over time.  
- Weakness: continuous-time formulation often yields limited empirical gains and can increase latency compared to the simpler threshold baseline.  
- Suggestion: present the non-temporal threshold variant as the core, easy-to-implement method and treat continuous time as an extension with guidance on when it helps.  
- Reporting gaps: 5-vote human baseline missing for sentiment and face tasks in some tables/figures; include consistently.  
- Reporting gaps: claims of “beats human performance” should be qualified (e.g., 5-vote remains best for NER); highlight best numbers appropriately.  
- Exposition/notation issue: unclear stopping criterion—specify whether prediction was returned at a fixed deadline, threshold, or via the policy’s return action, and state chosen thresholds/weights.  
- Exposition/notation issue: ensure model equations include dependency on s where appropriate; disambiguate symbols (e.g., q in F(q)) and correct the inequality/sign in the threshold baseline description.  
- Pros for acceptance: novel, principled, practical approach with demonstrated improvements and open-source release.  
- Cons against acceptance: clarity and presentation problems after continuous-time, reporting inconsistencies, and underspecified notation/stopping-criterion details.  
- Recommendation: Accept, contingent on fixing clarity/notation, delineating non-temporal vs continuous-time methods, and resolving reporting baseline inconsistencies.
