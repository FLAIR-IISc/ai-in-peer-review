

This paper proposes a hybrid framework that integrates crowdsourced labels with incrementally trained models to solve prediction tasks. The core strategy leverages the crowd heavily during early system stages, gradually reducing reliance as model confidence grows. This is formalized as a stochastic game where the system and crowd act as players: the system issues queries *q* for tokens *x* (with true label *y*), receiving responses *r* with utility *U(q,r)*. The system optimizes expected utility under a deadline *t_deadline*, dynamically determining query frequency and volume. Computing expected utility requires the dynamics model *P(y,r,t|x,q,s)* (Equation 4), but continuous time renders exact optimization intractable. The authors approximate this via Monte Carlo Tree Search and TD learning (Algorithm 1).  

Experiments evaluate the method (LENSE) against four baselines—majority vote (1, 3, 5 judges), online learning (oracle labels), and a "threshold" baseline (LENSE without continuous time, sending *m* queries until uncertainty drops below a threshold)—on NER, sentiment, and face identification tasks. LENSE achieves near-peak precision/recall/F1, outperforming all baselines except 5-vote crowdsourcing. Crucially, the threshold baseline matches LENSE’s performance with significantly lower NER latency (Table 2), and even surpasses it on face ID. The authors note LENSE’s efficiency in reducing crowd reliance over time (Figure 3) and its faster early accuracy gains versus online learning (Figure 4), as it strategically leverages crowd expertise when model uncertainty is high.  

This is a compelling paper that introduces the first principled, complete framework for hybrid crowd-model learning. Its results against strong baselines are impressive, and the approach holds significant potential for practical adoption. I strongly recommend acceptance. However, clarity issues risk diminishing its impact, particularly regarding the continuous time extension.  

The primary clarity concern stems from introducing continuous time mid-section (Section 3), which complicates the formulation and optimization without clear experimental benefits. Table 2 shows the threshold baseline achieves comparable performance to LENSE on NER (with lower latency) and even outperforms it on face ID. The authors attribute the NER’s marginal LENSE advantage to label interactions, but the face ID task—where interactions are minimal—also sees no clear LENSE benefit (l. 314). For practical applications, the threshold baseline (a principled hybrid system outperforming active/online learning) is likely sufficient. The paper would benefit from presenting the core method *without* continuous time first (simpler to understand/adopt), with continuous time as an optional extension.  

Additional clarity improvements are needed:  
1. **Baseline presentation**: The threshold baseline’s performance is consistently strong, even with label dependencies (e.g., NER). The paper should explicitly emphasize this to highlight the core method’s viability without the time complexity.  
2. **Missing data**: The 5-vote results are absent from Face ID and sentiment tables/figures (Tables 2, 3; Figure 4), despite claiming "beats human performance" (introduction). These should be included, and the 5-vote results (and threshold’s NER latency) should be highlighted as top performers.  
3. **Threshold baseline detail**: The uncertainty condition (l. 246–252) should be *(1 − p_θ(y_i|x)) · 0.3^m ≤ (1 − 0.88)* (not ≥ 0.88), as *m* reduces uncertainty.  
4. **Methodological details**:  
   - *t_deadline* usage in experiments is unspecified (l. 183–187). Was it an entropy threshold or fixed deadline?  
   - Equation (2) requires *s* in *P(y,r,t|x,q,s)*.  
   - Equation (5) uses confusing notation *q* in *F(q)*; replace with another symbol.  

The paper’s core contribution—hybrid learning via a principled stochastic game—is novel and impactful. The continuous time extension, while technically sound, adds complexity without demonstrable gains. Streamlining the presentation around the core method (without time) would enhance accessibility without weakening the contribution. The experimental claims are strong, but missing data and inconsistent baseline reporting require correction.  

In summary, this is an original, significant paper deserving of acceptance. Addressing the clarity issues—especially clarifying the threshold baseline’s strength and the minimal benefit of continuous time—will maximize its impact. The authors should revise to present the core method first, position continuous time as an optional refinement, and ensure all baseline results are consistently reported. With these fixes, the paper will be exceptionally clear and influential.
