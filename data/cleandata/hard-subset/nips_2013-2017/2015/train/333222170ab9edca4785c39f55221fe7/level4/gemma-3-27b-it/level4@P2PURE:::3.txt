This paper presents a hybrid approach for leveraging both crowdsourced labels and an incrementally (online) trained model to address prediction problems; the core idea is to heavily rely on the crowd during initial system ramp-up, learn from the acquired labels, and subsequently reduce reliance on the crowd as the model gains confidence. This is achieved through a sophisticated formulation of the problem as a stochastic game based on a CRF prediction model, where both the system and the crowd are players. The system issues one or more queries *q* for tokens *x* (with true label *y*) eliciting responses *r*, with a utility *U(q,r)* associated with each outcome; the system aims to select actions that maximize expected utility. Crucially, queries are not issued simultaneously, but at times *s* (with response times *t*); utility is maximized subject to a *t_deadline* constraint determining the number and rate of queries. 

Computing this expected utility necessitates utilizing the simulation dynamics model *P(y,r,t|x,q,s)* to estimate utilities as in (4). Given these utility values, the optimal action could be chosen; however, introducing continuous time renders this optimization intractable, prompting the use of an approximation based on Monte Carlo Tree Search and TD learning (Algorithm 1). Experiments were conducted on a simplified named entity recognition (NER) task, a sentiment recognition task, and a face identification task, comparing four methods: majority vote of *n* human judges (1, 3, 5), online learning (using true labels), a “threshold” baseline (the authors’ model without continuous time, issuing *m* queries until model prediction uncertainty falls below a threshold), and the authors’ full model (LENSE). In terms of precision/recall/F1, the full model outperforms all but the 5-vote crowdsourced version, although the “threshold” baseline performs nearly as well (and with lower latency for NER; Table 2). The authors also demonstrate that the model requires fewer crowdsourced labels over time with a sufficiently strong training signal (Figure 3), and that its accuracy surpasses online learning from the first example (Figure 4), due to the system leveraging crowd expertise while model uncertainty is high.

This is a very interesting and promising paper, likely to stimulate discussion, inspire further research, and facilitate practical adoption. While several heuristic approaches exist for learning from the crowd while training a system, this is the first complete and principled proposal I have encountered. The results against reasonable baselines are impressive, indicating the work is highly original and significant, and thus deserves acceptance to the conference. However, the paper’s clarity could be improved, potentially diminishing its impact. Many of these issues are correctable, even for the camera-ready version, with dedicated effort and potential adjustments to the presentation, particularly regarding the comparison of the “threshold” baseline and the full continuous-time model.

The primary clarity issue stems from the introduction of continuous time, beginning in Section 3. Although the motivation – the need to operate under both time and cost constraints – is reasonable, experimental benefits appear minimal while significantly increasing formulation complexity and the optimization procedure. In fact, Table 2 suggests the “threshold” baseline achieves substantially lower latency on NER with comparable performance (marginally worse); on face ID, it performs even better (though still roughly equivalent). The authors argue (l. 314) that the baseline’s success on the latter task is due to the absence of label interaction, yet the NER task, characterized by substantial label interaction, only exhibits marginal benefits from continuous time. For many practical applications, the “threshold” baseline will likely suffice, already representing a significant advance as a principled hybrid learning system surpassing active and online learning. The paper would benefit from emphasizing this, perhaps by developing the core method fully (without continuous time) and presenting the addition of continuous time as an extension. Similarly, in discussing results, the authors should highlight the strong performance of the “threshold” method, even with sequence dependencies. 

Several smaller issues affect the results reporting. First, the introduction claims the model “beats human performance” – which is inaccurate for NER with 5 votes; strangely, the 5-vote case is missing for Face-ID and Sentiment tasks in multiple tables and figures (the right half of Tables 2, Table 3 and Figure 4) and should be included. Second, results for the 5-vote case in Table 2 should be bolded to indicate best performance. The latency value for Threshold in NER should also be bolded for the same reason, as should the LENSE value for Face ID. More critically, the “Threshold” baseline is absent from the sentiment experiment and thus does not appear in Table 3 or Figure 4 – its inclusion is essential.

Finally, some minor points regarding the method description:
- While *t_deadline* is discussed (l.183-187), it’s unclear if/how it was used in the experiments, and what criteria determined when an answer was considered ready. Was it an entropic threshold (as in the baseline) or reaching a specific *t_deadline*?
- In equation (2), the distribution should include the variable *s* (i.e., *p(y,r,t|x,q,s)*).
- In equation (5), the use of *q* in *F(q)* is confusing – I initially assumed it referred to a query, but it represents a nominal distribution function; choose a different letter.
- In the description of the Threshold baseline (l.246-252), the expression should be *(1 - pθ(yi|x))*0.3^m <= (1-0.88)*, not >= 0.88, as increasing *m* reduces uncertainty.

This paper presents a hybrid approach for using both crowdsourced labels and an incrementally (online) trained model to address prediction problems, elegantly cast as a stochastic game that models many aspects of the data, true labels, and crowdworker responses, including the time at which queries are sent out and answers received. Impressive results are shown with respect to multiple human labelers, online learning with oracle labels, and a “threshold” baseline using the authors’ model but removing the dependence on continuous time that performs on par. The method is original, novel, and interesting, and as such the paper should be accepted, but there are some issues with clarity, particularly with respect to the introduction of continuous time, which greatly complicates the discussion and algorithmic mechanics yet seems to yield minimal benefits in performance.
