- Main contribution: a more principled Bayesian decision-theoretic formulation of an on-the-job learner that transitions from crowd labels to a learned model using MCTS to balance latency, cost, and accuracy.  
- Methodology: frames query selection as a decision problem, leverages Monte Carlo Tree Search and progressive widening to handle continuous time and intractable policy optimization.  
- Presentation: generally clear but missing specific implementation and experimental details.  
- Empirical results: improvements over a threshold-based heuristic are modest; authors provide open-source code for reproducibility.  
- Clarification needed (Eq. 2): ExpAcc(p) appears incomplete and should likely be conditioned on input x and query history; disappearance of y from p_R(r_i | y, q_i) inside the expectation is unclear.  
- Clarification needed (Algorithm 1): meaning of N(σ) is ambiguous—unclear if it denotes state visit count or something else.  
- Clarification needed (baseline thresholds): the choice of 0.3 and 0.88 for the threshold baseline requires justification.  
- Baselines and comparison: paper should compare to stronger state-of-the-art ML systems for each task to ensure gains aren’t primarily from human annotation quality.  
- Error analysis: lacking — needs analysis of cases where the on-the-job learner helps or fails relative to established baselines.  
- Argument for acceptance: novel principled framework, technically sound approach (MCTS + progressive widening), potential real-world impact, and open-source release.  
- Argument against acceptance: modest empirical gains, lack of implementation/experimental detail, and limited error analysis.
