The paper proposes an “on-the-job learning” setting in which the model can query annotators between receiving an example and issuing a prediction. The approach is clear for structured prediction, where individual components of a sequence can be queried. However, its application to the face recognition task is unclear: was the entire instance simply queried? In the non-structured case, there is no notion of adjacent positions to propagate information across examples, so it is not obvious how the proposed utility function is defined or justified in this setting. Clarification on how queries and utility are handled for non-structured tasks is needed.

The threshold baseline includes two parameters that appear arbitrary. How were these values selected (e.g., validation, heuristics), and how sensitive are the results to these choices? A sensitivity analysis would strengthen the evaluation.

An additional baseline using uncertainty sampling, in the spirit of active learning (e.g., margin or entropy-based querying), would be informative and provide a stronger point of comparison.

Although the paper emphasizes distinctions from active online learning, standard active learning could be adapted to this domain by treating the active set as a sequence: the model could be updated as responses arrive and produce a prediction at the end. While this would not capture the temporal nature of the decision process, it would allow leveraging established theory for query selection. A discussion or empirical comparison to such an adapted active learning approach would help position the contribution.

Overall, the framework appears novel, but several details are insufficiently specified, and its applicability seems restricted. Clarifying the non-structured application, justifying and analyzing baseline parameters, and adding a more principled active-learning-style baseline would substantially improve the paper.
