

This paper presents a novel framework for on-the-job learning, where a system achieves high accuracy from the outset without initial labeled data by strategically querying crowdsourcing for uncertain predictions. The authors formalize this as a stochastic game using Bayesian decision theory, balancing accuracy, latency, and cost, and propose an approximate solution via Monte Carlo Tree Search with progressive widening. The approach is evaluated across three tasks (NER, sentiment, image classification), demonstrating significant cost reductions (over an order of magnitude for NER) and accuracy gains (8% F1 over single-human labeling, 28% over online learning) compared to baselines.  

**Strengths:**  
- **Originality:** The integration of Bayesian decision theory with stochastic games for on-the-job learning—optimizing latency, cost, and accuracy simultaneously—represents a novel contribution distinct from prior work in online active learning (e.g., [8,9,10]) and active classification (e.g., [22,23,24]), which lack dynamic model improvement during test time.  
- **Significance:** The framework addresses a critical practical gap in deploying AI systems with scarce initial data (e.g., disaster response), offering tangible cost reductions while maintaining or improving accuracy over human-only or ML-only approaches.  
- **Quality:** Experiments are well-designed, with controlled crowdsourcing setups (e.g., frozen worker response pools) and strong empirical results across diverse tasks. The LENSE system implementation is reproducible (CodaLab link provided).  

**Weaknesses:**  
- **Clarity:** The example in Section 3 (Line 154) is overly abstract; a concrete illustration with specific query times, responses, and label marginals would greatly enhance readability.  
- **Terminology:** The "threshold baseline" is inconsistently labeled as "entropic" in Tables 2–3 (e.g., "entropic" in Table 2 vs. "threshold" in Table 3), causing confusion.  
- **Structure:** The related work section (Section 6) appears misplaced; it should be relocated to a dedicated section (e.g., Section 2) to contextualize contributions relative to NIPS proceedings (e.g., recent active learning work in NIPS 2022 [20,21]).  

**Pro/Con Acceptance:**  
*Pro:* Novel problem formulation, strong empirical validation, and clear practical impact.  
*Con:* Clarity issues and inconsistent terminology hinder reproducibility and reader comprehension.  

The paper makes a meaningful contribution to the intersection of active learning, crowdsourcing, and real-time deployment. With minor revisions to clarify the example and standardize terminology, it would be a strong fit for NIPS. I recommend acceptance pending these clarifications.
