This paper introduces a learning scenario termed "on the job learning," where an algorithm can query an annotator for labels between receiving an example and making a final prediction. While the proposed framework is interesting, the paper suffers from a lack of clarity in key areas and could benefit from a more thorough comparison to related work.

**Clarity on Application:**
While the application of the proposed algorithm to structured prediction tasks is clear, its implementation for the face recognition task lacks detail. It is not explained how the querying mechanism, which appears designed for elements within a sequence, is adapted to a non-structured setting. For instance, was the entire instance being queried? If so, it is unclear how the utility function, which seems to rely on propagating information between adjacent positions in a sequence, would remain effective in a non-structured context where such propagation is not possible. Clarifying this point is crucial for understanding the method's generalizability beyond sequential data.

**Baselines and Experimental Setup:**
The experimental comparison could be strengthened. The "threshold" baseline employs two parameters whose values appear arbitrary. The paper would benefit from a justification for these choices and a sensitivity analysis to demonstrate their impact on performance. Furthermore, to better contextualize the results, a comparison against a standard uncertainty sampling baseline, a common and powerful approach in active learning, would be highly informative.

**Positioning Relative to Active Learning:**
The authors draw a firm distinction between their setting and active online learning. However, this distinction may be less significant than presented, as standard active learning methods could plausibly be adapted to this domain. For instance, one could treat the incoming sequence as the active set under consideration, query a subset of instances based on a utility criterion, update the model upon receiving their labels, and then make predictions for the entire sequence. While such an adaptation might not explicitly model the temporal dependencies in the same way, it leverages a vast body of existing theory for selecting an optimal query set. A more thorough discussion, and ideally an experimental comparison to such an adapted active learning approach, would better justify the novelty and necessity of the proposed framework.

**Conclusion:**
In summary, the paper presents an interesting and seemingly novel framework. However, its contribution is currently diminished by a lack of clarity regarding its application to non-structured tasks and its unclear relationship to established active learning paradigms. The claims of novelty would be more convincing with a stronger set of baselines and a more detailed discussion of how this method differs from, and improves upon, adapted active learning strategies. As it stands, the framework's applicability appears somewhat restricted.
