This paper introduces a compelling framework for "on-the-job learning," a hybrid approach that combines real-time crowdsourcing with an online learning model to solve prediction tasks. The core idea is to rely heavily on human input when the model is new and uncertain, and then gradually reduce this reliance as the model learns from the crowd-provided labels and becomes more confident.

The problem is elegantly formulated as a stochastic game between the system and the crowd, grounded in Bayesian decision theory. For a given input, the system can choose to query the crowd for labels on specific parts of the input, wait for responses, or make a final prediction. The system's actions aim to maximize an expected utility function that balances accuracy, monetary cost, and latency. A key feature of the model is its handling of continuous time, where queries are issued at specific times `s` and responses arrive at later times `t`. Because computing the optimal policy is intractable, the authors propose an approximation based on Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) combined with progressive widening to handle the continuous-time variable.

The proposed system, LENSE, was evaluated on named-entity recognition (NER), sentiment analysis, and face classification tasks. It was compared against several baselines: human majority vote (1, 3, and 5 votes), a standard online learner given gold labels, and a "threshold" baseline that uses the authors' framework but without the complexities of continuous time. The results show that LENSE achieves high accuracy from the very first example, requires progressively fewer queries over time, and outperforms the online learning and 1-vote human baselines. Its performance is comparable to, though slightly below, the 5-vote human baseline, but at a fraction of the cost.

This is an excellent and thought-provoking paper that addresses a significant and practical problem in machine learning. While many systems have used heuristic methods to combine machine learning with crowdsourcing, this work is one of the first I have seen to propose a complete and principled, decision-theoretic framework for doing so. The results are impressive and demonstrate clear advantages over reasonable baselines. The work is highly original, significant, and likely to inspire further research and practical adoption. I believe it is a strong candidate for acceptance.

However, the paper's potential impact is somewhat hindered by issues of clarity, particularly concerning the central contribution of modeling continuous time. While the motivation is sound—in many real-world scenarios, latency is a critical factor—the inclusion of continuous time adds substantial complexity to the formulation, the discussion, and the optimization algorithm. Yet, the experimental benefit of this complexity appears to be marginal. For instance, in Table 2, the simpler "threshold" baseline achieves significantly lower latency on the NER task with only a negligible drop in F1 score. On the face classification task, the threshold baseline is both faster and slightly more accurate. While the authors suggest this is because face classification is a single-label task without strong label interactions, the benefit of the full time-aware model on the NER task, which is rich with such interactions, is still very small.

For many practical applications, the "threshold" baseline would be more than sufficient and represents a major contribution in its own right. The paper would have a greater impact if this were made clearer. To improve clarity, the authors might consider restructuring the presentation. A more accessible approach would be to first fully develop the core framework without continuous time (akin to the "threshold" baseline), which is a significant contribution in itself. The extension to continuous time could then be introduced as an additional layer of sophistication. While this is only a suggestion, I believe it would make the work more accessible and increase its adoption.

There are also several smaller issues regarding the experiments and their presentation that should be addressed:

*   **Missing Baselines:** For a complete comparison, the 5-vote human baseline results should be included for the Sentiment and Faces tasks across all relevant tables and figures (e.g., Table 2, Table 3, Figure 4). Similarly, the "Threshold" baseline is a crucial point of comparison and should be included in the sentiment task experiments (Table 3, Figure 4).
*   **Performance Claims:** The claim in the introduction that the system achieves a "boost in performance relative to the expert provided labels" should be qualified. While it outperforms a single expert (1-vote), the 5-vote human baseline still achieves the highest F1 score on the NER task.
*   **Table Presentation:** As a minor point, the best-performing result in each column of the tables should be bolded for readability. This would include the 5-vote F1 score on NER, the Threshold latency on NER, and the LENSE latency on the Faces task.

Finally, I have a few questions about the methodology:

*   The utility function incorporates costs related to time, but it is unclear what, if any, hard deadline (`t_deadline`) was used in the experiments. How did the system decide when to stop querying and return an answer? Was it based on crossing an uncertainty threshold, reaching a deadline, or some other criterion?
*   In the description of the Threshold baseline, the update rule appears to have an incorrect inequality: `(1−pθ(yi | x))×0.3^m ≥ 0.98`. Since increasing `m` (the number of queries) should decrease uncertainty, one would expect the inequality to be `≤` a small uncertainty value (e.g., `≤ 1 - 0.98`). Could the authors clarify this?

In summary, this paper presents a novel and significant contribution by formalizing the "on-the-job learning" problem within a principled, decision-theoretic framework. The results are impressive and clearly demonstrate the value of this hybrid human-machine approach. While the paper is a strong candidate for acceptance based on its originality and potential impact, its clarity and, by extension, its influence could be substantially improved by reconsidering the emphasis on the continuous-time model, which adds considerable complexity for what appears to be a limited performance gain over a simpler but highly effective version.
