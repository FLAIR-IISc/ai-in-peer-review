Summary
This paper formalizes an on-the-job learning framework that starts with no labeled data, uses real-time crowdsourcing at test time to reduce uncertainty, and gradually shifts reliance from the crowd to a learned model. The authors cast the query/wait/return decision as a stochastic game grounded in Bayesian decision theory, balancing accuracy, latency, and monetary cost. Because exact optimization is intractable, they approximate the policy with Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) and progressive widening to handle continuous time. The prediction model is a CRF; crowd responses are modeled as noisy labels with a response-time distribution. The system is evaluated on named-entity recognition (NER), sentiment classification, and face/image classification using a retainer pool for real-time responses. Reported results include substantial cost reductions relative to full human annotation on NER, improved performance over expert labels in that setting, and gains over a single-vote human baseline and an online learning baseline. An open-source implementation (LENSE) is provided.

Relation to prior work
The paper extends prior “on-the-job” and real-time crowd-in-the-loop systems by providing a principled decision-theoretic formulation and a practical search procedure. It contrasts with traditional online learning and online active learning, which typically do not query the oracle before committing to a prediction and therefore cannot guarantee high accuracy from the start. It is related to active classification (test-time feature acquisition) but here the policy adapts during deployment as the model improves, and it addresses structured outputs.

Strengths
- Principled decision-making: A clear Bayesian decision-theoretic utility that explicitly trades off accuracy, latency, and cost.
- Practical planning: Use of MCTS with progressive widening is a sensible approximation for the large, continuous-time state space.
- Structured prediction: Applying the framework to CRFs highlights the benefits of sharing information across labels.
- Real-time crowdsourcing: The retainer setup and asynchronous querying are well aligned with the use case.
- Empirical promise and open-source release: Cost reductions on NER and availability of code/data increase the work’s potential impact and reproducibility.

Weaknesses and clarity issues
- Some modeling details are underspecified or confusing (see questions below), particularly around the utility, conditioning, and environment model.
- The empirical gains relative to a simple threshold-based heuristic are modest in places, and the paper does not include sufficient error analysis to diagnose where the proposed policy helps most.
- The “online” machine baselines are not clearly positioned relative to state-of-the-art systems for each task, making it hard to assess how much improvement comes from crowd supervision versus better modeling.
- The crowd model is simplified (e.g., fixed 70% accuracy), with limited analysis of robustness to worker heterogeneity or calibration errors.
- Limited ablations: There is little discussion of sensitivity to the cost/time weights (wM, wT), the assumed worker accuracy, or the benefits of parallel vs. sequential querying that the framework is designed to trade off.

Specific questions and requests for clarification
1) Equation (2) and conditioning: Please clarify Eq. (2). Should the predictive distribution and ExpAcc depend on all observed variables, including the query issue times s (and possibly t)? Also, when marginalizing in Eq. (3), how does y “disappear” in pR(ri | y, qi)? A step-by-step derivation of p(r′ | x, r, q) from Eq. (3) would help.

2) Notation in Algorithm 1: The pseudocode uses N(·) for visit counts. In the text you sometimes refer to N(s), but the state is σ. Please standardize and define all variables used in UCT and progressive widening (e.g., what exactly is counted by N(σ), how is C(σ) sampled from Eq. (3) when some responses are unobserved?).

3) Threshold baseline constants: How were the constants chosen (e.g., the 0.3 reduction factor and the confidence threshold, written as 0.98 in the paper)? Are these derived from the assumed 70% crowd accuracy or tuned on held-out data? Please report sensitivity to these choices.

4) Strength of machine baselines: Are the “online” systems competitive with strong task-specific baselines (e.g., CRF/RNN for NER and sentiment with modern features)? If not, why were stronger baselines not included? If yes, then any improvement must come primarily from crowd supervision; please quantify in which regimes this occurs and provide error analysis illustrating typical cases the model fixes via targeted queries.

5) Environment model calibration: The response model assumes a fixed 70% accuracy. Did you estimate per-task or per-worker reliabilities? How sensitive is performance to miscalibration of pR, and can the system adaptively learn rater reliabilities?

6) Trade-offs and ablations: Please show how the policy changes with wM and wT, and analyze parallel vs. sequential querying (e.g., curves for accuracy vs. latency vs. cost). An ablation comparing your MCTS policy to a greedy myopic policy would contextualize the planning benefit.

7) Learning dynamics: Provide more detail on how pθ is updated online (frequency, optimizer settings, regularization), and whether you use gold labels or only crowd responses. How do you prevent feedback loops from noisy labels early on?

8) Evaluation protocol: Since you simulate responses from a frozen pool, please clarify how this affects variance and whether the results are representative of true live conditions. Also, for NER, indicate which subsets/labels are used and how “expert labels” were obtained.

Overall assessment
The paper addresses an important problem with a principled, clearly motivated framework and a plausible planning approach. The implementation and NER results are encouraging. However, several modeling and experimental details need clarification, and stronger empirical analysis (including ablations and error analysis) is necessary to fully substantiate the claims, especially beyond NER. With these revisions, the work could stimulate valuable discussion on deployable crowd-in-the-loop systems and is a promising step toward practical on-the-job learning.
