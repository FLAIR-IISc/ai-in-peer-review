- Introduces "on-the-job learning": deploy high-accuracy systems from zero training data by querying a crowd per example before predicting.  
- Formalizes the problem as a stochastic game using Bayesian decision theory to trade off accuracy, cost, and latency.  
- Computes an approximate optimal policy via Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) with progressive widening due to intractability.  
- Positioned relative to online and active learning, claiming novelty by actively seeking information prior to each prediction.  
- Strength: tackles a practical, important problem of achieving high accuracy from the first example.  
- Strength: principled, elegant formulation grounded in Bayesian decision theory.  
- Strength: strong empirical results (notably NER) showing large cost reductions and F1 gains over baselines.  
- Strength: reproducible work with open-source code and CodaLab worksheet.  
- Weakness: unclear how the method applies to non-structured tasks (e.g., Faces/images)—what input "parts" are queried and how utility is computed.  
- Weakness: threshold baseline uses seemingly arbitrary parameters (0.3 and 0.98) without justification or sensitivity analysis.  
- Weakness: lacks comparison to standard active learning baselines (e.g., uncertainty sampling) that could be adapted to this setting.  
- Concern: claimed novelty over adapted online active learning is not fully substantiated due to missing empirical comparisons.  
- Overall accept arguments: novel, well-motivated framework; sound technical approach; strong empirical gains; reproducible.  
- Overall reject arguments: limited clarity on generality to non-structured tasks, weaker experimental baselines/parameter justification, and insufficient comparison to active-learning adaptations.
