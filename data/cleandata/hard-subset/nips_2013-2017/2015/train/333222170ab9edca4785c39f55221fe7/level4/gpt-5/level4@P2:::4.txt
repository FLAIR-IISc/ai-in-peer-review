This paper proposes “on-the-job learning,” a deployment-time framework that starts with no training data, queries crowd workers in real time to reduce uncertainty before returning each prediction, and updates a model online so that reliance on the crowd diminishes over time. The authors cast the problem as a stochastic game grounded in Bayesian decision theory, balancing accuracy, latency, and monetary cost. Because the optimal policy is intractable, they approximate it via Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) with progressive widening to handle continuous time. Experiments on NER, sentiment classification, and image classification show that the approach (LENSE) reduces cost while improving or matching accuracy compared to human-only and machine-only baselines, with particularly strong results on NER.

Relation to prior work: The setting overlaps with online learning (continuous model updates), active learning (strategic selection of supervision), active classification/test-time feature acquisition (deciding which information to acquire at prediction time), and crowdsourcing with decision-theoretic control (e.g., POMDP-based methods). The paper most closely resembles active classification and crowd-POMDP approaches but differs by combining structured prediction with real-time crowd queries and a unified decision-theoretic objective over accuracy, time, and cost.

Strengths:
- Clear, principled objective that jointly trades off accuracy, latency, and monetary cost using Bayesian decision theory.
- Novel formulation as a stochastic game with an MCTS-based planner for test-time query decisions.
- Demonstrated cost reductions and accuracy improvements, especially on NER; open-source implementation.
- Thoughtful modeling of real-time constraints and asynchronous crowd responses, including latency modeling.

Weaknesses and points needing clarification:
- Applicability to non-structured tasks is under-specified. For the Faces task, what exactly is queried? Without structure, the model cannot propagate constraints across adjacent positions; please clarify how the utility and inference operate when y is a single label and whether any structural coupling (e.g., attribute groups) was used.
- Baselines may be weak or insufficiently justified:
  - Threshold baseline parameters (uncertainty reduction factor 0.3, target threshold 0.98) appear ad hoc. How were these chosen? Provide sensitivity analyses.
  - An uncertainty sampling baseline (e.g., query most uncertain components before prediction) and/or a VOI/expected error reduction heuristic would strengthen comparisons, especially for the unstructured tasks.
  - Active learning adapted to this setting: A simple variant could update the model as crowd responses arrive, then predict once a budget or confidence target is reached. While it would not model time explicitly, it directly competes on accuracy/cost trade-offs. Please add such a baseline or justify why it is inappropriate here.
- Environment assumptions:
  - The response model pR uses a fixed 0.7 accuracy. This seems crude across tasks and workers. Did you estimate per-task or per-worker reliabilities, and how sensitive are results to this prior? If the simulation reuses a frozen pool of pre-collected responses, is there any distribution shift relative to live runs?
  - Latency model pT: What priors/hyperparameters were used? Are results robust to changes in the latency distribution?
- Fairness and completeness of evaluation:
  - Compare to decision-theoretic crowd-control methods (e.g., TurKontrol) and test-time feature acquisition/active classification methods (value-of-information approaches) where applicable.
  - Report computational cost: planning latency per example, MCTS budget, and how performance scales with sequence length (NER) or class set size (Faces).
  - Clarify the “boosting performance relative to expert labels” claim on NER: how was “expert” defined, and did the system truly surpass expert quality or just match/aggregate multiple workers?
- Clarity: Some notation and footnotes (e.g., rules on issuing queries during waits, ExpAcc definition) could be tightened. Minor text issues (e.g., consistent description of label sets, typos) should be corrected.

Quality: The core idea and algorithm are sound and the reported results are promising, but stronger baselines, clearer non-structured task treatment, and sensitivity analyses (pR, pT, cost/time weights) are needed to fully substantiate the claims.

Clarity: Generally well organized; however, the application to non-structured settings, the precise utility components, and baseline choices require more detail. A brief schematic for the Faces/Sentiment pipelines would help.

Originality: Modeling on-the-job learning as a stochastic game with MCTS in a structured prediction setting is novel. The integration of latency, cost, and accuracy in a single decision-theoretic objective is compelling.

Significance: If generalized and supported by stronger comparisons, the approach could impact real-time ML systems that must launch with no labeled data. Current evidence is strongest for NER; broader applicability remains to be demonstrated.

Requested additions/analyses:
- Precisely describe the query/action space and policy for Faces and Sentiment; explain how information propagates without structure.
- Add uncertainty sampling and a simple active-learning-style “query-then-predict” baseline; discuss differences in time-awareness.
- Provide sensitivity analyses for threshold baseline parameters, pR, pT, and utility weights (wM, wT).
- Report planning overhead and scaling behavior.
- Consider per-worker reliability or hierarchical models for pR; discuss how LENSE would leverage known worker-quality variation.

Pros for acceptance:
- Principled and novel formulation; elegant use of MCTS for real-time query planning.
- Strong NER results with meaningful cost reductions; open-source release.
- Clear practical motivation for immediate deployment with limited resources.

Cons for acceptance:
- Incomplete and potentially weak baselines; missing uncertainty sampling/VOI comparisons.
- Unclear generality beyond structured tasks; Faces setup under-specified.
- Limited sensitivity analyses and environment realism (fixed 0.7 accuracy).
- Computational cost and scalability not fully characterized.

Overall: A promising and timely contribution with solid ideas and encouraging results on NER. I lean toward borderline accept, contingent on clarifying the non-structured tasks, strengthening baselines (especially uncertainty sampling/active variants), and adding sensitivity and computational analyses.
