- Proposes an on-the-job learning framework that interleaves model inference with real-time crowdsourcing to produce high-quality predictions from the start.  
- System selectively queries the crowd for uncertain parts, gathers noisy asynchronous responses, and returns predictions when confident, reducing crowd reliance over time.  
- Formalizes the problem as a stochastic game using a CRF prediction model and a Bayesian utility trading expected accuracy against monetary cost and latency.  
- Uses Monte Carlo Tree Search with UCT and progressive widening to approximate the intractable continuous-time optimal policy.  
- Evaluated on NER, sentiment, and face classification against majority-vote crowdsourcing, oracle online learning, a threshold baseline, and the full LENSE system.  
- LENSE achieves strong accuracy with substantially reduced cost versus crowdsourcing and outperforms machine-only baselines while maintaining high initial accuracy.  
- Situates work among online/active learning, active classification, crowdsourcing, and structured prediction, but uniquely treats labels as noisy and integrates continuous-time decision-making for structured outputs.  
- Quality: formulation and decision-theoretic objective are sound; MCTS and progressive widening are sensible approximations; empirical protocol is thoughtful.  
- Quality caveat: clarity gaps, notation inconsistencies, and missing/uneven baselines weaken evidence for continuous-time benefits and reproducibility.  
- Clarity: main concept is clear but continuous-time formulation complicates exposition; reported empirical gains from continuous time are modest, making complexity feel under-motivated.  
- Originality: high—unified integration of Bayesian decision theory, real-time crowdsourcing, and structured prediction with an approximate solution.  
- Significance: high—addresses practical need for accurate systems with zero initial data while managing time and cost; open-source code increases adoption potential.  
- Pros: principled joint optimization of accuracy, cost, and latency; effective MCTS approximation; strong empirical results and decreasing query rates; practical system with real-time crowd infrastructure.  
- Cons: continuous-time adds complexity with modest empirical benefit over threshold baseline; inconsistent reporting (missing rows/baselines, unclear bolding); some overstated claims about beating humans.  
- Cons (cont.): notational/editorial issues and incomplete details on decision thresholds, deadlines, and implementation specifics.  
- Suggestion 1: Reposition continuous time as an extension to a simpler core method and run targeted experiments showing when continuous time helps (tight deadlines, heterogeneous response times).  
- Suggestion 2: Improve reporting completeness and consistency—add missing 5-vote and threshold baseline results, and clearly highlight best metrics.  
- Suggestion 3: Clarify decision criteria and hyperparameters (return conditions, t_deadline, sensitivity to wM/wT and worker accuracy), and test robustness to worker heterogeneity and drift.  
- Suggestion 4: Provide more algorithmic details and ablations (MCTS rollout policy, depth limits, computational budget, overhead, remove progressive widening/parallel querying).  
- Suggestion 5: Fix notation and minor errors (conditioning on s, avoid q ambiguity, correct threshold baseline inequality, clean up equation references).  
- Suggestion 6: Discuss limits with low model capacity, convergence behavior of query rates, and generalization to other structured tasks with stronger models.  
- Recommendation: Accept, contingent on improved clarity, balanced positioning of continuous-time contribution, and complete baseline reporting.
