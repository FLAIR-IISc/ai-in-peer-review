- Introduces "on-the-job learning": deploying accurate predictors from zero training examples by querying a crowd while learning.
- Models the interaction as a stochastic game between the system and crowd, with strategic queries to resolve uncertainty.
- Uses Bayesian decision theory to balance accuracy, latency, and cost.
- Approximates the intractable optimal policy via Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS).
- Implements LENSE and evaluates on NER, sentiment, and image classification, showing large cost reductions and improved accuracy vs baselines.
- Makes a meaningful contribution bridging online learning, active learning, and crowdsourcing, addressing the lack of methods that start with no labeled data.
- Clearly connects to related work and distinguishes from active classification and standard online learning.
- Strengths: novel, well-motivated idea; sound theoretical foundation; strong empirical results (notably NER: 88.4% F1 with >10× cost reduction); open-source code and thorough evaluation.
- Major clarity issue: continuous time extension is poorly motivated and unnecessarily complicates the model without clear benefits.
- The simpler "threshold" baseline often performs nearly as well or better (e.g., similar or better F1 and latency on NER), but the paper understates this fact.
- Missing comparisons: 5-vote human baseline is omitted for Face-ID and Sentiment in key tables/figures, hindering result interpretation.
- Contradiction: the paper claims to "beat human performance" on NER, yet the 5-vote human baseline outperforms LENSE.
- Technical errors: incorrect inequality in threshold baseline description (sign error regarding m), Equation (2) omits time variable s, and Equation (5) uses confusing notation for q.
- Recommendation to reframe: present the crowdsourcing-to-bootstrap insight and threshold method as the core contribution, with continuous time as a minor optimization.
- Practical implication: the threshold method's simplicity and lower latency likely make it preferable in real applications.
- Overall verdict: strong, novel, and technically sound paper with high impact potential; acceptance recommended after clarifying presentation and addressing noted issues.
