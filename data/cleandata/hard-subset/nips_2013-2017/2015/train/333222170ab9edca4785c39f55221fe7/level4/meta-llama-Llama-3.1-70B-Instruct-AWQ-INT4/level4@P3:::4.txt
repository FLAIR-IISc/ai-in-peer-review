The authors propose a novel framework for on-the-job learning, where a system learns to make predictions by actively querying a crowd for labels in real-time. The framework is formalized as a stochastic game based on Bayesian decision theory, which allows for a principled trade-off between latency, cost, and accuracy. The authors develop an approximation algorithm based on Monte Carlo tree search to compute the optimal policy.

The paper is well-motivated, and the problem of on-the-job learning is relevant to many real-world applications. The authors provide a clear and thorough explanation of their framework and algorithm. The experiments are well-designed, and the results are impressive, showing significant cost reductions and accuracy improvements over baseline methods.

However, there are some aspects of the paper that require clarification. For instance, it is not clear how the algorithm is applied to the face recognition task, which is a non-structured prediction problem. The authors should provide more details on how the utility function is adapted for this task.

Additionally, the threshold baseline used in the experiments has two parameters that seem arbitrary. The authors should provide more information on how these parameters were chosen and how sensitive the results are to these parameters. A further baseline of uncertainty sampling, akin to that used in active learning, would be interesting to compare with the proposed framework.

The authors make a distinction between their framework and active online learning, but it is not entirely clear why active learning cannot be adapted to work in this domain. The authors should provide more discussion on why their framework is more suitable for on-the-job learning than active learning.

Overall, the paper presents a novel and interesting framework for on-the-job learning. However, some aspects of the paper require clarification, and the authors should provide more details on the applicability of their framework to different tasks.
