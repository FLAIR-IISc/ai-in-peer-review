

This paper introduces "on-the-job learning," a novel framework that combines crowdsourced labels with incrementally trained models to address prediction tasks without requiring initial labeled data. The core idea is to strategically leverage the crowd for uncertain predictions during early deployment, then gradually reduce reliance as the model improves. The authors formalize this as a stochastic game between the system and crowd, modeled through Bayesian decision theory to balance accuracy, latency, and cost objectives. The system strategically issues queries for uncertain input components, incorporating crowd responses to update its model. To address the computational intractability of the optimal policy, they develop an approximation using Monte Carlo Tree Search with progressive widening to handle continuous time variables.

The approach was evaluated on three tasks: named-entity recognition (NER), sentiment classification, and face classification. The authors compared their system (LENSE) against four baselines: majority vote of n human workers (n=1,3,5), online learning with oracle labels, a threshold baseline (which queries all at once until uncertainty drops below a threshold), and their full model. Results show LENSE outperforms all baselines except the 5-vote crowdsourcing approach on NER (achieving 88.4% F1), while reducing costs by more than an order of magnitude. On sentiment and face tasks, LENSE reduces costs by approximately a factor of 2 for comparable accuracy. The paper demonstrates that LENSE's query rate decreases over time as the model learns, with richer feature representations (RNN) leading to faster reduction in crowd dependency.

This is a significant contribution that addresses an important practical challenge in deploying accurate AI systems without initial labeled data. The framing as a stochastic game with Bayesian decision theory provides a principled approach to balancing multiple objectives, and the results against strong baselines are compelling. The open-source implementation (LENSE) should facilitate adoption in real-world applications.

However, the paper's clarity could be improved to maximize its impact. The introduction of continuous time (Section 3) substantially increases complexity without clear benefits in results. In Table 2, the threshold baseline achieves comparable performance to LENSE on NER (with lower latency) and even better performance on face classification. The authors acknowledge this in lines 314-315 but don't sufficiently emphasize that the threshold method is already a strong, simpler alternative. The paper would be more impactful if it presented the core method (without continuous time) first, then introduced continuous time as an optional extension.

Several presentation issues affect clarity. The 5-vote crowdsourcing baseline is missing for sentiment and face tasks in several tables and figures (right half of Tables 2, 3, and Figure 4), despite being the performance benchmark. It should be consistently included and highlighted as the best-performing baseline. The latency value for the threshold baseline on NER and LENSE on face classification should be similarly emphasized. The threshold baseline is also missing from the sentiment experiment results (Table 3 and Figure 4), which should be included for completeness.

Minor technical points require clarification:
- The paper mentions a "t_deadline" (lines 183-187) without specifying how it was determined or used in experiments.
- Equation (2) should include time variables s as part of the distribution (p(y,r,t|x,q,s)).
- Equation (5) uses confusing notation (q for a distribution function).
- The threshold baseline description (lines 246-252) appears to have a sign error: the condition should be (1 - pθ(yi|x))*0.3^m ≤ (1-0.88) rather than ≥ 0.88.

The paper is original, significant, and technically sound. The core idea is compelling and well-motivated, and the results demonstrate clear practical benefits. With minor revisions to improve clarity—particularly by emphasizing that the threshold baseline is already a strong alternative—the paper would be even more accessible and impactful. The work deserves acceptance, and these suggestions would enhance its potential for adoption and further research.
