The authors describe a scenario called "on-the-job learning", where requests are made to annotators in between receiving an example and outputting a prediction. This framework is applied to structured prediction tasks, such as named-entity recognition and sentiment classification, where elements of the sequence are queried to obtain labels. However, it is unclear how this framework is applied to non-structured tasks, such as face recognition, where the model does not propagate information between adjacent positions.

The authors model on-the-job learning as a stochastic game based on Bayesian decision theory, which allows them to balance latency, cost, and accuracy objectives in a principled way. They develop an approximation based on Monte Carlo Tree Search to compute the optimal policy. The results show that on-the-job learning outperforms machine and human-only comparisons on both quality and cost.

The paper is well-written, and the authors provide a clear explanation of their framework and its application to various tasks. However, there are a few areas that require clarification. Firstly, the threshold baseline used in the experiments has two parameters that seem arbitrary. The authors should provide more information on how these values were chosen and the sensitivity of the results to these parameters.

Additionally, it would be interesting to see a comparison with uncertainty sampling, a common approach in active learning. This would help to further establish the novelty of the on-the-job learning framework.

The authors make a distinction between their setting and active online learning, but it is not entirely clear why active learning could not be adapted to work in this domain. The model could be updated in the same fashion on receiving responses, and then output a prediction at the end. While this would not capture the temporal nature of the task, there is a significant body of theory on choosing the query set that could be drawn upon.

Overall, the paper presents a novel framework for on-the-job learning, but some details are not clear, and its applicability may be restricted. The authors should address the above concerns to strengthen their contribution.
