This paper tackles the problem of building high-accuracy classifiers from scratch, starting with zero labeled examples. A core challenge addressed is handling noisy crowd-sourced labels, a problem extensively studied, but with a novel focus on timing and latency optimization. This is a compelling problem with practical relevance.

The strength of this work lies in its comparison of classifier accuracy to human performance. The results presented in Table 2 are particularly insightful. The LENSE system’s outperformance of the 3-vote baseline, coupled with its underperformance relative to the 5-vote baseline, highlights a significant trade-off between the benefits of increased consensus in simple voting schemes versus the efficiency of the LENSE approach for denoising crowd responses. This nuanced comparison warrants further discussion.

However, the paper suffers from some clarity issues. The example provided in the paragraph beginning at Line 154 is abstract and difficult to follow. To improve readability, I recommend illustrating the concept with a concrete query example, accompanied by representative crowd responses and corresponding latency data mirroring a real-world scenario. This would greatly aid understanding of the methodology. 

Furthermore, the presentation of the threshold baseline in Tables 2 and 3 is inconsistent. It is alternately referred to as “entropic” and “threshold,” causing confusion. Maintaining a consistent nomenclature throughout the paper is crucial for clear communication.

I also question the placement of the related work in Section 5. Typically, related work provides context *before* detailing the proposed method. Moving this section earlier in the paper would improve its flow and allow readers to better understand the paper's contribution within the existing literature.

Overall, despite the compelling nature of the problem and the interesting novelty in considering latency, the explanations are somewhat opaque. Addressing the clarity issues mentioned above – the example at Line 154, inconsistent terminology in the tables, and the placement of related work – would significantly strengthen the paper.
