This paper addresses the problem of batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF), a crucial setting for improving interactive systems using historical data. The authors identify a significant flaw in the conventional unbiased risk estimator used within the Counterfactual Risk Minimization (CRM) framework, which they term "propensity overfitting". This issue arises when a model with high capacity learns to exploit the importance weights themselves, rather than minimizing the true loss, leading to severely biased and degenerate risk estimates. As a solution, the paper proposes replacing the standard estimator with a self-normalized risk estimator, a technique that uses the sum of importance weights as a multiplicative control variate. This leads to a new learning algorithm, Norm-POEM, which is an adaptation of the POEM algorithm from prior work [1]. The authors provide theoretical justification for the proposed estimator, showing it is equivariant, bounded, and consistent, thereby avoiding the anomalies of the unbiased estimator. Empirical evaluations on several multi-label classification datasets demonstrate that Norm-POEM consistently and substantially outperforms its predecessor.

The work is a direct follow-up to Swaminathan & Joachims (2015) [1], building upon the CRM principle and the POEM algorithm. The main novelty lies in identifying the specific failure mode of propensity overfitting and applying the self-normalized estimator—a known tool from the Monte Carlo literature [12, 19]—to solve this problem in the context of learning.

**Strengths:**

*   **Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The identification of the "propensity overfitting" problem is a key contribution, and it is explained clearly and convincingly with a simple yet effective example (Example 1). The proposed solution is well-principled, drawing from established statistical techniques (control variates). The authors are careful to note the properties of their proposed estimator, such as being biased but consistent (Theorem 2), and they provide a suitable variance estimator for use within the CRM objective.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The motivation is clear, the problem is carefully articulated, and the proposed solution follows logically. The connection to prior work is explicit, making it easy for the reader to understand the context and contribution.
*   **Significance:** The problem of learning from logged bandit data is of high practical importance for many large-scale applications (e.g., search, recommendations, advertising). Improving the robustness and performance of learning algorithms in this setting is a significant goal. The paper demonstrates substantial performance gains over a strong baseline [1], suggesting the identified problem is real and the proposed solution is effective.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Originality:** While the identification of the problem is novel and insightful, the core technical contribution is the application of a well-known estimator to an existing learning framework [1]. As such, the work feels more like a solid, incremental improvement or a correction to the prior method rather than a major conceptual advance.
*   **Experimental Scope:** The experiments, while cleanly executed and supportive of the paper's claims, could be more comprehensive. The evaluation uses the same "Supervised -> Bandit" simulation methodology and datasets as the original POEM paper. While this allows for a direct and fair comparison, it would have been more convincing to see the method validated on additional datasets, or ideally, on a real-world logged bandit feedback problem. This would better substantiate the claim of practical significance.
*   **Optimization Analysis:** The paper notes that Norm-POEM surprisingly converges faster than POEM, despite a more complex objective function. This is an interesting and valuable practical finding, but the analysis is brief. A deeper investigation into the properties of the optimization landscape or convergence behavior would strengthen the paper.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
1.  Identifies and provides a solution for a subtle but critical flaw in a widely used approach for learning from bandit feedback.
2.  The proposed solution is simple, elegant, and theoretically well-motivated.
3.  Demonstrates significant and consistent empirical improvements over the previous state-of-the-art on multiple datasets.
4.  The paper is very clearly written and presents a complete piece of work.

**Arguments for Rejection:**
1.  The contribution is largely incremental, serving as an important but direct follow-up to the authors' own prior work.
2.  The experimental validation is limited to simulated environments and could be strengthened with real-world applications to better assess the true impact.

**Detailed Comments and Suggestions:**

The empirical results presented are compelling. However, for Table 1, which reports the core generalization performance, only the mean over 10 runs is provided. To better understand the stability of the improvement, it would be very helpful to also report the standard deviation or standard error.

The authors state that Norm-POEM is robust to translations of the loss function, which is a major advantage. Table 2 provides excellent evidence for this. Could the authors also comment on its robustness to the scale of the loss?

In summary, this is a high-quality paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of counterfactual learning. It clearly identifies a significant problem with the standard approach and proposes a well-justified and effective solution. While the novelty is more incremental than groundbreaking, the work is well-executed and the results are significant enough to be of broad interest to researchers and practitioners working with logged feedback data.
