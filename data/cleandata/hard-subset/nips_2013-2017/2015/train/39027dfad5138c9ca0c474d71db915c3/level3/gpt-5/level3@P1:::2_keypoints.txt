- Problem: Studies batch learning from logged bandit feedback under CRM and identifies "propensity overfitting" when using unnormalized IPS in ERM over rich hypothesis classes.
- Proposal: Replace IPS with self-normalized importance sampling (SNIS) and introduce Norm-POEM, an algorithm using exponential-family stochastic policies with variance regularization adapted to SNIS.
- Theory/Estimator: Uses multiplicative control variates to produce a bounded, translation-equivariant estimator and derives an approximate variance formula tailored to the ratio estimator.
- Empirics: Norm-POEM consistently outperforms POEM across multi-label datasets, approaches supervised CRF performance, and shows robustness to loss translations; diagnostic Ŝ(h) supports the diagnosis.
- Literature connection: Properly connects to off-policy evaluation, causal inference, classic IS/self-normalization practice, and recent counterfactual learning work.
- Strength: Clearly identifies a practical failure mode of unnormalized IPS and gives an actionable, principled fix via self-normalization.
- Strength: The multiplicative control variate approach yields desirable estimator properties (boundedness, equivariance) that address empirical pathologies.
- Strength: Strong empirical evidence and practical algorithmic design (tractable gradients, competitive runtimes, code release).
- Weakness: Exposition around equation (2) is misleading; it conflates population identities with finite-sample Monte Carlo estimators and misstates translation invariance.
- Weakness: Example 1’s justification relies on that misinterpretation and should be reframed using standard IS concepts (weight variance and weight-sum deviation).
- Weakness: Practical edge cases not fully discussed—paper assumes full support; deterministic or near-zero propensities can cause zero/infinite weights and need safeguards (exploration, clipping, logging corrections).
- Weakness: Theoretical treatment of SNIS within CRM is largely heuristic (approximate variance, asymptotics); a finite-sample generalization bound is missing.
- Quality: Main algorithmic idea is technically sound and empirically validated, but conceptual discussion around IPS needs correction.
- Clarity suggestions: Explicitly separate E_h0[·] population identities from finite-sample estimators, reframe "propensity overfitting" in IS terms, and tighten Example 1.
- Originality: Self-normalization itself is known, but integrating SNIS into CRM-style learning for structured prediction with diagnostics and empirical validation is a novel, useful contribution.
- Significance: Findings are practically important for off-policy learning in recommender/search/ads and likely to be adopted due to simplicity and performance gains.
- Requested revisions: Fix Section 4 around (2), rework Example 1 to anchor in weight-sum deviation and variance, state full-support assumption and handling of near-zero propensities, and (if possible) compare to a strong doubly robust baseline and sensitivity to clipping M and λ.
- Pros for acceptance: Addresses an important failure mode, provides a simple principled fix with strong empirical gains, practical impact, and available code.
- Cons against acceptance: Expository inaccuracies around IPS, limited new finite-sample theory for CRM+SNIS, and some incremental aspects versus known IS techniques.
- Overall recommendation: Clear accept conditional on revising the exposition around equation (2), correcting Example 1, and addressing practical assumptions and safeguards.
