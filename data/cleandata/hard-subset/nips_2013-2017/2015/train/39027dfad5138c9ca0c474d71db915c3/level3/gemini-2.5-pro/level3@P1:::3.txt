This paper addresses the problem of batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF), a setting of high practical importance for improving interactive systems using historical data. The authors identify a critical issue with the conventional unbiased inverse propensity score (IPS) estimator used within the Counterfactual Risk Minimization (CRM) framework [1], which they term "Propensity Overfitting". This overfitting occurs when a complex model minimizes the estimated risk not by fitting the true loss function, but by exploiting the propensity weights—either by assigning high probability to points with negative loss already in the training set or by avoiding the training data entirely for points with positive loss.

To remedy this, the paper proposes replacing the standard IPS estimator with a self-normalized estimator. This estimator, derived using a multiplicative control variate, is a ratio of the IPS estimate of the risk and the IPS estimate of the sum of weights. The authors show that this self-normalized estimator avoids the anomalies of the unbiased estimator: it is equivariant to translations in the loss and its estimates are bounded within the range of the observed losses. While this estimator is biased in the finite-sample regime, it is strongly consistent. Building on this, the paper introduces a new learning algorithm, Norm-POEM, which integrates the self-normalized estimator and an appropriate variance estimate into the CRM objective. Experiments on several multi-label classification datasets, using a "Supervised-to-Bandit" conversion protocol, demonstrate that Norm-POEM consistently outperforms the previous state-of-the-art, POEM [1].

**Strengths (Arguments for acceptance):**

1.  **Clarity and Presentation:** The paper is exceptionally well-written, well-organized, and easy to follow. The motivation for the work is established clearly, and the "Propensity Overfitting" problem is introduced with a simple yet powerful illustrative example (Example 1) that makes the issue intuitive and compelling.

2.  **Problem Motivation and Quality:** The paper identifies and formalizes a genuine and subtle problem with a standard, widely-used technique in off-policy evaluation and learning. The proposed solution is theoretically well-grounded, drawing on established principles of control variates from the Monte Carlo literature. The analysis showing that the self-normalized estimator resolves the identified anomalies (equivariance, bounded estimates) is sound.

3.  **Empirical Results:** The experimental results, while limited in scope, are strong and convincing within their setting. Norm-POEM demonstrates consistent and often substantial improvements in generalization performance over the baseline POEM method across all datasets and training data sizes. The ablation studies effectively show that the self-normalized estimator is robust to loss translation (unlike POEM) and that CRM's variance regularization remains beneficial.

**Weaknesses (Arguments against acceptance):**

1.  **Originality and Incremental Contribution:** The primary contribution is the substitution of the standard IPS estimator with a self-normalized version within the CRM framework established in [1]. While effective, this makes the work a direct and arguably incremental follow-up to prior work rather than a completely novel approach. The hypothesis space, learning principle (CRM), and experimental methodology are all adopted directly from [1].

2.  **Scope of Experimental Evaluation:** The experimental evaluation, while showing positive results, could be significantly strengthened. The experiments are conducted on the same set of multi-label classification datasets and use the same "Supervised-to-Bandit" conversion protocol as the original POEM paper. To demonstrate broader significance and show that the gains are not an artifact of this specific setup, it would be beneficial to see results on new and different datasets. Most importantly, an evaluation on data from a real-world logged bandit feedback system (e.g., in advertising or recommendation) would be necessary to substantiate the claim that these ideas are ready for practitioners.

3.  **Limited Analysis of Optimization:** The paper notes that Norm-POEM converges faster than POEM in practice, despite a higher per-iteration computational cost due to the more complex variance term. This is an interesting and practically important observation. However, this aspect is not analyzed in depth. A more detailed discussion of the optimization landscape of the new objective or empirical convergence plots would be a valuable addition to support this claim and provide deeper insight.

**Detailed Comments and Suggestions:**

*   In Table 1, the authors report average test loss over 10 runs but do not include measures of variance (e.g., standard deviations). Including error bars or standard deviations would be crucial to assess the statistical significance of the reported improvements over the baseline.
*   The significance of the work would be greatly enhanced by moving beyond the "Supervised-to-Bandit" simulation framework from [1]. While this is a valuable tool for controlled experiments, demonstrating the method's effectiveness on at least one real-world BLBF task is critical for assessing its practical impact.
*   The discussion on the computational efficiency is intriguing. Could the authors provide convergence plots (e.g., objective value vs. iterations/wall-clock time) for both POEM and Norm-POEM on one of the datasets to visually support the claim of faster convergence?

In summary, this is a solid, well-executed paper that identifies a genuine problem with a standard technique in counterfactual learning and proposes a simple, effective, and well-motivated solution. The empirical results are strong and the presentation is excellent. The main limitations are the incremental nature of the contribution and the narrow scope of the experimental validation, which mirrors that of the prior work it builds upon. Despite these limitations, the identified problem of propensity overfitting and the demonstrated improvements are significant enough to be of clear interest to researchers and practitioners working in off-policy learning and evaluation. I recommend acceptance.
