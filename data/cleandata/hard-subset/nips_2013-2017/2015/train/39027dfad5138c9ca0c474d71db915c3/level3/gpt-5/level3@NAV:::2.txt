Summary: The paper addresses counterfactual learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF), building on CRM and POEM (ICML’15) to mitigate “propensity overfitting.” The authors propose replacing the conventional inverse propensity scoring (IPS) estimator with a self-normalized importance sampling (SN) estimator and derive Norm-POEM, a CRM-style learner for structured prediction with exponential models. This connects to importance weighting and off-policy evaluation, including self-normalized ratios and control variates studied in Monte Carlo and RL literatures.

Strengths: The problem is timely and relevant to NeurIPS work on off-policy evaluation/learning and multi-label prediction. Using a self-normalized estimator within CRM is sensible and well-motivated, yielding bounded and translation-equivariant empirical risks and improved robustness. The experiments are carefully executed on multiple multi-label datasets; Norm-POEM consistently outperforms POEM and approaches the full-information CRF baseline, which is compelling.

Weaknesses and requested revisions: The commentary and notation around equation (2) are misleading: the loss being averaged is not the expected quantity described, and the expectation/translation discussion is imprecise. Consequently, the claim that (2) exhibits an “anomaly” (non-invariance to translation of the loss) as a property of the estimator is technically incorrect under the paper’s own interpretation; the lack of finite-sample equivariance follows from the normalization mismatch, not from an inherent anomaly. Example 1 relies on this misunderstanding and, as stated, does not hold. The estimator in (2) is also highly sensitive to the denominator π; while full support is assumed in theory, in practice π can be extremely small (or zero if an action is outside the logging policy’s support), leading to instability unless clipping/safeguards are explicit. The modified estimator in (7) is preferable in principle because the double normalization mollifies these effects; please clarify the conditions (support, clipping, finite-sample behavior) under which consistency and variance formulas are valid, and revise the commentary around (2)/(4)/(7) accordingly.

Quality: Empirically strong; theoretical framing needs precision in expectations/normalization. Clarity: Generally well written, but the discussion surrounding (2) and Example 1 must be corrected. Originality: A thoughtful specialization of known SN estimators to CRM/BLBF with a new algorithmic instantiation. Significance: Likely to influence practice in counterfactual learning.

Pros for acceptance:
- Clear empirical gains: Norm-POEM > POEM, near-CRF performance.
- Principled variance reduction via self-normalization.
- Relevance to ongoing NeurIPS work in off-policy learning.

Cons:
- Misleading exposition around (2) and Example 1.
- Insufficient discussion of denominator/propensity safeguards.

Overall: Accept, contingent on the requested revisions to the commentary and examples.
