- Addresses batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF), builds on CRM/POEM, and proposes Norm-POEM using a self-normalized importance sampling estimator.  
- Identifies "propensity overfitting" as a problem where IPS can be manipulated by hypothesis complexity to bias importance weights.  
- Norm-POEM empirically outperforms POEM, showing substantial robustness and accuracy gains.  
- Norm-POEM often approaches the performance of a fully supervised CRF skyline despite learning from limited bandit feedback.  
- Experimental evaluation is thorough, with consistent wins across multiple multi-label datasets and robustness to loss translation.  
- Paper is generally well-written, clearly motivated, and experiments are well-described.  
- Novelty lies in applying the self-normalized estimator to CRM/BLBF and empirically demonstrating IPS instability in this context.  
- Main theoretical weakness: the paper misinterprets IPS behavior, confusing finite-sample variance with a fundamental non-equivariance of the estimator.  
- The claim that the IPS estimator is non-equivariant to constant loss shifts is misleading because the expectation is equivariant; the issue is variance.  
- Example 1’s pathology (arbitrarily negative R̂) illustrates high variance from small propensities, not a novel estimator anomaly.  
- A stronger, standard motivation is that the self-normalized (ratio) estimator reduces variance of importance weights; the paper should emphasize this.  
- Recommendation: accept, but revise Section 4 to replace the misleading "propensity overfitting"/non-equivariance argument with a variance-reduction justification for the self-normalized estimator.
