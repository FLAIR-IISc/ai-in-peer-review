## Review of "The Self-Normalized Estimator for Counterfactual Learning"

This paper addresses a critical issue in batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF): propensity overfitting in the commonly used counterfactual risk estimator. The authors demonstrate that this estimator can lead to severely biased risk estimates, particularly with complex hypothesis spaces. Their key contribution is a self-normalized estimator that elegantly avoids this problem, resulting in a new algorithm, Norm-POEM, that consistently outperforms the original POEM algorithm. 

The work builds directly on Swaminathan & Joachims (2015) and expands the CRM principle with a more robust estimator. This aligns well with the increasing interest in learning from historical data, a theme frequently explored at NIPS and related conferences (e.g., Dudík et al., 2011; Beygelzimer et al., 2011).  The problem of covariate shift and off-policy evaluation, highlighted in the related work, provides relevant context, although the authors clearly differentiate their contribution as a specific instance of overfitting within the BLBF framework. 

The paper’s strengths are substantial. The theoretical analysis is sound, clearly explaining the issue of propensity overfitting and demonstrating how the self-normalized estimator alleviates it.  The empirical evaluation across several datasets is convincing, showcasing consistent performance gains over POEM, and the finding that the algorithm is faster despite the added regularization is a significant practical benefit. The writing is, for the most part, clear and easy to follow, with a logical progression from problem definition to proposed solution and experimental validation. The originality of the formulation and the practical impact of improved training of POEM are both noteworthy.

A minor weakness lies in the depth of comparison to other approaches for mitigating propensity weighting issues (e.g., weighting clipping). While the authors reference them, a more detailed discussion of why their approach is superior in this context would be beneficial. Also, while the authors state Norm-POEM is competitive with full-information CRF learning, a direct comparison with a CRF trained on the same limited supervised data used to generate the logged bandit feedback would further strengthen the results.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Addresses a significant and previously unrecognised problem in BLBF.
*   Offers a novel and theoretically grounded solution with clear advantages.
*   Demonstrates strong empirical performance and faster training times.
*   Contributes a significantly new formulation of POEM with meaningful improvements.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   Comparison to other propensity weighting mitigation strategies could be more detailed.
*   A more direct comparison to a full-information CRF trained on limited supervised data would be useful.

**Overall:** This is a strong paper with a clear contribution to the field. The technical quality is high, the results are compelling, and the writing is clear. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the points raised above.
