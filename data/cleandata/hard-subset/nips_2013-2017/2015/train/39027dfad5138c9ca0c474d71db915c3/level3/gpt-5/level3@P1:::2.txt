This paper studies batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF) under the Counterfactual Risk Minimization (CRM) framework. It argues that the commonly used inverse propensity scoring (IPS) estimator within CRM is prone to what the authors term “propensity overfitting,” and proposes replacing IPS with a self-normalized importance sampling estimator (SNIS). This yields a new learning algorithm, Norm-POEM, that adopts exponential-family stochastic policies with CRM-style variance regularization and an approximate variance formula tailored to the ratio estimator. Empirically, Norm-POEM consistently outperforms POEM on several multi-label classification datasets and approaches the supervised CRF skyline. The work connects to off-policy evaluation and causal inference, classic importance sampling and control variates, and recent counterfactual learning (e.g., Dudík et al. ICML’11; Swaminathan & Joachims ICML’15). Using self-normalization in policy learning is consistent with established IS practice (e.g., Hesterberg; Owen; Kong), and the paper’s diagnostic Ŝ(h) links well to weight-sum stability used in the IS literature.

Strengths
- Clear identification of a practically important failure mode when using unnormalized IPS within ERM over rich hypothesis classes, with an actionable fix via self-normalization.
- Sound use of multiplicative control variates yielding an estimator that is bounded and equivariant to additive translations of the loss, addressing empirical pathologies.
- Strong empirical results: Norm-POEM dominates POEM across datasets and training sizes, and remains robust to loss translations; compelling diagnostic evidence via Ŝ(h).
- Practical algorithmic design: variance-regularized CRM objective adapted to SNIS; tractable gradients; competitive runtimes; code availability.

Weaknesses and requested clarifications
- Exposition around equation (2) is misleading. As written, the loss term in (2) is treated as if its empirical average were a direct “empirical risk” that should preserve translation equivariance. For unnormalized IS, finite-sample estimates obey E_h0[δ(x,y) h/h0] = R(h) in expectation, but the finite-sample Monte Carlo sum is not translation equivariant; it shifts by C times the average weight. This is a known property of IPS, not an “anomaly” per se. The paper should clearly separate the population identity from the finite-sample estimator and avoid interpreting (2) as an empirical risk with invariances analogous to supervised ERM.
- Consequently, the argument in Example 1 relies on that misinterpretation. The phenomenon the authors want to highlight—minimization of the weight sum Ŝ(h) rather than alignment with low-loss examples—is real and important, but the justification should be reframed in standard IS terms (instability due to weight variability and deviation of the weight-sum from 1), not as non-invariance of an “empirical risk” surrogate. As written, the example’s logic does not hold; please revise to ground it in weight instability and finite-sample behavior of IPS.
- Practical edge cases: the estimator in (2) is sensitive to denominators p_i; when logging policies lack full support (e.g., deterministic logs), weights can be zero or infinite. The paper assumes full support in theory and ensures stochastic logging in experiments; it would help to make these assumptions explicit and discuss safeguards (e.g., exploration, logging corrections, clipping) needed in deployments.
- Theoretical development for SNIS within CRM is largely heuristic (approximate variance, asymptotic arguments). A finite-sample generalization bound paralleling CRM for the self-normalized case would strengthen the contribution.

Quality
- The main algorithmic idea is technically sound and consistent with control variate theory. The empirical study is careful and aligns with the proposed diagnosis (Ŝ(h) near 1 for Norm-POEM).
- However, the discussion around (2), “anomalies,” and Example 1 should be corrected to avoid conceptual confusion between population expectations and finite-sample Monte Carlo properties.

Clarity
- Generally well written and organized, with useful diagnostics. Suggested revisions:
  - Clarify notation and expectations around (2); explicitly separate E_h0[·] identities from finite-sample estimators.
  - Reframe “propensity overfitting” using standard IS vocabulary (weight variance, weight-sum deviation).
  - Tighten Example 1 accordingly; demonstrate the effect without relying on translation arguments.

Originality
- Self-normalization is well known in IS, but its integration into CRM-style learning for structured prediction, with diagnostics and empirical validation against POEM, is a meaningful and novel contribution for the NIPS audience.

Significance
- The findings are practically important for off-policy learning from logs in recommender/search/ads. The approach is likely to be adopted, given its simplicity, robustness, and performance gains.

Requested revisions (if accepted)
- Revise Section 4 commentary around (2) and translation “anomalies”; ensure expectations and finite-sample properties are correctly stated.
- Rework Example 1 to avoid the incorrect presumption and anchor the phenomenon in weight-sum deviations and variance.
- Explicitly state and discuss the full-support assumption; add guidance for handling zero/near-zero propensities in practice.
- If space permits, include a comparison to a strong doubly robust baseline and a brief sensitivity analysis for clipping M and λ.

Pros for acceptance
- Addresses an important and common failure mode in counterfactual learning.
- Simple, principled fix with strong empirical benefits.
- Clear practical impact and availability of code.
- Fits well within NIPS topics on causal inference, off-policy evaluation, and structured prediction.

Cons against acceptance
- Expository inaccuracies around IPS may confuse readers and should be corrected.
- Limited new theory for CRM with SNIS; reliance on approximate variance and asymptotics.
- Incremental over known IS techniques, though the integration and validation are valuable.

Overall recommendation
- Clear accept, contingent on revising the exposition around equation (2), the “anomaly” claim, and Example 1 as noted above. The empirical gains and practical relevance make this a strong and useful contribution.
