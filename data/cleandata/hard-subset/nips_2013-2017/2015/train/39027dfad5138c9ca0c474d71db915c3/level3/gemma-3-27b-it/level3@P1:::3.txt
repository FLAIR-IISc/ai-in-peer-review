This paper identifies a critical issue – propensity overfitting – in the widely used counterfactual risk estimator for batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF). The authors demonstrate that this estimator can lead to degenerate risk estimates, particularly when dealing with complex hypothesis spaces. To address this, they introduce a self-normalized estimator based on multiplicative control variates, which provably avoids this overfitting problem. This estimator naturally leads to a new learning algorithm, Norm-POEM, evaluated on multi-label classification tasks, consistently outperforming prior approaches employing the conventional estimator.

The work builds upon the foundations laid in [1], extending the Counterfactual Risk Minimization (CRM) principle with a more robust estimator. BLBF is a compelling paradigm for learning from historical data, avoiding the need for interactive exploration, and has strong connections to off-policy evaluation in reinforcement learning and causal inference. The identified propensity overfitting issue is a novel and important observation. Prior work, including related areas like domain adaptation and off-policy evaluation (e.g., [7, 8, 9]), hasn’t explicitly highlighted this specific form of overfitting, particularly concerning the impact of hypothesis space capacity. The self-normalized estimator, while rooted in established importance sampling techniques like those found in financial applications [17] and policy iteration [18], is applied in a novel manner specifically to mitigate this BLBF-specific problem.

The strengths of this paper are considerable. Technically, the analysis appears sound, and the theoretical justification for the self-normalized estimator is well-presented. The introduction clearly articulates the problem and its relevance, connecting it to broader research areas. The presentation is exceptionally clear and well-organized; the paper is easy to follow, and the notation is consistent. The empirical evaluation, while limited in scope, demonstrates a consistent performance improvement with Norm-POEM. The authors are careful to acknowledge the limitations of their approach and discuss potential areas for future work.  The provision of code is a significant positive.

However, the paper also has some weaknesses. While the empirical results are convincing, the experiments are primarily conducted on benchmark datasets used in previous work [1].  A more extensive evaluation, potentially including datasets with varying characteristics and, crucially, application to real-world scenarios (e.g., ad placement, recommendation systems), would strengthen the claims about the practical significance of the proposed method.  The discussion of optimization – mentioning L-BFGS – is somewhat brief. Providing more details about the optimization process, including convergence behavior and sensitivity to hyperparameters, would be beneficial.  While variance regularization is mentioned, reporting the variance itself, perhaps across multiple runs, would offer greater insight into the algorithm's stability.  Furthermore, a deeper analysis of *why* Norm-POEM converges more quickly than POEM, beyond the observation about smaller weight magnitudes, would add valuable understanding. The paper touches upon the computational efficiency, but lacks a more detailed benchmarking analysis.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** The identification of propensity overfitting and the proposed self-normalized estimator represent a significant contribution to the field of BLBF.
*   **Technical Soundness:** The theoretical analysis is rigorous, and the proposed estimator is provably superior to existing methods in addressing the identified problem.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to understand.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The experimental results demonstrate consistent performance improvements.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   **Limited Scope of Experiments:** The evaluation is primarily limited to benchmark datasets.
*   **Lack of Real-World Applications:** The paper does not demonstrate the applicability of the proposed method to real-world problems.
*   **Optimization Details:**  Insufficient detail is provided regarding the optimization process.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with a clear and compelling contribution. The identification of propensity overfitting is a valuable insight, and the proposed self-normalized estimator offers a promising solution. While the scope of the experimental evaluation could be expanded, the existing results are convincing.  I believe this paper merits acceptance, contingent upon minor revisions to address the concerns outlined above – specifically, a discussion of variance across runs and a clearer articulation of the benefits of the self-normalized estimator in terms of optimization efficiency.  



**Recommendation:** Accept.
