Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper identifies a failure mode—propensity overfitting—of the conventional inverse propensity (IPS) counterfactual risk estimator used in batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF) and proposes a self-normalized IPS (SNIPS) estimator as a remedy. The key observation is that empirical risk minimization with IPS can exploit variability in importance weights to reduce the sum of weights rather than aligning with low loss, leading to degenerate solutions that are not bounded by the loss range and are not equivariant to loss translations. By introducing a multiplicative control variate and adopting self-normalized importance sampling, the authors obtain an estimator that is bounded and equivariant, and prove strong consistency. Instantiating CRM with SNIPS yields Norm-POEM, a learning algorithm for structured prediction with exponential-family policies and a variance-regularized objective. Empirical evaluations on multi-label classification datasets show consistent improvements over POEM (which uses IPS).

This work is closely related to prior NIPS/ICML work on importance weighting and counterfactual learning: IPS and clipping (Ionides), learning bounds for importance weighting (Cortes et al., NIPS), doubly robust estimation (Dudík et al., ICML), logged bandit learning (Strehl et al., NIPS), and CRM (Swaminathan & Joachims). While self-normalized IS (weighted IS) is classical in Monte Carlo (Kong; Owen), applying it to address propensity overfitting during learning and integrating it into CRM with a principled variance penalty is novel and insightful.

Strengths:
- Clear identification and analysis of propensity overfitting, with intuitive examples and theoretical framing.
- Well-motivated adoption of self-normalized IS; the estimator’s boundedness and equivariance neatly address the anomalies of IPS.
- Strong consistency proof and a practical variance estimator for SNIPS within CRM.
- A concrete algorithm (Norm-POEM) with empirical gains over POEM across multiple datasets, including sanity checks on susceptibility to loss translation and weight sums Ŝ(h).
- The topic is important for large-scale recommender/ads/search systems where BLBF is prevalent.

Weaknesses:
- Some clarity/notation issues (see detailed corrections) and a few confusing points about loss scaling and sign conventions.
- Limited comparison to alternative variance-reduction/evaluation methods (e.g., doubly robust, clipped+normalized hybrids) and limited sensitivity analyses (e.g., to clipping M).
- Theoretical generalization bounds are inherited from CRM with IPS; a bound tailored to SNIPS within CRM would strengthen the theory.

Quality:
- Technically sound overall; the SNIPS objective and variance estimate are standard and appropriately adapted. The consistency argument is correct. The empirical evaluation supports the claims and includes diagnostic checks (Ŝ(ĥ), translation experiments). A more thorough comparison against DR estimators and analysis of bias-variance trade-offs under varying M would enhance completeness.

Clarity:
- Generally well organized and readable, with convincing examples. A few spots need clarification (loss range/sign, stochastic loss modeling) and some typos/notation issues (see below). Explaining the Normal/delta-method approximations for the variance in more detail would help reproducibility.

Originality:
- The core idea—using multiplicative control variates/SNIPS to avoid propensity overfitting within CRM—is a novel and meaningful contribution. While SNIPS itself is well known, the diagnosis of propensity overfitting in learning and the demonstrated impact on BLBF are original.

Significance:
- Practically important for counterfactual learning from logs, a topic of strong interest in the NIPS community. Results suggest a drop-in improvement over POEM; ideas are likely to be adopted and extended (e.g., to DR-SN variants, structured policies, and safe policy improvement).

Specific comments, questions, and requested clarifications/corrections:
- Loss scaling and sign conventions:
  - The CRM bound section assumes δ ∈ [−1, 0]. Please clarify whether Norm-POEM’s training objective (Eqs. (3) and (8)) always uses a rescaled δ in [−1, 0], or whether you sometimes use positive losses. Table 1 and related text refer to “δ > 0”; please explain precisely how δ is scaled or translated in those experiments and why POEM’s performance is affected while Norm-POEM is not (by equivariance).
- Modeling δ as a random variable:
  - While you briefly mention stochastic losses, the core setup effectively treats δ(x, y) as deterministic. For completeness and correctness, please formalize δ as a random variable and define the true risk as E_{x∼Pr(X), y∼h(x), δ∼P(·|x,y)}[δ], i.e., R(h) = Ex Ey E[δ|x,y]. This aligns with label noise and bandit feedback noise in practice.
- Typos/notation:
  - Line 170–171: the importance weight should be h(y|x)/h0(y|x). Also, the nonlinearity and variance sensitivity of IPS estimators in learning were already noted in [1]; please acknowledge this connection when motivating propensity overfitting.
  - Line 276: should be argmin (you minimize risk), not argmax.
  - Line 319: replace overbars with hats for empirical quantities to match earlier conventions (e.g., use Ŝ(h) instead of S̄(h), R̂ instead of R̄).
- Methodology and analysis suggestions:
  - Compare against a doubly robust baseline (and, if feasible, a self-normalized DR variant), as DR methods often outperform IPS when outcome models are decent.
  - Provide sensitivity to clipping M and to λ. Since SNIPS reduces weight variance, how does optimal M shift relative to IPS?
  - Discuss robustness to imperfect support (small but nonzero propensities vs. true support violations). Does SNIPS help when h0 has near-zero mass?
  - Clarify computational aspects: per-iteration cost vs. POEM, number of L-BFGS iterations, and why SNIPS yields smaller ‖w‖ in practice.
  - If possible, provide a generalization guarantee that directly incorporates the SNIPS ratio structure (beyond plugging its variance into the CRM bound).

Pros (arguments for acceptance):
- Identifies an important and previously underappreciated failure mode in BLBF learning.
- Elegant, principled fix with strong theoretical and practical support.
- Consistent empirical improvements and diagnostic evidence.
- Clear relevance to NIPS topics in bandits, causal inference, and off-policy evaluation.

Cons (arguments against acceptance):
- Missing comparisons to DR-based baselines.
- Some clarity/notation issues and need for more detailed sensitivity analyses.
- No new generalization bound tailored to SNIPS-CRM (though objective-level variance regularization is used).

Overall assessment:
An interesting and useful follow-up to [1], with a compelling diagnosis (propensity overfitting), a principled remedy via multiplicative control variates, and clear empirical gains on an important problem. I recommend acceptance after addressing the clarity and comparison points above.
