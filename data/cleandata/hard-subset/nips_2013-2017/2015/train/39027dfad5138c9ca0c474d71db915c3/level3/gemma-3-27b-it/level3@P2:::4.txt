This paper identifies a significant problem – propensity overfitting – in the commonly used counterfactual risk estimator for batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF), and proposes a self-normalized estimator to mitigate it. The work builds directly upon the foundations laid in [1] (Swaminathan & Joachims, ICML 2015) and presents a compelling theoretical analysis alongside convincing empirical results demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed Normalized Policy Optimizer for Exponential Models (Norm-POEM). This is a valuable contribution to the BLBF literature, offering a practical and well-justified improvement over existing methods.

The paper begins by clearly articulating the challenges of learning from logged data, where only feedback for the actions taken by a historical policy is observed. It correctly positions BLBF within the broader context of causal inference, off-policy evaluation, and domain adaptation. The core contribution lies in the identification of propensity overfitting, a phenomenon where the risk estimate can be driven arbitrarily low (or high) by exploiting the propensity weights, rather than reflecting true performance. The authors demonstrate this with a clear and illustrative example. The proposed self-normalized estimator, leveraging a multiplicative control variate, elegantly addresses this issue by providing a more stable and equivariant risk estimate. 

The strengths of this work are numerous. The theoretical analysis is sound, building upon existing results [1, 13] and providing a rigorous justification for the proposed estimator. The connection to importance sampling and the discussion of variance reduction techniques are well-placed and informative. The empirical evaluation is thorough, employing a standard benchmark setup from [1] and consistently demonstrating Norm-POEM’s superior performance across multiple datasets. The ablation study in Section 7, demonstrating the continued benefit of variance regularization and the computational efficiency gains, further strengthens the conclusions.

However, there are a few areas that could benefit from clarification. First, it’s crucial to clarify whether Norm-POEM, like the CRM framework in [1], assumes δ(x,y) to be in the range [-1, 0]. The paper does not explicitly state this, and the experiments presented in Table 1 (where the reported loss values are positive) raise a question. What is the meaning of “delta > 0” in the context of those experiments, and how is the self-normalized estimator adapted when the loss function is not bounded in this way? 

Second, the formulation of the true risk presented in Equation (1) could be strengthened. Modeling δ(x, y) as a deterministic function is a simplification. It would be more accurate to acknowledge that δ(x, y) represents a realization of a random variable, and to include an expectation over label noise in the true risk setup: R(h) = E<sub>x~Pr(X), y~h(x), δ~p(δ|x,y)</sub>[δ(x, y)]. This clarifies that the loss is inherently noisy and influences how one interprets the implications of potential overfitting.

A few minor technical corrections would also enhance the paper. On line 170-171, the ratio should be h(y|x) / h<sub>0</sub>(y|x) to align with standard importance sampling notation. Furthermore, on line 276, “argmax” should be “argmin” as the CRM objective explicitly minimizes risk. Finally, on line 319, the bars over variables should be replaced with hats to maintain consistency with the notation used earlier in the paper.

Despite these minor points, this is a high-quality paper that makes a significant contribution to the field. The insightful analysis of propensity overfitting, the effective use of a multiplicative control variate, and the clear empirical improvement over POEM on an important problem are all commendable. The paper is well-written and logically organized, making it easy to follow the authors’ reasoning.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Addresses a crucial problem in BLBF – propensity overfitting.
*   Provides a theoretically sound and practically effective solution.
*   Demonstrates clear and consistent empirical improvements over existing methods.
*   Well-written and logically organized.
*   Builds directly upon a strong foundation established in prior work ([1]).

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Minor clarification needed regarding the assumed range of the loss function.
*   Minor technical corrections are required.
