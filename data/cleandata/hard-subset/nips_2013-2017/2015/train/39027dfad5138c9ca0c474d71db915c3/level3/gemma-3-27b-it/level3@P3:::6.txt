This paper addresses a critical issue in Counterfactual Risk Minimization (CRM) – propensity overfitting – which arises when learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF). The authors convincingly demonstrate that the standard unbiased estimator used in CRM can lead to severely biased risk estimates and overfitting, particularly with complex hypothesis spaces. Their proposed solution, a self-normalized estimator, offers a compelling alternative that addresses this issue by bounding variance and maintaining consistency. The theoretical analysis and empirical validation on multiple datasets strongly support their claims.

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound and presents a clear and well-motivated solution to a significant problem in BLBF. The theoretical results, particularly the consistency proof for the self-normalized estimator (Theorem 2), are valuable contributions. The empirical evaluation is thorough, utilizing a standard supervised-to-bandit conversion methodology and demonstrating consistent performance gains with Norm-POEM over the conventional CRM approach (POEM). The inclusion of ablation studies (Table 3) – investigating the necessity of variance regularization – strengthens the analysis. The authors are careful to acknowledge the computational cost of the self-normalized estimator and discuss its impact on training time. While the paper doesn’t delve deeply into the computational complexities of different implementations, the observation that Norm-POEM converges faster overall is noteworthy.

**Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and logically structured. The introduction clearly motivates the problem and positions the work within the broader context of causal inference and off-policy evaluation. The mathematical notation is generally clear, though a reader unfamiliar with importance sampling might benefit from more detailed explanations of some of the key concepts. The examples (e.g., Example 1) effectively illustrate the propensity overfitting problem. The description of Norm-POEM is concise and easy to follow.

**Originality:** The identification of propensity overfitting is a novel contribution. While importance sampling and self-normalization are established techniques, applying them to specifically address this issue within the CRM framework represents a significant advance. The work builds upon existing literature on BLBF and off-policy evaluation (cited appropriately) but offers a distinctly new perspective and solution. The use of multiplicative control variates in this context, and the resulting self-normalized estimator, distinguishes this work from prior approaches focused on additive control variates or clipping.

**Significance:** The results are important because propensity overfitting poses a fundamental limitation to applying CRM in real-world scenarios where logged data is often biased and the hypothesis space is complex. By mitigating this issue, the authors enable more reliable and effective learning from historical data, with implications for a wide range of applications including advertising, recommendation systems, and web search. The consistent empirical improvements demonstrated by Norm-POEM suggest a demonstrable advance in the state-of-the-art for BLBF. The code release further enhances the potential impact of this work.

**Strengths:**

*   Novel identification of propensity overfitting.
*   Effective self-normalized estimator to address the problem.
*   Strong theoretical justification (consistency proof).
*   Thorough empirical validation on multiple datasets.
*   Clear and well-written presentation.
*   Code release.

**Weaknesses:**

*   The computational complexity analysis is relatively brief; a more detailed comparison of the computational cost of Norm-POEM versus POEM would be valuable.
*   While the ablation study on variance regularization is helpful, further investigation into the optimal settings for the regularization parameter (λ) could be beneficial.
*   The discussion of limitations is limited. Exploring scenarios where the self-normalized estimator might *not* be effective or might require further refinement would strengthen the paper.



**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Addresses a significant and previously unrecognised problem in BLBF.
*   Presents a technically sound and well-motivated solution.
*   Provides strong theoretical and empirical evidence supporting the proposed approach.
*   Has the potential to impact a wide range of real-world applications.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The computational complexity analysis could be more detailed.
*   The paper could benefit from a more comprehensive discussion of limitations.



**Overall:** This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of learning from logged bandit feedback. Despite minor weaknesses, the originality, technical soundness, and potential impact of the work clearly outweigh any concerns. I recommend acceptance. While the reviewer expresses some uncertainty, the benefits of this work are significant enough to justify inclusion in NIPS.
