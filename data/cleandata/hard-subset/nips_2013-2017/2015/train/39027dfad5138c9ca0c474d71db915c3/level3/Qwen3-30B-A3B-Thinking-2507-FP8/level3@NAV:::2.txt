

This paper proposes a self-normalized estimator for counterfactual learning in batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF), addressing what the authors identify as "propensity overfitting" in conventional estimators. The work builds on the POEM framework from ICML 2015 and extends it to a new algorithm called Norm-POEM. The authors claim their estimator resolves issues with the standard inverse propensity estimator (Equation 2), demonstrating improved empirical performance across multiple multi-label classification datasets.

The paper's main strength lies in its empirical validation: Norm-POEM consistently outperforms POEM on all evaluated datasets, approaching CRF performance with full multi-label information. The theoretical justification for the self-normalized estimator (Equation 7) is largely sound, and the authors provide a clear demonstration of how it addresses the boundedness and equivariance properties missing in the conventional estimator.

However, the paper contains significant technical errors that undermine its main theoretical contribution. The commentary around Equation (2) is misleading: the loss in this equation is not the expected quantity, and the expectation interpretation is incorrect. Consequently, the claim that Equation (2) has an "anomaly" (non-invariance to translation of the loss) is technically incorrect. This invalidates Example 1, which is based on this misunderstanding. Additionally, the estimator in Equation (2) is sensitive to its denominator π, which can be zero if a sample was never seen in the prior—this issue is not adequately addressed in the paper.

The modified estimator in Equation (7) is indeed preferable, as the double denominator mollifies the effects of π, and the authors correctly note this. The paper would be much stronger with a revised discussion of Equation (2) and its implications.

In terms of quality, the empirical results are convincing, but the theoretical claims require correction. Clarity is generally good, though the technical errors create confusion. The work is original in its focus on propensity overfitting and the self-normalized approach. The significance is high, as BLBF is an important problem in real-world learning applications.

Overall, this is a strong paper that addresses a meaningful problem. With the noted revisions to the theoretical discussion, it would be a valuable contribution to the field. I recommend acceptance with major revisions to correct the technical inaccuracies in the theoretical discussion.
