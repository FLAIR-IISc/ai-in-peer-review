Summary and relation to prior work: The paper studies batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF) under the counterfactual risk minimization (CRM) framework. The standard approach uses inverse propensity (importance) weighting to estimate counterfactual risk; prior BLBF work has explored unbiased IPS, clipping/truncation, and doubly robust estimators, drawing from off-policy evaluation and covariate shift literatures. The authors identify a distinct failure mode—“propensity overfitting”—where limited exploration leads hypotheses to exploit rare or missing logged actions, yielding degenerate empirical risks. They propose replacing the usual estimator with a self-normalized importance estimator (SNIPS), viewing the sum of weights as a multiplicative control variate. They instantiate this in Norm-POEM and report consistent empirical gains on multi-label tasks, along with code.

Quality: I had limited time and could not verify all technical details or originality claims. That said, the core idea appears sensible: self-normalization is known to mitigate weight-fluctuation pathologies and here neatly addresses lack of equivariance and bounding issues that exacerbate propensity overfitting. The empirical evaluation is careful and aligns with the theory (e.g., Ŝ(h) near 1). However, I would appreciate a clearer, formal connection between “large weight variance” and “propensity overfitting,” beyond examples.

Clarity: Generally clear and well-organized. Requests:
- Please clarify the relationship between weight variance and propensity overfitting: Are they equivalent under certain loss ranges, or can Ŝ(h) deviate without large variance? A proposition linking deviations of Ŝ(h) from 1 to bias of R̂(h) and to Var(weights) would help.
- Line 199 / Example 1: when n is small relative to k, the behavior of R̂(ĥ) (e.g., the −k expression) is not fully transparent; spell out the assumptions on δ and p and how the bound follows, and reconcile with earlier loss-range assumptions.
- Prior BLBF often used clipping (hence biased estimators). Please qualify the statement that “virtually all” used an unbiased estimator.

Originality: The identification and naming of propensity overfitting in BLBF and its systematic treatment via self-normalization within CRM are valuable. While SNIPS itself is known, the articulation of this failure mode and its implications for learning are novel and useful.

Significance: Practical and timely; likely to influence off-policy learning practice. Code availability is appreciated.

Pros:
- Clear problem formulation; practical fix with strong intuition
- Bounded, equivariant estimator; consistent; empirical wins across datasets
- Code provided

Cons:
- Need tighter theory tying propensity overfitting to variance and generalization bounds
- Some claims about prior work need nuance; a few unclear derivations

Recommendation: Accept. Despite not fully checking technical soundness, the idea is compelling, the empirical evidence strong, and the contribution relevant to the NeurIPS community.
