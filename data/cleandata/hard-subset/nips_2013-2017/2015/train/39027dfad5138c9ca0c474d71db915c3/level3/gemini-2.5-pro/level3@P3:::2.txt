This paper addresses the problem of counterfactual learning from logged bandit feedback, building directly on the Counterfactual Risk Minimization (CRM) framework and the POEM algorithm from Swaminathan & Joachims (ICML 2015). The main contribution is the identification and diagnosis of a failure mode the authors call "Propensity Overfitting," where a learning algorithm minimizes the empirical risk not by fitting the losses, but by exploiting the importance weights. The authors argue that this problem stems from fundamental anomalies in the standard unbiased inverse propensity score (IPS) estimator. To resolve this, they propose using a self-normalized importance sampling estimator, which gives rise to a new algorithm, Norm-POEM. The paper provides a theoretical motivation for this new estimator and demonstrates its practical superiority through extensive experiments.

**Strengths:**

*   **Originality and Significance:** The paper makes a valuable contribution by identifying the subtle but important problem of propensity overfitting. This is a novel insight into a potential failure mode of learning from bandit feedback, which is highly relevant for many real-world applications like recommendation systems and ad placement. The proposed solution is principled and effectively addresses the identified problem.
*   **Technical Quality:** The proposed self-normalized estimator is well-motivated. Its properties, such as being equivariant to loss translations and having its estimates bounded by the range of the observed losses, are clear advantages for empirical risk minimization. The derivation of the Norm-POEM algorithm is a straightforward and sound application of the CRM principle to this improved estimator.
*   **Empirical Evaluation:** The experiments are thorough and provide strong evidence for the paper's claims. Norm-POEM consistently and significantly outperforms the previous state-of-the-art (POEM) on all datasets. The results are compelling, showing that the proposed method can approach the performance of a fully-supervised Conditional Random Field, which serves as a strong "skyline." The diagnostic experiments in Table 2 are particularly insightful, as they empirically verify that Norm-POEM avoids the identified overfitting pathology and is robust to loss transformations.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

The primary weakness of the paper lies not in its core idea or results, but in the exposition and technical framing of the problem.

1.  **Misleading Commentary on the IPS Estimator:** The commentary surrounding Equation (2) is confusing and technically imprecise. The paper claims the loss in (2) is "not the expected quantity" and that the "expectation interpretation is incorrect." This is misleading. R̂(h) is a Monte Carlo *estimate* of the true risk R(h). It is not an expectation itself, but its expectation *is* the true risk (i.e., E[R̂(h)] = R(h)), which is precisely what makes it an unbiased estimator. This crucial distinction between an estimator and its expectation should be clarified.

2.  **Imprecise "Anomaly" Claim:** The paper claims that the non-invariance of R̂(h) to a constant translation of the loss is an "anomaly." For a finite sample, replacing `δi` with `δi + C` yields a new estimate of `R̂(h) + C * Ŝ(h)`, which does not equal `R̂(h) + C` since `Ŝ(h)` is not guaranteed to be 1. While this property has the undesirable *consequence* that the empirical risk minimizer can change, labeling the property itself an "anomaly" is questionable. It is a known feature of the finite-sample IPS estimator. A more precise framing would be to describe this as a vulnerability of the *ERM procedure* when using this estimator, rather than a fundamental flaw in the estimator itself.

3.  **Motivation for Example 1:** While Example 1 is an excellent and powerful illustration of the propensity overfitting phenomenon, its motivation is based on the imprecise "anomaly" framing discussed above. The example's conclusion is correct and highlights a real danger, but the paper would be stronger if the motivation for the example were revised to focus on the practical consequences of the estimator's properties on the learning objective, rather than on the less precise claim of an anomaly.

4.  **Practical Robustness:** The paper correctly notes that the standard IPS estimator in (2) is highly sensitive to its denominator `pi`, which can be zero or near-zero if a sample was rarely or never seen under the logging policy. This leads to extremely high variance. A key practical advantage of the self-normalized estimator in (7) is that its denominator, `Σ h(yi|xi)/pi`, helps mollify the effect of individual samples with very small propensities, leading to a more stable and robust estimate. This is a significant strength and could be emphasized more directly as a core benefit of the proposed method.

**Recommendation:**

Overall, this is a convincing and well-executed paper that makes an important contribution to the field of counterfactual learning. The core ideas are sound, and the empirical results are strong. The weaknesses are primarily expositional and can be addressed through revision.

I recommend **acceptance**, contingent on the authors revising the paper to address the points noted above. Specifically, the commentary on the properties of the IPS estimator (Eq. 2), the framing of the "anomaly," and the motivation for Example 1 should be revised for greater technical precision. With these changes, the paper will represent an excellent contribution to the NIPS proceedings.
