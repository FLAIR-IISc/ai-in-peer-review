

# Review of "The Self-Normalized Estimator for Counterfactual Learning"

This paper identifies and addresses a critical issue in batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF) - a problem the authors term "propensity overfitting." The paper builds upon Swaminathan & Joachims (2015) and proposes a novel self-normalized risk estimator that avoids this problem, leading to the Norm-POEM algorithm. The authors demonstrate that the conventional unbiased estimator (Equation 2) used in BLBF can produce severely biased risk estimates for complex hypothesis spaces, particularly when the hypothesis space capacity allows fitting the propensity weights rather than the loss function. This is a significant problem that has been overlooked in prior work.

The paper makes several important contributions. First, it identifies propensity overfitting as a distinct problem from the more commonly known loss overfitting in supervised learning. The authors provide a clear theoretical analysis showing that the standard estimator lacks equivariance properties and can produce arbitrarily low risk estimates for overfitted hypotheses. Second, they propose the self-normalized risk estimator (Equation 7), which resolves these issues by making the risk estimate bounded within the loss range and equivariant to loss translations. Third, they develop Norm-POEM, a practical implementation of the CRM principle using their new estimator, and demonstrate through extensive experiments that it consistently outperforms the original POEM algorithm.

The paper's theoretical contributions are sound and well-motivated. The authors provide a clear proof that the self-normalized estimator is strongly consistent (Theorem 2), and they demonstrate through examples that it avoids the propensity overfitting problem that plagues the conventional estimator. The experimental results are compelling, showing that Norm-POEM outperforms POEM across multiple datasets (Scene, Yeast, TMC, LYRL) with consistent improvements in generalization performance. Notably, the paper shows that Norm-POEM avoids the propensity overfitting problem by maintaining Ŝ(ĥ) close to its expected value of 1 (Table 2), while POEM's estimates are severely biased. The paper also demonstrates that Norm-POEM is computationally efficient, often converging faster than POEM despite the added normalization step.

The paper is well-written and clearly organized, making a complex topic accessible. The authors effectively relate their work to prior research in counterfactual learning and importance sampling, citing relevant literature from NIPS, ICML, and other venues. The experiments are well-designed and follow the established Supervised 7→Bandit methodology, providing a fair comparison with the original POEM algorithm.

Strengths:
- Identifies and solves a previously overlooked problem (propensity overfitting)
- Provides clear theoretical justification for the proposed estimator
- Demonstrates significant empirical improvements over the state-of-the-art
- Shows the new algorithm is faster than the original POEM
- Well-written and clearly organized
- Makes a meaningful contribution to the BLBF literature

Weaknesses:
- The paper could more explicitly discuss the limitations of the self-normalized estimator (e.g., when the denominator approaches zero)
- The theoretical analysis of the variance estimator (Equation 8) is somewhat brief compared to the rest of the paper
- While the experiments are thorough, the paper could explore more diverse hypothesis spaces beyond the exponential family

The paper's contributions are original and significant. The identification of propensity overfitting is a novel insight, and the self-normalized estimator represents a clean, principled solution that improves upon the state-of-the-art. The authors have made a meaningful extension to the POEM framework that leads to both theoretical and practical improvements. The results are interesting and warrant further research, particularly in exploring the application of self-normalized estimators to other counterfactual learning settings.

The paper meets all four NIPS quality criteria:
- Quality: Technically sound with well-supported theoretical analysis and thorough experimental evaluation
- Clarity: Clearly written with logical flow and well-explained concepts
- Originality: Introduces a new problem (propensity overfitting) and a novel solution (self-normalized estimator)
- Significance: Addresses an important practical problem in BLBF with substantial performance improvements

The Norm-POEM algorithm represents a significant improvement over the original POEM, with better generalization performance, greater robustness to loss transformations, and faster convergence. The paper makes a strong case for the adoption of self-normalized estimators in BLBF settings, and the results suggest this approach could see widespread use in practice.

I strongly recommend acceptance of this paper. It makes a significant contribution to the field of counterfactual learning with well-motivated theory, compelling empirical results, and clear practical implications. The paper is of high quality, clearly written, and represents an important step forward in the development of robust algorithms for batch learning from logged bandit feedback.
