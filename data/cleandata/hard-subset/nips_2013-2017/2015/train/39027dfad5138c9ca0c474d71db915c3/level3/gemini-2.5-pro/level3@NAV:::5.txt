This paper addresses the problem of batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF), a crucial setting for improving interactive systems using historical data. The authors identify a subtle but severe flaw in the conventional unbiased risk estimator used in the Counterfactual Risk Minimization (CRM) principle, which they term "propensity overfitting." This form of overfitting occurs when a model minimizes the estimated risk not by learning the task, but by exploiting the propensity weights. Building directly on the POEM framework of Swaminathan & Joachims (2015), the paper proposes replacing the standard estimator with a self-normalized risk estimator, a technique from the importance sampling literature. This leads to a new algorithm, Norm-POEM, which is shown to be more robust and effective.

The paper’s strengths are numerous and significant.
-   **Quality:** The work is technically sound. The identification and clear articulation of the propensity overfitting problem is a valuable contribution in itself. Claims are well-supported by both theoretical arguments—showing the proposed estimator is equivariant and bounded—and a thorough empirical evaluation on several benchmark datasets. The experiments are well-designed and convincingly demonstrate the failings of the previous approach and the superiority of the proposed method.
-   **Originality:** While the self-normalized estimator is a known tool, its application to resolve the newly identified propensity overfitting issue within the CRM framework is highly novel. The paper successfully formulates a significantly improved version of POEM, clearly delineating its original contributions from prior work.
-   **Significance:** The results are highly impactful. Norm-POEM provides a substantial improvement in generalization performance over a state-of-the-art baseline. Furthermore, the surprising finding that the algorithm is often computationally faster than the original POEM, despite a more complex objective function, makes it a very attractive method for practical applications. This work significantly advances the state-of-the-art in counterfactual learning and is likely to be widely used and built upon by other researchers.

The paper is very well-written and easy to follow. A minor suggestion for improvement would be to slightly expand on the choice of L-BFGS for the non-convex objective, perhaps referencing its empirical success in similar settings.

Overall, this is an excellent paper that makes a clear, original, and important contribution to the field. It identifies a fundamental problem and proposes an elegant and effective solution, backed by strong evidence. I strongly recommend acceptance.
