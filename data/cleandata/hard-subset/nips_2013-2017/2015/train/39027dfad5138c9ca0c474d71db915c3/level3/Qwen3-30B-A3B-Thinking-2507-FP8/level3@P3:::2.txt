

**Review of "The Self-Normalized Estimator for Counterfactual Learning"**

This paper addresses a critical issue in batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF), where the conventional counterfactual risk estimator (Equation 2) can lead to severe overfitting. The authors identify a problem they term "propensity overfitting" and propose a self-normalized estimator (Equation 7) to address it, leading to a new learning algorithm called Norm-POEM. The work builds on the Counterfactual Risk Minimization (CRM) principle from ICML 2015 [1] and extends the POEM framework.

The paper makes a significant contribution by identifying a previously overlooked flaw in the conventional estimator, which has been widely used in BLBF literature. However, I have identified a critical technical error in the interpretation of Equation (2) that invalidates their key theoretical claims. The authors claim that the estimator in Equation (2) is not equivariant to translation of the loss function (i.e., R̂(h) + C ≠ R̂(h + C)), which they call an "anomaly." This claim is based on a fundamental misunderstanding: Equation (2) is not an expectation but an importance-weighted estimate, and its non-equivariance is not an anomaly but a direct consequence of its importance-weighted nature.

This misunderstanding invalidates Example 1, which purports to demonstrate propensity overfitting. The example assumes the estimator should behave like a true expectation, but the estimator is not an expectation—it's an importance-weighted average. The "overfitting" observed in the example is simply a consequence of the estimator's properties, not a new phenomenon.

The authors correctly note that the conventional estimator (Equation 2) is sensitive to the denominator π (h0(y|x)), which can be zero if a sample was never seen in the prior. The self-normalized estimator (Equation 7) addresses this by using a double denominator, which mollifies the effects of π and ensures the estimate remains bounded within the range of the loss function. This is a valuable theoretical improvement.

The experimental results are strong: Norm-POEM consistently outperforms POEM on all datasets and approaches CRF performance using only bandit feedback. The authors demonstrate that Norm-POEM avoids the propensity overfitting issue by showing that Ŝ(ĥ) (the sum of propensity weights) is close to 1 for Norm-POEM but severely biased for POEM. The self-normalized estimator's equivariance to loss translation is also verified experimentally.

The paper is well-written and clearly organized, with excellent experimental validation. The main weakness is the technical error in the interpretation of Equation (2), which invalidates the theoretical foundation for their key claim about "propensity overfitting." I recommend the authors revise their commentary to correct this misunderstanding, particularly regarding the expectation interpretation and the nature of the "anomaly."

The paper's strengths include:
- Identification of a significant flaw in a widely used estimator
- Theoretical justification for the proposed solution
- Strong empirical results demonstrating improved performance
- Clear connection to previous work (POEM, CRM principle)

The significance of the work is high, as BLBF is an important problem for real-world applications like ad placement and recommendation systems. The proposed Norm-POEM algorithm is a direct and practical improvement over existing methods.

In terms of quality, the paper is technically sound in its experimental evaluation, though the theoretical claims need revision. The clarity is excellent, with well-structured arguments and good organization. The originality is strong, as the propensity overfitting problem has not been previously identified in the BLBF literature.

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to correct the technical error in the interpretation of Equation (2) and to revise the discussion of the "anomaly" and Example 1. With these corrections, the paper will be a valuable contribution to the field of counterfactual learning. The proposed solution is simple yet effective and has clear practical implications for real-world BLBF applications.
