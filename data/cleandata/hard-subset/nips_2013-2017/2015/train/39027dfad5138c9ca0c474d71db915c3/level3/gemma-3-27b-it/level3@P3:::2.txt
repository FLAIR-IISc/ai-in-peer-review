This paper addresses the problem of propensity overfitting in batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF), a crucial area for training online systems from historical data. The authors identify a fundamental flaw in the widely-used counterfactual risk estimator (Equation 2), arguing its loss is not the expected quantity and its invariance properties are misrepresented. They propose a self-normalized estimator (Equation 7) to mitigate this issue, demonstrating improved performance with their Norm-POEM algorithm on multi-label classification tasks, achieving results competitive with full-information CRFs.

The paper builds directly upon previous work in BLBF, particularly POEM (Swaminathan & Joachims, ICML 2015), and connects to related fields like causal inference, domain adaptation, and off-policy evaluation. The motivation is well-articulated – the need for effective learning algorithms that can leverage the wealth of data available in historical logs without requiring interactive control. The connection to reinforcement learning through counterfactual estimation is also clearly established. The authors appropriately cite key papers in these areas (Bottou et al., Cortes & Mansour, Sutton & Barto).

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty & Insight:** The core finding regarding the misinterpretation of the conventional estimator and the resulting propensity overfitting is a significant contribution. Identifying this anomaly and providing a clear explanation is valuable.
* **Theoretical Justification:** The paper provides a theoretical foundation for the proposed self-normalized estimator, demonstrating its improved properties and consistency (Theorem 2).
* **Empirical Validation:** The experimental results consistently show Norm-POEM outperforms POEM across multiple datasets. Achieving performance close to full-information CRFs is a strong indication of the method's effectiveness. The ablation study (Table 3) further supports the value of variance regularization.
* **Clarity of Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the concepts and mathematical derivations. The connection between the theoretical findings and the practical algorithm (Norm-POEM) is well established.
* **Availability of Code:** Providing code (http://www.cs.cornell.edu/∼adith/POEM) enhances reproducibility and encourages further research.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Misleading Commentary around Equation (2):** The central claim of the paper – that the commentary and notation surrounding Equation (2) are misleading – is critical. However, the initial presentation of this issue lacks sufficient clarity. The phrasing "the loss in (2) is not the expected quantity" is vague and requires more precise explanation. While the authors later clarify the issue of non-equivariance under translation, the initial statement is confusing.
* **Example 1's Dependence on Incorrect Premise:** The authors acknowledge Example 1 is based on the flawed understanding of Equation (2). While it serves to illustrate the potential for overfitting, its validity is undermined by the initial incorrect premise, weakening its impact as a demonstrative example.
* **Sensitivity to π:** The sensitivity of the original estimator to the denominator π (Equation 2) is a valid concern, especially when a sample has never been seen in the prior. However, the discussion could benefit from a more concrete discussion of strategies for handling zero values of π.
* **Computational Cost Discussion:** While the paper states Norm-POEM’s runtime is “surprisingly faster,” a more detailed analysis comparing the computational complexity of Norm-POEM and POEM would be helpful. Specifically, discussing the impact of calculating the variance estimate (Equation 8) on overall runtime would add clarity.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper identifies and addresses a fundamental problem in a growing area of machine learning (BLBF).
* The proposed solution (self-normalized estimator) is theoretically sound and empirically effective.
* The experimental results are compelling and demonstrate a clear improvement over existing methods.
* The paper is well-written and clearly explains the technical details.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The initial presentation of the core issue surrounding Equation (2) is unclear and potentially misleading.
* Example 1 relies on a flawed premise, reducing its illustrative power.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a convincing and valuable contribution to the field of batch learning from logged bandit feedback. The identified issue of propensity overfitting is significant, and the proposed self-normalized estimator provides a promising solution. While the initial explanation of the problem with Equation (2) is somewhat unclear, the subsequent analysis and experimental results strongly support the authors’ claims.

**Recommendation:**

I recommend acceptance, contingent upon the authors revising their commentary around Equation (2) to provide a clearer and more precise explanation of the misinterpretation and the resulting issues. Specifically, they should explicitly state *why* the estimator is not equivariant and *how* this leads to the described overfitting behavior. The authors should also acknowledge the reliance of Example 1 on the incorrect premise and perhaps provide an alternative, independent illustration of propensity overfitting. Addressing these points will further strengthen an already strong paper.
