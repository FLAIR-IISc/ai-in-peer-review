

# Review of "The Self-Normalized Estimator for Counterfactual Learning"

This paper addresses an important issue in batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF), where the goal is to learn a new policy from historical data where only the actions taken by a logging policy are observed. The authors identify a previously overlooked problem they term "propensity overfitting" that occurs when using the conventional unbiased counterfactual risk estimator in empirical risk minimization. They propose a self-normalized risk estimator that avoids this problem and derive a new algorithm called Norm-POEM. This work builds on the CRM principle [1] and POEM [1], but identifies a critical flaw in the conventional estimator that has implications for the generalization performance of BLBF algorithms.

The paper makes several notable contributions. First, it clearly identifies the propensity overfitting problem, where the conventional estimator can produce risk estimates that are arbitrarily lower than the true risk, especially when the hypothesis space is large relative to the data. The authors provide a compelling theoretical analysis showing that their self-normalized estimator resolves all the anomalies of the conventional estimator, including the lack of equivariance and unbounded risk estimates. The empirical results on multi-label classification datasets demonstrate that Norm-POEM consistently outperforms the conventional POEM approach, with the proposed estimator avoiding the propensity overfitting issue as evidenced by the stability of Ŝ(ĥ) around its expected value of 1.

The paper is well-written and clearly organized, with a strong connection between theory and empirical results. The authors provide code for reproducibility, which is greatly appreciated. The theoretical arguments are sound, and the empirical evaluation is thorough, with consistent improvements across multiple datasets.

Regarding the four key criteria:

**Quality**: The paper presents a technically sound solution to a previously overlooked problem. The theoretical analysis is rigorous, with clear proofs of the estimator's properties. The empirical results support the theoretical claims, showing consistent improvements in generalization performance. The authors are careful to acknowledge the limitations of their approach, noting that variance regularization remains beneficial even with the self-normalized estimator.

**Clarity**: The paper is exceptionally well-written, with clear explanations of the problem and the proposed solution. The example in Section 4 effectively illustrates the propensity overfitting problem, though the reviewer found line 199 (regarding the behavior when n is small relative to k) somewhat unclear. The authors would benefit from a more detailed explanation of why the conventional estimator exhibits the observed behavior in the small n case.

**Originality**: The identification of propensity overfitting as a distinct problem from the standard importance weight variance issue represents a significant conceptual advance. While the self-normalized estimator has appeared in other contexts, the authors' application to BLBF and their identification of the specific problem it solves is novel. The paper clearly differentiates its contribution from prior work on counterfactual estimation.

**Significance**: The problem identified has important practical implications for BLBF algorithms used in real-world applications like ad placement and recommendation systems. The consistent empirical improvements across multiple datasets suggest this is a meaningful advance. The paper's approach provides a practical solution to a previously unrecognized issue that could improve the reliability of counterfactual learning methods.

The reviewer has a few concerns that should be addressed. First, the claim that prior BLBF works used an unbiased estimator is questionable, as many works (including POEM) use clipped importance weights, which introduces bias. The authors should clarify this distinction. Second, the relationship between large importance weight variance and propensity overfitting could be explained more clearly. The example in Section 4 is insightful but would benefit from additional explanation of why the conventional estimator behaves as it does when n is small relative to k.

Despite these concerns, the paper presents a valuable contribution that addresses a previously overlooked issue in BLBF. The proposed solution has both strong theoretical guarantees and practical empirical benefits. The authors have clearly demonstrated that the self-normalized estimator resolves the propensity overfitting problem and leads to improved generalization performance.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Identifies a previously overlooked problem with practical implications
- Provides a theoretically sound solution with clear empirical validation
- Well-written and clearly organized
- Includes code for reproducibility
- Consistent improvements across multiple datasets

Arguments against acceptance:
- Some claims about prior work could be clarified (e.g., the use of unbiased estimators)
- The connection between importance weight variance and propensity overfitting needs more explanation
- The example in Section 4 could be made clearer

Overall, this paper makes a significant contribution to the field of counterfactual learning. The identified problem is important, the proposed solution is elegant and effective, and the empirical results are convincing. The paper meets all the criteria for a high-quality NIPS submission. The reviewer recommends acceptance, with the understanding that the authors should address the minor clarifications requested.
