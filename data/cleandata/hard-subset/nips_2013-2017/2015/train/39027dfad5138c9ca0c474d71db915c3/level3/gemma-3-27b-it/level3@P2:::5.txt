This paper addresses a critical problem in batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF): propensity overfitting, which can lead to severely biased risk estimates when using the conventional counterfactual risk estimator. The authors convincingly demonstrate that this estimator, prevalent in prior work (e.g., Swaminathan & Joachims, 2015), suffers from anomalies that existing regularization techniques only partially address. They propose a self-normalized estimator as a solution, leading to a new learning algorithm, Normalized Policy Optimizer for Exponential Models (Norm-POEM). Extensive empirical evaluation on multi-label classification problems consistently shows Norm-POEM outperforms the original POEM algorithm.

The paper builds upon the existing body of work in BLBF, directly addressing a limitation of the approach outlined in Swaminathan & Joachims (2015). The connection to related areas like causal inference (Rosenbaum & Rubin, 1983), domain adaptation (Cortes et al., 2008), and off-policy evaluation (Dudík et al., 2011) is well-established. The authors appropriately position their work within this broader context. They also draw connections to importance sampling techniques and control variates (Hesterberg, 2005; Rubinstein & Kroese, 2008), offering a solid theoretical foundation for their proposed estimator. The reference to related work is thorough and demonstrates the authors’ understanding of the field.

**Strengths:**

* **Technical Soundness:** The paper presents a well-defined problem with a clear theoretical justification for the proposed solution. The derivation of the self-normalized estimator is sound, and the explanations of propensity overfitting are insightful. The theoretical guarantees regarding consistency (Theorem 2) further bolster the technical credibility.
* **Originality:** The identification of propensity overfitting as a distinct problem within BLBF is a novel contribution. The self-normalized estimator provides a genuinely new approach to address this issue, and Norm-POEM represents a significant extension of the POEM algorithm.
* **Significance:**  The demonstrated improvements over POEM are substantial and practically relevant. The potential for wider adoption due to the improved computational efficiency is a major positive. This work meaningfully advances the state-of-the-art in BLBF and provides a more robust and reliable method for learning from historical data.  The paper successfully demonstrates that their method is not just theoretically sound but delivers significant practical benefits.
* **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The introduction clearly motivates the problem and outlines the contributions. The mathematical notation is reasonably accessible, and the explanations of key concepts are understandable. The inclusion of illustrative examples (e.g., Example 1) is particularly helpful.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Variance Estimation Complexity:** While the paper acknowledges the increased computational cost of the variance estimation in Norm-POEM, a more detailed discussion of this trade-off would be beneficial. A deeper exploration of approximation techniques or alternative variance estimators could strengthen the work.
* **Limited Exploration of Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** While the authors mention hyperparameter calibration, a more systematic analysis of the sensitivity of performance to the choice of regularization parameters (λ and M) would be valuable.
* **Dataset Scope:** The experiments are limited to a relatively small set of datasets from the LibSVM repository. While these datasets are well-established, expanding the evaluation to more diverse and complex scenarios could provide further confidence in the generalizability of the approach.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper addresses a significant and previously unrecognised problem in BLBF.
* The proposed solution is theoretically sound and empirically validated.
* The results demonstrate a clear and substantial improvement over existing methods.
* The writing is clear and the presentation is well-organized.
* The algorithm shows promise for practical applications.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The computational cost of variance estimation is a potential limitation.
* The experimental evaluation could be broadened to include more diverse datasets.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of batch learning from logged bandit feedback. The authors have identified a critical limitation of existing methods and proposed a novel and effective solution. The theoretical analysis is sound, the empirical results are compelling, and the writing is clear. While there are minor areas for improvement, the overall quality of the work is excellent and warrants acceptance. I recommend accepting this paper for NIPS 2015.
