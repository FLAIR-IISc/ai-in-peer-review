This paper addresses the problem of batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF), a crucial setting for improving interactive systems using historical data. Building upon the Counterfactual Risk Minimization (CRM) principle from Swaminathan & Joachims (2015) [1], the authors identify a critical flaw in the conventional unbiased inverse propensity score (IPS) estimator. They term this issue "Propensity Overfitting," where a model can achieve an artificially low empirical risk by manipulating the propensity weights, rather than by fitting the underlying loss function. This can lead to degenerate solutions, a problem clearly and effectively illustrated in the paper's motivating example.

To solve this, the authors propose replacing the standard IPS estimator with a self-normalized risk estimator. This estimator, derived using a multiplicative control variate, is a ratio of the standard estimator and the sum of the importance weights. While this introduces bias, the authors show it has several desirable properties: it is equivariant to translations in the loss function, its value is always bounded within the range of the observed losses, and it is strongly consistent. This new estimator is then integrated into the CRM framework to create a new learning algorithm, Norm-POEM. Experiments on several multi-label classification tasks show that Norm-POEM consistently and substantially outperforms the original POEM algorithm, which uses the standard unbiased estimator.

**Strengths:**

*   **Problem Identification and Motivation (Quality):** The paper does an excellent job of identifying, naming, and illustrating the "Propensity Overfitting" problem. The example in Section 4 is exceptionally clear and provides strong motivation for the need for an alternative estimator. This is a subtle but important issue in counterfactual learning, and the authors' clear exposition is a significant contribution.
*   **Principled Solution (Quality):** The proposed solution is not an ad-hoc fix but is well-grounded in the statistical literature on control variates and ratio estimators. The paper clearly articulates the theoretical benefits of the self-normalized estimator (equivariance, bounded risk, consistency), directly linking them to the weaknesses of the standard estimator.
*   **Clarity and Presentation:** The paper is very well-written and organized. The logical flow from identifying the problem to proposing and evaluating the solution is easy to follow. The connection to prior work is clearly stated, and the technical details are presented with sufficient clarity for an expert to understand the method.
*   **Strong Empirical Results:** The experiments, while limited in scope, provide compelling evidence for the proposed method's effectiveness. The results in Table 1 show a consistent and often large performance improvement for Norm-POEM over POEM. The analysis in Table 2 is particularly strong, as it directly verifies the theoretical claims: Norm-POEM produces estimators where the sum of weights `Ŝ(h)` is stable and close to 1, and its performance is robust to loss-shifting, unlike the baseline which fails catastrophically in some cases.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

*   **Originality:** The primary weakness is that the work is an incremental improvement upon a previous paper [1]. The core learning principle (CRM), the hypothesis space, and the experimental framework are all inherited directly from [1]. The main novelty is the application of the self-normalized estimator—a known technique in the importance sampling literature—to the CRM framework. While the application is novel and the results are significant, this makes the contribution feel more like a follow-up or an important correction to a prior method rather than a fundamentally new approach.
*   **Experimental Scope and Significance:** The empirical evaluation, while internally convincing, could be stronger.
    *   **Lack of Variance Reporting:** The results in Table 1 are averaged over 10 runs, but no standard deviations or measures of variance are reported. Given the high variance inherent in counterfactual estimators, including this information is crucial for assessing the statistical significance of the reported gains. Please add error bars or standard deviations to the tables and plots.
    *   **Limited Datasets:** The experiments are conducted on the same set of multi-label classification datasets as the original POEM paper [1]. While this allows for a direct comparison, it limits the assessment of the method's general applicability. The significance of the work would be substantially strengthened by demonstrating its effectiveness on a wider range of problems, particularly on real-world logged bandit datasets from applications like advertising, search, or recommendation, where propensity overfitting is likely to be a real concern.
*   **Analysis of Optimization:** The paper notes that Norm-POEM surprisingly converges faster than POEM, despite a more complex per-iteration objective, because it finds solutions with smaller weight norms. This is an interesting and important practical finding. However, the analysis of this phenomenon is brief. A deeper discussion of the optimization landscape of the two objectives would be a valuable addition. Why does the self-normalized objective lead to better-behaved and more easily discoverable solutions? A brief analysis or even visualization of the objective function or gradient norms during training could provide valuable insight.

In summary, this is a high-quality, well-written paper that identifies an important problem in counterfactual learning and proposes a simple, principled, and effective solution. The main limitations are its incremental nature and the scope of the experimental validation. Despite these, the clear identification of the propensity overfitting problem and the demonstration of a robust fix represent a valuable contribution to the field of learning from logged bandit feedback.
