This paper addresses the important problem of counterfactual risk estimation in batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF), identifying and tackling a novel issue termed "propensity overfitting." The core idea – that simply minimizing the empirical risk with importance weights can lead to overfitting the propensity scores themselves, rather than the true loss – is insightful and well-motivated. While my time for this additional review was limited, and I may have missed subtleties, the approach presented seems sensible and potentially valuable.

The paper effectively contextualizes the problem within the broader landscape of causal inference, domain adaptation, and off-policy evaluation, referencing relevant work such as propensity score matching and importance sampling. The authors correctly point out the limitations of existing BLBF algorithms relying on unbiased counterfactual risk estimators, demonstrating that these estimators can exhibit anomalies leading to degeneracy and poor performance, particularly with complex hypothesis spaces. The proposed self-normalized estimator offers a promising solution, and the derivation of the Norm-POEM algorithm builds logically upon this foundation.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The identification of "propensity overfitting" as a distinct phenomenon is a significant contribution. While importance sampling is well-studied, recognizing this specific failure mode in the context of BLBF is novel.
*   **Theoretical grounding:** The paper provides a clear theoretical justification for the problem and the proposed solution. The explanation of why the conventional estimator can be problematic, and how the self-normalized estimator mitigates these issues, is well-presented.
*   **Practical algorithm:** Norm-POEM is a concrete algorithm built upon the theoretical foundations, and the empirical evaluation demonstrates its effectiveness.
*   **Implementation availability:** Providing code is a major plus, facilitating reproducibility and wider adoption of the proposed method.
*   **Clear writing:** The paper is generally well-written and the concepts are explained in a reasonably accessible manner.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Relationship between weight variance and propensity overfitting:** While the paper describes both phenomena, the exact *relationship* between large importance weight variance and propensity overfitting isn't entirely clear. Is high variance a *cause* of propensity overfitting, a *symptom*, or merely a correlated observation? A more explicit discussion of this link would strengthen the paper.
*   **Line 199 clarification needed:** The behavior of  `R̂(h^)` when *n* is small relative to *k* (number of possible labels) is unclear.  In this scenario, it seems the overfitted hypothesis could achieve extremely low empirical risk without necessarily reflecting true performance. Expanding on this point with a more intuitive explanation would be helpful.
*   **Unbiased counterfactual risk estimator claim:** The claim that prior BLBF works used an *unbiased* counterfactual risk estimator should be carefully re-examined. The use of clipping (as acknowledged in the related work section) explicitly introduces bias. The paper should either retract this claim or qualify it more accurately.
*   **Limited exploration of variance regularization:** While the paper notes that variance regularization is still helpful even with the self-normalized estimator, the exploration of this aspect seems somewhat limited. A more detailed analysis of the interplay between the self-normalization and variance regularization could provide further insights.



**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The paper addresses a significant problem in BLBF with a novel and theoretically sound approach.
*   The proposed self-normalized estimator appears to be a substantial improvement over existing methods, as evidenced by the empirical results.
*   The code availability promotes reproducibility and wider adoption.
*   The paper is well-written and clearly presents its ideas.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The relationship between weight variance and propensity overfitting could be more clearly articulated.
*   The clarification regarding line 199 is needed.
*   The claim about prior work using unbiased estimators needs revision.




**Overall Recommendation:**

Despite not having been able to verify all technical details due to time constraints, I recommend **acceptance** of this paper. The core idea is compelling, the proposed solution appears promising, and the empirical results are encouraging. Addressing the minor weaknesses outlined above would further strengthen the contribution, but the paper is currently of sufficient quality to warrant inclusion in NIPS. The identification of propensity overfitting is a valuable insight, and the Norm-POEM algorithm offers a practical and effective approach to mitigating this issue.
