This paper addresses the problem of batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF), a crucial setting for improving interactive systems using historical data. The authors build upon the Counterfactual Risk Minimization (CRM) principle, which is a standard approach for this problem. The paper's central contribution is the identification of a critical and previously overlooked failure mode of the conventional unbiased inverse propensity score (IPS) estimator used within CRM, which the authors term "propensity overfitting". They demonstrate, with a clear and compelling example, that models with high capacity can achieve arbitrarily low estimated risk not by fitting the true losses, but by manipulating the propensity weights, leading to poor generalization.

To solve this problem, the authors propose replacing the conventional unbiased estimator with a self-normalized importance sampling estimator. This estimator is derived in a principled way using multiplicative control variates and has several desirable properties: it is equivariant to translations in the loss function and its value is always bounded by the range of the observed losses, directly preventing the pathologies of the unbiased estimator. This new estimator is integrated into the CRM framework to create a new learning algorithm, the Normalized Policy Optimizer for Exponential Models (Norm-POEM), which is an extension of the prior POEM algorithm [1]. The paper provides a thorough empirical evaluation on several multi-label classification datasets, showing that Norm-POEM consistently and significantly outperforms the original POEM in terms of generalization, robustness, and even computational efficiency.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Quality:** The paper is of high technical quality. The identification of the propensity overfitting problem is a sharp and important insight. The proposed solution is theoretically well-grounded, drawing on established principles of control variates and ratio estimators from the statistics literature. Claims are rigorously supported by both theoretical arguments (e.g., consistency of the estimator) and a comprehensive set of experiments. The experimental methodology is sound, following the standard and effective "Supervised -> Bandit" conversion to allow for controlled and repeatable comparisons. The work is a complete and meaningful extension of previous research, presenting a clear problem and a well-validated solution.

**Clarity:** The paper is very well-written, clearly organized, and easy to follow. Section 4, which introduces the propensity overfitting problem, is particularly effective. It uses a simple, intuitive example to perfectly illustrate a subtle but severe flaw in the standard estimator, making the motivation for the rest of the paper exceptionally clear. The logical flow from the problem statement to the proposed solution and the resulting algorithm is seamless.

**Originality:** The contributions are highly original. While the self-normalized estimator is not new in itself, its application as a direct remedy for a specific type of *overfitting during learning* in the BLBF context is a novel and insightful contribution. The key original insight is the framing of the standard estimator's pathologies as "propensity overfitting," a new concept that neatly captures how model capacity can be misused in the counterfactual setting. This work is a significant and non-obvious extension of previous work like POEM [1], offering a substantially new and improved formulation.

**Significance:** The results presented are significant and likely to have a strong impact. The paper addresses a fundamental problem in a popular framework for learning from logged data. The proposed Norm-POEM algorithm is not just a marginal improvement; it provides substantial gains in accuracy and robustness over its state-of-the-art predecessor. Perhaps most compellingly, the experiments show that Norm-POEM can converge *faster* than POEM, despite a more complex objective function per iteration. This combination of improved accuracy, robustness, and efficiency makes the proposed method highly attractive for practitioners and is likely to lead to widespread use in applications like computational advertising and recommendation systems. The paper opens up interesting avenues for further research into robust counterfactual learning.

### Arguments for Acceptance:
*   **Pro:** The paper identifies and solves a critical, previously unaddressed flaw—propensity overfitting—in the widely used CRM framework for learning from bandit feedback.
*   **Pro:** The proposed algorithm, Norm-POEM, demonstrates significant and consistent empirical improvements over its state-of-the-art predecessor (POEM) in accuracy, robustness, and, surprisingly, training speed.
*   **Pro:** The contributions are original, the technical quality is high, and the potential impact on both research and practice is substantial.
*   **Pro:** The paper is exceptionally clear and presents a self-contained, complete piece of research.

### Arguments against Acceptance:
*   The experimental evaluation is performed on benchmark datasets converted to a bandit setting, rather than on real-world industrial logs. However, this is a very minor point, as this simulation paradigm is a well-established and necessary methodology for controlled and reproducible scientific comparison in this area.

Overall, this is an excellent paper that makes a strong and important contribution to the field of counterfactual learning. It is well-executed, clearly communicated, and the results are significant.
