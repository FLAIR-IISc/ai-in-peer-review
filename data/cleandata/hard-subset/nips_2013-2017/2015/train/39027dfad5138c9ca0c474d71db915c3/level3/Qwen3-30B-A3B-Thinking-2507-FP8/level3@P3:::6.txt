

# Review of "The Self-Normalized Estimator for Counterfactual Learning"

This paper addresses a critical gap in batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF), a setting where historical logs are used to train systems without interactive control (e.g., ad placement, recommendation systems). The authors identify a previously overlooked failure mode in conventional counterfactual risk minimization (CRM) approaches: propensity overfitting. This occurs when hypotheses exploit the importance weights in the risk estimator, leading to severely biased risk estimates. The paper proposes a self-normalized risk estimator that resolves this issue by using multiplicative control variates, which maintains consistency while controlling variance.

The work builds on Swaminathan and Joachims' 2013 NIPS paper on CRM, extending it to address a critical theoretical vulnerability in the risk estimator. It connects to broader literature on importance sampling (e.g., Hesterberg's work on self-normalized estimators), causal inference (e.g., propensity score matching), and off-policy evaluation in reinforcement learning. The paper's key contribution is the identification of propensity overfitting—a form of overfitting distinct from conventional loss overfitting—where hypotheses optimize not for low loss but for extreme importance weights.

The paper's theoretical results are strong: Theorem 2 proves the self-normalized estimator is strongly consistent and bounded within the loss range, unlike the conventional estimator. The empirical evaluation demonstrates consistent improvements across multiple multi-label classification datasets (Scene, Yeast, TMC, LYRL), with Norm-POEM (the proposed learning algorithm) outperforming the baseline POEM in all cases. The experiments also provide compelling evidence that propensity overfitting is indeed the mechanism causing POEM's failure, as shown by the deviation of Ŝ(ĥ) from 1 in POEM but not in Norm-POEM.

**Quality**: The paper is technically sound with well-supported theoretical and empirical results. The propensity overfitting counterexample is particularly compelling, and the theoretical analysis of the self-normalized estimator is rigorous. The authors carefully address limitations (e.g., the need for h0 to have full support over Y) and provide a thorough empirical evaluation. The only minor weakness is that the paper doesn't explore cases where h0 has limited support, though this is acknowledged as a requirement for their approach.

**Clarity**: The paper is exceptionally well-organized and clearly written. The problem is motivated effectively, the counterexample is easy to follow, and the connection between theory and experiments is clear. The notation is consistent, and the mathematical exposition is accessible to the intended audience. The discussion of how the self-normalized estimator resolves the equivariance issue (unlike the conventional estimator) is particularly well-explained.

**Originality**: The identification of propensity overfitting is novel and addresses a previously unrecognized failure mode in BLBF. The self-normalized estimator represents a clever application of multiplicative control variates to this problem, distinct from prior approaches like clipped importance weighting or additive control variates. The paper clearly differentiates its contribution from related work in Section 2 and the discussion of overfitting types.

**Significance**: The problem addressed has substantial practical importance for real-world BLBF applications. The proposed solution is immediately applicable, requires minimal changes to existing CRM frameworks, and consistently improves performance across multiple datasets. The paper makes a demonstrable advance in the state of the art by resolving a critical vulnerability in existing approaches.

**Arguments for acceptance**:
- Solves a previously overlooked but critical problem (propensity overfitting) in BLBF
- Theoretical analysis proves consistency and variance control
- Empirical results show consistent, significant improvements over state-of-the-art
- Well-motivated and clearly written with strong connections to prior work
- Practical implications for real-world systems using historical logs

**Arguments against acceptance**:
- The problem might be considered niche compared to broader NIPS topics
- The computational cost of the self-normalized estimator is slightly higher (though the paper shows it's still efficient)
- The limitation that h0 must have full support over Y could restrict applicability in some scenarios

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the BLBF literature. The identification of propensity overfitting is a substantial theoretical insight, and the proposed solution is both theoretically sound and empirically effective. The paper addresses a real problem with practical implications, and the results are convincing across multiple datasets. While the problem might not be as broadly appealing as some other NIPS topics, it represents a clear, important advance in a well-established area. I recommend acceptance. The reviewer is not fully certain due to the niche nature of the problem, but the technical quality and empirical results are compelling enough to support acceptance.
