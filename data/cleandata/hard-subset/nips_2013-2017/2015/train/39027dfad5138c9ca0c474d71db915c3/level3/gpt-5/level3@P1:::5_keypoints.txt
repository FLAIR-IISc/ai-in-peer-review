- Identifies "propensity overfitting" as a failure mode of IPS in CRM for batch learning from logged bandit feedback.  
- Proposes using a self-normalized (ratio) importance sampling estimator to replace standard IPS.  
- Instantiates Norm-POEM, a CRM algorithm for structured prediction with exponential-family models using the self-normalized estimator.  
- Core theoretical rationale: self-normalized estimator is bounded and equivariant to loss translations, preventing IPS degeneracy under finite samples.  
- Provides consistency results for the self-normalized estimator and an approximate variance estimate for use in CRM.  
- Empirically, Norm-POEM consistently outperforms POEM on supervised-to-bandit multi-label benchmarks.  
- Empirical evidence shows Norm-POEM avoids propensity overfitting (Ŝ(h) ≈ 1) and often converges faster in practice.  
- Builds on CRM/POEM literature and connects to classical importance sampling, control variates, and off-policy evaluation work.  
- Novelty: multiplicative control variates via self-normalization inside the learning objective to diagnose/mitigate a new overfitting mode.  
- Strength: clear identification and illustrative example of propensity overfitting with a simple Ŝ(h) diagnostic.  
- Strength: self-normalized estimator is a principled fix with boundedness, equivariance, and established consistency.  
- Strength: Norm-POEM integrates cleanly as a drop-in replacement with tractable gradients and a practical variance surrogate.  
- Strength: solid empirical gains across datasets and regimes, including robustness to loss translations and better calibration.  
- Strength: practical relevance and available code increase likelihood of adoption.  
- Weakness: theory is largely asymptotic; lacks finite-sample generalization bounds for CRM with the ratio estimator.  
- Weakness: experiments are limited to supervised-to-bandit simulations in multi-label classification, lacking real logging scenarios.  
- Weakness: missing comparisons to doubly robust estimators and other variance-reduction baselines (e.g., clipped IPS, defensive mixtures).  
- Weakness: limited sensitivity analysis for clipping parameter M and variance regularization λ; robustness curves not reported.  
- Weakness: assumption of full support in logging is central and behavior under partial support/deterministic logging is under-discussed.  
- Weakness: variance estimator relies on Normal approximations without empirical calibration evidence.  
- Weakness: could improve clarity with pseudocode, computational complexity summary, and wall-clock runtime reporting.  
- Quality: technically sound with solid theoretical support, but finite-sample CRM guarantees for ratio estimators are missing.  
- Clarity: generally well written; Section 4 example and Ŝ(h) diagnostic are pedagogically strong.  
- Originality: applying self-normalized importance sampling within CRM to address propensity overfitting is novel in BLBF.  
- Significance: high practical impact — simple, robust estimator likely to influence counterfactual learning practice.  
- Acceptance arguments: identifies important failure mode, offers principled and effective fix with strong empirical support and code availability.  
- Rejection arguments: lacks finite-sample bounds, limited baselines and experimental scope, and uncalibrated variance approximations.  
- Key questions for authors: provide finite-sample risk bounds or calibration, sensitivity guidance for M and λ, comparisons to DR/clipped IPS, behavior under partial support, and wall-clock runtimes.
