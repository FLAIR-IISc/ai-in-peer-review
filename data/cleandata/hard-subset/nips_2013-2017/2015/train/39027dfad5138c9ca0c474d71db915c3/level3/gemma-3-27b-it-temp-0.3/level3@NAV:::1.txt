## Review of "The Self-Normalized Estimator for Counterfactual Learning"

This paper addresses a critical issue in batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF): propensity overfitting. While existing work focuses on the variance of importance weights in counterfactual risk estimation, the authors highlight a related, and arguably more fundamental, problem – insufficient exploration leading to unreliable risk estimates when the hypothesis space is complex. The core idea, employing a self-normalized estimator as a replacement for the conventional unbiased estimator, is sensible and well-motivated. The authors convincingly argue that this approach mitigates propensity overfitting by providing a more stable and equivariant risk estimate. 

The paper is well-structured, clearly outlining the problem, proposed solution, and experimental validation. The connection to related work in causal inference, domain adaptation, and reinforcement learning is appropriately established. The theoretical justification, while not deeply scrutinized due to time constraints, appears sound, and the empirical results consistently demonstrate the superiority of Norm-POEM over the baseline POEM algorithm across several datasets. The provision of code is highly appreciated and facilitates reproducibility.

However, the relationship between large weight variance and propensity overfitting isn’t fully elucidated. While the authors explain *that* they are different, a more intuitive explanation of *how* they relate would strengthen the paper. Specifically, it's unclear if large variance is a *symptom* of propensity overfitting, or if they are distinct phenomena that happen to co-occur. 

I also found line 199 somewhat unclear. If *n* (sample size) is small relative to *k* (number of possible outputs), the behavior of  *R̂(ĥ)* seems unpredictable – the authors should elaborate on this scenario. Furthermore, the claim that prior BLBF works used an *unbiased* counterfactual risk estimator feels potentially inaccurate. Many implementations employ clipping, which introduces bias, and this should be acknowledged.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Addresses a significant and previously underappreciated problem in BLBF.
*   Proposes a sensible and theoretically motivated solution.
*   Empirically demonstrates consistent performance improvements.
*   Provides code for reproducibility.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   The relationship between weight variance and propensity overfitting could be clarified.
*   Technical details were not fully verified due to time constraints.
*   Potential inaccuracy regarding the unbiasedness of prior estimators.



Despite these minor concerns, and acknowledging my limited time for a thorough review, I recommend acceptance. The paper presents a valuable contribution to the field and addresses a practical issue with a well-reasoned and empirically validated solution.
