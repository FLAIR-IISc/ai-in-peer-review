This is an excellent paper that makes a significant contribution to the important problem of batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF). It clearly identifies a fundamental flaw in the standard counterfactual risk estimator, proposes a well-motivated and effective solution, and demonstrates its superiority through strong empirical results.

### Summary of the Paper

This paper addresses the problem of learning from logged bandit feedback, a setting crucial for improving interactive systems like search engines and recommender systems using historical data. The standard approach, Counterfactual Risk Minimization (CRM), relies on an unbiased inverse propensity score (IPS) estimator to evaluate the risk of new policies. The central contribution of this work is the identification and clear articulation of a severe failure mode of this standard estimator, which the authors term "Propensity Overfitting". They show that with a sufficiently complex hypothesis space, the IPS estimator can be minimized not by fitting the true losses, but by manipulating the propensity weights, leading to arbitrarily poor risk estimates and degenerate solutions.

To solve this problem, the authors propose replacing the standard IPS estimator with a self-normalized estimator. This alternative estimator, derived using a multiplicative control variate, is a ratio of the IPS estimate of the risk and the IPS estimate of the sum of weights. The authors show that this self-normalized estimator is equivariant to translations in the loss function, its value is always bounded by the range of the observed losses, and it is strongly consistent. These properties provably prevent the propensity overfitting problem.

Building on this improved estimator, the paper introduces a new learning algorithm, Normalized Policy Optimizer for Exponential Models (Norm-POEM), which integrates the self-normalized estimator into the CRM framework for structured output prediction. An extensive empirical evaluation on several multi-label classification tasks demonstrates that Norm-POEM consistently and substantially outperforms the previous state-of-the-art (POEM), which uses the standard estimator. The results confirm that Norm-POEM is more robust, learns better policies, and even converges faster in practice.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Quality:** The paper is of high technical quality. The analysis of propensity overfitting is insightful, and the illustrative example in Section 4 is very effective at building intuition for this critical problem. The proposed solution is principled, drawing on established variance reduction techniques from the Monte Carlo literature (control variates). The claims are well-supported by both theoretical arguments (equivariance, consistency of the estimator) and a thorough empirical evaluation. The experimental methodology is sound, using the "Supervised -> Bandit" conversion to allow for controlled and reproducible comparisons.

**Clarity:** The paper is very well-written and organized. The motivation is compelling, and the core idea of propensity overfitting is explained with exceptional clarity. The progression from identifying the problem to proposing a solution and then validating it empirically is logical and easy to follow. The paper provides sufficient detail for an expert to understand the contributions.

**Originality:** This is a key strength of the paper. While the self-normalized estimator is a known technique, the identification of the propensity overfitting problem in the BLBF context is a novel and important conceptual contribution. The application of this estimator to solve this specific problem is a creative and effective synthesis of ideas. This work is an insightful follow-up to previous work on CRM (e.g., [1]), significantly advancing the understanding of the subtleties of counterfactual learning.

**Significance:** The paper's contribution is highly significant. BLBF is a problem of immense practical importance, and this work addresses a fundamental weakness in the standard approach. The demonstrated failure of the unbiased IPS estimator should be a serious consideration for any researcher or practitioner in this area. Norm-POEM provides a practical, easy-to-implement, and demonstrably superior alternative to existing methods. The substantial performance improvements are compelling and suggest that these ideas are likely to be widely adopted and built upon.

### Arguments for Acceptance

*   Identifies and provides a clear analysis of a fundamental and previously overlooked problem (propensity overfitting) in a widely used estimator for counterfactual learning.
*   Proposes a simple, principled, and effective solution based on the self-normalized estimator.
*   The resulting algorithm, Norm-POEM, shows substantial and consistent empirical improvements over the prior state-of-the-art.
*   The work addresses a highly important and practical problem in machine learning.

### Arguments against Acceptance

The paper is very strong, and I do not have any major arguments against its acceptance. The points below are minor suggestions for improvement.

### Detailed Comments and Suggestions for Improvement

The authors have done an excellent job. I have only a few minor suggestions for clarification and correction that could further strengthen the manuscript:

1.  **Loss Function Formulation and Assumptions:** The CRM principle, as justified by Theorem 1, is presented for losses `δ(·, ·) ∈ [-1, 0]`. However, the experiments use Hamming loss, which is non-negative. Table 2 also explicitly discusses behavior for "δ > 0". Could you clarify whether the theoretical justification for the CRM objective (Eq. 3) still holds when the loss is not in the [-1, 0] range, or if the objective is simply used as a well-performing heuristic in this case?
2.  **Stochastic vs. Deterministic Loss:** On line 89, you mention that "the loss is the (noisy) realization of a stochastic random variable" but then immediately simplify by setting `δ(x, y) = E[δ | x, y]`. To improve the formalism, it might be clearer to explicitly carry the expectation over this noise in the definition of the true risk in Equation (1), e.g., `R(h) = E_x E_{y∼h(x)} [E[δ | x, y]]`.
3.  **Citation Clarification:** The non-equivariance of the standard estimator is an important point. You may consider adding a citation to [1] which also noted this issue, in addition to the current citation [19].
4.  **Minor Typos:**
    *   The learning objective for Norm-POEM in Section 6 is a minimization problem. The text should use `argmin` rather than `argmax` if that is what was intended.
    *   There appears to be a minor notational inconsistency in the tables or figures (e.g., bars vs. hats) that should be checked for consistency with the main text.
