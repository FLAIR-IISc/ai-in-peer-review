This paper identifies a critical issue – propensity overfitting – within the common framework of batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF) and proposes a self-normalized estimator to mitigate it. BLBF is a valuable technique for leveraging historical data when full, interactive control over a system is unavailable, a scenario prevalent in applications like ad placement and recommendation systems. The core problem addressed is that standard importance sampling-based counterfactual risk estimators, while unbiased, can exhibit high variance and, more insidiously, become susceptible to overfitting the propensity scores, especially with limited data and complex hypothesis spaces. The authors convincingly argue this isn’t simply a large-variance problem but a distinct phenomenon where the estimator can be heavily influenced by the distribution of the logged data, leading to poor generalization. Their proposed self-normalized estimator, leveraging multiplicative control variates, offers a promising solution by providing a bounded and equivariant estimate, thus alleviating the propensity overfitting issue.  The Norm-POEM algorithm, built upon this estimator, is demonstrated empirically to outperform existing methods.

The work builds upon a significant body of research in causal inference, off-policy evaluation, and domain adaptation (references [7, 8, 4]).  It clearly positions itself within the BLBF literature pioneered by Swaminathan & Joachims [1] and expands upon existing counterfactual estimators [6, 5, 11]. The connection to importance sampling and variance reduction techniques is well-established [12, 15, 16], and the authors appropriately contextualize their contribution within this landscape. While the paper acknowledges related work, a more detailed discussion regarding clipping techniques ([15, 5, 1]) and whether prior work *truly* relied solely on unbiased estimators (as stated in line 100) would be beneficial. It's possible some degree of bias was already present through weight clipping, potentially impacting the novelty claim. 

The strengths of this paper are numerous. The identification of propensity overfitting is a novel and insightful observation. The self-normalized estimator appears well-motivated and theoretically sound, offering a clear advantage over traditional approaches. The empirical results across multiple datasets consistently demonstrate the superior performance of Norm-POEM, and the ablation study (Table 3) effectively isolates the impact of variance regularization. Providing code availability is also a significant positive. The clarity of the exposition is generally good, though some sections (particularly those involving the mathematical derivations) could benefit from slightly more detailed explanations for readers less familiar with the underlying statistical concepts.

However, some weaknesses remain. While the explanation of propensity overfitting is compelling, the precise relationship between large importance weight variance and this specific overfitting issue isn’t entirely clear. A more intuitive explanation of *how* the estimator's instability leads to the observed behavior would be helpful.  The discussion surrounding Example 1 (lines 192-214) is somewhat difficult to follow.  Specifically, the reviewer (and potentially others) may struggle to fully grasp why `R̂(hoverfit)` becomes so negative when *n* is small relative to *k*, and how this translates to overfitting. A more illustrative example or visual representation might aid understanding. Further, the paper assumes a fully supportive historical policy h0. While stated, the implications and limitations of this assumption deserve further discussion.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* **Novelty:** The identification of propensity overfitting is a new and important problem in BLBF.
* **Significance:**  Addressing this issue has the potential to significantly improve the performance of real-world systems that rely on learning from historical logged data.
* **Soundness:** The proposed self-normalized estimator is theoretically grounded and empirically validated.
* **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and the key ideas are clearly presented.
* **Practicality:** The code availability enhances the reproducibility and impact of the work.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

* **Limited Technical Verification:** Due to time constraints, the reviewer was unable to fully verify all technical details.
* **Potential Overstatement of Novelty:**  The claim that prior works exclusively used unbiased estimators may require further scrutiny.
* **Minor Clarity Issues:** Some sections of the paper could benefit from more detailed explanations.



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite some minor concerns and the reviewer's limited capacity for a complete technical validation, this paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of batch learning from logged bandit feedback. The identified problem of propensity overfitting is significant, the proposed solution appears promising, and the empirical results are convincing.  I recommend **acceptance** with the caveat that authors might benefit from addressing the minor clarity issues and clarifying the relationship between large weight variance and propensity overfitting in a revised version.
