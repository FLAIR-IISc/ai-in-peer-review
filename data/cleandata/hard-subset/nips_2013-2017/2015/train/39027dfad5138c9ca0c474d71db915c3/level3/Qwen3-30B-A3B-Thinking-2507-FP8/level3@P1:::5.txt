

This paper identifies and addresses a critical flaw in the counterfactual risk estimation used in batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF), a setting that has gained significant attention at NIPS through works like Swaminathan & Joachims (2015) on POEM. The authors demonstrate that the conventional inverse propensity score estimator suffers from "propensity overfitting" when used in empirical risk minimization (ERM) for complex hypothesis spaces, where the risk estimate becomes arbitrarily biased. This is a novel form of overfitting distinct from standard loss overfitting in supervised learning, as the risk estimate can fall outside the natural range of the loss function. The authors propose a self-normalized risk estimator that resolves this issue by incorporating multiplicative control variates, leading to the Norm-POEM algorithm.

The paper's main contribution is both theoretically rigorous and empirically validated. The authors prove that the self-normalized estimator is equivariant (unlike the conventional estimator) and that its risk estimate remains bounded within the loss function's natural range. They show that the estimator avoids propensity overfitting by construction, as evidenced by the empirical observation that the sum of propensity weights Ŝ(h) remains close to its expected value of 1. This theoretical insight is significant because it addresses a previously unrecognized vulnerability in BLBF algorithms, which have been widely adopted for applications like ad placement and recommendation systems.

The quality of the paper is strong, with careful theoretical analysis and comprehensive experimental validation. The authors provide a thorough theoretical treatment of the propensity overfitting problem, including a clear example that demonstrates its severity. Their proof of the self-normalized estimator's consistency (Theorem 2) is concise and well-justified. The experiments on multi-label classification datasets (Scene, Yeast, TMC, LYRL) convincingly demonstrate that Norm-POEM consistently outperforms the original POEM across different training set sizes and loss functions. Notably, they show that Norm-POEM avoids the propensity overfitting problem as evidenced by Ŝ(h) being close to 1, while POEM exhibits severe bias in this metric. The paper also demonstrates that Norm-POEM is computationally efficient, converging faster than POEM despite the added normalization step, which is a significant practical advantage.

The clarity of the paper is excellent. The problem is well-motivated, the technical contributions are clearly presented, and the experimental setup is carefully designed to isolate the effects of the proposed estimator. The paper builds logically from the problem statement to the solution, with each section flowing naturally into the next. The writing is precise and accessible to NIPS readers, with appropriate mathematical notation and clear explanations of key concepts.

The originality of the contribution is strong. While BLBF has been studied extensively at NIPS (e.g., Swaminathan & Joachims 2015, Dudik et al. 2014), the propensity overfitting problem and the self-normalized estimator represent a novel insight that significantly improves upon existing work. The authors correctly position their work as extending the CRM principle from Swaminathan & Joachims (2015) rather than proposing a completely new framework, which is appropriate given the incremental but important nature of the contribution.

The significance of this work is high. BLBF is a crucial problem for real-world applications where interactive learning is impractical, and the authors demonstrate that their method substantially improves generalization performance across multiple datasets. The paper's findings have clear implications for practitioners developing bandit learning algorithms, and the Norm-POEM algorithm appears to be immediately applicable to existing BLBF systems. The computational efficiency of the method (faster convergence than POEM) further enhances its practical value.

One minor weakness is that the paper doesn't explore the theoretical limitations of the self-normalized estimator in more depth. While they show it avoids propensity overfitting, it would be valuable to understand under what conditions it might still fail or when the conventional estimator might be preferable. However, this is a minor point given the comprehensive empirical validation provided.

The paper is well-positioned within the NIPS community. It directly extends a highly cited NIPS 2015 paper (Swaminathan & Joachims), addresses a critical issue in the field, and provides both theoretical and empirical evidence for its claims. The work is clearly distinct from prior NIPS papers, with the propensity overfitting phenomenon and the self-normalized estimator representing new contributions.

Pro arguments for acceptance: 
1. Identifies and solves a previously unrecognized critical flaw in BLBF algorithms
2. Theoretical contribution is rigorous and significant
3. Empirical results are comprehensive and compelling
4. Algorithm is faster and more robust than the state-of-the-art
5. Clear writing and well-structured presentation

Con arguments against acceptance:
1. The problem addressed is somewhat narrow (focused on a specific estimator in BLBF)
2. Limited discussion of theoretical limitations of the proposed estimator

The paper makes a strong contribution to the field of batch learning from logged bandit feedback. It addresses a critical issue that has been overlooked in prior work, provides a theoretically sound solution, and demonstrates significant practical improvements. The work is well-executed, clearly presented, and has clear implications for both theory and practice. The significance of the contribution is high, as it directly improves the reliability and performance of a widely used learning paradigm. The paper meets all NIPS quality criteria and should be accepted.
