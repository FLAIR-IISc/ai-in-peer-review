This paper addresses a critical problem in batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF): propensity overfitting in the commonly used counterfactual risk estimator. The authors identify that this estimator, while unbiased, can exhibit significant variance and lead to poor generalization performance, particularly with complex hypothesis spaces. They propose a self-normalized estimator as a solution, demonstrating both theoretically and empirically its improved robustness and performance. The resulting learning algorithm, Norm-POEM, consistently outperforms the original POEM algorithm on multi-label classification tasks, approaching performance levels achievable with full supervised information.

The work builds upon the foundation laid by prior research in causal inference, domain adaptation, and off-policy evaluation, particularly the CRM principle introduced in Swaminathan and Joachims (ICML 2015). The authors effectively position their contribution within this landscape, correctly citing relevant work like propensity score matching (Rosenbaum & Rubin, 1983) and importance sampling techniques (e.g., Ionides, 2006). They acknowledge and address limitations of existing methods and clearly articulate the novelty of their approach – specifically, the use of a multiplicative control variate to mitigate propensity overfitting.

The strengths of this paper are numerous. The motivation is clear and compelling: BLBF is a valuable technique for leveraging historical data in online systems where full feedback is unavailable. The identification of propensity overfitting is a significant contribution, highlighting a previously overlooked issue in the application of counterfactual risk estimation. The self-normalized estimator proposed is a logical and well-justified solution, drawing upon established techniques from importance sampling. Furthermore, the experimental results are strong and consistent, demonstrating the practical benefits of Norm-POEM across multiple datasets. The inclusion of ablation studies (evaluating the necessity of variance regularization) and runtime comparisons further strengthen the analysis. The authors have also made their code publicly available, facilitating reproducibility and further research.

However, there are some points requiring attention. The mathematical commentary surrounding equation (2), the standard unbiased estimator, contains inaccuracies. The authors’ interpretation of the loss in this equation and claims about its expected value appear to be misleading, leading to an incorrect assertion regarding its non-invariance to loss translation. This mischaracterization subsequently affects the presentation of Example 1, which is built upon this flawed premise. While the authors are correct to point out the estimator’s sensitivity to small propensity weights (π), framing this as a novel “anomaly” is inaccurate given existing literature on the variance issues inherent in importance sampling. A clearer and more accurate description of the estimator’s properties would significantly strengthen the paper.

Despite these technical concerns, the core contribution of the paper – the self-normalized estimator and its application in Norm-POEM – remains valid and impactful. The self-normalized estimator (7) offers a pragmatic improvement by addressing the sensitivity to small propensity weights through a double-denominator approach. The experimental validation convincingly demonstrates its benefits in practice.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Addresses a significant practical problem in BLBF.
*   Proposes a well-motivated and theoretically sound solution (self-normalized estimator).
*   Demonstrates strong empirical performance improvements over existing methods.
*   Clear and well-organized presentation (aside from the noted technical inaccuracies).
*   Code availability promotes reproducibility and future research.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   Technical inaccuracies in the discussion of the original estimator (equation 2) and its properties.
*   Overstatement of the novelty of the identified issues related to estimator variance.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with a valuable contribution to the field of counterfactual learning. While the technical inaccuracies regarding the properties of the original estimator are concerning, they do not invalidate the core findings or the practical benefits of the proposed approach. I recommend acceptance, contingent upon the authors revising their commentary to accurately reflect the properties of the existing estimator and clarify their claims regarding its “anomalies.” If these revisions are made, the paper will represent a significant advance in the field and be a valuable addition to the NIPS proceedings.
