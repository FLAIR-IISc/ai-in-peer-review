

# Review of "The Self-Normalized Estimator for Counterfactual Learning"

This paper addresses a critical issue in batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF), a setting that has gained significant attention for its applicability to real-world systems like ad placement and recommendation engines. The authors identify a previously unrecognized problem—propensity overfitting—that plagues the conventional unbiased counterfactual risk estimator used in Counterfactual Risk Minimization (CRM) algorithms. They propose a self-normalized risk estimator that avoids this problem, leading to a new learning algorithm called Norm-POEM. The paper builds upon the foundational CRM framework introduced in [1] but identifies a significant limitation in the conventional estimator that has not been addressed in prior work.

The paper makes a strong theoretical contribution by formally defining propensity overfitting, providing a clear example that demonstrates its severity, and proving that the self-normalized estimator resolves the identified anomalies. The authors show that the conventional estimator is not equivariant under loss translation and can produce arbitrarily low risk estimates, while the self-normalized estimator preserves the loss range and is equivariant. The empirical evaluation on multi-label classification datasets demonstrates consistent improvements of Norm-POEM over the conventional POEM algorithm across multiple datasets and training sample sizes.

The paper excels in clarity and organization. The problem statement, theoretical analysis, and empirical results are presented in a logical flow that makes the contribution accessible to readers familiar with the BLBF literature. The examples illustrating propensity overfitting are particularly effective at conveying the problem's severity.

Regarding originality, the paper identifies a novel problem (propensity overfitting) that has not been previously recognized in the BLBF literature. While self-normalized estimators have been used in other contexts (e.g., financial applications), their application to BLBF to address this specific problem represents a meaningful contribution. The paper adequately references prior work, including the foundational CRM paper [1] and related work on importance sampling.

The significance of the paper is somewhat limited by the scope of the experiments. While the problem is clearly relevant to real-world applications, the paper lacks real-world applications beyond the controlled multi-label classification experiments. The authors demonstrate consistent improvements in controlled settings, but it remains unclear how these gains would translate to practical deployment in complex systems. The paper would benefit from more extensive experiments, particularly on additional datasets and with more realistic data distributions.

In terms of quality, the theoretical analysis is sound, and the empirical results support the claims. However, the paper could be strengthened by providing variance estimates for the repeated experiments (as requested by the reviewer), which would help assess the statistical significance of the improvements. The paper also does not deeply analyze the optimization properties of the Norm-POEM objective, which the reviewer notes could provide additional insights into why the proposed estimator leads to better generalization.

Strengths:
- Identifies and solves a previously unrecognized problem (propensity overfitting) in BLBF
- Provides clear theoretical analysis and proofs of the proposed estimator's advantages
- Demonstrates consistent empirical improvements over the conventional approach
- Well-written and clearly organized
- Builds upon and extends prior work in a meaningful way

Weaknesses:
- Limited to synthetic experiments on multi-label classification datasets without real-world applications
- Insufficient reporting of variance for repeated experiments
- Lacks deeper analysis of the optimization properties of the proposed learning algorithm
- Unclear how experimental gains translate to practical real-world systems

The paper makes a solid contribution to the BLBF literature, addressing a genuine limitation in the current approach. The proposed self-normalized estimator is theoretically sound and empirically effective. However, the significance could be enhanced by demonstrating the approach in more diverse and realistic settings.

Pro arguments for acceptance:
- The problem identified (propensity overfitting) is real and significant
- The solution is theoretically sound and empirically validated
- The paper is well-written and clearly presents its contribution
- The work extends prior CRM work in a meaningful way

Con arguments for rejection:
- The empirical evaluation is limited to a single class of problems (multi-label classification)
- The significance of the contribution is somewhat constrained by the lack of real-world applications
- The paper could benefit from more thorough analysis of the optimization process

Overall, this is a high-quality paper that makes a meaningful contribution to the BLBF literature. The problem is well-motivated, the solution is theoretically sound, and the empirical results demonstrate consistent improvements. While the significance could be strengthened with additional experiments in more realistic settings, the paper's contribution is valuable and should be accepted for presentation at NIPS. I recommend acceptance with the understanding that the authors could further strengthen the paper by addressing the variance reporting request and potentially expanding the empirical evaluation.
