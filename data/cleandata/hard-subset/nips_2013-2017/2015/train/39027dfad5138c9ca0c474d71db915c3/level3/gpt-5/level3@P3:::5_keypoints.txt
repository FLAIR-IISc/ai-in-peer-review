- Summary: Identifies propensity overfitting in CRM/POEM and proposes replacing IPS with a self-normalized importance sampling (SNIS) estimator, yielding Norm-POEM for structured prediction with improved stability and boundedness.

- Key theoretical claim: SNIS delivers bounded, loss-translation-equivariant, and strongly consistent counterfactual risk estimates; an approximate variance estimator is derived for regularization.

- Algorithmic contribution: Norm-POEM instantiates CRM with SNIS and optimizes the nonconvex objective via L-BFGS with importance-weight clipping.

- Empirical claim: Across multi-label datasets under supervised-to-bandit conversion, Norm-POEM outperforms POEM, controls propensity overfitting (Ŝ ≈ 1), is robust to loss translation, and converges faster.

- Strength — problem identification: Clear demonstration and diagnostic of propensity overfitting where IPS-based ERM exploits weight variability to drive degenerate empirical risk.

- Strength — principled remedy: Use of multiplicative control variates (SNIS) is well-motivated and restores boundedness and equivariance while remaining consistent.

- Strength — meaningful extension: Norm-POEM is substantially different from POEM in estimator and regularizer and improves training stability and generalization.

- Strength — empirical evidence: Results are consistent and compelling, showing better test risk and stability under loss translations.

- Strength — practicality: Optimization converges faster, parameter norms are smaller, and per-iteration cost is similar to POEM.

- Strength — clarity: Paper is mostly well written with appropriate related work positioning.

- Weakness — theory gap: No finite-sample CRM-style generalization bounds for SNIS with the proposed variance regularizer are provided.

- Weakness — variance estimator: The delta-method-based variance estimate lacks discussion of conditions for accuracy (effective sample size, tail behavior, clipping) and finite-sample guarantees.

- Weakness — baselines: Missing comparisons to modern off-policy estimators like doubly robust (DR), weighted DR, or other variance-reduced methods.

- Weakness — data realism: Evaluation relies on supervised-to-bandit conversion; no real logged-bandit datasets or stronger stress tests for sparse/heavy-tail propensities.

- Weakness — sensitivity analysis: Limited systematic analysis of clipping threshold M, regularization λ, and dependence on h0 quality; only brief treatment provided.

- Weakness — scope: Experiments are limited to multi-label linear-exponential models; computational implications for large/structured Y or nonlinear policies are not discussed.

- Quality assessment: Above-average technical quality with credible algorithmic instantiation and supportive experiments; main gap is lack of finite-sample bounds.

- Clarity assessment: Exposition is clear, especially on propensity overfitting and SNIS properties; minor fixes suggested for asymptotic assumptions and clipping effects.

- Originality: While SNIS is classical, the paper's formulation of propensity overfitting in BLBF and integration of SNIS into CRM is original and meaningful.

- Significance: Practically relevant fix for a common failure mode in counterfactual learning with a plausible path to adoption in CRM-like pipelines.

- Questions for authors: Can you provide finite-sample bounds for CRM with SNIS (possibly with clipping)? How does Norm-POEM compare to DR/WDR/SNDR? What are effective sample sizes and their correlation with performance? How sensitive are results to M and λ and are there principled selection rules?

- Suggestions: Add DR/WDR baselines, derive finite-sample risk/concentration results for SNIS-CRM, include real logged data or stress tests, expand sensitivity analyses, and discuss normalized DR extensions and larger policy classes.

- Arguments for acceptance: Addresses a real failure mode, provides a principled and empirically effective remedy, is well written, and likely to influence practice.

- Arguments against acceptance: Missing finite-sample theory and limited baselines/evaluation realism.

- Overall recommendation: Recommend acceptance due to originality, practical impact, and strong empirical results despite theoretical and evaluation gaps.
