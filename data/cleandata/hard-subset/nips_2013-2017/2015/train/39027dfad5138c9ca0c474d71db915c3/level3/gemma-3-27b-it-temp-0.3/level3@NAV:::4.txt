## Review of "The Self-Normalized Estimator for Counterfactual Learning"

This paper addresses a critical issue in batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF): propensity overfitting in the commonly used counterfactual risk estimator. The authors convincingly argue that this estimator, while unbiased, can lead to severely biased risk estimates and propose a self-normalized estimator as a remedy. This is a valuable contribution, building directly upon the work of Swaminathan & Joachims [1] and extending the understanding of challenges in off-policy evaluation. The proposed Norm-POEM algorithm, leveraging this new estimator, demonstrates consistent empirical improvements over POEM on multi-label classification tasks.

The strength of the paper lies in its insightful analysis of the problem. The examples illustrating propensity overfitting (Section 4) are particularly effective in demonstrating the issue’s severity and its distinction from standard overfitting. The theoretical justification for the self-normalized estimator, drawing on control variate techniques, is sound and well-explained. The empirical evaluation is thorough, mirroring the experimental setup of [1] for fair comparison and showcasing consistent gains across multiple datasets. The observation that Norm-POEM can converge faster than POEM due to the bounded risk estimates is a noteworthy practical benefit.

However, a few points require clarification. The paper states that delta(x,y) in NORM-POEM (equations 8 and 3) uses rescaled delta in [-1,0] like CRM, but doesn’t explicitly confirm this. Clarifying this point would strengthen the connection to prior work. Furthermore, the meaning of “delta > 0” in Table 1 experiments is unclear and requires explanation. The authors also make a strong claim that delta(x,y) should be modeled as a random variable, advocating for an expectation over delta in the true risk setup. While intuitively appealing, this requires more detailed justification and potentially a theoretical elaboration. 

Minor corrections are needed: line 170-171 should use h(y|x) / h0(y|x), and line 276 should use argmin instead of argmax. Additionally, line 319 should replace bars with hats to maintain consistency with earlier notation.

**Post-rebuttal assessment:** Quality: 6, Clarity: 6, Originality: 8, Significance: 8.

**Overall:** This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of counterfactual learning. The analysis is insightful, the proposed solution is effective, and the empirical results are compelling. Despite the minor points for clarification and correction, I recommend acceptance. The work is likely to be influential and will be of interest to researchers working on BLBF, off-policy evaluation, and related areas.
