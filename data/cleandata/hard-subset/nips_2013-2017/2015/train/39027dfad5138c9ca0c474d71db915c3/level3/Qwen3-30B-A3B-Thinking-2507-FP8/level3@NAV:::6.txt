

This paper identifies a critical failure mode in counterfactual risk minimization (CRM) for batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF), termed "propensity overfitting," where hypotheses exploit example generation probabilities to artificially minimize risk estimates. The authors demonstrate this problem occurs when the conventional importance-weighted risk estimator is used in empirical risk minimization, leading to severe overfitting where risk estimates can deviate arbitrarily from true risk. The paper proposes a self-normalized estimator that replaces the standard risk estimator with a multiplicative control variate, which provably preserves consistency while controlling variance. The authors derive a new learning algorithm, Norm-POEM, based on this estimator and provide theoretical guarantees and empirical validation.

The paper makes a strong contribution to the BLBF literature by addressing a previously unobserved failure mode that has significant practical implications. The theoretical analysis is sound, with clear proofs of consistency and variance control. The empirical evaluation across multiple multi-label classification datasets demonstrates consistent improvements over the standard CRM approach, with Norm-POEM achieving better generalization performance while avoiding the propensity overfitting problem. The authors also provide insightful analysis of how the self-normalized estimator maintains equivariance under loss transformations, a key advantage over conventional estimators.

Strengths include the clear identification of a novel problem, the elegant theoretical solution, and convincing empirical validation. The paper is well-organized, with clear connections to prior work in BLBF (e.g., POEM, off-policy evaluation) and proper contextualization of the contribution. The implementation details and experimental setup are thorough and well-motivated.

The main weakness is the limited discussion of the computational cost trade-offs. While the authors note Norm-POEM converges faster than POEM in practice, the theoretical complexity analysis could be strengthened. Additionally, the evaluation focuses primarily on multi-label classification; broader application domains would further establish significance.

The contribution is highly original, addressing a fundamental issue in CRM that previous BLBF work overlooked. The significance is substantial, as propensity overfitting could undermine the reliability of BLBF algorithms in real-world applications. The paper advances the state of the art by providing both a theoretical solution and a practical algorithm that demonstrably improves performance.

Overall, this is a high-quality paper that makes a significant contribution to the field. The proposed solution elegantly resolves a previously unaddressed problem with strong theoretical and empirical support. While I remain slightly uncertain about the broader applicability beyond the tested domains, the paper's strengths far outweigh this concern. I recommend acceptance.
