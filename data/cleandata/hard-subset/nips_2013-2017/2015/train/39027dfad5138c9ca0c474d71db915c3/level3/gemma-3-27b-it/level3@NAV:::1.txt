## Review of "The Self-Normalized Estimator for Counterfactual Learning"

This paper addresses a critical issue in batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF): propensity overfitting. While previous work focused on the large variance of importance weights, the authors correctly identify that the core problem is the inability to reliably estimate risk when the observed data provides insufficient exploration for a given hypothesis. This is a subtle but important distinction. The paper proposes a self-normalized estimator as a solution, and demonstrates through theoretical arguments and empirical evaluation that it mitigates propensity overfitting and improves performance on multi-label classification tasks.

The motivation is clearly presented, and the description of propensity overfitting with the illustrative example (Section 4) is particularly helpful in understanding the issue. The proposed self-normalized estimator is a sensible approach, drawing on well-established variance reduction techniques from importance sampling. The connection to existing work in causal inference, domain adaptation, and off-policy evaluation is also well-established. The empirical results consistently show improvements over the standard approach, and the ablation studies (Table 3) are useful for understanding the contribution of variance regularization. The availability of code is greatly appreciated and facilitates reproducibility.

However, my time for this additional review was limited, and I may have missed nuances. The relationship between large weight variance and propensity overfitting isn't fully elucidated – while the paper demonstrates they are related, a more direct explanation of *how* large variance manifests as overfitting would be helpful. Line 199’s discussion regarding `n` small relative to `k` feels unclear; the behavior of `R̂(h^)` in that regime warrants further clarification. Additionally, the claim that prior BLBF works used an *unbiased* counterfactual risk estimator (Section 2) seems questionable, given the common practice of clipping weights to address variance, which introduces bias. 

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Addresses a novel and important problem (propensity overfitting) in BLBF.
*   Proposed solution (self-normalized estimator) is theoretically grounded and practically effective.
*   Clear presentation, illustrative example, and code availability.
*   Empirical results consistently outperform existing methods.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Limited time prevented thorough verification of technical details.
*   Relationship between variance and overfitting could be more clearly explained.
*   Questionable claim regarding unbiasedness of previous estimators.
*   Behavior of estimator when n < k is unclear.



Despite these minor concerns and the limitations of my review, I recommend acceptance. The paper presents a valuable contribution to the field and addresses a real-world problem with a well-motivated and effective solution.
