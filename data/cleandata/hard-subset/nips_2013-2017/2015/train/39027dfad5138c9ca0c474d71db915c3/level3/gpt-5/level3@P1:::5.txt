This paper identifies “propensity overfitting” as a failure mode of the standard inverse propensity (IPS) counterfactual risk estimator used within Counterfactual Risk Minimization (CRM) for batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF). The authors propose replacing the conventional estimator with a self-normalized (weighted/ratio) importance sampling estimator and instantiate a CRM algorithm, Norm-POEM, for structured prediction with exponential-family models. The core technical rationale is that the self-normalized estimator is bounded and equivariant to loss translations, thereby removing degenerate behavior of IPS under finite samples. The paper provides a consistency result for the estimator and an approximate variance estimate to plug into CRM. Empirically, on multi-label classification benchmarks constructed via the standard supervised-to-bandit protocol, Norm-POEM consistently outperforms POEM, avoids the propensity overfitting pathology (as indicated by Ŝ(h) ≈ 1), and converges faster in practice.

Relation to prior work:
- Builds directly on CRM and POEM (Swaminathan & Joachims, ICML/NIPS-era), and connects to classical importance sampling, control variates, and self-normalized importance sampling (Hesterberg; Kong; Owen; Rubinstein & Kroese).
- Related to off-policy evaluation and learning (Dudík et al.; Thomas et al.), and to domain adaptation/importance weighting theory (Cortes et al.).
- Compared to prior bandit learning methods using IPS with clipping and variance regularization, the novelty is to use multiplicative control variates via self-normalization inside the learning objective and to diagnose/mitigate a new overfitting mode specific to propensity weights.

Strengths:
- Clear identification and explanation of propensity overfitting with a crisp illustrative example; the Ŝ(h) diagnostic is simple and compelling.
- Self-normalized estimator is a principled, well-founded fix: boundedness and equivariance address the anomalies of IPS that ERM can exploit; strong consistency is established.
- Well-integrated algorithmic contribution (Norm-POEM) that is a drop-in replacement for POEM’s estimator within the CRM template, with a reasonable variance surrogate and tractable gradients for exponential-family models.
- Solid empirical improvements across datasets and data regimes, including robustness to loss translations and superior calibration of Ŝ(h).
- Practical relevance: better generalization and often faster convergence despite higher per-iteration costs; code availability.

Weaknesses and suggestions:
- Theory is largely asymptotic. A finite-sample generalization bound for CRM with the self-normalized estimator (beyond the Normal/delta method variance approximation) would strengthen the paper and better parallel Theorem 1’s role in CRM.
- Experimental scope is limited to supervised-to-bandit simulations in multi-label classification. While standard, results on a real logging scenario (e.g., ranking/recommendation) or at least more diverse synthetic logging policies would make the case stronger.
- Baselines: comparisons to doubly robust estimators and/or other variance-reduced importance sampling schemes (e.g., clipped/thresholded IPS with systematic tuning, defensive mixtures) are missing. Since DR is designed to reduce variance/bias via additive control variates, showing that Norm-POEM still dominates would bolster significance.
- Sensitivity analysis: more systematic study of clipping parameter M and variance regularization λ would help practitioners; the paper notes both are tuned on held-out logs but does not report robustness curves.
- Assumption of full support in logging is central. A discussion of behavior and possible remedies under partial support/deterministic logging would be valuable.
- The variance estimator relies on Normal approximations; empirical validation of its calibration (e.g., coverage of confidence intervals or correlation with true generalization error) would increase confidence.
- Clarity: adding pseudocode for Norm-POEM and summarizing computational complexity and wall-clock run times per dataset would improve reproducibility.

Quality:
- Technically sound overall; the identification of propensity overfitting and the use of self-normalization are correct and well-motivated. Theoretical support (boundedness, equivariance, consistency) is solid, but finite-sample guarantees tailored to CRM with ratio estimators are lacking. Experiments convincingly demonstrate benefits in the tested setting.

Clarity:
- Generally well written and easy to follow. The example in Section 4 and the Ŝ(h) diagnostic are pedagogically strong. Minor improvements include providing algorithmic pseudocode, more detailed runtime metrics, and an explicit discussion of hyperparameter sensitivity.

Originality:
- While self-normalized importance sampling is classical, its diagnosis-driven adoption within CRM to mitigate propensity overfitting, and the formulation of Norm-POEM, are novel in the BLBF literature. The articulation of “propensity overfitting” as a distinct phenomenon is an original and useful contribution.

Significance:
- High practical impact for counterfactual learning from logs: a robust estimator that is simple to implement and consistently improves performance is likely to see adoption. The work advances the state of the art over POEM and can influence subsequent algorithms, including in off-policy RL and contextual bandits.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Identifies an important and underappreciated failure mode of widely used IPS-based CRM.
- Proposes a principled, simple, and effective fix with strong intuitive and empirical support.
- Consistent improvements across datasets and data sizes; robustness to loss transformations.
- Likely to be influential and useful in practice; code available.

Arguments against acceptance:
- No finite-sample generalization bound specific to the self-normalized estimator inside CRM.
- Limited baselines (no DR/alternative variance-reduction methods) and scope restricted to simulated bandit data.
- Reliance on Normal approximations for variance without calibration evidence.

Questions for the authors:
- Can you provide finite-sample risk bounds (e.g., via concentration for ratio estimators) or at least empirical calibration of the variance surrogate?
- How sensitive is performance to M and λ? Can you offer heuristic defaults or guidance?
- How does Norm-POEM compare to doubly robust estimators and to clipped IPS with strong regularization?
- What is the behavior under partial-support logging, and are there practical mitigations (e.g., hybrid estimators or exploration scavenging)?
- Can you report wall-clock times and iteration counts across datasets?

Overall assessment: Strong paper. The contribution is clear, original within the BLBF/CRM line of work, and practically impactful. I recommend acceptance after addressing baseline comparisons and, if possible, adding finite-sample analysis or empirical calibration for the variance estimator.
