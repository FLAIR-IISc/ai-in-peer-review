This paper addresses batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF) under the Counterfactual Risk Minimization (CRM) principle. The authors identify a failure mode of the commonly used inverse propensity risk estimator (IPS), namely “propensity overfitting,” where learning drives hypotheses to inflate or deflate importance weights to game the estimator, yielding pathological empirical risks and sensitivity to loss translations. They propose replacing IPS with a multiplicative control-variate version, the self-normalized importance sampling estimator (SNIPS), and instantiate it within CRM to obtain Norm-POEM for structured prediction with exponential-family (CRF-like) models. They provide a simple consistency theorem for SNIPS, derive an approximate variance formula to plug into CRM’s empirical-Bernstein-style regularization, and show empirically—on multi-label datasets using the supervised→bandit protocol—that Norm-POEM outperforms POEM and is robust to loss translations. The connection to prior work is appropriate: IPS and truncation [7, 15], importance-weighted learning bounds [8], CRM [1], off-policy evaluation [6, 14], and control variates/self-normalization in IS [12, 19, 22].

Quality
- Technical soundness: The propensity overfitting phenomenon is convincingly motivated (Example 1) and the diagnostic via the sum of weights Ŝ(h) is well grounded (E[Ŝ(h)]=1). The move to SNIPS is standard in Monte Carlo and addresses non-equivariance and boundedness issues, with strong consistency (Theorem 2). The CRM instantiation uses a reasonable approximate variance for a ratio estimator [22, 23] and includes clipping to ensure finite variance.
- Empirical support: Results consistently favor Norm-POEM over POEM across multiple datasets, show invariance to loss translations, and demonstrate improved runtime behavior via better-conditioned objectives. However, the experimental section would benefit from variance reporting (mean±std or confidence intervals over runs), ablations, and additional baselines (see below).
- Optimization details: The paper opts for L-BFGS on a non-convex, non-stochastic objective due to the ratio’s variance estimate. This is plausible but warrants deeper analysis: convergence behavior, sensitivity to initialization, and scalability compared to stochastic methods are only briefly discussed.

Clarity
- The paper is clearly written and well organized. The distinction between loss overfitting and propensity overfitting is crisp, the role of equivariance is nicely explained, and the relationship to control variates is pedagogical. Pointers to code increase reproducibility.
- Suggestions: Provide more guidance on selecting M and λ; include a brief derivation or appendix for the gradient of the SNIPS-CRM objective; and explicitly state computational complexity per iteration relative to CRF training, with wall-clock times and iteration counts.

Originality
- The individual ingredients—self-normalized IS, CRM, and CRF-like policy classes—are known. The novelty lies in identifying “propensity overfitting” as a learning-time pathology of IPS in CRM, arguing for SNIPS on both theoretical and practical grounds, and demonstrating that the resulting objective better regularizes learning in practice. As a follow-up to [1], the contribution is incremental but meaningful and well targeted to a real problem practitioners face.

Significance
- The problem is important for counterfactual learning in recommendation, ads, and search. Normalization is a simple, low-friction change with potentially broad impact. That said, the significance would be stronger with:
  - Comparisons to doubly robust/regression estimators [6, 16], including versions with clipping, and a discussion of how multiplicative versus additive control variates interact.
  - Experiments on real logged data (even small-scale) or additional domains beyond those in [1].
  - Statistical significance testing and sensitivity analyses (to exploration rate of h0, tail-heaviness of weights, clipping thresholds, and λ).

Pros (arguments for acceptance)
- Clearly identifies a consequential failure mode of a widely used estimator in BLBF and provides an elegant, principled fix.
- Strong empirical improvements over a relevant baseline (POEM), with robustness to loss translations and better-conditioned optimization.
- Solid connections to importance sampling theory; consistency result and variance plug-in are appropriate.
- Clear writing, good diagnostics (Ŝ(h) near 1), and available code.

Cons (arguments against acceptance)
- Experimental scope is limited to the supervised→bandit setup and the same family of tasks as [1], with few baselines; no real-world logs.
- Lack of statistical uncertainty reporting; unclear practical effect sizes across runs.
- Limited theoretical development around the CRM bound with SNIPS (e.g., no high-probability generalization bound tailoring the ratio estimator’s bias/variance).
- Optimization analysis is relatively light; no comparison to scalable stochastic variants or study of convergence/stability.

Requested revisions and suggestions
- Report mean±std (or 95% CI) over the 10 runs; add significance tests.
- Add baselines: doubly robust estimators [6] (with and without clipping), truncated IPS [15], and an ablation where only the normalization changes (keeping the rest of POEM identical), to isolate the effect of SNIPS.
- Provide sensitivity studies: effect of clipping M and λ; varying exploration in h0 and support violations; heavy-tailed weight scenarios.
- Include at least one real-world BLBF task (e.g., a small-scale recommendation log) or a synthetic RL off-policy domain to complement multi-label classification.
- Elaborate on optimization: initialization, convergence diagnostics, scaling with |Y| and feature dimension, and comparison to stochastic/quasi-stochastic alternatives.
- Discuss theoretical bias of SNIPS under clipping and provide practical guidance (e.g., bounds in terms of weight coefficient of variation, as in [19]).

Overall recommendation: Weak accept. The paper addresses a real and underappreciated issue with a principled, practical solution and demonstrates consistent improvements. Strengthening the experimental section and adding comparisons to DR/truncated baselines would solidify the significance. Confidence: medium.
