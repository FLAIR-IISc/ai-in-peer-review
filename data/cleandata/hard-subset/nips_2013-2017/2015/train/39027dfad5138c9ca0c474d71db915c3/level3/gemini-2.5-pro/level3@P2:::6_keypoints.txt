- Addresses batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF) and builds on Counterfactual Risk Minimization (CRM).
- Identifies a novel failure mode called "Propensity Overfitting" where IPS estimators are exploited to produce artificially low empirical risk.
- Shows propensity overfitting can produce arbitrarily poor risk estimates and degenerate solutions.
- Proposes replacing IPS with a self-normalized (ratio) estimator, treating the sum of importance weights as a control variate with expectation 1.
- Introduces Norm-POEM (Normalized Policy Optimizer for Exponential Models) as the CRM instantiation using the new estimator.
- The self-normalized estimator is consistent, equivariant to loss translations, and yields risk estimates bounded within observed loss range.
- Provides a clear illustrative example and theoretical analysis (including consistency, Theorem 2) supporting the claims.
- Experimental methodology is rigorous, using a supervised→bandit conversion to obtain ground-truth generalization performance.
- Empirical results show Norm-POEM consistently and significantly outperforms POEM and avoids propensity overfitting.
- Paper is exceptionally well-written, well-organized, and explains concepts (e.g., control variates) intuitively before formalization.
- Originality lies in identifying propensity overfitting in CRM and applying the self-normalized estimator as a learning-time fix, even though the estimator itself is not new.
- The work is significant for practitioners and researchers in off-policy/counterfactual learning and may influence future estimator scrutiny.
- Arguments for acceptance: novel problem identification, simple principled solution, strong theoretical and empirical support, and clear presentation.
- Arguments against acceptance: self-normalized estimator is not novel; experiments are simulated rather than using real logged bandit data.
- Additional concern: proposed variance estimator does not admit stochastic optimization—more discussion of computational trade-offs for large-scale datasets would help.
