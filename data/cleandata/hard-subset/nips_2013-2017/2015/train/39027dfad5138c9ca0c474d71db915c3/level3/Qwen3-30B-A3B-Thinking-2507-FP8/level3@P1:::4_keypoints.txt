- Identifies "propensity overfitting" in BLBF where conventional IPS estimators cause overfitting via propensity weights.
- Proposes a self-normalized risk estimator that avoids propensity overfitting using multiplicative control variates.
- Introduces Norm-POEM, replacing POEM’s estimator with the self-normalized version.
- Builds on Swaminathan & Joachims (2015 CRM) but uncovers a previously overlooked anomaly in the standard estimator.
- Theoretical analysis shows the conventional estimator can produce arbitrarily biased risk estimates, especially for complex hypothesis spaces.
- Distinguishes propensity overfitting from conventional overfitting: the issue stems from propensity weights, not loss function complexity.
- Self-normalized estimator properties: equivariant under loss translation, yields bounded risk estimates, and prevents propensity overfitting.
- Corrects a propensity-ratio sign/direction error (propensity should be h(y|x)/h0(y|x)), aligning with importance sampling literature.
- Derivation of the self-normalized estimator is clear and Theorem 2 proving strong consistency is technically sound.
- Experimental setup uses Supervised 7→ Bandit to create controlled logged bandit datasets from multi-label tasks.
- Empirical results (Table 1, Figure 1) show Norm-POEM consistently outperforms POEM, with larger gains as training size grows.
- Table 2 analysis shows Norm-POEM maintains normalized propensity weight near 1, while POEM exhibits severe bias.
- Variance regularization remains beneficial even with the self-normalized estimator (Table 3).
- Norm-POEM is computationally efficient and often converges faster than POEM.
- Requests minor clarifications: explicitly state loss rescaling to [-1,0], model δ(x,y) as random to reflect label noise, change argmax→argmin in optimization, and use hats instead of bars for consistency.
- Overall assessment: high significance and originality, good quality and clarity, minor fixes needed, recommend acceptance.
