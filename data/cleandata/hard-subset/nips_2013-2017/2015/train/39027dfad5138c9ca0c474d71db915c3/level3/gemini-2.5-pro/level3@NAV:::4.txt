This paper identifies and addresses a critical flaw, which it terms "propensity overfitting," in the conventional counterfactual risk estimator used for batch learning from logged bandit feedback. Building directly on the Counterfactual Risk Minimization (CRM) principle introduced in [1], the authors show how the standard inverse propensity score estimator can produce degenerate risk estimates by overfitting to the propensity weights rather than the loss signal. As a remedy, the paper proposes replacing this estimator with a self-normalized version, which acts as a multiplicative control variate. This leads to a new learning algorithm, Norm-POEM, which is shown to be more robust and equivariant to loss translations. The empirical evaluation on multi-label classification tasks convincingly demonstrates that Norm-POEM significantly outperforms its predecessor, POEM.

The primary strength of this work lies in its insightful diagnosis of the propensity overfitting problem, which is a novel and significant contribution to the field of counterfactual learning. The proposed solution, employing a self-normalized estimator, is elegant, well-motivated, and theoretically sound. The paper is generally well-written, and the experiments clearly demonstrate the practical benefits of the proposed method, showing substantial improvements in generalization performance.

While the work is strong, there are several areas where clarity could be improved and minor corrections are needed:

*   The authors should clarify whether NORM-POEM (equations (3) and (8)) assumes a rescaled loss `delta` in [-1,0] like the original CRM formulation. This is particularly relevant for understanding the "delta > 0" experiment in Table 1.
*   The theoretical framing could be strengthened. A more complete model would treat `delta(x,y)` as a random variable and include an expectation over label noise in the true risk formulation.
*   Several minor corrections would improve the manuscript:
    *   Lines 170–171: The argument about non-equivariance should use `h(y|x) / h_0(y|x)`, and it's worth noting this was previously observed in [1].
    *   Line 276: This should be an `argmin`, not `argmax`, as the objective is risk minimization.
    *   Line 319: The notation should use hats instead of bars to remain consistent with earlier sections.

Overall, this is an excellent and important follow-up to [1]. The analysis of propensity overfitting is insightful (Originality: 8), the use of a multiplicative control variate is effective, and the empirical results show a clear improvement on a significant problem (Significance: 8). The paper is technically solid and the claims are well-supported (Quality: 6). Despite the minor points needing clarification (Clarity: 6), I believe this is a strong contribution and recommend acceptance.
