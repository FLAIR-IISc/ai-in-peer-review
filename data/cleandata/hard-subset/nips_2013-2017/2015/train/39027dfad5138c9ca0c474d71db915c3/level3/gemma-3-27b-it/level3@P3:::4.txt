This paper addresses the important problem of propensity overfitting in batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF), a common scenario in online systems. The authors identify a key flaw in the widely used unbiased counterfactual risk estimator and propose a self-normalized estimator to mitigate this issue. They present Norm-POEM, a new learning algorithm based on this estimator, and demonstrate its effectiveness on multi-label classification tasks, consistently outperforming POEM. This is a compelling follow-up to Swaminathan & Joachims (ICML 2013) [1], and the analysis of propensity overfitting is insightful. The use of a multiplicative control variate is clever, and the empirical results are convincing.

The core idea is well-motivated and clearly presented. The problem of propensity overfitting is explained with a compelling example, and the proposed self-normalized estimator offers an elegant solution. The connection to importance sampling and control variates provides a strong theoretical foundation. The paper builds convincingly on prior work in BLBF [1, 2, 5, 6], causal inference [7], and importance sampling [11, 12, 19]. The experimental setup closely follows that of POEM [1], allowing for a fair comparison, and the inclusion of runtime comparisons is a welcome addition.

However, there are several points that require clarification and correction. First, concerning the rescaling of delta, it’s unclear if NORM-POEM (equations 8 and 3) utilizes a rescaled delta in the range [-1, 0] as does CRM. This should be clarified, as it impacts the interpretation of “delta > 0” in Table 1’s experiments. Specifically, a value of delta greater than zero implies what in the CRM setting? Is it simply a non-negative loss, or something different given the self-normalization?

Second, the authors treat delta(x, y) as a deterministic function. A more nuanced approach would be to model delta(x, y) as a random variable, acknowledging the inherent noise in the feedback. This would naturally lead to incorporating an expectation over delta in the true risk setup, potentially providing tighter theoretical bounds and a more robust learning algorithm.

Third, there is a correction needed on line 170-171. It should use h(y|x) / h0(y|x), not h(y|x) - h0(y|x). This is a critical correction as the former represents the likelihood ratio central to importance sampling, while the latter has no clear interpretation. Furthermore, the paper should acknowledge that the non-linearity of this ratio was previously noted in Beygelzimer & Langford [2].

Fourth, on line 276, “argmax” should be “argmin” since the risk is being minimized. This is a straightforward typographical error but important to correct.

Finally, a minor notation fix is needed on line 319, where bars should be replaced with hats to maintain consistency with earlier notation.

**Strengths:**

*   **Technical Soundness:** The paper is technically well-grounded, building on a solid foundation of related work. The proposed estimator is mathematically justified, and the empirical results support the claims.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the problem, the proposed solution, and the experimental setup.
*   **Originality:** The identification of propensity overfitting is a novel contribution, and the self-normalized estimator offers a new perspective on counterfactual learning.
*   **Significance:** The results are important and address a practical problem in online learning. The improvement over POEM is substantial, and the proposed algorithm is likely to be adopted by researchers and practitioners in the field.

**Weaknesses:**

*   Lack of clarity regarding delta rescaling.
*   Deterministic treatment of delta(x,y) should be revisited.
*   Typographical errors (lines 170-171, 276, 319)
*   Missing citation [2].

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Addresses a critical problem in BLBF with a novel solution.
*   Strong theoretical justification and empirical validation.
*   Clear and well-written presentation.
*   Significant improvement over existing methods.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   Minor technical clarifications and corrections are needed.
*   Potential benefit of modeling delta(x, y) as a random variable could be explored.

**Post-Rebuttal Assessment:**

The reviewer ratings of Quality: 6, Clarity: 6, Originality: 8, and Significance: 8 accurately reflect the paper's strengths and weaknesses. While not perfect, the paper represents a significant contribution to the field and addresses an important practical problem. The identified issues are relatively minor and can be easily addressed by the authors.



Overall, I recommend accepting this paper. The contributions are valuable, the presentation is clear, and the empirical results are convincing. Addressing the minor corrections and clarifications will further strengthen the paper and enhance its impact.
