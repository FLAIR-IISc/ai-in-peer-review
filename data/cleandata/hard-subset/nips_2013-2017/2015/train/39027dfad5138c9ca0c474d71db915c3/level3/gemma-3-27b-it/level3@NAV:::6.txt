## Review of "The Self-Normalized Estimator for Counterfactual Learning"

This paper addresses a critical problem in batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF): propensity overfitting, where standard counterfactual risk minimization (CRM) algorithms can exploit biases in the logged data leading to poor generalization. The authors convincingly argue that the conventional unbiased risk estimator used in prior work is susceptible to this overfitting, particularly with complex hypothesis spaces. They propose a self-normalized estimator as a remedy, theoretically demonstrating its consistency and variance control. Empirical validation on multi-label classification tasks consistently shows Norm-POEM, their algorithm based on the proposed estimator, outperforms the standard CRM approach (POEM). 

The work builds upon the growing body of research in off-policy evaluation and causal inference, particularly within the context of learning from observational data (e.g., [7, 8, 4]). It directly responds to challenges highlighted in previous BLBF work like POEM [1], extending the CRM framework with a novel estimator. The connection to importance sampling and self-normalization techniques [12, 19] is clearly articulated, providing valuable context for readers familiar with these areas.

A significant strength of this paper is the clear identification and articulation of "propensity overfitting," a previously unobserved failure mode. The examples provided effectively illustrate the issue, and the theoretical justification for the self-normalized estimator is sound. The experimental results are also compelling; Norm-POEM consistently shows improved performance and demonstrates robustness to loss translation, supporting the theoretical claims. The detailed comparison to POEM and analysis of Ŝ(h) values in Table 2 are particularly insightful.

However, the paper isn't without minor weaknesses. While the theoretical results are clear, the practical implications of the variance estimation in Section 6, specifically the challenges with stochastic optimization, could be further explored. It would also be beneficial to see a more detailed ablation study examining the individual contributions of the self-normalization and variance regularization components. Furthermore, while the experimental setup is well-defined, extending the evaluation to a broader range of datasets and potentially different hypothesis spaces would further strengthen the claims.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

* Addresses a significant and practical problem in BLBF.
* Presents a novel and theoretically justified solution.
* Strong empirical results demonstrate improved performance.
* Clear and well-written explanation of the problem and proposed solution.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

* Practical implications of variance estimation are not fully explored.
* Limited scope of experimental evaluation.



**Overall:** This is a strong paper with a valuable contribution to the field of learning from logged bandit feedback. Despite minor weaknesses, the clear articulation of the problem, the elegant solution, and the compelling experimental results outweigh the concerns. I recommend acceptance, though a few targeted revisions could further enhance the work.
