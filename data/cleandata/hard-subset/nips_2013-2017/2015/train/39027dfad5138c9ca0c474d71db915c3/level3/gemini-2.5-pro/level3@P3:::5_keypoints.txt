- Identifies a new failure mode, "propensity overfitting," in inverse propensity score (IPS) estimators for batch learning from logged bandit feedback.  
- Propensity overfitting occurs when learning exploits propensity weights (favoring large-weight examples for negative losses or avoiding them for positive losses), producing biased risk estimates and poor generalization.  
- Proposes replacing IPS with a self-normalized (ratio) estimator using the sum of propensity weights as a control variate.  
- The self-normalized estimator is equivariant to loss translations, bounded by observed loss range, and resolves the anomalies causing propensity overfitting.  
- Introduces Norm-POEM, a normalized policy optimizer for exponential models, as an improvement over POEM.  
- Originality/significance: novel formalization of propensity overfitting and a principled estimator-based solution advancing counterfactual learning.  
- Technical quality: solid theoretical arguments, a concrete motivating example, control-variates justification, and proved/argued estimator properties.  
- Experimental methodology: rigorous supervised-to-bandit conversion with strong empirical results showing lower generalization error, corrected propensity bias, and robustness to loss translations.  
- Clarity: paper is exceptionally well-written, intuitive, and well-structured with clear connections to prior work.  
- Practical impact: Norm-POEM consistently outperforms POEM across datasets and often trains faster due to better convergence.  
- Scalability concern: the variance term in Norm-POEM complicates stochastic optimization (e.g., SGD); discussion of large-scale adaptations would be helpful.  
- Bias-variance trade-off: the self-normalized estimator is consistent but biased in finite samples; more analysis comparing this bias to clipping in POEM is desirable.  
- Model generality: experiments focus on linear exponential models; commentary on applicability and challenges for non-linear models (e.g., deep networks) is needed.  
- Overall assessment: high-quality, significant contribution with minor weaknesses; strongly recommended for acceptance.
