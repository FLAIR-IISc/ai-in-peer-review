Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF) using importance sampling (IS) to reweight logged data for off-policy learning/evaluation. Prior BLBF algorithms often instantiate the Counterfactual Risk Minimization (CRM) principle with inverse propensity scoring (IPS), possibly with clipping and variance regularization, and related lines include covariate shift weighting, off-policy evaluation, and doubly robust (DR) estimators. The central contribution here is to identify a distinct failure mode—propensity overfitting—where empirical risk minimization (ERM) over expressive hypothesis classes exploits randomness in propensities rather than signal in the losses, producing degenerate IPS risk estimates. The authors propose replacing the conventional estimator with the self-normalized (SN) IS estimator (a multiplicative control variate), yielding bounded, equivariant estimates and strong consistency, and integrate it into a CRM-style learner, Norm-POEM, for structured prediction with exponential-family policies. Empirically, across several multi-label datasets using the supervised-to-bandit protocol, Norm-POEM consistently improves generalization versus POEM, its IPS-based counterpart, while keeping Ŝ(h) close to 1 and exhibiting robustness to loss translations. This connects classical Monte Carlo ideas (self-normalized IS) and control variates to the BLBF learning problem and provides evidence that the SN estimator mitigates propensity overfitting in practice.

Quality
- Theory: The paper makes a compelling case that IPS-based ERM can be pathological: the estimator is not equivariant, its finite-sample range can exceed the loss range, and ERM can “optimize the weights” via Ŝ(h) rather than align with low-loss regions. The SN estimator eliminates these anomalies (boundedness, equivariance) and is strongly consistent. The approximate variance formula for the ratio estimator is standard, and clipping is used to ensure finite variance. One theoretical gap is that the CRM bound is stated for the clipped IPS estimator; a corresponding finite-sample bound tailored to SN-IPS (or at least a careful translation showing how the approximate variance control in Eq. 8 enters the generalization guarantee) would strengthen the learning justification.
- Empirics: The experimental design follows prior BLBF work, compares fairly to POEM under the same optimizer (L-BFGS), and evaluates sensitivity to replay count and loss translations. It would be helpful to add DR-CRM baselines and explicitly tuned clipped-IPS baselines, as well as ablations isolating normalization vs clipping vs variance regularization. Reporting wall-clock times and iteration counts would substantiate the claimed optimization advantages.
- Technical correctness: I did not fully verify all derivations, but the use of SN-IS and variance approximations is standard in Monte Carlo. The assumption of full support of the logging policy is clearly stated and necessary.

Clarity
The paper is generally well written and well organized. Two clarifications would improve readability:
- Relationship between variance and propensity overfitting: Please make explicit how propensity overfitting differs from “just high variance.” High variance is a property of an estimator for a fixed h. Propensity overfitting arises because, during ERM over H, the optimizer can systematically select hypotheses for which realized Ŝ(h) deviates from its expectation (1), thereby biasing the empirical objective in a direction unrelated to true risk. This is partially correlated with large weight variance, but the phenomenon is about selection bias over H driven by weight fluctuations; normalization counteracts it by coupling numerator and denominator.
- Example around line 199: When n is small relative to k and xi are distinct, hoverfit sets f(xi)=yi, so h(yi|xi)=1 and pi=1/k under uniform logging, yielding weights of k. Since δ∈{−2,−1}, the empirical risk averages δi·k, which can be as low as roughly −k (or below in expectation, −k−1). This is the mechanism by which ERM prefers hoverfit over h∗; please spell out this computation more explicitly.

Originality
Self-normalized IS and control variates are classical. The novelty lies in (i) articulating propensity overfitting as a distinct learning-time pathology for IPS-based CRM, (ii) showing that SN-IS directly addresses the equivariance/range anomalies that enable it, and (iii) integrating SN-IS into a structured prediction learner with a practical variance-regularized objective. This seems a meaningful and useful reframing with moderate conceptual originality and solid practical implications.

Significance
BLBF is central to recommender systems, ads, and search, and the community routinely grapples with heavy-tailed propensities and estimator instability. A simple, well-motivated change of estimator that improves robustness and generalization is valuable. The empirical gains across multiple datasets and the diagnostic Ŝ(h) results are persuasive. I expect practitioners to adopt SN-IS within CRM-style learners.

Specific comments and questions
- Prior work characterization: The paper asserts that “virtually all existing works” used an unbiased estimator. Several BLBF papers do employ clipping and DR, which introduce bias. Please qualify this claim and distinguish learning-time objectives that use raw IPS versus clipped IPS/DR.
- Theoretical guarantees with SN: Can you provide a high-probability generalization bound analogous to the CRM bound but for the ratio estimator, or at least discuss the conditions under which the variance-regularized SN objective controls excess risk?
- Diagnostics: Ŝ(h)≈1 is a nice diagnostic. Consider recommending this as a general health check and showing plots of Ŝ(h) vs risk across candidate models.
- Baselines: Add DR-CRM and clipped-IPS-CRM (with tuned M) baselines; report sensitivity to M and λ; include confidence intervals.
- Computational claims: Provide wall-clock runtimes, iteration counts, and norms of w to substantiate the convergence discussion.
- Code availability is appreciated; please include instructions for reproducing tables and random seeds.

Pros
- Clear identification and explanation of propensity overfitting with convincing toy and empirical evidence.
- Simple, principled fix via self-normalized IS with strong practical benefits.
- Consistent improvements over POEM across datasets and training regimes.
- Useful diagnostics (Ŝ(h)) and robustness to loss translations.
- Code release.

Cons
- Lack of a dedicated finite-sample bound for the SN objective.
- Limited baselines (no DR-CRM), limited sensitivity analyses.
- Some presentation claims overgeneralize prior work; a few derivations could be explained more explicitly.

Recommendation
Accept. The paper addresses an important and pervasive issue in counterfactual learning with a principled, simple method that improves robustness and performance, and it will likely influence both practice and follow-on research.
