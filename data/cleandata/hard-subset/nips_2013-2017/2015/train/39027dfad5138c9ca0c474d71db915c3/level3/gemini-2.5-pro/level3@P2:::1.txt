This paper addresses the problem of Batch Learning from Logged Bandit Feedback (BLBF), a setting where a learner aims to find an optimal policy using historical interaction logs. The standard approach relies on Counterfactual Risk Minimization (CRM), which uses an importance-weighted risk estimator. The central contribution of this work is the identification of a critical failure mode of the conventional (inverse propensity score) estimator, which the authors term "Propensity Overfitting". This issue arises when a learner with a high-capacity hypothesis space minimizes the empirical risk not by fitting the observed losses, but by exploiting the propensity weights themselves—either by assigning high probability to points seen in the training data (for negative losses) or by avoiding them entirely (for positive losses). This can lead to arbitrarily poor risk estimates that are untethered from the actual range of the loss function.

To remedy this, the authors propose replacing the conventional estimator with a self-normalized (ratio) estimator, which is well-known in the Monte Carlo literature. This estimator is motivated as using the sum of importance weights as a multiplicative control variate. The authors show that this self-normalized estimator is equivariant to translations in the loss function and its estimates are naturally bounded by the range of the observed losses, thus directly resolving the pathologies that lead to propensity overfitting. They instantiate this idea in a new algorithm, Norm-POEM, and demonstrate through experiments on several multi-label classification tasks that it is substantially more robust and achieves better generalization performance than its predecessor, POEM, which uses the conventional estimator.

**Strengths:**

*   **Originality & Significance:** The key contribution—identifying, naming, and analyzing "propensity overfitting"—is novel and important. While the community is well aware of the high variance of importance sampling estimators, this paper provides a new and valuable lens through which to view their failure modes, specifically in a learning (i.e., optimization) context rather than just an evaluation context. It clearly distinguishes this issue from standard "loss overfitting" and shows how it can be particularly severe. This is a significant contribution that could influence how researchers approach optimization in off-policy and counterfactual settings.

*   **Quality:** The proposed solution is simple, elegant, and theoretically well-motivated. The connection to control variates and the analysis of the self-normalized estimator's desirable properties (equivariance, boundedness, consistency) are technically sound. The empirical evaluation is strong, using a controlled simulation environment that allows for a clear demonstration of the problem and the effectiveness of the proposed fix. The provision of code is a welcome addition that enhances reproducibility.

*   **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The illustrative example in Section 4 is particularly effective at building intuition for why propensity overfitting occurs and why it is a severe problem. The experiments are clearly described and the results convincingly support the paper's claims.

**Weaknesses and Questions for the Authors:**

I must preface my comments by noting that I had a limited amount of time for this review, and I may have misunderstood some of the nuances. My comments are based on an initial, though careful, reading.

1.  **Relationship between Propensity Overfitting and High Variance:** The paper could benefit from a more explicit discussion of the relationship between propensity overfitting and the classic problem of high-variance importance weights. A large deviation of the empirical sum of weights `Ŝ(h)` from its expectation of 1 is a classic indicator of a high-variance estimate. Is propensity overfitting simply a consequence of a learning algorithm exploiting this high variance within a flexible hypothesis class, or is there a more subtle distinction? Clarifying whether propensity overfitting is a cause, a symptom, or a co-occurring phenomenon with high variance would strengthen the paper's conceptual contribution.

2.  **Clarity of Prior Work's Estimators:** The paper states that "virtually all existing works on BLBF have focused on a particular unbiased estimator." While the foundational estimator is unbiased, many practical algorithms (e.g., [1, 5]) employ clipping on the importance weights, which introduces bias to control variance. The paper does mention this but could be more precise about whether the propensity overfitting problem applies equally to these biased-but-lower-variance estimators. The argument in line 186 that "thresholding schemes ... only exacerbate this effect" is interesting and could be expanded upon slightly.

3.  **Clarification on Example 1:** In the discussion of Example 1 (around line 199), the argument for how `hoverfit` achieves an arbitrarily low risk of `-k` is very quick. While the logic appears to hold (if all `x_i` are distinct, one can construct a policy that perfectly matches the observed `y_i`, yielding importance weights of `k`), spelling out the steps of the calculation more explicitly would make this powerful example more accessible and immediately convincing to all readers.

**Summary of Evaluation:**

*   **Quality:** The paper appears technically sound. The claims are well-supported by theoretical motivation and strong experimental results.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is well-written, though minor clarifications on the relationship between key concepts and on the details of the central example would be beneficial.
*   **Originality:** High. The identification and formalization of "propensity overfitting" is a novel and insightful contribution.
*   **Significance:** High. The work addresses a fundamental problem in a popular and important learning paradigm. The findings are likely to be influential for researchers and practitioners working on off-policy evaluation and learning.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Identifies a novel, subtle, and severe problem in counterfactual learning.
*   Proposes an elegant, well-motivated, and effective solution.
*   The solution is shown to be empirically superior and more robust than the prior state-of-the-art.
*   The paper is well-written and the findings are of high importance to the NIPS community.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   The relationship to the well-known problem of high-variance estimators could be drawn more clearly.
*   (Minor) The presentation of some of the technical arguments could be slightly more detailed for clarity.

**Recommendation:**

Despite my minor reservations, I find this to be an interesting and important paper. The identification of propensity overfitting is a valuable contribution, and the proposed solution is both principled and effective. I recommend acceptance.
