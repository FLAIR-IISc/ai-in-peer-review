

This paper identifies a critical flaw in the counterfactual risk estimation framework for batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF), proposes a novel self-normalized estimator to resolve it, and demonstrates significant empirical improvements. The work builds directly on Swaminathan & Joachims (NIPS 2015) but addresses a previously overlooked issue—propensity overfitting—that plagues the conventional unbiased estimator used in CRM (Counterfactual Risk Minimization) algorithms. The authors demonstrate that standard estimators can produce arbitrarily biased risk estimates when minimizing empirical risk over complex hypothesis spaces, leading to degenerate solutions that consistently outperform the true optimal policy in finite samples. This problem is distinct from standard loss overfitting and persists even under loss translation, as shown through rigorous theoretical examples (Section 4). The proposed self-normalized estimator (Equation 7) resolves these anomalies by leveraging multiplicative control variates, ensuring equivariance under loss translation and bounded risk estimates. This leads to Norm-POEM, a straightforward modification of POEM that replaces the risk estimator while retaining the same computational complexity.  

**Strengths:**  
*Quality:* The theoretical analysis is sound and compelling. Theorem 2 proves strong consistency of the self-normalized estimator, and Section 4’s examples rigorously demonstrate the propensity overfitting problem. The empirical evaluation (Section 7) is thorough, using standard BLBF benchmarks (Scene, Yeast, TMC, LYRL) and showing consistent improvements over POEM across all datasets and training sizes (Table 1, Figure 1). The runtime analysis (Section 7) is particularly valuable—Norm-POEM converges faster than POEM despite added normalization, due to better-behaved optimization (shorter ‖w‖ and bounded overfit risk).  
*Clarity:* The paper is exceptionally well-organized and readable. The propensity overfitting problem is introduced via a clear, intuitive example (Section 4), and the solution is motivated logically. Theoretical results are concisely presented, and the experimental setup is transparent and reproducible (identical to POEM’s, with hyperparameters calibrated identically).  
*Originality:* The identification of propensity overfitting as a novel failure mode in BLBF is highly original. While self-normalized estimators exist in importance sampling (cited from [19]), their application to counterfactual learning—specifically to fix this previously unaddressed vulnerability—is a significant contribution. The paper clearly differentiates this from prior work (e.g., clipping [15] or additive control variates [16]) and shows it resolves a fundamental flaw in the CRM framework.  
*Significance:* This work has immediate practical impact for real-world BLBF applications (ad placement, recommendations), where historical logs are ubiquitous. Norm-POEM consistently outperforms POEM across diverse datasets, offering both improved generalization and computational efficiency. The method requires minimal implementation changes (replacing one estimator), making it highly adoptable. The theoretical insights also advance the understanding of counterfactual learning, with implications beyond BLBF (e.g., off-policy evaluation in reinforcement learning).  

**Weaknesses:**  
The paper could briefly address why propensity overfitting was overlooked in prior work (e.g., POEM [1] or CRM [1]), though this is a minor point given the novelty of the problem. The variance regularization (λ) remains necessary (Table 3), but the authors correctly note this is expected and consistent with CRM.  

**Pro/Con for Acceptance:**  
*Pro:* Solves a critical, previously unaddressed flaw in a widely used BLBF framework; theoretical and empirical results are robust, clear, and impactful; offers a simple yet powerful fix with practical advantages (faster convergence, better generalization); directly addresses NIPS’s emphasis on high-impact, well-grounded contributions.  
*Con:* None substantial. The work is self-contained, well-executed, and significantly advances the state of the art.  

**Overall Recommendation:**  
This paper makes a strong, original contribution to the BLBF literature. It identifies a fundamental problem overlooked in prior work, proposes a theoretically sound and empirically validated solution, and demonstrates tangible improvements in both performance and efficiency. The quality of the analysis, clarity of exposition, and significance of the results align perfectly with NIPS’s standards. The paper is clearly written, well-structured, and makes a compelling case for the importance of the self-normalized estimator. It represents a meaningful extension of Swaminathan & Joachims (NIPS 2015) that advances the field significantly. I strongly recommend acceptance.
