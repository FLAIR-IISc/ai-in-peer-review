This paper addresses the problem of counterfactual learning, a specific setting of batch learning from logged bandit feedback. It builds directly upon previous work, particularly the Policy Optimizer for Exponential Models (POEM) from ICML 2015, by identifying and proposing a solution to a perceived problem termed "propensity overfitting" in the standard inverse propensity score (IPS) estimator. The authors propose replacing this conventional estimator with a self-normalized variant, leading to a new learning algorithm, Norm-POEM. The paper demonstrates through extensive experiments that Norm-POEM is more robust and achieves superior generalization performance.

The primary strengths of this paper are its strong empirical results and the principled nature of the proposed solution. The experiments are convincing, showing that Norm-POEM consistently outperforms the original POEM across all reported multi-label classification datasets. The fact that its performance approaches that of a Conditional Random Field trained on full-information feedback is a significant achievement and clearly demonstrates the practical value and significance of the contribution. Furthermore, the proposed self-normalized estimator from equation (7) is preferable in principle, as its double-denominator structure effectively mollifies the sensitivity to small propensity scores (π) in the denominator, a well-known issue with IPS estimators.

However, the paper suffers from a significant flaw in its theoretical motivation. The commentary surrounding equation (2) is misleading; this equation represents a finite sample Monte Carlo estimate of the risk, not the expected quantity itself. The authors' subsequent claim that this estimator has an "anomaly"—specifically, that it is not invariant to a constant translation of the loss—is therefore technically incorrect. This property is a known characteristic of the finite sample estimator, not a fundamental anomaly of the counterfactual risk. Consequently, Example 1, which is constructed entirely upon this flawed premise, does not hold and fails to compellingly illustrate the "propensity overfitting" problem as described.

Despite this flawed motivation, the proposed estimator is sound and the empirical results speak for themselves. The paper is a valuable contribution to the field. I recommend acceptance, but with the strong condition that the authors revise their commentary. The motivation for the self-normalized estimator should be reframed around its known benefits—equivariance, bounded estimates, and robustness to variance from the propensity weights—rather than the incorrect claims about the anomalies of the standard estimator.
