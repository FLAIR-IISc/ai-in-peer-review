This paper addresses batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF), an important setting for off-policy learning. The authors identify a key failure mode of the standard inverse propensity score (IPS) estimator, which they term "propensity overfitting." This problem, distinct from the well-known issue of high importance-weight variance, arises when a complex model exploits the logging policy to achieve an artificially low empirical risk, for instance by assigning probability to actions unobserved in the training data for a given context. To remedy this, the paper proposes using the self-normalized importance sampling estimator, which is a ratio estimator known to be more stable. This leads to a new learning algorithm, Norm-POEM, based on the Counterfactual Risk Minimization (CRM) framework. Empirical evaluations demonstrate that Norm-POEM significantly and consistently outperforms its predecessor.

**Quality and Significance:** The paper's primary strength is its clear identification and explanation of propensity overfitting. This is an important, subtle, and practical problem, and the conceptual framing is a valuable contribution to the field. The proposed solution is well-motivated, leveraging a known technique from the importance sampling literature and showing its direct applicability to avoiding this overfitting. The experimental results are strong, providing compelling evidence that the proposed method leads to better and more robust generalization.

**Originality:** While the self-normalized estimator is not new, its application to mitigate this specific, newly-identified "propensity overfitting" problem in the context of CRM is novel. The work builds directly upon previous research in BLBF (e.g., POEM) but makes a clear and significant improvement.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-written and the core argument is easy to follow. The inclusion of a simple, illustrative example is very effective. The provision of code is a major asset for reproducibility.

However, I have a few points for clarification:
1.  The relationship between propensity overfitting and high weight variance could be explained more clearly. It seems that high variance is a prerequisite for the extreme risk estimates that characterize propensity overfitting. A more detailed discussion on their interplay would be beneficial.
2.  The claim that prior BLBF works relied on an "unbiased" estimator should be nuanced. Many standard methods employ clipping or other thresholding schemes, which knowingly introduce bias to control variance.
3.  Regarding the example around line 199: While the calculation for `R̂(hoverfit)` is clear, the behavior of ERM when n is small relative to k could be elaborated upon.

Despite these minor points, and acknowledging that I had limited time for this review, the paper's contribution is strong. The problem it identifies is important, the solution is sensible, and the results are compelling. I recommend acceptance.
