This paper addresses a critical issue in batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF): propensity overfitting within the commonly used counterfactual risk estimator. The authors convincingly demonstrate that this estimator can lead to severely biased risk estimates, particularly with complex hypothesis spaces. Their proposed solution, a self-normalized estimator, elegantly mitigates this problem, resulting in a new learning algorithm, Norm-POEM, which empirically outperforms existing approaches.

The work is well-motivated, building directly upon the authors’ prior work [1] and acknowledging connections to relevant areas like causal inference, domain adaptation, and off-policy evaluation. The introduction clearly articulates the problem and its relevance to real-world applications such as ad placement and recommendation systems. The related work section is thorough, accurately positioning the contribution within the broader literature and identifying key differences from existing techniques. The mathematical presentation is clear and concise, making the core ideas accessible. The paper is well-written overall and easy to follow.

The key contribution is the self-normalized risk estimator. While leveraging existing concepts from importance sampling and control variates, the authors correctly identify and address a previously unrecognized form of overfitting specific to the BLBF setting. The resulting Norm-POEM algorithm represents a practical application of this estimator, and the experimental results demonstrate its effectiveness across several datasets.  The theoretical justification, particularly Theorem 2 guaranteeing strong consistency, adds further weight to the contribution.

However, the paper falls short in several areas.  While the empirical results are positive, the experimental evaluation feels somewhat limited. The datasets used are the same as in [1], and while the authors demonstrate consistency across these datasets, the generalizability to more diverse or real-world scenarios remains unclear.  The significance of these gains is therefore uncertain.  Demonstrating improvements on additional, more challenging datasets, or perhaps even datasets directly sourced from industry, would significantly strengthen the claims. 

Furthermore, the reviewer’s expectation for a more in-depth analysis of the training-objective optimization process was not met. The paper mentions the computational challenges of the variance estimator and the use of L-BFGS, but doesn't delve into details about the optimization landscape or the impact of different hyperparameter settings. Exploring alternative optimization strategies or a more detailed analysis of convergence behavior would be valuable. The variance reporting requested by the reviewer is absent; reporting the variance across repeated runs would provide crucial insights into the stability and reliability of the algorithm.  

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Addresses a significant and previously unrecognized problem in BLBF.
*   Presents a theoretically sound and elegant solution (self-normalized estimator).
*   Demonstrates empirical improvements over existing methods on established benchmarks.
*   Clear and well-written presentation.
*   Builds on strong prior work ([1]).

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   Limited experimental evaluation - relies heavily on datasets from [1].
*   Unclear real-world impact - needs demonstration on more diverse or real-world data.
*   Insufficient analysis of the optimization process.
*   Missing variance reporting for repeated runs.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a solid paper with a valuable contribution to the field of batch learning from bandit feedback. The identified problem of propensity overfitting is important, and the proposed solution is both theoretically sound and empirically effective. However, the limitations in the experimental evaluation and the lack of detailed analysis prevent me from giving a strong recommendation for acceptance *at this time*. I believe the paper has the potential to be a significant contribution to NIPS with some revisions addressing the points mentioned above. Specifically, expanding the experimental evaluation and providing variance estimates are crucial for establishing the robustness and generalizability of the proposed approach.



**Rating:** Weak Accept.  I would recommend acceptance contingent on major revisions addressing the concerns outlined above.
