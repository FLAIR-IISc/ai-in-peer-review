

This paper identifies a critical issue in batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF) - "propensity overfitting" - which occurs when using the conventional inverse propensity score estimator for counterfactual risk minimization. The authors propose a self-normalized risk estimator that avoids this problem, leading to a new algorithm called Norm-POEM, which consistently outperforms the previous POEM approach on multiple multi-label classification datasets.

The paper builds on Swaminathan and Joachims' 2015 NIPS paper (CRM principle) but identifies a previously overlooked anomaly in the conventional estimator. The authors demonstrate through theoretical analysis and experiments that the standard estimator can produce arbitrarily biased risk estimates, particularly when the hypothesis space is complex. This is distinct from conventional overfitting as it arises from the propensity weights rather than the loss function. The self-normalized estimator, which uses multiplicative control variates, resolves the key anomalies: it's equivariant under loss translation, produces bounded risk estimates, and avoids propensity overfitting.

The paper makes several important technical contributions. The theoretical analysis of propensity overfitting is insightful and well-motivated by concrete examples. The authors correctly note that the propensity score should be h(y|x)/h_0(y|x) (not h_0(y|x)/h(y|x) as in lines 170-171), which aligns with the importance sampling literature. The derivation of the self-normalized estimator is clear, and the proof of strong consistency (Theorem 2) is technically sound. The Norm-POEM algorithm is well-motivated as a natural extension of POEM that replaces the conventional estimator with the self-normalized version.

The experimental evaluation is thorough and convincing. The authors use the Supervised 7→ Bandit methodology to create controlled bandit feedback datasets from standard multi-label classification problems. The results in Table 1 show consistent improvements for Norm-POEM over POEM across multiple datasets, with the performance gap increasing as the training set size grows (Figure 1). The analysis in Table 2 provides strong evidence that Norm-POEM avoids propensity overfitting, as evidenced by the normalized propensity weight Ŝ(ĥ) being close to 1 (as expected), while POEM shows severe bias. The authors correctly note that variance regularization remains beneficial even with the self-normalized estimator (Table 3).

The paper would benefit from a few clarifications. First, the paper should explicitly state whether they rescale the loss to [-1,0] as in the CRM principle (Section 3), as the experiments in Table 1 reference "delta > 0" which requires explanation. Second, the authors should model δ(x,y) as a random variable with expectation over label noise, rather than a deterministic function, to better align with the true risk formulation. Third, the optimization in line 276 should use argmin rather than argmax since the risk is being minimized. Finally, the notation in line 319 should use hats instead of bars to maintain consistency with earlier conventions.

The paper is well-written and clearly organized, with a strong theoretical foundation and compelling empirical results. The main contribution is the identification and solution of propensity overfitting, which has been a subtle but important issue in the BLBF literature. The self-normalized estimator is a simple yet effective modification that yields significant practical improvements. The authors make a convincing case that this approach advances the state of the art in BLBF.

The paper's significance is high as BLBF is crucial for improving real-world interactive systems (ads, search, recommendations) using historical logs. The proposed method is both theoretically sound and empirically effective, with the added benefit of computational efficiency (Norm-POEM often converges faster than POEM). The authors' analysis of the problem's root causes provides valuable insights for the field.

This work builds meaningfully on prior NIPS contributions, particularly Swaminathan and Joachims' 2015 paper, while addressing a critical gap in the existing literature. The problem is well-motivated, the solution is elegant, and the empirical results are robust across multiple datasets and experimental conditions.

The paper has high quality (6/10), good clarity (6/10), and excellent originality (8/10) and significance (8/10). The key strength is the insightful analysis of propensity overfitting and the elegant solution using a self-normalized estimator. The main weakness is the need for minor technical clarifications as noted above, but these don't detract from the paper's core contribution.

Overall, this is a strong contribution to the field that provides both theoretical insights and practical improvements for batch learning from logged bandit feedback. The paper advances the state of the art in a demonstrable way and will be valuable to researchers and practitioners working with logged bandit feedback. I recommend acceptance.
