

# Review of "The Self-Normalized Estimator for Counterfactual Learning" (NIPS 2015)

This paper identifies a critical issue in batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF), termed "propensity overfitting," which arises when using conventional counterfactual risk estimators. The authors demonstrate that the standard unbiased importance sampling estimator (Equation 2) can produce severely biased risk estimates for certain hypotheses in complex hypothesis spaces, particularly when the training data has insufficient exploration (i.e., for a given x, few or no y values relevant to the new hypothesis are observed). They propose replacing this estimator with a self-normalized estimator (Equation 7) that avoids this problem and derive a new learning algorithm called Norm-POEM.

The paper builds upon prior work on counterfactual risk minimization (CRM) [1] and importance sampling [5, 11], but makes an important distinction by focusing on "propensity overfitting" rather than just the well-known issue of high variance in importance weights. The authors provide a compelling example (Example 1) showing how conventional estimators can produce arbitrarily low risk estimates for overfitted hypotheses, unlike supervised learning where risk estimates are bounded by the loss range.

**Strengths:**
1. The paper identifies a novel problem (propensity overfitting) that wasn't previously recognized in the BLBF literature, with clear theoretical and empirical justification.
2. The self-normalized estimator offers elegant theoretical properties: it's equivariant under loss translation, bounded within the loss range, and avoids the degeneracies of the conventional estimator.
3. The empirical evaluation is thorough, demonstrating consistent improvements of Norm-POEM over POEM across multiple datasets, with clear evidence that Norm-POEM avoids propensity overfitting (Table 2).
4. The authors provide code, which greatly facilitates reproducibility and adoption.
5. The paper is well-motivated and clearly explains the practical relevance to real-world applications like ad placement and recommendation systems.

**Weaknesses:**
1. The relationship between large weight variance and propensity overfitting is not sufficiently clarified. While the paper identifies propensity overfitting as a distinct problem, it's not entirely clear how it relates to the standard importance sampling variance issues discussed in Section 2.
2. The analysis in Section 4, particularly the claim that "the empirical risk R̂(h*) of h* concentrates around -2" (line 199), appears questionable when n is small relative to k. The behavior of R̂(h^) for small n seems unclear to the reviewer.
3. The paper claims prior BLBF works used "unbiased counterfactual risk estimators," but many prior methods (e.g., [15]) actually used clipped importance weights, which introduce bias. This requires clarification.
4. The theoretical analysis of the self-normalized estimator (Theorem 2) is correct but brief, and the connection to the practical benefits could be strengthened.

**Quality:** The paper presents a sound theoretical argument for the propensity overfitting problem and the self-normalized estimator. While I did not fully verify all technical details due to time constraints, the proposed solution appears logically sound and well-motivated. The empirical results support the theoretical claims.

**Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized, with clear explanations of the problem and solution. The example in Section 4 is particularly effective at illustrating the propensity overfitting problem. However, the connection between propensity overfitting and standard importance sampling variance issues could be made clearer.

**Originality:** This paper makes a significant contribution by identifying and addressing a previously unrecognized problem in BLBF. While the self-normalized estimator has been used in other contexts (e.g., [19]), its application to BLBF and the identification of propensity overfitting as a distinct problem constitutes a novel contribution to the field.

**Significance:** The problem identified has direct implications for real-world applications of BLBF, where poor generalization due to propensity overfitting could lead to suboptimal system performance. The proposed solution is practical, requires minimal changes to existing algorithms, and demonstrates consistent improvements across multiple datasets.

**Pro and Con Arguments for Acceptance:**
*Pro:* The paper identifies a genuine, previously unrecognized problem in BLBF with clear theoretical and empirical evidence. The proposed solution is elegant, well-motivated, and demonstrates consistent empirical improvements. The problem is significant for the field, and the solution is practical.
*Con:* Some aspects of the theoretical relationship between propensity overfitting and standard importance sampling issues could be clarified, and the claim about prior methods using unbiased estimators needs qualification.

**Recommendation:**
I recommend acceptance. Despite not having the time to fully verify all technical details, the paper presents a compelling argument for a previously unrecognized problem in BLBF with a sensible solution. The empirical results are convincing, and the code availability greatly enhances the paper's reproducibility. The paper makes a meaningful contribution to the field of counterfactual learning and has significant potential impact for practical applications. The minor concerns about theoretical connections and the prior work claim can be addressed in the camera-ready version.
