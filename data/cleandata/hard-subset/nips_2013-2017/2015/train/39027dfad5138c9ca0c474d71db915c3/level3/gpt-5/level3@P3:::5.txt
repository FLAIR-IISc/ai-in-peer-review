Summary and relation to prior work
The paper addresses batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF) under the counterfactual risk minimization (CRM) framework, building directly on Swaminathan & Joachims (2015, POEM). It identifies a concrete failure mode of the standard inverse propensity score (IPS) estimator when used inside empirical risk minimization over rich hypothesis classes: “propensity overfitting,” where the learner exploits variability in importance weights to drive empirical risk to degenerate values, often outside the loss range and non-equivariant to loss translations. The paper proposes replacing the conventional (clipped) IPS estimator with a self-normalized importance sampling (SNIS) estimator, using the sum of weights as a multiplicative control variate. This yields a counterfactual risk estimator that is bounded within the loss range and equivariant to loss translation, while remaining strongly consistent. Instantiating CRM with SNIS leads to a new learning algorithm, Norm-POEM, for structured prediction with exponential family policies. The authors provide an approximate variance estimator for the ratio estimator to plug into the CRM regularization term, and optimize the resulting non-convex objective via L-BFGS with clipping of importance weights. Experiments on several multi-label datasets created via the standard supervised-to-bandit protocol show consistent improvements over POEM, better control of propensity overfitting (measured via the normalized weight sum), robustness to loss translation, and faster convergence in practice. The work connects to classic variance reduction in importance sampling, weighted IS/off-policy evaluation, and to doubly robust and clipping approaches explored at NIPS/ICML/AISTATS/AAAI.

Strengths
- Clear identification and analysis of a real degeneracy in counterfactual learning (propensity overfitting), including an illustrative example and a practical diagnostic (deviation of the sum of normalized weights from 1).
- Principled remedy via multiplicative control variates leading to the self-normalized estimator; sound properties highlighted (boundedness, equivariance, strong consistency).
- Meaningful extension of CRM/POEM that substantially improves training stability and generalization; the formulation of Norm-POEM is significantly different from POEM in its estimator and regularizer.
- Empirical evidence is consistent and compelling: Norm-POEM improves test risk across datasets, remains stable under loss translation where POEM can fail catastrophically, and the selected models exhibit Ŝ(ĥ) ≈ 1 as desired.
- Practical impact: despite added regularization and a more complex variance term, optimization converges faster and to smaller parameter norms; per-iteration cost is similar to POEM and overall runtime is competitive.
- Writing is mostly clear and easy to follow; related work is appropriately cited and positioned.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Theory: While strong consistency of SNIS is stated, there is no finite-sample generalization bound for the CRM objective with the self-normalized estimator and its approximate variance term. The original CRM theory in POEM used empirical Bernstein arguments tailored to (clipped) IPS; extending that analysis to SNIS would strengthen the paper.
- Approximate variance: The variance estimator for the ratio estimator is based on normal approximations/delta method; discussion of conditions under which it is accurate (e.g., effective sample size, tail behavior under clipping) could be expanded, and finite-sample guarantees (even conservative ones) would help.
- Baselines: Missing comparisons to modern off-policy estimators beyond vanilla IPS/clipped IPS, e.g., doubly robust (DR), weighted DR/self-normalized DR, or variance-reduced methods that combine control variates additively and multiplicatively. Since DR is widely used, a head-to-head under the same CRM setup would clarify the space.
- Data realism: Evaluation uses the supervised-to-bandit conversion with a CRF logger trained on 5% data. While standard, results on truly logged datasets (or at least additional stress tests with sparser propensities, heavier-tail propensities, or different logging policies) would increase external validity.
- Sensitivity: More systematic sensitivity analyses for clipping threshold M and regularization λ, and for the quality/stochasticity of h0, would be useful (only some are briefly discussed).
- Scope: Experiments focus on multi-label classification with linear-exponential models; it would be helpful to comment on computational implications for larger/structured Y where partition functions are expensive, or for nonlinear policies.

Quality
- Above-average technical quality. The problem is well-formulated, the estimator is grounded in established IS theory, and the algorithmic instantiation is credible. Claims are supported by experiments that probe both performance and the proposed diagnostic. The main theoretical gap is the lack of finite-sample CRM-style bounds for SNIS with variance regularization.

Clarity
- The paper is clearly written and well-organized. The exposition of propensity overfitting, the diagnostic Ŝ(h), and the properties of SNIS are particularly clear. Minor suggestions: make explicit the conditions for the asymptotic normality assumptions used in the variance estimate; centralize the clipping strategy and its effect on bias.

Originality
- While self-normalization in IS is classical, the contribution here is original in this setting: formulating and analyzing propensity overfitting in BLBF, and integrating SNIS into the CRM learning objective for structured prediction. The resulting algorithm differs meaningfully from POEM and advances the state of the art in counterfactual learning.

Significance
- The results are interesting and practically relevant. The method addresses a common failure mode and yields better and faster training in benchmarks. Given the prevalence of counterfactual learning in recommendation/ads/search, the approach has a plausible path to widespread use, especially as a drop-in replacement within CRM/POEM-like pipelines.

Questions for the authors
- Can you provide a finite-sample generalization bound for CRM with SNIS (perhaps under clipping), analogous to the empirical Bernstein-style bound used for POEM?
- How does Norm-POEM compare to doubly robust and weighted/self-normalized DR variants under the same hypothesis class and tuning protocol?
- What are the observed effective sample sizes across datasets, and how do they correlate with variance estimates and performance?
- How sensitive are results to the clipping threshold M and λ? Are there principled selection rules beyond held-out counterfactual evaluation?

Suggestions for improvement
- Add comparisons to DR and WDR/SNDR baselines.
- Provide a finite-sample risk bound or at least concentration results for the SNIS-CRM objective with clipping.
- Include real logged-bandit data if possible, or stronger stress tests varying support and weight tails.
- Expand sensitivity analyses and provide guidelines for choosing M and λ.
- Discuss extensions to normalized DR within CRM, and to more complex policy classes.

Arguments for acceptance
- Addresses a real and under-appreciated failure mode in BLBF (propensity overfitting).
- Offers a principled, well-motivated fix with strong empirical gains and practical benefits.
- Clear writing and solid positioning with respect to prior work.
- Likely to influence practice in counterfactual learning.

Arguments against acceptance
- Missing finite-sample theory specific to the proposed CRM+SNIS objective.
- Limited baselines (no DR/WDR) and evaluation on simulated logs only.

Overall recommendation
- I recommend acceptance. The paper offers an original and practically important improvement over POEM, with above-average research quality, clear presentation, and results that warrant further research and likely adoption.
