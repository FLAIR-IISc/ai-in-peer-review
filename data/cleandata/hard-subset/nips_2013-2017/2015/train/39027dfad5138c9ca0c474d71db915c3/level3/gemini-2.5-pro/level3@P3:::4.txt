This paper addresses the problem of learning from logged bandit feedback, a crucial setting for improving interactive systems like search engines and recommender systems. The authors identify a key pathology in the conventional counterfactual risk estimator (the inverse propensity score estimator), which they term "propensity overfitting". This occurs when a model with high capacity minimizes the empirical risk not by fitting the loss, but by exploiting the propensity weights—either by assigning high probability to training examples with negative loss or by avoiding examples with positive loss.

To address this, the paper proposes replacing the standard estimator with a self-normalized estimator, a technique based on multiplicative control variates. This new estimator is shown to be equivariant to translations in the loss function and is bounded by the range of the loss, properties that the standard estimator lacks and which directly combat propensity overfitting. This leads to a new learning algorithm, Norm-POEM, which integrates the self-normalized estimator into the Counterfactual Risk Minimization (CRM) framework previously introduced in [1]. The empirical evaluation on several multi-label classification tasks demonstrates that Norm-POEM consistently and substantially outperforms the original POEM algorithm, showing improved generalization and robustness.

### Strengths

1.  **Insightful Problem Formulation:** The paper provides an excellent and clear diagnosis of "propensity overfitting." The analysis in Section 4, including the illustrative examples, convincingly demonstrates how the standard inverse propensity score estimator can lead to degenerate solutions, a subtle but severe problem for learning with complex models. This is an important contribution to the understanding of learning from bandit feedback.
2.  **Principled and Effective Solution:** The proposed solution—using a self-normalized estimator—is well-motivated by the control variate literature and directly targets the identified problem. The theoretical analysis shows that this estimator resolves the key anomalies of the unbiased estimator (equivariance, boundedness), and its strong consistency is established.
3.  **Strong Empirical Results:** The experiments are well-designed and provide compelling evidence for the effectiveness of Norm-POEM. It consistently outperforms the baseline POEM across multiple datasets and training data sizes. The ablation studies effectively demonstrate that the improved performance is indeed due to controlling propensity overfitting (Table 2) and that the CRM variance regularization remains beneficial (Table 3).
4.  **Important Follow-up Work:** This work is a significant and interesting follow-up to [1], addressing a critical weakness of the original CRM framework. By improving the core risk estimator, the authors make the overall approach more robust and practically applicable, advancing the state of the art in this important area.

### Weaknesses / Questions for the Authors

While the paper is strong, there are several points that could be clarified or corrected to improve its quality and impact.

1.  **Applicability of Theoretical Justification:** The CRM principle (Equation 3) is justified by Theorem 1, which explicitly assumes the loss `δ(·, ·) ∈ [-1, 0]`. However, the experiments in Table 1 use Hamming loss, which is non-negative. Table 2 further explores the effect of positive loss translations (`δ > 0`). Could the authors clarify whether the theoretical guarantees of Theorem 1 still hold for non-negative losses, or if the CRM objective is being applied as a well-performing heuristic in this more general setting?
2.  **Stochasticity of the Loss:** The paper notes (line 91) that the loss `δ(x, y)` is generally a noisy realization of a random variable and that the exposition can be extended by setting `δ(x, y) = E[δ | x, y]`. For greater formal clarity, it would be beneficial to model `δ(x, y)` as a random variable throughout and explicitly include an expectation over this randomness in the definition of the true risk. For example, `R(h) = E_{x~Pr(X), y~h(x), δ~P(δ|x,y)}[δ]`. This would make it clearer that the observed `δi` are single samples from this distribution and would more accurately reflect the stochastic nature of the problem.
3.  **Minor Corrections:**
    *   **Lines 170–171:** There appears to be a typo in the description of the correlation. The random variables of interest are the importance-weighted loss and the importance weight itself. The text should likely refer to `h(y|x) / h_0(y|x)` and `δ(x, y) * h(y|x) / h_0(y|x)`, not `h(y|x)h_0(y|x)`. The authors may also want to note that the non-linear relationship between the estimator and the weights was previously discussed in [1].
    *   **Line 276:** The CRM principle aims to find a hypothesis that *minimizes* a risk-plus-regularizer objective. The equation for `ĥCRM` should therefore use `argmin`, not `argmax`.
    *   **Line 319:** For notational consistency, the symbol for the learned hypothesis (e.g., in Table 2 captions) should be a hat (`ĥ`) to match the convention established in Equation (3).

### Overall Assessment

This is a well-executed paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of learning from logged bandit feedback. It identifies an important and previously under-appreciated problem with the standard counterfactual risk estimator and proposes an elegant, theoretically motivated, and empirically successful solution. The analysis is insightful and the results are strong. The identified weaknesses are minor and can be addressed in a final version. The work is original, significant, and likely to be influential for both researchers and practitioners working with logged data. I recommend acceptance.

**Quality:** 6/10 (Technically sound, but some clarifications on the theoretical assumptions and minor corrections are needed.)
**Clarity:** 6/10 (Generally well-written, but could be improved with the suggested notational clarifications and corrections.)
**Originality:** 8/10 (The identification and formalization of propensity overfitting are novel, as is the application of the self-normalized estimator in this learning context.)
**Significance:** 8/10 (The problem is of high practical importance, and the proposed solution represents a demonstrable advance over the state of the art, making the CRM framework substantially more robust.)
