- Addresses batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF) and a critical failure mode in CRM called propensity overfitting.  
- Identifies propensity overfitting: hypotheses exploit importance weights, producing severely biased risk estimates.  
- Proposes a self-normalized risk estimator using multiplicative control variates to control this failure mode.  
- Theoretical result (Theorem 2): self-normalized estimator is strongly consistent and bounded within the loss range.  
- Connects to importance sampling, causal inference, and off-policy evaluation literature while extending Swaminathan & Joachims (2013).  
- Empirical evaluation: Norm-POEM consistently outperforms POEM across multiple multi-label datasets (Scene, Yeast, TMC, LYRL).  
- Experiments show evidence that propensity overfitting causes POEM’s failure (Ŝ(ĥ) deviates from 1 in POEM but not Norm-POEM).  
- Technical quality is high: rigorous theory, compelling propensity-overfitting counterexample, and thorough empirical support.  
- Clear presentation: well-organized, accessible math, consistent notation, and good explanation of equivariance resolution.  
- Original contribution: novel identification of propensity overfitting and a distinct self-normalized solution vs. clipping/additive controls.  
- Practical significance: immediately applicable, minimal changes to CRM frameworks, and improves real-world BLBF systems.  
- Minor weakness: paper does not explore cases where logging policy h0 has limited support (requirement of full support acknowledged).  
- Other concerns: potentially niche topic, slightly higher computational cost for the estimator, and h0 full-support requirement may limit applicability.  
- Recommendation: overall strong paper with convincing theory and experiments; reviewer recommends acceptance despite some reservations about scope.
