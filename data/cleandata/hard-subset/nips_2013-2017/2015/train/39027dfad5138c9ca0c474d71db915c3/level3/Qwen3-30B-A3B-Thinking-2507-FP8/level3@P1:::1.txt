

This paper identifies a novel problem—propensity overfitting—in counterfactual risk estimation for batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF), and proposes a self-normalized estimator to address it. The setting involves learning from historical logs where only bandit feedback (not full supervision) is available for actions taken by a logging policy. Prior work on BLBF has primarily used an unbiased importance sampling estimator (R̂(h)) for counterfactual risk minimization (CRM), but the authors demonstrate that this estimator suffers from a previously unrecognized form of overfitting when optimizing over complex hypothesis spaces.

The key contribution is the identification of propensity overfitting, which occurs when the risk estimator can produce arbitrarily low (for negative losses) or high (for positive losses) estimates due to extreme importance weights, even when the true risk is bounded. The authors show that this problem arises because the conventional estimator minimizes the sum of weights Ŝ(h) = ∑ h(yi|xi)/pi rather than the true risk. They propose replacing the unbiased estimator with a self-normalized version R̂SN(h) = [∑ δi h(yi|xi)/pi] / [∑ h(yi|xi)/pi], which is equivariant under loss translation and always bounded within the loss range. This estimator avoids propensity overfitting by construction and leads to the Norm-POEM algorithm for structured output prediction.

The paper's strengths are significant. The identification of propensity overfitting fills an important gap in understanding limitations of existing BLBF methods. The self-normalized estimator is theoretically well-motivated, with a clear proof of consistency (Theorem 2) and principled derivation via multiplicative control variates. Empirically, Norm-POEM consistently outperforms POEM across multiple multi-label classification datasets (Scene, Yeast, TMC, LYRL), with statistically significant improvements. The authors also provide clear evidence that Norm-POEM avoids propensity overfitting (Table 2), as Ŝ(ĥ) remains close to 1 while POEM exhibits severe bias. The implementation is available, enhancing reproducibility.

However, several concerns warrant attention. The claim that prior BLBF works used an "unbiased" estimator appears inaccurate—most prior work (e.g., [1, 5, 6]) used clipped importance weights (R̂M(h)), which introduces bias. This undermines the paper's framing of the problem as a "severe problem of the counterfactual risk estimator typically used in BLBF." The authors should clarify whether their critique applies specifically to the unclipped estimator or to the general approach. 

The relationship between large weight variance and propensity overfitting requires deeper explanation. While the authors note that variance regularization in CRM combats propensity overfitting, they don't fully articulate why large weight variance *causes* the specific overfitting behavior they identify. For instance, in Example 1, the overfitting occurs when n is small relative to k (line 199), but the reviewer finds the behavior of R̂(hoverfit) unclear in this regime. A more detailed analysis of how insufficient exploration (few y values observed per x) interacts with the estimator would strengthen the argument.

The empirical section is thorough but could better address whether the improvement is due to the self-normalized estimator itself or the combination with CRM variance regularization. The paper shows that variance regularization still helps (Table 3), but it's unclear whether the self-normalized estimator alone would suffice. The runtime analysis is interesting (Norm-POEM converges faster than POEM), but the connection to propensity overfitting isn't explicitly drawn.

The paper is well-written and clear, with a logical flow from problem identification to solution to evaluation. The connection to causal inference (e.g., propensity score matching) and reinforcement learning (off-policy evaluation) is appropriately contextualized. The authors' focus on a practical problem (improving real-world recommendation systems) enhances significance, and the results demonstrate clear empirical advantages over prior work.

In terms of originality, the paper identifies a novel problem (propensity overfitting) not previously recognized in BLBF literature, though the self-normalized estimator itself is known in importance sampling. The key contribution is recognizing its relevance to BLBF and demonstrating its practical benefits.

The significance is high: propensity overfitting could undermine the reliability of BLBF algorithms in real applications. The authors provide a simple, effective fix that improves both theoretical guarantees and empirical performance. The paper advances the state of the art by resolving a previously unaddressed limitation in counterfactual learning.

Overall, the paper makes a valuable contribution to BLBF. While the framing of the problem could be refined to acknowledge prior work's use of clipped estimators, the core insight about propensity overfitting is novel and well-supported. The empirical results are convincing, and the code availability is a plus. Given the practical importance of the problem and the clear improvements demonstrated, the paper should be accepted. The minor concerns about framing and explanation do not undermine the paper's technical soundness or significance.
