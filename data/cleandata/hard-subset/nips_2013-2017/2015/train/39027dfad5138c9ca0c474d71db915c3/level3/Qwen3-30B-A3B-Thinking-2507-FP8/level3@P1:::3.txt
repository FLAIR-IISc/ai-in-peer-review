

The paper identifies a significant issue in batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF), specifically a previously unrecognized form of overfitting called "propensity overfitting" that plagues the conventional unbiased counterfactual risk estimator used in Counterfactual Risk Minimization (CRM) frameworks. Building on Swaminathan and Joachims' [1] POEM algorithm, the authors propose a self-normalized risk estimator that avoids this problem by using multiplicative control variates, leading to their Norm-POEM algorithm. The paper demonstrates that the conventional estimator suffers from severe anomalies: it can produce risk estimates outside the loss function's range and fails to be equivariant under loss translation, allowing hypotheses to overfit to propensity weights rather than actual loss patterns. The self-normalized estimator resolves these issues by being bounded within the loss range and equivariant under loss translation, while remaining strongly consistent.

The paper's theoretical contribution is strong, with clear proofs demonstrating the estimator's properties (Theorem 2) and a detailed analysis of the propensity overfitting problem through carefully constructed examples (Section 4). The authors convincingly show that propensity overfitting occurs regardless of loss translation, contrasting it with conventional loss overfitting in supervised learning. The Norm-POEM algorithm is well-motivated, with a clear derivation from the CRM principle using the new estimator, and the paper provides a tractable implementation using L-BFGS optimization. The empirical evaluation is thorough, comparing Norm-POEM against POEM across four multi-label classification datasets (Scene, Yeast, TMC, LYRL) using the supervised-to-bandit methodology from [1]. Results consistently show Norm-POEM outperforms POEM in terms of generalization performance (Table 1), with the self-normalized estimator successfully avoiding propensity overfitting as evidenced by Ŝ(ĥ) values close to 1 (Table 2). The paper also demonstrates that variance regularization remains beneficial even with the improved estimator (Table 3) and that Norm-POEM converges faster than POEM due to better optimization properties.

The paper is exceptionally well-written with clear organization, making the complex concepts accessible. The presentation of examples (particularly Example 1) effectively illustrates the propensity overfitting problem, and the connection to control variates from importance sampling literature is well-executed. The authors appropriately acknowledge related work, including [1] and [5], and situate their contribution within the broader context of causal inference and off-policy evaluation.

While the paper makes a valuable contribution to BLBF, there are several areas for improvement. The significance of the work is somewhat uncertain: while the problem is well-motivated and the experimental gains are consistent, the paper does not sufficiently demonstrate how these improvements translate to real-world applications beyond the controlled multi-label classification experiments. The authors claim their approach "consistently outperforms" POEM, but they do not provide variance estimates across the 10 repeated runs (Table 1), which would strengthen the statistical evidence for their claims. Additionally, the paper would benefit from more extensive experiments exploring the sensitivity of Norm-POEM to different logging policies (h0), including cases where h0 is less representative of the true optimal policy, and potentially real-world applications beyond the LibSVM datasets.

The paper's main novelty is the self-normalized risk estimator, which is a meaningful improvement to the CRM framework rather than a fundamentally new approach. While the authors build on [1], they correctly identify a critical flaw in the conventional estimator that was previously overlooked, and their solution is both theoretically sound and empirically effective. The connection to control variates in importance sampling is well-justified and adds theoretical depth to the contribution.

Quality is strong: the theoretical claims are well-supported, the experiments are comprehensive, and the authors are appropriately cautious about limitations (e.g., noting that the self-normalized estimator is biased but consistent). Clarity is excellent, with the paper being one of the clearest technical papers I've read on this topic. Originality is good, as the propensity overfitting problem and the self-normalized solution represent a significant refinement to the existing BLBF literature. Significance is moderate - the problem is relevant to important applications like ad placement and recommendation systems, but the paper doesn't sufficiently bridge the gap between the controlled experiments and real-world impact.

The paper makes a solid contribution to the BLBF literature, identifying and solving a previously unrecognized problem with important theoretical and practical implications. While the significance could be strengthened with more extensive experiments and real-world validation, the technical quality is high and the contribution is meaningful. The authors have clearly demonstrated that their approach avoids a critical flaw in existing methods, with consistent empirical improvements across multiple datasets. The paper would be strengthened by including variance estimates across runs and exploring additional experimental conditions, but as it stands, it presents a valuable and well-executed contribution to the field. I recommend acceptance with the understanding that additional experiments would further strengthen the significance argument.
