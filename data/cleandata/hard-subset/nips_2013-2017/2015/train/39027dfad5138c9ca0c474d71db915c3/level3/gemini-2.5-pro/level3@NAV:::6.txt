This paper addresses the problem of learning from logged bandit feedback, a critical area for improving interactive systems using historical data. The authors build upon the well-established Counterfactual Risk Minimization (CRM) principle. The main contribution is the identification and diagnosis of a subtle but severe failure mode in conventional CRM, which they term "propensity overfitting". This occurs when a complex hypothesis learns to exploit the importance weights themselves—by assigning high probability to examples that yield favorable weights—rather than learning to minimize the actual loss. This is a novel insight that clarifies a potential source of instability in off-policy learning.

To remedy this, the paper proposes replacing the standard unbiased risk estimator with a self-normalized estimator. This is a known technique from the importance sampling literature, but its application as a direct solution to propensity overfitting in CRM is new and elegant. The authors provide a sound theoretical justification, proving that their proposed estimator maintains consistency while being equivariant to loss translations and bounded by the loss range, thus avoiding the anomalies of the unbiased estimator.

**Strengths:**
*   **Originality and Significance:** The identification of "propensity overfitting" is a significant conceptual contribution. It provides a clear diagnosis for a problem that practitioners may have encountered without a formal name or explanation.
*   **Quality:** The proposed solution is technically sound, well-motivated, and principled. The connection to multiplicative control variates is clear and insightful. The claims are well-supported by both theoretical analysis (consistency proof) and strong empirical results on several multi-label classification tasks.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is very well-written and easy to follow. The motivating example in Section 4 is particularly effective at illustrating the core problem.

**Weaknesses:**
*   The primary weakness is that the proposed tool—the self-normalized estimator—is not itself novel. However, its application to this specific, newly identified problem in the context of CRM is a valuable contribution.
*   The experimental validation, while strong, is focused on multi-label classification. It would be beneficial to see results from other common bandit-feedback domains, like recommendation systems or ad placement, to further demonstrate the generality of the findings.

Overall, this is a strong paper that identifies an important problem and presents an effective, well-supported solution. It advances our understanding of the pitfalls of learning from logged bandit data and provides a practical tool to mitigate them. I recommend acceptance.
