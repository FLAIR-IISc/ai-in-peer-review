This paper identifies a critical issue – propensity overfitting – in batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF), a setting where evaluation relies on reweighting observed data to estimate the performance of a new policy. The authors argue that standard importance sampling techniques, while unbiased, can be severely impacted by this overfitting, particularly when the historical data lacks sufficient exploration. They propose a self-normalized estimator as a remedy, demonstrating its effectiveness through theoretical analysis and empirical evaluation on multi-label classification tasks.

The work builds upon the established framework of Counterfactual Risk Minimization (CRM) [1], and relates to broader areas of causal inference, domain adaptation, and off-policy evaluation [7, 8, 4]. While the core idea of importance sampling is well-known, the identification of *propensity* overfitting – a distinct issue from simply having large weight variance – is a valuable contribution. The authors effectively articulate how the capacity of the hypothesis space can exacerbate this problem, leading to misleading risk estimates. 

**Strengths:**

* **Important Problem:** Propensity overfitting, as described, is a genuine concern in BLBF and potentially other off-policy evaluation settings. The paper clearly highlights the limitations of standard estimators in such scenarios.
* **Sensible Solution:** The self-normalized estimator offers a pragmatic approach to address propensity overfitting, leveraging control variates to improve estimate stability and robustness. Its equivariance property, as demonstrated, is a particularly appealing characteristic.
* **Empirical Validation:** The experimental results consistently show that Norm-POEM, the algorithm built on the self-normalized estimator, outperforms the conventional approach. The inclusion of comparisons against a full-information CRF provides a useful benchmark.
* **Code Availability:** Providing code is a significant benefit, enabling reproducibility and facilitating further research.
* **Clear Presentation:** Despite the technical intricacies, the paper is generally well-written and logically structured.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Variance vs. Overfitting Connection:** The paper would benefit from a more detailed explanation of *how* large weight variance leads to propensity overfitting. While the examples illustrate the phenomenon, the precise mechanism linking the two could be elaborated. Is the high variance merely a symptom, or does it directly contribute to the overfitting by allowing certain hypotheses to dominate the estimate?
* **Claim about Prior Work:** The assertion that prior BLBF works universally employed the unbiased estimator (Equation 2) might be inaccurate. Many implementations likely utilize clipping techniques [15, 8, 5, 11], which, while reducing variance, introduce bias. Acknowledging this nuance would strengthen the discussion.
* **Example Clarification (Line 199):** The reviewer’s confusion regarding the behavior of  R̂(ĥ) in Example 1 when *n* is small relative to *k* is understandable. A clearer explanation of why an overfit hypothesis can achieve such a low empirical risk in this specific scenario would be helpful. The role of the limited sample size in enabling the construction of a hypothesis perfectly fitting the observed data, despite being poor in general, requires further clarification.
* **Theoretical Depth:** While the theorems provide a foundation, a deeper exploration of the theoretical properties of the self-normalized estimator, particularly in relation to the capacity of the hypothesis space, would be valuable. 



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* Addresses a relevant and important problem in BLBF.
* Proposes a sensible and theoretically motivated solution.
* Demonstrates strong empirical performance.
* Provides code for reproducibility.
* Contributes to a better understanding of the challenges in off-policy evaluation.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The connection between variance and overfitting could be clarified.
* The claim about prior work needs refinement.
* Further theoretical analysis of the estimator’s properties would be beneficial.



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite some minor weaknesses, I believe this paper represents a valuable contribution to the field of batch learning from logged bandit feedback. The identification of propensity overfitting is insightful, and the proposed self-normalized estimator offers a practical and effective solution. While I haven’t had time to thoroughly verify all technical details or assess the complete novelty of the approach, the core ideas are well-reasoned and supported by compelling empirical evidence. 

**Recommendation:** Accept.
