This paper identifies a critical and previously under-appreciated problem in batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF), which the authors term "propensity overfitting." This issue arises from the standard unbiased inverse propensity scoring (IPS) estimator used in Counterfactual Risk Minimization (CRM), as introduced in Swaminathan & Joachims (2015) [1]. The authors demonstrate that this estimator can produce arbitrarily poor risk estimates when the hypothesis space is sufficiently complex, as hypotheses can learn to exploit the propensity weights rather than the underlying losses. To address this, the paper proposes replacing the standard estimator with a self-normalized estimator, a technique derived from the use of multiplicative control variates in Monte Carlo methods. This leads to a new algorithm, Norm-POEM, which is empirically shown to be more robust and achieve better generalization than its predecessor, POEM [1]. The work is a direct and important follow-up to [1], clarifying a key failure mode of the original approach and providing a principled and effective solution.

**Strengths (Arguments for Acceptance):**

1.  **Insightful Problem Identification (Originality & Significance):** The primary contribution is the identification and clear articulation of "propensity overfitting." The paper provides a compelling and intuitive explanation, supported by a simple yet powerful illustrative example (Example 1), of how a learner can achieve an artificially low empirical risk by either concentrating probability mass on training examples (for negative losses) or avoiding them entirely (for positive losses). This is a subtle but severe failure mode for methods based on the standard IPS estimator, and bringing it to the community's attention is a significant contribution.

2.  **Principled and Elegant Solution (Quality & Originality):** The proposed solution—using a self-normalized risk estimator—is well-motivated and elegant. By drawing a connection to the control variates literature, the authors provide a principled way to correct for the degenerate behavior of the IPS estimator. The resulting self-normalized estimator has desirable theoretical properties, such as being equivariant to constant shifts in the loss and ensuring that risk estimates are bounded within the range of the observed losses, which directly addresses the anomalies of the unbiased estimator.

3.  **Strong Empirical Results (Quality & Significance):** The experiments are well-designed, following the "Supervised-to-Bandit" protocol from previous work, which allows for a controlled evaluation. The results convincingly demonstrate the superiority of the proposed Norm-POEM algorithm over the original POEM. Norm-POEM consistently achieves lower generalization error across multiple datasets and training set sizes. Crucially, the experiments in Table 2 directly verify the theoretical claims: Norm-POEM is robust to loss translation, whereas POEM's performance degrades catastrophically, and the sum of propensity weights Ŝ(h) remains close to its expected value of 1 for Norm-POEM, unlike for POEM. This provides strong evidence that the performance gain is indeed due to mitigating propensity overfitting.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

While the paper is strong, several points could be clarified or strengthened to improve the manuscript.

1.  **Modeling of the Loss Function:** The paper treats the loss δ(x, y) as a deterministic function. However, in many real-world settings, the feedback (e.g., click or no-click) is a noisy realization of an underlying stochastic process. A more general and realistic formulation would model δ as a random variable and define the true risk in Equation (1) with an additional expectation over this randomness: R(h) = E_x E_{y∼h(x)} [E[δ | x, y]]. This is a minor change to the setup but would strengthen the formalism and align it more closely with the typical assumptions in bandit literature.

2.  **Clarity on Loss Scaling and Experiments:** The original CRM framework [1] and its associated theory (Theorem 1) assume the loss δ is scaled to the range [-1, 0]. It is unclear if Norm-POEM also requires this assumption for its variance regularization term (Equation 8). This should be clarified. Furthermore, the experiments in Table 2 analyze performance for "δ > 0." This is somewhat ambiguous. Does this mean all losses were made positive via a constant shift? A more precise description of the loss transformation used would improve the clarity and reproducibility of these important results.

3.  **Minor Corrections and Notations:**
    *   Lines 170-171: The paper states that if δ is translated by a constant C, the unbiased estimator "need not equal R̂(h) + C." While true, the more direct issue is that the *minimizer* of R̂(h) can change, a nonlinearity also noted in [1].
    *   The paper should explicitly state that the importance weight is h(y|x) / h_0(y|x).
    *   Line 276: The training objective for Norm-POEM should use `argmin` not `argmax`, as it is minimizing a risk objective. This appears to be a typo.
    *   Line 319: The notation `bar(S)` is used in the table caption but `hat(S)` is used in the main text (Equation 4). For consistency, the notation should be unified, presumably using hats to match the convention for empirical estimates.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a well-written and impactful paper that makes a clear and significant contribution to the field of counterfactual learning. It identifies a crucial weakness in a widely used estimator, provides an insightful analysis of the problem, and proposes an effective and principled solution. The empirical results are strong and directly support the paper's claims. The weaknesses are minor and can be addressed with straightforward revisions. This work is an excellent follow-up to [1] and is likely to be highly influential for researchers and practitioners working with logged bandit feedback. I strongly recommend acceptance.
