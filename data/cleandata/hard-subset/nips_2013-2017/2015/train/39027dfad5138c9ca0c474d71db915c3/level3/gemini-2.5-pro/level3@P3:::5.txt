This paper addresses the problem of batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF), a crucial setting for improving interactive systems using historical data. The authors identify a significant and previously overlooked failure mode of the conventional inverse propensity score (IPS) estimator used in Counterfactual Risk Minimization (CRM), which they term "propensity overfitting." This issue arises because the learning algorithm can minimize the empirical risk by exploiting the propensity weights, either by placing probability mass on training examples with large weights (for negative losses) or avoiding them (for positive losses), leading to severely biased risk estimates and poor generalization.

To solve this problem, the paper proposes replacing the standard unbiased IPS estimator with a self-normalized estimator. This is elegantly framed as introducing the sum of propensity weights as a multiplicative control variate, resulting in a ratio estimator. This new estimator is shown to be equivariant to translations in the loss function and its estimates are naturally bounded by the range of the observed losses, thus directly resolving the anomalies that cause propensity overfitting. Building on this new estimator, the paper develops a new learning algorithm, Normalized Policy Optimizer for Exponential Models (Norm-POEM), which is a direct improvement upon the POEM algorithm from Swaminathan & Joachims (2015).

**Strengths:**

*   **Originality and Significance:** The primary contribution is the identification and clear formalization of "propensity overfitting." This is a novel insight into a subtle but severe problem with a foundational estimator in counterfactual learning. The proposed solution—using the self-normalized estimator within the CRM framework—is a significant and original reformulation that directly addresses the identified problem. The work meaningfully advances the state-of-the-art in learning from logged bandit data.
*   **Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The arguments for the failure of the unbiased estimator are made concrete with a clear example (Example 1). The motivation for the self-normalized estimator is well-grounded in the control variates literature, and its desirable properties (equivariance, bounded range, consistency) are proven or clearly argued. The experimental methodology is rigorous, following the well-established "Supervised to Bandit" conversion, which allows for controlled evaluation. The empirical results are strong and directly support the paper's claims, showing that Norm-POEM not only achieves substantially lower generalization error but also corrects the propensity bias (as measured by Ŝ(h)) and is robust to loss translations, unlike the original POEM.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow. The problem is motivated clearly, the proposed solution is explained intuitively, and the connection to prior work is explicit. The structure is logical, and the experiments are designed to convincingly answer the key research questions.
*   **Potential Impact:** The results are compelling. Norm-POEM consistently and significantly outperforms its predecessor across several datasets. A surprising and highly practical finding is that Norm-POEM is often *faster* to train than POEM, despite a more complex per-iteration objective, due to better convergence behavior. This combination of superior accuracy and competitive (or better) efficiency makes the proposed algorithm a strong candidate for widespread adoption by researchers and practitioners working with bandit feedback.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions:**

*   **Optimization at Scale:** The authors correctly note that the variance term in the Norm-POEM objective does not easily lend itself to stochastic optimization methods like SGD. While the experiments show that L-BFGS is very effective and even faster than POEM on the tested datasets, a brief discussion on the potential challenges or adaptations required for massive-scale datasets (where full-batch methods like L-BFGS may be infeasible) would be beneficial.
*   **Bias-Variance Trade-off:** The self-normalized estimator is consistent but biased for finite samples. The paper mentions this but could benefit from a more detailed discussion of the bias-variance trade-off it introduces, especially in relation to the bias introduced by clipping the importance weights in the standard POEM estimator. While the empirical results strongly favor the new estimator, a deeper theoretical or empirical characterization of this trade-off would be a valuable addition.
*   **Model Complexity:** The experiments and derivations are focused on linear exponential models. While the principles are general, it would be helpful for the authors to briefly comment on the applicability and potential challenges of using the self-normalized CRM principle with more complex, non-linear hypothesis spaces, such as deep neural networks.

**Summary of Evaluation:**

This is a high-quality paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of counterfactual learning. It identifies a critical flaw in a standard method and proposes an elegant, principled, and empirically powerful solution. The work is original, technically sound, and clearly presented. The resulting algorithm, Norm-POEM, is a substantial improvement over prior art in both accuracy and practical utility. The weaknesses are minor and relate more to avenues for future work than to flaws in the current paper. The results are interesting and are very likely to inspire further research into better estimators for learning from bandit feedback. I strongly recommend acceptance.
