

This paper addresses a critical issue in batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF) by identifying "propensity overfitting" as a distinct problem from standard importance weight variance. The authors propose replacing the conventional unbiased counterfactual risk estimator with a self-normalized estimator that avoids this problem, leading to their Norm-POEM algorithm for structured output prediction. This builds upon the CRM principle [1] and extends previous work on importance sampling in BLBF [5, 6].

The paper's main strengths are its clear identification of a previously unrecognized problem (propensity overfitting), the elegant solution using self-normalized estimators (which resolves key anomalies in conventional approaches), and compelling empirical validation across multiple datasets. The authors provide code and demonstrate consistent performance improvements over baseline methods. The theoretical arguments for the self-normalized estimator's superiority are well-motivated, particularly regarding its equivariance property and bounded risk estimates.

However, I have several concerns that need clarification. First, the relationship between large weight variance and propensity overfitting requires a more explicit explanation. While the paper identifies propensity overfitting as a distinct phenomenon, the connection to standard importance sampling challenges needs strengthening. Second, I'm confused by the analysis in line 199: when n is small relative to k, the behavior of \hat R(h^) appears unclear. Third, the claim that prior BLBF works used "unbiased" counterfactual risk estimators may be problematic, as many likely employed clipping (introducing bias) as discussed in [5, 15].

I did not verify all technical details due to time constraints, but the core idea appears sensible and the empirical results are convincing. The paper makes a meaningful contribution to the BLBF literature by addressing a subtle but important failure mode in existing approaches. While I cannot fully endorse the technical novelty claims without deeper verification, the proposed solution is both theoretically sound and empirically validated.

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a clear contribution to the field. I recommend acceptance, as the authors have identified and resolved a genuine problem in BLBF with a practical solution that significantly improves performance. The minor concerns noted above could be addressed in revision, but do not detract from the paper's overall quality and contribution.
