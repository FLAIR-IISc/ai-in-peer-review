This paper proposes a self-normalized estimator to address the problem of propensity overfitting in batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF). The authors identify a severe issue with the conventional counterfactual risk estimator used in prior works on BLBF, which can lead to degeneracies when used in Empirical Risk Minimization (ERM). The proposed self-normalized estimator provably avoids these degeneracies and is shown to be more robust than the conventional estimator.

The paper is well-motivated, and the problem of propensity overfitting is clearly explained through a series of examples. The authors also provide a thorough review of related work in the field, including counterfactual estimators, importance sampling, and control variates. The proposed self-normalized estimator is based on the idea of using a multiplicative control variate to reduce the variance of the estimator.

The experimental evaluation of the proposed method, called Norm-POEM, is thorough and well-designed. The authors compare Norm-POEM with the conventional POEM method on several multi-label classification datasets and show that Norm-POEM consistently outperforms POEM. The results also indicate that Norm-POEM is more robust to propensity overfitting and achieves better generalization performance.

However, there are some issues with the paper that need to be addressed. The commentary and notation around equation (2) are misleading, as the loss in (2) is not the expected quantity, and the expectation interpretation is incorrect. This mistake is carried over to the claim that (2) has an "anomaly" (non-invariance to translation of the loss), which is technically incorrect. Example 1 is based on this wrong presumption and therefore does not hold. The estimator in (2) is also sensitive to its denominator π, which can be zero if a sample was never seen in the prior.

In contrast, the modified estimator in (7) is preferable in principle because the double denominator mollifies the effects of π. The authors should revise their commentary to reflect these corrections.

Overall, the paper is convincing and worth publication, subject to the noted remarks. The proposed self-normalized estimator is a significant contribution to the field of BLBF, and the experimental evaluation is thorough and well-designed. The authors have demonstrated the effectiveness of their method in addressing the problem of propensity overfitting and achieving better generalization performance.
