

The paper identifies a critical flaw in counterfactual risk estimation for batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF), a core problem in deploying interactive systems (e.g., ad placement, recommendations) using historical logs. The authors demonstrate that the conventional unbiased importance-weighted estimator (e.g., used in POEM [1]) suffers from "propensity overfitting," where hypotheses exploit the logging policy's propensity weights to produce arbitrarily low risk estimates, even when the true risk is high. This occurs because the estimator's value depends on the sum of propensity weights Ŝ(h), which can deviate significantly from its expectation of 1 under the logging policy. Crucially, this overfitting persists even after loss translation or clipping thresholds (e.g., M in R̂M(h)), unlike standard loss overfitting in supervised learning. The authors propose a self-normalized estimator R̂SN(h) = [∑ δi h(yi|x_i)/p_i] / [∑ h(yi|x_i)/p_i], which leverages multiplicative control variates to ensure equivariance under loss translation and bounds the estimated risk within the loss range. Theoretical analysis (Theorem 2) proves consistency, while empirical results on multi-label classification datasets (Scene, Yeast, TMC, LYRL) show Norm-POEM (the CRM algorithm using R̂SN(h)) consistently outperforms POEM, with the most dramatic gains on TMC where POEM fails catastrophically (worse than random guessing). Norm-POEM also avoids the propensity overfitting symptom (Ŝ(h) ≈ 1) and achieves faster convergence despite higher per-iteration cost.

**Strengths**  
*Originality*: The paper correctly identifies propensity overfitting as a novel failure mode distinct from standard overfitting, filling a critical gap in prior BLBF work (e.g., POEM [1] and CRM [13]). While self-normalized estimators exist in importance sampling [19,22], their application to CRM and identification of propensity overfitting is new to NIPS.  
*Quality*: Theoretical arguments are sound (Theorems 1–2), with clear diagnostics for the overfitting problem (Example 1, Table 2). Empirical validation is thorough, using the standard Supervised 7→Bandit protocol [1] across four datasets, with statistical significance (10 runs) and ablation studies (e.g., variance regularization still benefits Norm-POEM).  
*Significance*: Solving propensity overfitting enables more reliable BLBF algorithms for real-world systems, where logged data is the primary training source. Norm-POEM achieves near-supervised performance (CRF "skyline") on all datasets, demonstrating tangible progress beyond prior methods.  
*Clarity*: The exposition is well-structured, though Example 1 could be strengthened with concrete numbers (e.g., k=1000, n=100). The connection between theory (Theorem 2) and practice (Table 1, Fig. 1) is exceptionally clear.

**Weaknesses**  
*Limited scope*: Experiments focus solely on multi-label classification; extension to other structured prediction tasks (e.g., ranking, sequence labeling) would further validate generality.  
*Computational detail*: While runtime is competitive, the paper does not explicitly compare convergence speed to stochastic-optimization variants of POEM [1], though the faster convergence is noted.  
*Minor clarity*: The distinction between "propensity overfitting" and "loss overfitting" is well-made, but the term "propensity" could be introduced earlier for readers less familiar with importance sampling.

**Pro/Con Acceptance**  
*Pro*:  
- Identifies and solves a serious, previously unaddressed flaw in BLBF (propensity overfitting).  
- Theoretical solution (self-normalized estimator) is elegant, provably consistent, and empirically superior.  
- Direct relevance to real-world deployment of interactive systems.  
- Strong empirical results across multiple datasets, with clear diagnostics for the proposed fix.  
*Con*:  
- Experimental scope limited to multi-label classification (though sufficient for the claim).  
- Minor opportunity to strengthen Example 1 with numerical illustration.

This paper makes a significant contribution to the BLBF literature. The propensity overfitting problem is a serious limitation of existing methods, and the authors provide a theoretically grounded, empirically validated solution that advances the state of the art. While the experimental scope could be broader, the core contribution is robust and impactful. The paper aligns perfectly with NIPS' focus on principled, high-impact machine learning research. It is technically sound, clearly presented, and addresses a well-motivated practical challenge. The proposed Norm-POEM algorithm is a valuable addition to the BLBF toolkit, with clear advantages over prior work. I strongly recommend acceptance.
