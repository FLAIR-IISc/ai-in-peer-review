

# Review for "The Self-Normalized Estimator for Counterfactual Learning"

This paper identifies a critical issue with the conventional counterfactual risk estimator used in batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF), termed "propensity overfitting," and proposes a self-normalized estimator that avoids this problem. The work builds on the Counterfactual Risk Minimization (CRM) principle introduced by Swaminathan and Joachims (ICML 2015), which was a significant contribution to the field of offline bandit learning. The authors extend this work by providing a deeper theoretical analysis of the risk estimator's behavior and introducing a novel self-normalized estimator that resolves key anomalies of the conventional approach.

The paper makes several important contributions. First, it rigorously identifies propensity overfitting as a new form of overfitting specific to BLBF, distinct from standard loss overfitting. The authors demonstrate through examples that the conventional estimator can produce severely biased risk estimates, even when the hypothesis space is simple. Second, they propose a self-normalized estimator (Equation 7) that is both consistent and equivariant, addressing the key anomalies of the conventional estimator. Third, they develop Norm-POEM, a practical algorithm that implements the new estimator, and demonstrate its superior empirical performance across multiple datasets.

The paper's theoretical analysis is strong, with careful examination of the estimator's properties and a proof of consistency (Theorem 2). The experimental evaluation is thorough, comparing Norm-POEM against POEM on multiple multi-label classification datasets using the standard Supervised 7→ Bandit methodology. The results consistently show Norm-POEM outperforms POEM, with particularly compelling evidence that it avoids propensity overfitting (Table 2).

**Strengths:**
- The identification of propensity overfitting is insightful and addresses a critical gap in understanding counterfactual learning.
- The self-normalized estimator is theoretically sound and resolves multiple anomalies of the conventional approach.
- The empirical results are convincing, with consistent improvements across multiple datasets and experimental conditions.
- The paper provides clear implementation details and computational analysis, making it reproducible.

**Weaknesses and Corrections:**

1. **Delta Range Clarification:** The paper states δ(·, ·) ∈ [-1, 0] in Section 3, but Table 1 reports "delta > 0" experiments. This is not contradictory but rather demonstrates the equivariance property of the self-normalized estimator. The authors should clarify that while the theoretical analysis uses δ ∈ [-1, 0], the empirical results use translated losses (δ > 0) to test the estimator's equivariance property. The self-normalized estimator remains effective regardless of loss translation.

2. **Loss as Random Variable:** The paper treats δ(x,y) as deterministic, but in practice, loss is often a noisy realization. The true risk should be defined as E[δ(x,y)] with expectation over both the data distribution and loss noise. The authors should explicitly include this expectation in their risk formulation.

3. **Correction to Equation (170-171):** The text states "h(y|x) / h_0(y|x)" but should be "h(y|x) / h_0(y|x)" (the denominator should be h_0(y|x), not h_0(y|x)).

4. **Correction to Equation (276):** The optimization should be "ĥCRM = argmin h∈H R̂M(h) + λ √ ˆV ar(uh)/n" rather than argmax, as the risk is being minimized.

5. **Notation Consistency:** Line 319 should replace "bars" with "hats" to match the earlier convention (e.g., R̂SN(h) instead of R̄SN(h)).

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a high-quality paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of offline bandit learning. The identification of propensity overfitting is novel and important, and the self-normalized estimator provides an elegant solution. The empirical results robustly support the theoretical claims, with Norm-POEM consistently outperforming POEM across multiple datasets.

The paper is clearly written and well-organized, with a logical progression from problem identification to theoretical solution to empirical validation. The authors have carefully addressed related work and positioned their contribution within the broader context of counterfactual learning.

I rate the paper:
- Quality: 6 (strong technical contribution with minor issues in presentation)
- Clarity: 6 (clear writing but with some notational inconsistencies)
- Originality: 8 (novel problem identification and solution)
- Significance: 8 (addresses a critical issue with practical implications for offline bandit learning)

The paper is a valuable extension to Swaminathan and Joachims' earlier work on CRM. The insights about propensity overfitting and the effective use of multiplicative control variates provide important theoretical and practical advances for the field. The empirical improvements over POEM on standard multi-label classification problems demonstrate the real-world impact of the proposed approach.

This paper makes a clear contribution to the state of the art in counterfactual learning and will likely be influential in both theoretical and applied contexts. The minor corrections suggested above would further strengthen the paper, but they do not detract from the overall quality and significance of the contribution. I recommend acceptance.
