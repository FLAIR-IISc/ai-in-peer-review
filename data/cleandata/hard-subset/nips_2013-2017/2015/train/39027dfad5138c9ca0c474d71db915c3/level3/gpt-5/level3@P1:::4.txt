Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF) under the Counterfactual Risk Minimization (CRM) paradigm of Swaminathan & Joachims [1]. It identifies a failure mode of the standard inverse propensity scoring (IPS) risk estimator when used inside ERM: “propensity overfitting,” where hypotheses exploit variability in importance weights rather than true loss. The authors propose replacing IPS (or clipped IPS) with a self-normalized IPS (SNIPS) estimator—i.e., a ratio estimator using the sum of weights as a multiplicative control variate—to obtain an equivariant, bounded risk estimate that mitigates this degeneracy. They instantiate this idea within CRM to yield Norm-POEM, a structured prediction learner over exponential-family (CRF-like) policies, and report consistent empirical gains over POEM on multi-label classification tasks. The approach is closely related to classic variance-reduction and control-variate methods in importance sampling (e.g., Hesterberg, Owen; SNIS/standardized weights), and to off-policy evaluation and doubly robust estimators (Dudík et al.). The paper contributes a careful diagnosis of a learning-pathology specific to BLBF and a principled estimator that addresses it.

Strengths
- Clear identification and formalization of “propensity overfitting” as distinct from standard loss overfitting; compelling toy example makes the issue concrete.
- Well-motivated use of multiplicative control variates; SNIPS brings boundedness and translation equivariance, directly addressing anomalies of IPS within ERM.
- Consistency result for the proposed estimator and a reasonable variance approximation for CRM regularization.
- Practical algorithm (Norm-POEM) with tractable gradients for exponential-family policies; competitive runtime.
- Solid empirical evidence on standard multi-label datasets; consistent improvements over POEM; diagnostics (Ŝ(ĥ)≈1) align with theory.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Finite-sample theory: The paper leverages CRM’s empirical Bernstein-style variance regularization but does not provide a dedicated generalization bound for the SNIPS-based objective. Given the bias of SNIPS, a bound (or at least a decomposition of bias/variance and how clipping M and λ control them) would strengthen the contribution.
- Comparisons: Including doubly robust (DR) or weighted DR baselines would broaden the empirical picture, since DR also mitigates weight variance and can be less sensitive to small propensities. A brief discussion of when SNIPS vs DR is preferable (e.g., quality of outcome models) would help practitioners.
- Sensitivity: More analysis of the sensitivity to M (clipping) and λ (variance regularization) would be useful, including failure cases and how the Ŝ diagnostic guides tuning.
- Assumptions on logging policy: Results hinge on full support and the practical effect of approximate support via clipping. Please discuss the impact of support violations and whether a “defensive mixture” logging policy would improve robustness.

Clarifications requested
- Loss scaling. CRM bounds in [1] assume δ∈[−1,0]. Please clarify whether Norm-POEM rescales δ to this range when instantiating Equations (3) and (8). In the experiments, you discuss “δ>0” settings (Table 1/2); specify whether this is due to an additive translation and confirm that SNIPS’s equivariance ensures invariance to such translations, while IPS-based POEM is affected.
- Stochastic losses. It would be cleaner to model δ(x,y) as a random variable with E[δ|x,y], and define the true risk as E_{x∼Pr(X), y∼h(x)} E[δ|x,y]. While you mention this informally, adopting this notation early would avoid confusion and align the analysis with noisy bandit feedback.
- Variance estimator. Equation (8) is an asymptotic approximation; please restate the conditions under which it is valid and whether you apply clipping inside the variance computation as well.

Specific corrections and notational issues
- Line 170–171: Replace h(y|x)h0(y|x) with h(y|x)/h0(y|x) inside the min{·,·} in the definition of u_h. This division is also consistent with [1]. Also, please note the nonlinearity of the CRM objective (already discussed in [1]) and how normalization further contributes to nonconvexity.
- Line 276: Use argmin (not argmax) for the training objective, since risk is minimized.
- Line 319: Replace bars with hats to match earlier convention for empirical estimates (e.g., use V̂ rather than V̄ if that is the intended sample estimate).
- Equation typesetting: Ensure consistent use of hats for Monte Carlo estimates (R̂, Ŝ), and clarify when clipping is applied to weights in both numerator and denominator of SNIPS.

Quality
- The methodology is technically sound, the diagnosis is insightful, and the estimator choice is principled. Theoretical support includes consistency and well-known properties of SNIPS; however, a finite-sample generalization bound for the SNIPS-CRM objective is missing. Empirics are convincing but could be broadened with DR comparisons and sensitivity analyses.

Clarity
- Generally clear and well-organized. Please address the loss scaling and stochastic-loss modeling, and fix the noted typos/notation. Adding a short “Practical tuning” paragraph (diagnosing Ŝ(h), setting M and λ) would help reproducibility.

Originality
- The specific identification of propensity overfitting in BLBF learning and the use of self-normalization within CRM for structured prediction provide a novel and valuable perspective, even though SNIPS itself is classical.

Significance
- BLBF is central for large-scale interactive systems. Demonstrating robust gains over POEM with simple, principled changes is impactful. The diagnostic Ŝ(h)≈1 is practical and likely to be adopted.

Arguments for acceptance
- Pinpoints a real and under-appreciated failure mode of IPS-based ERM.
- Provides a simple, principled fix with strong practical benefits and consistency.
- Demonstrates consistent empirical gains on relevant tasks with insightful diagnostics.

Arguments against acceptance
- Lack of a dedicated finite-sample bound for SNIPS-CRM.
- Limited baseline coverage (no DR) and limited sensitivity analyses.
- Minor clarity/notation issues.

Post-rebuttal assessment and ratings
- Quality: 6
- Clarity: 6
- Originality: 8
- Significance: 8

Overall: A strong and useful follow-up to [1]. The identification of propensity overfitting and the effective use of a multiplicative control variate are insightful, and the empirical improvements over POEM on an important problem are clear. I recommend acceptance after addressing the clarifications and corrections above.
