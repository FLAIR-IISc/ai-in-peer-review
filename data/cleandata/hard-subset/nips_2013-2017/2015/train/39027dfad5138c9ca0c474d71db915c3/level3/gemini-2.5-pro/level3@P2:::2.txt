### Summary of the Paper

This paper addresses the problem of batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF), a setting relevant for improving interactive systems using historical data. The authors build upon the Counterfactual Risk Minimization (CRM) principle, which has been previously applied in algorithms like POEM (ICML 2015). The central claim of this work is that the conventional inverse propensity score (IPS) estimator, used in POEM and other works, suffers from a severe "propensity overfitting" problem. This issue arises because the estimator is sensitive to the propensity weights, allowing models with high capacity to find degenerate solutions that minimize the empirical risk estimate without generalizing well.

To remedy this, the authors propose replacing the standard IPS estimator with a self-normalized estimator. This estimator, common in the importance sampling literature, uses the sum of the importance weights as a multiplicative control variate to normalize the risk estimate. The authors argue that this self-normalized estimator is equivariant to translations in the loss function and its value is bounded by the range of the loss, thus avoiding the anomalies of the conventional estimator. This leads to a new learning algorithm, Norm-POEM. Through extensive experiments on several multi-label classification datasets, the paper demonstrates that Norm-POEM consistently and significantly outperforms the previous state-of-the-art (POEM) and approaches the performance of a fully supervised model.

### Strengths

1.  **Significance and Impact:** The paper tackles a very important and practical problem. Learning from logged data is a core challenge in many industrial applications like computational advertising, search, and recommendation systems. Improving the robustness and performance of estimators in this setting is a significant contribution with high potential impact.

2.  **Empirical Results:** The experimental evaluation is thorough and convincing. Norm-POEM is shown to outperform the POEM baseline on all datasets, often by a large margin. The comparison against a "skyline" CRF model trained on full information effectively demonstrates the strength of the proposed method, showing it closes a substantial portion of the performance gap caused by only having access to bandit feedback.

3.  **Novelty and Principled Approach:** While the self-normalized estimator itself is not new, its application to mitigate estimation instability within the CRM framework for BLBF is a novel and well-motivated contribution. The resulting Norm-POEM algorithm is a natural and elegant modification of its predecessor. The principled motivation of using control variates to improve estimation is a strong point.

### Weaknesses and Areas for Improvement

The primary weakness of the paper lies in its theoretical motivation for the proposed estimator, specifically the arguments presented in Section 4.

1.  **Misleading Characterization of the IPS Estimator:** The paper's commentary around equation (2) and its claimed "anomalies" is technically misleading. The authors state that the unbiased estimator is not equivariant to a constant shift in the loss `δ`. While this is true for a finite sample realization of the estimator `R̂(h)`, it is not an "anomaly." The estimator remains unbiased in expectation, i.e., `E[R̂(h) + C] = E[R̂(h)] + C = R(h) + C`. The properties of a finite-sample estimate should not be confused with the properties of its expectation. This framing detracts from the paper's soundness.

2.  **Unconvincing "Propensity Overfitting" Example:** The core argument for "propensity overfitting" and the accompanying Example 1 are built on this flawed premise. The behavior described in the example is a manifestation of the well-known high variance of the IPS estimator, particularly when importance weights are large. Attributing this entirely to a lack of finite-sample equivariance is incorrect and weakens the paper's theoretical argument.

A more direct and technically sound motivation would be to frame the problem in terms of variance reduction. The standard IPS estimator (eq. 2) is known to be highly sensitive to the denominator `pi`, which can be very small for actions that were rarely taken by the logging policy. This leads to high variance and instability. The proposed self-normalized estimator (eq. 7) is a classic and effective technique for variance reduction in such cases. The normalization by the sum of weights effectively mollifies the influence of individual samples with extreme weights, leading to a more stable and robust estimate. I strongly urge the authors to revise their motivation in Section 4 to focus on this more standard and correct view of variance reduction, as it would significantly strengthen the paper without changing the proposed method or its excellent empirical results.

### Arguments For and Against Acceptance

*   **For:** The paper presents a simple, effective, and novel application of a known technique to a significant practical problem. The empirical results are strong, demonstrating a clear and substantial improvement over a recent, state-of-the-art baseline from a top-tier conference. The proposed algorithm, Norm-POEM, is a valuable contribution to the field of counterfactual learning.

*   **Against:** The theoretical motivation presented for the method is flawed. The arguments about estimator "anomalies" and "propensity overfitting" are based on a misunderstanding of the properties of unbiased estimators. This represents a significant weakness in the clarity and technical soundness of the paper's core argument.

### Conclusion

Overall, this is a strong paper with an important contribution. The proposed Norm-POEM algorithm is effective and the empirical results are convincing. While the technical motivation in Section 4 has some serious flaws, the core idea of using a self-normalized estimator is sound and the resulting method is valuable. Therefore, I recommend acceptance, but with the strong suggestion that the authors revise the motivation for their method along the lines suggested above. Reframing the problem in terms of variance reduction for the IPS estimator would make the paper's contribution clearer and more technically sound.
