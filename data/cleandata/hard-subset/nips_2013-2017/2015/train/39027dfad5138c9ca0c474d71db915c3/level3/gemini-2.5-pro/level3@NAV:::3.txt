This paper addresses the problem of batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF), a crucial setting for improving interactive systems using historical data. The authors identify a key pathology in the standard Counterfactual Risk Minimization (CRM) principle introduced in [1], which they term "propensity overfitting." This occurs when a model minimizes the counterfactual risk not by fitting the true loss but by exploiting the propensity weights. The proposed solution is to replace the conventional unbiased risk estimator with a self-normalized estimator, which uses the sum of importance weights as a multiplicative control variate.

The paper's primary strengths are its quality and clarity. The motivation is excellent; the "propensity overfitting" problem is clearly articulated with a simple yet effective example, providing strong intuition. The proposed self-normalized estimator is a theoretically sound and elegant solution to the identified issues of equivariance and unboundedness. The paper is very well-written and organized, making a technically complex topic accessible. The empirical results demonstrate that the proposed Norm-POEM algorithm consistently outperforms the baseline POEM from [1] on several benchmark datasets.

However, the paper has some weaknesses regarding its originality and significance. The contribution is largely an incremental improvement upon the CRM framework from [1], with the main novelty being the application of the self-normalized estimator—a known technique in importance sampling—to this specific learning problem. While the problem is important, the significance of the empirical gains remains somewhat uncertain. The experiments, while clean, are conducted on multi-label classification datasets converted to a bandit setting, following the exact protocol of [1]. It is unclear how these improvements would translate to large-scale, real-world applications where logging policies and reward structures can be substantially more complex.

To strengthen the paper, the authors should provide a more thorough experimental evaluation. Firstly, the results in Table 1 should include variance or standard deviation across the 10 runs to better assess the stability of the improvements. Secondly, expanding the experiments to include additional datasets, particularly real-world logged bandit datasets beyond those used in [1], would substantially bolster the claims of practical significance. A deeper, albeit brief, analysis of the non-convex training objective and why L-BFGS optimization is effective would also be beneficial. Overall, this is a high-quality, valuable contribution, but its impact could be more convincingly demonstrated with a broader empirical validation.
