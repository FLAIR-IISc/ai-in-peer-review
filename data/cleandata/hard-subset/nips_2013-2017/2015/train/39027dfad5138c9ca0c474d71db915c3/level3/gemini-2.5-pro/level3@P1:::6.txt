This paper addresses the problem of batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF), a setting of great practical importance for optimizing interactive systems using historical data. The standard approach in this area is Counterfactual Risk Minimization (CRM), which typically relies on an unbiased inverse propensity score (IPS) estimator to evaluate the risk of new policies.

The central contribution of this paper is the identification and clear articulation of a critical failure mode of this standard IPS estimator, which the authors term "Propensity Overfitting". They show that when learning with expressive hypothesis spaces, a learner can minimize the estimated risk not just by finding policies with low true loss, but by manipulating the importance weights themselves. For instance, with negative losses, a hypothesis can achieve an arbitrarily low empirical risk by assigning high probability to actions observed in the training data, effectively overfitting to the logging policy's choices. This issue is elegantly illustrated in Example 1. This is a significant problem because it means the empirical risk is no longer a reliable proxy for the true risk, potentially leading to the selection of highly suboptimal policies.

As a solution, the authors propose replacing the standard IPS estimator with the self-normalized importance sampling (SNIS) estimator. This estimator is a ratio of the IPS estimate of the total loss to the IPS estimate of the sum of importance weights. Drawing on established results from the statistics literature, the authors highlight several desirable properties of this estimator: it is bounded within the range of the loss function, it is equivariant to constant shifts in the loss, and while biased in finite samples, it is strongly consistent. These properties directly remedy the pathologies that enable propensity overfitting. The paper then instantiates the CRM principle with this new estimator, resulting in a learning algorithm called Norm-POEM. Empirical results on several multi-label classification tasks demonstrate that Norm-POEM consistently and substantially outperforms its predecessor (POEM), which uses the standard IPS estimator. The experiments convincingly show that Norm-POEM is robust to propensity overfitting and performs well across different dataset sizes.

**Strengths and Weaknesses**

**Quality:** The paper is of high technical quality. The identification of the propensity overfitting problem is a sharp and important insight. The claims are well-supported by both theoretical arguments and strong empirical results. The experimental methodology (Supervised -> Bandit) is sound and allows for a controlled evaluation. The experiments showing the effect of loss translation (Table 2) are particularly compelling and provide direct evidence for the theoretical claims about the estimator's equivariance (or lack thereof). The work is presented as a complete contribution, from problem identification to a practical algorithmic solution and thorough validation.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow. The authors do an excellent job of motivating the problem with a simple, intuitive example (Example 1) that makes the concept of propensity overfitting immediately clear. The structure is logical, guiding the reader from the problem with the standard estimator to the properties of the proposed solution and its empirical validation.

**Originality:** The core technical tool, the self-normalized estimator, is not new and is well-known in the statistics and Monte Carlo literature. However, the paper's originality lies in its application and framing. The key contribution is the identification of propensity overfitting as a specific failure mode in the context of counterfactual *learning* (as opposed to just evaluation) and demonstrating that the self-normalized estimator is a principled solution. This provides a valuable new lens through which to view the challenges of off-policy learning. The work is clearly positioned relative to prior work, particularly the original POEM paper [1], and demonstrates a significant and novel improvement.

**Significance:** The paper's contribution is highly significant. Learning from logged data is a cornerstone of improving many real-world systems (e.g., recommenders, search, ad placement). The propensity overfitting problem is likely to be a major obstacle, especially as researchers and practitioners move towards using more complex models like deep neural networks, which have the capacity to exploit such artifacts. By identifying this problem and providing a simple, effective, and easy-to-implement solution, this work provides an important contribution that is likely to be widely used and built upon by both researchers and practitioners.

**Weaknesses / Questions for Authors:**
1.  **Scalability:** The paper notes that the variance estimate for Norm-POEM (Eq. 8) is not amenable to stochastic optimization, and L-BFGS is used instead. While the experiments show this is efficient on the datasets tested, this could be a practical limitation for very large-scale problems where stochastic gradient methods are often a necessity. Could the authors comment on potential pathways to developing a scalable stochastic optimization algorithm for the Norm-POEM objective? Perhaps a different approximation of the variance could be used?

2.  **Theoretical Depth:** The primary theoretical result is a proof of consistency for the estimator. While the CRM framework is borrowed from prior work, it would strengthen the paper to include a more direct theoretical analysis (e.g., a new generalization bound) for learning with the self-normalized estimator, rather than simply plugging it into the existing CRM objective with an approximate variance formula. This could provide deeper insights into the bias-variance trade-offs involved.

Despite these minor points, the overall contribution is strong.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Identifies and provides a clear explanation for a novel, important, and likely widespread overfitting problem ("Propensity Overfitting") in counterfactual learning.
*   Proposes a simple, principled, and theoretically justified solution based on the self-normalized estimator.
*   Provides strong and convincing empirical evidence that the proposed method, Norm-POEM, solves the identified problem and significantly outperforms the previous state-of-the-art.
*   The work is highly significant for the field of learning from logged bandit feedback, with clear implications for both research and industrial applications.
*   The paper is extremely well-written and clear.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   The core technique (the self-normalized estimator) is a well-established tool in statistics, which slightly limits the deep technical novelty. The novelty is more in the application and problem formulation.
*   The proposed optimization method is not stochastic, which might limit its applicability on massive datasets.

In summary, this is an excellent paper. It identifies a subtle but critical problem with a standard method and provides a robust and well-validated solution. The strengths far outweigh the minor weaknesses. I recommend acceptance.
