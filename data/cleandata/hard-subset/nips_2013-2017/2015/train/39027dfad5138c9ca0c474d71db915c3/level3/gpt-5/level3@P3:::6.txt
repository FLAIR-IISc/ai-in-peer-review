Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF) via Counterfactual Risk Minimization (CRM), which leverages inverse propensity scoring (IPS) to correct for logging bias when learning policies from historical logs. The authors identify a previously underappreciated overfitting failure mode—propensity overfitting—where hypotheses exploit variation in example-generation probabilities, rather than structure in the loss, to minimize the IPS objective. In particular, hypotheses can increase or decrease support from the logged data through the importance weights, leading to anomalous empirical risks (e.g., estimates outside the loss range or non-equivariance to affine transformations of the loss). To address this, the paper proposes replacing the conventional (unbiased) IPS estimator with a multiplicative control variate, yielding the self-normalized IPS estimator (SNIPS). This “self-normalization” acts as a data-dependent scaling that stabilizes the weights, controlling variance while preserving consistency. Based on this estimator, the paper presents Norm-POEM, a CRM algorithm for structured prediction with linear-exponential family policies, along with an approximate variance estimator used for CRM’s variance regularization.

Relative to prior work, the paper builds on CRM (Swaminathan & Joachims), importance weighting under covariate shift, and RL off-policy evaluation. It distinguishes itself from clipping/truncation (which trades bias for variance) and additive control variates/doubly-robust estimators by using a multiplicative control variate (self-normalization), a standard variance reduction technique in Monte Carlo and off-policy evaluation, but here integrated into learning and CRM. The paper gives clean examples diagnosing propensity overfitting, proves strong consistency of the self-normalized risk estimator, and shows improved empirical performance over the non-normalized POEM across multiple multi-label datasets.

Strengths
- Clear identification and demonstration (via crisp examples) of a novel failure mode—propensity overfitting—when using IPS within ERM/CRM over rich hypothesis classes.
- Simple, principled fix using a self-normalized (multiplicative) estimator that:
  - Is bounded and equivariant to affine transformations of the loss.
  - Preserves consistency and empirically reduces variance-induced pathologies.
- Solid empirical evidence: the normalized CRM (Norm-POEM) consistently outperforms the non-normalized CRM (POEM) across datasets and data sizes; diagnostics show Ŝ(h) ≈ 1 as desired.
- Practical algorithmic contribution (Norm-POEM) with publicly available code and reasonable runtime; variance regularization remains beneficial.
- Good positioning within existing literatures (BLBF, off-policy evaluation, importance sampling, control variates).

Weaknesses
- The theoretical development for learning with the self-normalized estimator stops at strong consistency and an approximate variance formula. There is no new high-probability generalization bound for SNIPS+CRM analogous to prior CRM bounds; this leaves a gap between the diagnosis and a formal learning-theoretic guarantee under normalization.
- Empirical comparisons focus primarily on POEM; missing baselines include doubly-robust and modern stabilized estimators (e.g., DR, SWITCH-DR, weight clipping with careful tuning). This makes it harder to assess where normalization sits in the broader design space.
- Experiments use supervised→bandit simulation in multilabel classification; real logged-data results (ads/recs/search) or contextual bandit tasks with larger action spaces would strengthen the case.
- Sensitivity analyses for clipping parameter M and variance-regularization λ are somewhat light; more guidance on stable ranges and diagnostics would help practitioners.
- Optimization is non-convex and uses L-BFGS with full-batch gradients; while effective here, scalability to very large problems could be discussed further.

Quality
- Technically sound core: clear failure mode, correct use of multiplicative control variates, proof of strong consistency, and appropriate variance estimator for the ratio estimator. The empirical study is careful and consistent with the theory. The main limitation is the absence of a new generalization bound specialized to SNIPS in CRM.

Clarity
- Very well written and organized. The propensity overfitting examples are compelling. The discussion of equivariance and boundedness of SNIPS clarifies why normalization mitigates the anomalies. A few suggestions: explicitly state conditions ensuring finite variance under clipping; provide more intuition for the approximate variance formula; and offer practical heuristics for choosing M and λ.

Originality
- The self-normalized estimator is classical, but applying it to counterfactual learning within CRM, naming and analyzing propensity overfitting, and deriving the Norm-POEM objective constitute a novel and useful contribution. The combination and the problem framing are original and timely for BLBF.

Significance
- Addressing a central failure mode in IPS-driven learning is important. The fix is simple, widely applicable, and empirically effective, so others are likely to adopt it. While results are on simulated logs and a specific family of models, the ideas should transfer to broader BLBF settings.

Questions and suggestions for authors
- Can you provide a high-probability generalization bound for CRM with the self-normalized estimator (even if approximate or under additional moment assumptions)?
- How does Norm-POEM compare against doubly-robust and SWITCH-type estimators integrated with CRM?
- Provide sensitivity plots for M and λ and guidance for diagnostics when Ŝ(h) deviates from 1.
- Discuss scalability and possibilities for stochastic optimization of the ratio objective (e.g., via control variate batching or variance-reduced methods).

Pros for acceptance
- Identifies an important failure mode and offers a principled, effective solution.
- Strong empirical gains and diagnostic evidence that directly tie to the theory.
- Clear, well executed, and likely to influence practice in BLBF.

Cons against acceptance
- Missing learning-theoretic generalization guarantees tailored to the normalized estimator.
- Limited set of baselines and domains; no real logged-data results.
- Non-convex optimization details and scalability could be elaborated.

Recommendation
- Accept. This is a solid paper with a simple, impactful idea, clear theory for estimation, and convincing experiments. While I am not fully certain due to the missing generalization bound and limited baselines, the contribution is substantial and relevant.

Confidence
- Moderate.
