This paper addresses the problem of batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF), a setting of high practical importance for improving interactive systems using historical data. The authors build upon the Counterfactual Risk Minimization (CRM) principle, particularly the Policy Optimizer for Exponential Models (POEM) algorithm introduced by Swaminathan and Joachims (ICML 2015). The central thesis is that the standard inverse propensity score (IPS) estimator, used in POEM and other CRM-based approaches, suffers from a severe issue termed "propensity overfitting." This occurs when a complex hypothesis space allows a model to minimize the empirical risk not by fitting the losses, but by manipulating the importance weights—either by placing probability mass on training examples with negative losses or avoiding those with positive losses. To remedy this, the paper proposes replacing the standard IPS estimator with a self-normalized importance sampling estimator. This leads to a new algorithm, Norm-POEM, which is shown empirically to be substantially more robust and accurate than the original POEM.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Significance:** The paper tackles an important and practical problem. Improving the stability and performance of learning algorithms for BLBF has a direct impact on applications like web search, recommendation, and ad placement. The proposed method provides a tangible improvement over a strong and recent baseline.

2.  **Empirical Results:** The experimental evaluation is thorough and convincing. On several multi-label classification datasets, the proposed Norm-POEM algorithm consistently and significantly outperforms the POEM baseline. It is particularly impressive that the performance of Norm-POEM, which learns from limited bandit feedback, often approaches that of a "skyline" CRF model trained on full-information supervised data. The analysis in Table 2, showing Norm-POEM's robustness to loss translation, is a strong piece of evidence supporting the method's stability.

3.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is generally well-written and easy to follow. The problem is clearly motivated, the proposed solution is well-explained, and the connection to prior work is established. The experimental setup is described in sufficient detail.

4.  **Novelty:** While the self-normalized estimator is a known technique in the Monte Carlo and importance sampling literature, its application to the CRM framework for BLBF is novel. The identification and empirical demonstration of the instability of the standard IPS estimator within this learning context, and the subsequent proposal and validation of a more robust alternative, constitutes a solid contribution.

**Weaknesses and Detailed Comments:**

The primary weakness of the paper lies in its theoretical motivation for the proposed estimator, specifically the discussion surrounding "propensity overfitting" and the alleged "anomalies" of the standard estimator in Equation (2).

1.  **Misleading Interpretation of the IPS Estimator:** The commentary around Equation (2) and in Section 4 appears to be based on a misunderstanding of how the expectation of the estimator works. The paper claims that `R̂(h)` is not equivariant to a constant shift in the loss, `δ`. While a particular finite-sample realization `R̂(h)` on a fixed dataset `D` may not shift perfectly by a constant `C`, the *expectation* of the estimator, `E_D[R̂(h)]`, is indeed equivariant. Since `R̂(h)` is an unbiased estimator of `R(h) = E[δ(x,y)]`, it follows that `E_D[R̂(h) + C * Ŝ(h)] = R(h) + C * E_D[Ŝ(h)] = R(h) + C`. The finite-sample behavior is a matter of variance, not a fundamental "anomaly" of non-equivariance in expectation.

2.  **Flawed Justification in Example 1:** This misunderstanding undermines the central motivating example. Example 1 claims that `R̂(hoverfit)` can be made arbitrarily negative, far below the true minimum risk of -2. This is presented as a pathology unique to this estimator. However, this is simply an illustration of high variance, which is a well-known issue with IPS estimators, especially when the propensities `p_i` are small. The problem is not that the estimator is fundamentally broken in the way described, but that it is unstable on finite samples, a point that could be made more directly.

3.  **Alternative (and Stronger) Motivation:** The proposed self-normalized estimator in Equation (7) is indeed preferable, but for reasons that are more standard in the importance sampling literature. It is a ratio estimator, which is known to have lower variance than the standard IPS estimator when the importance weights themselves have high variance (as cited in [19]). The normalization by `Σ h(y_i|x_i)/p_i` effectively "mollifies" the effect of individual data points with very small propensities `p_i`. The authors should reframe their motivation around this well-established principle of variance reduction rather than the confusing and likely incorrect "propensity overfitting" and non-equivariance arguments.

If the paper is accepted, I strongly urge the authors to revise Section 4 to provide a more technically sound motivation for their otherwise excellent contribution. The core idea and the empirical results are strong enough to stand on their own with a clearer, more standard justification.

**Summary of Arguments for and against Acceptance:**

**Pro:**
*   The paper proposes a simple and effective modification to the CRM framework that yields significant performance improvements.
*   The empirical results are strong, consistent across multiple datasets, and demonstrate a clear advantage over a state-of-the-art baseline (POEM).
*   The problem of improving off-policy learning algorithms is highly relevant to the NIPS community.
*   The proposed Norm-POEM algorithm is practical and computationally feasible.

**Con:**
*   The main theoretical motivation for the new estimator (the "propensity overfitting" argument in Section 4) is based on a confusing and potentially incorrect interpretation of the properties of the standard IPS estimator. This section requires significant revision.

**Recommendation:**
Overall, the paper presents a valuable and convincing contribution. The empirical success of the Norm-POEM algorithm is undeniable and addresses a real-world problem. Despite the issues with the theoretical motivation, the proposed method is sound and the results are significant. Therefore, I recommend acceptance, with the strong suggestion that the authors revise the motivation in Section 4 to be more technically precise, focusing on the variance reduction properties of the self-normalized estimator.
