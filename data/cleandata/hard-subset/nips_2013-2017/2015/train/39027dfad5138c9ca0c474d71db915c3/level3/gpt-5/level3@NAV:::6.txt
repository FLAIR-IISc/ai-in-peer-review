This paper studies batch learning from logged bandit feedback under the Counterfactual Risk Minimization (CRM) framework and identifies a concrete overfitting failure mode of the standard inverse propensity score (IPS) estimator: hypotheses can “game” the sum of importance weights, leading to propensity overfitting. The authors propose replacing IPS within CRM by a multiplicative control variate (self-normalized importance sampling), yielding a self-normalized risk estimator that is equivariant to loss translation, bounded, and strongly consistent. They instantiate this within a structured prediction learner (Norm-POEM) and show improved generalization over POEM on several multi-label datasets. The work is well-situated relative to prior NIPS and related literature on importance weighting and BLBF, including CRM (Swaminathan & Joachims), variance reduction via clipping (Ionides), learning bounds for importance weighting (Cortes et al.), doubly robust evaluation (Dudík et al.), and self-normalized IS (Hesterberg, Owen). The main conceptual contribution is diagnosing propensity overfitting via deviations of the weight sum and addressing it with a ratio estimator inside the learning objective.

Quality: The theoretical claims (equivariance, boundedness, strong consistency) are correct and relevant. The empirical evidence is solid and shows consistent gains, including diagnostics (Ŝ near 1) that directly support the proposed mechanism. A limitation is the lack of finite-sample generalization guarantees for the CRM objective with the self-normalized estimator (contrast with the empirical-Bernstein bound used to motivate CRM for IPS), and the approximate variance estimator rests on asymptotic arguments.

Clarity: The paper is clearly written and well-organized. The exposition of propensity overfitting is compelling. It would help to more formally characterize when normalization introduces bias that could hurt finite-sample learning, and to detail guidance for choosing M and λ.

Originality: While self-normalized IS is classical, applying it inside CRM for counterfactual learning, identifying and formalizing propensity overfitting, and deriving Norm-POEM are novel and nontrivial.

Significance: Addressing overfitting in BLBF is important for practice (ads, recommendation, search). The method is simple, broadly applicable, and shows consistent gains.

Pros:
- Clear identification of a practical failure mode and a principled fix.
- Theoretical properties (consistency, boundedness) aligned with observed robustness.
- Strong empirical results and diagnostics.

Cons:
- No finite-sample generalization bound for the normalized CRM objective.
- Limited baselines (e.g., no doubly robust or clipped/DR comparisons).
- Experiments limited to multi-label classification.

Overall: I lean to accept; this is a good paper with meaningful contributions, though I am not fully certain due to the missing finite-sample theory and baselines.
