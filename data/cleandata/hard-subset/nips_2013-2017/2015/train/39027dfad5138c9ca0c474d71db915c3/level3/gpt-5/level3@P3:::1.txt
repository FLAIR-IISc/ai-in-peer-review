Summary and relation to prior work
The paper targets batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF), where we wish to evaluate and train a new policy from biased, partial-feedback logs using importance sampling. The authors argue that the conventional inverse-propensity-scored (IPS) estimator used within the Counterfactual Risk Minimization (CRM) framework can drive ERM toward a distinct failure mode they call propensity overfitting: the learner exploits deficiencies in exploration so that, for a given x, it over-emphasizes y values that were either rarely or never observed, yielding degenerate empirical risks. They propose replacing IPS with a self-normalized IPS (SNIPS) estimator, which uses the sum of weights as a multiplicative control variate, and instantiate this within a new learning algorithm, Norm-POEM. They prove strong consistency of SNIPS and emphasize its boundedness and equivariance to loss translation. Empirically, Norm-POEM outperforms POEM on several multi-label datasets, appears more robust to loss translations, and exhibits Ŝ(ĥ) values closer to 1.

This work is closely related to CRM/POEM (Swaminathan & Joachims, ICML’15), off-policy evaluation (Dudík et al., ICML’11; Thomas et al., AAAI’15), variance reduction via clipping/truncation (Ionides, 2008), and weighted/self-normalized importance sampling (Hesterberg, 1995; Kong, 1992; Rubinstein & Kroese). The key contribution is to reframe a well-known estimator (SNIPS) as a learning-time fix to a learning-specific failure mode (propensity overfitting), with both theoretical and empirical support.

Strengths
- Clear identification of a learning-time pathology beyond “just high variance”: propensity overfitting via the empirical sum of weights Ŝ(h).
- Sensible remedy using a classic control-variate technique (self-normalization) that directly counters deviations of Ŝ(h) from 1; bounded and equivariant finite-sample behavior is attractive in practice.
- Theoretical sanity checks (strong consistency) and a practical variance estimate via delta/Normal approximation.
- Consistent empirical gains across datasets; diagnostic reporting of Ŝ(ĥ) convincingly matches the proposed mechanism.
- Code availability is appreciated and increases reproducibility.

Weaknesses and questions
- Connection to variance: The paper treats propensity overfitting and high-variance IPS as distinct, but the relationship remains underspecified. Since Ŝ(h) deviating from 1 and large weight variance both arise from poor overlap, please clarify:
  - When does large weight variance necessarily imply high risk of propensity overfitting in ERM over a rich H?
  - Can you bound |Ŝ(h) − 1| in terms of the variance (or higher moments) of the weights and the effective sample size ESS = (∑ w)^2/∑ w^2? A discussion that decomposes error into loss vs weight contributions would help tie the two phenomena together.
  - Are there examples where weight variance is large but SNIPS offers limited protection because δ dominates, or where variance is moderate yet learning still overfits via Ŝ(h)?
- Line 199 / Example 1 confusion: The example shows that if n is small relative to k and all xi are distinct, hoverfit achieves R̂ = −k under IPS (and 0 after translating the loss). However:
  - Is the claim that ERM “will almost always select hoverfit over h∗” meant for λ = 0 (no variance regularization) only? Under CRM with a nonzero variance penalty (and especially with clipping M), one would expect hoverfit to be heavily penalized. Please clarify the intended setting (pure ERM vs CRM), and, if CRM is intended, explain why the penalty does not prevent selection of hoverfit.
  - After translating δ to be nonnegative, there are many hoverfit′ with R̂ = 0. How are ties broken in practice, and how does CRM’s variance regularizer treat these ties?
  - A small synthetic study varying n/k, λ, and M to show when ERM vs CRM vs Norm-POEM selects hoverfit would make the example more transparent.
- Prior work characterization: The abstract and intro suggest that “virtually all existing BLBF works” use an unbiased estimator. However, many works employ clipping/truncation or self-normalization for evaluation, which introduces bias; doubly robust variants are also common. Please nuance this claim and position your contribution relative to truncated IPS, DR estimators, and weighted/normalized variants explicitly.
- Technical details I did not fully verify due to time: the approximate variance estimator for SNIPS (Eq. 8) via delta/Normal approximations and the implications for the CRM bound; the asymptotic normality conditions under clipping; and the optimization details for nonconvex objectives under L-BFGS. The approach seems sensible, but I cannot vouch for all derivations.
- Empirical baselines and sensitivity:
  - Please include DR and truncated IPS (+CRM) baselines; also report a simple SNIPS-without-CRM variant to isolate the contribution of normalization vs CRM regularization.
  - Sensitivity of performance to clipping threshold M and λ would help practitioners.
  - Report ESS and the distribution of Ŝ(h) across hypotheses during training as additional diagnostics.

Quality
- The idea is technically sound and well-motivated; proofs provided are plausible but I did not check all details. The estimator’s known properties (boundedness, equivariance, consistency) support the claims. The characterization of propensity overfitting is illuminating, and the experiments are convincing, though a broader set of baselines would strengthen the case.

Clarity
- Generally clear and well-organized, with a helpful example. Please sharpen the discussion connecting weight variance, ESS, and propensity overfitting; clarify the ERM vs CRM setting in Example 1; and moderate claims about prior “unbiased” usage.

Originality
- Self-normalized IS is classical; the novelty is the diagnosis of propensity overfitting as a learning-time failure mode and the integration of SNIPS into CRM (Norm-POEM) with analysis and experiments. This is a meaningful conceptual and practical contribution.

Significance
- Important for practitioners training policies from logs in ranking, ads, and recommendation. The fix is simple, robust, and likely to be adopted. The diagnostic Ŝ(h) perspective is also useful.

Pros
- Identifies a real and under-discussed failure mode of IPS-based ERM.
- Simple, principled remedy with strong practical appeal.
- Empirical gains are consistent; diagnostics match theory.
- Code provided.

Cons
- Relationship to variance not fully formalized; missing ESS-based analysis.
- Some overstatements about prior “unbiased” estimators.
- Limited baseline coverage and sensitivity analyses.
- A few parts (Example 1, tie-breaking) could be clearer.

Overall recommendation
- Accept. Despite limited time to verify all technical details, I find the idea sound, the problem important, and the empirical evidence compelling. The paper is interesting and worth reading, and I expect the community to benefit from adopting self-normalized estimators within CRM-style learning from logs.
