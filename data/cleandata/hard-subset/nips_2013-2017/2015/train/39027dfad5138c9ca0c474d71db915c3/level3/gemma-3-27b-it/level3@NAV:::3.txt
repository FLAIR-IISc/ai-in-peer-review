## Review of “The Self-Normalized Estimator for Counterfactual Learning”

This paper addresses the important problem of batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF), specifically focusing on a propensity overfitting issue within the commonly used counterfactual risk estimator. The authors convincingly demonstrate this overfitting and propose a self-normalized estimator as a remedy, resulting in the Norm-POEM algorithm. The work builds directly upon their previous paper [1] and presents a theoretically sound and empirically validated improvement to existing BLBF methods. 

The paper is well-motivated, clearly written, and logically structured. The introduction effectively sets the stage, explaining the challenges of learning from logged data and the role of counterfactual estimation. The identification of “Propensity Overfitting” is a significant contribution, and the explanation, supported by a clear example, is easy to follow. The derivation of the self-normalized estimator and its theoretical properties are presented rigorously, and the connection to control variates is well-explained.

The experimental evaluation provides strong evidence that Norm-POEM outperforms POEM across several datasets. The ablation study, showcasing the continued benefit of variance regularization, and the analysis of Ŝ(ĥ) further solidify the claims. However, the experiments largely replicate the setup of [1]. While the improvement within that context is valuable, the significance of this work would be substantially increased by demonstrating its effectiveness on more diverse datasets, particularly those outside the multi-label classification domain. Real-world applications (e.g., advertising, recommendation systems) were mentioned in the introduction but remain unexplored in the experiments. 

A key weakness is the lack of variance reporting for repeated runs. While the authors average over 10 runs, reporting standard deviations or confidence intervals would provide a more comprehensive understanding of the results' statistical significance.  Furthermore, while the authors discuss the computational efficiency gains, a more detailed runtime comparison, perhaps with other state-of-the-art BLBF algorithms, would be beneficial. 

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Addresses a relevant and important problem in the field of learning from logged data.
*   Presents a novel and theoretically grounded solution (the self-normalized estimator) to a demonstrated issue (Propensity Overfitting).
*   Empirically demonstrates the effectiveness of the proposed method, consistently outperforming the baseline.
*   Clear and well-written presentation.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Experiments are limited in scope, primarily replicating results from [1].
*   Lack of variance reporting hinders a full assessment of the results’ reliability.
*   Significance is somewhat uncertain as real-world applicability remains unproven.



**Overall Assessment:** This is a high-quality paper with a clear contribution. While further experimentation and statistical rigor would strengthen the work, the identified problem and proposed solution are valuable. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the concerns raised above, particularly the need for broader experimental evaluation and variance reporting.
