- Studies batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF) and diagnoses "propensity overfitting" in IPS-based CRM due to non-equivariance and unbounded variance.
- Proposes using self-normalized importance sampling (SNIS) as a multiplicative control-variate, yielding a biased but consistent, bounded, and equivariant risk estimator.
- Introduces Norm-POEM: same exponential-family hypothesis and variance regularizer as POEM but replacing IPS with SNIS and an approximate variance estimate.
- Provides theoretical analysis of SNIS properties (boundedness, equivariance, strong consistency) and a diagnostic Ŝ(h) based on normalized weight sums.
- Empirically evaluates on supervised→bandit multi-label benchmarks using replay; Norm-POEM consistently outperforms POEM and shows Ŝ(h) ≈ 1.
- Well-motivated identification of a concrete failure mode with clear toy example and actionable Ŝ(h) diagnostic.
- The self-normalizing solution is principled, theoretically sound, and directly addresses IPS anomalies.
- Empirical results are compelling: consistent improvements, robustness to loss translation, and better alignment of Ŝ(h) with expectations.
- Clear writing, presentation, and good connection to prior work in off-policy evaluation and variance reduction.
- Incremental contribution relative to POEM: main novelty is plugging SNIS into an existing CRM framework and model class.
- Limited experimental scope: same benchmarks as prior work, no real logged datasets, and no tasks beyond multi-label classification.
- Optimization analysis is weak: nonconvex objective uses L-BFGS without study of convergence, sensitivity, or failure modes.
- Evaluation reporting lacks dispersion measures, ablations on clipping threshold M and regularization λ, and comparisons to strong alternatives.
- Technical arguments for propensity overfitting and SNIS properties are convincing, though finite-sample CRM guarantees with SNIS are not provided.
- Empirical support is positive but narrow; averages without variance make statistical significance unclear.
- Paper is coherent and implementable as a follow-up, but richer baselines and optimization analysis would strengthen claims.
- Writing and organization are strong; Ŝ(h) diagnostic and loss-translation experiments are clear and reproducible.
- Originality is modest: problem framing and CRM from prior work, SNIS is standard; novelty is formalizing propensity overfitting and applying SNIS in CRM.
- Significance is high for BLBF research, but practical impact is uncertain without real-world logs or broader benchmarks.
- Suggested experiments: report standard deviations/confidence intervals and per-dataset error bars over repeated runs.
- Suggested baselines/ablations: clipped IPS with varied M, doubly robust learning, sensitivity to λ and M, and unnormalized IPS with strong regularization.
- Add a real-world logged application or public off-policy benchmark to demonstrate practical impact.
- Provide detailed optimization diagnostics: convergence behavior, robustness to initialization, gradient issues, and wall-clock comparisons.
- Discuss finite-sample bias–variance trade-offs for SNIS vs IPS and give guidance for choosing M and λ.
- Explore hybrid estimators (e.g., self-normalized doubly robust) and whether multiplicative control variates compose with additive ones.
- Arguments for acceptance: identifies an important failure mode, proposes a principled fix with theoretical justification, and shows consistent empirical gains.
- Arguments for rejection: incremental over prior work, narrow experimental scope without variance reporting or broad baselines, and limited analysis of training dynamics.
- Overall: high-quality, well-motivated follow-up that mitigates a real pathology and improves performance, but acceptance should be contingent on stronger experiments and deeper optimization/hyperparameter analysis.
