The paper "The Self-Normalized Estimator for Counterfactual Learning" addresses the problem of batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF), which is an important area of research with applications in various online systems. The authors identify a severe issue with the conventional counterfactual risk estimator used in prior works on BLBF, namely the propensity overfitting problem, and propose an alternative estimator that avoids this problem. The proposed self-normalized estimator is shown to be superior to the conventional estimator in various aspects, including its ability to avoid propensity overfitting and its robustness to the translation of losses.

The paper is well-written, and the presentation is clear. The authors provide a thorough analysis of the problem and the proposed solution, including theoretical results and empirical evaluations. The experiments are conducted on several multi-label classification problems, and the results show that the proposed self-normalized estimator consistently outperforms the conventional estimator.

The main contribution of the paper is the proposal of the self-normalized risk estimator, which is a novel solution to the propensity overfitting problem. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of this estimator through theoretical analysis and empirical evaluations. The paper is a follow-up work on prior research [1], and the main novelty is the self-normalizing risk estimator, which improves empirical performance.

The significance of the paper is uncertain, as it is unclear how the experimental gains translate to real-world applications. While the problem addressed in the paper is relevant, the experimental setup is limited to simulated bandit feedback datasets, and it is unclear whether the proposed solution will generalize to more complex and realistic scenarios. Furthermore, the paper lacks variance reporting for repeated runs, which makes it difficult to assess the reliability of the results.

To strengthen the paper, the authors should consider conducting more extensive experiments, including additional and real-world applications beyond those in [1]. This would help to demonstrate the practical significance of the proposed solution and its potential impact on real-world applications. Additionally, the authors should provide more details on the optimization of the training objective, including the choice of hyperparameters and the convergence of the optimization algorithm.

Overall, the paper is a high-quality, well-motivated work that addresses an important problem in BLBF. While it has some limitations, the proposed self-normalized estimator is a promising solution that warrants further investigation.
