This paper presents a significant contribution to the field of batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF) by identifying a severe problem with the conventional counterfactual risk estimator and proposing an alternative estimator that avoids this issue. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach through an extensive empirical evaluation, showing improved generalization performance and robustness.

The main ideas of the paper can be summarized as follows. The authors first introduce the concept of BLBF and the Counterfactual Risk Minimization (CRM) principle, which provides a general recipe for designing BLBF algorithms. They then identify a problem with the conventional unbiased risk estimator used in prior works on BLBF, which they term "propensity overfitting." This problem occurs when the capacity of the hypothesis space allows for overfitting of the propensity weights, leading to severely biased risk estimates.

To address this issue, the authors propose the use of a multiplicative control variate, which leads to the self-normalized risk estimator. This estimator is shown to avoid the anomalies of the conventional estimator and is strongly consistent, approaching the desired expectation when the sample size is large. The authors then derive a new learning algorithm, called Norm-POEM, based on the CRM principle using the self-normalized estimator.

The empirical evaluation of Norm-POEM shows that it consistently outperforms the conventional estimator, POEM, on several multi-label classification problems. The results also indicate that Norm-POEM avoids propensity overfitting, as the average sum of propensity weights for the selected hypothesis is close to its known expectation of 1. Furthermore, the authors demonstrate that the improved self-normalized estimator no longer requires variance regularization, and the runtime of Norm-POEM is surprisingly faster than POEM.

The strengths of the paper include its insightful analysis of propensity overfitting, the effective use of a multiplicative control variate, and the clear empirical improvement over POEM on an important problem. The paper is well-written and easy to follow, with a clear structure and concise language.

However, there are a few minor issues that need to be addressed. The authors should clarify whether Norm-POEM uses a rescaled delta in [-1,0] like CRM and explain the meaning of "delta > 0" in Table 1 experiments. Additionally, the authors should consider modeling delta(x,y) as a random variable with an expectation over label noise rather than a deterministic function and add an expectation over delta in the true risk setup.

In terms of related work, the authors provide a thorough review of the literature on BLBF and the CRM principle. They also discuss the relationship between BLBF and other areas, such as off-policy evaluation in reinforcement learning and learning under covariate shift. However, the authors could further elaborate on the differences between their approach and other methods that use control variates, such as the doubly robust estimator.

Overall, the paper presents a significant contribution to the field of BLBF and demonstrates the effectiveness of the proposed approach through an extensive empirical evaluation. With some minor revisions to address the issues mentioned above, the paper is ready for publication.
