

This paper addresses a critical issue in batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF), where the conventional counterfactual risk estimator (Equation 2) suffers from a purported "propensity overfitting" problem. The authors propose a self-normalized estimator (Equation 7) that avoids this issue and introduce Norm-POEM, a new learning algorithm based on this estimator. The paper builds on prior work in counterfactual learning, particularly the POEM algorithm from ICML 2015, and demonstrates empirical improvements across multiple multi-label classification datasets.

The paper's main contribution is the identification of a problem with the conventional inverse propensity score estimator and its replacement with a self-normalized variant. However, there is a fundamental misunderstanding in the paper's interpretation of the conventional estimator (Equation 2). The authors claim this estimator exhibits "anomalies" including non-invariance to translation of the loss function, but this claim is based on an incorrect interpretation of the estimator's expectation. The loss δ(x,y) in Equation 2 is not an expected quantity but rather the observed loss value for the specific action taken in the logged data. The expectation interpretation of Equation 2 is technically incorrect, as the estimator is designed to approximate the true risk R(h) = E[δ(x,y)], not to represent the expectation of the observed loss. Consequently, Example 1, which purports to demonstrate propensity overfitting, is invalid because it's based on this misunderstanding. The authors incorrectly assume that translating the loss function should affect the risk estimate in a specific way, but this misinterpretation invalidates their example.

The paper correctly identifies that the conventional estimator (Equation 2) is sensitive to the denominator π = h0(y|x), which can be zero if a particular action was never observed under the logging policy. The self-normalized estimator (Equation 7) indeed addresses this issue by normalizing with the sum of the propensity weights, which makes it more robust to extreme values of π. This is a valid and useful improvement to the conventional estimator.

The experimental results are strong and convincing. Norm-POEM consistently outperforms POEM across all datasets (Scene, Yeast, TMC, LYRL) and approaches the performance of full-information CRF models. The authors demonstrate that Norm-POEM's self-normalized estimator keeps Ŝ(h) close to its expected value of 1, while POEM's estimator shows severe bias. The runtime analysis also shows Norm-POEM converges faster than POEM, which is a practical advantage.

The paper is well-written and clear, with good organization and sufficient detail for reproducibility. The theoretical analysis of the self-normalized estimator (Theorem 2) is sound and correctly establishes its consistency. The connection to control variates and importance sampling literature is appropriate, though the paper could have more explicitly acknowledged that self-normalized estimators have been studied in the importance sampling literature before.

The main weakness is the technical error in interpreting Equation 2, which invalidates the paper's central claim about the "anomaly" in the conventional estimator. This is a significant issue because it undermines the motivation for the paper's main contribution. The authors should revise their commentary to correctly explain the estimator's behavior, noting that the conventional estimator is not inherently flawed in the way they claim, but rather that the self-normalized variant provides practical advantages in terms of robustness to extreme propensity weights.

In terms of originality, the paper builds on the POEM algorithm from ICML 2015, and while the self-normalized estimator is a useful modification, it's not entirely novel in the broader context of importance sampling. However, the specific application to counterfactual learning and the empirical validation are valuable contributions to the field.

The significance of the paper is high, as BLBF is an important problem for real-world applications like ad placement and recommendation systems. The empirical results show that Norm-POEM provides consistent improvements over the state-of-the-art POEM algorithm, which is a meaningful contribution to the field.

The paper's main strength is its strong empirical evaluation, which demonstrates clear practical benefits of the proposed method. The weakness is the technical error in the motivation for the method. If the authors correct this error in the final version, the paper would be a valuable contribution to the literature on counterfactual learning.

Pro: Strong empirical results showing consistent improvements over the state-of-the-art; clear presentation of the self-normalized estimator; practical application to real-world problems.

Con: Fundamental misunderstanding of the conventional estimator's behavior invalidates the paper's central claim about "anomalies"; Example 1 is based on an incorrect interpretation.

Overall, the paper makes a valuable contribution to counterfactual learning, but requires revision to correct the technical misinterpretation of the conventional estimator. With this correction, the paper would be a strong candidate for publication at NIPS. The empirical evidence supporting the self-normalized estimator is compelling, and the Norm-POEM algorithm represents a meaningful improvement over existing methods for batch learning from logged bandit feedback.
