## Review of "The Self-Normalized Estimator for Counterfactual Learning"

This paper addresses the important problem of batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF), a common scenario in many real-world applications where full interaction is not possible. The authors identify a critical issue – propensity overfitting – in the commonly used counterfactual risk estimator, and propose a novel self-normalized estimator to mitigate this. The core idea of using multiplicative control variates to achieve a self-normalizing effect is well-motivated and theoretically sound, building upon prior work in importance sampling and causal inference. The resulting Norm-POEM algorithm is presented clearly and demonstrates empirical improvements over existing methods on standard multi-label classification datasets, mirroring the experimental setup of their previous work [1].

The strengths of this paper lie in its clear identification of a previously unrecognised problem (propensity overfitting) and the elegant solution proposed. The theoretical analysis, while concise, provides a convincing argument for the benefits of the self-normalized estimator. The writing is excellent, making the complex topic accessible. The connection to related work in causal inference, importance sampling, and off-policy evaluation is well-established. 

However, the paper feels somewhat limited in its experimental evaluation. While the results consistently show improvements, the gains are not overwhelmingly large, and the significance of these gains in real-world applications remains unclear. The experiments largely replicate the setup from [1], and the reviewer would have appreciated a more extensive evaluation, including datasets beyond those previously used, and ideally, a demonstration of the method’s effectiveness on a more realistic, real-world application (e.g., ad placement, recommendation systems). Reporting variance across multiple runs would also strengthen the conclusions. The analysis of the training objective optimization process is also lacking; a deeper dive into *why* Norm-POEM converges faster and to a different solution (smaller ||w||) would be valuable. 

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Addresses a relevant and important problem in BLBF.
*   Presents a novel and theoretically justified solution.
*   Clear writing and well-organized presentation.
*   Demonstrates empirical improvements over existing methods.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Experimental evaluation is limited in scope and depth.
*   Significance of experimental gains to real-world applications is unclear.
*   Insufficient analysis of the training objective optimization.



Overall, this is a high-quality paper with a valuable contribution. While the experimental evaluation could be more comprehensive, the identified problem and proposed solution are significant and warrant acceptance. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the points raised regarding experimental scope and analysis.
