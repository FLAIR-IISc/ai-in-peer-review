Summary
The paper tackles batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF) under the Counterfactual Risk Minimization (CRM) paradigm. It diagnoses a systematic failure mode of the standard inverse propensity scoring (IPS) estimator used for counterfactual risk—“propensity overfitting”—where learning over complex hypothesis spaces can exploit the variability of importance weights to produce degenerate ERM solutions. The authors propose replacing the unbiased IPS estimator with a multiplicative control-variate (self-normalized) estimator (SN-IPS/WIS). This estimator is biased but consistent, bounded, and equivariant under translations of the loss, and it uses the known expectation of the sum of weights as a control variate. They instantiate this in a new algorithm, Norm-POEM, within a linear-exponential (CRF-like) hypothesis class and a CRM-style objective that includes an approximate variance penalty for the ratio estimator. Experiments on multi-label datasets (Scene, Yeast, TMC, LYRL) show consistent improvements over POEM (the CRM method that uses IPS), reduced sensitivity to loss translation, better Ŝ(h) diagnostics (closer to 1), and competitive runtime.

Relation to prior work
The work builds directly on CRM/POEM [1] and the extensive literature on importance sampling, variance reduction, and control variates [12, 19, 21–23]. It connects BLBF to off-policy evaluation (OPE) and domain adaptation [4, 8, 11, 14], and positions the proposed estimator as a principled fix when fluctuations in weights dominate variance. While self-normalization (WIS/SNIS) is classical, the paper’s contribution is to (i) identify and formalize “propensity overfitting” in learning (beyond evaluation), (ii) argue that self-normalization directly addresses the equivariance/boundedness anomalies driving this issue, and (iii) integrate SN-IPS into a CRM learning objective for structured prediction.

Strengths
- Clear problem identification: the propensity overfitting phenomenon is well motivated with crisp examples and diagnostics via Ŝ(h).
- Methodologically sound: using multiplicative control variates to enforce normalization is an apt and well-justified solution; the estimator’s equivariance and boundedness are compelling benefits for learning.
- Algorithmic contribution: Norm-POEM is a concrete instantiation with a tractable objective, approximate variance estimate, and practical training.
- Consistent empirical gains: across datasets and replay counts, Norm-POEM outperforms POEM; sensitivity analyses with translated losses agree with theory.
- Good presentation and writing; the paper is easy to follow.

Weaknesses
- Limited experimental scope: datasets and setup mostly mirror [1]. It remains unclear how the improvements translate to real-world BLBF tasks (ads, recommender, search) with large action spaces and heavy-tailed propensities.
- Missing baselines: no comparison to doubly robust (DR) learning/evaluation [6], regression control variates, or more advanced weight stabilization (e.g., capped/clipped IPS + DR). Including DR-WIS or DR with clipping would strengthen the case.
- Variance and stability reporting: the paper reports averages but not variability across runs. Confidence intervals, standard deviations, and effective sample size (ESS) would help quantify robustness.
- Optimization analysis: the objective is non-convex with a ratio estimator variance term; more details on optimization dynamics (e.g., landscapes, convergence diagnostics, sensitivity to initialization) would increase confidence.
- Theoretical gap: while SN-IPS consistency is shown, there is no generalization bound for the self-normalized CRM objective analogous to [1]. Even a discussion or partial result (e.g., bias–variance trade-offs, asymptotic bias O(1/n)) would help.

Quality
- Technically sound and well grounded in classical IS/control-variate theory. The propensity overfitting examples are convincing. The approximate variance formula is standard, but its calibration and bias in finite-sample heavy-tailed regimes should be empirically assessed. Clipping M and λ tuning are discussed, but sensitivity plots and ESS would be informative. The claimed runtime advantage deserves more granular reporting (iterations, wall-clock, Z(x) costs).

Clarity
- The manuscript is clearly written, logically structured, and appropriately cites relevant literatures. Suggestions:
  - Report standard deviations or confidence intervals over the 10 runs.
  - Include ESS and Ŝ(h) distributions across runs.
  - Detail the chosen M and λ, and provide sensitivity curves.
  - Clarify when Z(x) is tractable and any approximations used.

Originality
- The self-normalized estimator is known in IS; the novelty lies in diagnosing propensity overfitting for learning and integrating SN-IPS into CRM for structured output with a variance-regularized objective. As a follow-up to [1], the conceptual framing and learning instantiation are a meaningful extension, though not radically new.

Significance
- The problem is important and common in BLBF/OPE. The proposed fix is simple, principled, and practically beneficial. However, without broader evaluations (e.g., real-world logs, larger action spaces, DR baselines), the ultimate impact on deployed systems remains uncertain.

Requests and questions for the authors
- Please report variance (std/CI) across the 10 runs and include ESS and Ŝ(h) distributions. Are the gains statistically significant?
- Add DR baselines (DR-IPS, DR-WIS; with/without clipping) and possibly regression control variates to contextualize gains.
- Provide ablations on M (clipping) and λ (variance penalty), with sensitivity curves.
- Include at least one real-world or semi-synthetic large-action experiment (e.g., ads/recs) with heavy-tailed propensities; analyze robustness to logging policy quality and support violations.
- Calibrate the approximate variance estimator: does it match empirical variance? Any under/over-confidence?
- Discuss (or provide) a generalization guarantee for the SN-CRM objective or a bound highlighting the bias–variance trade-off.
- Report optimization details: iteration counts, convergence criteria, initialization, and wall-clock breakdowns.

Pros
- Identifies a real and underappreciated failure mode (propensity overfitting) in counterfactual learning.
- Simple, principled estimator that fixes key anomalies (equivariance, boundedness).
- Consistent empirical improvements and strong diagnostics.
- Clear exposition and practical algorithm.

Cons
- Experimental scope is narrow; no DR baselines; limited evidence for real-world impact.
- Lack of formal generalization analysis for the self-normalized CRM objective.
- Limited reporting on variability, sensitivity, and optimization behavior.

Overall assessment
A solid and well-presented follow-up to [1] that addresses an important pitfall in BLBF with a principled estimator and shows consistent gains. I lean positive, but I strongly encourage broader experiments (including DR baselines and real-world or large-action scenarios) and more comprehensive variability/sensitivity reporting to solidify the significance of the contribution.
