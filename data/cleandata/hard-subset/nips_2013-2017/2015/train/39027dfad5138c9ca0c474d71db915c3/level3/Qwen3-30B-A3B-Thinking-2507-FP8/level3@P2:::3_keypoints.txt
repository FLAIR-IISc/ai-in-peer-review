- Addresses propensity overfitting in batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF), a previously unrecognized problem.
- Proposes a self-normalized risk estimator and a new learning algorithm (Norm-POEM) to avoid propensity overfitting.
- Formally defines propensity overfitting and provides a clear illustrative example showing its severity.
- Proves the conventional estimator is not equivariant under loss translation and can yield arbitrarily low risk estimates.
- Shows the self-normalized estimator preserves loss range and is equivariant, resolving identified anomalies.
- Empirical evaluation on multi-label classification datasets shows consistent improvements of Norm-POEM over POEM across datasets and sample sizes.
- Paper is well-written, clearly organized, and presents theory and experiments in a logical, accessible flow.
- Examples illustrating propensity overfitting are effective and help convey the problem’s importance.
- Originality: identifies a novel problem in BLBF and meaningfully applies self-normalization to this context.
- Adequately references prior CRM and importance-sampling literature while extending prior work.
- Significance is limited by experimental scope—only controlled multi-label tasks without real-world deployments.
- Lacks variance estimates for repeated experiments, making statistical significance of improvements unclear.
- Does not deeply analyze optimization properties of Norm-POEM, leaving questions about why it generalizes better.
- Unclear how empirical gains will translate to complex, real-world systems and data distributions.
- Strengths: novel problem identification, strong theoretical analysis, consistent empirical gains, clear writing, meaningful extension of prior work.
- Weaknesses: limited experimental diversity, missing variance reporting, insufficient optimization analysis, unclear practical impact.
- Pro accept arguments: problem is real/significant, solution is theoretically sound and empirically validated, well-written, extends CRM meaningfully.
- Con accept arguments: evaluation limited to one problem class, constrained significance without real-world tests, optimization analysis incomplete.
- Overall recommendation: accept (NIPS) with suggested improvements on variance reporting and broader, more realistic experiments.
