Summary
The paper addresses learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF) via counterfactual risk minimization (CRM). The standard approach uses inverse propensity scoring (IPS) to estimate the risk of a new policy from logs collected by a historical policy with full support. The authors identify a distinct failure mode they call propensity overfitting: when the hypothesis class is rich, empirical risk minimization over IPS can choose models that exploit the importance weights themselves (e.g., by putting probability mass on actions with tiny logging propensities or by avoiding logged actions), yielding degenerate empirical risks that fall outside the loss range or that change under additive translations of the loss. As a remedy, they propose using a multiplicative control variate, leading to a self-normalized IPS estimator (SNIPS) as the counterfactual risk estimator inside CRM. They prove strong consistency, discuss desirable finite-sample properties (boundedness and equivariance), and instantiate a learning algorithm, Norm-POEM, for exponential-family (CRF-like) policies with a variance-regularized CRM objective using an approximate variance formula for the ratio estimator. Experiments on several multilabel datasets (Supervised→Bandit setup) show that Norm-POEM consistently outperforms POEM (which uses IPS), is robust to loss translations, yields Ŝ(ĥ) close to 1 (diagnostic of avoiding propensity overfitting), and has competitive runtime. Code is provided.

Relation to prior work
- BLBF and CRM: Builds directly on [1] (POEM; empirical Bernstein-style variance regularization) and related importance-weighted risk minimization [8,13].
- Off-policy evaluation: Connects to IPS, clipping/truncation [15], and doubly robust (DR) estimators [6].
- Self-normalized importance sampling (SNIPS): Classic in Monte Carlo [12,19,22,23]; also seen in RL and financial Monte Carlo; the paper’s novelty is to highlight propensity overfitting in learning (not just evaluation) and to integrate SNIPS into the learning objective with analysis of its qualitative benefits.
- The paper could better delineate how its use of SNIPS differs from prior BLBF work that employed clipping and DR-based control variates; the conceptual contribution is to show that normalization directly mitigates the propensity-overfitting failure mode.

Strengths
- Identifies and articulates a real and under-discussed failure mode (propensity overfitting) distinct from “just high variance.”
- Proposes a simple, principled fix (SNIPS) with clear finite-sample advantages: boundedness and equivariance.
- Strong law consistency proof and a reasonable approximate variance estimate to enable CRM regularization.
- Clean experimental evidence across multiple datasets; helpful diagnostic Ŝ(h) analyses; robustness to loss translations is convincing.
- Practical impact: easy drop-in replacement for IPS; code availability improves reproducibility.

Weaknesses and questions
- Relationship to variance: While propensity overfitting is induced by weight variability, it is not fully clarified how it differs from “large-variance IPS” in general. A more explicit statement would help: propensity overfitting arises because ERM over a rich H can drive Ŝ(h) far from 1 (e.g., by matching or avoiding logged actions), which correlates with, but is not identical to, having large variance under a fixed h.
- Bounds: The approach relies on an approximate variance for the ratio estimator; a generalization bound analogous to the CRM result in [1] but specialized to SNIPS would strengthen the theory. Even a high-level theorem with assumptions and proof sketch would be valuable.
- Baselines: Comparison is primarily to POEM (IPS). Since the paper discusses DR and clipping, empirical comparisons to (i) clipped IPS-POEM, (ii) DR-based learners, and (iii) self-normalized DR would better situate the contribution.
- Claim of unbiased estimator usage in prior BLBF: Many works in practice use clipping/truncation (introducing bias). Please clarify wording (“unbiased IPS family” vs “exactly unbiased”), and position SNIPS relative to clipped-IPS learners.
- Example clarity (the “k vs n” argument): The hoverfit example could be clearer. For uniform logging with pi=1/k, choosing h that places probability 1 on the logged actions yields terms δi/ pi = k δi. Since δi ≤ −1, the empirical risk can be ≤ −k, hence arbitrarily low as k grows. A short expectation calculation explaining why the sample with distinct xi makes h match each yi would help readers.
- Scope and scalability: Discussion of scalability to very large action spaces and training with stochastic optimization is brief. Some practical guidance on when L-BFGS suffices versus mini-batch approximations would be useful.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The use of control variates and SNIPS is principled; the consistency claim is standard. The approximate variance formula is reasonable, but a formal learning bound for the SNIPS-CRM objective is missing. Empirical methodology mirrors prior work and is solid.
- Clarity: Generally well written. I recommend tightening the distinction between loss overfitting and propensity overfitting, and adding the requested clarifications above (variance link; example derivation; prior-work “unbiased” claim).
- Originality: While SNIPS is known, identifying propensity overfitting as a learning-time pathology and remedying it via normalization within CRM is a useful and, to my knowledge, novel angle.
- Significance: The fix is simple and seems to matter in practice; results are consistent across datasets. Many BLBF applications (recommendation, ads) can benefit.

Suggestions for the authors
- Add a section explicitly relating propensity overfitting to importance-weight variance: provide a small lemma or visualization showing how ERM over H can push Ŝ(h) away from 1 even when per-h variance is moderate.
- Provide a brief generalization result (even with assumptions) for the SNIPS-CRM objective, or at least a discussion of when the approximate variance is reliable.
- Include baselines: clipped-IPS POEM, DR learners, and self-normalized DR.
- Clarify the language on “unbiased estimator” in prior work and the practical prevalence of clipping.
- Expand the example’s derivation; show both negative-loss and translated-loss cases algebraically.
- Report sensitivity to M and λ, and discuss hyperparameter selection bias under counterfactual validation.

Reproducibility
- Code provided; datasets standard; hyperparameter tuning protocol described. This is commendable.

Pros for acceptance
- Clear identification of an important failure mode with a principled and practical remedy.
- Strong empirical evidence and diagnostics; robust improvements over POEM.
- Simple method likely to be adopted by practitioners; code available.

Cons for acceptance
- Lack of a dedicated generalization bound for SNIPS-CRM; limited baselines beyond POEM.
- Some claims and examples would benefit from clearer exposition.

Overall recommendation
- I recommend acceptance. Despite limited time to verify every technical detail and novelty claim, the idea is sensible, well-motivated, and empirically validated; it addresses a real pain point in BLBF and should be of interest to the NIPS community.

Reviewer confidence
- Moderate: I may have missed nuances in the technical sections due to time constraints.
