This paper addresses a critical issue in batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF): propensity overfitting in the commonly used counterfactual risk estimator. The authors demonstrate that this estimator can be susceptible to overfitting the propensity weights, leading to poor generalization performance, even when the underlying loss function is well-behaved. To mitigate this, they propose a self-normalized estimator and a corresponding learning algorithm, Norm-POEM, which consistently outperforms existing methods on multi-label classification tasks.

The paper is well-motivated, building upon the established framework of CRM [1] and relating BLBF to broader areas like causal inference, domain adaptation, and off-policy evaluation. The introduction effectively highlights the practical relevance of learning from historical logs in online systems. The authors clearly articulate the problem of propensity overfitting with a compelling example (Example 1) and convincingly argue why existing solutions like thresholding are insufficient. The proposed self-normalized estimator is well-grounded in importance sampling theory and provides a theoretically sound alternative to the conventional estimator. The presentation of Norm-POEM, leveraging linear rules and a CRF-like hypothesis space, is clear and logical. The experimental evaluation is thorough, utilizing a standard benchmark setup and demonstrating consistent improvements over POEM [1]. The ablation study (Table 3) and analysis of Ŝ(ĥ) (Table 2) further strengthen the claims, providing insights into the mechanism by which Norm-POEM avoids overfitting.

The strengths of the paper are numerous. Technically, the analysis is rigorous, and the proofs (referenced to previous work) appear solid. The identification of propensity overfitting is a significant contribution, as it reveals a subtle but crucial flaw in the standard approach to BLBF. The self-normalized estimator offers an elegant solution, and the empirical results convincingly demonstrate its effectiveness. The clarity of the writing is commendable, with clear explanations of complex concepts and well-organized sections. The code availability is a welcome addition.

However, the paper does have some weaknesses. While the theoretical analysis establishes consistency, a more detailed understanding of the statistical properties of the self-normalized estimator – beyond the asymptotic guarantees – would be valuable. The experiments, while comprehensive, primarily focus on multi-label classification. Exploring the performance of Norm-POEM on other types of problems (e.g., contextual bandits with continuous actions) would broaden the applicability of the findings. A discussion of the computational cost of the self-normalized estimator, beyond the observation that it converges faster, could be beneficial, particularly concerning the approximate variance estimate and the use of L-BFGS. While the connection to broader literature on importance sampling is made, a more nuanced discussion of how this work extends or deviates from existing techniques in that field would be helpful. The fact that the variance regularization (λ) still helps with the self-normalized estimator could be explored further - does this indicate that propensity overfitting is not *fully* eliminated, but just substantially reduced?

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novel identification of propensity overfitting, a previously overlooked issue in BLBF.
*   Theoretically sound self-normalized estimator that addresses the identified problem.
*   Consistent and statistically significant empirical improvements over existing methods.
*   Clear and well-written presentation of the technical details and experimental results.
*   Practical relevance and potential impact on real-world applications of BLBF.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

*   The theoretical analysis, while solid, could benefit from a more detailed characterization of the estimator’s statistical properties.
*   The experimental evaluation is limited to multi-label classification, and broader applicability needs to be demonstrated.
*   The computational complexity analysis could be more thorough.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of learning from logged bandit feedback. The identification of propensity overfitting and the proposed self-normalized estimator represent a significant advance in the state of the art. While there are some minor weaknesses, the strengths of the paper outweigh them considerably. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions. Specifically, I suggest the authors consider adding a discussion of the limitations of the current experimental evaluation and potentially exploring the performance of Norm-POEM on additional problem domains. A deeper exploration into the impact of the variance regularization with the self-normalized estimator would also be worthwhile.
