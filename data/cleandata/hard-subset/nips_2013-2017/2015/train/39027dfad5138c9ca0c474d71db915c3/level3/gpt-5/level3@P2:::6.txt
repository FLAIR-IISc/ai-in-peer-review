Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF) under the Counterfactual Risk Minimization (CRM) framework, where risk is estimated via importance weighting of logged data collected by a historical policy. The authors identify a failure mode they call propensity overfitting: when optimizing the conventional inverse propensity (IPS) risk estimator over rich hypothesis classes, the learner can drive the empirical risk by manipulating the sum of importance weights rather than fitting low-loss examples, leading to degenerate solutions sensitive to loss translations and potentially unbounded empirical risks. They diagnose this via the statistic Ŝ(h), the average normalized weight, which should have expectation 1 but can deviate substantially for overfitting hypotheses.

As a remedy, they propose replacing IPS with the self-normalized importance sampling (SNIS) risk estimator, leveraging multiplicative control variates. This estimator is bounded, equivariant to additive translations of the loss, and strongly consistent (though biased in finite samples). They integrate it into CRM to yield Norm-POEM, using an approximate variance estimator for the ratio estimator in the empirical Bernstein-style regularization. Empirical results on multilabel classification datasets using the supervised-to-bandit protocol show consistent improvements over POEM and robustness to loss translation, along with faster convergence.

The paper is well positioned relative to counterfactual evaluation and learning from logged bandits (e.g., CRM, doubly robust, clipping) and to classic variance reduction in importance sampling and control variates. The self-normalized estimator is known in Monte Carlo, sequential Monte Carlo, and off-policy evaluation (e.g., weighted importance sampling); here the novelty is its principled use within CRM to address propensity overfitting in learning, and the resulting practical algorithm.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The propensity overfitting phenomenon is clearly demonstrated (both through a simple constructed example and diagnostics), and the properties of the SN estimator (boundedness, equivariance, strong consistency) are correct and well supported. The variance estimator used for the ratio estimator is a standard approximation (delta method / normal approximation).
- Theoretical coverage: While the paper motivates CRM via an empirical Bernstein bound, the adaptation to the SN estimator is done via an approximate variance; a corresponding finite-sample generalization bound for SN-CRM is not provided. This is the main theoretical gap: it would strengthen the work to show a bound analogous to prior CRM results, even if with mild additional conditions.
- Empirical evaluation: The experiments are thorough within the supervised→bandit simulation protocol, demonstrate the claimed failure modes for IPS-CRM, and show robust gains for Norm-POEM. Ablations on variance regularization and diagnostics via Ŝ(ĥ) are helpful. However, comparisons to strong alternative estimators (e.g., doubly robust and its self-normalized/weighted variants) are missing, and experiments on real logs would further validate applicability.

Clarity
The paper is clearly written and well organized. The diagnosis via Ŝ(h) is intuitive. The presentation of the SN estimator and its properties is accessible. Suggestions for clarity:
- Provide pseudocode for Norm-POEM and explicitly list hyperparameters and tuning protocol.
- Clearly state the clipping scheme for SN (how M is applied in numerator/denominator) and discuss induced bias.
- Summarize computational complexity per iteration and the practical conditions under which Z(x) is tractable.

Originality
Identifying propensity overfitting as a distinct failure mode of IPS-CRM and showing that SNIS addresses it within a CRM learning objective is novel and insightful. The SN estimator itself is classical, but its integration and the diagnostic perspective are original contributions in the BLBF literature.

Significance
Counterfactual learning from logs is central to search, recommendation, and ads. The failure mode addressed here is practically important, and the proposed fix is simple, principled, and effective. The work should be of interest to both practitioners and researchers working on off-policy learning and evaluation.

Strengths
- Clear identification and analysis of a practically relevant failure mode (propensity overfitting).
- Principled, minimal modification (SNIS) with desirable properties (boundedness, equivariance, consistency).
- Effective integration into CRM and a concrete algorithm (Norm-POEM).
- Empirical results show consistent improvements and robustness to loss translation; helpful diagnostics.
- Code availability and reasonable computational profile.

Weaknesses
- No finite-sample generalization bound for SN-CRM; reliance on approximate variance and asymptotics.
- Experimental comparisons lack strong baselines such as doubly robust (DR) and self-normalized DR/weighted DR.
- Evaluation is limited to simulated bandit feedback; no experiments on real-world logs.
- Limited discussion of the bias–variance trade-off introduced by normalization and clipping in the SN estimator.
- Some related work on weighted importance sampling in RL (e.g., Precup, Sutton, Singh, 2000/2001) could be cited for completeness.

Questions and suggestions for the authors
- Can you provide a finite-sample bound for SN-CRM, perhaps leveraging a concentration result for ratio estimators with clipped weights?
- How exactly is clipping applied for SN—do you clip the weights before both numerator and denominator? Please discuss the induced bias formally or empirically.
- How does Norm-POEM compare to DR and clipped DR, and to self-normalized DR, under the same protocol?
- Sensitivity analysis: How sensitive are results to λ and M? Are there heuristics for choosing M that balance variance and bias for SN?
- Can you include at least one experiment on real logged data with known propensities?

Pros for acceptance
- Addresses an important and underappreciated failure mode in BLBF.
- Simple, theoretically motivated fix that improves robustness and performance.
- Clear diagnostics and convincing empirical evidence within the chosen protocol.
- Likely to influence practice in counterfactual learning from logs.

Cons for acceptance
- Missing finite-sample theory for the proposed CRM objective with SN.
- Missing comparisons to DR-family baselines and self-normalized DR.
- No real-world logged-data experiments.

Overall recommendation
Lean accept. This is a solid, well-motivated contribution that advances the practice of counterfactual learning by making CRM more robust. Strengthening the theoretical guarantees for SN-CRM and broadening empirical comparisons would further improve the paper, but the current version already offers valuable insights and a practically useful algorithm.
