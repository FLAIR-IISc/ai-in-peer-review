Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF), where training must proceed from historical logs generated by a stochastic logging policy. It critiques the conventional counterfactual risk estimator used in Counterfactual Risk Minimization (CRM) and related off-policy learning methodsÔÇönamely inverse propensity scoring (IPS), possibly with clippingÔÇöshowing that empirical risk minimization with IPS can ÔÇťpropensity overfit.ÔÇŁ The core issue is that the IPS estimator is not equivariant to shifts of the loss and has unbounded variance; in finite samples, ERM can prefer hypotheses that arbitrarily inflate/deflate importance weights rather than genuinely improving loss. The paper proposes a multiplicative control-variate solution: the self-normalized importance sampling (SNIS) estimator, used here as a self-normalized risk estimator within CRM. This yields a biased but consistent estimator that is bounded, equivariant, and empirically more robust.

Building on [1] (POEM), the authors introduce Norm-POEM, which keeps the same exponential-family hypothesis class and the CRM-style variance regularization, but replaces IPS with the self-normalized estimator and a corresponding approximate variance estimate. They analyze the estimatorÔÇÖs properties (boundedness, equivariance, strong consistency), give a diagnostic based on the normalized weights sum S╠é(h), and evaluate on multi-label classification datasets using the standard ÔÇťsupervisedÔćĺbanditÔÇŁ replay methodology also used in [1]. Norm-POEM consistently outperforms POEM and exhibits S╠é(h) close to 1, supporting the propensity-overfitting diagnosis. The work is well-situated within prior literatures on off-policy evaluation, importance sampling, and control variates, and clearly distinguishes multiplicative control variates (SNIS) from additive ones (e.g., doubly robust estimators).

Strengths
- Well-motivated identification of a concrete failure mode (propensity overfitting) for IPS-based CRM. The toy example and S╠é(h) diagnostic make the phenomenon clear and actionable.
- The self-normalizing solution is principled (rooted in Monte Carlo/SNIS), theoretically sound (consistency), and addresses the precise anomalies (lack of equivariance, unboundedness).
- Empirically compelling: consistent improvements over POEM across datasets, robustness to loss translation, and better alignment of S╠é(h) with its expectation.
- Clear writing and presentation; the paper is easy to follow, with explicit connections to prior work in BLBF, off-policy evaluation, and variance reduction.

Weaknesses
- Incremental relative to [1]: the main novelty is the self-normalized risk estimator plugged into the CRM framework, with similar model class and training setup.
- Limited experimental scope: essentially the same supervisedÔćĺbandit multi-label benchmarks as [1], no real logged applications, and no tasks beyond the multi-label setting.
- Optimization details are under-analyzed: the objective is nonconvex, uses L-BFGS without stochastic optimization; there is little exploration of convergence behavior, sensitivity, or failure modes.
- Evaluation reporting would benefit from variance/standard deviations over repeated runs, ablations on clipping threshold M and regularization ╬╗, and comparisons to strong alternatives (e.g., clipped IPS baselines, additive control variates/doubly robust learning objectives).

Quality
- Technical soundness: Arguments for propensity overfitting are convincing; the properties of SNIS are standard and correctly applied; the consistency theorem is appropriate. The approximate variance formula is reasonable, though finite-sample guarantees for the CRM objective with SNIS are not developed.
- Empirical support: Clear, positive results versus POEM, but limited breadth. Reporting averages without dispersion makes it hard to judge statistical significance and robustness.
- Completeness: As a follow-up to [1], the work is a coherent, implementable improvement. However, a deeper analysis of optimization and richer baselines would strengthen the claims.

Clarity
- The paper is well written, well organized, and easy to reproduce from. The S╠é(h) diagnostic and translation-of-loss experiments crisply illustrate the problem and solution.

Originality
- Problem framing and CRM are from [1]; SNIS as a control variate is classic. The novelty lies in recognizing and formalizing propensity overfitting within CRM and deploying SNIS as a learning objective with a tailored variance regularizer. This is a thoughtful, if modest, conceptual advance with a practical algorithmic consequence.

Significance
- The topic is important for off-policy learning in interactive systems. The empirical gains look meaningful, but the lack of real-world logs and broader applications makes it hard to assess real-world impact. The proposed approach is likely to be adopted by researchers in BLBF; practitioner adoption would be bolstered by demonstrations beyond synthetic replay.

Requests and suggestions
- Report dispersion (standard deviation or confidence intervals) over repeated trials (the paper averages over 10 runs). Include per-dataset boxplots or error bars.
- Expand baselines: clipped IPS with varied M; doubly robust estimators for learning; sensitivity to ╬╗ and M; ablations on the variance regularizer; an unnormalized IPS baseline with strong regularization to separate estimator vs regularization effects.
- Add at least one real-world logged application (e.g., recommendation or ad placement) or a public off-policy evaluation benchmark with known propensities.
- Provide more detailed analysis of optimization: convergence diagnostics, robustness to initialization, gradient pathologies, and wall-clock vs. iterations; clarify when L-BFGS fails/succeeds.
- Discuss finite-sample trade-offs: quantify biasÔÇôvariance for SNIS vs IPS under typical propensity dispersion; provide guidance for choosing M, ╬╗.
- Consider hybrid estimators (e.g., self-normalized doubly robust) and whether the multiplicative control-variate idea composes with additive control variates.

Arguments for acceptance
- Identifies and explains an important failure mode for a widely used estimator in CRM.
- Proposes a principled, simple, and effective fix with clear theoretical justification.
- Demonstrates consistent empirical improvements and desirable robustness properties.
- Clear and careful presentation.

Arguments for rejection
- Incremental over [1]; primary novelty is an established estimator integrated into the prior framework.
- Experimental scope is narrow and lacks real-world validation; no variance reporting or broad baselines.
- Limited analysis of the nonconvex optimization objective and training dynamics.

Overall
A high-quality and well-motivated follow-up to [1] with a principled estimator that improves empirical performance and mitigates a real pathology. I lean positive, contingent on stronger experiments (variance reporting and broader/real-world evaluations) and deeper analysis of optimization and hyperparameter sensitivity.
