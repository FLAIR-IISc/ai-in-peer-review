

This paper investigates a critical issue in batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF): the propensity overfitting problem that plagues conventional counterfactual risk minimization (CRM) approaches. The authors identify that the standard unbiased importance sampling estimator (Equation 2) suffers from anomalous behavior when used in empirical risk minimization, leading to severely biased risk estimates for complex hypothesis spaces. They propose a self-normalized risk estimator (Equation 7) using multiplicative control variates, which they prove avoids these degeneracies while maintaining strong consistency. The paper develops Norm-POEM, a learning algorithm based on this estimator, and demonstrates empirical improvements over the conventional POEM approach across multiple multi-label classification datasets.

The paper makes a technically sound contribution that builds upon prior work [1] by addressing a previously overlooked vulnerability in BLBF. The theoretical analysis is rigorous, and the experimental evaluation is well-designed to validate the core claims. The writing is clear and well-organized, making the technical content accessible to readers.

However, the paper has notable limitations. While the proposed self-normalized estimator is theoretically sound, the significance of the work remains uncertain. The experimental results are confined to simulated bandit feedback on standard supervised datasets, with no real-world applications or deeper analysis of how these gains translate to practical settings. The authors claim "consistent" improvements over POEM, but the paper lacks variance reporting across repeated runs (e.g., standard deviations) and provides limited discussion of the training-objective optimization dynamics. The paper also doesn't sufficiently explore how the proposed method might scale to more complex hypothesis spaces or larger-scale applications.

The novelty is incremental, extending prior work [1] with a specific technical improvement rather than introducing a fundamentally new paradigm. While the problem addressed is relevant to real-world applications like ad placement and recommendation systems, the paper fails to convincingly demonstrate how these improvements would benefit practitioners. The authors should provide more extensive experiments, including real-world applications, and report variance metrics for all results.

Overall, this is a high-quality, well-motivated paper with clear theoretical contributions, but its significance is undermined by limited experimental scope and insufficient real-world validation. The work is technically sound and well-written, but the lack of deeper analysis and real-world evidence makes its impact less compelling.
