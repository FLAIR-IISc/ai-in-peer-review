**Summary**

This paper addresses the problem of batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF), a crucial setting for improving interactive systems like recommenders or ad platforms using historical data. The authors build upon the Counterfactual Risk Minimization (CRM) principle, which is a standard approach for this problem. The central contribution is the identification of a severe and previously overlooked failure mode in the conventional inverse propensity score (IPS) estimator used within CRM, which they term "Propensity Overfitting". This overfitting occurs when a complex hypothesis exploits the structure of the importance weights to achieve an artificially low empirical risk, not by fitting the observed losses, but by assigning high probability to training examples where the logging policy assigned low probability. This can lead to arbitrarily poor risk estimates and degenerate solutions.

To solve this problem, the authors propose replacing the standard unbiased IPS estimator with a self-normalized (or ratio) estimator. This is motivated by framing the sum of importance weights as a multiplicative control variate with a known expectation of 1. This new estimator is shown to be consistent and has desirable properties that the standard estimator lacks: it is equivariant to translations in the loss function and its estimates are always bounded within the range of the observed losses. The authors instantiate this approach within the CRM framework to create a new learning algorithm, Normalized Policy Optimizer for Exponential Models (Norm-POEM). Through a series of well-controlled experiments on multi-label classification tasks, they demonstrate that Norm-POEM consistently and significantly outperforms its predecessor (POEM), is robust to loss transformations, and successfully avoids the propensity overfitting problem.

**Quality**

The paper is of high technical quality. The claims are well-supported by a combination of a clear illustrative example, theoretical analysis, and thorough empirical evaluation.

*   The identification of the "Propensity Overfitting" problem is sharp and insightful. Example 1 provides a crystal-clear illustration of how the standard IPS estimator can fail in a way that is fundamentally different from traditional overfitting in supervised learning.
*   The proposed solution—the self-normalized estimator—is principled and theoretically sound. The authors correctly show that while the estimator introduces a small bias, it is strongly consistent (Theorem 2) and resolves the key anomalies of the standard estimator (equivariance, boundedness).
*   The experimental methodology is rigorous. By using the "Supervised -> Bandit" conversion, the authors create a controlled environment where they can measure true generalization performance, providing a solid ground truth for evaluating their claims. The experiments convincingly show that the performance gains of Norm-POEM are directly linked to its ability to avoid propensity overfitting, as evidenced by the analysis of Ŝ(h).

**Clarity**

The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The logical flow is easy to follow, starting from the problem motivation, moving to the proposed solution, and finishing with empirical validation. The authors define the problem setting and notation clearly. The core idea of propensity overfitting is explained with an intuitive example before being formalized, which greatly aids reader comprehension. The connection between the self-normalized estimator and control variates provides a strong conceptual grounding for the proposed method. The experimental section is clear, and the results presented in the tables and figures directly support the paper's central arguments.

**Originality**

The main contribution of this paper is not the invention of the self-normalized estimator itself, which is a well-known technique in the Monte Carlo and survey statistics literature. Rather, the originality lies in:
1.  Identifying and clearly articulating the "Propensity Overfitting" problem as a distinct and serious failure mode for learning with the standard IPS estimator. To my knowledge, this specific type of overfitting in the context of CRM has not been previously identified in the literature.
2.  The novel application of the self-normalized estimator as a direct and elegant solution to this problem within a learning (as opposed to just evaluation) framework.

This work clearly distinguishes itself from prior work on CRM (e.g., [1]) by diagnosing and fixing a fundamental flaw in its core estimator. The related work section is comprehensive and appropriately positions the paper's contributions.

**Significance**

The paper's results are highly significant. Learning from logged bandit feedback is a problem of immense practical importance, and the CRM framework is a leading approach. By exposing a critical vulnerability in the standard methodology and providing a simple, robust, and effective fix, this work represents a substantial advance for the field. Practitioners using or developing systems based on off-policy or counterfactual learning are likely to benefit directly from these insights and adopt the proposed estimator. The paper could influence future research by encouraging a more critical examination of the properties of estimators used in counterfactual learning, beyond simple unbiasedness.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Identifies a novel, subtle, and important problem ("Propensity Overfitting") in a widely used learning framework.
*   Proposes a simple, theoretically sound, and empirically validated solution that significantly improves robustness and performance.
*   The paper is extremely well-written and the core concepts are presented with exceptional clarity.
*   The findings are significant for both researchers and practitioners working on learning from logged data.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   The core technical tool (the self-normalized estimator) is not new, though its application to solve this specific learning problem is.
*   The experiments are conducted in a simulated bandit setting. While this is a strong and justified methodology for this type of work, results on a real-world logged bandit dataset would further strengthen the claims.
*   The paper notes that the proposed variance estimator does not admit stochastic optimization. A slightly more detailed discussion on the computational trade-offs for very large-scale datasets would be welcome.

Overall, the arguments for acceptance far outweigh the minor points against. This is a strong paper that makes a clear and valuable contribution.
