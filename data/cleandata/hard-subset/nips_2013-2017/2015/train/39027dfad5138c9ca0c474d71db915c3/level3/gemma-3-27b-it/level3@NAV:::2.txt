## Review of "The Self-Normalized Estimator for Counterfactual Learning"

This paper addresses a critical problem in batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF): propensity overfitting in the commonly used counterfactual risk estimator. The authors convincingly argue that this estimator, while unbiased, can lead to poor generalization due to its sensitivity to the historical policy’s propensity scores, particularly in high-capacity hypothesis spaces. They propose a self-normalized estimator as a remedy, demonstrating its theoretical advantages – specifically, equivariance and bounded risk – and empirically validating it through experiments on multi-label classification datasets. 

The paper is well-motivated, situating the problem within the broader contexts of causal inference, domain adaptation, and off-policy evaluation. The connection to previous work on importance sampling and counterfactual risk minimization is thoroughly discussed, effectively placing the contribution in context. The introduction of "Propensity Overfitting" as a distinct phenomenon is insightful, and the illustrative Example 1 effectively highlights the issue. The derivation of the self-normalized estimator is clear, and the discussion of its properties, including equivariance, is particularly valuable. 

However, the commentary surrounding Equation (2) is misleading and fundamentally undermines the correctness of the initial arguments. The loss in (2) *is* the expected quantity, and the claim of non-invariance to translation is incorrect. This impacts the validity of Example 1, which is built upon this erroneous premise.  Furthermore, the sensitivity to the denominator π in (2) is a common characteristic of importance sampling estimators and isn't a novel issue uniquely addressed by the self-normalized version. While the modified estimator in (7) is a sensible improvement – mollifying the effects of π is a beneficial approach – the theoretical justification initially presented is flawed.

The experimental results are compelling, demonstrating that Norm-POEM consistently outperforms POEM and approaches the performance of a full-information CRF. This provides strong empirical evidence for the effectiveness of the proposed approach, despite the theoretical misstep. 

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Addresses a relevant and important problem in BLBF.
*   Proposes a plausible and empirically validated solution (self-normalized estimator).
*   Demonstrates strong performance improvements over existing methods.
*   Clear writing and well-organized presentation.

**Arguments against acceptance (mitigated by revision):**

*   The core theoretical argument concerning Equation (2) and its properties is fundamentally incorrect.



**Overall:** This is a valuable contribution to the field, but its acceptance is contingent upon the authors carefully revising the commentary surrounding Equation (2) and addressing the associated inaccuracies. If these revisions are made, the paper is worthy of publication at NIPS. I strongly request the authors clarify the theoretical foundation of their claims.
