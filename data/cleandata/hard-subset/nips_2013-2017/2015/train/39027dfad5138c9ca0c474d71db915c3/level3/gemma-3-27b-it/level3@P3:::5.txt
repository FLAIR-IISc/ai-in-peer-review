This paper addresses a critical issue in batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF): propensity overfitting, a phenomenon where the standard counterfactual risk estimator can lead to severely biased risk estimates and poor generalization. The authors propose a self-normalized estimator as an alternative, demonstrably avoiding this problem and resulting in a new algorithm, Norm-POEM, which significantly outperforms the original POEM.

The paper builds directly on prior work in BLBF, particularly Swaminathan & Joachims (2015), and contextualizes its contribution within the broader fields of causal inference, domain adaptation, and off-policy evaluation. It correctly identifies the limitations of existing importance sampling-based estimators and articulates how these limitations manifest in the context of learning from historical logs. The connection to related concepts like propensity score matching and doubly robust estimators is appropriately acknowledged. The problem statement is clearly motivated by the practical need for learning from existing system logs, such as those generated by ad platforms and recommendation systems.

**Strengths:**

* **Technical Soundness:** The core technical contribution – the self-normalized estimator – is well-motivated and theoretically justified. The proof of Theorem 2 establishes the strong consistency of the estimator. The explanation of propensity overfitting is particularly insightful, detailing *why* the conventional estimator fails and illustrating this with a compelling example.
* **Originality:** The formulation of the self-normalized estimator as a direct response to propensity overfitting is a novel and important contribution. While self-normalization has been used in other contexts (financial applications, reinforcement learning), its application to address this specific problem within BLBF is original. The resulting Norm-POEM algorithm represents a significant refinement of the POEM framework.
* **Significance:** The potential impact of this work is substantial. The improved robustness and faster training speed of Norm-POEM, as demonstrated empirically, suggest it could be widely adopted for real-world applications. Addressing propensity overfitting is crucial for reliably learning from historical data, a common scenario in many online systems.
* **Clarity:** The paper is, for the most part, exceptionally well-written and easy to follow. The exposition is clear, and the mathematical notation is generally well-defined. The inclusion of examples helps to illustrate the key concepts. The connection to existing algorithms (POEM) makes the contribution more accessible.
* **Quality of Experiments:** The empirical evaluation is thorough and well-designed, following the established "Supervised → Bandit" methodology. The consistent outperformance of Norm-POEM over POEM across multiple datasets is convincing. The ablation study (Table 3) further strengthens the findings. The inclusion of runtime comparisons is also valuable.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Variance Estimator Complexity:** While the authors acknowledge the increased computational cost of the variance estimator in Norm-POEM, and surprisingly find faster convergence, a more detailed analysis of this complexity (perhaps relating it to dataset size or feature dimensionality) would be beneficial.
* **Limited Discussion of Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** While the paper mentions hyperparameter calibration, a more in-depth discussion of the sensitivity of Norm-POEM’s performance to the choice of λ and M would be helpful.
* **Connections to Existing Variance Reduction Techniques:** While the paper cites related work on variance reduction, the connection to other, more recent techniques could be more explicitly drawn.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper addresses a significant and previously overlooked problem in BLBF.
* The proposed solution (self-normalized estimator) is theoretically sound, empirically validated, and demonstrably improves upon the state-of-the-art.
* The writing is clear, the experiments are well-executed, and the results are compelling.
* The algorithm (Norm-POEM) is practical and has the potential for widespread adoption.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

* The increased complexity of the variance estimation could be a barrier to adoption, though the observed faster convergence mitigates this concern.
* The paper could benefit from a more detailed analysis of hyperparameter sensitivity.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a high-quality paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of batch learning from logged bandit feedback. The problem of propensity overfitting is clearly articulated, the proposed solution is elegant and effective, and the experimental results are convincing. The paper meets all the criteria for acceptance to NIPS and is likely to have a lasting impact on the field. I strongly recommend acceptance.
