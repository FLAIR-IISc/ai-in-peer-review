

# Review for "The Self-Normalized Estimator for Counterfactual Learning"

This paper addresses an important problem in batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF), where the conventional counterfactual risk estimator suffers from propensity overfitting when used in Empirical Risk Minimization (ERM). The authors propose replacing the standard unbiased estimator with a self-normalized estimator that avoids this issue, leading to a new algorithm called Norm-POEM. This work builds upon the Counterfactual Risk Minimization (CRM) principle introduced by Swaminathan and Joachims (ICML 2015), which itself extended the POEM algorithm for structured output prediction.

The paper's main technical contribution is the identification of a problem with the conventional estimator (Equation 2) and the proposal of a self-normalized alternative (Equation 7). The authors demonstrate that their approach avoids the propensity overfitting problem and achieves superior empirical performance across multiple multi-label classification datasets.

**Strengths:**
The paper makes a significant practical contribution to counterfactual learning, which is highly relevant to real-world applications like ad placement and recommendation systems. The empirical evaluation is thorough and convincing, showing consistent improvements of Norm-POEM over POEM across multiple datasets (Scene, Yeast, TMC, LYRL), with results approaching those of full-information CRF (the "skyline" benchmark). The authors provide clear experimental evidence that Norm-POEM avoids the propensity overfitting problem (Table 2), with the self-normalized estimator maintaining the expected value of the propensity weights (Ŝ(h) ≈ 1), unlike POEM. The theoretical justification for the self-normalized estimator (Theorem 2) is sound and demonstrates strong consistency.

**Weaknesses:**
The paper contains a significant technical error in its analysis of the conventional estimator. The commentary around Equation (2) incorrectly interprets the loss as an expectation, and the claim of an "anomaly" (non-invariance to translation of the loss) is based on this misunderstanding. Example 1, which is central to the paper's motivation, is invalid due to this error. The authors claim that translating the loss by a constant C should shift R̂(h) by C, but this is only true for the true risk R(h), not for the finite-sample estimator R̂(h). The estimator in Equation (2) is correctly defined as an unbiased estimator of R(h), but the authors incorrectly claim it's not equivariant, when in fact it's the finite-sample estimator that has this property (not the true risk).

The paper would benefit from a clearer exposition of the problem, particularly clarifying the distinction between the true risk (which is equivariant) and the finite-sample estimator (which is not). The proposed self-normalized estimator (Equation 7) is theoretically sound and preferable, but the paper's initial motivation for this approach is undermined by the technical error in the analysis.

**Quality:** The empirical evaluation is strong and well-executed, with careful experimental design and consistent results across multiple datasets. The theoretical results (Theorem 2) are sound. However, the theoretical analysis of the problem contains errors that need correction.

**Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and clear, but the technical error in the analysis of the conventional estimator creates confusion. The authors should revise their explanation of the problem to clarify the distinction between the true risk and the finite-sample estimator.

**Originality:** The self-normalized estimator is a novel application in the context of counterfactual learning, building on prior work in importance sampling and control variates. The paper clearly differentiates itself from previous work (POEM, CRM) by addressing a previously overlooked issue.

**Significance:** The paper addresses a real problem in counterfactual learning with practical implications for real-world applications. The improvements are substantial (consistent across datasets) and approach the performance of full-information learning, which is significant for the field.

**Arguments for acceptance:**
- The self-normalized estimator is theoretically sound and addresses a real problem in counterfactual learning.
- The empirical results are strong, consistent, and show significant improvements over existing methods.
- The paper has clear practical relevance to important applications like recommendation systems and ad placement.
- The proposed Norm-POEM algorithm is well-motivated and implemented.

**Arguments against acceptance (minor):**
- The initial analysis of the problem contains technical errors that need correction.
- The paper's motivation for the problem is weakened by these errors.
- The authors should clarify their explanation of the estimator's properties.

**Recommendation:**
I recommend acceptance with minor revisions. The paper makes a valuable contribution to the field of counterfactual learning, and the empirical results are compelling. The technical error in the analysis should be corrected in the final version, which would strengthen the paper's theoretical foundation. The authors should revise their explanation of the problem to clarify the distinction between the true risk and the finite-sample estimator, and correct the misleading commentary around Equation (2) and Example 1.

The paper is well-suited for NIPS as it addresses an important practical problem with sound theoretical and empirical results. The proposed self-normalized estimator is a valuable addition to the counterfactual learning toolkit, and the Norm-POEM algorithm provides a clear practical implementation of this idea. With the minor revisions noted, this paper would make a strong contribution to the literature.
