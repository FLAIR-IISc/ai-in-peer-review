This paper addresses the problem of batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF), a crucial setting for improving interactive systems using historical data. The authors identify a significant and previously overlooked issue with the conventional unbiased counterfactual risk estimator, which forms the basis of the Counterfactual Risk Minimization (CRM) principle and algorithms like POEM [1]. They term this issue "Propensity Overfitting," where a model with high capacity can achieve an artificially low estimated risk not by fitting the true loss function, but by exploiting the structure of the importance weights. Specifically, for negative losses, the model learns to assign high probability to actions observed in the training data to maximize the weights, while for positive losses, it learns to predict unseen actions to drive the weights (and thus the estimated risk) to zero.

To remedy this, the paper proposes replacing the standard unbiased estimator with a self-normalized importance sampling estimator. This estimator, a ratio of the weighted loss sum to the weight sum, is shown to possess several desirable properties that the unbiased estimator lacks. It is equivariant to constant shifts in the loss function, and its estimates are naturally bounded within the range of the observed losses. While the estimator is biased in the finite sample regime, it is strongly consistent. Building on this improved estimator, the authors formulate a new learning algorithm, Norm-POEM, which integrates the self-normalized risk and a corresponding variance estimator into the CRM framework. The empirical evaluation on several multi-label classification tasks shows that Norm-POEM consistently and significantly outperforms the original POEM algorithm.

**Strengths:**

*   **Quality and Significance:** The primary contribution of this work is the identification and clear articulation of the propensity overfitting problem. This is a subtle but severe flaw in the standard approach to BLBF that can lead to catastrophic failures, as demonstrated in the paper's experiments. The diagnosis of this problem is a significant contribution in itself, providing valuable insight for any researcher working with importance sampling-based estimators in machine learning.
*   **Originality:** The proposed solution is both elegant and effective. While the self-normalized estimator is a known technique in the statistics literature for variance reduction, its application here as a direct remedy for propensity overfitting in a learning context is novel and well-motivated. The paper successfully connects the theoretical properties of the estimator (equivariance, boundedness) to the practical problem it aims to solve. This represents a meaningful and non-trivial extension of the work by Swaminathan & Joachims [1].
*   **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow. The authors use a simple and effective toy example (Example 1) to build strong intuition for why propensity overfitting occurs and how it differs from standard "loss overfitting" in supervised learning. The logical flow from problem identification to the proposed solution is clear and compelling.
*   **Empirical Results:** The experimental validation is thorough and convincing. The results in Table 1 and Figure 1 clearly demonstrate that Norm-POEM achieves substantially better generalization performance than POEM. More importantly, the diagnostic experiments in Table 2 directly confirm the paper's central hypothesis: Norm-POEM successfully controls for propensity overfitting (as seen by Ŝ(ĥ) being close to 1) and is robust to loss translations, whereas POEM is not. The finding that Norm-POEM is often computationally *faster* than POEM, despite a more complex objective, is a surprising and significant practical advantage that makes the proposed method highly attractive for real-world use.

**Weaknesses:**

*   The experiments are conducted using the standard "Supervised-to-Bandit" conversion protocol. While this is a necessary and well-established method for controlled evaluation in this domain (as it provides access to the ground-truth risk for evaluation), the paper's claims would be further strengthened by demonstrating the effectiveness of Norm-POEM on a real-world logged bandit dataset, where the logging policy is not known analytically and may have complex biases.
*   The paper notes that the variance of the self-normalized estimator is an approximation based on the delta method and that the overall optimization problem is non-convex. While the use of L-BFGS is a reasonable and standard choice, a brief discussion on the sensitivity of the algorithm to initialization or the potential for local minima would add to the completeness of the work. However, this is a minor point, as the strong empirical results suggest the method is practically robust.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a high-quality paper that makes a clear and important contribution to the field of counterfactual learning. It identifies a fundamental problem with the most common estimator used for learning from bandit feedback, proposes a theoretically-grounded and principled solution, and demonstrates its superiority through strong empirical results. The resulting algorithm, Norm-POEM, is not only more accurate but also more robust and computationally efficient than its predecessor. The work is well-presented, the claims are well-supported, and the results are likely to be of high interest to both researchers and practitioners working on improving online systems from log data. This paper is a clear advance on the state of the art and warrants further research. I strongly recommend acceptance.
