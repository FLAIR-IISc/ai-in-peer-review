Summary and relation to prior work
The paper addresses batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF) within the Counterfactual Risk Minimization (CRM) framework. Prior BLBF/CRM work (notably Swaminathan & Joachims, ICML 2015) has relied on the inverse propensity scoring (IPS) risk estimator (possibly with clipping), which is unbiased but high variance. The paper identifies a previously underappreciated failure mode—propensity overfitting—where ERM over complex hypothesis classes exploits variability in importance weights, leading to degenerate minimizers and pathological risk estimates (e.g., estimates outside the loss range and non-equivariance to loss translation).

To remedy this, the authors propose using a multiplicative control variate, yielding a self-normalized (weighted) importance sampling estimator for risk (SNIPS). SNIPS is bounded and equivariant, and while biased, it is strongly consistent. They integrate SNIPS into CRM, derive a sample variance estimator for the ratio estimator, and instantiate a learning algorithm, Norm-POEM, for structured prediction with exponential-family (CRF-like) policies trained via L-BFGS. Empirically, Norm-POEM consistently outperforms POEM on multi-label classification datasets under a supervised-to-bandit simulation protocol, is more robust to loss translation, avoids severe deviations in the sum of weights Ŝ(h), and often converges faster despite a heavier per-iteration cost.

The work is situated against BLBF, importance weighting and variance reduction (Rosenbaum & Rubin; Cortes et al.; Ionides), doubly robust methods (Dudík et al.), off-policy evaluation bounds (Thomas et al.), and weighted/self-normalized IS (Hesterberg; Kong; Rubinstein & Kroese). The paper’s main novelty is to diagnose propensity overfitting during learning and replace IPS with SNIPS within CRM, yielding practical and consistent improvements.

Quality
- Theory: The propensity overfitting phenomenon is convincingly illustrated via a constructive example and linked to non-equivariance and weight-sum deviations under IPS. The proposed switch to SNIPS is well motivated by control variates, and the consistency theorem follows from the SLLN for numerator and denominator. The variance estimator for the ratio estimator is a standard large-sample approximation. However, the paper does not provide a new finite-sample generalization bound for SNIPS within CRM analogous to the empirical Bernstein-style bound for IPS-CRM; the variance regularizer is justified asymptotically but not analyzed with finite-sample confidence. The effects of clipping M on bias for SNIPS are discussed pragmatically but not bounded. These omissions are limitations but are somewhat mitigated by the empirical evidence and by prior literature on ratio estimators.
- Empirics: The experimental design follows established BLBF practice and tests multiple datasets, analyzes loss translation, examines Ŝ(ĥ), shows learning curves, and studies the role of variance regularization. Norm-POEM consistently dominates POEM and is computationally competitive. That said, comparisons do not include doubly robust baselines (e.g., DR/DR with clipping or self-normalization), which would be natural and potentially strong alternatives. Experiments are conducted on simulated bandit feedback from supervised datasets; results on real logs would further strengthen claims. Reporting of variability (error bars) and sensitivity to M and λ could be expanded.
- Algorithmic details: The optimization is non-convex but the use of L-BFGS is reasonable and standard; convergence behavior is discussed qualitatively. The reliance on a tractable partition function is clear.

Clarity
The paper is clearly written, well organized, and easy to follow. The propensity overfitting example is particularly illuminating. The derivation of SNIPS and the variance estimator is concise and informative. Suggested minor improvements:
- More explicit discussion of how the original CRM generalization bound is adapted (or not) to the SNIPS objective and whether any finite-sample guarantees can be stated.
- Include runtime tables and standard deviations for performance metrics.
- Briefly discuss sensitivity to mis-specified propensities.

Originality
High. While SNIPS itself is classical, its explicit diagnosis and use to fix propensity overfitting within CRM for counterfactual learning is novel. The identification and formalization of “propensity overfitting” as distinct from loss overfitting is a useful conceptual contribution, and the Norm-POEM formulation is a meaningful extension of POEM.

Significance
High. BLBF is central to modern interactive systems. A robust, practical fix that improves both stability and performance over a widely used baseline is impactful. The technique is simple, broadly applicable, and likely to be adopted. The paper opens avenues for theory (finite-sample analysis for SNIPS-CRM) and for combining with DR estimators.

Pros
- Clear diagnosis of a real and important failure mode (propensity overfitting) with intuitive examples.
- Principled remedy via self-normalized IS; bounded, equivariant, and consistent estimator.
- Practical algorithm (Norm-POEM) with variance regularization and tractable optimization.
- Strong empirical improvements over POEM across datasets; robust to loss translation.
- Competitive runtime; likely to see broad use.

Cons
- No finite-sample generalization bound for the SNIPS-based CRM objective; reliance on asymptotic variance approximation.
- Missing comparisons to strong baselines such as doubly robust (DR) and clipped/normalized DR.
- Experiments are on supervised-to-bandit simulations only; no real-log evaluation.
- Limited analysis of sensitivity to propensity misspecification and support violations.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide a finite-sample analysis (even partial) for SNIPS-CRM, or at least concentration bounds for the ratio estimator in this setting.
- Add DR baselines (with and without self-normalization/clipping) and discuss trade-offs.
- Include experiments assessing robustness to noisy or biased propensities and partial support.
- Report error bars, sensitivity to M and λ, and wall-clock runtimes.
- Clarify how the variance regularizer interacts with normalization and clipping.

Recommendation
Accept. The paper identifies a critical issue in counterfactual learning, proposes a simple and principled fix grounded in established Monte Carlo methodology, and demonstrates consistent and meaningful empirical gains over a strong baseline. Despite some theoretical and experimental gaps (notably finite-sample guarantees and missing DR comparisons), the contribution is original, clear, and significant for the NeurIPS community.

Reviewer confidence: Medium-high (3.5/5).
