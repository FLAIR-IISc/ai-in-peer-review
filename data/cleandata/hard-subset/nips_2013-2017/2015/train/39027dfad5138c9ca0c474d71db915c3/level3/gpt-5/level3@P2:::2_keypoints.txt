- Summary: Replaces IPS with self-normalized IS (SNIS) to form Norm-POEM for CRM-based batch learning from logged bandit feedback, improving robustness and approaching CRF performance.
- Relation to prior work: Builds on causal propensity scoring, off-policy evaluation, and IS variance-reduction (multiplicative control variates, SNIS).

Strengths
- Problem relevance: Tackles robust counterfactual learning from logs, important for search, recommender, and advertising systems.
- Sound idea: SNIS to temper importance-weight fluctuations is well-motivated and aligns with IS practice.
- Empirical gains: Norm-POEM consistently outperforms POEM across multi-label datasets and is robust to loss translations.
- Practicality: Uses same hypothesis class as POEM, tractable optimization, reasonable runtime, and public code.

Weaknesses / requested revisions
- Eq. (2) notation: Conflates realized loss δ(x,y) with its expectation; needs clear distinction between stochastic loss, E[δ | x,y], and estimator target.
- Translation-invariance framing: Framing IPS’s finite-sample non-equivariance as an “anomaly” is misleading; it reflects variance rather than an expectation-level flaw.
- Example 1: Overstates IPS pathology; should be reworked to attribute effects to weight concentration and model capacity, not IPS error.
- Propensity zeros: Must acknowledge practical issues with zero/near-zero logged propensities and explain assumptions and handling (clipping, estimation error).
- Theory scope: Theorem 2 (SNIS consistency) is standard; paper should characterize regimes where SNIS strictly improves MSE over IPS and compare to clipped IPS and doubly robust estimators.
- Variance regularizer: Discuss reliability and finite-sample sensitivity of the approximate variance in Eq. (8) and to clipping M.

Quality
- Technical soundness: Estimator and algorithm are grounded in IS theory, but narrative around Eq. (2) and “anomaly” needs correction.
- Empirical support: Strong and consistent, but would benefit from broader baselines (e.g., DR variants) or justification for preferring SNIS over DR in structured outputs.

Clarity
- Generally well-written; requested clarifications include precise definitions of δ and E[δ|x,y], reframing “propensity overfitting,” tightening Example 1, and stating full-support conditions and practical propensity acquisition.

Originality
- Incremental: SNIS and multiplicative control variates are known; novelty is integrating SNIS into CRM and diagnosing “propensity overfitting” in this context.

Significance
- Practically important: Improves robustness, stability, and generalization for logged bandit learning with clear real-world utility.

Pros for acceptance
- Principled estimator addressing a central problem, strong empirical results, practical implications, and available code.

Cons for acceptance
- Overstated claims about IPS “anomalies,” limited theoretical MSE/regret comparisons among estimators, and narrower set of baselines.

Questions for authors
- Provide conditions (weight variance vs. loss variance) guaranteeing SNIS-CRM improves MSE or generalization over IPS-CRM.
- How sensitive is Norm-POEM to clipping threshold M and λ; guidance beyond validation?
- Was a doubly robust CRM variant compared; if not, feasibility and expected performance?
- How are propensities logged in synthetic setup and how would misspecified/estimated propensities be handled in practice?

Minor comments
- Clarify notation around Eq. (2), recast translation discussion as finite-sample variance issue, rework Example 1, and discuss SNIS bias vs unbiased IPS and why bias is acceptable in CRM.

Overall recommendation
- Accept conditional on revising exposition around Eq. (2), translation discussion, and Example 1; methodological contribution and empirical gains justify acceptance.
