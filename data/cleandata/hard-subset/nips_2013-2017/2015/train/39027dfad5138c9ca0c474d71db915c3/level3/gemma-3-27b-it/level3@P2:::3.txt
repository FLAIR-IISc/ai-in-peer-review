This paper identifies a significant problem – propensity overfitting – in the application of counterfactual risk minimization (CRM) for batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF), a setting increasingly relevant for training online systems from historical data. The authors demonstrate that the commonly used unbiased risk estimator suffers from this issue and propose a self-normalized estimator as a solution, grounded in control variate techniques. This leads to a new learning algorithm, Norm-POEM, and empirical results on multi-label classification problems demonstrate consistent performance gains over the standard approach.

The work builds logically upon prior research in BLBF, particularly [1], and correctly positions itself within the broader context of causal inference, domain adaptation, and off-policy evaluation. The introduction effectively motivates the problem and highlights the limitations of existing approaches. The paper is exceptionally well-written and clearly explains both the theoretical challenges and the proposed solutions. The mathematical derivations are concise and easy to follow, and the connection to existing techniques like importance sampling is clearly articulated. The authors also diligently acknowledge and discuss related work, including relevant literature on variance reduction techniques and propensity score matching.

**Strengths:**

* **Strong Theoretical Motivation:** The identification and formalization of propensity overfitting is a key contribution. The analysis convincingly demonstrates why the standard estimator can fail and provides a principled justification for the proposed self-normalized alternative.
* **Clear and Accessible Presentation:** The paper is remarkably well-written. Complex concepts are explained clearly and concisely, making the work accessible to a broad audience within the NIPS community.
* **Solid Empirical Validation:** The experiments on multiple datasets consistently show that Norm-POEM outperforms POEM, lending credence to the theoretical claims. The setup, mirroring the "Supervised 7→ Bandit" methodology from previous work, allows for a fair comparison.
* **Practical Algorithm:** Norm-POEM is presented as a concrete learning algorithm with clear implementation details, making it readily usable by other researchers.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Scope of Experiments:** While the experiments are well-executed, they primarily replicate the setting of prior work [1] with multi-label classification datasets. The authors acknowledge this but further exploration, including experiments on more diverse datasets and particularly real-world applications, would significantly strengthen the paper’s impact.
* **Insufficient Analysis of Training Dynamics:**  The observation that Norm-POEM converges faster than POEM is intriguing but lacks a deeper analysis. Understanding *why* this is the case – for example, examining the behavior of the optimization landscape or the gradients – could provide valuable insights.  More detailed reporting of the training process, beyond runtime, would be beneficial.
* **Variance of Results:** The paper would benefit from reporting the variance of results across multiple runs (beyond the average performance reported in Table 1). This would provide a clearer picture of the robustness of the performance gains.
* **Significance is Moderately Convincing:** While the problem addressed is relevant, the magnitude of the performance gains achieved isn't overwhelmingly substantial. It remains unclear how these gains would translate to tangible improvements in real-world systems where the complexities of data collection and implementation may outweigh the benefits.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper addresses a theoretically important problem in a rapidly growing area of research (BLBF).
* The proposed solution is well-motivated, technically sound, and empirically validated.
* The writing is excellent and the presentation is clear.
* The code is available, promoting reproducibility.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The experimental evaluation is somewhat limited in scope.
* The significance of the results, while positive, isn't definitively established.
* The analysis of the training process could be more in-depth.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of batch learning from logged bandit feedback. The identification and mitigation of propensity overfitting is a significant advance, and the self-normalized estimator provides a principled solution. While the experimental evaluation could be broadened and the analysis of training dynamics deepened, the paper’s strengths outweigh its weaknesses. I believe this work is of sufficient quality to be accepted to NIPS, with a recommendation to the authors to consider expanding the experimental evaluation in future work.

**Recommendation:** Accept.
