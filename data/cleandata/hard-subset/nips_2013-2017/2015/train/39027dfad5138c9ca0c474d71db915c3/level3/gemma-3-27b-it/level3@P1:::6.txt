This paper addresses a critical challenge in learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF): the propensity to overfit when using standard counterfactual risk estimators. The authors convincingly demonstrate that conventional estimators, while unbiased, can exhibit severe instability and lead to poor generalization, particularly with complex hypothesis spaces. They attribute this to a novel form of overfitting, termed "Propensity Overfitting," where the estimator exploits the historical policy's sampling probabilities rather than focusing on true risk minimization. To address this, they propose a self-normalized estimator, motivated by control variate techniques, and demonstrate its theoretical properties – specifically, consistency and improved variance control.  Empirical evaluation on multi-label classification problems consistently shows that their new learning algorithm, Norm-POEM, outperforms existing methods.

The work builds directly on the established CRM framework [1] for BLBF, positioning itself within a growing body of research on off-policy evaluation and causal inference [2, 3, 4]. It clearly articulates its connection to existing work on importance sampling [5, 6, 7, 8, 9, 10, 11, 12], highlighting how the proposed solution addresses limitations of prior approaches, particularly regarding variance control and susceptibility to exploitation of the historical policy. The identification of Propensity Overfitting as a distinct failure mode is a significant contribution, going beyond simply observing high variance in the estimator. The authors effectively contrast this with traditional Loss Overfitting in supervised learning, providing a nuanced understanding of the challenges in BLBF.

The strengths of this paper are considerable.  The technical analysis is sound, providing a theoretical justification for the proposed estimator and rigorously establishing its desirable properties. The authors demonstrate a strong understanding of the theoretical underpinnings of importance sampling and counterfactual estimation. The empirical results are compelling, consistently showing performance gains across multiple datasets.  The detailed experimental setup, replicating the methodology of prior work [1], strengthens the validity of the conclusions. Importantly, the authors not only present performance improvements but also provide diagnostic analysis (Table 2) that confirms the reduction in Propensity Overfitting with their proposed method. The code availability is also a strong positive.

However, there are a few areas where the paper could be improved. While the authors acknowledge the computational cost of the self-normalized estimator, a more detailed discussion of its practical implications would be beneficial.  The use of L-BFGS, while justified, could be a barrier to adoption for large-scale problems. Exploring alternative optimization methods or approximation techniques might be valuable. The connection to work on doubly-robust estimators [6] could be more explicitly drawn, discussing how the self-normalized estimator relates to and potentially improves upon these approaches. Finally, while the empirical results are strong, further analysis of the sensitivity of the method to the choice of hyperparameters (M and λ) would increase confidence in its robustness. 

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** The identification and mitigation of Propensity Overfitting represents a significant advancement in the field of BLBF.
*   **Technical Soundness:** The theoretical analysis is rigorous and the proposed estimator is well-motivated.
*   **Empirical Validation:**  Consistent performance gains across multiple datasets demonstrate the effectiveness of the approach.
*   **Significance:** The problem addressed is highly relevant to real-world applications of online learning and the proposed solution has the potential to improve the performance of many deployed systems.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Potential computational cost might limit scalability to very large datasets.
*   The paper focuses primarily on multi-label classification. Exploring the performance on other problem domains would strengthen the generalizability of the findings.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of learning from logged bandit feedback. The authors clearly identify a critical issue, propose an effective solution, and provide compelling theoretical and empirical evidence to support their claims. Despite some minor areas for improvement, the paper's strengths significantly outweigh its weaknesses. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the points outlined above.
