Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper addresses counterfactual learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF), building on the Counterfactual Risk Minimization (CRM) framework (Swaminathan & Joachims, ICML 2015). The authors argue that the conventional inverse propensity scoring (IPS) estimator used in POEM suffers from “propensity overfitting,” and propose replacing it with a self-normalized importance sampling estimator. Instantiating CRM with this estimator yields Norm-POEM, a structured prediction method akin to CRFs trained with bandit feedback. The approach connects to long-standing work on importance sampling, control variates, and self-normalized estimators, and is empirically evaluated on multi-label datasets, where Norm-POEM consistently outperforms POEM and approaches full-information CRF performance.

Main comments and technical correctness:
- Misleading commentary around Eq. (2): The text around (2) conflates properties of the target expectation with finite-sample properties of the unnormalized IS estimator. The IPS estimator in (2) is an unbiased Monte Carlo estimator of the expected risk, but its finite-sample behavior is governed by the realized sum of importance weights. Interpreting (2) as if the sample estimate must inherit expectation-level invariances (e.g., exact translation equivariance) is incorrect. Finite-sample IS is not invariant to additive translations of the loss unless the weights are normalized.

- “Anomaly” claim: The paper’s claim that (2) exhibits an “anomaly” (non-invariance to translation) is technically inaccurate as stated. Non-equivariance to translation is a well-known and expected property of unnormalized IS estimators; it is not an anomaly relative to the expectation, but a consequence of not normalizing the weights. This should be reframed to avoid suggesting a conceptual flaw in IPS beyond its known finite-sample limitations.

- Example 1: The example’s reasoning implicitly relies on the incorrect presumption above. As such, parts of the argument do not hold as evidence of a conceptual anomaly. The example can be reframed to illustrate the genuine issue—finite-sample instability driven by the variability and sum of weights—without invoking incorrect expectation-level invariance.

- Sensitivity to π: The estimator in (2) is acutely sensitive to the denominator π = h0(y|x). In practice π may be extremely small, and can be zero if an action was never supported by the logging policy, leading to infinite or undefined importance weights. While the paper assumes full support (as it must), it should discuss practical safeguards: exploration in logging, weight clipping, floors on π, or model constraints to avoid weight explosion.

- Preference for the modified estimator (7): The self-normalized estimator divides the weighted loss by the sum of weights, effectively introducing a “double denominator” that softens the impact of small π by renormalizing their aggregate effect. This delivers boundedness, translation equivariance, and reduced variance when weight fluctuations dominate—properties that directly counter propensity overfitting. These are standard, well-understood advantages of self-normalized IS and are appropriate here.

- Requested revision if accepted: Please revise the commentary around (2) to accurately reflect finite-sample properties of unnormalized IS, remove the “anomaly” language, and adjust Example 1 so that it no longer depends on an incorrect expectation interpretation. Also clarify the assumptions and practical handling of zero or near-zero propensities.

Evaluation:
- Quality: Strong empirical results and a principled estimator choice. The variance estimate and CRM integration are reasonable. However, theoretical commentary around (2) and Example 1 should be corrected. A brief discussion of bias–variance trade-offs and conditions under which SN-IPS dominates IPS (and the role of clipping M) would strengthen the paper.

- Clarity: Generally well-organized and readable, with helpful connections to control variates and CRM. Clarity would substantially improve with the revisions above. Explicitly state the positivity assumption, how π=0 cases are precluded, and how clipping interacts with self-normalization.

- Originality: The self-normalized estimator is classic, but its integration into CRM for structured output learning with a tailored variance proxy and a practical optimizer is a useful and novel synthesis. The “propensity overfitting” diagnosis, when framed correctly as a finite-sample IS issue, is a clear and instructive lens.

- Significance: Counterfactual learning from logs is important in practice. The empirical gains are consistent and meaningful; Norm-POEM outperforms POEM across datasets and approaches full-information CRF performance using only bandit feedback. This will likely influence practitioners’ estimator choices in BLBF.

Pros:
- Clear, principled estimator swap that addresses real finite-sample issues.
- Strong, consistent empirical improvements; robust to loss translation.
- Practical training recipe with competitive runtime; CRM regularization still helps.
- Good positioning within prior work on IS, control variates, and CRM.

Cons:
- Mischaracterization of IPS finite-sample properties and “anomaly” language.
- Example 1 relies on that mischaracterization; needs revision.
- Limited discussion of positivity and handling of π≈0 in practice.
- Theoretical guidance on clipping M and bias of SN-IPS could be expanded.

Recommendation:
Accept, conditional on revisions. The contribution is convincing and practically valuable. Please:
- Correct the discussion around Eq. (2), remove “anomaly” framing, and revise Example 1.
- Clarify assumptions and safeguards for π, including zero/near-zero propensities.
- Briefly discuss the bias–variance trade-off of SN-IPS and the role of clipping M.

Additional suggestions:
- Include an ablation on clipping thresholds and their effect on POEM vs. Norm-POEM.
- Consider a small comparison to a doubly robust baseline, or at least discuss the complementary nature of SN-IPS and DR estimators.
