

# Review for "The Self-Normalized Estimator for Counterfactual Learning"

This paper identifies a critical flaw in the conventional counterfactual risk estimator used in batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF), a problem previously overlooked in the literature. The authors demonstrate that the standard unbiased estimator suffers from "propensity overfitting" when used in empirical risk minimization (ERM) for complex hypothesis spaces. They propose replacing this estimator with a self-normalized version that avoids this problem, leading to a new learning algorithm called Norm-POEM. This work builds upon Swaminathan and Joachims' CRM principle [1] for BLBF, which was presented at ICML 2013, and makes a significant theoretical contribution by identifying a previously unrecognized issue with the conventional estimator.

**Quality (6/10):** The paper is technically sound with well-supported theoretical analysis. The authors rigorously prove that the self-normalized estimator avoids propensity overfitting, is equivariant under loss translation, and remains bounded within the loss range. The experimental results consistently demonstrate Norm-POEM's superiority over the conventional approach (POEM) across multiple datasets. However, the paper would benefit from greater care in notation (e.g., line 170-171 should use h(y|x)/h₀(y|x) instead of h(y|x)h₀(y|x)), and the use of "argmax" on line 276 should be corrected to "argmin" since the goal is risk minimization. The paper also would be strengthened by modeling delta(x,y) as a random variable with expectation (rather than a deterministic function) to better reflect real-world scenarios with label noise.

**Clarity (6/10):** The paper is generally well-written and clearly organized, though some notational inconsistencies (line 319 should use hats instead of bars) and ambiguous statements about the loss function (e.g., "delta > 0" in Table 1) require clarification. The explanation of propensity overfitting is particularly clear and well-motivated through concrete examples. The empirical evaluation is thorough and well-structured, making the results accessible and convincing. The connection to control variates from importance sampling literature is insightful and well-explained.

**Originality (8/10):** This is a strong contribution with high originality. The identification of "propensity overfitting" as a distinct problem from conventional loss overfitting is novel and insightful. The use of multiplicative control variates to derive the self-normalized estimator is a clever application of established techniques to a new problem. The paper clearly differentiates itself from prior work, particularly Swaminathan & Joachims [1], by addressing a previously overlooked issue in the CRM framework. The approach is a significant improvement over existing methods rather than merely a minor modification.

**Significance (8/10):** The problem addressed is highly significant for practical applications of BLBF in real-world systems (e.g., ad placement, recommendation systems). The paper demonstrates that the proposed method consistently outperforms the conventional approach across multiple datasets, which is a substantial practical contribution. The theoretical insights about propensity overfitting deepen our understanding of counterfactual learning and have implications for other areas of causal inference. The Norm-POEM algorithm provides a practical solution that can be readily applied to improve existing BLBF systems, making this work immediately relevant to practitioners.

**Strengths:** The paper makes a clear, important contribution by identifying and solving a previously overlooked problem in BLBF. The theoretical analysis is rigorous and convincing, and the empirical results consistently demonstrate the superiority of the proposed method. The connection to control variates in importance sampling is insightful, and the paper provides a principled solution rather than a heuristic fix. The experimental evaluation is comprehensive and well-designed, using the standard Supervised 7→Bandit methodology to ensure fair comparison.

**Weaknesses:** The paper would benefit from greater care with notation (as noted above) and a clearer explanation of the loss function setup (e.g., why delta is reported as positive in Table 1 when the loss function is defined as negative). While the paper mentions that the self-normalized estimator "is not unbiased," it doesn't fully address when this trade-off is acceptable or how the bias compares to the variance reduction achieved. The authors could also more clearly explain why the self-normalized estimator avoids propensity overfitting beyond stating it's "equivariant."

**Arguments for acceptance:** The paper identifies a critical, previously overlooked problem in BLBF with significant practical implications. The solution is theoretically sound, well-motivated, and empirically validated. The work builds upon and improves the CRM framework in a meaningful way, making it highly relevant to the NIPS community.

**Arguments against acceptance:** The paper contains some notational inconsistencies that need correction, and the loss function setup could be clarified. However, these are minor issues that do not detract from the core contribution.

This is an important follow-up to Swaminathan & Joachims' CRM work [1], with insightful theoretical analysis of propensity overfitting, effective use of multiplicative control variates, and clear empirical improvement over the state-of-the-art. The paper makes a significant contribution to the field of counterfactual learning and has clear potential for practical impact in real-world applications of BLBF. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the notational issues.
