## Review of "The Self-Normalized Estimator for Counterfactual Learning"

This paper addresses a critical issue in batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF): propensity overfitting. The authors demonstrate that the commonly used unbiased counterfactual risk estimator can lead to severely biased risk estimates, particularly with complex hypothesis spaces, due to exploiting the importance weights. They propose a self-normalized estimator as a solution, theoretically proving its consistency and variance control, and empirically validating its superior performance on multi-label classification tasks.

The work builds upon the established CRM framework [1] and connects to broader areas like causal inference, domain adaptation, and off-policy evaluation [2, 4, 7, 8]. The identification of propensity overfitting as a distinct failure mode is a novel contribution, going beyond standard concerns about variance in importance sampling. The proposed self-normalized estimator, drawing from financial applications and reinforcement learning [12, 17, 18], offers a principled way to mitigate this issue. The Norm-POEM algorithm, built upon this estimator, provides a concrete implementation and demonstrates practical improvements over existing methods like POEM [1]. 

The strengths of the paper lie in its clear problem definition, rigorous theoretical analysis, and convincing empirical results. The authors meticulously demonstrate the limitations of the conventional estimator and showcase the benefits of their proposed solution across multiple datasets. The inclusion of ablation studies (Table 3) and analysis of the Ŝ(ĥ) values (Table 2) strengthens the claims and provides valuable insights. The paper is well-written and logically structured, making it easy to follow the technical details.

However, a lingering concern is the practical significance of the performance gains. While consistently outperforming POEM, the absolute improvements reported in Table 1 appear modest in some cases. Further investigation into the scenarios where Norm-POEM provides the most substantial benefits would be valuable. Additionally, the computational complexity analysis, while noting faster convergence, could benefit from a more detailed comparison of per-iteration costs. 

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel identification of propensity overfitting.
*   Strong theoretical guarantees for the self-normalized estimator.
*   Consistent empirical improvements over existing methods.
*   Clear and well-written presentation.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Modest absolute performance gains in some experiments.
*   Computational complexity analysis could be more detailed.

**Overall:**

Despite these minor concerns, this is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of learning from logged bandit feedback. The identified problem of propensity overfitting is significant, and the proposed solution is both theoretically sound and empirically effective. I recommend acceptance, though with the caveat that further investigation into the practical impact and computational aspects would be beneficial.
