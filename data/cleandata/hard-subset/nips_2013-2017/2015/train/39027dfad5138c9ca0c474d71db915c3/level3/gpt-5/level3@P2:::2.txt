Summary and relation to prior work
The paper addresses batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF) within the Counterfactual Risk Minimization (CRM) framework. It argues that the commonly used inverse propensity scoring (IPS) risk estimator employed in POEM (Swaminathan & Joachims, ICML’15) is prone to what the authors call propensity overfitting and proposes replacing it with a self-normalized importance sampling (SNIS) estimator. This leads to a new algorithm, Norm-POEM, for structured output prediction with exponential-family models. The work is related to causal inference via propensity scores, off-policy evaluation in RL, and variance-reduction in importance sampling, in particular multiplicative control variates and SNIS (Kong’92; Owen’13; Hesterberg’95). Empirically, Norm-POEM improves over POEM across several multi-label datasets, approaching the performance of a full-information CRF baseline.

Strengths
- Clear and relevant problem: robust counterfactual learning from logs is central to search, recommender, and advertising systems, squarely within NIPS scope.
- Sound idea: employing SNIS to temper weight fluctuations is well-motivated and aligns with established IS practice when weight variance dominates.
- Empirical gains: consistently better generalization than POEM on multiple datasets; robustness to loss translations; reasonable runtime and convergence characteristics.
- Practicality: same hypothesis class as POEM, tractable optimization, public code.

Weaknesses and requested revisions
- Misleading commentary around Eq. (2): The notation and interpretation of expectations conflates realized losses δ(x, y) and their expectation; please clearly distinguish the stochastic loss, its conditional expectation E[δ | x, y], and what the estimator targets. As written, some statements about what (2) “estimates” on finite samples are confusing.
- “Anomaly” claim re translation-invariance: The paper claims that the IPS estimator in (2) has an anomaly because it is not equivariant under additive translations of δ on finite samples. In expectation, IPS is translation equivariant; the lack of finite-sample equivariance reflects variance, not a conceptual anomaly. Please revise the framing to avoid implying a flaw in unbiasedness or expectation-level invariance.
- Example 1: This example rests on the above framing and overstates the issue as a pathology of IPS rather than a manifestation of high-variance, weight-driven ERM behavior. As written, it does not convincingly isolate a mistake in IPS; rather, it highlights known issues of weight concentration and model capacity. Please rework the example and its conclusions to reflect this, or provide a corrected argument that does not hinge on the misunderstanding in (2).
- Propensity and zero denominators: While the theory assumes full support, the discussion should acknowledge that in practice misspecified or deterministic logging can induce zero or near-zero propensities, leading to instability for (2). Clarify the assumptions under which π > 0 for logged actions and how you handle real-world violations (estimation error, clipping, or logging bugs).
- Theory scope: The consistency of SNIS (Theorem 2) is standard. It would strengthen the paper to characterize when SNIS has strictly lower MSE than IPS for CRM objectives (e.g., regimes of weight variance vs. reward variance), and to discuss the bias-variance tradeoff relative to clipped IPS and doubly robust estimators.
- Variance term: The approximate variance in Eq. (8) is reasonable, but its use as a CRM regularizer deserves a brief discussion of reliability for finite samples and sensitivity to clipping M.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The proposed estimator and algorithm are grounded in established IS theory. The CRM instantiation with SNIS is reasonable. However, the narrative around Eq. (2) and the translation “anomaly” needs correction to avoid suggesting an error where there is a variance issue. The empirical evaluation is thorough within the chosen setting and supports the claims.
- Empirical support: Strong, with consistent wins for Norm-POEM. It would be helpful to include additional baselines (e.g., DR-POEM-like variants) or at least a brief discussion of why SNIS is preferable to DR in your structured-output setup.

Clarity
- Generally well-written and organized. Requested clarifications:
  - Precisely define δ, E[δ | x, y], and which is used in optimization and analysis.
  - Reframe “propensity overfitting” as a weight-variance/capacity-induced phenomenon, not a flaw in IPS per se.
  - Tighten Example 1 and the discussion of translation effects.
  - State the conditions for full support and how propensities are obtained in practice.

Originality
- The SNIS estimator and multiplicative control variates are known; the novelty lies in diagnosing “propensity overfitting” within CRM and integrating SNIS into a structured-output learning algorithm with a CRM-style variance penalty. This is an incremental but useful contribution with clear practical impact.

Significance
- Important for practitioners using logged bandit data: the method improves robustness, training stability, and generalization. The consistent empirical gains and approach to CRF performance suggest real-world utility. The conceptual contribution will likely influence how counterfactual objectives are regularized.

Pros for acceptance
- Addresses a central problem in counterfactual learning with a principled, well-motivated estimator.
- Strong and consistent empirical results across datasets.
- Clear practical implications and available code.
- Integrates established IS variance-reduction into CRM in a way that demonstrably matters.

Cons for acceptance
- Overstated claims about “anomalies” and a problematic example; portions of the exposition need correction to avoid technical misunderstandings.
- Limited theoretical comparison of MSE/regret between IPS, clipped IPS, SNIS, and DR within CRM.
- Baselines could be broader.

Questions for the authors
- Can you provide conditions (in terms of weight variance and loss variance) under which SNIS-CRM is guaranteed to improve MSE or generalization bounds relative to IPS-CRM?
- How sensitive is Norm-POEM to the clipping threshold M and λ? Any guidance beyond validation?
- Did you compare against a doubly robust CRM variant? If not, can you comment on feasibility and expected performance in your setting?
- How are propensities logged in your synthetic setup, and how would you handle misspecified or estimated propensities in real systems?

Minor comments and edits
- Clarify notation and expectations around Eq. (2).
- Recast the translation discussion as a finite-sample variance issue; avoid calling it an “anomaly.”
- Rework Example 1 accordingly.
- Briefly discuss the bias of SNIS vs. unbiased IPS and why the bias is acceptable in CRM.

Overall recommendation
Accept, conditional on revising the exposition around Eq. (2), the translation discussion, and Example 1. The methodological contribution is principled, the empirical gains are convincing, and the paper is relevant and impactful for counterfactual learning from logs.
