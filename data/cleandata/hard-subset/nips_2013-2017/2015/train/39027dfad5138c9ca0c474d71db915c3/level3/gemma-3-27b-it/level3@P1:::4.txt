The paper identifies a critical issue – propensity overfitting – in counterfactual risk estimation, a cornerstone of batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF). It argues that the commonly used unbiased estimator suffers from this problem when applied to complex hypothesis spaces, leading to poor generalization. The authors propose a self-normalized estimator as a remedy, demonstrating its theoretical advantages and empirical effectiveness through a new algorithm, Normalized Policy Optimizer for Exponential Models (Norm-POEM), applied to multi-label classification tasks. This work builds directly upon prior research in BLBF, particularly [1], and relates to broader areas like causal inference, domain adaptation, and off-policy evaluation.

The core strength of this paper lies in its insightful analysis of the propensity overfitting problem. The authors convincingly demonstrate, through a compelling example and subsequent experimental validation, that the conventional estimator can indeed lead to severely biased risk estimates and consequently, poor performance. The introduction of the self-normalized estimator is a clever solution, leveraging multiplicative control variates to mitigate this overfitting. This is not merely a theoretical fix; the empirical results consistently show Norm-POEM outperforming POEM [1], highlighting the practical significance of the proposed approach. The connection to existing work in importance sampling and the justification for using the self-normalized estimator in this specific context are well-articulated. The paper is particularly commendable for its thorough experimental evaluation, closely following the supervised-to-bandit methodology established in previous work, allowing for a clear comparison with existing methods.

However, a few points could be clarified to enhance the paper's quality. First, the treatment of the loss δ(x, y) could be strengthened. While the paper acknowledges it as potentially noisy, explicitly modeling δ as a *random variable* with an expectation over the label noise would provide a more rigorous foundation for the risk formulation. This would clarify the expected value calculations and provide a more complete theoretical picture.  Specifically, the true risk should be expressed as E<sub>x,y</sub>[δ(x, y)], emphasizing the expectation over the stochastic loss.

Furthermore, certain details within the experimental setup require clarification. The meaning of "delta > 0" in Table 1 should be explicitly defined.  It’s also important to clarify whether Norm-POEM, like CRM, rescales δ to the range [-1, 0]. This is crucial for understanding the assumptions underlying the theoretical guarantees and the interpretation of the experimental results. 

There are a few minor technical corrections needed. On line 170-171, the ratio should be h(y|x) / h<sub>0</sub>(y|x), aligning with standard importance sampling notation.  The authors should also acknowledge that this nonlinearity was previously noted in [1]. Line 276 contains a typo; it should state `argmin` instead of `argmax` as the objective is to *minimize* the risk. Finally, on line 319, the notation should be consistent with earlier use of hats (^) instead of bars (|) for empirical estimates.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** The identification and analysis of propensity overfitting is a novel contribution to the field of BLBF.
*   **Significance:** Addressing this issue significantly improves the performance and robustness of learning algorithms in a crucial setting (learning from logged data).
*   **Soundness:** The theoretical justification for the self-normalized estimator and the empirical validation are convincing.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly presents the problem, proposed solution, and experimental results.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Minor technical clarifications:** The points regarding the loss function, experimental details, and notation require clarification.
*   **Potential for more in-depth theoretical analysis:** While the paper provides a solid theoretical foundation, exploring the conditions under which the self-normalized estimator guarantees improved performance could be beneficial.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper presenting an important contribution to the field of batch learning from logged bandit feedback. The identification of propensity overfitting is insightful, and the proposed self-normalized estimator provides an effective solution.  While a few clarifications and minor corrections are needed, the overall quality of the work is high. The paper offers a significant improvement over existing methods and has the potential to influence future research in this area.
