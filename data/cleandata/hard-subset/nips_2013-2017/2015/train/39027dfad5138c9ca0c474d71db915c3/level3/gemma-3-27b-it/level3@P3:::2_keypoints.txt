- Paper addresses propensity overfitting in batch learning from logged bandit feedback and identifies a fundamental flaw in the conventional counterfactual risk estimator (Eq. 2).  
- Authors argue the loss in Eq. 2 is misinterpreted (not the expected quantity) and its claimed invariance properties are incorrect.  
- They propose a self-normalized estimator (Eq. 7) and derive the Norm-POEM algorithm to mitigate propensity overfitting.  
- Theoretical results (Theorem 2) show improved properties and consistency for the proposed estimator.  
- Empirical results: Norm-POEM consistently outperforms POEM on multi-label classification and approaches full-information CRF performance.  
- Ablation study supports the importance of variance regularization for the method’s success.  
- Paper builds on POEM and connects to causal inference, domain adaptation, and off-policy evaluation; motivation and RL connections are well explained.  
- Presentation is generally clear and math/derivations are well explained; code is provided for reproducibility.  
- Weakness: Initial commentary around Eq. 2 is vague and confusing; the claim “loss in (2) is not the expected quantity” needs a sharper explanation.  
- Weakness: Example 1 depends on an incorrect premise about Eq. 2, undermining its illustrative power.  
- Weakness: Discussion of sensitivity to π (denominator) lacks concrete strategies for handling zero or unseen-probability cases.  
- Weakness: Paper claims Norm-POEM is faster but lacks detailed computational-complexity analysis, especially cost of variance estimate (Eq. 8).  
- Arguments for acceptance: identifies a fundamental problem, offers a theoretically sound and empirically effective solution, and is well-written with reproducible code.  
- Arguments against acceptance: unclear initial presentation and reliance on a flawed example reduce persuasive strength.  
- Overall assessment: Convincing and valuable contribution; recommend acceptance conditional on clearer explanation of Eq. 2’s non-equivariance, explicit link to overfitting, acknowledgement/fix for Example 1, and more detail on computational cost.
