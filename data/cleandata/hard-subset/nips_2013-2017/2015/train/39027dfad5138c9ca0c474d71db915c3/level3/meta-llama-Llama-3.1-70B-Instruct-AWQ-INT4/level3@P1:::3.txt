This paper addresses the problem of batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF), which is an instance of causal inference. The authors identify a severe issue with the conventional counterfactual risk estimator used in prior works on BLBF, known as propensity overfitting. This problem occurs when the capacity of the hypothesis space allows overfitting of the propensity weights, leading to severely biased risk estimates.

The authors propose a solution to this problem by introducing a multiplicative control variate, which yields a self-normalized risk estimator. This estimator is shown to be more accurate than the conventional unbiased estimator, especially when fluctuations in the sampling weights dominate the fluctuations in the loss function. The self-normalized estimator is also equivariant, meaning that it is invariant to translations of the loss function.

The authors derive a new learning algorithm, called Norm-POEM, which uses the self-normalized risk estimator and the Counterfactual Risk Minimization (CRM) principle. Norm-POEM is shown to outperform the conventional POEM algorithm in several multi-label classification problems.

The paper is well-written and clearly presents the problem, the proposed solution, and the experimental results. The authors provide a thorough analysis of the self-normalized estimator and its properties, which is a significant contribution to the field.

However, the reviewer expected more experiments and a deeper analysis of the training-objective optimization. The paper could benefit from additional experiments on real-world applications and a more extensive analysis of the performance of Norm-POEM in different scenarios.

The significance of the paper is uncertain, as it is unclear how the experimental gains translate to real-world applications. The reviewer requests variance reporting for repeated runs and more extensive experiments, especially on additional and real-world applications beyond those in [1].

In terms of related work, the paper is a follow-up to [1], and the main novelty is the self-normalizing risk estimator, which improves empirical performance. The authors provide a thorough review of the relevant literature and clearly explain how their work differs from previous contributions.

Overall, the paper is a high-quality contribution to the field of BLBF, and the proposed solution has the potential to improve the performance of BLBF algorithms in real-world applications. However, further experiments and analysis are needed to fully understand the significance and impact of the proposed solution.
