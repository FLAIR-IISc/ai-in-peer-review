This paper addresses the problem of learning from logged bandit feedback, a crucial setting for improving interactive systems like search engines and ad platforms. The standard approach, Counterfactual Risk Minimization (CRM), uses an importance-weighted estimator to evaluate new policies from historical data. The main contribution of this work is the identification of a critical failure mode of this standard estimator, which the authors term "Propensity Overfitting". This occurs when a sufficiently complex hypothesis exploits the importance weights `h(y|x)/p` to achieve an artificially low (or even unbounded) empirical risk, a phenomenon distinct from standard overfitting of the loss function.

To solve this problem, the authors propose replacing the standard unbiased estimator with a self-normalized one. This is a ratio estimator where the importance-weighted sum of losses is normalized by the sum of the importance weights. This idea is motivated by the use of multiplicative control variates in Monte Carlo methods. The paper shows that this self-normalized estimator is consistent, equivariant to shifts in the loss function, and naturally bounds the estimated risk within the range of observed losses, thereby directly mitigating the propensity overfitting issue. The authors instantiate this approach in a new algorithm, Norm-POEM, and demonstrate through experiments on multi-label classification tasks that it consistently and significantly outperforms its predecessor based on the standard estimator.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Clear Identification of a Significant Problem:** The paper's primary strength is its clear articulation and diagnosis of the "propensity overfitting" problem. This is a subtle but severe issue that can lead to catastrophic failures in counterfactual learning. The illustrative example in Section 4 is simple and highly effective at demonstrating how a learner can achieve an arbitrarily low risk estimate by manipulating propensity scores. This contribution is valuable to both researchers and practitioners in the field, as it highlights a critical pitfall of a widely used technique.

2.  **Principled and Effective Solution:** The proposed solution—using a self-normalized estimator—is elegant, well-motivated, and directly addresses the identified problem. By framing it as an application of control variates, the authors connect the solution to established statistical techniques. The theoretical properties of the estimator (consistency, equivariance, bounded range) are shown to resolve the anomalies of the standard estimator.

3.  **Strong and Convincing Empirical Evaluation:** The experiments are well-designed and provide strong support for the paper's claims. The authors not only show that Norm-POEM achieves better generalization performance than the standard POEM (Table 1, Figure 1) but also include diagnostic experiments that confirm the underlying mechanism. Table 2 compellingly demonstrates that Norm-POEM corrects the bias in the sum of propensity weights (`Ŝ(h)`) and is robust to loss translations, while the standard approach is not. This level of empirical rigor, which verifies *why* the method works, is commendable.

4.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow. The logical flow from problem identification to solution and empirical validation is clear and persuasive.

**Weaknesses and Questions for the Authors:**

1.  **Interaction with the CRM Bound:** The CRM principle and its associated generalization bound (Theorem 1) are derived for the unbiased importance sampling estimator. The proposed self-normalized estimator is, as the authors note, biased for finite samples. The paper substitutes this biased estimator into the CRM objective `argmin(R̂ + λ * sqrt(Var))` without a formal justification of how this substitution affects the theoretical underpinnings of the learning principle. While the approach is intuitively appealing and works well empirically, the paper would be stronger with a discussion on the theoretical implications of using a biased estimator within a framework originally justified for an unbiased one. Does the original bound still provide a meaningful guideline?

2.  **Nature of the Estimator's Bias:** While the paper proves consistency, a deeper discussion on the finite-sample bias would be beneficial. The bias of a ratio estimator is a well-studied topic. How does this bias trade off against the reduction in variance, especially for smaller sample sizes? Understanding this trade-off is crucial for practitioners.

3.  **Scope and Generality:** The propensity overfitting problem is demonstrated with deterministic hypotheses and then addressed in the context of linear exponential family models (Norm-POEM). It seems likely that this problem would be even more severe for higher-capacity models like deep neural networks, which can more easily assign near-zero or near-one probabilities. A brief discussion on the applicability and potential challenges of this approach for other, more complex hypothesis classes would broaden the paper's impact.

**Summary of Review:**

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a clear and important contribution to the field of counterfactual learning. It identifies a critical and previously underappreciated problem with the standard counterfactual risk estimator and proposes a simple, elegant, and effective solution. The claims are well-supported by both theoretical reasoning and a thorough set of experiments. While a deeper theoretical treatment of using a biased estimator within the CRM framework would strengthen the paper, the clarity of the problem statement and the convincing empirical results make this a valuable contribution. I recommend acceptance.
