

# Review of "The Self-Normalized Estimator for Counterfactual Learning"

This paper addresses a critical issue in batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF), a setting increasingly relevant for real-world applications like ad placement and recommendation systems. The authors identify a previously overlooked problem—propensity overfitting—in the conventional counterfactual risk estimator used in the Counterfactual Risk Minimization (CRM) framework. They propose a self-normalized estimator that avoids this issue, leading to a new algorithm called Norm-POEM. The paper makes a clear contribution to the BLBF literature by identifying and solving a fundamental problem that affects the reliability of existing methods.

The paper builds upon previous NIPS work on CRM (Swaminathan & Joachims, 2015) and POEM (Swaminathan & Joachims, 2015), which established the CRM principle for BLBF. While prior work focused on variance reduction techniques for the unbiased risk estimator, this paper identifies a novel failure mode—propensity overfitting—that occurs when the estimator's risk can be artificially minimized by exploiting the propensity weights, rather than the actual loss values. The authors demonstrate this through a carefully constructed example showing how the conventional estimator can produce arbitrarily low risk estimates for overfitted hypotheses.

The main theoretical contribution is the self-normalized risk estimator, which uses multiplicative control variates to maintain consistency while avoiding the anomalies of the conventional estimator. The authors prove that this estimator is strongly consistent and equivariant (unlike the conventional estimator), and they provide a detailed analysis of how it resolves the propensity overfitting problem. The empirical evaluation on multi-label classification datasets demonstrates that Norm-POEM consistently outperforms the conventional POEM algorithm across multiple datasets and training sample sizes, with the self-normalized estimator effectively controlling the propensity overfitting issue as evidenced by the empirical estimates of the propensity sum Ŝ(h) being close to 1.

Strengths:
1. The paper identifies a novel and significant problem (propensity overfitting) that affects the reliability of existing BLBF methods, which has not been previously recognized in the literature.
2. The theoretical analysis is rigorous, with clear proofs of consistency and properties of the self-normalized estimator.
3. The empirical evaluation is thorough, demonstrating consistent performance gains across multiple datasets and experimental conditions.
4. The paper provides a clear connection between the theoretical properties of the estimator and the empirical results, showing that the self-normalized estimator effectively avoids propensity overfitting.

Weaknesses:
1. The paper does not explore the computational complexity of the self-normalized estimator in detail, though the experiments show it's comparable to POEM.
2. The connection to related work on control variates in importance sampling could be strengthened with more specific citations to recent NIPS papers on this topic.
3. While the paper demonstrates the approach works for structured output prediction, the applicability to other hypothesis spaces beyond linear models is not thoroughly explored.

Quality: The paper is technically sound with well-supported theoretical claims and thorough empirical validation. The authors are careful to acknowledge limitations, including the continued need for variance regularization.

Clarity: The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized, with clear explanations of the problem, the proposed solution, and the empirical results. The example illustrating propensity overfitting is particularly effective in making the problem concrete.

Originality: This work is highly original in identifying propensity overfitting as a distinct failure mode in BLBF. The application of self-normalized estimators to this problem represents a novel combination of techniques from importance sampling and counterfactual learning.

Significance: The problem addressed is significant for practical applications of BLBF, where unreliable risk estimates can lead to poor policy learning. The proposed solution leads to demonstrably better generalization performance, advancing the state of the art in this important area of machine learning.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Identifies and solves a previously overlooked problem in BLBF that affects the reliability of existing methods
- Provides rigorous theoretical analysis and thorough empirical validation
- Demonstrates consistent performance improvements across multiple datasets
- Makes a clear contribution to the understanding of counterfactual learning

Arguments against acceptance:
- Limited exploration of computational complexity and broader applicability
- Could strengthen connections to recent NIPS work on control variates

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of counterfactual learning. The problem identification is novel and important, the solution is theoretically sound and empirically validated, and the paper is exceptionally well-written. The authors have clearly distinguished their work from previous contributions while building upon them in a meaningful way. I believe this paper advances the state of the art in BLBF and will be of interest to the NIPS community. I recommend acceptance.
