I found the paper "The Self-Normalized Estimator for Counterfactual Learning" to be an interesting and well-written contribution to the field of batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF). The authors identify a significant problem with the conventional unbiased risk estimator used in prior works on BLBF, which they term "propensity overfitting." They propose a novel solution to this problem by introducing a self-normalized risk estimator, which they show to be more robust and effective in practice.

The paper is well-organized and clearly written, making it easy to follow the authors' arguments and understand the technical details. The authors provide a thorough review of related work in the field, which helps to situate their contribution within the broader context of BLBF research.

One of the strengths of the paper is its ability to balance theoretical and empirical contributions. The authors provide a rigorous theoretical analysis of the self-normalized estimator, including a proof of its consistency and a bound on its variance. They also present an extensive empirical evaluation of the estimator, which demonstrates its effectiveness in practice.

I appreciate that the authors provide code for their proposed method, which will facilitate replication and extension of their work by other researchers.

My main suggestion for improvement is to provide a clearer explanation of the relationship between large weight variance and propensity overfitting. While the authors discuss these concepts separately, it is not entirely clear how they are related, and a more explicit discussion of this relationship would be helpful.

Additionally, I had some confusion about line 199, where the behavior of the estimator seems unclear when n is small relative to k. The authors may want to consider adding additional explanation or clarification on this point.

Finally, I question the claim that prior BLBF works used an unbiased counterfactual risk estimator, noting that some of these works may have used clipping (which introduces bias). The authors may want to consider addressing this point in their discussion of related work.

Overall, I recommend acceptance of this paper, despite not having fully checked the technical soundness of the work due to time constraints. The paper makes a significant contribution to the field of BLBF, and its strengths outweigh its weaknesses.
