- Paper identifies "propensity overfitting" in CRM: IPS-based empirical risk can be arbitrarily manipulated when optimizing over rich hypothesis classes.  
- Shows IPS can give arbitrarily optimistic/pessimistic estimates and lacks equivariance under loss translation.  
- Proposes replacing IPS with self-normalized importance sampling (SNIS/SNIPS) to restore boundedness and loss-translation equivariance.  
- Instantiates CRM with SNIS to produce Norm-POEM for structured prediction using exponential-family linear rules.  
- Derives an approximate variance estimate for the SNIS ratio estimator to retain variance-regularized CRM objectives and optimizes with L-BFGS.  
- Empirically, Norm-POEM consistently outperforms POEM on multi-label datasets, is robust to loss translations, keeps weight sums near expectation, and converges faster.  
- Situates work within BLBF/causal inference and importance-weighting literature; SNIS is classical but applied here to a CRM-specific failure mode.  
- Technical soundness: propensity overfitting is convincingly demonstrated; SNIS justification and strong consistency are appropriate, but finite-sample SNIS-based generalization bounds are missing.  
- Empirical evaluation: follows established protocols with ablations and diagnostics, but baseline set is limited (no doubly robust or clipped/defensive/mixture IS).  
- Assumptions/limitations: requires full support of logging policy and still uses weight clipping for finite variance.  
- Clarity: well-written, good exposition and reproducibility; suggest labeling SNIS as weighted IPS/SNIPS and clarifying conditions for the variance approximation.  
- Originality: conceptual novelty in formalizing propensity overfitting and integrating SNIS into CRM; overall contribution is meaningful but incremental.  
- Significance: practical impact likely high—recommendation to use SNIS within CRM could influence BLBF practice.  
- Pros: identifies a real failure mode, offers a simple principled fix, shows consistent empirical gains, provides diagnostics and released code.  
- Cons: modest theoretical novelty, reliance on approximate variance, limited baselines and real-logged-data evaluation, sensitivity to clipping/regularization not deeply analyzed.  
- Suggested improvements: compare to doubly robust and defensive/mixture IS, report sensitivity to M and λ, derive finite-sample bounds for SNIS-CRM, test on real logged data and partial-support scenarios, add runtime/memory and weight-norm analyses.  
- Recommendation: Accept — practical, well-motivated contribution with consistent empirical benefits despite incremental theory.
