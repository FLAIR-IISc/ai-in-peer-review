- Identifies propensity overfitting in Counterfactual Risk Minimization (CRM) from logged bandit feedback, causing biased risk estimates and overfitting in complex hypothesis spaces.  
- Proposes a self-normalized estimator (Norm-POEM) that bounds variance while preserving consistency to mitigate propensity overfitting.  
- Provides a theoretical consistency proof for the self-normalized estimator (Theorem 2) and supporting analysis.  
- Empirically validates Norm-POEM on multiple datasets using supervised-to-bandit conversion, showing consistent gains over POEM.  
- Includes ablation studies (Table 3) demonstrating the necessity and effect of variance regularization.  
- Notes higher per-iteration computational cost but reports faster overall convergence for Norm-POEM; computational complexity analysis is brief.  
- Paper is well-written and logically structured with clear motivation and positioning in causal/off-policy literature.  
- Mathematical notation is generally clear, though importance sampling concepts could use more explanation for unfamiliar readers.  
- Example 1 effectively illustrates the propensity overfitting issue.  
- Novel contribution in identifying propensity overfitting and applying self-normalization/multiplicative control variates within CRM.  
- Builds on existing BLBF and off-policy evaluation literature while distinguishing itself from additive control variate or clipping approaches.  
- Addresses a significant practical limitation of CRM, improving reliability of learning from logged data across applications (ads, recommendation, search).  
- Public code release enhances reproducibility and potential impact.  
- Strengths: novelty, strong theoretical justification, thorough empirical validation, clear presentation, and code release.  
- Weakness: limited analysis of computational complexity and lack of deeper exploration of optimal regularization parameter (λ).  
- Weakness: limited discussion of limitations or scenarios where the self-normalized estimator may underperform.  
- Arguments for acceptance: recognizes a significant problem, offers a sound solution with solid theory and experiments, and has broad applicability.  
- Arguments against acceptance: needs more detailed computational analysis and a more comprehensive limitations discussion.  
- Overall: strong paper with valuable contributions; recommended for acceptance despite minor weaknesses.
