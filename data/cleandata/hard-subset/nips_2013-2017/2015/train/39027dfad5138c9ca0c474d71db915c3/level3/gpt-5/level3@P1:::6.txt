This paper studies batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF) under the Counterfactual Risk Minimization (CRM) framework. The authors identify a failure mode of the standard inverse propensity scoring (IPS) estimator used within CRM: when optimizing over rich hypothesis classes, empirical risk minimization can “overfit the propensities,” leading to degenerate estimates and poor generalization. They term this propensity overfitting and provide illustrative examples showing how IPS risk estimates can become arbitrarily optimistic (or pessimistic) due to weight manipulation, and how IPS lacks equivariance under loss translation. To address this, they propose replacing IPS with the self-normalized importance sampling (SNIS) estimator (a multiplicative control variate), yielding a bounded, loss-translation-equivariant, strongly consistent risk estimator. Instantiating CRM with SNIS leads to Normalized POEM (Norm-POEM) for structured prediction with exponential-family linear rules. They derive an approximate variance estimate for the ratio estimator to retain CRM’s variance-regularized objective and optimize it with L-BFGS. Empirically, on several multi-label datasets using a supervised-to-bandit protocol, Norm-POEM consistently outperforms POEM, appears robust to loss translations, keeps the sum of weights close to its expectation, and converges faster in practice.

Relation to prior work: The work is firmly grounded in the BLBF/causal inference literature. It builds on CRM (Swaminathan & Joachims, ICML) and connects to importance weighting under covariate shift (Cortes et al., NIPS), off-policy evaluation (Dudík et al.; Thomas et al.), and variance reduction via clipping (Ionides) and control variates (Hesterberg; Kong; Owen). Self-normalized IS (aka weighted IS) is a classic technique in Monte Carlo; the paper’s contribution is to diagnose a CRM-specific failure mode (propensity overfitting) and to integrate SNIS within CRM, showing both conceptual and empirical benefits for counterfactual learning.

Quality:
- Technical soundness: The propensity overfitting phenomenon is convincingly demonstrated via a clean construction and by diagnostic Ŝ(h) measurements. The use of SNIS to address equivariance and boundedness is appropriate and well-justified. The strong consistency result is standard for SNIS; the empirical variance formula is an approximation (delta method/CLT), which is reasonable but should be flagged as such when used for regularization. The paper stops short of providing new finite-sample generalization bounds calibrated to SNIS-based CRM, which would strengthen the theory.
- Empirical evaluation: The experiments follow established supervised-to-bandit methodology, cover multiple multilabel datasets, and include repeated trials and ablations (with/without variance regularization, loss translations). Results consistently favor Norm-POEM and provide clear diagnostics (Ŝ near 1, robustness to translation). Baselines are limited to POEM; omitting doubly robust and clipped/defensive/mixture IS baselines is a missed opportunity.
- Assumptions: Full support of the logging policy is required; weight clipping is still used to ensure finite variance.

Clarity:
- The paper is clearly written and well-organized. The exposition of propensity overfitting and the role of Ŝ(h) as a diagnostic is pedagogically strong. Pseudocode/algorithmic details are sufficient for reproduction, and the availability of code is appreciated. Minor suggestions: explicitly label SNIS as “weighted IPS/SNIPS” to ease cross-referencing to prior literature; clarify the conditions under which the approximate variance is reliable.

Originality:
- SNIS itself is not new; the novelty lies in identifying and formalizing propensity overfitting within CRM, proving that SNIS eliminates key anomalies (boundedness, equivariance), and integrating it into a practical CRM learner. This is a meaningful but incremental contribution. The “Norm-POEM” instantiation is a natural evolution of POEM.

Significance:
- Learning from logged bandit data is important in ads, recommendation, and search. A robust estimator that avoids a practical failure mode is valuable. Given the pervasiveness of IPS in BLBF, the recommendation to use SNIS (with variance regularization) is likely to influence practice. The empirical gains and diagnostics suggest this can be a new default within CRM for structured prediction; broader baselines would help cement the impact.

Pros:
- Identifies a real and underappreciated failure mode (propensity overfitting) with clear examples and diagnostics.
- Simple, principled fix via a well-known estimator that restores boundedness and equivariance.
- Consistent empirical improvements across datasets; robust to loss translations; informative diagnostics (Ŝ ≈ 1).
- Practical algorithm with released code; optimization converges reliably.

Cons:
- Theoretical novelty is modest; the main estimator is classical, and the consistency result is standard.
- No new finite-sample generalization bounds for SNIS-based CRM; reliance on approximate variance.
- Experimental baselines are limited (no doubly robust/mixture/defensive IS comparisons; no real logged data).
- Assumes full support; limited discussion of behavior under support violations beyond clipping.
- Sensitivity to clipping threshold M and λ is not deeply analyzed.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Add comparisons to doubly robust estimators and defensive/mixture IS; report sensitivity to M and λ.
- Provide a finite-sample generalization bound tailored to SNIS within CRM (e.g., using empirical Bernstein for ratio estimators).
- Evaluate on at least one real logged dataset, or simulate partial support violations to stress-test robustness.
- Include runtime/memory tables and a study of weight norms to substantiate computational claims.
- Explore explicit regularization/constraints on Ŝ(h) (e.g., penalizing deviations from 1) as an alternative to full normalization.

Recommendation: Accept. Despite incremental theoretical novelty, the paper makes a clear, well-motivated, and practically impactful contribution by diagnosing propensity overfitting and showing that self-normalization within CRM remedies it, with consistent empirical gains and solid clarity.
