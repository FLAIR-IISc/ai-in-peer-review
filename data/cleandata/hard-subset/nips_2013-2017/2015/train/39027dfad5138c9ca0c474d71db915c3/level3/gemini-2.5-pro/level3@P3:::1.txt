This paper addresses the problem of learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF), where a new policy must be learned from historical data collected by a different, potentially suboptimal, policy. The standard approach relies on Counterfactual Risk Minimization (CRM), which uses an importance-weighted risk estimator. The authors identify a critical failure mode of the conventional inverse propensity score (IPS) estimator, which they term "propensity overfitting". This issue is distinct from the well-studied problem of high variance in importance weights. Propensity overfitting occurs when the hypothesis space is complex enough to find a policy that achieves an artificially low empirical risk by exploiting the logging probabilities—for instance, by assigning high probability to actions in the log that had negative loss, or by assigning probability to actions *not* in the log to achieve a near-zero risk estimate for positive losses.

To solve this, the authors propose replacing the standard IPS estimator with a self-normalized (or ratio) estimator, which is well-known in the Monte Carlo literature. This estimator is shown to be equivariant to shifts in the loss function and is naturally bounded within the range of the observed losses, properties that the standard estimator lacks and which directly combat propensity overfitting. They instantiate this idea in a new algorithm, Norm-POEM, and demonstrate through experiments on multi-label classification tasks that it significantly and consistently outperforms a strong baseline (POEM) that uses the conventional (clipped) IPS estimator.

**Strengths:**
*   The paper clearly identifies and illustrates an important, and perhaps underappreciated, problem in off-policy learning. The concept of "propensity overfitting" is a useful contribution that provides a new lens through which to view the challenges of learning from logged data, moving beyond the standard discussion of variance.
*   The proposed solution of using a self-normalized estimator is elegant, well-motivated, and directly addresses the identified pathologies of the standard estimator. The connection to the control variates literature provides a principled foundation for the approach.
*   The empirical evaluation is strong. Norm-POEM shows substantial gains over the baseline POEM across multiple datasets. The analysis in Table 2, which shows the sum of propensity weights `Ŝ(ĥ)` for the learned hypothesis, provides compelling evidence that the proposed method is indeed fixing the targeted problem.
*   The authors have made their code available, which is commendable and facilitates reproducibility and future work.

**Weaknesses and Questions for Clarification:**

*Disclaimer: This was an additional paper assigned late in the process, so I had limited time for a thorough review. My apologies if I have missed or misunderstood key details. I was not able to fully verify all technical details or conduct an exhaustive novelty check, but the core ideas appear sound.*

1.  **Relationship between Propensity Overfitting and Variance:** The paper distinguishes "propensity overfitting" from the high-variance problem. While intuitively different, the relationship could be explained more clearly. Is propensity overfitting a direct consequence of an estimator being susceptible to high-variance weights, or can these problems arise independently? For example, is it possible to have propensity overfitting even when the importance weights themselves have low variance? A more detailed discussion would strengthen the paper's conceptual framing.

2.  **Clarity on Example 1 (line 199):** The example of propensity overfitting is very insightful. The paper states that if `n` is small relative to `k`, an `hoverfit` hypothesis can be found with an estimated risk `R̂` as low as `-k`. This demonstrates the pathology well. However, it would be helpful to briefly comment on why the variance-regularization term in the full CRM objective (Eq. 3) is insufficient to prevent this. One might assume that `hoverfit` would also have a very high empirical variance `V̂ar(uh)`, which should penalize it. A short explanation of why this regularization fails in this scenario would make the argument more complete.

3.  **Positioning vs. Prior Work with Clipping:** The paper frames the problem by pointing out flaws in the "unbiased" IPS estimator. However, it quickly acknowledges that in practice, biased variants like clipping are used to control variance, and the main baseline, POEM, uses such a clipped estimator. The claim that prior work has focused primarily on an "unbiased estimator" could be seen as slightly overstating the case, since practitioners are well aware of the need for bias-variance trade-offs. The argument would be stronger if it more directly addressed why clipping is an insufficient solution to *propensity overfitting* specifically, rather than just framing the self-normalized estimator as a fix for the pathologies of the purely unbiased estimator.

**Overall Recommendation:**

This is an interesting and well-executed paper. It makes a valuable conceptual contribution by identifying and naming the problem of propensity overfitting and proposes a simple, principled, and empirically effective solution. Despite my minor requests for clarification, the core message is clear and compelling. The paper addresses a significant problem in a practical learning setting, and the results suggest that the proposed method offers a substantial improvement in robustness and performance. I recommend acceptance.
