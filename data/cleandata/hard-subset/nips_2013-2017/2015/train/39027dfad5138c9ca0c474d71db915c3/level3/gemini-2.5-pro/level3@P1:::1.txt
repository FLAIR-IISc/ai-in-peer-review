This paper addresses the problem of batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF), a setting of high practical importance for improving interactive systems using historical data. The standard approach relies on counterfactual risk minimization (CRM), which uses an importance-sampling-based estimator to evaluate the performance of a new policy based on data logged by an old one. The authors argue that this conventional unbiased estimator suffers from a severe issue they term "propensity overfitting." This problem arises when a hypothesis with high capacity learns to manipulate the propensity weights themselves, rather than minimizing the true loss. For example, with negative losses, the model can achieve an arbitrarily low estimated risk by assigning high probability to actions observed in the training data, regardless of their actual loss.

To remedy this, the authors propose replacing the standard inverse propensity score (IPS) estimator with a self-normalized version. This is achieved by introducing the sum of the importance weights as a multiplicative control variate, effectively creating a ratio estimator where the risk is normalized by the sum of weights. This self-normalized estimator is shown to be equivariant to translations of the loss function and bounded within the range of the loss, properties which the standard estimator lacks and which are key to preventing propensity overfitting. The authors instantiate this new estimator within the CRM framework to develop a new algorithm, Norm-POEM, for structured output prediction with linear models. Empirical results on several multi-label classification tasks demonstrate that Norm-POEM consistently and substantially outperforms the previous POEM algorithm, which uses the standard unbiased estimator.

**Strengths:**
1.  **Significance:** The paper tackles a crucial and practical problem. Learning effectively from logged data is a key challenge in many industrial applications like recommendation, search, and advertising. Improving the robustness of learning algorithms in this setting is a significant contribution.
2.  **Originality & Quality:** The identification and formalization of "propensity overfitting" is a novel and insightful contribution. It clearly distinguishes this issue from the well-known problem of high variance in importance sampling. While high variance is about the instability of the estimate, propensity overfitting is a more systematic failure mode where the learning objective itself encourages degenerate solutions. The proposed solution is elegant, well-motivated by the control variates literature, and its desirable theoretical properties (equivariance, boundedness, consistency) are clearly established.
3.  **Clarity & Empirical Validation:** The paper is well-written and the problem is clearly illustrated with a compelling example (Example 1). The experimental setup is sound, following the established "Supervised to Bandit" protocol, which allows for controlled evaluation. The results are strong and compellingly demonstrate the practical benefits of the proposed estimator, showing that Norm-POEM is not only more accurate but also robust to simple translations of the loss function that cause the baseline method to fail catastrophically.
4.  **Reproducibility:** The authors are to be commended for making their code available, which greatly aids in reproducibility and encourages adoption of the method.

**Weaknesses and Questions for the Authors:**
First, I should preface my comments by noting that I had limited time for this review and may have missed or misunderstood certain points. My overall impression is positive, but I have a few points that could benefit from clarification.

1.  **Relationship between Propensity Overfitting and High Variance:** The paper does a good job of introducing propensity overfitting as a distinct concept. However, the two issues are clearly related, as a model that overfits propensities will necessarily induce very large (or very small) importance weights, which in turn leads to high variance of the risk estimate. A more direct discussion of this relationship would be beneficial. For example, does the variance regularization term in the original CRM objective already implicitly penalize propensity overfitting? The results suggest it is not sufficient, but a clearer explanation of *why* it is insufficient would strengthen the argument for the new estimator.

2.  **Clarity of Example 1:** In Example 1 (around line 199), the argument for the case where `n` is small relative to `k` was not immediately clear. The paper states that if all `xi` in the sample are distinct, then a hypothesis `hoverfit` exists with an estimated risk of `-k`. The text then claims this scenario is "quite likely if n is small relative to k". If `k` is the size of the input space and `n << k`, sampling with replacement makes it highly probable that all `xi` are distinct. The resulting risk estimate of `-k` is a very clear example of the problem. Perhaps a slightly more explicit walk-through of this part of the example would help the reader.

3.  **Positioning Relative to Prior Work:** The paper states that "virtually all existing works on BLBF have focused on a particular unbiased estimator." While the vanilla IPS estimator is indeed unbiased, many practical approaches cited in the paper (e.g., those using clipping/truncation) explicitly trade a small amount of bias for a large reduction in variance. It might be more precise to acknowledge that while the foundational estimator is unbiased, common practice often involves biased variants. The paper's contribution could then be framed even more strongly as addressing a fundamental issue with the importance weighting mechanism itself, which is not fully resolved by simple variance-reduction techniques like clipping.

**Summary of Evaluation:**
- **Quality:** High. The paper appears technically sound. The claims are well-supported by both theoretical arguments and strong experimental results.
- **Clarity:** High. The paper is well-written and easy to follow. The core problem and proposed solution are clearly motivated.
- **Originality:** High. The identification of propensity overfitting is a novel and important conceptual contribution. The use of the self-normalized estimator in this context is also novel and effective.
- **Significance:** High. The work addresses a widespread and important problem. The proposed method offers a substantial improvement in robustness and performance, making it likely that others will use and build upon these ideas.

Despite my minor questions, I believe this is a strong paper with a valuable contribution to the field of counterfactual learning. The problem it identifies is subtle but severe, and the proposed solution is principled and empirically effective. I recommend acceptance.
