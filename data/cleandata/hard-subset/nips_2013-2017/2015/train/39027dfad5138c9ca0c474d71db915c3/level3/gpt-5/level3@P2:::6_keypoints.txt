- Paper identifies "propensity overfitting" in IPS-based CRM: learners can exploit importance weights to drive empirical risk instead of fitting low-loss examples.  
- Diagnostic statistic Ŝ(h) (average normalized weight) reveals deviation from expected 1 under overfitting hypotheses.  
- Proposed remedy: replace IPS with self-normalized importance sampling (SNIS) estimator within CRM, yielding Norm-POEM.  
- SNIS properties: bounded, equivariant to additive loss translations, and strongly consistent (biased in finite samples).  
- Norm-POEM uses an approximate variance estimator (delta-method) for empirical Bernstein-style regularization.  
- Empirical results (supervised→bandit protocol) show Norm-POEM improves over POEM, is robust to loss translation, and converges faster.  
- Paper is well positioned with respect to CRM, doubly robust methods, clipping, and classical variance reduction literature.  
- Novelty lies in diagnosing propensity overfitting and integrating SNIS into CRM; SNIS itself is a known estimator.  
- Technical soundness: propensity overfitting and SNIS properties are convincingly demonstrated and supported.  
- Main theoretical gap: no finite-sample generalization bound for SN-CRM; reliance on asymptotic/approximate variance.  
- Empirical evaluation is thorough within simulation but lacks comparisons to DR-family baselines and self-normalized DR variants.  
- No experiments on real logged data, limiting demonstrated applicability to practical logs.  
- Clarity is good overall; suggestions include adding pseudocode, hyperparameter/tuning details, and clipping specification.  
- Request clearer description of clipping for SN (numerator/denominator) and discussion of induced bias.  
- Suggest reporting computational complexity per iteration and conditions for tractable Z(x).  
- Strengths: clear failure-mode identification, principled minimal fix, concrete algorithm, strong empirical diagnostics, and code availability.  
- Weaknesses: missing finite-sample theory, limited baseline comparisons and real-data experiments, and limited discussion of normalization/clipping bias–variance trade-off.  
- Recommendation: lean accept — solid, practical contribution that would be strengthened by theoretical guarantees and broader empirical comparisons.
