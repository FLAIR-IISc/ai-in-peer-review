## Review of "The Self-Normalized Estimator for Counterfactual Learning"

This paper addresses a critical issue in batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF): propensity overfitting in the standard counterfactual risk estimator. The authors convincingly demonstrate that this estimator can lead to severely biased risk estimates, particularly with complex hypothesis spaces, and propose a self-normalized estimator as a remedy. This is a valuable contribution as BLBF is increasingly relevant for training online systems with historical data. The paper builds logically on prior work in causal inference, off-policy evaluation, and counterfactual learning (e.g., [1, 5, 6]), and clearly positions itself within this landscape.

The core strength of the paper lies in its insightful analysis of the propensity overfitting problem and the elegant solution offered by the self-normalized estimator. The explanations are well-reasoned, and the examples provided (e.g., Example 1) effectively illustrate the issue. The empirical evaluation is thorough, consistently demonstrating Norm-POEM’s superior performance over POEM across multiple datasets. The ablation study (Table 3) clarifying the continued need for variance regularization is also a positive aspect.

However, there are a few points that need clarification. First, the paper states that NORM-POEM uses rescaled delta in [-1,0] like CRM, but doesn’t explicitly clarify *how* delta is rescaled in equation (8). A short explanation would be helpful. Also, the meaning of “delta > 0” in Table 1 experiments remains unclear; a brief elaboration would increase readability. The authors should also address the reviewer’s suggested correction to line 170-171 (using h(y|x) / h0(y|x)) and line 276 (using argmin instead of argmax). The notation fix on line 319 (bars to hats) is also necessary.

Furthermore, while the authors acknowledge that the self-normalized estimator is not unbiased, they argue its strong consistency.  A stronger theoretical argument for *why* modeling delta(x,y) as a deterministic function is problematic and instead should be viewed as a random variable with an expectation over label noise would bolster the paper’s claims.  Including an expectation over delta in the true risk setup could further strengthen the theoretical foundation. The paper also mentions prior work noting nonlinearity [1], but doesn’t fully contextualize its relevance here; a more thorough discussion would be valuable.

**Overall:** Despite these minor points, this is a strong paper with significant potential impact. The identified problem of propensity overfitting is crucial, and the proposed solution is both theoretically sound and empirically validated. 

**Pro:**
*   Addresses a significant problem in BLBF.
*   Provides a novel and effective solution (self-normalized estimator).
*   Strong empirical results demonstrating improved performance.
*   Clear and well-written explanation of the problem and proposed solution.

**Con:**
*   Requires clarification on rescaling of delta and the meaning of "delta > 0".
*   Minor corrections needed as outlined by the supervisor.
*   Theoretical argument regarding delta as a random variable could be strengthened.
