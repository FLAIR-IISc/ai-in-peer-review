### Summary of the Paper

This paper addresses the problem of Batch Learning from Logged Bandit Feedback (BLBF). The authors identify a novel failure mode of standard counterfactual risk estimators based on Inverse Propensity Scoring (IPS), which they term "Propensity Overfitting". This overfitting occurs when a learning algorithm minimizes the estimated risk not by finding actions with low intrinsic loss, but by exploiting the structure of the propensity weights—for instance, by assigning high probability to actions observed in the training data (for negative losses) or avoiding them entirely (for positive losses). This can lead to arbitrarily poor risk estimates and degenerate solutions.

To solve this, the authors propose replacing the standard IPS estimator with the Self-Normalized Estimator (SNE), a well-known ratio estimator from the importance sampling literature. They show that the SNE is equivariant to shifts in the loss function and its estimates are bounded within the range of the loss, thus directly mitigating the pathologies of Propensity Overfitting. They incorporate this estimator into the Counterfactual Risk Minimization (CRM) framework to develop a new learning algorithm, Norm-POEM. Through extensive experiments on multi-label classification tasks, they demonstrate that Norm-POEM significantly outperforms its predecessor (POEM), is more robust, and successfully controls for Propensity Overfitting.

### Strengths (Reasons for Acceptance)

1.  **Novel and Important Problem Identification:** The core contribution of this paper is the identification and clear articulation of "Propensity Overfitting". This is a subtle but critical issue in off-policy learning that has not been explicitly addressed in prior BLBF literature. The illustrative example in Section 4 is exceptionally clear and convincing, demonstrating how standard Empirical Risk Minimization (ERM) can lead to degenerate solutions that are disconnected from the true risk. This is a valuable conceptual contribution to the field.

2.  **Principled and Elegant Solution:** The proposed solution is not an ad-hoc heuristic but is well-grounded in the statistical literature on control variates and ratio estimators. The insight to use the sum of importance weights, `Ŝ(h)`, as a multiplicative control variate (since its expectation is known to be 1) is elegant. The resulting Self-Normalized Estimator directly addresses the identified pathologies (lack of equivariance, unbounded estimates) in a principled manner.

3.  **Strong and Convincing Empirical Evaluation:** The experimental section is thorough and well-designed. The authors follow a controlled "Supervised -> Bandit" protocol, allowing for precise evaluation. The key claims are directly tested and validated:
    *   **Generalization:** Norm-POEM consistently and significantly outperforms POEM across multiple datasets and data sizes (Table 1, Figure 1).
    *   **Propensity Overfitting Control:** Table 2 provides direct evidence that Norm-POEM keeps `Ŝ(h)` centered around its expected value of 1, while POEM does not.
    *   **Robustness:** The experiments showing invariance to loss translation (Table 2, right) provide a powerful demonstration of the SNE's practical benefits over the standard IPS estimator, which fails catastrophically.

4.  **Clarity and Presentation:** The paper is exceptionally well-written. The motivation is clear, the problem is introduced logically, the proposed solution is well-explained, and the experiments directly support the paper's central thesis. The logical flow from problem to solution is easy to follow.

### Weaknesses (Points for Improvement)

1.  **Theoretical Justification for the CRM Objective:** The paper plugs the SNE and its approximate variance into the CRM objective (Equation 3), which is motivated by a high-probability bound (Theorem 1) derived for the *unbiased* IPS estimator. However, the SNE is a biased estimator (though consistent). It is not immediately clear that minimizing this objective is theoretically sound. The original bound relies on properties of sums of i.i.d. random variables (via Bernstein's inequality) which may not hold in the same way for a ratio of sums. A more rigorous justification or a new learning bound tailored to the SNE would significantly strengthen the paper's theoretical foundations.

2.  **Limited Comparison to Other Baselines:** The primary baseline is POEM [1], which uses the standard (clipped) IPS estimator. While this is the most direct comparison, other advanced off-policy estimators exist. In particular, the Doubly Robust (DR) estimator [6] is a standard and powerful alternative that combines a direct model with an IPS component. It would be valuable to understand how DR fares against Propensity Overfitting and how its performance compares to Norm-POEM. A DR estimator might also mitigate this issue, albeit through different mechanisms.

3.  **Computational Scalability:** The authors note that the variance term in their objective (Equation 8) does not admit stochastic optimization, necessitating the use of a batch optimizer like L-BFGS. While they observe faster convergence in practice on their datasets, this could be a significant limitation for truly large-scale problems where full-batch methods are infeasible. A discussion of potential approximations or alternative optimization strategies for scaling Norm-POEM would be beneficial.

### Detailed Evaluation

*   **Quality:** The paper is of high quality. The identification of Propensity Overfitting is a solid contribution, and the empirical work is sound and convincing. The main weakness lies in the theoretical gap of applying a bound for an unbiased estimator to a biased one without a formal justification. However, the empirical success provides strong evidence for the method's validity.

*   **Clarity:** Excellent. The paper is a model of clarity in its writing and organization. The arguments are easy to follow, and the experiments are clearly described and interpreted.

*   **Originality:** The Self-Normalized Estimator itself is not new, a fact the authors correctly acknowledge. The originality lies in (a) identifying the specific problem of Propensity Overfitting in the context of *learning* (as opposed to just evaluation) and (b) proposing the SNE as a principled solution to this specific learning pathology. This connection is novel and insightful.

*   **Significance:** High. BLBF is a problem of immense practical importance for web search, recommender systems, and online advertising. Propensity Overfitting is a real and dangerous pitfall of a standard technique. The proposed solution is simple to implement, empirically effective, and addresses a fundamental flaw in the existing approach. This work is likely to be influential and widely adopted by practitioners and researchers in the field.

In conclusion, this is a strong paper with a clear and important message. It identifies a previously overlooked but critical problem in counterfactual learning and provides an effective, principled solution. While the theoretical justification could be tightened, the conceptual insight and the strength of the empirical results make this a significant contribution to the field.
