## Review of "The Self-Normalized Estimator for Counterfactual Learning"

This paper addresses the critical problem of learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF), framing it within the context of counterfactual estimation. The authors identify a novel form of overfitting – “Propensity Overfitting” – that plagues existing estimators and propose a self-normalized estimator to mitigate this issue. The theoretical justification and empirical validation appear promising, focusing on improvements to existing CRM-based algorithms.

**Summary of Strengths:**

* **Identifies a novel issue:** The concept of Propensity Overfitting is a significant contribution, highlighting a vulnerability in standard counterfactual estimation techniques for BLBF that has been previously overlooked. The illustrative examples are effective in demonstrating this problem.
* **Theoretical grounding:** The proposed self-normalized estimator is presented with a solid theoretical foundation, drawing upon control variate techniques and establishing consistency.
* **Empirical validation:** The experimental results, using a standard benchmark setup, consistently demonstrate the superiority of Norm-POEM (the algorithm utilizing the proposed estimator) over POEM. The ablation study regarding variance regularization is also insightful.
* **Clear connection to existing work:** The paper effectively positions the work within the broader landscape of causal inference, off-policy evaluation, and importance sampling, citing relevant literature.

**Summary of Weaknesses:**

* **Presentation of Theorem 1:** The justification for referencing Theorems 1 & 6 of [1, 13] without explicitly stating the relevant portions within the text feels lazy. A brief summarization or citation of specific equations/claims is expected.
* **Notational inconsistencies:** The variable `p` is used to represent both a propensity score *and* a probability in the context of the hypothesis space definition. This is confusing. `h0(y|x)` is consistently denoted as `p` in the equations while also representing a probabilistic outcome.
* **Overly reliant on [1]:** While building on prior work is expected, the paper heavily leans on [1] for justification and methodology, sometimes to a fault. While the authors present an improvement, the description feels less self-contained as a result.
* **Computational Complexity Discussion Lacks Detail:** While stating that Norm-POEM is competitive with POEM in runtime despite increased per-iteration cost, the paper lacks detailed analysis. What aspects of the self-normalized estimator enable faster convergence? Is the L-BFGS optimization substantially different in practice? 
* **Minor Typo/Grammatical Issues:** Throughout the paper, several minor grammatical errors and typos were noted (e.g., “the unbiased estimator of Equation (2) has two anomalies” should read “the unbiased risk estimator…”). These detract from the overall polish of the manuscript.
* **Missing Discussion of Alternative Variance Estimators:** The paper focuses primarily on the variance estimate presented in Equation (8). However, other approximations exist, and a brief discussion of their trade-offs would strengthen the analysis.
* **Limited Exploration of Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper states that hyper-parameters are calibrated on a hold-out set, but a sensitivity analysis (e.g., showing performance variations with different λ values) is absent. This limits confidence in the robustness of the results.
* **Software Availability Issues:** The URL provided for the Norm-POEM software implementation is broken ("http://www.cs.cornell.edu/∼adith/POEM").

**Detailed Comments & Questions:**

* **Section 3:** The definition of the hypothesis space `H` feels somewhat unnecessarily formal, and the notation could be simplified.
* **Section 4, Example 1:** While a helpful illustration, the specific choice of the loss function feels contrived. Is this a general phenomenon, or is it amplified by this particular construction?
* **Section 5:** The transition from identifying Ŝ(h) as a potential indicator of overfitting to using it as a control variate is somewhat abrupt. A more detailed explanation of the rationale would be beneficial.
* **Section 6:** Why was L-BFGS chosen specifically? A brief justification, perhaps referencing its performance on similar optimization problems, is warranted.
* **Section 7:** The table captions could be more descriptive. For example, Table 2’s caption does not fully explain what the values represent. The observation that Norm-POEM consistently outperforms POEM is noted, but a statistical significance test would reinforce this claim.

**Pro & Con Acceptance:**

**Pro:**

* Novel identification and mitigation of Propensity Overfitting.
* Strong theoretical foundation for the proposed estimator.
* Consistent empirical improvements over existing approaches.
* Addresses a practically important problem in batch learning.

**Con:**

* Presentation contains minor inconsistencies and lacks sufficient detail in certain areas.
* Heavy reliance on previous work ([1]) makes the paper less self-contained.
* Limited discussion of computational complexity and hyperparameter sensitivity.
* Several minor errors in writing and a broken software link.



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite its strengths, the paper suffers from a number of minor flaws that, collectively, detract from its overall quality. The core idea is novel and valuable, and the empirical results are convincing. However, the level of polish and attention to detail expected for a NIPS publication is not consistently met. **I lean towards rejection**, but would be open to reconsideration if the authors address the identified weaknesses in a thorough revision. The paper has potential, but currently falls short of the high standards for acceptance. I'm hesitant to recommend acceptance because of the sheer number of detailed issues, despite acknowledging the significance of the core contribution.
