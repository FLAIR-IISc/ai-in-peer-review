**Summary:**
This paper addresses the problem of Batch Learning from Logged Bandit Feedback (BLBF), a crucial setting for improving interactive systems using historical data. The authors identify a novel and significant failure mode of standard counterfactual risk estimators based on Inverse Propensity Scoring (IPS), which they term "Propensity Overfitting." They show that because the standard IPS estimator is not equivariant to shifts in the loss function, Empirical Risk Minimization (ERM) can select degenerate hypotheses that achieve an artificially low risk estimate by manipulating propensity weights rather than accurately modeling the underlying loss. To solve this, they propose replacing the standard IPS estimator with the Self-Normalized (SN) estimator, a well-known ratio estimator from the importance sampling literature. They demonstrate that the SN estimator is equivariant, bounded, and consistent, thereby provably avoiding propensity overfitting. They integrate this estimator into the Counterfactual Risk Minimization (CRM) framework to create a new learning algorithm, Norm-POEM, and provide extensive empirical evidence showing that it significantly outperforms its predecessor (POEM) in terms of generalization and robustness.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Clear Identification of a Critical Problem (Significance & Originality):** The paper's primary contribution is the identification and clear articulation of "Propensity Overfitting." This is a subtle but fundamental problem with applying ERM to standard counterfactual estimators that has been largely overlooked. The illustrative example in Section 4 is exceptionally clear and convincingly demonstrates how a learner can achieve an arbitrarily low estimated risk by either concentrating probability on training examples (for negative losses) or avoiding them entirely (for positive losses). This is a novel and important insight for the off-policy learning community.

2.  **Elegant and Principled Solution (Quality & Significance):** The proposed solution is simple, elegant, and theoretically sound. Instead of introducing a complex new model or heuristic, the authors advocate for using the self-normalized estimator, a classic statistical tool. They provide clear reasoning for why this choice directly remedies the identified problem: the estimator's equivariance to loss translation and its values being bounded within the range of the observed losses. This connects the learning problem back to fundamental principles of Monte Carlo estimation.

3.  **Strong and Thorough Empirical Validation (Quality):** The experimental section is a model of good scientific practice. The authors not only show that their proposed method, Norm-POEM, achieves lower generalization error than the baseline (Table 1, Figure 1), but they also include diagnostic experiments that directly confirm their hypothesis. Table 2's results on the sum of weights (`Ŝ(h)`) and the effect of loss translation provide direct evidence that Norm-POEM is indeed controlling for propensity overfitting while the baseline is not. The robustness shown across different datasets is compelling. The surprising finding that Norm-POEM is also computationally efficient adds to the practical appeal.

4.  **Exceptional Clarity and Organization (Clarity):** The paper is extremely well-written and easy to follow. It logically progresses from the background of BLBF, to the identification of the propensity overfitting problem, to the principled solution, and finally to empirical verification. The concepts are explained with precision, and the motivation is consistently strong.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Limited Novelty of the Estimator Itself (Originality):** While the identification of the problem is novel, the proposed solution—the self-normalized estimator—is a well-established technique in the importance sampling literature. The authors are transparent about this and provide appropriate citations (e.g., [12, 19, 22]). The core novelty lies in diagnosing its utility for *learning* via ERM in the BLBF setting, not in inventing the estimator. This is a minor point, as the contribution is in the insight and application, but it's worth noting.

2.  **Lack of Comparison to Other Advanced Estimators (Quality):** The experiments focus exclusively on comparing Norm-POEM (based on the SN estimator) to POEM (based on the clipped IPS estimator). However, the literature on off-policy evaluation contains other powerful variance-reduction techniques, most notably the Doubly Robust (DR) estimator [6]. DR estimators combine a regression model with importance sampling and are often considered state-of-the-art. It is plausible that DR estimators are also less susceptible to propensity overfitting. A discussion and/or empirical comparison against a DR-based learning algorithm would have more firmly established the place of Norm-POEM within the broader landscape of counterfactual learning methods.

3.  **Computational Scalability Concerns (Quality):** The authors note that the variance estimate for the self-normalized estimator (Equation 8) does not readily admit stochastic optimization, forcing the use of batch methods like L-BFGS. While they report fast convergence in their experiments, this could be a practical limitation for scaling to extremely large datasets where stochastic gradient methods are often required. A brief discussion of potential approximations or future work to enable stochastic optimization would strengthen the paper's practical outlook.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Identifies and provides a clear explanation for a fundamental, previously overlooked problem in counterfactual learning.
*   Proposes a simple, principled, and highly effective solution.
*   Provides comprehensive and convincing empirical evidence, including diagnostic experiments that validate the core claims.
*   The paper is exceptionally well-written and will be a valuable contribution to the NIPS community, likely influencing future work in off-policy and bandit learning.

**Arguments for Rejection:**
*   The proposed estimator is not new, though its application in this context is.
*   The empirical comparison is limited to a single baseline, omitting other relevant methods like Doubly Robust estimators.

**Conclusion:**
This is an excellent paper that makes a clear and significant contribution. The identification of propensity overfitting is a genuine insight, and the proposed solution is both elegant and effective. The strengths, particularly the clarity of the problem formulation and the quality of the empirical evaluation, far outweigh the minor weaknesses. The work advances the state of the art in batch learning from bandit feedback in a demonstrable way and is highly likely to be impactful. I strongly recommend acceptance.
