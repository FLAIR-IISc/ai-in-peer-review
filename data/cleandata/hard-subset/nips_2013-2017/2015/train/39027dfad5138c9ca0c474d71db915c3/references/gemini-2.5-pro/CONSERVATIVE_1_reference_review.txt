This paper addresses the problem of batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF). The authors identify a specific failure mode of the conventional inverse propensity score (IPS) estimator when used for empirical risk minimization, which they term "Propensity Overfitting". This issue arises because the learning objective can be minimized by manipulating the sum of importance weights, leading to degenerate solutions with poor generalization. To remedy this, the paper proposes replacing the standard IPS estimator with the self-normalized (SN) importance sampling estimator, a well-established technique from the statistics and Monte Carlo literature. This estimator normalizes the weighted losses by the sum of the importance weights. The authors incorporate this SN estimator into the Counterfactual Risk Minimization (CRM) framework, resulting in a new learning algorithm called Norm-POEM. An empirical evaluation on several multi-label classification datasets shows that Norm-POEM consistently outperforms its direct predecessor, POEM, which is based on the standard (unnormalized) IPS estimator.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Problem Identification:** The paper clearly articulates and demonstrates a significant practical problem with the naive use of IPS estimators in a learning context. The "Propensity Overfitting" example in Section 4 is insightful and provides strong motivation for the work. It highlights how learners with high capacity can exploit the estimator's structure to produce arbitrarily low (and meaningless) risk estimates on the training set.

2.  **Principled Solution:** The proposed solution is methodologically sound. The use of the self-normalized estimator is not an ad-hoc fix but rather a standard statistical tool that is well-suited to the problem. The authors correctly point out its desirable properties, such as equivariance to loss translation and its estimates being bounded within the range of the observed losses, which directly counter the identified pathologies of the standard IPS estimator.

3.  **Empirical Validation:** The experiments effectively demonstrate the superiority of Norm-POEM over the POEM baseline. The results in Table 1 show a consistent and often substantial improvement in generalization performance. Furthermore, Table 2 provides convincing evidence that this improvement is indeed due to the mitigation of propensity overfitting, as the sum of weights `Ŝ(ĥ)` for Norm-POEM remains close to its expectation of 1, unlike for POEM.

4.  **Clarity:** The paper is well-written, clearly structured, and easy to follow. The motivation, proposed method, and experimental results are presented logically.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Insufficient Baselines:** The primary weakness of this work lies in its experimental comparison. The paper almost exclusively compares Norm-POEM to POEM, which uses the basic and often high-variance IPS estimator. While this is a direct and necessary comparison, it is not sufficient. The field of off-policy evaluation has developed more sophisticated estimators to handle the variance of IPS. The most prominent among these is the doubly robust (DR) estimator [6], which is mentioned in the related work but conspicuously absent from the experiments. DR estimators are known to be highly effective and are a standard baseline in this area. Without a comparison to a learning algorithm based on a DR estimator, the paper's claims of significance are not fully substantiated. It remains unclear whether Norm-POEM offers an advantage over other established methods for robust counterfactual learning or if it simply improves upon a weak baseline.

2.  **Limited Novelty:** The core technical component of this paper, the self-normalized estimator, is a classic, decades-old technique from the importance sampling literature [12, 19, 22]. The contribution here is its application to the CRM learning framework and the coining of the "Propensity Overfitting" phenomenon. While this is a valid contribution, the paper could be more explicit about the fact that the estimator itself is not novel. The work is more of an effective application of an established statistical tool to solve a problem in a machine learning context rather than the development of a fundamentally new estimation method.

3.  **Superficial Theoretical Analysis:** The theoretical contributions are modest. Theorem 2 establishes the consistency of the self-normalized estimator, but this is a standard result that follows directly from the law of large numbers. The original CRM paper [1] provided a generalization error bound to motivate its objective function. A similar, non-trivial bound for the Norm-POEM objective would have significantly strengthened this paper's theoretical foundation. As it stands, the justification for the variance regularization term relies on analogy and the use of a standard delta-method approximation for the variance, rather than a rigorous learning-theoretic argument specific to the new estimator.

**Summary of Pros and Cons:**

**Pro:**
*   Identifies and clearly explains an important practical problem ("Propensity Overfitting").
*   Proposes a methodologically sound solution based on a classic, well-understood estimator.
*   Provides strong empirical evidence that the proposed method solves the identified problem and outperforms its direct predecessor.
*   The paper is well-written and clear.

**Con:**
*   The experimental evaluation lacks a crucial baseline comparison against doubly robust estimators, which are a standard and powerful alternative in this domain.
*   The technical novelty is limited, as the core method is an existing statistical tool.
*   The theoretical analysis is not deep and mostly restates known properties of the estimator.

**Recommendation:**

While the paper makes a solid contribution by identifying an important issue and demonstrating an effective solution, its claim to significance is hampered by an incomplete experimental evaluation. The failure to compare against established and powerful baselines like doubly robust methods is a critical omission for a paper submitted to a top-tier venue like NIPS. The work demonstrates an improvement over the specific POEM algorithm, but it does not adequately position itself within the broader context of robust off-policy learning. For these reasons, I lean towards rejection. The paper would be much stronger if it included a thorough comparison with a DR-based learner.
