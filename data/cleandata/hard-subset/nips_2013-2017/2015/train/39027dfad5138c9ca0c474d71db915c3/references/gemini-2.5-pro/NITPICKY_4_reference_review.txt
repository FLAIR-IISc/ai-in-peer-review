**Summary of the paper**

This paper addresses the problem of batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF). The authors identify a specific failure mode of learning algorithms based on Empirical Risk Minimization (ERM) with the standard inverse propensity score (IPS) estimator. They term this failure mode "Propensity Overfitting," where the model learns to exploit the propensity weights in the training sample to achieve an artificially low empirical risk, rather than learning to minimize the true loss. This can happen by assigning high probability to training examples with negative loss, or avoiding training examples with positive loss. The authors propose to replace the standard IPS estimator with the self-normalized (SN) importance sampling estimator, which is a ratio estimator. They argue that this estimator is equivariant to translations in the loss and is bounded by the range of the loss, thus mitigating Propensity Overfitting. They incorporate this SN estimator into the Counterfactual Risk Minimization (CRM) framework, leading to a new algorithm called Norm-POEM. Empirical results on several multi-label classification datasets show that Norm-POEM substantially outperforms its predecessor, POEM, which uses the standard IPS estimator.

**Strengths**

1.  **Problem Identification:** The paper clearly articulates a subtle yet significant problem in counterfactual learning. The concept of "Propensity Overfitting" is intuitive and the illustrative example in Section 4 is effective at demonstrating the pathologies of the standard IPS estimator when used for ERM.
2.  **Principled Solution:** The proposed solutionŌĆöusing the self-normalized estimatorŌĆöis not an ad-hoc fix but a well-established technique from the Monte Carlo literature. The paper does a good job of motivating its properties, such as equivariance and consistency (Theorem 2), and explaining why they directly address the identified problem.
3.  **Strong Empirical Results:** The experimental evaluation appears thorough and convincing. The reported improvements of Norm-POEM over the baseline POEM are substantial and consistent across different datasets and conditions. The analysis in Section 7 provides compelling evidence that the performance gain is indeed due to the mitigation of Propensity Overfitting, as shown by the analysis of S╠é(h╠é) and the robustness to loss translation.

**Weaknesses**

Despite the clear contribution, the paper suffers from a significant number of minor flaws, ranging from presentational sloppiness to a lack of technical depth, which collectively diminish its quality.

1.  **Presentation and Clarity:** The manuscript is riddled with typographical errors and formatting inconsistencies that suggest a lack of careful proofreading.
    *   The bibliography is particularly problematic. Reference [1] contains placeholder commas ("In ICML,, , ,"). Several references lack authors, venues, or years (e.g., [3], [4], [7]). Reference [19] has a typo ("distributions."). This level of carelessness in the bibliography is unacceptable for a top-tier conference submission.
    *   There are numerous typos in the main text: `╬┤ : X ├ŚY 7ŌåÆ R` should be `ŌåÆ` (Section 3); the piecewise function in Example 1 is malformed; a key sentence in Section 5 contains a typo `h(y|x)h0(y|x)` where it should be a ratio `h(y|x)/h0(y|x)`.
    *   The paper refers to "Table 1", "Table 2", "Table 3", and "Figure 1", none of which are present in the document. Presenting key results only in running text is poor form and hinders readability.
    *   The provided URL in Section 6 contains an incorrectly rendered tilde character (`Ōł╝`).

2.  **Originality and Relation to Prior Work:** While the *application* is novel, the core technical toolŌĆöthe self-normalized estimatorŌĆöis a classic method in importance sampling. The authors cite relevant work ([12], [19], [22]) but could be more explicit in framing their contribution as the successful application and analysis of this existing tool to solve a specific learning problem, rather than presenting it as a wholly new proposal. The originality lies in the connection to "Propensity Overfitting," not in the estimator itself.

3.  **Technical Depth and Justification:**
    *   **Theoretical Analysis:** The paper's main theoretical result is Theorem 2, which shows the consistency of the self-normalized estimator. This is a standard and expected result for any reasonable estimator and does not represent a deep contribution. A much more valuable result, which is conspicuously absent, would be a finite-sample generalization bound for the CRM principle with the self-normalized estimator, analogous to Theorem 1 for the standard estimator. Without such a bound, the theoretical justification for the Norm-POEM objective (i.e., minimizing the SN risk plus a term related to its variance) is purely heuristic.
    *   **Variance Estimation:** The paper uses a delta method approximation for the variance of the ratio estimator (Eq. 8). The authors then claim that weight clipping can guarantee the existence of this variance. This connection is asserted rather than proven. A more rigorous discussion on how clipping the terms in the numerator and denominator impacts the variance of the ratio would be necessary to make this claim sound.
    *   **Optimization:** The authors state they use L-BFGS for a "non-convex and non-smooth" objective. L-BFGS is a quasi-Newton method designed for *smooth* optimization. The objective becomes non-smooth from the `min` function used for weight clipping. While citing papers that use L-BFGS for non-smooth problems ([25], [26]) provides some support, a brief justification for why this is acceptable here (e.g., the non-smooth points are rarely encountered, or the effect is negligible) is needed. Simply stating it is "well supported" is insufficient.

4.  **Reproducibility:** The experimental setup is described as "identical to POEM [1]". While concise, this forces the reader to consult another paper for crucial details. Key parameters such as the hyperparameter search space for `M` and `╬╗`, convergence criteria for L-BFGS, and details of the dataset splits should be included in the appendix, at a minimum, to ensure the work is self-contained and reproducible.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   The paper identifies and provides a compelling name for an important and previously underappreciated problem in counterfactual learning.
*   The proposed solution is simple, effective, and principled.
*   The empirical results are strong and clearly demonstrate the advantages of the proposed method over a recent state-of-the-art baseline.

**Arguments for Rejection:**
*   The paper exhibits a notable lack of polish, with numerous typos and significant formatting errors, especially in the bibliography.
*   The theoretical contribution is limited. The lack of a finite-sample generalization bound for the proposed estimator is a major gap.
*   Key methodological choices (e.g., the use of L-BFGS for a non-smooth problem) are not sufficiently justified.
*   The paper is not self-contained, omitting key experimental details and failing to include its own figures and tables.

**Decision**

While the core idea of this paper is valuable and the empirical results are promising, the execution is flawed. The combination of superficial theoretical analysis, missing methodological justifications, and a striking number of presentational errors leaves a poor impression. The work feels preliminary and rushed. A paper at NIPS should not only present a good idea but also execute it with technical rigor and scholarly care. This submission falls short of that standard. I recommend **rejection**, but I would strongly encourage the authors to revise the paper to address the issues raised, as the underlying work has the potential to be a strong contribution to the field.
