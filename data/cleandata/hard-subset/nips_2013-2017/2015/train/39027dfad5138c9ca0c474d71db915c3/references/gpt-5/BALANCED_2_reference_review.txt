Summary and relation to prior work
This paper studies batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF) and argues that the conventional inverse propensity score (IPS) counterfactual risk estimator used within counterfactual risk minimization (CRM) can induce a new failure mode the authors call Propensity Overfitting. Intuitively, because the IPS empirical risk depends on the sum of importance weights, hypotheses can minimize the risk by manipulating the weight mass rather than modeling the loss, which leads to severe finite-sample pathologies (non-equivariance under translation, estimates outside the loss range, and degenerate ERM solutions). As a remedy, the paper proposes a multiplicative control variate: use the sum of weights as a self-normalizing denominator (the self-normalized/weighted importance sampling estimator, WIS/SNIPS). The resulting estimator is bounded, equivariant to additive loss shifts, and strongly consistent. Building on this, the authors propose Norm-POEM, which instantiates CRM using the self-normalized estimator and an approximate variance term for the ratio estimator. Experiments on multilabel classification datasets created via a “supervised→bandit” protocol show improved generalization over POEM; diagnostic statistics (sum of weights) suggest reduced Propensity Overfitting. The paper relates to importance weighting under covariate shift, off-policy evaluation in RL, and variance reduction via clipping and control variates; it cites doubly robust (DR) evaluation and weighted importance sampling (e.g., Kong 1992; Hesterberg 1995).

Strengths
- Clear identification and illustration (via a crisp synthetic example) of a practically important pathology when using IPS for ERM with finite samples.
- The proposed fix—self-normalized IPS—is principled, simple, and aligns with long-standing Monte Carlo/IS practice; its boundedness and equivariance are highly desirable in learning objectives.
- Solid diagnostics (Ŝ(h) ≈ 1) corroborate the claimed mechanism of improvement; the translation-invariance experiments are convincing.
- Empirically, Norm-POEM consistently outperforms POEM on multiple datasets across data sizes, and appears to converge faster in practice.
- The work is squarely within scope for NeurIPS (off-policy learning, causal inference, and importance weighting).

Weaknesses
Quality
- Theory: The core new theoretical result is strong consistency of the self-normalized estimator (well known in IS). The CRM generalization bound (Theorem 1) is inherited from prior work for capped IPS; there is no analogous generalization bound for the self-normalized objective actually optimized. The proposed variance term is an approximation (delta/normal) without guarantees in finite samples. This weakens the theoretical justification of Norm-POEM relative to POEM.
- Baselines: The experimental comparison omits standard and competitive off-policy estimators designed to mitigate weight variance and bias, notably doubly robust (DR), clipped DR, and weighted DR/WIS-DR (and “MAGIC”). Given that SNIPS is a known technique, comparisons to DR variants are essential to establish significance.
- Realism of setting: The logging policy is fully known and has full support; there is no study of estimated propensities, support violations, or misspecification—central issues in practice. The evaluation uses simulated bandit feedback from supervised data only.
- Bias/variance trade-off: While boundedness and translation equivariance are good, self-normalization introduces bias. There is no quantitative analysis of this bias (e.g., finite-sample bounds or conditions under which SNIPS dominates IPS/DR), nor an exploration of regimes where loss variance dominates weight variance (where SNIPS can underperform).

Clarity
- Generally well written and organized; the Propensity Overfitting example is effective.
- Some notational/typo issues (e.g., “δ(·, ·) ∈ [5,4]” appears to mean a bounded interval; please correct). Several references are incomplete.
- Training details are light: initialization, stopping criteria, and hyperparameter search ranges are not fully specified; the code link is helpful but not sufficient for reproducibility at review time.

Originality
- The use of self-normalized importance sampling is not new; the contribution is in its explicit diagnosis of “Propensity Overfitting” and its integration into the CRM learning objective for BLBF. This is a modest, but useful, conceptual and algorithmic advance.

Significance
- The problem is important; the proposed fix is simple and likely to be adopted. However, the lack of comparisons to DR/WDR and the absence of theory for the actual learning objective limit the strength of the claim that Norm-POEM advances the state of the art.

Questions and suggestions for the authors
- Provide a generalization bound tailored to the self-normalized objective (e.g., concentration for ratio estimators with bounded/clipped weights, possibly via delta method plus empirical-Bernstein style control, or self-normalized inequalities).
- Add DR, clipped-DR, and weighted-DR baselines. If computing a model for DR is costly in multilabel settings, discuss and approximate; otherwise, include results.
- Study estimated propensities (learned logging policies) and partial support violations; include diagnostics and recovery strategies (e.g., overlap checks, pessimism/robustness).
- Quantify finite-sample bias of SNIPS and characterize regimes (loss variance vs weight variance) where normalization helps/hurts; include ablations varying clipping M and λ, and scale/shift of losses.
- Discuss sensitivity to misspecified logging propensities; can normalization exacerbate bias when p is misestimated?
- Clarify optimization details (initialization, L-BFGS settings) and report wall-clock times and iterations.
- Fix typos and complete citations.

Pros
- Identifies a real and underappreciated IPS-for-learning pathology; offers a principled, practical fix.
- Bounded, equivariant objective improves stability and training behavior.
- Consistent empirical gains over POEM; compelling diagnostics.

Cons
- Missing key baselines (DR/WDR); limited experimental scope (simulated logs only).
- No generalization theory for the proposed objective; variance estimate is heuristic.
- No analysis of SNIPS bias or failure modes; assumptions (full support, known propensities) are strong.

Recommendation: Weak Reject
The idea is valuable and likely impactful in practice, but the paper lacks comparisons to standard DR variants and does not provide theoretical support for the self-normalized CRM objective it optimizes. With stronger empirical baselines (including DR/WDR) and at least a partial generalization analysis for the ratio-based objective, I would be inclined toward acceptance.
