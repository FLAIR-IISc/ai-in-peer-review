This paper tackles a critical issue in learning from logged data – propensity overfitting – and proposes a clever solution using self-normalized estimation. The core idea of recognizing that simply minimizing empirical risk in this setting can lead to degenerate solutions, driven not by accurate modeling of the reward but by exploiting biases in the logging policy, is insightful. The connection to importance sampling and the identification of this novel overfitting phenomenon are significant contributions. While the presentation is dense and the empirical validation, while comprehensive, doesn’t dramatically *outperform* existing methods in terms of absolute numbers, the theoretical grounding and novelty of the approach are strong enough to warrant acceptance.

**Summary:** The authors identify a new form of overfitting (propensity overfitting) that arises when applying Empirical Risk Minimization to counterfactual risk estimation in batch learning from logged bandit feedback. They demonstrate that standard importance sampling-based estimators are susceptible to this issue and propose a self-normalized estimator to mitigate it, drawing inspiration from financial mathematics. The theoretical analysis provides justification for the improved estimator, and experiments on several datasets confirm its robustness and generalization performance.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The identification of propensity overfitting is a genuinely novel contribution. This isn't a minor tweak to existing methods; it's a fundamental issue in this learning paradigm that hasn’t been previously recognized.
*   **Theoretical Soundness:** The paper provides a clear and well-motivated theoretical analysis. The connection to importance sampling and the justification for the self-normalized estimator are convincing. The theoretical argument for avoiding degeneracies is particularly strong.
*   **Clear Problem Definition:** The problem of learning from logged bandit feedback is clearly defined, and the motivation for addressing the limitations of existing approaches is well-articulated.
*   **Practical Relevance:** The problem addressed is highly relevant to real-world applications involving large-scale logged data, such as recommendation systems and advertising platforms.
*   **Connection to Existing Literature:** The authors effectively connect their work to relevant literature in causal inference, off-policy evaluation, and importance sampling.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Presentation:** The paper is dense and can be challenging to follow. The mathematical notation is heavy, and the explanation of the self-normalized estimator could be more intuitive. A more gradual introduction of concepts would improve readability.
*   **Empirical Results – Incremental Improvement:** While the empirical results demonstrate the benefits of the proposed approach, the performance gains over existing methods (like POEM) are not always dramatic. The improvements are often consistent but not overwhelmingly large.
*   **Computational Complexity:** The discussion of computational complexity feels somewhat glossed over. While they mention the potential for faster convergence, a more detailed analysis of the computational trade-offs would be valuable.
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The sensitivity of the method to hyperparameters (M and lambda) isn’t thoroughly explored.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

*   The identified problem of propensity overfitting is a fundamental issue in batch counterfactual learning.
*   The proposed solution, the self-normalized estimator, is theoretically well-motivated and demonstrably effective.
*   The paper contributes a new perspective on the interplay between importance sampling, risk estimation, and generalization in offline learning settings.
*   The insights provided are likely to influence future research in this area.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The empirical improvements, while consistent, are not always substantial.
*   The paper’s density and technicality may limit its accessibility to a broader audience.
*   The computational implications of the proposed approach require further investigation.



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite some minor flaws in presentation and the incremental nature of the empirical improvements, this paper presents a significant and novel contribution to the field of learning from logged bandit feedback. The identification of propensity overfitting and the development of the self-normalized estimator are both important advancements. I believe that the theoretical insights and potential impact of this work outweigh its weaknesses, and I recommend **acceptance**. The work is a welcome addition to NIPS, offering a fresh perspective on a challenging problem and paving the way for future research.
