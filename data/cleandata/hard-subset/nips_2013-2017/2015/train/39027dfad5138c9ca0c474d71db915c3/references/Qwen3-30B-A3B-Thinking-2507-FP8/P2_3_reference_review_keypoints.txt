- Paper identifies a novel problem in batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF) called "Propensity Overfitting," where importance weights cause severely biased risk estimates.
- Shows conventional counterfactual risk estimators can produce arbitrarily low, misleading risk estimates (Example 1).
- Proposes a Self-Normalized Risk Estimator using multiplicative control variates to address propensity-driven bias.
- Derives a new algorithm, Norm-POEM, based on the self-normalized estimator.
- Provides rigorous theoretical analysis, including consistency of the self-normalized estimator (Theorem 2) and explanations of equivariance/boundedness fixes.
- Empirical evaluation is thorough: standard benchmarks, multiple runs, proper hyperparameter tuning, and comparisons to state-of-the-art POEM.
- Experiments show Norm-POEM consistently outperforms POEM across datasets and training sizes, with evidence that gains stem from controlling Propensity Overfitting (Table 2).
- Paper clearly distinguishes Propensity Overfitting from Loss Overfitting and situates contributions relative to prior work in BLBF, causal inference, and off-policy evaluation.
- Writing and organization are excellent; problem statement, technical development, and experiments are clearly presented and reproducible.
- Originality: novel application of self-normalized estimators to counterfactual learning and first characterization of Propensity Overfitting.
- Significance: high practical impact for recommender systems and ad platforms; Norm-POEM is compatible with existing BLBF frameworks.
- Strengths: clear problem identification, rigorous theory, strong empirical validation, practical algorithmic improvement, excellent presentation.
- Weaknesses: computational cost not fully analyzed for very large datasets; theory limited to specific stochastic contextual bandit setting.
- Additional minor concern: paper could better explain why self-normalization reduces need for clipping and the role of variance regularization (Table 3).
- Recommendation: strong accept for NeurIPS — technically sound, novel, well-validated, and practically significant.
