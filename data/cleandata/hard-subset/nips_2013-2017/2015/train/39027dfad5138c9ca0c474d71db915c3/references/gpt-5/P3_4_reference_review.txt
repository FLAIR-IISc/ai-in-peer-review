Summary
The paper addresses batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF) and identifies a failure mode of inverse propensity scoring (IPS) when used within an ERM/CRM objective: propensity overfitting. The authors argue that IPS-based empirical risk can be driven to degenerate values by manipulating the sum of importance weights, leading to non-equivariance and unbounded estimates that interact poorly with finite-sample ERM. They propose to use a multiplicative control variate yielding the self-normalized importance sampling (SNIS) estimator as the empirical risk: a ratio estimator that is bounded and equivariant to additive translations of the loss. They prove strong consistency of SNIS and present Norm-POEM, which plugs SNIS and an approximate variance estimate into the CRM principle. Experiments on multi-label classification (Supervised→Bandit) show improved generalization, robustness to loss translation, and better behavior of the weight-sum diagnostic compared to POEM.

Strengths
- Clear diagnosis of a real and under-discussed failure mode (propensity overfitting) when doing ERM with IPS; the synthetic example is compelling and aligns with practitioners’ experiences with heavy-tailed IPS.
- Adoption of self-normalized IS is well-motivated: it restores boundedness and translation equivariance and empirically stabilizes learning.
- Norm-POEM is a practical algorithm with publicly available code; optimization and variance-regularization choices are well explained.
- Experiments systematically test generalization, the Ŝ(h) diagnostic, effect of loss translation, data size, and runtime; the method consistently improves over POEM.

Weaknesses
- Theoretical development is limited: no finite-sample generalization bound is provided for the SNIS-based objective. The CRM bound cited (Theorem 1) applies to clipped IPS, not to the ratio estimator central to this paper.
- Comparisons to stronger baselines are missing. Doubly robust (DR) and weighted/normalized DR estimators are standard for off-policy evaluation and learning, often outperforming IPS/SNIS by reducing bias-variance tradeoffs; they are not evaluated here.
- The diagnostic E[Ŝ(h)]=1 and some claims rely on known/accurate logging propensities and full support. The paper does not analyze robustness to misspecified or estimated propensities, a common practical reality.
- The experimental setting, while standard, is restricted to simulated logs on multi-label classification with CRF-like models. No ranking/recommender or real logged datasets are included, limiting external validity.
- The variance estimator for SNIS is approximate; the implications for optimization (bias in gradients, convergence guarantees) are not analyzed.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The identification of anomalies (non-boundedness, non-equivariance) of IPS and their implications for ERM is correct and important. Using SNIS to fix these properties is standard in IS and off-policy evaluation. The strong law consistency proof is straightforward. However, the main theoretical gap is the lack of a high-probability generalization bound for the SNIS-based empirical risk and its variance-regularized objective. The paper asserts that SNIS “provably avoids degeneracies,” but the formal result provided stops at boundedness/equivariance/consistency rather than a finite-sample control tailored to learning. Given CRM’s original contribution was a bound-driven objective, this is a notable omission.
- Empirical support: Results convincingly show improved stability and performance relative to POEM and validate the Ŝ diagnostic and translation invariance. Yet, conclusions about significance would be stronger with DR/WDR baselines and sensitivity to propensity errors.

Clarity
- The paper is well written and organized. The propensity overfitting example is instructive. The connection to control variates and the intuition behind SNIS are clear. Some parts could be strengthened:
  - Explicitly separate which guarantees transfer from IPS-CRM to SNIS-CRM and which do not; avoid suggesting Theorem 1 implies anything for SNIS.
  - Discuss the bias of SNIS more explicitly and its dependence on weight variance and clipping M.
  - Provide gradient expressions or an appendix for the SNIS+variance objective to aid reproducibility.

Originality
- SNIS and multiplicative control variates are classical; applying SNIS to counterfactual learning is known in off-policy evaluation. The novelty lies in articulating “propensity overfitting” as a learning pathology of IPS-ERM/CRM, arguing for self-normalization within the CRM framework, and demonstrating that this design choice materially improves learning. This is an incremental but meaningful contribution.

Significance
- BLBF and off-policy learning from logs are central problems at NeurIPS. Replacing IPS with SNIS in learning objectives is a simple, impactful change that practitioners can adopt. However, the lack of DR/WDR comparisons and a finite-sample theory for SNIS-CRM limits the breadth of the claim. With those additions, the contribution could be more influential.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide a finite-sample generalization analysis for SNIS-based empirical risk (e.g., concentration for self-normalized IS or PAC-Bayesian/empirical Bernstein style bounds adapted to ratios), possibly under clipping.
- Include comparisons to DR and weighted/normalized DR estimators for learning (and to stabilized IPS), with and without clipping.
- Study robustness to misspecified/estimated propensities and partial support, as E[Ŝ(h)]=1 no longer holds; analyze how SNIS behaves versus IPS/DR under misspecification.
- Add experiments on ranking/recommendation or a real logged dataset, and include ablations on M, λ, and the effect of loss translation at different scales.
- Clarify optimization: discuss mini-batch approximations for the ratio objective, convergence behavior, and the impact of the approximate variance estimator on gradients.

Recommendation: Weak accept
- The paper raises an important issue (propensity overfitting), offers a principled and practically effective fix (self-normalization), and demonstrates clear empirical gains over a strong baseline. Strengthening theory for the SNIS-CRM objective and adding DR-based baselines would elevate the contribution further. Confidence: high.
