### Summary and Contributions

This paper addresses the problem of batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF), a crucial setting for improving interactive systems like search and recommendation. The standard approach, Counterfactual Risk Minimization (CRM), relies on an Inverse Propensity Score (IPS) estimator to evaluate new policies using historical data. The authors identify a critical and previously overlooked failure mode of this approach, which they term "Propensity Overfitting". They show that with a sufficiently powerful hypothesis space, a learning algorithm can minimize the estimated risk not by learning to predict low-loss actions, but by pathologically manipulating the importance weights—either by assigning high probability to actions already in the log (for negative losses) or by avoiding them entirely (for positive losses). This leads to severely biased risk estimates and poor generalization.

The core contribution of the paper is to propose a simple and elegant solution: replacing the standard IPS estimator with the Self-Normalized (SN) importance sampling estimator. This estimator, framed as using a multiplicative control variate, is a ratio of the IPS-estimated total loss to the sum of the importance weights. The authors show that the SN estimator resolves the key issues of the IPS estimator: it is equivariant to shifts in the loss function and its estimates are guaranteed to lie within the range of the observed losses, thus preventing the extreme values characteristic of propensity overfitting. They integrate this new estimator into the CRM framework to create a new algorithm, Norm-POEM. Through a series of well-designed experiments on multi-label classification tasks, they demonstrate that Norm-POEM significantly outperforms its predecessor, is robust to transformations of the loss function, and effectively controls the propensity overfitting problem it was designed to solve.

### Strengths

1.  **Novel and Important Problem Identification:** The clear articulation of "Propensity Overfitting" is a significant contribution in itself. It provides a new lens through which to understand the pathologies of off-policy learning. The example in Section 4 is intuitive and powerfully illustrates how standard Empirical Risk Minimization with an IPS estimator can fail in ways not seen in supervised learning. This insight is valuable for both researchers and practitioners working with logged bandit data.

2.  **Principled and Effective Solution:** The proposed solution of using the self-normalized estimator is well-motivated and elegant. Rather than an ad-hoc fix, it leverages a classic tool from the Monte Carlo literature that directly addresses the identified weaknesses of the IPS estimator (lack of shift-equivariance, unbounded estimates). The theoretical properties highlighted—consistency, equivariance, and boundedness—are precisely what is needed to combat propensity overfitting.

3.  **Strong and Thorough Empirical Validation:** The experimental section is a model of clarity and rigor. The authors use a well-established "supervised-to-bandit" conversion protocol, which allows for controlled evaluation. The results are compelling: Norm-POEM consistently and substantially outperforms the baseline across multiple datasets and training set sizes. Crucially, the authors include diagnostic experiments that directly support their claims, showing that Norm-POEM successfully keeps the sum of importance weights centered around its expectation (Table 2, left) and is robust to loss shifting, a key failure mode for the baseline (Table 2, right).

4.  **Clarity of Presentation:** The paper is exceptionally well-written, well-organized, and easy to follow. The motivation is established clearly, the technical details are presented concisely, and the connection between the theory and the experiments is explicit.

### Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement

1.  **Lack of a Formal Learning Bound for the Proposed Objective:** The paper motivates its learning algorithm, Norm-POEM, using the CRM principle from [1], which is based on a finite-sample generalization bound (Theorem 1). However, that bound applies to the standard (capped) IPS estimator. The self-normalized estimator is a ratio estimator and is biased in the finite-sample regime. It is not clear whether a similar high-probability generalization bound can be derived for the Norm-POEM objective. While the strong empirical results provide confidence in the approach, the paper would be strengthened by a discussion of the theoretical challenges in deriving a formal learning-theoretic justification for the proposed objective. Even without a full proof, acknowledging this gap and discussing the potential difficulties would be valuable.

2.  **Comparison to Doubly Robust Methods:** The paper focuses on improving upon IPS-style estimators. Another major class of estimators for off-policy evaluation are Doubly Robust (DR) estimators [6], which also use control variates (albeit additive ones) to reduce variance. A discussion of how DR estimators might behave with respect to propensity overfitting would be insightful. Furthermore, including a DR-based learning algorithm as a baseline in the experiments would provide a more complete picture of where Norm-POEM stands in the broader landscape of counterfactual learning methods.

3.  **Computational Considerations:** The authors note that the variance term for the self-normalized estimator does not readily admit stochastic optimization, requiring a batch optimizer like L-BFGS. While they report that this converges quickly in their experiments, this could be a practical limitation on datasets that are too large to fit in memory. It would be helpful to explicitly acknowledge this potential scalability issue as a direction for future work, perhaps speculating on ways the objective could be modified or approximated to allow for stochastic updates.

### Decision

This is an excellent paper that makes a clear and significant contribution. It identifies a fundamental problem in counterfactual learning, proposes a principled and effective solution, and backs it up with strong and convincing empirical evidence. The weaknesses are minor and relate more to avenues for future work than to flaws in the current contribution. The paper is well-written, technically sound, and addresses a problem of high importance to the NeurIPS community. I strongly recommend acceptance.
