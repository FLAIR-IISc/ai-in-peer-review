

# Peer Review: "The Self-Normalized Estimator for Counterfactual Learning"

## Summary
This paper identifies a previously unacknowledged problem—Propensity Overfitting—in conventional counterfactual risk estimators used for batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF). The authors demonstrate that standard importance sampling estimators can produce arbitrarily biased risk estimates, even when the loss function is bounded, due to the capacity of the hypothesis space to overfit the propensity weights. They propose a self-normalized risk estimator that uses multiplicative control variates to address this issue. This estimator is theoretically sound (consistent, equivariant, and bounded) and leads to a new learning algorithm called Norm-POEM. The authors provide theoretical guarantees and extensive empirical evidence across multiple datasets showing that Norm-POEM consistently outperforms existing methods like POEM. The work builds on the CRM principle established in prior BLBF work (e.g., POEM [1]) but addresses a critical gap in the theoretical understanding of risk estimation.

## Strengths

1. **Significant Problem Identification**: The paper identifies a fundamental flaw in the standard counterfactual risk estimation approach that has been overlooked in the literature. The Propensity Overfitting example (Example 1) is particularly compelling and clearly demonstrates why conventional estimators can fail catastrophically even with bounded loss functions.

2. **Theoretical Soundness**: The theoretical contributions are strong. The paper provides a clear proof (Theorem 2) that the self-normalized estimator is consistent and resolves the key anomalies of the conventional estimator. The distinction between Propensity Overfitting and Loss Overfitting is conceptually important and well-motivated.

3. **Empirical Rigor**: The experimental evaluation is thorough and well-designed. The authors systematically test Norm-POEM across multiple datasets, verify that it avoids Propensity Overfitting (Table 2), demonstrate the necessity of variance regularization (Table 3), and show computational advantages (faster convergence than POEM). The use of the Supervised 7→ Bandit methodology [1] ensures a fair comparison.

4. **Clear Connection to Prior Work**: The paper properly situates its contributions within the BLBF literature, acknowledging prior work on counterfactual estimation (e.g., [5, 6, 11]) while clearly identifying the gap it addresses. The relationship to control variates in importance sampling [19] is appropriately discussed.

5. **Practical Impact**: The Norm-POEM algorithm is immediately applicable to real-world systems that rely on logged bandit feedback (e.g., ad placement, recommender systems), and the paper demonstrates clear improvements in generalization performance.

## Weaknesses

1. **Computational Complexity Analysis**: While the paper mentions that Norm-POEM converges faster than POEM, it lacks a detailed computational complexity analysis. The authors note that the variance estimate (Equation 8) doesn't admit stochastic optimization, but don't fully explore the computational trade-offs or how this might scale to very large datasets.

2. **Sensitivity to Hyperparameters**: The paper mentions that hyperparameters M and λ can be calibrated via counterfactual evaluation, but doesn't provide analysis of how sensitive the method is to these choices. It would be valuable to understand whether the improved performance is robust across a range of hyperparameter values.

3. **Comparison to Other Variance Reduction Techniques**: While the paper mentions control variates in [17, 18, 19], it doesn't fully contrast the multiplicative control variates used in their approach with other variance reduction techniques like additive control variates or doubly robust estimators [6].

4. **Real-World Data Validation**: The experiments use a standard supervised-to-bandit conversion method [1], but the paper doesn't validate the approach on truly real-world bandit feedback data, which might have different characteristics than the simulated data.

5. **Theoretical Limits of the Approach**: The paper demonstrates the advantages of the self-normalized estimator, but doesn't explore potential limitations or scenarios where it might not provide improvement (e.g., when the propensity weights are already well-behaved).

## Quality

The paper is technically sound, with clear theoretical contributions and a thorough empirical evaluation. The claims are well-supported by both theory and experiments. The authors carefully address the strengths and weaknesses of their approach, particularly in Table 2 where they show how POEM is sensitive to loss translations while Norm-POEM is not. The paper is complete and not merely a position paper.

## Clarity

The paper is well-written and well-organized. The problem statement is clear from the beginning, and the distinction between Propensity Overfitting and Loss Overfitting is well-motivated. The theoretical contributions are presented logically, and the experimental results are clearly presented with appropriate visualizations. The connection to prior work is well-explained, and the paper provides sufficient detail for reproducibility.

## Originality

This work makes a significant original contribution by identifying and solving a previously unrecognized problem in counterfactual learning. While the self-normalized estimator has been used in other contexts (e.g., [12, 19]), the application to BLBF and the theoretical characterization of Propensity Overfitting is novel. The paper clearly differentiates itself from prior work by showing that the conventional estimator has fundamental anomalies that can lead to degenerate behavior.

## Significance

The results are highly significant for the field of counterfactual learning. The problem of Propensity Overfitting is fundamental to the reliability of counterfactual learning methods, and the proposed solution directly addresses this issue. The empirical improvements demonstrated across multiple datasets suggest that Norm-POEM will have practical impact in real-world systems that rely on logged bandit feedback. The paper advances the state of the art in BLBF in a demonstrable way.

## Recommendation

I recommend acceptance of this paper. The work addresses a fundamental problem in a well-studied area, provides a theoretically sound solution, and demonstrates clear empirical improvements. The paper is of high quality, well-written, and makes a significant contribution to the field of counterfactual learning. While there are some areas for further exploration (computational complexity, real-world validation), these do not detract from the paper's core contributions.

## Arguments for Acceptance
- Identifies and solves a critical, previously overlooked problem in BLBF
- Provides strong theoretical guarantees for the proposed estimator
- Demonstrates consistent empirical improvements across multiple datasets
- Well-written, clear organization, and thorough evaluation
- Direct practical impact on real-world systems using logged bandit feedback

## Arguments Against Acceptance (Minor)
- Limited analysis of computational complexity
- No validation on truly real-world bandit data
- Could provide more detailed comparison with other variance reduction techniques

This paper represents a solid, significant contribution to the field of counterfactual learning. It should be accepted for presentation at NIPS.
