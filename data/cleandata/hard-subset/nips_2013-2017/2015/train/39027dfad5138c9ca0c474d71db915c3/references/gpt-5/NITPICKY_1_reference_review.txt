Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF) and argues that the standard inverse propensity scoring (IPS) risk estimator used within ERM/CRM can “propensity overfit,” yielding degenerate minimizers due to instability of the importance weights. The authors propose using a multiplicative control variate: a self-normalized importance sampling (SNIS) estimator for the counterfactual risk. They show this estimator is bounded and equivariant under additive translations of the loss, provide strong consistency (via SLLN), and integrate it into a CRM-style training objective (“Norm-POEM”) with an approximate variance estimate. Empirically, on multilabel classification datasets under a supervised→bandit protocol, Norm-POEM is reported to outperform POEM and be more robust to loss translations and weight sum deviations Ŝ(h).

Use of SNIS/weighted importance sampling (WIS) is classical in Monte Carlo and off-policy evaluation (e.g., Hesterberg 1995; Kong 1992; broad RL literature including Precup et al., Sutton & Singh; WDR/MAGIC variants). The paper’s position is: SNIS specifically addresses “propensity overfitting” within the CRM framework and improves practical learning.

Strengths
- Clear identification and illustration (Example 1) of a failure mode of IPS-ERM due to weight instability; “propensity overfitting” is a helpful diagnostic framing via Ŝ(h).
- Adoption of SNIS yields bounded, translation-equivariant risk estimates; the consistency argument is correct under stated assumptions.
- Practical algorithm and empirical evidence that normalization can materially improve generalization under the BLBF protocol; robustness to loss translations is convincingly motivated.
- The variance surrogate for SNIS is standard and implementable; optimization with L-BFGS is pragmatic.

Weaknesses and detailed concerns
Technical/theoretical
- Novelty: Using SNIS/weighted IPS to stabilize off-policy evaluation/learning is well known in Monte Carlo and RL. The paper cites Hesterberg/Kong but omits much of the RL lineage where normalized/weighted IPS is standard (e.g., Precup, Sutton & Singh 2000; per-decision and weighted IS; more recent WDR/MAGIC). The conceptual contribution (naming “propensity overfitting” and plugging SNIS into CRM) feels incremental.
- CRM bound gap: The CRM principle in Eq. (3) is justified by Theorem 1 for capped IPS R̂M. For Norm-POEM, the estimator and the variance proxy (Eq. 8) are different; there is no generalization bound analogous to Theorem 1. The method therefore lacks the theoretical support that motivates CRM. Claims that the new estimator “provably avoids these degeneracies” overreach; boundedness/equivariance address some anomalies but no learning-theoretic guarantee is provided for the ERM minimizer under SNIS-CRM.
- Assumptions and edge cases: Theorem 2 requires full support of h0 and implicitly that the denominator average converges to 1. However:
  - If H includes deterministic policies with zero probability on observed yi, the SN estimator can be undefined (denominator 0). Later the CRF class ensures positivity, but this restriction should be explicit.
  - With clipping M for variance control, the SN estimator is then based on clipped weights; its expectation changes and the consistency (Theorem 2) no longer holds for fixed M. The paper does not clarify bias/consistency trade-offs for SNIS with clipping nor whether the variance formula (Eq. 8) remains appropriate under clipping.
- Variance estimate: Eq. (8) is an asymptotic delta-method/normal approximation. The CRM objective now regularizes using this proxy, but no justification (e.g., a Bernstein-style bound for ratio estimators) is provided. This undercuts the theoretical story.
- “Resolves all the problems”: SNIS bounds the ERM-minimizer’s estimated risk and ensures equivariance, but SNIS can still overfit by concentrating normalized weights on a few low-loss samples (limited only by the minimum observed loss). The claim that it “resolves all the problems” of IPS is too strong.

Empirical
- Baselines: No comparisons to strong alternatives common in off-policy learning/evaluation:
  - Doubly robust (DR) and weighted DR/WDR (Li et al.; Dudík et al.; Thomas & Brunskill variants).
  - Clipped IPS vs. stabilized/normalized weights from causal inference (stabilized IPW).
  - Other variance-reduction schemes (e.g., control variates additive DR, shrinkage, MAGIC).
Limiting to POEM makes the case for significance weaker.
- Statistical reporting: The text mentions “averaged over 10 runs,” but no standard deviations/confidence intervals are provided; significance is unclear. The figures/tables referenced (Table 1–3, Figure 1) are not present in the submission excerpt; please ensure they are included and legible in the camera-ready format.
- Protocol limits: Supervised→bandit with a stochastic CRF logger on 5% labels is convenient but may not reflect real logs where support violations and severe propensities occur. Consider stress tests with heavier-tailed propensities, deterministic loggers, or real logged datasets if available.
- Hyperparameters: Values/ranges for M and λ, selection procedure, and sensitivity analyses are not detailed. Given that clipping and λ interact with SNIS bias/variance, this analysis is important.
- Runtime: Claims of faster convergence are anecdotal; please provide iteration counts, wall-clock times, and gradients/line-search stats across datasets.

Clarity/presentation
- Notational inconsistencies and small errors:
  - Section 4: “Even if δ(·, ·) ∈ [5,4]” is nonsensical; likely a typo for a bounded interval.
  - Theorem 1 assumes δ ∈ [−1, 0], but Example 1 uses {−2, −1}; discuss rescaling explicitly.
  - Eq. (5) mixes sums/integrals and indexes awkwardly; clarify measure and support (discrete Y).
  - Several places use h(y|x)/h0(y|x) with missing parentheses or spacing; define Ŝ(h) carefully.
- References are incomplete/inconsistent: many entries lack years/venues/pages; [19] entry is malformed; several ICML/NIPS entries show “, , ,”. Important RL citations on weighted IS/DR are missing.
- The code URL points to POEM rather than a Norm-POEM repo; ensure an anonymized, working link and sufficient details for reproduction.
- Formatting: Typos (“i i.d.∼”), inconsistent math fonts (V ar), and equation punctuation should be cleaned up.

Questions for the authors
1) Can you provide a CRM-style generalization bound for the SNIS objective (or at least a high-probability guarantee that justifies the variance regularizer in Eq. (8))? If not, can you temper claims and position Norm-POEM as heuristic but robust?
2) How does Norm-POEM compare to DR and WDR baselines on your datasets? Does normalization still dominate?
3) What is the impact of clipping M in the SNIS objective on bias/consistency and on the empirical performance? Please provide ablations over M and λ.
4) Can you guarantee denominator positivity for all h ∈ H during training? If ensured by CRF parametrization, state this clearly; if not, how do you handle zero denominators?
5) Please include error bars and full tables/figures, and clarify the optimization details (iterations, tolerances).

Pros
- Clear identification of a real failure mode; simple, principled fix with strong intuition.
- Boundedness and equivariance are desirable properties for learning with IPS.
- Empirical gains over POEM across several datasets.

Cons
- Limited novelty relative to established weighted IS/WIS in off-policy evaluation.
- Missing theoretical guarantees for the SNIS-based CRM objective.
- Incomplete experimental comparisons; limited statistical reporting.
- Numerous presentation and reference issues.

Recommendation: Weak reject
While the practical message (normalize the weights) is sensible and the empirical results are promising, the contribution feels incremental, theoretical support for the new objective is lacking, and the paper has many presentation and citation issues. Strengthening theory (or clearly positioning as a practical heuristic), adding DR/WDR baselines with rigorous statistical reporting, clarifying assumptions/edge cases, and fixing the writing/refs would move this to acceptability.
