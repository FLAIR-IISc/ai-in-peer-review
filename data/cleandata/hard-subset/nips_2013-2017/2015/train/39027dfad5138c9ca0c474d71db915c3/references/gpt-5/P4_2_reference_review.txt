Summary and relation to prior work
The paper addresses batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF) by identifying a failure mode of the standard inverse propensity score (IPS) risk estimator when used within empirical risk minimization (ERM). The authors term this failure mode Propensity Overfitting: with finite data, IPS-based empirical risk can be driven arbitrarily low (or high) by manipulating importance weights, leading to degenerate ERM solutions. They propose a self-normalized importance sampling (SNIS) risk estimator (a multiplicative control variate) to mitigate this issue, yielding an estimator that is bounded by the loss range and equivariant to loss translations. They prove strong consistency of the self-normalized estimator and integrate it into the Counterfactual Risk Minimization (CRM) framework, resulting in a method dubbed Norm-POEM (analogous to POEM but replacing IPS with SNIS). They derive an approximate variance estimator for SNIS (via Normal/delta approximations) for use as a CRM regularizer and use L-BFGS for optimization. Experiments on multi-label datasets (Scene, Yeast, TMC, LYRL) using the supervised-to-bandit methodology show improved generalization over POEM, better calibration of the propensity-weight sum Ŝ(h) near its expected value 1, robustness to loss translation, and competitive runtime.

This contribution sits at the intersection of off-policy evaluation/learning and importance sampling. The identified pathology relates to known issues of high-variance IPS and weight instability. The proposed solution builds on classic self-normalized IS (a.k.a. weighted IS) from the Monte Carlo literature (e.g., Hesterberg 1998; Kong 1992; Owen 2013) and is related to control variates and variance reduction used in RL (e.g., TRPO trust-region style normalization). Prior contextual bandit work has explored IPS, weight clipping/truncation, and doubly robust (DR) estimators (e.g., Dudík et al. 2011; Strehl et al. 2010; Cortes et al. 2010). The paper’s novelty lies in showing how SNIS specifically addresses propensity overfitting within ERM for BLBF and in instantiating a CRM-style learner that leverages SNIS.

Strengths
- Clear identification and exposition of Propensity Overfitting through a simple, instructive example demonstrating IPS’s lack of boundedness and non-equivariance, with practical implications for ERM.
- Sound motivation for using a multiplicative control variate; the self-normalized estimator has well-understood properties (bounded, equivariant, often lower variance when weight fluctuations dominate).
- Strong consistency proof for SNIS in this setting and a clear diagnostic via Ŝ(h) whose expectation is 1.
- Integration into a learning algorithm (Norm-POEM) with a reasonable approximate variance estimator and practical optimization strategy.
- Empirical evidence across multiple datasets that Norm-POEM improves generalization, stabilizes Ŝ(h), is robust to loss translation, and remains efficient.

Weaknesses
- Theoretical development for learning is incomplete: there is no generalization bound analogous to the CRM bound for the SNIS-based objective, nor an analysis of how SNIS bias interacts with ERM and variance regularization. The variance estimator is heuristic (Normal/delta) without finite-sample guarantees.
- Limited baselines. The experiments compare mainly to POEM. Missing are strong off-policy learning/evaluation baselines such as doubly robust (DR), normalized DR, switch-DR, clipped IPS with tuned thresholds, and pure SNIS without CRM beyond the brief Norm-IPS ablation. Given the extensive literature, these comparisons are important.
- Experimental reporting could be more rigorous: confidence intervals/significance tests, sensitivity to clipping threshold M and λ, and robustness to varying stochasticity/coverage in h0 beyond qualitative statements.
- Claims such as “provably avoids degeneracies” are accurate for boundedness/equivariance, but the avoidance of propensity overfitting in ERM is not theoretically quantified (e.g., conditions under which ERM with SNIS cannot select degenerate solutions).
- The objective and optimization details could be more explicit (complete objective formula, discussion of non-convexity, convergence behavior, and handling of cases where Ŝ(h) is near zero).

Evaluation by NeurIPS criteria
- Quality: The identification of the IPS pathology and the SNIS remedy are technically sound and consistent with established IS theory. The strong consistency statement is correct. However, the learning theory is partial: there is no uniform convergence/generalization bound for the SNIS-CRM objective, and the variance regularizer’s efficacy rests on approximations. Empirically, results are positive but lack comprehensive baselines and statistical analysis.
- Clarity: Generally well written and organized. The propensity overfitting example, the role of Ŝ(h), and the estimator properties are clear. The method section could provide the exact training objective and algorithmic steps. Experimental setup is described, but tables/figures (in the excerpt) are referenced rather than shown; ensure all results include uncertainty estimates.
- Originality: Using SNIS itself is not novel; it is a standard estimator in off-policy evaluation. The novelty lies in diagnosing propensity overfitting for ERM in BLBF, arguing for SNIS to address it, and designing a SNIS-based CRM learner. This is an incremental but meaningful contribution bridging IS theory and BLBF learning practice.
- Significance: BLBF is important for recommender/ads/search. If broadly validated, the approach can improve robustness of counterfactual learning pipelines. The significance would be strengthened by theory for the SNIS-CRM objective and by comparisons to DR/switching estimators, and by results on real logs.

Questions for the authors
- Can you provide a generalization bound for the SNIS-based objective (even with clipping), or at least a uniform convergence argument quantifying the bias-variance trade-off in learning?
- How sensitive is Norm-POEM to the clipping threshold M and λ? Please include sensitivity plots and CIs.
- How does Norm-POEM compare to DR/normalized DR or switch-DR in both evaluation and learning?
- What happens when Ŝ(h) is very small for some h during training? Do you impose safeguards (e.g., lower-bounding denominators)?
- Can the normalization be combined with doubly-robust control variates (normalized DR) to further reduce variance?

Pros for acceptance
- Clear problem identification; principled and well-motivated estimator.
- Simple change with strong practical impact; consistent empirical gains and improved robustness.
- Bridges importance sampling best practices and counterfactual learning.

Cons for acceptance
- Missing theoretical learning guarantees for the proposed objective.
- Limited baselines and statistical rigor in experiments.
- Novelty is moderate since SNIS is standard; contribution is primarily in application/diagnosis.

Overall assessment
A solid and practically relevant contribution that diagnoses a real pathology of IPS-based ERM and proposes a principled, well-known remedy adapted to BLBF, with positive empirical evidence. Strengthening theory for the SNIS-CRM objective and expanding baselines would substantially improve the work.
