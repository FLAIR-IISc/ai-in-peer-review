Summary and relation to prior work
This paper addresses batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF) by diagnosing a failure mode of inverse propensity scoring (IPS) when used as an ERM objective, which the authors term Propensity Overfitting: the ERM objective can be minimized by manipulating importance weights rather than aligning with low loss examples, leading to degenerate solutions. The paper proposes using a multiplicative control variate to derive a self-normalized importance sampling (SNIS) estimator for counterfactual risk, yielding a new objective (Norm-POEM) and an approximate variance regularizer within the Counterfactual Risk Minimization (CRM) framework. The authors argue that the self-normalized estimator is bounded, equivariant to translation, and strongly consistent, thereby avoiding the anomalies of the unbiased IPS estimator. Experiments on supervised-to-bandit conversions for multilabel classification (Scene, Yeast, TMC, LYRL) show that Norm-POEM improves generalization and robustness, and is less sensitive to loss translation and weight clipping.

The ideas are rooted in classic importance sampling and control variates (Trotter & Tukey; Hesterberg; Owen; Kong), and in counterfactual learning and off-policy evaluation (Dudík et al.; Strehl et al.; Swaminathan & Joachims; Thomas et al.). Self-normalized IPS/WIS is well known in Monte Carlo and has been advocated in contextual bandit evaluation; the paper’s novelty is in (i) identifying and illustrating propensity overfitting when using IPS as a learning objective and (ii) integrating SNIS into a CRM-style learning algorithm with variance regularization for structured prediction.

Strengths
- Clear diagnosis of a real and under-discussed problem (Propensity Overfitting) with instructive examples showing how IPS-based ERM can have arbitrarily bad minima and can depend spuriously on loss translation.
- Applying self-normalization addresses these pathologies: the estimator becomes bounded and translation-equivariant, properties that are desirable for learning objectives.
- Simple, principled estimator (SNIS) with strong consistency; the control variate argument is standard but well adapted to this setting.
- Empirical evidence shows consistent gains over POEM across datasets and training sizes; useful diagnostics via Ŝ(h) around 1 validate the mechanism.
- Practical algorithmic choices (variance regularization, L-BFGS) and discussion of optimization behavior.

Weaknesses and detailed assessment

Quality
- Theoretical development is incomplete for learning. While Theorem 2 (consistency) is standard, there is no finite-sample generalization bound or CRM-style guarantee tailored to the self-normalized objective. The CRM bound cited (from [1], [13]) applies to (truncated) unbiased IPS, not SNIS. Plugging the SN estimator and its approximate variance into the CRM objective is plausible but theoretically ungrounded here.
- The variance estimator (Eq. 8) relies on a Normal/delta-method approximation. No discussion of its accuracy in small samples, nor confidence intervals for model selection, is provided. Alternatives like bootstrap or concentration for self-normalized ratios could strengthen claims.
- The method still requires clipping M and regularization λ. Sensitivity analyses are limited; formal guidance on selecting M and how it trades bias/variance in the learning objective is missing.
- Experimental evaluation lacks several strong baselines: doubly robust (DR), clipped-DR, switch-DR, MAGIC, or other modern off-policy learning estimators, as well as SNIPS/weighted IPS used directly as an objective. The comparison is mainly to POEM and an unregularized variant.
- Some details are underspecified: exact choice of M, λ tuning protocol and variance estimator behavior on validation; wall-clock runtimes; robustness to misspecified logging propensities.

Clarity
- The paper is generally well written and easy to follow, with a clear diagnosis and motivation. However:
  - There are minor typos (e.g., δ ∈ [5,4]) and some references are incomplete (missing years/venues).
  - The statement “provably avoids these degeneracies” is stronger than what is proven; boundedness/equivariance/consistency are shown, but no finite-sample guarantee against propensity overfitting is provided.
  - The link to code includes a non-anonymized URL, which violates double-blind reviewing.

Originality
- Using SNIS in contextual bandits and off-policy evaluation is not new; the novelty lies in elevating SNIS to the learning objective, connecting it to propensity overfitting, and integrating it with CRM/structured prediction. The conceptual contribution (propensity overfitting) and the empirical demonstration are valuable, though the estimator itself is standard.

Significance
- Practical relevance is high: objectives that are bounded and translation-equivariant are more stable and robust. The observed gains and diagnostic behavior are compelling.
- However, the impact would be greater with stronger theory (finite-sample learning guarantees) and broader empirical validation (more baselines, real logs, ablations).

Pros for acceptance
- Clear identification of a subtle but important failure mode of IPS-based learning.
- Simple, principled fix with strong intuitive and empirical support.
- Robust empirical improvements over a strong prior method (POEM), with diagnostic evidence that the mechanism is working.

Cons for acceptance
- Limited theoretical guarantees for the proposed learning objective; CRM bound not adapted to SNIS.
- Narrow experimental comparison set; absence of DR/switch-DR/MAGIC baselines.
- Anonymity breach via code URL; some presentation polish needed.
- All experiments are supervised-to-bandit; no real-world logs.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide a finite-sample generalization bound for the SNIS-based objective, or at least a learning-to-evaluate guarantee that explicitly incorporates the bias of SNIS and the effect of clipping.
- Compare against DR, clipped-DR, switch-DR, MAGIC, and SNIPS-based learning baselines; consider hybrid objectives (e.g., SNDR).
- Add sensitivity analyses for M and λ; report CIs via bootstrap or asymptotic variance and provide guidance for hyperparameter selection.
- Include at least one experiment on real logged data with known or simulatable counterfactuals; assess robustness when logging has low support or heavy-tailed propensities.
- Report wall-clock runtimes, iteration counts, gradient norms, and effect on ||w||; include ablations on the variance regularizer.
- Fix typos, complete citations, and remove non-anonymized links for submission.

Recommendation
Weak reject. The paper addresses an important problem and offers a practical and effective solution with solid empirical evidence, but the theoretical footing for learning with the self-normalized objective is incomplete, and the experimental comparisons are too narrow for NeurIPS standards. With stronger finite-sample analysis and broader baselines (and addressing anonymity), this would be a strong submission.

Reviewer confidence: medium.
