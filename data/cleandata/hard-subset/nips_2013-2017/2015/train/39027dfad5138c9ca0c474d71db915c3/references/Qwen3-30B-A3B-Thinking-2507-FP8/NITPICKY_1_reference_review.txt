

# Review of "The Self-Normalized Estimator for Counterfactual Learning"

This paper identifies "propensity overfitting" as a previously overlooked issue in counterfactual learning for batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF) and proposes a self-normalized risk estimator to address it. The authors develop a new learning algorithm called Norm-POEM that uses this estimator and provide empirical evidence of improved performance over the baseline POEM algorithm.

## Strengths
- The paper identifies a novel problem (propensity overfitting) that has been overlooked in prior BLBF work.
- The theoretical motivation for the self-normalized estimator is grounded in importance sampling literature.
- The empirical results demonstrate consistent improvements over POEM across multiple datasets.
- The paper is well-organized with a logical progression from problem statement to solution to evaluation.

## Weaknesses

### Theoretical Inconsistencies
1. **Inconsistent Theorem Claims**: The paper claims in Section 5 that "the self-normalized risk estimator R̂SN(h) in Equation (7) resolves all the problems of the unbiased estimator R̂(h) from Equation (2) identified in Section 4." However, Section 4 identifies two anomalies: (1) R̂(h) can take values outside the range of δ, and (2) R̂(h) is not equivariant to constant shifts in δ. Theorem 2 only proves consistency, but does not demonstrate how the estimator resolves the non-equivariance issue. The paper fails to explicitly prove that R̂SN(h) is equivariant.

2. **Incomplete Proof**: The proof of Theorem 2 (consistency) is incomplete. The paper states "Pr(limn→∞ R̂SN(h) = R(h)) = 1" but does not properly justify why the ratio of two convergent sequences converges to the ratio of their limits. The continuous mapping theorem should be explicitly referenced.

### Empirical Validation Issues
1. **Unsubstantiated Claims**: In Section 7, the paper states "Ŝ(ĥ) is close to its known expectation of 1 for Norm-POEM, while it is severely biased for POEM" (Table 2, left). However, the table doesn't provide actual values for POEM's Ŝ(ĥ), making this claim unsubstantiated.

2. **Contradictory Statements**: The paper claims "the improved self-normalized estimator no longer requires variance regularization" (Section 7), but Table 3 shows that variance regularization still helps, contradicting this claim. This inconsistency undermines the paper's core argument.

### Technical Inaccuracies and Notation Issues
1. **Incorrect Citation**: The paper cites [19] (Hesterberg) for the claim about the self-normalized estimator's performance, but Hesterberg's work is about "Weighted average importance sampling," not self-normalized estimators. The correct reference should be [12] (Trotter and Tukey).

2. **Inconsistent Notation**: The paper uses inconsistent notation for importance weights. In Section 3, pi is defined as h0(yi|xi), but the estimator uses "h(yi|xi)/pi" which should be "h(yi|xi)/h0(yi|xi)" for consistency with importance sampling theory. This confusion appears multiple times throughout the paper.

3. **Overstated Claim**: The paper claims "R̂SN(h) is always bounded within the range of δ" (Section 5), but this is not true when the weights h(yi|xi)/pi can become arbitrarily large. The paper should clarify the conditions under which this holds.

4. **Misleading Statement**: The paper states "the self-normalized risk estimator is not unbiased (E[R̂(h)/Ŝ(h)] ≠ R(h)/E[Ŝ(h)] in general), but it is strongly consistent." This is misleading - the estimator is not unbiased for R(h), but the paper doesn't clarify that it's consistent for R(h), not for R(h)/E[Ŝ(h)].

### Minor but Significant Formatting Issues
1. **Theorem Numbering**: The paper references "Theorem 1" in Section 3 but doesn't clearly state it's Theorem 1 in the text. The theorem numbering should be consistent with the standard practice of stating the theorem number before the statement.

2. **Inconsistent Subscripting**: The paper uses inconsistent subscripting for estimators (R̂(h) vs R̂SN(h) vs R̂M(h)) without clear explanation of what the subscripts represent.

## Overall Assessment
The paper identifies an interesting problem in BLBF, but the theoretical claims are not fully supported, the empirical validation lacks statistical significance tests, and there are multiple technical inaccuracies that undermine the paper's credibility. The paper's claims about the self-normalized estimator's properties are overstated, and the contradictory statements about variance regularization weaken the core contribution.

While the empirical results are promising, the theoretical gaps and technical inconsistencies are significant enough to question the validity of the proposed approach. The paper would benefit from a more rigorous theoretical treatment, consistent notation, and better empirical validation before it could be considered for publication.

Given the substantial number of minor but critical technical flaws and inconsistencies that prevent the reader from fully trusting the paper's claims, I recommend rejection.
