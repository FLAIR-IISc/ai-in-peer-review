Summary
The paper addresses batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF) and, in particular, a failure mode of empirical risk minimization (ERM) when using the standard inverse propensity scoring (IPS) estimator. The authors identify “propensity overfitting”: the IPS empirical risk can be driven to extreme values by manipulating the sum of importance weights rather than truly exploiting low-loss examples, leading to degenerate ERM solutions and anomalies such as lack of range-boundedness and lack of equivariance under loss translation. To mitigate this, the paper proposes using a multiplicative control variate yielding the self-normalized importance sampling (SNIS) estimator for counterfactual risk. The SN estimator is bounded within the loss range and equivariant under translation; it is strongly consistent (Theorem 2) though biased in finite samples. The estimator is incorporated into the Counterfactual Risk Minimization (CRM) framework to produce Norm-POEM: a structured prediction learner with a variance-regularized objective based on the SN estimator and an approximate (delta-method/normal-approximation) variance. Experiments on multi-label classification datasets (supervised-to-bandit protocol) show Norm-POEM consistently outperforms POEM (IPS-based) in generalization, reduces deviations of the sum of weights toward 1, is robust to loss translations, and often converges faster in practice.

Relation to prior work
The work builds on counterfactual learning and CRM (Swaminathan & Joachims), importance weighting under covariate shift, and off-policy evaluation in RL. It connects to classic SNIS (weighted importance sampling), widely studied in Monte Carlo and RL evaluation, and to variance reduction techniques such as clipping and control variates. The paper contrasts with prior additive control variate approaches (regression/doubly robust estimators) and previous CRM instantiations that used unbiased IPS. The novelty is applying multiplicative control variates/SNIS to learning (not only evaluation) to fix IPS pathologies in ERM and integrating this into a CRM-style objective for structured prediction.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The identification of propensity overfitting is well-motivated and the toy example is illuminating. Using SNIS to fix equivariance and boundedness is standard but well-justified here. Theorem 2 (consistency) is correct but straightforward. The empirical variance formula and CRM objective use an asymptotic/normal approximation, which is reasonable but weakens theoretical guarantees.
- Theoretical coverage: The paper reuses the CRM bound (Theorem 1) for clipped IPS but does not provide an analogous finite-sample generalization bound for the SN estimator. Since the learning objective uses the SN estimator and its sample variance, a new bound for ratio estimators (or a careful adaptation) would substantially strengthen the contribution. Currently, the theoretical story for learning is more heuristic than for evaluation.
- Empirical support: The experiments support the claims: improved test risk vs POEM, much better control of Ŝ(h) near 1, robustness under loss translations, and a useful ablation showing variance regularization still helps. However, the setup is limited to supervised-to-bandit with synthetic logging and only Hamming loss; stronger baselines and more diverse settings would increase confidence.

Clarity
The paper is generally clear and well-organized. The exposition of anomalies of IPS, diagnostic statistic Ŝ(h), and the derivation of the SN estimator are accessible. Some minor issues to fix:
- Typographical issues (e.g., “δ ∈ [5,4]” likely a typo for a bounded interval; some missing details in references).
- Provide explicit gradients/implementation details for the Norm-POEM objective (even in an appendix) to facilitate reproduction.
- Report wall-clock times and iteration counts to substantiate the runtime claims.
- Include confidence intervals or standard errors for the reported averages.

Originality
The SN estimator itself is classical; the main novelty is diagnosing the specific ERM failure mode (propensity overfitting) and showing that SNIS corrects the key anomalies in the context of counterfactual learning, then integrating this into a CRM-like training objective for structured prediction. While incremental in theory, the conceptual contribution and practical implications for BLBF are meaningful and timely. Related work is broadly covered, though see suggestions below.

Significance
The problem (robust counterfactual learning from logs) is important for recommender systems, ads, and other interactive systems. The fix is simple to implement, theoretically motivated, and empirically beneficial. Given how common IPS-based objectives are, this work can influence practice by steering learners toward SNIS-based objectives and diagnostics like monitoring Ŝ(h). Broader impact would be higher with stronger finite-sample theory and more comprehensive empirical validation.

Pros (arguments for acceptance)
- Clear identification and demonstration of a real failure mode of IPS-based ERM.
- Simple, principled remedy using SNIS with strong practical benefits: boundedness, equivariance, consistency.
- Integration into a CRM-inspired objective and structured prediction setting.
- Solid empirical gains over POEM and diagnostic evidence that propensity overfitting is mitigated.
- Code availability and practical relevance.

Cons (arguments against acceptance)
- Limited theoretical novelty; no finite-sample generalization bound for the SN-based learner.
- Variance term relies on normal approximation; no guarantees analogous to prior CRM bounds.
- Experimental scope limited to supervised-to-bandit with synthetic logging; no real logged datasets.
- Missing comparisons to stronger off-policy baselines (e.g., doubly robust and self-normalized DR variants, SWITCH-DR/MAGIC-type estimators), which could matter when losses/signals are noisy.
- Sensitivity analyses (clipping M, λ) and quantitative runtime comparisons are limited.

Suggestions and questions for the authors
- Provide a finite-sample generalization analysis tailored to the self-normalized estimator (e.g., concentration for ratios or a bound involving deviations of Ŝ(h); even a PAC-Bayes style argument would help).
- Add baselines: doubly robust and self-normalized DR estimators used for learning objectives; clipped vs unclipped SN; possibly SWITCH-DR/MAGIC-style hybrids if applicable.
- Include sensitivity plots for M and λ, and report wall-clock times and iterations to convergence.
- Evaluate on at least one real logged dataset (even small-scale) to demonstrate robustness beyond the supervised-to-bandit protocol.
- Clarify how deterministic hypotheses are handled when logging has limited support; discuss failure modes when pi is very small and illustrate clipping effects.
- Minor: fix typos, complete references, and provide pseudocode and gradient expressions in an appendix.

Overall recommendation
Weak accept. The paper identifies an important and underappreciated pathology of IPS-based ERM and proposes a simple, well-motivated solution that demonstrably improves robustness and performance. While the theoretical contribution is limited and the empirical scope could be broader, the work is likely to be useful to practitioners and to stimulate further research on principled, finite-sample guarantees for self-normalized counterfactual learning.
