Summary
The paper addresses batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF) by identifying a failure mode of inverse propensity score (IPS)-based empirical risk minimization (ERM), termed Propensity Overfitting. The authors show that standard IPS estimators can exhibit non-equivariance and unboundedness that ERM can exploit by manipulating the sum of importance weights Ŝ(h), leading to degenerate solutions. To remedy this, they propose replacing IPS with a self-normalized importance sampling estimator (SNIPS) within the Counterfactual Risk Minimization (CRM) framework, yielding a new algorithm, Norm-POEM. They argue (i) SNIPS is bounded and equivariant, (ii) strongly consistent, and (iii) better aligned with the learning objective when fluctuations in weights dominate. They further provide an approximate variance estimator for SNIPS to instantiate the CRM regularizer. Experiments on multi-label classification using the supervised→bandit protocol show that Norm-POEM improves generalization and is robust to loss translations, avoids propensity overfitting (Ŝ ≈ 1), and converges efficiently.

Relation to prior work
The work builds on CRM and POEM (Swaminathan & Joachims), importance weighting under covariate shift, off-policy evaluation, and variance-reduction techniques including clipping and doubly robust estimators. SNIPS (ratio/self-normalized IS) is classical in Monte Carlo and has been used in policy evaluation; the novelty here is integrating SNIPS into learning (not just evaluation), diagnosing propensity overfitting, and showing empirical gains in structured prediction. The paper situates itself against clipped IPS, additive control variates (DR), and empirical Bernstein bounds in CRM.

Evaluation

Quality
- The technical diagnosis of propensity overfitting is clear and insightful, with a simple, compelling example that exposes how ERM can minimize Ŝ instead of loss.
- The proposed estimator is well-motivated: self-normalization enforces equivariance and bounds predicted risk within the loss range; Theorem 2 establishes strong consistency.
- The variance proxy for SNIPS is standard (ratio estimator via delta/normal approximations) and supports a CRM-style regularizer.
- Empirical results are consistent: Norm-POEM improves test loss vs. POEM across datasets and is robust to loss translations; Ŝ(ĥ) stays near 1; training converges quickly.
- However, there is no finite-sample generalization bound for SNIPS+CRM analogous to Theorem 1 for IPS. The learning objective relies on an approximate variance and clipping without a uniform convergence guarantee. This is a notable theoretical gap given the paper’s learning focus.
- Comparisons omit strong baselines such as doubly robust (DR) or self-normalized DR, and recent high-confidence OPE estimators, which could clarify whether SNIPS alone is the key driver.

Clarity
- The paper is generally clear, well-organized, and uses consistent notation. The propensity overfitting example is pedagogical.
- Minor issues: a few typos (e.g., loss ranges), and the relationship between clipping M and existence of variance for SNIPS could be expanded. A short discussion on bias introduced by self-normalization and clipping in the learning objective would aid readers.

Originality
- Using SNIPS for evaluation is classic, but the articulation of “propensity overfitting” and the systematic integration of SNIPS into the CRM-based learning objective for structured output prediction is a novel and useful contribution.
- The identification of equivariance and boundedness as desirable properties for counterfactual risk estimators used in ERM is conceptually valuable.

Significance
- The problem is important for counterfactual learning and off-policy evaluation, with direct relevance to information retrieval, recommendation, and ads.
- The method is simple and practical, likely to be adopted. The empirical gains and robustness are meaningful.
- The lack of finite-sample guarantees tempers the theoretical significance; stronger baselines and more diverse datasets (including real logs, ranking/reco settings) would strengthen impact.

Pros
- Clear identification and formalization of propensity overfitting; compelling diagnostic Ŝ(h).
- Principled use of a multiplicative control variate to obtain a bounded, equivariant estimator.
- Strong, consistent empirical improvements over POEM; robustness to loss translation.
- Simple to implement; training remains efficient.

Cons
- No finite-sample learning bound for SNIPS+CRM; reliance on asymptotic arguments and approximate variance.
- Missing comparisons to DR/normalized-DR and other strong baselines.
- Experiments limited to supervised→bandit multi-label classification; no real-world logs or ranking/recommendation tasks.
- Limited ablation on λ, M, clipping sensitivity, and behavior policy support.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide a finite-sample generalization bound for SNIPS within CRM (e.g., via empirical Bernstein/delta method plus a control of normalization denominator), or at least a uniform stability analysis to complement Theorem 2.
- Compare against DR and self-normalized DR estimators, and report ablations: IPS vs. SNIPS with/without CRM regularization, effect of clipping M and λ.
- Evaluate on real logged data (e.g., ads/reco/search) and tasks where logging propensities are near-zero to probe robustness under heavy-tailed weights.
- Report confidence intervals and conduct sensitivity to loss transformations and scaling.
- Consider explicitly regularizing deviations of Ŝ(h) from 1 as an auxiliary penalty and compare against SNIPS-only training.
- Clarify optimization details (initialization, stopping, handling of non-convexity), and document code for reproducibility.

Decision
Accept. The paper identifies an important failure mode of IPS-based learning and proposes a simple, well-motivated fix using self-normalization, yielding robust empirical gains. While theoretical guarantees for the learning objective are incomplete and baselines could be broader, the conceptual clarity and practical impact justify acceptance.
