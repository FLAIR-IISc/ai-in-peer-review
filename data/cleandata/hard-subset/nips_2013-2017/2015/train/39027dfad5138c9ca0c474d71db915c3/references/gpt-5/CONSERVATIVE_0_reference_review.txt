Summary
The paper addresses batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF) via counterfactual risk minimization (CRM). It identifies a failure mode of inverse propensity weighting (IPS) in ERM—termed “propensity overfitting”—arising from the non-equivariance and unboundedness of the vanilla IPS risk estimator. The authors propose replacing IPS with the self-normalized importance sampling estimator (SNIPS), i.e., a multiplicative control variate using the sum of importance weights as a normalizer. They argue SNIPS is bounded, equivariant to shifts in the loss, and strongly consistent, and claim it “provably avoids degeneracies.” Building on this, they propose a learning algorithm, Norm-POEM, which plugs SNIPS and an approximate variance estimate into the CRM objective and optimizes it for a CRF-like hypothesis class. Experiments on supervised-to-bandit multi-label datasets show improved test loss versus POEM (IPS-based CRM), reduced sensitivity to loss translation, and suggest faster convergence with L-BFGS.

Relation to prior work
Self-normalized (a.k.a. weighted) importance sampling is classical (Tukey-Trotter; Hesterberg) and has been widely used in off-policy evaluation, including in RL and contextual bandits. The paper correctly situates SNIPS within importance sampling and mentions related variance reduction ideas (clipping, doubly robust). However, the theoretical properties emphasized here (boundedness, equivariance, consistency) are standard for SNIPS. The primary novelty appears to be: (i) articulating “propensity overfitting” in the BLBF/ERM setting; and (ii) integrating SNIPS into the CRM framework with a specific variance proxy and demonstrating empirical gains on structured prediction. The connection to existing off-policy evaluation work that uses normalized or doubly robust estimators (e.g., weighted/normalized DR) is underdeveloped, and experimental comparisons are missing.

Strengths
- Clear diagnosis of IPS pathologies in ERM (non-equivariance, unbounded risk leading to degenerate solutions); the toy example is instructive.
- Sensible application of a well-established estimator (SNIPS) to mitigate these issues; properties like boundedness and shift-equivariance directly address the identified anomalies.
- Empirical results show consistent improvements over POEM across several datasets, with a careful “supervised-to-bandit” protocol and reasonable hyperparameter selection.
- The Ŝ diagnostic is appropriate and the observed stability of Norm-POEM to loss translation aligns with theory.
- Code availability is noted.

Weaknesses
- Theoretical contribution is limited. The key properties of SNIPS are known, and Theorem 2 is a straightforward application of the SLLN. The claim that SNIPS “provably avoids degeneracies” is not supported by finite-sample analysis or generalization bounds within the CRM setting. The paper reuses the CRM idea but does not provide a bound tailored to ratio estimators (including bias terms and appropriate concentration for ratios).
- The proposed variance estimate for SNIPS (Eq. 8) is an approximation via Normal/delta arguments. The learning objective hinges on this proxy, yet there is no finite-sample justification or sensitivity analysis. Moreover, the method still clips weights (M), which introduces bias; the interaction between normalization and clipping is not analyzed.
- Baselines are too narrow. Missing:
  - Doubly robust estimators (DR) and their weighted/normalized variants for policy learning/evaluation.
  - Clipped IPS with stronger tuning strategies (e.g., adaptive clipping), and alternative stabilizations (e.g., weight shrinkage, defensive mixtures).
  - Switch estimators or model-based components (even a simple direct method blended with IPS/DR).
These are standard in off-policy evaluation; only comparing to POEM makes it hard to assess broader significance.
- The experimental setting is entirely simulated (supervised-to-bandit), with a specific hypothesis class and Hamming loss. While common, it limits external validity. There are no results on real logged data, no stress tests under severe support mismatch, and no controlled studies varying overlap or the logging policy determinism beyond brief statements.
- Scalability: the paper states stochastic optimization is not applicable for the SNIPS variance objective, relying on L-BFGS. For large-scale applications, this is a practical drawback. Claims of faster convergence are anecdotal; there are no convergence plots, wall-clock comparisons, or complexity analyses.
- Overstatements: “resolves all the problems of the unbiased estimator” is too strong given residual issues (bias under clipping, potential instability under extreme weight variance, and lack of finite-sample guarantees).

Questions for the authors
1. Can you provide a finite-sample generalization bound for the CRM objective with SNIPS, accounting for ratio concentration and estimator bias (especially with clipping)? Even a high-level bound with explicit bias-variance terms would strengthen the claims.
2. How sensitive is Norm-POEM to the choice of M and λ under SNIPS compared to IPS? Please include ablations and failure cases.
3. Why not include DR and weighted DR baselines? A simple outcome model (e.g., logistic regression per label) is easy to add in your setting.
4. How does the method perform when the logging policy violates full support (deterministic regions)? Does SNIPS still help relative to IPS under heavy truncation?
5. Can you show runtime scaling with number of examples and labels, and compare to stochastic POEM variants?

Suggestions for improvement
- Add DR and normalized DR baselines, and possibly switch-DR or model-assisted estimators. Report both estimation and learning performance.
- Provide a more principled variance regularizer for ratio estimators or derive a bound-driven objective; at minimum, quantify the bias from clipping under SNIPS.
- Include experiments varying overlap and weight dispersion (controlled logging stochasticity), with diagnostic plots of weight distributions and Ŝ(h).
- Report wall-clock times, iteration counts, and convergence curves.
- Temper claims about “resolving all problems,” and clarify the scope: SNIPS addresses certain anomalies but does not eliminate high-variance issues when overlap is poor.

Clarity
The paper is generally well written and organized, with intuitive examples and clear notation. Some derivations (Eq. 8) are sketched; providing more detail or an appendix would help. The relation to prior OPE work using normalized estimators could be expanded.

Originality
Moderate. The use of SNIPS in this context is sensible but not conceptually new. The articulation of “propensity overfitting” in ERM is a useful perspective, and the empirical integration into CRM for structured prediction is a practical contribution.

Significance
Potentially useful for practitioners using CRM-like objectives. However, without stronger theoretical backing and broader baselines (notably DR), the impact is limited.

Overall evaluation
Borderline reject. The paper applies a well-known estimator to a known problem, yielding empirical gains over a single baseline but lacking rigorous finite-sample theory and comprehensive comparisons. With added baselines, tighter analysis for the CRM objective with SNIPS, and stronger experimental validation, this could become a solid contribution.
