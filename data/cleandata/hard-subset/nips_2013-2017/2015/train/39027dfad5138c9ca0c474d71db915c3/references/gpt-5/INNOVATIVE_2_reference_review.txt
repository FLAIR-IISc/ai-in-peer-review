Summary
The paper targets batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF) and the use of counterfactual estimators within the Counterfactual Risk Minimization (CRM) framework. It identifies a concrete failure mode of the standard inverse propensity (IPS) estimator when used for ERM: “propensity overfitting,” whereby hypotheses exploit the variance of importance weights to drive empirical risk estimates to degenerate values (including violating bounds and translation non-equivariance). The paper proposes a principled fix via multiplicative control variates: the self-normalized estimator (a.k.a. self-normalized importance sampling, SNIS). This yields a self-normalized counterfactual risk estimator that is bounded, equivariant under translations, and strongly consistent. The authors then instantiate CRM with this estimator and an approximate variance estimate to derive Norm-POEM, a learning algorithm for structured prediction with exponential-family policies. Experiments on multiple multilabel datasets constructed via “supervised → bandit” simulation show improved generalization, robustness to loss translation, reduction of Ŝ(h) bias, and competitive training time compared to POEM. The paper argues that the estimator’s properties directly address propensity overfitting and translate into better performance.

Relation to prior work
The work sits at the intersection of importance weighting for covariate shift/BLBF, CRM (Swaminathan & Joachims), off-policy evaluation in RL, and classic IS variance reduction. Self-normalized IS and ratio estimators are well-known in Monte Carlo (Hesterberg; Kong; Owen; Rubinstein & Kroese) and used in RL policy iteration/practical OPE. The novelty here is to (i) diagnose propensity overfitting as a learning-time pathology of IPS-based ERM, (ii) argue that SNIS resolves the specific anomalies (boundedness, equivariance), and (iii) integrate SNIS into CRM with an implementable variance regularizer in a structured prediction setup.

Strengths
- Clear and compelling identification of “propensity overfitting,” with crisp illustrative examples that connect estimator anomalies to ERM behavior.
- Simple, principled fix via multiplicative control variates; the estimator is bounded and translation-equivariant, directly addressing the diagnosed issues.
- Theoretical properties are appropriate and honest: strong consistency is established; boundedness/equivariance explained; variance control via clipping is discussed.
- Practical integration into a CRM objective and a concrete algorithm (Norm-POEM) for exponential-family policies, with tractable gradients and an approximate variance estimate.
- Empirically persuasive: consistent improvements over POEM across datasets, robustness to loss translations, Ŝ(ĥ) near 1, and competitive runtime.
- The contribution is broadly applicable: any counterfactual learning pipeline suffering from weight variance can benefit, not just the specific CRF instantiation.

Weaknesses and questions
- Novelty is incremental with respect to estimators: self-normalization is classic. The main conceptual novelty lies in articulating “propensity overfitting” and showing its remedy within CRM, rather than inventing a new estimator.
- Theory is limited to consistency; no finite-sample generalization bounds tailored to the self-normalized CRM objective are provided. An empirical Bernstein-style bound adapted to the ratio estimator would strengthen the story.
- Experimental scope is narrow: “supervised → bandit” multilabel classification with Hamming loss and CRF-like policies, and a single logging policy construction (5% of data, multiple replays). Real-world logged datasets or harder OPE regimes (heavier-tailed propensities) would be compelling.
- Baselines miss strong competitors: doubly-robust (DR) and weighted/self-normalized DR (e.g., regression estimators with control variates) are not compared, though closely related and sometimes superior under modelability of rewards.
- The variance estimator used for SNIS is approximate and asymptotic; practical guidance on reliability/clipping M, and its sensitivity, is limited. Ablations over M and λ, and diagnostics when weight distributions are extremely skewed, would help.
- Optimization is non-convex and non-stochastic; while L-BFGS worked well here, a discussion of local minima and initialization sensitivity would be useful.
- The evaluation protocol tunes hyperparameters via counterfactual evaluation; potential selection bias or overfitting to the estimator used for selection is not discussed.

Quality
Technically sound overall. The consistency result is straightforward but correct. The discussion of equivariance and boundedness is accurate and important for learning-time behavior. The empirical findings are consistent with the theory. The main missing technical piece is a finite-sample bound for the self-normalized CRM objective, which would parallel the original CRM results and solidify the regularization choice.

Clarity
Well-written and easy to follow. The propensity overfitting examples are excellent. A few suggestions:
- Define “equivariance” more formally when first introduced.
- Be explicit early that the proposed estimator is the standard self-normalized IS/ratio estimator; this helps readers connect to a large literature.
- Provide algorithmic pseudocode and gradient expressions in an appendix for reproducibility.

Originality
Moderate. The estimator itself is not new; the paper’s originality is in naming/diagnosing propensity overfitting for IPS-based ERM and in showing that multiplicative control variates cleanly fix it within CRM with practical benefits. This conceptual reframing is useful and likely to influence practice.

Significance
High practical significance for BLBF and off-policy learning: the fix is simple, broadly applicable, and improves robustness. Given the prevalence of counterfactual learning in recommender systems and ads, this can see adoption. The work also nudges the community to move beyond raw IPS for learning.

Reasons to accept
- Clear identification of an important failure mode with a principled, effective remedy.
- Simple, general technique with immediate practical impact.
- Solid empirical improvements and robustness.
- Conceptual contribution (propensity overfitting) likely to influence how practitioners design objectives.

Reasons to reject
- Estimator novelty is limited; theoretical contributions beyond consistency are modest.
- Missing comparisons to DR/WDR and lack of real-world logs reduce evidentiary weight.

Questions for authors
- Can you provide or outline a finite-sample CRM-style bound for the self-normalized objective?
- How does Norm-POEM compare to DR and weighted/self-normalized DR variants?
- How sensitive are results to clipping M and the weight distribution’s tail behavior?
- Any results on real logged datasets or settings with severe propensities?

Overall recommendation
Lean accept. Despite limited estimator novelty, the paper makes a clear, impactful conceptual contribution and delivers a practical, robust improvement to counterfactual learning. The community will benefit from adopting self-normalized objectives to avoid propensity overfitting.
