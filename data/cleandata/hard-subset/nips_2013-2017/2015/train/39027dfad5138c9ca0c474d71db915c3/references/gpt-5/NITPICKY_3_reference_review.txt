Summary
The paper tackles batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF) for contextual/structured prediction. It argues that inverse propensity scoring (IPS) used within the Counterfactual Risk Minimization (CRM) framework can suffer from a distinct failure mode—“propensity overfitting,” where the ERM objective exploits variability in importance weights rather than true loss structure. To mitigate this, the authors propose using a multiplicative control variate, yielding the self-normalized importance sampling (SNIS/SNIPS) estimator for risk. They show SNIPS is equivariant to loss translation, bounded within the loss range, and strongly consistent. They then instantiate a learning algorithm (Norm-POEM) by plugging SNIPS and a delta-method variance estimate into a CRM-style objective, and empirically report better generalization than POEM on multi-label datasets, with improved robustness and runtime.

Strengths
- Clear diagnosis of a real issue (propensity overfitting) that is practically important when learning with logged bandit feedback.
- Sensible proposal: replace IPS by the well-known SNIS estimator; the translation-equivariance and boundedness properties are indeed desirable for ERM.
- Simple consistency argument (SLLN for ratio) is correct under standard integrability conditions.
- Empirical results (as described) suggest benefits in generalization and robustness; they also include sanity diagnostics via Ŝ(h)≈1.
- The paper is well aligned with NeurIPS topics (off-policy evaluation/learning, causal inference, structured prediction), and builds on prior CRM work.

Weaknesses and concerns
Methodological/theoretical
- The key theoretical bound (Theorem 1) is for capped IPS, not for the proposed SNIPS-based objective. The paper then “substitutes” a Normal-approximation variance estimate (Eq. 8) into CRM without providing a corresponding generalization guarantee. This is a central gap: the original CRM appeal is its high-probability control via empirical Bernstein; here, the theory no longer connects to the actual learning objective.
- Assumptions for Theorem 2 are incomplete. Strong consistency of the ratio estimator requires finite first moments (and typically bounded or square-integrable losses). Earlier, δ is assumed in [−1,0] for the bound, but the paper then discusses arbitrary translations. Please state explicit assumptions (e.g., bounded δ or E|δ|<∞ and finite second moments of weights).
- Clipping and normalization: the text suggests clipping “to guarantee finite variance,” but the exact estimator used in training is not formally defined. If clipping is applied, the stated equivariance and boundedness properties may change. Please provide the precise definition of the trained estimator (numerator and denominator both clipped? only weights?).
- The variance estimator (Eq. 8) is asymptotic (delta/Normal approximation). There is no analysis of its bias or reliability in finite samples, nor a uniform convergence argument across H. Existing self-normalized concentration inequalities or ratio bounds could be leveraged to re-derive a CRM-like bound; without this, the learning objective’s theoretical footing is weak.
- The “propensity overfitting” example is useful but somewhat contrived; with realistic clipping M, the catastrophe is attenuated. The paper should analyze the tradeoff (bias vs. robustness) for IPS vs SNIPS under clipping.

Empirical
- Critical experimental information is missing in the submission text: the referenced Table 1/2/3 and Figure 1 are not included; no numerical results, standard errors, or confidence intervals are shown. This prevents verification of claims and assessment of effect sizes and variability.
- Baselines are too limited. Given the focus on variance reduction and robustness, comparisons to doubly robust/DR-learning methods and strong off-policy estimators (e.g., DR, SWITCH/DRos, WDR/MAGIC) are needed, even if framed for contextual bandits/structured outputs.
- Hyperparameter selection details (grids for λ, M), initialization, stopping criteria, and sensitivity analyses are not provided. The claimed runtime advantage lacks wall-clock numbers and hardware details.
- The logging policy assumption (stationary, full support) is strong. The paper acknowledges clipping but does not explore violations of support or nonstationarity, which are common in logs.

Clarity/presentation
- Several notational and consistency issues:
  - δ ranges: Section 3 assumes δ∈[−1,0], Section 4 refers to δ∈[5,4] (likely a typo, decreasing interval), examples use {−2,−1}, experiments use Hamming loss in [0,1]. Unify and state translation/scaling clearly relative to each estimator’s properties.
  - Define precisely Ŝ(h) and its use. Eq. (7) uses sums; earlier Ŝ(h) is an average (1/n). Be explicit to avoid confusion.
  - Precisely define the clipped SNIPS estimator used in training.
- Many references are incomplete or malformed (missing years/venues/pages; inconsistent formatting; some entries appear truncated).
- The code URL points to “POEM” rather than a dedicated Norm-POEM repo; reproducibility would benefit from a working link with scripts and seeds.

Originality and significance
- Using SNIS to stabilize importance-weighted estimation is classical; the novelty lies in highlighting “propensity overfitting” in CRM and proposing a SNIS-based learning objective. This is a reasonable and useful incremental contribution, but the theoretical advance is limited without a new generalization bound for the actual objective.
- If the empirical gains are robust and substantial, the contribution would be practically significant; however, the absence of results/tables in the submission text makes it impossible to judge.

Detailed suggestions
- Provide a generalization analysis for the SNIPS-based objective, ideally leveraging self-normalized concentration or ratio inequalities to obtain a CRM-style bound that matches the proposed training criterion.
- Precisely define the estimator under clipping and analyze its bias and equivariance properties.
- Expand baselines to include DR and recent robust OPE/L algorithms; report CIs, effect sizes, and ablations over λ and M; include wall-clock runtime with hardware specs.
- Unify assumptions on δ and weights; clearly state conditions for consistency and finite variance.
- Include the missing tables/figures; provide seeds and a functioning code repository.
- Fix typos, notation, and references.

Pros
- Identifies and illustrates a real failure mode of IPS-based ERM.
- Sensible, well-motivated fix with desirable properties (boundedness, equivariance).
- Consistency proof is straightforward and correct given standard assumptions.
- Promising empirical story (if corroborated).

Cons
- No theoretical bound corresponding to the proposed objective; reliance on asymptotic variance undermines CRM’s appeal.
- Estimator used in practice (with clipping) not precisely specified/analyzed.
- Missing experimental details/tables; limited baselines; no CIs/timings.
- Numerous presentation/reference issues.

Recommendation
Reject. The idea is sound and likely useful, but the paper in its current form has significant theoretical and empirical gaps, and numerous presentation problems. With a proper generalization analysis for the SNIPS-based objective, complete and convincing experiments (including DR baselines), and cleaned-up presentation, this could become a strong contribution. Confidence: high.
