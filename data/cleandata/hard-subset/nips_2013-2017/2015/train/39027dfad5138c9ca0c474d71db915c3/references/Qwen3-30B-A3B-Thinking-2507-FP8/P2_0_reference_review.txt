

# Review: The Self-Normalized Estimator for Counterfactual Learning

## Summary and Context

This paper addresses a critical but previously overlooked issue in batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF), a key problem for improving interactive systems like search engines and recommender systems. The authors identify "Propensity Overfitting" - a new form of overfitting that occurs when using conventional counterfactual risk estimators (like inverse propensity scoring) for empirical risk minimization (ERM) in BLBF settings. They propose a self-normalized risk estimator that provably avoids this issue and derive a new learning algorithm called Norm-POEM that uses this estimator. The paper provides theoretical analysis, clear examples of the problem, and extensive empirical validation across multiple datasets.

## Quality

The paper demonstrates strong technical quality. The identification of Propensity Overfitting is well-motivated through clear examples that reveal fundamental flaws in the conventional estimator (Section 4), showing how risk estimates can fall outside the loss range and fail to be equivariant under loss translation. The theoretical contribution (Theorem 2) establishes the consistency of the self-normalized estimator, and the experimental validation is thorough and well-designed. The authors carefully compare against the state-of-the-art POEM algorithm, using the standard Supervised 7→ Bandit methodology to isolate the effect of their estimator.

The paper acknowledges limitations appropriately, noting that variance regularization remains necessary (Table 3) and providing detailed analysis of how the self-normalized estimator avoids the specific anomalies identified in Section 4. The empirical results are convincing, showing consistent improvements across multiple datasets and training sizes (Table 1, Figure 1). The authors are honest about the computational trade-offs, noting that while the normalization increases per-iteration cost, optimization converges faster.

## Clarity

The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The problem statement is clear from the outset, with a strong motivation about the practical importance of BLBF for real-world systems. The identification of Propensity Overfitting is made accessible through a concrete example (Example 1) that demonstrates the severity of the issue. The transition from problem identification to solution is logical and well-supported.

The theoretical development is presented with sufficient detail for experts to follow, and the experimental methodology is described precisely enough to be reproducible. The figures and tables (particularly Table 2 showing the deviation of Ŝ(h) from 1) effectively illustrate the key insights. The paper follows a clear structure: problem statement → related work → formalization → problem identification → solution → algorithm derivation → empirical validation.

## Originality

This paper makes a genuinely novel contribution. While importance sampling and counterfactual estimators have been studied extensively in BLBF and off-policy evaluation, the specific problem of Propensity Overfitting was not previously identified or characterized. The paper's insight that conventional estimators can be "minimized not only by putting large probability mass on examples with low loss but by maximizing the sum of weights" is novel and insightful.

The use of multiplicative control variates to derive the self-normalized estimator is a clever application of established techniques from importance sampling literature (citing Hesterberg [19]), but the specific application to this problem in BLBF is new. The Norm-POEM algorithm is a natural extension of POEM that incorporates this insight, but the key contribution is the identification of the problem and the theoretical solution.

## Significance

The significance of this work is substantial. Propensity Overfitting is a fundamental limitation of current BLBF methods that has likely affected the reliability of many prior studies. The proposed solution is both theoretically sound and practically effective, with consistent empirical improvements across multiple datasets. The self-normalized estimator's properties (equivariance, boundedness) make it more robust and reliable for real-world applications.

The paper advances the state of the art in counterfactual learning by addressing a previously overlooked but critical issue. The authors demonstrate that their solution is not just a theoretical improvement but translates to better generalization performance in practice. The fact that Norm-POEM consistently outperforms POEM across all datasets (Table 1) and training sizes (Figure 1) is particularly compelling.

## Strengths and Weaknesses

**Strengths:**
- Clear identification of a new, important problem (Propensity Overfitting)
- Theoretical justification with a proof of consistency
- Practical solution with a new algorithm (Norm-POEM)
- Comprehensive empirical validation showing consistent improvements
- Strong connection to established techniques in importance sampling

**Weaknesses:**
- Limited theoretical analysis beyond consistency (no finite-sample bounds or comparison of error rates)
- The computational cost analysis could be more detailed, though the empirical runtime results are convincing
- The experiments are limited to classification datasets; broader validation would strengthen the claims

## Recommendation

This is an excellent paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of counterfactual learning. The identification of Propensity Overfitting is a major theoretical insight, and the self-normalized estimator provides a practical solution that demonstrably improves performance. The paper is technically sound, well-written, and addresses an important problem with real-world relevance.

I recommend acceptance at NeurIPS. The paper is clearly superior to many others in the field for its combination of theoretical insight and empirical validation. The authors have made a strong case for the importance of their contribution, and the paper advances the state of the art in a demonstrable way.

The paper meets all four criteria at a high level: it's technically sound (Quality), clearly written (Clarity), makes a novel contribution (Originality), and addresses an important problem with practical significance (Significance). The weaknesses noted are minor in comparison to the paper's strong merits.
