This paper addresses the problem of batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF), a crucial setting for improving interactive systems like search and recommendation. The authors identify a novel failure mode in existing counterfactual risk minimization (CRM) approaches, which they term "Propensity Overfitting". This occurs when a model with sufficient capacity learns to minimize the estimated risk not by fitting the underlying loss function, but by manipulating the importance sampling weights—either by assigning high probability to training examples with negative loss, or zero probability to those with positive loss. This can lead to arbitrarily poor risk estimates and degenerate solutions.

To address this, the paper proposes replacing the standard inverse propensity score (IPS) estimator with the Self-Normalized (SN) estimator, a well-known ratio estimator from the Monte Carlo literature. The authors correctly point out the desirable properties of the SN estimator: it is equivariant to affine transformations of the loss and its estimates are guaranteed to lie within the range of the observed losses, thus directly preventing the pathological behavior of Propensity Overfitting. They integrate this estimator into the CRM framework, deriving a new learning algorithm called Norm-POEM. Through a series of well-controlled experiments on multi-label classification tasks, they demonstrate that Norm-POEM significantly outperforms its predecessor (POEM), is robust to loss translations that cause POEM to fail, and effectively controls the propensity weights as intended.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Novel and Important Problem Formulation:** The primary contribution is the identification and clear articulation of "Propensity Overfitting". The example in Section 4 is exceptionally clear and provides a powerful intuition for why standard ERM with an unbiased importance-weighted estimator can fail catastrophically. This is a subtle but critical insight that has been largely overlooked in the BLBF literature, which has focused more on the variance of the estimator rather than its potential for adversarial manipulation by the learner.

2.  **Principled and Elegant Solution:** The proposed solution—using the self-normalized estimator—is simple, well-motivated, and directly targets the identified problem. While the SN estimator itself is not new, its application as a remedy for this specific type of overfitting in a learning context is novel and insightful. The connection to multiplicative control variates provides a solid theoretical foundation.

3.  **Strong and Convincing Empirical Evaluation:** The experiments are thoughtfully designed and provide compelling evidence for the paper's claims.
    *   The "Supervised -> Bandit" methodology allows for controlled evaluation against a known ground truth.
    *   The results in Table 1 and Figure 1 clearly show superior generalization performance for Norm-POEM over POEM.
    *   Crucially, Table 2 directly verifies the central hypothesis: Norm-POEM corrects the bias in the sum of weights `Ŝ(h)` and is robust to loss transformations, whereas POEM is not. This closes the loop and confirms that the performance gains are indeed due to mitigating Propensity Overfitting.
    *   The ablation study showing that variance regularization is still beneficial (Table 3) is a good inclusion.

4.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is exceptionally well-written. The motivation is established early, the problem is illustrated with a concrete example, the solution is presented logically, and the experiments directly support the claims. The paper is easy to follow and a pleasure to read.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Limited Discussion of Computational Trade-offs:** The paper notes that the variance estimate for Norm-POEM (Eq. 8) does not admit stochastic optimization, forcing the use of a batch optimizer like L-BFGS. While the authors report faster convergence in practice on their datasets, this is a potentially significant limitation for scaling to massive datasets where SGD-based methods are dominant. A more thorough discussion of this trade-off would strengthen the paper. For instance, are there approximations to the variance term that could restore the possibility of stochastic optimization?

2.  **Scope of Baselines:** The comparison is focused entirely on POEM, which uses the standard IPS estimator. While this is the most direct and relevant baseline, the field of off-policy evaluation has other powerful estimators, notably the Doubly Robust (DR) estimator, which also uses control variates (additive ones) to reduce variance. A brief discussion or comparison to a DR-based learning algorithm would help situate Norm-POEM within the broader landscape of off-policy learning methods and better establish its relative advantages.

3.  **Novelty of the Estimator:** As acknowledged, the self-normalized estimator is a classic tool. The paper's novelty lies in diagnosing the Propensity Overfitting problem and applying this tool as a solution within a learning framework. This is a strong contribution, but it is more of a conceptual and applicational one than a purely technical one. This is a minor point, as the contribution is significant regardless.

**Evaluation Summary:**

*   **Quality:** High. The paper is technically sound. The claims are well-supported by both theoretical reasoning and rigorous experimental results.
*   **Clarity:** Excellent. The writing is clear, concise, and well-organized. The core ideas are communicated very effectively.
*   **Originality:** Good. While the core estimator is not new, the identification and analysis of Propensity Overfitting is a novel and important contribution to the field of counterfactual learning. The application of the SN estimator in this context is new and well-motivated.
*   **Significance:** High. Learning from logged data is a problem of immense practical importance. Propensity Overfitting is a fundamental issue that this paper is the first to clearly diagnose and solve. The proposed method, Norm-POEM, is a simple and effective replacement for previous approaches that demonstrably improves robustness and performance. Researchers and practitioners working on off-policy learning are very likely to adopt these ideas and build upon this work.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Identifies and provides a clear explanation for a previously under-appreciated but critical failure mode in counterfactual learning.
*   Proposes a simple, principled, and effective solution with strong theoretical justification.
*   Provides comprehensive and convincing empirical evidence that the proposed method not only outperforms the state-of-the-art but does so for the precise reasons hypothesized.
*   The paper is extremely well-written and advances our understanding of the nuances of learning from bandit feedback.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   The computational approach (L-BFGS) may not scale as well as the stochastic methods it compares against, a point that could be discussed more deeply.
*   The experimental comparison could have been slightly broader by including a Doubly Robust baseline.

In conclusion, this is an excellent paper that makes a significant contribution to our understanding of learning from logged bandit feedback. The identification of Propensity Overfitting is a key insight, and the proposed solution is both elegant and effective. The weaknesses are minor and do not detract from the overall strength of the work. I strongly recommend acceptance.
