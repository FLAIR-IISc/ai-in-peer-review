

# Review of "The Self-Normalized Estimator for Counterfactual Learning"

This paper identifies a novel problem called "Propensity Overfitting" in batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF) and proposes a solution using a self-normalized risk estimator. The authors correctly situate their work within the context of prior NIPS/ICML work on BLBF (e.g., POEM [1]), off-policy evaluation in reinforcement learning, and importance sampling techniques. They make a compelling argument that conventional counterfactual risk estimators suffer from severe anomalies that lead to degenerate risk estimates, particularly when used in Empirical Risk Minimization (ERM) for BLBF. The proposed self-normalized estimator using multiplicative control variates resolves these anomalies, and the authors derive a new algorithm (Norm-POEM) based on this estimator.

**Strengths:**
1. **Clear identification of a novel problem:** The paper effectively identifies and formalizes "Propensity Overfitting" as a distinct problem from conventional overfitting, with a well-constructed example demonstrating how the conventional estimator can produce arbitrarily biased risk estimates. This is a significant theoretical contribution to the field.

2. **Strong theoretical foundation:** The authors provide a rigorous proof (Theorem 2) of the strong consistency of the self-normalized estimator, along with a clear explanation of why it avoids the anomalies of the conventional estimator. The connection to control variates in importance sampling literature (e.g., Hesterberg [19]) is well-motivated.

3. **Thorough empirical evaluation:** The experiments across multiple datasets (Scene, Yeast, TMC, LYRL) demonstrate consistent improvements of Norm-POEM over POEM in generalization performance. The authors effectively verify that the improved performance is directly linked to avoiding Propensity Overfitting by showing that Norm-POEM maintains Ŝ(ĥ) close to 1 (as theoretically predicted), while POEM's estimates are severely biased.

4. **Practical implementation:** The paper provides clear implementation details for Norm-POEM, including the hypothesis space, variance estimator, and training objective. The computational efficiency claim (runtime comparable to full-information CRF) is well-supported by empirical results.

**Weaknesses:**
1. **Limited discussion of why Propensity Overfitting was previously overlooked:** While the paper convincingly demonstrates the existence of Propensity Overfitting, it doesn't sufficiently explain why this problem wasn't identified in prior BLBF work (e.g., in POEM [1] or other related papers). A more thorough comparison with previous methods' behavior would strengthen the novelty claim.

2. **Incomplete analysis of computational cost:** The paper mentions that the self-normalized estimator has a higher per-iteration computational cost but converges faster. However, it doesn't provide a detailed theoretical or empirical analysis of the trade-off between per-iteration cost and iteration count. A more comprehensive computational analysis would be valuable.

3. **Limited exploration of theoretical properties:** While the paper proves strong consistency, it doesn't provide a theoretical analysis of the estimator's variance or bias compared to the conventional estimator. A more complete theoretical comparison (e.g., bias-variance trade-off) would strengthen the theoretical contribution.

4. **Potential scope for broader impact:** The paper focuses on structured output prediction, but the proposed estimator could potentially be applied to other counterfactual learning settings (e.g., reinforcement learning). A brief discussion of this broader applicability would increase the paper's significance.

**Recommendation:**
The paper makes a significant contribution to the field of counterfactual learning by identifying a previously overlooked problem (Propensity Overfitting) and providing a theoretically sound and empirically validated solution. The theoretical analysis is strong, the experimental evaluation is thorough, and the proposed method is practically implementable with clear benefits over prior work.

The main weaknesses are the limited explanation for why Propensity Overfitting was previously overlooked and the incomplete theoretical analysis of the estimator's properties. However, these do not undermine the paper's core contribution. The paper represents a solid, well-executed piece of research that advances the state of the art in BLBF.

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the weaknesses noted above. Specifically, the authors should:
1. Add a paragraph explaining why Propensity Overfitting wasn't identified in prior BLBF work.
2. Include a more detailed theoretical comparison of the variance/bias properties of the two estimators.
3. Provide additional computational analysis comparing the total time complexity of the two methods.

These revisions would strengthen the paper without requiring major rework, and would make the contribution even clearer and more impactful for the NIPS community.
