This paper addresses the problem of batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF), a crucial setting for improving interactive systems like search and recommendation. The authors identify a novel and subtle failure mode of standard counterfactual risk estimators based on importance sampling, which they term "Propensity Overfitting." This occurs when a learning algorithm with sufficient capacity minimizes the empirical risk not by fitting the true loss function, but by exploiting the importance weights—either by placing probability mass only on training examples (for negative losses) or by avoiding them entirely (for positive losses). This can lead to arbitrarily poor risk estimates and catastrophic generalization failure.

To solve this, the authors propose replacing the standard inverse propensity score (IPS) estimator with the self-normalized estimator, which is derived by using the sum of importance weights as a multiplicative control variate. They show that this estimator is equivariant to translations in the loss function and its estimates are bounded within the range of observed losses, provably avoiding the anomalies that cause Propensity Overfitting. They integrate this estimator into the Counterfactual Risk Minimization (CRM) framework to develop a new learning algorithm, Norm-POEM. The work builds directly on prior work in BLBF, particularly the POEM algorithm [1], but its central contribution is the diagnosis of Propensity Overfitting and the demonstration that the well-known self-normalized estimator is an elegant and effective solution in this learning context.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Strengths:**

1.  **Excellent Problem Formulation and Diagnosis:** The paper's primary strength is the clear identification and explanation of "Propensity Overfitting." The example in Section 4 is exceptionally illustrative and convincing, demonstrating how standard Empirical Risk Minimization (ERM) with an unbiased IPS estimator can fail in ways not typically seen in supervised learning. This is a significant conceptual contribution that highlights a real, practical pitfall for anyone applying these methods.

2.  **Principled and Elegant Solution:** The proposed solution—using the self-normalized estimator—is not an ad-hoc fix but is well-motivated by control variate theory. The paper clearly articulates the theoretical advantages of this estimator (equivariance, bounded range, consistency), directly linking each property to a specific flaw in the standard IPS estimator.

3.  **Strong and Convincing Empirical Evaluation:** The experiments are very well-designed. The use of the "Supervised-to-Bandit" protocol allows for controlled evaluation against a known ground truth. The results are compelling:
    *   Table 1 and Figure 1 clearly show that Norm-POEM consistently achieves better generalization performance than the baseline POEM.
    *   Table 2 provides the "smoking gun" evidence for the paper's central thesis. It demonstrates that Norm-POEM corrects the severe bias in the sum of importance weights (`Ŝ(h)`) observed in POEM and, crucially, that Norm-POEM's performance is invariant to loss translation while POEM's performance collapses. This experiment directly validates the theoretical motivation.
    *   The paper also responsibly checks that variance regularization is still beneficial (Table 3) and analyzes computational performance.

4.  **High Clarity and Readability:** The paper is extremely well-written, well-organized, and easy to follow. The authors build their argument logically from problem to solution to validation, making a complex topic accessible.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Limited Comparison to Alternatives:** While the paper mentions doubly robust (DR) estimators in related work, it does not compare against them empirically. DR estimators are a standard and powerful technique for off-policy evaluation and learning that also aim to reduce variance and bias. It would be valuable to understand if Propensity Overfitting also affects DR estimators, and how Norm-POEM compares to a DR-based learning algorithm. This would provide a more complete picture of the landscape of solutions.

2.  **Scalability Concerns:** The authors note that their objective requires a batch optimizer like L-BFGS and does not admit stochastic optimization. While they show it converges surprisingly fast on the tested datasets, this could be a practical limitation for much larger datasets where stochastic gradient descent (SGD) is often the only feasible option. A brief discussion on potential approximations or future work to enable stochastic optimization would strengthen the paper's practical relevance.

3.  **Novelty of the Estimator:** The self-normalized estimator itself is a classic tool in the importance sampling literature. The authors are upfront about this. The novelty lies entirely in the diagnosis of Propensity Overfitting in the context of counterfactual *learning* and the application of this estimator as a solution. While this is a strong conceptual contribution, it is worth noting that the core statistical tool is not new.

### Detailed Comments and Evaluation

*   **Quality:** The paper is of high technical quality. The claims are well-supported by a clear theoretical argument and a thorough, targeted empirical study. The work is complete and the authors are careful to evaluate their method's properties from multiple angles (generalization, robustness, runtime).

*   **Clarity:** The clarity is excellent. The writing is precise, the structure is logical, and the key ideas are communicated effectively. The illustrative example in Section 4 is a standout piece of exposition. The paper provides enough information for an expert to understand and likely reproduce the work.

*   **Originality:** The primary originality lies in the identification of the Propensity Overfitting problem. This is a novel insight into the failure modes of ERM in BLBF settings. While the proposed estimator is not new, its application to solve this specific, newly-diagnosed problem within a learning framework is an original and valuable contribution. The connection between the estimator's properties (like equivariance) and the learning problem's pathologies is particularly insightful.

*   **Significance:** The work is highly significant. Counterfactual learning from logged data is a problem of immense practical importance. This paper identifies a critical flaw in a standard approach and provides a simple, robust, and effective solution. Researchers and practitioners working on off-policy learning, recommender systems, and computational advertising are likely to find this work immediately useful. The concept of Propensity Overfitting should influence how people think about and apply importance sampling-based learning algorithms, likely leading to more robust models.

### Summary of Pros and Cons

**Pros:**
*   Identifies and clearly explains a novel, important failure mode: "Propensity Overfitting."
*   Proposes an elegant, principled solution using the self-normalized estimator.
*   Provides strong, targeted empirical evidence that directly validates the core claims.
*   Exceptionally well-written and easy to understand.

**Cons:**
*   Lacks empirical comparison to other strong baselines like doubly robust methods.
*   The proposed optimization method is not stochastic, which may limit scalability.
*   The core estimator is a known statistical tool, so the novelty is in the diagnosis and application.

**Recommendation:** I strongly recommend acceptance. The paper makes a clear, insightful, and significant contribution to the field of counterfactual learning. It identifies a real and previously unarticulated problem and provides a well-motivated and empirically validated solution. The strengths far outweigh the minor weaknesses.
