### Summary

This paper addresses the problem of batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF), a setting where a new policy must be learned from historical data collected by a different, fixed logging policy. The standard approach for this problem is Counterfactual Risk Minimization (CRM), which performs Empirical Risk Minimization (ERM) using an unbiased Inverse Propensity Score (IPS) estimator of the policy's risk.

The main contribution of this work is the identification and formalization of a critical failure mode of this standard approach, which the authors term "Propensity Overfitting". They demonstrate through a clear example and theoretical argument that ERM with the standard IPS estimator can select hypotheses that minimize the estimated risk not by fitting the true loss function, but by exploiting the propensity weights to produce arbitrarily low (or high) risk estimates. This is because the standard IPS estimator is not equivariant to shifts in the loss function and its value is not bounded by the range of the observed losses.

To resolve this issue, the paper proposes replacing the standard IPS estimator with the Self-Normalized Estimator, a well-known ratio estimator from the importance sampling literature. This estimator is derived using a multiplicative control variate and has several desirable properties: it is shift-equivariant, its estimates are guaranteed to lie within the range of the observed losses, and it is strongly consistent. By incorporating this self-normalized estimator and its approximate variance into the CRM framework, the authors derive a new learning algorithm called Norm-POEM.

An extensive empirical evaluation on several multi-label classification datasets, using a standard supervised-to-bandit conversion protocol, demonstrates that Norm-POEM significantly outperforms its predecessor, POEM [1], in terms of generalization performance. The experiments confirm that Norm-POEM is robust to shifts in the loss function and successfully mitigates propensity overfitting, leading to more stable and accurate learning.

### Quality and Technical Soundness

The paper is technically sound. The claims are well-supported by both theoretical arguments and empirical results.

*   The identification of the Propensity Overfitting problem is a key insight and is very clearly explained. The provided example (Example 1) is simple and effective at illustrating the pathology of the standard IPS estimator when used for ERM.
*   The motivation for using the self-normalized estimator is sound. Its properties—equivariance, boundedness, and consistency (Theorem 2)—directly address the identified weaknesses of the standard estimator. The connection to multiplicative control variates is an elegant framing.
*   The experimental methodology is rigorous, following the established "Supervised -> Bandit" protocol from prior work [1, 2]. The experiments are comprehensive and directly test the paper's central claims. They not only show that Norm-POEM achieves better generalization but also provide diagnostic results (Table 2) demonstrating that it indeed avoids propensity overfitting and is robust to loss translations, thereby causally linking the improved methodology to the improved performance.
*   A potential weakness is the justification for using a biased estimator within the CRM framework. The CRM principle (Theorem 1) is derived from a generalization bound for an *unbiased* estimator. The paper substitutes the (finitely-biased) self-normalized estimator and its variance into the CRM objective without re-deriving the theoretical motivation from a new bound. While this is a reasonable heuristic that works well empirically, a discussion of the theoretical implications of this substitution would strengthen the paper's technical depth.

### Clarity

The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized.

*   The problem statement and motivation are clear and compelling. The authors do an excellent job of building intuition before presenting the technical solution.
*   The structure is logical, flowing from the problem definition (BLBF), to the identification of a specific failure mode (Propensity Overfitting), to the proposed solution (Self-Normalized Estimator), and finally to the algorithm (Norm-POEM) and its evaluation.
*   The use of Example 1 is particularly effective in making the abstract concept of propensity overfitting concrete and understandable.
*   The experimental section is clear, reproducible, and effectively communicates the key results through well-designed tables and figures.

### Originality and Significance

*   **Originality:** While the self-normalized estimator itself is a classic tool from the statistics and Monte Carlo literature [12, 19], its application to counterfactual *learning* and the analysis in this context are novel. The primary conceptual contribution is the identification, naming, and clear diagnosis of "Propensity Overfitting" as a distinct problem from standard "Loss Overfitting" in supervised learning. This is a novel and important insight for the field of off-policy and counterfactual learning.
*   **Significance:** The paper addresses a fundamental problem in a widely applicable learning setting. The identified issue affects any learning algorithm that performs ERM on an IPS-style objective. The proposed solution is simple, principled, and shown to be highly effective. Given the increasing interest in learning from logged data in applications like search, recommendation, and ad placement, this work is likely to have a significant impact. The improved robustness and performance of Norm-POEM make it a strong candidate for practical use, and the insights on propensity overfitting will likely inform future research in this area. The contribution represents a clear and demonstrable advance over the state of the art [1].

### Arguments for and Against Acceptance

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
1.  **Novel and Important Problem Formulation:** The paper clearly identifies and articulates "Propensity Overfitting," a fundamental and previously under-appreciated problem in counterfactual learning.
2.  **Effective and Principled Solution:** It proposes a simple and elegant solution that demonstrably fixes the problem, leading to substantial gains in generalization performance and robustness.
3.  **High Quality and Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written, with strong theoretical motivation and a rigorous, convincing empirical evaluation.
4.  **Significant Impact:** The findings are highly relevant to a broad and active research area and are likely to be widely adopted by both researchers and practitioners working with logged bandit feedback.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**
1.  **Limited Technical Novelty of the Estimator:** The core technical component, the self-normalized estimator, is not new. The contribution lies in its application and analysis in a new context.
2.  **Theoretical Gap:** The theoretical justification for plugging a biased estimator into the CRM learning objective, which was derived for an unbiased one, is not fully developed.
3.  **Scalability Concerns:** The proposed objective is not amenable to stochastic gradient methods, which might limit its applicability to extremely large-scale datasets where full-batch methods like L-BFGS are infeasible. The paper mentions favorable convergence but a more direct comparison of wall-clock time would be beneficial.

Overall, the strengths of this paper—particularly the novel problem identification and the clarity and significance of the results—far outweigh the weaknesses. It is a strong contribution to the field.
