Summary
The paper addresses batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF) by diagnosing a failure mode of inverse propensity scoring (IPS) when used inside empirical risk minimization (ERM). The authors term this “propensity overfitting”: models can game the importance weights to drive the IPS risk estimate arbitrarily low or high, leading to severe degeneracies (e.g., non-equivariance under loss translations and unbounded estimates). To mitigate this, they propose using a multiplicative control variate—the sum of importance weights—yielding the self-normalized IPS (SNIPS) risk estimator for both evaluation and learning. They integrate this estimator into the CRM framework and derive a new training objective (Norm-POEM), with an approximate variance regularizer. They argue SNIPS restores boundedness and translation equivariance, and prove strong consistency. Empirically, on several multi-label datasets under a supervised→bandit simulation, Norm-POEM outperforms POEM, appears robust to loss translations, and converges faster in practice.

Relation to prior work
Importance weighting and variance reduction via clipping and control variates are well-established; self-normalized IS is classic (Hesterberg, Kong, Owen, Rubinstein & Kroese) and widely used in off-policy evaluation. CRM and POEM (Swaminathan & Joachims) provide the learning bound and variance-regularized objective for IPS. Doubly robust (DR) and regression estimators (Dudík et al.) are also standard for OPE and learning under partial feedback. The paper’s conceptual novelty is framing “propensity overfitting” and advocating SNIPS for training in structured contextual bandits; algorithmically it is an incremental variant of POEM that swaps IPS for SNIPS and uses an approximate variance.

Strengths
- Clear diagnosis of IPS pathologies inside ERM (unboundedness, non-equivariance) and a crisp, established fix via self-normalization.
- Theoretical properties emphasized (boundedness, translation equivariance, strong consistency) are correct and important for practice.
- The propensity-overfitting examples are instructive and connect well to observed empirical behavior (Ŝ(h) deviating from 1).
- Empirical results show consistent gains over POEM on multiple datasets and robustness to loss translations; implementation practicalities (L-BFGS convergence, hyperparameter tuning) are discussed.

Weaknesses
Quality
- The theoretical development is limited. The main finite-sample generalization bound (CRM/empirical Bernstein) is imported from prior work for IPS with clipping. No analogous bound is provided for the SNIPS-based objective; the variance regularizer uses a Normal/delta-method approximation without guarantees. Claims that SN “provably avoids the anomalies” are true for boundedness and equivariance but do not translate into a new generalization error bound or finite-sample risk control.
- Theorem 2 (consistency) is immediate from the SLLN; no rates or high-confidence guarantees are provided, and the interaction with clipping M (which induces bias) is not analyzed.
- The denominator of SNIPS can be small in finite samples; while clipping is suggested to ensure finite variance, the bias–variance tradeoff of SNIPS-with-clipping is not quantified.
- Empirical evidence is limited to a supervised-to-bandit simulation with a stochastic logging CRF trained on 5% of data. No evaluation under propensity misspecification, deterministic logging, covariate shift, or real logs; these are common in practice.
- Baselines are too narrow. DR/augmented IPS (and their weighted variants) are natural and often stronger baselines; propensity clipping strategies and weight stabilization beyond a single M should be compared; the Offset Tree and other reduction-based baselines are not included for learning. Given that SNIPS is established in OPE, comparisons to DR and WDR are essential.
- Statistical rigor of experiments is limited: while 10 runs are mentioned, there are no confidence intervals or significance tests; sensitivity to λ and M is not thoroughly explored; the effect of the approximate variance regularizer on calibration/coverage is not assessed.

Clarity
- Generally clear and well written. The exposition of propensity overfitting is useful. Minor typos (e.g., Section 4 “δ ∈ [5,4]”). The code link appears to point to POEM rather than explicitly to Norm-POEM; please provide a reproducible package.
- Some implementation details are missing: how is clipping applied in the denominator for SNIPS? How are gradient stability and numerical issues handled when the denominator is small?

Originality
- Using self-normalized IS is not novel; the contribution is its integration into the CRM training objective and the framing of “propensity overfitting”. The algorithmic change from POEM is modest. The work would be stronger with new theory specialized to SNIPS-CRM or new insights beyond the well-known properties of SN.

Significance
- The problem is important. If validated against stronger baselines and backed by finite-sample guarantees, this could influence practice. As is, the significance is constrained by incremental novelty and limited evaluation scope.

Questions for the authors
1. Can you provide a finite-sample generalization bound for the SNIPS-based CRM objective, perhaps via self-normalized concentration inequalities or PAC-Bayes? How does the bias introduced by clipping enter the bound?
2. How does Norm-POEM compare to DR-learning and weighted DR in both evaluation and learning? Please include these baselines.
3. How sensitive are results to M and λ? Provide ablations and CIs.
4. How do results change with deterministic logging or misspecified/estimated propensities?
5. Can you report the distribution of the denominator Ŝ(h) at solutions, to verify robustness beyond the mean?

Pros
- Addresses a real pathology of IPS in ERM with a principled, established fix.
- Clear exposition and simple method; bounded, translation-equivariant estimator.
- Consistent empirical gains over POEM; robustness to loss translation; practical optimization.

Cons
- Limited novelty relative to established SNIPS; main theorem is trivial; no new finite-sample guarantees for the proposed objective.
- Narrow baselines (omits DR/WDR) and limited experimental settings; lack of statistical analysis.
- Potential instability of SNIPS not fully addressed; clipping bias unquantified.

Recommendation
Weak reject. The idea of using self-normalization inside CRM is sensible and likely beneficial, but the current paper lacks rigorous finite-sample theory for the proposed objective and omits key baselines (DR/WDR). Strengthening the theoretical guarantees and expanding the empirical validation against established alternatives would make this a solid contribution. Confidence: high.
