- Paper studies batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF) and a failure mode of ERM with IPS called “propensity overfitting.”
- Propensity overfitting: IPS risk can be driven to extreme values by manipulating importance weight sums, causing degenerate ERM solutions and anomalies (unboundedness, non-equivariance).
- Proposed remedy: use a multiplicative control variate — the self-normalized importance sampling (SNIS) estimator — which is bounded and equivariant under loss translation.
- The SN estimator is strongly consistent (Theorem 2) but biased in finite samples.
- Introduces Norm-POEM: a CRM-style structured prediction learner using SN estimator and an approximate (delta-method/normal) variance regularizer.
- Empirically, Norm-POEM outperforms POEM, keeps the sum of weights near 1, is robust to loss translations, and often converges faster.
- Builds on prior work in counterfactual learning, CRM, importance weighting, and SNIS from Monte Carlo/RL literature.
- Novelty lies in diagnosing propensity overfitting in ERM and applying SNIS within learning (not just evaluation) and integrating it into CRM.
- Technical soundness: pathology identification and toy example are convincing; SNIS justification is standard but well-argued; Theorem 2 is straightforward.
- Theoretical gap: no finite-sample generalization bound for the SN-based learner or ratio estimators, weakening theoretical guarantees for learning.
- Variance term and CRM objective rely on asymptotic normal approximation, reducing theoretical rigor.
- Empirical support is positive but limited to supervised-to-bandit protocol, synthetic logging, and Hamming loss.
- Clarity is good; presentation of IPS anomalies, Ŝ(h), and SN derivation is accessible.
- Minor clarity issues: typos, missing reference details, and lack of explicit gradients/implementation specifics for Norm-POEM.
- Practical reproducibility needs wall-clock times, iteration counts, and reported confidence intervals/standard errors.
- Pros: clear identification of a real IPS failure mode, simple principled remedy, integration into CRM, solid empirical gains, and code availability.
- Cons: limited theoretical novelty, no finite-sample bound, reliance on normal approximation, limited experimental scope, missing stronger baselines (DR, SWITCH-DR/MAGIC), and sparse sensitivity/runtime analyses.
- Suggestions: add finite-sample analysis for SN, include doubly robust and self-normalized DR baselines, sensitivity plots for clipping and λ, runtime reporting, evaluation on real logged data, discussion of small-propensity failures, and append pseudocode/gradients.
- Overall recommendation: Weak accept — important practical contribution with limited theoretical depth and empirical breadth but likely useful to practitioners.
