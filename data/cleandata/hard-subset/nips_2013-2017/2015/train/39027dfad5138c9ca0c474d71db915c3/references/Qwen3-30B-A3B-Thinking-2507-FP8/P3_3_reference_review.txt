

# Peer Review: "The Self-Normalized Estimator for Counterfactual Learning"

## Summary and Context
This paper addresses a critical gap in Batch Learning from Logged Bandit Feedback (BLBF), a fundamental problem in counterfactual learning for interactive systems. The authors identify a previously unrecognized form of overfitting—Propensity Overfitting—that plagues conventional counterfactual risk estimators used in Empirical Risk Minimization (ERM) for BLBF. They propose a self-normalized risk estimator based on multiplicative control variates, which provably avoids the degeneracies of existing estimators, and introduce Norm-POEM, a learning algorithm built upon this estimator. The work extends the CRM principle from prior BLBF literature (e.g., POEM [1]) by addressing a subtle but severe theoretical flaw.

## Strengths

1. **Novel Problem Identification**: The paper makes a strong case for Propensity Overfitting as a distinct and serious problem. Their examples (Section 4) compellingly demonstrate how conventional estimators can produce arbitrarily low risk estimates that violate the boundedness of loss functions—a clear departure from standard overfitting in supervised learning.

2. **Theoretical Soundness**: The theoretical contributions are well-justified. Theorem 2 establishes the consistency of the self-normalized estimator, and the paper correctly identifies how the self-normalized form resolves the equivariance issue (unlike the conventional estimator). The connection to control variates in importance sampling (Section 5) is well-motivated and appropriately cited.

3. **Empirical Validation**: The experiments (Section 7) are thorough and convincing. Norm-POEM consistently outperforms POEM across multiple datasets, and the analysis of $\hat{S}(\hat{h})$ (Table 2) directly confirms the theoretical claims about Propensity Overfitting. The demonstration that Norm-POEM is unaffected by loss translations (Table 2, right) is particularly strong evidence.

4. **Practical Impact**: The paper addresses a real-world limitation in counterfactual learning algorithms that could have significant implications for deployed systems. The proposed solution requires minimal changes to existing frameworks (Norm-POEM is analogous to POEM with a different estimator), making it highly applicable.

## Weaknesses and Concerns

1. **Limited Theoretical Guarantees**: While Theorem 2 establishes consistency, the paper lacks finite-sample bounds for the self-normalized estimator. For practitioners, knowing the rate of convergence or sample complexity would be valuable. The authors rely on the empirical evidence to support their claims, which is reasonable but could be strengthened.

2. **Computational Efficiency Claims**: The paper states Norm-POEM is "surprisingly faster" than POEM, but the evidence is limited to convergence iterations (Section 7). A detailed runtime comparison (wall-clock time, memory usage) would better substantiate this claim. The increased per-iteration cost of the self-normalized estimator could offset fewer iterations in practice.

3. **Handling Extreme Propensity Scores**: The paper doesn't fully address how the self-normalized estimator behaves when propensity scores are extremely small (a common issue in real-world logging). While they mention thresholding (Section 6), the analysis of this critical edge case is superficial.

4. **Connection to Prior Importance Sampling Work**: The paper cites Hesterberg [19] on self-normalized estimators, but doesn't deeply engage with this literature. The authors could better position their contribution relative to established importance sampling techniques (e.g., how their estimator differs from the "defensive mixture" approach in [19]).

## Critical Assessment

The paper makes a significant contribution by identifying a previously overlooked problem (Propensity Overfitting) that undermines the reliability of counterfactual learning algorithms. The self-normalized estimator is a natural yet insightful extension of existing importance sampling techniques, and the authors provide strong theoretical and empirical justification for its use.

The main limitation is the lack of deeper theoretical analysis of the estimator's properties beyond consistency. While the empirical results are compelling, the absence of finite-sample guarantees leaves room for doubt about performance in small-sample regimes—a common concern in practice.

The paper is well-written, clearly structured, and appropriately positioned relative to related work. It builds on the POEM framework [1] while addressing a critical gap. The authors are careful to acknowledge the limitations of their approach (e.g., needing variance regularization, Section 7), which demonstrates intellectual honesty.

## Significance and Impact

This work addresses a fundamental issue in counterfactual learning that has practical consequences for real-world systems. The Propensity Overfitting problem could lead to severely suboptimal policies in applications like recommendation systems and ad placement. The self-normalized estimator provides a simple, principled fix that requires minimal changes to existing frameworks.

The paper advances the state of the art in BLBF by resolving a previously unaddressed source of bias. Given the widespread use of counterfactual learning in industry, this work has clear potential for impact.

## Recommendation

This is a strong paper that makes a clear, novel contribution to the field. The identification of Propensity Overfitting is significant, and the self-normalized estimator provides a theoretically sound and empirically effective solution. While the paper would benefit from more detailed theoretical guarantees and computational analysis, these are minor concerns given the overall quality and significance of the work.

**Recommendation**: Accept. This paper makes a valuable contribution to the BLBF literature and provides a practical solution to a previously overlooked problem. The work is technically sound, well-motivated, and has clear implications for both theory and practice.
