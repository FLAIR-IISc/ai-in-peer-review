**Summary of the paper**

This paper addresses the problem of Batch Learning from Logged Bandit Feedback (BLBF). The authors identify a specific failure mode of standard counterfactual risk estimators based on importance sampling, which they term "Propensity Overfitting". This phenomenon occurs when a model with high capacity minimizes the empirical risk not by fitting the loss, but by manipulating the propensity weights, leading to degenerate risk estimates. To counteract this, the paper proposes replacing the standard unbiased Inverse Propensity Score (IPS) estimator with the Self-Normalized (ratio) estimator, a well-known technique from the Monte Carlo literature. This estimator is shown to be equivariant to loss translations and bounded by the range of the observed losses, thus mitigating Propensity Overfitting. The authors incorporate this new estimator into the Counterfactual Risk Minimization (CRM) framework to create a new algorithm, Norm-POEM. Empirical results on several multi-label classification datasets demonstrate that Norm-POEM is more robust and achieves better generalization performance than its predecessor, POEM.

**Strengths**

1.  **Problem Significance:** The paper tackles a crucial and practical problem. The instability of importance sampling estimators is a well-known bottleneck in off-policy evaluation and learning, and any principled method to improve their robustness is a valuable contribution.
2.  **Clear Problem Formulation:** The "Propensity Overfitting" problem is illustrated with a clear and compelling example (Example 1). This effectively builds intuition for why standard Empirical Risk Minimization with an unbiased estimator can fail catastrophically in the BLBF setting.
3.  **Principled Solution:** The choice of the self-normalized estimator is well-motivated. Its properties—equivariance to loss translation and having its estimates bounded within the range of the loss—directly address the anomalies of the standard IPS estimator highlighted by the authors.
4.  **Strong Empirical Results:** The experiments are well-designed, following the "Supervised → Bandit" protocol, which allows for controlled evaluation. The results in Table 1 and Figure 1 show a consistent and significant performance improvement of Norm-POEM over the POEM baseline. Table 2 provides convincing evidence that the proposed method indeed controls the sum of propensity weights `Ŝ(h)` and is robust to loss-shifting, validating the paper's core claims.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Technical Soundness and Clarity:** There appear to be several technical inaccuracies and presentation issues that undermine the paper's quality.
    *   The CRM objective in Equation (3) is presented as `R̂_M(h) + λ * sqrt(V̂ar(uh))/n`. The regularization term's units do not match the risk term. Typically, such bounds would involve `sqrt(V̂ar/n)`, making the regularizer a standard deviation of the mean. This discrepancy, whether a typo or a misunderstanding of the original bound from [1, 13], is a significant flaw.
    *   The proof of Theorem 1 is deferred entirely to prior work. While acceptable for standard results, the theorem is central to the motivation of the CRM principle, and its formulation should be self-contained and precise.
    *   The justification for the variance estimator in Equation (8) is also offloaded to external references. A brief derivation or at least a more detailed explanation of the delta method approximation would greatly improve the paper's self-containedness.
    *   The claim that L-BFGS is "well supported" for non-smooth optimization is an overstatement. L-BFGS is a quasi-Newton method designed for smooth objectives, and its application to non-smooth problems is heuristic.

2.  **Originality:** While the application is novel, the core technical component—the self-normalized estimator—is a classic technique in importance sampling [12, 19]. The phenomenon of "Propensity Overfitting" is also closely related to the known high variance and instability of the Horvitz-Thompson estimator. The paper's contribution lies more in the framing of this problem within the BLBF learning context and its integration into the CRM framework, rather than the invention of a fundamentally new estimator.

3.  **Attention to Detail and Formatting:** The manuscript suffers from a lack of polish, which detracts from its overall quality.
    *   There are numerous formatting errors in the bibliography (e.g., "In ICML,, , ,").
    *   A non-standard symbol is used for function mapping ("7→" instead of `→` in Section 3).
    *   In Table 2 (right), under the "TMC" column for POEM, a test error of "-2.16" is reported for the `δ-2` loss transformation. Test error (Hamming loss) cannot be negative. This is likely a copy-paste error from the `Ŝ(h)` table, but such a mistake in the main results table is concerning.
    *   The paper claims "we observed NormPOEM dominated POEM even after l2−regularization" but provides no data to support this, weakening the claim.

**Decision**

While the paper presents a good idea with promising results, the numerous technical inaccuracies, clarity issues, and lack of meticulousness in the presentation prevent me from recommending acceptance in its current form. The error in the formulation of the core CRM objective (Equation 3) and the reporting error in the main results table (Table 2) are particularly serious.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Addresses an important, practical problem in counterfactual learning.
*   The core idea is simple, intuitive, and empirically shown to be effective.
*   The results demonstrate a clear and substantial improvement over a recent, relevant baseline.

**Arguments for Rejection:**
*   Significant technical unclarity/error in the formulation of the main learning objective (Equation 3).
*   The paper is not self-contained, frequently deferring proofs and derivations of key equations.
*   The originality is limited, as the main technical tool is a standard method from another field.
*   A substantial number of minor flaws, including formatting errors, typos in the results table, and unsubstantiated claims, indicate a lack of careful preparation.

I recommend **rejection**. The paper has a strong foundation, but it needs a thorough revision to address the technical and presentational shortcomings before it meets the high standards of NIPS. A revised version that corrects the formulation of the CRM objective, clarifies derivations, fixes the reporting and formatting errors, and more carefully contextualizes its contribution with respect to classic importance sampling literature would be a strong candidate.
