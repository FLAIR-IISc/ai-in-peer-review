- Paper studies batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF) and diagnoses "propensity overfitting" when IPS is used inside ERM/CRM.
- Propensity overfitting: hypotheses manipulate importance weights, causing degenerate, non-equivariant, and unbounded empirical risks with IPS.
- Authors propose replacing IPS with self-normalized (SNIPS/weighted IS), treating sum of weights as a multiplicative control variate.
- SNIPS is shown to be bounded, translation-equivariant, and strongly consistent.
- They derive a Norm-POEM algorithm: CRM with SNIPS plus an approximate variance regularizer for structured prediction (CRF-like models).
- Experiments on multilabel datasets show Norm-POEM improves generalization, is robust to loss translation, and avoids propensity overfitting (sum of weights near 1).
- Runtime and optimization behavior are discussed and shown to be competitive in practice.
- Builds on CRM/POEM, importance sampling, and off-policy evaluation literature; SNIPS itself is standard in OPE.
- Main novelty is formalizing propensity overfitting and integrating SNIPS into the learning objective with variance regularization.
- Theoretical contribution addresses estimator anomalies but lacks a finite-sample generalization bound specific to SNIPS-CRM.
- No uniform deviation bound for the ratio estimator over the hypothesis class; strong consistency alone is insufficient for learning guarantees.
- The variance estimator is heuristic (delta-method/normal approximation) with no bias or concentration analysis.
- The objective is nonconvex and non-smooth; L-BFGS works empirically but convergence guarantees or diagnostics are not provided.
- Experimental baselines are limited; missing comparisons to DR/WDR, SWITCH/DR heuristics, MAGIC, and other modern OPE learners.
- Sensitivity analysis for clipping parameter M and regularization λ is brief; more systematic ablations are needed.
- Logging policy is simulated from supervised data and only evaluates CRF-like multiclass multilabel datasets; no real logged data or extreme propensity regimes tested.
- Minor clarity issues and typos; Weighted IS nomenclature and denominator-stability assumptions should be clarified earlier.
- Technically sound in motivation and estimator properties, but lacks learning-theoretic analysis and theoretical justification for variance approximations.
- Generally well written with strong examples and pedagogical control variate perspective; more optimization and hyperparameter detail would aid reproducibility.
- Originality is moderate: SNIPS is classical, but the propensity overfitting diagnosis and Norm-POEM integration are novel and useful.
- Practical significance is good; SNIPS is simple and improves robustness, though theoretical and experimental breadth limits the strength of claims.
- Suggested improvements: provide CRM-style generalization bounds for SNIPS, compare to DR/WDR and hybrid OPE methods, report sensitivity to M/λ and Ŝ(h) distributions, test more extreme logging policies, and clarify terminology/typos.
- Pros for acceptance: clear diagnosis, principled simple fix, empirical gains, and bounded/equivariant estimator addressing IPS anomalies.
- Cons for acceptance: missing finite-sample theory, limited baselines and simulated logging data, heuristic variance estimate and nonconvex optimization without guarantees.
- Recommendation: Weak accept (paper likely to be adopted despite limitations).
- Reviewer confidence: 7/10.
