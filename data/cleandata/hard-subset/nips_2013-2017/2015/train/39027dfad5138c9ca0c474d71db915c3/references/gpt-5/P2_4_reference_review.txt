Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper addresses batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF) and off-policy evaluation. It identifies a new pathology—propensity overfitting—arising when the standard inverse propensity score (IPS) risk estimator is used inside ERM/CRM. The core observation is that the IPS empirical risk can be driven by manipulating the sum of importance weights Ŝ(h), not only by aligning with low-loss examples, leading to extreme and non-equivariant estimates. The paper proposes using a multiplicative control variate (the sum of weights) to form a self-normalized IPS (SNIPS) risk estimator, and builds a learning algorithm (Norm-POEM) that plugs SNIPS and an approximate variance estimate into the CRM objective. Theoretical claims include boundedness and equivariance of the estimator and almost sure consistency (given full support). Empirically, on simulated-logged multilabel datasets, Norm-POEM improves generalization versus POEM, is robust to loss translation, and appears computationally competitive.

SN/self-normalized IS is well-known in Monte Carlo and RL (Hesterberg, Kong, TRPO-style normalization); doubly robust (DR) and clipped IPS baselines are also established. The main novelty is diagnosing propensity overfitting in BLBF ERM and demonstrating that SNIPS mitigates it in both theory (equivariance/boundedness) and practice, along with a concrete algorithm.

Quality:
- Technical soundness: The identification of propensity overfitting via Ŝ(h) is compelling and correctly reasoned; Example 1 clearly shows how IPS ERM can select degenerate hypotheses. The SNIPS estimator and its properties (boundedness, equivariance, strong consistency under full support) are standard and correctly stated; the consistency proof is straightforward. The variance estimator for a ratio estimator via normal/delta approximations is reasonable but approximate; the paper is clear that clipping is used to ensure finite variance.
- Claims vs. support: The claim that SNIPS “resolves all problems” of the unbiased estimator is too strong. SNIPS still introduces finite-sample bias and can suffer when denominators are small; clipping trades bias/variance. However, the key anomalies (lack of equivariance and unbounded empirical risk) are indeed addressed, and the experiments support robustness improvements and better generalization.
- Completeness: The work provides a full algorithm (Norm-POEM), training details (L-BFGS), and empirical evaluation, but misses comparisons to strong alternatives (DR, weighted/normalized DR). No formal generalization bound is given for CRM with SNIPS analogous to the bound used to motivate CRM, which weakens the theoretical completeness.

Clarity:
- The paper is generally clear, well-structured, and accessible. The propensity overfitting diagnosis via Ŝ(h) and the translation pathology are explained well. Notation is occasionally heavy but consistent.
- Suggestions:
  - Make explicit earlier that full support of h0 is required for both IPS and SNIPS consistency and discuss practical implications for exploration.
  - Provide a short derivation or appendix details for the gradient of the Norm-POEM objective and practical tricks to avoid small denominators.
  - Clarify hyperparameter selection: which estimator is used to select M, λ on the validation logs, and how selection bias is mitigated (e.g., separate validation logs).
  - Report confidence intervals and effect sizes in tables, and show weight distributions or Ŝ(h) histograms to visualize the mitigation of propensity overfitting.

Originality:
- Moderate. Self-normalized IS is classical, and its use in off-policy evaluation is not new. The novelty lies in: (i) articulating “propensity overfitting” as a distinct ERM pathology in BLBF, (ii) linking it to the lack of equivariance/unboundedness of IPS, and (iii) showing that SNIPS within CRM yields practical and stable learning (Norm-POEM). This framing and empirical demonstration are valuable, even if the estimator itself is not new.

Significance:
- The problem—robust off-policy learning from logs—is important in recommender systems, ads, and contextual bandits. The diagnosis of propensity overfitting is insightful and likely to influence how practitioners design objectives and diagnostics (e.g., tracking Ŝ(h)). Norm-POEM seems easy to adopt for CRF-like models and improves robustness. The lack of comparisons to DR/WDR and absence of real-world logging data limit the immediate impact; nonetheless, the contribution is likely to be used and inspire follow-up work (e.g., normalized DR objectives and theory).

Pros:
- Clear identification and demonstration of a real IPS-ERM pathology (propensity overfitting).
- Simple, principled fix via self-normalization with strong practical benefits.
- Theoretical properties that directly address the anomalies (boundedness, equivariance, consistency).
- Solid empirical gains across multiple datasets; robustness to loss translation; useful diagnostic Ŝ(h) analysis.
- Practical algorithm (Norm-POEM) with code.

Cons:
- Limited novelty in the estimator; missing comparisons to DR/weighted DR and other stabilized IPS variants.
- No new generalization bounds for CRM with SNIPS; variance estimator is approximate.
- Evaluation relies on simulated logs; no real-world datasets; limited ablations (e.g., sensitivity to M, weight distributions).
- Potential instability when denominators are small is not analyzed; guidance on clipping vs. bias is light.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Add baselines: DR, truncated DR, and weighted/normalized DR; discuss when SNIPS vs DR is preferable.
- Provide a CRM-style bound or at least a high-level argument showing how normalization interacts with the empirical Bernstein term to control propensity overfitting.
- Include analyses of the effect of M and the distribution of importance weights; report CIs.
- Clarify model selection protocol and guard against overfitting to logged validation data.

Overall assessment:
A well-argued, practically relevant paper that diagnoses an important failure mode of IPS-based ERM and offers a simple, effective remedy with both theoretical and empirical support. Despite limited novelty in the estimator and missing comparisons to DR baselines, I find the contribution valuable to the NeurIPS audience.

Recommendation: Weak accept.
