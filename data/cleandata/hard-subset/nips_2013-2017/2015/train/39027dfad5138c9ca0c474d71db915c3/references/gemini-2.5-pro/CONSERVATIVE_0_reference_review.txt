**Summary:**
This paper addresses the problem of batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF). The authors identify a specific failure mode of the conventional inverse propensity score (IPS) estimator used in this setting, which they term "Propensity Overfitting". This occurs when an empirical risk minimization (ERM) procedure learns a policy that minimizes the risk estimate by manipulating the importance weights, rather than by fitting the underlying losses. For example, for negative losses, a policy can achieve an arbitrarily low estimated risk by assigning high probability to actions observed in the training data, regardless of their associated loss. To address this, the authors propose replacing the standard IPS estimator with the self-normalized estimator, a well-known ratio estimator from the importance sampling literature. This estimator normalizes the weighted sum of losses by the sum of the importance weights. The authors show that this estimator is equivariant to shifts in the loss function and its estimates are bounded by the range of the observed losses, thus resolving the identified anomalies. They embed this new estimator within the Counterfactual Risk Minimization (CRM) framework to develop a new algorithm, Norm-POEM. Empirical results on several multi-label classification datasets show that Norm-POEM substantially and consistently outperforms POEM, a prior state-of-the-art method based on the standard IPS estimator.

**Strengths:**
1.  **Clear Problem Identification:** The paper's primary contribution is the clear identification and articulation of the "Propensity Overfitting" problem. The toy example in Section 4 is simple, clear, and highly effective at demonstrating how standard ERM over an IPS estimator can lead to degenerate solutions. This is a subtle but important issue that has not been adequately addressed in prior BLBF literature.

2.  **Principled Solution:** The proposed solution is not an ad-hoc fix but is grounded in established statistical principles. The use of the self-normalized estimator as a multiplicative control variate is a standard technique for variance reduction in Monte Carlo estimation. Applying this sound, established method to the newly identified problem in counterfactual learning is a strong and methodologically sound contribution. The properties of the estimator (equivariance, bounded estimates) directly address the weaknesses of the standard IPS estimator.

3.  **Thorough and Convincing Experiments:** The experimental evaluation is a major strength of the paper.
    *   The comparison against POEM is the correct and most informative baseline, as it isolates the effect of the new estimator.
    *   The results in Table 1 and Figure 1 show a clear and consistent performance improvement for Norm-POEM.
    *   Most importantly, the diagnostic experiments in Table 2 provide direct evidence for the paper's central claim. They show that POEM indeed suffers from propensity overfitting (Ŝ(h) deviates significantly from 1) and is sensitive to loss translation, while Norm-POEM is robust to both. This level of targeted validation is commendable and adds significant weight to the authors' conclusions.

**Weaknesses:**
1.  **Limited Novelty of the Estimator:** While the application is novel and insightful, the self-normalized estimator itself is a classic tool in statistics and importance sampling. The paper appropriately cites prior work (e.g., Hesterberg, Kong, Trotter & Tukey), but the core technical machinery is not new. The main novelty lies in identifying the problem in the BLBF context and demonstrating the effectiveness of this existing tool.

2.  **Limited Theoretical Analysis:** The theoretical contribution is modest. Theorem 1 is a restatement of a bound from prior work. Theorem 2 establishes the strong consistency of the self-normalized estimator, which is a standard result and a straightforward application of the Strong Law of Large Numbers. The paper would have been substantially stronger if it provided a more in-depth theoretical analysis, such as finite-sample bounds on the bias and variance of the self-normalized estimator in the context of the CRM objective. The variance estimate used (Equation 8) is an approximation based on the delta method, and a discussion of the implications of this approximation would have been beneficial.

3.  **Scope of Empirical Evaluation:** The experiments are well-controlled but are limited to multi-label classification tasks where the action space is structured but of moderate size. It would be valuable to see if the observed benefits hold in settings with very large, unstructured action spaces or different logging policies, where propensity mismatch could be more extreme.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   The paper identifies a clear and significant flaw in a standard method used for learning from logged bandit feedback.
*   The proposed solution is simple, principled, and rooted in established statistical methods.
*   The empirical evidence is exceptionally strong and directly validates the paper's core hypotheses, showing substantial improvements over a state-of-the-art baseline.
*   The work is well-written, easy to follow, and likely to be impactful for researchers and practitioners working on off-policy evaluation and learning.

**Arguments for Rejection:**
*   The core technical component (the self-normalized estimator) is not novel.
*   The theoretical contribution beyond establishing consistency is limited.

**Conclusion:**
This is a solid and well-executed paper. While the proposed estimator is not new, its application to counterfactual learning is novel, well-motivated, and shown to be highly effective. The key contribution is the clear diagnosis of the "Propensity Overfitting" problem, which is a valuable insight for the community. The empirical validation is rigorous and provides compelling evidence that the proposed method is a significant improvement over prior work. The weaknesses regarding theoretical depth and the novelty of the estimator itself are present but are outweighed by the clarity of the problem formulation and the strength of the empirical results. This paper addresses a real problem with a sound method and demonstrates its value convincingly. I recommend acceptance.
