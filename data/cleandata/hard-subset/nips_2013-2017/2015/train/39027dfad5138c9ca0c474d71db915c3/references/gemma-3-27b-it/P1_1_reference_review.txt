## Peer Review: The Self-Normalized Estimator for Counterfactual Learning

**Summary:**

This paper tackles the problem of batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF), a common scenario in real-world interactive systems. The authors identify a novel issue – "Propensity Overfitting" – in standard counterfactual risk estimation techniques used in this context. They demonstrate that this overfitting leads to biased risk estimates and poor generalization. To address this, they propose a “Self-Normalized Risk Estimator” (SNRE), drawing inspiration from control variate techniques in importance sampling.  They theoretically analyze the SNRE, demonstrating its improved properties, and empirically validate its superior performance on several benchmark datasets compared to existing methods (specifically POEM).

**Relation to Previous Work:**

The paper builds directly upon existing work in BLBF, counterfactual estimation, and off-policy evaluation (references [1, 2, 4, 5, 6]).  It acknowledges the connection to causal inference techniques like propensity score matching [7] and domain adaptation [8].  The authors correctly position their work within the broader landscape of importance sampling and variance reduction techniques [11, 15, 16, 17, 18, 19], specifically highlighting the relevance of self-normalization [12]. The identification of Propensity Overfitting is a novel contribution that clearly distinguishes this work from prior art.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty & Insight:** The identification and formalization of "Propensity Overfitting" is a significant contribution. It explains observed performance issues in BLBF and provides a clear motivation for the proposed solution.
* **Theoretical Justification:** The paper provides a strong theoretical foundation for the SNRE, demonstrating its improved properties compared to standard estimators.  Theorem 2 guaranteeing consistency is a valuable result.
* **Empirical Validation:**  The extensive experimental evaluation on multiple datasets consistently shows that Norm-POEM (the algorithm utilizing the SNRE) outperforms the baseline POEM, even exhibiting faster convergence. The ablation studies (Table 3) providing insights into the necessity of variance regularization are well-designed.
* **Clarity of Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The introduction clearly sets the stage, and the technical details are presented in a logical manner. The examples illustrating Propensity Overfitting (Section 4) are particularly helpful.
* **Practical Implementation & Availability:** The authors provide a link to the software implementing Norm-POEM, which enhances the reproducibility and impact of the work.
* **Equivariance Property:** The observation that the SNRE is equivariant under loss translation is a valuable property that mitigates potential biases.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Computational Complexity:** While the authors mention that Norm-POEM is surprisingly faster than POEM, a more detailed analysis of the computational complexity – particularly the impact of the variance estimation – would be beneficial.  It’s not immediately clear *why* it converges faster.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:**  The paper mentions calibration of hyperparameters (M and lambda) via a validation set. It would be helpful to discuss the sensitivity of the results to these hyperparameters. How robust is the performance to sub-optimal hyperparameter choices?
* **Limited Comparison:** The comparison is primarily with POEM.  While this is a strong baseline, it would be valuable to compare against other relevant BLBF or off-policy evaluation techniques (e.g., doubly robust estimators [6] or recent advancements in importance weighting) to provide a more comprehensive benchmark.
* **Assumption of Full Support:** The theoretical guarantees rely on the assumption that the historical policy has full support over Y. While stated, the practical implications of relaxing this assumption are not discussed.  Real-world datasets often have limited coverage.



**Arguments for and against acceptance:**

**For Acceptance:**

* The paper addresses an important and practically relevant problem in a theoretically sound manner.
* The identification of Propensity Overfitting is a significant insight.
* The empirical results are compelling and consistently demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed approach.
* The code release promotes reproducibility and facilitates future research.

**Against Acceptance:**

* The computational complexity analysis could be more thorough.
* The comparison is limited primarily to one baseline method.
* The assumption of full support may limit the applicability of the theoretical guarantees.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of batch learning from logged bandit feedback. The identification of Propensity Overfitting is a novel and important insight, and the proposed Self-Normalized Estimator effectively addresses this issue. The theoretical analysis and empirical validation are both well-executed. While there are a few minor weaknesses, they do not detract significantly from the overall quality of the work.  I believe this paper is suitable for acceptance to NIPS.

**Recommendation:** **Accept**

**Confidence:** 8/10
