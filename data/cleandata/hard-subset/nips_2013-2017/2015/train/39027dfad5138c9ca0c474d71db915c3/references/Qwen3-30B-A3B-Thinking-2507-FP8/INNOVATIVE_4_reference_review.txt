

# Review of "The Self-Normalized Estimator for Counterfactual Learning"

This paper identifies a fundamental, previously overlooked issue in batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF): "Propensity Overfitting," a novel form of overfitting that plagues conventional counterfactual risk estimators. The authors demonstrate that standard importance sampling-based estimators can produce severely biased risk estimates when used in Empirical Risk Minimization (ERM), particularly when the loss function is negative. They propose a self-normalized estimator using multiplicative control variates that provably avoids this problem, leading to the new Norm-POEM learning algorithm.

The core novelty lies in recognizing Propensity Overfitting as a distinct phenomenon from conventional Loss Overfitting. The paper's theoretical analysis (Section 4) convincingly demonstrates this through carefully constructed examples where the standard estimator fails catastrophically, producing risk estimates arbitrarily lower than the true minimum risk. This is a significant conceptual advance in understanding counterfactual learning limitations. The proposed self-normalized estimator (Equation 7) is elegant and theoretically grounded, with strong consistency guarantees (Theorem 2) and desirable properties like equivariance and boundedness within the loss range.

The authors make a compelling case for the significance of their work by showing how the self-normalized estimator resolves the identified problems while maintaining the benefits of counterfactual learning. The empirical validation (Section 7) across multiple datasets demonstrates consistent improvements in generalization performance over the state-of-the-art POEM algorithm, with Norm-POEM consistently outperforming POEM across all metrics. The experiments also directly validate the theoretical claims by showing that Norm-POEM maintains Ŝ(h) close to 1 (as expected) while POEM exhibits severe bias.

The paper's strengths are substantial: the identification of a previously unrecognized problem is highly novel and valuable, the theoretical justification is rigorous, and the empirical results convincingly demonstrate practical significance. The connection to control variates from importance sampling literature (Section 5) is well-motivated and shows deep understanding of the field. The paper also acknowledges limitations appropriately, noting that variance regularization remains necessary despite the improved estimator.

Minor weaknesses include the computational cost of the self-normalized estimator (though the paper shows it converges faster in practice) and the fact that the theoretical analysis focuses primarily on the estimator itself rather than the full learning algorithm. However, as a reviewer who prioritizes novelty over methodological perfection, these are acceptable trade-offs given the paper's significant conceptual contribution.

This work stands out as a bold conceptual advance rather than an incremental improvement. While counterfactual learning has been studied extensively, the identification of Propensity Overfitting as a distinct problem is genuinely novel and addresses a fundamental limitation in the field. The paper successfully bridges theoretical insights with practical improvements, demonstrating how understanding the problem leads to better algorithms.

The paper's contribution is significant because it fundamentally changes how we think about counterfactual risk estimation in BLBF. The problem identification alone would be valuable, but the authors go further by providing a theoretically sound and empirically validated solution. This work has the potential to influence future research in counterfactual learning, off-policy evaluation, and causal inference.

Compared to previous NIPS work on counterfactual learning (e.g., the POEM paper [1]), this paper makes a clear conceptual leap rather than a methodological refinement. The problem statement is refreshingly original, and the solution is both elegant and effective.

I believe this paper makes a substantial contribution to the field with its novel identification of Propensity Overfitting and the self-normalized estimator solution. The empirical results robustly support the theoretical claims, and the paper is well-written and clearly organized. While the computational aspects could be explored further, the core conceptual contribution is sufficiently significant to warrant acceptance.

**Recommendation: Accept**

**Strengths:**
- Novel identification of Propensity Overfitting as a fundamental problem in BLBF
- Elegant theoretical solution using multiplicative control variates
- Strong theoretical guarantees (equivariance, boundedness, consistency)
- Comprehensive empirical validation showing consistent performance improvements
- Clear differentiation from prior work on importance sampling and counterfactual learning

**Weaknesses:**
- Slightly higher computational cost per iteration (though faster convergence in practice)
- Limited theoretical analysis of the full learning algorithm rather than just the estimator
- Could further explore connections to other variance reduction techniques

The paper's novelty and conceptual contribution outweigh these minor limitations. This is exactly the kind of bold, novel work that advances the field in meaningful ways.
