

# Peer Review: "The Self-Normalized Estimator for Counterfactual Learning"

## Summary
This paper identifies a previously unrecognized problem in batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF), termed "Propensity Overfitting," which occurs when using conventional unbiased risk estimators (e.g., inverse propensity score estimators) for counterfactual risk minimization. The authors propose a self-normalized risk estimator based on multiplicative control variates that provably avoids this degeneracy. They derive a new learning algorithm, Norm-POEM, which replaces the conventional estimator with their proposed method. The paper provides theoretical analysis showing the self-normalized estimator is equivariant and bounded, along with empirical results demonstrating consistent improvements over the previous state-of-the-art (POEM) across multiple benchmark datasets.

## Strengths

**Theoretical Contribution:** The identification of Propensity Overfitting is a significant theoretical contribution. The authors clearly demonstrate how conventional risk estimators can produce arbitrarily low (or high) risk estimates for overfitted hypotheses, unlike supervised learning where overfitting is bounded. The examples in Section 4 are particularly compelling in illustrating this previously unexplored phenomenon.

**Practical Impact:** The self-normalized estimator addresses a critical practical problem in counterfactual learning. The theoretical analysis (Theorem 2) and empirical results (Table 1, Figure 1) consistently show that Norm-POEM outperforms POEM across multiple datasets, with the self-normalized estimator providing robustness to loss translations and avoiding the propensity overfitting problem.

**Clarity and Organization:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The problem statement, theoretical analysis, and empirical results flow logically. The examples illustrating Propensity Overfitting are particularly effective in making a complex concept accessible.

**Empirical Rigor:** The experimental design follows the established Supervised 7→ Bandit methodology from prior work [1, 2]. The authors perform thorough ablation studies (Tables 2-3), showing not only that Norm-POEM outperforms POEM but also that it specifically addresses the Propensity Overfitting problem (via Ŝ(h) values) and that variance regularization remains beneficial.

## Weaknesses

**Theoretical Novelty:** While the identification of Propensity Overfitting is novel, the self-normalized estimator itself is not new in the importance sampling literature [12, 19, 22, 23]. The primary contribution is the application of this established technique to BLBF, which is valuable but represents a more incremental theoretical advance than the paper might suggest. The connection to existing work on self-normalized estimators in importance sampling could be more explicitly discussed.

**Empirical Scope:** The experiments are limited to multi-label classification datasets from the LibSVM repository. While these are standard benchmarks for BLBF, it would strengthen the paper to include results on more diverse data, particularly real-world datasets from ad placement or recommendation systems where BLBF is most commonly applied.

**Computational Efficiency Claim:** The paper claims Norm-POEM is computationally efficient, but the justification is somewhat vague. While it states that "the runtime of Norm-POEM is surprisingly faster than POEM," it doesn't provide concrete timing measurements comparing the two methods. The statement that "optimization tends to converge in fewer iterations" would be more convincing with actual iteration counts and runtime comparisons.

**Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper mentions that hyperparameters M (clipping threshold) and λ (variance regularization strength) are calibrated via counterfactual evaluation on a held-out set, but doesn't discuss how sensitive the method is to these choices. A brief analysis of the sensitivity of performance to these parameters would be valuable.

## Significance and Context

The paper makes a significant contribution to the field of counterfactual learning. The Propensity Overfitting problem is a serious limitation of existing BLBF methods, and the self-normalized estimator provides a simple yet effective solution. The connection between control variates in importance sampling and counterfactual learning is particularly insightful and could inspire future work in related areas.

The paper sits well within the NIPS tradition of addressing fundamental challenges in machine learning with both theoretical rigor and practical impact. It builds on the important work of Swaminathan and Joachims [1] (POEM), but identifies a critical flaw in their approach that had not been previously recognized.

## Overall Assessment

This is a strong paper that makes a clear, novel contribution to the field of counterfactual learning. The identification of Propensity Overfitting is significant, and the proposed solution is theoretically sound and empirically validated. The paper is well-written and addresses a real problem with practical impact.

The main limitations are the somewhat incremental theoretical contribution (the self-normalized estimator is not new) and the limited empirical scope (only multi-label classification datasets). However, these do not undermine the paper's main contribution, which is the identification of Propensity Overfitting and the demonstration that the self-normalized estimator effectively addresses this problem.

## Recommendation

I recommend acceptance. The paper makes a valuable contribution to the field, with clear theoretical insights and strong empirical results. The identification of Propensity Overfitting fills an important gap in our understanding of counterfactual learning, and the proposed solution is both elegant and effective. While the theoretical novelty is somewhat incremental, the practical impact and the clarity with which the problem is articulated make this a significant contribution to the literature.

The authors should address the empirical scope limitation in future work by testing on more diverse datasets, but this is not required for acceptance given the thoroughness of the current experiments. The paper is well-suited for NIPS as it addresses a fundamental challenge in machine learning with both theoretical rigor and practical relevance.

**Summary of Strengths vs. Weaknesses:**

*Strengths:*
- Clear identification and analysis of Propensity Overfitting
- Theoretical proof of desirable properties of self-normalized estimator
- Comprehensive empirical validation across multiple datasets
- Well-written and logically organized

*Weaknesses:*
- Theoretical novelty is somewhat incremental (self-normalized estimator is not new)
- Limited empirical scope (only multi-label classification datasets)
- Vague claims about computational efficiency without concrete timing data

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a meaningful contribution to counterfactual learning.
