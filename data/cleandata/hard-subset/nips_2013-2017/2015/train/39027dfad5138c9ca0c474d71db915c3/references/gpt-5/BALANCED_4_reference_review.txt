Summary and relation to prior work
This paper studies batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF) and identifies a failure mode of standard inverse propensity scoring (IPS) when used inside empirical risk minimization (ERM): propensity overfitting. The core observation is that the conventional (possibly clipped) IPS risk estimator encourages hypotheses to manipulate the sum of importance weights Ŝ(h)=1/n ∑ h(yi|xi)/pi rather than align with low loss, causing degenerate ERM solutions. The paper proposes using a multiplicative control variate—the sum of propensity weights—to construct a self-normalized risk estimator (SNIPS): R̂SN(h)=∑ δi w_i / ∑ w_i with w_i=h(yi|xi)/pi. This estimator is bounded, equivariant to loss translations, and strongly consistent, which alleviates the identified anomalies. Building on CRM (Counterfactual Risk Minimization), the paper introduces Norm-POEM, which replaces IPS with SNIPS within the CRM objective, uses an approximate finite-sample variance estimator for SNIPS, and optimizes a non-convex objective with L-BFGS. Experiments on multilabel classification tasks (Scene, Yeast, TMC, LYRL) under the standard “Supervised→Bandit” protocol show improved generalization relative to POEM, reduced deviation of Ŝ(h) from 1, lower sensitivity to loss translations, and competitive or better runtimes.

The work is grounded in causal/IPS literature (Rosenbaum & Rubin), BLBF/CRM (Swaminathan & Joachims), and off-policy evaluation (Dudík et al., Thomas et al.). The use of self-normalization connects to weighted/normalized importance sampling known in Monte Carlo (Hesterberg; Kong; Owen) and RL off-policy evaluation (e.g., weighted IS/wis; please consider citing Precup, Sutton, and Singh, 2000, for weighted IS in RL). The proposed contribution is to (i) diagnose propensity overfitting as a learning-time pathology of IPS-based ERM and (ii) advocate SNIPS as the default estimator within CRM for both evaluation and learning.

Quality
- Positive: The diagnosis of propensity overfitting is crisp, with a concrete example showing how ERM can select degenerate hypotheses by inflating/deflating weight sums, and a practical diagnostic via Ŝ(h). The shift to SNIPS is well motivated by control variates theory and known benefits when weight variability dominates. SNIPS’s boundedness and equivariance directly target the anomalies. Experiments consistently favor Norm-POEM over POEM across datasets and training sizes, and show that Ŝ(ĥ) stays near 1 for Norm-POEM.
- Concerns: 
  - The theoretical development for learning is limited. While Theorem 2 (consistency) is standard, there is no finite-sample generalization bound for the SNIPS-based CRM objective analogous to Theorem 1 for truncated IPS. Given that SNIPS is biased, a discussion of how its bias appears in a CRM-style bound (and its dependence on M, λ, and weight variability) would materially strengthen the paper.
  - The variance estimator used for SNIPS is approximate and justified via asymptotic normality/delta method. Finite-sample behavior and conditions ensuring concentration would be important, especially since this variance serves as a regularizer.
  - Baselines are incomplete. Doubly robust and weighted/normalized DR estimators (and minimax/regularized IPS approaches) are relevant off-policy learners and should be compared. The paper cites DR and minimax OPE but does not include empirical baselines. This weakens the empirical case for Norm-POEM as a default choice.
  - The assumption of full support is standard but strong; discussion/experiments on partial support (e.g., mixture logging, exploration scavenging, or adding support via smoothing) would increase practical relevance.
  - Some notational and range inconsistencies (δ in [-1,0] in Theorem 1 vs other ranges earlier) should be cleaned up.

Clarity
Overall well written and easy to follow. The narrative from anomaly→diagnosis→control variate→algorithm is clear. Suggestions:
- Clearly define capacity C(H) used in the bound and all assumptions on δ’s range in one place.
- Be explicit about the clipping scheme used with SNIPS (M) and its effect on bias/variance and the CRM penalty.
- Provide pseudo-code for Norm-POEM and the exact objective to improve reproducibility.

Originality
The self-normalized estimator itself is well known in IS/OPE; the novelty lies in identifying “propensity overfitting” as a learning-time failure mode, systematically using Ŝ(h) as a diagnostic, and integrating SNIPS into a CRM learning objective for structured prediction. This is an incremental but meaningful contribution that clarifies a practical pitfall and offers a principled fix.

Significance
BLBF is broadly important in recommendation, ads, and interactive systems. Showing that normalization stabilizes ERM and improves generalization is practically impactful. However, the significance would be stronger with:
- Comparisons to DR and weighted DR learners.
- Tests under harsher propensities (heavy-tailed weights) and partial support.
- Additional domains (e.g., contextual bandits beyond multilabel CRFs) to demonstrate breadth.

Pros for acceptance
- Clear identification and explanation of a real failure mode (propensity overfitting).
- Principled, simple fix grounded in control variates with strong intuitive benefits (boundedness, equivariance).
- Consistent empirical improvements and diagnostics aligning with theory.
- Practical algorithmic instantiation with competitive runtime.

Cons for acceptance
- Lack of finite-sample generalization guarantees for the SNIPS-based CRM objective.
- Missing strong baselines (DR/weighted DR/minimax IPS), limiting claims of superiority.
- Limited evaluation scope (single hypothesis class and domain).
- Reliance on approximate variance and asymptotics without a rigorous analysis of regularization effects.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide a finite-sample bound for SNIPS-based CRM, quantifying bias and variance terms and showing how the variance penalty controls generalization.
- Add baselines: (weighted) DR estimators for learning, minimax/regularized IPS, and ablate clipping M and λ.
- Evaluate under partial support and high weight variance; report diagnostics (effective sample size, distribution of normalized weights).
- Clarify assumptions and notation; add missing references (e.g., Precup et al. on weighted IS in RL).
- Include additional domains/hypothesis classes to demonstrate robustness.

Overall recommendation: Weak accept. The paper offers a clear conceptual advance and practical win by diagnosing propensity overfitting and adopting self-normalization within CRM. Strengthening theory and baselines would make this a solid accept. Reviewer confidence: medium-high.
