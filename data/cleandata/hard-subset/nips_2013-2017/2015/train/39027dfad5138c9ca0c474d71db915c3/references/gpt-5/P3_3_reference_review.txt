Summary
The paper tackles batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF) and identifies a concrete failure mode of inverse propensity scoring (IPS) when used as an ERM objective: propensity overfitting. The core insight is that the IPS empirical risk can be driven to extreme values by manipulating the sum of importance weights rather than truly aligning with low loss. The authors propose a principled fix using a multiplicative control variate: self-normalized importance sampling (SNIPS). They show that (i) the sum of propensity weights Ŝ(h) has expectation 1, (ii) using Ŝ(h) as a control variate yields a self-normalized risk estimator that is bounded and equivariant to translations of the loss, and (iii) the estimator is strongly consistent. They then instantiate CRM with SNIPS to create Norm-POEM, present an approximate variance estimator for the ratio estimator, and empirically demonstrate improved generalization and robustness over POEM across multilabel datasets in a supervised-to-bandit simulation.

Relation to prior work
The paper is grounded in CRM (Swaminathan & Joachims), importance weighting under covariate shift, and off-policy evaluation. The proposed estimator is the well-known weighted (self-normalized) importance sampling estimator from Monte Carlo, with known advantages when weight variance dominates reward variance (Hesterberg, Owen, Kong). The work connects the diagnostic E[Ŝ]=1 to detect/penalize propensity overfitting and integrates SNIPS into BLBF learning, contrasting with prior IPS-based learning and variance regularization, and complementary to additive control variates like doubly robust (Dudík et al.). The paper cites relevant work; however, comparisons to DR-style learners are missing.

Strengths
- Clear identification and articulation of propensity overfitting, with simple, convincing examples showing the pathologies of IPS-based ERM (unboundedness, non-equivariance) that persist under loss translations.
- The use of SNIPS as a multiplicative control variate is principled, addresses the exact anomalies (boundedness and translation equivariance), and the strong consistency argument is correct.
- Integrating SNIPS into CRM (Norm-POEM) is natural; the approximate variance estimator for the ratio estimator is standard and appropriate.
- Empirical evidence shows consistent improvements over POEM on multiple datasets, robustness to loss translation, and better alignment of Ŝ(ĥ) with its expectation 1—directly supporting the “propensity overfitting” diagnosis.
- Practical observations on optimization (LBFGS convergence, smaller parameter norms) are useful to practitioners.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Theoretical novelty is modest. SNIPS is classical; the main new theoretical component is the diagnosis of propensity overfitting plus the observation that SNIPS fixes the identified anomalies. There is no new finite-sample generalization bound specialized to SNIPS-based CRM, nor concentration results for the ratio estimator in this learning setting.
- The CRM bound used (Theorem 1) is inherited from IPS; the paper does not provide an analogous bound for the SNIPS objective. Since the training objective changes materially, a learning-theoretic justification beyond asymptotics would strengthen the contribution.
- The variance estimator used for the CRM regularizer is an asymptotic approximation. The paper lacks discussion of its bias/variance in finite samples and how clipping is incorporated in the self-normalized setting (is the denominator also clipped?). Clarifying the exact estimator used in experiments (clipped/unclipped SNIPS) is important.
- Baselines are limited. Missing are DR and weighted DR learners, clipped-IPS + regularization variants, and modern counterfactual learning algorithms that combine clipping/switching with DR (even if not all were contemporaneous, DR was). Without these, it is hard to conclude that SNIPS is the preferred fix versus additive control variates.
- Experimental scope is narrow. The setting is supervised-to-bandit with multilabel Hamming loss and relatively small action spaces (factorized assumption). More realistic high-variance logging (heavier-tailed propensities), larger action spaces, and non-factorized structures would better test SNIPS’ purported advantage. Reporting wall-clock times would also substantiate the efficiency claims.
- Assumptions: full support logging and i.i.d. samples may be unrealistic in practice; the paper could discuss sensitivity to support violations and exploration rates.

Quality
Technically sound overall. The diagnosis and proposed estimator are correct; the consistency result is standard. However, the lack of finite-sample learning guarantees for Norm-POEM and limited baselines reduce the strength of the claims. Experimental methodology otherwise mirrors prior work and is carefully executed.

Clarity
Generally well-written and organized. The propensity overfitting examples are clear and compelling. Suggestions:
- Precisely state whether SNIPS weights are clipped in both numerator and denominator in practice.
- Provide a short derivation or clearer reference for the variance approximation, and discuss when it is reliable.
- Explicitly connect the CRM penalty to Ŝ(h) fluctuations to better justify the chosen regularizer for SNIPS.

Originality
Moderate. The estimator itself is not new; the main novelty is recognizing and formalizing propensity overfitting in BLBF ERM and integrating SNIPS into CRM learning. This is a useful, if incremental, contribution.

Significance
Moderate to high for practitioners of counterfactual learning from logs: SNIPS is easy to adopt, directly addresses a real failure mode, and empirically improves robustness. The absence of DR baselines and theory for the new objective tempers the impact.

Questions for the authors
- Do you clip the SNIPS weights in both numerator and denominator? How sensitive are results to M?
- How does Norm-POEM compare to DR-based learners and to IPS with clipping plus stronger regularization?
- Can you provide any finite-sample concentration or stability result for the SNIPS-based CRM objective (perhaps via self-normalized concentration inequalities)?
- What happens under support mismatch or very peaky logging policies?

Suggestions for improvement
- Include DR and clipped-IPS baselines; report wall-clock time.
- Add sensitivity analyses for M, λ, and loss scaling.
- Clarify implementation details of the variance regularizer and its role in preventing residual overfitting.
- Discuss applicability to larger, structured action spaces and to real logs.

Pros
- Identifies and explains a real pathology of IPS-based ERM.
- Proposes a simple, principled fix with clear benefits (boundedness, equivariance).
- Solid empirical gains and diagnostics aligned with theory.

Cons
- Limited theoretical development for the new training objective.
- Missing strong baselines (DR).
- Narrow experimental scope and incomplete reporting on optimization/runtime.

Recommendation
Weak accept. The paper makes a practical, well-motivated improvement to counterfactual learning objectives with clear empirical benefits, despite modest novelty and missing baselines. With added comparisons and clarifications, it would be a solid contribution.
