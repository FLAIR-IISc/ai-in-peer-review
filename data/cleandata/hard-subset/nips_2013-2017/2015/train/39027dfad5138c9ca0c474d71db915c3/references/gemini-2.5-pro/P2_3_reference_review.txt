### Paper Summary

This paper addresses the problem of Batch Learning from Logged Bandit Feedback (BLBF), a setting crucial for improving interactive systems using historical data. The authors identify a novel failure mode in standard counterfactual learning algorithms based on Inverse Propensity Score (IPS) estimators, which they term "Propensity Overfitting." This occurs when an Empirical Risk Minimization (ERM) procedure minimizes the risk estimate not by learning a good policy, but by exploiting the structure of the propensity weights—for instance, by assigning high probability mass to training examples with large weights when losses are negative.

To remedy this, the paper proposes replacing the standard IPS estimator with the Self-Normalized Estimator (SNE), a well-known ratio estimator from the importance sampling literature. The authors argue and demonstrate that the SNE is equivariant to translations of the loss function and its estimates are naturally bounded by the range of observed losses, thus preventing the degenerate solutions characteristic of Propensity Overfitting. They incorporate this estimator into the Counterfactual Risk Minimization (CRM) framework, resulting in a new learning algorithm called Norm-POEM. Through a series of well-controlled experiments on multi-label classification datasets, they show that Norm-POEM significantly outperforms its predecessor (POEM), is more robust to transformations of the loss function, and successfully avoids Propensity Overfitting, leading to better generalization.

### Review

#### Quality

The paper is of high technical quality. The claims are well-supported by a combination of theoretical reasoning, an insightful illustrative example, and strong empirical evidence.

*   **Soundness:** The identification of "Propensity Overfitting" is a key contribution and is convincingly demonstrated in Example 1. The logic is sound: the standard IPS estimator is not equivariant to loss translations, and its value is unbounded, which creates opportunities for a flexible hypothesis class to find degenerate solutions that have little to do with the true risk. The proposed solution, using the self-normalized estimator, is principled. The properties of the SNE (equivariance, boundedness, consistency) are correctly stated and directly address the identified weaknesses of the standard estimator.
*   **Completeness:** The work is a complete and self-contained contribution. It diagnoses a problem, proposes a solution, integrates it into a state-of-the-art framework (CRM), and provides a thorough empirical evaluation. The experiments are particularly well-designed, testing not just for improved generalization but also directly verifying the claims about avoiding propensity overfitting (by inspecting Ŝ(h)) and robustness to loss-shifting.
*   **Honesty:** The authors are careful to evaluate their work. They acknowledge that the self-normalized estimator itself is not new, but its application to this specific problem is. They also fairly evaluate the continued need for variance regularization (Table 3), showing that the SNE is not a silver bullet but rather a better foundation for the CRM principle. One minor point is that the paper relies on the original CRM generalization bound (Theorem 1), which was derived for the standard capped IPS estimator. While the CRM principle is used as a motivation, a direct theoretical analysis of a generalization bound for the proposed Norm-POEM objective, which uses a ratio estimator, would further strengthen the work, though its absence does not detract significantly from the paper's core message.

#### Clarity

The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The narrative flows logically from the general problem of BLBF to the specific issue of Propensity Overfitting, and then to the proposed solution and its validation.

*   **Organization:** The structure is clear and easy to follow. The introduction and related work sections effectively situate the paper within the broader context of off-policy evaluation and learning. Section 4, which introduces Propensity Overfitting, is the highlight of the paper, using a simple yet powerful example to build intuition. The transition to the self-normalized estimator in Section 5 feels natural and well-motivated.
*   **Readability:** The mathematical notation is standard and consistent. The concepts are explained with admirable clarity, making a potentially dense topic accessible. The experimental section is transparent about its setup (the "Supervised → Bandit" methodology), allowing for reproducibility and clear interpretation of the results.

#### Originality

The originality of this paper is strong, though it lies more in the problem formulation and the novel application of existing tools than in the invention of entirely new techniques.

*   **Novelty:** The primary novel contribution is the identification, naming, and formalization of "Propensity Overfitting." While experienced practitioners may have encountered similar phenomena, this paper crystallizes the concept, explains its cause, and provides a clear diagnostic (the deviation of Ŝ(h) from 1). This is a significant conceptual contribution to the field of counterfactual learning.
*   **Relation to Prior Work:** The paper does an excellent job of citing related work and positioning its contribution. It correctly identifies the SNE as a classic technique in Monte Carlo methods but argues for its novel use in mitigating this specific overfitting pathology in a learning context. The work is presented as a direct improvement upon the POEM algorithm [1], which is appropriate and clearly delineates its place in the literature.

#### Significance

The results presented in this paper are highly significant and likely to have a substantial impact on both research and practice in counterfactual learning.

*   **Impact:** The problem of BLBF is of immense practical importance for web search, recommendation, and online advertising. The standard IPS estimator is the foundational tool in this area. By identifying a critical and subtle failure mode and providing a simple, robust, and effective fix, this paper offers a valuable contribution. The proposed Norm-POEM algorithm demonstrates substantially better and more stable performance than its predecessor, making it a strong candidate for practical deployment.
*   **Advancement:** The paper clearly advances the state of the art. It moves beyond simply managing the variance of counterfactual estimators (e.g., via clipping or doubly robust methods) to address a more fundamental structural problem with how these estimators interact with ERM. The insights about equivariance and boundedness will likely influence the design of future off-policy learning algorithms.

#### Arguments for Acceptance:

1.  **Novel and Insightful Problem Formulation:** The paper identifies and clearly articulates "Propensity Overfitting," a previously under-appreciated but critical failure mode for counterfactual learning.
2.  **Principled and Effective Solution:** The proposed use of the self-normalized estimator is an elegant solution that directly addresses the identified problem's root causes (lack of equivariance and unboundedness).
3.  **Strong Empirical Validation:** The experiments are thorough, well-controlled, and convincingly demonstrate that the proposed method (Norm-POEM) not only achieves better generalization but also specifically fixes the propensity overfitting issue.
4.  **High Clarity and Readability:** The paper is exceptionally well-written, making a complex topic accessible and the contributions easy to understand.
5.  **Significant Practical Implications:** The work provides a more robust and reliable algorithm for the important problem of learning from logged bandit data, which could be immediately useful to practitioners.

#### Arguments for Rejection:

1.  **Limited Novelty of the Estimator:** The core component, the self-normalized estimator, is a well-established technique. The novelty is in its application and analysis in this context. (This is a very minor point, as the application is highly insightful).
2.  **Computational Scalability:** The paper notes that the variance estimate for Norm-POEM is not amenable to stochastic optimization. While they observe faster convergence in practice with L-BFGS, this might limit its applicability on extremely large-scale datasets where SGD is the only feasible option.

**Recommendation:** Strong Accept. The paper makes a clear, insightful, and significant contribution to the field of counterfactual learning. Its strengths far outweigh its minor weaknesses.
