This paper addresses the problem of batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF), identifying a novel issue termed “Propensity Overfitting” in the standard counterfactual risk estimator. The authors propose a Self-Normalized Risk Estimator, theoretically motivated and empirically validated, to mitigate this overfitting and improve generalization performance.

The paper is well-structured and clearly motivated. It correctly identifies the limitations of existing counterfactual estimators in the BLBF setting and proposes a sensible solution based on control variates. The connection to existing literature in causal inference, off-policy evaluation, and importance sampling is properly established. The theoretical analysis, while concise, provides a convincing argument for the benefits of the proposed estimator. The experimental setup closely follows previous work (POEM) allowing for a direct comparison, and the results demonstrate the superiority of Norm-POEM across various datasets. The inclusion of ablation studies (e.g., examining the necessity of variance regularization) strengthens the empirical evaluation.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The identification of “Propensity Overfitting” as a distinct issue in BLBF is a valuable contribution. The self-normalized estimator provides a novel way to address this problem.
*   **Theoretical Soundness:** The paper provides theoretical justification for the proposed estimator, linking it to existing results on variance reduction techniques and providing a theorem demonstrating consistency.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and clearly explains complex concepts. The notation is consistent and the arguments are easy to follow.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The experiments are well-designed and provide strong evidence for the effectiveness of the proposed approach, consistently outperforming the baseline (POEM).
*   **Practical Relevance:** The problem of learning from logged data is highly relevant in many real-world applications (e.g., recommender systems, advertising).

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Computational Complexity:** While the authors claim Norm-POEM is computationally efficient, the discussion of the computational cost is somewhat superficial. A more detailed analysis, especially compared to existing methods, would be beneficial. The practical implications of the need for L-BFGS instead of stochastic gradient descent could be expanded on.
*   **Limited Scope of Experiments:** The experiments focus primarily on synthetic datasets constructed using a CRF as the logging policy (h0). While this allows for a controlled comparison, it's unclear how well the proposed method generalizes to more complex scenarios with different data distributions and policy structures.
*   **Dependence on Full Support:** The theoretical guarantees rely on the assumption of full support of the logging policy (h0).  A discussion of the robustness of the method when this assumption is violated would be valuable.
*   **Variance Estimation Details:** The use of an *approximate* variance estimate for the self-normalized risk estimator is mentioned, but a more detailed justification for this approximation and its potential impact on the theoretical guarantees would be helpful.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper addresses an important and challenging problem in machine learning with a novel and theoretically sound approach.
*   The empirical results are compelling and demonstrate the practical benefits of the proposed method.
*   The paper is exceptionally well-written and clearly presents its contributions.
*   The identified "Propensity Overfitting" is a potentially significant issue in BLBF, and addressing it is a valuable contribution to the field.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

*   The computational cost discussion is limited.
*   The experimental evaluation primarily relies on synthetic datasets.
*   The assumption of full support for the logging policy may limit the applicability of the method in certain real-world scenarios.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of batch learning from logged bandit feedback. The identification and mitigation of “Propensity Overfitting” is a novel and important result, and the proposed self-normalized estimator is well-motivated and empirically validated. Despite minor weaknesses related to computational complexity, experimental scope, and theoretical assumptions, the strengths of the paper significantly outweigh its weaknesses.

**Recommendation:**

Strong Accept.
