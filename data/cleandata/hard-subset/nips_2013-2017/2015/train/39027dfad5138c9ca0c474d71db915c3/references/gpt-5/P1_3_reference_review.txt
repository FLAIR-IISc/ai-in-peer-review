Summary
This paper addresses learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF) by identifying a new failure mode of inverse propensity scoring (IPS)-based empirical risk minimization (ERM), which the authors term propensity overfitting. They show that the standard (clipped) IPS estimator used in CRM (Counterfactual Risk Minimization) can incentivize hypotheses that (i) overweight or (ii) avoid the logged actions, leading to degenerate empirical risks that are not bounded by the loss range and are not translation-equivariant. To mitigate this, the paper proposes using a multiplicative control variate—the sum of importance weights—to derive the self-normalized estimator (SNIPS) for counterfactual risk, yielding a new learning objective, Norm-POEM (a modified version of POEM), that couples the SN estimator with a variance penalty in the CRM spirit. The paper proves strong consistency of the estimator, highlights desirable properties (boundedness, equivariance), provides an approximate variance estimator for the ratio estimator, and reports empirical gains across multilabel classification datasets produced via a supervised-to-bandit protocol. The method improves generalization vs. POEM and shows robustness to loss translations, aligning with theory.

Relation to prior work
The work is grounded in counterfactual evaluation/learning and importance sampling (Rosenbaum & Rubin; Dudík et al.; Bottou et al.; Cortes et al.; Li et al.; Thomas et al.). Using self-normalized importance sampling (Hesterberg; Owen; Rubinstein & Kroese; Kong) is classic in evaluation, and the novelty here is deploying it inside an ERM/CRM learning objective to prevent learning-induced weight pathologies. The paper positions itself relative to clipping, regression/additive control-variates (DR), and discusses variance reduction and defensive mixtures. The “propensity overfitting” phenomenon is a clear conceptual contribution that connects importance-weight fluctuations to selection bias during learning.

Strengths
- Clear identification and formalization of a practically important failure mode (propensity overfitting) with an intuitive, compelling toy example and diagnostics via S(h) = average weight sum.
- Using a multiplicative control variate to derive a self-normalized counterfactual risk for learning is natural, principled, and leads to immediate improvements: bounded estimates, translation equivariance, and strong consistency.
- The proposed Norm-POEM is simple to implement (same hypothesis class as POEM/CRFs), uses familiar regularization knobs (clipping M, variance penalty λ), and appears computationally practical.
- Experiments across several multilabel benchmarks demonstrate consistent and nontrivial improvements over POEM, especially robustness to loss translation and better calibration of S(h).
- The exposition is generally clear and well-situated within the literature.

Weaknesses and questions
- Theory: While the paper proves consistency and discusses desirable properties, it does not provide a finite-sample generalization bound for the SN-based CRM objective, analogous to the empirical-Bernstein style bounds used to motivate CRM for IPS. The objective’s variance penalty (Eq. 8) is derived via asymptotic normal approximations/delta method, but a high-probability bound (even if looser) for the ratio estimator would strengthen the learning justification.
- Claims such as “provably avoids degeneracies” are accurate for anomalies like unboundedness and non-equivariance, but a stronger statement about propensity overfitting (beyond the qualitative argument and diagnostics) would benefit from a formal finite-sample guarantee or impossibility discussion.
- Baselines: The empirical comparison is limited to POEM and a CRF skyline. Given the focus on robustness, comparisons to doubly robust (DR) learning baselines, weighted/normalized DR (WDR), defensive mixture/weight stabilization, or other off-policy learning schemes (e.g., cost-sensitive reductions like Offset Tree) are important to contextualize gains.
- Experimental scope: All results are in supervised-to-bandit simulations with a softmax behavior policy. It would be valuable to test sensitivity to coverage (support gaps, heavier-tailed propensities), different logging stochasticity/quality beyond replay count, and ideally some real logged data (even small-scale).
- Hyperparameter tuning and validation: Tuning λ and M by counterfactual evaluation on held-out logs is standard, but can itself be estimator-dependent; more details on the validation estimator (SN vs IPS, clipping) and sensitivity analyses would help.
- Minor clarity/typos: In Sec. 4, “δ(·, ·) ∈ [5,4]” appears to be a typo. Please clean up small inconsistencies and provide pseudocode for Norm-POEM for reproducibility.

Quality
Technically sound overall, with correct properties of SNIPS and a convincing diagnosis of IPS-based ERM anomalies. The main theoretical gap is the absence of a finite-sample generalization bound for the proposed objective; the variance penalty is heuristic/asymptotic. Empirical results are coherent and support claims, but a stronger baseline set and sensitivity analyses would increase confidence.

Clarity
Well organized and readable. The core ideas are explained cleanly, the example is illuminating, and the connection to control variates is well made. Providing more implementation details (gradient expressions, complexity) and pseudocode would further improve reproducibility.

Originality
Self-normalized IS is not new, but applying it inside a CRM learning objective to directly combat propensity overfitting is a meaningful and timely contribution. The identification and articulation of “propensity overfitting” as a distinct failure mode in BLBF learning is novel and insightful.

Significance
Addressing robustness in counterfactual learning is important for practical deployment. The improvements are consistent and the method is simple enough to be adopted. With stronger theory and broader empirical comparisons, the impact could be substantial.

Suggestions for improvement
- Derive a finite-sample generalization bound (even under clipping) for the SN-based objective, or adapt existing high-probability analyses for ratio estimators to the CRM setting.
- Add baselines: DR-based learners (and normalized DR), defensive mixtures, and cost-sensitive reductions; include stronger ablations on M and λ.
- Explore settings with poorer support and larger action spaces; add real logged datasets if possible.
- Provide pseudocode and additional implementation details; include error bars.
- Clarify the validation/evaluation procedure and estimator used for hyperparameter tuning; discuss potential selection bias.

Arguments for acceptance
- Identifies and addresses a real and underappreciated failure mode in BLBF learning.
- Proposes a simple, principled fix with strong intuitive and empirical support.
- Consistent improvements and robustness across datasets; practical and computationally reasonable.
- Clear exposition and connection to established variance reduction methods.

Arguments against acceptance
- Lacks a finite-sample generalization theory for the proposed objective; variance penalty is asymptotic.
- Limited baselines; no comparison to DR/WDR or other robust off-policy learners.
- Evaluation restricted to simulated bandit logs; limited sensitivity analyses.

Overall recommendation
Weak accept. The paper offers a clear conceptual advance and a practical method with compelling empirical benefits. Strengthening theory and expanding baselines would make it even more impactful.
