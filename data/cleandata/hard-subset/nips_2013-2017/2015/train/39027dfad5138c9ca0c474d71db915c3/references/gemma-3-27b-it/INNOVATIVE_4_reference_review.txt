This paper tackles a critical issue in learning from logged data: propensity overfitting, a novel form of overfitting arising from the use of importance sampling-based counterfactual risk estimation. The core idea – leveraging a self-normalized estimator to mitigate this overfitting – is both insightful and theoretically grounded. While the empirical evaluation isn't exhaustive, the consistent performance gains across several datasets, coupled with the compelling diagnostics demonstrating the bias in standard estimators, makes this a highly promising contribution. I find the identification of this new overfitting phenomenon and the proposed solution particularly noteworthy.

**Summary:** The authors identify a previously unrecognized issue, "propensity overfitting," when applying standard importance sampling techniques for off-policy evaluation (and consequently, batch learning from logged bandit feedback). This overfitting isn’t tied to fitting the loss function itself, but to exploiting the propensity weights, especially when they are low. They propose and theoretically justify a self-normalized estimator to address this, and empirically demonstrate improved performance and robustness.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The identification of propensity overfitting is a significant contribution. This is not a subtle bug fix; it reveals a fundamental limitation of existing methods in a burgeoning area of research.
*   **Theoretical Soundness:** The paper provides a clear theoretical justification for the self-normalized estimator and its advantage in avoiding the identified overfitting. The connection to existing work on control variates is well-articulated.
*   **Practical Relevance:** Batch learning from logged data is becoming increasingly important. A more robust estimator directly addresses a real-world challenge.
*   **Clear Presentation:** The paper is well-structured and the core ideas are explained clearly, despite the technical complexity of the topic.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The experiments, while not extensive, consistently demonstrate the benefits of the proposed approach, and the diagnostic experiments clearly showcase the problem it solves.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Limited Scope of Experiments:** While the results are positive, the evaluation is limited to a set of relatively standard datasets. Exploring more complex, real-world datasets would strengthen the claims.
*   **Computational Complexity:** The discussion of computational complexity is a bit hand-wavy. A more detailed analysis of the computational overhead introduced by the self-normalized estimator would be beneficial. While they state runtimes are competitive, a more rigorous comparison is warranted.
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The impact of the clipping parameter *M* on performance is not fully explored. A more detailed sensitivity analysis would be helpful.
*   **Minor Clarity Issues:** The notation can be a little dense at times, and a slightly more pedagogical approach to the mathematical derivations could enhance accessibility. For instance, clarifying why the conventional estimator *isn't* equivariant earlier on would be useful.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper introduces a novel and important problem (propensity overfitting) in a rapidly growing area of research.
*   The proposed solution (self-normalized estimator) is theoretically sound and empirically validated.
*   The paper is well-written and clearly presents a complex topic.
*   The potential impact on the field is significant.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The empirical evaluation is limited in scope.
*   The computational complexity analysis is not fully detailed.
*   The paper doesn't explore the full range of potential applications and extensions.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that identifies a critical issue and proposes a compelling solution. The novelty of the identified problem and the elegance of the proposed solution outweigh the minor weaknesses in the empirical evaluation and computational analysis. While more extensive experimentation would be ideal, the existing results are sufficient to demonstrate the value of this contribution. I believe this work has the potential to significantly impact the field of learning from logged data and off-policy evaluation.

**Recommendation:** Accept. I would encourage the authors to consider expanding the empirical evaluation in future work and providing a more detailed analysis of the computational complexity.
