### Summary

This paper addresses the problem of batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF), a crucial setting for improving interactive systems like search and recommendation. The authors identify a specific and previously unarticulated failure mode of standard counterfactual risk minimization, which they term "Propensity Overfitting". This occurs when a high-capacity model minimizes the inverse propensity scored (IPS) risk estimate not by learning a good policy, but by exploiting the propensity weights themselves—either by assigning high probability to training examples (for negative losses) or by avoiding them entirely (for positive losses), leading to degenerate and severely biased risk estimates.

As a remedy, the paper proposes replacing the standard IPS estimator with the Self-Normalized (SN) importance sampling estimator. This classic ratio estimator normalizes the weighted losses by the sum of the importance weights. The authors show that the SN estimator's desirable properties—namely, that its estimates are bounded within the range of the observed losses and are equivariant to affine transformations of the loss—directly counteract Propensity Overfitting. They incorporate this estimator into the Counterfactual Risk Minimization (CRM) framework to create a new learning algorithm, Norm-POEM. Extensive experiments on several multi-label classification datasets demonstrate that Norm-POEM consistently and significantly outperforms its predecessor (POEM), while also being more robust and computationally efficient in practice.

### Strengths

1.  **Novel and Important Problem Formulation:** The key contribution of this paper is the identification and clear articulation of "Propensity Overfitting". While the high variance of IPS estimators is well-known, this work provides a sharp new perspective by framing it as an overfitting problem that can be exploited by expressive models during empirical risk minimization. The example in Section 4 is exceptionally clear and powerful, illustrating how ERM can lead to pathological solutions with arbitrarily low estimated risk, a phenomenon distinct from standard "loss overfitting". This is a significant conceptual insight for the field of off-policy learning.

2.  **Principled and Elegant Solution:** The proposed solution is not an ad-hoc heuristic but is based on the well-established self-normalized estimator from the Monte Carlo literature. The paper does an excellent job of connecting the theoretical properties of this estimator (equivariance, boundedness) directly to the identified problem. By showing that the SN estimator is immune to the pathologies that cause Propensity Overfitting, the authors make a compelling case for its adoption in BLBF learning settings.

3.  **Strong and Convincing Empirical Validation:** The experimental evaluation is thorough and well-designed.
    *   **Performance:** Norm-POEM demonstrates substantial and consistent gains in generalization performance over the baseline POEM across multiple datasets and data sizes (Table 1, Figure 1).
    *   **Diagnostics:** The experiments go beyond simply showing better performance. The results in Table 2 provide direct evidence for the paper's central claims. They show that Norm-POEM successfully keeps the sum of propensity weights (Ŝ(h)) centered around its expectation of 1, while POEM does not. Crucially, the experiments on loss translation confirm the SN estimator's equivariance and the brittleness of the standard IPS estimator, which fails catastrophically on some datasets after a simple loss shift.
    *   **Efficiency:** The finding that Norm-POEM converges faster than POEM is a valuable practical result, mitigating concerns about the more complex per-iteration cost of the SN estimator's variance term.

4.  **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow. The motivation is clear, the problem is explained with a simple yet powerful example, the solution is logically derived, and the experimental results are presented effectively.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Limited Comparison to Alternative Variance Reduction Methods:** The paper focuses on comparing Norm-POEM with the vanilla IPS estimator (as used in POEM). However, another major class of estimators for off-policy evaluation are the Doubly Robust (DR) estimators, which use a learned reward model as an additive control variate. While mentioned in related work, a deeper discussion or an experimental comparison would have strengthened the paper. It would be interesting to analyze whether DR methods are also susceptible to a form of Propensity Overfitting or if the learned reward model provides a form of regularization against it. This is not a fatal flaw but would have provided a more complete picture of the landscape.

2.  **Scalability and Optimization:** The paper notes that the variance term for the SN estimator does not admit stochastic optimization and relies instead on L-BFGS. While the experiments show this is effective and even faster on the tested datasets, this might become a bottleneck for truly massive-scale industrial datasets where stochastic gradient methods are often preferred. A brief discussion on the challenges and potential avenues for developing a scalable, stochastic optimization algorithm for the Norm-POEM objective would be a valuable addition.

### Overall Assessment

This is an excellent paper that makes a clear, significant, and well-supported contribution to the field of counterfactual learning. The identification of Propensity Overfitting is a novel insight that clarifies a critical weakness of a widely used class of methods. The proposed solution is simple, principled, and shown to be highly effective. The paper is technically sound, clearly written, and the results are likely to be of high interest to both researchers and practitioners working on learning from logged data. The strengths heavily outweigh the minor weaknesses, which are primarily avenues for future work. I strongly recommend acceptance.
