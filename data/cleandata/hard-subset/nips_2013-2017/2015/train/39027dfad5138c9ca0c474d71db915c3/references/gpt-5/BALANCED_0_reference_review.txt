Summary and relation to prior work
The paper addresses batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF) via counterfactual risk minimization (CRM). It identifies a failure mode of the standard inverse propensity scoring (IPS) estimator used as a learning objective, which the authors term propensity overfitting: the learner exploits variability in importance weights rather than true loss structure, leading to pathological ERM solutions (e.g., unboundedly low empirical risk for negative losses, or trivial “avoid-the-logs” solutions after loss translation). The paper proposes replacing IPS with the self-normalized importance sampling (SNIS) estimator as the training objective, yielding a bounded, shift-equivariant, and strongly consistent estimator. The sum of normalized weights Ŝ(h) is used as a diagnostic (its expectation is 1), and the paper argues that large deviations flag propensity overfitting. Building on prior CRM work (POEM), the authors instantiate Norm-POEM by combining SNIS with a variance-penalized objective and a linear-exponential family hypothesis (CRF-like model), optimized by L-BFGS. Experiments on multi-label datasets using the supervised-to-bandit protocol show improved generalization and robustness to loss translation compared to POEM.

This work sits at the intersection of causal inference/IPS, domain adaptation, and off-policy evaluation. It connects to known use of self-normalization in importance sampling (Hesterberg; Kong; Rubinstein & Kroese) and high-confidence OPE (Thomas et al.), as well as doubly robust and regression estimators (Dudík et al.). The CRM framework and POEM are directly built upon (Swaminathan & Joachims, ICML).

Strengths
- Clear identification and demonstration (with a crisp example) of a learning-time failure mode (propensity overfitting) that is not apparent from standard IPS variance analyses; the Ŝ(h) diagnostic is well-motivated.
- Simple, principled fix via multiplicative control variate (SNIS) that confers desirable properties: boundedness, shift equivariance, and strong consistency; aligns with best practices in IS when weight fluctuations dominate.
- Practical algorithm (Norm-POEM) with minimal changes to POEM; code availability; uses a tractable exponential-family model and a variance-regularized objective.
- Empirical results consistently favor Norm-POEM across datasets and training set sizes; robustness to loss translation demonstrated; improved stability (Ŝ≈1) and faster convergence behavior are compelling.

Weaknesses and questions
Quality
- Finite-sample theory for the SNIS-based CRM objective is missing. The generalization bound in prior CRM work applies to IPS; here, you use an approximate variance for a ratio estimator without providing a corresponding uniform bound or high-probability guarantee. This weakens the theoretical story relative to POEM.
- The combination of clipping with self-normalization is mentioned to ensure finite variance, but the induced bias is not analyzed. Guidance on bias-variance tradeoffs for M under SNIS would be valuable.
- Baselines are limited to POEM and an unregularized variant. Absent are comparisons to doubly robust (DR), weighted DR (a.k.a. self-normalized DR/WDR), SWITCH/DRos, or other stabilized/clipped estimators commonly used in OPE/BLBF. These could close the gap between theory and practice and test whether gains are due to self-normalization per se versus other stabilization strategies.
- Experiments use simulated logs from supervised datasets; no real logged data evaluation is presented. While common, this limits external validity.

Clarity
- Overall well-written and well-structured; the propensity overfitting example is excellent. However:
  - Several references are incomplete (missing years/venues) and some notation around loss ranges is inconsistent (e.g., δ∈[−1,0] vs “[5,4]” typographical slip).
  - The “CRF” hypothesis appears to factor across labels (essentially independent logistic regressions); clarifying this factorization and tractability assumptions would help.
  - Details on hyperparameter selection (λ, M) and sensitivity analysis are brief; please report ranges/grids and include CIs in tables/plots.

Originality
- Applying SNIS as the training objective in CRM and explicitly framing “propensity overfitting” as a learning pathology are the main novel contributions. While SNIS itself is standard in IS/OPE, the focused diagnosis and its consequences for ERM in BLBF are insightful. Novelty is moderate but meaningful.

Significance
- The proposed change is simple, robust, and likely to be adopted by practitioners doing BLBF/OPE where IPS-based learning is brittle. The gains shown suggest practical impact. The lack of finite-sample theory specific to the SNIS objective and limited baselines reduce the theoretical and empirical breadth, but the contribution is still valuable.

Pros for acceptance
- Clear problem identification (propensity overfitting) with convincing examples.
- Simple, principled solution grounded in established IS practice.
- Consistent empirical improvements and robustness to loss translation.
- Code availability and applicability to common BLBF settings.

Cons for acceptance
- No finite-sample generalization guarantees for the SNIS-based CRM objective.
- Missing comparisons to DR/WDR/SWITCH-style methods and other stabilized estimators.
- Evaluation restricted to simulated logs; limited ablations and sensitivity analyses.
- Some presentation and bibliographic polish needed.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide a finite-sample bound or at least a stability/generalization analysis tailored to ratio estimators (e.g., via delta method/empirical process tools or concentration for self-normalized sums). Alternatively, add a constrained variant enforcing Ŝ(h)≈1 and analyze it.
- Include baselines: DR and weighted DR (self-normalized DR), SWITCH/DRos, clipped IPS without normalization, and perhaps direct-modeling (DM) where appropriate.
- Analyze and report the bias from clipping under SNIS, with guidance for selecting M; add sensitivity plots for λ and M.
- Add real logged-data experiments (e.g., from recommendation or ad-click datasets) if available; otherwise, vary logging policy coverage and stochasticity more systematically.
- Report confidence intervals and statistical tests; provide ablations on optimization (SGD vs L-BFGS) and runtime breakdowns.
- Fix references and minor notation inconsistencies; clarify factorization assumptions of the “CRF” model.

Recommendation: Weak accept
This is a solid, well-motivated contribution that identifies an important failure mode and offers a simple, effective fix with clear empirical benefits. The main reservation is the lack of finite-sample guarantees and limited baselines; addressing these would strengthen the paper further. Nonetheless, the practical significance and clarity merit acceptance.
