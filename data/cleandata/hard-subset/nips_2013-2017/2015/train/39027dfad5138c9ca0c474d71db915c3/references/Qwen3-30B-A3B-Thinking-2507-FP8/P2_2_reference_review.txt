

# Review: The Self-Normalized Estimator for Counterfactual Learning

## Summary and Context
This paper identifies a critical flaw in current counterfactual learning methods for batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF), which is central to many real-world interactive systems like recommendation engines and ad platforms. The authors identify "Propensity Overfitting," a previously unrecognized form of overfitting that occurs when using conventional unbiased risk estimators (importance sampling) in empirical risk minimization. They propose a self-normalized risk estimator that uses multiplicative control variates to address this problem, leading to a new learning algorithm called Norm-POEM. This work builds on prior BLBF research (e.g., POEM [1]) but makes a significant theoretical contribution by identifying and solving a previously overlooked problem.

## Strengths

**Quality:** The paper demonstrates strong technical quality. The authors clearly define the Propensity Overfitting problem through a compelling theoretical example (Example 1) that shows how conventional estimators can produce arbitrarily low risk estimates. They provide a rigorous theoretical justification for their approach with Theorem 2, proving the consistency of their self-normalized estimator. The empirical evaluation is comprehensive and well-designed, following the established "Supervised 7→ Bandit" methodology from prior work [1]. Their results consistently show Norm-POEM outperforming POEM across multiple datasets (Scene, Yeast, TMC, LYRL), with clear evidence that the improved performance is directly due to avoiding Propensity Overfitting (Table 2).

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The problem statement is clear from the introduction, and the related work section properly contextualizes the contribution within BLBF and causal inference literature. The technical details are presented logically, with theorems and proofs appropriately placed. The experimental section is transparent about methodology and results, making it easy for readers to understand and potentially replicate the work. The key innovation (self-normalized estimator) is clearly explained with sufficient mathematical detail.

**Originality:** This paper makes a significant original contribution. The concept of "Propensity Overfitting" is entirely novel - it's a distinct form of overfitting that affects counterfactual learning but doesn't occur in standard supervised learning. While self-normalized estimators have been used in other contexts (e.g., importance sampling in finance [17]), the authors' application to BLBF is novel. The paper clearly differentiates from prior work, particularly POEM [1], and properly references related work in causal inference and counterfactual learning.

**Significance:** The problem addressed is highly significant for the field of counterfactual learning, which is crucial for many real-world applications. Propensity Overfitting represents a fundamental limitation in current BLBF methods that can lead to severely misleading risk estimates. The authors' solution is both theoretically sound and empirically effective, with Norm-POEM consistently outperforming POEM across multiple datasets. The paper advances the state of the art in BLBF in a demonstrable way, providing a simple yet powerful solution to a previously overlooked problem that affects the reliability of counterfactual learning methods.

## Weaknesses

**Theoretical Depth:** While the consistency proof (Theorem 2) is solid, the paper lacks generalization bounds for the Norm-POEM algorithm. The authors prove the estimator is consistent but don't provide finite-sample guarantees or error bounds for the learning algorithm itself. This would strengthen the theoretical contribution.

**Computational Analysis:** The paper mentions that Norm-POEM is "surprisingly faster" than POEM but doesn't provide a detailed computational analysis. A more thorough discussion of the computational complexity trade-offs would be valuable, especially since the self-normalized estimator requires computing a normalization term that might be expensive in some settings.

**Assumption Limitations:** The paper assumes the logging policy has full support over the action space (h0(y|x) > 0 for all y), which is a strong assumption. While common in the literature, it's worth discussing how the method would perform when this assumption is violated (e.g., in real-world scenarios where some actions are rarely taken).

## Overall Assessment

This is a high-quality paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of counterfactual learning. The identification of Propensity Overfitting is a novel insight that addresses a fundamental limitation in current BLBF methods. The proposed self-normalized estimator is theoretically sound, and the empirical results convincingly demonstrate its superiority over existing approaches.

The paper excels in clarity and organization, making complex concepts accessible to the NeurIPS audience. It's well-positioned to advance the field by providing a simple yet powerful solution to a previously overlooked problem. The significance of the contribution is high, as it addresses a critical issue that affects the reliability of counterfactual learning in real-world applications.

The main weaknesses are the limited theoretical analysis of the learning algorithm (beyond consistency) and insufficient computational complexity discussion. However, these do not detract significantly from the paper's overall contribution.

## Recommendation

**Accept**. This paper makes a strong contribution to the field with novel insights, sound theory, and compelling empirical results. It clearly advances the state of the art in batch learning from logged bandit feedback and provides a solution to a previously unrecognized problem that has significant practical implications. The paper is well-written, properly contextualized in the literature, and addresses an important problem in a demonstrably effective way.

The novelty of the Propensity Overfitting concept, combined with the practical improvements demonstrated in experiments, makes this a significant contribution that would be of interest to the NeurIPS community. The paper should be accepted as it meets all the criteria for a high-quality NeurIPS paper.
