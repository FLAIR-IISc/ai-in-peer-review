### Summary

This paper identifies a critical and previously overlooked problem in batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF), which the authors term "Propensity Overfitting". This issue arises when the standard unbiased inverse propensity score (IPS) estimator is used for empirical risk minimization (ERM). Models with high capacity can learn to minimize the estimated risk by manipulating the propensity weights, rather than by accurately modeling the true loss function. This can lead to degenerate risk estimates and poor generalization. To address this, the authors propose replacing the standard IPS estimator with the self-normalized (SN) importance sampling estimator. They show that this estimator is equivariant to loss translations and its estimates are bounded by the range of the loss, thereby resolving the anomalies that enable propensity overfitting. This leads to a new learning algorithm, Norm-POEM, based on the Counterfactual Risk Minimization (CRM) principle. The empirical evaluation across several multi-label classification datasets demonstrates that Norm-POEM consistently and significantly outperforms its predecessor (POEM), is robust to loss transformations, and successfully mitigates propensity overfitting.

### Strengths

1.  **Novel and Important Problem Formulation:** The identification and clear articulation of "Propensity Overfitting" is a significant contribution. The paper provides a compelling, simple example in Section 4 that illustrates how ERM with the standard IPS estimator can fail in ways not typically seen in supervised learning. This insight is valuable for the off-policy evaluation and learning community.
2.  **Principled and Effective Solution:** The proposed solution—using the self-normalized estimator—is not ad-hoc. It is a well-established technique from the importance sampling literature that elegantly addresses the identified pathologies of the unbiased estimator (lack of equivariance, unbounded estimates). The authors clearly connect the properties of the SN estimator to the problem of propensity overfitting.
3.  **Strong Empirical Evidence:** The experimental section provides convincing evidence supporting the paper's claims. In particular, Table 2 is a very effective demonstration of both the existence of propensity overfitting in the baseline (POEM) and the robustness of the proposed Norm-POEM to loss translations. The consistent performance improvements shown in Table 1 and Figure 1 across multiple datasets and training sizes further strengthen the paper's contribution.

### Weaknesses

Despite the strong core contribution, the manuscript is plagued by a substantial number of errors, ranging from minor typos to significant technical inaccuracies. This lack of polish and precision detracts considerably from the paper's quality and raises concerns about its overall carefulness.

1.  **Technical Inaccuracies and Typos:** The paper contains several careless errors that undermine its technical credibility.
    *   In Section 4, the loss range is given as "δ(·, ·) ∈ [5,4]", which is an empty set and contradicts the range of [-1, 0] stated in Section 3.
    *   In Section 5, the derivation of E[Ŝ(h)] = 1 is incorrect. The expression `∫ 1 Pr(xi)dxi` is not meaningful or correct.
    *   Also in Section 5, the motivation for the ratio estimator is presented with the nonsensical formula `EX [W (X)] = E[W (X)]E[V (X)] v`.
    *   In Section 3, the CRM objective in Equation (3) includes `1/n` as a multiplier for the standard deviation term, whereas it should likely be `1/√n` to represent the standard error of the mean. This is a potentially significant error in the problem formulation.
    *   The argument in Section 4 that `R̂(hoverfit) ≤ −k ln` is confusingly written and the term `ln` appears to be a typo.
    *   Miscellaneous typos like "δ : X ×Y 7→ R" and "Supervised 7→ Bandit" are present.

2.  **Lack of Experimental Rigor:**
    *   The main results in Table 1 and Figure 1 are reported without standard deviations or error bars. This is a critical omission, as it prevents an assessment of the statistical significance of the claimed improvements over the baseline.
    *   The authors state that experiments were run without L2-regularization. While they claim Norm-POEM still dominates with it, omitting it from the primary comparison is unconventional and may not represent the strongest possible baseline performance, casting some doubt on the magnitude of the improvement.

3.  **Clarity and Reproducibility Issues:**
    *   The paper states that the variance term in Equation (8) "does not admit stochastic optimization" without any explanation, leaving the reader to guess the reason (presumably the sums over the full dataset in the denominator).
    *   The provided URL for the software implementation is a non-functional placeholder (`http://www.cs.cornell.edu/~adith/POEM`), which hinders reproducibility.

4.  **Sloppy Formatting and Referencing:** The bibliography is exceptionally poorly formatted. Many entries are incomplete, missing page numbers, conference proceedings titles, or publishers (e.g., [1], [2], [5], [6], etc.). This level of disregard for citation standards is unacceptable for a top-tier publication.

### Decision

**Reject.**

The core idea of this paper is excellent, and the central empirical results appear strong. The identification of propensity overfitting is a notable contribution. However, the manuscript in its current form does not meet the quality standards for NIPS. The sheer volume of technical inaccuracies, typographical errors, omissions in experimental reporting (e.g., error bars), and profoundly sloppy referencing demonstrates a lack of care that fatally undermines the work. While the central thesis is likely correct and important, the paper requires a thorough and meticulous revision to correct the numerous flaws before it can be reconsidered. I would be open to reviewing a substantially improved version in the future.
