The paper addresses the problem of batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF), highlighting a critical issue – propensity overfitting – in conventional counterfactual risk estimation. The authors propose a self-normalized risk estimator and demonstrate, both theoretically and empirically, that it mitigates this overfitting, leading to improved generalization performance.

The core idea is sound and builds upon established concepts in causal inference, off-policy evaluation, and importance sampling. The identification of "propensity overfitting" as a novel form of overfitting specific to BLBF is a significant contribution, and the subsequent proposal of the self-normalized estimator as a direct remedy is well-motivated. The connection to existing work in control variates, particularly the self-normalized estimator used in financial applications, provides a strong theoretical foundation.

However, despite the novelty of the identified problem and the potential of the proposed solution, the paper suffers from a surprising number of flaws, ranging from superficial to substantial, hindering its overall impact. These flaws, while individually minor, accumulate to a point where accepting the paper would be problematic.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The identification of propensity overfitting is a genuinely novel contribution to the field of BLBF.
*   **Theoretical Soundness:** The proposed self-normalized estimator is well-motivated by control variate techniques and supported by a theorem demonstrating its consistency.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The experimental results consistently demonstrate that Norm-POEM outperforms POEM across multiple datasets.
*   **Clear Problem Statement:** The introduction clearly articulates the challenges of learning from logged bandit feedback and motivates the need for a more robust risk estimator.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Presentation of Theorem 1:** The theorem is stated without sufficient context. The connection between the parameters C(H) and the hypothesis space’s complexity is not adequately explained. Referencing Theorem 1 of [1] and the proof of Theorem 6 of [13] without including the crucial details within the text is insufficient. It feels like a black box.
*   **Incomplete Explanation of Example 1:** While Example 1 effectively illustrates propensity overfitting, the reasoning behind why simply translating the loss *also* exacerbates the problem is not fully elucidated. The authors state it "intuitively" occurs due to maximizing/minimizing the propensity weights, but a more rigorous explanation is needed.
*   **Lack of Justification for L-BFGS:** The choice of L-BFGS is stated as being "well-supported," but no references are provided. A brief discussion of why other optimization algorithms were not considered would strengthen this justification.
*   **Minor Typographical Errors & Inconsistencies:** Throughout the paper, there are minor typographical errors (e.g. "hitherto" appears awkwardly) and inconsistencies in notation. This gives the impression of a rushed submission. Specifically, the subscript "lin" for the hypothesis space seems unmotivated and is dropped later in the text.
*   **Equivariance Claim Requires Qualification:** The claim that R̂SN(h) is equivariant needs clarification. While it is true that adding a constant shifts the estimate, this holds only if the underlying distribution of δ is unchanged. The authors don't address potential issues if the constant shift changes the data distribution.
*   **Experimental Details Insufficient:** The description of the experimental setup, while broadly similar to POEM, lacks sufficient detail for reproducibility. Key parameters (e.g., the exact CRF parameters used to generate the logged data) are not fully specified.
*   **Limited Ablation Studies:** While the authors demonstrate that variance regularization is still beneficial, a more thorough ablation study analyzing the contribution of each component of Norm-POEM (e.g., the self-normalized estimator vs. variance regularization) would be valuable.
*   **Overstated Claims of Runtime Improvement:** The claim that Norm-POEM is "surprisingly faster" than POEM is not strongly supported by the experimental results. A more detailed runtime analysis is needed.

**Pro & Con Acceptance:**

**Pro:**

*   Novel identification of a previously unrecognized overfitting phenomenon.
*   A theoretically sound and empirically validated solution to the identified problem.
*   Potential to significantly improve the performance of learning algorithms for BLBF.

**Con:**

*   Numerous minor flaws in presentation and explanation.
*   Insufficient detail in the experimental setup and analysis.
*   Lack of rigorous justification for certain design choices (e.g., L-BFGS).
*   The accumulated effect of these flaws detracts from the overall quality of the paper.



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite the novelty and potential of the work, I recommend **rejection**. The paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of BLBF, but the numerous flaws, while individually minor, collectively undermine its quality. The lack of rigor in certain explanations, the insufficient experimental detail, and the typographical errors suggest a lack of polish that is unacceptable for a NIPS publication. I believe that addressing these issues would significantly strengthen the paper and make it a worthwhile contribution to the community, but in its current form, it falls short of the standards expected for acceptance. The authors should address the comments thoroughly and resubmit in the future.
