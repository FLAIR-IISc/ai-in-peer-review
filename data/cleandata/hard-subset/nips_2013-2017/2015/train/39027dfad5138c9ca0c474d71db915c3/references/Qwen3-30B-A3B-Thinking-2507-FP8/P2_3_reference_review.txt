

# Review of "The Self-Normalized Estimator for Counterfactual Learning"

## Summary and Context

This paper identifies a previously unrecognized problem in batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF), which is critical for improving interactive systems like recommender systems and ad platforms. The authors demonstrate that conventional counterfactual risk estimators suffer from "Propensity Overfitting," where the empirical risk estimator can produce severely biased risk estimates due to the way importance sampling weights interact with the hypothesis space. They propose a Self-Normalized Risk Estimator using multiplicative control variates to address this issue, derive a new learning algorithm called Norm-POEM, and provide theoretical guarantees and empirical validation.

The paper builds on foundational work in BLBF (e.g., [1, 6, 5]) and counterfactual inference, but makes a clear distinction by identifying a novel overfitting phenomenon that wasn't previously characterized. The authors effectively relate their work to related concepts like causal inference, domain adaptation, and off-policy evaluation in reinforcement learning.

## Quality

The paper demonstrates strong technical quality. The authors clearly define Propensity Overfitting through a compelling example (Example 1), where the conventional estimator produces arbitrarily low risk estimates that don't reflect the true risk. This is a significant contribution because it reveals a fundamental flaw in existing BLBF approaches that could lead to severe performance degradation.

The theoretical analysis is rigorous. Theorem 2 proves the consistency of the self-normalized estimator, and the paper carefully explains how it resolves the equivariance and boundedness issues of the conventional estimator. The empirical evaluation (Section 7) is thorough and well-designed, using standard benchmark datasets and comparing to the state-of-the-art POEM algorithm. The experiments consistently show Norm-POEM outperforming POEM across multiple datasets and training sizes, with additional evidence that the improvement comes from better control of Propensity Overfitting (Table 2).

The authors are careful to acknowledge limitations, such as the need for variance regularization (Table 3) and the computational trade-offs (Section 7). The experimental methodology follows standard practices in the field, including proper hyperparameter calibration and multiple runs.

## Clarity

The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The problem statement is clear from the outset, with the introduction effectively framing the practical importance of BLBF. The technical exposition is logically structured: problem definition (Section 3), identification of the problem (Section 4), solution proposal (Section 5), algorithm derivation (Section 6), and empirical validation (Section 7).

The key concepts are explained with sufficient detail and examples. The distinction between Propensity Overfitting and Loss Overfitting is particularly well-explained, making the novel contribution clear to readers. The experimental setup is described in sufficient detail for reproducibility, and the results are presented clearly with appropriate figures (Figure 1) and tables (Tables 1-3).

## Originality

This paper makes a significant original contribution by identifying and characterizing Propensity Overfitting, a previously unrecognized issue in BLBF. While self-normalized estimators have been used in other contexts (e.g., finance [17]), their application to counterfactual learning with the specific problem of Propensity Overfitting is novel.

The paper effectively differentiates its contribution from related work in Section 2. It acknowledges that importance sampling has been studied extensively, but points out that the specific form of overfitting they identify was previously overlooked. The authors also provide a clear theoretical explanation for why this overfitting occurs (Section 4), which is not present in prior work.

## Significance

The significance of this work is high. Propensity Overfitting is a fundamental problem that can cause BLBF algorithms to fail catastrophically in practice (as shown in Table 2, where POEM performs worse than random guessing on TMC). The authors' solution directly addresses this issue and leads to consistent improvements in generalization performance across multiple datasets.

The practical implications are substantial for the many real-world systems that rely on logged bandit feedback (recommender systems, ad platforms, etc.). The Norm-POEM algorithm is designed to be compatible with existing BLBF frameworks, making it relatively easy to adopt in practice. The paper shows that the improved estimator leads to better robustness and generalization without requiring major changes to the underlying learning framework.

## Strengths and Weaknesses

**Strengths:**
- Clear identification and characterization of a novel problem (Propensity Overfitting)
- Rigorous theoretical analysis with meaningful guarantees
- Thorough empirical validation across multiple datasets
- Practical implementation that improves upon state-of-the-art methods
- Well-written and logically organized presentation

**Weaknesses:**
- The computational cost of the self-normalized estimator is not fully analyzed for extremely large datasets (though the paper claims convergence is faster)
- The theoretical analysis is limited to the specific stochastic, cardinal, contextual bandit setting without addressing more complex settings
- The paper could better explain why the self-normalized estimator doesn't require the same level of clipping as the conventional estimator

## Recommendation

This is a strong paper that makes a clear and significant contribution to the field of counterfactual learning. The identification of Propensity Overfitting is novel and important, and the proposed solution is both theoretically sound and empirically validated. The paper is well-written, carefully argued, and addresses a practical problem with substantial real-world impact.

The paper meets all the criteria for acceptance at NeurIPS. It is technically sound (Quality), well-written (Clarity), makes a clear original contribution (Originality), and addresses an important problem with significant practical implications (Significance).

I recommend acceptance with confidence. The paper advances the state of the art in BLBF by addressing a previously unrecognized problem that can severely impact the performance of existing algorithms, and provides both theoretical and empirical evidence for the effectiveness of their solution.
