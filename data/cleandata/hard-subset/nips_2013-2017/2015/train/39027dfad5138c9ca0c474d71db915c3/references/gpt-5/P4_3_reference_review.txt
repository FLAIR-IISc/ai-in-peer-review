Summary and relation to prior work
The paper addresses batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF), where only the outcome of the action taken by a historical logging policy is observed. Prior work frames BLBF via counterfactual risk minimization (CRM), typically using inverse propensity scoring (IPS) with clipping to trade bias/variance, and variance-regularized ERM motivated by empirical Bernstein bounds (e.g., POEM). The authors identify a failure mode of IPS-based ERM—propensity overfitting—where minimizing the IPS estimator can be achieved by manipulating the sum of importance weights rather than aligning with low loss, leading to degenerate estimates and pathological dependence on loss translations. They propose replacing IPS with a self-normalized importance sampling (SNIS) risk estimator (a multiplicative control variate), yielding an estimator that is bounded and equivariant under additive shifts of the loss, and strongly consistent. They integrate this estimator into CRM, deriving a new algorithm, Norm-POEM, with an approximate variance estimator for the ratio estimator, and optimize it using L-BFGS. Experiments on multilabel classification datasets constructed via the Supervised→Bandit protocol show improved generalization over POEM, robustness to loss translations, better calibration of the sum of normalized weights Ŝ(h), and competitive runtimes.

The approach builds on extensive literature: IPS and clipping [Rosenbaum & Rubin; Ionides], CRM and learning bounds [Swaminathan & Joachims; Cortes et al.; Maurer & Pontil], doubly robust estimators [Dudík et al.], and self-normalized/weighted importance sampling [Trotter & Tukey; Hesterberg; Kong; Owen; Rubinstein & Kroese]. The paper’s novelty lies in diagnosing propensity overfitting within BLBF/CRM and using SNIS as a risk estimator for learning (not just evaluation) to eliminate anomalies of IPS in ERM.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The identification of propensity overfitting is well-motivated with a clear synthetic example. The SNIS estimator is correctly derived as a ratio estimator with Ŝ(h) as a control variate with known expectation 1. The boundedness and equivariance properties straightforwardly address the IPS anomalies discussed, and strong consistency is correctly established by the SLLN. The variance estimator for SNIS uses a standard normal/delta-method approximation; the need for clipping to ensure finite variance is acknowledged.
- The integration into CRM is plausible, but the theory is less complete: the original CRM generalization bounds were derived for (clipped) IPS; the paper does not provide a matching finite-sample generalization bound for the SNIS-based objective. Using an approximate variance inside CRM is heuristic from a theoretical standpoint.
- Empirics: The experimental protocol follows prior work, and the reported metrics (test Hamming loss, Ŝ(h), sensitivity to loss translations) support the claims. However, the baseline coverage is limited (primarily POEM vs. Norm-POEM), and results lack uncertainty estimates.

Clarity
The paper is generally clear and well-structured. Definitions of the setting, hypotheses, and estimators are precise, and the propensity overfitting phenomenon is explained intuitively and formally. Minor points:
- Theorem 1 assumes δ ∈ [−1,0] whereas the example uses [−2,−1]; although the authors note that translation/scaling can address this, aligning notation would help.
- The CRF factorization underlying tractable partition functions in multilabel settings could be stated explicitly (independent per-label logistic factors).
- The hyperparameter selection procedure (which estimator is used for validation risk) could be specified.

Originality
- The self-normalized estimator is classical in importance sampling and off-policy evaluation. The new contribution is its principled use inside CRM for learning, the identification and analysis of propensity overfitting in IPS-driven ERM, and the formulation of Norm-POEM with an appropriate variance estimator.
- The “propensity overfitting” diagnosis and Ŝ(h) diagnostic are useful conceptual contributions for the BLBF community.

Significance
- Practical significance: The proposed estimator addresses a real and recurrent failure mode in BLBF, improving robustness to loss transformations and extreme weights—a relevant issue for recommender/ads/search logs.
- Empirical significance: Improvements over POEM are consistent on several datasets and under data size variation. However, broader significance would be strengthened by comparisons with doubly robust and recent strong off-policy learners (e.g., DR, SWITCH-DR, weighted DR) and by inclusion of confidence intervals.
- The absence of a new non-asymptotic generalization bound for SNIS within CRM limits the theoretical impact.

Strengths
- Clear identification of a subtle yet important failure mode (propensity overfitting) with instructive examples.
- Sound and simple remedy via SNIS that removes IPS anomalies (boundedness, equivariance) and is strongly consistent.
- Integration into a practical learning algorithm with variance regularization and efficient optimization.
- Empirical evidence that aligns with theory: improved generalization, robustness to loss translation, Ŝ(h) close to 1.

Weaknesses and questions
- Theory: No finite-sample generalization guarantees analogous to CRM bounds for the SNIS-based objective; use of an approximate variance within CRM is heuristic.
- Baselines: Missing comparisons to doubly robust estimators and recent off-policy learning methods, which are standard in this area.
- Evaluation reporting: No variance/error bars over runs; runtime claims lack quantitative tables.
- Sensitivity: Limited ablations on clipping threshold M and λ, and on logging policy properties beyond the reported scenarios.
- Scope: Experiments are limited to supervised-to-bandit multilabel tasks; results on real logged bandit data would increase external validity.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide a finite-sample analysis for SNIS-based CRM (e.g., a bound using ratio-estimator concentration or self-normalized concentration inequalities).
- Add baselines: DR and weighted/self-normalized DR; compare against clipped IPS variants used in practice; include SNIPS terminology to connect to bandit literature.
- Report standard errors/CI over runs; include quantitative runtime tables and iteration counts.
- Conduct ablations: sensitivity to M and λ; effect of varying logging policy entropy and coverage.
- Clarify the tractability assumptions for CRF partition functions in the multilabel case (factorization).
- Specify the estimator used for hyperparameter selection on the validation set to avoid selection bias.

Arguments for acceptance
- Addresses a well-known pain point in BLBF with a principled and easy-to-implement estimator.
- Demonstrates consistent empirical gains and improved robustness across datasets and data sizes.
- Provides practical diagnostics (Ŝ(h)) and a learning recipe likely to be adopted.

Arguments against acceptance
- Theoretical contribution is incomplete: lacks non-asymptotic guarantees for the proposed CRM objective.
- Empirical evaluation omits key contemporary baselines (DR and variants) and uncertainty reporting.
- Experiments limited to synthetic bandit scenarios derived from supervised data.

Overall assessment
On balance, the paper makes a useful and practically relevant contribution by diagnosing propensity overfitting and showing that self-normalization materially improves counterfactual learning. The absence of a matching finite-sample theory and limited baseline coverage are notable gaps. If space permits addressing baseline comparisons and adding quantitative runtime/uncertainty reporting, the paper would be strengthened. As it stands, it is a solid, incremental contribution with clear practical value.
