**Summary**

This paper addresses the problem of batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF), a crucial setting for improving interactive systems using historical data. The authors identify a novel and significant failure mode of standard counterfactual risk estimators based on importance sampling, which they term "Propensity Overfitting." This phenomenon occurs when a learning algorithm, under the Empirical Risk Minimization (ERM) principle, minimizes the estimated risk not by fitting the observed losses, but by manipulating the propensity weights. For instance, with negative losses, the learner might assign extremely high probability to observed actions to drive the estimated risk to negative infinity. To solve this, the authors propose replacing the standard inverse propensity score (IPS) estimator with the self-normalized estimator, a classic ratio estimator from the importance sampling literature that uses the sum of importance weights as a multiplicative control variate. They show that this estimator is equivariant to loss translations, bounded within the range of the loss, and consistent, thereby directly mitigating Propensity Overfitting. They embed this new estimator into the Counterfactual Risk Minimization (CRM) framework to create a new algorithm, Norm-POEM, and demonstrate through extensive experiments that it is substantially more robust and achieves better generalization performance than prior work.

**Strengths (Arguments for Acceptance)**

1.  **High Originality and Significance of the Core Idea:** The primary contribution of this paper is the identification, formalization, and naming of "Propensity Overfitting." This is a profound and novel insight into a fundamental weakness of applying ERM with standard importance-sampling-based estimators. While overfitting is well-understood in supervised learning ("Loss Overfitting"), this paper clearly articulates a distinct and dangerous failure mode unique to the counterfactual setting. The simple yet powerful example in Section 4 makes the concept startlingly clear. This is a significant conceptual advance that will likely change how researchers think about and diagnose problems in off-policy learning and evaluation.

2.  **Elegant and Principled Solution:** The proposed solution—using the self-normalized estimator—is not a complex, ad-hoc fix. It is a principled application of a well-understood statistical tool (multiplicative control variates) that directly targets the identified problem. The paper clearly explains why this estimator resolves the pathologies of the standard IPS estimator: it normalizes away the effect of manipulating the sum of propensities, is equivariant to shifts in the loss function, and its estimates are naturally bounded. This elegance is a major strength.

3.  **Strong and Convincing Empirical Validation:** The experiments are well-designed and compelling. By using the "Supervised -> Bandit" conversion, the authors create a controlled environment where the ground truth is known, allowing for a clear demonstration of the problem and the solution's efficacy. The results in Table 2 are particularly damning for the standard approach and powerfully illustrate the robustness of Norm-POEM to loss translation, a key theoretical property. The consistent performance improvement over the prior state-of-the-art (POEM) across multiple datasets and sample sizes provides strong evidence for the practical value of the proposed method.

4.  **Clarity and Presentation:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow. The motivation is clear, the problem is precisely defined, and the proposed solution is logically derived. The connection to prior work is well-articulated. This clarity makes a significant conceptual contribution accessible to a broad audience.

**Weaknesses (Points for Improvement)**

1.  **Limited Theoretical Novelty of the Estimator Itself:** The self-normalized estimator is a classic tool in the Monte Carlo and survey statistics literature. The novelty here lies entirely in its application to diagnose and solve the newly identified problem of Propensity Overfitting in a learning context. While I believe this application is more than sufficient for acceptance, the authors could be slightly more explicit that the tool is old, but the problem and its solution in this context are new.

2.  **Lack of a Finite-Sample Generalization Bound:** The original CRM principle (and the POEM algorithm) was motivated by a finite-sample, data-dependent generalization bound (Theorem 1). The current work provides a proof of consistency for the new estimator (Theorem 2) but does not provide an analogous finite-sample bound for the proposed Norm-POEM objective. The variance term used in the objective is an approximation based on the delta method. While the empirical results are excellent, a formal theoretical justification in the form of a generalization bound would make the work more complete. Given the strength of the core idea, this is a minor weakness but would be a valuable direction for future work.

3.  **Computational Considerations:** The paper states that the new objective does not admit stochastic optimization, unlike the original POEM. While it is noted that Norm-POEM converges faster in practice using L-BFGS, a deeper discussion of the optimization landscape and the computational trade-offs would be beneficial.

**Recommendation: Strong Accept**

This is an excellent paper that makes a significant and fundamental contribution to the field of counterfactual learning. The identification of "Propensity Overfitting" is a bold and important new concept that clarifies a critical pathology in a widely used class of methods. The proposed solution is simple, elegant, and effective. While the underlying estimator is not new, its application here is highly novel and impactful. The paper prioritizes a deep conceptual insight over incremental methodological refinement, which is precisely the kind of work that pushes the field forward. The empirical evidence is strong and unambiguous. This paper addresses a key problem in a better way, advances the state of the art, and provides ideas that many researchers are likely to build upon. I recommend it for acceptance without hesitation.
