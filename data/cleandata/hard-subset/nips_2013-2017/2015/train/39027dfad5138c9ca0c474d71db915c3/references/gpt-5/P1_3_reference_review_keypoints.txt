- Identifies a new failure mode, “propensity overfitting,” for IPS-based ERM in learning from logged bandit feedback.
- Shows clipped IPS can incentivize hypotheses that overweight or avoid logged actions, producing degenerate empirical risks that are unbounded and not translation-equivariant.
- Proposes a multiplicative control variate (sum of importance weights) to derive a self-normalized counterfactual risk (SNIPS) and a new learning objective, Norm-POEM.
- Norm-POEM couples the SN estimator with a variance penalty in the CRM spirit to mitigate propensity overfitting.
- Proves strong consistency and shows desirable properties (boundedness, translation equivariance) for the SN-based estimator; provides an approximate variance estimator for the ratio estimator.
- Empirical results on multilabel supervised-to-bandit benchmarks show consistent improvements over POEM, robustness to loss translation, and better calibration of S(h).
- Positions work within established IS and counterfactual learning literature; novelty lies in deploying self-normalized IS inside ERM to prevent learning-induced weight pathologies.
- Strength: clear identification and formalization of propensity overfitting with an intuitive toy example and diagnostics (S(h)).
- Strength: multiplicative control variate approach is principled and yields immediate practical benefits.
- Strength: Norm-POEM is simple to implement, computationally practical, and uses familiar regularization knobs (clipping M, variance penalty λ).
- Strength: Experiments demonstrate nontrivial, consistent gains and improved robustness compared to POEM.
- Weakness: Lacks finite-sample generalization bounds for the SN-based CRM objective; variance penalty is justified asymptotically via the delta method.
- Weakness: Claims of “provably avoids degeneracies” would be stronger with formal finite-sample guarantees about propensity overfitting.
- Weakness: Empirical baselines are limited—missing comparisons to doubly robust (DR/WDR), defensive mixtures, weighted stabilization, and cost-sensitive reductions.
- Weakness: Evaluation is restricted to supervised-to-bandit simulations with a softmax logging policy; needs sensitivity tests for support gaps, heavy-tailed propensities, and real logged data.
- Weakness: Hyperparameter tuning/validation details are underspecified; choice of validation estimator (SN vs IPS, clipping) and sensitivity analyses are needed.
- Minor issues: typos (e.g., “δ(·, ·) ∈ [5,4]”) and missing pseudocode/implementation details for reproducibility.
- Quality: Technically sound and convincing empirically, but main theoretical gap is absence of finite-sample learning guarantees.
- Clarity: Well organized and readable; would benefit from more implementation details (gradients, complexity) and pseudocode.
- Originality: Self-normalized IS is classic, but applying it within CRM to combat propensity overfitting and naming the phenomenon is a meaningful contribution.
- Significance: Addresses an important robustness problem in counterfactual learning with a simple, adoptable method; impact would grow with stronger theory and broader experiments.
- Suggestions: derive finite-sample bounds (possibly with clipping), add DR/WDR and other baselines, test poorer support and larger action spaces, include real logged datasets, provide pseudocode and error bars, and clarify hyperparameter validation.
- Arguments for acceptance: identifies and fixes a real failure mode with a simple, principled method and shows consistent empirical benefits.
- Arguments against acceptance: lacks finite-sample theory, limited baselines, and restricted empirical evaluation.
- Overall recommendation: Weak accept.
