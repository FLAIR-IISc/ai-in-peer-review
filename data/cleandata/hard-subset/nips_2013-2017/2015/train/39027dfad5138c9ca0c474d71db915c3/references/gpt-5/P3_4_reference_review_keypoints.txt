- Paper identifies "propensity overfitting" as a failure mode of IPS within ERM/CRM and proposes SNIS (self-normalized importance sampling) to fix it.  
- IPS can be driven to degenerate/unbounded empirical risks via manipulation of importance-weight sums, causing non-equivariance and poor finite-sample ERM behavior.  
- SNIS (multiplicative control variate) restores boundedness and translation equivariance, and the authors prove strong consistency for SNIS.  
- Norm-POEM plugs SNIS and an approximate variance estimate into CRM to build a practical algorithm.  
- Empirical results on simulated multi-label classification show improved generalization, robustness to loss translation, and better Ŝ(h) diagnostic behavior versus POEM.  
- Clear and compelling diagnosis with a synthetic example that matches practitioner experience of heavy-tailed IPS.  
- Use of SNIS is well-motivated and empirically stabilizes learning.  
- Norm-POEM is practical, well-explained, and the code is publicly available.  
- Experiments systematically evaluate generalization, diagnostics, translation effects, data size, and runtime.  
- Lack of finite-sample generalization bounds for the SNIS-based objective; cited CRM bound applies to clipped IPS, not SNIS.  
- Missing comparisons to stronger baselines like doubly robust (DR) and weighted/normalized DR estimators.  
- Reliance on known/accurate logging propensities and full support; robustness to propensity misspecification is not analyzed.  
- Experimental scope is limited to simulated multi-label logs; no ranking/recommender tasks or real logged datasets are evaluated.  
- The SNIS variance estimator is approximate and its impact on optimization, gradient bias, and convergence is not studied.  
- Paper is well written and organized, but should explicitly separate which IPS-CRM guarantees carry over to SNIS-CRM and discuss SNIS bias dependence on weight variance/clipping.  
- Novelty is incremental: SNIS and control variates are classical, but articulating propensity overfitting and applying self-normalization within CRM is a meaningful contribution.  
- Significance is high for BLBF practice, but impact is limited by missing DR baselines and lack of finite-sample theory.  
- Suggested improvements: finite-sample/concentration analysis for SNIS, add DR/WDR baselines, test robustness to estimated/misspecified propensities, evaluate on real logged/ranking datasets, and clarify optimization/gradient details.  
- Recommendation: Weak accept — important and practical contribution, but would be stronger with added theory and baselines (high reviewer confidence).
