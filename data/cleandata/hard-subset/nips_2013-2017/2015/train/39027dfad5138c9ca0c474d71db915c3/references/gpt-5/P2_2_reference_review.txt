Summary
The paper addresses batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF) via counterfactual risk minimization (CRM). It identifies a failure mode of the standard inverse propensity scoring (IPS) estimator when used inside ERM—termed Propensity Overfitting—where the learner exploits variability in importance weights, leading to degenerate and non-equivariant risk estimates. The authors propose using a multiplicative control variate to derive the self-normalized IPS (SNIPS) estimator for counterfactual risk, argue that it eliminates key anomalies (boundedness, equivariance), prove strong consistency, and integrate it into a CRM-style learning algorithm (Norm-POEM) with an approximate variance penalty. Empirically, Norm-POEM outperforms POEM on several multilabel datasets in a supervised-to-bandit simulation, is robust to loss translations, better controls the sum of weights Ŝ(h), and is competitive in runtime.

Relation to prior work: Importance sampling (IS), clipping, control variates, and self-normalized IS are well-known in Monte Carlo and off-policy evaluation (Hesterberg; Owen; Kong). CRM for BLBF (Swaminathan & Joachims) uses IPS and empirical Bernstein-style variance regularization. Doubly robust (DR) estimators are another line (Dudík et al.). This paper’s novelty is to (i) articulate the Propensity Overfitting phenomenon for BLBF, (ii) adopt SNIPS as the counterfactual risk estimator within CRM, and (iii) instantiate and evaluate Norm-POEM with a variance penalty tailored to SNIPS.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The diagnosis of IPS anomalies (unboundedness, non-equivariance) is correct, and the constructed example convincingly shows ERM can chase the weights rather than the loss. The use of SNIPS directly addresses these issues: it is bounded, equivariant to additive shifts of the loss, and consistent (proof via SLLN is straightforward and correct). The approximate variance estimator for the ratio uses standard delta/normal approximations.
- Support for claims: The empirical results align with the theory: SNIPS stabilizes Ŝ(h)≈1, robustness to loss translation is demonstrated, and test risk improves over POEM across datasets and data sizes. The runtime observations (fewer iterations despite higher per-iteration cost) are plausible and supported qualitatively.
- Limitations: Finite-sample theory is limited. While the paper uses a CRM-style objective with an estimated variance for SNIPS, it does not provide a generalization error bound for the ratio estimator analogous to Theorem 1 for IPS, nor a uniform control of the bias-variance tradeoff introduced by normalization and clipping. The claim that SNIPS “provably avoids degeneracies” is accurate for the identified anomalies, but there is no formal guarantee that ERM with SNIPS cannot overfit in other ways (e.g., via correlation between loss and weights under finite samples). The variance estimator is approximate, and its impact on optimization and calibration is not theoretically characterized. Assumptions (full support; well-estimated logging propensities) are critical and could be emphasized.

Clarity
- The paper is clearly written and well organized. The Propensity Overfitting example is lucid and illustrates the failure sharply. The rationale for multiplicative control variates and the move to SNIPS is compelling and easy to follow. The empirical setup mirrors prior work to enable a clean comparison.
- A few places could be tightened: provide pseudocode or a clearer summary of the training objective and gradients; explicitly discuss conditions ensuring finite variance of SNIPS and the effect of clipping M; expand on how Z(x) stays tractable (it appears the CRF factorizes into independent logistic labels, which is important to state clearly).

Originality
- The use of self-normalized IS itself is not novel, but identifying and formalizing “Propensity Overfitting” in BLBF and showing that SNIPS directly neutralizes the key anomalies is a useful conceptual contribution. Integrating SNIPS into CRM (Norm-POEM) and demonstrating the practical benefits in structured prediction is a nontrivial adaptation.
- Related work is broadly cited; however, empirical comparisons to DR/wDR or more modern stabilized estimators are missing, which would situate the contribution more precisely.

Significance
- BLBF and counterfactual learning are important in recommender/ads/search. Stabilizing off-policy learning objectives is practically impactful. The paper’s insights—use SNIPS to avoid IPS pathologies inside ERM—are likely to influence practice and subsequent work.
- The empirical gains over POEM are consistent and meaningful, though the evaluation remains in supervised-to-bandit simulations with one family of logging policies. Additional baselines (DR, clipped DR, SNDR) and ablations on sensitivity to M and λ would strengthen the claim of broad superiority.

Pros
- Clear identification and demonstration of a real failure mode (Propensity Overfitting).
- Principled fix via SNIPS with desirable properties (bounded, equivariant, consistent).
- Integration into CRM with a workable variance penalty and practical optimizer.
- Consistent empirical improvements and robustness to loss translation.
- Public code and reproducibility.

Cons
- No finite-sample generalization bound tailored to SNIPS-CRM; theoretical guarantees lag behind those for IPS-CRM.
- Bias-variance tradeoffs from normalization and clipping are not analyzed theoretically.
- Missing comparisons to (self-normalized) doubly robust and other stabilized estimators.
- Evaluation limited to simulated bandit logs from supervised datasets; limited diversity of logging policies and action spaces.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide a finite-sample generalization bound for SNIPS (e.g., a ratio-concentration-based CRM bound), or at least a high-level analysis of the bias-variance impact on the CRM objective.
- Add DR/wDR baselines and report sensitivity to clipping M and λ.
- Clarify model class (factorization, tractability of Z), optimization details, and provide pseudocode.
- Discuss limitations when propensities are small/misspecified and how SNIPS behaves under model mismatch.

Overall assessment
The paper is technically sound, well written, and addresses a real and important problem with a principled and effective approach. Despite limited finite-sample theory and missing DR baselines, the conceptual contribution and empirical evidence are strong. I recommend acceptance.
