This paper addresses the problem of batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF), a crucial setting for improving interactive systems using historical data. The authors identify a novel failure mode, which they term "Propensity Overfitting," that affects learning algorithms based on the standard inverse propensity score (IPS) estimator. This type of overfitting occurs when a model with sufficient capacity minimizes its estimated risk not by fitting the true loss function, but by manipulating the importance weights—either by assigning high probability to examples seen in the training data (for negative losses) or by avoiding them entirely (for positive losses). This can lead to arbitrarily poor risk estimates and degenerate solutions.

To solve this problem, the authors propose replacing the standard IPS estimator with the self-normalized importance sampling estimator. This is a classic ratio estimator from the Monte Carlo literature, which the authors motivate as using the sum of importance weights as a multiplicative control variate. This self-normalized estimator is shown to be equivariant to translations in the loss function and its estimates are naturally bounded by the range of the observed losses, properties not held by the standard IPS estimator. These properties directly mitigate Propensity Overfitting. The authors incorporate this new estimator into the Counterfactual Risk Minimization (CRM) framework, leading to a new algorithm called Norm-POEM. Extensive experiments on several multi-label classification datasets demonstrate that Norm-POEM consistently and significantly outperforms its predecessor (POEM), validating the practical importance of addressing Propensity Overfitting.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Significance and Clarity of the Problem:** The paper makes a significant contribution by identifying, clearly defining, and illustrating the problem of Propensity Overfitting. The example in Section 4 is simple, intuitive, and highly effective at demonstrating how standard counterfactual risk minimization can fail in ways not typically seen in supervised learning. This is a subtle but critical issue for the field of off-policy learning, and this paper brings it to the forefront with exceptional clarity.

2.  **Principled and Effective Solution:** The proposed solution is elegant and well-motivated. Instead of an ad-hoc fix, the authors leverage a well-established technique—the self-normalized estimator—and provide a clear rationale for its suitability. The analysis in Section 5, highlighting the estimator's equivariance and boundedness properties, directly connects the solution to the identified problem. The strong consistency proof (Theorem 2) provides basic theoretical grounding.

3.  **High-Quality Empirical Evaluation:** The experimental section is thorough and convincing. The authors use a controlled and reproducible "Supervised-to-Bandit" setup. The results in Table 1 and Figure 1 clearly show that Norm-POEM yields substantial improvements in generalization performance over the previous state-of-the-art. Crucially, the analysis goes beyond simply showing better performance; Table 2 provides compelling evidence that the improvement is indeed due to the mitigation of Propensity Overfitting. The ablation study on the necessity of variance regularization (Table 3) and the discussion on computational efficiency further strengthen the empirical claims.

4.  **Clarity of Writing:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The logical flow from the introduction of BLBF, to the specific problem of Propensity Overfitting, and finally to the proposed solution and its empirical validation, is easy to follow.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

1.  **Theoretical Justification for the Learning Algorithm:** The CRM principle, which Norm-POEM is based on, is motivated by a finite-sample generalization bound (Theorem 1) that applies to the standard clipped IPS estimator. However, the paper does not provide an analogous bound for the proposed self-normalized estimator. The learning objective for Norm-POEM is thus a heuristic combination of the CRM principle with a new estimator and a new variance approximation. While the paper proves the estimator's consistency, the lack of a finite-sample bound for the full learning objective is a theoretical gap. The paper would be significantly stronger if it could provide such a bound, or at least discuss the challenges associated with deriving one for ratio estimators (e.g., leveraging concentration inequalities for self-normalized estimators).

2.  **Originality of the Core Technique:** While the identification of Propensity Overfitting is novel and important, the core technical tool—the self-normalized estimator—is a classic method in the importance sampling literature. The paper's novelty lies in its application to a learning (rather than purely evaluative) context within the CRM framework. The authors should be careful to precisely frame their contribution in this light.

3.  **Scalability Concerns:** The paper notes that the proposed variance estimator (Equation 8) does not readily admit stochastic optimization, necessitating the use of a batch optimizer like L-BFGS. While the authors report that Norm-POEM converges in fewer iterations, this could still be a practical limitation for massive datasets where stochastic gradient methods are often the only feasible option. A discussion of potential avenues for developing a scalable, stochastic version of the objective would be a valuable addition for future work.

**Summary of Pro/Con Arguments:**

*   **Pro:**
    *   Identifies a novel, significant, and subtle problem ("Propensity Overfitting") in a popular learning framework.
    *   Proposes a simple, principled, and highly effective solution based on a theoretically sound estimator.
    *   Provides strong and thorough empirical evidence that not only shows the method works, but *why* it works.
    *   The paper is exceptionally clear and well-written.

*   **Con:**
    *   The theoretical justification for the final learning objective is incomplete, as the motivating generalization bound (Theorem 1) does not apply to the proposed estimator.
    *   The core estimator is not new, though its application in this context is.
    *   The reliance on batch optimization may present scalability challenges.

**Decision:**

This is a very strong paper. The identification and solution of the Propensity Overfitting problem is a clear and important contribution to the field of counterfactual learning. The empirical results are compelling, and the paper is written with outstanding clarity. While the theoretical analysis could be deepened with a finite-sample bound tailored to the new objective, the existing contributions are more than sufficient for acceptance. The paper addresses a real and previously underappreciated flaw in a standard method and provides a robust and practical solution. It is likely to be influential and to be built upon by other researchers in the field.
