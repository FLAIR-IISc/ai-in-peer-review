### Summary of Main Ideas and Relation to Prior Work

This paper addresses the problem of batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF), where the goal is to learn a new policy from historical interaction logs generated by a different, existing policy. The paper builds directly upon the Counterfactual Risk Minimization (CRM) principle, particularly as instantiated in the POEM algorithm of Swaminathan & Joachims (2015). The CRM approach uses an importance-sampling based estimator of a policy's risk and minimizes a generalization bound on this risk, which includes both the empirical risk and a variance-based regularization term.

The central contribution of this work is the identification and formalization of a critical failure mode of the standard inverse propensity score (IPS) estimator when used within an Empirical Risk Minimization (ERM) framework. The authors term this problem "Propensity Overfitting." They demonstrate through a clear example and theoretical argument that the IPS estimator can be driven to arbitrarily low values not only by finding policies that achieve low loss, but also by finding policies that manipulate the sum of the importance weights. This can lead to degenerate solutions that are artifacts of the finite sample and do not generalize well.

To remedy this, the authors propose replacing the standard IPS estimator with the self-normalized estimator. This estimator, known in the Monte Carlo literature as a ratio estimator, uses the sum of the importance weights as a multiplicative control variate to normalize the risk estimate. The paper shows that this estimator resolves the anomalies of the standard estimator: it is equivariant to translations of the loss function and its estimates are guaranteed to lie within the range of the observed losses, thus preventing the extreme values characteristic of Propensity Overfitting. While the self-normalized estimator is biased for finite samples, the authors prove it is strongly consistent.

Based on this improved estimator, the paper develops a new learning algorithm called Norm-POEM. This algorithm applies the CRM principle using the self-normalized risk estimator and an appropriate approximate variance estimator. The experiments, conducted on several multi-label classification datasets adapted to the BLBF setting, demonstrate that Norm-POEM significantly and consistently outperforms the previous state-of-the-art (POEM), is robust to the kind of loss transformations that cause POEM to fail, and converges efficiently in practice.

### Strengths

1.  **Clear Identification of a Significant Problem:** The paper makes a valuable conceptual contribution by identifying, naming, and clearly explaining the "Propensity Overfitting" problem. The example provided in Section 4 is insightful and convincingly demonstrates how standard ERM with an unbiased IPS estimator can lead to pathological solutions. This is an important and subtle issue that has been largely overlooked in the BLBF literature.

2.  **Principled and Effective Solution:** The proposed solution, employing the self-normalized estimator, is not an ad-hoc heuristic but a well-established technique from the importance sampling literature that directly addresses the identified problem. The paper does an excellent job of motivating why this estimator's properties (equivariance, bounded range) are desirable for learning and proves its strong consistency.

3.  **Thorough and Convincing Empirical Evaluation:** The experimental setup is sound, following the established "Supervised to Bandit" protocol which allows for controlled and reproducible evaluation. The results are strong and directly support the paper's central claims. Norm-POEM consistently outperforms the baseline, POEM. More importantly, the diagnostic experiments in Table 2 provide compelling evidence that Norm-POEM successfully mitigates Propensity Overfitting by keeping the sum of weights (Ŝ(ĥ)) close to its expectation and by being robust to loss-shifting, a setting where the baseline fails catastrophically.

4.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow. The motivation is clearly laid out, the problem is defined precisely, the proposed solution is logically derived, and the experimental results are presented effectively. The paper situates its contributions well within the existing literature.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Limited Theoretical Novelty:** The primary technical tool, the self-normalized estimator, is a classic technique in Monte Carlo methods. The paper's novelty lies in its application to solve the newly identified Propensity Overfitting problem within the CRM learning framework. While this is a significant contribution, the paper does not provide a new finite-sample generalization bound for the proposed Norm-POEM objective, which would be the analogue to Theorem 1 for the original POEM. Such a bound would provide a stronger theoretical justification for the specific form of the Norm-POEM objective function, which currently relies on an approximate variance estimator (Eq. 8).

2.  **Scalability Concerns:** The authors note that the proposed objective, due to the form of the variance estimator, does not readily admit stochastic optimization and must be optimized with a batch method like L-BFGS. While the experiments show faster convergence in terms of iterations on the tested datasets, the inability to use stochastic gradient methods could be a significant limitation for scaling to extremely large datasets where batch methods are computationally infeasible. A more detailed discussion of this trade-off would be beneficial.

### Assessment

This is a high-quality paper that makes a clear and important contribution to the field of learning from bandit feedback.

**Arguments for acceptance:**
*   It identifies and provides a remedy for a critical, previously unarticulated failure mode ("Propensity Overfitting") in a popular and practical learning paradigm (CRM).
*   The proposed solution is principled, simple to implement, and shown to be highly effective through strong empirical results.
*   The work substantially improves upon the state-of-the-art (POEM), demonstrating superior generalization and robustness.
*   The paper is very well-written and presents its ideas with exceptional clarity.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
*   The core estimator is not new, limiting the paper's fundamental technical originality.
*   The theoretical analysis could be strengthened with a finite-sample generalization bound for the proposed learning principle.

In summary, the strengths of this paper, particularly the clear articulation of a significant problem and the demonstration of an effective and principled solution, far outweigh its weaknesses. The work addresses a difficult problem in a better way than previous research and advances the state of the art in a demonstrable way. The insights regarding Propensity Overfitting are likely to be influential for future research in counterfactual learning and off-policy evaluation.
