Summary
The paper addresses batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF) and identifies a pathology of inverse propensity scoring (IPS) when used as a counterfactual risk estimator inside empirical risk minimization (ERM)/CRM: “propensity overfitting,” where the objective is minimized by manipulating the sum of importance weights rather than modeling loss. The authors propose to replace IPS with the self-normalized importance sampling estimator (SNIPS) as a multiplicative control variate, yielding a bounded, translation-equivariant risk estimate. They prove almost-sure consistency (via SLLN), derive an approximate variance estimate for CRM regularization, and instantiate a learning algorithm (Norm-POEM) for structured prediction using a CRF-like hypothesis class. Experiments on multi-label classification datasets created via a supervised-to-bandit protocol show improved generalization vs. POEM across datasets and robustness to loss translation; Ŝ(h) stays near 1 for Norm-POEM and deviates for POEM.

Relation to prior work
SNIPS/weighted importance sampling is classical in Monte Carlo and has been used in off-policy evaluation in RL and elsewhere. The paper correctly cites Hesterberg and others, as well as prior BLBF work (POEM, DR, truncation). The main novelty is integrating SNIPS into CRM for learning and empirically demonstrating mitigation of propensity overfitting in this setting.

Strengths
- Clear diagnosis of a real and under-discussed failure mode (“propensity overfitting”), with simple illustrative examples.
- Sensible, well-known remedy (self-normalization) that directly targets the issue; boundedness and translation equivariance are attractive when using ERM.
- Empirical evidence that Norm-POEM improves test risk vs. POEM and is robust to loss translation; diagnostic Ŝ(h) is compelling.
- Practical considerations (variance estimate, clipping, optimization with L-BFGS) are discussed; code is available.

Weaknesses
- Limited theoretical advance. Theorem 2 (consistency) is immediate from SLLN; no finite-sample generalization analysis analogous to the CRM bound is provided for the SN estimator. The claim that SN “resolves all problems” is overstated: SNIPS introduces bias and does not address support violations or misspecified propensities.
- Incomplete baselines. The most relevant competing estimators (doubly robust and its variants, e.g., DR/DRos, Switch-DR, hybrid/defensive mixtures, more modern OPE techniques) are not compared. Since DR often dominates IPS/SNIPS in practice, the empirical case for significance is weakened.
- Experimental scope. All results use supervised-to-bandit conversion on classification datasets with a learned logging policy that likely has near-full support. No real-world logs, no evaluation under misspecified propensities or near-deterministic logging—scenarios where SNIPS can behave differently. No confidence intervals/significance tests are reported.
- Variance regularization for the ratio estimator is only approximated; its impact is not theoretically justified, and sensitivity to clipping parameter M and λ is not thoroughly analyzed. Hyperparameter tuning via counterfactual evaluation could itself be biased; no safeguards (e.g., nested validation or robust selection) are discussed.
- Some notational issues and small inconsistencies (e.g., δ ranges, occasional typos). The reliance on exact propensities is assumed but not stress-tested; the effect of logging-policy estimation error is not studied.

Quality
Technically sound in its use of standard SNIPS and variance approximations, but theory is thin beyond standard properties. The empirical methodology is reasonable but misses important baselines and robustness checks. Some claims appear stronger than what is supported.

Clarity
Generally clear and well organized. The propensity-overfitting examples are effective. However, more explicit derivation of the variance estimate and clearer discussion of bias-variance trade-offs under clipping would help. Include pseudocode or a succinct algorithmic summary and add confidence intervals in tables/figures.

Originality
Moderate. Using SNIPS in this BLBF/CRM context is a natural extension of well-known ideas. The main contribution is identifying and naming the propensity-overfitting pathology for learning and showing empirically that SNIPS mitigates it within POEM-like training.

Significance
Potentially useful for practitioners using BLBF with IPS-based ERM, but impact is limited by incremental novelty and missing comparisons to stronger modern OPE estimators. Broader significance would be higher with rigorous finite-sample analysis and real-world validation.

Questions for the authors
1. Can you provide a finite-sample generalization bound for CRM with the SN estimator (even under clipping), analogous to the original CRM bounds?
2. How does Norm-POEM compare to doubly robust estimators (with and without self-normalization), Switch-DR, and defensive-mixture approaches?
3. How sensitive are results to misspecified or noisy propensities? What happens if h0 is near-deterministic (weak support)?
4. Please report confidence intervals and statistical tests for the performance differences, and include ablations over M and λ with sensitivity plots.
5. Could you quantify effective sample size (ESS) and relate it to observed performance?
6. How do results change if hyperparameters are selected with nested validation to reduce selection bias?

Pros
- Identifies a real issue (propensity overfitting) with clear diagnostics.
- Simple, principled fix with desirable properties; consistent empirical improvements over POEM.
- Practical algorithm and code; reasonable runtime.

Cons
- Limited novelty beyond applying SNIPS; theory lacks finite-sample guarantees tailored to CRM.
- Missing strong baselines (DR variants); experiments confined to simulated logs.
- Overstated claims; no robustness to propensity errors or support violations.

Recommendation
Borderline reject. The paper is well written and practically motivated, and I appreciate the clear diagnosis and the clean fix. However, given NeurIPS standards, the contribution feels incremental without stronger theory and broader empirical validation against state-of-the-art OPE baselines and under more realistic logging conditions. Strengthening these aspects could make a compelling submission.
