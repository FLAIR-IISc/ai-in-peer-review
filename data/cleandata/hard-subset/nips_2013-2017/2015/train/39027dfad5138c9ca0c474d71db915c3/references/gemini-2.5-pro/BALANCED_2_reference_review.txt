**Summary of the Paper**

This paper addresses the problem of Batch Learning from Logged Bandit Feedback (BLBF), a setting where a learner aims to find an optimal policy using historical data logged by a different, potentially suboptimal, policy. The authors build upon prior work in counterfactual risk minimization (CRM), which typically employs an importance-sampling (IPS) based estimator for the policy's risk.

The central contribution of this work is the identification and formalization of a critical failure mode of the standard IPS estimator, which the authors term "Propensity Overfitting". They demonstrate with a clear, didactic example that a learner minimizing the IPS risk estimate can learn a degenerate policy that does not focus on minimizing the true loss, but rather on exploiting the structure of the propensity weights. This can lead to arbitrarily poor risk estimates and catastrophic generalization performance. The authors insightfully diagnose this issue as a consequence of the IPS estimator's lack of equivariance to shifts in the loss function.

To remedy this, the paper proposes replacing the standard IPS estimator with the self-normalized importance sampling estimator. This estimator, which normalizes the weighted sum of losses by the sum of importance weights, is shown to be equivariant, bounded within the range of observed losses, and strongly consistent. The authors integrate this new estimator into the CRM framework to develop a new learning algorithm called Norm-POEM. Through a series of well-designed experiments on multi-label classification tasks, they demonstrate that Norm-POEM not only avoids propensity overfitting but also achieves significantly better and more robust generalization performance than its predecessor, POEM, which uses the standard IPS estimator.

**Strengths**

1.  **Significant Conceptual Contribution:** The key strength of this paper is the identification and clear explanation of "Propensity Overfitting". This is a subtle but fundamental problem in counterfactual learning that, while perhaps implicitly understood by some experts, has not been so clearly articulated and demonstrated before. Example 1 is exceptionally effective at building intuition for why standard ERM with an IPS estimator can fail in ways that are distinct from traditional supervised learning. This is a valuable contribution to the field's understanding.

2.  **Principled and Elegant Solution:** The proposed solution—employing the self-normalized estimator—is simple, well-motivated, and elegant. It directly addresses the identified pathologies of the standard estimator (lack of equivariance and unboundedness) and has strong theoretical grounding in the statistics literature on Monte Carlo methods. Its application in a learning context (as opposed to just evaluation) is novel and compelling.

3.  **High-Quality Empirical Evaluation:** The experiments are thorough and convincingly support the paper's claims. The "Supervised -> Bandit" evaluation protocol provides a controlled setting to measure true generalization error. The results in Table 1 and Figure 1 clearly show the performance benefits of Norm-POEM. Crucially, the analysis in Table 2 goes beyond just showing improved performance and provides direct evidence that Norm-POEM mitigates propensity overfitting (by checking that $\hat{S}(\hat{h}) \approx 1$) and is robust to loss translations, confirming the paper's central hypotheses.

4.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is extremely well-written and easy to follow. The problem is introduced clearly, the motivation is built step-by-step, the proposed solution is well-explained, and the experiments are directly tied to the claims. This makes the paper's contributions accessible and impactful.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement**

While this is a strong paper, there are a few areas where the discussion could be expanded to further strengthen the work.

1.  **Discussion on Bias:** The paper correctly notes that the self-normalized estimator is biased for finite samples, unlike the standard IPS estimator. However, the implications of this bias are not discussed in detail. It would be beneficial to add a brief discussion about the nature of this bias. For instance, the bias is known to be of order $O(1/n)$ and depends on the covariance between the numerator and the denominator. Acknowledging this trade-off (accepting a small, typically low-order bias to eliminate pathological overfitting and reduce variance) more explicitly would provide a more complete picture for the reader.

2.  **Comparison to Other Variance Reduction Techniques:** The paper positions itself against the standard IPS estimator and its clipped variants. It would be valuable to relate the proposed approach to other major families of counterfactual estimators, particularly doubly robust (DR) methods [6]. DR estimators also use a form of control variate (an additive one based on a reward model) to reduce variance. A short discussion on the relative merits of using a multiplicative control variate (as in this paper) versus an additive one would better contextualize the contribution. For example, Norm-POEM has the advantage of not requiring the training of an auxiliary direct model for the losses, which can be a strength if such a model is difficult to specify or learn accurately.

3.  **Optimization Details:** The objective function for Norm-POEM is non-convex. The authors state that they use L-BFGS, which is a reasonable choice. However, a brief comment on the practical behavior of the optimization would be helpful. For instance, did the authors observe issues with local minima? The excellent results suggest this is not a major practical impediment, but acknowledging the non-convexity and its potential implications would be good practice.

**Arguments for Acceptance (Pro):**
*   Identifies, names, and clearly explains a fundamental and previously under-appreciated problem in counterfactual learning ("Propensity Overfitting").
*   Proposes an elegant, theoretically sound, and empirically effective solution.
*   The work is a significant step forward for making counterfactual learning more robust and reliable.
*   The paper is exceptionally clear and well-written.
*   The empirical evaluation is strong, rigorous, and directly validates the core claims.

**Arguments against Acceptance (Con):**
*   The self-normalized estimator itself is not new, though its application to solve this specific learning problem is.
*   The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion of the estimator's bias and a comparison to doubly robust methods.

**Decision**

This is an excellent paper that makes a clear and significant contribution to the field of counterfactual learning. The identification of propensity overfitting is a key insight, and the proposed Norm-POEM algorithm is a well-justified and effective solution. The weaknesses are minor and relate to expanding the discussion rather than any fundamental flaws in the methodology or results. The paper is of high quality, original, significant, and should be of great interest to researchers and practitioners working on off-policy evaluation and learning. I strongly recommend acceptance.
