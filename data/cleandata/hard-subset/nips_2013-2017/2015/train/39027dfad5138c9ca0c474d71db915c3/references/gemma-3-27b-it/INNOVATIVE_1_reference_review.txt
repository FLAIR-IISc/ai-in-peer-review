This paper tackles a crucial problem in learning from observational data – propensity overfitting – that arises when applying standard counterfactual risk estimation techniques to logged bandit feedback. The identification of this novel form of overfitting is the paper’s strongest contribution, and the proposed self-normalized estimator provides a compelling solution with strong theoretical justification. The empirical results convincingly demonstrate the benefits of the proposed approach, showing improved robustness and generalization compared to existing methods. 

The core idea of using a multiplicative control variate to address the instability of importance sampling estimates is clever and well-motivated. The connection to existing work on self-normalization, and its applicability to this specific context of propensity overfitting, is clearly articulated. The theoretical analysis, while building upon prior work (Theorem 1), correctly identifies the limitations of existing methods and formally establishes the benefits of the self-normalized estimator (Theorem 2).  The experimental setup is rigorous, following established methodologies for evaluating batch learning from bandit feedback, and the results on multiple datasets are compelling. The fact that Norm-POEM is *faster* than POEM, despite the increased per-iteration cost, is a surprising and welcome result.

However, the paper is not without its shortcomings. While the explanation of propensity overfitting is insightful, the initial examples (Section 4) feel somewhat contrived and might not immediately resonate with readers unfamiliar with the intricacies of importance sampling. A more intuitive explanation relating the issue directly to the limitations of ERM in non-identifiable settings might be more effective.  The discussion around the computational efficiency gains could be more detailed – understanding *why* the faster convergence occurs would be valuable.  Furthermore, while the experiments are comprehensive, the analysis could be extended to include a more in-depth exploration of the hyperparameter sensitivity, especially regarding the clipping parameter *M*.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

* **Novelty:** The identification of “propensity overfitting” is a significant new insight into the challenges of learning from logged bandit feedback.  This is not a minor tweak to existing methods, but a fundamental understanding of a previously unrecognised problem.
* **Significance:**  Addressing propensity overfitting is crucial for reliably learning from real-world observational data, making this work highly relevant to a broad range of applications. The self-normalized estimator offers a practical and theoretically sound solution.
* **Soundness:** The theoretical analysis provides a solid foundation for the proposed approach, and the empirical results strongly support the claims.
* **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized, despite some areas where the explanation could be streamlined.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

* **Initial Examples:** The initial motivating examples feel somewhat artificial and might hinder understanding for a broader audience.
* **Computational Details:** The explanation of the speedup in optimization requires further elaboration.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** More detailed analysis of the hyperparameter *M* would strengthen the empirical validation.



**Overall:**

This is a strong paper that introduces a novel and significant contribution to the field of learning from observational data. While there are minor areas for improvement, the originality and potential impact of the work outweigh these concerns. I believe this paper is a valuable addition to NIPS and recommend **acceptance**.  The authors have identified a crucial problem and proposed a solution that is both theoretically grounded and empirically validated.  The boldness of tackling a subtle and fundamental issue with observational data, rather than incremental improvements, is particularly commendable.
