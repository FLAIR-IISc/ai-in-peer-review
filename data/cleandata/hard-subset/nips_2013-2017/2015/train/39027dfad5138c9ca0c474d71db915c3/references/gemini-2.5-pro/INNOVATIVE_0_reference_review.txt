**Summary:**
This paper addresses the problem of batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF). The authors identify a critical and previously overlooked failure mode in standard counterfactual risk minimization (CRM) approaches, which they term "Propensity Overfitting". This issue arises because the classic inverse propensity score (IPS) estimator, while unbiased, is not equivariant to shifts in the loss function and can produce risk estimates outside the true range of the loss. This allows a learning algorithm with sufficient capacity to minimize the empirical risk not by fitting the loss, but by manipulating the propensity weights—for instance, by assigning high probability to training examples with negative loss, driving the empirical risk to arbitrarily low values.

To solve this fundamental problem, the paper proposes replacing the standard IPS estimator with the self-normalized (SN) estimator. This estimator, framed as an application of multiplicative control variates, is a ratio of the standard estimator and the sum of the importance weights. The authors show that this SN estimator is equivariant, its estimates are naturally bounded within the range of the loss, and it is strongly consistent. These properties directly remedy the pathologies that cause propensity overfitting. They embed this new estimator within the CRM framework to create a new learning algorithm, Norm-POEM, and demonstrate through a series of well-designed experiments that it is robust to propensity overfitting and achieves significantly better generalization performance than the prior state-of-the-art.

**Strengths (Arguments for Acceptance):**

1.  **High Novelty and Conceptual Significance:** The primary contribution of this paper is the identification and formalization of "Propensity Overfitting". This is a genuinely new and important concept for the counterfactual learning community. While overfitting is a familiar concept, this paper elegantly demonstrates a distinct and pernicious form unique to importance-sampling-based learning. The simple example in Section 4 is brilliant and clearly illustrates how ERM can go catastrophically wrong in a way that is entirely different from standard supervised learning. This is a significant conceptual leap that fundamentally changes how we should think about the stability of learning with bandit feedback.

2.  **Elegant and Principled Solution:** The proposed solution is not an ad-hoc fix but a principled one, grounded in the theory of Monte Carlo methods. By identifying the root cause in the estimator's properties (lack of equivariance), the authors motivate the use of the self-normalized estimator, a classic but under-utilized tool in this ML context, which directly corrects these flaws. The connection drawn to control variates provides a solid theoretical foundation. The solution is simple, elegant, and directly targets the identified problem.

3.  **Strong and Insightful Empirical Validation:** The experiments are excellent. They do not merely show that Norm-POEM outperforms POEM on a few benchmarks. Instead, they are carefully designed to verify the paper's core claims. The results in Table 2 are particularly compelling: they directly measure the sum of propensity weights Ŝ(h), confirming that POEM suffers from severe bias in this term while Norm-POEM remains stable near the expected value of 1. Furthermore, the experiments testing the effect of translating the loss function provide a direct and convincing demonstration of the SN estimator's equivariance and the brittleness of the standard approach. This is exactly the kind of targeted evaluation that builds confidence in a new idea.

4.  **Clarity and Impact:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow. The motivation is clear, the problem is crisply defined, and the solution is logically derived. This work has the potential to be highly influential, as it calls into question the default use of the standard IPS estimator for *learning* (as opposed to just evaluation) and provides a robust, easy-to-implement alternative. Researchers and practitioners in off-policy reinforcement learning, causal inference, and computational advertising are likely to adopt these ideas.

**Weaknesses (Minor Points):**

1.  **Finite-Sample Bias:** The self-normalized estimator is known to be biased for finite samples, unlike the IPS estimator. The paper acknowledges this and proves consistency, which is the most important theoretical property. However, a deeper discussion of the potential effects of this bias in small-data regimes would have been welcome. Given the catastrophic failures of the "unbiased" estimator demonstrated in the paper, this trade-off seems overwhelmingly favorable, but it is a trade-off nonetheless.

2.  **Optimization:** The paper notes that the proposed objective does not easily admit stochastic optimization and relies on L-BFGS. While the experiments show this is not a practical limitation and can even lead to faster convergence, it might limit scalability to truly massive datasets where SGD is the only feasible option. This is a minor point, as the core contribution is the estimator, not the optimization algorithm, but it's worth noting.

**Recommendation: Accept**

This is an outstanding paper that exemplifies the kind of research NIPS should showcase. It takes a bold step back from an established method, identifies a deep and previously unappreciated flaw, and proposes an elegant, principled, and effective solution. The conceptual contribution of identifying "Propensity Overfitting" is significant and is likely to change the conversation around learning from logged data. The work is clear, technically sound, and convincingly validated. The minor weaknesses are far outweighed by the novelty and importance of the core idea. I strongly recommend acceptance.
