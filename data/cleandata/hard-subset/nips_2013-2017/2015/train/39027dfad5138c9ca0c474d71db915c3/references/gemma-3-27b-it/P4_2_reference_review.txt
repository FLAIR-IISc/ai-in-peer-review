This paper addresses the problem of batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF), highlighting a previously unrecognized issue called "Propensity Overfitting" in the standard counterfactual risk estimator. The authors propose a Self-Normalized Risk Estimator to mitigate this problem and demonstrate its effectiveness both theoretically and empirically.

The paper is well-motivated, connecting BLBF to related areas like causal inference, off-policy evaluation, and domain adaptation. The identification of Propensity Overfitting is a novel contribution, clearly explained with a compelling example. The proposed Self-Normalized estimator, leveraging control variates, is a sensible approach to address the identified issue, drawing on existing literature in importance sampling. The experimental validation, using a well-established "Supervised 7→ Bandit" methodology, provides strong evidence supporting the effectiveness of the proposed method. The comparison to POEM and the analysis of the Ŝ(h) values further solidify the claims. The connection to CRM and the discussion of computational efficiency are also valuable.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The identification of Propensity Overfitting is a novel and significant contribution to the field of BLBF.
*   **Technical Soundness:** The theoretical analysis, including the proof of Theorem 2, is rigorous and supports the proposed solution. The use of control variates is well-justified.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the problem, the proposed solution, and the experimental setup. The example in Section 4 is particularly helpful.
*   **Significance:** Addressing the issue of Propensity Overfitting has the potential to significantly improve the performance and robustness of learning algorithms from logged bandit feedback, with broad implications for real-world applications.
*   **Empirical Validation:** Thorough experimentation demonstrates the efficacy of the proposed method, consistently outperforming the baseline POEM algorithm. The ablation studies examining the impact of variance regularization are insightful.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Computational Cost Discussion:** While the paper states Norm-POEM's runtime is comparable to POEM, a more detailed discussion of the computational complexity of the variance estimate (Equation 8) and its impact on scalability would be beneficial.
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper mentions hyperparameter calibration, but doesn’t provide detailed insights into the sensitivity of the results to the choices of λ and M.
*   **Limited Dataset Variety:** The experiments are conducted on a limited set of datasets. Extending the evaluation to more diverse and larger datasets would strengthen the conclusions.
*   **Connection to Existing Literature on Equivariance:** The discussion of equivariance (or lack thereof) is important, but could be strengthened by a more thorough connection to the broader literature on robust estimators and equivariant risk measures, particularly in causal inference.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper addresses a significant and previously unrecognized problem in BLBF.
*   The proposed solution is technically sound, theoretically justified, and empirically validated.
*   The experimental results are compelling and demonstrate a clear improvement over existing methods.
*   The work has the potential to have a significant impact on the field of learning from logged data.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   The computational complexity discussion could be more detailed.
*   The hyperparameter sensitivity analysis is limited.
*   The experimental evaluation could benefit from a wider range of datasets.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of batch learning from logged bandit feedback. The identification of Propensity Overfitting is a novel and important finding, and the proposed Self-Normalized estimator provides an effective solution. While there are some minor weaknesses, the strengths of the paper clearly outweigh them. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions. Specifically, the authors should expand on the computational complexity discussion, provide more details on hyperparameter sensitivity, and consider including results on a larger and more diverse set of datasets.
