This paper addresses the problem of batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF), a crucial setting for improving interactive systems like search and recommendation. The authors identify a novel and critical failure mode in the standard approach, which uses Empirical Risk Minimization (ERM) on an importance-weighted (or inverse propensity score) risk estimator. They term this failure mode "Propensity Overfitting," where a high-capacity model learns to minimize the estimated risk not by fitting the true loss function, but by manipulating the importance weights themselves. To solve this, they propose replacing the standard unbiased estimator with the self-normalized importance sampling estimator, which is a classic ratio estimator from the Monte Carlo literature. They integrate this estimator into the Counterfactual Risk Minimization (CRM) framework, resulting in a new algorithm called Norm-POEM. The paper provides a clear theoretical motivation for the self-normalized estimator's desirable properties (e.g., equivariance, bounded estimates) and presents strong empirical results demonstrating its superiority over the previous state-of-the-art.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Novel Problem Identification:** The paper's most significant contribution is the clear identification and articulation of "Propensity Overfitting." The authors provide a simple yet powerful example (Example 1) that perfectly illustrates how ERM with the standard inverse propensity score estimator can lead to degenerate solutions with arbitrarily low (and meaningless) risk estimates. This is a subtle but fundamental problem that has been largely overlooked in the BLBF literature, and its clear exposition is a major service to the community.

2.  **Principled and Effective Solution:** The proposed solution is not an ad-hoc heuristic but is grounded in the well-established statistical technique of control variates. The choice of the self-normalized estimator directly addresses the identified anomalies of the standard estimator. It is equivariant to loss translations and guarantees that risk estimates remain within the range of observed losses, effectively preventing the kind of overfitting described. This provides a much more stable and robust foundation for learning.

3.  **Strong and Convincing Empirical Evaluation:** The experimental setup, using the "Supervised -> Bandit" methodology, is well-controlled and allows for a clear comparison against a known ground truth. The results are compelling:
    *   Norm-POEM consistently outperforms the baseline (POEM) in terms of generalization error.
    *   The experiments directly validate the theoretical claims. Table 2 shows that the sum of propensity weights Ŝ(h) for the baseline is wildly off from its expectation of 1, confirming that Propensity Overfitting is occurring, while Norm-POEM remains stable and close to 1.
    *   The experiments on loss translation demonstrate the practical importance of the equivariance property, as POEM's performance degrades catastrophically while Norm-POEM's is unaffected.
    *   The finding that Norm-POEM can be computationally more efficient due to faster convergence is a surprising and valuable practical result.

4.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is exceptionally well-written. The motivation is clear, the problem is precisely defined, and the proposed solution is logically derived. The flow from identifying the problem (Section 4) to proposing the solution (Section 5) and instantiating it as a learning algorithm (Section 6) is seamless.

**Weaknesses and Questions for Authors:**

1.  **Novelty of the Estimator:** The self-normalized estimator itself is a classic tool in the importance sampling and survey statistics literature. The authors acknowledge this by citing relevant work (e.g., Hesterberg, Kong). While the application to solve Propensity Overfitting in BLBF is certainly novel and insightful, the paper could be slightly clearer in positioning the core novelty as the diagnosis of the problem and the successful application of this existing statistical tool, rather than the invention of the estimator itself.

2.  **Bias of the Estimator:** The self-normalized estimator is known to be biased for finite sample sizes, although it is consistent. The paper proves consistency but does not discuss the nature or magnitude of this finite-sample bias. While the empirical results suggest that the bias introduced is less harmful than the high variance and overfitting potential of the unbiased estimator, a brief discussion of the bias-variance trade-off, perhaps citing work by Kong et al. or Owen, would strengthen the theoretical exposition. Is it possible that for very small `n`, this bias could be problematic?

3.  **Optimization and Scalability:** The authors note that the variance term for Norm-POEM does not admit stochastic optimization, necessitating the use of a batch optimizer like L-BFGS. While they report faster convergence in their experiments, this could be a limitation for massive datasets where SGD-based methods are often the only feasible option. It would be valuable to comment on the prospects for developing a scalable, perhaps stochastic, optimization procedure for the Norm-POEM objective.

**Evaluation Summary:**

*   **Quality:** High. The paper is technically sound. The claims are well-supported by both theoretical arguments (equivariance, consistency) and a thorough, well-designed set of experiments. It is a complete and polished piece of work.
*   **Clarity:** Excellent. The paper is a model of clarity. The writing is precise, the organization is logical, and the core concepts are explained with exceptional lucidity.
*   **Originality:** High. The key original contribution is the identification and diagnosis of Propensity Overfitting, a crucial and previously unarticulated problem in counterfactual learning. The application of the self-normalized estimator as a solution is a novel and highly effective idea in this context.
*   **Significance:** High. Learning from logged bandit feedback is a problem of immense practical importance. This paper addresses a fundamental weakness in the standard approach and provides a robust, practical, and demonstrably superior alternative. The insights regarding Propensity Overfitting are likely to influence future work in off-policy evaluation and learning. Researchers and practitioners working with logged data will likely find this work immediately useful.

**Recommendation:**

This is a strong paper that makes a clear and important contribution. It identifies a fundamental problem, proposes an elegant and principled solution, and validates it with convincing experiments. I strongly recommend acceptance.
