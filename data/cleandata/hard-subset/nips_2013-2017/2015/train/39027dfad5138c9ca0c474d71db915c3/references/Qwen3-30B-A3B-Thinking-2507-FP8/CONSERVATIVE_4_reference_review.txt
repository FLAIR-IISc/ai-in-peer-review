

# Review of "The Self-Normalized Estimator for Counterfactual Learning"

This paper proposes a "Self-Normalized Estimator" to address a problem the authors call "Propensity Overfitting" in batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF). The authors argue that conventional counterfactual estimators exhibit severe anomalies leading to degenerate risk estimates, and they propose replacing the standard risk estimator with a self-normalized version that they claim avoids these issues. They derive a new algorithm called Norm-POEM based on this estimator and report empirical improvements over the existing POEM algorithm.

The paper makes a theoretically interesting observation about a pathological case of overfitting in counterfactual learning that seems to be a special case of the well-known issues with importance sampling. However, I have significant concerns about the paper's claims and the evidence supporting them.

**Strengths:**
- The paper identifies a theoretically interesting problem in counterfactual learning that deserves attention.
- The self-normalized estimator is shown to have desirable theoretical properties (consistency, equivariance, bounded risk estimates).
- The empirical results show consistent improvements over POEM across multiple datasets, which is promising.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Problem formulation is overly narrow:** The "Propensity Overfitting" problem is demonstrated using a highly contrived example (Section 4) that may not reflect real-world scenarios. The authors use a specific loss function (δ(x,y) = -2 if y=x, -1 otherwise) and a hypothesis space of deterministic functions, which is not representative of typical BLBF applications. This raises serious questions about how prevalent and severe this problem is in practice.

2. **Lack of comparison to established methods:** The paper fails to compare against other well-established approaches for handling high-variance importance sampling, such as Doubly Robust Estimators [6] or variance reduction techniques that have been extensively studied in the literature. The paper only compares to POEM, which is a specific implementation of the CRM principle, but not to the broader set of techniques that address importance sampling challenges.

3. **Insufficient empirical validation:** The empirical evaluation is limited to a few datasets from the LibSVM repository using the "Supervised 7→ Bandit" methodology, which creates a controlled synthetic setting rather than real-world data. The paper doesn't demonstrate the approach on real-world logged bandit feedback data from applications like recommendation systems or ad platforms, which would be necessary to establish practical relevance.

4. **Misleading claims about "provably avoiding degeneracies":** While the self-normalized estimator has better theoretical properties in the specific pathological case, the paper overstates the significance of this. The conventional estimator with thresholding (R̂M(h)) is a standard approach that addresses the variance issues the paper identifies, and the paper doesn't properly analyze how their method compares to this established technique.

5. **Lack of statistical significance testing:** The reported performance improvements (Table 1) lack statistical significance testing, making it difficult to determine whether the observed improvements are meaningful or simply due to random variation.

6. **Inadequate discussion of trade-offs:** The paper claims the self-normalized estimator "provably avoids degeneracies" but doesn't properly address the trade-offs. The self-normalized estimator is not unbiased, and the paper doesn't explore the bias-variance trade-off in detail. The claim that "the improved estimator leads to significantly improved generalization performance" is not sufficiently supported by the evidence.

7. **Overstated novelty:** The self-normalized estimator has been studied in the statistics literature for decades (e.g., [19, 22, 23] cited by the authors), and the paper doesn't adequately contextualize their contribution within this broader literature. They present it as a novel solution to a problem, but the technique itself is not new.

**Overall assessment:**

While the paper identifies an interesting theoretical issue in counterfactual learning, the evidence supporting the practical significance of this problem and the superiority of their proposed solution is insufficient. The paper relies on a highly specific pathological case to motivate a new approach, but fails to demonstrate how common this problem is in real-world applications or how their method compares to established techniques for addressing importance sampling challenges.

The empirical evaluation is limited to a controlled synthetic setting, and the paper doesn't provide sufficient evidence that the proposed method would be beneficial in practical applications like recommendation systems or ad platforms. The improvements over POEM are modest and not statistically validated, and the paper doesn't provide a thorough analysis of the computational trade-offs.

As a reviewer who prefers established methods and is skeptical of new approaches without extensive validation, I find the paper's claims to be overstated. The problem they identify may be theoretically interesting, but it's not clear that it represents a significant practical challenge in BLBF that requires a new estimator. The paper would need to demonstrate that Propensity Overfitting is a common and severe problem in real-world applications, and that their solution provides substantial practical benefits over established methods to justify its adoption.

I recommend rejection, as the paper does not provide sufficient evidence to support its claims and the contribution appears incremental rather than transformative for the field. The authors should first demonstrate the prevalence and severity of Propensity Overfitting in real-world BLBF applications before proposing a new estimator to address it.
