### Review of "The Self-Normalized Estimator for Counterfactual Learning"

**Summary**

This paper addresses the problem of batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF). The authors identify a critical failure mode in learning algorithms that perform Empirical Risk Minimization (ERM) using the standard inverse propensity score (IPS) estimator. They term this failure mode "Propensity Overfitting," where the learning algorithm minimizes the estimated risk not by fitting the underlying loss function, but by exploiting the propensity weights—either by assigning high probability to examples with negative loss or zero probability to examples with positive loss. This can lead to severely biased risk estimates and poor generalization.

To remedy this, the authors propose replacing the standard IPS estimator with the self-normalized importance sampling (SNIS) estimator. This estimator, a well-known ratio estimator from the Monte Carlo literature, normalizes the weighted losses by the sum of the importance weights. The authors show that this estimator is equivariant to shifts in the loss function and its estimates are naturally bounded within the range of the observed losses, thus directly mitigating the mechanisms behind propensity overfitting. They incorporate this estimator into the Counterfactual Risk Minimization (CRM) framework, leading to a new algorithm called Norm-POEM. Empirical results on several multi-label classification tasks demonstrate that Norm-POEM significantly outperforms its predecessor (POEM), is more robust, and successfully avoids the identified overfitting problem.

**Quality**

The paper is technically sound. The diagnosis of "Propensity Overfitting" is clear, well-motivated by a simple yet compelling example, and convincingly demonstrated in the experiments (Table 2). The proposed solution—employing the self-normalized estimator—is a principled and appropriate application of an established statistical technique to address this specific problem. The theoretical claims, such as the consistency of the estimator, are standard but correctly stated and provide a solid foundation for the method.

However, the experimental evaluation, while strong in demonstrating the advantage over the direct baseline (POEM), is lacking in breadth. The field of off-policy evaluation has long recognized the high variance of the IPS estimator and has developed powerful alternatives. The most prominent among these is the Doubly Robust (DR) estimator [6], which combines a regression model with importance sampling to reduce variance. The DR estimator is a standard and often state-of-the-art baseline in this domain. Its omission from the experimental comparison is a significant weakness. Without this comparison, it is difficult to ascertain whether Norm-POEM advances the state of the art or simply fixes a known flaw in the POEM framework while still potentially underperforming other established methods. The claims of superiority are only demonstrated relative to a method that has been shown to be fundamentally flawed.

**Clarity**

The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The motivation for the work is presented clearly in the introduction, and the "Propensity Overfitting" problem is explained with an excellent illustrative example in Section 4. The connection between this problem and the properties of the standard IPS estimator is well-argued. The transition to the self-normalized estimator as a solution feels natural and well-justified. The description of the Norm-POEM algorithm and the experimental setup are detailed enough to allow for reproducibility.

**Originality**

The main contribution of this paper is not the invention of a new estimator, but rather the identification and clear articulation of the "Propensity Overfitting" problem in the context of ERM for BLBF, and the application of a known, but seemingly underutilized, estimator to solve it. The self-normalized estimator itself is a classic tool from the importance sampling literature [12, 19]. While the authors correctly cite this prior work, the novelty lies in demonstrating how this specific tool elegantly resolves the structural issues (e.g., lack of equivariance, unbounded estimates) that cause propensity overfitting during learning, a context different from its traditional use for pure evaluation. This reframing of a known variance issue as a specific type of learning-induced overfitting is a useful conceptual contribution.

**Significance**

The paper addresses a significant and practical problem. As learning from logged data becomes increasingly common, understanding and mitigating the pathologies of counterfactual estimators is of high importance. The finding that a naive ERM approach with IPS can fail catastrophically is a crucial lesson. The proposed solution is simple, effective, and does not add significant conceptual or computational complexity compared to the baseline. Researchers and practitioners using IPS-based learning objectives would likely benefit from adopting the self-normalized estimator.

However, the ultimate significance is tempered by the limited experimental comparison mentioned under "Quality." If established methods like the Doubly Robust estimator are not susceptible to propensity overfitting and perform better, then the impact of this work would be primarily as a diagnostic tool and an incremental improvement to the specific POEM/CRM line of work, rather than a broadly applicable state-of-the-art method.

**Strengths and Weaknesses**

**Strengths:**
1.  **Clear Problem Diagnosis:** The paper provides a clear and compelling analysis of "Propensity Overfitting," a critical and previously under-appreciated failure mode for counterfactual learning.
2.  **Principled Solution:** The proposed use of the self-normalized estimator is a methodologically sound and elegant solution that directly addresses the identified pathologies of the standard IPS estimator.
3.  **Strong Empirical Evidence:** The experiments convincingly demonstrate the existence of the problem and the effectiveness of the proposed solution compared to the direct baseline (POEM). The robustness to loss translation is a particularly strong result.
4.  **High Clarity:** The paper is very well-written, making a complex topic accessible and the arguments easy to follow.

**Weaknesses:**
1.  **Limited Baselines:** The lack of an experimental comparison to other established and powerful off-policy estimators, particularly the Doubly Robust (DR) estimator, is a major omission. This makes it impossible to judge the method's performance in the broader context of the field.
2.  **Modest Originality of the Core Technique:** The central tool, the self-normalized estimator, is not new. The primary contribution is its application and analysis in a learning context, which may be seen as incremental by some.

**Conclusion**

This is a good paper that makes a valuable contribution by identifying and providing a simple, sound solution for a serious problem in counterfactual learning. The analysis of propensity overfitting is insightful, and the empirical results are convincing in their own context. My main reservation stems from the narrowness of the experimental comparison. While the paper convincingly shows that Norm-POEM is superior to POEM, the lack of comparison against stronger, widely-accepted baselines like Doubly Robust methods makes it difficult to endorse the proposed method as a general-purpose solution. Nonetheless, the paper's clear diagnosis of a subtle but important issue is a solid contribution to the field. I would recommend acceptance, but I strongly urge the authors to include a comparison with a DR baseline in the final version to properly contextualize their method's performance.
