

This paper identifies a novel problem—*Propensity Overfitting*—in counterfactual learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF), where conventional unbiased risk estimators (e.g., inverse propensity weighting) produce severely biased risk estimates during Empirical Risk Minimization (ERM). The authors propose a *Self-Normalized Risk Estimator* using multiplicative control variates to resolve this issue, proving its strong consistency and equivariance properties. They develop *Norm-POEM*, a learning algorithm based on this estimator, and demonstrate consistent empirical improvements over prior methods (e.g., POEM) across multiple datasets.

**Strengths:**  
1. **Originality & Problem Identification:** The paper’s core contribution—*Propensity Overfitting*—is novel and well-motivated. The example in Section 4 (e.g., risk estimates arbitrarily lower than true risk) clearly exposes a critical flaw unaddressed in prior BLBF work (e.g., [1,5,6]). This problem is distinct from known overfitting types (e.g., Loss Overfitting) and significantly impacts generalization.  
2. **Theoretical Rigor:** Theoretical analysis is sound. Theorem 2 proves strong consistency of the self-normalized estimator, and the equivariance argument (Section 5) resolves key anomalies of prior estimators. The connection to control variates in importance sampling (citing [19]) is well-reasoned.  
3. **Empirical Validation:** Experiments (Section 7) are thorough and convincing. Norm-POEM consistently outperforms POEM (Table 1, Figure 1), avoids Propensity Overfitting (Table 2), and achieves faster convergence (Section 7). The use of supervised datasets converted to bandit feedback via replay aligns with established BLBF benchmarks [1,2].  
4. **Practical Impact:** Norm-POEM requires no new hyperparameters beyond POEM (e.g., *M* for clipping), has comparable runtime to POEM, and is implemented in open-source code. The runtime advantage (Section 7) is a notable practical contribution.

**Weaknesses:**  
1. **Limited Theoretical Scope:** While the paper proves consistency and equivariance, it lacks a *variance analysis* for the self-normalized estimator. The claim that it "avoids degeneracies" relies on empirical evidence (Table 2), but a theoretical bound on variance reduction (e.g., compared to POEM) would strengthen the argument. The variance estimate in Equation (8) also lacks stability guarantees when the denominator is small.  
2. **Comparison Gaps:** The paper does not compare against *doubly robust estimators* (e.g., [6]), which are state-of-the-art in counterfactual evaluation. Such a comparison would clarify whether Norm-POEM’s gains are unique to Propensity Overfitting mitigation or stem from general variance reduction.  
3. **Clipping Dependency:** The self-normalized estimator still requires clipping (*M*), and the paper does not analyze how *M* interacts with Propensity Overfitting mitigation (e.g., does larger *M* reduce bias but increase variance?). Section 7 notes variance regularization (*λ*) remains necessary (Table 3), but the interplay between *M*, *λ*, and the self-normalized estimator is underexplored.  
4. **Real-World Scalability:** Experiments use modest datasets (e.g., Scene, Yeast), but BLBF often involves *massive* logs (e.g., ad platforms). The paper does not address computational scalability (e.g., distributed training) or robustness to *extreme* covariate shift (beyond the controlled replay setup).

**Significance & Impact:**  
The problem of Propensity Overfitting is significant for real-world applications (e.g., ad systems, recommenders), where biased risk estimates lead to suboptimal policies. Norm-POEM’s consistent gains across datasets (e.g., TMC where POEM fails catastrophically) demonstrate tangible progress. However, the paper’s scope is limited to *batch* learning; the extension to *online* settings (e.g., contextual bandits with adaptive logging) would further elevate its impact.

**Recommendation:**  
**Accept**. This is a high-quality, original contribution that identifies a critical gap in BLBF and provides a theoretically grounded, empirically validated solution. The weaknesses are addressable in revision (e.g., adding variance analysis, comparing to doubly robust estimators), but the core contribution is sufficiently novel and impactful for NIPS. The paper’s clarity, thoroughness, and practical relevance align well with NIPS standards.

**Suggestions for Revision:**  
1. Add a theoretical variance comparison between the self-normalized estimator and POEM’s estimator (e.g., asymptotic bounds under specific assumptions).  
2. Include a comparison with doubly robust estimators [6] to isolate the contribution of Propensity Overfitting mitigation.  
3. Discuss how *M* and *λ* interact with the self-normalized estimator (e.g., via ablation studies).  
4. Briefly address scalability to larger datasets (e.g., via synthetic experiments with 1M+ samples).  

This work advances the state of the art in counterfactual learning and deserves publication at NIPS.
