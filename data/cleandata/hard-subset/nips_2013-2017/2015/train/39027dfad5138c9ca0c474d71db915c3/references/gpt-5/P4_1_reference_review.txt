Summary and relation to prior work
The paper addresses batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF), where only bandit feedback from a historical policy is available. Prior work (e.g., CRM/POEM) uses inverse propensity scoring (IPS) with clipping as a counterfactual risk estimator and regularizes via empirical Bernstein-style variance terms to control generalization. The authors identify a systematic failure mode of IPS in the ERM setting—Propensity Overfitting—where the empirical risk can be driven to degenerate values by manipulating the sum of importance weights, leading to highly biased minimizers. They argue that this stems from two anomalies of the standard IPS estimator: lack of boundedness and lack of translation equivariance.

To address this, they propose replacing IPS with the self-normalized importance sampling (SNIPS) estimator, viewed as using a multiplicative control variate whose expectation is known. SNIPS is a classical estimator in importance sampling (Hesterberg; Kong; Rubinstein & Kroese) and has been used in off-policy evaluation and policy iteration in RL. The authors show that SNIPS (a) is translation-equivariant, (b) is bounded by the loss range, and (c) is strongly consistent, thereby mitigating the identified degeneracies. They instantiate this within the CRM framework by defining Norm-POEM, which uses SNIPS in the objective along with an approximate variance estimator for the ratio estimator (via normal approximation/delta method) and clipping to ensure finite variance. Experiments on multilabel datasets (converted to bandit feedback via a logging CRF) show consistent improvements over POEM, robustness to loss translations, and closer adherence of the sum of weights to its expected value.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The identification of Propensity Overfitting is well-motivated and illustrated with a clear synthetic example. The use of SNIPS is appropriate and supported by known properties: boundedness, equivariance, and strong consistency (Theorem 2). The approximate variance estimator for the ratio estimator is standard and justified. The learning objective is reasonable and follows the CRM template, with clipping to control variance.
- Theoretical depth: The paper leverages existing theory (empirical Bernstein bounds for CRM; known properties of SNIPS). It does not provide a new generalization bound specialized to the SNIPS-based objective nor a formal result proving elimination of Propensity Overfitting under capacity/coverage assumptions. Claims that the approach “provably avoids anomalies” are accurate for boundedness/equivariance but not for a complete learning-theoretic guarantee relative to ERM degeneracies.
- Experimental evidence: The experiments are in a controlled “supervised-to-bandit” setup and follow prior work (POEM). Results consistently favor Norm-POEM across datasets and replay counts, and diagnostics on Ŝ(h) are compelling. However, comparisons are limited to POEM; there is no evaluation against doubly robust (DR) baselines or other variance-reduction schemes (e.g., DR with SNIPS/normalized weights). Confidence intervals/significance testing are not reported. Runtime claims are qualitative; concrete timings are not shown.

Clarity
The paper is clearly written and well-organized. The example illustrating Propensity Overfitting is effective. The connection between Ŝ(h) and overfitting is explained cleanly. Derivations and estimator definitions are standard and readable. Minor issues:
- A few typos (e.g., Section 4: “δ(·, ·) ∈ [5,4]” seems a typo; elsewhere δ is in [−1,0]). 
- It would help to specify precisely whether clipping is applied consistently to both numerator and denominator in SNIPS when used in training.
- The variance estimator’s derivation is referenced but could benefit from a brief derivation or clearer assumptions (finite variance conditions, role of clipping).

Originality
Applying SNIPS as a learning-time risk estimator within CRM to mitigate Propensity Overfitting in BLBF appears novel, as is the explicit articulation and diagnosis of Propensity Overfitting in ERM for logged bandit data. The estimator itself and the variance estimation techniques are well-known. The conceptual contribution (identifying the degeneracy and showing how SNIPS addresses it) is meaningful, though the theoretical novelty beyond known SNIPS properties is limited.

Significance
The problem is important: IPS-based ERM for BLBF is widely used and can be brittle. A practical, principled modification that improves robustness and generalization is valuable. The improvements shown across multiple datasets are consistent and the diagnostics corroborate the mechanism. Broader impact may extend to off-policy learning in contextual bandits and RL policy optimization, where normalized estimators are often used for evaluation but less so for optimization. The lack of comparisons to DR-style methods and the absence of a tailored generalization bound temper claims of state-of-the-art advancement.

Pros
- Clear identification and demonstration of Propensity Overfitting in IPS-based ERM.
- Principled fix via a classical estimator (SNIPS) with desirable boundedness/equivariance.
- Strong consistency proof (standard but appropriate).
- Empirical gains across datasets and sample sizes; robust to loss translation; diagnostic alignment (Ŝ close to 1).
- Simple drop-in replacement within CRM; practical optimization with L-BFGS.

Cons
- Limited theoretical development specific to the new objective (no new generalization bounds; no formal guarantee against ERM degeneracy beyond estimator properties).
- Baselines are limited; no comparison to DR/doubly robust learning or other advanced off-policy estimators.
- Experimental setting is simulated from supervised data; lack of real logged bandit datasets.
- Variance estimator is approximate; no analysis of its accuracy or sensitivity to clipping M and λ.
- Runtime claims lack quantitative evidence.

Suggestions/questions for authors
- Provide comparisons to DR-based learners (e.g., doubly robust IPS, clipped DR, or normalized DR), and to alternative variance-control strategies.
- Include confidence intervals or statistical tests for reported improvements and report actual runtimes.
- Clarify how clipping is applied in the SNIPS objective (both numerator/denominator) and study sensitivity to M and λ.
- Consider a learning-theoretic bound tailored to the SNIPS-based CRM objective (e.g., using ratio concentration or self-normalized processes), to complement the empirical findings.
- Fix minor typos and explicitly state assumptions (full support/coverage, bounded losses, finite variance).

Overall recommendation
Weak accept. The paper addresses a real and under-discussed failure mode of IPS-based BLBF and offers a principled, effective remedy with solid empirical support. The main limitation is the modest theoretical novelty and limited baselines; addressing these would strengthen the contribution.
