This paper addresses the important problem of learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF) and identifies a novel issue termed “Propensity Overfitting” that affects standard counterfactual risk estimation techniques. The authors propose a Self-Normalized Risk Estimator to mitigate this overfitting and demonstrate its effectiveness both theoretically and empirically.

The paper is well-motivated, clearly building upon prior work in causal inference, off-policy evaluation, and batch reinforcement learning. It correctly identifies a weakness in existing approaches to BLBF – the potential for instability and bias when using inverse propensity weighting with complex hypothesis spaces. The introduction of Propensity Overfitting as a distinct phenomenon is insightful. The proposed Self-Normalized estimator is a natural extension of existing variance reduction techniques (specifically, self-normalization in importance sampling) and the theoretical justification provided is sound. The experimental validation, utilizing a standard supervised-to-bandit framework, convincingly shows the improvements offered by the proposed method over POEM. The connection to equivariant estimators and the analysis of how the self-normalized estimator resolves issues with loss translation are particularly strong points. The code availability is also a positive aspect.

However, several aspects could be strengthened. While the theoretical analysis establishes the consistency of the estimator, a more detailed analysis of its variance properties, especially in relation to the capacity of the hypothesis space, would be beneficial. The empirical evaluation, although thorough, primarily focuses on relatively small datasets (in terms of features and instances).  Extending the experiments to larger, more realistic datasets would further bolster the claims. The discussion of computational complexity, while noting faster convergence, could be more rigorous. A more detailed comparison of the computational cost of evaluating the variance estimator (Equation 8) versus the original POEM’s variance estimate would be helpful.  The paper assumes full support of the logging policy, which is a strong assumption. A discussion of the implications of violating this assumption and potential mitigations would be valuable.  Additionally, the examples provided to illustrate propensity overfitting (Example 1) feel somewhat contrived. While they effectively demonstrate the issue, they don't necessarily reflect the types of scenarios encountered in real-world applications.  Finally, a more detailed discussion of the hyperparameters (M and λ) and their sensitivity could be included.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** Identifies and formalizes a previously unrecognised issue (Propensity Overfitting) in BLBF.
*   **Theoretical Soundness:** Provides a clear theoretical justification for the proposed Self-Normalized estimator, building upon existing literature on importance sampling and control variates.
*   **Empirical Validation:** Demonstrates a consistent and significant performance improvement over a strong baseline (POEM) on a standard benchmark.
*   **Practical Relevance:** Addresses a core challenge in learning from observational data with significant applications in areas like advertising, recommendation systems, and healthcare.
*   **Clarity and Presentation:** The paper is well-written, clearly structured, and easy to follow.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Limited Scalability Demonstration:** The empirical evaluation is conducted on relatively small datasets.
*   **Strong Assumption of Full Support:** The reliance on full support of the logging policy may limit the applicability of the method.
*   **Contrived Example:** The example illustrating propensity overfitting feels artificial.
*   **Variance Analysis could be deepened**: While consistency is shown, a more complete variance analysis would be valuable.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a significant contribution to the field of learning from logged bandit feedback. The identification of Propensity Overfitting and the proposed solution are novel and well-supported by both theory and experiments. While there are areas for improvement, the strengths of the paper clearly outweigh its weaknesses.  I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions addressing the points raised above, particularly expanding the empirical evaluation and discussing the implications of the full support assumption.  The authors have provided a valuable tool for practitioners and researchers working with observational data.
