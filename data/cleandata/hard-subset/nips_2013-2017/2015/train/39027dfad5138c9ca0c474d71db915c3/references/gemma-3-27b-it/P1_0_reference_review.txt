## Peer Review: The Self-Normalized Estimator for Counterfactual Learning

**Summary:**

This paper addresses the problem of batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF), a common scenario in many interactive systems. The authors identify a novel form of overfitting, termed "Propensity Overfitting," that plagues existing counterfactual risk estimators used in this setting. They propose a "Self-Normalized Risk Estimator" based on multiplicative control variates to mitigate this issue, drawing inspiration from financial applications and reinforcement learning. They demonstrate theoretically that their estimator avoids the identified degeneracy and empirically show improved generalization performance and robustness across several datasets. The core idea is sound, and the paper presents a well-motivated and technically rigorous solution to a previously unrecognised problem in BLBF.

**Relation to Previous Work:**

The paper appropriately situates its work within the broader context of causal inference, domain adaptation, and off-policy evaluation. It clearly acknowledges the connections to existing counterfactual estimators (e.g., those by Dudík et al. and Bottou et al.) and highlights how their approach differs. The referencing is comprehensive and covers relevant literature on importance sampling, control variates, and previous work in BLBF (particularly POEM by Swaminathan & Joachims). The connection to the self-normalized estimator from the importance sampling literature is well explained.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The identification of Propensity Overfitting is a significant contribution. This is a subtle issue that hasn't been previously recognised in the BLBF/off-policy evaluation literature.
* **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper provides a clear theoretical justification for the proposed estimator, demonstrating its improved properties compared to existing methods. The proof of Theorem 2 is concise and strengthens the argument.
* **Empirical Validation:** The experimental results are compelling. The consistent outperformance of Norm-POEM over POEM across multiple datasets, coupled with the analysis of Ŝ(h) and its relation to the propensity overfitting problem, provides strong evidence for the effectiveness of the proposed approach.
* **Clarity of Explanation:** The paper is generally well-written and logically organized. The explanation of the self-normalized estimator and the intuition behind propensity overfitting are particularly clear.
* **Practical Relevance:** The problem of learning from logged data is highly relevant to many real-world applications, making this work potentially impactful. The computational efficiency discussion adds to its practicality.
* **Equivariance Property:** The demonstration of the estimator’s equivariance to loss translations is a valuable theoretical and practical insight.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Computational Complexity:** While the authors claim the runtime is comparable to POEM, the discussion of the variance estimation (Equation 8) being harder to optimize is a concern. A more detailed analysis of the computational cost, especially compared to alternative variance reduction techniques, would be beneficial.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** While the authors address hyperparameter tuning, a more detailed sensitivity analysis to the choice of *M* (clipping parameter) and *λ* (regularization strength) would be useful. How robust are the results to suboptimal hyperparameter settings?
* **Limited Scope of Experiments:** The experiments primarily focus on datasets from the LibSVM repository.  Exploring the performance of Norm-POEM on more diverse and realistic datasets (e.g., those from recommender systems or advertising platforms) would further strengthen the empirical evaluation.
* **Assumption of Full Support:** The assumption of full support for the historical policy h0 is stated, but the impact of violating this assumption isn’t thoroughly discussed. In practice, h0 might have limited coverage of the action space.
* **Theoretical Link to CRM:** While the paper builds upon the CRM principle, the link between preventing propensity overfitting and improving the bounds in Theorem 1 could be further elucidated.  How does the self-normalized estimator specifically lead to a tighter bound?



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper identifies and addresses a significant and previously unrecognised issue in batch learning from logged bandit feedback.
* The proposed self-normalized estimator is theoretically sound and empirically validated.
* The paper is well-written, clearly explained, and provides a comprehensive overview of related work.
* The results have the potential to improve the performance of learning algorithms in many real-world applications.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The computational complexity discussion could be more thorough.
* The experiments could be expanded to include more diverse datasets.
* The robustness to hyperparameter settings could be further investigated.

**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a strong contribution to the field of batch learning from logged bandit feedback. The identification of Propensity Overfitting is a significant insight, and the proposed self-normalized estimator provides an elegant and effective solution. While there are a few minor weaknesses, the strengths of the paper clearly outweigh them. 

**Recommendation:**

**Accept with Minor Revisions.** I recommend accepting this paper for NIPS, contingent on the authors addressing the weaknesses outlined above. Specifically, I suggest providing a more detailed discussion of the computational complexity, conducting a sensitivity analysis of the hyperparameters, and considering experiments on more diverse datasets.
