## Review of "The Self-Normalized Estimator for Counterfactual Learning"

**Summary:**

This paper addresses a critical problem in batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF): propensity overfitting, a novel form of overfitting arising from the use of standard importance sampling estimators. The authors demonstrate that conventional estimators can produce severely biased risk estimates, even with unbiased losses. To mitigate this, they propose a self-normalized risk estimator, drawing inspiration from financial applications and reinforcement learning. They provide theoretical justification for their approach, demonstrating consistency, and empirically validate it across several datasets, showing improved generalization and robustness compared to existing methods like POEM.

**Strengths:**

* **Identifies a novel and important problem:** The paper highlights a previously unrecognized issue – propensity overfitting – that significantly impacts the reliability of counterfactual risk estimation in BLBF. This is a substantial contribution to the field.
* **Elegant theoretical solution:** The proposed self-normalized estimator offers a principled and theoretically sound solution to the identified problem. The explanation connecting it to control variates and self-normalization techniques is clear and convincing.  The consistency proof (Theorem 2) is a welcome addition.
* **Strong empirical validation:** The experiments are well-designed, following the established "Supervised → Bandit" methodology.  The results consistently demonstrate the superiority of Norm-POEM over POEM, with clear evidence that the improved performance is linked to reduced susceptibility to propensity overfitting (as shown in Table 2).
* **Clear and well-written:** The paper is generally well-organized and clearly written. The authors effectively explain complex concepts and relate their work to existing literature.
* **Practical Implementation:** Providing a link to the code fosters reproducibility and allows the community to quickly benefit from their contribution.
* **Equivariance property:** The demonstration that the self-normalized estimator is equivariant is a significant advantage, addressing a weakness of the conventional estimator.



**Weaknesses:**

* **Computational Complexity:** While the authors state that Norm-POEM is surprisingly faster than POEM, the explanation is somewhat hand-wavy.  A more detailed analysis of the computational complexity, and potentially profiling results, would be beneficial. The comment regarding needing L-BFGS and the difficulty of stochastic optimization could be expanded on.
* **Limited Scope of Experiments:**  The experiments focus on datasets with relatively low dimensionality and use a specific feature map.  Exploring the performance of Norm-POEM on larger, more complex datasets, and with different feature representations, would strengthen the empirical validation.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:**  The paper mentions hyperparameter calibration, but doesn't delve into the sensitivity of the performance to these parameters (M and λ). A brief analysis of this sensitivity would be valuable.
* **Connection to Existing Literature on Robust Estimation:** While the paper cites related work on importance sampling and control variates, it could benefit from a more thorough discussion of connections to the broader literature on robust estimation and outlier detection. Techniques for handling extreme weights are common in other fields, and relating this work to those approaches could provide further insights.
* **Variance Estimation:** The approximate variance estimate used in Norm-POEM, while standard, is still an approximation. Discussing the potential impact of this approximation on the performance of the algorithm would be helpful.



**Originality:**

The identification of propensity overfitting is a novel and significant contribution. While the self-normalized estimator itself is not entirely new (it has been used in other domains), its application to the specific problem of BLBF and its theoretical justification in this context are original. The combination of this estimator with the CRM principle and the development of Norm-POEM represents a valuable advance in the field.

**Significance:**

This work is highly significant. The problem of learning from logged bandit feedback is increasingly important in many real-world applications. Propensity overfitting represents a fundamental challenge to the reliable application of counterfactual learning in this setting.  The proposed solution is both theoretically sound and empirically validated, making it likely to be adopted by researchers and practitioners working in this area.  The potential for improved performance in systems like recommender engines and ad platforms is substantial.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* Addresses a critical, previously unrecognised problem in BLBF.
* Presents a novel and theoretically grounded solution.
* Demonstrates strong empirical performance across multiple datasets.
* Clear, well-written and well-organized.
* Code availability promotes reproducibility.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* Computational complexity analysis could be more detailed.
* Experimental scope could be broadened.
* Hyperparameter sensitivity analysis would strengthen the results.




**Overall Assessment:**

I strongly recommend accepting this paper. It represents a significant advance in the field of batch learning from logged bandit feedback, addressing a critical problem with a novel and effective solution. While there are some minor areas for improvement, the strengths of the paper far outweigh its weaknesses. This is a high-quality contribution that is likely to have a lasting impact on the field.

**Rating:** 8.5/10 (Strong Accept)
