Summary
This paper studies batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF). The authors diagnose a failure mode of inverse propensity scoring (IPS) when used inside empirical risk minimization for learning, which they term “propensity overfitting”: the IPS objective can be driven by manipulation of importance weights rather than by true loss reduction, and is not equivariant to loss shifts. They propose replacing IPS with the self-normalized importance sampling estimator (SNIPS), treating the sum of weights as a multiplicative control variate. They argue SNIPS is bounded and equivariant, and (trivially) strongly consistent, thereby avoiding the anomalies of IPS. They instantiate a learning algorithm, Norm-POEM, which mirrors POEM’s CRM framework but uses SNIPS plus an asymptotic variance estimate in the regularizer. Experiments on standard multi-label datasets using the “supervised→bandit” methodology show Norm-POEM outperforms POEM and is more robust to loss translations.

Relation to prior work
- The paper is well-situated within BLBF and OPE. The use of importance sampling, clipping, control variates, and doubly robust methods are appropriately cited (Rosenbaum & Rubin; Dudík et al.; Hesterberg; Ionides; Owen).
- Self-normalized importance sampling (also known as weighted IPS) is a long-standing technique in IS/OPE; its variance/bias trade-offs and practical benefits are well-documented (e.g., Hesterberg; Owen; Kong).
- POEM/CRM is prior work by the authors; this paper replaces IPS with SNIPS within that framework.
- The “propensity overfitting” phenomenon is a useful articulation of known IPS pathologies (weight explosion, lack of equivariance), illustrated here with a clear example.

Strengths
- Clear exposition of IPS pathologies when used for learning, with simple, instructive examples.
- The proposal is principled: SNIPS as a multiplicative control variate directly targets the identified degeneracy (sum of weights deviating from 1), restoring boundedness and translation equivariance.
- The Norm-POEM instantiation is natural, and the variance estimate for the ratio estimator is standard.
- Empirical results consistently show improvements over POEM across datasets and training sizes; the diagnostic Ŝ(h) aligns with theory.
- The paper is generally well-written and easy to follow.

Weaknesses and concerns
Quality
- Theoretical novelty is limited. The strong consistency of SNIPS and its equivariance/boundedness are well-known; the paper does not provide new finite-sample guarantees or a CRM-style generalization bound tailored to the SNIPS objective. The asymptotic variance plugged into CRM lacks a corresponding finite-sample justification akin to the empirical-Bernstein bound used in POEM.
- The claim that SNIPS “provably avoids degeneracies” is overstated. While it avoids the specific IPS anomalies (range violation, lack of equivariance), SNIPS can still be unstable when the denominator is small and remains biased; the paper mitigates this with clipping but does not analyze residual bias-variance tradeoffs or derive conditions under which “propensity overfitting” cannot occur in finite samples.
- Baselines are too narrow. Comparison is primarily to POEM. Missing are:
  - Doubly robust (DR) learning objectives (e.g., Dudík et al.; later SWITCH-DR-type baselines), which are known to substantially stabilize OPE/learning when a decent reward model exists.
  - Weighted IPS/SNIPS variants already used in contextual bandits, and combinations with clipping/defensive mixtures.
  - High-confidence OPE-informed learning or trust-region style constraints (Thomas et al.; TRPO-inspired constraints) that directly control distribution shift.
- Experimental setting is simulated via “supervised→bandit” with a stochastic logging policy trained on 5% labels. This is standard but may favor methods robust to loss translation while not capturing common real-world issues (partial support, misspecified propensities). No real logged data experiments are provided.
- Hyperparameter selection via counterfactual evaluation on held-out logs is sensitive; the paper does not assess selection bias or report confidence intervals. Reported results lack error bars.

Clarity
- Overall clear, but some claims (avoid degeneracies) should be tempered and precisely scoped (what degeneracies, under what conditions).
- The variance estimator (Eq. 8) is introduced via asymptotic arguments; a brief derivation or appendix would help.
- Runtime claims are qualitative; actual wall-clock and iteration counts should be reported.

Originality
- The main algorithmic change is replacing IPS with SNIPS inside CRM. Given SNIPS is a standard estimator in OPE and the finite-sample CRM theory is not extended, the methodological novelty is incremental.
- The articulation and empirical diagnosis of “propensity overfitting” are useful, but conceptually close to known IPS pathologies.

Significance
- If robust across stronger baselines and settings, the result would be practically useful for BLBF. As is, the limited comparisons and lack of finite-sample theory temper impact.

Questions for the authors
- Can you provide a finite-sample generalization bound for the SNIPS-based CRM objective, analogous to the empirical-Bernstein bound used in POEM?
- How does Norm-POEM compare to DR-based learning objectives (with a simple reward model) and to SWITCH-DR variants?
- How sensitive are results to clipping M and λ? Please provide ablation and confidence intervals.
- What happens under more realistic logging (partial support or deterministic with exploration scavenging)? Any safeguards?
- Can you report actual runtime numbers and iterations across datasets?

Suggestions for improvement
- Add DR and SWITCH-DR baselines; include SNIPS+DR for completeness.
- Provide finite-sample analysis (even under simplifying assumptions) showing how normalization interacts with CRM to prevent the specific propensity manipulation you highlight.
- Include real-world logged bandit datasets or stronger semi-synthetic protocols with heavier-tailed propensities and partial support.
- Report error bars, hyperparameter sensitivity, and wall-clock times.
- Calibrate claims around “provably avoids degeneracies” to the precise properties shown (boundedness, equivariance, consistency).

Pros
- Clear problem diagnosis; simple, principled fix via a well-established technique.
- Consistent empirical gains over POEM; diagnostics support the proposed mechanism.
- Good clarity and relevance to BLBF.

Cons
- Limited novelty; SNIPS is standard and finite-sample theory specific to the learning objective is missing.
- Narrow baselines; no DR comparisons; no real logged data.
- Overstated claims about degeneracy avoidance; lack of rigorous guarantees in the finite-sample learning setting.

Recommendation: Leaning to reject in current form. The idea is sensible and practically promising, but the contribution is incremental and not yet validated against stronger baselines or with supporting finite-sample analysis tailored to the proposed objective. Strengthening theory and experiments as suggested could make this a solid contribution.
