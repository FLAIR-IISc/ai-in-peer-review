This paper addresses the problem of batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF), a critical area for improving interactive systems. The core contribution is identifying and mitigating a novel form of overfitting, termed "Propensity Overfitting," which arises when using conventional counterfactual risk estimators. The authors propose a Self-Normalized Risk Estimator and demonstrate its theoretical properties and empirical effectiveness. 

While the problem addressed is significant and the proposed solution has merit, the paper suffers from a surprising number of minor, yet persistent, flaws that detract from its overall impact. My assessment is heavily influenced by a feeling that these flaws suggest a lack of meticulous attention to detail, despite the paper’s focus on nuanced issues in statistical estimation.

**Strengths:**

* **Important Problem:** BLBF is a vital area with clear practical relevance.
* **Novelty:** The identification of Propensity Overfitting is a genuine contribution, highlighting a subtle issue with existing methods.
* **Theoretical Justification:** The paper provides a formal justification for the proposed self-normalized estimator.
* **Empirical Validation:** The experiments demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed approach across multiple datasets.
* **Clear Explanation of Core Ideas:** The core idea of propensity overfitting and the motivation behind the self-normalized estimator are well explained.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Notational Inconsistencies:** Throughout the paper, there are minor notational inconsistencies. For instance, δ(x, y) is sometimes defined as a loss and sometimes as a stochastic random variable. While subtle, these inconsistencies necessitate careful tracking by the reader.
* **Redundant Explanations:** The introduction repeats information already well-established in the related work section. Specifically, the connection to off-policy evaluation and causal inference is stated multiple times.
* **Theorem 1 Citation:** Theorem 1 references Theorem 1 and Theorem 6 of [1, 13] *within* the proof itself, which is unusual and makes verification difficult.  It reads like a placeholder for a more complete justification.
* **Equation (4) Label:** Equation (4) is labeled as 'Ŝ(h)' but it is never explicitly defined outside of its appearance in the equation itself. A brief explanation would improve readability.
* **Equivariance Argument:** The claim that R̂SN(h) is equivariant is not fully convincing. While the *estimator* is equivariant, the *optimization process* (e.g., L-BFGS) is not necessarily equivariant, potentially introducing bias. This nuance is overlooked.
* **Overstated Claims:** The statement that Norm-POEM is “surprisingly faster” than POEM is not fully supported by the experimental results. The runtime improvements appear marginal and not consistently observed. The reason for the speed-up, tied to smaller weight norms, feels speculative.
* **Lack of Ablation Studies:** The paper doesn’t provide an ablation study to dissect the impact of specific components of Norm-POEM. For example, a comparison of Norm-POEM with and without the variance regularization (λ) would be valuable.
* **Formatting Issues:** Minor formatting errors are present. For example, inconsistent spacing around operators and commas in several equations.
* **Reference Formatting:** Some reference formatting is inconsistent (e.g., capitalization in titles).
* **Hyperparameter Tuning Details:** The description of hyperparameter tuning is brief. More details about the validation set size and tuning process would increase confidence in the results.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* Addresses an important and under-explored issue in BLBF.
* Presents a novel and theoretically sound solution.
* Demonstrates empirically improved performance over existing methods.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* A plethora of minor technical and presentational flaws indicates a lack of meticulousness.
* The claimed performance improvements are not substantial enough to outweigh these flaws.
* The argument regarding runtime efficiency is not convincingly supported.
* The paper feels somewhat rushed, lacking the polish expected of a NIPS submission.



**Overall Assessment:**

I find myself in a difficult position. The core ideas of this paper are strong and represent a valuable contribution to the field. However, the numerous minor flaws, while individually insignificant, collectively create a sense of sloppiness. This is particularly concerning given the paper’s focus on precise statistical estimation. 

I lean towards **rejection**. While the work is promising, the level of polish and attention to detail is below the standard expected for NIPS. The authors should address the identified issues, conduct more thorough experiments, and provide a more nuanced discussion of their results before resubmitting.  The theoretical foundations and empirical validation are strong enough to merit reconsideration if these issues are adequately addressed.
