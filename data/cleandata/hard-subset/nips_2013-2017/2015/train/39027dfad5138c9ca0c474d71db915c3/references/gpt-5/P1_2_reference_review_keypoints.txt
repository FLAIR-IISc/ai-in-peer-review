- Addresses batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF) and identifies a failure mode called "propensity overfitting" in IPS-based ERM.  
- Shows propensity overfitting arises from IPS anomalies: lack of boundedness and translation equivariance.  
- Proposes using self-normalized importance sampling (SNIS) as a control variate to produce a bounded, equivariant (but biased) risk estimator.  
- Proves strong consistency (SLLN) for the SNIS-based estimator and provides an asymptotic variance approximation for CRM regularization.  
- Introduces Norm-POEM: same hypothesis class as POEM but using SNIS and its variance estimator, optimized with L-BFGS.  
- Experiments on multilabel supervised-to-bandit datasets show improved generalization, robustness to loss translations, S(h) near 1, and competitive runtime.  
- Notes SNIS and related diagnostics are well-known in off-policy evaluation; contribution is articulating propensity overfitting and integrating SNIS into CRM learning.  
- Theory is solid in characterizing IPS anomalies and motivating SNIS, but lacks new non-asymptotic/finitesample bounds specialized to SNIS.  
- The variance term used for CRM is an asymptotic approximation; finite-sample justification is not provided.  
- Method details are clear: Norm-POEM objective, clipping M and λ tuned via held-out counterfactual evaluation, and reasonable optimization choice.  
- Empirics are careful (10 runs, ablations, diagnostics) but miss strong baselines (DR, WDR, truncated IS, defensive mixtures) and statistical significance testing.  
- Experimental scope is limited to supervised-to-bandit multilabel data; no real logged data, RL tasks, or low-support/near-deterministic logging-policy regimes.  
- Paper is well written; propensity overfitting example and S(h) diagnostic are clearly explained.  
- Requests clearer statements on denominator stability for SNIS (support/clipping conditions) and more computational cost/convergence details.  
- Originality: SNIS itself is not novel, but naming propensity overfitting and integrating SNIS into CRM with practical gains is a useful contribution.  
- Significance: addresses an important area and offers a simple, practical fix likely to influence practice, though empirical claims are weakened by missing baselines.  
- Pros: clear identification/demonstration of propensity overfitting, principled SNIS remedy, seamless CRM integration, consistent empirical gains, code and tractable optimization.  
- Cons: no finite-sample SNIS-CRM bound, missing DR/WDR baselines, limited experimental diversity, sensitivity analyses and significance tests lacking, and shallow discussion on denominator stability/bias-variance trade-offs.  
- Suggestions/questions: report sensitivity to M and λ or adaptive schemes; compare to DR/WDR and normalized DR variants; consider S(h)-based penalties; seek finite-sample self-normalized concentration bounds; analyze logging-policy low-support robustness; add significance/runtime metrics.  
- Overall assessment: valuable and practically impactful identification and remedy for IPS propensity overfitting; recommend weak accept if stronger baselines and empirical/theoretical gaps are addressed.
