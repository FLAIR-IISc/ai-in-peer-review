

# Review of "The Self-Normalized Estimator for Counterfactual Learning"

## Summary
This paper identifies a new form of overfitting called "Propensity Overfitting" in counterfactual learning from logged bandit feedback. The authors propose a self-normalized risk estimator that addresses this issue by using multiplicative control variates. They develop a new learning algorithm called Norm-POEM that uses this estimator and show through theoretical analysis and experiments that it outperforms existing methods like POEM. The paper builds on prior work in counterfactual evaluation and batch learning from bandit feedback, particularly the CRM principle introduced in [1].

## Strengths
The paper identifies a genuine and important problem in counterfactual learning that has been overlooked in prior work. The theoretical analysis of Propensity Overfitting is sound and well-motivated through clear examples. The proposed self-normalized estimator has desirable properties (equivariance, boundedness) that address the identified problems. The empirical results demonstrate consistent improvements over POEM across multiple datasets, which is compelling evidence for the practical value of the approach.

## Weaknesses
Despite the paper's strong theoretical contributions and empirical results, I identify several critical issues that prevent me from recommending acceptance:

1. **Notational inconsistencies**: The paper inconsistently uses "h(y|x)" to denote both a probability distribution and a probability value. In Section 3, the hypothesis space is defined as "h(Y|x) that define a probability distribution over Y," but then "h(y|x)" is used as a probability value in the risk estimator (Equation 1). This is confusing and technically incorrect.

2. **Ambiguous example in Section 4**: The example demonstrating Propensity Overfitting assumes that all y_i ≠ x_i in the sample, but this is not stated explicitly. The paper claims "when drawing a training sample D = ((x1, y1, δ1, p1), ..., (xn, yn, δn, pn)), let us first consider the special case where all xi in the sample are distinct." However, for the overfitting to occur as described, it must also be that y_i ≠ x_i for all i, which isn't specified. This omission weakens the example's validity.

3. **Incorrect reference attribution**: In Section 2, the paper states "importance weights can be 'clipped' [15] to trade-off bias against variance in the estimators [5]." However, reference [5] is "Counterfactual reasoning and learning systems: the example of computational advertising" by Bottou et al., which does not discuss clipping of importance weights. Reference [5] is actually used for counterfactual estimators in general, not for clipping, which is properly attributed to [15] in the text.

4. **Theoretical proof error**: In the proof of Theorem 2, the paper claims "The numerator of R̂SN(h) in (7) are i.i.d. observations with mean R(h)." The numerator is a sum of terms, not individual observations. It should state "The terms in the numerator are i.i.d. observations with mean R(h)."

5. **Experimental presentation issues**: The paper reports "test set loss" without specifying whether it's the expected loss or the average loss. In Table 1, the results are presented without statistical significance tests, which is standard practice in machine learning research. The paper claims "Norm-POEM consistently beats POEM" but doesn't provide evidence that the differences are statistically significant.

6. **Minor but pervasive formatting errors**: The reference list contains inconsistent formatting, including a diacritic in "Dudı́k" without corresponding diacritic in the citation (reference #6), and inconsistent use of "et al." in references. The paper also uses "h(y|x)" as a probability in some places but as a distribution in others.

7. **Mischaracterization of the self-normalized estimator**: The paper states "the self-normalized risk estimator is not unbiased (E[R̂(h)/Ŝ(h)] ≠ R(h)/E[Ŝ(h)] in general)" which is misleading. The estimator is biased, but the correct statement should be E[R̂SN(h)] ≠ R(h), not the ratio of expectations.

## Recommendation
While the paper presents an interesting theoretical contribution with practical implications, the accumulated minor technical errors and inconsistencies undermine confidence in the paper's rigor. In a field as precise as machine learning, such details matter significantly. The paper's claim of "provable" avoidance of degeneracies is weakened by the technical inconsistencies identified above.

I recommend rejection. The paper has significant potential but requires careful revision to address these technical issues before it can be considered for publication at NIPS. The authors should carefully proofread the manuscript, clarify the example in Section 4, correct the reference attribution, fix the proof of Theorem 2, and provide statistical significance for the experimental results.
