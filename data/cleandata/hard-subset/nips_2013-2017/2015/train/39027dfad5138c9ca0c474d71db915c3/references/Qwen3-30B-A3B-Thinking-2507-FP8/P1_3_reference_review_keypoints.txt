- Identifies a critical flaw in conventional counterfactual risk estimation called "propensity overfitting," where inverse propensity weighting can yield arbitrarily low risk estimates.  
- Proposes a self-normalized risk estimator using multiplicative control variates and introduces the Norm-POEM learning algorithm to fix this issue.  
- Provides rigorous theoretical analysis showing the self-normalized estimator’s properties (equivariance, boundedness, consistency) and characterizing propensity overfitting.  
- Empirical results across multiple datasets show Norm-POEM consistently outperforms POEM and avoids propensity overfitting.  
- Clearly distinguishes "loss overfitting" from the newly identified "propensity overfitting," improving conceptual clarity.  
- Experiments use a sound supervised-to-bandit methodology and validate theoretical predictions (e.g., invariance to loss translations).  
- Lacks a formal generalization bound or deeper theoretical guarantees specifically for the Norm-POEM algorithm.  
- Notes higher per-iteration computational cost for the self-normalized estimator; more detailed complexity analysis is needed for large-scale use.  
- Discussion of related work is incomplete—insufficient contrast with recent self-normalized importance sampling advances in reinforcement learning and other domains.  
- Provides limited implementation/optimization details (e.g., iteration counts, convergence behavior, optimization landscape).  
- Overall recommendation: strong, novel contribution with convincing theory and empirical validation—should be accepted.  
- Arguments for acceptance: identifies and solves a new problem, rigorous theory, consistent empirical gains, clear practical solution.  
- Arguments against acceptance (minor): limited Norm-POEM-specific theory, need for deeper computational and contextual discussion.
