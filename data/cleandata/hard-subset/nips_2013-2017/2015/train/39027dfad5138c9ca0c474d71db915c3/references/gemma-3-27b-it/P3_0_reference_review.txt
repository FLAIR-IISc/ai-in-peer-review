## Review of "The Self-Normalized Estimator for Counterfactual Learning"

**Summary:**

This paper addresses a critical issue in batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF): propensity overfitting. The authors demonstrate that the standard counterfactual risk estimator, commonly used in this setting, suffers from a novel form of overfitting where the estimator can be driven arbitrarily far from the true risk. To mitigate this, they propose a self-normalized risk estimator, leveraging ideas from importance sampling and control variates. They provide theoretical justification for their approach, demonstrating its ability to avoid the identified overfitting, and empirically validate its superior performance across several datasets, showing improved generalization and robustness.

**Related Work & Context:**

The paper correctly positions itself within the burgeoning field of BLBF, highlighting its connection to causal inference, domain adaptation, and off-policy evaluation in RL. The authors provide a good overview of existing counterfactual estimators and their limitations. Importantly, they appropriately connect their work to the broader literature on importance sampling and variance reduction techniques, particularly the self-normalized estimator. The acknowledgement of prior work on propensity score matching and its limitations is also important. The references are comprehensive and relevant.

**Strengths:**

* **Novel Problem Identification:** The identification of "propensity overfitting" is a significant contribution. This is a subtle yet crucial issue that wasn't previously recognized in the BLBF literature, and the clear explanation and illustrative examples are particularly effective.
* **Elegant Solution:** The proposed self-normalized estimator is a well-motivated and elegant solution to the identified problem. The theoretical justification and connection to control variates are strong.
* **Strong Empirical Validation:** The experimental results are compelling. The consistent outperformance of Norm-POEM over POEM across various datasets, and the diagnostic analysis demonstrating the impact of propensity overfitting, strongly support the authors’ claims. The runtime comparison is a helpful addition.
* **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper provides a solid theoretical foundation, including a theorem demonstrating consistency of the estimator.
* **Practical Implementation:** The availability of the code is a significant benefit to the research community.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Computational Complexity:** While the authors acknowledge the increased per-iteration cost, the discussion of computational complexity feels somewhat superficial. A more detailed analysis, perhaps comparing the scaling with dataset size and model complexity, would be beneficial. The claim that convergence is faster *despite* increased per-iteration cost feels surprising and warrants further explanation. Why does shorter ‖w‖ lead to faster convergence?
* **Variance Estimation:** The use of an approximate variance estimate (Equation 8) is a potential limitation. While justified by the difficulty of obtaining an exact estimate, it would be helpful to discuss the potential impact of this approximation on the theoretical guarantees and empirical performance. Is the approximation accurate enough for the results to be trustworthy?
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:**  The paper mentions hyperparameter calibration but doesn’t delve into the sensitivity of the results to the choice of M and λ. A sensitivity analysis would strengthen the robustness of the findings.
* **Limited Scope of Experiments:** The experiments primarily focus on a specific experimental setup (supervised datasets converted to BLBF). While this allows for a controlled comparison, it would be valuable to explore the performance of Norm-POEM on more realistic, naturally occurring logged bandit feedback datasets.



**Originality:**

The work is highly original. While the self-normalized estimator exists in the importance sampling literature, its application to address propensity overfitting in BLBF is novel and insightful. The rigorous theoretical analysis and comprehensive empirical evaluation further enhance the originality of the contribution.

**Significance:**

The findings are significant. Propensity overfitting is a fundamental issue in BLBF that can severely hinder the performance of learning algorithms. By providing a robust and theoretically sound solution, this paper advances the state of the art in this important area.  The potential impact extends to numerous practical applications, including recommender systems, ad placement, and search engines.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that addresses an important and previously overlooked problem in batch learning from logged bandit feedback. The proposed solution is elegant, theoretically well-grounded, and empirically validated. While some minor improvements could be made regarding the computational complexity analysis and hyperparameter sensitivity, the overall quality of the work is excellent.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* Addresses a fundamental and previously unrecognized problem in BLBF.
* Provides a novel and elegant solution with strong theoretical guarantees.
* Demonstrates significant improvements in generalization performance through comprehensive empirical evaluation.
* Is clearly written and well-organized.
* Provides accessible code for reproducibility.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*  Computational complexity analysis is somewhat superficial.
*  Reliance on an approximate variance estimate.
*  Experiments primarily focused on a specific setup and could benefit from more realistic datasets.

**Rating:**

**Strong Accept (8/10)** - This paper is a valuable contribution to the field and should be accepted to NIPS. The identified problem and proposed solution are both significant, and the empirical results are compelling. Addressing the minor weaknesses outlined above would further strengthen the paper.
