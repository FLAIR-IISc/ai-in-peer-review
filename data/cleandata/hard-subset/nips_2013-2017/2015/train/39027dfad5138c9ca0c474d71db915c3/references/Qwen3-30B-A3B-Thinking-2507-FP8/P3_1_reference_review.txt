

# Peer Review: "The Self-Normalized Estimator for Counterfactual Learning"

## Summary and Relation to Prior Work

This paper identifies a previously unrecognized problem in batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF): "Propensity Overfitting," where conventional inverse propensity score (IPS) estimators for counterfactual risk minimization can produce unbounded risk estimates that are not equivariant to loss translations. This differs fundamentally from conventional loss overfitting in supervised learning. The authors propose replacing the standard IPS estimator with a self-normalized estimator using multiplicative control variates, which they theoretically show avoids these anomalies and empirically demonstrate improves performance. The work builds on established BLBF literature (e.g., POEM [1]), importance sampling [12], and control variates [21], but identifies a critical flaw in current practice that has not been previously recognized.

## Strengths

The paper's strongest contribution is the clear identification and formalization of Propensity Overfitting as a distinct problem in BLBF. The theoretical analysis (Section 4) is compelling, with the example demonstrating how conventional estimators can produce arbitrarily low risk estimates for overfitted hypotheses. This is a significant conceptual advance that explains why previous methods like POEM may fail in practice.

The proposed self-normalized estimator (Equation 7) is theoretically sound and addresses the identified problem. Theorem 2 correctly establishes strong consistency, and the empirical results (Section 7) provide robust evidence that the new estimator consistently outperforms POEM across multiple datasets. The demonstration that Norm-POEM (the new algorithm) avoids the sensitivity to loss translations (Table 2) is particularly convincing.

The paper also makes a valuable connection to importance sampling literature, noting that the self-normalized estimator has been used in finance [17] but not previously applied to BLBF. This interdisciplinary perspective strengthens the contribution.

## Weaknesses

Despite its strengths, the paper has several weaknesses that warrant consideration. First, the theoretical analysis lacks a detailed bias-variance trade-off analysis for the self-normalized estimator. While the paper correctly states it's not unbiased, it doesn't quantify this bias or explain why it doesn't harm performance compared to the unbiased IPS estimator that suffers from Propensity Overfitting. This is a critical omission for a theoretically grounded contribution.

Second, the computational analysis is superficial. The paper notes that "the improved robustness of Norm-POEM permits fast convergence during training even without stochastic optimization," but provides no evidence of computational complexity comparisons with POEM. The claim that "the runtime of Norm-POEM is surprisingly faster than POEM" needs stronger empirical support, especially since the self-normalized estimator requires additional computation per iteration.

Third, the empirical evaluation, while comprehensive across multiple datasets, lacks analysis of how the method performs under more challenging conditions—such as when the logging policy has very low support for certain actions (violating the full-support assumption) or with extremely high-dimensional feature spaces. This would strengthen the argument for real-world applicability.

## Originality and Significance

The identification of Propensity Overfitting represents a significant conceptual advance in the BLBF literature. While the self-normalized estimator has appeared in other contexts [12, 19], the application to BLBF and the recognition of this specific overfitting phenomenon is novel. The paper clearly differentiates itself from prior work on counterfactual learning (e.g., [5, 6, 1]) by addressing a previously unrecognized limitation.

The significance is substantial for practical applications of BLBF in industry (ad placement, recommendation systems), where the proposed method consistently outperforms existing approaches. The paper's theoretical guarantees and empirical validation provide a strong foundation for adoption in real-world systems.

## Overall Assessment

This is a high-quality paper with a novel theoretical contribution that addresses a genuine limitation in current BLBF methods. The identification of Propensity Overfitting is compelling, and the proposed solution is well-justified both theoretically and empirically. The paper is clearly written and makes a strong case for the superiority of the self-normalized estimator.

The main weaknesses are the lack of detailed bias-variance analysis and superficial computational comparison. However, these do not undermine the core contribution. The paper makes a significant advancement in understanding and addressing a fundamental challenge in counterfactual learning.

## Recommendation

I recommend acceptance. The paper makes a clear, important contribution to the field of counterfactual learning by identifying and solving a previously unrecognized problem. The theoretical and empirical results are robust, and the implications for practical applications are significant. While the paper could benefit from more detailed analysis of the bias introduced by the self-normalized estimator, this does not detract from the overall quality and significance of the work.

The work is highly relevant to NIPS' focus on machine learning theory and applications, and it advances the state of the art in a demonstrable way. The paper is well-written, technically sound, and makes a significant contribution to the literature on batch learning from logged bandit feedback.
