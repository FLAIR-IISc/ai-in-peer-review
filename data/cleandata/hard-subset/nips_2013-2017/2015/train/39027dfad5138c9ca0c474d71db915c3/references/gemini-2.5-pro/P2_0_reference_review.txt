### **Summary**

This paper addresses the problem of batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF), a crucial setting for improving interactive systems using historical data. The authors identify a novel and subtle failure mode of standard counterfactual risk estimators, which they term "Propensity Overfitting." This issue arises because the unbiased inverse propensity score (IPS) estimator can be minimized not only by finding a policy that minimizes the true loss but also by finding a policy that manipulates the sum of importance weights. This can lead to severely biased risk estimates and poor generalization.

To solve this, the paper proposes replacing the standard IPS estimator with a self-normalized (ratio) estimator, which is derived using multiplicative control variates. This estimator is shown to be equivariant to loss translations and bounded within the range of the observed losses, thus provably avoiding the anomalies that cause Propensity Overfitting. The authors then incorporate this new estimator into the Counterfactual Risk Minimization (CRM) framework to develop a new learning algorithm called Norm-POEM. Through extensive experiments on several multi-label classification datasets, they demonstrate that Norm-POEM significantly and consistently outperforms its predecessor (POEM), showing improved generalization performance and robustness, particularly with respect to translations of the loss function.

### **Evaluation**

#### **Quality**
The paper is of high technical quality. The claims are well-supported by both theoretical reasoning and rigorous empirical validation.

*   **Theoretical Soundness:** The identification of "Propensity Overfitting" is a key theoretical contribution, and the provided example (Example 1) is a crystal-clear illustration of the problem. The proposed solution is principled, drawing on the well-established statistical literature on control variates and ratio estimators. While the self-normalized estimator is known to be biased in finite samples, the authors correctly state its strong consistency (Theorem 2), which is a sufficient theoretical justification for its use in an ERM-style framework. The variance approximation for the ratio estimator is also standard.
*   **Experimental Soundness:** The experimental methodology is excellent. The "Supervised -> Bandit" simulation framework is a strong standard for this research area, as it allows for controlled experiments and exact evaluation of the learned policy's true risk. The comparison is made against the most relevant and state-of-the-art baseline (POEM). The experiments convincingly demonstrate not only that Norm-POEM performs better (Table 1, Figure 1) but *why* it performs better—by showing that it corrects the propensity score bias of POEM (Table 2, left) and is robust to loss translations (Table 2, right). The inclusion of an experiment showing that variance regularization is still beneficial (Table 3) is a good ablation study.
*   **Completeness:** The work feels complete. It identifies a problem, proposes a solution, provides theoretical grounding, instantiates it in a practical algorithm, and validates it thoroughly. The authors are also honest about the limitations, noting the computational trade-off that the new objective is not amenable to stochastic optimization, although they find this is not a practical issue on their datasets.

#### **Clarity**
The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow. The organization is logical, guiding the reader from the general problem of BLBF to the specific issue of Propensity Overfitting, and then to the proposed solution and its validation. The prose is clear, and the mathematical notation is consistent. The motivation in Section 4 is particularly strong; it makes the core problem intuitive before diving into the formal solution. The experimental section is also clear, with each table and figure designed to answer a specific question about the algorithm's performance.

#### **Originality**
The paper demonstrates strong originality. While the self-normalized estimator itself is not new to the field of statistics and Monte Carlo methods, its application to solve a newly identified problem in counterfactual learning is novel and insightful.

*   The primary original contribution is the identification and formalization of "Propensity Overfitting." To my knowledge, this specific failure mode, distinct from standard "loss overfitting," has not been previously discussed in the BLBF literature. This is an important conceptual contribution.
*   The connection between the estimator's lack of equivariance and this overfitting phenomenon is a sharp insight.
*   While POEM [1] introduced the application of the CRM principle to BLBF, this work represents a significant and non-trivial improvement by fixing a fundamental flaw in its underlying estimator. The work is clearly situated within the existing literature and correctly credits prior work on both BLBF and importance sampling techniques.

#### **Significance**
The significance of this work is high. Learning from logged data is a problem of immense practical importance for companies operating search engines, recommender systems, and ad platforms.

*   The identified problem of Propensity Overfitting is likely a real, if previously undiagnosed, issue in practical applications. The proposed Norm-POEM algorithm provides a simple and robust solution.
*   The method appears to be a direct "drop-in" replacement for previous approaches based on the standard IPS estimator, offering superior performance with the same hyper-parameters and comparable computational cost. This lowers the barrier to adoption for practitioners.
*   The insights from this paper are likely to influence future work in off-policy evaluation and learning. Researchers will now be more aware of this potential pitfall and the benefits of using stabilized or self-normalized estimators in their learning objectives. The paper advances the state of the art in a demonstrable way.

### **Strengths and Weaknesses**

**Strengths:**
1.  **Novel Problem Identification:** The paper clearly articulates a novel and important problem, "Propensity Overfitting," that affects standard counterfactual learning methods.
2.  **Principled and Effective Solution:** The proposed self-normalized estimator is a simple, well-justified, and empirically effective solution that directly addresses the identified problem.
3.  **Strong Empirical Validation:** The experiments are thorough, well-designed, and provide convincing evidence for the superiority of Norm-POEM over the previous state-of-the-art.
4.  **Exceptional Clarity:** The paper is very well-written, making a potentially complex topic accessible and the contributions easy to appreciate.
5.  **High Practical Impact:** The work addresses a real-world problem and provides a practical algorithm that could be readily adopted by practitioners.

**Weaknesses:**
1.  **Limited Scalability Discussion:** The paper notes that the variance estimator in Norm-POEM is not amenable to stochastic gradient descent. While the authors find that L-BFGS is fast enough for their experiments, a more detailed discussion of potential scalability challenges on truly massive datasets and possible workarounds would have been welcome.
2.  **Asymptotic Analysis:** The theoretical justification relies on consistency (an asymptotic property). While sufficient for this work, finite-sample bounds would provide stronger theoretical guarantees. This is a minor point, however, as such analysis is often complex and could be the subject of future work.

### **Final Recommendation**

This is an excellent paper that makes a clear and significant contribution to the field of counterfactual learning. It identifies a subtle but critical problem with existing methods and proposes an elegant and effective solution. The claims are strongly supported by both theory and extensive experiments. I recommend **Strong Accept**.
