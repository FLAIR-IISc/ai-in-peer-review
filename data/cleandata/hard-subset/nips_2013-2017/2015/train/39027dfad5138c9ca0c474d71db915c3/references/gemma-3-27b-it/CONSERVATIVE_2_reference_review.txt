This paper addresses the problem of learning from logged bandit feedback, a crucial area for improving interactive systems. The authors identify a novel issue – “Propensity Overfitting” – in the standard counterfactual risk estimation approach and propose a self-normalized estimator to mitigate it. The motivation is well-explained, and the connection to related fields like causal inference and off-policy evaluation is clearly drawn.

However, while the identification of Propensity Overfitting is potentially valuable, the paper feels somewhat incremental and lacks the depth of rigorous justification expected at NIPS. The theoretical analysis, while present, relies heavily on citing existing theorems ([1, 13]) without substantial new contributions beyond identifying that these theorems *apply* to this new form of overfitting. The introduction of the self-normalized estimator is sensible, drawing upon established techniques from importance sampling, but the justification for *why* it specifically avoids Propensity Overfitting beyond being equivariant feels somewhat hand-wavy. The claim of resolving “all the problems” of the unbiased estimator is an overstatement.

The experimental evaluation is the most compelling aspect, demonstrating consistent improvements over POEM. However, the experimental setup mirrors that of the POEM paper [1] very closely, which raises concerns about simply extending the existing empirical validation rather than providing truly independent evidence. The runtime comparison is interesting but not central to the core contribution. Moreover, the datasets used are relatively standard, and the gains, while consistent, aren’t dramatic enough to immediately suggest a breakthrough. I am also concerned that the parameter tuning for both POEM and Norm-POEM was performed on a hold-out from the logged bandit dataset, rather than a completely separate validation dataset. This could lead to overly optimistic results.

**Strengths:**

*   Identifies a previously unrecognised issue (Propensity Overfitting) in a relevant problem setting.
*   Proposes a sensible solution (Self-Normalized Estimator) based on established techniques.
*   Provides empirical evidence of improved performance over a strong baseline (POEM).
*   Clear writing and well-defined problem statement.

**Weaknesses:**

*   Theoretical contribution feels incremental; largely applies existing theory to a new context.
*   Justification for *why* the self-normalized estimator avoids overfitting isn’t fully convincing.
*   Experimental evaluation relies heavily on replicating the setup from a prior paper.
*   Gains in performance, while consistent, are not overwhelmingly significant.
*   Parameter tuning could be biased due to using the bandit log as a validation set.
*   The discussion of computational efficiency feels secondary to the core contribution.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper addresses a practically relevant problem with a reasonable solution.
*   The empirical results are consistently positive, even if not groundbreaking.
*   The identification of Propensity Overfitting is a potentially useful contribution to the field.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The theoretical contribution is limited.
*   The paper feels more like an engineering improvement than a fundamental advance.
*   The experimental validation isn't sufficiently independent.
*   The paper doesn't present a compelling argument for why other approaches wouldn't also address Propensity Overfitting.



**Overall Assessment:**

I lean towards **reject** with encouragement to revise and resubmit. While the paper is technically sound and the identified problem is interesting, it doesn't meet the bar for originality and significance expected at NIPS. The authors need to strengthen the theoretical justification for the self-normalized estimator, conduct more independent and extensive experimental validation, and demonstrate that their approach offers a substantial advantage over existing methods.  A more thorough comparison with other propensity weighting techniques (e.g., those employing clipping or other regularization methods) would also be beneficial. I suggest the authors focus on addressing the weaknesses listed above, and further demonstrating the robustness and generalizability of their approach before resubmitting.
