Summary and contributions
The paper addresses batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF) and the use of counterfactual risk estimators within ERM. It argues that the conventional inverse propensity scoring (IPS) risk estimator used in prior BLBF/CRM work exhibits pathologies when used inside learning: its finite-sample estimates are not bounded by the loss range, are not translation-equivariant, and can be minimized by manipulating propensity weights rather than true loss—what the authors term “propensity overfitting.” The paper proposes to replace IPS with the self-normalized importance sampling (SNIS) estimator (a multiplicative control variate), yielding a self-normalized risk estimator that is bounded and equivariant, and is strongly consistent. They integrate this into the CRM framework, resulting in Norm-POEM, including an approximate variance estimate for the ratio estimator to instantiate a variance-regularized objective. Empirically, on supervised-to-bandit multi-label benchmarks, Norm-POEM improves generalization relative to POEM and appears more robust to loss translation; it also reduces Ŝ(h) deviation from 1 and converges faster in practice.

Relation to prior work
Importance sampling, clipping, and variance reduction via control variates are well-known in OPE and Monte Carlo (Hesterberg; Kong; Dudík et al.; Thomas et al.; Ionides). Self-normalized (a.k.a. weighted) IS is standard in RL OPE and sequential Monte Carlo. The novelty here lies in diagnosing “propensity overfitting” within ERM for BLBF and integrating SNIS into the CRM learning objective for structured prediction. The paper correctly notes that prior CRM bounds were derived for IPS and that SNIS is preferable when weight fluctuations dominate. The connection to defensive mixtures, DR estimators, and trust region methods is touched on but not explored experimentally.

Quality
- Theory: The examples clearly expose IPS pathologies in ERM; the “propensity overfitting” phenomenon is convincingly illustrated and linked to deviations of Ŝ(h) from 1. The SNIS estimator’s boundedness and equivariance are correct and important. The strong consistency statement is standard and sound. However, the learning-theoretic justification for Norm-POEM is weaker than for POEM: the CRM generalization bound used to motivate variance regularization is for IPS; the paper does not provide an analogous high-probability bound for the SN ratio estimator nor a uniform convergence argument over H. The variance estimator is an asymptotic approximation; there is no finite-sample guarantee tying the Norm-POEM objective to generalization. Clipping is required to bound variance, but the induced bias is not analyzed for the ratio estimator.
- Method/optimization: Using L-BFGS on the nonconvex objective is reasonable, and gradients are tractable for CRF-like models. The claim of faster convergence is plausible (bounded losses temper weight blow-up), but runtimes are reported qualitatively rather than with quantitative tables.
- Experiments: The supervised-to-bandit protocol is standard and appropriate for controlled study. The results supporting improved generalization, robustness to loss translation, and Ŝ(h) stabilization are compelling. That said, key baselines are missing: doubly robust (DR) estimators (both standard and self-normalized), and strong clipping or capping variants of POEM often used in practice. Confidence intervals are not reported. The analysis of sensitivity to M, λ is limited, and there is no evaluation on real logs or on settings without full support.

Clarity
The paper is clearly written and well organized. The motivation, anomalies with IPS, and the SNIS fix are communicated crisply. Definitions and notation are mostly consistent. A few points could be clarified: how validation for hyperparameters avoids bias in BLBF; precise conditions under which the asymptotic variance formula is reliable; and whether the same normalization/clipping is applied consistently across train/validation/test evaluation.

Originality
Using SNIS itself is not new; it is a standard tool in OPE. The original contribution is to (i) articulate “propensity overfitting” as a failure mode of IPS-based ERM with concrete examples, (ii) argue for SNIS as a structural fix with desirable invariances within learning, and (iii) integrate it into CRM for structured prediction with an implementable objective and show empirical gains. This is an incremental but meaningful advance rather than a wholly novel estimator.

Significance
BLBF is practically important (ads, recommenders), and avoiding degenerate learning is consequential. The work offers a simple, principled modification that improves robustness and often accuracy, with minimal changes to existing pipelines. However, the lack of a new finite-sample learning bound for the SN objective and the absence of DR baselines temper the impact. If extended with stronger theory and broader comparisons, this could become a standard recommendation.

Pros
- Clear diagnosis of IPS pathologies in ERM and the Ŝ(h) diagnostic.
- Principled, simple fix via self-normalization with strong qualitative advantages (boundedness, equivariance).
- Integration into CRM and a practical algorithm for structured outputs.
- Empirical gains across datasets; robustness to loss translation; reduced Ŝ(h) deviations.

Cons
- No high-probability learning bound or uniform convergence guarantees for the SN objective; reliance on asymptotic variance approximations.
- Missing comparisons to DR and self-normalized DR, and stronger IPS baselines (e.g., tuned clipping).
- Limited empirical breadth (no real logs; no discrete large-action or sparse support settings); lack of runtime/CI reporting.
- Bias–variance trade-off of clipping under SNIS not analyzed.

Questions for authors
- Can you provide a generalization bound tailored to the SN ratio estimator (e.g., via empirical process techniques for ratios or self-normalized concentration)?
- How sensitive are results to M and λ? Can you show calibration curves and Ŝ(h) distributions?
- How does Norm-POEM compare to DR and SN-DR on your benchmarks?
- Are runtimes and numbers of L-BFGS iterations reproducible across runs? Please report quantitative runtimes.
- How does the method behave when h0 lacks full support (common in logs), beyond clipping?

Suggestions
- Add DR and SN-DR baselines, and a thorough ablation on clipping thresholds.
- Report confidence intervals and runtimes.
- Discuss extensions to per-decision normalization in sequential settings and connections to trust-region methods.
- Provide a finite-sample analysis (even in a restricted setting) to support the CRM objective with SNIS.

Recommendation: Weak accept (6). The paper is well written, identifies a real and underappreciated failure mode, and proposes a simple, principled remedy that improves robustness and performance. Strengthening theoretical guarantees and experimental comparisons would further solidify the contribution. Confidence: medium-high.
