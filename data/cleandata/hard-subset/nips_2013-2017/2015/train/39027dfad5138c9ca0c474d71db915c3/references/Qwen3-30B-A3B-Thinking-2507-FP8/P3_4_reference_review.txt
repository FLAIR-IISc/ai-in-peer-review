

# Peer Review: "The Self-Normalized Estimator for Counterfactual Learning"

This paper identifies a previously overlooked problem in batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF), called "Propensity Overfitting," where conventional counterfactual risk estimators produce severely biased risk estimates by overemphasizing samples based on their propensity scores. The authors propose a self-normalized risk estimator using multiplicative control variates, which they prove avoids the anomalies of conventional estimators. They derive a new learning algorithm, Norm-POEM, and demonstrate its superior performance on multiple datasets.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novel Problem Identification:** The paper compellingly identifies Propensity Overfitting as a distinct form of overfitting that has not been previously addressed in BLBF. The examples in Section 4 are particularly effective in demonstrating how conventional estimators can produce risk estimates arbitrarily lower (or higher) than the true risk, unlike standard supervised learning overfitting.

2. **Theoretical Soundness:** The theoretical analysis is rigorous. The authors prove the self-normalized estimator is consistent (Theorem 2) and equivariant (unlike conventional estimators), which are crucial properties for a risk estimator. The connection to control variates in importance sampling is well-motivated and properly contextualized in the literature.

3. **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The empirical validation is thorough and well-designed. Using multiple datasets from LibSVM, the authors demonstrate consistent improvements of Norm-POEM over POEM across different problem sizes and dataset characteristics. The analysis of Ŝ(h) in Table 2 provides compelling evidence that the proposed estimator avoids Propensity Overfitting.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Lack of Context on Why This Was Overlooked:** The paper fails to adequately explain why previous work (including [1, 5, 6]) didn't identify Propensity Overfitting. The related work section mentions prior counterfactual estimators but doesn't discuss why they didn't face this issue or how Propensity Overfitting differs from overfitting concerns they addressed. This weakens the novelty claim.

2. **Limited Theoretical Analysis of Variance:** While the authors prove consistency, they don't provide finite-sample bounds for the self-normalized estimator or analyze how its variance compares to the conventional estimator. This is significant because variance is a key concern in importance sampling, and the CRM principle explicitly addresses variance regularization.

3. **Insufficient Discussion of Limitations:** The paper doesn't discuss potential limitations, such as performance when the logging policy h0 has very low support for certain actions (a common challenge in BLBF). The authors should address whether the self-normalized estimator handles cases with extremely small propensity scores better than conventional methods.

4. **Superficial Runtime Analysis:** While the paper claims Norm-POEM is "surprisingly faster" than POEM, the explanation for this is somewhat superficial. The authors note that Norm-POEM converges to a smaller ||w|| but don't provide a detailed analysis of why this leads to faster convergence.

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper makes a valuable contribution to counterfactual learning by identifying and solving a previously overlooked problem. The theoretical foundation is strong, and the empirical results are convincing across multiple datasets. The proposed self-normalized estimator is a simple yet powerful modification that resolves a critical issue in conventional approaches.

The paper advances the state of the art in BLBF and provides a useful tool for practitioners. While not perfect, the novelty, theoretical soundness, and empirical validation outweigh the weaknesses. The main contribution is significant within the BLBF literature, and the paper would be a valuable addition to the NIPS proceedings.

**Recommendation:** Accept. This is a strong paper that makes a meaningful contribution to the field. The identification of Propensity Overfitting is novel and important, and the proposed solution is both theoretically sound and empirically effective.

**Specific Suggestions:**

1. Expand the related work section to better contextualize why Propensity Overfitting wasn't previously identified, comparing with existing overfitting analyses in BLBF.

2. Add theoretical analysis of the variance of the self-normalized estimator, including finite-sample bounds.

3. Discuss the behavior of the self-normalized estimator when the logging policy has very low support for certain actions (e.g., when p_i is extremely small for some samples).

4. Provide a more detailed explanation of why Norm-POEM converges faster than POEM, particularly regarding the relationship between ||w|| and convergence rate.

5. Clarify how the self-normalized estimator relates to other recent counterfactual learning approaches, such as doubly robust estimators [6].

The paper is well-written, technically sound, and addresses an important problem in counterfactual learning. With minor revisions to address the theoretical and contextual gaps, it would be an excellent contribution to NIPS.
