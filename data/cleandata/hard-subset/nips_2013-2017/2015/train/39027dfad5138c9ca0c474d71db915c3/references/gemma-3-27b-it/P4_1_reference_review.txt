This paper addresses the problem of batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF) and identifies a novel issue called “Propensity Overfitting” that can degrade the performance of standard counterfactual risk estimators. The authors propose a “Self-Normalized Risk Estimator” based on multiplicative control variates to mitigate this problem and demonstrate improved performance both theoretically and empirically.

The paper is well-motivated, clearly explaining the challenges of learning from logged data and relating it to existing work in causal inference, off-policy evaluation, and domain adaptation. The introduction clearly frames the problem and highlights the limitations of existing approaches. The related work section is thorough, covering relevant literature on counterfactual estimation, importance sampling, and control variates. The technical presentation is generally strong. The problem of Propensity Overfitting is well-defined with a compelling example. The proposed solution, utilizing the self-normalized estimator, is justified theoretically and supported by experimental results. The experimental setup follows established methodologies from previous work (POEM), allowing for a fair comparison. 

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The identification of “Propensity Overfitting” as a distinct issue in BLBF is a novel contribution. The self-normalized estimator provides a new perspective on addressing the biases introduced by importance sampling in this setting.
*   **Technical Soundness:** The theoretical analysis appears rigorous, building on established concepts from importance sampling and control variates. The proof of Theorem 2 is concise and convincing.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and logically organized. The concepts are explained clearly, making the paper accessible to a broad audience. The use of examples helps to illustrate the key ideas.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The experimental results convincingly demonstrate the benefits of the proposed approach. The comparison with the existing POEM algorithm provides a strong baseline. The ablation study (Table 3) provides additional insights into the importance of variance regularization.
*   **Practical Relevance:**  The problem addressed is highly relevant to many real-world applications involving historical data from interactive systems.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Computational Complexity:** The discussion of computational efficiency is a bit vague. While the authors claim that Norm-POEM’s runtime is comparable to POEM and a full-information CRF, it would be helpful to provide more detailed runtime comparisons, particularly showing the impact of the self-normalization step on overall performance.
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper mentions calibrating hyperparameters (M and lambda) on a held-out validation set, but doesn’t delve into the sensitivity of the results to these parameters.  A brief discussion of how the performance varies with different hyperparameter settings would be beneficial.
*   **Limited Scope of Experiments:** While the experiments are well-conducted, they are limited to a relatively small set of datasets.  Exploring the performance on a wider range of datasets with different characteristics could further strengthen the conclusions.
*   **Connection to Existing Literature on Equivariance:** The authors highlight that the self-normalized estimator is equivariant. While mentioned, a deeper connection and discussion of existing work on equivariance in machine learning would be valuable.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper addresses a significant problem in the field of learning from logged data.
*   The proposed solution is both novel and theoretically sound.
*   The empirical results provide strong evidence of the benefits of the proposed approach.
*   The paper is well-written and clearly explains the key ideas.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   The computational complexity of the proposed approach is not fully explored.
*   The experimental evaluation could be more comprehensive.
*   The connection to existing literature on equivariance could be strengthened.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of learning from logged bandit feedback. The identification of Propensity Overfitting and the proposed solution based on the self-normalized estimator are both novel and significant. While there are a few minor weaknesses, the strengths of the paper outweigh them. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the points raised above regarding computational complexity, hyperparameter sensitivity, and a more in-depth discussion of equivariance.
