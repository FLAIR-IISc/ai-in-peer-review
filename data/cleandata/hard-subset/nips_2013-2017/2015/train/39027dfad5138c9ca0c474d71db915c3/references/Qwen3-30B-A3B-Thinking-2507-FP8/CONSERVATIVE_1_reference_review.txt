

# Review of "The Self-Normalized Estimator for Counterfactual Learning"

This paper identifies a problem they term "Propensity Overfitting" in counterfactual learning from logged bandit feedback, and proposes a self-normalized estimator as a solution. The authors argue that conventional importance sampling estimators can produce severely biased risk estimates due to overfitting of propensity weights, and claim their self-normalized estimator (a well-known technique in importance sampling literature) resolves these issues.

The paper's main contribution is the application of a standard self-normalized importance sampling estimator (which has been studied for decades in statistics and Monte Carlo methods, as cited in their references [12, 19, 22, 23]) to the specific context of batch learning from logged bandit feedback. The authors develop a learning algorithm called Norm-POEM that uses this estimator in place of the conventional one.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written and clearly organized, with a thorough theoretical analysis of the proposed estimator.
- The empirical evaluation is comprehensive, comparing against the POEM baseline across multiple datasets.
- The authors provide convincing evidence that their estimator avoids the specific bias issues they identify, with Ŝ(h) values closer to 1 for Norm-POEM than for POEM.
- The consistency result (Theorem 2) is technically sound and correctly establishes the estimator's convergence properties.

**Weaknesses:**
1. **Lack of novelty:** The self-normalized estimator they propose is not new to the importance sampling literature. Hesterberg's work [19] and others have long established that self-normalized estimators can reduce variance when weight fluctuations dominate, and that they are equivariant (which the authors correctly identify as a benefit). The paper fails to properly contextualize their contribution within this extensive literature. The claim that this is a "new form of overfitting" (Propensity Overfitting) appears to mischaracterize a well-known issue in importance sampling.

2. **Misrepresentation of the problem:** The "Propensity Overfitting" problem described in Section 4 is essentially a manifestation of the known bias-variance tradeoff in importance sampling. Standard practice in the field (as evidenced by prior work like [15, 5, 1]) has always involved clipping importance weights to control variance. The paper's examples of overfitting could easily be mitigated by standard clipping techniques, which POEM already employs. The authors do not convincingly demonstrate why their approach is superior to standard clipping.

3. **Inadequate comparison to established methods:** While the paper compares to POEM (which uses a clipped estimator), it does not compare to other established approaches that handle importance sampling variance, such as doubly robust estimators [6] or more sophisticated variance reduction techniques. This limits the significance of their claimed improvements.

4. **Marginal empirical gains:** The empirical results show consistent but modest improvements over POEM. For instance, on the Yeast dataset, Norm-POEM achieves 0.378 vs. POEM's 0.381 (Table 1). The paper does not demonstrate that these differences are statistically significant or practically meaningful in real-world applications.

5. **Overstated claims:** The paper claims their estimator "avoids all the problems of the unbiased estimator," but this is misleading. The self-normalized estimator has known bias (unlike the unbiased estimator with clipping), and the paper does not provide a thorough analysis of when this bias becomes problematic.

**Recommendation:**
The paper presents a technically sound application of a well-known technique to a specific problem in counterfactual learning. However, the contribution is not sufficiently novel or significant to warrant acceptance at NIPS. The "new" problem they identify has been well-understood in the importance sampling literature for decades, and the solution they propose is a standard technique that should have been acknowledged as such in the related work.

The paper's main value lies in providing a clear empirical demonstration of the issues they identify, but this is not sufficient to overcome the lack of novelty. The authors would need to substantially strengthen their claims about novelty and significance, and provide a more thorough comparison to established methods in the field.

**Pro and Con for Acceptance:**
- *Pro:* Well-executed empirical validation of a known technique in a specific context; clear theoretical analysis of the estimator's properties.
- *Con:* The core contribution is not novel; the problem identified is not new; the empirical improvements are marginal and could be achieved with standard clipping techniques; the paper fails to properly contextualize its contribution within existing importance sampling literature.

This paper would be better suited for a more applied venue where the focus is on specific applications rather than theoretical novelty. The ideas presented are valuable but not sufficiently innovative for NIPS.
