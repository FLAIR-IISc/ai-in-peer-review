

# Review: The Self-Normalized Estimator for Counterfactual Learning

## Summary
This paper identifies a critical issue in batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF) called "Propensity Overfitting," where conventional counterfactual risk estimators can produce arbitrarily low risk estimates due to the propensity weights. The authors propose a Self-Normalized Risk Estimator using multiplicative control variates to address this problem, which they prove is consistent and avoids the anomalies of the conventional estimator. They develop a new learning algorithm called Norm-POEM based on this estimator and demonstrate through extensive empirical evaluation that it significantly outperforms the state-of-the-art POEM algorithm across multiple datasets.

## Quality

The paper is technically sound with well-supported theoretical claims and thorough empirical validation. The identification of Propensity Overfitting through a concrete example (Example 1) is particularly compelling and clearly demonstrates the limitations of existing methods. The authors provide a solid theoretical foundation with Theorem 2 establishing the consistency of their estimator, and they thoughtfully address the limitations of their approach (e.g., the need for variance regularization even with their estimator).

The empirical evaluation is comprehensive, following the standard Supervised 7→ Bandit methodology used in prior work [1]. The results across multiple datasets (Scene, Yeast, TMC, LYRL) consistently show that Norm-POEM outperforms POEM, with the improvement being statistically significant. The authors also provide valuable ablation studies (Table 2) showing that Norm-POEM successfully avoids the propensity overfitting problem, as evidenced by the self-normalized estimator's close adherence to the expected value of 1 for Ŝ(h).

## Clarity

The paper is exceptionally well-written and well-organized. The problem statement is clear, and the authors effectively motivate the need for their approach by contrasting Propensity Overfitting with the more familiar Loss Overfitting in supervised learning. The mathematical exposition is precise, with consistent notation throughout. The connection between control variates and the self-normalized estimator is elegantly explained, and the theoretical results are presented in a way that is accessible to readers familiar with the field.

The experimental setup is described with sufficient detail to allow for reproducibility, and the results are presented clearly through tables and figures. The paper's structure effectively guides the reader from problem identification through theoretical development to empirical validation.

## Originality

This work makes a significant original contribution by identifying and naming a previously unrecognized problem (Propensity Overfitting) in counterfactual learning. While self-normalized estimators have been used in importance sampling before (e.g., [19, 22, 23]), the authors' application of this technique to counterfactual learning in the BLBF setting is novel and insightful. The distinction between Propensity Overfitting and Loss Overfitting is particularly valuable, as it clarifies a fundamental issue in counterfactual learning that previous work has not adequately addressed.

The paper also makes a clear contribution to the literature by connecting their approach to established techniques in financial applications and reinforcement learning (e.g., [18]), demonstrating that their method is grounded in a broader theoretical framework.

## Significance

The significance of this work is substantial. Propensity Overfitting is a serious problem that can lead to degenerate risk estimates and poor generalization performance, which is a critical concern for real-world applications like recommendation systems and ad placement. The authors' solution provides a simple yet effective fix that improves performance across multiple datasets without requiring major changes to the learning framework.

The empirical results are particularly compelling, showing that Norm-POEM not only avoids Propensity Overfitting but also converges faster than POEM (as evidenced by shorter optimization paths and smaller norm of the weight vector). The computational efficiency of their method (surprisingly faster than POEM despite the normalization) is an important practical advantage.

## Weaknesses

While the paper is strong overall, there are a few areas for improvement:

1. The paper could provide a more detailed analysis of the bias-variance tradeoff of the self-normalized estimator compared to the clipped unbiased estimator. While they mention that the self-normalized estimator is not unbiased, they don't quantify this bias or discuss how it compares to the bias introduced by clipping.

2. The theoretical analysis focuses primarily on consistency, but a more detailed comparison of the bias and variance of the self-normalized estimator versus the conventional estimator would strengthen the theoretical contribution.

3. The paper doesn't discuss how sensitive the method is to the choice of the clipping parameter M, which could be important for practitioners implementing this approach.

## Recommendation

This paper makes a significant contribution to the field of counterfactual learning, identifying a previously unrecognized problem (Propensity Overfitting) and providing a simple yet effective solution. The theoretical analysis is sound, the empirical evaluation is thorough and convincing, and the practical implications are clear. The work is both technically rigorous and practically relevant, making it a strong candidate for acceptance at NeurIPS.

The paper addresses a critical issue in a field with significant real-world applications, and the solution is elegant and implementable. While there are minor areas for improvement in the theoretical analysis, these do not detract from the overall quality and significance of the work.

**Strengths:** Clear identification of a new problem (Propensity Overfitting), elegant theoretical solution using self-normalized estimators, thorough empirical validation showing consistent improvements over state-of-the-art, and practical computational efficiency.

**Weaknesses:** Limited discussion of bias-variance tradeoff for the self-normalized estimator, and minimal analysis of sensitivity to the clipping parameter.

I recommend acceptance of this paper as it makes a clear, significant contribution to the field of counterfactual learning with strong theoretical and empirical support.
