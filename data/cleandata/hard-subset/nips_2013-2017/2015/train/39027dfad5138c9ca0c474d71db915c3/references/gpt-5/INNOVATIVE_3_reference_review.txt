Summary
This paper tackles batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF) and identifies a previously underappreciated failure mode of inverse propensity scoring (IPS)-based empirical risk minimization: propensity overfitting. The authors show that standard (and clipped) IPS estimators can drive ERM toward hypotheses that exploit the sampling weights rather than the loss, leading to severe, even unboundedly bad, finite-sample behavior and lack of equivariance to loss translations. They propose using multiplicative control variates—specifically, the self-normalized importance sampling estimator (SNIPS)—as a counterfactual risk estimator. The resulting self-normalized risk estimator is bounded and equivariant, and is strongly consistent. Building on CRM, they instantiate a new learning algorithm, Norm-POEM, that optimizes the self-normalized empirical risk plus an approximate variance penalty. Experiments on multiple multilabel datasets (simulated BLBF via “Supervised → Bandit”) show consistent gains over POEM, robustness to loss translations, reduced propensity overfitting measured via the sum of weights Ŝ(h), and competitive runtime.

Relation to prior work
Self-normalized importance sampling (aka weighted importance sampling) is classical in Monte Carlo and has appeared in off-policy evaluation; the paper cites Hesterberg, Kong, and others. The novelty here lies less in the estimator itself and more in (i) crisply diagnosing propensity overfitting as a structural degeneracy of IPS-driven ERM, (ii) showing that self-normalization directly addresses these anomalies (boundedness, equivariance) in the learning objective, and (iii) integrating SNIPS into the CRM framework to yield a practical algorithm with empirical wins. The work complements prior variance-reduction approaches (clipping, DR) and learning bounds for IPS-based CRM.

Strengths
- Conceptual novelty and clarity: The identification and formalization of “propensity overfitting” is insightful and broadly useful. The example makes the degeneracy vivid and explains real-world failures many practitioners have seen.
- Simple, strong idea: Replace IPS with SNIPS to fix equivariance and bound the ERM objective. This is a bold but elegant shift that meaningfully changes the objective’s geometry.
- Theoretical properties: Equivariance and boundedness are important for ERM; strong consistency is established. The control-variate perspective is well-motivated.
- Practical algorithm: Norm-POEM is a drop-in successor to POEM within the same modeling family, with a workable approximate variance estimator and tractable optimization.
- Empirical evidence: Across several datasets and data regimes, Norm-POEM outperforms POEM, is robust to loss translations (consistent with theory), shows Ŝ(ĥ) near 1, and converges faster in practice.

Weaknesses and questions
- Finite-sample guarantees: The CRM bound employed (Theorem 1) is for IPS with clipping. The proposed learning objective for SNIPS uses an approximate variance via delta/normal approximations, but there is no analogous high-probability finite-sample generalization bound for the ratio estimator. Even a bias-aware bound (e.g., via delta method with empirical Bernstein on numerator/denominator) or a discussion of conditions under which SN-CRM enjoys similar guarantees would strengthen the theoretical foundation.
- Interaction with clipping: SNIPS plus clipping/truncation is mentioned as needed to ensure finite variance, but the paper should clarify whether the theoretical properties (equivariance, strong consistency) and the diagnostic Ŝ(h) ≈ 1 still hold when weights are clipped, and how M affects bias in the self-normalized setting.
- Baselines: Since DR and additive control variates are discussed, it would be useful to include weighted doubly robust (WDR/SNDR) or Switch-DR as baselines, as they often provide favorable bias-variance tradeoffs in OPE/BLBF. This would help position the proposed estimator among contemporary alternatives.
- Scope of experiments: The evaluation focuses on multilabel classification with Hamming loss and synthetic logging policies. Additional tests with (i) other structured losses, (ii) different logging-policy supports/qualities, and (iii) truly logged data (or semi-synthetic with realistic propensities) would increase external validity. Also report numeric Ŝ(ĥ) and performance tables referenced in the text.
- Optimization details: The objective is nonconvex and uses L-BFGS. While convergence is empirically fast, a brief ablation on initialization, regularization sensitivity (λ, M), and stability across runs would help practitioners reproduce the gains.
- Referencing and polish: A few references are incomplete; please ensure consistent formatting and, if space permits, acknowledge related “SNIPS” usage in contextual bandits/off-policy learning beyond the cited Monte Carlo literature.

Quality
Technically sound overall, with a solid problem diagnosis and a principled estimator choice. Theory is correct for consistency and properties, but finite-sample analysis for the learning objective is the main gap. Empirics are convincing within scope.

Clarity
Generally clear and well-structured. The propensity overfitting narrative and diagnostic Ŝ(h) are compelling. Minor suggestions: explicitly state when expectations are over the logging policy vs. the input distribution; clarify treatment of clipping in SNIPS and its impact on properties; ensure all tables/figures and references are complete.

Originality
Medium-to-high. The estimator is classical, but the identification of propensity overfitting and the re-foundation of CRM learning on a self-normalized objective is a novel and impactful direction that can reshape practice in BLBF.

Significance
High practical significance for counterfactual learning, contextual bandits, and off-policy evaluation. The proposed fix is simple to adopt and addresses a pervasive failure mode. Likely to influence subsequent work (e.g., SN variants of DR objectives, new generalization bounds for ratio estimators).

Pros for acceptance
- Clear, important problem diagnosis (propensity overfitting) with a principled, simple remedy.
- Strong conceptual contribution with consistent empirical improvements and robustness.
- Broad relevance to counterfactual learning and OPE communities.

Cons against acceptance
- Lack of finite-sample generalization guarantees for the self-normalized CRM objective.
- Limited baselines (no weighted DR) and scope of datasets.
- Some clarity/polish issues and missing quantitative details in the text.

Recommendation
Accept. The paper introduces a meaningful conceptual advance and a practical algorithmic shift that addresses a fundamental instability in IPS-based counterfactual learning. The novelty and potential impact outweigh the incomplete finite-sample theory and narrower experimental scope. Constructive additions in a camera-ready version (theoretical discussion on SN-CRM bounds, clarifications on clipping, stronger baselines) would further strengthen the work.

Confidence
High.
