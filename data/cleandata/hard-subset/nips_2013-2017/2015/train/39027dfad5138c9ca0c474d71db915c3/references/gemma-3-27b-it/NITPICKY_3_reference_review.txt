The paper addresses the important problem of batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF), highlighting a critical issue – Propensity Overfitting – in existing counterfactual risk estimation methods. The authors propose a Self-Normalized Risk Estimator (SNRE) and demonstrate, both theoretically and empirically, its ability to mitigate this overfitting. While the motivation is strong and the proposed solution appears promising, the paper suffers from a multitude of minor flaws and inconsistencies that, cumulatively, detract from its overall quality.

The introduction is generally well-written but lacks sufficient concrete examples to illustrate the severity of Propensity Overfitting beyond the abstract. The connection to off-policy evaluation and reinforcement learning is stated but not thoroughly explored, missing an opportunity to contextualize the work further within the broader ML landscape.

The related work section is adequate but somewhat disorganized. While covering relevant areas, it lacks a critical assessment of the strengths and weaknesses of the cited works in relation to the specific problem of propensity overfitting. The statement that all counterfactual estimators "can be traced back to What-If simulations" feels reductive and lacks nuance. A more thorough discussion of existing variance reduction techniques beyond clipping and additive control variates would be beneficial.

The technical presentation in Section 3 is generally sound, accurately representing the CRM principle and existing estimators. However, the notation is occasionally inconsistent. For example, the use of both ‘h(x)’ and ‘h(y|x)’ to denote probability distributions is confusing.  Theorem 1 is presented without sufficient justification for the reader; referencing the referenced theorems [1, 13] isn’t a substitute for explaining the underlying logic *within* the paper. The theorem also appears to present a fairly standard Bernstein bound and doesn’t specifically highlight the novelty within the context of BLBF.

Section 4, detailing Propensity Overfitting, is the core of the paper, yet it is presented in a somewhat convoluted manner. Example 1, while illustrative, is overly simplistic and relies on specific conditions (distinct xi values) that may not hold in real-world datasets. The claim that the problem is “ubiquitous” (Section 7) is not adequately substantiated within Section 4 itself; it relies entirely on deferred empirical evidence. The explanations regarding loss translation and its effect on overfitting, while correct, feel somewhat repetitive and could be more concisely presented. The assertion that CRM variance regularization combats both Loss and Propensity Overfitting feels hand-wavy and lacks detailed justification.

The presentation of the Self-Normalized estimator (Section 5) is clear and well-motivated. The connection to existing importance sampling literature is appropriately highlighted. Theorem 2, establishing consistency, is straightforward but lacks a discussion of its practical implications—specifically, the rate of convergence. The justification for using L-BFGS in Section 6 is weak; while commonly used, a more detailed rationale would be helpful.

Section 7, the experimental evaluation, is the weakest part of the paper. The experimental setup is largely borrowed from prior work [1], which is positive, but the analysis is superficial. The claim of consistent outperformance of Norm-POEM is not supported by sufficiently detailed statistical analysis (e.g., confidence intervals, p-values). The runtime comparison feels anecdotal and lacks a rigorous benchmarking.  The tables are poorly formatted and lack clear captions.  The use of datasets with varying dimensionality (p) and label space size (q) without careful consideration of their potential impact on the results is concerning. The observation about shorter ‖w‖ in Norm-POEM, while interesting, is presented without a clear explanation of its significance. The fact that performance is similar to a CRF should be emphasized as a limitation – SNRE is *solving* the problem of using off-policy data to *approximate* a supervised CRF. The gap should be larger.

Numerous minor stylistic issues exist throughout the paper: inconsistent use of abbreviations (e.g., BLBF), occasional grammatical errors, and a lack of polish in the overall writing style. The reference list is comprehensive but could benefit from better formatting.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Addresses a genuine and important problem in the field of learning from logged data.
*   Proposes a theoretically sound and potentially effective solution (SNRE).
*   Provides empirical evidence supporting the benefits of the proposed approach.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The technical presentation is marred by inconsistencies and a lack of clarity.
*   The experimental evaluation is superficial and lacks statistical rigor.
*   The paper suffers from numerous minor flaws and stylistic issues.
*   The significance of the results is overstated relative to the improvements achieved.
*   The connection to related work is not thoroughly explored.



**Overall Assessment:** Reject. While the paper identifies a valid problem and proposes a reasonable solution, the numerous flaws and inconsistencies detract significantly from its overall quality. The lack of rigor in the experimental evaluation and the superficial presentation of the theoretical results do not meet the standards for acceptance at NIPS. A major revision addressing the concerns raised above would be necessary for reconsideration.
