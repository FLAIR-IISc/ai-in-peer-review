This paper addresses the problem of Batch Learning from Logged Bandit Feedback (BLBF), where a new policy must be learned from a fixed dataset generated by a different, historical policy. The authors build upon the Counterfactual Risk Minimization (CRM) principle, which learns a new policy by minimizing an importance-weighted risk estimate, regularized by its empirical variance.

The key contribution of this work is the identification and formalization of a novel failure mode in this setting, which the authors term "Propensity Overfitting". They demonstrate through a clear example that standard unbiased risk estimators can be minimized not only by finding actions with low loss, but also by finding policies that exploit the importance weights themselves—for instance, by assigning high probability to training examples with negative loss, driving the risk estimate to arbitrarily low values. This is a subtle but critical issue that is distinct from standard "loss overfitting".

To solve this problem, the paper proposes replacing the standard unbiased importance sampling estimator with a Self-Normalized estimator. This estimator is derived from the statistical literature on control variates, where the sum of the importance weights (which has a known expectation of 1) is used as a multiplicative control variate. The authors show that this self-normalized estimator is equivariant to shifts in the loss function and is always bounded by the range of the observed losses, thus directly resolving the anomalies that lead to propensity overfitting. They incorporate this new estimator into the CRM framework to create a new learning algorithm, Norm-POEM, and demonstrate its effectiveness through a series of well-controlled experiments on multi-label classification tasks.

### Strengths

1.  **Clear Identification of an Important Problem:** The paper's primary strength is its clear articulation of "Propensity Overfitting". This is a genuine and previously under-appreciated problem in counterfactual learning. The illustrative example in Section 4 is highly effective at demonstrating how standard estimators can produce pathologically over-optimistic risk estimates, leading ERM-style approaches astray. This is a valuable conceptual contribution to the field.

2.  **Principled and Elegant Solution:** The proposed solution is not an ad-hoc heuristic. The use of the self-normalized estimator, grounded in the theory of control variates, is a principled way to address the identified problem. The paper clearly explains the theoretical benefits of this estimator, namely its equivariance to loss translation and its bounded nature, which directly counter the identified failure modes of the unbiased estimator.

3.  **Strong and Convincing Empirical Results:** The experiments are well-designed, following the established "Supervised -> Bandit" protocol which allows for controlled evaluation. The results provide strong evidence for the paper's claims:
    *   Norm-POEM consistently and significantly outperforms the baseline (POEM) in terms of generalization error (Table 1, Figure 1).
    *   The experiments directly validate the core hypothesis by showing that Norm-POEM successfully controls the sum of importance weights `S_hat(h)`, keeping it close to its expected value of 1, while the baseline does not (Table 2).
    *   The robustness of Norm-POEM to loss translation is empirically confirmed, highlighting a key practical advantage (Table 2).

4.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow. The motivation is clear, the technical sections are precise, and the connection between the problem and the proposed solution is laid out logically.

### Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement

1.  **Lack of a Formal Generalization Bound:** The original CRM framework (used by POEM) is motivated by a formal generalization bound based on an empirical Bernstein inequality (Theorem 1). The proposed Norm-POEM objective is presented as an instantiation of the CRM *principle*, but no corresponding generalization bound is derived for the self-normalized estimator and its approximate variance. While the approach is intuitively sound and empirically successful, a formal theoretical justification in the form of a high-probability bound would significantly strengthen the paper's contribution and place it on the same theoretical footing as its predecessor.

2.  **Analysis of the Estimator's Bias:** The paper correctly notes that the self-normalized estimator is biased for finite samples, although it is strongly consistent. A more detailed discussion of this bias would be beneficial. For example, how does the bias scale with the sample size `n` or the mismatch between the logging and target policies? Understanding the bias-variance trade-off introduced by this estimator more formally would provide a more complete picture.

3.  **Computational Scalability:** The authors note that the variance term for Norm-POEM (Eq. 8) does not readily admit stochastic optimization, forcing them to use a batch method like L-BFGS. While they observe faster convergence in their experiments, this could be a significant limitation for datasets at a scale where stochastic gradient methods are a necessity. A discussion of this potential limitation and possible future work on developing scalable, perhaps stochastic, approximations for the Norm-POEM objective would be a valuable addition.

4.  **Novelty of the Estimator:** While the *application* is novel and the analysis of propensity overfitting is a key contribution, it is important to be clear that the self-normalized estimator itself is a well-known tool in the broader statistics and Monte Carlo literature. The paper does a good job of citing this prior work, but the framing should consistently emphasize the novelty in the context of counterfactual *learning* and the problem it solves, rather than the estimator itself.

### Summary

Overall, this is a high-quality paper that makes a clear and significant contribution. It identifies a subtle but important flaw in a standard approach to batch learning from bandit feedback and proposes an elegant, principled, and empirically effective solution. The strengths—particularly the insightful problem formulation and strong empirical validation—far outweigh the weaknesses. The lack of a formal generalization bound is a missed opportunity for deeper theoretical impact, but the existing contributions are more than sufficient for acceptance. This work is likely to be influential for researchers and practitioners working on off-policy evaluation and learning.
