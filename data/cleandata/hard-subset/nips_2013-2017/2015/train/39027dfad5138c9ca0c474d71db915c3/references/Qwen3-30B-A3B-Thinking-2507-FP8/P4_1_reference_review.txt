

# Review: The Self-Normalized Estimator for Counterfactual Learning

This paper identifies a previously unrecognized problem in batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF) called "Propensity Overfitting" and proposes a solution using a self-normalized risk estimator. The authors demonstrate that conventional counterfactual risk estimators used in BLBF suffer from anomalies that lead to degenerate risk estimates, particularly when using Empirical Risk Minimization (ERM). They propose replacing the standard inverse propensity estimator with a self-normalized estimator based on multiplicative control variates, which avoids these anomalies and leads to improved generalization performance. The authors derive a new learning algorithm called Norm-POEM that incorporates this estimator and validate it empirically across multiple datasets.

The paper builds upon previous work on counterfactual learning (e.g., POEM [1]) and causal inference (e.g., propensity score matching [7]), but identifies a novel problem that was not previously recognized in the BLBF literature. The authors make a clear distinction between the conventional "Loss Overfitting" in supervised learning and the newly identified "Propensity Overfitting" in BLBF, which arises due to the estimator's failure to maintain equivariance under loss translation and its unbounded risk estimates.

**Strengths:**
1. The paper makes a strong theoretical contribution by identifying and formally defining Propensity Overfitting, a problem that plagues existing BLBF approaches. The example in Section 4 effectively demonstrates how conventional estimators can produce arbitrarily biased risk estimates.

2. The theoretical analysis (Section 5) is sound, with clear proofs showing that the self-normalized estimator resolves the identified anomalies. The authors prove the estimator's consistency and demonstrate its equivariance property, which the conventional estimator lacks.

3. The empirical evaluation (Section 7) is comprehensive and well-designed. The authors use a standardized experimental setup with multiple datasets from the LibSVM repository and show consistent improvements across all datasets. The ablation studies (Table 2) clearly demonstrate that Norm-POEM avoids Propensity Overfitting while POEM does not.

4. The paper is well-written and clearly organized, with sufficient mathematical detail to allow reproduction of the results. The connection between the theoretical contributions and the empirical results is well-maintained throughout.

**Weaknesses:**
1. The paper does not sufficiently compare with other recent approaches to counterfactual learning, such as doubly robust estimators [6] or recent advances in off-policy evaluation [14]. While the authors cite these works in the related work section, they don't explicitly position their contribution relative to these approaches.

2. The computational efficiency claim ("surprisingly faster than POEM") could be better supported. The paper notes that Norm-POEM converges in fewer iterations but doesn't provide detailed runtime analysis or compare the per-iteration cost more thoroughly.

3. The paper doesn't fully explore the limitations of the proposed approach. For instance, it's unclear how the method performs when the logging policy h0 has very limited support over the action space, which is a common scenario in practice.

4. The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion of how the self-normalized estimator relates to other variance reduction techniques in importance sampling, particularly those that have been successful in practice.

**Recommendation:**

This paper makes a significant contribution to the field of counterfactual learning by identifying a previously unrecognized problem (Propensity Overfitting) and providing a theoretically sound solution. The proposed self-normalized estimator addresses a fundamental limitation of existing approaches, and the empirical results consistently demonstrate improved performance across multiple datasets.

The paper is technically sound, well-written, and provides both theoretical guarantees and empirical validation. While the comparison with other recent approaches could be strengthened, the contribution is clearly novel and addresses an important gap in the literature.

**Arguments for acceptance:**
- Identifies a novel problem (Propensity Overfitting) not previously recognized in BLBF literature
- Provides a theoretically sound solution with clear proofs of advantages
- Demonstrates consistent empirical improvements across multiple datasets
- Well-written with clear connections between theory and practice

**Arguments against acceptance:**
- Limited comparison with recent counterfactual learning approaches beyond POEM
- Computational efficiency claims could be better supported with more detailed analysis
- Limited discussion of potential limitations in practical scenarios

Overall, this paper makes a strong contribution to the field of counterfactual learning that addresses a fundamental problem in the existing literature. The theoretical insights and empirical validation are compelling, and the work is well-positioned to advance the state-of-the-art in batch learning from logged bandit feedback. The paper meets all four criteria (Quality, Clarity, Originality, Significance) at a high level.
