

# Review of "The Self-Normalized Estimator for Counterfactual Learning"

This paper identifies "Propensity Overfitting" as a previously unrecognized problem in counterfactual learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF) and proposes a self-normalized risk estimator to address it. The authors argue that conventional importance sampling estimators exhibit severe anomalies, particularly when used in Empirical Risk Minimization (ERM), leading to degenerate risk estimates. They propose replacing the standard estimator with a self-normalized version that provably avoids these issues and demonstrate improved performance with their Norm-POEM algorithm.

**Strengths:**
The paper identifies a genuine problem in counterfactual learning that has been overlooked in prior work. The theoretical analysis of Propensity Overfitting (Section 4) is insightful, and the self-normalized estimator (Equation 7) appears to resolve the identified anomalies. The empirical evaluation (Section 7) shows consistent improvements over the baseline POEM algorithm across multiple datasets.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Critical Notational Inconsistency:** In Equation (1), the risk is defined as R(h) = E_h[δ(x, y)], which is ambiguous. The notation E_h suggests expectation with respect to h, but h is a hypothesis (a function), not a distribution. This should be E_{(x,y)~h}[δ(x,y)] or similar. This fundamental notational error appears throughout the paper and undermines the mathematical rigor.

2. **Theoretical Gap:** The paper claims the self-normalized estimator "resolves all the problems" of the unbiased estimator (Section 5), but fails to explicitly connect its properties to the Propensity Overfitting problem identified in Example 1. The self-normalized estimator's boundedness and equivariance properties address certain anomalies, but the paper doesn't demonstrate how these specifically prevent the specific overfitting behavior described in Example 1.

3. **Missing Empirical Details:** The paper references "Table 1," "Figure 1," and "Table 2" but does not include these in the provided text. Without access to these results, it's impossible to verify the empirical claims. The paper should include these tables/figures or at least describe the key findings in the text.

4. **Statistical Significance:** The empirical results claim "consistent" improvements but lack statistical significance testing. Without p-values or confidence intervals, it's unclear whether the observed improvements are meaningful or due to random fluctuations.

5. **Ambiguous Terminology:** The paper uses "Ŝ(h)" without clearly defining it in Section 7 (Table 2 refers to "average Ŝ(ĥ)" without defining Ŝ). The notation is inconsistent with the earlier definition of Ŝ(h) in Equation (4).

6. **Inconsistent Notation:** The paper uses both "h0" and "h0" (with and without subscript) without consistency, creating confusion.

7. **Reference Errors:** The reference for "Counterfactual risk minimization" is incomplete (missing year, pages). The citation [19] (Hesterberg) is incorrectly attributed to self-normalized estimators when it discusses weighted average importance sampling.

8. **Algorithmic Ambiguity:** The feature map φ(x, y) = x ⊗ y (Section 6) is ambiguous for multi-label classification. For datasets with multiple labels, it's unclear how this tensor product would be handled in practice.

**Overall Assessment:**

While the paper identifies a potentially important problem in counterfactual learning, the numerous technical inconsistencies, missing empirical details, and theoretical gaps significantly undermine the paper's credibility. The notational errors and inconsistencies are particularly concerning as they appear to be fundamental issues rather than minor typographical errors. The paper's claims about the self-normalized estimator resolving Propensity Overfitting are not sufficiently supported by the theoretical or empirical evidence presented.

The paper has potential value but requires substantial revision to address these fundamental issues before it can be considered for publication. The current version contains too many technical inconsistencies that would make it difficult for other researchers to understand, verify, or build upon the work.

**Recommendation: Reject**

*Pro:*
- Identifies a previously unrecognized problem (Propensity Overfitting)
- Theoretical analysis of the problem is insightful
- Empirical results show consistent improvements over baseline

*Con:*
- Fundamental notational inconsistencies
- Missing empirical details (tables/figures)
- Lack of statistical significance testing
- Theoretical gap between problem and proposed solution
- Inconsistent terminology and notation
- Reference errors

The paper's technical weaknesses are substantial enough to warrant rejection, despite its potentially valuable contribution to counterfactual learning. The author should address the notational issues and provide complete empirical results before resubmitting.
