

# Review of "The Self-Normalized Estimator for Counterfactual Learning"

This paper identifies what the authors call "Propensity Overfitting" in counterfactual risk estimation for batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF). They propose a "Self-Normalized Risk Estimator" using multiplicative control variates, which they claim resolves this issue. The authors derive a new algorithm called Norm-POEM and provide theoretical and empirical evidence supporting their approach.

The paper makes a reasonable attempt to formalize a problem that has been observed in importance sampling contexts. The authors demonstrate that conventional inverse propensity estimators can produce severely biased risk estimates for certain hypothesis spaces, which they term "Propensity Overfitting." They correctly identify that this is distinct from standard loss overfitting in supervised learning.

The theoretical contribution (Theorem 2) is technically sound, showing the self-normalized estimator is strongly consistent. The authors also provide a clear explanation of why this estimator avoids the anomalies of the conventional estimator (e.g., equivariance to loss translation). The empirical evaluation on standard datasets shows consistent improvement over POEM (the baseline algorithm), with the self-normalized estimator producing risk estimates closer to the expected value of 1 for the propensity weights.

However, this paper raises several significant concerns that prevent me from recommending acceptance. First, the claimed novelty of "Propensity Overfitting" is questionable. The paper cites prior work on importance sampling [19, 22, 23] that discusses similar issues with self-normalized estimators. The authors fail to adequately distinguish their specific problem from well-known challenges in importance sampling, such as the variance issues that self-normalized estimators are designed to address. The problem they describe appears to be a special case of the general importance sampling challenge, not a previously unobserved phenomenon.

Second, the empirical validation is insufficiently thorough. The authors test on only four datasets from the LibSVM repository, which is a narrow scope for a claim of "ubiquitous" overfitting. More critically, they don't compare against a broader range of baselines beyond POEM. For instance, they don't compare against other variance-reduction techniques like clipped importance sampling or doubly robust estimators that have been well-studied in the literature [5, 11, 15]. The claim that "Norm-POEM dominates POEM even after l2-regularization" is based on limited evidence without sufficient statistical significance testing.

Third, the paper doesn't convincingly establish the significance of the problem in real-world applications. While they demonstrate the problem theoretically and on synthetic examples, they don't provide evidence that Propensity Overfitting is a common issue in practical BLBF settings. The datasets they use (Scene, Yeast, TMC, LYRL) are standard for supervised-to-bandit conversion, but this doesn't necessarily reflect the characteristics of real-world log data.

The computational analysis is also superficial. The authors claim Norm-POEM runs faster than POEM, but they don't provide detailed runtime analysis or show how this compares to other methods. The claim that "the runtime of Norm-POEM is on the same order of magnitude as those of a full-information CRF" is not supported with concrete measurements.

The paper also fails to adequately connect to related work. While they cite important papers on BLBF, they don't sufficiently engage with the extensive literature on importance sampling and control variates [21, 22, 23]. The relationship between their self-normalized estimator and existing work in importance sampling is underdeveloped.

The most significant concern is the lack of comprehensive validation against established baselines. The paper claims their method "provides substantially better generalization performance," but without proper comparison to the full range of counterfactual learning approaches, this claim is premature. The empirical results show marginal improvements (e.g., 0.01-0.03 reduction in Hamming loss), which may not be statistically significant or practically meaningful.

While the paper makes a technically sound contribution to the theoretical understanding of counterfactual risk estimation, it falls short of providing sufficient evidence to justify the claim that this is a significant problem requiring a new approach. The proposed solution appears to be a straightforward application of existing importance sampling techniques rather than a novel method.

Strengths:
- Clear theoretical exposition of the problem
- Well-structured paper with consistent notation
- Strong theoretical guarantee (Theorem 2)
- Consistent empirical improvements over the baseline

Weaknesses:
- Questionable novelty of the identified problem
- Insufficient empirical validation against established baselines
- Limited scope of empirical evaluation
- Superficial computational analysis
- Inadequate connection to existing importance sampling literature

In conclusion, while the paper presents a technically correct method, the evidence supporting its significance and novelty is insufficient. The authors claim to identify a new problem and propose a solution, but fail to demonstrate that this problem is widespread or that their solution offers substantial advantages over established importance sampling techniques. The paper would benefit from more comprehensive empirical validation against a wider range of baselines and a clearer distinction from existing importance sampling methods. Given these concerns, I cannot recommend acceptance at NIPS.
