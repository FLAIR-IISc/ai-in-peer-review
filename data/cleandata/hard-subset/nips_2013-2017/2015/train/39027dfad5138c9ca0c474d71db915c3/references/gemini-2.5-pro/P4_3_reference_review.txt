### Summary

This paper addresses the problem of Batch Learning from Logged Bandit Feedback (BLBF), a setting where a learner aims to find an optimal policy using a fixed dataset collected by a different, historical policy. The paper builds upon the Counterfactual Risk Minimization (CRM) principle, a state-of-the-art approach that minimizes an upper bound on the true risk of a policy. The standard CRM framework, as exemplified by methods like POEM [1], relies on the inverse propensity score (IPS) estimator to evaluate the risk of a new policy from historical data.

The central contribution of this work is the identification and formalization of a critical failure mode of this approach, which the authors term "Propensity Overfitting". They demonstrate through a compelling example and theoretical argument that the standard IPS estimator has undesirable properties: it is not equivariant to constant shifts in the loss function and its value is not bounded by the range of the loss. This allows an Expressive Risk Minimization (ERM) learner to find policies with artificially low empirical risk by manipulating the importance weights, rather than by accurately modeling the underlying losses. For instance, a model can learn to assign high probability to actions seen in the training data if losses are negative, purely to inflate the importance weights and drive the estimated risk down, irrespective of the true expected loss.

To remedy this issue, the authors propose replacing the standard IPS estimator with the Self-Normalized Estimator. This estimator, known in the statistics literature [12, 19], is motivated as a ratio estimator derived using a multiplicative control variate. The paper shows that this self-normalized estimator is equivariant to loss translations and is always bounded within the range of the observed losses, thus directly resolving the anomalies that cause Propensity Overfitting. While the estimator introduces a small bias for finite samples, it is strongly consistent. The authors then integrate this estimator into the CRM framework, deriving a new learning algorithm called Norm-POEM. The paper concludes with an extensive empirical evaluation on several multi-label classification datasets, demonstrating that Norm-POEM significantly and consistently outperforms POEM in terms of generalization performance and robustness, particularly with respect to shifts in the loss function.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Quality:** The paper is of high technical quality.

*   **Strengths:** The theoretical claims are well-supported. The identification of the "Propensity Overfitting" problem is a sharp and insightful diagnosis of a fundamental weakness in prior methods. The properties of the proposed self-normalized estimator (equivariance, boundedness, consistency) are correctly established and directly address the identified problem. The experimental methodology is rigorous, following the established "Supervised to Bandit" protocol [1, 2], which allows for controlled and reproducible evaluation. The experiments are thorough, comparing against a strong baseline (POEM), analyzing the key diagnostic `Ŝ(h)`, testing for robustness to loss translation, and even including an ablation study on the necessity of variance regularization. The results are strong and consistently support the paper's central thesis.

*   **Weaknesses:** The theoretical results themselves are not deeply novel. Theorem 2 is a direct application of the Strong Law of Large Numbers, and the variance estimate in Equation (8) is a standard approximation for ratio estimators based on the delta method or a Normal approximation [21, 23]. A more detailed discussion on the impact of this approximation on the validity of the CRM bound (Theorem 1) would have further strengthened the theoretical grounding of the final algorithm.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally clear and well-written.

*   **Strengths:** The organization is excellent, logically progressing from the problem setting, to the identification of a specific failure mode, to the proposed solution, and finally to empirical validation. The motivation is compelling, and the core idea of Propensity Overfitting is made remarkably intuitive through the use of a simple, concrete example (Example 1). The writing is precise, and the mathematical notation is consistent and easy to follow. The paper provides sufficient detail for an expert to understand the method and potentially reproduce the results.

*   **Weaknesses:** There are no significant weaknesses in clarity. A minor point for improvement could be to briefly elaborate on why the variance estimator in Equation (8) does not admit stochastic optimization, as this is stated as a key difference from the original POEM but not fully explained.

**Originality:** The paper makes a significant original contribution.

*   **Strengths:** The primary originality lies in the identification, diagnosis, and naming of "Propensity Overfitting". While the self-normalized estimator is a known tool in statistics, its application to mitigate this specific and newly-identified overfitting problem within the context of counterfactual *learning* is novel and insightful. This work is not merely an application of an existing estimator; it provides a new conceptual understanding of a failure mode in BLBF and demonstrates how to fix it. This successfully reframes the choice of estimator from one of simple variance reduction to one of ensuring robust learning and preventing pathological solutions. The work clearly delineates how it advances beyond prior work like POEM [1].

*   **Weaknesses:** The core components—the CRM principle and the self-normalized estimator—are themselves not new. The novelty is in the synthesis and the new understanding that this combination provides for the problem of counterfactual learning.

**Significance:** The results presented in this paper are highly significant.

*   **Strengths:** The paper addresses a fundamental problem in the practical application of machine learning to improve systems based on logged data, a scenario of immense practical importance. The identified Propensity Overfitting problem is likely a real and previously underappreciated issue in many applications. The proposed solution is principled, easy to understand, and empirically shown to be highly effective and robust, yielding substantial performance gains over the prior state-of-the-art. The resulting algorithm, Norm-POEM, appears to be a direct and superior replacement for its predecessor. It is highly likely that researchers and practitioners in off-policy evaluation and BLBF will adopt this estimator and build upon the insights of this paper.

*   **Weaknesses:** The experiments are conducted on multi-label classification benchmarks. While this is a standard and valid approach for controlled studies, demonstrating the method's effectiveness on a real-world interactive system (e.g., in computational advertising or recommendations) would further solidify its practical significance.

### Arguments for Acceptance

1.  The paper identifies and clearly articulates a novel, fundamental, and likely widespread problem in counterfactual learning, "Propensity Overfitting".
2.  It proposes a principled and elegant solution using the self-normalized estimator, which directly remedies the diagnosed flaws of the standard IPS estimator.
3.  The empirical results are comprehensive and show significant, consistent improvements in generalization and robustness over a strong state-of-the-art baseline.
4.  The paper is exceptionally well-written and clear, making an important contribution highly accessible.
5.  The findings are of high significance to anyone working on learning from logged bandit data, a problem of major practical relevance.

### Arguments Against Acceptance

1.  The main technical components (the self-normalized estimator and the CRM framework) are adapted from prior work; the novelty is in their combination and the motivation for it.
2.  The formal theoretical contributions (e.g., the consistency proof) are straightforward applications of existing statistical results.
