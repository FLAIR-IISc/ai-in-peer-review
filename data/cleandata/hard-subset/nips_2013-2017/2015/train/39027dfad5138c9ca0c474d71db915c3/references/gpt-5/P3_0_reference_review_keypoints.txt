- Paper identifies pathologies of IPS as a risk estimator in ERM for BLBF, including unbounded finite-sample estimates and lack of translation-equivariance.  
- Authors define and illustrate "propensity overfitting," where learners exploit propensity weights instead of true loss.  
- Proposal: replace IPS with self-normalized IS (SNIS) to produce a bounded, equivariant, and strongly consistent self-normalized risk estimator.  
- Integrate SNIS into CRM to produce Norm-POEM and derive an approximate variance-regularized objective using an asymptotic variance estimate.  
- Empirical results on supervised-to-bandit multi-label benchmarks show Norm-POEM improves generalization, is more robust to loss translation, stabilizes Ŝ(h) near 1, and converges faster.  
- Relation to prior work: SNIS and control variates are standard in OPE/MC; novelty is diagnosing propensity overfitting in ERM and integrating SNIS into CRM for structured prediction.  
- Theory: examples convincingly expose IPS failures; SNIS boundedness/equivariance and strong consistency are sound.  
- Theoretical gap: no high-probability/generalization bound or uniform convergence argument for the SN ratio estimator over hypothesis class H.  
- Variance estimator is asymptotic; no finite-sample guarantees or analysis of clipping-induced bias for the ratio estimator.  
- Method/optimization: L-BFGS on the nonconvex objective is reasonable and gradients are tractable; faster convergence claim is plausible but runtimes lack quantitative reporting.  
- Experiments: protocol is appropriate and results compelling, but key baselines (DR, SN-DR, strongly clipped POEM) are missing and confidence intervals are not reported.  
- Empirical sensitivity analysis is limited (M, λ) and no evaluation on real logged data or settings without full support.  
- Clarity: paper is well written and organized; some clarifications requested about validation bias, variance formula conditions, and consistent application of normalization/clipping.  
- Originality: use of SNIS is not novel, but framing propensity overfitting and integrating SNIS into CRM is a meaningful incremental contribution.  
- Significance: practically important and offers a simple, robust fix, but impact is tempered by missing theory and broader comparisons.  
- Pros: clear diagnosis and diagnostic Ŝ(h); principled SNIS fix with desirable invariances; practical Norm-POEM algorithm; empirical robustness and gains.  
- Cons: lack of finite-sample learning guarantees; missing DR and SN-DR baselines and stronger IPS baselines; limited empirical breadth and no runtime/CIs; no bias–variance analysis of clipping.  
- Questions for authors: provide SN-specific generalization bounds, sensitivity plots for M and λ, comparisons to DR and SN-DR, quantitative runtimes/L-BFGS iterations, and behavior when h0 lacks full support.  
- Suggestions: add DR and SN-DR baselines, ablate clipping thresholds, report confidence intervals and runtimes, discuss sequential/per-decision normalization and trust-region links, and pursue finite-sample analysis.  
- Recommendation: Weak accept (6) — solid, well-written paper addressing a real failure mode with a simple fix, but needs stronger theory and broader experimental comparisons.
