## Review of "The Self-Normalized Estimator for Counterfactual Learning"

**Summary:**

This paper addresses a critical issue in batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF): propensity overfitting. The authors identify that standard counterfactual risk estimators, while theoretically sound, can exhibit problematic behavior leading to severely biased risk estimates and poor generalization. They propose a self-normalized risk estimator, drawing from importance sampling literature, which provably mitigates this issue.  The paper presents a theoretical analysis and empirical validation demonstrating the benefits of their approach (Norm-POEM) over existing methods (POEM) on several benchmark datasets.

**Strengths:**

* **Identifies a novel and important problem:** Propensity overfitting is a genuinely insightful observation regarding the behavior of standard estimators in BLBF, and a weakness not previously highlighted in the literature. This is a significant contribution.
* **Strong theoretical justification:** The paper provides a clear and concise theoretical argument for why the self-normalized estimator avoids the identified overfitting issue. The connection to existing results on importance sampling and control variates is well-established. Theorem 2 establishing consistency is a valuable addition.
* **Empirical validation:** The experimental results are compelling. Norm-POEM consistently outperforms POEM across a variety of datasets, and the analysis demonstrating the impact of propensity overfitting (Table 2) strongly supports the authors' claims. The comparison to a "skyline" CRF performance provides useful context.
* **Clear and well-written:** The paper is generally very well-written and organized. The explanation of the problem, the proposed solution, and the experimental setup are all clear and easy to follow.
* **Practical Algorithm:** Norm-POEM appears to be a relatively straightforward modification to existing BLBF algorithms, making it potentially easy to adopt and implement. The runtime comparison is also encouraging.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited novelty of the core technique:** While the *application* to BLBF and the *identification* of propensity overfitting are novel, the self-normalized estimator itself is a known technique from the importance sampling literature. The paper acknowledges this, but could perhaps emphasize the non-triviality of recognizing its relevance and efficacy in this specific context.
* **Computational complexity:** While the authors mention that the runtime is comparable to POEM, the variance estimation (Equation 8) appears more complex.  A more detailed analysis of the computational cost, potentially including scalability to larger datasets, would be beneficial.
* **Hyperparameter sensitivity:**  The discussion of hyperparameter tuning (M and lambda) is brief. It would be helpful to see an analysis of the sensitivity of the results to these parameters, or a more detailed discussion of how they can be effectively chosen.
* **Missing related work:** The citation of recent work on doubly robust estimators [6] feels a bit cursory. A more thorough discussion of how the self-normalized estimator relates to doubly robust methods might be valuable.




**Assessment based on NIPS criteria:**

* **Quality:** The paper is technically sound, with a clear theoretical justification and strong empirical support for its claims. The analysis is careful and addresses both strengths and weaknesses.  It feels like a complete piece of work. **(Strong)**
* **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized, making it easy to understand the core ideas and contributions. **(Strong)**
* **Originality:** While the self-normalized estimator isn't entirely new, the application to BLBF and the identification of propensity overfitting are novel and significant.  The paper clearly differentiates its work from prior contributions. **(Good)**
* **Significance:** The problem of propensity overfitting is important for the broader field of learning from logged data.  The proposed solution offers a practical and effective way to mitigate this issue, and could have a significant impact on the development of more robust and reliable BLBF algorithms. **(Strong)**

**Arguments for acceptance:**

* Addresses a significant and previously unrecognised problem in BLBF.
* Provides a theoretically sound and empirically validated solution.
* Well-written and clearly explains complex concepts.
* Has the potential to be widely adopted by researchers and practitioners.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

* Core technique (self-normalization) is not novel in itself.
* Computational complexity of variance estimation could be a concern.
* Limited discussion of hyperparameter sensitivity.

**Overall Recommendation:**

**Strong Accept.** This is a high-quality paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of learning from logged data. The identification of propensity overfitting is a novel and important observation, and the proposed solution is both theoretically sound and empirically effective. While the self-normalized estimator itself is not entirely new, its application to BLBF is a valuable contribution. I believe this paper is well-suited for presentation at NIPS. I would recommend the authors address the minor weaknesses mentioned above (computational complexity analysis, hyperparameter sensitivity) in a final version.
