This paper addresses the problem of batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF), a setting where data is collected passively and suffers from both bandit feedback and selection bias. The authors identify a novel issue – “Propensity Overfitting” – arising from the use of standard importance sampling estimators in this context, and propose a self-normalized estimator to mitigate this. The theoretical analysis appears sound, and the empirical evaluation demonstrates improved performance compared to a baseline (POEM) across several datasets.

While the identified problem of propensity overfitting is interesting and the proposed solution appears theoretically well-motivated, I have significant reservations about the overall novelty and practical impact of this work. The paper leans heavily on established concepts from causal inference, reinforcement learning, and importance sampling, and the presented contribution feels incremental rather than transformative.

**Strengths:**

*   **Clear Problem Definition:** The paper clearly articulates the challenges of BLBF and motivates the need for robust estimators.
*   **Theoretical Soundness:** The analysis of propensity overfitting and the derivation of the self-normalized estimator appear rigorous and well-justified. The proof of Theorem 2 is concise and leverages existing results effectively.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The experimental results consistently demonstrate the superiority of Norm-POEM over POEM across multiple datasets, suggesting practical benefits. The diagnostic analysis confirming the presence of propensity overfitting is also valuable.
*   **Well-Written:** The paper is generally well-written and organized, making it reasonably easy to follow the key ideas.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Limited Novelty:** The core idea of using a self-normalized estimator is not entirely new (see references [12, 19, 22, 23]). The contribution lies in recognizing its applicability to the specific problem of propensity overfitting in BLBF, but the core technique itself is borrowed from other fields. The "new form of propensity overfitting" described feels less like a fundamentally new phenomenon and more like a specific manifestation of a well-known issue in importance sampling.
*   **Incremental Improvement:** While the empirical results are positive, the performance gains over POEM appear modest. The improvements do not seem substantial enough to warrant significant attention, especially given the complexity added by the self-normalized estimator.
*   **Limited Baselines:** The comparison is limited to a single baseline, POEM. While POEM is a reasonable choice, evaluating against other established methods for off-policy evaluation (e.g., doubly robust estimators, trimmed importance sampling) would strengthen the claims of improvement.
*   **Computational Complexity:** The authors acknowledge increased computational cost and highlight the need for L-BFGS. Given the potential scaling issues, a more thorough discussion of the computational limitations and potential optimizations would be beneficial.
*   **Overreliance on Existing Theory:** The paper heavily relies on existing theoretical results without providing substantial new theoretical insights beyond the analysis of propensity overfitting.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper identifies a practical issue (propensity overfitting) that affects the performance of existing BLBF algorithms.
*   The proposed solution (self-normalized estimator) is theoretically sound and empirically validated.
*   The paper is well-written and clearly presents the key ideas.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

*   The core novelty is limited, as the self-normalized estimator is a known technique.
*   The performance gains over the baseline are modest.
*   The evaluation is limited to a single baseline.
*   The computational complexity and scalability are not thoroughly addressed.

**Overall Assessment:**

I lean towards **rejecting** this paper. While technically sound and well-presented, it lacks the significant novelty and impact expected of a NIPS publication. The contribution feels incremental, and the limited comparison to other baselines weakens the claims of improvement. The identified problem of propensity overfitting is interesting, but the solution leverages established techniques without providing substantial new theoretical insights. I believe the paper would be more suitable for a conference with a lower bar for novelty, or as a technical report. A major revision with a more comprehensive evaluation against a wider range of baselines, a more in-depth discussion of computational complexity, and a stronger emphasis on the unique aspects of the identified overfitting phenomenon might warrant reconsideration.
