This paper addresses the important problem of learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF), focusing on a critical issue: propensity overfitting, which can lead to severely biased risk estimates and poor generalization. The authors convincingly demonstrate this problem and propose a self-normalized risk estimator to mitigate it, offering both theoretical guarantees and empirical validation.

**Summary & Relation to Prior Work:**

The paper builds upon the growing body of work on BLBF, a problem closely linked to off-policy evaluation in reinforcement learning and covariate shift correction. It correctly identifies limitations in existing counterfactual risk estimators, specifically highlighting how they can be vulnerable to overfitting the propensity scores. Prior work (e.g., [1, 5, 6]) established the foundation of using counterfactual estimators for BLBF and explored variance reduction techniques. This work extends this line of research by identifying a *new* mode of overfitting—propensity overfitting—and introducing a self-normalized estimator, drawing inspiration from financial applications and reinforcement learning (specifically, [12, 17, 18, 19]). The authors appropriately position their contribution within this context, providing relevant citations and clearly articulating the novelty of their approach.

**Strengths:**

*   **Clear Problem Statement & Motivation:** The paper clearly articulates the issue of propensity overfitting and its detrimental effects on ERM in the BLBF setting. The intuitive explanation and illustrative example (Example 1) are particularly helpful.
*   **Novelty & Theoretical Contribution:** The introduction of the self-normalized risk estimator is a significant contribution. The theoretical analysis, including Theorem 2, provides strong justification for its use and addresses the limitations of existing estimators.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The experimental results are compelling. The paper demonstrates consistent performance improvements of Norm-POEM over POEM across multiple datasets, along with insightful analyses confirming that the improvements are indeed due to reduced propensity overfitting. The ablation study (Table 3) further strengthens the conclusions.
*   **Sound Methodology:** The experimental setup closely follows the supervised-to-bandit methodology of previous work [2], allowing for a fair comparison and reproducible results.
*   **Well-Written and Organized:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly structured. The organization is logical, and the notation is consistent. The connection to related work is clearly established.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Computational Cost Discussion:** While the paper mentions that Norm-POEM is surprisingly faster, the detailed computational complexity analysis is lacking. A more in-depth discussion of the runtime trade-offs between the two estimators (conventional vs. self-normalized) would be valuable, especially concerning the variance estimate calculation.
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper states hyper-parameters were calibrated, but lacks details on the calibration procedure. A brief discussion on the sensitivity of performance to the choice of M and λ would be helpful. How robust is the approach to suboptimal hyperparameter settings?
*   **Limited Scope of Experiments:** The experiments primarily focus on datasets from LibSVM. Exploring the performance of Norm-POEM on more diverse datasets (e.g., recommender systems, ad click prediction) could strengthen the generalizability claims.
*   **CRM Variance Estimator Complexity:** The variance estimation step for the self-normalized estimator appears complex. While the paper addresses computational efficiency, a more detailed discussion of potential approximation techniques for the variance estimate would be beneficial, especially for large-scale datasets.
*   **Equivariance Discussion:** While the equivariance of the self-normalized estimator is a significant advantage, the paper could benefit from a more detailed explanation of *why* this property is crucial in preventing overfitting, potentially linking it more directly to the limitations of the original estimator.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

*   Addresses a significant and previously overlooked problem (propensity overfitting) in BLBF.
*   Proposes a novel and theoretically sound solution (self-normalized risk estimator).
*   Demonstrates compelling empirical evidence of improved performance.
*   Contributes to the growing body of knowledge on learning from logged data.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   Computational cost discussion lacks detail.
*   Hyperparameter tuning details are insufficient.
*   Experiments are limited in scope.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with a clear contribution to the field of learning from logged bandit feedback. The authors identify a critical issue, propose a principled solution, and provide compelling evidence of its effectiveness. While there are minor weaknesses regarding computational cost analysis and hyperparameter sensitivity, they do not detract significantly from the overall quality of the work. The paper is well-written and makes a valuable contribution that is likely to be of interest to researchers in machine learning, causal inference, and reinforcement learning.

**Recommendation:**

Accept. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions, specifically addressing the computational cost discussion, providing more details on hyperparameter tuning, and potentially expanding the experimental evaluation.
