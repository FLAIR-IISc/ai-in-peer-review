Summary and relation to prior work
The paper addresses batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF), where only bandit feedback from a historical policy is available and the goal is to learn a new policy. Prior work uses importance sampling (IPS) estimators, with capping/clipping and empirical-Bernstein-based CRM regularization (e.g., POEM). The authors identify a specific failure mode of IPS-based empirical risk minimization they call propensity overfitting: the ERM solution exploits the propensity weights to drive the empirical risk to degenerate values, either by over-representing or avoiding logged actions depending on the loss translation. They show this is tied to two known anomalies of the standard IPS estimator: lack of boundedness and lack of translation equivariance.

To remedy this, they propose replacing IPS with the self-normalized (weighted) importance sampling estimator (SNIS), i.e., use the ratio of weighted losses to the sum of weights. They formalize how the sum of weights has expected value 1 and use it as a multiplicative control variate, leading to a risk estimator that is bounded and equivariant (though biased). They prove strong consistency (SLLN) and provide an approximate variance estimator for SNIS to instantiate the CRM principle. The resulting algorithm, Norm-POEM, mirrors POEM’s hypothesis class (stochastic linear/CRF-like) and objective but uses SNIS and its variance estimate (optimized via L-BFGS). Experiments on multilabel datasets (Scene, Yeast, TMC, LYRL) using the supervised-to-bandit protocol show improved generalization over POEM, robustness to loss translations, S(h) close to 1 (diagnostic for avoiding propensity overfitting), and competitive or better runtime.

The proposed estimator and diagnostics are well-known in importance sampling and off-policy evaluation (self-normalization, weighted IS; e.g., Hesterberg, Kong, Owen), but the paper’s contribution is to articulate and demonstrate propensity overfitting in BLBF/CRM and to integrate SNIS into a practical learning objective.

Quality
- Theory: The paper correctly characterizes anomalies of IPS, motivates SNIS via control variates, shows equivariance/boundedness, and proves strong consistency. The empirical-Bernstein bound (CRM) is cited from prior work; there is no new non-asymptotic bound specialized to SNIS. The variance formula used for CRM regularization is an asymptotic approximation; finite-sample justification is not provided.
- Method: The Norm-POEM objective is clearly specified, with clipping M and regularization λ tuned via held-out counterfactual evaluation. Optimization with L-BFGS is reasonable.
- Empirics: Experiments are careful within the chosen protocol, include 10 runs, ablations on variance regularization (Norm-IPS), and diagnostic S(h). However, baselines are limited: comparisons to doubly robust (DR)/weighted doubly robust, truncated IS variants, or defensive mixture are missing. No statistical significance testing is reported. The logging policy is stochastic and trained on 5% labels; results under low-support or near-deterministic h0 are not reported.

Clarity
The paper is well written and organized. The propensity overfitting example is illuminating. The relationship between S(h), estimator degeneracy, and overfitting is clearly explained. Some details that could be clarified:
- Explicitly state conditions ensuring denominator stability for SNIS (e.g., support assumptions and clipping strategy).
- Provide more details on the computational costs per iteration and convergence behavior (beyond qualitative statements).

Originality
Using SNIS and control variates is not novel per se, but:
- Identifying and naming “propensity overfitting” as a structural overfitting mode in BLBF-ERM is insightful.
- Integrating SNIS into CRM-based learning and showing practical benefits, including equivariance and boundedness impacting ERM, is a useful and novel application to this setting.
- The diagnostic use of S(h) to detect overfitting during learning is appropriate and practically relevant.

Significance
The work addresses an important and active area (off-policy learning/counterfactual learning). The proposed change is simple, well-motivated, and yields consistent improvements in the presented tasks. Given how widely IPS-based CRM is used, showing that SNIS alleviates a real degeneracy and improves robustness is likely to influence practice. The lack of comparisons to DR/WDR reduces the strength of the empirical claim, but the conceptual contribution (propensity overfitting and a principled fix) is still valuable.

Pros
- Clear identification and compelling demonstration of propensity overfitting with IPS-ERM.
- Principled proposal using a well-understood control variate (SNIS) that ensures boundedness/equivariance and strong consistency.
- Seamless integration into CRM with a practical variance estimator.
- Consistent empirical gains across datasets; robustness to loss translations; informative diagnostics (S(h) near 1).
- Code availability and tractable optimization.

Cons
- No finite-sample generalization bound tailored to the SNIS-based objective; variance term is asymptotic.
- Missing strong baselines from off-policy evaluation/learning (DR, weighted DR, SWITCH-DR, more clipping schemes).
- Experimental setting limited to supervised-to-bandit multilabel classification; no real logged data or RL tasks; limited exploration of low-support regimes.
- Sensitivity analyses for M and λ are not detailed; no significance tests.
- Discussion of denominator stability and potential bias-variance trade-offs of SNIS could be deeper.

Questions and suggestions
- How sensitive are results to M and λ? Can you provide guidance or an adaptive scheme (e.g., variance-constrained clipping)?
- Can you report comparisons to doubly robust and weighted/doubly robust normalized estimators? These are strong baselines in off-policy evaluation and could change conclusions.
- Is there a way to incorporate S(h)-based constraints or penalty directly in the objective?
- Can you provide a finite-sample bound for SNIS-CRM (perhaps via ratio concentration or self-normalized concentration inequalities)?
- What happens under logging policies with low or no support (violating assumptions)? Any robustness strategies (e.g., logging policy mixtures)?
- Please include statistical significance testing and more detailed runtime metrics.

Overall assessment
This paper makes a clear, practically impactful point: IPS-based CRM can propensity-overfit, and the self-normalized estimator is a principled, effective remedy that improves robustness and generalization. While the estimator is classical, the identification of the failure mode and its integration into counterfactual learning are valuable. The main weaknesses are missing stronger baselines and finite-sample theory tailored to the new objective. I lean toward acceptance provided the authors add comparisons to DR/WDR and strengthen the empirical evaluation and discussion.

Recommendation: Weak accept.
