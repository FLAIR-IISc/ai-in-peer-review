Summary
The paper addresses batch learning from logged bandit feedback and argues that the conventional inverse propensity scoring (IPS) estimator used within Counterfactual Risk Minimization (CRM) suffers from a previously underappreciated pathology (“propensity overfitting”). The authors propose replacing IPS with the self-normalized importance sampling estimator (SNIPS), using the sum of importance weights as a multiplicative control variate, and instantiate a learning algorithm (Norm-POEM) that combines SNIPS with a variance-regularized CRM objective. They argue SNIPS is bounded and equivariant, thereby avoiding degenerate ERM behavior, prove almost-sure consistency, propose an approximate variance estimator for use in the CRM penalty, and present experiments on supervised-to-bandit conversions of multilabel datasets showing improved generalization and robustness relative to POEM.

Relation to prior work
Weighted/self-normalized importance sampling (aka WIS/SNIPS) is well known in Monte Carlo and RL off-policy evaluation (e.g., Hesterberg; Kong; Owen). Prior contextual bandit work also explored clipping, doubly robust estimators, and high-confidence OPE. The paper positions SNIPS as a learning-time replacement within CRM and coins “propensity overfitting” to describe weight degeneracy effects when learning. While the use of SNIPS for evaluation is established, integrating SNIPS into a CRM objective for learning is the main claimed novelty. However, the paper does not provide a new generalization bound tailored to SNIPS-CRM.

Strengths
- Clear articulation and demonstration (via examples) of a real failure mode of IPS-driven ERM.
- Sensible proposal to use a multiplicative control variate; R̂SN is bounded and equivariant, which addresses key anomalies of IPS.
- Consistency result (albeit minimal) and an approximate variance estimator for the ratio estimator to instantiate CRM.
- Empirical results (as described) suggest improved stability, better test performance, and Ŝ(ĥ) ≈ 1, consistent with the diagnostics.

Weaknesses and detailed comments
Technical correctness and theoretical completeness
1) Missing finite-sample generalization guarantees for SNIPS-CRM. The bound in Theorem 1 is for clipped IPS; the paper neither adapts nor derives an analogous bound for the SNIPS-based objective it actually optimizes. Given that the central claim is that propensity overfitting is “provably” avoided, a finite-sample guarantee (or at least a bound that depends on the concentration of standardized weights) is needed. As written, claims overreach the presented theory.

2) Theorem 2 (consistency) assumptions are underspecified. Full support of h0 is insufficient; one needs E|δ(x,y) h/h0| < ∞ (bounded loss suffices), and the denominator’s limit must be nonzero. Please state and prove under explicit integrability/boundedness assumptions. If clipping is applied, consistency may be lost unless M = M(n) → ∞; this is not discussed.

3) Variance estimator (Eq. 8) is presented without derivation or conditions. The cited Normal/delta method approximations require moment conditions; clarify when this estimator is reliable, and whether the extra variability from clipping is accounted for. Also discuss the common SNIPS variance approximation Var ≈ (1/n) Var[W−θV]/(E[V])^2.

4) Clipping with SNIPS. You suggest thresholding weights “analogous to R̂M” to ensure finite variance, but the exact implementation is unclear (clip both numerator and denominator? impact on bias/consistency?). Please specify and analyze.

5) Objective complexity. The claim that per-iteration cost matches that of supervised CRFs is questionable: the variance penalty couples all data points (sums in numerator/denominator), precluding stochastic optimization and requiring full passes. Provide a more precise complexity analysis and runtimes.

Experimental issues and reproducibility
6) Missing quantitative results. The manuscript references Tables 1–3 and Figure 1 but does not show them, preventing verification of the claimed gains, runtime improvements, or statistical significance. Include full tables with means, standard errors, and significance tests.

7) Baselines. No comparisons to doubly robust estimators (DR), clipped IPS-CRM variants, or recent high-confidence OPE learners. Given extensive prior work, these are important baselines.

8) Supervised→bandit protocol details are sparse. How is Hamming loss scaled to satisfy the bounded-loss assumptions (several sections assume δ ∈ [−1,0])? The text later varies loss translations; please standardize and report the normalization used for training and evaluation.

9) Hyperparameter selection. Validation uses counterfactual evaluation on held-out logs; specify which estimator is used for model selection and whether it introduces selection bias. Report sensitivity to M and λ.

10) Code and reproducibility. The provided URL appears to point to POEM, not Norm-POEM. Please release the exact implementation and scripts used to generate the reported results.

Clarity, notation, and presentation
11) Multiple typos/inconsistencies:
- “δ(·, ·) ∈ [5,4]” seems erroneous (likely [−5,4] or [−1,0]).
- Mixing δ and Δ for loss; unify notation.
- Use of δ ∈ [−1,0] in Theorem 1 but nonnegative Hamming losses in experiments; reconcile.
- Several reference entries are incomplete (years, venues, pages missing), e.g., many “In ICML, , ,”.
- Minor grammatical/formatting issues (“consistenly”, stray spaces in Pr(X ), etc.).
These detract from readability and should be fixed.

12) Overclaiming language. Phrases like “resolves all the problems” are too strong; SNIPS remains biased finite-sample and can be high-variance under heavy-tailed weights.

Significance and originality
The idea to replace IPS with SNIPS in learning is sensible but incremental given the extensive literature on WIS/SNIPS and DR estimators in off-policy evaluation. The empirical benefit is plausibly meaningful, but without the actual tables/plots and stronger theory, the significance is hard to assess.

Pros for acceptance
- Identifies an important practical pathology with a compelling illustrative example.
- Simple, well-motivated fix with clear diagnostics (Ŝ(h)).
- Empirical narrative claims consistent improvements and robustness.

Cons for acceptance
- Lacks finite-sample generalization guarantees for the proposed objective.
- Incomplete and sometimes incorrect presentation (typos, notation, references).
- Missing quantitative results, baselines, and statistical analyses.
- Ambiguity around clipping, variance estimation, and computational claims.
- Modest novelty relative to known SNIPS/WIS and DR methods.

Recommendation: Reject (borderline). The core idea is promising and likely useful in practice, but the paper in its current form does not meet NeurIPS standards for rigor and presentation. Strengthening theory for SNIPS-CRM, adding complete experiments with strong baselines and significance tests, clarifying clipping/variance details, and fixing presentation issues would substantially improve the submission.

Confidence: High.
