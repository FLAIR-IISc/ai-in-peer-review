Summary
The paper addresses learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF) via counterfactual risk minimization (CRM). It identifies a failure mode of the standard inverse propensity scoring (IPS) estimator when used inside ERM—termed propensity overfitting—where the learner implicitly optimizes the sum of importance weights, leading to degenerate empirical risks (unbounded, non-equivariant under loss translations). The authors propose using a multiplicative control variate—self-normalized importance sampling (SNIS)—to obtain a self-normalized risk estimator. They argue this estimator is bounded and equivariant, and is strongly consistent. Building on this, they implement Norm-POEM, which substitutes SNIS and an approximate variance estimator into the CRM objective and optimizes it with L-BFGS. Experiments on multilabel datasets (via supervised→bandit simulation) show consistent gains over POEM, improved robustness to loss translations, and diagnostics (normalized weight sum near 1) consistent with reduced propensity overfitting.

Relation to prior work
The paper situates BLBF within off-policy evaluation and covariate shift, and cites classic IPS, clipping, and doubly robust (DR) estimators. SNIS/weighted IS is long-standing in Monte Carlo and RL (e.g., Kong 1992; Hesterberg; Owen; Rubinstein/Kroese) and is used in RL policy iteration and safe policy optimization. The main novelty here is: (a) formalizing and diagnosing propensity overfitting in BLBF/CRM with concrete examples, and (b) showing that SNIS specifically mitigates those anomalies within a learning algorithm, along with a practical variance-regularized objective.

Quality
- Theory: The “propensity overfitting” phenomenon is convincingly demonstrated via a clean construction (Example 1) and by analyzing how ERM with IPS can exploit weight sums. The properties claimed for SNIS—boundedness, equivariance, and (strong) consistency—are standard and correctly argued. However, the paper does not provide a finite-sample generalization bound for the SNIS-based learning objective analogous to the CRM bound they rely on for clipped IPS (Theorem 1 is inherited from prior work for capped IPS, not for SNIS). The variance expression used (Eq. 8) is a standard normal/delta-method approximation for ratio estimators; this is appropriate but heuristic in finite samples. The role of clipping M under SNIS and its bias-variance tradeoff are not analyzed theoretically.
- Method: Norm-POEM is a direct, well-motivated substitution of SNIS and an approximate variance regularizer into CRM. Optimization details are reasonable (L-BFGS), and the objective is implementable when CRF partition functions are tractable. The diagnostic Ŝ(h)≈1 is a useful sanity check.
- Experiments: The supervised→bandit protocol is well-established and allows controlled study. Norm-POEM consistently outperforms POEM and is robust to loss translations; Ŝ(h) supports the propensity overfitting diagnosis. However, baselines are limited. Given the problem framing, comparisons to DR and related estimators (e.g., regression, Switch-DR) are notably absent, and would strengthen claims. An ablation on the contribution of normalization vs variance regularization, and sensitivity to M and λ, is only partially addressed (Norm-IPS included, but no DR). Real logged data or larger action spaces would increase external validity.

Clarity
The paper is generally clear, well-organized, and readable. The exposition of propensity overfitting and the motivation for SNIS are crisp. Minor issues: there appears to be a typo around δ ranges (“[5,4]”); clearly state loss ranges consistently. Some details on hyperparameter search ranges and stopping criteria could be expanded, though code availability helps reproducibility.

Originality
Using SNIS is not novel per se, and its advantages (variance reduction when weight fluctuations dominate, bounded/equivariant behavior) are known. The novel contribution lies in identifying and formalizing “propensity overfitting” within CRM/BLBF, articulating how IPS-based ERM exploits weight sums, and demonstrating that SNIS addresses the identified anomalies when used for learning (not just evaluation). The integration into a robust training objective and the empirical evidence are valuable, albeit incremental on the estimator side.

Significance
BLBF and off-policy learning are important for recommender systems, ads, and RL. The identified pathology is practically relevant, and the proposed fix is simple, principled, and effective. The work will likely influence practitioners to prefer self-normalized estimators (with appropriate regularization) for counterfactual learning, improving robustness. The lack of a new finite-sample learning bound for SNIS and the limited baselines somewhat limit theoretical and empirical impact, but the contribution is still meaningful.

Pros
- Clear diagnosis of a real failure mode (propensity overfitting) with concrete examples and diagnostics.
- Simple, principled remedy via SNIS with strong practical appeal; bounded and equivariant behavior avoids pathologies of IPS in ERM.
- Consistent empirical gains over POEM across datasets; robustness to loss translations; useful diagnostic Ŝ(h).
- Practical algorithm (Norm-POEM) with available code; optimization is feasible and converges quickly in practice.

Cons
- Limited novelty on the estimator: SNIS is well-known; theoretical results are standard (consistency) and heuristic (variance approximation).
- No finite-sample generalization bound for the SNIS-based CRM objective; clipping/regularization under SNIS not analyzed theoretically.
- Experimental baselines omit DR/regression-based and switch estimators; no real logged datasets.
- Some notation/typo issues; modest detail on hyperparameter tuning.

Questions and suggestions
- Provide a generalization bound (e.g., an empirical Bernstein-style bound) tailored to SNIS or a justification that the proposed variance regularizer meaningfully upper-bounds excess risk.
- Include DR and Switch-DR baselines, and report effective sample size (ESS) to quantify weight degeneracy.
- Analyze sensitivity to M and λ; discuss principled selection (e.g., via validation criteria tied to ESS).
- Cite additional RL literature on weighted importance sampling and per-decision IS (e.g., Precup et al.) and clarify relation to WIS in RL.

Recommendation
Weak accept. The core estimator is known, but the paper makes a clear, well-argued case that self-normalization addresses a practically important failure mode of IPS-based CRM, and demonstrates consistent empirical benefits. Strengthening theoretical guarantees for the learning objective and adding stronger baselines would further improve the paper.
