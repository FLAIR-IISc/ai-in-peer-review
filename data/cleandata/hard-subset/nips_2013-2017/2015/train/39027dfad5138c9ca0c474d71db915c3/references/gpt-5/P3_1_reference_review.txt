Summary and relation to prior work
The paper addresses batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF) and the use of inverse propensity scoring (IPS) for counterfactual risk minimization (CRM). The authors identify a failure mode they term propensity overfitting: when using IPS-based ERM, hypotheses can exploit the importance weights, yielding degenerate empirical risks (e.g., unboundedly low for negative losses, or zero by avoiding observed actions under loss shifts). They argue that IPS is non-equivariant and unbounded, exacerbating this issue, and propose replacing the standard estimator with the self-normalized (weighted) importance sampling estimator (SNIPS), used as a multiplicative control variate. This yields a risk estimate that is bounded and translation-equivariant, and consistent. Building on this, they propose Norm-POEM, which optimizes a CRM-style objective with an approximate variance penalty for the ratio estimator, and evaluate it on multilabel classification datasets using a supervised-to-bandit simulation. They show improved generalization relative to POEM (IPS-based CRM), robustness to loss translations, and better diagnostic behavior via the sum of weights Ŝ(h).

The proposal connects classic ideas from importance sampling (self-normalization; Hesterberg 1995; Owen 2013) to counterfactual learning. It relates to off-policy evaluation and learning (Dudík et al. 2011, Strehl et al. 2010, Thomas et al. 2015) and prior BLBF/CRM work (Swaminathan & Joachims 2015). The paper’s conceptual contribution is to formalize “propensity overfitting” and advocate SNIPS within a CRM training objective.

Strengths
- Clear articulation of a genuine and important failure mode of IPS-based ERM in BLBF, illustrated by a simple example that reveals non-equivariance and unboundedness issues.
- Use of a well-founded variance reduction/control variate technique (self-normalization) to mitigate that failure mode. The resulting estimator is bounded and translation-equivariant; the consistency claim is correct and immediate.
- Integration into a practical learning algorithm (Norm-POEM) with an approximate variance penalty and weight clipping for stability, and a coherent discussion of computational aspects.
- Empirical results consistently favor Norm-POEM over POEM across several datasets; diagnostic evidence (Ŝ close to 1) supports the claimed mitigation of propensity overfitting. The loss-translation experiments convincingly show POEM’s brittleness vs. Norm-POEM’s invariance.

Weaknesses and questions
- Limited novelty of the core estimator: SNIPS (a.k.a. weighted IPS/WIS) is a standard tool in IS/OPE. The main novelty here is framing “propensity overfitting” and applying SNIPS inside CRM. The paper would benefit from a deeper theoretical treatment of learning with SNIPS (beyond consistency), e.g., a finite-sample generalization bound for the ratio estimator analogous to the CRM bound used for IPS. Currently, the CRM objective with the approximate variance of SNIPS lacks a supporting finite-sample guarantee.
- Comparisons are narrow. Key baselines are missing:
  - Doubly robust (DR) and self-normalized DR (a.k.a. WDR) estimators, which are widely used and often outperform IPS/SNIPS when a decent reward model is available.
  - Clipped IPS/SNIPS (with consistent clipping in numerator and denominator) as pure estimators without CRM, to separate estimator effects from the CRM variance penalty.
  - Other robust off-policy learning approaches (e.g., offset tree, exploration scavenging, cost-proportionate weighting) in the same setting.
- Treatment of clipping in the ratio estimator is underspecified. For SNIPS, if weights are clipped, the denominator must be clipped in the same way to preserve the control variate property. The paper mentions clipping for variance control but does not clearly define the exact objective used during training and validation when clipping is applied, nor its bias implications.
- The theoretical claim “provably avoids degeneracies” is partially supported (boundedness/equivariance/consistency) but does not address finite-sample risk estimation error under high-variance propensities; counterexamples where SNIPS can still be biased and high-variance are not discussed. Moreover, the empirical Bernstein-style CRM bound is reused from prior work but does not apply directly to a ratio estimator; this gap should be acknowledged and, ideally, bridged.
- Experimental scope: synthetic-to-bandit simulation with CRF logging policies trained on 5% of the data is convenient but limited. Results on real logged bandit data, or at least stress tests with heavier-tailed propensities (e.g., deterministic or near-deterministic logging, extreme action spaces), would strengthen claims about robustness. Statistical reporting (variances/confidence intervals) is light; some splits (e.g., LYRL test size) look unusual and deserve clarification.
- Modeling clarity: The hypothesis class is described as exponential family with partition function over Y. For multilabel Y = {0,1}^q, exact normalization is intractable unless the model factorizes (independent per-label logistic). The paper hints at this but should be explicit about the factorization used in both training and evaluation to ensure reproducibility.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide a finite-sample learning bound or at least a high-level argument for the CRM objective with SNIPS, possibly leveraging ratio concentration inequalities or delta-method-based empirical Bernstein bounds for ratios.
- Add DR and WDR/SNDR baselines, and show ablations with and without clipping in numerator/denominator. Include sensitivity analyses over the clipping threshold M and the variance-regularization coefficient λ.
- Clarify the exact clipped SNIPS objective used, including consistent clipping in both numerator and denominator, and discuss the induced bias.
- Expand experiments to include scenarios with more extreme propensities and, if possible, a real logged dataset; report standard errors/confidence intervals.
- Explicitly state the factorization structure used for tractable Z(x) and ensure the “CRF” terminology does not imply structured dependencies that are not implemented.

Overall assessment
- Quality: Solid and careful empirically; theoretical section is correct but modest (consistency and qualitative properties). Missing finite-sample learning guarantees and key baselines.
- Clarity: Generally well written; a few places need precision (clipping; model factorization).
- Originality: Incremental but meaningful: articulates a new failure mode within BLBF and applies a classic estimator in a learning objective.
- Significance: Practical importance for counterfactual learning; likely to influence how practitioners stabilize IPS-based training objectives.

Arguments for acceptance
- Identifies and convincingly demonstrates an important pitfall of IPS-based CRM.
- Offers a simple, principled, and effective fix with clear practical benefits.
- Empirical improvements are consistent and diagnostics support the mechanism.

Arguments against acceptance
- Limited novelty relative to established SNIPS; theoretical contributions stop short of finite-sample guarantees for learning.
- Missing strong baselines (DR/WDR), ablations, and broader experimental validation.

Recommendation: Weak accept, contingent on clarifying clipping, adding DR/WDR comparisons, and tightening the theoretical/expository gaps. Confidence: High.
