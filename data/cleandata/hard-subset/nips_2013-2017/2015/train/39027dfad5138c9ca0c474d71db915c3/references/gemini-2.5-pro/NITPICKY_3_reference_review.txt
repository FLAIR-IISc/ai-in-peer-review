**Summary of the Paper:**

This paper addresses the problem of Batch Learning from Logged Bandit Feedback (BLBF). The authors identify a novel failure mode of standard counterfactual risk estimators based on Inverse Propensity Scoring (IPS), which they term "Propensity Overfitting". This occurs when a learning algorithm, following the Empirical Risk Minimization (ERM) principle, minimizes the risk estimate not by fitting the loss, but by exploiting the propensity weights—for example, by assigning probability mass to unseen actions to achieve an artificially low or even negative risk estimate. The authors demonstrate this issue with a clear illustrative example.

To remedy this, the paper proposes replacing the standard IPS estimator with the Self-Normalized (SN) estimator, a well-known ratio estimator from the importance sampling literature that uses the sum of importance weights as a multiplicative control variate. The authors argue that the SN estimator is bounded within the range of the loss, is equivariant to translations of the loss function, and is strongly consistent, thereby resolving the anomalies that lead to Propensity Overfitting. They embed this estimator into the Counterfactual Risk Minimization (CRM) framework, deriving an approximate variance and proposing a new learning algorithm, "Norm-POEM". Empirical results on several multi-label classification datasets show that Norm-POEM consistently outperforms its predecessor (POEM), is robust to loss translations that cause POEM to fail, and converges faster in practice.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Problem Identification:** The main contribution is the clear identification and formalization of "Propensity Overfitting". This is a subtle but critical issue in counterfactual learning that, to my knowledge, has not been explicitly addressed in this way before. The example in Section 4 is highly effective at illustrating the degeneracy of the standard unbiased estimator.
2.  **Principled Solution:** The proposed solution of using the self-normalized estimator is well-motivated and principled. It connects the BLBF problem to established statistical techniques (control variates, ratio estimators) and elegantly resolves the identified issues of unboundedness and non-equivariance of the standard estimator.
3.  **Strong Empirical Results:** The experiments provide compelling evidence for the paper's claims. Norm-POEM consistently outperforms the baseline, and the ablation study in Table 2, showing Norm-POEM's robustness to loss translation while POEM fails catastrophically, is a powerful demonstration of the practical importance of addressing Propensity Overfitting.

**Weaknesses:**

Despite the strong central idea, the manuscript is marred by a significant number of minor flaws, ranging from formatting issues to a lack of technical depth in key areas.

1.  **Clarity and Presentation:**
    *   The paper suffers from a lack of polish. The reference list is poorly formatted, with numerous entries missing essential information (e.g., "[1] In ICML,, , ,") or containing typos. This level of carelessness is concerning for a top-tier conference submission.
    *   Notation is sometimes inconsistent or confusing. For example, the range of the loss function is stated as `[−1, 0]` for Theorem 1, then as `[5,4]` (a typo) in Section 4, and is different again in Example 1. This makes the theoretical exposition difficult to follow.
    *   The statement in Section 3, `δ(x, y) = E [δ | x, y]`, appears to conflate a random variable with its expectation and is imprecisely worded.

2.  **Methodological Depth:**
    *   While the SN estimator is a good choice, it is a classic tool. The paper frames it as a novel proposal, but the originality lies more in its application to diagnose and solve Propensity Overfitting within the CRM framework, not in the estimator itself. The connection to existing literature on the Hajek estimator could be made more explicit.
    *   The SN estimator is biased for finite samples. The paper mentions this only in passing before asserting its consistency. A more thorough discussion of the bias-variance trade-off being made is warranted. How significant can this bias be, and under what conditions?
    *   The variance estimator in Equation (8) is a Taylor series approximation (via the delta method). The paper provides no discussion of the quality of this approximation or its potential limitations, which could impact the performance of the CRM regularizer, especially in small-sample or high-variance regimes.
    *   The learning algorithm still relies on clipping the importance weights (the hyperparameter `M`), which seems to partially undercut the elegance of using a self-normalizing estimator that is inherently designed to handle fluctuating weights. The interplay between normalization and clipping is not explored.

3.  **Experimental Analysis:**
    *   The claim that Norm-POEM is "surprisingly faster" than POEM is presented without quantitative support. Plots of the objective function versus wall-clock time or iteration count, as well as statistics on the final learned model norms (`||w||`), are needed to substantiate this claim.
    *   The experimental setting uses a logging policy `h0` from the same model class as the learned policy `h`. The performance of the method should be evaluated in more challenging scenarios, such as when `h0` is misspecified or exhibits substantially different characteristics from the optimal policy.

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper introduces an important and well-articulated problem, Propensity Overfitting, and presents a simple, effective, and empirically validated solution. The core idea is strong and relevant to the NIPS community.

However, the manuscript in its current form does not meet the high standards expected for NIPS. The numerous minor errors in formatting, notation, and presentation detract significantly from the quality of the work and suggest a rushed preparation. Furthermore, the discussion lacks technical depth regarding the theoretical trade-offs (bias of the estimator) and the approximations used (variance estimation). While the core contribution is valuable, the paper would require a major revision to address these shortcomings. My recommendation is therefore to reject, with strong encouragement for the authors to revise and resubmit their work.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Identifies a novel and important problem ("Propensity Overfitting") in a popular learning setting.
*   The proposed solution is simple, principled, and shown to be highly effective empirically.
*   The results are significant and likely to influence future work in off-policy learning and evaluation.

**Arguments for Rejection:**
*   The paper is poorly polished, with numerous formatting and notational errors that undermine its professionalism.
*   Key technical details are glossed over, including the bias of the proposed estimator and the validity of the variance approximation.
*   The experimental analysis, while positive, could be more rigorous (e.g., providing quantitative evidence for runtime claims).
