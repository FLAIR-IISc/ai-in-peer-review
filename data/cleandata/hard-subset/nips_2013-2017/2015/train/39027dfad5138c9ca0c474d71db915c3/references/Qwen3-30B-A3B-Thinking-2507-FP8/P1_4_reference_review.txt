

# Peer Review: "The Self-Normalized Estimator for Counterfactual Learning"

## Summary

This paper identifies a previously uncharacterized problem in batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF) called "Propensity Overfitting," which occurs when using conventional counterfactual risk estimators in Empirical Risk Minimization (ERM). The authors propose a novel Self-Normalized Risk Estimator that uses multiplicative control variates to address this issue. The paper demonstrates theoretically that this estimator avoids the anomalies of conventional estimators (unbounded risk estimates, lack of equivariance) and empirically shows that their derived learning algorithm, Norm-POEM, outperforms the previous state-of-the-art method (POEM) across multiple datasets. The work builds on the CRM principle from [1] but identifies a critical limitation of the conventional estimator that the authors term "Propensity Overfitting," which is distinct from standard overfitting in supervised learning.

## Quality

The paper is technically sound with rigorous theoretical analysis. The authors clearly define Propensity Overfitting through a compelling example and demonstrate its prevalence across multiple datasets. The theoretical guarantees for the self-normalized estimator (Theorem 2) are well-supported, and the paper provides a thorough explanation of why the proposed estimator avoids the anomalies of conventional importance sampling estimators. The experimental evaluation is comprehensive, using multiple standard BLBF datasets with controlled settings, and demonstrates consistent improvements over POEM across different dataset sizes and loss transformations. The authors also thoughtfully address potential weaknesses, such as the computational cost of the new estimator and the continued need for variance regularization.

## Clarity

The paper is exceptionally well-organized and clearly written. The problem statement, theoretical contributions, and experimental results are presented in a logical flow that builds a strong case for the proposed method. The key concepts (Propensity Overfitting, self-normalized estimator) are introduced with clear examples and well-explained motivation. The notation is consistent throughout, and the experimental setup is sufficiently detailed for reproducibility. The comparison between the conventional and proposed estimators is particularly clear, with the paper explicitly highlighting the theoretical and practical advantages of the self-normalized approach.

## Originality

The paper makes a significant original contribution by identifying and formalizing "Propensity Overfitting" as a distinct problem in BLBF that affects conventional counterfactual risk estimators. This is a novel insight that previous work did not recognize. The proposed solution using multiplicative control variates for self-normalization is also original, drawing on techniques from importance sampling literature but applying them in a new context to solve a previously unaddressed problem. While the paper builds on the CRM principle from [1], the identification of Propensity Overfitting and the corresponding solution represent a meaningful advance beyond previous work on BLBF.

## Significance

The paper addresses a problem with significant practical implications for interactive systems (recommendation engines, ad platforms, etc.) that rely on logged bandit feedback. The demonstrated improvements in generalization performance and robustness across multiple datasets provide strong evidence that the proposed approach is not just theoretically interesting but practically valuable. The paper's insights about the limitations of conventional estimators will likely influence future work in counterfactual learning. The results are significant enough that they could change how practitioners approach BLBF problems, moving beyond the current state-of-the-art methods.

## Strengths

1. Identification of Propensity Overfitting as a novel problem in BLBF
2. Clear theoretical justification for the self-normalized estimator
3. Comprehensive experimental validation across multiple datasets
4. Practical implementation of Norm-POEM with clear comparison to POEM
5. Demonstrated robustness to loss transformations (a key advantage over previous methods)
6. Detailed analysis of why the proposed approach avoids the anomalies of conventional estimators

## Weaknesses

1. The paper could more explicitly discuss the limitations of the self-normalized estimator (e.g., when the denominator might approach zero in practice)
2. The theoretical analysis of the variance of the self-normalized estimator could be more detailed
3. The computational cost of the self-normalized estimator is acknowledged but not thoroughly analyzed compared to POEM

## Overall Assessment

This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of counterfactual learning. The identification of Propensity Overfitting as a critical problem in BLBF is novel and well-justified, and the proposed solution is theoretically sound and empirically validated. The paper advances the state-of-the-art in BLBF and provides valuable theoretical insights that will likely influence future research in the area. The quality of the theoretical analysis, the thoroughness of the experimental evaluation, and the clear writing make this a compelling contribution to the field.

## Arguments for Acceptance

- Clearly identifies a novel problem (Propensity Overfitting) not previously recognized in BLBF literature
- Proposes a theoretically grounded solution with strong empirical validation
- Demonstrates consistent improvements over the previous state-of-the-art
- Provides valuable theoretical insights about the limitations of conventional estimators
- Well-written, clear, and technically sound

## Arguments Against Acceptance

- The computational cost of the self-normalized estimator is slightly higher than the conventional estimator (though the paper shows it still converges faster in practice)
- The paper could provide more detailed theoretical analysis of the variance of the self-normalized estimator

Overall, the strengths of this paper significantly outweigh the weaknesses. The novel problem identification, solid theoretical foundation, and comprehensive empirical validation make this a highly significant contribution to the field of counterfactual learning. I strongly recommend acceptance for NIPS.
