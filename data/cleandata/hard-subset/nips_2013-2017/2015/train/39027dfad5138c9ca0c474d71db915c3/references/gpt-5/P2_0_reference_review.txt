Summary and relation to prior work
The paper addresses batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF) and, in particular, a failure mode of importance-weighted empirical risk minimization used in prior work (e.g., POEM/CRM). The authors identify “propensity overfitting,” where the empirical IPS risk can be driven to degenerate values by manipulating the sum of importance weights, leading to extreme bias and lack of equivariance under loss translations. They propose replacing the standard IPS risk with a self-normalized IPS (SNIPS) risk estimator, leveraging multiplicative control variates. They prove basic properties (equivariance, boundedness within loss range, strong consistency) and integrate SNIPS into the CRM learning principle, yielding the “Norm-POEM” algorithm with an approximate variance estimator for use as a data-dependent regularizer. Empirically, on multilabel datasets using a supervised-to-bandit protocol, Norm-POEM consistently outperforms POEM, appears more robust to loss translations, and shows better calibration of the sum of weights.

This connects to a large literature on importance sampling, control variates, and off-policy evaluation. Self-normalization (a.k.a. weighted IPS) is classic in the Monte Carlo/OPE literature (e.g., Hesterberg; Owen; Kong). The paper’s novelty lies in diagnosing propensity overfitting in BLBF/CRM and operationalizing SNIPS as a learning objective with a variance regularizer in structured prediction settings, rather than proposing a new estimator per se.

Strengths
- Clear identification and illustration (via a simple example) of “propensity overfitting” as distinct from “loss overfitting,” highlighting practical pathologies of IPS risk minimization that are not remedied by clipping alone.
- Adoption of SNIPS is well-motivated: it fixes lack of equivariance, bounds the empirical risk within the loss range, and uses a known control-variate identity (E[Ŝ]=1) that directly targets the degeneracy.
- Integration into CRM yields a practical algorithm (Norm-POEM) with a variance-regularized objective. The approximate variance estimator for ratios is standard and justified.
- Empirical results across several datasets are consistent: better generalization than POEM, robustness to loss translations, improved Ŝ calibration, and competitive runtime.
- Writing is generally clear and well-structured; the problem statement and solution are easy to follow.

Weaknesses
- Theoretical contribution is modest. Strong consistency of SNIPS and its properties are well-known; the paper does not provide a new finite-sample generalization bound for the SNIPS-CRM objective analogous to Theorem 1 (the bound is still stated for clipped IPS). Thus, the central theoretical support for the new learning objective remains asymptotic/heuristic.
- The variance estimator used is asymptotic and requires clipping to ensure finite variance; there is no analysis of the bias-variance trade-off induced by self-normalization plus clipping in the learning setting, nor a bound that captures the effect of normalization on the variance regularizer.
- Comparisons are limited. Given the known advantages of doubly robust estimators and additive control variates, a direct empirical comparison to DR-based learning (and, where possible, weighted/normalized DR) would strengthen the case. The paper cites DR but does not compare to it in learning performance.
- Evaluation is solely in a supervised-to-bandit simulation with a CRF logging policy trained on 5% of labels. While standard in this area, the absence of real logged data or broader tasks limits claims about practical impact. Sensitivity analyses for hyperparameters (clipping M, λ) are not deeply explored.
- Some notational inconsistencies and minor issues (e.g., δ ranges, occasional mixing of loss/reward language) could be polished; the theory section at one point has an apparent typo in the loss range.

Assessment by criteria
- Quality: Technically sound in its diagnosis and use of SNIPS; claims about equivariance, boundedness, and consistency are correct. However, the learning theory contribution is incomplete: no new finite-sample bound for the SNIPS-regularized objective, and reliance on approximate variance estimation. Experiments are careful but limited in scope and baselines.
- Clarity: Clear exposition of the problem and solution, with an illustrative example. The algorithmic instantiation is described well enough to reproduce (and code is said to be available). Some minor clarity issues as noted above.
- Originality: The estimator itself is not new; the novelty is in the identification of propensity overfitting in BLBF and the principled replacement of IPS with SNIPS inside CRM, plus the empirical demonstration that this matters. This is an incremental yet meaningful conceptual and algorithmic contribution.
- Significance: Propensity overfitting is an important and practical issue in counterfactual learning. Showing that SNIPS materially improves learning stability and accuracy is useful to practitioners. The absence of stronger finite-sample guarantees and broader comparisons tempers the theoretical/significance claims, but the work is likely to be adopted in BLBF pipelines.

Questions and suggestions for authors
- Can you provide a finite-sample generalization bound (even under simplifying assumptions) for the SNIPS-CRM objective, or at least a concentration inequality that quantifies the impact of normalization on the variance term?
- Please compare against doubly robust learning objectives (and, if feasible, weighted/normalized DR or switch estimators) to disentangle the benefits of normalization vs. model-based control variates.
- Include sensitivity analyses for M and λ, and ablations showing the effects of normalization, clipping, and variance regularization separately.
- Report variability across runs (std errors) and confidence intervals for estimated risks to support robustness claims.
- Clarify minor notational issues and unify loss range assumptions.

Pros
- Identifies and addresses a real failure mode (propensity overfitting) with a principled, practical fix.
- Simple change (SNIPS) with clear theoretical properties improves robustness.
- Consistent empirical gains, better calibration, and reasonable computational cost.

Cons
- Limited novelty in the estimator; main theory is asymptotic and lacks new finite-sample bounds.
- Missing comparisons to DR-based learners and other variance-reduction techniques.
- Evaluation scope limited to simulated logs on multilabel datasets.

Overall recommendation: Weak accept. The paper offers a clear, useful improvement to a widely used methodology and highlights an underappreciated failure mode. While the theoretical advances are incremental and experiments could be broader, the contribution is practical and likely to influence future BLBF work.

Confidence: High.
