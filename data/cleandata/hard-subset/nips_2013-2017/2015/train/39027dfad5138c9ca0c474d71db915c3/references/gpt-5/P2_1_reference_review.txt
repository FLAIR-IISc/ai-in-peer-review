Summary
The paper addresses batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF) via counterfactual risk minimization (CRM). It identifies a failure mode of the standard inverse propensity scoring (IPS) estimator when used inside ERM—Propensity Overfitting—where hypotheses can exploit variability in importance weights to drive empirical risk estimates far outside the true loss range or to depend on arbitrary loss translations. The authors propose replacing IPS with the self-normalized IPS (SNIPS) estimator, motivated as a multiplicative control variate that enforces the sample propensity sum to be near its expectation. They show SNIPS is bounded and equivariant to loss translations, and strongly consistent. They then instantiate a learning algorithm, Norm-POEM, by plugging SNIPS and an approximate variance estimator into the CRM principle and optimizing a CRF-like exponential-family hypothesis with L-BFGS. Experiments on multi-label datasets demonstrate improved generalization and robustness relative to POEM (IPS-based CRM), reduced sensitivity to loss translation, better calibration of the propensity-sum statistic, and competitive runtime.

Relation to prior work
- The paper is grounded in importance sampling for off-policy evaluation (OPE) and causal inference, drawing on classic self-normalization (Hesterberg; Kong; Rubinstein & Kroese). The CRM framework and POEM baseline follow Swaminathan & Joachims (ICML).
- Connections to variance reduction (clipping), additive control variates (regression/doubly robust), and empirical-Bernstein generalization bounds are discussed.
- The key novelty is not SNIPS per se (well-known), but the analysis and practical use of SNIPS as a learning objective within CRM to avoid propensity overfitting, plus an empirical demonstration that this materially improves BLBF.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The identification and articulation of propensity overfitting are clear and well justified (Example 1 is compelling). The properties of SNIPS (boundedness, translation equivariance, strong consistency) are correct and relevant for learning. Using the sample propensity sum as a diagnostic and as a control variate is principled.
- However, the theoretical development for learning is limited. The main theorem (consistency) is standard; there is no finite-sample generalization/bias-variance bound for the SNIPS-based CRM objective analogous to the empirical-Bernstein bounds that motivated POEM. The variance estimator used for regularization (ratio estimator asymptotics) is approximate and invoked without high-probability guarantees. The interplay between clipping and normalization (bias/variance trade-offs) is not analyzed.
- Experiments are solidly executed within the supervised-to-bandit protocol, with multiple datasets, calibration on held-out logged data, and diagnostics (Ŝ(h)). The empirical results consistently favor Norm-POEM and align with the theoretical claims about robustness and translation invariance. Nonetheless, comparisons omit doubly robust (DR) learning baselines, which are directly relevant competitors when additive control variates are available.

Clarity
- The paper is well written and organized. The propensity overfitting phenomenon is explained intuitively and with a concrete example. The derivation of SNIPS as a control variate is clear, and the properties are well motivated. Algorithmic details (hypothesis class, optimization, variance estimator) are described sufficiently to reproduce, and code is provided. A few experimental details (hyperparameter search ranges, sensitivity to clipping M) could be expanded.

Originality
- The SNIPS estimator itself is not new. The contribution lies in (i) identifying propensity overfitting as a distinct and practically important failure mode of IPS-based ERM; (ii) arguing and demonstrating that self-normalization specifically mitigates it for learning; and (iii) integrating SNIPS into a CRM-style learner with empirical variance regularization. This is a novel and useful combination, albeit incremental relative to prior OPE/CRM literature.

Significance
- BLBF is important for recommender, ads, and other interactive systems; robust off-policy learning is impactful. The proposed modification is simple, principled, and yields consistent gains and improved robustness in experiments. Given how commonly IPS-based learning is used, the recommendation to prefer SNIPS within ERM could influence practice. The lack of new finite-sample theory tempers the theoretical significance, and missing comparisons to DR methods limit empirical generality.

Pros
- Clear identification and diagnosis of propensity overfitting; compelling example.
- Principled remedy via self-normalization with intuitive properties (boundedness, equivariance).
- Practical algorithm (Norm-POEM) with demonstrated improvements across datasets; robustness to loss translation; better calibration of propensity sums.
- Good alignment between theory, diagnostics, and empirical outcomes; code available.

Cons
- Limited new theory for learning: no high-probability generalization bounds for SNIPS-based CRM; reliance on asymptotic variance approximations.
- Incomplete empirical comparisons: no DR or DR+SNIPS baselines; limited exploration of clipping and weight instability regimes.
- Some optimization and scalability considerations are empirical and setup-specific (L-BFGS vs SGD); broader applicability not fully established.
- The main estimator (SNIPS) is known; novelty is primarily in applying it to avoid overfitting in learning.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide a finite-sample bound for SNIPS-based CRM (e.g., concentration for self-normalized IS) or at least bias/variance trade-off analysis under clipping.
- Compare against DR learning methods and DR-SNIPS variants; include modern robust OPE baselines if feasible.
- Analyze sensitivity to clipping M and logging policy support; include regimes with heavier-tailed weights.
- Explore explicit regularization or constraints on Ŝ(h) ≈ 1 as an alternative or complement to normalization.
- Add ablations on optimization (SGD vs L-BFGS) and larger-scale settings.

Overall assessment
This is a well-executed, practically relevant paper that identifies an important failure mode of IPS-based ERM and offers a simple, principled fix with consistent empirical gains. The main weakness is limited theoretical novelty and missing comparisons to DR-based learners. I lean toward acceptance for its clarity, practical impact, and empirical evidence, while encouraging stronger finite-sample analysis and broader baselines.
