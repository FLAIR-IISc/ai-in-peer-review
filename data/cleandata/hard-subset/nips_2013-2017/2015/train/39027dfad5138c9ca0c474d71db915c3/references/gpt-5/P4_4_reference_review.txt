Summary and relation to prior work
The paper addresses batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF) via counterfactual risk minimization (CRM). Prior work typically estimates the risk of a target policy using inverse propensity scoring (IPS) with optional clipping, and regularizes by an empirical Bernstein-style variance term (e.g., POEM). The authors identify a failure mode of IPS when used for ERM in finite samples, which they term Propensity Overfitting: the IPS objective can be minimized by manipulating the sum of importance weights rather than aligning with low-loss actions, leading to degenerate empirical risks that are unbounded and non-equivariant under loss translations. They propose using a multiplicative control variate—the sum of normalized propensities—to form a self-normalized importance sampling estimator (SNIS, also known as WIS), yielding a self-normalized risk estimator (SNIPS) that is bounded within the loss range and equivariant. They prove strong consistency and argue (drawing on known IS results) that self-normalization reduces variance when weight fluctuations dominate.

Building on this, they instantiate a CRM-style learning algorithm, Norm-POEM, using SNIPS as the risk estimator and an approximate variance estimate for the ratio estimator, optimized over a CRF-like exponential-family hypothesis class via L-BFGS. Experiments on multilabel classification datasets (supervised-to-bandit protocol) show that Norm-POEM improves generalization over POEM, is robust to loss translations, and yields propensity-weight sums near their expected value, diagnosing reduced propensity overfitting.

The work relates to: IPS and truncated/clipped IPS in causal inference and domain adaptation; high-confidence off-policy evaluation; doubly robust (DR) estimators (additive control variates); and classic results on self-normalized IS (SNIS/WIS) and control variates. Its key novelty is articulating propensity overfitting in IPS-based ERM and proposing SNIPS within CRM to mitigate it.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The failure mode of IPS is convincingly illustrated by a constructed example and linked to known anomalies (unboundedness, non-equivariance). The use of SNIS is appropriate; boundedness and equivariance directly address the anomalies. Consistency (Theorem 2) follows from LLN for numerator and denominator with full support. The variance estimate for the ratio estimator is standard (normal approximation/delta method), and clipping is used to ensure finite variance. However, the paper does not provide a formal generalization bound for the SNIPS-based objective analogous to the CRM bound given for IPS. Thus, the learning-theoretic justification of the specific variance-regularized objective is heuristic (albeit principled).
- Empirical support: The experimental protocol is standard for BLBF. Reported results indicate consistent gains over POEM, robustness to loss translations, and diagnostic S(h) near 1. The runtime observations are plausible. Missing details include confidence intervals or statistical tests, sensitivity to clipping M and λ, and ablations comparing with/without variance regularization for SNIPS across datasets.
- Scope of baselines: The paper compares to POEM but not to DR-based learners/estimators (e.g., doubly robust or weighted/doubly robust variants), which are relevant additive control variates known to reduce variance and bias in OPE. A comparison to DR (and possibly a weighted/normalized DR estimator) would strengthen claims about robustness and generalization.

Clarity
The paper is generally clear and well-organized. The exposition of propensity overfitting and the role of self-normalization is accessible. Notation is consistent with prior work. Minor comments:
- Early sections sometimes assume δ in [−1,0] (for the bound) but later example uses {−2,−1}; clarifying when rescaling/translation is applied would help.
- Provide pseudo-code for Norm-POEM and explicitly state how clipping interacts with self-normalization.
- Report standard errors/CI in tables and learning curves.

Originality
Self-normalized importance sampling is a well-established technique in Monte Carlo and has been used in off-policy evaluation. The originality here lies in:
- Identifying and formalizing propensity overfitting as a distinct failure mode for IPS-based ERM.
- Arguing for and integrating SNIPS into the CRM learning principle (Norm-POEM) for structured prediction, with theoretical properties (boundedness, equivariance, consistency) leveraged to prevent degeneracy.
This is an incremental but meaningful methodological contribution with clear practical implications.

Significance
The work addresses a practical and recurring issue in counterfactual learning and off-policy evaluation. Preventing degeneracies in ERM with logged bandit data is important for interactive systems (recsys, ads, ranking). Given the widespread use of IPS in BLBF, demonstrating the benefits of SNIPS within a learning objective and diagnosing propensity overfitting increase the reliability of counterfactual learning. The absence of a new generalization bound for the SNIPS objective and limited baselines temper the theoretical and empirical impact, but the proposed method appears useful and likely to be adopted.

Pros for acceptance
- Clear identification and analysis of a real failure mode (propensity overfitting) in IPS-based ERM.
- Principled remedy via self-normalization with desirable properties (boundedness, equivariance, consistency).
- Concrete algorithm (Norm-POEM) with improved empirical performance and robustness across datasets.
- Diagnostic use of Ŝ(h) is insightful and practical.

Cons for acceptance
- No formal generalization error bound for the SNIPS-based CRM objective; variance regularization is justified heuristically.
- Limited baselines; no comparison to DR/WDR or hybrid control variate methods.
- Empirical reporting lacks uncertainty quantification and detailed sensitivity analyses.
- Full-support assumption and the effect of clipping on bias in SNIPS-based learning are not analyzed theoretically.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide a learning bound or at least a PAC-style argument tailored to ratio estimators in CRM, clarifying the role of the variance term and clipping.
- Compare against doubly robust estimators and, if possible, a weighted/normalized DR variant; discuss combining multiplicative and additive control variates.
- Report standard errors/CI, conduct sensitivity analyses for M and λ, and include ablations on the variance regularizer.
- Discuss behavior when logging policies lack full support and connections to pessimistic/robust objectives.

Overall assessment: In scope for NeurIPS. The paper offers a well-motivated and practically relevant improvement to counterfactual learning with solid theoretical grounding for the estimator and convincing empirical evidence, though with some missing theoretical and baseline comparisons. I lean toward acceptance.
