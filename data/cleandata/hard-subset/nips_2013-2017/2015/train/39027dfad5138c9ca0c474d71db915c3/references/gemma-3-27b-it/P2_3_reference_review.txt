## Review of "The Self-Normalized Estimator for Counterfactual Learning"

**Summary:**

This paper addresses a critical issue in batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF): propensity overfitting. The authors demonstrate that standard counterfactual risk estimators used in this setting can lead to severely biased risk estimates due to overfitting the propensity scores, even when the loss function itself is well-behaved. To mitigate this, they propose a self-normalized risk estimator, leveraging techniques from importance sampling and control variates. They provide theoretical justification for its improved properties and demonstrate empirically that it leads to better generalization and robustness in several experiments.

**Relation to Previous Work:**

The paper builds upon the existing literature in BLBF, off-policy evaluation, and causal inference. It correctly positions itself relative to work on propensity score matching, doubly-robust estimators, and previous counterfactual learning approaches (e.g., POEM). The connection to self-normalized importance sampling is well-explained, and the paper successfully highlights the novelty of identifying and addressing *propensity* overfitting – a phenomenon not previously described in this context.  The references are comprehensive and cover relevant areas.

**Strengths:**

* **Identifies a novel and important problem:** Propensity overfitting is a significant issue that can undermine the performance of BLBF algorithms. This paper is the first to clearly articulate and analyze this phenomenon.
* **Sound Theoretical Foundation:** The paper provides theoretical justification for why the self-normalized estimator avoids the issues of the standard estimator. While the proofs are referred to existing work, the explanation of the intuition behind the improvement is clear.
* **Strong Empirical Validation:** The experimental results consistently demonstrate the superior performance of Norm-POEM (the proposed algorithm) over POEM across multiple datasets. The ablation studies (showing impact of variance regularization) and analysis of Ŝ(h) further support the theoretical claims.
* **Clear Writing and Organization:** The paper is generally well-written and logically organized. The introduction clearly motivates the problem, and the technical details are presented in a comprehensible manner.
* **Practical Implications:** The proposed method offers a practical solution for improving the performance of learning algorithms in real-world scenarios where data is collected via logged bandit feedback (e.g., ad placement, recommender systems).

**Weaknesses:**

* **Computational Complexity:** While the paper acknowledges the increased per-iteration cost of the self-normalized estimator, it doesn't provide a detailed comparative analysis of computational requirements (beyond observing faster *convergence*). A more thorough investigation of the runtime scaling would be valuable.
* **Limited Scope of Experiments:** The experiments primarily focus on adapting the POEM framework. While this provides a clear comparison, exploring the effectiveness of the self-normalized estimator with different base algorithms or hypothesis spaces would strengthen the results.
* **Parameter Sensitivity:** The paper mentions hyperparameter tuning but doesn’t delve deeply into the sensitivity of the results to different settings of λ and M.  A sensitivity analysis would be useful.
* **Equivariance Claim Qualification:** While the authors rightly point out the estimator is equivariant, it's worth noting that this property is contingent on the accurate estimation of the self-normalized term.  Any biases in that estimate could re-introduce non-equivariance issues.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that addresses an important problem with a well-motivated and theoretically sound solution. The empirical results convincingly demonstrate the benefits of the proposed self-normalized estimator. While there are some minor weaknesses, the novelty, clarity, and significance of the work make it a valuable contribution to the field. 

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* Addresses a significant and previously unrecognised problem in BLBF.
* Provides a theoretically grounded solution with compelling empirical support.
* Clear and well-written presentation.
* Has practical implications for real-world applications.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* Computational complexity could be a barrier to adoption.
* Limited scope of experiments.
* Parameter sensitivity needs further exploration.

**Rating:**

**Strong Accept (8/10)**

I believe this paper is of high quality and is suitable for presentation at NIPS. The contributions are significant, and the paper is well-executed. Addressing the minor weaknesses outlined above would further strengthen the work.
