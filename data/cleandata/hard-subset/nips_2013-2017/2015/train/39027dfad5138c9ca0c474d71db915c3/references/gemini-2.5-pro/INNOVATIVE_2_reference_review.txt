### Summary

This paper addresses the problem of batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF). The authors identify a novel and critical failure mode of existing methods based on Counterfactual Risk Minimization (CRM), which they term "Propensity Overfitting". This issue arises because the standard inverse propensity score (IPS) estimator is not equivariant to shifts in the loss function and is unbounded. Consequently, an empirical risk minimizer can achieve an artificially low risk estimate not by fitting the true loss, but by manipulating the propensity weights—for instance, by assigning high probability to observed actions for negative losses or avoiding them for positive losses. To solve this, the paper proposes replacing the standard IPS estimator with the self-normalized importance sampling (SNIS) estimator. This ratio estimator is provably equivariant and bounded, directly resolving the anomalies that lead to propensity overfitting. The authors embed this new estimator within the CRM framework to create a new algorithm, Norm-POEM, and demonstrate through extensive experiments that it is substantially more robust and achieves significantly better generalization performance than its predecessor.

### Strengths

1.  **High Novelty and Conceptual Contribution:** The primary strength of this paper is the identification and formalization of "Propensity Overfitting." This is a subtle, non-obvious, yet fundamental problem in counterfactual learning that, to my knowledge, has not been explicitly articulated before. Distinguishing it from standard "Loss Overfitting" provides the community with new vocabulary and a deeper understanding of the pathologies of learning with importance sampling. This is a significant conceptual contribution that reframes how we should think about the stability of counterfactual learning objectives.

2.  **Elegant and Principled Solution:** The proposal to use the self-normalized estimator is a bold and elegant solution. While SNIS is a known tool in the Monte Carlo literature, its application here as a *learning objective* to cure a specific overfitting pathology is highly novel. The solution is not an ad-hoc fix but is deeply principled, directly addressing the identified lack of equivariance and boundedness in the standard estimator. This demonstrates a strong command of the underlying statistical principles.

3.  **Significant Practical Impact:** The problem the paper tackles is not merely a theoretical curiosity. The experiments compellingly demonstrate that the standard CRM approach (POEM) can fail catastrophically, producing models that are worse than random guessing under simple, plausible loss transformations (Table 2). In contrast, Norm-POEM is shown to be robust and consistently high-performing. Given the increasing importance of learning from logged data in real-world systems (e.g., recommenders, search), this work provides a crucial correction and a much more reliable tool for practitioners.

4.  **Clarity and Quality of Exposition:** The paper is exceptionally well-written. The authors use a simple yet powerful illustrative example (Example 1) to build intuition for propensity overfitting. The theoretical motivation is clear, and the logical flow from problem identification to the proposed solution is seamless. The experiments are well-designed and the results are presented clearly, strongly supporting the paper's claims.

### Weaknesses

While the paper is strong, there are a few points that could be further developed:

1.  **Analysis of the Optimization Objective:** The resulting Norm-POEM objective is non-convex. The paper pragmatically uses L-BFGS for optimization and achieves excellent results. However, a deeper analysis of the optimization landscape, even if preliminary, would strengthen the paper. For instance, does the self-normalization introduce or alleviate issues like local minima compared to the standard objective?

2.  **Theoretical Guarantees of the CRM Bound:** The paper adapts the CRM principle by plugging in the self-normalized estimator and an approximate variance term derived via the delta method. While this is a standard and reasonable approach, the original CRM generalization bounds rely on properties of the unbiased IPS estimator. A more formal theoretical treatment deriving a new bound for the self-normalized case, or at least a discussion of the theoretical implications of using a biased (though consistent) estimator, would make the work more complete. However, given the strength of the core idea and the empirical results, this is a minor point that does not detract from the paper's main contribution.

### Recommendation

**Accept**

This paper presents a clear, novel, and significant contribution to the field of counterfactual learning. The identification of "Propensity Overfitting" is a genuine insight that advances our understanding of the challenges in this domain. The proposed solution, using the self-normalized estimator, is both elegant in its simplicity and powerful in its effect. The work is a perfect example of identifying a fundamental flaw in an existing framework and offering a principled, robust, and empirically superior solution. This is not an incremental improvement; it is a critical correction that will likely influence future work in off-policy learning and evaluation. The paper is bold in its claims and backs them up with convincing evidence. It is exactly the kind of high-impact, conceptually-driven work that NIPS should champion.
