Summary
The paper studies batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF). It argues that the commonly used inverse propensity scoring (IPS) risk estimator can cause a new failure mode, “propensity overfitting,” when used in empirical risk minimization (ERM)/CRM. The authors propose using a multiplicative control variate, yielding the self-normalized (aka weighted) importance sampling estimator (SN-IPS) for risk, and incorporate it into the CRM framework to form Norm-POEM. They give a consistency result and an approximate variance estimator for the ratio estimator, and empirically claim improved generalization and robustness over POEM on multi-label datasets generated via the supervised→bandit protocol. They also use Ŝ(h) (the sum of normalized weights) as a diagnostic for propensity overfitting.

Relation to prior work: Importance weighting, weight clipping, control variates, and self-normalized IS are classic (Hesterberg 1995; Owen; Rubinstein & Kroese), and importance weighting with control variates/doubly robust estimators are standard in off-policy evaluation (Dudík et al. 2011). The work’s main novelty is to name and illustrate “propensity overfitting,” to argue that SN-IPS alleviates it, and to plug SN-IPS into CRM.

Strengths
- Clear identification and illustration (Example 1) of a failure mode of IPS-based ERM tied to weight degeneracy; the diagnostic Ŝ(h) is sensible and informative.
- Using SN-IPS brings boundedness and translation equivariance, which directly addresses anomalies of IPS-based empirical risk; the convex-combination property is desirable.
- Consistency of SN-IPS is correct and well-known; the paper emphasizes the practical implication in BLBF.
- Integrating SN-IPS into CRM (Norm-POEM) is natural, and empirical results (as described) suggest consistent gains over POEM.
- Discussion of variance regularization within CRM is appropriate; the approximate variance for ratio estimators is standard.

Weaknesses
1) Novelty and positioning:
- The core estimator (SN-IPS) and its benefits are standard; the use of multiplicative control variates and normalization to mitigate weight variance is well-established. The paper needs to more clearly delineate what is new beyond (i) articulating propensity overfitting and (ii) plugging SN-IPS into CRM. Related ideas in off-policy evaluation (weight stabilization, normalized estimators, defensive mixtures) are not thoroughly connected.
- The claim that SN “resolves all problems” of IPS is overstated. SN-IPS can still be high variance, and can still behave poorly when denominator sums are small; it reduces, but does not eliminate, weight degeneracy.

2) Theory is limited:
- Beyond a restatement of the CRM bound for clipped IPS (Theorem 1) from prior work and a trivial strong consistency claim for SN-IPS (Theorem 2), there is no finite-sample analysis for Norm-POEM. If the contribution is to use SN-IPS within CRM, a matching generalization bound (ratio concentration, e.g., via empirical process theory for self-normalized estimators) is needed to justify the objective and regularizer. The approximate variance (Eq. 8) via normal/delta method is standard but unquantified; no finite-sample risk bounds or high-probability controls are provided.
- Clipping in the SN estimator is mentioned but not formally defined. Is the algorithm optimizing a clipped normalized estimator R̂SN,M(h) (clipping in numerator and denominator)? How does this interact with the variance estimate and CRM penalty? No formal conditions ensure existence of variance and stability when using clipping.

3) Experimental shortcomings:
- Comparisons are only to POEM (and an unregularized SN baseline). Stronger baselines are missing: clipped IPS with tuned M; doubly robust (DR) and regression estimators; normalized/weighted DR; defensive mixture or weight stabilization. Without these, it is difficult to assess practical significance.
- Tables and figures are referenced (Table 1, Table 2, Table 3, Figure 1), but they are missing from the submission text provided. Claims like “POEM fails catastrophically (worse than random)” and “surprisingly faster runtime” are unsubstantiated without numbers, confidence intervals, and ablations.
- Experimental protocol details are insufficient: exact hyperparameters, M and λ grids, initialization, number of L-BFGS iterations, stopping criteria, wall-clock times, and standard errors are not reported. The “replay training data 4 times” setting induces dependence; discuss whether/how this affects the i.i.d. assumptions in the variance estimates and CRM.
- The CRF hypothesis is stated, but the used model appears to factor per label (x ⊗ y with independent sigmoid/logistic), so “structured” aspects are limited. Please clarify.

4) Clarity, notation, and correctness issues:
- There are numerous typographical and mathematical inconsistencies:
  - Section 4: “δ(·, ·) ∈ [5,4]” is nonsensical; likely meant [−1,0] or [0,1].
  - Loss is referred to as negative-valued in places (e.g., Example 1) but later translated; settle on a consistent sign convention (a [0,1] loss avoids confusion).
  - Eq. (5) derivation for E[Ŝ(h)] mixes discrete/continuous notation and has incorrect integrals (∫1 Pr(xi)dxi). Please rewrite carefully using sums or well-defined densities.
  - Notation oscillates between h(x), h(y|x), h0(Y|x), U(Y), etc.; define and stick to a consistent notation.
  - Theorem references are vague (e.g., “Refer Theorem 1 of [1]…” without restating needed conditions).
  - Section 6: the objective is described as “non-convex and non-smooth,” yet the presented components are smooth; if non-smoothness arises from clipping, clarify and justify L-BFGS use.
- References are incomplete (missing years/venues/pages), and several entries are malformed.

5) Anonymity and reproducibility:
- The code link contains identifying information (a named Cornell URL), violating double-blind review.
- Without the missing tables/figures, code, and detailed hyperparameters, the results are not reproducible.

Questions for the authors
- Precisely define the training objective optimized: is the estimator in Eq. (7) clipped? If so, how? Do you normalize by the sum of clipped weights?
- Can you provide a finite-sample bound for the CRM objective with SN-IPS (e.g., a self-normalized Bernstein-type inequality for ratios)?
- How sensitive is Norm-POEM to M and λ? Provide ablations and CIs.
- Add baselines: clipped IPS, DR, and a regression-based estimator. How does Norm-POEM compare?
- Report actual numbers (test Hamming loss, Ŝ(h), run times), with error bars, and include the missing tables/figures.

Pros
- Highlights a real and under-appreciated failure mode of IPS-ERM; Ŝ diagnostic is useful.
- SN-IPS brings compelling practical properties (boundedness, translation equivariance).
- Conceptual integration into CRM is natural; empirical narrative suggests benefits.

Cons
- Limited novelty relative to well-known SN-IPS; theoretical development insufficient.
- Missing/unsupported empirical evidence; weak baselines.
- Numerous clarity/notation/reference issues; anonymity violation.

Recommendation: Reject (encourage resubmission after substantial revision)

Confidence: High (familiar with importance sampling, off-policy evaluation, and CRM)
