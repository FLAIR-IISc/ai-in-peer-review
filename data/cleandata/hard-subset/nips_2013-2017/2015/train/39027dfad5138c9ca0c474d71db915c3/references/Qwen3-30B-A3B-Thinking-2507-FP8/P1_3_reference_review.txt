

# Peer Review: "The Self-Normalized Estimator for Counterfactual Learning"

## Summary
This paper identifies a critical flaw in conventional counterfactual risk estimation for batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF), which is a fundamental problem in improving interactive systems like recommendation engines and ad platforms. The authors introduce the novel concept of "propensity overfitting," where the standard inverse propensity score estimator can produce arbitrarily low risk estimates due to the way it handles importance weights. They propose a self-normalized risk estimator that uses multiplicative control variates to address this issue, leading to a new learning algorithm called Norm-POEM. The paper makes a significant theoretical contribution by characterizing a previously unrecognized form of overfitting in counterfactual learning, and demonstrates empirically that their approach consistently outperforms existing methods across multiple datasets. This work builds on prior BLBF literature (e.g., POEM [1]) but fundamentally addresses a limitation that has been overlooked in previous approaches.

## Strengths

1. **Theoretical Novelty and Rigor**: The paper's identification of "propensity overfitting" as a distinct form of overfitting is highly original and well-justified. The theoretical analysis in Sections 4 and 5 is rigorous, demonstrating clear limitations of the conventional estimator and proving desirable properties of the self-normalized estimator (equivariance, boundedness, consistency).

2. **Practical Impact**: The authors have developed a practical algorithm (Norm-POEM) that directly addresses a real-world problem in deploying counterfactual learning systems. The experimental results (Section 7) are comprehensive and consistently show that Norm-POEM outperforms POEM across multiple datasets and experimental conditions.

3. **Clarity of Contribution**: The paper clearly distinguishes between "loss overfitting" (common in supervised learning) and "propensity overfitting" (the new problem), making the contribution easy to understand and appreciate. The theoretical and empirical evidence for the superiority of the self-normalized estimator is compelling.

4. **Thorough Experiments**: The experimental design is well-considered, using the supervised-to-bandit methodology from prior work [1] for fair comparison. The authors go beyond simple performance metrics to demonstrate that Norm-POEM avoids the propensity overfitting problem (Table 2) and that their estimator is unaffected by loss translations (as predicted by theory).

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited Theoretical Analysis of Norm-POEM**: While the paper provides a strong theoretical foundation for the self-normalized estimator, it doesn't provide a formal generalization bound for the Norm-POEM algorithm itself. The connection between the self-normalized estimator and the learning guarantees of the CRM principle could be strengthened with additional theoretical analysis.

2. **Computational Complexity Concerns**: The paper notes that the self-normalized estimator has a higher per-iteration computational cost than the standard estimator, though they observe faster convergence in practice. A more detailed analysis of the computational complexity tradeoffs would be valuable, especially for large-scale applications.

3. **Broader Context of Self-Normalized Estimators**: While the paper references the use of self-normalized estimators in financial applications and reinforcement learning, it doesn't sufficiently contrast with recent work on self-normalized importance sampling in other domains. A more thorough discussion of how this work relates to, say, the recent advances in importance sampling for reinforcement learning would strengthen the paper.

4. **Implementation Details**: The paper mentions that Norm-POEM converges faster than POEM, but doesn't provide sufficient detail on the optimization behavior. More information about the number of iterations required for convergence or the specific properties of the optimization landscape would be helpful.

## Recommendation

This paper makes a significant contribution to the field of counterfactual learning by identifying a previously unrecognized form of overfitting and providing a theoretically grounded solution. The problem is important, the solution is novel, and the empirical results are convincing. The paper is well-written, clearly structured, and makes a strong case for the superiority of the self-normalized estimator.

The weaknesses identified are not substantial enough to outweigh the paper's strong theoretical contribution and empirical validation. The paper clearly advances the state of the art in BLBF and should be accepted at NIPS.

## Arguments for Acceptance
- Identifies and solves a previously unrecognized problem in counterfactual learning (propensity overfitting)
- Theoretical analysis is rigorous and well-supported
- Empirical results consistently show superior performance over existing methods
- Clear distinction between conventional overfitting and the new form of overfitting
- Practical, implementable solution with clear benefits for real-world applications

## Arguments Against Acceptance (Minimal)
- Slightly limited theoretical analysis of the Norm-POEM algorithm itself
- Could provide more detailed computational complexity analysis
- Broader context of self-normalized estimators could be expanded

This paper represents a solid contribution that advances our understanding of counterfactual learning. The problem is well-motivated, the solution is novel, and the empirical validation is comprehensive. The authors have made a clear and important contribution to the field that will likely influence future work in counterfactual learning and batch learning from bandit feedback.
