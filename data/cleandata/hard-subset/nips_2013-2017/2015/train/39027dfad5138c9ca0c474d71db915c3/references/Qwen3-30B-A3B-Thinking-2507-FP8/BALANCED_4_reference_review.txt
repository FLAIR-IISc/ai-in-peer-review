

# Review of "The Self-Normalized Estimator for Counterfactual Learning"

## Summary
This paper addresses a critical issue in batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF), a key problem in interactive systems like recommendation engines and ad platforms. The authors identify a previously unrecognized problem they term "Propensity Overfitting" that plagues conventional counterfactual risk estimators used in Empirical Risk Minimization (ERM) for BLBF. They propose a Self-Normalized Risk Estimator using multiplicative control variates to resolve this issue, derive a new learning algorithm (Norm-POEM), and demonstrate its superiority through theoretical analysis and empirical evaluation. This work builds upon the CRM principle established in prior NIPS papers (e.g., Swaminathan and Joachims, ICML 2015), but makes a significant contribution by identifying and solving a novel problem that was not previously recognized in the BLBF literature.

## Strengths

1. **Novel Problem Identification**: The paper's most significant contribution is the identification of Propensity Overfitting as a previously unrecognized phenomenon in BLBF. The authors provide compelling theoretical arguments and examples demonstrating how conventional estimators can produce arbitrarily biased risk estimates, which is a serious issue for practical applications.

2. **Theoretical Rigor**: The paper provides strong theoretical justification for the proposed estimator, including proofs of its desirable properties (equivariance, boundedness) and consistency. The connection to control variates and self-normalized importance sampling is well-motivated and properly contextualized.

3. **Comprehensive Evaluation**: The experimental section is thorough, comparing Norm-POEM against POEM across multiple datasets (Scene, Yeast, TMC, LYRL) using the established Supervised 7→Bandit methodology. The results consistently show Norm-POEM outperforms POEM, with additional evidence that the improvement is directly linked to avoiding Propensity Overfitting (e.g., Ŝ(h) values close to 1 for Norm-POEM).

4. **Practical Impact**: The paper addresses a real-world problem with immediate practical implications for companies that rely on logged bandit feedback to improve their interactive systems. The proposed method is implemented and available, making it accessible for practitioners.

## Weaknesses

1. **Incremental Nature of Contribution**: While the identification of Propensity Overfitting is novel, the core estimator (self-normalized importance sampling) has been used in other contexts (e.g., finance, reinforcement learning). The paper could better clarify how this application to BLBF differs from prior uses of the technique.

2. **Limited Theoretical Comparison**: The paper doesn't provide theoretical bounds on the generalization error of Norm-POEM compared to POEM. While they show the estimator avoids certain anomalies, a formal comparison of the generalization performance would strengthen the theoretical contribution.

3. **Computational Efficiency Claim**: The claim that "Norm-POEM is surprisingly faster than POEM" needs more justification. The normalization step should increase computational cost, so the authors should elaborate on why the reduced number of iterations outweighs this cost.

4. **Lack of Discussion on Limitations**: The paper doesn't address how the method behaves when the logging policy h0 does not have full support over Y (a common scenario in practice), which would weaken the theoretical guarantees.

## Overall Assessment

This paper makes a valuable contribution to the BLBF literature by identifying and solving a previously unrecognized problem. The theoretical analysis is sound, the empirical evaluation is comprehensive, and the practical implications are significant. The paper advances the state of the art in counterfactual learning by providing a more robust estimator that consistently outperforms existing methods.

While the contribution is somewhat incremental (building on existing importance sampling techniques), the identification of Propensity Overfitting is a significant insight that addresses a real problem in the field. The paper is well-written, clearly structured, and provides sufficient detail for reproducibility.

## Recommendation

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions. The paper addresses an important problem with a well-motivated solution and provides strong empirical validation. To strengthen the paper, the authors should:

1. More clearly distinguish their contribution from prior uses of self-normalized importance sampling in other domains.
2. Provide a more detailed explanation for the computational efficiency claim.
3. Discuss the limitations of their approach when the logging policy has limited support.
4. Add a brief theoretical comparison of the generalization error bounds for Norm-POEM versus POEM.

These revisions would further enhance the paper's contribution and make it an even stronger addition to the NIPS proceedings. The work has clear significance for both theoretical understanding and practical application of counterfactual learning in real-world interactive systems.
