Summary
The paper targets batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF) and identifies a failure mode of inverse propensity weighting (IPS) when used as a training objective: Propensity Overfitting. The authors show that ERM on IPS (even with clipping) can exploit fluctuations in importance weights, yielding degenerate, non-equivariant, and unbounded empirical risks, which leads to severe overfitting unrelated to the loss signal. They propose using a multiplicative control variate to form a self-normalized importance sampling (SNIS) estimator for counterfactual risk. The resulting self-normalized risk estimator is bounded and equivariant to additive shifts of the loss, and is strongly consistent. They integrate this estimator into the CRM principle, deriving Norm-POEM, which minimizes the SNIS risk plus an empirical variance penalty. Empirically, Norm-POEM improves robustness and generalization over POEM across multiple multilabel datasets under a standard supervised→bandit protocol, and is less sensitive to loss translations. The paper also discusses optimization considerations and provides code.

Relation to prior work
SNIS (a.k.a. weighted importance sampling) is classic in Monte Carlo and RL off-policy evaluation; its lower variance relative to IPS when weights dominate is known. Doubly-robust estimators and clipping are standard alternatives. The contribution here is to: (1) crisply diagnose propensity overfitting as a learning-time pathology of IPS objectives; (2) argue that SNIS directly addresses it; and (3) show that plugging SNIS into CRM yields a practical algorithm with better behavior. This is a concrete and useful reframing for BLBF/CRM.

Strengths
- Conceptual clarity and novelty in identifying “propensity overfitting” as distinct from loss overfitting. The example is compelling and explains puzzling practical failures of IPS-based training.
- Clean estimator choice with desirable properties: boundedness and equivariance eliminate pathological ERM incentives; strong consistency holds under standard assumptions.
- Practical algorithmic impact: Norm-POEM is a small change to POEM with substantial robustness gains. The variance-regularized objective is well-motivated, and the variance estimator is standard for ratio estimators.
- Empirical validation indicates consistent improvements across datasets and training sizes, reduced sensitivity to loss translation, and better-calibrated sum of weights Ŝ(h)≈1.
- Good positioning within IS/OPE literature; acknowledges control variates, clipping, and doubly robust lines, and connects to CRM generalization bounds.

Weaknesses
- Incremental at some level: SNIS is long-standing and widely used for off-policy evaluation and, in RL, for more stable policy improvement. The main novelty lies in clearly articulating propensity overfitting and formalizing SNIS within CRM as a training objective. A stronger theoretical story (e.g., generalization bounds specific to SNIS-CRM) would elevate the contribution.
- Limited baselines: The experiments mostly compare against POEM. Missing are strong off-policy learning baselines such as doubly robust (DR) training objectives, clipped-IPS with tuned clipping, or weighted DR. Without these, it is hard to disentangle how much of the gain is due to normalization vs the presence/strength of variance regularization.
- Variance estimation and optimization: The variance term for SNIS uses an approximate normal/delta-method variance for ratio estimators and requires clipping for finite variance. While standard, a discussion of sensitivity to M and λ and ablations would be useful. Also, using a coupled ratio objective complicates stochastic optimization; the reliance on L-BFGS is reasonable but might not scale or handle non-convexity in other settings.
- Scope of evaluation: Only supervised→bandit simulations on multilabel data are reported. No results on real logged policies or on settings with severe logging mismatch, and no analyses under model misspecification or label noise. This limits external validity.
- Theorem 2 is straightforward (SLLN); more substantive theory (e.g., excess risk bounds under SNIS-CRM) is absent.

Questions for the authors
- How does Norm-POEM compare to doubly-robust learning objectives, including their self-normalized variants? Can you add WDR comparisons?
- Can you provide sensitivity plots over M (clipping) and λ, and, ideally, a principled way to tune M?
- How stable is L-BFGS optimization as the output space grows? Any evidence on larger, structured spaces?
- Could you quantify the bias-variance trade-off introduced by normalization in the training objective (not just evaluation), possibly via small-sample simulations?
- Are there scenarios where SNIS hurts (e.g., when δ dominates weight variability), and how does CRM handle that?

Minor comments
- Please complete/correct some references (years/proceedings are missing) and consider citing older RL work on weighted/normalized importance sampling for policy improvement (e.g., Precup et al., “Off-Policy Evaluation…”, and related WIS-based policy search).
- Clarify whether normalization uses per-dataset or mini-batch sums, and the implications for scalability.

Broader impact and significance
The diagnosis of propensity overfitting and the normalization-based fix are simple, persuasive, and likely to influence how practitioners train with logged bandit feedback. Despite using a known estimator, the paper advances the field by reframing a core pathology and offering a practical, theoretically motivated remedy that plugs into CRM. This is exactly the kind of conceptually bold but implementable idea that can change practice.

Recommendation: Weak accept
- Pros: Clear articulation of a new failure mode; principled, effective fix; consistent empirical gains; likely practical impact in BLBF and off-policy learning.
- Cons: The estimator is well-known; missing comparisons to DR/WDR; limited theoretical development beyond consistency; evaluation scope could be broader.

Confidence: High.
