### Summary of the Paper

This paper addresses the problem of Batch Learning from Logged Bandit Feedback (BLBF), where a new policy must be learned from a fixed dataset generated by a different, historical policy. The standard approach, known as Counterfactual Risk Minimization (CRM), involves minimizing an unbiased risk estimator based on Inverse Propensity Scoring (IPS). The authors identify a critical flaw in this approach, which they term "Propensity Overfitting." This phenomenon occurs when the learning algorithm minimizes the estimated risk not by finding a policy that incurs low loss, but by manipulating the propensity weights themselves—either by assigning high probability to points in the training set (for negative losses) or by avoiding them (for positive losses). This leads to severely biased risk estimates and poor generalization.

To solve this problem, the authors propose replacing the standard IPS estimator with the Self-Normalized Importance Sampling (SNIS) estimator. This estimator, framed as an application of multiplicative control variates, is a ratio of the standard IPS estimator and the sum of the importance weights. The paper demonstrates that the SNIS estimator is equivariant to translations in the loss function and its estimates are bounded by the range of the loss, thereby inherently preventing the anomalies that cause Propensity Overfitting. Based on this improved estimator, the paper develops a new learning algorithm called Norm-POEM, which adapts the CRM principle to the self-normalized case. The authors provide strong empirical evidence on several multi-label classification datasets, showing that Norm-POEM consistently and substantially outperforms its predecessor (POEM) and is robust to variations in the loss function.

This work builds directly upon previous research in BLBF, particularly the CRM framework introduced by Swaminathan and Joachims (2015). It also connects to a broad literature on off-policy evaluation in reinforcement learning and variance reduction techniques in Monte Carlo methods. While the self-normalized estimator is a known technique in statistics (e.g., Hesterberg, 1995; Owen, 2013), this paper's primary contribution is to identify Propensity Overfitting as a key failure mode in the *learning* setting and to demonstrate that the self-normalized estimator provides an elegant and effective solution.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The diagnosis of the Propensity Overfitting problem is insightful and well-supported by the clear and convincing Example 1. The proposed solution is principled, drawing from established statistical methods (control variates). The claims are strongly supported by a comprehensive set of experiments that are well-designed and directly test the central hypotheses of the paper (e.g., robustness to loss translation, control of the sum of propensities Ŝ(h)). The authors are honest about the limitations, for instance, noting that the variance estimator for Norm-POEM does not lend itself to stochastic optimization. The work is a complete and well-executed piece of research. A minor weakness in the theoretical analysis is the lack of a finite-sample generalization bound for the proposed Norm-POEM objective, analogous to Theorem 1 for the original CRM. While Theorem 2 establishes consistency, a finite-sample analysis would provide a more rigorous justification for the proposed CRM-style objective.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The motivation for the work is established clearly in the introduction. The core problem, Propensity Overfitting, is explained with remarkable clarity through a simple but powerful example. The transition from identifying the problem to proposing the solution via control variates is logical and easy to follow. The experimental setup and results are presented transparently, allowing for a clear interpretation of the outcomes. The level of detail provided should be sufficient for an expert to understand and potentially reproduce the work.

**Originality:** The primary original contribution is the identification, naming, and analysis of "Propensity Overfitting" as a distinct challenge in counterfactual learning. While overfitting is a general concept, this paper articulates a specific mechanism unique to learning with importance weights. Although the self-normalized estimator is not new, its application to solve this particular learning pathology is novel and insightful. The paper does an excellent job of placing its contributions in the context of related work in BLBF, off-policy evaluation, and importance sampling, making it clear how it differs from and builds upon previous contributions.

**Significance:** The results presented are highly significant for the field of counterfactual learning and off-policy evaluation. Propensity Overfitting is likely a general and previously under-appreciated problem that affects any method relying on ERM with a standard IPS estimator. The proposed solution, Norm-POEM, is not only effective but also conceptually simple and relatively easy to implement, making it likely to be adopted by researchers and practitioners. The paper demonstrates a substantial improvement over the prior state-of-the-art (POEM) on multiple datasets. By providing a more robust estimator, this work makes learning from logged bandit feedback more reliable and practical, advancing the state of the art in a demonstrable way.

### Arguments For and Against Acceptance

**Arguments for acceptance:**

1.  **Novel Conceptual Contribution:** The paper identifies and clearly formalizes "Propensity Overfitting," a fundamental and previously overlooked problem in counterfactual risk minimization. This provides a new and important perspective on the challenges of off-policy learning.
2.  **Effective and Principled Solution:** The proposed self-normalized estimator is a well-motivated and elegant solution that provably corrects the anomalies of the standard IPS estimator.
3.  **Strong Empirical Validation:** The experiments are thorough and provide compelling evidence that the proposed method, Norm-POEM, significantly outperforms the previous state-of-the-art and is robust to experimental conditions where the standard approach fails catastrophically.
4.  **High Clarity and Impact:** The paper is exceptionally well-written, making a complex topic accessible. The findings are significant and likely to have a direct impact on how researchers and practitioners approach learning from logged bandit feedback.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

1.  **Limited Theoretical Novelty:** The self-normalized estimator is a well-known tool in the statistics and Monte Carlo literature. Furthermore, the paper does not provide a finite-sample generalization bound for the new learning objective, relying instead on consistency arguments and analogy to the original CRM principle.
2.  **Potential Computational Scalability Issues:** The proposed objective is not amenable to stochastic gradient descent, which may limit its applicability to extremely large-scale problems. While the authors report faster convergence in practice with L-BFGS, this trade-off could be a concern.

Overall, the arguments for acceptance are substantially stronger. The conceptual insight into the nature of overfitting in this domain and the demonstration of a robust, practical solution represent a significant contribution to the field.
