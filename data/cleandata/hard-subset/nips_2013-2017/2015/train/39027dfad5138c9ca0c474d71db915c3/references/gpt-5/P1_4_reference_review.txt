Summary
The paper addresses batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF), where only bandit feedback from a historical policy is available. It diagnoses a failure mode of inverse propensity scoring (IPS) when used inside empirical risk minimization (ERM)/Counterfactual Risk Minimization (CRM): propensity overfitting, where the hypothesis manipulates importance weights rather than modeling loss, leading to degenerate, non-equivariant, and unbounded empirical risks. The authors propose replacing the standard IPS estimator with the self-normalized (weighted) importance sampling estimator (SNIPS), treating the sum of weights as a multiplicative control variate. They show SNIPS is bounded and translation-equivariant, and provide a strong consistency statement. They then instantiate CRM with SNIPS and an approximate variance estimator to obtain a new learning algorithm, Norm-POEM, for structured prediction (CRF-like hypothesis class). Experiments on multilabel datasets show improved generalization, robustness to loss translation, avoidance of propensity overfitting (measured via the sum of weights), and competitive runtime.

Relation to prior work
The work builds on CRM/POEM for BLBF, importance sampling with clipping, and off-policy evaluation (OPE). The self-normalized estimator is well-known in IS/OPE (a.k.a. weighted IS), and multiplicative control variates are standard. The paper’s main novelty is recognizing and formalizing “propensity overfitting” within BLBF/CRM and integrating SNIPS into the learning objective with a variance regularizer. Related OPE techniques such as doubly robust (DR), weighted DR, and recent high-confidence and minimax approaches are cited, though not empirically compared.

Strengths
- Clear and compelling diagnosis of propensity overfitting with simple examples, including invariance issues and unboundedness of IPS in ERM.
- Use of SNIPS as a principled, simple fix that directly targets the identified pathology; good intuition via control variates.
- Theoretical properties that directly address the anomalies: boundedness, translation equivariance, strong consistency.
- Practical algorithm (Norm-POEM) that can be dropped into the CRM framework; variance regularization remains, with a reasonable approximate variance formula for the ratio estimator.
- Empirical results consistently show improved generalization vs. POEM across datasets and training set sizes, and demonstrate that SNIPS mitigates propensity overfitting (sum of weights close to 1) and is robust to loss translation.
- Useful discussion of optimization behavior and runtime in practice.

Weaknesses
- Theory for learning with SNIPS under CRM is incomplete. The paper reuses prior CRM bounds (for clipped IPS) but does not provide a finite-sample generalization bound tailored to the SNIPS objective and its estimator-specific variance, nor a uniform deviation bound for the ratio estimator over H. The strong consistency of the estimator is not a substitute for learning guarantees.
- The variance estimate used (normal/delta-method approximation) is heuristic; no analysis of its bias or concentration is provided. The objective is nonconvex and non-smooth, and while L-BFGS works empirically, convergence guarantees or diagnostics are not discussed.
- Experimental comparisons are limited. Key baselines missing include DR and its weighted variants (WDR), SWITCH/DR-like clipping heuristics, MAGIC, and other modern OPE learners that address variance/bias tradeoffs; even if not all are directly in the same BLBF setting, DR is very relevant. This weakens claims about superiority beyond POEM.
- Sensitivity analysis around clipping M and regularization λ is brief. Since clipping is used to bound variance and ensure well-behaved denominators, more systematic ablations would help, as would reporting S(h) distributions and the effect of M on bias/variance and accuracy.
- The logging policy is simulated (supervised to bandit) and only CRF-like multiclass multilabel datasets are considered. No real logged data or more challenging exploration regimes (e.g., very peaky propensities) are shown. Generality across policy stochasticity/quality is only briefly mentioned.
- Minor clarity issues: a few typos (e.g., δ(·, ·) ∈ [5,4] likely means [−1,0]); the connection to Weighted Importance Sampling nomenclature could be made explicit earlier; assumptions ensuring denominator stability (especially with clipping) could be stated more clearly.

Quality
Technically sound in motivation and estimator properties; the propensity overfitting diagnosis is convincing. However, learning-theoretic analysis specific to SNIPS-CRM is missing, and approximations in the variance term are not theoretically justified. The experiments are solid within scope but lack stronger baselines and broader settings.

Clarity
Generally well written and organized. The examples are excellent and the control variate perspective is pedagogical. Some derivations are deferred or heuristic; more detail on optimization and hyperparameter selection would help reproducibility. Make explicit that the proposed estimator is the standard weighted/self-normalized IS.

Originality
Moderate. SNIPS itself is classical; the main novelty is identifying “propensity overfitting” in BLBF/CRM and showing that SNIPS directly addresses it, plus the specific Norm-POEM objective and empirical validation. The conceptual contribution is valuable despite the estimator being known.

Significance
Practical significance is good: SNIPS is simple and appears to materially improve robustness in BLBF. The conceptual framing is likely to influence how the community designs counterfactual learners. The lack of stronger baselines and theory limits the strength of the claim but does not negate usefulness.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide a CRM-style generalization bound for the SNIPS objective (e.g., via ratio concentration inequalities or self-normalized concentration, or by analyzing a clipped, normalized estimator).
- Compare against DR/WDR and at least one recent OPE learner that blends direct modeling with IS (e.g., DR with clipping/switching).
- Report sensitivity to M and λ, distributions of Ŝ(h), and behavior when Ŝ is small; include ablations with more extreme logging policies (lower support).
- Clarify and correct typos; explicitly connect to Weighted IS terminology in OPE literature.
- Discuss scalability and provide guidance for large-scale optimization (e.g., mini-batch approximations to the ratio or stabilized objectives).

Pros for acceptance
- Clear problem diagnosis; simple, principled fix with strong intuition.
- Demonstrated empirical gains and robustness across datasets.
- Bounded and equivariant estimator addresses key anomalies of IPS in ERM.

Cons for acceptance
- Missing finite-sample learning theory tailored to SNIPS-CRM.
- Limited baselines (no DR/WDR) and only simulated logging data.
- Heuristic variance estimate and nonconvex optimization without guarantees.

Recommendation
Weak accept. The paper addresses a real and underappreciated failure mode and offers a simple, effective solution likely to be adopted, despite theoretical and experimental breadth limitations.

Confidence
7/10.
