

# Review: The Self-Normalized Estimator for Counterfactual Learning

## Summary of Main Ideas

This paper addresses a critical issue in batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF), a key problem for improving interactive systems like recommendation engines and ad platforms. The authors identify a previously unnoted form of overfitting called "Propensity Overfitting" that affects conventional counterfactual risk estimators (based on importance sampling) when used in Empirical Risk Minimization (ERM). This problem occurs because the unbiased risk estimator can produce arbitrarily low risk estimates for hypotheses that overrepresent or underrepresent certain actions in the logged data, regardless of the loss function transformation. The authors propose a Self-Normalized Risk Estimator using multiplicative control variates that resolves these anomalies and avoids Propensity Overfitting. They derive a new learning algorithm, Norm-POEM, that uses this estimator within the Counterfactual Risk Minimization (CRM) framework. The paper provides theoretical guarantees for the estimator's consistency and demonstrates empirical improvements over the baseline POEM algorithm across multiple datasets.

The work builds on prior NIPS contributions in BLBF (Swaminathan & Joachims, 2015), counterfactual estimation (Dudik et al., 2014), and importance sampling (Hesterberg, 1995), while making a novel contribution by identifying Propensity Overfitting as a distinct problem from the well-known Loss Overfitting in supervised learning.

## Strengths

**Quality:** The paper provides a rigorous theoretical analysis of Propensity Overfitting, including a clear example demonstrating the problem's severity. The authors prove that the proposed Self-Normalized estimator avoids the anomalies of the conventional estimator (Theorem 2) and is strongly consistent. The empirical evaluation is thorough, using multiple datasets from the LibSVM repository and the Supervised 7→Bandit methodology, with results consistently showing Norm-POEM outperforming POEM across various metrics. The paper is technically sound with well-supported claims.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-organized and clearly written. The problem statement, motivation, and theoretical contributions are presented logically. The distinction between Propensity Overfitting and Loss Overfitting is particularly well-explained, with concrete examples that make the problem accessible. The experimental setup is described in sufficient detail to allow reproduction, and the results are presented clearly through tables and figures.

**Originality:** The identification of Propensity Overfitting represents a significant conceptual contribution that differentiates this work from prior NIPS papers on BLBF. While self-normalized estimators have been used in other contexts (e.g., Hesterberg, 1995), their application to counterfactual learning in BLBF is novel. The paper explicitly contrasts their approach with prior work on control variates in importance sampling and demonstrates how their method resolves a previously unaddressed problem.

**Significance:** The problem of BLBF is highly significant for practical applications, and the proposed solution addresses a fundamental limitation of existing methods. The empirical results show consistent improvements across multiple datasets, suggesting the method could be broadly applicable. The computational efficiency of Norm-POEM (faster convergence than POEM) further enhances its practical significance, as the method can be applied to structured prediction problems with minimal computational overhead.

## Weaknesses

**Clarity:** The paper could provide more intuition about why Propensity Overfitting occurs specifically in the BLBF setting, beyond the example provided. While the example is effective, a more general explanation of the conditions that lead to this problem would strengthen the argument.

**Originality:** The paper could better distinguish their work from related approaches in causal inference. While they cite relevant work, a more explicit comparison to methods like propensity score matching (Rosenbaum & Rubin, 1983) would clarify the novelty within the broader causal inference literature.

**Significance:** The paper does not discuss potential limitations or failure cases of the proposed method. It would be valuable to understand whether the benefits of Norm-POEM hold under more challenging scenarios (e.g., highly skewed logging policies or extreme label imbalances).

## Pro and Con Acceptance Arguments

**Pro Acceptance:**
- Identifies and resolves a previously unnoted problem (Propensity Overfitting) that affects the reliability of counterfactual learning methods
- Provides strong theoretical guarantees for the proposed estimator
- Demonstrates consistent empirical improvements across multiple datasets and problem settings
- The method (Norm-POEM) is computationally efficient and can be applied to structured prediction problems
- Clear, well-organized presentation that makes the contribution accessible

**Con Acceptance:**
- Limited discussion of potential failure cases or limitations of the proposed method
- Could provide more detailed explanation of why Propensity Overfitting occurs in the BLBF context
- The connection to broader causal inference literature could be strengthened

## Overall Assessment

This paper makes a significant contribution to the field of batch learning from logged bandit feedback by identifying a previously unrecognized form of overfitting (Propensity Overfitting) and providing a theoretically grounded solution. The theoretical analysis is rigorous, the empirical evaluation is comprehensive, and the proposed method (Norm-POEM) demonstrates clear practical advantages over existing approaches. The paper is well-written, clearly organized, and makes a distinct contribution that advances the state of the art in counterfactual learning.

The paper addresses all four key criteria for NIPS submissions effectively: it is technically sound (Quality), clearly written (Clarity), presents a novel problem and solution (Originality), and solves a significant practical problem with demonstrable improvements (Significance). The work is substantially different from previous NIPS papers on BLBF and represents a meaningful advancement in the field.
