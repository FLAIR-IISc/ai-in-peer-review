This paper addresses the problem of batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF). The authors first identify a critical and previously overlooked failure mode of the standard counterfactual risk estimator (based on inverse propensity scoring, IPS) when used for empirical risk minimization (ERM). They term this failure mode "Propensity Overfitting," where the learning algorithm minimizes the estimated risk not by learning a good policy, but by manipulating the importance weights to produce arbitrarily low risk estimates. This is a fundamental issue distinct from standard overfitting on the loss values.

To solve this, the authors propose replacing the standard IPS estimator with the self-normalized importance sampling estimator. This estimator, framed as using the sum of importance weights as a multiplicative control variate, normalizes the risk estimate. The authors show that this self-normalized estimator is equivariant to shifts in the loss function and is always bounded within the range of the loss, thereby provably avoiding the anomalies that lead to Propensity Overfitting. They incorporate this new estimator into the Counterfactual Risk Minimization (CRM) framework to create a new learning algorithm, Norm-POEM. Extensive experiments on several datasets demonstrate that Norm-POEM significantly outperforms the previous state-of-the-art (POEM), and that this improvement is directly attributable to its robustness against Propensity Overfitting.

### Strengths

1.  **Major Conceptual Contribution:** The most significant strength of this paper is the identification and clear formalization of "Propensity Overfitting." This is a novel, insightful, and frankly, quite alarming observation about a widely used technique. It provides a crisp explanation for potential instabilities and poor performance when applying ERM to IPS-based objectives. Naming and diagnosing this problem is a substantial contribution in its own right and is likely to influence future work in off-policy learning and evaluation. This is precisely the kind of bold, foundational insight that the field needs.

2.  **Elegant and Principled Solution:** The proposed solution—employing the self-normalized estimator—is not an ad-hoc patch. It is a well-established technique from the Monte Carlo literature that, as the authors convincingly argue, is the perfect tool for the job. The connection they draw between the properties of this estimator (equivariance, boundedness) and the direct mitigation of Propensity Overfitting is elegant and compelling. It's a beautiful example of identifying a fundamental problem and applying a principled solution.

3.  **Strong and Illuminating Experiments:** The empirical evaluation is excellent. The authors don't just present a table showing their method has a lower error. They design experiments (e.g., translating the loss function) specifically to validate their core hypothesis. The results in Table 2 are particularly striking, showing that the baseline method (POEM) fails catastrophically under loss translation, while Norm-POEM's performance is unaffected. This provides direct, causal evidence that they have correctly identified and fixed the root problem.

4.  **High Significance and Impact:** This work challenges the standard practice of naively minimizing an unbiased risk estimate in BLBF settings. Given the simplicity and effectiveness of the proposed fix, the self-normalized estimator could and should become the default choice for this class of problems. The paper addresses a difficult and practical problem, advances the state of the art in a demonstrable way, and the ideas are highly likely to be used and built upon by other researchers.

### Weaknesses

While this is a strong paper, there are minor points that could be addressed:

1.  **Novelty of the Estimator:** The self-normalized estimator itself is not new. It is a well-known tool in the importance sampling and survey statistics literature. The authors are clear about this, but the paper's main novelty lies in diagnosing Propensity Overfitting and connecting it to this specific solution in the context of *learning*. A slightly more explicit framing of the contribution as the analysis of a learning failure mode and its novel resolution, rather than the invention of an estimator, would be even more precise.

2.  **Reliance on Simulated Data:** The experiments use the standard "Supervised -> Bandit" conversion protocol. This is a necessary and well-accepted methodology for performing controlled experiments where the ground truth is known. However, the ultimate test for methods in this domain is performance on real-world logged data from an interactive system. While not a reason to reject the paper, as the controlled experiments are very convincing, demonstrating the effect on a real-world problem would make the work unassailable.

3.  **Variance Approximation:** The paper uses a standard Taylor series/Delta method approximation for the variance of the ratio estimator to plug into the CRM objective. This is a reasonable and practical choice. However, a brief discussion on the quality of this approximation and its potential impact on the optimization would add completeness. Given the strong empirical results, this is a minor point, but one worth noting for practitioners.

### Recommendation: Strong Accept

This is an excellent paper and a clear accept for NIPS. Its primary contribution—the identification of Propensity Overfitting—is a novel and important concept that deepens our understanding of the pitfalls of counterfactual learning. The proposed solution is simple, elegant, and empirically shown to be highly effective. The paper is well-written, the arguments are clear, and the experiments are thoughtfully designed to support the central claims.

While one could point to the fact that the core estimator is not new or that the experiments are on converted datasets, these are minor quibbles in the face of the paper's significant conceptual contribution. This work prioritizes a bold new idea over incremental methodological refinement, and it succeeds admirably. It addresses a real and subtle problem with a principled solution, pushing the field forward in a meaningful way. This is exactly the kind of research that NIPS should champion.
