This paper addresses the important problem of batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF), focusing on a critical issue – propensity overfitting – that plagues conventional counterfactual risk estimators. The authors convincingly argue that existing estimators can be severely biased, leading to poor generalization, and propose a self-normalized risk estimator to mitigate this. The theoretical analysis, demonstrating the estimator’s properties, is well-presented, and the empirical evaluation provides strong support for the claims.

**Summary of the Paper & Relation to Prior Work:**

The paper builds upon the growing body of work on learning from logged data, framed as off-policy evaluation in reinforcement learning or domain adaptation. It correctly identifies the challenges of using importance sampling-based estimators (like those in [6, 5]) due to their susceptibility to high variance and, more critically, the newly identified propensity overfitting. The authors cleverly adapt a self-normalized estimator from the importance sampling literature [12, 19], demonstrating its effectiveness in addressing this overfitting phenomenon.  They also relate their work to broader concepts like control variates [16, 17], and Structural Risk Minimization [20], providing a strong context for their contribution.  The connection to POEM [1] is also very clear, and the paper positions Norm-POEM as an improvement over it.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty and Significance:** Identifying propensity overfitting is a significant contribution. This isn’t simply a variance issue; it's a fundamentally different failure mode for estimators in this setting. The proposed self-normalized estimator offers a principled solution.
* **Theoretical Rigor:**  The paper provides a solid theoretical foundation for the proposed estimator, including a theorem demonstrating its consistency. The discussion of equivariance is insightful and helps explain the benefits of the self-normalized approach.
* **Empirical Validation:**  The experimental results are compelling.  The consistent outperformance of Norm-POEM across multiple datasets, along with the diagnostic tests showing reduced bias in the estimator (Ŝ(ĥ) close to 1), strongly support the claims.
* **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the technical concepts. The examples (e.g., Example 1) are helpful for illustrating the problem of propensity overfitting.
* **Practicality:** The authors provide implementation details and show that the proposed method is computationally efficient and competitive with existing approaches.
* **Thoroughness:** The extensive comparison with POEM, and analysis of hyperparameter settings and runtime, demonstrates a careful and thorough investigation.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Assumptions:** The paper relies on the assumption of full support of the historical policy h0. While common in this literature, it is a strong assumption that may not always hold in practice.  A discussion of the implications of violating this assumption would be valuable.
* **Complexity of Variance Estimation:** While the authors acknowledge the computational challenges of the variance estimator in Norm-POEM, the explanation could be more detailed. Specifically, how do they handle scaling to larger datasets and hypothesis spaces?
* **Limited Exploration of Alternative Solutions:**  The paper focuses heavily on the self-normalized estimator. While justified given its effectiveness, a brief discussion of other potential approaches to mitigate propensity overfitting (e.g., different regularization techniques) could strengthen the argument.
* **Presentation of Results:** While the tables are comprehensive, some of the performance differences are relatively small. Visualizations (e.g., error bars) could help to better illustrate the statistical significance of the results.  More detailed analysis of *why* Norm-POEM is better on specific datasets would be valuable.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* Addresses a critical, previously unrecognised issue in BLBF.
* Provides a theoretically sound and empirically validated solution.
* Offers significant practical benefits, outperforming a strong baseline (POEM).
* Well-written and clearly presented.
* Has the potential to impact a wide range of applications.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* Relies on a strong assumption (full support of historical policy).
* Variance estimation in Norm-POEM could be a practical bottleneck for very large problems.
* Could benefit from a more thorough exploration of alternative mitigation strategies.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of learning from logged bandit feedback. The identification of propensity overfitting and the development of the self-normalized estimator are novel and important. The thorough theoretical analysis and empirical evaluation provide strong evidence for the effectiveness of the proposed approach. While there are some minor weaknesses, they do not detract significantly from the overall quality of the work.

**Recommendation:**

**Accept.** I believe this paper is worthy of acceptance to NeurIPS. The contribution is significant, the work is well-executed, and the paper is clearly written. The authors have addressed a key challenge in the field and provided a compelling solution. I encourage the authors to consider addressing the weaknesses outlined above in future work.
