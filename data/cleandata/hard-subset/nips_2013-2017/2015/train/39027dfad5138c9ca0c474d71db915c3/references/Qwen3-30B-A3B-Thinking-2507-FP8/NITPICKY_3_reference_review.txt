

# Review of "The Self-Normalized Estimator for Counterfactual Learning"

This paper introduces the concept of "propensity overfitting" in counterfactual learning from logged bandit feedback and proposes a self-normalized risk estimator to address it. The authors identify a novel issue where conventional importance sampling estimators can produce severely biased risk estimates for the empirical risk minimizer, and they demonstrate that their self-normalized estimator resolves this problem both theoretically and empirically. The paper builds on prior work on Counterfactual Risk Minimization (CRM) and proposes a new learning algorithm called Norm-POEM.

## Strengths
The paper makes a valuable contribution by identifying a previously overlooked problem in counterfactual learning. The theoretical analysis of propensity overfitting is well-constructed, and the proposed self-normalized estimator has clear advantages over the conventional approach, particularly in its equivariance property and bounded risk estimates. The empirical evaluation is thorough, with consistent improvements across multiple datasets and experimental settings. The paper is well-written and logically structured, with a clear progression from problem identification to theoretical solution to empirical validation.

## Weaknesses
While the paper presents a novel and potentially important contribution, I identify several technical inconsistencies and minor flaws that prevent me from recommending acceptance:

1. **Notational inconsistencies**: The paper uses inconsistent notation for the importance weights. In Equation (2), the estimator is defined as R̂(h) = 1/n ∑ δi h(yi|x)/pi, where pi = h0(yi|xi). However, in Equation (4), Ŝ(h) = 1/n ∑ h(yi|xi)/pi. This notation implies that h(yi|xi)/pi is the importance weight, but the paper never clearly defines this as the importance weight. This creates confusion about what the terms represent.

2. **Theoretical precision issues**: In the proof of Theorem 2, the authors state "The numerator of R̂SN(h) in (7) are i.i.d. observations with mean R(h)." This is inaccurate - the expected value of each term in the numerator is R(h), so the expected value of the numerator is nR(h), not R(h). This imprecision undermines the theoretical rigor of the paper.

3. **Ambiguous terminology**: The paper introduces "propensity overfitting" but fails to clearly distinguish it from other types of overfitting. The distinction between "loss overfitting" and "propensity overfitting" is not sufficiently clarified, making it difficult to assess the novelty of the problem.

4. **Citation issues**: The reference to Hesterberg's work [19] is incomplete - the citation in the paper states "Weighted average importance sampling and defensive mixture, Tim Hesterberg, distributions. Technometrics," which is clearly a formatting error. The correct citation should be "Weighted Average Importance Sampling and Defensive Mixture" by Tim Hesterberg in Technometrics (1988).

5. **Missing definitions**: The acronym "CRM" is used extensively without definition (it should be "Counterfactual Risk Minimization" or similar). Similarly, "SN" in "R̂SN(h)" is never defined as "Self-Normalized" in the paper.

6. **Experimental details**: The paper mentions "Supervised 7→ Bandit methodology" but fails to cite the relevant paper that describes this methodology. While this might be considered minor, it's important for reproducibility and context.

7. **Technical accuracy**: The paper claims "the self-normalized estimator is not unbiased" but provides no proof or explanation for this claim. A brief justification would strengthen the theoretical argument.

8. **Hyperparameter discussion**: The paper states "hyper-parameters λ, M were calibrated as recommended," but does not specify what "recommended" means or how they were calibrated. This lack of detail reduces reproducibility.

## Overall Assessment
This paper identifies a genuine issue in counterfactual learning and proposes a potentially valuable solution. The theoretical arguments are mostly sound, and the empirical results are compelling. However, the minor technical inconsistencies and imprecisions in notation, definitions, and citations are concerning for a NIPS submission. As a perfectionist reviewer, I believe these issues, while individually small, collectively undermine the paper's technical rigor and clarity.

The paper's contribution is significant enough that it would likely be accepted at a conference with less stringent standards, but for NIPS, where attention to technical detail is paramount, these flaws are unacceptable. The paper would benefit from careful proofreading to address the notational inconsistencies, clarify the theoretical arguments, and provide complete citations.

The paper is well-written and makes a valuable contribution to the field, but the technical inconsistencies prevent me from recommending acceptance. I would recommend rejection with the suggestion that the authors revise the paper to address the technical precision issues before resubmission.

## Recommendation
Reject
