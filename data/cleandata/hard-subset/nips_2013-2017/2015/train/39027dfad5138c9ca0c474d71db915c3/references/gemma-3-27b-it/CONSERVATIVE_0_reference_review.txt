This paper addresses the important problem of learning from logged bandit feedback, a common scenario in many real-world systems. The authors identify a novel issue – Propensity Overfitting – in standard counterfactual risk estimation and propose a Self-Normalized estimator to mitigate it. While the identified problem is interesting and the proposed solution is theoretically motivated, I have significant reservations about the paper’s overall contribution and the strength of the empirical validation.

The core idea of Propensity Overfitting, where the risk estimator can be minimized by overfitting to the propensity weights rather than the true reward, is presented clearly. The examples illustrating this phenomenon are helpful. However, the theoretical justification, while building on existing work in importance sampling and causal inference, feels somewhat incremental. The link to existing overfitting phenomena (Loss Overfitting) is alluded to, but not deeply explored, leaving the reader wondering whether this is truly a *new* form of overfitting or a manifestation of known issues in high-dimensional estimation.

The introduction of the Self-Normalized estimator is a reasonable approach given the identified problem, and the theoretical guarantee of consistency is reassuring. However, the claim that this estimator *resolves* all problems of the unbiased estimator feels overstated. The paper acknowledges that variance regularization remains necessary, indicating that the self-normalization alone isn't a complete solution. Furthermore, the benefits of self-normalization are well-known in importance sampling literature (references [12, 19, 22, 23] are relevant), raising questions about the novelty of applying it specifically to this BLBF context. 

My primary concern lies with the empirical evaluation. The experimental setup, mirroring that of POEM [1], is appreciated for consistency, but the datasets are relatively small and the performance gains, while consistently observed, appear modest. The claim that Norm-POEM is "surprisingly faster" than POEM is also weak without a detailed runtime analysis. The experiments primarily demonstrate that Norm-POEM avoids catastrophic failure modes of POEM, which isn't necessarily evidence of a substantial improvement in overall performance.  The analysis of Ŝ(h) confirms the theoretical predictions, but it doesn't provide insights into *why* Norm-POEM achieves better generalization. It simply confirms that it’s less prone to the identified overfitting problem.  I'm unconvinced that the reported speed improvements are solely attributable to a shorter ‖w‖; a more thorough profiling analysis would be needed to support this claim. 

The paper lacks a comparison to simpler baselines, such as directly thresholding propensity weights (as mentioned in the introduction) with careful cross-validation.  Demonstrating superiority over such a simple approach would strengthen the argument for the complexity of the self-normalized estimator. The performance of a standard CRF, acting as a “skyline”, is reported, but a more direct comparison with other off-policy evaluation methods, even if they are known to have limitations, would be valuable.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Identifies a novel issue (Propensity Overfitting) in batch learning from logged bandit feedback.
*   Proposes a theoretically sound solution (Self-Normalized estimator) with consistency guarantees.
*   Demonstrates consistent performance improvements over a specific baseline (POEM) across multiple datasets.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The identified overfitting issue might be a specific instance of more general phenomena in importance sampling.
*   The theoretical contribution feels incremental.
*   The empirical evaluation is limited in scope and lacks comparison to simpler baselines.
*   Performance gains are modest, and the speed improvements require further investigation.
*   The paper relies heavily on previous work (POEM) without providing a compelling justification for the added complexity of the self-normalized estimator.



**Overall Assessment:** Reject.

While the paper addresses a relevant problem and proposes a reasonable solution, the contributions are not substantial enough to warrant acceptance to NIPS. The theoretical novelty is limited, and the empirical validation, while positive, isn’t sufficiently compelling. The paper reads more like a technical report detailing an extension of existing work than a breakthrough contribution to the field. I would encourage the authors to expand the empirical evaluation, compare to more baselines, and provide a more in-depth analysis of the observed performance gains before resubmitting.
