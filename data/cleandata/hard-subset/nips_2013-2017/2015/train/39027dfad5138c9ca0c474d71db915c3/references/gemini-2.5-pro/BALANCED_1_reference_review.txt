### Summary and Relation to Prior Work

This paper addresses the problem of Batch Learning from Logged Bandit Feedback (BLBF), a crucial setting for improving interactive systems using historical data. The authors build upon the Counterfactual Risk Minimization (CRM) principle, a state-of-the-art approach for this problem introduced by Swaminathan & Joachims (2015). The core contribution is the identification of a critical failure mode in existing CRM methods that use the standard Inverse Propensity Score (IPS) estimator. The authors term this failure mode "Propensity Overfitting," where the learning algorithm minimizes the estimated risk not by finding a good policy, but by exploiting the structure of the propensity weights to produce an arbitrarily low, and highly biased, risk estimate.

To solve this, the authors propose replacing the standard IPS estimator with the Self-Normalized (SN) importance sampling estimator. This estimator, a well-known ratio estimator from the Monte Carlo literature (e.g., Hesterberg, 1995; Owen, 2013), normalizes the weighted losses by the sum of the importance weights. The paper demonstrates that this simple change resolves the anomalies of the IPS estimator: the SN estimator is bounded by the range of the observed losses and is equivariant to affine transformations of the loss function. The authors then instantiate the CRM principle with this new estimator and its corresponding variance approximation, resulting in a new learning algorithm called Norm-POEM. The paper's relation to prior work is well-established, correctly positioning itself as an improvement upon the POEM algorithm [1] and connecting its core technique to the broader statistics and reinforcement learning literature on importance sampling and control variates [6, 12, 19].

### Strengths

1.  **Clear and Compelling Problem Identification (Significance & Clarity):** The paper's primary strength is its sharp diagnosis of "Propensity Overfitting." The authors provide a simple yet powerful illustrative example in Section 4 that clearly demonstrates how Empirical Risk Minimization with the standard IPS estimator can lead to degenerate solutions. This is a subtle but fundamental problem that may not have been fully appreciated by the community, and this paper does an excellent job of crystallizing, naming, and analyzing it. This contribution is significant as it provides a clear warning and a path forward for practitioners and researchers in off-policy learning.

2.  **Principled and Elegant Solution (Quality & Originality):** The proposed solution—using the self-normalized estimator—is elegant, well-motivated, and directly addresses the identified issues. While the estimator itself is not novel, its application to the CRM framework for BLBF is. The authors provide sound theoretical arguments for its superiority in this context, highlighting its boundedness and equivariance properties, and prove its strong consistency. The derivation of the Norm-POEM algorithm by incorporating the SN estimator and its variance into the CRM objective is a logical and well-executed extension of prior work.

3.  **Thorough and Convincing Empirical Evaluation (Quality):** The experiments are well-designed and executed, following the established "Supervised-to-Bandit" protocol for reproducible research in this area. The results strongly support the paper's claims. Norm-POEM consistently and significantly outperforms its direct predecessor, POEM. Crucially, the experiments directly test the core hypotheses: Table 2 shows that Norm-POEM corrects the bias in the sum of weights (`Ŝ(h)`) and is robust to loss translation, a key failure mode for POEM. The findings on faster convergence and improved generalization across various datasets and data sizes are also compelling.

4.  **High-Quality Presentation (Clarity):** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow. The motivation is clear, the problem is explained intuitively, the solution is presented logically, and the experimental results are interpreted effectively. The structure of the paper guides the reader seamlessly from the problem's context to its solution and validation.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Limited Novelty of the Core Estimator:** The main technical tool, the self-normalized estimator, is a standard technique in the statistics and Monte Carlo literature. The paper is transparent about this, citing relevant work. However, this means the core technical novelty is in the application and analysis within the BLBF learning context, rather than the invention of a new statistical tool. While this does not diminish the paper's overall contribution, it is a point to consider when evaluating originality.

2.  **Limited Discussion on Alternatives:** The paper focuses on the self-normalized estimator as a solution. It would be beneficial to include a brief discussion comparing it to other advanced variance reduction techniques in the context of CRM. For instance, how would a CRM algorithm based on Doubly Robust (DR) estimators [6] fare against Propensity Overfitting? A DR estimator also uses a control variate (the regression model) and might be less susceptible to this issue. While a full empirical comparison is likely beyond the scope of this work, a short conceptual discussion would strengthen the paper by placing the proposed solution in a broader context of variance reduction methods.

### Summary of Arguments

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Identifies and clearly explains a fundamental and previously under-appreciated problem ("Propensity Overfitting") in a popular class of counterfactual learning algorithms.
*   Proposes a simple, principled, and highly effective solution that demonstrably fixes the problem.
*   The empirical results are strong, consistent, and directly support the paper's central claims, showing significant improvements over a state-of-the-art baseline.
*   The paper is very well-written and will be of high interest and practical value to researchers and practitioners in off-policy learning, reinforcement learning, and causal inference.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   The core statistical technique (the self-normalized estimator) is not new, which limits the paper's fundamental technical novelty.

### Decision and Constructive Feedback

This is a strong paper that makes a clear and significant contribution to the field of counterfactual learning. The identification and analysis of the Propensity Overfitting problem is a valuable insight, and the proposed Norm-POEM algorithm appears to be a robust and direct improvement over existing methods. While the core estimator is not new, its novel application and the rigorous demonstration of its benefits in this learning setting constitute a solid contribution worthy of publication at NeurIPS.

**Recommendation:** Accept.

**Suggestions for Improvement:**
*   As mentioned in the weaknesses, a brief discussion in the related work or conclusion about how other variance reduction techniques, particularly Doubly Robust estimators, might interact with the Propensity Overfitting problem would be insightful. This could help contextualize the contribution and point towards interesting future research directions (e.g., combining self-normalization with DR estimation).
*   The variance of the self-normalized estimator (Equation 8) is based on an approximation (e.g., the delta method). A comment on the quality or limitations of this approximation, especially for smaller `n`, could add nuance to the technical presentation.
*   The claim that Norm-POEM is computationally faster due to better convergence properties is interesting. If space permits, a plot showing the training objective vs. iterations for both POEM and Norm-POEM on one of the datasets would be a great way to visually substantiate this claim.
