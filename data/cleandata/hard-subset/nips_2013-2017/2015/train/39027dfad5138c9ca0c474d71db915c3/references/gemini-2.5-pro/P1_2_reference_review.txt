### Summary of the Paper

This paper addresses the problem of Batch Learning from Logged Bandit Feedback (BLBF), a crucial setting for improving interactive systems like recommenders and ad platforms using historical data. The authors identify a novel and critical failure mode in standard counterfactual learning methods that rely on Inverse Propensity Score (IPS) estimators. They term this problem "Propensity Overfitting," where a learning algorithm, in its attempt to minimize empirical risk, learns to exploit the propensity weights themselves rather than minimizing the true underlying loss. This can lead to degenerate solutions with arbitrarily poor risk estimates. For example, a model might learn to place all its probability mass on training examples (for negative losses) or avoid them entirely (for positive losses), manipulating the sum of importance weights to drive the estimated risk down.

To solve this, the authors propose replacing the standard unbiased IPS estimator with the Self-Normalized estimator. This estimator, which arises from using the sum of propensity weights as a multiplicative control variate, is a ratio of the weighted sum of losses to the weighted sum of probabilities. The authors show that this estimator is equivariant to translations in the loss function and is always bounded within the range of observed losses, thereby provably avoiding the anomalies that cause Propensity Overfitting. They incorporate this new estimator into the Counterfactual Risk Minimization (CRM) framework, leading to a new algorithm called Norm-POEM. Extensive experiments on several multi-label classification datasets, using a well-controlled "supervised-to-bandit" conversion, demonstrate that Norm-POEM significantly outperforms its predecessor (POEM) in terms of generalization performance and is robust to the issues that plague the standard IPS estimator.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Strengths:**

1.  **Clear Identification of a Fundamental Problem:** The core contribution of this paper is the identification and clear articulation of "Propensity Overfitting." This is a subtle but severe issue that can undermine counterfactual learning. The illustrative example in Section 4 is exceptionally clear and convincing, demonstrating how standard Empirical Risk Minimization (ERM) on an IPS-estimated risk can lead to pathologically bad solutions. Naming and formalizing this problem is a significant conceptual contribution to the field.
2.  **Principled and Elegant Solution:** The proposed solution is not an ad-hoc heuristic but is based on the well-established statistical technique of control variates. The resulting Self-Normalized estimator directly addresses the root cause of propensity overfitting by normalizing by the empirical sum of weights, Ŝ(h). The theoretical properties highlighted—equivariance and being bounded by the loss range—are precisely what is needed to fix the identified anomalies. The proof of consistency (Theorem 2) provides theoretical grounding.
3.  **Strong and Convincing Empirical Evaluation:** The experiments are thoughtfully designed and provide compelling evidence for the paper's claims.
    *   The "supervised-to-bandit" protocol allows for reliable evaluation of generalization performance.
    *   Norm-POEM consistently and significantly outperforms the baseline (POEM) across multiple datasets and data sizes (Table 1, Figure 1).
    *   Most importantly, the experiments in Table 2 directly test the central hypothesis. They show that Ŝ(h) for POEM is severely biased while it is stable for Norm-POEM, and that Norm-POEM is robust to loss translations while POEM's performance catastrophically degrades. This is a very strong piece of evidence.
    *   The authors also include a valuable ablation study (Table 3) showing that variance regularization from the CRM principle is still beneficial on top of the self-normalized estimator.
4.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is extremely well-written and easy to follow. The narrative flows logically from the background, to the identification of a problem, the proposal of a principled solution, and finally, a thorough empirical validation.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Limited Novelty of the Estimator Itself:** The self-normalized estimator is a known tool in the Monte Carlo and survey statistics literature. The paper's novelty lies in its application to diagnose and solve propensity overfitting in BLBF, not in the invention of the estimator. The authors are transparent about this, citing prior work, but it's worth noting.
2.  **Lack of Deeper Theoretical Analysis of the CRM Bound:** The paper adopts the CRM principle and its associated generalization bound (Theorem 1) from prior work, which was developed for an unbiased estimator. The self-normalized estimator is biased (though consistent). The paper does not theoretically analyze how this bias might affect the validity or tightness of the CRM generalization bound. While the empirical results are strong, a discussion on the theoretical implications of using a biased estimator within this specific bounding framework would have strengthened the paper.
3.  **Computational Considerations:** The authors note that their objective function is not amenable to stochastic gradient descent (SGD) due to the form of the variance estimator, requiring batch methods like L-BFGS. While they observe faster convergence in practice on their datasets, this could be a limitation for massive-scale problems where SGD is often the only feasible optimization method. A brief discussion on potential stochastic approximations could be valuable for practitioners.

### Arguments for and against acceptance

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Identifies and provides a clear explanation of a fundamental, previously unnamed problem ("Propensity Overfitting") in a widely used class of learning algorithms.
*   Proposes an elegant, principled, and highly effective solution.
*   The experimental results are comprehensive and strongly support the paper's claims, including targeted experiments that directly validate the diagnosis of the problem.
*   The paper is exceptionally clear and well-written, making a complex topic accessible.
*   The work is highly significant for anyone working on off-policy evaluation and learning, a problem of great practical importance. It is very likely to be influential.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The core statistical tool (the self-normalized estimator) is not new, though its application here is novel and insightful.
*   The theoretical analysis could be more complete, particularly regarding the interaction between the biased estimator and the CRM generalization bound framework.

### Overall Assessment and Recommendation

This is an excellent paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of counterfactual learning. The identification and clear explanation of "Propensity Overfitting" is a major strength and provides a valuable new lens through which to understand the challenges of learning from logged bandit data. The proposed solution, Norm-POEM, is simple, principled, and shown to be highly effective. The experimental validation is rigorous and compelling. The weaknesses are minor in comparison to the strengths. The paper is of high quality, clarity, originality, and significance. It addresses a difficult and important problem, advances the state of the art in a demonstrable way, and the insights are likely to be widely used by both researchers and practitioners.

**Recommendation: Accept**
