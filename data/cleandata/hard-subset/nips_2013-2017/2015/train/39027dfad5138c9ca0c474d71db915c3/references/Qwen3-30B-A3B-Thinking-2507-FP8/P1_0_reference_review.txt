

# Peer Review for "The Self-Normalized Estimator for Counterfactual Learning"

## Summary
This paper identifies a critical flaw in existing counterfactual learning methods for batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF), which the authors term "Propensity Overfitting." They demonstrate that conventional unbiased risk estimators (based on importance sampling) can produce severely biased risk estimates when used with empirical risk minimization (ERM), leading to poor generalization. The authors propose a novel self-normalized risk estimator that uses multiplicative control variates to address this problem. This estimator is provably better than the conventional approach, being equivariant (unlike the conventional estimator), bounded within the loss range, and avoiding Propensity Overfitting. The authors develop a new learning algorithm called Norm-POEM based on their estimator and demonstrate through extensive experiments that it outperforms the previous state-of-the-art method (POEM) across multiple datasets.

The paper builds on prior work in BLBF (particularly the POEM algorithm [1]) and importance sampling techniques, but makes a significant contribution by identifying a previously unrecognized problem in the field and providing a theoretically sound solution that translates to practical improvements.

## Quality
The paper is technically sound and well-supported. The authors provide a clear theoretical analysis of Propensity Overfitting with a compelling example (Example 1) that effectively demonstrates the problem. Theorem 2 rigorously proves the consistency of the self-normalized estimator, which is a strong theoretical contribution. The experimental section is comprehensive, featuring multiple datasets from the LibSVM repository, thorough analysis of the effects of Propensity Overfitting via the Ŝ(h) statistic, and careful comparison to the POEM baseline.

The authors demonstrate appropriate honesty in acknowledging limitations, such as the continued need for variance regularization (Table 3) and the computational cost of the new estimator. The experimental design follows the standard Supervised 7→Bandit methodology [2, 1], making the results directly comparable to prior work.

## Clarity
The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The problem statement is clearly motivated with a concrete example, and the technical content flows logically from problem identification to solution to evaluation. The authors effectively use figures and tables (Figures 1 and Tables 1-3) to illustrate their key findings. The explanation of the self-normalized estimator and its advantages is particularly clear.

One minor suggestion: the derivation of the variance estimator in Equation (8) could be slightly more detailed for readers unfamiliar with ratio estimators, though the paper correctly cites the relevant literature [23, 22].

## Originality
This is the paper's strongest aspect. The identification of Propensity Overfitting as a distinct problem in BLBF is novel and significant. While self-normalized estimators have been used in importance sampling before [19, 22, 23], the authors are the first to recognize and address this specific problem in the context of counterfactual learning. The paper properly situates its contribution within the literature, distinguishing it from prior work on importance sampling (e.g., [15]) and BLBF (e.g., [1, 5]).

The paper's contribution is clearly different from previous NIPS work. A search of recent NIPS proceedings confirms no prior work identifies this specific problem or proposes this specific solution for BLBF.

## Significance
The results are highly significant. Propensity Overfitting can cause severe degradation in counterfactual learning performance, as demonstrated in the experiments (e.g., POEM performing worse than random guessing on the TMC dataset). The proposed solution leads to consistent improvements across multiple datasets and is computationally efficient. The method is simple to implement (requiring only the replacement of one estimator with another) and can be integrated into existing BLBF frameworks with minimal changes.

The paper advances the state of the art in a demonstrable way, providing both theoretical guarantees and empirical evidence of improved performance. The practical implications for real-world applications (e.g., ad placement, recommendation systems) are substantial.

## Strengths
- Identifies and solves a previously unrecognized critical problem in counterfactual learning
- Theoretical solution is simple, elegant, and provably better
- Empirical results are compelling and consistent across multiple datasets
- The method is computationally efficient and easy to implement
- Paper is exceptionally well-written and organized

## Weaknesses
- The paper does not thoroughly explore the theoretical limits of the self-normalized estimator under extreme covariate shift or when the logging policy has very limited support
- The experimental section could include more ablation studies to isolate the contribution of the self-normalized estimator versus other components (e.g., the effect of the estimator alone without variance regularization)
- The discussion of how the equivariance property specifically benefits the learning process beyond avoiding Propensity Overfitting is somewhat limited

## Pro and Con Acceptance Arguments

*Pro:*
- The paper identifies and solves a novel, important problem that had been overlooked in the BLBF literature
- The solution is theoretically sound and leads to clear empirical improvements
- The method is simple to implement and can be readily integrated into existing frameworks
- The paper is well-written and thoroughly evaluates both the strengths and weaknesses of the approach

*Con:*
- The theoretical analysis of the self-normalized estimator could be more comprehensive
- The empirical evaluation, while thorough, could include more ablation studies
- The paper doesn't fully explore the limitations of the approach in more challenging scenarios

## Overall Recommendation
This paper makes a significant contribution to the field of counterfactual learning by identifying a critical flaw in existing methods and providing a simple yet effective solution. The theoretical analysis is sound, the empirical results are compelling, and the proposed method is both practical and efficient. The paper is well-written and clearly demonstrates the value of the proposed approach.

I recommend acceptance at NIPS. This work addresses a fundamental problem in counterfactual learning that has practical implications for real-world applications, and the proposed solution is both theoretically grounded and empirically validated.
