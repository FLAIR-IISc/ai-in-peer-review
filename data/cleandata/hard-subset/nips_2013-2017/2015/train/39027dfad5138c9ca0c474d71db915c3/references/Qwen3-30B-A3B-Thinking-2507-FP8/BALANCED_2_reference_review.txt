

# Review of "The Self-Normalized Estimator for Counterfactual Learning"

## Summary
This paper addresses a critical issue in batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF), a setting central to improving interactive systems like recommender systems and ad platforms. The authors identify and formalize "Propensity Overfitting," a previously overlooked problem where conventional counterfactual risk estimators (like the inverse propensity score estimator) produce severely biased risk estimates during Empirical Risk Minimization (ERM). They propose a Self-Normalized Risk Estimator using multiplicative control variates that resolves the anomalies of the conventional estimator. The paper derives a new learning algorithm, Norm-POEM, based on this estimator and demonstrates its superiority over existing methods (like POEM) through extensive empirical evaluation on multiple benchmark datasets. The work builds on and extends the CRM principle from prior BLBF literature [1, 5, 6, 13] and makes a significant contribution to the understanding of counterfactual learning.

## Strengths
1. **High theoretical quality**: The paper makes a clear, well-justified contribution to the theoretical understanding of counterfactual learning. The identification of Propensity Overfitting as a distinct problem from conventional Loss Overfitting is insightful and well-documented with compelling examples. The theoretical analysis (Theorem 2) of the self-normalized estimator's strong consistency is rigorous.

2. **Strong empirical validation**: The experiments are thorough, well-designed, and directly address the theoretical claims. The authors demonstrate consistent improvements of Norm-POEM over POEM across multiple datasets and metrics. The ablation studies (Table 2) provide convincing evidence that the improvements are due to better control over Propensity Overfitting.

3. **Clear practical impact**: The paper demonstrates that the proposed method is not only theoretically sound but also practically useful, with comparable computational cost to existing methods and significantly better generalization performance.

4. **Good related work**: The paper provides a comprehensive review of relevant literature, correctly situating the contribution within the broader context of counterfactual learning, importance sampling, and causal inference.

## Weaknesses
1. **Limited theoretical analysis of bias/variance**: While the paper proves strong consistency of the estimator, it lacks finite-sample bias and variance bounds that would better characterize the estimator's behavior in practical settings. The connection between the estimator's properties and the observed empirical improvements could be strengthened with more theoretical analysis.

2. **Incomplete comparison with alternative variance reduction techniques**: The paper focuses on comparing with POEM but doesn't compare with other variance reduction techniques (e.g., doubly robust estimators [6], or other control variate approaches) that might also mitigate Propensity Overfitting.

3. **Lack of sensitivity analysis**: The paper doesn't thoroughly explore how sensitive the method is to hyperparameter choices (particularly M for clipping), which could be important for practitioners. The claim that "variance regularization still helps" (Table 3) needs more justification.

4. **Limited scope of experiments**: The experiments are limited to multi-label classification datasets. It would strengthen the argument if the method were tested on more diverse tasks, such as recommendation systems or reinforcement learning benchmarks.

## Arguments for and against Acceptance

**Arguments for acceptance:**
- The paper identifies a significant and previously overlooked problem in counterfactual learning (Propensity Overfitting) that affects the reliability of existing methods.
- The proposed solution is theoretically sound, well-justified, and demonstrates consistent empirical improvements.
- The work is highly relevant to the NeurIPS community, with direct applications to important real-world problems.
- The paper is well-written and clearly organized, making the contribution accessible to readers.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
- The theoretical analysis could be strengthened with more detailed bias/variance bounds.
- The empirical evaluation could be expanded to include more diverse tasks and comparative analysis with additional variance reduction techniques.
- The paper doesn't fully address how the method scales to very large datasets or high-dimensional feature spaces.

## Recommendations
1. **Strengthen theoretical analysis**: Add a finite-sample bound for the bias and variance of the self-normalized estimator to better connect theory with empirical results.

2. **Expand empirical evaluation**: Include comparisons with additional variance reduction techniques (e.g., doubly robust estimators) and test on at least one additional domain (e.g., a recommendation system dataset).

3. **Provide more sensitivity analysis**: Include a thorough analysis of how hyperparameters (particularly M and λ) affect performance, and discuss practical guidelines for their selection.

4. **Clarify computational cost**: Provide more detailed analysis of the computational complexity of the self-normalized estimator compared to the conventional estimator, including runtime measurements for larger datasets.

This paper makes a valuable contribution to the field of counterfactual learning with clear theoretical insights and strong empirical validation. While the theoretical analysis could be strengthened and the empirical evaluation expanded, the core contribution is significant and well-executed. With the suggested improvements, this paper would be an excellent fit for NeurIPS. I recommend acceptance.
