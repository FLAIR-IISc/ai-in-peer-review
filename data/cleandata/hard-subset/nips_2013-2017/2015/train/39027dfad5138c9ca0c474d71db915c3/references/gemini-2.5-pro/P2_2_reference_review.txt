**Summary**

This paper identifies and addresses a critical failure mode in batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF) which the authors term "Propensity Overfitting". Standard counterfactual risk estimators, such as the Inverse Propensity Score (IPS) estimator, can be minimized not just by fitting the underlying loss function, but by exploiting the propensity weights themselves. For instance, with negative losses, a model can achieve an arbitrarily low estimated risk by placing all its probability mass on the observed actions in the training data. The authors demonstrate this pathology with a clear and compelling example.

To solve this, the paper proposes using the self-normalized (SN) importance sampling estimator. This is derived by using the sum of propensity weights as a multiplicative control variate, which is known to have an expected value of 1. The resulting SN estimator has several desirable properties that the standard IPS estimator lacks: it is equivariant to shifts in the loss function and its estimates are always bounded within the range of the observed losses. The authors then incorporate this new estimator and a corresponding variance approximation into the Counterfactual Risk Minimization (CRM) framework, leading to a new learning algorithm called Norm-POEM. Extensive experiments on several datasets show that Norm-POEM significantly outperforms the previous state-of-the-art (POEM), is more robust, and directly mitigates the propensity overfitting problem it was designed to solve.

**Strengths**

1.  **Significance & Originality:** The primary contribution is the clear identification, naming, and diagnosis of "Propensity Overfitting". This is a subtle yet fundamental problem in learning with importance-weighted estimators. While the self-normalized estimator is a known tool in the statistics literature, its application to solve this specific, well-articulated problem in counterfactual learning is novel and significant. It provides a crisp explanation for a type of instability that many researchers and practitioners in this area have likely observed.

2.  **Quality:** The paper is technically sound and presents a complete piece of work.
    *   The motivation is excellent; the toy example in Section 4 perfectly illustrates the core problem, making the subsequent solution feel necessary and elegant.
    *   The proposed solution directly addresses the identified weaknesses of the standard estimator. The properties of equivariance and boundedness are crucial and well-explained.
    *   The experimental evaluation is thorough and convincing. The methodology is sound, and the comparisons to a strong baseline (POEM, from the same authors' prior work) are fair. Crucially, the experiments go beyond simply showing improved generalization error. The analysis in Table 2, which directly measures the sum of propensities `Ŝ(h)` and tests robustness to loss translation, provides direct evidence that the proposed method works for the reasons claimed by the theory. This is a hallmark of excellent empirical work.

3.  **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The logical flow from problem definition to theoretical solution to algorithmic implementation and finally to empirical validation is seamless and easy to follow. The mathematical notation is clear and consistent. The authors do a great job of building intuition before presenting formal results.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Limited Novelty of the Estimator Itself:** The main technical tool, the self-normalized estimator, is not new. The authors are transparent about this and correctly cite its origins in the Monte Carlo literature. The primary novelty is therefore in the *connection* of this existing tool to the *newly identified problem* of propensity overfitting in the BLBF setting. This is a minor weakness, as the contribution of identifying the problem and providing a principled solution is substantial on its own.

2.  **Computational Scalability:** The authors note that the proposed Norm-POEM objective is not amenable to stochastic gradient descent, unlike the original POEM algorithm. While they show that L-BFGS converges quickly in their experiments, the inability to use stochastic methods could be a limitation for applications with extremely large datasets where full passes over the data are prohibitively expensive. A brief discussion of this potential limitation or possible future work on stochastic approximations would strengthen the paper.

3.  **Experiments on Simulated Data:** The experiments use the standard and well-accepted "supervised-to-bandit" conversion protocol. This allows for controlled evaluation and is a clear strength. However, demonstrating the method's effectiveness on a real-world logged bandit dataset, where propensities might be noisy or mis-specified, would further bolster the paper's practical significance. This is more of a suggestion for future work than a flaw in the current paper.

**Arguments for Acceptance (Pros):**
*   Identifies and provides a clear diagnosis of a fundamental problem ("Propensity Overfitting") in a popular learning setting.
*   Proposes an elegant, principled, and effective solution based on the self-normalized estimator.
*   The solution is shown to have superior theoretical properties (equivariance, boundedness) that directly fix the identified problem.
*   Empirical results are strong and consistent across multiple datasets, showing significant improvement over a state-of-the-art baseline.
*   The paper includes excellent diagnostic experiments that validate the theoretical claims, connecting the "why" with the "what".
*   The paper is extremely well-written and a pleasure to read.

**Arguments for Rejection (Cons):**
*   The core statistical tool (the self-normalized estimator) is not new, though its application and motivation in this context are.
*   The proposed algorithm is not optimized via stochastic methods, potentially limiting its scalability compared to some alternatives.

**Conclusion**

This is an excellent paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of counterfactual learning. The identification of propensity overfitting is a key insight, and the proposed solution is simple, effective, and well-justified by both theory and experiments. The paper is a model of clarity and rigorous research. The weaknesses are minor and do not detract from the overall strength of the work. I strongly recommend acceptance.
