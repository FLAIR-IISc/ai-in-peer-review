This paper addresses the problem of batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF), identifying and tackling a novel issue termed “Propensity Overfitting” in standard counterfactual risk estimation. The authors propose a Self-Normalized Risk Estimator (SNRE) and demonstrate both theoretically and empirically that it mitigates this overfitting and improves generalization performance.

The paper is well-motivated, building upon established work in causal inference, off-policy evaluation, and reinforcement learning. The connection to existing challenges in importance sampling and covariate shift is clearly articulated. The problem of propensity overfitting is convincingly explained with a clear example, highlighting how conventional estimators can lead to counterintuitive results. The proposed SNRE is a logical extension of existing control variate techniques, drawing on successful applications in finance and reinforcement learning. The experimental validation, utilizing a supervised-to-bandit framework, provides a strong empirical basis for the claims, comparing SNRE against a baseline (POEM) across multiple datasets. The inclusion of ablation studies examining the necessity of variance regularization and analysis of runtime efficiency further strengthens the evaluation.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** Identifying and formally analyzing propensity overfitting is a significant contribution. It exposes a previously unrecognized vulnerability in the standard approach to BLBF.
* **Theoretical Soundness:**  The paper provides a theoretical justification for the SNRE, proving consistency. The use of existing theorems (e.g., from [1, 13]) is appropriate and well-explained.
* **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The notation is consistent, and the key concepts are explained in a relatively accessible manner. The example illustrating propensity overfitting is particularly helpful.
* **Empirical Validation:** The experimental results consistently demonstrate the superiority of SNRE over the baseline POEM, both in terms of generalization performance and robustness. The ablation studies and runtime analysis add further credibility.
* **Practical Relevance:**  BLBF is a highly relevant problem in many real-world applications (e.g., recommendation systems, advertising). The proposed solution offers a potentially valuable tool for improving the performance of such systems.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Scope of Experiments:** While the supervised-to-bandit setup is a standard approach for evaluating BLBF algorithms, it may not fully capture the complexities of real-world logged data. Exploring the performance on more diverse and realistic datasets would be beneficial.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The performance of both POEM and Norm-POEM relies on careful hyperparameter tuning (M and λ). The paper briefly mentions calibration on a hold-out set but doesn’t extensively discuss the sensitivity of the results to these parameters.  A more thorough analysis of hyperparameter sensitivity would strengthen the conclusions.
* **Computational Cost Discussion:** While the paper notes that Norm-POEM is surprisingly faster, a more detailed breakdown of the computational complexity and runtime comparisons would be useful, particularly for larger datasets.
* **Connection to Existing Literature on Robust Estimation:**  The problem of propensity overfitting bears some resemblance to challenges in robust statistical estimation, where outliers or influential observations can severely distort estimates.  Explicitly connecting the work to this broader literature (e.g., M-estimation, trimmed estimators) could provide additional insights.
* **Minor Clarity Issue:** The transition between discussing the general case (δ(x, y) ∈ [-1, 0]) and the examples with δ(x, y) taking on specific values feels a bit abrupt.  A smoother explanation would improve readability.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper identifies a novel and important problem in BLBF that has been previously overlooked.
* The proposed solution, the Self-Normalized Risk Estimator, is theoretically sound and empirically validated.
* The experimental results are compelling and demonstrate a clear improvement over existing methods.
* The paper is well-written and clearly presented, making it accessible to a broad audience.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The experimental evaluation, while strong, is limited to a supervised-to-bandit setup, which may not fully reflect real-world scenarios.
* The discussion of hyperparameter sensitivity and computational cost could be more thorough.
* The connection to related work in robust estimation could be further explored.

**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a significant contribution to the field of batch learning from logged bandit feedback. The identification of propensity overfitting is a novel insight, and the proposed SNRE offers a practical and effective solution. While there are some minor weaknesses, the strengths of the paper outweigh them.  I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions.  Specifically, the authors should consider:

1.  Expanding the experimental evaluation to include more diverse datasets or real-world logged data, if possible.
2.  Providing a more detailed analysis of hyperparameter sensitivity.
3.  Adding a discussion of the connection to related work in robust estimation.
4.  Clarifying the transition in Section 4 regarding the loss function values.
