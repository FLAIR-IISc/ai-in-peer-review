

This paper identifies a previously unrecognized issue called "Propensity Overfitting" in batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF), a critical problem in real-world interactive systems like search engines and recommendation platforms. The authors demonstrate that conventional counterfactual risk estimators used in Empirical Risk Minimization (ERM) exhibit severe anomalies leading to biased risk estimates, distinct from standard Loss Overfitting in supervised learning. They propose a Self-Normalized Risk Estimator using multiplicative control variates, prove its theoretical properties, and derive a new learning algorithm called Norm-POEM. The work builds on prior BLBF research, particularly Swaminathan and Joachims' CRM principle [1], while extending importance sampling techniques to address a novel overfitting phenomenon.

Quality: The paper is technically sound with rigorous theoretical analysis. The authors clearly define Propensity Overfitting (Section 4), provide a concrete example demonstrating its severity (Example 1), and prove the Self-Normalized Risk Estimator's strong consistency (Theorem 2). The empirical evaluation is comprehensive, using multiple datasets from LibSVM (Scene, Yeast, TMC, LYRL) with 10 runs per dataset. The results consistently show Norm-POEM outperforms POEM in generalization performance (Table 1), avoids Propensity Overfitting (Table 2), and maintains computational efficiency (Section 7). The paper appropriately addresses potential concerns like the necessity of variance regularization (Table 3) and the impact of loss translation.

Clarity: The paper is exceptionally well-organized and clearly written. The problem statement (Section 1) logically leads to the identification of Propensity Overfitting (Section 4), followed by the solution (Section 5) and empirical validation (Section 7). The key concepts (Propensity Overfitting vs. Loss Overfitting) are well-explained with concrete examples. The mathematical derivations are precise, and the experimental setup is thoroughly described. The authors provide sufficient detail for experts to reproduce the results, including implementation details and hyperparameter calibration procedures.

Originality: The paper makes a significant contribution by identifying Propensity Overfitting as a new type of overfitting in BLBF, distinct from existing concepts. While self-normalized estimators have appeared in other contexts [19, 22, 23], their application to BLBF to specifically address Propensity Overfitting is novel. The paper clearly differentiates this work from prior importance sampling approaches [5, 11, 15] and demonstrates how the proposed estimator resolves previously unaddressed anomalies. The authors properly cite related work, including the seminal BLBF papers [1, 5, 6] and self-normalized estimators [19].

Significance: The problem addressed is highly relevant to real-world applications of counterfactual learning in interactive systems. The proposed solution provides substantial practical benefits, with consistent improvements in generalization performance across multiple datasets. The theoretical guarantees (boundedness, equivariance, strong consistency) provide a solid foundation for the method. The paper advances the state of the art in BLBF, with Norm-POEM offering a robust, efficient alternative to existing methods that handles a previously unaddressed failure mode.

Strengths: Clear identification of a novel problem; sound theoretical analysis with proofs; comprehensive empirical validation across multiple datasets; practical implementation with computational efficiency; well-written and well-organized.

Weaknesses: The paper could better compare with doubly robust estimators [6] in terms of bias-variance tradeoffs, though the authors do acknowledge this in related work. The theoretical analysis of generalization error bounds for Norm-POEM could be expanded beyond strong consistency to provide more precise guarantees.

Arguments for acceptance: The paper identifies a significant previously overlooked problem in BLBF; proposes a theoretically sound solution with rigorous proofs; demonstrates consistent empirical improvements over state-of-the-art; provides clear implementation details; and advances the field with practical contributions.

Arguments against acceptance: The comparison with doubly robust estimators is limited, though this is a minor point given the paper's focus on a specific overfitting issue. The theoretical analysis could be slightly extended, but this is not a major weakness.

Overall, this paper makes a strong contribution to the field of counterfactual learning. It identifies a critical problem that has been overlooked in prior work, provides a theoretically grounded solution, and demonstrates significant practical improvements. The work is technically sound, clearly presented, and addresses an important problem with real-world relevance. The paper advances the state of the art in BLBF and should be accepted for publication at NIPS.
