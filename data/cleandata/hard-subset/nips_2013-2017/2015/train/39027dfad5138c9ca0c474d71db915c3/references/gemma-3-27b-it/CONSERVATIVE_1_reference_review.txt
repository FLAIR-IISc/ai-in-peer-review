This paper addresses the important problem of learning from logged bandit feedback, a common scenario in many real-world systems. The authors identify a novel issue – propensity overfitting – in the standard counterfactual risk estimation approach and propose a self-normalized estimator to mitigate it. While the problem statement is well-motivated and the proposed solution has theoretical appeal, I find the paper ultimately falls short of convincing me of its practical significance, primarily due to the reliance on a somewhat artificial experimental setup and a lack of comparison to simpler, more established baselines.

The core idea of propensity overfitting is intriguing. The authors clearly articulate how standard estimators can be susceptible to fitting the propensity weights rather than the true reward function, leading to poor generalization. The self-normalized estimator is a reasonable attempt to address this, drawing upon techniques from financial mathematics and reinforcement learning. The theoretical justification, specifically Theorem 2 guaranteeing strong consistency, is a positive aspect.

However, my primary concern lies with the experimental evaluation. The setup, using supervised datasets and simulating bandit feedback via a CRF, feels contrived. While the “Supervised 7→ Bandit” methodology is common, the extent to which the findings translate to real-world logged data scenarios is unclear. The datasets chosen are relatively small and may not adequately stress-test the proposed method. More importantly, the comparison is almost exclusively to POEM [1], the authors’ prior work. Where are the comparisons to simpler, well-established methods like propensity score weighting with basic clipping, or doubly-robust estimators? Demonstrating a clear advantage over these baselines would significantly strengthen the paper. The fact that Norm-POEM outperforms POEM is not particularly surprising given the identified limitations of POEM, but doesn't necessarily demonstrate a fundamental advance.

The claims of computational efficiency are also somewhat suspect. While the authors state that Norm-POEM is faster, this seems to stem from converging to a simpler solution (smaller ||w||) rather than algorithmic improvements. This is more of a side-effect than a deliberate optimization. Furthermore, the reliance on L-BFGS, while justified, limits scalability.

The clarity of the paper is good, and the writing is generally concise. However, the presentation of the theoretical results feels dense and could benefit from more intuitive explanations. The connection between the mathematical formalism and the practical implications of propensity overfitting could be strengthened.

Regarding originality, identifying propensity overfitting is a novel contribution. However, the self-normalized estimator itself is not new, simply adapted from other fields. The core novelty lies in applying it to this specific problem and proving its beneficial properties in this context. 

Finally, the significance of this work is questionable. While propensity overfitting is a valid concern, the degree to which it hinders practical applications remains unclear. The paper doesn't convincingly demonstrate that this problem is widespread or that the proposed solution offers a substantial improvement over existing techniques in realistic scenarios.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Identifies a novel problem – propensity overfitting – in counterfactual learning.
*   Proposes a theoretically sound solution – the self-normalized estimator – with a consistency guarantee.
*   Demonstrates improved performance over the authors’ prior work (POEM).

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Experimental evaluation relies on a somewhat artificial setup.
*   Lacks comparison to simpler, more established baseline methods.
*   Claims of computational efficiency are not fully convincing.
*   The overall significance of the work is questionable, with limited demonstration of real-world impact.



**Overall assessment:** Reject. 

While the paper presents a technically sound and potentially interesting idea, the lack of thorough empirical validation against established baselines and the reliance on a somewhat contrived experimental setup prevent me from recommending acceptance at this time. The authors need to demonstrate that propensity overfitting is a significant issue in practical scenarios and that the proposed solution offers a substantial and demonstrable advantage over existing techniques. I encourage the authors to address these concerns and resubmit a revised version of the paper.
