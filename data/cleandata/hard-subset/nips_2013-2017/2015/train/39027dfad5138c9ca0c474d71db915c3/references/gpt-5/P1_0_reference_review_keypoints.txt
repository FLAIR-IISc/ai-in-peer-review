- Paper identifies "Propensity Overfitting": IPS-based ERM can game importance weights, causing non-equivariance and unbounded finite-sample degeneracies.
- Proposes self-normalized IPS (SN-IPS) via a multiplicative control variate to produce bounded, equivariant, and strongly consistent counterfactual risk estimates.
- Integrates SN-IPS into a CRM algorithm for structured prediction (Norm-POEM) and provides an approximate variance estimator to enable variance regularization.
- Empirical evaluation (supervised-to-bandit conversion on multi-label tasks) shows consistent gains over POEM, robustness to loss translation, and improved optimization behavior.
- Situates work in off-policy evaluation and causal inference, noting SN-IPS/WIS is well-known in Monte Carlo and RL literature.
- Contribution framed as bringing SN-IPS into CRM to address a specific overfitting pathology rather than inventing a new estimator.
- Strength: clear diagnosis and illustrative example of propensity overfitting demonstrating the failure mode of IPS-based ERM.
- Strength: conceptually simple, principled remedy that fixes non-equivariance and bounds finite-sample estimates.
- Strength: practical algorithm (Norm-POEM) with variance regularization preserving CRM principles.
- Strength: diagnostics and empirical evidence (e.g., Ŝ(ĥ) ≈ 1) support the method’s ability to control propensity overfitting.
- Weakness: theoretical contribution modest — strong consistency is standard for WIS and no new finite-sample CRM-style generalization bound is provided for SN-IPS.
- Weakness: variance estimation relies on a Normal/delta-method approximation with no analysis of its accuracy or comparison to alternatives (jackknife/bootstrap).
- Weakness: limited baselines — comparisons only to POEM and a supervised skyline, lacking DR, weighted DR, and clipped IPS baselines.
- Weakness: experiments use standard supervised-to-bandit conversion; lacking real-world heavy-tailed domains (e.g., recommendations) to stress-test benefits.
- Weakness: method still needs full support or clipping; little analysis of clipping-induced bias or guidance on choosing clipping threshold M.
- Weakness: some implementation/reporting gaps — no wall-clock runtimes, no statistical significance tests, and limited ablation on hyperparameters (λ, M).
- Quality: technically sound with a compelling diagnosis and correct fix, but limited novel theory and missing finite-sample guarantees.
- Clarity: paper is well-written and organized with an effective propensity-overfitting example, though loss sign conventions vary across sections.
- Originality: using SN-IPS in off-policy evaluation isn’t new; novelty is in identifying propensity overfitting and integrating SN-IPS into CRM for structured prediction.
- Significance: addresses an important practical failure mode and offers a simple, adoptable fix likely to influence practitioners.
- Suggestions: derive or discuss finite-sample CRM bounds for SN-IPS (possibly with clipping) and analyze when variance regularization controls generalization.
- Suggestions: add baselines (DR, weighted DR, clipped IPS), run statistical significance tests, and include ablations on λ and M.
- Suggestions: analyze clipping bias/variance trade-offs and provide practical guidelines for selecting M and λ.
- Suggestions: report runtimes, iteration counts, convergence plots, and test on domains with heavy-tailed weight distributions.
- Pros: clear problem diagnosis, simple principled solution, consistent empirical gains, and good relevance to NeurIPS audience.
- Cons: limited new theory, narrow experimental baselines/domains, heuristic variance estimation, and little guidance on hyperparameters/clipping.
- Recommendation: Weak accept — practical, well-motivated improvement with medium confidence despite modest theoretical novelty and limited baselines.
