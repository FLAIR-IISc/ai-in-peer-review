**Summary**

This paper addresses the problem of batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF). The authors identify a critical failure mode in the standard Counterfactual Risk Minimization (CRM) approach, which relies on an unbiased inverse propensity score (IPS) estimator. They term this issue "Propensity Overfitting," where the learning algorithm can achieve an artificially low empirical risk by manipulating the propensity weights rather than minimizing the actual loss. This occurs because the standard IPS estimator is not equivariant to shifts in the loss function and its value is not bounded by the range of the loss. To remedy this, the authors propose replacing the standard IPS estimator with the self-normalized estimator, a well-established ratio estimator from the Monte Carlo literature. They integrate this estimator into the CRM framework to create a new learning algorithm, Norm-POEM. Through a series of well-controlled experiments, they demonstrate that their proposed approach effectively mitigates Propensity Overfitting, leading to substantially improved and more robust generalization performance compared to the standard method.

**Strengths and Weaknesses**

The paper's primary strength lies in its clear diagnosis of a subtle yet severe problem and the proposal of a principled, effective solution. The concept of "Propensity Overfitting" is a valuable contribution, as it articulates a specific type of model misspecification that can occur in counterfactual learning, distinct from standard supervised learning overfitting. The illustrative example in Section 4 is highly effective in demonstrating how ERM with the standard IPS estimator can lead to degenerate solutions.

The proposed solution is methodologically sound. Rather than introducing an ad-hoc heuristic, the authors leverage a well-established statistical tool—the self-normalized (or ratio) estimator. The properties of this estimator (equivariance to affine loss transformations, boundedness, and consistency) are precisely what is needed to address the identified anomalies of the standard IPS estimator. The adoption of an existing, proven method is preferable to an entirely new and unproven one, as its properties are better understood.

The empirical evaluation is thorough and convincing. The experiments are conducted in a controlled setting that allows for clear comparison and analysis. The results in Table 1 and Figure 1 show a consistent and significant performance improvement for Norm-POEM over the baseline POEM. Crucially, the diagnostic experiments in Table 2 provide direct evidence for the authors' central claim: Norm-POEM is robust to loss translations and avoids the dangerous deviation of the sum of propensity weights from its expected value of 1, confirming that it successfully mitigates Propensity Overfitting.

The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized, making a complex topic accessible. The arguments are presented logically, from problem identification to solution and validation.

However, the paper has some weaknesses that should be addressed.

1.  **Originality of the Estimator:** The main technical contribution is the application of the self-normalized estimator to the BLBF problem. This estimator itself is not novel and is a standard tool in the importance sampling literature (e.g., [19, 22]). While the authors correctly cite this prior work, the paper's contribution should be carefully framed as the identification of a problem in a specific learning setting and the successful application of an appropriate, existing statistical tool, rather than the development of a fundamentally new estimation technique.

2.  **Bias-Variance Discussion:** The standard IPS estimator is unbiased, while the proposed self-normalized estimator is known to be biased for finite sample sizes. The authors acknowledge this and prove its consistency (Theorem 2). However, the paper would be strengthened by a more explicit discussion of the bias introduced. The key benefit of a ratio estimator is often a reduction in mean squared error (MSE), where a small increase in bias is more than compensated by a large reduction in variance. A more formal discussion of this trade-off, perhaps referencing existing statistical theory on the conditions under which the ratio estimator's MSE is lower than the unbiased estimator's, would provide deeper theoretical grounding for the observed empirical improvements.

3.  **Computational Scalability:** The authors note that their proposed variance estimator (Eq. 8) does not readily admit stochastic optimization, forcing them to use a batch method like L-BFGS. While they report that faster convergence makes up for the per-iteration cost in their experiments, this could be a significant limitation for larger-scale problems where SGD-based methods are essential. A discussion of potential avenues for creating a scalable, stochastic version of the Norm-POEM objective would enhance the paper's practical impact.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Identifies and clearly explains a significant and previously unarticulated problem ("Propensity Overfitting") in a widely used class of counterfactual learning algorithms.
*   The proposed solution is simple, principled, and based on a methodologically sound and established statistical estimator.
*   The experimental evidence is strong, comprehensive, and directly supports the paper's central claims, demonstrating substantial improvements in robustness and generalization.

**Arguments for Rejection:**
*   The core technical tool (the self-normalized estimator) is not novel, limiting the paper's fundamental originality.
*   The theoretical analysis could be more complete, particularly regarding the bias-variance trade-off of the proposed estimator.
*   Potential computational scaling issues are noted but not fully addressed.

**Conclusion**

Overall, this is a high-quality paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of learning from logged bandit feedback. The identification and formalization of Propensity Overfitting is an important insight, and the proposed Norm-POEM algorithm provides a robust and effective solution. While the core estimator is not new, its application in this context is novel and well-motivated. The strengths, particularly the clarity of the problem diagnosis and the strength of the empirical results, outweigh the weaknesses. I recommend acceptance.
