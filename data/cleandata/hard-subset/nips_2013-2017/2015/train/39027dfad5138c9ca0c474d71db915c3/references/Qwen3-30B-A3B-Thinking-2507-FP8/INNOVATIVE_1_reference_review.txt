

# Review of "The Self-Normalized Estimator for Counterfactual Learning"

This paper identifies and solves a previously unrecognized problem in counterfactual learning: Propensity Overfitting. The authors demonstrate that the conventional importance sampling estimator used in batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF) suffers from severe anomalies that cause degenerate risk estimates when used in Empirical Risk Minimization (ERM). Specifically, they show that standard estimators can produce risk estimates arbitrarily lower than the true risk, even when the loss function is bounded. The authors propose a Self-Normalized Risk Estimator using multiplicative control variates that resolves these anomalies and provides theoretical guarantees of consistency and equivariance. They derive a new learning algorithm called Norm-POEM that incorporates this estimator and demonstrate its superior performance across multiple benchmark datasets.

The paper makes a significant contribution by identifying a novel problem in a well-studied area. While previous work on BLBF has focused on variance reduction techniques and thresholding (e.g., POEM, CRM), this paper reveals a fundamental flaw in the underlying estimator that has been overlooked. The authors clearly distinguish their work from prior approaches by showing that Propensity Overfitting occurs regardless of loss transformations, unlike conventional Loss Overfitting. The Self-Normalized estimator they propose is theoretically sound (Theorem 2 proves strong consistency) and empirically validated across multiple datasets.

Strengths of the paper include:
1. **Novel problem identification**: Propensity Overfitting is a genuinely new problem in BLBF, not previously recognized in the literature.
2. **Elegant solution**: The self-normalized estimator is conceptually simple yet powerful, using a natural control variate approach that resolves the identified anomalies.
3. **Strong empirical validation**: The authors demonstrate consistent improvements over POEM across multiple datasets and experimental conditions, including robustness to loss translation and better generalization with more data.
4. **Theoretical clarity**: The paper provides clear theoretical justification for both the problem and the solution, with well-constructed theorems and proofs.
5. **Practical implementation**: Norm-POEM is computationally efficient and requires minimal modification to existing algorithms, making it immediately applicable to real-world systems.

Weaknesses are relatively minor:
1. The paper doesn't fully explore the theoretical limits of the self-normalized estimator in extreme cases (e.g., when the denominator approaches zero).
2. While the authors claim computational efficiency, a more detailed comparison of training time versus POEM would strengthen the claims.
3. The experimental section could have included additional ablation studies on the impact of different hyperparameters.

The paper is well-written, clearly organized, and makes an important contribution to the field. The problem is clearly articulated, the solution is novel and elegant, and the empirical results convincingly demonstrate the advantages of the proposed approach. The authors have carefully situated their work within the literature, acknowledging the connections to prior work while clearly differentiating their novel contribution.

This paper stands out for its bold approach to identifying a fundamental flaw in a standard technique and providing a simple yet powerful solution. The problem of Propensity Overfitting is significant for anyone working on counterfactual learning in real-world applications (advertising, recommendation systems, etc.), and the proposed solution has immediate practical value. The paper's theoretical insights also advance our understanding of counterfactual learning, going beyond incremental improvements to establish a new conceptual framework.

While the paper is not perfect (minor computational details could be expanded), the novelty and significance of the contribution far outweigh these minor concerns. The authors have identified a previously unrecognized problem and provided a clean, effective solution that improves upon the state-of-the-art in a demonstrable way.

This paper represents a significant contribution to the field of counterfactual learning and would be valuable to researchers and practitioners working on batch learning from logged bandit feedback. The problem identification and solution are both novel, the theoretical foundation is solid, and the empirical results are convincing. The authors have made a clear and important step forward in the field.

I recommend acceptance of this paper at NIPS. The novelty of the problem and solution, combined with strong theoretical and empirical validation, make it a valuable contribution to the field. The paper exemplifies the kind of bold, novel thinking that advances machine learning research, and its practical implications for real-world systems make it particularly significant.
