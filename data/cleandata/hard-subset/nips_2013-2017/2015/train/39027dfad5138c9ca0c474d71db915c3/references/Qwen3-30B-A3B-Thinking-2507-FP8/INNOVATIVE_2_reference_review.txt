

# Review of "The Self-Normalized Estimator for Counterfactual Learning"

This paper identifies and solves a previously unrecognized problem in batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF), a fundamental setting for improving interactive systems like recommendation engines and ad platforms. The authors introduce the concept of "Propensity Overfitting" - a novel form of overfitting that occurs when using conventional counterfactual risk estimators (e.g., inverse propensity score) in empirical risk minimization. Unlike traditional loss overfitting, propensity overfitting arises from the propensity weights themselves, causing risk estimates to be arbitrarily biased (e.g., much lower than the true risk) when using negative loss functions. This is a significant conceptual advance that explains why existing methods like POEM can fail catastrophically in certain settings.

The authors propose a self-normalized risk estimator (Equation 7) that uses multiplicative control variates to resolve the anomalies of conventional estimators. The key insight is that the sum of propensity weights has an expected value of 1, which the self-normalized estimator leverages to create a bounded, equivariant estimator. This simple yet profound modification addresses the core problem without requiring additional hyperparameters beyond those already used in existing methods. The authors prove the estimator's consistency (Theorem 2) and derive a new learning algorithm, Norm-POEM, that incorporates this estimator into the CRM principle.

The paper's strength lies in its clear identification of a fundamental flaw in the standard approach to counterfactual learning, which has been overlooked in prior work despite extensive research on importance sampling. The authors demonstrate that conventional estimators suffer from propensity overfitting across multiple datasets and loss transformations, while their proposed estimator consistently avoids this issue. The empirical results are compelling: Norm-POEM consistently outperforms POEM across multiple datasets (Table 1, Figure 1), and the average Ŝ(ĥ) for Norm-POEM remains close to 1 (Table 2), confirming the theoretical analysis.

This work is highly significant because it not only solves a previously unrecognized problem but also provides a principled solution that improves both the theoretical foundations and practical performance of counterfactual learning. The self-normalized estimator is elegant in its simplicity, requiring no additional complexity beyond standard importance sampling methods. The authors demonstrate that the improved estimator leads to substantially better generalization without requiring major algorithmic changes to existing approaches.

The paper is well-written, with clear motivation, thorough theoretical analysis, and comprehensive empirical validation. The experiments follow standard BLBF evaluation protocols, using supervised datasets transformed into bandit feedback settings as in prior work [1]. The authors carefully validate their claims across multiple datasets (Scene, Yeast, TMC, LYRL) and demonstrate robustness to different loss transformations.

The main novelty of this paper lies in identifying the propensity overfitting problem and proposing a simple yet powerful solution using self-normalized estimators. While self-normalized estimators have been used in importance sampling before [12, 19, 22, 23], this is the first work to recognize their critical importance for avoiding propensity overfitting in counterfactual learning. This represents a significant conceptual advance rather than just a technical improvement.

The paper's contributions are substantial: (1) identification of a previously unrecognized problem in BLBF; (2) a principled solution using self-normalized estimators; (3) theoretical guarantees for the new estimator; and (4) empirical validation showing improved performance. This is not a minor incremental improvement but a fundamental rethinking of how counterfactual risk estimation should be performed.

I note that the paper could be strengthened by more detailed discussion of the computational implications of the self-normalized estimator (beyond the brief mention of faster convergence), and a more formal definition of "equivariance" for the estimator. However, these are minor points that don't detract from the paper's substantial contribution.

In summary, this paper makes a significant contribution to the field by identifying and solving a previously overlooked problem in counterfactual learning. The novelty of the problem identification, the elegance of the solution, and the strong empirical validation make this a highly significant contribution to the literature on counterfactual inference and batch learning from bandit feedback. The paper clearly advances the state of the art in a demonstrable way, and the proposed approach has clear practical implications for improving real-world interactive systems.

Given the reviewer's preference for novel concepts over methodological perfection, and the paper's clear identification of a new problem with a principled solution that significantly outperforms existing methods, I strongly recommend acceptance. This paper represents a meaningful advance in understanding and addressing the fundamental challenges of counterfactual learning.
