

# Review of "The Self-Normalized Estimator for Counterfactual Learning"

This paper identifies a previously unrecognized problem in batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF) called "Propensity Overfitting," which causes severe anomalies in conventional counterfactual risk estimators. The authors demonstrate that standard importance sampling estimators can produce arbitrarily low risk estimates for certain hypotheses, even when these hypotheses perform poorly in reality. They propose a novel solution using a self-normalized risk estimator with multiplicative control variates, which they theoretically prove avoids these degeneracies and provides improved generalization performance.

The paper makes a significant conceptual contribution by identifying a new failure mode in counterfactual learning that has implications for the entire field. Prior work on BLBF has focused on variance reduction and bias-variance tradeoffs, but the authors uncover a fundamental issue with the unbiased risk estimator itself. Their self-normalized estimator resolves the key anomalies identified (non-equivariance to loss translation and unbounded risk estimates) while maintaining strong consistency. The resulting Norm-POEM algorithm demonstrates consistent improvements over existing methods across multiple datasets.

The theoretical contributions are compelling, particularly Theorem 2 which establishes the strong consistency of the self-normalized estimator. The authors carefully distinguish their problem (propensity overfitting) from conventional overfitting (loss overfitting) through rigorous examples and analysis. The empirical results provide strong validation, showing that Norm-POEM consistently outperforms POEM across datasets and training sizes, and crucially, that the proposed estimator successfully avoids propensity overfitting as evidenced by Ŝ(h) values close to their expected value of 1.

This paper stands out for its conceptual novelty in identifying a new problem and proposing a fundamentally different solution. The self-normalized approach represents a fresh perspective on counterfactual learning, moving beyond incremental improvements to importance sampling methods. The authors convincingly demonstrate that this is not merely a technical tweak but a substantial conceptual advance with practical implications.

Strengths:
- Identifies a previously unaddressed fundamental problem (propensity overfitting) in BLBF
- Proposes a novel solution with clear theoretical justification
- Demonstrates consistent empirical improvements across multiple datasets
- Provides careful theoretical analysis of the problem and solution
- Clearly differentiates from related work on importance sampling

Weaknesses:
- The computational complexity of the self-normalized variance estimator could be further discussed
- The paper could elaborate more on why propensity overfitting occurs in the first place beyond the examples
- While the empirical results are strong, including additional real-world datasets would further strengthen the case

The paper is clearly written and well-organized, with a logical progression from problem identification to solution to validation. The theoretical claims are well-supported, and the empirical results convincingly demonstrate the practical value of the proposed approach.

As a reviewer who values novelty and bold conceptual contributions, I find this paper exceptionally strong. While some papers in the BLBF literature have made incremental improvements to importance sampling techniques, this work identifies a fundamental flaw in the standard approach and proposes a novel solution that fundamentally changes how we think about counterfactual risk estimation. The authors don't just improve an existing method—they identify a new problem and provide a new framework for addressing it.

The significance of this work is substantial. Propensity overfitting appears to be a ubiquitous problem in BLBF that has gone unnoticed in prior literature. The self-normalized estimator not only resolves this issue but also leads to improved generalization performance in practice. This work has the potential to change how counterfactual learning is approached in both academic research and industrial applications.

I recommend acceptance. The novelty of the problem identification and the elegance of the proposed solution make this a strong contribution to the field. While the paper could benefit from slightly more discussion on the computational aspects and the theoretical foundations of propensity overfitting, these are minor concerns that do not detract from the paper's core conceptual contribution. The authors have provided a clear, theoretically grounded, and empirically validated solution to a problem that has been overlooked in the literature for years.

This work stands out as a prime example of the kind of bold, novel contribution that NIPS should be highlighting—identifying a new problem that has significant implications for the field and providing a principled solution that advances the state of the art.
