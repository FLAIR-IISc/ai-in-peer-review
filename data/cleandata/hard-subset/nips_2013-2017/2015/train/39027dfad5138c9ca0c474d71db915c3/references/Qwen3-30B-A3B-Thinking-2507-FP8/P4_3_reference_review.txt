

**Review of "The Self-Normalized Estimator for Counterfactual Learning"**

This paper identifies a previously unrecognized problem in batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF) called "Propensity Overfitting" that affects conventional counterfactual risk estimators. The authors propose a self-normalized risk estimator using multiplicative control variates to resolve this issue, and derive a new learning algorithm called Norm-POEM. The work builds on the authors' prior POEM algorithm [1] and extends the CRM (Counterfactual Risk Minimization) principle, while connecting to off-policy evaluation in reinforcement learning [4, 5] and importance sampling techniques [11, 12].

**Strengths:**

*Technical Soundness:* The paper provides a rigorous theoretical analysis of Propensity Overfitting, demonstrating through concrete examples (Example 1) that conventional estimators can produce arbitrarily biased risk estimates. The self-normalized estimator (Equation 7) is proven to be strongly consistent (Theorem 2) and resolves the key anomalies identified in Section 4. The empirical evaluation is comprehensive, with results across four benchmark datasets (Scene, Yeast, TMC, LYRL) showing consistent improvements over POEM in generalization performance. The authors also demonstrate that Norm-POEM avoids the loss-translation sensitivity of POEM (Table 2) and maintains robust performance across varying logging policy qualities.

*Clarity:* The paper is exceptionally well-structured and clearly written. The problem statement is motivated with a compelling example, and the solution is presented in a logical sequence. Mathematical notation is consistent and well-defined throughout. The empirical results are presented with appropriate tables and figures, and the connection between theory and practice is well-maintained.

*Originality:* The identification of Propensity Overfitting as a distinct form of overfitting from Loss Overfitting is novel and significant. While self-normalized estimators have been used in other domains (e.g., finance [17]), their application to counterfactual learning in BLBF is original. The connection to multiplicative control variates in importance sampling [12, 19, 22] is particularly insightful and well-motivated for this specific context.

*Significance:* The problem addressed has substantial practical implications for interactive systems that rely on logged bandit feedback. The proposed solution leads to demonstrably improved generalization performance across multiple datasets, with Norm-POEM consistently outperforming POEM (Table 1, Figure 1). The method is computationally efficient, with runtime comparable to previous methods while providing substantially better performance.

**Weaknesses:**

*Computational Analysis:* While the paper notes that Norm-POEM is "surprisingly faster" than POEM, it lacks a detailed computational complexity analysis. The authors should provide more specific discussion of the per-iteration computational cost and how it scales with problem size.

*Theoretical Scope:* The theoretical analysis is limited to the specific setting described in the paper. A broader discussion of how the estimator performs under different distributional assumptions or with more complex hypothesis spaces would strengthen the theoretical contribution.

*Differentiation from Related Work:* The paper could better differentiate their approach from related variance reduction techniques in importance sampling, such as doubly robust estimators [6] or clipped importance sampling [15], particularly regarding their relationship to the self-normalized estimator.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
- The paper identifies and solves a significant, previously overlooked problem in counterfactual learning
- The theoretical analysis is rigorous and well-supported by empirical evidence
- The proposed method (Norm-POEM) demonstrates consistent improvements over the state-of-the-art
- The contribution advances the state of the art in BLBF, a topic of substantial interest to the NeurIPS community
- The paper is clearly written and well-structured, making the contribution accessible

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
- The computational analysis is somewhat superficial
- The theoretical scope could be broader
- The differentiation from related variance reduction techniques is not fully developed

**Overall Assessment:**
This paper makes a substantial contribution to the field of counterfactual learning by identifying and resolving a previously unrecognized problem with serious practical implications. The self-normalized estimator provides a theoretically sound solution that translates to improved empirical performance across multiple datasets. The paper is technically sound, well-written, and addresses a problem of significant importance to the NeurIPS community. The minor weaknesses identified do not detract from the paper's overall contribution, which advances the state of the art in counterfactual learning for batch learning from logged bandit feedback.

I recommend acceptance.
