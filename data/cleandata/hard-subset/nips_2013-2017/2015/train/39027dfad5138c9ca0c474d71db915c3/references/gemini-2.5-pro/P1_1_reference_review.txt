### **Summary of the Paper**

This paper addresses the problem of Batch Learning from Logged Bandit Feedback (BLBF), a crucial setting for improving interactive systems using historical data. The authors identify a novel failure mode of standard counterfactual estimators based on Inverse Propensity Scoring (IPS), which they term "Propensity Overfitting". This issue arises because the learning objective can be minimized not just by finding a policy with low true loss, but also by manipulating the learned policy probabilities to exploit the structure of the propensity weights in the training sample. For instance, with negative losses, a model can achieve an arbitrarily low empirical risk by assigning high probability to actions observed in the logs, irrespective of their true loss.

To address this, the paper proposes replacing the standard IPS estimator with the Self-Normalized estimator, a well-known ratio estimator from the importance sampling literature that uses the sum of importance weights as a multiplicative control variate. The authors show that this estimator is equivariant to shifts in the loss function and its estimates are naturally bounded by the range of the observed losses, thus provably avoiding the pathologies of propensity overfitting. Building on the Counterfactual Risk Minimization (CRM) framework of Swaminathan & Joachims (2015), they develop a new learning algorithm called Norm-POEM. Through a series of well-controlled experiments on multi-label classification datasets converted to a bandit setting, they demonstrate that Norm-POEM significantly outperforms its predecessor (POEM), showing improved generalization, robustness to loss transformations, and even faster convergence in practice.

### **Evaluation**

This is an excellent paper that makes a clear, insightful, and significant contribution to the field of counterfactual learning. It identifies a subtle but critical problem with a standard approach and proposes a simple, theoretically-grounded, and empirically effective solution.

#### **Quality**
The paper is of high technical quality.
*   **Soundness:** The diagnosis of "Propensity Overfitting" is sound and convincingly illustrated with a clear example in Section 4. The argument is intuitive and identifies a real degeneracy in using unbounded IPS estimators within an ERM framework. The proposed solution, the self-normalized estimator, is a well-established technique, and its properties (equivariance, consistency, bounded range) are correctly stated and proven where necessary (Theorem 2). The derivation of the Norm-POEM objective by incorporating this estimator and its approximate variance into the CRM principle is straightforward and well-executed.
*   **Completeness:** The work feels complete. It identifies a problem, proposes a principled solution, instantiates it in a concrete algorithm, and provides a thorough empirical evaluation that directly validates its claims. The experiments are well-designed, comparing directly against the relevant baseline (POEM) and testing the specific claims about robustness to loss-shifting.
*   **Honesty:** The authors are careful to position their work. They acknowledge that the self-normalized estimator is not new, citing classic work from the statistics and Monte Carlo literature. The contribution is framed correctly as identifying the propensity overfitting problem and showing that this estimator is the right tool to solve it in the context of BLBF. They also fairly evaluate the continued need for variance regularization (Table 3), showing that the new estimator is not a silver bullet, but rather a better foundation for the CRM principle.

#### **Clarity**
The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow.
*   The motivation is built up logically, starting from the general BLBF problem, to the standard IPS estimator, and then to the specific failure mode of propensity overfitting.
*   The example in Section 4 is crystal clear and perfectly illustrates the core problem. It is one of the highlights of the paper.
*   The explanation of the self-normalized estimator and why its properties directly address the identified problem is very lucid.
*   The experimental setup and results are presented clearly, with tables and plots that are easy to interpret and directly support the paper's central theses.

#### **Originality**
The primary originality of this paper lies in the insightful identification and formalization of the "Propensity Overfitting" problem. While practitioners may have had intuitions about the instability of IPS estimators, this paper gives the problem a name, a clear explanation, and a compelling example of how it manifests as a distinct form of overfitting within a learning context.

The solution itself—the self-normalized estimator—is not original. However, its application to solve this specific learning problem is novel and powerful. The paper does an excellent job of connecting a known statistical tool to a modern machine learning problem, demonstrating a deep understanding of both the problem's pathology and the tool's corrective properties. This is a valuable form of originality that bridges different fields. The resulting algorithm, Norm-POEM, is a novel and important contribution to the BLBF literature.

#### **Significance**
The significance of this work is high.
*   **Impact:** BLBF is a problem of immense practical importance for companies operating search engines, ad platforms, and recommender systems. The instability of IPS estimators is a major hurdle for their practical application. This paper provides a much more robust and reliable method, which could be readily adopted by practitioners.
*   **Advancement of the State of the Art:** The paper clearly demonstrates a substantial improvement over previous state-of-the-art methods like POEM. The results in Table 2, showing that POEM can fail catastrophically under simple loss transformations while Norm-POEM remains stable, are particularly striking. This work establishes a new, stronger baseline for learning from logged bandit feedback.
*   **Future Research:** The clarity of the "Propensity Overfitting" problem formulation is likely to influence how researchers think about and design new counterfactual learning algorithms, encouraging a focus on estimator stability and robustness beyond just unbiasedness.

### **Arguments for and against acceptance**

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
1.  **Novel Problem Formulation:** The paper identifies and clearly articulates "Propensity Overfitting," a critical and previously under-appreciated failure mode for counterfactual learning.
2.  **Principled and Elegant Solution:** The proposed solution is simple, theoretically sound, and directly targets the identified problem by leveraging a well-motivated statistical technique.
3.  **Strong Empirical Results:** The experiments are thorough and provide compelling evidence that the proposed method (Norm-POEM) significantly improves generalization performance and, crucially, robustness over the prior state-of-the-art.
4.  **High Clarity and Readability:** The paper is exceptionally well-written, making a technical contribution accessible and easy to understand.
5.  **High Practical Significance:** The work addresses a real-world problem and provides a practical, effective algorithm that is likely to be used by both researchers and practitioners.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
1.  **Novelty of the Estimator:** The core technical tool, the self-normalized estimator, is not new. However, the paper is transparent about this, and the novelty lies in its diagnosis and application. This is a very minor weakness.
2.  **Simulated Experiments:** The experiments are conducted in a controlled "Supervised -> Bandit" setting. While this is a strong methodology for isolating variables and validating the approach, results on a real-world logged bandit dataset would further strengthen the claims. This is more a suggestion for future work than a flaw in the current paper.

### **Conclusion**

This is a strong paper that should be accepted. It makes a clear and important contribution by identifying a fundamental problem in counterfactual learning and offering a robust, principled, and empirically superior solution. It is well-written, technically sound, and will be of interest to a broad audience at NIPS working on causal inference, reinforcement learning, and the application of machine learning to interactive systems.
