Summary
The paper addresses batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF) and identifies a failure mode of empirical risk minimization (ERM) with inverse propensity scoring (IPS), which the authors term “propensity overfitting.” They argue that the unbiased IPS risk estimator can yield degenerate ERM solutions due to weight variability, lack of equivariance to loss translation, and unboundedness. They propose replacing IPS with the self-normalized importance sampling (SNIS) estimator (a multiplicative control variate), show that it is bounded and equivariant, and prove strong consistency. They integrate SNIS into the Counterfactual Risk Minimization (CRM) framework to produce Norm-POEM, using an approximate variance estimate for regularization. Experiments on multi-label datasets (converted to bandit feedback) suggest Norm-POEM consistently outperforms POEM in test risk, is robust to loss translations, reduces propensity overfitting as measured by the sum of weights Ŝ(h), and is computationally competitive.

Relation to prior work
The work builds on CRM/POEM (Swaminathan & Joachims) and a large literature on importance sampling, variance reduction, clipping, and doubly robust estimation (Dudík et al.; Ionides; Hesterberg; Kong; Owen). Self-normalized (weighted) IS is a classical tool with known advantages when weight fluctuations dominate, and the paper’s use here is close in spirit to WIS in off-policy evaluation. The paper positions “propensity overfitting” as a new conceptualization of weight degeneracy/overfitting in ERM over policies.

Strengths
- Clear identification and illustration (via a constructive example) of a genuine pitfall of ERM with IPS in BLBF.
- Using SNIS to enforce boundedness and equivariance is sensible and well-motivated; the consistency argument is straightforward and correct under bounded losses and full support.
- Empirical results (as described) indicate improved generalization, robustness to loss translation, and better-behaved Ŝ(h) near 1.
- Practical integration into a CRF-like stochastic policy class, with a usable approximate variance estimate, and competitive runtimes.

Weaknesses and detailed comments

Quality
- Theoretical support for the proposed learning objective is incomplete. The CRM bound (Theorem 1) applies to clipped IPS R̂M, not to the self-normalized estimator. The paper uses an approximate variance for SNIS and then applies the CRM principle, but provides no finite-sample generalization bound for the SN objective. The claim that the self-normalized estimator “provably avoids” the anomalies is true for boundedness/equivariance/consistency, but there is no theorem quantifying reduced overfitting risk for the ERM solution in finite samples.
- Clipping/thresholding for SNIS is mentioned but not specified. If weights are clipped, the correct SNIS analogue is sum δi min{M, wi} / sum min{M, wi}. The paper never explicitly states whether Eq. (7) uses clipped or unclipped weights, which matters for variance existence and bias.
- Variance estimator (Eq. 8) is introduced without derivation details or conditions; the Normal/delta-method approximation is reasonable but its calibration and effect on regularization are not studied (no coverage, CIs, or ablation on variance-estimation accuracy).
- Experimental baselines are limited. The paper compares to POEM but not to other strong off-policy estimators/learners: doubly robust (DR), switch-DR, clipped-DR, or optimally tuned clipping; nor to weighted IPS/WIS learning variants that normalize weights (which are essentially what SNIS is). Without these, it is hard to assess the true gain.
- The propensity-overfitting example, while illustrative, uses an extreme deterministic class and uniform logging; it would help to quantify the phenomenon on realistic settings beyond the provided Ŝ(h) summaries.

Clarity
- Several notation and numerical inconsistencies:
  - Loss range is stated as δ ∈ [−1, 0] but Example 1 uses {−2, −1}. Later, δ ∈ [5,4] appears (likely a typo).
  - Ambiguous notation: “E [ R̂(h) Ŝ(h) ] 6= R(h) E[Ŝ(h)]” conflates estimator and numerator; clarify.
  - In “Control variates,” the reference to Trotter & Tukey for SNIS seems off; Hesterberg, Owen, and Kong are the standard citations.
  - In Section 6, whether the self-normalized estimator and variance use clipped weights is unclear.
- References are often incomplete or malformed (missing years/pages; typos: “distributions. Technometrics”; inconsistent capitalization; missing venues).
- Minor typos/grammar: “consistenly,” “NormPOEM” vs “Norm-POEM,” “l2−regularization,” inconsistent spacing/math formatting.
- The datasets table includes suspicious counts (e.g., LYRL ntest=781265) without explanation.

Originality
- The core methodological move—using self-normalized (weighted) IS—is well-known in off-policy evaluation and Monte Carlo, including its boundedness and equivariance. Framing the issue as “propensity overfitting” is a useful articulation, but the algorithmic novelty is modest: plug SNIS into CRM and use an approximate variance.
- The paper would be stronger by providing a new finite-sample learning bound (or at least an ESS-based regularizer) tailored to SNIS, beyond the conceptual argument.

Significance
- The observation that normalized weights mitigate IPS-induced ERM pathologies is important for practice. However, without stronger theoretical support and broader baselines, the impact is limited. The idea is likely to be adopted, but its novelty relative to WIS/normalized IPS in learning seems incremental.

Anonymity violation
- The paper contains a direct code URL with an author-identifying domain (Cornell/~adith). This violates the double-blind policy and is grounds for rejection.

Questions for authors
1. Do you use clipped weights in Eq. (7)? If so, please state the exact estimator (normalized clipped IPS) and analyze its bias/variance; if not, how do you ensure finite variance?
2. Can you provide a finite-sample generalization bound for the SN-CRM objective, or at least an ESS-based regularizer derived from concentration on Ŝ(h)?
3. Please compare against DR/switch-DR and normalized/clipped variants, and report sensitivity to M and λ.
4. Clarify the dataset sizes and provide full experimental details (splits, seeds, runtime, convergence criteria, gradient formulas).
5. Remove identifying information and anonymize the code link for compliance.

Pros
- Clear problem identification; intuitive fix grounded in classical IS.
- Boundedness and equivariance properties address real IPS pathologies.
- Empirically promising and practically simple to adopt.
- Conceptual link to Ŝ(h) and diagnostics is useful.

Cons
- Double-blind violation.
- Incomplete theory for the proposed learning objective (no finite-sample bound).
- Limited baselines; incomplete experimental reporting (tables/figures missing here).
- Numerous formatting/notation/reference issues.

Recommendation
Score: 4 (Reject)
Confidence: 0.8

Rationale: The paper tackles an important problem and presents a sensible, practically useful idea. However, the contribution is incremental relative to well-known WIS/SNIS; theoretical support for the learning objective is insufficient; baselines are incomplete; and there is a clear anonymity violation. With a proper finite-sample analysis, broader comparisons, and polished presentation (and anonymization), this work could be competitive.
