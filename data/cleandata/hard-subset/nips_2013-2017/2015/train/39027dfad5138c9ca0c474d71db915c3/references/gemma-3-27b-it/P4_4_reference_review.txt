This paper addresses the problem of batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF), highlighting a novel form of overfitting called "Propensity Overfitting" in the commonly used counterfactual risk estimator. The authors propose a Self-Normalized Risk Estimator to mitigate this issue and demonstrate its effectiveness through theoretical analysis and empirical evaluation.

The paper is well-structured and clearly motivated. The introduction effectively frames the problem within the context of interactive systems and relates it to existing work in causal inference, domain adaptation, and off-policy evaluation. The connection to reinforcement learning concepts like counterfactual estimation is also clearly explained. The authors carefully review related work, positioning their contribution within the existing landscape and identifying limitations of previous approaches. The mathematical notation is generally clear, and the derivations are presented in a logical manner. The inclusion of Theorem 1 and 2 provides a strong theoretical foundation for their approach. The experimental setup mirrors previous work (POEM), facilitating fair comparison and leveraging established methodologies. The comprehensive empirical evaluation across multiple datasets strengthens the conclusions.

A key strength of the paper lies in identifying and formalizing Propensity Overfitting, a previously unrecognized issue in BLBF. The authors not only diagnose the problem but also propose a theoretically sound and practically effective solution. The self-normalized estimator is well-motivated and appears to address the identified anomalies of the conventional estimator. The experimental results consistently show improved performance with Norm-POEM compared to POEM, demonstrating the practical benefits of their approach. The authors appropriately acknowledge the limitations of their work and areas for future research. The availability of code is a significant positive aspect, fostering reproducibility and further research.

However, the paper could benefit from some improvements. While the explanation of Propensity Overfitting is relatively clear, the intuition behind *why* it occurs could be further elaborated. A more intuitive explanation of the multiplicative control variate and how it specifically addresses the problem would be valuable. The discussion surrounding the computational cost, while mentioning faster convergence, could be more detailed – quantifying the runtime difference would be helpful. The experiments primarily focus on comparison with POEM. Exploring comparisons with other BLBF or off-policy evaluation methods (e.g., those based on direct method or doubly robust estimators) would further contextualize the performance of Norm-POEM. The explanation of the choice of L-BFGS optimization is somewhat brief; a more detailed justification, particularly regarding its suitability for non-convex and non-smooth objectives, would be beneficial. Finally, while the examples in Section 4 are illustrative, they feel somewhat contrived. A discussion of how Propensity Overfitting manifests in real-world datasets would strengthen the argument.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** Identifies and addresses a novel form of overfitting (Propensity Overfitting) in BLBF.
*   **Theoretical Soundness:** The self-normalized estimator is theoretically justified and shown to avoid the anomalies of existing estimators.
*   **Empirical Validation:** Extensive experiments demonstrate consistently improved performance compared to a strong baseline (POEM).
*   **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and clearly presents the problem, proposed solution, and experimental results.
*   **Reproducibility:** Code availability enhances reproducibility and encourages further research.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Intuition:** The underlying intuition of why Propensity Overfitting occurs could be strengthened.
*   **Comparison:** Limited comparison with alternative BLBF/off-policy evaluation methods.
*   **Computational Detail:** A more detailed analysis of the computational cost and runtime would be beneficial.
*   **Real-world Manifestation:** A discussion of how Propensity Overfitting manifests in real-world datasets is missing.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that presents a significant contribution to the field of batch learning from logged bandit feedback. The identified problem of Propensity Overfitting is novel and important, and the proposed solution is theoretically sound and empirically validated. While there are minor areas for improvement, the strengths of the paper outweigh the weaknesses. I recommend acceptance.
