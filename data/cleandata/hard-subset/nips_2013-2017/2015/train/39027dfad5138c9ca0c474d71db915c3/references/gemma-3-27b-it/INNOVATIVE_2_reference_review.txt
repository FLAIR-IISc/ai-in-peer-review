This paper tackles a crucial problem in leveraging logged data for improving interactive systems – batch learning from logged bandit feedback. The core idea, identifying and mitigating "Propensity Overfitting," is genuinely novel and a significant contribution. While the presentation is dense, the authors convincingly argue that standard counterfactual estimators suffer from a previously unobserved bias, and their proposed Self-Normalized Estimator elegantly addresses this issue. The connection to multiplicative control variates and self-normalization techniques from other fields (finance, RL) is well-motivated. The empirical results, while presented in a fairly standard “supervised-to-bandit” setup, demonstrate consistent improvements over the baseline POEM algorithm, and the ablation studies regarding variance regularization and runtime are valuable.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The identification of Propensity Overfitting is a genuinely new insight, exposing a flaw in existing approaches to batch counterfactual learning.
*   **Theoretical Soundness:** The argument for why the self-normalized estimator avoids this overfitting is clearly presented and builds on established concepts. Theorem 2 provides a solid guarantee of consistency.
*   **Elegance:** The solution—employing a self-normalized estimator—is a clean and conceptually appealing fix. Connecting it to existing work in importance sampling strengthens this.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The experimental results consistently show improved performance compared to a strong baseline (POEM), and the ablation studies provide insightful analysis.
*   **Practical Relevance:** The problem addressed is highly relevant to many real-world applications involving log data (recommendation systems, ad platforms, etc.).

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Presentation:** The paper is quite dense and requires significant effort from the reader to fully grasp the nuances. The mathematical notation is heavy, and the flow could be improved.
*   **Limited Scope of Experiments:** The experimental setup, while well-established, relies on a specific “supervised-to-bandit” transformation. It would be beneficial to see results on more diverse datasets or in a more realistic offline evaluation setting.
*   **Variance Estimation Complexity:**  While the authors acknowledge it, the computational cost of the variance estimate in Norm-POEM (Equation 8) is a concern, and the claim of faster convergence needs further investigation.
*   **Lack of Detailed Error Analysis:** While the paper demonstrates *that* Norm-POEM performs better, a more detailed analysis of *why* – in terms of the specific biases being reduced – would be valuable.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

*   The paper identifies a novel and important problem in counterfactual learning.
*   The proposed solution is theoretically sound and empirically validated.
*   The work has the potential to significantly impact the field of batch reinforcement learning and offline policy evaluation.
*   The inherent novelty of the contribution outweighs some minor weaknesses in presentation and experimental scope.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The paper is dense and may be difficult for some readers to fully understand.
*   The experimental setup is somewhat limited in its scope.
*   The computational complexity of the variance estimation could be a practical barrier.

**Overall Assessment:**

Despite some minor weaknesses, this paper presents a significant and novel contribution to the field of batch counterfactual learning. The identification of Propensity Overfitting is a crucial insight, and the proposed Self-Normalized Estimator provides an elegant and effective solution. I highly value the boldness of tackling this problem and the theoretical and empirical support for the proposed approach. The paper’s impact is likely to be high, as it addresses a fundamental limitation of existing methods.

**Recommendation:**

**Accept.** I recommend accepting this paper, despite the density of the presentation and the limited scope of the experiments. The novelty and potential impact of the work are substantial, and the authors demonstrate a clear understanding of the problem and a convincing solution.  I suggest the authors consider simplifying the presentation and expanding the experimental evaluation in future work.
