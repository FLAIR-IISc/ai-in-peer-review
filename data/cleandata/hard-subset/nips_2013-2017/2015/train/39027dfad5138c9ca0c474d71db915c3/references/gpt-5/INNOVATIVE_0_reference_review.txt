Summary
The paper tackles batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF), focusing on the degeneracy of the standard inverse propensity score (IPS) risk estimator when used inside ERM/CRM. The authors diagnose a phenomenon they call “propensity overfitting,” where hypotheses exploit variability in importance weights rather than genuine signal in losses, leading to pathological empirical risks (unbounded, non-equivariant to translation) and poor generalization. They propose replacing the vanilla IPS with a multiplicative control-variate approach—the self-normalized importance sampling estimator (SNIS), yielding a self-normalized risk estimator that is bounded, shift-equivariant, and strongly consistent. They integrate this estimator into the CRM framework to produce Norm-POEM, derive a practical variance estimate via a delta/normal approximation, and optimize a nonconvex objective using L-BFGS. Experiments on multi-label classification datasets using a supervised-to-bandit protocol show improved test performance, robustness to loss translations, reduced weight-degeneracy (Ŝ(h) ≈ 1), and competitive runtimes compared to POEM.

Relation to prior work
The work builds on counterfactual risk minimization (POEM) and a long line of IS-based off-policy evaluation methods. The self-normalized estimator is well-known in Monte Carlo, importance sampling, and off-policy evaluation, and has been used in RL and finance. The novelty here is (i) identifying and formalizing propensity overfitting as a concrete degeneracy when using IPS within ERM/CRM, (ii) advocating and validating SNIS as the risk estimator for learning (not only evaluation), and (iii) showing practical gains with a structured predictor (CRF-like). The paper situates itself reasonably in the literature but could more directly relate to weighted importance sampling (WIS), weighted/doubly robust (WDR/DR) estimators and recent off-policy evaluation advances.

Strengths
- Clear and important problem: propensity overfitting is compelling, well-motivated by examples, and empirically observed.
- Simple, principled fix: using a multiplicative control variate leading to a self-normalized estimator is elegant and addresses the key anomalies (boundedness and shift equivariance).
- Theoretical sanity: proofs of consistency and properties; discussion of variance and clipping when necessary.
- Practical algorithm (Norm-POEM): integrates SNIS into CRM with a tractable variance proxy and optimization; same modeling capacity as POEM.
- Empirical evidence: consistent and nontrivial improvements across multiple datasets and sample sizes; strong robustness to loss translation; diagnostic Ŝ(ĥ) near 1 indicates reduced propensity overfitting; runtime competitive or better due to faster convergence.
- Clarity: overall well-written and easy to follow; the motivating examples are effective.

Weaknesses and questions
- Missing finite-sample learning guarantees for SNIS-CRM: Theorem 1 (empirical Bernstein bound) applies to clipped IPS; an analogous finite-sample bound for the self-normalized estimator (with clipping) is not provided. This leaves a gap between the proposed objective and a formal generalization guarantee.
- Variance estimator and optimization: The variance used for CRM is an asymptotic/delta-method proxy, and the objective is nonconvex. While empirically fine, more discussion on when the normal approximation is reliable and on sensitivity to clipping M would strengthen the case.
- Baselines: No comparisons to doubly robust or weighted doubly robust methods, which are known to be more stable than IPS; normalization and DR can be combined (WDR). This is especially relevant since the claimed robustness is a hallmark of DR/WDR.
- Experimental scope: All experiments use supervised-to-bandit simulation with a stochastic CRF logger of full support; real logged data or settings with sparser support would be informative. How does Norm-POEM behave when support violations are handled only via clipping?
- Novelty relative to WIS/WDR: The idea of ratio normalization to control weight degeneracy is established. The chief novelty is deploying SNIS inside learning/CRM and diagnosing its benefits. It would help to articulate how this differs from using WDR for learning and whether a “Norm-POEM + DR” variant yields further gains.
- Minor clarity issues/typos: Section 4 contains “δ(·, ·) ∈ [5,4]” (likely a sign typo). Some references are incomplete. Please clarify the exact conditions for Theorem 2 (bounded losses, finite second moments with clipping).

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide a finite-sample generalization bound for the SNIS-based objective (with clipping), possibly via empirical Bernstein or PAC-Bayes for ratio estimators, to mirror POEM’s guarantees.
- Include comparisons to DR and WDR (and potentially SWITCH/MIS) under the same hypothesis class and optimization regime.
- Add sensitivity analyses for M and λ, and report stability under more extreme propensities (e.g., colder logging policies).
- Explore a hybrid CRM penalty that directly regularizes deviations of Ŝ(h) from 1 alongside variance regularization.
- Evaluate on at least one real logged bandit dataset to demonstrate practical impact.

Significance
The paper addresses a real and underappreciated failure mode of IPS-based learning from logs and offers a simple, broadly applicable fix with clear theoretical and empirical merits. Given the prevalence of off-policy learning in recommender systems, search, and RL, this contribution is likely to influence practice and inspire follow-up work (e.g., combining normalization with DR, developing finite-sample guarantees for ratio-based CRM).

Recommendation
Accept. While the core estimator is known, the diagnosis of propensity overfitting and the integration of self-normalization into the learning objective constitute a meaningful and practically important advance. The empirical gains and robustness are convincing. Strengthening the theoretical guarantees and baseline comparisons would further improve the paper, but the conceptual contribution and demonstrated impact justify acceptance.

Confidence
Moderate.
