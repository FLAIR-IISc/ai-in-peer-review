This paper addresses the problem of batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF). The authors identify a specific failure mode of existing methods based on Counterfactual Risk Minimization (CRM), which they term "Propensity Overfitting". This occurs when a model with high capacity minimizes the empirical risk not by fitting the loss, but by manipulating the inverse propensity weights. To address this, they propose replacing the standard unbiased inverse propensity score (IPS) estimator with the self-normalized importance sampling estimator. They incorporate this estimator into the CRM framework to create a new algorithm, Norm-POEM. The theoretical analysis shows that this new estimator is bounded, equivariant to loss translations, and strongly consistent. Empirical results on several multi-label classification datasets demonstrate that Norm-POEM outperforms its predecessor (POEM) and is robust to the issues that define Propensity Overfitting.

### Strengths:

1.  **Clear Problem Identification:** The paper provides a very clear and compelling motivation for its work. The "Propensity Overfitting" problem is illustrated with a simple yet powerful example in Section 4 that highlights the pathologies of the standard unbiased risk estimator when used for minimization. This is a valuable conceptual contribution.

2.  **Principled Solution:** The proposed solution—using the self-normalized estimator—is not an ad-hoc fix but is well-grounded in the importance sampling literature. The authors correctly identify the estimator's desirable properties (equivariance, bounded range) and show how they directly counteract the identified problem.

3.  **Strong Empirical Validation:** The experimental setup is well-designed, using the established "Supervised to Bandit" protocol which allows for controlled evaluation. The results are convincing: Table 1 and Figure 1 show consistent performance gains over the baseline, and Table 2 provides direct evidence that Norm-POEM solves the propensity overfitting problem by being robust to loss translations, a key claim of the paper.

### Weaknesses:

Despite the clear contribution and strong results, the paper suffers from a significant number of technical inaccuracies, minor errors, and presentational issues that detract from its overall quality. A paper at this level should be held to a higher standard of rigor and precision.

1.  **Technical Sloppiness:** Several key mathematical expressions and definitions contain errors.
    *   In the definition of the deterministic hypothesis in Example 1 (Section 4), the function is incomplete: `hf(y|x) = { if f(x) = y 0 otherwise.` The value for the first case is missing (it should be 1). This is a critical definition for understanding the core motivating example.
    *   The derivation of E[Ŝ(h)] = 1 in Equation (5) contains an incorrect integral. The expression `∫ 1 Pr(xi)dxi` is nonsensical. The integral should be over `yi`, and the correct derivation would be `E[h(yi|xi)/h0(yi|xi)] = ∫ (h(y|x)/h0(y|x)) * h0(y|x) dy = ∫ h(y|x) dy = 1`. Such a fundamental error in a key expectation is concerning.
    *   The claim in Example 1 that `R̂(hoverfit) ≤ −k ln` is presented without clear justification and the notation `ln` is ambiguous. A more careful derivation or explanation is needed.

2.  **Overstated Claims and Lack of Nuance:**
    *   The paper claims the self-normalized estimator "resolves all the problems" of the unbiased estimator. This is an overstatement. The self-normalized (ratio) estimator is known to be biased for finite samples, a crucial trade-off that the paper fails to discuss. The analysis is limited to asymptotic consistency (Theorem 2), which ignores this important finite-sample bias characteristic.
    *   The novelty of "Propensity Overfitting" is perhaps slightly exaggerated. While the framing within an ERM context is useful, the underlying issues of high variance and instability of the IPS estimator are well-known in the off-policy evaluation and causal inference literature. Similarly, the self-normalized estimator is a classic technique. The paper's novelty lies in the specific application and analysis for *learning* in the BLBF setting, which should be stated with more precision.

3.  **Minor Presentation and Formatting Issues:**
    *   The formatting of references is highly inconsistent. Some entries have full first names, others have initials. Some list the conference or journal, while others do not.
    *   There are minor notational inconsistencies, such as using `pi ' 0` instead of `pi ≈ 0` (Section 3) and the unusual arrow `7→` in the definition of the loss function.
    *   The description of the Norm-POEM objective in Section 6 is slightly ambiguous. The equations for the estimator (7) and variance (8) are uncapped, but the text states that weight capping is necessary to guarantee finite variance and that the method is "analogous to R̂M(h)". It should be made explicit how the capping `M` is incorporated into Equations (7) and (8).

### Summary of Pros and Cons:

**Pro:**
*   Identifies and clearly explains an important failure mode in BLBF (Propensity Overfitting).
*   Proposes an elegant and principled solution using a well-established statistical estimator.
*   Provides strong and convincing empirical evidence supporting the proposed method's effectiveness and robustness.

**Con:**
*   Contains several technical errors in key definitions and derivations (Example 1, Eq. 5).
*   Overstates claims about the proposed estimator by ignoring its finite-sample bias.
*   Slightly overstates the novelty of the underlying concepts (instability of IPS, ratio estimators).
*   Exhibits a general lack of polish in its presentation, including inconsistent references and minor notational issues.

### Decision:

While the core idea of this paper is valuable and the empirical results are strong, the number of technical errors and the lack of precision in its theoretical exposition are too substantial to ignore. These are not merely cosmetic issues; they undermine the reader's confidence in the technical soundness of the work. A paper presenting a principled, theoretically-grounded method must itself be technically flawless. The identified flaws, particularly the incorrect integral in a core proof and the incomplete definition in the main motivating example, are significant. Therefore, I cannot recommend acceptance in its current form. However, the work shows considerable promise. If the authors were to perform a thorough revision to correct all technical inaccuracies, moderate their claims, and polish the presentation, a future submission would be very compelling.

**Recommendation: Reject**
