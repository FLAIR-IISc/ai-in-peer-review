

# Review of "The Self-Normalized Estimator for Counterfactual Learning"

This paper proposes a "Self-Normalized Risk Estimator" to address a purported problem called "Propensity Overfitting" in batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF). The authors claim that conventional counterfactual estimators suffer from this issue, leading to degenerate risk estimates during Empirical Risk Minimization (ERM), and propose replacing the standard importance sampling estimator with a self-normalized version that they claim avoids these anomalies.

## Summary and Relation to Previous Work

The paper builds on established work in BLBF, particularly the Counterfactual Risk Minimization (CRM) framework introduced in prior NIPS/ICML papers (e.g., [1, 6, 13]). The authors identify what they call "Propensity Overfitting" - a form of overfitting where the standard estimator can produce risk estimates arbitrarily lower than the true risk, even when the loss function is bounded. They propose using a self-normalized estimator (previously studied in importance sampling literature [12, 19, 22, 23]) as a remedy, and develop a new algorithm called Norm-POEM based on this estimator.

While the self-normalized estimator has been discussed in the importance sampling literature (e.g., [12, 19, 22]), the paper's contribution is limited to applying it to BLBF and identifying Propensity Overfitting as a problem specific to this context. The authors don't provide sufficient evidence that Propensity Overfitting is a significant issue in real-world BLBF applications, rather than a contrived pathology.

## Strengths

1. The paper is clearly written and well-structured.
2. The theoretical analysis (Theorem 2) shows that the self-normalized estimator is strongly consistent, which is a valid theoretical property.
3. The empirical evaluation on four standard datasets (Scene, Yeast, TMC, LYRL) provides some evidence of improved performance over POEM.

## Weaknesses

1. **Questionable Problem Definition**: The "Propensity Overfitting" problem is demonstrated through a highly contrived example (Example 1) that relies on pathological conditions unlikely to occur in real-world applications. In particular, the example requires a deterministic hypothesis space with full support over a large output space, and a logging policy with uniform distribution over outputs. This is an artificial setup that doesn't reflect typical bandit feedback scenarios where the logging policy is not uniform and the hypothesis space is constrained. The authors fail to demonstrate that this problem occurs in realistic settings.

2. **Insufficient Empirical Validation**: The experimental validation is limited to four datasets from the LibSVM repository, and the bandit feedback is simulated using a CRF trained on 5% of the supervised data. This is a very specific setup that doesn't reflect the diversity of real-world logging systems. The improvements over POEM are marginal (Table 1 shows differences of 0.01-0.03 in Hamming loss), and the paper doesn't compare against other state-of-the-art BLBF methods beyond POEM. The authors claim "significant improvements" but provide no statistical significance testing to support this claim.

3. **Theoretical Claims Overstated**: The paper claims the self-normalized estimator "provably avoids" Propensity Overfitting, but the only theoretical result (Theorem 2) shows consistency, not that the estimator avoids Propensity Overfitting in finite samples. The paper provides no theoretical proof that their estimator avoids Propensity Overfitting, only empirical evidence that it performs better in their specific experimental setup.

4. **Lack of Practical Justification**: The authors acknowledge that the self-normalized estimator has higher computational cost (Section 6), yet they claim it "surprisingly" converges faster than POEM. They provide no evidence that this speedup is consistent across different datasets or problem sizes. The marginal performance gains do not appear to justify the added complexity.

5. **Insufficient Comparison to Existing Work**: The paper doesn't sufficiently distinguish its contribution from prior work on self-normalized importance sampling. While they cite [19] and [22], they don't fully acknowledge that the self-normalized estimator has been used in other contexts (e.g., finance, reinforcement learning) without identifying Propensity Overfitting as a new problem.

## Recommendation

This paper presents a limited contribution that builds on well-established techniques (self-normalized importance sampling) without providing sufficient evidence that the problem they identify (Propensity Overfitting) is significant in realistic BLBF applications. The empirical validation is insufficient, the theoretical claims are overstated, and the practical significance of the proposed method is questionable given the marginal performance improvements and increased computational cost.

While the self-normalized estimator is a valid technique, the paper fails to demonstrate that it solves a meaningful problem in the BLBF context that isn't already addressed by existing methods. The authors have not provided the extensive evidence and thorough validation required to justify a new methodological contribution.

I recommend rejection. The paper would be strengthened by:
1. Demonstrating Propensity Overfitting in realistic bandit feedback scenarios
2. Providing a more comprehensive comparison against state-of-the-art BLBF methods
3. Offering a rigorous theoretical proof that the estimator avoids Propensity Overfitting
4. Conducting experiments on more diverse datasets with real-world logging policies

As a reviewer who prioritizes established methods and rigorous validation, I find this paper's claims unsubstantiated by sufficient evidence. The contribution appears to be an incremental application of a known technique rather than a significant advancement in the field.
