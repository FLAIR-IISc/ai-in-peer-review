## Review of "The Self-Normalized Estimator for Counterfactual Learning"

**Summary:**

This paper tackles a crucial problem in learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF): propensity overfitting, a novel form of overfitting arising from the use of standard importance sampling-based counterfactual risk estimators. The authors demonstrate that this overfitting can lead to severely biased risk estimates and poor generalization.  They propose a self-normalized risk estimator, borrowed from the financial mathematics and reinforcement learning literature, which mitigates this issue by being equivariant to loss translations and inherently bounded.  They support their theoretical analysis with extensive empirical results on several datasets, demonstrating improved performance and robustness compared to existing methods like POEM.

**Relation to Prior Work:**

The paper sits squarely within the growing body of work on BLBF, closely related to off-policy evaluation in RL and causal inference. It builds upon previous approaches utilizing counterfactual estimators (e.g., [6, 5]) and the CRM principle [1].  The authors effectively position their work against these prior approaches, highlighting the limitations of existing techniques and the novelty of their proposed solution.  The connection to self-normalized estimators from other fields (finance, RL) is well-explained, and they properly acknowledge the related literature on importance sampling and variance reduction techniques. The paper thoughtfully contrasts propensity overfitting with traditional loss overfitting, clarifying the specific challenges in the BLBF context.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The identification and analysis of "propensity overfitting" is a significant contribution.  This is a subtle but crucial point, highlighting a fundamental limitation of existing BLBF algorithms.
* **Theoretical Soundness:**  The analysis convincingly explains why standard estimators fail and provides a theoretical justification for the improved performance of the self-normalized estimator. The theorem proving consistency is valuable.
* **Practical Impact:**  The proposed self-normalized estimator, implemented in Norm-POEM, demonstrably improves performance across multiple datasets. The observation that it *also* appears to be computationally more efficient than previous methods is a significant bonus.
* **Clarity and Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The motivation is clearly articulated, the technical details are well-explained, and the experimental setup is thorough. The examples illustrating propensity overfitting (Example 1) are particularly helpful.
* **Thorough Experimentation:** The experiments are well-designed, following the established Supervised->Bandit methodology and including comprehensive comparisons with POEM and a CRF baseline. The ablation study examining the necessity of variance regularization is insightful.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Computational Complexity:** While the authors report faster convergence, a deeper dive into the computational complexity of the variance estimation in Equation (8) would be helpful.  The statement that it "does not admit stochastic optimization" raises concerns, although they acknowledge its practical efficiency.  Specifically, how does the computational cost scale with the size of the dataset and the complexity of the hypothesis space?
* **Parameter Sensitivity:** The choice of the clipping parameter *M* is crucial.  The paper mentions calibration on a hold-out set, but doesn't discuss the sensitivity of the results to this parameter. A more detailed analysis of *M*'s impact on performance would strengthen the work.
* **Limited Scope of Experiments:** While the experiments are comprehensive, they focus exclusively on datasets used in previous work [1]. Exploring datasets with different characteristics (e.g., higher dimensionality, more complex relationships between features and labels) could further validate the robustness of Norm-POEM.
* **Norm-POEM details:** The paper refers to using L-BFGS for optimization.  Details on the specific implementation (learning rate schedule, convergence criteria) would increase reproducibility.



**Arguments for and against acceptance:**

**Pro:**

* Addresses a fundamental problem in BLBF (propensity overfitting).
* Proposes a theoretically sound and practically effective solution.
* Demonstrates clear improvements over existing state-of-the-art methods.
* Well-written and clearly presented.
* Solid empirical validation.

**Con:**

* Computational complexity of variance estimation warrants further investigation.
* Limited exploration of parameter sensitivity (especially *M*).
* Experiments could benefit from broader dataset coverage.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of learning from logged bandit feedback. The identification of propensity overfitting is a key insight, and the proposed self-normalized estimator provides a compelling solution. The theoretical analysis is solid, and the experimental results are convincing. While there are some minor weaknesses regarding computational complexity and parameter sensitivity, they do not detract significantly from the overall quality of the work. 

**Recommendation:**

**Accept with minor revisions.** 

I recommend acceptance, contingent on the authors addressing the following:

1.  **Expand the discussion of computational complexity:** Provide a more detailed analysis of the computational cost of the variance estimation in Equation (8).
2.  **Investigate parameter sensitivity:**  Include a brief analysis of the sensitivity of the results to the choice of the clipping parameter *M*.
3. **Clarify Implementation details:** Add a brief section describing the specific implementation details of L-BFGS optimization in Norm-POEM.



This paper is a valuable addition to the NIPS program and has the potential to significantly impact research in BLBF and related areas.
