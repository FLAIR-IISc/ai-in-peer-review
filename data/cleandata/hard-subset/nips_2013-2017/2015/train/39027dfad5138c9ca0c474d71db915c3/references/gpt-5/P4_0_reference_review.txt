Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF) and highlights a failure mode of the standard inverse propensity score (IPS) estimator when used inside empirical risk minimization (ERM). The authors term this failure “propensity overfitting”: ERM can minimize IPS-based risk by manipulating importance weights rather than aligning with low-loss outcomes, yielding degenerate risk estimates that can lie outside the loss range and are not translation-equivariant. They propose a multiplicative control variate approach leading to the self-normalized importance sampling estimator (SNIPS) as a replacement for IPS within the Counterfactual Risk Minimization (CRM) framework. They show SNIPS is bounded and translation-equivariant, and give a consistency statement (Theorem 2). Building on POEM (Swaminathan & Joachims, ICML), they instantiate Norm-POEM: stochastic linear policies (CRF-like), CRM objective with an approximate variance estimate for SNIPS (via Normal/delta method), weight clipping to control variance, and L-BFGS optimization. Using supervised→bandit simulations on multi-label datasets, they report that Norm-POEM consistently improves generalization over POEM, is robust to loss translations, avoids extreme Ŝ(h) values (sum of weights), and converges faster in practice.

The work is related to causal inference (propensity scoring), domain adaptation via importance weighting, and off-policy evaluation in RL. The estimator is a well-known “weighted/normalized importance sampling” (Kong 1992; Hesterberg 1995; Rubinstein & Kroese; Owen), used in policy evaluation and in policy optimization contexts (e.g., TRPO employs normalized ratios locally). Doubly robust estimators and clipping-based IPS variants have been studied for variance reduction; the paper argues that multiplicative control variates (SNIPS) directly address propensity overfitting and integrates them into a learning algorithm for BLBF.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The failure mode of IPS inside ERM is convincingly illustrated (Example 1), including dependence on loss translation and on the sum of weights Ŝ(h). The use of SNIPS (ratio estimator) to restore boundedness and equivariance is correct, and the almost-sure consistency claim follows from the Strong Law. The approximate variance formula for SNIPS is standard but relies on asymptotic normality and finite variance; the paper mitigates with clipping. However, the generalization error bound motivating CRM (Theorem 1) applies to the clipped IPS estimator, not to SNIPS. The paper does not provide an analogous high-probability bound for the ratio estimator or prove that the combined Norm-POEM objective inherits the CRM guarantees. As a result, the theoretical support for the learning objective is incomplete (though the estimator-level properties are clear).
- Empirics: The experiments follow the setup in prior BLBF work and show consistent improvements over POEM, including diagnostics on Ŝ(h) and robustness to loss translation. Comparisons are limited to POEM; important baselines such as doubly robust (DR), weighted DR, SWITCH/DRos, or other clipping/truncation schemes are missing. Confidence intervals or statistical tests are not reported. Evaluation is confined to supervised→bandit simulations on multi-label datasets; no real logged data or RL domains are presented. Reporting of runtime and convergence is qualitative.

Clarity
The manuscript is generally clear and well-organized. The exposition of propensity overfitting, the role of Ŝ(h) as a diagnostic, and the properties of SNIPS are easy to follow. The term “equivariant” is defined informally but sufficiently. One limitation is that the CRM bound is cited for IPS while the proposed algorithm uses SNIPS; this distinction should be made explicit earlier, with a discussion of what is and is not theoretically guaranteed. Some references are incomplete; standardize citation formatting.

Originality
Self-normalized importance sampling is not new, nor is its use in off-policy evaluation/optimization. The novelty here lies in: (i) identifying and formalizing “propensity overfitting” as a distinct degeneracy of IPS-ERM, (ii) arguing that SNIPS addresses it in learning, and (iii) integrating SNIPS into the CRM-based training (Norm-POEM) with an empirical variance penalty. This is an incremental but meaningful contribution, particularly the clear diagnosis and the empirical demonstration within BLBF.

Significance
If widely adopted, replacing IPS with SNIPS in BLBF objectives could improve robustness, especially under high weight variability and in practice where loss translations occur. The paper provides evidence on standard datasets and clarifies a pitfall that practitioners may overlook. However, the absence of a SNIPS-specific generalization bound and limited baselines reduces the theoretical and empirical breadth. Overall significance is moderate.

Pros
- Clear identification and demonstration of “propensity overfitting” for IPS-ERM.
- Sound proposal of SNIPS to regain boundedness and translation equivariance.
- Consistency of SNIPS formally stated; intuitive variance estimator provided.
- Empirical gains over POEM across datasets; improved diagnostics (Ŝ(h) near 1) and robustness to loss translation.
- Practical training recipe (L-BFGS) and code availability.

Cons
- No high-probability generalization bound for the SNIPS-based objective; CRM guarantee does not directly carry over.
- Empirical comparison limited to POEM; omits DR and recent robust OPE/OPL baselines.
- Simulated logging only; no real logged data or RL tasks; no ablations on clipping M and λ sensitivity; no statistical significance reporting.
- Variance estimator relies on asymptotics; conditions for finite variance and the impact of clipping not fully analyzed.
- Claims that SNIPS “provably avoids degeneracies” are only partially formalized (estimator-level properties, not learning-level guarantees).

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide a generalization error bound for the SNIPS-based objective (e.g., ratio concentration or PAC-Bayes/empirical Bernstein style bounds adapted to self-normalized estimators), or clearly delimit the scope of guarantees.
- Add baselines: DR, WDR, SWITCH/DRos, clipped IPS variants, and report statistical significance.
- Evaluate on at least one real logged bandit dataset and, if possible, an RL off-policy evaluation/learning setting.
- Include sensitivity analyses for M and λ, and report runtime quantitatively.
- Discuss conditions ensuring finite variance under clipping and how they affect bias.
- Standardize references and complete missing bibliographic details.

Overall assessment
The paper presents a well-motivated and practically useful modification to CRM-based BLBF by substituting IPS with SNIPS, backed by a clear diagnosis of IPS-induced degeneracies and consistent empirical improvements over a strong prior method. The main weakness is the lack of a SNIPS-specific generalization bound and limited empirical breadth. With additional theoretical support or broader comparisons, the contribution would be stronger.
