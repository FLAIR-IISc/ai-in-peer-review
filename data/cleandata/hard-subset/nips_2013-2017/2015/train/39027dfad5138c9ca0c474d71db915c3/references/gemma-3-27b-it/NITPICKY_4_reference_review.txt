## Review of "The Self-Normalized Estimator for Counterfactual Learning"

This paper addresses a critical problem in learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF): propensity overfitting, a newly identified phenomenon causing biased risk estimates when employing standard counterfactual estimators. The authors propose a self-normalized estimator, drawing inspiration from financial mathematics and reinforcement learning, and demonstrate both theoretically and empirically its superiority over existing methods.

The core idea—using a multiplicative control variate to address the instability of importance sampling weights—is clever and potentially impactful. The paper is structured logically, starting with a clear introduction to BLBF and its challenges, proceeding to related work, a detailed formalization of the problem, the proposed solution, and finally, comprehensive experiments. The connection to existing literature in causal inference, domain adaptation, and off-policy evaluation is well established.

However, while the ideas are promising, the paper suffers from a multitude of shortcomings, many stemming from a lack of precision and an overreliance on hand-waving arguments. The theoretical contributions, while present, are presented in a somewhat incomplete and opaque manner.  For example, Theorem 1 simply *refers* to theorems in prior work without providing sufficient detail to assess its applicability or the underlying assumptions.  The proof sketch is also insufficient.  The crucial step of establishing the boundedness of the variance in the self-normalized estimator, required for asymptotic normality (and hence the validity of many subsequent arguments), is acknowledged but not rigorously demonstrated. The statement that it can be “guaranteed” by thresholding feels like a post-hoc justification rather than a central part of the theoretical development.

The experimental section, while extensive, is also problematic.  The choice of datasets is standard, but the description of the experimental setup lacks crucial details. For instance, the statement "h0 has varying performance (trained on random 5% subsets)" is vague; what is the range of performance achieved?  How does this variation impact the results?  The claim of faster convergence of Norm-POEM is intriguing but not supported by sufficiently detailed runtime comparisons (e.g., plots showing convergence curves). The impact of the hyperparameter M (clipping threshold) is not thoroughly explored – a sensitivity analysis is missing. The claim that the self-normalized estimator is unaffected by translations of the loss function, while theoretically sound, is demonstrated with a rather contrived example.  A more robust demonstration across different loss functions and datasets would be more convincing.  Furthermore, the statement that “Norm-POEM consistently beats POEM” is too strong given the limited number of runs (10) and the potentially high variance in the results. The statistical significance of these improvements isn't adequately addressed.

The writing quality is uneven. While the introduction is well-written, sections detailing the theoretical aspects become dense and difficult to follow.  There are numerous instances of imprecise language (e.g., “sufficiently large k,” “surprisingly faster”). The use of mathematical notation is inconsistent. For example, δ(x,y) is sometimes treated as a random variable and sometimes as a deterministic function, leading to confusion. There are also minor formatting issues throughout (e.g., inconsistent spacing, slightly awkward phrasing).

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Addresses a significant and relevant problem in BLBF.
*   Proposes a novel and potentially effective solution (self-normalized estimator).
*   Extensive experimental evaluation suggesting performance improvements.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   Theoretical development is incomplete and lacks sufficient rigor.
*   Experimental evaluation lacks detail and statistical rigor.
*   Writing quality is uneven and contains numerous imprecisions.
*   Overstated claims and a lack of thorough sensitivity analysis.
*   Numerous minor formatting and notational inconsistencies.



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite the potential of the idea, the paper, in its current form, falls short of NIPS standards. The theoretical contributions need to be significantly strengthened, the experimental evaluation needs to be more rigorous and detailed, and the writing needs to be clarified. The paper feels rushed and lacks the polish expected of a NIPS submission.  The sheer number of minor issues distracts from the core contribution. 

**Recommendation:** **Reject**.  I recommend rejection, but encourage the authors to address the identified weaknesses and resubmit a revised version.  A significant overhaul, especially of the theoretical foundations and experimental validation, is necessary.
