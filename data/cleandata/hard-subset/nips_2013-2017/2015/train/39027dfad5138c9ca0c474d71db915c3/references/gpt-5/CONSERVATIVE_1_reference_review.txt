Summary
The paper addresses batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF) and argues that the standard inverse propensity score (IPS) estimator used within counterfactual risk minimization (CRM) can lead to a new failure mode the authors term Propensity Overfitting. They propose using a multiplicative control variate (the sum of importance weights) to form a self-normalized importance sampling (SNIS) estimator for risk, and then plug this estimator and an approximate variance into a CRM-style objective, yielding “Norm-POEM.” They highlight three properties of the SN estimator: boundedness, translation equivariance, and strong consistency, and argue these mitigate the IPS anomalies that cause propensity overfitting. Experiments on supervised-to-bandit variants of several multilabel datasets show Norm-POEM outperforms POEM and is robust to loss shifts; diagnostics based on the sum of weights Ŝ(h) suggest Norm-POEM avoids the failure mode.

Relation to prior work
The work is grounded in importance sampling for off-policy evaluation, with references to clipping, control variates, and doubly robust estimators. The proposed estimator is the well-known self-normalized IS (also called weighted IS) from the Monte Carlo literature and has been used in RL policy evaluation. The novelty claimed lies in (i) identifying “propensity overfitting” in BLBF/CRM and (ii) integrating SNIS into a CRM-style learning objective.

Strengths
- Clear articulation and demonstration (via a constructed example) of a real pitfall with IPS-based ERM in BLBF.
- Using SNIS addresses two practical anomalies: lack of bounding and non-equivariance under loss translations; these are standard but important points for learning with weighted samples.
- Empirical results consistently outperform POEM on the tested settings; the diagnostic Ŝ(h) analysis is convincing and aligns with the stated motivation.
- The proposed method is simple, uses the same hyperparameters as POEM, and seems practically robust.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Limited novelty: The core estimator (SNIS) and its properties (boundedness, translation equivariance, strong consistency) are classical. Theorem 2 is a direct application of SLLN for ratios. The main conceptual contribution is naming and illustrating “propensity overfitting,” but there is little new theory beyond standard SNIS facts.
- Lack of finite-sample/generalization guarantees with SNIS in CRM: The CRM bound (Theorem 1) is inherited from prior work for clipped IPS; the paper does not provide an analogous uniform generalization bound for the self-normalized estimator. The variance term used (Eq. 8) is asymptotic/approximate (delta-method/Normal approximation), and it is unclear whether the resulting objective actually controls generalization error as in [1,13]. The claim that the SN estimator “provably avoids these degeneracies” is only partially supported (boundedness and equivariance) and not tied to a finite-sample ERM analysis across H.
- Baselines are too narrow: The paper compares to POEM and an unregularized Norm-IPS ablation. Missing are widely used and relevant alternatives for off-policy learning/evaluation: doubly robust policy learning/estimation, regression estimators/additive control variates, clipped/WIS baselines optimized similarly, and recent high-confidence OPE approaches. Since SNIS is standard, one would expect at least a DR baseline; without it, it’s difficult to assess practical advantage.
- Experimental scope: All results use supervised-to-bandit conversion on multilabel classification with factorized models (essentially independent labels). It is unclear whether the approach scales to more structured settings where partition functions are intractable. The synthetic logging setup (CRF trained on 5%) is standard but limited; there are no real logged datasets, no analysis under severe support mismatch, and no sensitivity studies for M (clipping) and λ. Claims about runtime and convergence are qualitative; quantitative timing comparisons and fairness controls (e.g., same optimizer and stopping criteria) should be reported.
- Methodological clarity: While the paper states that learning is “tractable whenever the corresponding supervised CRF can be learnt efficiently,” for the multilabel case this typically requires independence assumptions; these should be explicitly stated. The variance estimator’s validity depends on finite variance and asymptotic normality; conditions and diagnostics for when this holds in BLBF would strengthen the paper.
- Minor presentation issues: Some typos (e.g., δ ∈ [5,4]), incomplete citation details, and missing figures/tables in the submission text hinder verification.

Quality
Technically sound at the level of using SNIS and the approximate variance, and the propensity-overfitting example is instructive. However, there is a gap between the theoretical claims (robustness “provably avoids degeneracies”) and what is actually proved (classical SNIS properties); there is no finite-sample learning bound tailored to the proposed objective.

Clarity
Generally well-written and easy to follow. The core idea and its motivation are clear. The paper would benefit from more precise statements about modeling assumptions for structured outputs, clearer exposition of the optimization details and convergence criteria, and a more careful discussion of the bias–variance trade-off introduced by SNIS and clipping.

Originality
Moderate. Applying SNIS to BLBF/CRM and highlighting propensity overfitting is useful, but the estimator and properties are established, and the theoretical additions are light.

Significance
Potentially useful in practice for BLBF, but the current empirical scope and missing baselines limit the strength of the claim. Without a CRM-style bound for SNIS or stronger empirical comparisons, it is hard to conclude that this advances the state of the art beyond an incremental improvement over POEM.

Questions for the authors
- Can you provide a finite-sample generalization bound for the SNIS-based CRM objective (perhaps using empirical Bernstein-style arguments for ratio estimators)?
- How does Norm-POEM compare to doubly robust policy learning methods on your benchmarks?
- Please report sensitivity to M and λ, and to the quality/stochasticity of the logging policy, including severe support mismatch.
- Clarify the modeling assumptions that make Z(x) tractable in your structured setting; are labels modeled independently?
- Provide quantitative runtime numbers and iteration counts across methods.
- Can you include experiments on a real logged bandit dataset?

Pros
- Identifies and explains a real failure mode of IPS-based ERM in BLBF.
- Simple, well-motivated fix using a standard estimator; bounded and translation-equivariant.
- Consistent empirical improvements over POEM; useful diagnostic metric Ŝ(h).

Cons
- Core estimator and properties are not novel; theory beyond standard SNIS is minimal.
- No finite-sample generalization guarantees for the proposed learning objective.
- Missing strong baselines (e.g., DR), limited experimental scope, and lack of sensitivity analyses.

Recommendation
Reject (borderline). The idea is sensible and likely useful, but to meet NeurIPS standards, the paper needs (i) a CRM-relevant finite-sample analysis for the self-normalized objective, and/or (ii) stronger empirical validation including DR and other baselines, sensitivity analyses, and broader tasks or real logs. I encourage resubmission after addressing these points.

Confidence: High.
