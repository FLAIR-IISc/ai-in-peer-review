### Paper Summary

This paper addresses the problem of Batch Learning from Logged Bandit Feedback (BLBF), where the goal is to learn a new policy from historical interaction logs. The standard approach is to use Counterfactual Risk Minimization (CRM), which minimizes an importance-weighted risk estimate, typically regularized by the estimate's variance. The authors identify a critical and previously unarticulated failure mode of the standard Inverse Propensity Score (IPS) estimator used in CRM, which they term "Propensity Overfitting". This occurs when a high-capacity hypothesis space allows the learner to minimize the empirical risk not by finding actions with low loss, but by manipulating the importance weights themselves—either by assigning high probability to actions that happen to be in the training set (for negative losses) or by avoiding them entirely (for positive losses). This can lead to arbitrarily poor risk estimates and catastrophic generalization.

To solve this problem, the authors propose replacing the standard IPS estimator with the Self-Normalized Estimator, a well-known ratio estimator from the importance sampling literature. This estimator normalizes the weighted sum of losses by the sum of the importance weights. The paper shows that this estimator is equivariant to translations in the loss function and is always bounded by the range of the observed losses, thus directly resolving the anomalies that cause Propensity Overfitting. The authors then instantiate the CRM principle with this new estimator and its corresponding variance, creating a new learning algorithm called Norm-POEM. Extensive experiments on several multi-label classification datasets, converted to a bandit setting, demonstrate that Norm-POEM significantly outperforms its predecessor (POEM), is robust to loss translations, and effectively controls Propensity Overfitting, leading to superior generalization performance.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Strengths:**

1.  **Clear Identification of a Novel and Important Problem (Originality & Significance):** The core contribution of this paper is the identification and clear articulation of "Propensity Overfitting". This is a subtle but fundamental problem with applying ERM to importance-weighted objectives. The examples provided in Section 4 are simple, intuitive, and powerfully illustrate how standard CRM can fail in ways that are completely alien to supervised learning. This is a significant insight for the off-policy evaluation and learning community.

2.  **Principled and Elegant Solution (Quality):** The proposed solution—using the self-normalized estimator—is not an ad-hoc fix but a principled application of control variates from the Monte Carlo literature. The authors clearly explain why this estimator resolves the identified issues: it is equivariant to loss shifts and its estimates are bounded within the range of the observed losses. The connection to known statistical techniques lends credibility to the approach.

3.  **Strong and Convincing Empirical Evaluation (Quality):** The experiments are exceptionally well-designed. They go beyond simply showing that Norm-POEM has lower test error than POEM (Table 1, Figure 1). The analysis in Table 2 is the most compelling part of the evaluation, as it directly verifies the paper's central hypothesis. It shows that POEM indeed produces biased sums of propensity weights (`Ŝ(h)`) and is highly sensitive to loss translation, while Norm-POEM is stable and robust as predicted by theory. This provides strong evidence that the performance gains are indeed due to mitigating Propensity Overfitting.

4.  **Exceptional Clarity and Organization (Clarity):** The paper is extremely well-written and easy to follow. It starts by setting up the standard framework, clearly isolates the problem with a dedicated section and illustrative examples, presents the solution, and then validates it empirically. The logical flow is compelling and makes the paper's contributions easy to appreciate.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Novelty of the Estimator Itself:** As the authors correctly acknowledge, the self-normalized estimator is a standard tool in importance sampling. The novelty lies in diagnosing the specific problem of Propensity Overfitting in the context of BLBF and applying this estimator as the solution. While this is a very valuable contribution, the core technical tool is not new.

2.  **Computational Scalability:** The paper notes that the variance estimate for the self-normalized estimator (Eq. 8) does not readily admit stochastic optimization, requiring batch methods like L-BFGS. While the authors find it converges surprisingly fast on their datasets, this could be a limitation for massive-scale industrial applications where SGD is often the only feasible optimizer. A brief discussion of potential approximations or future work towards a stochastic version would have strengthened this aspect.

3.  **Simulated Experimental Setting:** The experiments use the "Supervised -> Bandit" simulation protocol. This is a standard and necessary methodology for controlled evaluation in this area, allowing for access to a ground truth for evaluation. However, the true challenges of real-world logged data (e.g., non-stationarity, complex logging policies) are not fully captured. Demonstrating the method's effectiveness on a large-scale real-world bandit dataset would make the results even more impactful, though the current experiments are already very strong.

### Detailed Comments

The diagnosis of Propensity Overfitting is the highlight of this work. It elegantly explains why practitioners using IPS-based learning might see puzzlingly poor performance, especially with flexible models. The insight that the learner is overfitting the sampling distribution (`p_i`) rather than the loss signal (`δ_i`) is crucial.

The choice of the self-normalized estimator as a remedy is very well-motivated. Its properties of equivariance and being range-respecting are precisely what is needed to plug the holes in the standard IPS estimator. The consistency proof (Theorem 2) provides the necessary theoretical reassurance.

The experiment showing that CRM variance regularization is still beneficial for the self-normalized estimator (Norm-POEM vs. Norm-IPS in Table 3) is a good inclusion, as it demonstrates that the two components—the improved estimator and the variance regularization—are complementary.

### Summary of Recommendation

This is an excellent paper that makes a clear and significant contribution to the field of counterfactual learning. It identifies a critical, previously overlooked problem ("Propensity Overfitting") in a popular learning framework and proposes a principled, effective, and elegant solution. The claims are strongly supported by a well-designed and compelling set of experiments. The paper is exceptionally well-written and a pleasure to read. Despite the core estimation technique being known in statistics, its application to solve this newly-identified problem in machine learning is highly novel and impactful. This work is likely to be influential and should be of great interest to both researchers and practitioners working with logged feedback data. I strongly recommend acceptance.
