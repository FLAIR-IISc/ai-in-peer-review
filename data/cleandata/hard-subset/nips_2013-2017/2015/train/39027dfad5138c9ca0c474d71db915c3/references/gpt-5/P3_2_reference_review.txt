Summary
The paper tackles batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF) and identifies a failure mode of inverse propensity scoring (IPS)-based empirical risk minimization: propensity overfitting. The root cause is that the standard IPS estimator is not range-bounded or equivariant under translations of the loss, allowing ERM to exploit the weight sum rather than structure in the loss. The authors propose using a multiplicative control variate leading to the self-normalized IPS (SNIPS) estimator for risk, which is bounded and equivariant, and show strong consistency. They instantiate a learning algorithm, Norm-POEM, by replacing the IPS estimator in the CRM framework with the self-normalized estimator and an approximate variance estimate, and optimize a nonconvex objective via L-BFGS. Experiments on multi-label classification datasets (supervised→bandit protocol) show consistent gains over POEM, better stability under loss translations, avoidance of the propensity overfitting signature Ŝ(h)≠1, and competitive runtimes.

Relation to prior work
The work sits at the intersection of off-policy evaluation in RL and domain adaptation via importance weighting. It builds on CRM and POEM (Swaminathan & Joachims) and connects to classic IS variance reduction (Hesterberg; Owen; Kong) and DR estimators (Dudík et al.). Self-normalized IS has long been used for evaluation; its explicit use inside learning objectives for BLBF and the identification of propensity overfitting in ERM are the main conceptual advances here. The paper cites relevant foundations (empirical Bernstein bounds; high-confidence OPE), though comparisons to modern DR/WDR and hybrid clipping-switch estimators are missing experimentally.

Strengths
- Clear identification and articulation of “propensity overfitting” with a crisp synthetic example and diagnostics via the weight-sum Ŝ(h); the link to IPS non-equivariance is insightful and broadly useful.
- The proposed fix (SNIPS) is principled, simple, and well grounded in IS literature. The boundedness and equivariance properties directly address the pathologies.
- Strong consistency proof is straightforward but necessary; the empirical results show practically meaningful and robust gains across datasets and under loss translations.
- The integration into CRM and a tractable instantiation (Norm-POEM) are well motivated; the empirical runtime observations are encouraging.
- The analysis and diagnostics (Ŝ(h) near 1 for Norm-POEM, large deviations for POEM) nicely support the main hypothesis.

Weaknesses and questions
- Theory for learning with SNIPS is underdeveloped. The CRM bound (Theorem 1) applies to capped IPS, not to the self-normalized ratio estimator. The paper then optimizes a CRM-like objective with an approximate variance (delta method/normal approximation) but provides no high-probability generalization bound that captures the finite-sample bias of SNIPS. At minimum, a bound decomposing bias and variance terms for SNIPS with clipping, uniform over H, would strengthen the contribution. Existing SNIS bias results (e.g., O(1/n) under moment conditions) could be adapted; high-probability self-normalized concentration exists in the IS literature and could be cited or derived.
- Limited baselines. A fair comparison should include doubly robust (DR) and weighted doubly robust (WDR) estimators, especially since DR is designed to reduce variance while maintaining small bias, and DR can be combined with self-normalization. Other competitive off-policy learners/evaluators (e.g., truncation/clipping alone, SWITCH-DR) are absent. This weakens claims of general superiority.
- The empirical setting is the supervised→bandit replay protocol with synthetic logging from a CRF trained on 5% of data. While standard, it does not test two crucial practical issues: (i) partial or no support (zero propensities), (ii) propensity misspecification/noise. The method still assumes full support (and uses clipping), but its behavior under insufficient coverage is not explored. Including experiments with degraded coverage and mis-logged propensities would increase practical relevance.
- The nonconvex optimization of a ratio objective can be delicate. The paper reports L-BFGS works well, but details on initialization, convergence diagnostics, and sensitivity are sparse. A brief ablation on initialization and the effect of λ and M would help.
- The variance estimator (Eq. 8) is approximate and relies on asymptotic normality; yet it is also used as a regularizer during training on the same data. Some discussion of its stability, especially under heavy-tailed weights and clipping, and empirical calibration (e.g., via bootstrap) would be valuable.
- Related work could better situate the approach w.r.t. conservative or trust-region policy improvement methods that explicitly regularize towards the logging policy (e.g., CPI/TRPO-style KL constraints). These methods also mitigate propensity overfitting; a conceptual comparison would be helpful.

Clarity
The paper is generally well written and organized. The example and the diagnostic Ŝ(h) are very helpful. A few suggestions:
- Explicitly distinguish three estimators early: IPS, clipped IPS, and SNIPS (with/without clipping), and consistently state which is used where.
- State assumptions for Theorem 2 (finite variance/moment conditions under clipping) precisely.
- Provide gradients of the Norm-POEM objective in the appendix for reproducibility.

Significance
The identification of propensity overfitting and the proposal to use self-normalization within learning are conceptually significant and practically impactful. The estimator’s boundedness/equivariance properties matter for ERM stability, and the empirical results corroborate the claims. With stronger theoretical guarantees for learning with SNIPS and broader baselines, this could set a standard practice in BLBF.

Reproducibility
The description of datasets, protocol, hyperparameters, and optimization is adequate; code availability is a plus. Including seeds, exact preprocessing, and full hyperparameter grids would further help.

Pros for acceptance
- Clear problem diagnosis with compelling theoretical and empirical motivation.
- Simple, principled fix grounded in classic IS theory with demonstrable gains.
- Practical algorithmic instantiation with competitive runtime and robustness.

Cons for acceptance
- Lack of generalization bounds tailored to the biased ratio estimator used in learning.
- Missing comparisons to DR/WDR and other strong off-policy baselines.
- Evaluation limited to simulated logging with full support; no tests of coverage/misspecification.

Recommendation
Weak accept. The paper addresses an important and common failure mode in BLBF with a principled estimator and delivers robust empirical improvements. Strengthening theory for SNIPS-based learning and broadening baselines would make this a clear accept. Confidence: high.
