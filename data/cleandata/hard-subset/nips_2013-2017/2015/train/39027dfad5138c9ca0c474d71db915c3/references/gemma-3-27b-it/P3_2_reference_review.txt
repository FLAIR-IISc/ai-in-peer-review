## Review of "The Self-Normalized Estimator for Counterfactual Learning"

**Summary:**

This paper tackles a critical challenge in learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF): propensity overfitting, a novel form of bias introduced by the standard counterfactual risk estimator. The authors demonstrate that this overfitting leads to degenerate solutions and propose a self-normalized estimator, drawing inspiration from financial mathematics and reinforcement learning, to mitigate this issue. They provide theoretical guarantees for their estimator and empirically validate its improved performance across several datasets, showcasing its robustness and practical benefits.

**Relation to Prior Work:**

The paper builds on a substantial body of work at the intersection of causal inference, off-policy evaluation, and batch reinforcement learning. It correctly positions itself within the landscape of techniques like propensity score matching, importance sampling, and doubly-robust estimators. The authors effectively highlight how their work extends these approaches by identifying a previously unaddressed overfitting phenomenon and providing a novel solution.  The connection to self-normalization techniques used in finance (cited appropriately) is a clever and insightful contribution. The paper appropriately cites foundational work like [Bottou et al., 2013] and [Dudík et al., 2014] and recent advancements in the field [Thomas et al., 2016, Li et al., 2017].  The reference to POEM [Swaminathan & Joachims, 2015] is also crucial as this work directly extends it.

**Strengths:**

* **Identification of a Novel Problem:** The core strength of this paper is the clear articulation and theoretical justification of “propensity overfitting.” This is a significant contribution to the understanding of biases in BLBF. The examples provided (Example 1) are illustrative and effectively convey the problem.
* **Elegant Solution:**  The proposed self-normalized estimator is a well-motivated and mathematically sound solution.  Leveraging multiplicative control variates is a smart and effective approach.  The demonstration of equivariance is a particularly valuable property.
* **Strong Theoretical Foundation:** The paper provides a solid theoretical basis for the proposed estimator, including a theorem establishing consistency.
* **Compelling Empirical Validation:** The experiments demonstrate convincingly that the self-normalized estimator leads to improved performance, particularly in scenarios where propensity overfitting is severe. The comparison to POEM is particularly effective, showing a clear advantage. The additional analysis of Ŝ(h) confirms the theoretical intuition.
* **Clear and Well-Written:**  The paper is generally well-written and organized. The notation is consistent, and the technical details are presented clearly.
* **Reproducibility:** The availability of the code is a strong positive.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Computational Complexity:** While the authors mention the computational cost, a more detailed discussion of the practical implications would be beneficial. Specifically, a comparison of the computational complexity of calculating the variance estimate in Equation (8) versus POEM's approach would be valuable. Is there a significant overhead?
* **Parameter Sensitivity:** The performance of the self-normalized estimator (and consequently Norm-POEM) may be sensitive to the choice of the clipping parameter M. A more in-depth analysis of the impact of M on performance and a discussion of how to choose an appropriate value would be helpful.
* **Limited Scope of Experiments:**  While the experiments are well-designed and convincing, they are primarily based on datasets from LibSVM. Expanding the experimental evaluation to more diverse datasets (e.g., real-world recommender system logs or ad click data) would strengthen the conclusions.
* **CRM Variance Regularization:** The authors mention that variance regularization is still necessary even with the improved estimator.  However, they don't delve into *why* this is the case.  Is there a remaining source of variance that regularization addresses, or is it simply a conservative approach?  A more detailed discussion would be insightful.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* Addresses a significant and previously unrecognised problem in BLBF.
* Proposes a theoretically sound and practically effective solution.
* Demonstrates clear improvements over state-of-the-art methods through rigorous experimentation.
* Well-written and clearly presented.
* Code available for reproducibility.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* Computational complexity of the estimator could be a practical limitation.
* Parameter sensitivity requires careful tuning.
* Limited scope of experimental evaluation.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of learning from logged bandit feedback. The identification of propensity overfitting is a key insight, and the proposed self-normalized estimator offers a compelling solution. Despite some minor weaknesses, the theoretical soundness, empirical validation, and clarity of presentation make this paper deserving of acceptance.  I believe this paper will be highly influential in this area.

**Rating:** Accept (Strongly)
