Summary
This paper tackles batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF) and, in particular, the failure modes of empirical risk minimization (ERM) when using inverse propensity scoring (IPS). The authors identify a distinctive overfitting phenomenon—propensity overfitting—where ERM exploits variability in importance weights rather than structure in the losses, yielding degenerate estimates (non-equivariance, estimates outside the loss range). They propose using a multiplicative control variate, leading to the self-normalized importance sampling estimator (SNIPS) for counterfactual risk. This estimator is bounded, equivariant to translations of the loss, and strongly consistent. They integrate it into the counterfactual risk minimization (CRM) framework to derive Norm-POEM, a practical algorithm for structured prediction with a CRF-like hypothesis class, using an approximate variance estimate for the ratio estimator and L-BFGS optimization. Experiments on multi-label datasets demonstrate improved generalization and robustness over POEM, especially in avoiding propensity overfitting (as diagnosed by the sum of normalized weights Ŝ(h) ≈ 1).

Relation to prior work
Off-policy evaluation via IPS, clipping, additive control variates (regression/doubly robust), and empirical Bernstein-based CRM are well-established. Self-normalization in importance sampling is classic (Monte Carlo and RL policy iteration). The paper positions its contribution as: (i) articulating propensity overfitting as a failure mode of IPS-based ERM, (ii) advocating SNIPS for counterfactual learning rather than mere evaluation, and (iii) showing its practical benefits within CRM. This is a meaningful differentiation from prior BLBF works using unbiased IPS or clipping and from DR-style estimators (additive control variates).

Strengths
- Conceptual novelty: The paper clearly formalizes and demonstrates “propensity overfitting,” a failure mode specific to ERM with logged bandit data, supported by an instructive synthetic example.
- Simple, principled fix: Using SNIPS as a multiplicative control variate is elegant and immediately addresses the two IPS anomalies (non-equivariance, unboundedness). The estimator remains strongly consistent and bounded within the loss range.
- Practical integration: The Norm-POEM objective is a direct drop-in to CRM with a tractable approximate variance for the ratio estimator and retains the same hyperparameters. Despite a higher per-iteration cost, optimization is reported to converge faster and to more stable solutions.
- Empirical evidence: Across multiple datasets and varying sample sizes, Norm-POEM outperforms POEM and is robust to loss translations; Ŝ(h) stays near 1, confirming mitigation of propensity overfitting.
- Clarity: The paper is well written and ties the diagnostic Ŝ(h) and estimator properties to observed training behavior.

Weaknesses
- Scope of novelty: Self-normalized IS is well known; the core estimator is not new. The primary novelty lies in problem framing (propensity overfitting) and integrating SNIPS into CRM for learning. Some readers may view this as incremental unless the framing is emphasized as the main contribution.
- Missing finite-sample theory: While strong consistency and equivariance are valuable, the paper does not provide a CRM-style finite-sample generalization bound tailored to the self-normalized estimator (or a proof that the proposed variance-regularized objective controls generalization in finite samples). The variance estimator is approximate/asymptotic, and the impact of clipping M on bias is not theoretically quantified.
- Baselines and evaluation breadth: Experiments compare mainly against POEM. Given the literature, comparisons to doubly robust (DR), weighted/normalized DR, and modern variance-reduction schemes (e.g., clipped/weighted IS variants) would strengthen the empirical case. Real logged data would further validate practical impact.
- Optimization and stability: The objective is non-convex and the variance estimate precludes stochastic optimization; while L-BFGS works empirically, there are no convergence/stability guarantees. Analysis of denominator stability (small Ŝ) and sensitivity to M, λ is limited.
- Positioning with prior SNIS/RL work: While related work is covered, the connection to known issues of weight degeneracy in IS and to self-normalized estimators in off-policy evaluation could be tightened to avoid over-claiming novelty.

Questions for the authors
- Can you provide a finite-sample generalization bound for SNIPS within the CRM framework, or at least a discussion of conditions under which the proposed variance-regularized objective guarantees improved generalization over IPS?
- How does Norm-POEM compare to DR and weighted DR (normalized DR) in both evaluation and learning settings? Can you include these baselines?
- How sensitive is performance to clipping threshold M and λ? Please include ablations and report the frequency of small denominators and their mitigation.
- Are there scenarios where SNIPS underperforms IPS (e.g., when loss variance dominates weight variance)? Can you characterize such regimes theoretically or empirically?
- Can you evaluate on a real logged dataset (e.g., recommendations/ads) to corroborate the Supervised→Bandit findings?
- Please release code and scripts to facilitate reproduction, and clarify the validation protocol to avoid selection bias in hyperparameter tuning with logged data.

Pro/Con for acceptance
Pro:
- Clear identification and analysis of a new failure mode (propensity overfitting) that matters in practice.
- Clean, principled remedy with strong practical benefits and favorable properties (boundedness, equivariance).
- Consistent empirical wins and diagnostic alignment (Ŝ near 1).

Con:
- Core estimator is known; theoretical contributions are limited to consistency and qualitative arguments.
- Missing stronger baselines and real-world evaluation; finite-sample guarantees are absent.
- Optimization relies on approximations and lacks theoretical convergence analysis.

Recommendation
Accept (borderline). Despite limited theoretical novelty around the estimator itself, the paper introduces a compelling conceptual failure mode and demonstrates that a simple, principled modification meaningfully improves counterfactual learning. The direction is important and likely to influence practice; I am willing to overlook some missing comparisons and finite-sample theory for the conceptual clarity and empirical impact.

Confidence
High.
