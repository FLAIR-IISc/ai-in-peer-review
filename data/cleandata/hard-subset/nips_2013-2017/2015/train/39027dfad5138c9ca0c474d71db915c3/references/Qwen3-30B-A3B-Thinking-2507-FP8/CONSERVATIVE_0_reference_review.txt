

# Review of "The Self-Normalized Estimator for Counterfactual Learning"

This paper identifies a problem they call "Propensity Overfitting" in counterfactual learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF), where conventional inverse propensity score estimators lead to biased risk estimates when used with Empirical Risk Minimization (ERM). The authors propose replacing the standard estimator with a "self-normalized" estimator using multiplicative control variates, derive a new algorithm called Norm-POEM, and provide theoretical and empirical validation.

**Strengths:**
The paper clearly identifies a genuine issue in the BLBF literature that previous work (e.g., POEM) did not fully address. The theoretical analysis (Theorem 2) demonstrating the consistency of the self-normalized estimator is sound, and the empirical results consistently show Norm-POEM outperforms POEM across multiple datasets. The paper is well-organized, clearly written, and provides a thorough explanation of the Propensity Overfitting problem through a convincing example. The connection to importance sampling literature (citing [12, 19, 22, 23]) provides solid theoretical grounding.

**Weaknesses:**
The primary concern is the degree to which this constitutes a significant methodological advance. The self-normalized estimator has been previously studied in importance sampling [19, 22, 23], and the paper doesn't sufficiently explain why this approach wasn't adopted in prior BLBF work. The empirical validation, while consistent, is limited to comparison against POEM alone. More importantly, the paper doesn't compare against recent state-of-the-art BLBF methods (e.g., Doubly Robust estimators [6, 11] or recent improvements [13, 14]), which would better establish the significance of their contribution.

The theoretical claims require closer examination. While Theorem 2 shows consistency, the paper doesn't provide bounds on the bias or variance improvements over conventional estimators, which would be crucial for establishing the practical advantage. The claim that the self-normalized estimator "provably avoids these degeneracies" is overstated - the paper shows it avoids the specific anomalies (non-equivariance, unbounded risk estimates), but doesn't establish that Propensity Overfitting is completely eliminated in all scenarios.

The computational efficiency claim ("surprisingly faster than POEM") is under-supported. The paper notes that Norm-POEM converges in fewer iterations, but provides no concrete runtime comparisons or analysis of the per-iteration cost increase from the normalization term. Without this, it's difficult to assess the practical computational trade-offs.

**Major Concerns:**
The most significant concern is that the paper doesn't adequately address why Propensity Overfitting wasn't observed or addressed in prior BLBF work. The example in Section 4 is artificial and doesn't reflect typical BLBF scenarios where loss functions are usually non-negative (e.g., 0-1 classification loss). The authors claim this problem is "ubiquitous" (Section 4), but their empirical evidence doesn't convincingly demonstrate this across realistic datasets.

The paper's strongest contribution appears to be the careful identification of a specific failure mode in conventional estimators. However, the proposed solution is not sufficiently novel, and the empirical validation is limited to comparison against a single baseline (POEM) that itself is not the current state-of-the-art for BLBF. The authors could have strengthened their case by demonstrating how their approach improves upon more recent methods.

**Recommendation:**
This is a technically sound paper that identifies a genuine problem in the BLBF literature. However, the proposed solution is not sufficiently novel, and the empirical validation doesn't adequately demonstrate the significance of the contribution relative to recent advances in the field. The paper would benefit from:

1. Comparison against more recent BLBF baselines (e.g., Doubly Robust estimators)
2. More thorough theoretical analysis of bias/variance improvements
3. Better justification for why Propensity Overfitting wasn't observed in prior work
4. Concrete computational efficiency analysis

Without these improvements, the paper presents a minor technical improvement rather than a significant advance. While the findings are interesting and the paper is well-written, it doesn't meet the bar for a major contribution to NIPS. I recommend rejection, but with the strong caveat that the problem identification is valuable and could form the basis for a stronger paper with additional validation against current state-of-the-art methods.
