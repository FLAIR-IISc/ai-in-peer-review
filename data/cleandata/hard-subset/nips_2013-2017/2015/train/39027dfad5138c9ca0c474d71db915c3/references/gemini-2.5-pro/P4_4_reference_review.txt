**Summary of the Paper**

This paper addresses the problem of batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF), where the goal is to learn a new policy from historical interaction logs. The authors build upon the Counterfactual Risk Minimization (CRM) principle, which learns by minimizing an upper bound on the true risk, typically estimated using an Inverse Propensity Score (IPS) estimator.

The central contribution of this paper is the identification and formalization of a novel failure mode in this learning paradigm, which the authors term "Propensity Overfitting." They demonstrate that the standard IPS estimator has anomalies—it is not equivariant to shifts in the loss function and its value can be outside the range of the loss. When used within an Empirical Risk Minimization (ERM) framework, these anomalies allow a learning algorithm to find policies with artificially low estimated risk by manipulating the sum of propensity weights, rather than by accurately modeling the underlying loss. This can lead to poor generalization performance.

To remedy this, the paper proposes replacing the standard IPS estimator with the Self-Normalized Estimator, a known ratio estimator from the importance sampling literature that uses the sum of importance weights as a multiplicative control variate. The authors show that this estimator is equivariant, its estimates are bounded within the range of the loss, and it is strongly consistent. By integrating this self-normalized estimator and an appropriate variance approximation into the CRM framework, they derive a new learning algorithm called Norm-POEM. An extensive empirical evaluation on several multi-label classification tasks shows that Norm-POEM significantly outperforms its predecessor, POEM [1], achieving better generalization and demonstrating robustness to the issues that plague the standard IPS estimator.

**Strengths and Weaknesses**

**Quality:** The paper is of high technical quality. The claims are well-supported by both theoretical reasoning and empirical evidence.
*   The identification of "Propensity Overfitting" is sharp and well-articulated. The example in Section 4 provides a clear and convincing illustration of how standard ERM can fail in the BLBF setting in a way that is distinct from conventional overfitting.
*   The theoretical properties of the proposed self-normalized estimator (equivariance, boundedness, consistency) are correctly identified and directly address the diagnosed problems of the standard IPS estimator. The proof of consistency is straightforward but sound.
*   The experimental methodology ("Supervised to Bandit") is rigorous and allows for controlled and reproducible evaluation. The experiments are comprehensive, directly testing the key claims: improved generalization (Table 1, Figure 1), mitigation of propensity overfitting (Table 2, left), robustness to loss transformations (Table 2, right), and the continued utility of variance regularization (Table 3).
*   A minor point is that the variance estimator for the self-normalized risk (Equation 8) is an approximation. While this is a standard and practical approach (e.g., using the delta method), the paper does not analyze the theoretical implications of this approximation on the validity of the CRM generalization bound. However, the strong empirical results suggest this is not a practical concern.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized.
*   The progression of ideas is logical: the paper introduces the BLBF setting, clearly explains the subtle but critical problem of propensity overfitting, presents a principled solution, derives a practical algorithm, and validates it empirically.
*   The motivating example in Section 4 is a key strength, making a potentially complex issue highly intuitive.
*   The relationship to prior work is clearly explained. The authors are forthright about the origins of the self-normalized estimator in the statistics literature, correctly framing their contribution as its application to solve a specific learning problem.
*   The experimental section is easy to follow, and the tables and figures effectively communicate the main results.

**Originality:** The work contains significant novel contributions.
*   While the self-normalized estimator is not new, its application to counterfactual *learning* to explicitly solve the problem of propensity overfitting is novel. The primary conceptual contribution is the identification and diagnosis of propensity overfitting itself. Prior work in this area has focused primarily on the high variance of IPS estimators, whereas this paper highlights a different and important structural bias that arises during ERM.
*   The resulting algorithm, Norm-POEM, is a novel and meaningful advancement over the previous state-of-the-art, POEM. It represents a non-trivial modification that remedies a fundamental weakness in the underlying estimation approach.

**Significance:** The results presented are important and likely to have an impact on the field.
*   Learning from logged bandit data is a problem of high practical relevance in numerous industrial applications (e.g., recommender systems, ad placement, search).
*   The paper addresses a fundamental issue in the standard ERM/CRM approach to this problem. The insights about propensity overfitting are likely to be valuable to both researchers and practitioners.
*   The proposed solution is elegant, simple to implement, and demonstrably effective. The fact that it improves performance while also proving to be computationally efficient makes it a strong candidate for practical adoption. The work significantly advances the state of the art in batch learning from bandit feedback.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
1.  **Novel Problem Formulation:** The paper identifies and clearly formalizes "Propensity Overfitting," a subtle but critical failure mode for counterfactual risk minimization that has been overlooked in prior work.
2.  **Principled and Effective Solution:** The proposed solution, using the self-normalized estimator, is well-motivated by statistical theory and directly addresses the identified problem.
3.  **Strong Empirical Validation:** The experiments are thorough and provide convincing evidence that the proposed Norm-POEM algorithm substantially outperforms the previous state-of-the-art, is more robust, and directly mitigates propensity overfitting.
4.  **High Clarity and Readability:** The paper is exceptionally well-written, making the contributions easy to understand and appreciate.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
1.  **Existing Core Component:** The central technical tool, the self-normalized estimator, is a well-established method in the importance sampling literature. The paper's novelty lies in its application and the problem it solves, rather than the invention of a new estimator.

In conclusion, the paper makes a significant contribution to the field of counterfactual learning. The strengths, particularly the insightful problem diagnosis and strong empirical results, far outweigh the minor weakness regarding the novelty of the core estimator. This is a complete and high-quality piece of work that is well-suited for NIPS.
