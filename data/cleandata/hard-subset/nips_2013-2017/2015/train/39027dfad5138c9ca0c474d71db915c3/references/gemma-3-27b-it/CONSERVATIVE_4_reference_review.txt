## Review of "The Self-Normalized Estimator for Counterfactual Learning"

This paper addresses the critical problem of learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF), framed as a challenge of off-policy evaluation. The authors identify a novel form of overfitting – “Propensity Overfitting” – that plagues existing counterfactual risk estimators used in this context and propose a “Self-Normalized Risk Estimator” as a remedy. The theoretical justification, based on control variates, appears sound, and the empirical evaluation demonstrates improved performance over a baseline (POEM) on standard datasets. 

However, while the identified problem and proposed solution are interesting, the paper lacks the thoroughness and rigorous validation expected for a NIPS submission, particularly given the somewhat unconventional nature of the proposed estimator. My overall assessment is skeptical, leaning towards rejection.

**Strengths:**

* **Identified a potentially important issue:** Propensity Overfitting is a well-articulated and seemingly genuine problem that has been overlooked in previous work on BLBF. The examples provided clearly demonstrate the issue.
* **Sound theoretical foundation:** The use of multiplicative control variates and the self-normalized estimator is theoretically motivated and builds upon established techniques from importance sampling.
* **Empirical improvements:** The empirical results consistently show improvements over the POEM baseline, suggesting that the proposed estimator offers a practical benefit.
* **Clear presentation:** The core ideas are well-explained, and the paper is generally easy to follow, particularly for those familiar with counterfactual learning and importance sampling.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Baseline Comparison:** The empirical evaluation *only* compares against POEM. While POEM is a relevant baseline, it’s insufficient to demonstrate the robustness and generalizability of the proposed method. A comparison against simpler, established methods like propensity score matching, or doubly-robust estimators (mentioned in the related work) is crucial.  Failing to do so leaves open the possibility that the gains are specific to the comparison chosen and not indicative of a broader improvement.
* **Lack of Sensitivity Analysis:** The paper reports hyperparameter tuning but doesn’t provide a detailed sensitivity analysis. How do the results change with different values of *M* and *λ*? Are there specific dataset characteristics that make the method more or less effective?  This is especially important given the theoretical discussion about variance regularization.
* **Overstated Claims:** The language used to describe the benefits of the self-normalized estimator is often quite strong (e.g., “resolves all the problems,” “provably avoids”). The theoretical guarantees are limited to consistency; the paper doesn’t establish tighter bounds on the estimation error or demonstrate superior sample complexity.
* **Computational Efficiency Claim:** The claim that Norm-POEM is “surprisingly faster” than POEM is not convincingly supported. The discussion focuses on a reduction in the number of iterations, but doesn’t present actual wall-clock times or a detailed computational analysis. The statement that the convergence to shorter ||w|| is a reason for speedup is hand-wavy.
* **Insufficient Ablation Study:** While the paper does mention an experiment removing variance regularization, a more thorough ablation study would be valuable.  For example, what happens if you use the self-normalized estimator *without* the CRM principle?  This would help isolate the contribution of each component.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper identifies a previously unrecognized issue in BLBF and proposes a theoretically sound solution.
* The empirical results demonstrate consistent improvements over a relevant baseline.
* The method has the potential to be useful in real-world applications where learning from logged data is essential.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The empirical evaluation is insufficiently comprehensive.
* The claims are overstated and lack sufficient theoretical support.
* The paper doesn’t adequately address the limitations of the proposed method.
* The analysis lacks depth, and crucial questions about sensitivity and computational efficiency remain unanswered.



**Overall Recommendation:** **Reject**

This paper presents an interesting idea and promising initial results. However, it falls short of the standards for NIPS acceptance due to the limited scope of the empirical evaluation, the overstated claims, and the lack of a thorough analysis. I believe the authors need to significantly strengthen the experimental validation and provide more rigorous theoretical justification before this work would be suitable for presentation at NIPS.  Specifically, a comparison against established off-policy evaluation methods, a detailed sensitivity analysis of hyperparameters, and a more nuanced discussion of the theoretical limitations are necessary.
