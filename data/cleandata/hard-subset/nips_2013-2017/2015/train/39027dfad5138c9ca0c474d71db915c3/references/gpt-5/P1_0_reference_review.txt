Summary and contributions
The paper addresses batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF) and, in particular, the use of importance sampling (IPS) estimators for counterfactual risk minimization (CRM). The authors identify a new failure mode, Propensity Overfitting, whereby empirical risk minimization with the standard IPS estimator prefers hypotheses that game the importance weights rather than the loss, leading to severe finite-sample degeneracies (non-equivariance and unboundedness). They propose to use a multiplicative control variate to derive a self-normalized (weighted) importance sampling estimator (SN-IPS) for counterfactual risk, which is bounded and equivariant to loss translation, and strongly consistent. They integrate this estimator into a CRM algorithm for structured prediction, dubbed Norm-POEM, and provide an approximate variance estimator for the ratio estimator to retain variance regularization. Experiments on multi-label classification datasets constructed via supervised-to-bandit conversion show consistent improvements over POEM, reduced sensitivity to loss translation, and better runtime behavior.

Relation to prior work
The paper situates itself within causal inference and off-policy evaluation, building on importance sampling, propensity score methods, and CRM bounds. The self-normalized estimator has a long history in Monte Carlo and RL as weighted importance sampling (WIS). The manuscript cites Hesterberg, Owen, and related sources, and positions the contribution as bringing SN-IPS into CRM-based learning to counteract a specific overfitting pathology. It also connects to variance reduction methods (clipping, doubly robust), though empirical comparisons to DR methods are not provided.

Strengths
- Clear identification and articulation of Propensity Overfitting, with an instructive example that demonstrates how standard IPS-based ERM can severely underestimate risk or overfit by manipulating weights rather than loss.
- Conceptually simple, principled remedy via multiplicative control variates leading to self-normalized risk estimation. SN-IPS directly fixes non-equivariance and ensures bounded finite-sample estimates.
- Integration into a practical learning algorithm (Norm-POEM) with an approximate variance estimator and variance regularization, preserving the CRM spirit while addressing the underlying degeneracy.
- Empirical results show consistent and meaningful gains over POEM across datasets and training sizes; compelling diagnostic evidence (Ŝ(ĥ) ≈ 1) that the method controls propensity overfitting; robustness to loss translation; and favorable optimization behavior.

Weaknesses and questions
- Theory is limited. Theorem 2 (strong consistency) is standard for WIS; there is no new finite-sample generalization bound for the SN estimator in the CRM setting. The main bound (Theorem 1) is inherited from prior work and applies to capped IPS, not to the ratio estimator used here. It would strengthen the paper to derive a CRM-style bound tailored to SN-IPS (even with clipping).
- Variance estimation: the paper uses a Normal/delta-method approximation for the ratio estimator. No discussion of its accuracy in the regimes considered or of alternative variance estimators (e.g., jackknife, bootstrap) is provided. Given the emphasis on variance-aware CRM, more principled uncertainty estimates would be valuable.
- Baselines: comparisons are limited to POEM and a supervised “skyline.” Given the discussion of control variates and prior off-policy estimators, experimental comparisons to clipped IPS without normalization, doubly robust or weighted doubly robust estimators, and perhaps self-normalized DR would be informative.
- Scope and realism: experiments rely on supervised-to-bandit conversion in multi-label classification with a stochastic CRF logging policy trained on 5% data. This is a standard setup, but evaluation on domains where weight variability is extreme (e.g., recommendation or contextual ads) would better test the purported advantage of SN-IPS when weight fluctuations dominate.
- Support assumptions and clipping: the method still relies on full support (or clipping M) to ensure finite variance. There is little analysis of the bias introduced by clipping within the SN framework, or guidance on choosing M beyond validation.
- Some implementation details are light: no wall-clock times, no statistical significance tests, and no ablation on λ and M; claims about l2-regularization effects are not quantified.

Quality
Technically sound overall. The diagnosis of the pathology is compelling and the proposed fix is correct and well-motivated. However, the theoretical contribution is modest (largely known properties of WIS) and finite-sample guarantees tailored to the proposed objective are missing. Experiments are solid within their scope but could be broader and include stronger baselines.

Clarity
The paper is clearly written and well-organized. The propensity overfitting example is particularly effective. Notation is mostly consistent, though the sign conventions for losses change across sections (δ ∈ [−1,0] vs. {−2, −1} and translated variants). Proofs are minimal; referencing is appropriate for known results.

Originality
Using self-normalized IS in off-policy evaluation is not new; the novelty lies in identifying propensity overfitting in IPS-based ERM and integrating SN-IPS into CRM for structured prediction. This is a meaningful, if incremental, contribution. The “equivariance” perspective and its implications for ERM are insightful.

Significance
Addressing a practical failure mode of IPS-based BLBF is important. The proposed solution is simple to adopt and appears to reliably improve performance and robustness. The work is likely to influence practitioners using CRM/IPS for counterfactual learning.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide a finite-sample generalization bound for SN-IPS within CRM (even with assumptions or clipping), or at least discuss when the variance regularizer meaningfully controls generalization with the ratio estimator.
- Include baselines with doubly robust, weighted doubly robust, and clipped (non-normalized) IPS, and report statistical significance.
- Analyze the impact of clipping M on bias/variance for SN-IPS; provide guidelines for setting M and λ; include ablations.
- Report wall-clock runtimes and iteration counts; include convergence plots.
- Consider additional tasks with heavy-tailed weight distributions (e.g., recommendation logs) to stress-test normalization benefits.
- Minor: unify loss sign conventions; clarify availability/URL of code.

Pros
- Clear problem diagnosis (propensity overfitting) with strong intuition
- Simple, principled fix (self-normalization) with theoretical and practical benefits
- Consistent empirical gains and robustness to loss translation
- Good fit to NeurIPS scope; likely useful to the community

Cons
- Limited new theory; lack of CRM bound for SN-IPS
- Narrow experimental baselines; limited domains
- Heuristic variance estimation without calibration analysis
- Limited guidance on hyperparameters and clipping-induced bias

Recommendation: Weak accept. The paper offers a practical and well-motivated improvement to counterfactual learning with clear empirical benefits, despite modest theoretical novelty and limited baselines. My confidence is medium.
