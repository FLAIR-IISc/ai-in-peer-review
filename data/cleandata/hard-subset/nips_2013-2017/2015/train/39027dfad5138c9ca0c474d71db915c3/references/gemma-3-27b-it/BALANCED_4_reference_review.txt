This paper addresses the problem of batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF), focusing on a crucial issue: propensity overfitting. The authors demonstrate that standard counterfactual risk estimators used in this setting can be severely biased due to this phenomenon, leading to poor generalization. They propose a Self-Normalized Risk Estimator, inspired by techniques from financial mathematics and reinforcement learning, and theoretically prove that it avoids these degeneracies. Extensive empirical evaluation validates the theoretical findings, showing improved performance and robustness compared to existing methods.

The paper is well-motivated and tackles a significant problem in the field of learning from observational data. The connection to related work in causal inference, off-policy evaluation, and importance sampling is clearly established. The introduction of “Propensity Overfitting” as a distinct issue is insightful and highlights a subtle but critical flaw in existing approaches. The theoretical analysis, particularly the proof of Theorem 2, is a strong point, solidifying the benefits of the proposed self-normalized estimator. The experimental setup closely follows previous work (POEM), allowing for a fair comparison, and the results convincingly demonstrate the superiority of Norm-POEM.  The inclusion of runtime comparison is also valuable.

However, there are several areas where the paper could be improved. 

**Weaknesses:**

* **Clarity of the Explanation of Propensity Overfitting:** While the authors illustrate Propensity Overfitting with a compelling example, the intuitive explanation remains somewhat abstract.  Expanding on *why* the estimator is not equivariant and the consequences of this for optimization would be helpful. The explanation links to translation invariance, but further clarifying how capacity allows for exploiting this non-equivariance would enhance understanding.
* **Limited Scope of Experiments:** The experiments are primarily conducted on datasets used in previous work (POEM). While providing a baseline, exploring datasets with different characteristics (e.g., varying degrees of bias, different dimensionality) would strengthen the conclusions. Specifically, the reported improvement is substantial, and testing the robustness of this improvement across diverse datasets is important.  
* **Computational Complexity Discussion:** The paper states Norm-POEM’s runtime is on the same order as a CRF but doesn’t provide detailed runtime numbers or scalability analysis.  A more thorough examination of the computational cost, especially concerning the variance estimation, is needed. How does the algorithm scale with the number of features, labels, and data points?
* **Lack of Error Bar/Statistical Significance:** While the authors report average Hamming loss, the lack of error bars or statistical significance tests makes it difficult to assess the reliability of the performance differences. Providing confidence intervals or p-values would add rigor to the empirical evaluation.
* **Hyperparameter Tuning Details:** While the authors mention hyperparameter calibration on a held-out set, more details about the specific search strategies and ranges used for the hyperparameters (M and lambda) would be beneficial for reproducibility.
* **CRM connection feels a bit hand-wavy**: The connection to CRM principle is stated, and equations are provided, but the role of the self-normalized estimator in *specifically* improving the CRM bound is not fully elucidated.  How does the self-normalization relate to the variance term in the CRM bound?

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:**  Identifying and addressing Propensity Overfitting is a novel contribution.
* **Theoretical Rigor:** The theoretical analysis provides a strong foundation for the proposed method.
* **Empirical Validation:** The experimental results convincingly demonstrate the benefits of the self-normalized estimator.
* **Clarity of Writing:**  Overall, the paper is well-written and clearly explains the technical concepts.
* **Reproducibility:** Code availability is a significant strength.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper addresses a crucial and often overlooked issue in batch learning from bandit feedback.
* The proposed solution is theoretically sound and empirically validated.
* The paper is well-written and clearly explains the technical concepts.
* The code availability promotes reproducibility and facilitates further research.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The experimental evaluation could be more comprehensive.
* The explanation of Propensity Overfitting could be clearer.
* The lack of error bars/statistical significance limits the confidence in the empirical results.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this is a strong paper with significant potential to impact the field of learning from observational data. While there are some weaknesses, the novelty, theoretical rigor, and empirical validation outweigh them. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions, specifically addressing the clarity of the explanation of Propensity Overfitting, expanding the experimental evaluation with more diverse datasets, providing statistical significance tests, and offering more detail on the computational complexity and hyperparameter tuning process.  A more detailed connection between self-normalization and the CRM bound would also be appreciated.
