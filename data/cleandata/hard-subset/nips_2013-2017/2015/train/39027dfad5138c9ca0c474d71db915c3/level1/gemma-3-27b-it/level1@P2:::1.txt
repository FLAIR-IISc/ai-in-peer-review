This paper tackles a critical issue in batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF): propensity overfitting. The authors convincingly demonstrate that the commonly used unbiased counterfactual risk estimator suffers from this problem, leading to poor generalization. They propose a self-normalized estimator as a remedy, theoretically justifying its robustness and empirically validating its effectiveness on several multi-label classification tasks.

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The identification of propensity overfitting is a novel and important contribution. The theoretical analysis supporting the self-normalized estimator is well-presented and clearly demonstrates why it avoids the pitfalls of the standard estimator. The experimental evaluation is thorough, utilizing established datasets and a well-defined experimental setup (following the "Supervised 7→ Bandit" methodology). The ablation studies, specifically isolating the impact of variance regularization and demonstrating equivariance, significantly strengthen the claims. The comparison with POEM and a full-information CRF provides valuable context. The inclusion of runtime analysis is a plus. Minor concerns exist regarding the complexity of the variance estimation for the self-normalized estimator, but the authors acknowledge and address this by noting faster convergence despite increased per-iteration cost.

**Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The introduction effectively motivates the problem and situates it within the broader context of causal inference and off-policy evaluation. The mathematical notation is standard and the derivations are relatively easy to follow. The examples illustrating propensity overfitting (Example 1) are particularly helpful. However, the detailed mathematical derivations in Section 5 could be slightly more streamlined for readers less familiar with importance sampling and control variates. A more intuitive explanation of *why* the self-normalized estimator avoids the issues with the standard estimator *before* delving into the mathematical formalism could improve clarity.

**Originality:** The identification of propensity overfitting is a novel contribution. While the self-normalized estimator itself is known in importance sampling literature (referenced appropriately), its application to the BLBF setting and the theoretical analysis of its properties in this context are original. The Norm-POEM algorithm represents a novel instantiation of the CRM principle using the proposed estimator. The paper carefully distinguishes its contribution from existing work in causal inference, domain adaptation, and off-policy evaluation, demonstrating a clear understanding of the relevant literature.

**Significance:** The results are significant. Propensity overfitting is a fundamental problem in BLBF, and the proposed solution demonstrably improves generalization performance. The potential impact extends beyond the specific application of structured output prediction, as the self-normalized estimator could be applied to other BLBF algorithms. The thorough empirical evaluation and ablation studies provide strong evidence for the practical utility of the approach. The fact that Norm-POEM achieves competitive runtime with existing methods makes it even more appealing. This work directly addresses a critical challenge in learning from observational data, which is increasingly relevant in many real-world applications.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

*   Novel and important identification of propensity overfitting.
*   Strong theoretical justification for the proposed self-normalized estimator.
*   Comprehensive empirical validation demonstrating significant performance improvements.
*   Clear and well-organized presentation of the method and results.
*   Careful comparison with related work and ablation studies to isolate the impact of key components.
*   Competitive runtime performance.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   Mathematical derivations in Section 5 could be more streamlined for broader accessibility.
*   The variance estimation for the self-normalized estimator is complex, though acknowledged and mitigated by faster convergence.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that addresses an important problem with a novel and theoretically sound solution. The empirical results are compelling and demonstrate the practical significance of the work. I recommend acceptance.
