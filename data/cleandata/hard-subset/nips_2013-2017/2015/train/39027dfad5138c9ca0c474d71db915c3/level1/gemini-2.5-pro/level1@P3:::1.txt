This paper addresses the problem of batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF), a crucial setting for improving interactive systems like search and recommendation engines using historical data. The authors identify a critical and previously overlooked failure mode of the standard counterfactual risk estimator, which is based on inverse propensity scoring (IPS). They term this failure mode "propensity overfitting," where a sufficiently complex model can achieve an artificially low empirical risk by manipulating the importance weights, rather than by fitting the true loss function. This can lead to degenerate solutions with poor generalization.

To solve this problem, the authors propose replacing the standard unbiased IPS estimator with the self-normalized estimator, a well-known ratio estimator from the importance sampling literature. They show that this estimator has several desirable properties that the standard estimator lacks: its estimates are guaranteed to lie within the range of the observed losses, and it is equivariant to constant shifts in the loss function. While the self-normalized estimator is biased for finite samples, it is strongly consistent. Building on this improved estimator, the paper develops a new learning algorithm, Norm-POEM, by incorporating the self-normalized estimator and its corresponding variance estimate into the Counterfactual Risk Minimization (CRM) framework previously proposed by Swaminathan & Joachims (ICML 2015). Through a series of well-designed experiments on multi-label classification tasks, the authors demonstrate that Norm-POEM is robust to propensity overfitting and consistently outperforms its predecessor, which uses the standard estimator.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Significant Conceptual Contribution:** The primary strength of this paper is the identification and clear articulation of the "propensity overfitting" problem. This is a fundamental issue that can affect any learning algorithm using importance sampling with a flexible hypothesis class. The illustrative example in Section 4 is exceptionally clear and provides powerful intuition for why standard Empirical Risk Minimization with the IPS estimator can fail so spectacularly. This is a valuable insight for the broader community working on off-policy evaluation and learning.

2.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is extremely well-written and easy to follow. The motivation is clear, the problem is precisely defined, the proposed solution is elegantly derived, and the experiments directly support the paper's central claims. The distinction between "loss overfitting" and "propensity overfitting" is a useful conceptual framing.

3.  **Technical Quality and Rigorous Evaluation:** The proposed solution is simple, well-grounded in the statistical literature on control variates, and its desirable properties (boundedness, equivariance, consistency) are proven. The empirical evaluation is thorough and convincing. By using the "Supervised -> Bandit" protocol, the authors conduct a controlled study that allows for a clear comparison of generalization performance. The experiments in Table 2, which show the value of the sum of weights Ŝ(h) and the robustness to loss translation, provide direct and compelling evidence that Norm-POEM indeed solves the identified problem.

4.  **Originality:** While the self-normalized estimator is not a new invention, its application as a direct remedy for the propensity overfitting problem in the context of counterfactual *learning* is novel. The paper effectively connects a known tool from the importance sampling literature to a specific, critical failure mode in a machine learning setting, resulting in a practical and high-performing algorithm.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Discussion of Bias:** The paper notes that the self-normalized estimator is biased for finite samples. A more detailed discussion of this bias would be beneficial. For example, how does the magnitude of the bias depend on the sample size or the mismatch between the logging and target policies? While the experiments show that this bias is not detrimental in practice (and may even be helpful, akin to regularization), a deeper theoretical or empirical characterization of the bias-variance trade-off for this specific estimator in a learning context would strengthen the paper.

2.  **Scalability and Optimization:** The authors mention that the variance term for the self-normalized estimator is not amenable to stochastic gradient descent (SGD), leading them to use L-BFGS. While L-BFGS proved effective and even faster in their experiments, the inability to use SGD could be a limitation for extremely large-scale problems where full batch or mini-batch gradients are computationally prohibitive. A brief discussion on the potential for scalable optimization, perhaps through approximations or alternative variance estimators, would be a welcome addition.

3.  **Generalization to other model classes:** The experiments focus on linear exponential models (CRF-like). While the propensity overfitting problem seems general, it would be useful to briefly discuss how it might manifest in other popular and high-capacity model classes, such as deep neural networks. The dynamics of overfitting might be different, and it's worth considering if the self-normalized estimator would be equally effective in that context.

**Summary of Arguments:**

*   **Pro-acceptance:**
    *   Identifies a fundamental and important problem (propensity overfitting) in counterfactual learning.
    *   Proposes a simple, elegant, and effective solution grounded in established statistical methods.
    *   The paper is exceptionally clear, well-written, and well-organized.
    *   Empirical results are strong and directly support the central claims, demonstrating substantial improvements over prior art.

*   **Con-acceptance:**
    *   The proposed estimator is known in the statistics literature, reducing the core technical novelty. (However, its application and analysis in this context are novel and significant.)
    *   The discussion on the estimator's bias and the algorithm's scalability could be more detailed.

**Conclusion:**

This is an excellent paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of learning from bandit feedback. The identification and formalization of propensity overfitting is a key insight, and the proposed solution is both principled and empirically validated to be highly effective. The weaknesses are minor and relate more to potential extensions than to flaws in the current work. The paper is of high quality, original in its framing and application, and significant for both researchers and practitioners working with logged data. It clearly meets the standards for publication at NIPS.
