- Proposes using self-normalized importance sampling (SNIS) within CRM to mitigate propensity overfitting in batch learning from logged bandit feedback.
- Identifies propensity overfitting: ERM can exploit variability in importance weights instead of fitting low-loss examples.
- Instantiates Norm-POEM for structured output prediction (CRF-like models) using the SNIS-based learning objective.
- Shows SNIS is bounded, equivariant under loss translation, and strongly consistent under stated assumptions.
- Empirically demonstrates improved generalization over POEM, reduced deviation of the weight-sum diagnostic Ŝ(h) from one, and robustness to loss translations on supervised-to-bandit datasets.
- The use of SNIS and its properties are standard in Monte Carlo/OPE, but applying it to BLBF/CRM and formalizing propensity overfitting is the paper’s main novelty.
- Technical arguments are sound; the diagnostic Ŝ(h) expectation and variance approximation via delta/normal are reasonable though approximate.
- Lacks finite-sample generalization bounds for SNIS-based CRM analogous to prior IPS bounds, leaving bias-variance trade-offs mainly empirical.
- Experimental setup (supervised-to-bandit, multiple datasets, 10 runs) is appropriate, but omits comparisons to doubly robust/regression-based baselines and significance testing.
- Paper is clearly written and organized; implementation details are sufficient but could use pseudocode and more hyperparameter/compute details.
- Contribution is practically significant and easy to adopt, but empirical scope is limited (no real logged data) and theoretical guarantees are incomplete.
- Pros: clear diagnosis of failure mode, practical SNIS integration, boundedness/equivariance benefits, consistent empirical gains, simple implementation.
- Cons: no SNIS-specific finite-sample guarantees, limited baselines (no DR), reliance on approximate variance/clipping, experiments restricted to simulations and missing statistical uncertainty reporting.
- Suggested improvements: derive CRM-style finite-sample bounds for SNIS, compare to doubly robust/control-variate methods, provide ablations on clipping/λ, add confidence intervals and real logged-data experiments.
- Overall: technically sound, moderate novelty, empirically supportive of the main claim; acceptance favored for relevance to off-policy learning despite limited theory and baselines.
