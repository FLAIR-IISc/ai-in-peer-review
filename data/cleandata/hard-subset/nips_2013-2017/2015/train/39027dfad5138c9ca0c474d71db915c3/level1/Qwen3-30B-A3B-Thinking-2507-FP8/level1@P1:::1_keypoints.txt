- Identifies a critical flaw ("propensity overfitting") in the standard counterfactual risk estimator that can make estimates arbitrarily lower than true risk.  
- Shows this failure is distinct from supervised overfitting and that the conventional estimator is not equivariant under loss translation.  
- Proposes a self-normalized estimator R̂SN that is equivariant under loss translation and bounded within the loss range.  
- Derives Norm-POEM, a learning algorithm based on the self-normalized estimator, with theoretical guarantees addressing the identified anomaly.  
- Empirical results on multiple multi-label datasets (Scene, Yeast, TMC, LYRL) show Norm-POEM consistently outperforms POEM and controls propensity overfitting.  
- Demonstrates Ŝ(h) ≈ 1 for Norm-POEM but severe bias for POEM, linking improved performance to reduced propensity overfitting.  
- Builds on CRM and prior counterfactual estimation work, filling a gap by addressing a previously unrecognized form of overfitting.  
- Paper is very well-written, clearly organized, and connects theory and experiments effectively.  
- Norm-POEM is presented as a practical, plug-and-play alternative with comparable runtime to POEM.  
- Experimental scope is limited to multi-label classification and lacks broader structured prediction tasks.  
- Lacks a detailed computational complexity comparison and analysis of the added cost from the self-normalized denominator.  
- Does not sufficiently compare to other variance-reduction techniques (e.g., doubly robust estimators, additive control variates).  
- Missing a thorough hyperparameter sensitivity analysis for λ and M, which were tuned via counterfactual evaluation.  
- Recommendation: strong accept — significant, theoretically sound contribution with practical impact.  
- Arguments for acceptance: identifies and solves a novel problem, provides provable advantages, shows empirical superiority, and offers a practical algorithm.  
- Arguments against acceptance: limited experimental diversity, incomplete computational analysis, and insufficient comparison with related techniques.  
- Overall assessment: excellent quality, originality, clarity, and significance; meets conference criteria and is valuable for researchers and practitioners.
