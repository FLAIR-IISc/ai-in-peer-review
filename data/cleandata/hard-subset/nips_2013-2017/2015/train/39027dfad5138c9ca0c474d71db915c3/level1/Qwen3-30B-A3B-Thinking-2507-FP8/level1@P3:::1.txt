

# Peer Review: "The Self-Normalized Estimator for Counterfactual Learning"

This paper identifies a critical flaw in the conventional counterfactual risk estimator used in batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF), introducing a novel self-normalized estimator that avoids this issue. The authors demonstrate that the standard inverse propensity score estimator suffers from "propensity overfitting" - a phenomenon where the risk estimate can become severely biased for complex hypothesis spaces, even when the loss is bounded. They propose replacing the conventional estimator with a self-normalized version, which maintains equivariance under loss translation and bounds the overfitted risk, and derive a new learning algorithm (Norm-POEM) based on this estimator.

The paper builds on the Counterfactual Risk Minimization (CRM) framework established in prior NIPS work (Swaminathan & Joachims, 2015; Swaminathan & Joachims, 2013), but identifies a previously overlooked limitation of the conventional estimator. The authors' theoretical analysis of propensity overfitting is particularly compelling, with clear examples demonstrating how the conventional estimator can produce arbitrarily low risk estimates that don't reflect the true risk. This problem has significant practical implications for applications like ad placement and recommendation systems where BLBF is commonly used.

**Strengths**

1. **Theoretical Contribution**: The paper makes a strong theoretical contribution by identifying propensity overfitting as a distinct problem from standard overfitting in supervised learning. The examples provided (Section 4) are particularly effective in illustrating the severity of the problem, and the proof of consistency for the self-normalized estimator (Theorem 2) is rigorous and well-motivated.

2. **Practical Impact**: The proposed solution (Norm-POEM) demonstrates consistent empirical improvements over the conventional POEM algorithm across multiple datasets (Table 1, Figure 1). The authors convincingly show that Norm-POEM avoids the propensity overfitting problem (Table 2), and the implementation details are sufficiently clear for reproducibility.

3. **Clarity and Organization**: The paper is exceptionally well-organized, with a clear progression from problem statement to theoretical analysis to empirical validation. The distinction between "loss overfitting" and "propensity overfitting" is particularly well-explained, and the examples make the theoretical points accessible.

4. **Critical Evaluation**: The authors are careful to acknowledge limitations, such as the need for variance regularization even with the self-normalized estimator (Table 3) and the computational trade-offs (Section 7).

**Weaknesses**

1. **Limited Theoretical Analysis of the Self-Normalized Estimator**: While the paper proves consistency of the self-normalized estimator, it doesn't provide a theoretical analysis of its generalization properties compared to the conventional estimator. A bound on the expected generalization error for the self-normalized estimator would strengthen the theoretical contribution.

2. **Empirical Comparison Scope**: The comparison is primarily against POEM (the conventional CRM algorithm), but doesn't include other relevant counterfactual learning approaches like doubly robust estimators (Dudik et al., 2014) or the offset tree (Beygelzimer & Langford, 2009). A more comprehensive comparison would better establish the significance of the contribution.

3. **Computational Complexity Analysis**: The paper mentions that Norm-POEM converges faster in practice but doesn't provide a detailed complexity analysis. The authors state that "the per-iteration cost of optimizing the Norm-POEM objective has the same complexity as the per-iteration cost of POEM with L-BFGS," but this isn't fully justified given the normalization step.

4. **Assumption Limitations**: The consistency proof (Theorem 2) requires that the logging policy h0 has full support over Y. While this is a common assumption in BLBF, it's worth discussing how the method might perform when this assumption is violated (e.g., in real-world settings with limited exploration).

**Overall Assessment**

The paper makes a significant contribution to the BLBF literature by identifying a previously overlooked problem (propensity overfitting) that affects the reliability of counterfactual learning algorithms. The self-normalized estimator is theoretically well-motivated and empirically validated across multiple datasets. The distinction between propensity overfitting and standard overfitting is a valuable conceptual contribution that clarifies an important limitation of existing approaches.

The paper is of high quality, with strong theoretical analysis, clear writing, and compelling empirical results. It represents a meaningful advance over prior work on CRM and BLBF, and the insights it provides will be valuable for both researchers and practitioners in the field.

**Pro Acceptance**
- Identifies a novel and important problem in BLBF that has been overlooked in prior work
- Provides a theoretically sound solution with strong empirical validation
- Clear, well-organized presentation with effective examples
- Practical impact on real-world applications of BLBF

**Con Acceptance**
- Limited comparison to alternative counterfactual learning methods
- Incomplete theoretical analysis of generalization properties
- Computational complexity claims require more justification

This paper makes a substantial contribution to the field of counterfactual learning and deserves publication at NIPS. The problem identified is both theoretically interesting and practically important, and the solution proposed is well-justified and effective. The paper is well-written, technically sound, and provides valuable insights that will inform future research in BLBF. While the empirical comparison could be expanded to include more baselines, the current results are sufficiently compelling to demonstrate the value of the proposed approach.
