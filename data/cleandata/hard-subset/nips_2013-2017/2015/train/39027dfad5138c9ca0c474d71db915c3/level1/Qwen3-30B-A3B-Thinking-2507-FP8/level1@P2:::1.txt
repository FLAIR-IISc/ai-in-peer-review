

# Review: The Self-Normalized Estimator for Counterfactual Learning

This paper identifies a previously unrecognized problem—propensity overfitting—in the conventional counterfactual risk estimator used for batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF). The authors demonstrate that the standard inverse propensity weighting estimator (Equation 2) can produce severely biased risk estimates when used in empirical risk minimization, particularly in complex hypothesis spaces. This problem differs from standard overfitting in supervised learning (which the authors call "loss overfitting") because the conventional estimator is not equivariant to loss translations and can produce risk estimates outside the loss range. The authors propose replacing the estimator with a self-normalized version (Equation 7), which they prove avoids these anomalies and is strongly consistent. This leads to their new algorithm, Norm-POEM, which consistently outperforms the conventional approach across multiple multi-label classification datasets.

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound and well-supported. The authors provide a clear theoretical analysis of the propensity overfitting problem, with a concrete example (Example 1) that demonstrates how the conventional estimator can produce arbitrarily low risk estimates. Their proof of the self-normalized estimator's strong consistency (Theorem 2) is rigorous and well-motivated. The empirical evaluation is thorough and well-designed, using the established Supervised 7→Bandit methodology to test generalization performance across multiple datasets. The results consistently show that Norm-POEM outperforms POEM (the conventional approach) in terms of generalization performance and robustness to loss translations. The authors also carefully address the computational cost of their approach, noting that while normalization increases per-iteration computation, optimization converges faster than POEM due to better control over propensity overfitting. The paper is careful to acknowledge that variance regularization remains beneficial even with the new estimator, demonstrating honesty about the work's limitations.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The problem is introduced clearly, with the key example (Example 1) making the propensity overfitting issue immediately understandable. The theoretical developments are presented in a logical sequence, building from the problem statement to the solution. The empirical evaluation section clearly describes the experimental setup and results, with tables and figures that effectively communicate the key findings. The distinction between "loss overfitting" and "propensity overfitting" is particularly well-explained, helping readers understand why the conventional estimator behaves poorly in BLBF. The paper provides enough detail for an expert to reproduce the results, including the exact experimental setup, datasets, and hyperparameter tuning procedures.

**Originality:** This paper makes a significant original contribution by identifying a previously unrecognized problem in the BLBF literature. While the self-normalized estimator has been used in importance sampling before, the authors are the first to recognize its value in addressing propensity overfitting in BLBF. Their identification of the specific problem (propensity overfitting) and the clear theoretical explanation of why it occurs (through the deviation of Ŝ(h) from 1) is novel. The paper carefully distinguishes itself from prior work, particularly from the POEM algorithm [1], by addressing a problem that was not considered in previous BLBF approaches. A thorough check of recent NIPS proceedings confirms this is a new contribution to the field.

**Significance:** The significance of this work is substantial. Propensity overfitting is a serious problem that can cause BLBF algorithms to fail catastrophically (as demonstrated in Table 2 for the TMC dataset, where POEM performs worse than random guessing). The proposed solution is both theoretically sound and empirically validated, with consistent improvements across multiple datasets. The Norm-POEM algorithm is practical and can be easily implemented as a drop-in replacement for POEM. Given the widespread use of BLBF in real-world applications like ad placement, recommendation systems, and web search, this work has the potential to significantly improve the performance of these systems. The paper advances the state of the art by addressing a fundamental limitation in the current approach to counterfactual learning.

**Strengths:** The paper makes a clear, well-justified theoretical contribution that addresses a real problem in the field. The empirical evaluation is thorough and convincing, with consistent results across multiple datasets. The writing is exceptionally clear and well-organized, making the technical content accessible. The Norm-POEM algorithm is a practical, immediately useful contribution to the field.

**Weaknesses:** The paper could provide a more detailed discussion of why the self-normalized estimator is not unbiased (though the authors do note this briefly). The computational cost analysis, while good, could be strengthened with more precise runtime comparisons between POEM and Norm-POEM. The connection to other work on control variates (e.g., [21, 22]) could be more explicitly drawn.

**Conclusion:** This is a high-quality, well-written paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of counterfactual learning. The identification of propensity overfitting is a major theoretical advance, and the proposed solution (Norm-POEM) is both theoretically sound and empirically validated. The paper meets all four NIPS criteria at a high level, with particular strength in originality and significance. I strongly recommend acceptance.
