This paper tackles counterfactual learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF) by identifying a failure mode of the standard inverse propensity score (IPS) risk estimator—propensity overfitting—and proposing a self-normalized importance sampling (SNIS) alternative within the Counterfactual Risk Minimization (CRM) framework. The key idea is to treat the sum of importance weights as a multiplicative control variate, yielding a self-normalized risk estimator that is bounded and equivariant to loss translations, thereby avoiding degenerate ERM solutions that exploit weight fluctuations. The authors instantiate this estimator in a structured prediction algorithm, Norm-POEM, with linear exponential-family policies and a variance-regularized CRM objective. Empirically, on supervised-to-bandit conversions of multi-label datasets, Norm-POEM consistently outperforms POEM (which uses unbiased IPS), and diagnostics show that the normalized weights concentrate near their expected value. The work connects to importance weighting and off-policy evaluation (e.g., Rosenbaum & Rubin; Cortes et al., NIPS’10; Strehl et al., NIPS’10; Dudík et al.; Hesterberg; Owen), and to CRM (Swaminathan & Joachims).

Quality: The technical contribution is sound and carefully argued. The “propensity overfitting” phenomenon is convincingly demonstrated via a constructed example and empirical diagnostics. The SNIS estimator’s desirable properties (boundedness, equivariance, strong consistency) are correct, and the variance approximation follows standard ratio-estimator analysis. However, the paper lacks finite-sample, high-probability generalization bounds tailored to the SNIS objective (analogous to the CRM bound given for clipped IPS), and does not quantify the bias-variance trade-off vs. clipping or doubly robust alternatives.

Clarity: The paper is clearly written, well organized, and pedagogical. The diagnostic Ŝ(h) tool is compelling. Minor suggestions: explicitly relate the proposed estimator to weighted/normalized IPS used in off-policy evaluation, and clearly separate asymptotic claims from finite-sample approximations.

Originality: While self-normalization is classical, its principled integration into a learning objective for BLBF to prevent propensity overfitting, together with the identification and analysis of this failure mode, is novel and valuable.

Significance: The contribution is practically important for counterfactual learning in recommender/ads/search, and likely to influence methodology. That said, experiments are limited to simulated bandit feedback and comparisons primarily against POEM; broader baselines (e.g., clipped IPS variants, doubly robust and self-normalized DR) would strengthen significance.

Pros for acceptance:
- Identifies an important and underappreciated failure mode of IPS in learning.
- Simple, principled fix via self-normalization with strong practical impact.
- Clear diagnostics and consistent empirical gains.
- Applicable to structured prediction settings.

Cons for acceptance:
- No finite-sample generalization bound specific to SNIS-CRM.
- Limited baselines (missing DR/DR+SN, SWITCH-like methods).
- Evaluation on simulated logs only; scalability and robustness to real logging policies not shown.
- Sensitivity to clipping M and λ not thoroughly analyzed.

Overall: A strong, well-motivated contribution that advances counterfactual learning; I recommend acceptance after addressing the empirical baselines and clarifying theoretical guarantees.
