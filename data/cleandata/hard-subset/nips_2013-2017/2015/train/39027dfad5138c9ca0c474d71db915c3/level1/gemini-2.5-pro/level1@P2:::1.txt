This paper addresses the problem of batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF), a crucial setting for improving interactive systems using historical data. The authors identify a fundamental and previously overlooked issue with the standard inverse propensity score (IPS) estimator when used within a risk minimization framework. They term this issue "Propensity Overfitting," where a sufficiently complex model can achieve an artificially low empirical risk not by fitting the true loss function, but by manipulating the propensity weights. For example, with negative losses, the model learns to assign high probability to points observed in the training set, driving the estimated risk towards negative infinity. The paper proposes a principled solution: replacing the standard IPS estimator with the self-normalized estimator, which is motivated as a ratio estimator using the sum of importance weights as a multiplicative control variate. This estimator is shown to be equivariant to shifts in the loss function and always produces estimates bounded by the range of observed losses, thus directly resolving the propensity overfitting problem. Building on this, the authors develop a new learning algorithm, Norm-POEM, which integrates the self-normalized estimator and a corresponding variance estimate into the Counterfactual Risk Minimization (CRM) principle. The paper provides extensive empirical validation on several multi-label classification tasks, demonstrating that Norm-POEM consistently and significantly outperforms its predecessor (POEM), which uses the standard estimator.

This work builds directly upon recent research in counterfactual learning, particularly the CRM principle introduced in [1]. While the self-normalized estimator itself is a classic tool in the importance sampling literature [12, 19, 22], its application to mitigate a specific overfitting pathology in a machine learning context is, to my knowledge, novel. The paper does an excellent job of positioning its contribution relative to other variance reduction techniques like clipping [15] and doubly robust methods [6], clarifying that its use of a multiplicative control variate addresses a different failure mode than these alternatives.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Quality:** The paper is technically sound and presents a complete piece of work. The central contribution is the clear identification and explanation of the "Propensity Overfitting" problem. The illustrative example in Section 4 is exceptionally effective at demonstrating this subtle but severe flaw in the standard unbiased estimator. The proposed solution is not an ad-hoc fix but is well-grounded in statistical theory (control variates). The claims are strongly supported by a combination of theoretical arguments (e.g., consistency in Theorem 2, discussion of equivariance) and a thorough, well-designed empirical evaluation. The experiments are particularly compelling because they directly test the core hypotheses: Table 2's verification that the sum of weights `Ŝ(h)` remains close to 1 for Norm-POEM and its robustness to loss translation are direct confirmations of the theory.

2.  **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The logical flow from identifying a critical problem, to proposing a principled solution, to developing a concrete algorithm, and finally to validating it empirically is clear and easy to follow. The authors have made a complex topic accessible without sacrificing technical rigor. The distinction between "Loss Overfitting" and "Propensity Overfitting" is a useful conceptual contribution in itself.

3.  **Originality & Significance:** The primary originality lies in identifying propensity overfitting as a key obstacle in counterfactual learning. While others have focused on the high variance of the IPS estimator, this paper shows that for learning with expressive models, there is a more fundamental problem of bias induced by the learning process itself. This is a significant insight that is likely to have a substantial impact on the field. Researchers and practitioners using IPS-based estimators for learning will need to be aware of this issue. The proposed Norm-POEM algorithm is a significant advance over the previous state-of-the-art, and its effectiveness and conceptual simplicity make it likely to be widely adopted. The problem setting is of high practical importance for web search, recommendation, and computational advertising, amplifying the paper's potential impact.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  The variance estimator for the self-normalized risk (Equation 8) is based on an asymptotic approximation (e.g., the delta method). While this is a standard technique, the paper could benefit from a brief discussion on the potential limitations of this approximation, especially in the small-to-medium sample regime, and how it might interact with the CRM objective. Does a poor variance estimate early in training negatively affect optimization?

2.  The final optimization objective for Norm-POEM is non-convex. The authors state they use L-BFGS, which is a reasonable choice, but the potential issue of local minima is not discussed. While the empirical results are strong, some characterization of the optimization landscape or sensitivity to initialization would strengthen the work, though this is a minor point.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   **Pro:** The paper identifies and provides a clear diagnosis of a novel, fundamental, and important problem in counterfactual learning.
*   **Pro:** The proposed solution is elegant, principled, and shown to be highly effective in comprehensive experiments.
*   **Pro:** The work represents a significant advance over prior art and is likely to have a high impact on both research and practice.
*   **Pro:** The manuscript is a model of clarity and high-quality scientific communication.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   **Con:** The core statistical tool (the self-normalized estimator) is not new, though its application in this context is.
*   **Con:** The theoretical analysis of the learning algorithm's optimization properties (e.g., dealing with non-convexity and the variance approximation) is light.

In summary, this is an outstanding paper that makes a significant and timely contribution. The strengths far outweigh the minor weaknesses. It clearly identifies a critical flaw in a widely used method and provides an effective and well-justified solution. It should be of great interest to the NIPS community. I strongly recommend acceptance.
