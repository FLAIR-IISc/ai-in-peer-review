- Problem addressed: batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF) and finding optimal policies from historical data collected by a different logging policy.  
- Main contribution: identifies a critical failure mode called "Propensity Overfitting" when using IPS within CRM with expressive models.  
- Failure mechanism: models can manipulate propensity weights to drive empirical IPS risk arbitrarily low without reducing true loss.  
- Example demonstration: shows concentrating mass on logged examples for negative losses or avoiding them for positive losses produces misleadingly low risk.  
- Distinction: this pathology is different from standard overfitting on loss values.  
- Proposed remedy: replace IPS with the self-normalized estimator (a multiplicative control variate) to prevent propensity manipulation.  
- Theoretical properties: the self-normalized estimator is bounded by the loss range, equivariant to constant loss shifts, and strongly consistent.  
- Algorithmic result: derives Norm-POEM by integrating the self-normalized estimator and a variance approximation into the CRM framework (building on POEM).  
- Empirical findings: on multi-label datasets with supervised-to-bandit conversion, Norm-POEM consistently outperforms POEM and keeps learned propensity weights near the expected sum of one.  
- Robustness claim: Norm-POEM is empirically robust to translations of the loss, unlike the standard IPS estimator.  
- Quality assessment: technically sound, well-motivated, with correct theoretical claims and rigorous experimental methodology.  
- Clarity: paper is very well-written and organized; Example 1 and figures/tables effectively communicate ideas.  
- Originality: novel in diagnosing "Propensity Overfitting" in counterfactual policy learning and applying a known estimator to solve it.  
- Significance: addresses a major practical barrier for off-policy learning and offers a practically useful algorithm likely to influence future work.  
- Arguments for acceptance: identifies a fundamental overlooked problem, proposes a principled effective solution, has strong theory and experiments, and is clearly written.  
- Arguments against acceptance: lacks deeper discussion of bias introduced by self-normalization, may not scale due to batch optimizer reliance, and is validated only on simulated rather than real logged bandit data.
