This paper addresses the problem of batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF), a setting where a learner aims to find an optimal policy using historical data from a different, potentially suboptimal, logging policy. The central contribution is the identification of a critical failure mode in the conventional counterfactual risk estimator, which the authors term "Propensity Overfitting". This issue arises when using the standard unbiased Inverse Propensity Score (IPS) estimator within a Counterfactual Risk Minimization (CRM) framework, particularly with expressive hypothesis spaces. As illustrated in a clear example, a model can achieve an arbitrarily low empirical risk estimate not by minimizing the true loss, but by manipulating the propensity weights, either by concentrating probability mass on logged examples (for negative losses) or by avoiding them entirely (for positive losses). This behavior is distinct from traditional overfitting on the loss values.

To remedy this, the paper proposes replacing the standard IPS estimator with the self-normalized estimator, a known technique from the importance sampling literature that can be framed as using a multiplicative control variate. The authors show that this self-normalized estimator is bounded by the range of the loss function, is equivariant to constant shifts in the loss, and is strongly consistent. By incorporating this improved estimator and an appropriate variance approximation into the CRM principle, they derive a new learning algorithm called Normalized Policy Optimizer for Exponential Models (Norm-POEM). This work builds directly on the POEM algorithm [1], which introduced the CRM principle for BLBF, but shows that the choice of estimator within that framework is crucial.

The empirical evaluation, conducted on several multi-label classification datasets using a controlled "supervised-to-bandit" conversion, systematically demonstrates the benefits of the proposed approach. Norm-POEM is shown to consistently outperform the original POEM, achieving better generalization performance. The experiments also directly verify that Norm-POEM mitigates propensity overfitting, as the sum of its learned propensity weights remains close to the theoretical expectation of one, unlike for POEM. Furthermore, the results confirm the theoretical claim that Norm-POEM is robust to translations of the loss function, a property the standard estimator lacks.

**Quality:**
The paper is technically sound. The identification and formalization of the "Propensity Overfitting" problem are insightful and well-argued. The motivation for using the self-normalized estimator as a solution is principled and grounded in the established theory of control variates and ratio estimators. The theoretical claims about the properties of the self-normalized estimator (equivariance, boundedness, consistency) are correct and appropriately proven or cited. The experimental methodology is rigorous, following established protocols for evaluating BLBF algorithms in a controlled setting, and the results convincingly support the paper's central claims. The work is a complete and self-contained contribution.

**Clarity:**
The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The motivation is established clearly in the introduction. The "Propensity Overfitting" problem is introduced with a simple yet powerful example (Example 1) that makes the abstract concept concrete and easy to grasp. The transition from identifying the problem to proposing the solution via control variates is logical and smooth. The description of the Norm-POEM algorithm and the experimental setup is detailed enough to allow for a solid understanding of the work. The tables and figures are clear and effectively communicate the key results.

**Originality:**
While the self-normalized estimator is a known tool in the broader statistics and Monte Carlo literature, its application to the problem of *learning* from bandit feedback to explicitly prevent the newly-identified "Propensity Overfitting" is a novel contribution. The key originality lies not in the invention of a new estimator, but in the diagnosis of a fundamental problem with the standard approach in the context of policy optimization and the successful application of an appropriate, principled remedy. The paper clearly distinguishes its contribution from prior work on variance reduction in importance sampling by focusing on this specific overfitting pathology that arises from the interaction between model capacity and the estimator during optimization.

**Significance:**
The paper's contribution is highly significant. The problem of estimator instability is a major practical barrier to deploying systems learned from logged data. By identifying, naming, and solving the propensity overfitting problem, the paper provides a crucial insight and a practical tool for researchers and practitioners in areas that rely on off-policy learning, such as recommender systems, ad placement, and search ranking. The proposed Norm-POEM algorithm represents a demonstrable advance over the previous state-of-the-art method. The findings are likely to influence the design of future algorithms for learning from bandit feedback and encourage a more critical assessment of the estimators used in counterfactual learning.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Identifies and clearly explains a fundamental, previously overlooked problem ("Propensity Overfitting") in a standard and widely used counterfactual learning framework.
*   Proposes a simple, principled, and effective solution based on the self-normalized estimator, which demonstrably resolves the identified problem.
*   The work is technically sound, with solid theoretical motivation and strong empirical support.
*   The paper is very well-written, making a complex topic accessible.
*   The contribution is significant and has high potential impact for both research and practice in off-policy learning.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   The paper could benefit from a deeper discussion of the bias introduced by the self-normalized estimator, particularly in relation to the finite-sample performance and how it trades off against the reduction in variance and robustness.
*   The proposed algorithm relies on a batch optimizer (L-BFGS) due to the nature of the variance estimate, which may limit its scalability compared to stochastic methods. While the paper notes faster convergence in practice, a discussion of scalability to massive datasets would be valuable.
*   The experiments, while well-controlled, are limited to simulated bandit feedback from supervised datasets. Validation on a real-world logged bandit dataset would further strengthen the claims of practical significance.

Overall, the arguments for acceptance strongly outweigh the arguments against. The paper makes a clear, insightful, and significant contribution to the field of learning from bandit feedback.
