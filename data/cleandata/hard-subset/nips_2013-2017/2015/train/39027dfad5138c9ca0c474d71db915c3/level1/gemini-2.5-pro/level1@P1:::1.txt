### **Summary**

This paper addresses the problem of batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF), a setting crucial for improving interactive systems using historical data. The authors identify a critical and previously overlooked flaw in the standard inverse propensity score (IPS) estimator used within the Counterfactual Risk Minimization (CRM) framework. They term this flaw "Propensity Overfitting," where a learning algorithm with a complex hypothesis space can achieve an artificially low empirical risk not by finding a good policy, but by manipulating the propensity weights—either by assigning high probability to examples with negative loss or by avoiding training examples with positive loss. This leads to severely biased risk estimates and poor generalization.

As a remedy, the authors propose replacing the standard IPS estimator with the self-normalized importance sampling estimator. This estimator, which is a ratio of the standard estimator and the sum of the importance weights, is motivated by the use of multiplicative control variates from the Monte Carlo literature. The authors show that this self-normalized estimator is equivariant to translations of the loss function and is naturally bounded by the range of the loss, thereby resolving the anomalies that lead to propensity overfitting. They integrate this new estimator into the CRM framework to create a new learning algorithm, Norm-POEM, for structured prediction with linear exponential family models. Through a series of well-designed experiments on multi-label classification tasks, they demonstrate that Norm-POEM significantly outperforms its predecessor (POEM), is robust to transformations of the loss function, and effectively mitigates the propensity overfitting problem.

### **Relation to Prior Work**

The paper is well-situated within the literature on off-policy learning and evaluation. It directly builds upon the recent Counterfactual Risk Minimization (CRM) principle and the POEM algorithm [1]. The core idea of using importance sampling for counterfactual estimation is classical, with roots in causal inference [7] and off-policy reinforcement learning [4, 6, 11]. The proposed self-normalized estimator is also a well-known technique in the statistics and Monte Carlo simulation literature [12, 19, 21], where it is recognized for its superior variance properties when sampling weights fluctuate significantly. The primary novelty of this work is not the estimator itself, but the identification of the propensity overfitting problem in the specific context of *learning* (as opposed to just evaluation) and the demonstration that the self-normalized estimator provides a principled and effective solution. This connects the practical problem of learning from logged data to a subtle but important statistical pathology of the standard IPS estimator.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Significance and Originality:** The paper's main contribution—the identification and clear articulation of "Propensity Overfitting"—is highly significant. It exposes a fundamental weakness in the direct application of ERM with the standard IPS estimator, which has been the de facto approach in BLBF. The illustrative example in Section 4 is exceptionally clear and convincing. This insight is likely to be impactful for researchers and practitioners working on off-policy learning.

2.  **Technical Quality:** The proposed solution is simple, elegant, and theoretically sound. Grounding the self-normalized estimator in the theory of control variates provides a principled motivation. The authors correctly analyze its key properties (equivariance, boundedness, consistency) that directly address the identified problem. The derivation of the Norm-POEM algorithm is a straightforward but complete application of the CRM principle with the new estimator.

3.  **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written, well-organized, and easy to follow. The motivation is clear, the problem is explained with a simple yet powerful example, the solution is logically derived, and the experiments are designed to directly validate the paper's central claims.

4.  **Empirical Evaluation:** The experiments are thorough and convincing. They don't just show that Norm-POEM outperforms POEM in terms of generalization error (Table 1, Figure 1). Crucially, they provide direct evidence for the underlying claims: Table 2 demonstrates that Norm-POEM successfully controls the sum of propensity weights (Ŝ(h) ≈ 1) and is robust to loss translations, precisely where POEM fails dramatically. This provides strong support that the performance gains are indeed due to mitigating propensity overfitting.

### **Weaknesses**

1.  **Limited Theoretical Analysis:** While the consistency proof for the self-normalized estimator is provided, the paper would benefit from a more in-depth theoretical analysis. For instance, the original CRM framework relies on an empirical Bernstein bound; it is unclear how the bias introduced by the self-normalized estimator affects this bound. A finite-sample analysis comparing the generalization error of CRM with the standard vs. self-normalized estimator would significantly strengthen the paper's theoretical contribution.

2.  **Computational Scalability:** The authors note that the variance estimate for the self-normalized estimator does not easily admit stochastic optimization, forcing them to use a batch method (L-BFGS). While they report that Norm-POEM converges faster in practice on their datasets, the lack of a stochastic variant could be a limitation for applying this method to truly massive datasets where batch optimization is infeasible. A more detailed discussion of this trade-off would be valuable.

3.  **Real-World Data:** The experiments are conducted using a "Supervised-to-Bandit" conversion protocol on standard classification datasets. While this is an established and valuable methodology for controlled evaluation, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed method on a real-world logged bandit dataset (e.g., from advertising, search, or recommendation systems) would make the empirical results even more compelling.

### **Arguments For/Against Acceptance**

*   **Pro:**
    *   Identifies a novel, important, and subtle problem (propensity overfitting) in a growing field.
    *   Proposes a principled, effective, and simple solution based on a well-established statistical tool.
    *   Provides strong and well-designed empirical evidence that directly supports the paper's core claims.
    *   The paper is written with exceptional clarity.

*   **Con:**
    *   The core estimator is not new, though its application and analysis in this context are.
    *   The theoretical analysis could be deepened to provide finite-sample guarantees within the CRM framework.
    *   The reliance on batch optimization may limit scalability compared to prior work.

### **Conclusion**

This is an excellent paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of learning from logged bandit feedback. The identification of propensity overfitting is a key insight that clarifies a source of instability and poor performance in existing methods. The proposed solution is both principled and empirically shown to be highly effective. While the theoretical analysis could be extended, the clarity of the problem formulation and the strength of the empirical results make this a high-quality submission. The paper addresses a difficult problem in a better way than previous research and advances the state of the art. It is a must-read for anyone working in off-policy evaluation and learning. I strongly recommend acceptance.
