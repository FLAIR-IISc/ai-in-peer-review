

This paper identifies a critical flaw in the conventional counterfactual risk estimator used in batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF), which is widely applied in recommendation systems and ad placement. The authors demonstrate that the standard unbiased estimator suffers from "propensity overfitting" when learning over complex hypothesis spaces—a phenomenon where risk estimates become arbitrarily biased, unlike conventional supervised learning overfitting. They propose a self-normalized risk estimator using multiplicative control variates, which avoids this problem by maintaining equivariance and bounded risk estimates. This leads to Norm-POEM, a new learning algorithm that consistently outperforms the conventional POEM approach across multiple multi-label classification datasets.

The paper makes a significant contribution by identifying a previously unrecognized problem (propensity overfitting) in a well-studied BLBF framework. The theoretical analysis (Theorem 2) convincingly demonstrates the self-normalized estimator's consistency and resolves the anomalies of the conventional estimator. The empirical evaluation is thorough, with careful ablation studies showing that Norm-POEM avoids propensity overfitting (as evidenced by Ŝ(h) values close to 1) and achieves better generalization across datasets. The authors also provide a detailed comparison with related work, properly situating their contribution within the NIPS literature (e.g., referencing Swaminathan & Joachims' CRM principle from ICML 2015).

Strengths include the clear identification of a meaningful problem, strong theoretical foundation, and compelling empirical results that demonstrate practical significance. The paper also addresses potential concerns about computational efficiency, showing Norm-POEM converges faster than POEM despite the normalization step. The writing is clear and well-organized, with sufficient technical detail to reproduce the results.

Weaknesses are minimal but include the limited scope of experiments (only multi-label classification datasets) and insufficient discussion of the estimator's behavior in settings with highly skewed propensity scores. While the paper demonstrates superiority over POEM, it doesn't compare against more recent BLBF methods like Doubly Robust estimators (Dudik et al., ICML 2014).

Arguments for acceptance: Novel problem identification, strong theoretical and empirical validation, practical significance for real-world bandit learning applications. Arguments against acceptance: Limited experimental scope and minimal comparison with state-of-the-art BLBF methods. However, the paper's core contribution—identifying and solving propensity overfitting—is sufficiently impactful to warrant acceptance, especially given the growing importance of BLBF in industry applications. The paper meets all four criteria: high quality (rigorous analysis), clarity (well-structured), originality (new problem formulation), and significance (improved performance in critical applications).
