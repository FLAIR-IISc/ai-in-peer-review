Summary and relation to prior work
The paper addresses batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF) under the Counterfactual Risk Minimization (CRM) framework. It identifies a failure mode of the conventional inverse propensity score (IPS) risk estimator used for learning, which the authors term propensity overfitting: ERM over complex hypothesis classes can exploit variability in importance weights, yielding degenerate empirical risks (non-equivariance and unboundedness) and selecting hypotheses that either over-represent or avoid logged actions, depending on the sign/translation of the loss. The paper proposes replacing IPS with the self-normalized importance sampling estimator (SNIPS), viewing the sum of importance weights as a multiplicative control variate. This yields a risk estimator that is bounded within the loss range, equivariant to additive loss shifts, and strongly consistent. The authors instantiate this idea in a learning algorithm, Norm-POEM, by plugging the SNIPS risk and its approximate variance into the CRM objective for exponential-family policies with linear features, optimized via L-BFGS. Empirical results on several multilabel datasets show consistent improvements over POEM (IPS-based CRM), reduced sensitivity to loss translation, better diagnostics via the normalized weight sum Ŝ(h), and competitive runtimes.

The work connects to importance sampling with variance reduction (weight clipping, control variates), doubly robust estimation for off-policy evaluation, and prior BLBF/CRM work. The use of self-normalized IS is well-known in Monte Carlo and RL off-policy evaluation (Kong, Hesterberg, Owen; also Precup et al. 2000/2001 in RL weighted IS), but this paper’s contribution is to diagnose propensity overfitting in learning and to integrate SNIPS into CRM with a practical algorithm.

Strengths
- Clear identification and explanation of a real failure mode (propensity overfitting), with an illustrative toy example and a practical diagnostic Ŝ(h) whose expectation is known to be 1.
- Simple, principled fix: using SNIPS as a multiplicative control variate yields bounded and translation-equivariant empirical risk, addressing the anomalies of IPS that drive the overfitting.
- Sound theoretical properties: strong consistency of SNIPS is established; boundedness and equivariance are immediate and important for ERM stability.
- Practical algorithmic instantiation (Norm-POEM) with sensible variance regularization under CRM; implementation details are clear and reuse the exponential-family structure.
- Compelling empirical evidence: consistent gains over POEM across datasets, robustness to loss translations, Ŝ(h) close to 1 as predicted, and useful ablations (showing variance regularization still helps). The runtime behavior is promising.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Theory: While strong consistency is shown, the CRM story here lacks a finite-sample, high-probability generalization bound tailored to the ratio estimator. The paper relies on a delta-method variance approximation (and clipping) rather than a bound analogous to the empirical-Bernstein-style analysis in prior CRM work. Even a conservative concentration result for self-normalized IS (or a PAC-Bayesian treatment) would strengthen the claims and justify the variance regularizer more rigorously.
- Baselines: The experimental comparison focuses on POEM and an unregularized variant. Missing are:
  - Doubly robust learning baselines (e.g., using DR risk or its clipped/weighted variants) which are widely used in off-policy evaluation and would test whether SNIPS still dominates when outcome models are exploited.
  - Weighted/normalized DR (if known to the authors), or at least a discussion of combining multiplicative and additive control variates.
  - Additional IPS stabilizations beyond clipping (e.g., weight shrinkage/defensive mixtures).
- Scope of “structured” outputs: The experiments factorize over labels (φ(x,y)=x⊗y), making the partition function tractable. While this is reasonable, the method’s practicality for genuinely structured spaces with intractable Z(x) is not demonstrated. A discussion (or small experiment with approximate inference) would temper or substantiate the generality claims.
- Sensitivity and implementation details: The paper notes clipping inside SNIPS to ensure finite variance, but does not present sensitivity analyses for M and λ, or clarify whether clipping is applied symmetrically in numerator/denominator (this matters). Reporting stability across a range of M and the effect of logging policy entropy would be useful. Likewise, explicit runtime numbers would support the efficiency claim.
- Related work coverage: The connection to weighted importance sampling as used in RL (Precup, Sutton, Singh) should be explicitly acknowledged; the paper cites Kong/Hesterberg/Owen, but the RL lineage is directly relevant given the off-policy setting. TRPO is orthogonal here; consider tightening the related work to focus on off-policy evaluation/learning.
- Bias-variance trade-offs: The statement that SNIPS “resolves all problems” of IPS is a bit strong; while it fixes boundedness and equivariance, SNIPS introduces small-sample bias and can still suffer when weights are extremely heavy-tailed. A nuanced discussion of when SNIPS helps (weight fluctuations dominate) versus when DR may be better would guide practitioners.

Quality
Technically sound overall. The diagnostic and fix are well-motivated, and the algorithm is coherent. The main theoretical limitation is the absence of finite-sample guarantees for the CRM with a ratio estimator. Experiments are careful and support the claims, though baselines could be broader.

Clarity
Clearly written and well-organized. The propensity overfitting phenomenon and the role of Ŝ(h) are explained crisply. Minor clarifications needed around clipping within SNIPS and the exact factorization used in the “CRF” model.

Originality
Moderate to strong. Self-normalization is classic, but its use as a learning objective within CRM, the identification of propensity overfitting in BLBF, and the translation-invariance argument are novel and insightful.

Significance
High practical relevance for counterfactual learning in IR/ads/recommendation. The fix is simple and likely to be adopted. Broader significance would be amplified by stronger theory and additional baselines.

Pros for acceptance
- Identifies and explains an important failure mode of IPS-based learning.
- Proposes a simple, principled estimator that directly addresses the issue.
- Strong empirical improvements and robust behavior across datasets.
- Easy to integrate into existing CRM-based learners.

Cons against acceptance
- Lacks finite-sample generalization bounds for the SNIPS-CRM objective.
- Experimental baselines could be expanded (DR, additional stabilizations).
- Claims about structured outputs are not tested beyond independent labels.
- Limited sensitivity/runtime analysis and some missing related citations.

Questions for the authors
- Can you provide a concentration inequality or PAC-style bound for the SNIPS-based CRM objective (perhaps with clipping), or justify the variance regularizer theoretically?
- How exactly is clipping applied within the ratio estimator, and how sensitive are results to M and λ?
- Have you tried combining self-normalization with doubly robust control variates?
- Can you report wall-clock runtimes and parameter norms to substantiate the optimization claims?
- How does performance vary with the entropy/coverage of the logging policy, and under more extreme weight distributions?

Overall recommendation
A solid and practically important contribution. Despite theoretical gaps and limited baselines, the paper advances BLBF by identifying propensity overfitting and offering an effective remedy. I lean toward acceptance, contingent on clarifying theory and adding at least a DR baseline or discussion.
