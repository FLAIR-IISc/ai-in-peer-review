- Problem: Addresses counterfactual learning from logged bandit feedback and a failure mode of IPS called “propensity overfitting.”
- Failure mode: IPS can lead to degenerate ERM solutions by exploiting fluctuations in importance weights.
- Solution: Proposes a self-normalized importance sampling (SNIS) estimator treating the sum of weights as a multiplicative control variate.
- Estimator properties: SNIS is bounded, equivariant to loss translations, and strongly consistent; variance approximated via standard ratio-estimator analysis.
- Algorithm: Instantiates the estimator in Norm-POEM for structured prediction with linear exponential-family policies and variance-regularized CRM.
- Empirical results: On supervised→bandit conversions of multi-label datasets, Norm-POEM consistently outperforms POEM (unbiased IPS).
- Diagnostics: Normalized weights concentrate near their expectation, supporting reduced propensity overfitting.
- Literature connection: Relates to prior work on importance weighting/off-policy evaluation and CRM (e.g., Rosenbaum & Rubin; Cortes et al.; Swaminathan & Joachims).
- Technical quality: Sound arguments and convincing constructed example demonstrating propensity overfitting.
- Theoretical gap: Lacks finite-sample, high-probability generalization bounds specific to the SNIS-CRM objective.
- Comparative gap: Does not quantify bias–variance trade-offs versus clipping, doubly robust, or self-normalized DR alternatives.
- Clarity: Well written, organized, and pedagogical; diagnostic Ŝ(h) is compelling.
- Minor suggestions: Explicitly relate to weighted/normalized IPS in OPE and separate asymptotic claims from finite-sample approximations.
- Originality/significance: Novel, principled integration of self-normalization into learning objectives; practically important for recommender/ads/search.
- Empirical limitations: Experiments limited to simulated logs and comparisons mainly against POEM; missing baselines (DR, clipped IPS, SWITCH) and real-logger evaluations.
- Recommendation: Strong accept with revisions to add broader baselines and clarify theoretical guarantees.
