Summary and relation to prior work
The paper addresses batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF) and the Counterfactual Risk Minimization (CRM) framework. It identifies a failure mode of the conventional inverse propensity scoring (IPS) risk estimator used in CRM—dubbed propensity overfitting—where ERM can drive the sum of importance weights away from its expected value, leading to degenerate, non-equivariant, and unbounded empirical risks. The authors propose replacing IPS with the self-normalized importance sampling estimator (SNIPS), yielding a bounded and translation-equivariant risk estimate and arguing this avoids the anomaly. They build a learning algorithm, Norm-POEM, instantiating CRM with SNIPS and a variance penalty derived from asymptotic approximations, for exponential-family stochastic policies over structured outputs. Experiments on multi-label classification via a supervised-to-bandit setup show consistent improvements over POEM and robustness to loss translations, with diagnostics indicating Ŝ(h) near 1.

The work is well-situated in Monte Carlo/IS literature (e.g., self-normalized estimators and control variates: Trotter & Tukey, Hesterberg, Owen; truncation/clipping; defensive mixtures) and in off-policy evaluation/learning (Dudík et al. doubly robust, Li et al., Thomas et al.). It extends prior CRM work (Swaminathan & Joachims) and connects to domain adaptation bounds (Cortes et al.). The identification of propensity overfitting as a learning-time phenomenon due to the estimator’s non-equivariance is insightful and, to my knowledge, not made explicit in prior BLBF learning papers.

Quality
- Theory: The diagnosis of propensity overfitting is clear and compelling, with a simple constructed example showing the ERM pathologies of IPS (unboundedness, lack of translation equivariance). The proposal to use SNIPS is well justified: boundedness, translation equivariance, and strong consistency are standard properties; the paper adapts them to the CRM context. The chosen variance penalty for SNIPS follows established asymptotic variance/Delta-method derivations for ratio estimators.
  However, the finite-sample generalization analysis lags the elegance of the original CRM bounds. The main bound stated (Theorem 1) is for capped IPS, not for the self-normalized estimator; the paper then optimizes CRM with a variance surrogate for SNIPS without a corresponding finite-sample excess risk bound. Since SNIPS is biased, some discussion of how the bias manifests in model selection and how the variance regularizer controls the trade-off would strengthen the theory. A tailored concentration result (even with clipping) for the ratio estimator in this learning setting would materially improve rigor.
- Empirics: The experimental design is solid and mirrors prior work to enable a fair comparison, including replay, same features/hypothesis class, and evaluation protocol. Results consistently favor Norm-POEM, including a convincing translation-invariance stress test and Ŝ(ĥ) diagnostics verifying mitigation of propensity overfitting. Runtime observations are plausible and practically useful.
  Nonetheless, baselines are limited. Given the causal/IS literature, comparisons to doubly robust (DR), weighted/self-normalized DR, and clipped IPS with tuned M would be informative. Since SNIPS reduces variance by normalizing weights, defensive mixtures or other variance reduction schemes are also relevant comparators. Finally, while supervised-to-bandit is reasonable for control, real logged data (even in a small case) or synthetic settings with known propensities would bolster external validity.

Clarity
The paper is clearly written and well-organized. The propensity overfitting example is particularly effective. The connection between Ŝ(h) as a control variate and the SNIPS ratio estimator is clean and easy to follow. Suggestions:
- Be explicit earlier that clipping is also applied to SNIPS weights and specify precisely how M enters Eq. (7) and Eq. (8).
- Provide pseudo-code for Norm-POEM (objective, gradients, and training loop), and report convergence criteria.
- Include standard deviations or confidence intervals for test losses in tables/figures.
- Clarify validation protocol for λ and M under counterfactual evaluation to avoid inadvertent biases.

Originality
Diagnosing propensity overfitting in IPS-based learning and leveraging SNIPS inside CRM for structured prediction is novel and useful. While SNIPS itself is classical, its explicit use as both a diagnostic (via Ŝ(h)) and a learning objective with variance regularization in CRM is, to my knowledge, new at NeurIPS. The empirical demonstration of translation robustness and bounded overfitting is a valuable contribution.

Significance
The contribution is practically impactful: IPS-based BLBF has seen adoption in interactive systems, and the revealed pathology is consequential for learning quality. Showing that a simple replacement with SNIPS substantially improves robustness and accuracy is likely to influence practice and future research. The significance would be amplified by broader baselines and at least one real-world logged dataset.

Pros
- Clear identification and explanation of a non-obvious failure mode (propensity overfitting).
- Principled, simple fix via self-normalization with strong intuitive and theoretical support.
- Consistent empirical gains across datasets; robustness to loss translations.
- Useful diagnostic (Ŝ(h)) and practical training insights (faster convergence, smaller norms).
- Open-source implementation.

Cons
- No finite-sample generalization bound for the SNIPS-based CRM objective; reliance on asymptotic variance approximations.
- Limited baselines (missing DR, weighted DR, defensive mixture, well-tuned clipping).
- Experiments limited to supervised-to-bandit; no real logged data or synthetic ground-truth propensity stress tests.
- Some implementation details (clipping within SNIPS, variance estimator behavior under clipping) could be clearer.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide a bound (or at least a discussion with empirical calibration) for the SNIPS-CRM objective under clipping, quantifying bias-variance trade-offs.
- Add DR and weighted DR baselines; include defensive mixture or weight stabilization baselines.
- Report sensitivity to M and λ; include error bars.
- Include at least one experiment on real logs or a synthetic setting with known propensities and controlled support violations.
- Clarify training details and present pseudo-code.

Overall evaluation
A strong paper with a clear problem diagnosis and an effective, principled solution. Despite the missing finite-sample theory for the new objective and limited baselines, the work advances BLBF practice and understanding. I lean toward acceptance, contingent on strengthening baselines and clarifying theory/exposition as suggested.
