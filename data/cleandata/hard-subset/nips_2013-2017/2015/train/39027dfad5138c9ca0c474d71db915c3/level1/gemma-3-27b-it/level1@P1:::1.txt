This paper addresses a crucial problem in batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF): propensity overfitting in the commonly used counterfactual risk estimator. The authors convincingly demonstrate that this estimator can lead to severely biased risk estimates, especially with complex hypothesis spaces. They propose a self-normalized estimator as a remedy, providing both theoretical justification and empirical evidence of its superior performance on multi-label classification tasks.

The paper is well-structured and clearly written. It starts with a strong motivation, laying out the challenges of learning from logged data and the importance of counterfactual reasoning. The connection to related work, including causal inference, domain adaptation, and off-policy evaluation, is well-established. The mathematical formulation is rigorous, and the presentation of the self-normalized estimator and its properties is clear and concise. The experimental setup closely follows previous work (POEM), enabling a fair comparison, and the results are compelling. The inclusion of ablation studies (CRM variance regularization) and analysis of Ŝ(ĥ) strengthens the conclusions.

**Strengths:**

*   **Important Problem:** The identification and analysis of propensity overfitting is a significant contribution to the BLBF literature. This issue hasn’t been thoroughly addressed previously, and its impact can be substantial.
*   **Sound Theoretical Foundation:** The authors provide a solid theoretical basis for their proposed self-normalized estimator, demonstrating its advantages over the conventional estimator in terms of bias and variance.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The experimental results consistently show that Norm-POEM outperforms POEM across multiple datasets, confirming the practical benefits of the proposed approach. The analysis of Ŝ(ĥ) provides valuable insight into the mechanism behind the performance improvement.
*   **Clear Writing and Organization:** The paper is easy to follow, with a logical flow of ideas and a well-defined structure.
*   **Reproducibility:** Code availability is a positive aspect promoting reproducibility and further research.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Computational Complexity:** While the authors claim runtime improvements, the discussion of the computational cost of the self-normalized estimator and its variance estimation could be more detailed. The transition from theoretical improvements to faster practical convergence needs further elaboration.  A more thorough computational analysis, including scaling with dataset and model size, would be beneficial.
*   **Limited Scope of Experiments:** The experiments are primarily focused on multi-label classification using a specific hypothesis space (CRFs). Expanding the evaluation to other problem domains and model types would strengthen the generalizability of the findings. The robustness to different logging policies (h0) also deserves investigation.
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:**  Although hyperparameter tuning is mentioned, the sensitivity of Norm-POEM's performance to the choices of *M* and *λ* isn't fully explored.  A more systematic hyperparameter study could provide further insights.
*   **Connection to Existing Importance Sampling Literature:** While the paper references relevant work on self-normalized estimators, the discussion could benefit from a more in-depth comparison with other variance reduction techniques (e.g., weighted empirical likelihood) in the context of BLBF.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper addresses a significant and previously under-appreciated issue in BLBF.
*   The proposed solution is theoretically sound and empirically validated.
*   The paper is well-written and clearly presented.
*   The code availability promotes reproducibility and further research.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   The computational complexity analysis is not sufficiently detailed.
*   The experimental scope is somewhat limited.
*   The hyperparameter sensitivity is not fully explored.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper makes a strong contribution to the field of batch learning from logged bandit feedback. The identification of propensity overfitting and the development of the self-normalized estimator are significant advances. While there are some minor weaknesses, the strengths of the paper outweigh them. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions.  Specifically, I suggest the authors provide a more detailed discussion of the computational complexity, expand the experimental evaluation (potentially including different model architectures or domains), and perform a more systematic hyperparameter study.
