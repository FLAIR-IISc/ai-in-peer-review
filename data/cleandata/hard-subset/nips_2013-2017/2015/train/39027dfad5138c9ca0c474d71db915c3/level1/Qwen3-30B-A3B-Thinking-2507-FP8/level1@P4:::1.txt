

This paper addresses a critical problem in batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF), a setting relevant to real-world applications like ad placement and recommendation systems. The authors identify a previously unrecognized issue called "propensity overfitting" in the conventional counterfactual risk estimator used in counterfactual risk minimization (CRM) algorithms. This problem occurs when the unbiased importance sampling estimator (Equation 2) leads to severely biased risk estimates for hypotheses with complex capacity, particularly when the loss function is negative. The authors propose a self-normalized risk estimator (Equation 7) based on multiplicative control variates that resolves this issue, leading to their Norm-POEM algorithm. The paper demonstrates theoretically and empirically that this approach avoids propensity overfitting, produces more reliable risk estimates, and consistently outperforms existing methods.

The paper builds on prior work in BLBF, particularly the CRM principle established in [1] and related work on importance sampling estimators [5, 6, 11]. The authors correctly position their contribution as identifying a new form of overfitting (propensity overfitting) that was not previously recognized in the BLBF literature, distinguishing it from the more commonly studied loss overfitting in supervised learning. They provide a thorough comparison with related approaches, including clipped importance sampling [15] and doubly robust estimators [6], and correctly note that their approach differs by using multiplicative control variates rather than additive ones.

Strengths: The paper makes a clear and important theoretical contribution by identifying a previously overlooked problem in BLBF. The formal definition of propensity overfitting and the examples provided in Section 4 are particularly compelling and well-justified. The theoretical analysis (Theorems 1-2) is sound and clearly demonstrates the advantages of the self-normalized estimator. The empirical evaluation is comprehensive, using multiple standard multi-label classification datasets and carefully designed experiments to verify the theoretical claims. The results consistently show that Norm-POEM outperforms POEM (which uses the conventional estimator) across different datasets, training sample sizes, and loss transformations. The paper also provides a detailed analysis of how Norm-POEM avoids propensity overfitting through the behavior of Ŝ(h), as shown in Table 2.

Weaknesses: While the paper provides a strong theoretical foundation, it could benefit from a more detailed discussion of the computational cost of the self-normalized estimator. The authors mention that the variance estimate in Equation (8) does not admit stochastic optimization, but the paper does not provide sufficient justification for why L-BFGS works well in practice despite this limitation. The paper also does not thoroughly explore the sensitivity of the approach to the choice of the control variate beyond the theoretical expectation that it should be correlated with the importance weights. Additionally, while the paper claims the approach is applicable to structured output prediction, the empirical evaluation is limited to multi-label classification, and it would be valuable to see results on more complex structured prediction tasks.

The paper is well-written and clearly organized, with a logical flow from problem identification to theoretical solution to empirical validation. The technical details are presented with sufficient rigor for a NIPS audience, and the authors provide enough information for readers to understand and potentially reproduce their results. The experimental setup is carefully designed and follows the established methodology in the BLBF literature [1, 2].

The contribution is novel in identifying propensity overfitting as a distinct problem from conventional overfitting and proposing a self-normalized estimator to address it. The authors have properly cited relevant prior work and clearly distinguish their contribution from existing methods. This appears to be a significant improvement over previous approaches, as demonstrated by the consistent empirical gains.

The significance of this work is substantial, as BLBF is a critical problem for real-world applications where interactive learning is impractical. By addressing a fundamental limitation in current approaches, this work enables more reliable and effective learning from historical logged data. The Norm-POEM algorithm provides a practical solution that outperforms existing methods without requiring significant computational overhead, as demonstrated in the runtime analysis.

Pro arguments for acceptance: Clear identification of a previously unrecognized problem in BLBF; sound theoretical analysis demonstrating the advantages of the proposed approach; comprehensive empirical validation across multiple datasets showing consistent improvements; practical algorithm with minimal computational overhead; significant relevance to real-world applications.

Con arguments against acceptance: Limited empirical evaluation on structured prediction tasks beyond multi-label classification; some details about computational complexity and optimization are not fully explained; the paper would benefit from more discussion about the choice of control variate.

Overall, this is a high-quality paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of counterfactual learning. The problem identified is important, the proposed solution is novel and theoretically sound, and the empirical results provide convincing evidence of the approach's superiority. The paper is well-written and clearly presented. The weaknesses identified are relatively minor and do not detract from the paper's overall quality and contribution. This paper should be accepted to NIPS.
