Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper addresses batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF) under the Counterfactual Risk Minimization (CRM) framework. Prior BLBF work largely relies on importance sampling (IS) with inverse propensity scoring (IPS) to construct unbiased counterfactual risk estimators, sometimes with clipping to control variance. The authors identify a failure mode—propensity overfitting—arising when ERM over complex hypothesis classes exploits variability in importance weights rather than fitting low-loss examples. They propose replacing the unbiased estimator with a self-normalized importance sampling (SNIS, or “ratio”) estimator, which uses the known expectation of the propensity weight sum as a multiplicative control variate. They show the SN estimator is bounded and equivariant under loss translation and is strongly consistent. They then instantiate CRM with SNIS to obtain a learning algorithm, Norm-POEM, for structured output prediction using exponential family (CRF-like) models. Empirical studies on multilabel classification datasets (Scene, Yeast, TMC, LYRL) using a supervised-to-bandit protocol show improved generalization compared to POEM, reduced deviations of the weight-sum diagnostic Ŝ(h) from one, and robustness to loss translations. The work is related to importance sampling variance reduction (clipping, control variates), doubly robust estimators, and high-confidence off-policy evaluation. Its main contribution is to bring SNIS (widely known in Monte Carlo, RL/OPE) into the BLBF learning objective and to diagnose and mitigate propensity overfitting.

Quality:
- Technical soundness: The identification of propensity overfitting is well motivated via a clear synthetic example. The use of a ratio estimator is standard, and the properties cited (boundedness, equivariance, strong consistency) are correct under the stated assumptions. The diagnostic Ŝ(h) having expectation 1 is correct and central to the approach. The proposed variance estimate for SNIS via delta/normal approximation is standard, though approximate. The learning objective follows CRM with a variance regularizer adapted to SNIS.
- Theoretical completeness: While the paper shows consistency and qualitative benefits (boundedness/equivariance), it does not provide finite-sample generalization bounds for the SNIS-based CRM objective analogous to prior IPS-based bounds. The reliance on approximate variance (and clipping) means bias-variance trade-offs are handled empirically rather than with guarantees. A formal treatment of how SNIS mitigates propensity overfitting during ERM (e.g., bounds coupling deviations in Ŝ(h) and risk estimation error, or a CRM-style bound specific to SNIS) would strengthen the theory.
- Experimental methodology: The supervised-to-bandit setup follows prior work and is appropriate for controlled comparisons. Multiple datasets, averaged over 10 runs, comparisons to POEM and an ERM skyline (full-information CRF) are sound. The evaluation of Ŝ(ĥ) and invariance to loss translation directly tests the core claim. However, comparisons to doubly robust estimators or other OPE/learning methods (e.g., DR, regression-based control variates) are absent; these would be informative given the paper’s framing around variance reduction and bias control. Confidence intervals or statistical tests for performance differences would also help.

Clarity:
The paper is clearly written and well organized. The exposition of BLBF/CRM, the propensity overfitting phenomenon, and the properties of SNIS is accessible. Example 1 is effective. The variance formula and training objective are presented with enough detail to implement, and optimization choices (L-BFGS) are justified. Suggested clarifications:
- Provide a precise definition of propensity overfitting and conditions under which it arises.
- State assumptions for consistency (support and bounded moments) and implications of clipping on asymptotic bias.
- Include pseudocode for Norm-POEM and more detail on hyperparameter selection, computational costs, and convergence behavior.
- Report confidence intervals/standard errors and, if possible, significance tests.

Originality:
Using self-normalized IS as a multiplicative control variate is known in Monte Carlo and RL/OPE, and the properties of SNIS are classical. The novelty here is (i) identifying and formalizing propensity overfitting in BLBF learning with IPS, (ii) integrating SNIS into the CRM learning objective for structured prediction, and (iii) demonstrating empirical benefits and robustness to loss translations. This is an incremental but meaningful contribution: it translates a well-established IS variant into the BLBF learning setting and shows it resolves a concrete failure mode of IPS-based ERM.

Significance:
The work addresses a practical and important issue for off-policy learning from logs in applications like ads/recs/search. The proposed change is simple to adopt and shows consistent empirical gains over the strongest prior BLBF baseline (POEM) with improved robustness diagnostics. However, the empirical scope is limited to synthetic bandit feedback derived from supervised datasets and a CRF-like hypothesis family; no real-world logs are shown, and comparisons to DR-style learning are missing. A finite-sample bound for SNIS-CRM would increase impact.

Pros:
- Clear identification and diagnosis of propensity overfitting.
- Sound, well-motivated use of self-normalized IS; boundedness and equivariance are practically important.
- Consistent empirical improvements over POEM and robustness to loss translations; Ŝ(h) diagnostic validates the mechanism.
- Simple to implement within existing CRM frameworks; code availability.

Cons:
- No finite-sample generalization bound tailored to SNIS within CRM; reliance on approximate variance estimates.
- Limited baselines (no doubly robust/regression estimators, no recent high-confidence OPE-based learners).
- Experiments confined to supervised-to-bandit simulations; no real logged data; lack of statistical significance reporting.
- Discussion of clipping-induced bias and conditions for consistency could be more rigorous.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Develop a CRM-style generalization bound for SNIS, possibly leveraging empirical Bernstein-type arguments for ratio estimators.
- Compare against doubly robust learning and other control-variate approaches; include ablations on clipping level M and λ.
- Provide statistical uncertainty on reported metrics and, if feasible, results on real logged data.
- Clarify the structured model used (independence assumptions, tractability of Z) and the scalability of optimization.

Overall assessment:
Technically sound and clearly presented, with moderate novelty and practical relevance. The empirical evidence supports the main claim that SNIS mitigates propensity overfitting and improves generalization in BLBF within the CRM framework. The lack of finite-sample theory and broader baselines limits the strength of the contribution but does not undermine its utility. Arguments for acceptance outweigh those against, especially for the NeurIPS audience interested in off-policy learning from logs.
