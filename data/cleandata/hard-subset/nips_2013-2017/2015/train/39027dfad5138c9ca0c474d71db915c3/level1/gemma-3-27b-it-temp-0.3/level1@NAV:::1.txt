## Review of "The Self-Normalized Estimator for Counterfactual Learning"

This paper addresses a critical issue in Batch Learning from Logged Bandit Feedback (BLBF): propensity overfitting in the commonly used counterfactual risk estimator. The authors convincingly demonstrate that this estimator can lead to severely biased risk estimates, particularly with complex hypothesis spaces. They propose a self-normalized estimator as a solution, theoretically grounding its advantages and empirically validating its effectiveness through experiments on multi-label classification problems. The work builds upon the established CRM principle [1] and connects to broader areas like causal inference, domain adaptation, and off-policy evaluation [2, 4, 7, 8].

The paper’s strength lies in its clear identification and articulation of a novel overfitting phenomenon – propensity overfitting – and its elegant solution. The theoretical analysis demonstrating the estimator’s properties (equivariance, consistency) is well-presented. The experimental results consistently show Norm-POEM outperforming the conventional estimator, and the ablation studies (Table 3) provide valuable insights into the importance of variance regularization. The connection to existing importance sampling techniques [12, 19] and the discussion of computational efficiency are also commendable. 

However, the paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion of the practical implications of the choice of 'M' (clipping parameter) and its sensitivity to different datasets. While the authors mention calibration on a held-out set, a more in-depth analysis of this parameter’s impact would be valuable. Additionally, the explanation of why Norm-POEM converges faster despite the increased per-iteration cost could be expanded. While the authors hint at the smaller norm of the learned weights, a more thorough investigation would strengthen this claim. The presentation of the theoretical results, particularly Theorem 1, could be more accessible by providing more intuition behind the terms and their significance.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Addresses a significant and previously unrecognised problem in BLBF.
*   Provides a theoretically sound and empirically validated solution.
*   Clear and well-written presentation of the problem, proposed solution, and experimental results.
*   Strong connection to related work and a clear demonstration of improvement over existing methods.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The discussion of the clipping parameter 'M' could be more detailed.
*   Further explanation of the faster convergence of Norm-POEM is needed.
*   Theoretical results could be presented with more intuition.



**Overall:** This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of learning from logged bandit feedback. The identified problem is important, the proposed solution is elegant and effective, and the experimental validation is convincing. I recommend acceptance.
