- Addresses batch learning from logged bandit feedback under CRM; diagnoses "propensity overfitting" of IPS and proposes SNIPS as a replacement.  
- Frames SNIPS as using the sum of importance weights as a multiplicative control variate, yielding bounded, translation-equivariant, and consistent risk estimates.  
- Introduces Norm-POEM: plugs SNIPS risk and an approximate variance term into CRM for exponential-family policies, optimized with L-BFGS.  
- Empirical results on multilabel datasets show consistent improvements over POEM, reduced sensitivity to loss translation, and useful diagnostics via Ŝ(h).  
- Connects to existing variance-reduction and off-policy estimation work (weight clipping, control variates, doubly robust methods); notes SNIPS is well-known in MC/RL but novel in this CRM learning context.  
- Strength: Clear identification and illustration of propensity overfitting, plus a diagnostic Ŝ(h) whose expectation equals 1.  
- Strength: Simple, principled fix (SNIPS) that addresses IPS anomalies (boundedness and equivariance).  
- Strength: Theoretical property—strong consistency of SNIPS is established; boundedness and equivariance promote ERM stability.  
- Strength: Practical algorithmic instantiation with clear implementation details leveraging exponential-family structure.  
- Strength: Compelling empirical evidence including ablations and promising runtime behavior.  
- Weakness: Lacks finite-sample, high-probability generalization bounds tailored to the ratio estimator; relies on delta-method variance approximation and clipping.  
- Weakness: Experimental baselines are limited—missing doubly robust learning baselines and other IPS stabilizations (weight shrinkage, defensive mixtures).  
- Weakness: Scope of structured outputs is limited to factorized multilabels with tractable partition functions; generality to intractable Z(x) not demonstrated.  
- Weakness: Insufficient sensitivity analysis and implementation detail on clipping (M, λ) and whether clipping is symmetric; explicit runtime numbers are missing.  
- Weakness: Related work should more explicitly acknowledge RL lineage of weighted IS (Precup et al.) and tighten focus on off-policy evaluation/learning.  
- Weakness: Overstates SNIPS benefits—introduces small-sample bias and can still fail with heavy-tailed weights; needs nuanced bias–variance discussion and when DR might be preferable.  
- Quality: Technically sound and coherent, but main theoretical limitation is absence of finite-sample CRM guarantees for the ratio estimator.  
- Clarity: Well-written and organized; propensity overfitting and Ŝ(h) are explained clearly, with minor clarifications needed on clipping and factorization.  
- Originality: Moderate–strong—self-normalization is classic but diagnosing propensity overfitting and integrating SNIPS into CRM is novel and insightful.  
- Significance: High practical relevance for counterfactual learning in IR/ads/recommendation; likely to be adopted with stronger theory and baselines.  
- Pros for acceptance: Identifies an important failure mode, proposes a principled fix, shows strong empirical gains, and is easy to integrate into existing CRM learners.  
- Cons against acceptance: Missing finite-sample bounds, limited experimental baselines, untested claims on structured outputs, and limited sensitivity/runtime analyses.  
- Key questions for authors: Provide concentration/PAC bounds for SNIPS-CRM (with clipping)? Clarify clipping application and sensitivity to M/λ? Tried combining SNIPS with doubly robust control variates? Report wall-clock runtimes and performance vs. logging-policy entropy?  
- Overall recommendation: Solid, practically important contribution with a lean toward acceptance contingent on clarifying theory and adding at least a DR baseline or discussion.
