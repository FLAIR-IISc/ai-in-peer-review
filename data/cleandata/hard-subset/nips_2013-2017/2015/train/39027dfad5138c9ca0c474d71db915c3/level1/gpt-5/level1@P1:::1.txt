Summary and relation to prior work
The paper addresses batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF) under the Counterfactual Risk Minimization (CRM) framework. Prior BLBF work typically optimizes an unbiased inverse propensity score (IPS) estimator, possibly with clipping, within a variance-regularized ERM objective (e.g., POEM). The authors identify a failure mode of IPS-based ERM they call propensity overfitting: the learned policy exploits variability of importance weights to drive the empirical risk arbitrarily low or high, depending on loss translation, leading to poor generalization. They propose replacing IPS with the self-normalized importance sampling estimator (SNIPS), viewing the sum of weights as a multiplicative control variate. They argue SNIPS avoids the anomalies of IPS (equivariance to loss translation, bounded estimates) and is strongly consistent. They then instantiate CRM with SNIPS and an approximate variance estimator to obtain Norm-POEM for structured prediction with exponential family models. Experiments on multi-label datasets (Scene, Yeast, TMC, LYRL) using the supervised→bandit protocol show consistent improvements over POEM, stability under loss translation, better diagnostics Ŝ(h)≈1, and competitive runtime.

This work is grounded in a rich literature: IPS and clipped IPS in BLBF/causal inference (Rosenbaum & Rubin; Strehl et al.; Li et al.; Cortes et al.), CRM (Swaminathan & Joachims), control variates and self-normalized IS (Tukey & Trotter; Hesterberg; Owen), and off-policy evaluation (Dudík et al. doubly robust; Thomas et al. high-confidence OPE). The novelty is not the estimator itself, but identifying and mitigating propensity overfitting within policy learning.

Quality
- Theory: The paper clearly explains why IPS-based ERM can be degenerate, with an instructive example. It proves strong consistency of SNIPS and explains boundedness and equivariance—important properties when optimizing an empirical objective. However, there is no finite-sample generalization bound for CRM with SNIPS analogous to prior results for IPS-based CRM, and the variance regularizer is based on an approximation. The interaction of clipping with SNIPS is not analyzed theoretically. A discussion of potential bias when the denominator is small (and guarantees that the variance exists under clipping) is helpful but not formalized in a generalization guarantee.
- Methodology: The Norm-POEM objective is well specified, and variance regularization remains beneficial empirically. The choice of L-BFGS for non-convex objectives is reasonable.
- Experiments: The supervised→bandit protocol is appropriate and consistent with prior work. Results demonstrate gains across datasets and robustness to loss translation, with a useful diagnostic via Ŝ(h). However, baselines are limited: there is no comparison to doubly robust or weighted/normalized DR estimators (even if contemporary variants), nor to other variance-reduced IPS variants beyond POEM. Sensitivity analyses for M and λ, and ablations for clipping thresholds, are limited in the text. Error bars or significance tests are not reported.

Clarity
The paper is clearly written and well organized. The propensity overfitting phenomenon and the benefits of SNIPS are explained intuitively and formally. Assumptions (full support of logging policy) are explicit. Some implementation details could be expanded (e.g., how the structured exponential-family model factorizes to make Z(x) tractable for multi-label; details of hyperparameter search and stability; numerical safeguards when ∑ weights is small). Figures/tables (referenced) appear to support claims; including confidence intervals would improve clarity.

Originality
The key estimator (SNIPS) is known. The main contribution is the identification of propensity overfitting in IPS-based CRM and the principled use of SNIPS within learning, along with diagnostics and a new objective (Norm-POEM). This is a novel and useful combination for BLBF learning, though the theoretical contribution is more incremental than foundational.

Significance
The work addresses a real and important problem in counterfactual learning, with practical implications for recommender systems, ads, and search. The identified failure mode is compelling and likely affects many IPS-based learners. The proposed fix is simple, widely applicable, and yields consistent empirical benefits. Strengthening theory and baselines would further increase impact, but as it stands, the paper advances the state of practice and understanding in BLBF.

Strengths
- Clear identification and exposition of a critical failure mode (propensity overfitting) with concrete examples and diagnostics.
- Simple, well-motivated fix via self-normalization; bounded, equivariant, strongly consistent estimator.
- Practical algorithm (Norm-POEM) with variance regularization and tractable optimization.
- Consistent empirical improvements and robustness to loss translation; code availability; reasonable runtime behavior.

Weaknesses
- Limited theoretical guarantees for CRM with SNIPS (no finite-sample bound analogous to prior CRM results; approximate variance).
- Experimental baselines miss DR/normalized DR (where available), regression-based or model-based baselines, and more severe/realistic logging policies (partial support).
- Limited sensitivity analyses (effect of clipping M, denominator small events, high-variance rewards).
- The “structured” setting appears to reduce to independent-label models; scalability to truly structured outputs with intractable Z(x) is not demonstrated.

Questions and suggestions
- Can you derive an empirical Bernstein-style generalization bound for SNIPS-based CRM (with clipping) to parallel Theorem 1? Even a bias-aware bound would be valuable.
- How sensitive is Norm-POEM to the clipping threshold M and λ? Please include plots and confidence intervals.
- Compare against doubly robust estimators (and if feasible, weighted/normalized DR) under the same setup.
- Discuss/experiment with partial-support logging (propensity zero) and practical remedies (e.g., exploration, learned propensity smoothing).
- Clarify the multi-label model factorization and computation of Z(x), and report on scalability to larger q or genuinely structured Y.
- Provide safeguards when the denominator is small (e.g., normalization floor, mixture with uniform logging).

Arguments for acceptance
- Identifies an important and underappreciated failure mode in a widely used learning setup.
- Offers a principled, simple, and broadly applicable remedy that improves robustness.
- Solid empirical evidence on multiple datasets; diagnostic matches theory; code available.
- Clear writing; likely to influence practitioners of off-policy learning.

Arguments against acceptance
- The estimator is known; novelty lies mainly in application and empirical demonstration.
- Missing finite-sample theory for the new CRM objective; reliance on approximate variance.
- Incomplete experimental comparison set and sensitivity analysis; evaluation limited to one family of tasks/models.

Overall recommendation
A strong, practice-relevant contribution that clarifies and remedies a critical issue in counterfactual learning. While theoretical development and baselines could be strengthened, the clarity of the problem, simplicity of the solution, and consistent empirical gains make this paper a good fit for NIPS. I lean toward acceptance.
