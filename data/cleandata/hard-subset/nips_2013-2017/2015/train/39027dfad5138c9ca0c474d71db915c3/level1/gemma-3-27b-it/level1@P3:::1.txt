This paper addresses a crucial issue in batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF): propensity overfitting, a phenomenon where the standard counterfactual risk estimator can lead to severely biased risk estimates, especially with complex hypothesis spaces. The authors convincingly demonstrate this problem and propose a self-normalized estimator as a remedy, implemented in a new algorithm, Norm-POEM. The theoretical justification and empirical validation are strong, and the paper is well-written and clearly organized.

**Summary & Relation to Prior Work:**

The paper builds directly upon the CRM framework [1] for BLBF, which itself draws connections to off-policy evaluation in RL [4] and causal inference. Previous work largely relied on the unbiased estimator described in [6, 5, 1]. This paper directly challenges that consensus, arguing – and demonstrating – that this estimator suffers from a previously unrecognized form of overfitting. The self-normalized estimator is adapted from importance sampling literature [12, 19], but its application to address *propensity* overfitting in the context of BLBF is novel. The paper appropriately positions itself within related work, referencing relevant areas like covariate shift [8], doubly robust estimators [6], and importance sampling variance reduction techniques [15].  The connection to financial applications of self-normalized estimators [17] is a nice touch, providing broader context.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty and Significance:** Identifying and addressing propensity overfitting is a significant contribution. This problem is fundamental to the success of BLBF and has been largely overlooked. The self-normalized estimator offers a compelling solution.
* **Strong Theoretical Justification:** The paper provides a clear explanation of why the standard estimator fails and why the self-normalized estimator avoids this failure. Theorem 2 establishes strong consistency, reassuring the reader of the estimator's asymptotic behavior.
* **Comprehensive Empirical Evaluation:** The experiments are well-designed, using a standard methodology [2] and a variety of datasets. The results consistently demonstrate the superiority of Norm-POEM over POEM. The ablation studies (Table 3) are valuable in understanding the impact of variance regularization. The analysis of Ŝ(ĥ) (Table 2) provides direct evidence supporting the claim of reduced propensity overfitting.
* **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is exceptionally well-written. The introduction clearly motivates the problem, the related work section is thorough, and the methodology is presented in a logical and accessible manner. Mathematical notation is used appropriately and consistently.
* **Practical Considerations:** The authors address computational efficiency and provide readily available code, enhancing the paper’s impact.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** While the authors mention calibration of hyperparameters M and λ, the process could be elaborated further.  A more detailed discussion of the sensitivity of performance to these parameters would be beneficial.  How does performance change with different choices, and are there guidelines for choosing appropriate values based on dataset characteristics?
* **Limited Scope of Experimentation:** The experiments focus solely on multi-label classification with a specific feature representation (φ(x, y) = x ⊗ y).  Exploring other hypothesis spaces or feature representations could broaden the applicability of the findings. Would the benefits of the self-normalized estimator persist in different problem settings (e.g., regression, different structured prediction tasks)?
* **Variance Estimator Complexity:**  The empirical variance estimator (Equation 8) appears computationally expensive. The authors acknowledge this but don’t delve into potential approximations or efficient implementations.  Investigating methods to reduce the computational burden of the variance estimation would be valuable.
* **Connection to Existing Variance Reduction Techniques:** While the paper references variance reduction techniques, it doesn't deeply explore the relationship between the self-normalized estimator and other methods like clipped importance sampling [15]. A more comprehensive comparison could reveal insights into the strengths and weaknesses of each approach.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* Addresses a critical and previously unrecognised issue in BLBF.
* Provides a theoretically sound and empirically validated solution.
* Well-written, clear, and well-organized.
* Makes a significant contribution to the field of learning from logged data.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* Limited scope of experimental evaluation.
* Computational complexity of the variance estimator.
* Lack of detailed discussion on hyperparameter sensitivity.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of batch learning from logged bandit feedback. The identified problem of propensity overfitting is important, and the proposed self-normalized estimator offers a compelling solution. The empirical results are convincing, and the paper is exceptionally well-written. While there are minor weaknesses, they do not detract significantly from the overall quality of the work.  I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions addressing the points raised above (primarily expanding the discussion of hyperparameter tuning and exploring potential approximations to the variance estimator).
