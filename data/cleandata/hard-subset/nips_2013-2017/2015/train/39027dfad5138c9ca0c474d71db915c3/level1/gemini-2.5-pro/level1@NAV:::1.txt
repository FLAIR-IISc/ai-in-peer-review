This paper identifies a critical failure mode, termed "Propensity Overfitting," in the standard inverse propensity score (IPS) estimator used for batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF). The authors show that when learning over complex hypothesis spaces, models can minimize the empirical risk by manipulating propensity weights rather than fitting the true loss function, leading to unboundedly optimistic risk estimates. To address this, they propose replacing the standard estimator with a self-normalized (ratio) estimator, a known technique from the Monte Carlo literature. This estimator normalizes the weighted losses by the sum of the weights. The authors integrate this robust estimator into the Counterfactual Risk Minimization (CRM) framework of Swaminathan & Joachims (ICML 2015), resulting in a new algorithm, Norm-POEM. This work is a direct and important extension of prior research on counterfactual learning, a topic of significant interest to the NIPS community.

The paper’s strengths are numerous.
**Pro:**
*   **Significance & Clarity:** The primary contribution is the clear identification and diagnosis of a fundamental and previously overlooked problem. The concept of Propensity Overfitting is explained exceptionally well, both intuitively and through a compelling and simple example.
*   **Quality:** The proposed solution is elegant, well-grounded in statistical theory (control variates), and provably resolves the anomalies of the standard estimator. It possesses desirable properties like equivariance to loss translation and produces estimates bounded within the loss range.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The experimental results are comprehensive and convincingly demonstrate that Norm-POEM not only outperforms its predecessor in generalization but is also robust to the loss-shifting pathologies that cause the standard approach to fail catastrophically. The finding that it also converges faster is a significant practical advantage.

The paper is of high quality, and its weaknesses are minor.
**Con:**
*   **Originality:** The self-normalized estimator itself is not novel, as the authors appropriately acknowledge. The core novelty lies in identifying the specific problem it solves in the *learning* context and analyzing its benefits, which is a solid contribution.
*   **Scalability Discussion:** The authors note their variance estimate precludes stochastic optimization, necessitating batch methods like L-BFGS. While experiments show this is effective and even faster on the tested datasets, a brief discussion on potential scalability limitations for massive-scale problems, where stochastic methods are often essential, would strengthen the paper.

In summary, this is an excellent paper that makes a clear and significant contribution. It addresses a crucial problem with a principled solution backed by strong theoretical motivation and compelling empirical evidence. The work is well-written and will likely influence future research in off-policy learning and evaluation. I strongly recommend acceptance.
