

**Review of "The Self-Normalized Estimator for Counterfactual Learning"**

**Summary**  
This paper addresses a critical issue in batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF), a setting with significant real-world applications in ad placement, recommendation systems, and online learning. The authors identify a previously unrecognized problem—propensity overfitting—in the conventional counterfactual risk estimator used in the Counterfactual Risk Minimization (CRM) framework [1]. This problem causes severe bias in risk estimates when optimizing over complex hypothesis spaces, leading to suboptimal generalization. The authors propose a self-normalized risk estimator that avoids this issue by incorporating multiplicative control variates, which they theoretically justify and empirically validate through a new algorithm, Norm-POEM. This work builds on and extends the CRM principle introduced in [1], demonstrating that their estimator resolves key anomalies present in prior approaches.

**Quality**  
The paper presents a technically sound contribution with strong theoretical foundations. The authors rigorously demonstrate that the conventional estimator exhibits a propensity overfitting problem that can lead to arbitrarily biased risk estimates, even when using standard variance regularization. Their theoretical analysis is thorough, with clear definitions and well-structured proofs (Theorems 1 and 2). The empirical evaluation is comprehensive, using multiple multi-label classification datasets and comparing against the baseline POEM algorithm. The results consistently show that Norm-POEM outperforms POEM across all datasets and training sample sizes, with the self-normalized estimator effectively preventing propensity overfitting as evidenced by Ŝ(ĥ) being close to its expected value of 1. The paper is complete and well-supported, though I would have appreciated additional analysis of the optimization landscape and convergence properties of Norm-POEM, particularly given the non-convex nature of the training objective. The authors acknowledge that the variance regularization still provides benefits (Table 3), but a deeper exploration of the trade-offs between the self-normalized estimator and variance regularization would strengthen the theoretical contribution.

**Clarity**  
The paper is exceptionally well-written and well-organized. The authors clearly define the problem, introduce necessary notation, and systematically develop their arguments. The examples illustrating propensity overfitting (Example 1) are particularly effective in demonstrating the severity of the problem. The explanation of control variates and the self-normalized estimator is accessible, even for readers unfamiliar with importance sampling. The experimental setup is well-described, and the results are presented with sufficient detail to be reproducible. The only minor suggestion would be to include standard deviations in the reported results (Table 1) to better convey the stability of the performance improvements.

**Originality**  
This work makes a significant contribution to the BLBF literature by identifying a previously unrecognized problem (propensity overfitting) and providing a theoretically grounded solution. While the paper builds on the CRM framework [1], the modification to the risk estimator is novel and has substantial practical implications. The use of multiplicative control variates to create a self-normalized estimator is a clever adaptation of established techniques from importance sampling literature [19, 22], but the authors demonstrate its specific utility for BLBF in a way that is distinct from prior work. The paper's originality is further reinforced by the empirical evidence showing consistent performance improvements across multiple datasets, which goes beyond the incremental contributions typical in follow-up work.

**Significance**  
The significance of this work is substantial. BLBF is a critical problem for real-world applications where interactive learning is impractical, and the authors' solution directly addresses a fundamental limitation in current approaches. While the paper focuses on multi-label classification, the theoretical insights about propensity overfitting are general and could benefit other BLBF applications. The empirical results demonstrate that the proposed method not only avoids the theoretical pitfalls but also delivers tangible improvements in generalization performance. The authors' claim that Norm-POEM is competitive with full-information CRFs (the "skyline" for what's achievable in BLBF) is well-supported. The paper would be even more impactful if it included one real-world application beyond the supervised-to-bandit conversion experiments, but the significance is still high given the problem's relevance and the demonstrated improvements.

**Specific Comments**  
- Line 365: Please report standard deviations for the results in Table 1 to better convey the stability of the performance improvements.
- The paper would benefit from a more detailed discussion of the computational efficiency of Norm-POEM, particularly given the authors' observation that it converges faster than POEM despite higher per-iteration cost.
- The comparison to other variance reduction techniques (e.g., clipped importance weights) is somewhat brief; a more thorough discussion of how the self-normalized estimator compares to these alternatives would strengthen the contribution.

**Recommendation**  
This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the BLBF literature. The identification of propensity overfitting and the introduction of the self-normalized estimator address a significant gap in the current understanding of counterfactual learning. The theoretical arguments are sound, the empirical validation is comprehensive, and the writing is excellent. While the paper would be strengthened by additional experimental analysis and some discussion of the optimization properties, these are minor points in what is otherwise a high-quality contribution. I recommend acceptance.
