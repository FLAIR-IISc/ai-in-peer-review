I had limited time to complete this review due to my current travel schedule, which is unfortunate given that I found this paper quite engaging. As a result, I may have overlooked or misinterpreted certain points, and I apologize in advance if this is the case.

When observing data from a given hypothesis, we may be interested in evaluating a different hypothesis. For non-deterministic hypotheses, such as conditional distributions, we can estimate the loss under the hypothesis being investigated by reweighting the data points, as seen in the equation between (1) and (2).

The authors appear to address a problem related to, yet distinct from, the issue of large variance in the weights. They term this problem "propensity overfitting." The provided examples suggest that this problem arises when there is insufficient exploration, resulting in only a few or even no observations of y for a given x that correspond to the new hypothesis being evaluated. A similar argument explains the large variance of the weights, so it would be beneficial to elaborate on the relationship between these two problems in more detail.

Due to time constraints, I was unable to thoroughly examine the details or assess the novelty of the proposed solution, but the underlying idea seems reasonable.

I have a few specific comments on the paper:

- Line 199: I am unclear about this point. If n is small compared to k, we would expect to see only a few data points where xi = yi, correct? In that case, shouldn't \hat R(h^) be even smaller than -2? Am I missing something?

- The statement "unbiased counterfactual risk estimator used in prior works on BLBF [4, 5, 1]" seems potentially misleading. Although I am not familiar with all three papers, I suspect that they may employ techniques like clipping, which would result in biased estimation with reduced variance.

I appreciate that the authors have made their code available.

In summary, I was unable to thoroughly verify the technical soundness of the paper. However, it undoubtedly presents interesting ideas that warrant further consideration. Therefore, I suggest accepting the paper.
