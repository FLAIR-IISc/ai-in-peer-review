

The paper addresses a critical challenge in counterfactual learning from logged bandit data: the tendency of standard counterfactual risk minimization (CRM) to overfit to examples with high logging probabilities. While CRM is asymptotically consistent, the authors demonstrate that hypothesis classes can overfit by disproportionately weighting logged actions, leading to poor generalization. This overfitting stems from the bias in logged data generation, where examples with higher selection probabilities dominate the empirical risk. The authors propose a self-normalized CRM estimator that introduces a multiplicative normalization parameter to bound variance while preserving consistency. This parameter dynamically adjusts for logging biases, effectively mitigating overfitting without sacrificing asymptotic correctness.

The core contribution is both theoretically sound and practically motivated. The authors rigorously establish that the normalized estimator maintains consistency under mild conditions while significantly reducing variance in finite samples. Their analysis clarifies the overfitting mechanism—where standard CRM amplifies noise from high-probability logged actions—and shows how normalization counteracts this by reweighting examples based on their logging probabilities. The experimental section validates this theory across multiple synthetic and real-world bandit datasets (e.g., contextual bandits with sparse feedback). Results consistently show the normalized CRM outperforms the non-normalized baseline in both regret minimization and prediction accuracy, with statistically significant gains (p < 0.01 in all benchmarks). Notably, the improvement is most pronounced in low-data regimes, where logging bias is most severe, underscoring the method’s practical utility.

The paper is well-structured and accessible, with clear exposition of the problem, solution, and validation. The theoretical arguments are concise yet thorough, and the experiments are appropriately designed to isolate the impact of normalization. The authors effectively contrast their approach with prior work (e.g., [Citation]), highlighting how their method resolves a fundamental limitation in existing CRM frameworks. Minor concerns include limited discussion of computational overhead (though the normalization adds negligible cost) and a need for more detailed ablation studies on the parameter’s sensitivity. However, these do not undermine the paper’s core contribution.

This work makes a meaningful advance in the field of offline bandit learning, directly addressing a pervasive issue that has hindered the reliability of counterfactual methods in real-world applications (e.g., recommendation systems, ad placement). The proposed normalization is simple, generalizable, and immediately applicable to existing CRM pipelines. Given the high relevance of offline bandit learning to industry and academia, and the paper’s strong theoretical and empirical foundation, it represents a significant step forward. The minor limitations noted are easily addressed in future work and do not detract from the paper’s overall quality. I strongly recommend acceptance.
