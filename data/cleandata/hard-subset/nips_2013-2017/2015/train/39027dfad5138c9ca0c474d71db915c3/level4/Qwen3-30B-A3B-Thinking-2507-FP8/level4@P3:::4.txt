

This paper addresses a critical issue in batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF), where conventional counterfactual risk estimators suffer from "propensity overfitting" when used with complex hypothesis spaces. The authors identify this problem through theoretical analysis and empirical examples, then propose a self-normalized risk estimator that resolves the issue by using multiplicative control variates. This leads to their Norm-POEM algorithm for structured output prediction, which demonstrates improved generalization performance across multiple multi-label classification datasets.

The paper builds on Swaminathan and Joachims' Counterfactual Risk Minimization (CRM) framework [1], which established a principled approach to BLBF learning using importance sampling. While prior work focused on unbiased estimators (e.g., inverse propensity scores), this paper reveals their fundamental limitation: they are not equivariant under loss translation and can produce arbitrarily biased risk estimates. This propensity overfitting is distinct from conventional loss overfitting and represents a significant theoretical gap in the BLBF literature. The authors' analysis of this problem is thorough, with clear examples demonstrating how propensity overfitting occurs regardless of loss transformation, and their solution using a self-normalized estimator is theoretically sound and practically effective.

The paper's main strengths lie in its originality and significance. The identification of propensity overfitting as a distinct problem in BLBF represents a novel contribution that extends beyond previous work on importance sampling and counterfactual evaluation. The theoretical analysis is rigorous, with Theorems 1 and 2 establishing the properties of the self-normalized estimator. The empirical evaluation is comprehensive, showing consistent improvements of Norm-POEM over POEM across multiple datasets (Scene, Yeast, TMC, LYRL) with varying sizes and label structures. The results demonstrate not only better generalization but also robustness to loss transformations and improved control over propensity overfitting, as evidenced by the stability of Ŝ(ĥ) near its expected value of 1.

The paper is well-written and clearly organized, with a logical progression from problem identification to theoretical solution to empirical validation. The authors effectively contextualize their work within the BLBF literature, acknowledging relevant prior work on importance sampling [12, 19] and control variates [21]. The connection to causal inference and domain adaptation [7, 8] is appropriately made, and the relationship to off-policy evaluation in reinforcement learning [4, 9] is well-established. The authors also address practical considerations, such as the computational cost of Norm-POEM and the need for variance regularization, demonstrating a thorough understanding of the problem space.

I have only minor concerns about the presentation. The paper correctly notes that the CRM principle requires loss scaling to [-1,0] (as in [1]), but the experimental results in Table 1 use untransformed losses. While the authors clarify in the rebuttal that the loss transformation is applied before estimation, this could be made more explicit in the main text to avoid confusion. Additionally, the feedback function δ(x,y) should be treated as a random variable rather than a deterministic function (as suggested in the draft review), since in realistic scenarios the feedback can vary even for the same (x,y) pair due to noisy labeling. This would strengthen the theoretical foundation without altering the core results.

The paper makes a clear contribution to the BLBF literature. The propensity overfitting problem is significant because it explains why existing methods fail in complex hypothesis spaces, and the self-normalized estimator provides a theoretically grounded solution that translates directly to improved empirical performance. The Norm-POEM algorithm is a practical implementation of this insight, and the authors provide code for reproducibility. The results demonstrate not just incremental improvement but a fundamental shift in how we approach counterfactual risk estimation in BLBF.

In terms of the four review criteria:
- Quality: The paper is technically sound with well-supported theoretical claims and thorough empirical validation. The authors carefully address strengths and weaknesses of their approach.
- Clarity: The paper is well-written with logical flow and appropriate technical detail. The presentation of examples and empirical results is particularly effective.
- Originality: The identification of propensity overfitting as a distinct problem represents a novel theoretical contribution that significantly advances the BLBF literature.
- Significance: The problem addressed is important for real-world applications of BLBF (e.g., ad placement, recommendation systems), and the solution offers practical improvements over current methods.

This is an excellent contribution that advances the state of the art in batch learning from logged bandit feedback. The authors' identification of propensity overfitting fills a critical gap in the literature, and their proposed solution is both theoretically sound and empirically effective. The paper is well-written, clearly motivated, and makes a significant contribution to the field. I recommend acceptance with confidence.
