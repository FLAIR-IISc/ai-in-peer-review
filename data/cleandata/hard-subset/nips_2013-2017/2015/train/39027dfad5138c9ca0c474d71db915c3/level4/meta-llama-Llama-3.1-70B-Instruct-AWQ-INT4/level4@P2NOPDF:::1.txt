I had limited time to complete this review, which is unfortunate given the paper's interesting content. As a result, I may have missed or misinterpreted certain points, and I apologize if this is the case.

The paper addresses the problem of evaluating a different hypothesis based on observed data from a given hypothesis. This can be achieved by reweighting data points, as shown in the equation between (1) and (2), particularly when dealing with non-deterministic hypotheses such as conditional distributions.

The authors tackle a problem related to, yet distinct from, the issue of large variance in weights, which they term "propensity overfitting." The provided examples suggest that this problem arises when there is insufficient exploration, resulting in few or no observations of y corresponding to the new hypothesis being evaluated for a given x. A similar argument explains the large variance of weights, so a more detailed explanation of the relationship between these two problems would be beneficial.

Although I did not have the opportunity to examine the proposed solution in detail or assess its novelty, the underlying idea appears sensible.

There are a few specific points that require clarification:

* Line 199: If n is small compared to k, we would expect to see only a few data points with xi = yi. Consequently, should \hat R(h^) not be even smaller than -2? I may be missing something, and further explanation would be helpful.
* The statement regarding the "unbiased counterfactual risk estimator used in prior works on BLBF [4, 5, 1]" seems questionable. While I am not familiar with all three papers, I suspect that they may employ techniques like clipping, which can lead to biased estimation with reduced variance.

I appreciate the availability of the code, which facilitates further exploration and verification of the results.

In summary, although I could not thoroughly assess the paper's technical soundness, it undoubtedly presents interesting ideas worthy of consideration. Therefore, I suggest acceptance.
