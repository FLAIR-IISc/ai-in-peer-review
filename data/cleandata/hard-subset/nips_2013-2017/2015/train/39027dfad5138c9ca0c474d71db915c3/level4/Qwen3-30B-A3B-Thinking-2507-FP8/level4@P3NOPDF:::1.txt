

This paper addresses a compelling problem in off-policy evaluation: estimating the risk under a target hypothesis when data is generated under a different behavior policy. The core idea of mitigating "propensity overfitting" through regularization appears promising, particularly for scenarios with limited exploration. However, the relationship between propensity overfitting and the well-known issue of high-variance importance weights requires clearer articulation to establish the novelty and scope of the contribution.

The authors define propensity overfitting as occurring when insufficient exploration leads to sparse data for certain input regions (e.g., few or no observations for a given \(x\) under the target hypothesis). While this phenomenon intuitively relates to the high variance of importance weights (which also stems from sparse data), the paper does not explicitly clarify how these two concepts connect or diverge. Specifically, high variance in weights arises from the estimator's sensitivity to rare samples, whereas propensity overfitting appears to describe a *modeling error* where the estimated propensity score overfits to the limited exploration data. The authors should explicitly state whether propensity overfitting is a *cause* of high weight variance (e.g., overfitted propensities amplify weight instability) or a distinct phenomenon requiring separate mitigation. A concise comparative discussion would significantly strengthen the motivation.

Regarding technical details, I was unable to fully verify the novelty or correctness of the proposed regularization method due to time constraints. The core idea—regularizing the propensity score estimation to reduce overfitting—seems reasonable. However, two points warrant clarification:

1.  **Line 199**: The expression \(\hat{R}(h) = \frac{1}{n} \sum_{i=1}^n \mathbb{I}(x_i = y_i) \hat{w}_i\) is confusing when \(n \ll k\). If \(n\) is small relative to the number of classes \(k\), the indicator \(\mathbb{I}(x_i = y_i)\) will frequently be zero (since \(x_i\) and \(y_i\) are unlikely to match), making \(\hat{R}(h)\) small. However, the text states \(\hat{R}(h)\) should be "even smaller than -2," which is impossible for a risk estimate bounded below by 0. This suggests a possible typo or misinterpretation in the example; the authors should clarify the intended scenario.

2.  **Claim on unbiased estimators (Section 2)**: The statement that prior works like [4, 5, 1] use an "unbiased counterfactual risk estimator" requires nuance. While these works may present unbiased estimators *in theory*, practical implementations often employ clipping or truncation to reduce variance, introducing bias. The claim that prior works "do not use" such techniques is likely inaccurate. A more precise statement acknowledging common variance-reduction practices would be appropriate.

Despite these points, the paper’s core contribution—identifying and addressing propensity overfitting as a distinct challenge beyond weight variance—deserves attention. The approach of regularizing the propensity score estimation is conceptually sound and aligns with broader trends in robust off-policy learning. The availability of code is a significant strength, facilitating reproducibility and further investigation.

In summary, while I was unable to fully assess the technical soundness or novelty due to time constraints, the paper presents a clear and timely problem with a plausible solution. The key gap lies in precisely defining the relationship between propensity overfitting and weight variance, which is critical for establishing the work’s significance. Addressing this would greatly enhance the paper. Given the promising direction and the importance of the problem, I recommend acceptance. The authors should resolve the points above in revision to strengthen the contribution.
