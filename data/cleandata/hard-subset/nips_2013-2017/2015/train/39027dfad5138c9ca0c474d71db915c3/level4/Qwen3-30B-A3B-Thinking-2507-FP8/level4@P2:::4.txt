

This paper addresses a critical issue in batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF), where the conventional counterfactual risk estimator suffers from a previously uncharacterized "propensity overfitting" problem when used with complex hypothesis spaces. The authors identify that this overfitting occurs because the standard estimator can produce arbitrarily low risk estimates when the hypothesis space is sufficiently expressive, even when the true risk remains bounded. To resolve this, they propose replacing the conventional estimator with a self-normalized risk estimator that incorporates multiplicative control variates. This estimator avoids the anomalies of the conventional approach, maintains equivariance to loss translations, and produces bounded risk estimates. The authors develop a new learning algorithm called Norm-POEM that leverages this estimator within the Counterfactual Risk Minimization (CRM) framework, demonstrating consistent performance improvements over the original POEM algorithm across multiple multi-label classification datasets.

The paper builds on the foundational work of Swaminathan and Joachims (2015) on CRM for BLBF, extending their framework by identifying a critical flaw in the conventional risk estimator that wasn't previously recognized. While the CRM principle was established in prior work, the authors uncover a new form of overfitting specific to the BLBF setting that undermines the reliability of the estimator. This is a significant contribution to the field, as it addresses a fundamental limitation in existing BLBF approaches that could lead to misleading risk estimates and poor generalization.

**Strengths:**
- **Originality (8/10):** The identification of "propensity overfitting" as a distinct problem from conventional loss overfitting is novel and well-justified through both theoretical analysis and empirical examples. The self-normalized estimator approach represents a meaningful technical contribution to the field.
- **Significance (8/10):** The problem addressed is highly relevant to practical applications of BLBF in online systems (e.g., ad placement, recommendation systems), where log data is abundant but feedback is limited. The empirical results consistently show improved performance across multiple datasets, demonstrating that the theoretical improvements translate to practical gains.
- **Quality (6/10):** The theoretical analysis is sound, with clear proofs of the estimator's properties (e.g., strong consistency, equivariance). The empirical evaluation is thorough, covering multiple datasets, and the results consistently support the authors' claims. However, the quality is somewhat limited by the lack of clear explanation regarding the loss scaling (see below).

**Weaknesses:**
- **Clarity (6/10):** The paper would benefit from greater clarity in several key areas. The authors should explicitly state whether their method requires scaling the loss to [-1,0] as in the original CRM paper (Swaminathan & Joachims, 2015). The experimental results (Table 1) appear to use loss values greater than zero, which contradicts the theoretical foundation presented in Section 4.1 of [1] and the original CRM paper. This inconsistency needs clarification.
- **Methodological Rigor:** The paper treats the feedback δ(x,y) as a deterministic function (line 110), but in most practical scenarios, the feedback would be a random variable (e.g., due to noisy labeling). The risk definition in Equation (1) should include an additional expectation over the randomness of the feedback to be more general and accurate.
- **Notational Consistency:** There are minor notational inconsistencies, such as the formula in line 170-171 (should be h(y|x)/h₀(y|x), not h(y|x)h₀(y|x)) and the use of "argmax" (line 276) instead of "argmin" for risk minimization. The notation for the estimated propensity weight in line 319 should use hats instead of bars for consistency.

**Recommendation:**
This is a well-executed follow-up to the influential CRM work that identifies and resolves a significant limitation in the current BLBF framework. The authors provide a compelling theoretical analysis of propensity overfitting and demonstrate its practical importance through extensive empirical validation. While the paper is strong in its core contribution, the lack of clarity regarding loss scaling and the treatment of feedback as a random variable are notable weaknesses that should be addressed in the final version.

The paper makes a significant contribution to the field of counterfactual learning by:
1. Identifying a novel form of overfitting specific to BLBF settings
2. Proposing a theoretically sound solution using self-normalized estimation
3. Demonstrating consistent empirical improvements over existing methods

I recommend acceptance. The work is important for the field, addresses a genuine limitation in current methods, and provides a clear path forward for practitioners working with logged bandit feedback. The minor clarity issues noted above are easily addressable in the final version.

Pro: Novel problem identification (propensity overfitting), strong theoretical foundation, consistent empirical improvements, practical relevance to important applications
Con: Minor notational inconsistencies, unclear loss scaling requirements, feedback treated as deterministic rather than random variable
