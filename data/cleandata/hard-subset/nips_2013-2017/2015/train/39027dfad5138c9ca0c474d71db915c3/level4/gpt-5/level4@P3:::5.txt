Summary
This paper addresses batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF), where the learner observes outcomes only for actions taken by a historical logging policy. Most prior BLBF methods rely on inverse propensity scoring (IPS) to form an unbiased counterfactual risk estimator, and the Counterfactual Risk Minimization (CRM) principle regularizes by the empirical variance of this estimator (e.g., POEM). The authors identify a failure mode of IPS when used for ERM over rich hypothesis spaces: propensity overfitting. In finite samples, IPS risk can be driven to extreme values by manipulating the sum of importance weights, leading to degenerate solutions and sensitivity to loss translations.

To remedy this, the paper proposes a self-normalized importance sampling estimator (SNIPS) as the counterfactual risk estimate, using the sum of propensities as a multiplicative control variate. This yields an estimator that is bounded and equivariant to loss translation, with a simple consistency argument. Building on CRM, the authors instantiate a new learning algorithm, Norm-POEM, for structured prediction with exponential-family policies. They provide an approximate variance estimator for SNIPS, optimize a nonconvex objective via L-BFGS, and evaluate on multi-label classification tasks using the supervised-to-bandit protocol. Empirically, Norm-POEM consistently outperforms POEM across datasets and training sizes, shows robustness to loss translations, exhibits Ŝ(h) close to 1 (mitigating propensity overfitting), and is competitive in runtime.

Relation to prior work
The paper is well-situated within BLBF, causal inference, and off-policy evaluation. It connects to IPS, clipping, and variance reduction techniques, as well as doubly robust methods. The proposed use of multiplicative control variates (self-normalization) is a standard tool in Monte Carlo estimation but has not, to my knowledge, been deployed in BLBF learning objectives to counteract propensity overfitting. The work builds directly on POEM/CRM and complements prior analyses on importance weighting and domain adaptation.

Strengths
- Clear identification and analysis of propensity overfitting in IPS-based ERM, including instructive examples that highlight non-equivariance and unboundedness issues.
- A simple, principled fix: use self-normalization (SNIPS), which enforces bounded, translation-equivariant estimates and directly controls the weight-sum deviation.
- Sound theoretical properties for the estimator (consistency) and a compelling diagnostic via Ŝ(h), which matches the empirical findings.
- A concrete algorithm (Norm-POEM) with a tractable objective and variance estimate, applicable to structured policies in the exponential family.
- Strong empirical results on multiple datasets: consistent gains over POEM, robustness to loss translations, and reasonable runtime despite a more involved estimator.
- The study carefully controls a potential confounder by using the same optimizer (L-BFGS) across methods.

Weaknesses and questions
- Finite-sample guarantees: While the paper provides a consistency result for SNIPS, it does not present a finite-sample generalization bound for the CRM objective instantiated with SNIPS analogous to the earlier Bernstein-style bound used to motivate CRM. A discussion or partial result would strengthen the theoretical story.
- Variance estimation: The variance term for SNIPS is derived using asymptotic approximations. How sensitive are the results to this approximation, especially in small-n or heavy-tailed weight regimes? Empirical ablations on the quality of the variance estimate would be useful.
- Bias-variance trade-off: SNIPS is biased in finite samples. The paper mentions and uses clipping M to ensure finite variance, but a systematic sensitivity analysis over M and λ (and their interaction with policy support) would be valuable.
- Baselines: The empirical comparison focuses on POEM vs. Norm-POEM. Given the related literature, comparisons to clipped IPS and doubly robust estimators (e.g., DR/WDR variants) would help clarify when SNIPS provides the largest benefit.
- Scope of evaluation: The experiments use the supervised-to-bandit protocol on multi-label classification. While standard, additional experiments on real-world logs or other structured tasks (e.g., ranking) would improve external validity.
- Assumptions: The method requires accurate propensities and full support of the logging policy. A discussion of robustness to misspecified propensities would be important for practice.
- Double-blind issue: The paper includes a public code link that appears to reveal author identity. Please anonymize for review.

Quality
The work is technically sound overall, with a clear derivation of the estimator, a sensible approximate variance, and a well-motivated algorithm. The propensity overfitting analysis is convincing, and experiments support the claims. The main theoretical gap is the absence of a finite-sample learning bound for the SNIPS-based CRM objective.

Clarity
The paper is generally well written and organized. The propensity overfitting examples effectively illustrate the problem. I recommend clarifying: (i) the conditions under which the variance approximation is reliable; (ii) a brief comparison to doubly robust estimators; and (iii) a consolidated discussion of hyperparameter selection for M and λ.

Originality
While self-normalization is a known variance-reduction technique, applying it to BLBF learning to directly address propensity overfitting is a novel and insightful contribution. The formulation of Norm-POEM and the diagnostic via Ŝ(h) are original within this context.

Significance
The contribution is significant. Propensity overfitting is a practical issue in counterfactual learning, and the proposed solution is simple, principled, and empirically effective. This work is likely to influence subsequent BLBF algorithms and deployments.

Pros for acceptance
- Identifies an important failure mode of widely used estimators in BLBF.
- Provides a principled estimator that fixes key anomalies (boundedness, equivariance) and is consistent.
- Demonstrates consistent empirical gains with strong diagnostics and reasonable computational cost.
- Likely to have practical impact in counterfactual learning applications.

Cons for acceptance
- Lacks a finite-sample generalization bound for the SNIPS-based CRM objective.
- Limited empirical baselines and domains; robustness to propensity misspecification not studied.
- Variance estimate relies on asymptotic approximations; small-sample behavior could be discussed more.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide, if possible, a finite-sample bound or at least a discussion of challenges and partial results for SNIPS-based CRM.
- Add comparisons to clipped IPS and doubly robust baselines.
- Include sensitivity analyses for M and λ, and an experiment on robustness to propensity noise.
- Anonymize any code or URLs in the submission to preserve double-blind review.

Overall assessment
This is a strong paper that makes a clear, well-motivated, and practically relevant contribution to counterfactual learning. With minor additions and clarifications, it would be an excellent fit for NIPS.
