This paper addresses a critical problem in counterfactual learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF): propensity overfitting. The authors demonstrate that the conventionally used unbiased risk estimator can exhibit severe overfitting, particularly with complex hypothesis spaces, leading to biased risk estimates. To mitigate this, they propose a self-normalized risk estimator leveraging multiplicative control variates, which provably avoids this degeneracy. This leads to a new learning algorithm, Norm-POEM, evaluated on multi-label classification tasks, consistently outperforming approaches based on the conventional estimator.

The core idea builds upon the Counterfactual Risk Minimization (CRM) principle [1] and recognizes that standard importance sampling techniques, while unbiased, are vulnerable to instability when applied to learning. This vulnerability stems from the potential for overfitting the propensity weights—essentially, fitting the model to *how* the data was collected rather than the underlying signal. The proposed self-normalized estimator offers a compelling solution by effectively re-weighting the samples and ensuring equivariance to loss translations, a property lacking in the conventional estimator.  This relates to broader work in causal inference [7] and off-policy evaluation in reinforcement learning [4], where similar challenges arise. The connection to existing variance reduction techniques for importance sampling [15, 16] and self-normalization [12, 19] is clearly articulated, providing a strong foundation for their proposed approach.

**Strengths:**

*   **Identifies a novel and important problem:** Propensity overfitting is a significant issue in BLBF that has not been previously recognized.
*   **Elegant solution:** The self-normalized estimator offers a theoretically sound and practical solution to the identified problem. The proof of consistency (Theorem 2) is a valuable contribution.
*   **Strong empirical validation:** The experimental results demonstrate consistent improvements over the baseline POEM algorithm across multiple datasets, showcasing the effectiveness of Norm-POEM. The ablation study on variance regularization (Table 3) is insightful.
*   **Clear presentation:** The paper is well-written and clearly explains the motivation, technical details, and experimental setup.
*   **Availability of code:** Providing publicly available software is commendable and promotes reproducibility.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Computational complexity:** While the paper notes the runtime of Norm-POEM is comparable to POEM, a more detailed discussion of the computational cost of calculating the self-normalized estimator, especially for large-scale datasets and complex hypothesis spaces, would be beneficial.
*   **Hyperparameter sensitivity:** The influence of the clipping parameter M and the regularization parameter λ could be explored in more detail. While calibrated via a validation set, understanding their impact on performance in various scenarios would be valuable.
*   **Limited scope of experiments:** The experiments are focused on multi-label classification. Exploring the performance of Norm-POEM on other types of structured prediction tasks would strengthen the conclusions.



**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The paper addresses a significant and previously unaddressed problem in counterfactual learning.
*   The proposed solution is theoretically sound and empirically validated.
*   The results demonstrate a clear improvement over existing methods.
*   The paper is well-written and clearly presented.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The computational complexity of the self-normalized estimator might limit its scalability to very large datasets.
*   The experiments are primarily focused on one type of task.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that presents a valuable contribution to the field of counterfactual learning. The identified problem of propensity overfitting is important, and the proposed solution is both theoretically sound and empirically effective. While there are minor limitations regarding computational complexity and scope of experiments, the strengths of the paper significantly outweigh the weaknesses. I recommend acceptance.
