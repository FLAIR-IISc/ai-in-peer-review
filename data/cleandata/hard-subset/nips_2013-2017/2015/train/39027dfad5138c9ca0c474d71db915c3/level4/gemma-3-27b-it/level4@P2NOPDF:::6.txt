Counterfactual risk minimization (CRM) is a technique for learning in bandit settings from offline, logged data, addressing the inherent bias in how such data is generated. This paper identifies a significant overfitting problem with standard CRM: the hypothesis class can become overly sensitive to the data generation probability, leading to poor generalization. The authors propose a solution involving a multiplicative parameter that effectively bounds the variance of the estimator, while importantly preserving the consistency properties of CRM. 

The core contribution lies in introducing a self-normalized estimator for counterfactual learning, specifically designed to mitigate this overfitting issue where the learned hypothesis may exhibit excessive confidence in the logged data. This is achieved by normalizing the counterfactual estimates, leading to a more robust and generalizable model. 

Experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed, normalized CRM approach, consistently outperforming the standard, non-normalized variant. The improvement suggests that controlling the variance through this multiplicative parameter is crucial for practical application.

Overall, this is a strong paper that addresses a critical limitation of counterfactual risk minimization. The identified overfitting problem is insightful, and the proposed solution is both theoretically sound – maintaining consistency is a key strength – and empirically validated. While not groundbreaking, the work represents a valuable contribution to the field and I recommend acceptance.
