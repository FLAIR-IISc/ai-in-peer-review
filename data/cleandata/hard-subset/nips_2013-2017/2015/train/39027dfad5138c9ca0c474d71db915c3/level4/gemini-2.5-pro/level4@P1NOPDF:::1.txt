Due to travel constraints, this is a lighter review than I would typically provide. I found the paper quite interesting, but I may have missed or misunderstood some details, for which I apologize in advance.

The paper addresses the problem of evaluating a target hypothesis using data logged from a different, behavioral hypothesis. When these hypotheses are non-deterministic (e.g., conditional distributions), the standard approach is to use importance weighting to estimate the loss of the target hypothesis, as described in the equation between (1) and (2).

The authors introduce the problem of "propensity overfitting," which they distinguish from the well-known issue of high variance in importance weights. As I understand it, propensity overfitting occurs when there is insufficient exploration in the logged data. Specifically, for a given context x, the data may contain few or no examples of the actions (y) that the new hypothesis would have taken. While this is closely related to the cause of high-variance estimators, the precise relationship between propensity overfitting and high variance is not fully elucidated. A more detailed explanation of this connection would strengthen the paper.

While I did not have sufficient time to verify the technical details or the novelty of the proposed solution, the underlying idea appears sensible.

Specific points:
*   **Line 199:** I was confused by this statement. If n is small compared to k, my understanding is that we would observe very few data points where x_i = y_i. If so, shouldn't R̂(ĥ) be even smaller than -2? Perhaps I have misunderstood a key detail here.

*   **"unbiased counterfactual risk estimator used in prior works on BLBF [4, 5, 1]":** I find this characterization questionable. While I am not familiar with all three references, it is common practice in this area to use techniques like weight clipping, which intentionally introduce bias to reduce variance. The authors should clarify if these prior works truly used strictly unbiased estimators.

*   I appreciate the authors making their code available.

In summary, while my time constraints prevented a thorough verification of the paper's technical soundness and novelty, the core ideas presented are intriguing and thought-provoking. The paper identifies a potentially important problem in off-policy evaluation. Despite my outstanding questions, I believe the paper's interesting direction merits further discussion in the community, and I therefore lean towards acceptance.
