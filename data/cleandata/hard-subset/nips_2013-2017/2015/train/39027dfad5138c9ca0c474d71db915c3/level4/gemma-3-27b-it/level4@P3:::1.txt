This paper identifies a significant problem – propensity overfitting – in counterfactual risk estimation, a core component of batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF). The authors demonstrate that the commonly used unbiased estimator suffers from this issue, particularly when learning with complex hypothesis spaces, and propose a self-normalized estimator to mitigate it. They instantiate this with a new learning algorithm, Norm-POEM, and demonstrate consistent performance gains over existing methods on multi-label classification tasks.

The work builds upon the established framework of Counterfactual Risk Minimization (CRM) [1] and connects to broader areas like causal inference, off-policy evaluation in reinforcement learning [4], and domain adaptation [8]. The introduction clearly motivates the problem of learning from historical logs, highlighting the challenges of bandit feedback and the need for counterfactual estimators. The paper appropriately references relevant work, positioning itself within the existing literature.

**Strengths:**

* **Identifies a novel issue:** The concept of “propensity overfitting” provides a valuable new lens through which to view the challenges of BLBF. This goes beyond the traditional concern of high variance in importance sampling weights.
* **Theoretical grounding:** The paper offers theoretical justification for the self-normalized estimator and demonstrates its advantages over the conventional estimator, particularly in addressing the identified overfitting problem. Theorem 2 provides a strong consistency guarantee.
* **Empirical validation:** The experimental results consistently demonstrate the superior performance of Norm-POEM across multiple datasets. The inclusion of comparisons to a full-information CRF provides a useful benchmark. The analysis showing improved robustness and reduced sensitivity to loss translation is compelling.
* **Practical considerations:** The authors acknowledge the computational cost of the self-normalized estimator but show that it does not necessarily translate to longer training times due to improved convergence properties. Code availability is a significant plus.
* **Clear writing:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains complex concepts. The examples provided (e.g., Example 1) effectively illustrate the propensity overfitting phenomenon.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Relationship to existing variance reduction techniques:** While the paper acknowledges techniques like clipping [15, 5], the discussion of the relationship between propensity overfitting and the high variance issue could be more nuanced. Specifically, elaborating on *how* propensity overfitting differs from, and potentially exacerbates, the variance problem would strengthen the argument.
* **Clarification needed (l. 199):** The question raised in the original review regarding the example on lines 199-201 remains valid. A more detailed explanation of why  `R̂(hoverfit)` would be smaller than -2 in the specific scenario would improve clarity.
* **Fairness of statement (l. 212):** The assertion that prior work doesn't employ clipping requires more careful consideration. While some might not explicitly state it, clipping or similar techniques are often used to manage variance in practice. A more accurate phrasing acknowledging the common use of variance reduction techniques would be preferable.
* **Limited exploration of hyperparameter sensitivity:** The paper states hyperparameters were calibrated on a hold-out set, but further exploration of their impact and potential sensitivity would be valuable.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper addresses a critical, previously unrecognised problem in BLBF.
* The proposed solution, the self-normalized estimator, is theoretically sound and empirically validated.
* Norm-POEM demonstrably outperforms existing methods.
* The paper is well-written and clearly presents its contributions.
* Code availability enhances reproducibility and facilitates future research.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The connection between propensity overfitting and traditional variance issues could be more thoroughly explored.
* A minor clarification regarding the example in lines 199-201 is needed.
* The statement about prior work not utilizing clipping might be inaccurate.



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite a few minor weaknesses, this is a strong paper with a significant contribution to the field of batch learning from logged bandit feedback. The identification of propensity overfitting and the proposed self-normalized estimator represent a valuable advancement. The empirical results are compelling, and the paper is well-written and clearly presented. I recommend **acceptance**.
