Summary
This paper addresses batch learning from logged bandit feedback, an important problem for applications such as ad ranking. It identifies an overfitting issue in the recently proposed Counterfactual Risk Minimization (CRM) principle and proposes a self-normalized variant based on multiplicative control variates. While this estimator is biased, it substantially reduces variance and appears to mitigate overfitting in the settings studied.

Quality
The paper is high-quality and well motivated. The methodological contribution is clear, and the empirical results show notable gains over CRM. However, as a follow-up to [1], I expected a broader experimental evaluation and deeper analysis of the training objective and its optimization. In particular, the brief discussion around line 415 leaves open questions about convergence behavior, regularization, and sensitivity to hyperparameters. Additional ablations and diagnostics would strengthen the case for the proposed approach and help practitioners adopt it.

Clarity
The presentation is clear and the paper is well written. The motivation for self-normalization and the intuition behind multiplicative control variates are well conveyed. The notation is consistent, and the connection to CRM is made explicit.

Originality
Although this work is a follow-up to [1], the modification of the risk estimator to be self-normalizing is a meaningful and nontrivial contribution. The introduction of multiplicative control variates in this context is well motivated and appears to be the key driver of the empirical improvements. The discussion of bias–variance trade-offs is appropriate, though it would benefit from a more detailed theoretical or empirical analysis.

Significance
Learning from logged bandit feedback is highly relevant, and the paper tackles a known weakness of CRM with a practical solution. The reported experimental improvements are substantial. That said, it remains unclear how these gains translate to a wider range of real-world applications beyond those considered in [1]. Demonstrating robustness across additional domains and datasets would increase the impact and practical significance.

Various Remarks and Suggestions
- Experiments and reporting:
  - If multiple runs were conducted (as implied), please report variability across runs (e.g., standard deviations, confidence intervals), and consider including statistical significance tests.
  - Provide ablations isolating the effect of self-normalization versus other design choices (e.g., clipping, regularization). A comparison to standard IPS, clipped IPS, and doubly robust estimators would clarify where the gains are coming from.
  - Include sensitivity analyses for key hyperparameters (e.g., normalization strength, regularization coefficients) and for propensity score misspecification.
  - Expand the set of applications beyond those in [1]; ideally include at least one real-world dataset or production-like evaluation to demonstrate external validity.

- Optimization details:
  - Elaborate on the optimization of the proposed training objective beyond the short paragraph around line 415: objective properties (convexity/nonconvexity), gradient estimators, variance reduction techniques, learning-rate schedules, regularization, initialization, and stopping criteria.
  - Discuss computational cost relative to CRM (e.g., per-iteration complexity, convergence speed).

- Bias–variance trade-off:
  - Provide more quantitative analysis of the bias introduced by self-normalization and how it affects policy selection, especially in finite-sample regimes.
  - Clarify conditions under which self-normalization may degrade performance (e.g., extreme propensities, small sample sizes) and any practical safeguards.

- Practical considerations:
  - Comment on propensity estimation and calibration when logging propensities are not known exactly. How sensitive is the method to errors in propensities?
  - Discuss compatibility with structured prediction or ranking losses and any implications for large action spaces.
  - If applicable, provide guidance on choosing normalization strategies in practice.

Overall Assessment
This is a solid, well-written, and timely paper that identifies a concrete shortcoming in CRM and proposes a principled, practical fix via self-normalized estimation with multiplicative control variates. The empirical results are compelling within the tested settings. To reach its full potential and maximize impact, the paper should include broader and more rigorous experiments, more detailed optimization guidance, and a deeper examination of the bias–variance trade-off. With these enhancements, the contribution would be both robust and highly relevant to practitioners working with logged bandit data.
