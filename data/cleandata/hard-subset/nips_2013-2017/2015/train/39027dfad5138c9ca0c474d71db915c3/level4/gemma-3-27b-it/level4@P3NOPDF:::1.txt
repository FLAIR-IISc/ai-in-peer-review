This paper addresses the problem of evaluating a hypothesis given data generated from a different hypothesis, a common scenario in off-policy evaluation and related fields. The core idea – identifying and mitigating “propensity overfitting” – is intuitive and potentially valuable. While my review is somewhat limited due to travel constraints, and I may have missed nuances, I found the paper’s central concept interesting and recommend acceptance.

The authors observe that when evaluating a new hypothesis based on reweighted data (as in, for example, estimating the loss according to equation (2) given data from the distribution described near equation (1)), a key challenge arises: insufficient exploration under the new hypothesis can lead to overly optimistic risk estimates. This issue, termed “propensity overfitting,” is distinct from, yet related to, the problem of high variance in importance weights. The paper effectively illustrates this distinction with examples where limited coverage of the state-action space for certain outcomes leads to unreliable estimates. A more detailed discussion explicitly connecting propensity overfitting to the variance of importance weights would strengthen the paper; clarifying the conditions under which each dominates or co-occurs would be particularly insightful.

The proposed solution appears sensible, though I did not have sufficient time to thoroughly assess its technical details or novelty. The core idea of addressing this overfitting issue is well-motivated, and the paper presents a reasonable approach.

I have a few specific questions and concerns:

* **Line 199:** I am uncertain about the reasoning presented. If *n* is small compared to *k*, it seems we observe very few data points where *x<sub>i</sub>* = *y<sub>i</sub>*. In this case, wouldn’t  \hat{R}(h^) be even *smaller* than -2? I would appreciate clarification on this point.

* **Unbiasedness Claim:** The statement that prior work ([4, 5, 1]) uses an “unbiased counterfactual risk estimator” requires scrutiny. While these works aim for unbiased estimation, it’s doubtful that they entirely avoid techniques like clipping, which inherently introduce bias to reduce variance. A more nuanced phrasing acknowledging potential trade-offs between bias and variance in prior approaches would be more accurate.

Despite these minor concerns, I appreciate the clarity of the presentation and the authors’ focus on a practical problem. The availability of code is a significant positive, facilitating reproducibility and further research. 

In summary, while I was unable to perform a completely comprehensive evaluation due to time constraints, the paper presents a compelling idea with potential for significant impact. I recommend acceptance and encourage the authors to address the points raised regarding the connection to importance weight variance and the unbiasedness claim in future revisions.
