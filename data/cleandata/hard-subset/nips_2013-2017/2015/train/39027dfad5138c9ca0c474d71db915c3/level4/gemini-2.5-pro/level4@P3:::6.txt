### Summary of the Paper

This paper addresses the problem of Batch Learning from Logged Bandit Feedback (BLBF), a setting crucial for improving online systems like ad placement and search engines using historical data. The standard approach, Counterfactual Risk Minimization (CRM), relies on an unbiased inverse propensity score (IPS) estimator to evaluate and optimize new policies. The authors' central contribution is the identification of a critical and previously overlooked failure mode of this conventional estimator, which they term "Propensity Overfitting." They demonstrate that with a sufficiently complex hypothesis space, a learner can achieve an artificially low empirical risk not by accurately modeling the true loss, but by manipulating the propensity weights—for instance, by placing probability mass on actions unobserved in the training data (for positive losses) or by concentrating mass on the observed logged actions (for negative losses).

To solve this problem, the paper proposes replacing the standard IPS estimator with a self-normalized (or ratio) estimator. This alternative estimator, derived using a multiplicative control variate, is shown to be equivariant to shifts in the loss function and is naturally bounded by the range of the observed losses, thus provably avoiding the anomalies that lead to propensity overfitting. The authors instantiate this idea within the CRM framework to develop a new algorithm, the Normalized Policy Optimizer for Exponential Models (Norm-POEM), for structured output prediction. Comprehensive experiments on several multi-label classification datasets show that Norm-POEM consistently and significantly outperforms its predecessor (POEM), which uses the conventional estimator, demonstrating the practical impact of addressing propensity overfitting.

### Strengths and Arguments for Acceptance

This is a well-executed and insightful paper that makes a strong contribution to the field of counterfactual learning.

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The paper's primary strength is the identification and clear articulation of the "Propensity Overfitting" problem. While the components of the solution (e.g., the self-normalized estimator) are known in the statistics literature, their application to diagnose and solve this specific, subtle, and severe failure mode in a learning context (CRM) is novel and important. BLBF is an area of significant practical interest, and identifying such a fundamental flaw in the standard approach is a major conceptual advance. The problem is illustrated with a simple yet powerful example (Example 1) that makes the issue exceptionally clear.

2.  **Quality:** The proposed solution is elegant, principled, and theoretically sound. The use of a control variate to derive the self-normalized estimator is a well-motivated fix for the diagnosed problem. The authors correctly analyze its desirable properties, such as equivariance and boundedness, which directly counteract the weaknesses of the unbiased estimator. The consistency proof (Theorem 2) establishes the asymptotic correctness of the estimator. The experimental evaluation is thorough and convincing; it is designed not just to show that the proposed method is better, but to empirically validate the paper's core hypothesis. The experiments that directly measure the sum of propensity weights (Table 2) and test robustness to loss translation provide direct evidence that Norm-POEM is indeed fixing the propensity overfitting problem.

3.  **Clarity:** The paper is very clearly written and well-organized. The authors do an excellent job of motivating the problem, explaining the subtle mechanism of propensity overfitting, and presenting their solution in a logical, step-by-step manner. The connection between the theoretical properties of the estimators and the empirical results is explicitly and effectively drawn. The paper is easy to follow for readers familiar with the domain and provides sufficient background for others to grasp the main ideas.

### Weaknesses and Questions for the Authors

The paper is very strong, and the weaknesses are minor. They are better framed as suggestions for future work or points for clarification.

1.  **Bias of the Self-Normalized Estimator:** The paper correctly notes that the self-normalized estimator is biased for finite samples, although it is strongly consistent. While the empirical results are compelling, it would be valuable to briefly discuss the potential implications of this bias, especially in small-sample regimes. Could this bias be problematic under certain conditions, and how does it trade-off against the estimator's lower variance and robustness to propensity overfitting?

2.  **Scope of Experiments:** The experiments use the "Supervised -> Bandit" conversion methodology, which is a standard and well-accepted protocol for controlled evaluation in this area. However, the paper's claims would be further strengthened by demonstrating the effectiveness of Norm-POEM on a real-world logged bandit dataset, where the logging policy and data distributions might have more complex characteristics than those in the simulated setup.

3.  **Variance Estimation:** The paper uses an approximate variance estimate for the ratio estimator (Equation 8) based on the delta method or a Normal approximation. While standard, this is still an approximation. It would be interesting to comment on the quality of this approximation and whether more advanced techniques for variance estimation of ratio estimators (e.g., bootstrap methods) could offer any further benefits, even if at a higher computational cost.

### Final Recommendation

This is a high-quality paper that I recommend for acceptance. It makes a significant and novel conceptual contribution by identifying the problem of propensity overfitting in counterfactual learning. The proposed solution is principled, technically sound, and empirically shown to lead to substantial improvements in generalization performance and robustness. The work is a clear advancement of the state-of-the-art in batch learning from bandit feedback and is likely to be influential for both researchers and practitioners working in this important area.
