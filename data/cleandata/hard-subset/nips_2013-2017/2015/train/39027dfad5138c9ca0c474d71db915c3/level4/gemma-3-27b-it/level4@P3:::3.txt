This paper addresses the important problem of batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF), a setting crucial for applications like ad ranking and recommendation systems. The authors identify a novel “propensity overfitting” issue within the widely-used Counterfactual Risk Minimization (CRM) principle and propose a solution based on a self-normalized risk estimator employing multiplicative control variates. This results in a biased, yet demonstrably more robust, estimator.

**Quality:** This is a high-quality paper with strong theoretical motivation and empirical validation. The identified propensity overfitting problem is insightful and the proposed solution is well-justified. As a follow-up to [1], the paper logically extends existing work and presents a significant refinement to the CRM principle. While the empirical evaluation is thorough, expanding the experimental analysis, particularly regarding the optimization of the training objective (beyond the discussion on lines 415-422), would further strengthen the contribution. Exploring the sensitivity of the method to different hyperparameter settings and providing more detailed ablation studies could also be beneficial.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and clearly presented. The authors provide sufficient background information and effectively explain complex concepts. The organization is logical, making it easy to follow the line of reasoning. The notation is consistent and the mathematical derivations are generally accessible. 

**Originality:** This work builds upon [1] but introduces a significant and novel modification to the risk estimator – the self-normalizing approach. This change demonstrably improves performance across several multi-label classification problems. The identification of “propensity overfitting” as a distinct phenomenon is a valuable contribution, and the analysis convincingly demonstrates its impact. The paper clearly differentiates itself from prior work and adequately references related literature.

**Significance:** Learning from logged data is a highly relevant and impactful problem. While the experimental improvements are substantial, assessing the practical transferability of these gains to real-world applications remains a challenge. It would be valuable to discuss potential limitations and scenarios where the proposed approach might be less effective. Demonstrating the method’s scalability and robustness on larger, more complex datasets would further highlight its significance. The potential impact of avoiding propensity overfitting is considerable, as it could lead to more reliable and effective online learning systems.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Addresses an important and practical problem (BLBF).
*   Identifies a novel and significant issue (propensity overfitting).
*   Proposes a theoretically sound and empirically validated solution (self-normalized estimator).
*   Demonstrates consistent performance improvements over existing methods.
*   The paper is well-written, clear, and logically organized.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Experiments are largely based on datasets and methodology similar to [1].  Expanding the experimental scope with more diverse datasets or real-world applications would strengthen the paper.
*   A deeper analysis of the optimization process and hyperparameter sensitivity would be beneficial.
*   While the improvements are clear, a more detailed discussion of the potential limitations and practical challenges of applying this method in different scenarios would be valuable.




**Overall Assessment:** This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of batch learning from logged bandit feedback. The identified problem of propensity overfitting is important, and the proposed solution is both theoretically sound and empirically effective. While further experimentation and analysis would be beneficial, the paper as it stands is of high quality and deserves acceptance.
