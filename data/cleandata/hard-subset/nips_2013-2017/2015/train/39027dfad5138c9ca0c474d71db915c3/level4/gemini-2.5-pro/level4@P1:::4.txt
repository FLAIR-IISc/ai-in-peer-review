### **Summary**

This paper identifies and addresses a critical problem in batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF), termed "propensity overfitting." The authors show that the conventional unbiased counterfactual risk estimator, widely used in this setting, can be manipulated by hypotheses that focus on minimizing or maximizing the sum of propensity weights rather than minimizing the true loss. This leads to severely biased risk estimates and poor generalization, especially with complex hypothesis spaces.

As a solution, the paper proposes replacing the conventional estimator with a self-normalized risk estimator. This approach uses the sum of importance weights as a multiplicative control variate, which elegantly resolves the identified issues. The resulting estimator is equivariant to translations of the loss function and is always bounded by the range of the loss, analogous to supervised learning. The authors instantiate this approach within the Counterfactual Risk Minimization (CRM) framework to develop a new learning algorithm, Norm-POEM. Empirical results on several multi-label classification tasks demonstrate that Norm-POEM consistently and significantly outperforms its predecessor, POEM, confirming the practical benefits of the proposed estimator.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Clear Problem Identification and Analysis:** The paper provides a clear and insightful analysis of "propensity overfitting," a subtle but important failure mode of the standard inverse propensity score estimator in a learning context. The illustrative example in Section 4 is highly effective at demonstrating how a model can achieve an arbitrarily low estimated risk that is completely disconnected from its true performance.

2.  **Elegant and Principled Solution:** The proposal to use a self-normalized estimator is a well-motivated and principled solution. By framing it as an application of multiplicative control variates, the authors connect their solution to a classic variance reduction technique. The theoretical properties of the new estimator (equivariance, boundedness, and consistency) directly address the anomalies of the unbiased estimator.

3.  **Strong Empirical Results:** The experiments provide compelling evidence for the effectiveness of Norm-POEM. It consistently outperforms the previous state-of-the-art (POEM) across multiple datasets and training set sizes. The diagnostic experiments in Table 2 are particularly convincing, as they directly confirm that Norm-POEM avoids the pathological behavior predicted by the paper's analysis (i.e., it is robust to loss translation and keeps the sum of weights, Ŝ(h), close to its expected value of 1).

4.  **Significance and Impact:** This work is an important follow-up to the influential CRM framework [1]. Given the growing interest in learning from logged data in applications like recommendation systems, search, and online advertising, a more robust and reliable learning principle is a significant contribution. The identified problem and proposed solution are likely to be of broad interest to researchers and practitioners in this area.

### **Weaknesses and Questions for the Authors**

1.  **Loss Scaling Assumption:** The original CRM framework in [1] (Section 4.1) relies on the assumption that the loss δ is scaled to the range [-1, 0] to derive its generalization bounds. Does the proposed Norm-POEM algorithm also require this assumption for the CRM objective (Eq. 3) and the variance estimate (Eq. 8) to be valid? If so, the experimental results in Table 2, which analyze performance with translated losses (implying δ > 0), are potentially confusing. Could the authors clarify whether the losses were rescaled before being used in the Norm-POEM objective and how to interpret these specific results in light of the theoretical requirements of the CRM principle?

2.  **Stochasticity of Feedback:** The paper defines the loss δ(x, y) as a deterministic function (line 110), later noting this can be extended by setting δ(x, y) = E[δ|x, y]. However, treating the observed feedback as a single realization of a random variable seems more general and natural for many real-world settings. For instance, in a classification task with inherent label noise, the same input x and action y could receive different losses on different occasions in the log. While the proposed estimators may not change, defining the true risk in Equation (1) with an explicit expectation over the stochasticity of the loss (e.g., R(h) = Eₓ E_{y∼h(x)} E_δ[δ(x, y)]) would strengthen the problem formulation and make it more broadly applicable.

### **Minor Comments**

*   **Line 170-171:** The term `δ(x, y) h(y|x)h0(y|x)` should likely be a ratio: `δ(x, y) h(y|x)/h0(y|x)`.
*   **Line 171:** It might be worth explicitly noting that the lack of equivariance (or linearity with respect to constant shifts) of the unbiased estimator was also pointed out in [5].
*   **Line 319:** In the definition of `ˆV ar(R̂SN (h))`, the notation uses bars for `δi − R̂SN(h)`. To be consistent with the rest of the paper, this should probably use hats.

### **Conclusion**

This is a well-written and valuable contribution that addresses a significant flaw in a popular approach to learning from logged bandit feedback. The analysis of propensity overfitting is insightful, the proposed self-normalized estimator is an elegant solution, and the empirical results clearly demonstrate its superiority over the previous state-of-the-art. I recommend acceptance.
