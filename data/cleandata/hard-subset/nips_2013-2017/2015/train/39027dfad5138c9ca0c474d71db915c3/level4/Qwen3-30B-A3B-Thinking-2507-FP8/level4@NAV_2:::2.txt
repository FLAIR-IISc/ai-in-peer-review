

This paper identifies a previously overlooked issue with the conventional counterfactual risk estimator used in batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF), proposing a self-normalized estimator that avoids this problem. The authors demonstrate that the conventional estimator suffers from "propensity overfitting" when used in empirical risk minimization (ERM) for complex hypothesis spaces, which leads to severely biased risk estimates. They introduce Norm-POEM, a new learning algorithm based on the counterfactual risk minimization principle that employs their proposed self-normalized risk estimator. The paper provides theoretical justification for the new estimator (including Theorem 2 showing strong consistency) and demonstrates through extensive experiments that Norm-POEM consistently outperforms the conventional POEM approach across multiple multi-label classification datasets.

The paper relates well to previous work at NIPS and elsewhere, particularly building upon the Counterfactual Risk Minimization framework introduced in ICML 2015 by Swaminathan and Joachims [1]. The authors correctly identify and analyze the specific problem of propensity overfitting, which distinguishes their work from prior approaches that have focused on variance reduction techniques like importance weight clipping.

Strengths:
- The identification of propensity overfitting as a novel problem in BLBF is significant and well-justified
- The theoretical analysis is sound, with clear proofs of the estimator's desirable properties
- The empirical evaluation is thorough, showing consistent improvements across multiple datasets and training sizes
- The paper is clearly written with a logical structure that makes the contributions accessible
- The approach has practical relevance for real-world applications like ad placement and recommendation systems

Weaknesses:
- The paper does not fully explore the computational trade-offs of the self-normalized estimator compared to the conventional approach
- The analysis of when the self-normalized estimator provides the most significant improvements is somewhat limited
- The connection to broader theoretical frameworks in machine learning could be strengthened

The paper is technically sound and makes a clear contribution to the field. The authors carefully evaluate both strengths and weaknesses of their approach, and the experimental results provide convincing evidence of the proposed method's superiority. The problem addressed is important for practitioners working with logged bandit feedback data, and the solution offers a simple yet effective improvement to existing algorithms.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Identifies and solves a previously unrecognized problem (propensity overfitting) in BLBF
- Theoretical analysis is rigorous and well-motivated
- Empirical results consistently demonstrate the superiority of the proposed method
- Clear writing and organization
- Practical significance for real-world applications

Arguments against acceptance:
- The computational cost analysis could be more comprehensive
- The paper does not explore the limitations of the approach in depth

This paper makes a meaningful contribution to the field of counterfactual learning, addressing a practical problem with a theoretically sound and empirically validated solution. It is well-suited for publication at NIPS.
