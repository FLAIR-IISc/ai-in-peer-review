- Addresses batch learning from logged bandit feedback and proposes a self-normalized CRM variant using multiplicative control variates to reduce variance at the cost of bias.  
- Empirical results show substantial gains over standard CRM and appear to mitigate overfitting in studied settings.  
- Paper is well motivated, methodologically clear, and well written with consistent notation and explicit connection to CRM.  
- Expected broader experimental evaluation and deeper analysis of training objective, convergence, regularization, and hyperparameter sensitivity.  
- The brief discussion of optimization (around line 415) is insufficient—needs details on objective properties, gradient estimators, schedules, initialization, and stopping.  
- Additional ablations and diagnostics are needed to isolate effects of self-normalization versus clipping, regularization, or other design choices.  
- Recommend reporting variability across runs (std. devs/confidence intervals) and including statistical significance tests.  
- Compare against standard IPS, clipped IPS, and doubly robust estimators to clarify sources of improvement.  
- Include sensitivity analyses for normalization strength, regularization coefficients, and propensity-score misspecification.  
- Provide more quantitative analysis of the bias introduced by self-normalization and its impact on policy selection in finite samples.  
- Clarify conditions where self-normalization may degrade performance (extreme propensities, small samples) and suggest practical safeguards.  
- Discuss computational cost and per-iteration complexity relative to CRM and report convergence speed.  
- Address propensity estimation and calibration when logging propensities are imperfect and measure method sensitivity to such errors.  
- Discuss compatibility with structured prediction/ranking losses and implications for large action spaces.  
- Offer practical guidance on choosing normalization strategies and hyperparameters for practitioners.  
- Overall: solid, timely contribution that meaningfully improves CRM in tested settings but needs broader, more rigorous experiments and deeper optimization/bias–variance analysis to maximize impact.
