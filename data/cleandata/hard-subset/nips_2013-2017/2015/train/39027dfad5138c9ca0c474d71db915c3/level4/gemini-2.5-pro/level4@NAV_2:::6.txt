This paper addresses the problem of batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF), a setting crucial for improving interactive systems using historical data. The authors build upon the Counterfactual Risk Minimization (CRM) principle, which is a standard approach in this domain. The paper's primary contribution is the identification and formalization of a critical issue with the conventional unbiased risk estimator used in CRM, which they term "Propensity Overfitting". They clearly demonstrate, both theoretically and with a simple but powerful example, how a sufficiently complex hypothesis space can minimize the estimated risk not by learning a better policy, but by exploiting the propensity weights in the training data. This leads to degenerate solutions where the model either over-represents or avoids the training examples, depending on the sign of the loss.

To address this problem, the authors propose replacing the standard unbiased estimator with a self-normalized risk estimator, a technique based on multiplicative control variates that is well-known in the Monte Carlo literature but novel in this specific learning context. This choice is well-motivated, as the self-normalized estimator is equivariant to translations in the loss function and its estimates are naturally bounded by the range of the observed losses, thus directly resolving the pathologies that cause propensity overfitting. This leads to a new learning algorithm, Norm-POEM, for structured prediction with linear models. The empirical evaluation on several multi-label classification datasets convincingly shows that Norm-POEM consistently and significantly outperforms its predecessor, POEM, which uses the conventional estimator.

**Strengths:**

*   **Significance and Originality:** The key strength of this paper is the identification of the propensity overfitting problem. This is a subtle but severe flaw in a widely used estimator for counterfactual learning, and bringing it to light is a significant contribution to the field. While the proposed solution—the self-normalized estimator—is not new in itself, its application to diagnose and fix this specific issue in the BLBF setting is novel and insightful.
*   **Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The arguments are clear, the theoretical claims (e.g., consistency of the estimator) are well-supported, and the analysis of the conventional estimator's failures is rigorous. The experimental methodology is strong, using a controlled simulation environment that allows for a clear demonstration of the proposed method's advantages. The results strongly support the central claims of the paper, showing not only improved generalization performance but also directly verifying that propensity overfitting is being controlled (as seen in the `Ŝ(h)` values in Table 2).
*   **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow. The authors do an excellent job of motivating the problem, with Example 1 being particularly illuminating. The progression from problem identification to the proposed solution and the resulting algorithm is logical and coherent.

**Weaknesses:**

*   The proposed Norm-POEM objective is non-convex and its variance term (Equation 8) does not lend itself to stochastic optimization methods. The authors state that faster convergence using a batch optimizer like L-BFGS makes up for the higher per-iteration cost. While the reported runtimes are competitive, a more detailed discussion on the computational trade-offs, especially for massive datasets where stochastic methods are often preferred, would have been beneficial.
*   The work focuses on linear exponential family models. A brief discussion on the challenges or potential pathways for applying this principle to more complex, non-linear models such as deep neural networks would have increased the paper's scope and impact.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

1.  The paper identifies and clearly explains a fundamental problem in a popular framework for learning from logged bandit data.
2.  It proposes an elegant, principled, and effective solution that directly corrects the identified deficiencies of the standard estimator.
3.  The empirical results are comprehensive and provide strong evidence for the proposed method's superior performance and robustness.
4.  The work is highly significant for anyone working on off-policy evaluation and learning, a critical area for real-world applications like recommender systems and online advertising.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

1.  The core statistical tool (the self-normalized estimator) is an existing technique, reducing the fundamental novelty slightly. However, its application and the problem diagnosis are highly original.
2.  The computational scalability with respect to the optimization method could be a potential limitation, although the experiments suggest it is not a practical issue for the datasets tested.

In summary, this is an excellent paper that makes a clear and important contribution. It identifies a significant problem, proposes a well-justified solution, and demonstrates its effectiveness thoroughly. The strengths far outweigh the minor weaknesses. I strongly recommend acceptance.
