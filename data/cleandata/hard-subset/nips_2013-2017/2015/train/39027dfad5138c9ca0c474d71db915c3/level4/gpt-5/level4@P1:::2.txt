Summary and relation to prior work
This paper addresses batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF) within the Counterfactual Risk Minimization (CRM) framework. The authors argue that the standard inverse propensity score (IPS) estimator used in prior BLBF work (e.g., ERM-style CRM with importance weighting) suffers from “propensity overfitting,” especially over complex hypothesis classes. They propose replacing the conventional IPS estimator (Eq. 2) with a self-normalized importance sampling estimator (Eq. 7), leveraging multiplicative control variates. This leads to a new algorithm, Norm-POEM, an exponential-family model trained under CRM with a variance penalty adapted to the self-normalized estimator. The paper positions itself relative to importance sampling, off-policy evaluation, and variance-reduction techniques (e.g., clipping, control variates, doubly robust estimation) and compares against POEM [1] and CRF “skyline” models on multi-label classification datasets.

Main strengths
- Clear problem motivation: logs provide biased, partial-information feedback; IPS can have high variance and be unstable when propensities are small. The proposed self-normalized estimator directly attenuates the impact of extreme propensities.
- Conceptual contribution: recasting BLBF estimation through multiplicative control variates is natural and connects to well-known variance reduction in Monte Carlo.
- Practical algorithm: Norm-POEM is a direct drop-in replacement for POEM within CRM, with a carefully derived variance proxy for the ratio estimator.
- Empirical results: Across multiple multi-label datasets, Norm-POEM consistently outperforms POEM and is competitive with supervised CRF baselines. Results are robust to loss translations and show favorable runtime due to faster convergence.
- Relevance: Fits squarely within NIPS topics (bandits, off-policy evaluation, importance sampling, structured prediction).

Technical concerns and clarifications
- Commentary around Eq. (2) and translation invariance: The paper claims the IPS estimator (Eq. 2) exhibits “anomalies” because it is not equivariant under additive translations of the loss, and builds Example 1 on this premise. This reasoning appears technically incorrect or at least misleading. In an expectation of the form E[f(X)] = sum f(x) p(x), the “expected quantity” is f(x), not the probability measure; invariance properties must be interpreted relative to the estimand. In Eq. (2), the estimator targets the expected loss under the target policy via importance weighting; the lack of exact finite-sample equivariance under loss translation is not, by itself, an “anomaly” of the estimator. Consequently, the argument that Example 1 reveals a fundamental flaw in Eq. (2) because it is not translation-invariant is not convincing.
- Practical instability of Eq. (2): That said, the key practical issue with Eq. (2) is its sensitivity to small propensities pi in the denominator; when propensities approach zero, variance explodes and estimates become unreliable. On this front, the self-normalized estimator in Eq. (7) is indeed preferable in principle, as normalization dampens the impact of extreme weights and enforces boundedness and (finite-sample) translation equivariance of the estimate.
- Recommendation to adjust exposition: I strongly encourage the authors to revise the discussion that labels the non-equivariance of Eq. (2) as an “anomaly,” and to disentangle the variance/weight-instability critique (which is strong and well-founded) from claims about translation invariance (which are not needed to motivate the new estimator and, as currently written, are technically inaccurate).

Empirical evaluation
- The supervised-to-bandit setup, datasets, Hamming loss, and CRF “skyline” are appropriate and consistent with prior work (e.g., POEM). Norm-POEM beats POEM across datasets and training sizes and is robust to loss translations, matching the theoretical advantages of the self-normalized estimator.
- The analysis of Ŝ(ĥ) (sum of normalized weights) supports the claim that Norm-POEM better controls propensity overfitting and behaves stably across loss translations, whereas POEM can select degenerate solutions.
- Runtime observations are plausible: although per-iteration cost increases, improved robustness reduces the number of iterations. Using L-BFGS across methods controls for optimizer effects.

Quality
- The theoretical move to self-normalization is sound and well-justified by established Monte Carlo literature. The finite-sample variance proxy and clipping are reasonable. However, the paper should correct the technical narrative around Eq. (2) and the role of loss translation.
- Proof sketches and references are appropriate; claims about strong consistency of the self-normalized estimator are standard.

Clarity
- Generally well-written and well-organized, with thorough related work. The main clarity issue is the framing of “anomalies” of Eq. (2): this should be toned down and technically corrected. Example 1 should be reframed to emphasize variance/weight instability (and the optimizer exploiting weight sums) rather than translation invariance.

Originality
- Using self-normalization in CRM for BLBF and deriving Norm-POEM is a meaningful and timely extension. The connection to propensity overfitting is a useful conceptual contribution, provided the exposition is corrected.

Significance
- Important for practitioners relying on logged bandit data. The method is simple to adopt, improves stability and generalization, and is likely to be used.

Pros for acceptance
- Solid, relevant contribution with clear practical impact.
- Strong empirical gains over a recent baseline (POEM).
- Bridges CRM with well-established variance reduction via control variates.

Cons and requests for revision
- Technically misleading discussion of translation invariance and “anomalies” attributed to Eq. (2); Example 1 depends on this framing.
- Please revise the exposition to:
  - Remove or correct claims that non-equivariance of Eq. (2) is an anomaly.
  - Re-express Example 1 to focus on variance/weight instability and propensity overfitting driven by extreme importance weights.
  - Clarify conditions under which self-normalization introduces bias and how clipping interacts with consistency and variance in practice.

Overall assessment
Overall, the paper is convincing and worth publishing, contingent on revising the commentary around Eq. (2) and the associated example. The methodological contribution and empirical evidence are strong, and the proposed estimator is likely to be adopted. I encourage the authors to adjust the technical exposition accordingly if the paper is accepted.
