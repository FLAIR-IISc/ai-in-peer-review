This paper identifies a critical issue – propensity overfitting – in the commonly used counterfactual risk estimator for batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF), and proposes a novel self-normalized estimator to address it. Building upon the foundational work of Swaminathan and Joachims (2015), the authors demonstrate significant improvements in algorithm performance and stability.

**Quality:** The technical quality of this work is high. The authors clearly articulate the problem of propensity overfitting, provide a compelling theoretical justification for their proposed self-normalized estimator, and rigorously evaluate its performance empirically. The connections to related areas like causal inference, importance sampling, and off-policy evaluation are well-established and appropriately cited. The mathematical derivations, including Theorem 1 and Theorem 2, appear sound and contribute to the overall rigor. The experimental results convincingly demonstrate the effectiveness of Norm-POEM.

**Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly organized. The introduction effectively motivates the problem and situates it within the broader context of learning from historical data. The notation is consistent and the technical concepts are explained in a reasonably accessible manner, even for readers not deeply familiar with the BLBF literature. However, some sections (e.g., the discussion of variance estimation in Section 6) could benefit from further clarification. For example, expanding on the practical implications of the Normal approximation argument for the variance estimate could be useful.

**Originality:** The paper presents a genuinely original contribution. While building upon existing techniques, the identification of propensity overfitting as a distinct phenomenon and the development of the self-normalized estimator to specifically combat it represent a significant advancement in the field. The Norm-POEM algorithm, derived from this new estimator, provides a practical and effective solution for BLBF. The authors appropriately acknowledge related work (e.g., Rosenbaum & Rubin, 1983; Cortes et al., 2008) and clearly differentiate their contributions.

**Significance:** The results presented in this paper are highly significant. The problem of learning from logged bandit feedback is increasingly relevant in many real-world applications (e.g., ad placement, recommendation systems). The propensity overfitting issue identified by the authors represents a substantial hurdle to achieving reliable performance in these settings. The proposed solution – Norm-POEM – offers a promising path towards overcoming this hurdle. The observation that Norm-POEM can be faster than the original POEM, despite incorporating additional regularization, is particularly noteworthy and suggests practical advantages for deployment. This work has the potential to influence future research and development in the area of off-policy evaluation and batch reinforcement learning.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Addresses a critical, previously unrecognised problem (propensity overfitting) in BLBF.
*   Provides a theoretically sound and empirically validated solution (self-normalized estimator and Norm-POEM).
*   Demonstrates significant performance improvements over existing methods (POEM).
*   Offers a computationally efficient algorithm with practical advantages.
*   Well-written and clearly presented.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The discussion of variance estimation could be more detailed and accessible.
*   While comprehensive, the experimental evaluation could be expanded to include a wider range of datasets and scenarios.



**Overall:** This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of batch learning from logged bandit feedback. I recommend acceptance.
