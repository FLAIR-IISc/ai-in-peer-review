### Summary

This paper addresses the problem of Batch Learning from Logged Bandit Feedback (BLBF), where a learning system is trained using historical interaction logs. The standard approach, Counterfactual Risk Minimization (CRM), relies on an unbiased inverse propensity score (IPS) estimator to evaluate the risk of new policies. The authors identify a significant issue with this conventional estimator, which they term "Propensity Overfitting." This problem arises when a model with high capacity learns to exploit the structure of the propensity weights rather than minimizing the true loss, leading to severely biased risk estimates and poor generalization.

To address this, the paper proposes replacing the standard IPS estimator with a Self-Normalized estimator. This estimator, a form of ratio estimator well-known in the importance sampling literature, uses the sum of propensity weights as a multiplicative control variate. The authors show that this self-normalized estimator is equivariant to translations in the loss function and its estimates are bounded within the range of the loss, thereby provably avoiding the anomalies that cause propensity overfitting.

Building on this improved estimator, the paper introduces a new learning algorithm, the Normalized Policy Optimizer for Exponential Models (Norm-POEM), which adapts the CRM framework to use the self-normalized risk and a corresponding variance estimate. The empirical evaluation on several multi-label classification tasks demonstrates that Norm-POEM consistently and significantly outperforms the previous state-of-the-art method (POEM), which uses the conventional estimator.

### Strengths

1.  **Problem Significance:** The paper tackles a well-defined and important problem. Learning from logged bandit data is a critical task for improving real-world interactive systems like recommenders and ad platforms. The issue of estimator instability and overfitting is a central challenge in this domain.

2.  **Clear Diagnosis and Solution:** The identification and illustration of "Propensity Overfitting" is a key strength. The examples provided in Section 4 are clear and effectively demonstrate how the conventional unbiased estimator can fail catastrophically, producing risk estimates that are arbitrarily low. The proposed solution—employing the self-normalized estimator—is principled, drawing from established techniques in Monte Carlo methods, and directly addresses the identified weaknesses (equivariance, bounded estimates).

3.  **Strong Empirical Results:** The experimental evaluation is thorough and convincing. Norm-POEM demonstrates substantial and consistent performance gains over the baseline POEM across four different datasets. The results in Table 2, which show the stability of Norm-POEM under loss translation while POEM's performance collapses, provide compelling evidence that the self-normalized estimator successfully mitigates the propensity overfitting problem. The fact that Norm-POEM's performance approaches that of a fully supervised CRF ("skyline") highlights the effectiveness of the approach.

### Weaknesses and Comments for Authors

The primary concern with the paper lies in the framing of its theoretical motivation regarding the "anomalies" of the unbiased estimator in Equation (2).

1.  **Motivation for Estimator "Anomalies":** The paper argues that it is an "anomaly" for the unbiased estimator `R_hat(h)` not to be shift-invariant (i.e., `R_hat(h) -> R_hat(h) + C` when `δ -> δ + C`). This argument is potentially misleading. An expectation is defined as `E[f(X)] = Σ f(x)p(x)`. In the case of the true risk `R(h)`, the expectation is over the loss `δ`, so linearity and shift-invariance hold. However, the estimator in Equation (2) is a Monte Carlo approximation of `E_h0[δ * h(y|x)/h0(y|x)]`. Here, the quantity whose expectation is being estimated is the entire product `δ_i * h(y_i|x_i)/p_i`, not just the loss `δ_i`. It is therefore not an "anomaly" that the estimator for this product is not shift-invariant with respect to `δ_i`; it is a direct consequence of its mathematical form.

2.  **Reframing the Argument:** The argument would be stronger if it focused less on the perceived "anomaly" of shift-invariance and more on the well-known high-variance issues of the IPS estimator. The core problem, which the paper correctly identifies in practice, is that the estimator is highly sensitive to the propensity scores `p_i`. When `p_i` is small, the importance weights can explode, leading to extreme variance and instability. The self-normalized estimator in Equation (7) elegantly mitigates this by dividing by the sum of the importance weights, effectively re-normalizing the contribution of each sample. This is arguably the more fundamental reason for its superior stability and performance, a point which is somewhat understated in favor of the shift-invariance argument. Example 1 is powerful, but its conclusion—that severe overfitting occurs—is better explained by the model's ability to exploit large importance weights rather than by a lack of shift-invariance.

I strongly suggest that the authors consider revising this part of the narrative. The proposed method and its empirical success are convincing on their own. Grounding the motivation more firmly in the well-understood variance problems of IPS estimators, which the self-normalized estimator is designed to combat, would make the paper's theoretical contribution even more sound and impactful.

### Overall Assessment

This is a well-written paper that makes a clear and valuable contribution to the field of counterfactual learning. It successfully identifies a critical failure mode of the standard IPS estimator in a learning context and proposes an effective and principled solution. The empirical results are strong and convincingly support the paper's claims. Despite the minor weakness in the framing of the theoretical motivation, the overall quality, clarity, originality, and significance of the work are high. The paper is a solid incremental improvement on recent work [1] and is likely to be influential for researchers and practitioners working with logged bandit feedback. I recommend acceptance.
