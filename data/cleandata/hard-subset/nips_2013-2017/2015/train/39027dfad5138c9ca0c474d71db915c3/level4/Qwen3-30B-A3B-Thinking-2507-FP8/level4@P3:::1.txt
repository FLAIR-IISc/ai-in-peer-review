

This paper identifies a critical flaw in the standard counterfactual risk estimator used for batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF), introducing the concept of "propensity overfitting" and proposing a self-normalized estimator to resolve it. The authors demonstrate that conventional unbiased estimators (e.g., inverse propensity weighting) can produce severely biased risk estimates when minimizing empirical risk over complex hypothesis spaces, particularly when the logging policy lacks sufficient exploration. This problem manifests as a form of overfitting where risk estimates become unbounded below (for negative losses) or fail to reflect true risk under loss transformations—contrasting sharply with supervised learning. The proposed solution replaces the estimator with a self-normalized variant (Equation 7), which is equivariant under loss translation and bounded within the loss range. The resulting Norm-POEM algorithm for structured prediction is rigorously analyzed and empirically validated across multiple multi-label classification datasets, consistently outperforming prior methods like POEM.

**Strengths:**  
*Quality & Technical Soundness:* The paper makes a compelling theoretical contribution by formally identifying propensity overfitting (Section 4) and proving the self-normalized estimator resolves its key anomalies (Theorem 2). The empirical evaluation (Section 7) is thorough, with clear ablation studies showing Norm-POEM’s robustness to loss translation (Table 2), avoidance of propensity overfitting (via $\hat{S}(\hat{h}) \approx 1$), and superior generalization (Table 1). The analysis of variance regularization’s continued necessity (Table 3) and computational efficiency (Section 7) further strengthens the case.  
*Originality:* The problem of propensity overfitting is novel and distinct from known variance issues in importance sampling (e.g., clipped estimators [5, 15]). While prior work [1, 6, 11] focuses on variance reduction, this paper reveals a deeper structural flaw in the estimator’s behavior under ERM—a significant conceptual advance. The self-normalized estimator (used in finance [17, 19]) is repurposed in a theoretically grounded way for BLBF, making the approach both novel and well-motivated.  
*Significance:* The findings directly address a critical gap in BLBF, a core problem for real-world systems (e.g., ad placement, recommendations). Norm-POEM’s consistent empirical gains across datasets (Scene, Yeast, TMC, LYRL) and compatibility with existing frameworks (e.g., CRFs [24]) suggest immediate practical impact. The paper advances the state-of-the-art by enabling more reliable counterfactual learning in data-scarce settings where exploration is limited.

**Weaknesses:**  
*Clarity:* The distinction between propensity overfitting and variance-related issues (e.g., clipped importance weights) is initially subtle. The draft review correctly notes this; the authors could clarify that propensity overfitting arises *despite* clipping (Section 4’s Example 1 shows clipping fails to prevent degenerate estimates). A brief comparison table contrasting the two problems would aid readers.  
*Technical Detail:* The claim that prior work uses "unbiased" estimators (line 199 in draft) is misleading. The paper acknowledges clipping in [1, 5, 15] but correctly identifies the *unclipped* estimator’s inherent flaw. The draft reviewer’s confusion about Example 1 (line 199) stems from misinterpreting the overfitting mechanism: when $n \ll k$, the overfitting hypothesis $h_{\text{overfit}}$ assigns $f(x_i)=y_i$ for all $i$, yielding $\hat{R}(h_{\text{overfit}}) = -k$ (not smaller than $-2$), which *is* unbounded below. The authors’ example is correct.  
*Minor:* The paper could briefly discuss whether propensity overfitting occurs in *online* bandit feedback (e.g., via adaptive policies), though this is outside the BLBF scope.

**Recommendation:**  
This paper makes a high-impact contribution to counterfactual learning. It identifies a fundamental flaw in widely used estimators, provides a theoretically sound solution, and demonstrates robust empirical improvements. The work is technically rigorous, well-motivated, and directly addresses a critical challenge in applying BLBF to real-world systems. While the exposition of propensity overfitting could be slightly clearer, the core ideas are compelling and well-supported. The empirical validation is comprehensive and addresses key concerns (e.g., loss translation, variance regularization).  

**Pro:**  
- Novel problem identification (propensity overfitting)  
- Theoretically proven solution (self-normalized estimator)  
- Strong empirical results across multiple datasets  
- Practical implementation (code available)  
- Clear significance for real-world bandit learning  

**Con:**  
- Slight ambiguity in differentiating from variance issues (easily resolved)  
- Example 1 requires careful reading (but is mathematically sound)  

The paper meets all NIPS criteria: it is technically sound (Quality), clearly written (Clarity), conceptually original (Originality), and addresses a significant problem with broad applicability (Significance). It advances the state of the art in BLBF by resolving a previously overlooked failure mode. I strongly recommend acceptance.
