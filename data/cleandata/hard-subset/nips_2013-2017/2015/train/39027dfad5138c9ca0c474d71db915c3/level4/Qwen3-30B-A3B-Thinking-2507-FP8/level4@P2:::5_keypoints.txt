- Identifies a new problem in batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF) called "propensity overfitting" that breaks conventional unbiased counterfactual risk estimators.  
- Builds on Swaminathan & Joachims (2015) CRM framework and critiques the conventional inverse propensity estimator (Equation 2).  
- Shows the conventional estimator yields severe anomalies in ERM over complex hypothesis spaces, producing arbitrarily low biased risk estimates.  
- Proposes a self-normalized risk estimator (Equation 7) that avoids these anomalies via multiplicative control variates.  
- Proves the self-normalized estimator is equivariant under loss translation and yields risk estimates bounded by the loss range.  
- Formalizes propensity overfitting theoretically and demonstrates it differs fundamentally from standard supervised loss overfitting.  
- Introduces Norm-POEM, which replaces the conventional estimator with the self-normalized estimator while preserving the CRM principle.  
- Provides rigorous theoretical analysis, including a proof of strong consistency for the self-normalized estimator (Theorem 2).  
- Empirically demonstrates consistent improvements of Norm-POEM over POEM across multiple multi-label datasets (Scene, Yeast, TMC, LYRL).  
- Shows Norm-POEM achieves better generalization while being computationally more efficient than POEM.  
- Includes ablation studies confirming invariance to loss translation (Table 2) and continued benefit of variance regularization (Table 3).  
- Paper is well-written, logically organized, and connects theory to experiments clearly.  
- Related work is well-contextualized within causal inference, off-policy evaluation, and importance sampling literature.  
- Experimental setup follows prior BLBF methodology and includes implementation details and software to support reproducibility.  
- Potential weakness: limited discussion on performance under extremely sparse logging policies with very small h0(y|x).  
- Potential weakness: computational complexity analysis could be expanded to give precise per-iteration cost bounds relative to POEM.  
- NIPS criteria assessment: high quality (rigorous theory + experiments), clear writing, original contribution (identification of propensity overfitting), and significant practical impact.  
- Pro for acceptance: resolves a fundamental limitation in prior BLBF work with solid theory and strong empirical validation.  
- Con for rejection: none of substantial importance noted by the reviewer.  
- Overall verdict: high-quality, significant contribution with potential for real-world impact in advertising and recommendation systems.
