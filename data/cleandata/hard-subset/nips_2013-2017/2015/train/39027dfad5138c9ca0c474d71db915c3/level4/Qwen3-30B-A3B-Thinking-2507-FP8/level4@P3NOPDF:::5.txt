

The paper proposes a novel method for batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF) that effectively addresses the propensity overfitting problem inherent in prior approaches. Building on Swaminathan and Joachims (2015), the authors introduce a significant extension to the POEM algorithm, yielding substantially improved performance in offline learning scenarios.  

The research quality is above average. The proposed extension not only resolves a critical limitation of the original POEM framework but also delivers measurable empirical gains. Crucially, the authors demonstrate that their method achieves faster convergence than POEM despite incorporating a more sophisticated regularization mechanism—a counterintuitive yet highly valuable advancement. The theoretical analysis is sound, and the experimental validation across multiple datasets robustly supports the claims.  

The paper is exceptionally well-written and logically structured. Key contributions are clearly articulated, and the technical exposition is accessible without sacrificing rigor. The organization facilitates easy comprehension of the methodological innovations, and the figures effectively illustrate the algorithmic improvements.  

The contribution is highly original. While the work extends Swaminathan and Joachims (2015), the authors present a fundamentally new formulation for handling propensity overfitting in BLBF. This is not merely a minor tweak but a principled rethinking of the regularization strategy, resulting in a distinct algorithmic paradigm. The novelty is evident in both the theoretical framework and the practical implementation, making it a meaningful advancement over existing approaches.  

The significance of this work is substantial. The proposed method’s ability to outperform POEM while reducing computational overhead addresses a pressing bottleneck in real-world applications of offline bandit learning. Given the growing reliance on logged data in recommendation systems, ad placement, and other large-scale decision-making contexts, this algorithm has strong potential for immediate adoption. The results warrant further investigation into its scalability and adaptability to more complex bandit settings.  

This paper makes a valuable contribution to the field. It elegantly solves a persistent problem in BLBF with a method that is both theoretically grounded and empirically superior. The clarity of presentation, combined with the strength of the results, makes it a compelling read for the NeurIPS community. I recommend acceptance without reservation.
