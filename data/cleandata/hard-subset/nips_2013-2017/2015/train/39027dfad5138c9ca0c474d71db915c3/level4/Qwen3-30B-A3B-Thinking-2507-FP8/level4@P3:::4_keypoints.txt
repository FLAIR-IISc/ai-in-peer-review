- Identifies "propensity overfitting" in batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF) as a new failure mode for counterfactual risk estimators in complex hypothesis spaces.  
- Shows inverse-propensity (unbiased) estimators are not equivariant under loss translation and can yield arbitrarily biased risk estimates.  
- Distinguishes propensity overfitting from conventional loss overfitting and argues it fills a theoretical gap in BLBF literature.  
- Proposes a self-normalized risk estimator using multiplicative control variates to correct propensity overfitting.  
- Develops Norm-POEM, a practical algorithm for structured output prediction based on the self-normalized estimator.  
- Provides rigorous theoretical analysis (Theorems 1 and 2) establishing properties of the self-normalized estimator.  
- Empirically demonstrates consistent generalization improvements of Norm-POEM over POEM across multiple multi-label datasets (Scene, Yeast, TMC, LYRL).  
- Shows robustness to loss transformations and improved control over propensity overfitting, with Ŝ(ĥ) stable near its expected value of 1.  
- Clearly situates work within CRM and importance-sampling literature and connects to causal inference, domain adaptation, and off-policy evaluation.  
- Addresses practical considerations such as computational cost and the need for variance regularization.  
- Minor presentation concern: loss-scaling to [-1,0] should be made more explicit in the main text (experiments use transformed losses but this was unclear).  
- Minor theoretical suggestion: treat the feedback function δ(x,y) as a random variable to reflect realistic noisy labeling; this does not change core results.  
- Provides code for reproducibility and argues the contribution is both theoretically significant and practically useful.  
- Overall evaluation: technically sound, original, well-written, significant for real-world BLBF applications; recommends acceptance with confidence.
