Due to travel constraints, my review of this paper is necessarily brief. However, I found the work interesting and potentially valuable, though I acknowledge the possibility of having missed nuanced details. I apologize if any misinterpretations are present.

The paper investigates a challenge arising when evaluating a hypothesis given data generated from a different one. Specifically, when dealing with non-deterministic hypotheses (e.g., conditional distributions), the authors propose reweighting data points to estimate the loss under the hypothesis being evaluated – a technique related to, but distinct from, addressing the large variance of these weights, as illustrated between equations (1) and (2). The authors identify a phenomenon they term "propensity overfitting," which appears to occur when there is insufficient exploration; that is, when the dataset lacks examples of outcomes (y) that are likely under the new hypothesis being considered, given a particular input (x). While a similar logic underlies explanations for high weight variance, a more detailed discussion clarifying the relationship between these two issues would strengthen the paper.

I was unable to thoroughly examine the technical details or rigorously assess the novelty of the proposed solution. Nevertheless, the core idea appears sound and promising. 

I have a few specific questions and comments:

* **Line 199:** I am unclear about the reasoning here. If *n* is small relative to *k*, shouldn't we observe few data points where *x<sub>i</sub> = y<sub>i</sub>*? If so, wouldn’t  *R̂(ĥ)* be even smaller (more negative) than -2? I may be misunderstanding something.

* **Regarding prior work:** The authors state they use an "unbiased counterfactual risk estimator used in prior works on BLBF [4, 5, 1]". I question whether “unbiased” is an accurate characterization. While I am not intimately familiar with all three cited papers, I suspect they likely employ techniques such as clipping, which introduce bias in exchange for reduced variance. 

* **Code Availability:** I appreciate the authors’ commitment to reproducibility by making their code publicly available.

In summary, while I haven’t been able to conduct a fully comprehensive technical evaluation, the paper presents intriguing ideas that warrant further consideration. Based on my initial reading, I recommend acceptance.
