This paper presents an interesting follow-up to [1], focusing on addressing propensity overfitting in off-policy evaluation, specifically within the context of batch learning from logged bandit feedback – a setting receiving increasing attention. The authors propose using a multiplicative control variate to reduce this overfitting, and their analysis of the phenomenon is insightful. The experimental results demonstrate clear improvements over POEM.

However, I have a few questions and comments. First, regarding the rescaling of the loss: Section 4.1 of [1] indicates the CRM approach functions optimally when delta falls within the range [-1, 0], necessitating a rescaling of the general loss to fit this range. I assume the same constraint applies here to equations (8) and (3), utilizing this rescaled delta in [-1, 0]. If this is correct, the use of "delta > 0" in the experiments presented in Table 1 is unclear and should be clarified in the rebuttal.

Second, the setup for “batch learning from logged bandit feedback” in Section 3 (and drawing from [1]) feels incomplete. The feedback, delta(x, y), appears to be modeled as a deterministic function (line 110). However, I believe it should be treated as a random variable. Consider a classification scenario where labels are unknown and obtained through a loss calculation after prediction. A “god agent” labels each input x with y’, and the loss Delta(y’, y) is observed. If labeling is noisy—a common occurrence in classification—multiple instances of the same (x, y) pair may yield different delta(x, y) values due to varying labels (y’). Therefore, delta(x, y) should generally be understood as a random variable. For a fixed x and played y, its mean should represent the expected value of Delta(y’, y) where y’ is distributed according to the true (and potentially noisy) labeling distribution p(y’|x). While this doesn't necessarily affect the risk estimator (2), the setup in (1) representing the true risk should ideally include an expectation over the randomness of delta for greater generality.

Third, on line 170-171, I believe the ratio should be expressed as h(y|x) / h_0(y|x). Additionally, it would be beneficial to acknowledge that the lack of linearity was previously identified in [1]. 

Finally, on line 276, “argmax” should be replaced with “argmin” as the objective is risk minimization. On line 319, please replace bars with hats in the notation to maintain consistency with earlier conventions.
