This paper addresses a critical issue in batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF), a setting where a learner aims to train a new policy using historical data from a different, existing policy. The core of the paper is the identification of a failure mode in the standard inverse propensity score (IPS) estimator used for counterfactual risk minimization, which the authors term "propensity overfitting." This problem arises because the IPS estimator can be minimized not only by finding a hypothesis with low intrinsic loss but also by selecting hypotheses that exploit the propensity weights to produce arbitrarily low (or high) risk estimates, especially when the hypothesis space is complex.

To solve this, the authors propose replacing the standard IPS estimator with a self-normalized estimator. This alternative estimator, which has roots in the control variates literature for Monte Carlo methods, normalizes the weighted sum of losses by the sum of the importance weights. This simple change yields an estimator that is equivariant to shifts in the loss function and bounded by the range of the loss, thereby provably avoiding the anomalies that lead to propensity overfitting. The paper introduces a new learning algorithm, Normalized Policy Optimizer for Exponential Models (Norm-POEM), which incorporates this self-normalized estimator into the Counterfactual Risk Minimization (CRM) framework. The empirical evaluation on several multi-label classification tasks demonstrates that Norm-POEM consistently and significantly outperforms its predecessor (POEM), which uses the standard estimator.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The key contribution of this paper is the identification and clear articulation of the "propensity overfitting" problem. While the high variance of IPS estimators is a well-known challenge, this paper provides a novel and insightful perspective on a distinct failure mode that occurs during *learning*, where the capacity of the hypothesis space is used to overfit the propensity scores themselves. This is a subtle but important problem for the field of counterfactual learning, and the paper's clear diagnosis is a significant contribution.

2.  **Quality of the Proposed Solution:** The proposal to use a self-normalized estimator is elegant, well-principled, and effective. The authors correctly connect it to the established technique of multiplicative control variates and clearly explain its theoretical benefits, such as equivariance and boundedness, which directly address the identified problem. The resulting algorithm, Norm-POEM, is a natural and well-justified extension of the CRM principle.

3.  **Clarity and Presentation:** The paper is very well-written and easy to follow. The motivating examples in Section 4 are particularly effective, providing a clear and compelling illustration of how propensity overfitting can lead to degenerate solutions. The theoretical arguments are presented concisely, and the connection to prior work is well-established.

4.  **Empirical Soundness:** The experimental evaluation is thorough and convincing. By using the "Supervised to Bandit" protocol, the authors provide a controlled environment to clearly demonstrate the advantages of their proposed method. The results in Tables 1, 2, and 3 strongly support the paper's claims: Norm-POEM not only achieves better generalization performance but is also robust to transformations of the loss function, directly confirming that it mitigates propensity overfitting where the standard approach fails. The fact that code is made available is also a major plus for reproducibility.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

1.  **Relation between Propensity Overfitting and High Variance:** The paper distinguishes propensity overfitting from the well-known high-variance problem of the IPS estimator. While they are conceptually different (one is a bias issue in the learned hypothesis, the other a variance issue of the estimator itself), they are also deeply related, as both are exacerbated by small propensity scores. The paper would be strengthened by a more detailed discussion of this relationship. For instance, is propensity overfitting a specific consequence of high-variance estimates being exploited by a high-capacity learner? Clarifying this connection would further refine the paper's contribution.

2.  **Characterization of Prior Work:** The paper states that "virtually all existing works on BLBF have focused on a particular unbiased estimator." This characterization may be slightly too strong. Many established methods for off-policy evaluation and learning employ techniques like weight clipping or capping (e.g., as in R̂_M(h)), which intentionally introduce bias to control variance. It might be more precise to state that prior work is based on estimators that are *derived from* an unbiased one, but that these practical variants also fail to address propensity overfitting.

3.  **Clarification on Example 1:** The calculation in Example 1 that leads to R̂(hoverfit) ≤ -k could be spelled out more explicitly. For a reader quickly scanning the paper, it may not be immediately obvious how a hypothesis that perfectly fits the observed data `(xi, yi)` leads to this specific value, particularly how the losses `δi` and propensities `pi` combine. A brief, step-by-step derivation would improve clarity.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Identifies a novel, important, and subtle problem in counterfactual learning (propensity overfitting).
*   Proposes a simple, theoretically sound, and principled solution (the self-normalized estimator).
*   The resulting algorithm, Norm-POEM, is shown to be empirically superior to the state-of-the-art baseline in a well-controlled experimental setup.
*   The paper is exceptionally well-written and the ideas are clearly communicated.
*   The contribution is significant and likely to influence future research in learning from bandit feedback and off-policy evaluation.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   The distinction between the newly identified problem and the classic high-variance issue of importance sampling could be drawn more sharply.

**Recommendation:**
This is an excellent paper that makes a clear and valuable contribution to the field of counterfactual learning. It identifies a real problem, proposes an elegant solution, and provides strong theoretical and empirical evidence to support its claims. The weaknesses are minor and mainly relate to opportunities for further clarification. I strongly recommend acceptance.
