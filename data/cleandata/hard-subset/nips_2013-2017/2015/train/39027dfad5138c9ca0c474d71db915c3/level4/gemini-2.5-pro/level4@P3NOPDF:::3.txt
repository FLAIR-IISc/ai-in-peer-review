### **Summary**

This paper addresses the problem of batch learning from logged bandit feedback, a setting with significant practical relevance in applications such as ad ranking. Building upon the recently proposed Counterfactual Risk Minimization (CRM) principle [1], the authors identify a key "overfitting" issue that can arise from the high variance of the standard importance-weighted risk estimator. To address this, they propose a novel, self-normalizing risk estimator based on multiplicative control variates. While this modification introduces bias to the estimator, it is shown to be highly effective in the empirical evaluation.

### **Quality and Significance**

The paper is of high quality, well-motivated, and clearly written. The problem of learning effective policies from historical logged data is highly significant, and the paper's focus on improving the stability and performance of offline policy learners is a valuable contribution to the field. The identification of the overfitting issue in the original CRM formulation is insightful, and the proposed self-normalizing estimator is an elegant solution. The strong empirical results on the studied datasets demonstrate that this modification can lead to substantial performance gains over the baseline.

### **Areas for Improvement**

While the paper presents a solid contribution, there are several areas where it could be strengthened:

1.  **Experimental Scope:** The experimental evaluation, while convincing on the chosen datasets, feels somewhat limited. As this is a direct follow-up to [1], the evaluation would be more compelling if it included a broader range of experiments beyond those already studied in the original work. Demonstrating the method's effectiveness on new, real-world applications would significantly bolster the claims of its practical utility. It is currently difficult to assess how the substantial improvements observed here would translate to more complex, real-world scenarios.

2.  **Analysis of Optimization:** The paper would benefit from a more detailed analysis of the optimization of the proposed training objective. The discussion around line 415 is brief, and a deeper exploration of the optimization landscape, convergence properties, or sensitivity to hyperparameters would add considerable technical depth.

3.  **Reporting of Variance:** A minor but important point regarding the experimental results: the authors state that they run several repetitions (line 365), but the plots do not include any measure of variance (e.g., error bars or confidence intervals). Including these would provide a more complete picture of the algorithm's stability and help quantify the statistical significance of the reported improvements.

### **Originality**

The work is a follow-up to [1], but the proposed modification to the risk estimator is novel and impactful. The introduction of the self-normalizing term makes a significant difference in the experiments, highlighting an important and previously unaddressed weakness in the CRM framework.

### **Conclusion**

Overall, this is a solid and well-written paper that makes a relevant and novel contribution to the important problem of offline learning from bandit feedback. My main reservations concern the scope of the experimental evaluation. I would have appreciated more extensive experiments, particularly on applications not previously seen in [1], to better demonstrate the generalizability and real-world impact of the proposed method.
