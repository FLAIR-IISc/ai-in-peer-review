This review was conducted under significant time constraints, so my assessment is based on a high-level reading of the paper. I apologize in advance if I have missed or misunderstood any key details.

The paper addresses an important problem in off-policy evaluation. When evaluating a new hypothesis (policy) using data generated from a different, existing hypothesis, a standard approach is to use importance sampling to reweight the observed data. The authors identify a specific failure mode in this setting, which they term "propensity overfitting." My understanding is that this issue arises when the data-generating policy has insufficient exploration. Specifically, for a given context `x`, the set of observed outcomes `y` may not include those that are probable under the new hypothesis being evaluated.

This concept of "propensity overfitting" appears closely related to the well-known problem of high variance in importance sampling estimators, which also stems from a mismatch in support between the two distributions. A more detailed discussion clarifying the distinction and relationship between propensity overfitting and the high-variance problem would significantly strengthen the paper's motivation. For instance, is propensity overfitting a specific cause of high variance, or a distinct issue altogether?

While I was not able to delve into the technical details or verify the novelty of the proposed solution, the core idea appears intuitive and sensible as a remedy for the problem described.

I have a few specific points of confusion and comments:

1.  Regarding the argument on line 199: I was unable to follow the reasoning. If `n` is small compared to `k`, wouldn't we expect very few data points where `xi = yi`? In this scenario, my understanding is that the estimated risk `\hat R(h^)` would be significantly smaller than -2. Could the authors clarify this point?

2.  The paper claims that prior works on "Bandits with logged bandit feedback" (BLBF) [1, 4, 5] used an "unbiased counterfactual risk estimator." This characterization of prior work may not be entirely accurate. It is common practice in this field to use techniques like weight clipping to control variance, which intentionally introduces bias. It would be valuable for the authors to confirm whether the cited works truly abstain from any such variance-reduction techniques.

On a positive note, I appreciate the authors' commitment to reproducibility by making their code available.

In summary, this paper presents interesting and thought-provoking ideas on a relevant problem. Due to time limitations, I could not perform a thorough technical validation of the proposed method. However, the problem formulation is compelling, and the direction of the solution seems promising. I believe the paper's contributions are sufficient to warrant further discussion in the community. Therefore, I lean towards acceptance, contingent on the authors addressing the relationship between "propensity overfitting" and estimator variance more clearly.
