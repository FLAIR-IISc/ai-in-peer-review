

Due to travel constraints, this review is brief, but I found the paper highly interesting. I regret that time limitations may have prevented me from fully engaging with the technical details, and I apologize for any misinterpretations.  

The paper addresses a key challenge in counterfactual evaluation: when assessing a new hypothesis (e.g., a conditional distribution) using data generated under a different hypothesis, reweighting techniques can estimate the target loss (as noted between equations 1 and 2). The authors introduce "propensity overfitting" as a distinct issue from the well-known problem of high weight variance. They argue that propensity overfitting arises when insufficient exploration occurs—specifically, when for a given input \(x\), the data contains very few or no observations corresponding to the new hypothesis. Given that similar reasoning underlies high weight variance (e.g., sparse support for the target hypothesis), the authors should clarify the precise relationship between these two phenomena. A more detailed discussion would strengthen the paper’s conceptual foundation.  

I did not have time to fully verify the technical novelty or correctness of the proposed solution, but the core idea appears sound and promising.  

**Specific concerns:**  
- **Line 199:** The claim that \(\hat{R}(\hat{h})\) becomes "even smaller than -2" when \(n \ll k\) is unclear. If \(n\) is small relative to \(k\), observations with \(x_i = y_i\) would be scarce, making \(\hat{R}(\hat{h})\) less reliable—but why would it be *smaller* than -2? I may be misunderstanding the context.  
- **Line 201:** The statement that prior BLBF work [4, 5, 1] uses "unbiased counterfactual risk estimators" appears inaccurate. I suspect these works employ clipping (or similar techniques) to reduce variance, which inherently introduces bias. A citation confirming unbiasedness would be necessary to support this claim.  

**Positive notes:**  
- The paper’s focus on propensity overfitting is novel and addresses a meaningful gap.  
- I appreciate the availability of code, which enhances reproducibility.  

**Summary and recommendation:**  
While I was unable to conduct a thorough technical review due to time constraints, the paper presents compelling ideas that merit further consideration. The core problem is well-motivated, and the proposed framework offers a fresh perspective on counterfactual evaluation. Given the potential impact of the approach and the clarity of the problem statement, I recommend **acceptance**. The authors should address the relationship between propensity overfitting and weight variance, clarify line 199, and revise the claim about unbiased estimators to align with standard practice. These minor points do not undermine the paper’s value, and I look forward to seeing the final version.
