This paper identifies a significant issue – propensity overfitting – in the commonly used counterfactual risk estimator for batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF), and proposes a compelling solution using a self-normalized estimator. The BLBF setting, where learning occurs from historical data without active intervention, is highly relevant to practical applications like ad placement and recommendation systems, making this work impactful. The authors clearly articulate how the conventional estimator can lead to degenerate risk estimates and demonstrate that their self-normalized estimator elegantly avoids this problem, offering both theoretical guarantees and empirical improvements. 

The paper is well-structured, beginning with a clear introduction to the problem of learning from logged bandit feedback and its connection to counterfactual reasoning. The authors effectively relate their work to existing literature in causal inference, domain adaptation, and off-policy evaluation, properly citing relevant papers [1, 2, 4, 7, 8]. The detailed explanation of the propensity overfitting issue, illustrated with a clarifying example (Example 1), is particularly strong. The proposed self-normalized estimator builds upon established techniques like importance sampling and control variates [12, 17, 19], and the derivation is presented logically. The connection to the CRM principle [1] and the development of the Norm-POEM algorithm are well-motivated.

A key strength of this work lies in its thorough empirical validation. The experiments, following the established "Supervised → Bandit" methodology [2], convincingly demonstrate the superiority of Norm-POEM over the conventional estimator across multiple datasets. The ablation studies and analyses (e.g., examining Ŝ(ĥ) in Table 2) provide further insights into the benefits of the proposed approach and confirm that it addresses the identified propensity overfitting problem. The authors also acknowledge the computational costs associated with the new estimator and show that the gains in robustness outweigh these costs, even leading to faster convergence in some cases.

However, there are a few areas where the paper could be strengthened. While the theoretical justification for the self-normalized estimator is sound (Theorem 2), the discussion of its consistency properties could be more detailed. Additionally, the practical implications of choosing the hyperparameter *M* (clipping threshold) are somewhat understated; a more in-depth discussion of its sensitivity and a potential adaptive strategy could be valuable. Finally, while the authors compare their method to POEM [1], a brief comparison to other relevant BLBF algorithms (e.g., those based on doubly robust estimators [6]) would provide additional context.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Addresses a critical issue (propensity overfitting) in a widely used technique (counterfactual risk minimization).
*   Proposes a theoretically sound and empirically validated solution (self-normalized estimator).
*   Clear and well-structured presentation with a strong connection to relevant literature.
*   Thorough experimental evaluation demonstrating significant performance improvements.
*   The method is practically relevant and addresses a core challenge in learning from logged data.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The theoretical discussion of consistency could be more detailed.
*   The sensitivity of the hyperparameter *M* is not fully explored.
*   Limited comparison to other relevant BLBF algorithms beyond POEM.

**Overall:**

Despite these minor points, this is a high-quality paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of learning from logged bandit feedback. The identified problem of propensity overfitting is important, the proposed solution is elegant and effective, and the experimental results are compelling. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the points raised above.
