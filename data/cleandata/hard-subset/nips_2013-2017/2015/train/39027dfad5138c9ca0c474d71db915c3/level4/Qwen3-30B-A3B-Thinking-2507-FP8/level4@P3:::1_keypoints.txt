- Identifies a critical flaw called "propensity overfitting" in standard counterfactual risk estimators for batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF).  
- Shows conventional unbiased estimators (e.g., inverse propensity weighting) can produce severely biased risk estimates when minimizing empirical risk over complex hypothesis spaces.  
- Demonstrates the problem arises especially when the logging policy lacks sufficient exploration, leading to overfitting of the estimator rather than the true risk.  
- Explains the estimator failure can make risk estimates unbounded below for negative losses and non-equivariant under loss translations, unlike supervised learning.  
- Proposes a self-normalized estimator (Equation 7) that is equivariant under loss translation and bounded within the loss range.  
- Proves theoretically (Theorem 2) that the self-normalized estimator resolves the key anomalies of propensity overfitting.  
- Introduces Norm-POEM for structured prediction that uses the self-normalized estimator.  
- Provides rigorous empirical validation across multiple multi-label datasets (Scene, Yeast, TMC, LYRL) where Norm-POEM consistently outperforms prior methods like POEM.  
- Empirical ablation studies show robustness to loss translation, avoidance of propensity overfitting (via \hat{S}(\hat{h}) ≈ 1), and improved generalization (Tables 1–3).  
- Shows variance regularization remains necessary and the proposed method is computationally efficient (Section 7).  
- Argues the problem is novel and distinct from known variance/importance-sampling issues; prior variance-reduction work does not address propensity overfitting.  
- Notes the self-normalized estimator has precedents in finance but is repurposed here with new theoretical grounding for BLBF.  
- Weakness: distinction between propensity overfitting and variance-related issues is initially subtle and could be clarified (e.g., a comparison table).  
- Weakness: phrasing that prior work uses "unbiased" estimators is misleading; clipping and prior methods should be more precisely discussed.  
- Example 1 caused reviewer confusion but is mathematically correct—the overfitting hypothesis can yield arbitrarily low empirical risk when n ≪ k.  
- Minor suggestion: briefly discuss whether propensity overfitting could appear in online/adaptive bandit settings, even if outside BLBF scope.  
- Recommendation: technically rigorous, novel, and practically significant paper deserving strong acceptance.
