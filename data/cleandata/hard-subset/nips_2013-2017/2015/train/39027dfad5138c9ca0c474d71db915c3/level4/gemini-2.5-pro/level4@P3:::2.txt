### **Summary**

This paper addresses the problem of Batch Learning from Logged Bandit Feedback (BLBF), a crucial setting for improving interactive systems like search engines and ad platforms using historical data. The authors identify a significant issue with the conventional unbiased risk estimator used in the Counterfactual Risk Minimization (CRM) principle, which is a standard approach in this domain. They term this issue "Propensity Overfitting," where a model with high capacity can achieve a deceptively low empirical risk by manipulating the importance sampling weights, rather than by actually modeling the underlying loss function well.

To solve this problem, the paper proposes replacing the standard inverse propensity score estimator with a self-normalized (or ratio) estimator. This alternative estimator is shown to be equivariant to shifts in the loss function and naturally constrains risk estimates within the observed loss range, thus preventing the extreme values that characterize propensity overfitting. The authors integrate this new estimator into the CRM framework to create a new learning algorithm, the Normalized Policy Optimizer for Exponential Models (Norm-POEM). Through extensive experiments on several multi-label classification datasets, they demonstrate that Norm-POEM consistently and significantly outperforms its predecessor (POEM), which uses the conventional estimator, and approaches the performance of a skyline model trained on full information.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Clear Identification of a Critical Problem:** The paper's main strength lies in its clear diagnosis of a subtle yet severe failure mode in a widely-used counterfactual learning approach. The concept of "Propensity Overfitting" is well-motivated and convincingly illustrated through a simple but powerful example in Section 4. This insight is valuable to researchers and practitioners using importance sampling-based estimators for off-policy learning and evaluation.

2.  **Elegant and Principled Solution:** The proposed solution—using a self-normalized estimator—is simple, well-justified, and elegant. While this estimator is known in the broader statistics literature, its application to mitigate this specific overfitting problem within the CRM learning framework is novel and insightful. The authors provide good theoretical grounding, showing that the estimator is strongly consistent and resolves the "anomalies" of the unbiased estimator.

3.  **Strong and Comprehensive Empirical Validation:** The experimental results are thorough and compelling.
    *   Norm-POEM consistently outperforms the baseline POEM across four different datasets, various sample sizes, and different logging policies (as reported).
    *   The experiments directly validate the paper's core claim by showing that Norm-POEM produces properly-calibrated sum-of-weights (`Ŝ(h)`) values close to 1, while POEM suffers from severe bias.
    *   The results demonstrating Norm-POEM's invariance to loss translation further strengthen the argument and highlight its robustness.
    *   The ablation showing that variance regularization is still beneficial on top of the self-normalized estimator is a good inclusion.

### **Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement**

1.  **Framing of the Unbiased Estimator's "Anomalies":** The motivation for the new estimator rests on the argument that the standard unbiased estimator (Eq. 2) has "anomalies," such as not being equivariant to a constant shift in the loss. While technically correct for the finite-sample estimator, this property stems directly from the mechanics of importance sampling, where the sum of weights on a finite sample does not necessarily equal the sample size (though its expectation is the sample size). Some readers might find calling this an "anomaly" slightly strong, as it is a well-known characteristic of the estimator. The argument could be strengthened by framing this less as a fundamental flaw and more as a practical vulnerability that makes the estimator susceptible to the propensity overfitting problem, which is the paper's key insight.

2.  **Scope of the Contribution:** The paper is presented primarily as an improvement over POEM [1], a specific algorithm from a recent ICML paper. This makes for a very clean and direct comparison, but it slightly undersells the generality of the findings. The problem of propensity overfitting is likely to affect other importance-sampling-based off-policy learning algorithms, particularly those operating over complex hypothesis spaces (e.g., in deep reinforcement learning). A brief discussion on the broader applicability of the self-normalized estimator as a remedy in other contexts would enhance the paper's significance.

3.  **Discussion of Computational Scalability:** The paper notes that the variance of the self-normalized estimator (Eq. 8) does not admit a straightforward stochastic optimization approach, unlike the original POEM. Although the experiments show that L-BFGS converges surprisingly fast, this could become a limitation on truly massive datasets where stochastic methods are essential. A more detailed discussion of this trade-off and potential avenues for scalable optimization of the Norm-POEM objective would be a valuable addition.

### **Arguments For and Against Acceptance**

*   **Arguments for acceptance:**
    *   The paper identifies and solves a significant, practical problem in the important area of learning from logged bandit data.
    *   The proposed solution is simple, well-motivated, and demonstrates strong, consistent empirical improvements over a state-of-the-art baseline.
    *   The work is of high quality, clearly written, and the claims are well-supported by both theory and experiments.

*   **Arguments against acceptance:**
    *   The core technical contribution could be viewed as incremental, as it involves substituting a well-known statistical estimator into an existing learning framework. However, the insight into *why* this substitution is necessary and effective is novel and important.

### **Conclusion**

Overall, this is a strong and well-executed paper. It makes a clear and valuable contribution by identifying the problem of propensity overfitting and providing an effective, principled solution. The experimental results are convincing and demonstrate a substantial improvement in robustness and generalization performance. Despite the somewhat narrow framing, the insights are likely to be impactful for the broader field of off-policy evaluation and learning. I recommend acceptance.
