- Paper studies batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF) and identifies propensity overfitting when using IPS, proposing SNIPS and Norm-POEM for CRM with structured prediction.
- Builds on BLBF/CRM, importance weighting under covariate shift, and off-policy evaluation; novelty is diagnosing propensity overfitting and advocating SNIPS as a control variate.
- Strength: clearly identifies a practical failure mode (propensity overfitting) with convincing thought experiment and S(h) diagnostic.
- Strength: principled estimator choice—SNIPS reduces sensitivity to propensity variability, is bounded and translation-equivariant in finite samples.
- Strength: provides strong consistency for SNIPS and an approximate variance estimate enabling CRM-style variance regularization.
- Strength: empirical gains—Norm-POEM consistently outperforms POEM across datasets, sizes, and loss translations with competitive runtimes.
- Strength: practical and simple to implement (plug-in estimator swap) with applicability beyond structured multilabel tasks.
- Weakness: lacks finite-sample generalization bounds for CRM with SNIPS analogous to IPS-based CRM bounds.
- Weakness: variance estimate relies on Normal/delta-method approximations; needs empirical calibration or ablations comparing variance estimators.
- Weakness: baseline comparisons are limited—missing doubly robust (DR), weighted/optimized DR, and carefully tuned clipped-IPS.
- Weakness: assumes full support (nonzero propensities); needs discussion of practical logging strategies and sensitivity to very small propensities.
- Weakness: experimental scope limited to supervised-to-bandit protocol; would benefit from at least one real logged-bandit dataset and reporting variance (std. devs or CIs).
- Weakness: exposition should clarify IPS non-equivariance is a finite-sample ERM issue (expectation remains unbiased) to avoid conceptual confusion.
- Quality: technical content is sound; analysis correctly notes bias-variance trade-off of self-normalization though approximate variance formulas need more justification.
- Clarity: paper is mostly clear and organized but should define S(h) early, give proof sketches for variance/asymptotic claims, and fix minor typos/style issues.
- Originality: applying SNIPS centrally within CRM and explicitly diagnosing propensity overfitting is novel and useful relative to prior work.
- Significance: likely to influence counterfactual learning practice—SNIPS is simple, robust, and improves structured prediction performance.
- Pros for acceptance: addresses an underappreciated IPS failure mode, offers a simple principled remedy, shows consistent empirical improvements, and has strong conceptual grounding.
- Cons for acceptance: missing finite-sample guarantees, limited baselines/real logs, and insufficient analysis of variance estimation and hyperparameter sensitivity.
- Key suggestions: clarify finite-sample nature of IPS non-equivariance and connect to ERM; include DR/optimized DR baselines and sensitivity analyses for clipping/λ.
- Key suggestions: report variability (std. devs/CIs), add at least one real logged-bandit dataset if possible, and show S(h) diagnostics during training.
- Key suggestions: discuss logging-policy requirements to ensure full support and how tiny propensities affect SNIPS vs. IPS.
- Key suggestions: consider partial or conditional CRM generalization bounds for ratio estimators and check code-link anonymity for double-blind review.
- Overall recommendation: Accept—important issue identified, principled practical remedy with consistent empirical benefits; would be stronger with the above clarifications and additional baselines.
