

This paper identifies a critical flaw in the conventional counterfactual risk estimator used for batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF), where the estimator suffers from a previously unrecognized form of overfitting termed "propensity overfitting." The authors demonstrate that when using the standard unbiased estimator for Empirical Risk Minimization (ERM) in BLBF, the risk estimate can become severely biased for complex hypothesis spaces—producing estimates that are arbitrarily lower (or higher) than the true risk, even when the loss function is bounded. They propose a self-normalized risk estimator that avoids this problem by incorporating multiplicative control variates, leading to a new learning algorithm called Norm-POEM for structured output prediction.

The paper builds upon the Counterfactual Risk Minimization (CRM) framework established in prior NIPS work (Swaminathan & Joachims, ICML 2015), but makes a significant conceptual contribution by identifying propensity overfitting as a distinct problem from conventional loss overfitting in supervised learning. The authors provide a thorough theoretical analysis showing that the standard estimator is not equivariant (i.e., translating the loss function by a constant doesn't translate the estimator by the same amount), which enables the problematic overfitting behavior. They prove that their self-normalized estimator resolves all these anomalies—it is equivariant, bounded within the loss range, and strongly consistent.

The experimental evaluation is particularly convincing. Using the Supervised 7→Bandit methodology on standard multi-label classification datasets, the authors demonstrate that Norm-POEM consistently outperforms POEM (the conventional approach) across multiple datasets and training sample sizes. The key evidence for the theoretical claims comes from Table 2, which shows that Norm-POEM's propensity weight sum Ŝ(h) is close to its expected value of 1, while POEM's estimate is severely biased. Furthermore, as predicted by theory, Norm-POEM remains unaffected by loss translations, while POEM's performance degrades catastrophically (e.g., on the TMC dataset, POEM performs worse than random guessing). The paper also provides a clear explanation of why Norm-POEM converges faster than POEM—because its risk estimates are bounded, preventing the need for excessively large weight magnitudes.

The paper is exceptionally well-written and structured. The authors clearly define the problem, systematically analyze the limitations of existing approaches, derive their solution, and provide thorough empirical validation. The related work section effectively contextualizes the contribution within the literature on counterfactual learning and importance sampling, properly acknowledging connections to control variates and self-normalized estimators in the importance sampling literature.

Strengths:
1. The identification of propensity overfitting as a novel and significant problem represents a substantial conceptual contribution to the BLBF literature.
2. The theoretical analysis is rigorous and clearly demonstrates how the self-normalized estimator resolves multiple anomalies of the standard approach.
3. The experimental results are comprehensive, well-designed, and provide compelling evidence for the practical benefits of the proposed method.
4. The paper is exceptionally clear and well-organized, making complex technical concepts accessible to readers.

Weaknesses:
1. The comparison with related work on variance reduction techniques (e.g., doubly robust estimators) could be more detailed, though the authors do acknowledge connections to additive control variates.
2. The computational complexity discussion of the self-normalized variance estimator is brief, though the authors do note that optimization still converges efficiently despite the increased per-iteration cost.

The significance of this work is high, as BLBF is crucial for improving interactive systems (e.g., ad placement, recommendation systems) using historical logs. The paper provides a practical solution to a previously overlooked problem, with clear benefits for practitioners. The authors' claim that the self-normalized estimator "neatly avoids" propensity overfitting is well-supported by both their theoretical analysis and empirical results.

Pro: Novel problem identification, strong theoretical foundation, compelling empirical validation, clear writing, practical significance.
Con: Minor limitations in comparison with related work, brief discussion of computational complexity.

This is a strong contribution to the field that addresses a significant gap in the BLBF literature. The paper demonstrates both theoretical depth and practical impact, with results that are likely to be widely adopted in future work on counterfactual learning. The authors have successfully identified a fundamental limitation of existing approaches and provided an elegant solution that improves both theoretical guarantees and empirical performance. The paper should be accepted for publication at NIPS.
