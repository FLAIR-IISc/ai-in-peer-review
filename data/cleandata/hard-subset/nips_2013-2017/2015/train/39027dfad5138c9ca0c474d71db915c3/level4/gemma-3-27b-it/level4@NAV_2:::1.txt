This paper identifies a significant problem – propensity overfitting – in the commonly used counterfactual risk estimator for batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF), and proposes a self-normalized estimator to mitigate this issue. The core idea builds upon the counterfactual risk minimization (CRM) principle [1] and addresses a critical limitation of existing BLBF algorithms. By framing BLBF as a causal inference problem, the authors connect it to related areas like off-policy evaluation in reinforcement learning [4] and domain adaptation [8], correctly positioning their work within the broader research landscape. They highlight that while importance sampling techniques are prevalent in these areas, they can be susceptible to overfitting, specifically a novel form they term “propensity overfitting,” distinct from traditional loss overfitting. Their proposed solution, a self-normalized estimator, leverages control variates [16, 17] to achieve a more stable and robust risk estimation. This leads to a new learning algorithm, Norm-POEM, which is evaluated on multi-label classification problems and demonstrated to consistently outperform the conventional estimator.

The paper’s strength lies in its clear identification of a subtle but important problem with existing BLBF methods. The explanation of propensity overfitting, illustrated with a compelling example (Section 4), is particularly insightful. The theoretical justification for the self-normalized estimator, connecting it to established results in importance sampling, is solid. The empirical evaluation is well-conducted, using a standard experimental setup [2, 1] and demonstrating consistent improvements over POEM. The availability of code further enhances the reproducibility and impact of the work. The paper’s clarity is commendable; the authors present a complex topic in a well-organized and understandable manner. The connection to existing literature is thorough, and the discussion of related work is relevant and insightful.

However, while the paper convincingly demonstrates the benefits of the self-normalized estimator, the relationship between propensity overfitting and the high variance of importance weights could be explored in more detail. The authors acknowledge this connection but a more explicit discussion would strengthen the paper. Furthermore, while the example in Section 4 is illustrative, it relies on specific conditions (distinct xi values). Expanding the discussion to consider more realistic scenarios and demonstrating the robustness of the phenomenon would be beneficial. The claim that prior works haven't considered techniques like clipping (e.g., [15, 5, 11]) to address variance requires further clarification – many do utilize such methods. Finally, while the runtime comparison is encouraging, a more detailed computational analysis, particularly in relation to the cost of computing the partition function Z(x) in Norm-POEM, would be valuable.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Addresses a significant and previously unrecognised problem in BLBF.
*   Proposes a theoretically sound and empirically validated solution.
*   Clear and well-written presentation of complex concepts.
*   Thorough connection to related work.
*   Availability of code promotes reproducibility and impact.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Relationship between propensity overfitting and high variance could be more thoroughly explained.
*   Example illustrating propensity overfitting relies on specific conditions.
*   Claim about prior work’s use of clipping needs clarification.
*   More detailed computational analysis would be beneficial.

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of learning from logged bandit feedback. Despite minor areas for improvement, the novelty of the identified problem, the soundness of the proposed solution, and the clear presentation justify acceptance. I recommend accepting this paper for presentation at NIPS.
