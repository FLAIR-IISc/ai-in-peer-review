### Summary

This paper addresses the problem of batch learning from logged bandit feedback, a setting highly relevant to applications such as ad ranking. The authors identify an "overfitting" issue in the recently proposed Counterfactual Risk Minimization (CRM) principle and propose a solution based on multiplicative control variates. This modification results in a biased but empirically more stable estimator.

### Quality

The paper is of high quality and is well-motivated. However, as this is follow-up work to [1], the paper would be strengthened by more extensive experiments and a deeper analysis of the optimization of the training objective, expanding on the brief discussion around line 415.

### Clarity

The presentation is clear, and the paper is well-written.

### Originality

While this is follow-up work on [1], it introduces a novel modification to the risk estimator by making it self-normalizing. This change appears to yield significant performance improvements in the authors' experiments.

### Significance

The problem of learning from logged data is important and relevant. However, it remains an open question whether the substantial experimental improvements shown here will translate to similar gains in real-world applications.

### Various Remarks

Overall, this is a solid, well-written paper presenting a relevant and novel contribution. The authors correctly identify a weakness in the Counterfactual Risk Minimization (CRM) principle and propose a compelling solution.

My main suggestions for improvement concern the experimental evaluation. As follow-up work, the paper would be more convincing with an expanded set of experiments that go beyond the applications presented in [1]. In particular, including results from real-world datasets would significantly strengthen the paper's claims about the method's practical utility.

Additionally, a minor but important point regarding the current results (line 365): given that experiments were run with several repetitions, the plots and tables should include measures of variance (e.g., error bars or standard deviations) to allow readers to properly assess the stability and statistical significance of the reported improvements.
