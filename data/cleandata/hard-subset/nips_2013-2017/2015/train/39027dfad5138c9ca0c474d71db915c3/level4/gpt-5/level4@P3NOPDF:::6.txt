Summary
The paper studies counterfactual risk minimization for learning from logged bandit feedback. It highlights a key failure mode of standard inverse propensity scoring (IPS): although IPS is unbiased and consistent, empirical risk minimization with IPS can overfit by exploiting examples with small logging probabilities, leading to high-variance estimates and poor generalization. To address this, the authors propose a self-normalized (multiplicative) estimator together with a variance-control mechanism that bounds the estimator’s variance while preserving consistency. Empirically, the normalized counterfactual approach outperforms the non-normalized baseline.

Strengths
- Clear problem motivation: The paper articulates why naive IPS-based learning can overfit via propensity-weighted examples, a practical issue in logged bandit settings.
- Principled estimator: The proposed self-normalized estimator reduces variance by normalizing the propensity weights, mitigating the impact of rare actions without introducing significant bias. The multiplicative variance-control parameter further stabilizes learning.
- Theoretical grounding: The work argues that the proposed procedure maintains consistency while reducing overfitting risks tied to small propensities. The emphasis on variance control is well justified.
- Empirical evidence: Experiments show consistent gains of the normalized approach over the non-normalized counterpart, supporting the claim that variance control improves learning from logged data.

Weaknesses and questions
- Clarity of the estimator and objective: While the paper motivates self-normalization well, it would benefit from a more explicit presentation of the objective being optimized (e.g., the exact form of the normalized risk and the role of the multiplicative parameter in the training criterion). Making the connection between the estimator, its variance, and the learning algorithm fully explicit would help readers reproduce the approach.
- Assumptions on logging policy: The approach relies on accurate propensities and positivity (nonzero support). The paper should more clearly state these assumptions and discuss robustness to misspecified propensities.
- Bias-variance trade-off: Self-normalization can introduce small bias while reducing variance. The paper asserts consistency, but a more detailed discussion of finite-sample bias and how it scales with normalization would be valuable. Similarly, guidance on selecting the multiplicative parameter (and its sensitivity) would help practitioners.
- Experimental scope: While the reported results are promising, it would be useful to see a broader set of tasks, ablations (e.g., varying the distribution of propensities and the frequency of rare actions), and sensitivity analyses for the normalization/variance-control parameter.
- Relation to prior estimators: The paper could better situate the contribution relative to existing counterfactual estimators (e.g., standard IPS, clipped IPS, self-normalized IPS, and related variance-regularized objectives) to clarify novelty and the conceptual differences between normalization and alternative variance-reduction techniques.

Quality
The problem is important and the proposed solution is sound and relevant to practical counterfactual learning. The argument that normalization curbs overfitting to rare actions is compelling, and the methodology is consistent with established techniques for variance reduction. The empirical results align with the theory.

Clarity
Overall well written, but some derivations and definitions would benefit from being made more explicit, especially:
- A clear definition of the normalized risk estimator and its unbiasedness/consistency properties.
- The exact training objective, including the multiplicative variance-control term, and how it is optimized.
- A concise statement of assumptions on the logging policy and data collection process.

Impact
This work addresses a real and recurring issue in logged bandit learning. The proposed approach offers a practical, theoretically motivated improvement that should be broadly useful in counterfactual policy learning and off-policy evaluation/optimization.

Recommendation
Weak accept. The paper tackles an important problem, provides a principled solution that maintains consistency while controlling variance, and demonstrates empirical benefits over a strong baseline. With clearer exposition of the estimator, assumptions, and parameter choices, the paper would be even stronger, but in its current form it makes a meaningful and timely contribution.

Minor comments and suggestions
- Provide pseudocode or a concise algorithmic description of the training procedure, including normalization and the variance-control parameter.
- Include a brief discussion on how to tune the multiplicative parameter (e.g., via validation on counterfactual estimates or proxy metrics) and report its sensitivity.
- Report confidence intervals or statistical tests in the experiments to substantiate improvements.
- Add a short section contrasting normalization with clipping and other variance-reduction strategies, and clarify when each is preferable.
