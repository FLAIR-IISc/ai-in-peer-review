This paper identifies a significant problem – propensity overfitting – in the commonly used counterfactual risk estimator for batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF), and proposes a self-normalized estimator to mitigate this issue. BLBF is a crucial setting for leveraging historical data from online systems (e.g., ad placement, recommendation) where full-information feedback is unavailable, relying instead on data generated by a previous policy. The core idea builds on counterfactual reasoning, attempting to estimate the performance of a new policy using data from an existing one. Existing methods, while unbiased, can suffer from high variance, and the authors demonstrate a further issue: even with unbiased estimation, the estimator can be misleadingly optimistic due to overfitting to the propensity scores, particularly when there is limited exploration of the action space. The proposed self-normalized estimator addresses this by incorporating control variates, leading to a new algorithm, Norm-POEM, which is empirically shown to outperform the conventional estimator on multi-label classification tasks.

The paper’s connection to prior work is well-established, drawing links to causal inference, covariate shift adaptation, and off-policy evaluation in reinforcement learning. It appropriately cites key papers in these areas [1, 2, 4, 7, 8]. The authors correctly position their contribution as extending beyond simply addressing high variance, and highlighting a distinct overfitting phenomenon. Their analysis builds on existing importance sampling techniques [11, 12, 15] and variance reduction methods [6, 16, 17].

**Strengths:**

*   **Identifies a Novel Problem:** The concept of “propensity overfitting” is a valuable contribution. While high variance in importance sampling is well-known, the authors highlight a subtle but critical issue related to the interaction between estimator bias and the hypothesis space capacity.
*   **Sound Theoretical Motivation:** The derivation of the self-normalized estimator, leveraging control variates, appears well-reasoned and grounded in established statistical principles. The arguments for why the estimator avoids the identified overfitting problem are convincing.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The experimental results consistently demonstrate the superior performance of Norm-POEM across multiple datasets. The comparison against POEM [1] provides a strong baseline. The additional analysis showcasing the impact on Ŝ(ĥ) – demonstrating how the estimator stabilizes – is particularly insightful.
*   **Practical Considerations:** The authors acknowledge the computational cost and discuss the practical implementation details, including hyperparameter tuning and optimization techniques. The availability of code is a significant positive.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Relationship to Variance Reduction:** While the authors acknowledge the connection to variance reduction techniques, a more in-depth discussion of *how* propensity overfitting relates to, and differs from, standard variance issues would be helpful. Clarifying this distinction could further strengthen the paper’s contribution.
*   **Example Clarification:** The example in Section 4, while illustrative, could benefit from a more detailed explanation of why R̂(hoverfit) becomes so negative. The current explanation relies on the reader filling in some gaps. Specifically, clarifying why having distinct xi is crucial would be valuable.
*   **Fairness of Prior Work Citation:** The statement regarding prior works not using techniques like clipping is potentially overstated. A more nuanced discussion of different strategies employed for variance reduction in previous studies would be beneficial.
*  **Limited Scope of Experiments:** While the results are compelling, the experiments focus on a specific set of multi-label classification problems. Exploring the performance of Norm-POEM in other BLBF scenarios or with different data distributions would further enhance the generalizability of the findings.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper addresses an important and previously unrecognized problem in batch learning from logged bandit feedback.
*   The proposed solution is theoretically sound and empirically validated.
*   The results demonstrate a clear improvement over existing methods.
*   The paper is well-written and clearly explains the key concepts.
*   The availability of code facilitates reproducibility and further research.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The connection between propensity overfitting and standard variance issues could be clarified further.
*   The example in Section 4 requires a more detailed explanation.
*   The experimental evaluation could be broadened to demonstrate broader applicability.

**Overall Assessment:**

Despite a few minor weaknesses, this is a strong paper with a significant contribution to the field of batch learning from logged bandit feedback. The authors identify a novel problem, propose a principled solution, and provide compelling empirical evidence to support their claims. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions to address the points mentioned above regarding clarification of the relationship to variance reduction and the example in Section 4. The potential impact of this work is substantial, and it is likely to be of significant interest to researchers and practitioners working in this area.
