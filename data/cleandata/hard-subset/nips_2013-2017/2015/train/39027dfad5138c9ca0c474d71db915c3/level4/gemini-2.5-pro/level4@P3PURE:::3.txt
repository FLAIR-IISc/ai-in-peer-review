### Review

**Summary**

This paper studies the problem of batch learning from logged bandit feedback, a setting highly relevant to applications such as ad ranking. The authors identify an "overfitting" problem in the recently proposed Counterfactual Risk Minimization (CRM) principle and propose a solution based on multiplicative control variates. This modification results in a new, albeit biased, self-normalizing risk estimator.

**Quality and Clarity**

The paper is of high quality, well-motivated, and clearly written. The presentation is easy to follow, and the arguments are well-articulated.

**Originality and Significance**

This work is a follow-up to the research presented in [1]. Its primary contribution is the modification of the CRM risk estimator to be self-normalizing, a change that appears to make a significant difference in the experiments studied by the authors. The problem of learning from logged data is important and relevant; however, it is difficult to assess from the current experiments how the substantial improvements reported here will translate to more complex, real-world applications.

**Detailed Comments and Suggestions for Improvement**

Overall, this is a solid paper that presents a relevant and novel contribution. The core idea is well-executed, and the results are promising. However, the work could be strengthened in a few key areas:

*   **Expanded Experiments:** As this is follow-up work on [1], the paper would be more convincing with a broader set of experiments. I would have appreciated seeing the method evaluated on additional applications beyond those already explored in [1], particularly including some actual real-world datasets. This would help demonstrate the generalizability of the proposed solution.
*   **Analysis of Optimization:** The paper would benefit from a more detailed analysis of the optimization of the training objective, expanding on the discussion currently found in the paragraph on line 415.
*   **Reporting Variance:** On line 365, it is mentioned that several repetitions were run for the experiments. Please include a measure of variance (e.g., standard deviation or error bars) in the results. This is crucial for understanding the stability and statistical significance of the reported improvements.
