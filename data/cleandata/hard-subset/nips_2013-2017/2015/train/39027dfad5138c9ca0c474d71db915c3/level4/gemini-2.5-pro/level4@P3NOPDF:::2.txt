This paper presents Norm-POEM, a novel approach for "counterfactual learning," a scenario that can be viewed as a specialization of multi-label learning with bandit feedback. In this setting, instead of observing a full vector of 0/1 labels for each class, the learner only receives feedback for a single class per sample. The paper's primary contribution is to improve upon the POEM estimator, which was recently presented by the authors of reference [1]. The experimental results are strong, showing that Norm-POEM consistently outperforms the original POEM estimator across all datasets and performs competitively against a CRF model that uses full multi-label supervision.

While the empirical results are convincing, the paper's theoretical motivation for the proposed estimator is a significant point of concern. The core argument hinges on an alleged "anomaly" in the POEM estimator defined in Equation (2). Specifically, the authors claim that the estimator is flawed because it is not invariant to a translation of the loss function. This claim appears to be technically incorrect.

An expectation is generally defined as E[f(x)] = ∫ f(x) p(x) dx, where f(x) is the quantity whose expectation is being taken, and p(x) is the probability measure. The property of translation invariance applies to the expected quantity, f(x). However, in the formulation of Equation (2), the loss term is an integral part of the probability measure itself, not the quantity being averaged. As such, the expectation is taken over a constant value (one), weighted by a probability distribution that is dependent on the loss. Therefore, the argument that the estimator should be invariant to a translation of the loss function is not applicable in this context and seems to stem from a misinterpretation of the estimator's structure.

This foundational issue invalidates the subsequent argument, including the conclusions drawn from Example 1, which is constructed specifically to illustrate this supposed anomaly. The central theoretical premise used to motivate the new Norm-POEM estimator is therefore unconvincing.

Despite the flawed theoretical justification, the proposed Norm-POEM estimator in Equation (7) is well-motivated from a practical standpoint. A key weakness of the original estimator in Equation (2) is its sensitivity to the denominator term, `pi` (the propensity score), which can be zero or near-zero if a sample has not been observed under the logging policy. This can lead to high variance or instability. The modified estimator in Equation (7) introduces a normalization that effectively mollifies the influence of small `pi` values through a "double denominator" structure. This provides a much more compelling and sound justification for the proposed method, as it directly addresses a known practical weakness of inverse propensity scoring estimators.

In summary, this paper introduces an empirically successful and practically well-founded modification to the POEM estimator. However, the theoretical justification provided for this modification is fundamentally flawed. The paper would be significantly strengthened if the authors were to replace the current motivation based on the "anomaly" of loss translation with the more robust and practical justification regarding estimator stability.

Given the strong empirical results, the paper has the potential for publication. However, this is contingent on a major revision of the paper's core theoretical argument to accurately reflect the true benefits of the proposed normalization.
