

Due to travel commitments, I was able to complete only a brief supplementary review of this paper. I regret that I couldn’t dedicate more time to it, as I found the work genuinely interesting. If I’ve overlooked or misinterpreted any points, I apologize in advance.  

The paper addresses a scenario where, given observed data under a hypothesis, one seeks to evaluate a different hypothesis—particularly when these hypotheses are non-deterministic (e.g., conditional distributions). The authors note that the loss under the target hypothesis can be estimated via data reweighting (see the equation between (1) and (2)).  

However, the problem tackled—termed "propensity overfitting"—appears related but distinct from the well-known issue of high variance in importance weights. The authors demonstrate that propensity overfitting arises when exploration is insufficient: for a given input $x$, there are very few or no observed $y$ values corresponding to the hypothesis being evaluated. Since this mirrors the conditions causing high weight variance, the relationship between these two phenomena warrants clearer explanation.  

I did not have sufficient time to rigorously verify the technical soundness or novelty of the proposed solution, though its core idea appears plausible.  

Specific concerns:  
- **Line 199**: If $n$ is small relative to $k$, we observe few data points with $x_i = y_i$. In this case, $\hat{R}(\hat{h})$ should be even smaller than $-2$, not larger. Am I misinterpreting the setup?  
- **Citation claim**: The statement that prior works on BLBF [4, 5, 1] use an "unbiased counterfactual risk estimator" seems questionable. While I haven’t examined all three papers, I suspect they employ techniques like clipping to reduce variance, which inherently introduces bias. The authors should clarify this point.  

The availability of code is appreciated.  

In summary, I was unable to fully assess the paper’s technical validity. Nevertheless, the ideas presented are intriguing and merit further consideration. I recommend acceptance.
