- Addresses propensity overfitting in batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF), where conventional counterfactual risk estimators can give arbitrarily low (misleading) risk estimates with expressive hypothesis spaces.  
- Identifies that standard estimators can understate true risk due to propensity overfitting, a previously uncharacterized failure mode in BLBF.  
- Proposes a self-normalized risk estimator using multiplicative control variates to produce bounded, translation-equivariant risk estimates.  
- Develops Norm-POEM, a learning algorithm that integrates the self-normalized estimator into the CRM framework.  
- Empirical results show consistent performance improvements of Norm-POEM over POEM on multiple multi-label classification datasets.  
- Builds on Swaminathan & Joachims (2015) CRM foundation but uncovers a new estimator-specific overfitting issue not previously recognized.  
- Originality rated high (8/10): identifying propensity overfitting and the self-normalized solution is novel and well-justified.  
- Significance rated high (8/10): problem is practically important for systems using logged bandit data; empirical gains support practical relevance.  
- Quality rated moderate (6/10): solid theoretical proofs and thorough experiments, but some explanatory gaps remain.  
- Clarity issues: unclear whether loss must be scaled to [-1,0] as in prior CRM work; Table 1 seems to contradict theoretical assumptions.  
- Methodological concern: treats feedback δ(x,y) as deterministic, whereas real feedback is often stochastic—risk definition should include expectation over feedback noise.  
- Notational inconsistencies: several minor errors (e.g., ratio vs. product, argmax vs. argmin, hat vs. bar for estimates) need correction.  
- Recommendation: accept—important contribution with manageable revisions addressing clarity and modeling of stochastic feedback.  
- Pros summary: novel problem identification, strong theory, consistent empirical improvements, practical relevance.  
- Cons summary: minor notation issues, unclear loss-scaling requirements, and treating feedback as deterministic rather than random.
