

Counterfactual risk minimization (CRM) addresses the challenge of learning from logged bandit data by correcting for selection bias introduced during the logging process. While CRM is theoretically consistent, the paper identifies a critical overfitting issue: hypothesis classes can exploit the logging distribution's generation probabilities, leading to poor generalization. Specifically, models may overfit by assigning excessive weight to logged examples with high logging probabilities, rather than learning the true reward structure.  

The authors propose a normalized CRM approach that introduces a multiplicative parameter to bound the estimator's variance while preserving consistency. This normalization explicitly accounts for the logging distribution's influence, preventing the hypothesis from disproportionately favoring logged examples. The paper rigorously analyzes the theoretical properties of this normalized estimator and demonstrates its superiority over the standard CRM formulation.  

Empirical evaluations across multiple bandit datasets confirm that the normalized CRM technique consistently outperforms the non-normalized variant. The results show significant improvements in both accuracy and robustness, particularly in settings with sparse logging or skewed action distributions. The experimental design is thorough, including ablation studies that isolate the impact of normalization and comparisons against relevant baselines.  

The paper makes a clear, well-motivated contribution to the literature on offline bandit learning. The identification of the overfitting mechanism and the elegant solution via normalization are both novel and practically significant. The theoretical analysis is sound, and the experimental validation strongly supports the proposed method. While the core idea builds on existing CRM frameworks, the normalization approach provides a simple yet effective fix for a previously overlooked practical limitation.  

This work is well-written, technically solid, and addresses a meaningful gap in the literature. The proposed method is immediately applicable to real-world scenarios where logged bandit data is the only available resource, such as recommendation systems or online advertising. Given the increasing prevalence of offline learning settings, this contribution is timely and impactful.  

The paper should be accepted. It offers a clear, practical improvement to a foundational technique with strong theoretical backing and empirical validation. The minor concerns (e.g., limited exploration of hyperparameter sensitivity in experiments) do not detract from the overall strength of the contribution. The normalized CRM framework provides a valuable tool for practitioners and researchers alike, and the results are sufficiently compelling to warrant publication at NIPS.
