This paper addresses a critical problem in batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF): propensity overfitting in the commonly used counterfactual risk estimator. Building upon the foundation laid by Swaminathan & Joachims (2015), the authors propose a self-normalized estimator that demonstrably mitigates this issue and leads to a significantly improved learning algorithm, Norm-POEM.

The core idea is to replace the conventional estimator with a multiplicative control variate, resulting in a risk estimate that is both more stable and equivariant – a key advantage highlighted in the paper. This theoretical insight translates directly into empirical gains, as demonstrated through extensive experimentation on multi-label classification problems. Norm-POEM consistently outperforms the original POEM algorithm, achieving better generalization performance and, surprisingly, often exhibiting faster convergence during training. 

**Quality:** The paper presents a technically sound contribution. The analysis of propensity overfitting is insightful, and the proposed self-normalized estimator is well-motivated and rigorously justified. The empirical evaluation is thorough, employing a standard experimental setup (following Swaminathan & Joachims, 2015) and demonstrating consistent improvements across multiple datasets. The theoretical grounding, including Theorem 1 and Theorem 2, provides a strong foundation for the proposed method.

**Clarity:** The paper is, for the most part, clearly written and well-organized. The authors effectively explain the problem of propensity overfitting and the motivation behind their proposed solution. The mathematical notation is generally accessible, and the flow of ideas is logical. While the level of detail in some sections (e.g., the derivation of the variance estimator) might be challenging for readers unfamiliar with the field, the authors provide sufficient context to understand the key concepts.

**Originality:** The contribution of this paper is significant. While building on existing work in BLBF and importance sampling, the identification and mitigation of propensity overfitting represent a novel and important advancement. The self-normalized estimator is not merely a minor tweak but a fundamental improvement to the standard approach, and the Norm-POEM algorithm effectively leverages this improvement. The exploration of equivariance as a desirable property of the risk estimator is particularly noteworthy.

**Significance:** The results presented in this paper are highly promising. The improvements in generalization performance and training speed demonstrated by Norm-POEM have the potential to significantly impact the practical application of BLBF techniques in areas such as ad placement, recommendation systems, and web search. The authors clearly articulate the relevance of their work and its potential for future research. The observation that Norm-POEM can be faster *despite* incorporating additional regularization is particularly impressive and warrants further investigation. 

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Addresses a significant and previously unrecognised problem (propensity overfitting) in BLBF.
*   Presents a theoretically sound and empirically validated solution (self-normalized estimator & Norm-POEM).
*   Demonstrates consistent performance gains over existing state-of-the-art methods (POEM).
*   The results suggest potential for broader impact in applications relying on learning from logged data.
*   The paper is well-written and clearly articulates the contributions.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The derivation of the variance estimator (Equation 8) may be difficult to follow for some readers.
*   While the experiments are thorough, exploring a wider range of datasets or application domains could further strengthen the conclusions.
*   The computational complexity of the algorithm, while promisingly faster than POEM, could benefit from a more detailed analysis.



**Overall Assessment:** This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of batch learning from logged bandit feedback. The authors have identified a critical problem, proposed an elegant solution, and provided compelling evidence of its effectiveness. I recommend acceptance with high confidence.
