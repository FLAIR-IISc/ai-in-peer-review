

Summary  
This paper addresses batch learning from logged bandit feedback, a critical challenge in applications like ad ranking. It identifies an overfitting issue in the Counterfactual Risk Minimization (CRM) principle and proposes a solution using multiplicative control variates, yielding a self-normalizing risk estimator (which introduces bias).  

Quality  
The work is high-quality, well-motivated, and builds logically on prior work [1]. As a follow-up, it would have been strengthened by additional experiments and deeper analysis of the training objective’s optimization—beyond the brief mention at line 415.  

Clarity  
The paper is exceptionally well-written, with a clear structure and precise technical exposition.  

Originality  
While extending [1], the core innovation lies in modifying the risk estimator to be self-normalizing. This change demonstrably improves empirical performance in the authors’ experiments, distinguishing it from prior approaches.  

Significance  
The problem of learning from logged bandit data is highly relevant. However, the substantial experimental gains reported require clearer justification for real-world applicability. The paper does not sufficiently bridge the gap between the controlled experiments and practical deployment challenges (e.g., in ad systems or recommendation engines), leaving the broader impact somewhat ambiguous.  

Major Comments  
1. **Experimental Rigor**: At line 365, the authors report mean results across multiple repetitions but omit variance metrics. Including standard deviations or confidence intervals would significantly strengthen the claims about statistical significance.  
2. **Practical Validation**: The experiments primarily replicate settings from [1]. To bolster significance, additional real-world applications (beyond synthetic or benchmark datasets) would be valuable. For instance, demonstrating the method on a publicly available ad-click dataset or a novel industry use case would better justify its practical utility.  
3. **Bias-Performance Trade-off**: The self-normalizing estimator’s bias is acknowledged but not thoroughly analyzed. A brief discussion of how this bias scales with dataset size or logging policies—alongside its empirical impact on regret—would address potential concerns about the estimator’s reliability.  

Minor Comments  
- Line 415: The optimization details are insufficient. Please elaborate on how the training objective is minimized (e.g., optimization algorithm, convergence criteria) and why the chosen approach is suitable for the biased estimator.  
- The term "overfitting" in the context of CRM could be clarified (e.g., specify whether it refers to overfitting to the logging policy or to noise in the logged data).  
- The introduction of multiplicative control variates is technically sound, but a concise intuition for *why* this choice resolves the overfitting issue (beyond the mathematical derivation) would aid readability.  

Overall, this is a solid contribution that advances the state of the art in batch bandit learning. The self-normalizing estimator represents a meaningful improvement, though the path to real-world adoption requires more concrete validation. With minor revisions addressing the points above—particularly adding variance metrics, expanding experimental scope, and clarifying the bias trade-off—the paper would be significantly strengthened.
