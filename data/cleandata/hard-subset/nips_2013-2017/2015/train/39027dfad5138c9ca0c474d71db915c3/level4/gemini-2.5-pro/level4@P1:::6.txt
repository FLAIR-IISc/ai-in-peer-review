This paper addresses a critical issue in batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF), a setting crucial for improving interactive systems using historical data. The authors identify a subtle but severe failure mode in the conventional inverse propensity score (IPS) estimator, which is the foundation of the Counterfactual Risk Minimization (CRM) principle. They term this issue "Propensity Overfitting," where a model with high capacity can achieve an artificially low estimated risk not by making better predictions, but by manipulating the importance weights to either over-represent or under-represent training examples. This can lead to degenerate solutions and poor generalization.

As a remedy, the authors propose replacing the standard IPS estimator with the self-normalized importance sampling estimator. This estimator, derived using a multiplicative control variate, is shown to be equivariant to shifts in the loss function and naturally constrains risk estimates to lie within the range of the observed losses, thus resolving the pathologies of the standard estimator. This leads to a new learning algorithm, the Normalized Policy Optimizer for Exponential Models (Norm-POEM). The paper provides a thorough empirical evaluation on several multi-label classification tasks, demonstrating that Norm-POEM consistently and significantly outperforms its predecessor (POEM), which uses the conventional estimator.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Significance and Impact:** The paper identifies and clearly formalizes a fundamental problem in a learning setting of high practical importance. Propensity overfitting is a genuine and previously under-appreciated risk in counterfactual learning. The demonstrated failures of the standard estimator are alarming, and the proposed solution provides a robust and principled alternative. This work is likely to be highly influential for researchers and practitioners working on off-policy evaluation and learning from logged data.

2.  **Clarity and Presentation:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow. The "Propensity Overfitting" problem is introduced with a simple yet powerful illustrative example (Example 1) that makes the core issue crystal clear. The motivation for using the self-normalized estimator is well-argued, connecting a classic technique from the statistics literature to a modern machine learning problem.

3.  **Technical Quality:** The proposed solution is elegant and theoretically sound. The authors correctly identify the desirable properties of the self-normalized estimator (equivariance, boundedness, consistency) that directly address the weaknesses of the standard IPS estimator. The derivation of the Norm-POEM algorithm and its variance estimator is a solid contribution.

4.  **Empirical Evaluation:** The experiments are thoughtfully designed and convincingly support the paper's claims. The authors not only show that Norm-POEM achieves better generalization performance (Table 1, Figure 1) but also provide direct evidence that it successfully mitigates propensity overfitting by inspecting the sum of importance weights and testing for robustness to loss translation (Table 2). This level of diagnostic evaluation adds significant credibility to the results.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Originality of the Estimator:** The primary technical tool, the self-normalized estimator, is a well-established method in the importance sampling literature. The main novelty lies in identifying its suitability for solving the propensity overfitting problem in the context of counterfactual *learning* and building a practical algorithm around it. While this is a valuable contribution, the core statistical tool itself is not new.

2.  **Optimization:** The paper notes that the training objective for Norm-POEM is non-convex and is optimized using L-BFGS. While this is a pragmatic choice, a brief discussion of the optimization landscape or potential challenges like local minima would have been beneficial. The empirical observation that Norm-POEM converges faster than POEM is interesting but remains an observation without deeper analysis.

**Recommendation:**

This is an excellent paper that makes a clear, significant, and well-supported contribution. It tackles a subtle but critical problem in counterfactual learning, provides an elegant and effective solution, and validates its claims with a strong set of experiments. The weaknesses are minor and do not detract from the overall quality and impact of the work. The paper is well-written, technically solid, and addresses a topic of considerable interest to the NIPS community. I strongly recommend acceptance.
