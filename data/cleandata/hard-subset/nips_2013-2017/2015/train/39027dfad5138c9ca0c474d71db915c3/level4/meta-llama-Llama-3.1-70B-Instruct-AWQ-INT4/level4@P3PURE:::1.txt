When evaluating a hypothesis based on observed data, it is often desirable to assess a different hypothesis. In cases where these hypotheses are non-deterministic (e.g., conditional distributions), estimating the loss under the hypothesis in question can be achieved by reweighting data points, as illustrated between equations (1) and (2).

The authors appear to address a problem related to, yet distinct from, the issue of large variance in weights. They term this problem "propensity overfitting." The provided examples suggest that this issue arises when there is insufficient exploration, resulting in only a few or even no observations of y for a given x that correspond to the new hypothesis being evaluated. A similar argument explains the large variance of weights, so a more detailed explanation of the relationship between these two problems would be beneficial.

Due to time constraints, I was unable to thoroughly examine the details or assess the novelty of the proposed solution. However, the underlying idea seems sensible.
