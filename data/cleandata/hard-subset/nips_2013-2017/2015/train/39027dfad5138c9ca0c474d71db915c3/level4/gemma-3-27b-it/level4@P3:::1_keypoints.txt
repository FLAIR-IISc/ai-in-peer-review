- Identifies propensity overfitting in counterfactual risk estimation for batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF).
- Shows the commonly used unbiased estimator suffers from propensity overfitting, especially with complex hypothesis spaces.
- Proposes a self-normalized estimator to mitigate propensity overfitting.
- Instantiates the estimator in a new algorithm, Norm-POEM, with consistent performance gains on multi-label classification tasks.
- Builds on the Counterfactual Risk Minimization (CRM) framework and connects to causal inference, off-policy RL, and domain adaptation.
- Introduction is well-motivated and the paper is appropriately positioned within related work.
- Provides theoretical justification and a consistency guarantee for the self-normalized estimator (Theorem 2).
- Empirical results show Norm-POEM outperforms existing methods and a full-information CRF benchmark.
- Analysis demonstrates improved robustness and reduced sensitivity to loss translation.
- Addresses practical concerns: acknowledges computational cost but shows improved convergence; code is available.
- Paper is clearly written with effective examples (e.g., Example 1) illustrating propensity overfitting.
- Weakness: discussion of relationship between propensity overfitting and existing variance-reduction techniques (e.g., clipping) is insufficiently nuanced.
- Weakness: needs clarification of the example on lines 199–201 (why R̂(hoverfit) would be smaller than -2).
- Weakness: statement that prior work does not employ clipping is potentially inaccurate and should be rephrased.
- Weakness: limited exploration of hyperparameter sensitivity despite calibration on a hold-out set.
- Arguments for acceptance: identifies a critical, previously unrecognized problem; solution is theoretically sound and empirically validated; strong writing and available code.
- Arguments against acceptance: incomplete connection to traditional variance issues, minor clarifications needed, and an overstated claim about prior use of clipping.
- Overall assessment: strong paper with a significant contribution; recommendation to accept despite minor issues.
