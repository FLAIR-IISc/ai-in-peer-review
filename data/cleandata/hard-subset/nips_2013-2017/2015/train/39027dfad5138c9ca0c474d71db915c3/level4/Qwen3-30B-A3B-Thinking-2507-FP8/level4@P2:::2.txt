

The paper identifies a critical issue with the conventional counterfactual risk estimator used in batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF), proposing a self-normalized estimator that resolves this problem. The authors demonstrate that the standard inverse propensity estimator (Equation 2) suffers from "propensity overfitting" when used in empirical risk minimization (ERM), where the risk estimate can become arbitrarily biased for certain hypothesis spaces. They introduce a self-normalized estimator (Equation 7) that avoids this issue by incorporating multiplicative control variates, and develop a new learning algorithm called Norm-POEM that implements this estimator within the Counterfactual Risk Minimization (CRM) framework.

The paper's main contribution is significant as it addresses a previously overlooked problem that can lead to severely degraded performance in BLBF applications. The authors provide a thorough theoretical analysis of the propensity overfitting problem, including a compelling example (Example 1) that demonstrates how the conventional estimator can produce risk estimates arbitrarily lower than the true minimum risk. They prove that the self-normalized estimator is equivariant to loss translations and bounded within the range of the loss function, properties that the conventional estimator lacks. The empirical evaluation across multiple multi-label classification datasets consistently shows that Norm-POEM outperforms the conventional POEM algorithm, with results approaching those of a full-information CRF.

The paper excels in quality, providing a technically sound analysis with clear theoretical guarantees (Theorems 1 and 2) and thorough empirical validation. The authors carefully distinguish their work from prior literature, demonstrating how their self-normalized estimator resolves issues that thresholding schemes and variance regularization alone cannot address. The experimental methodology is well-designed, using the Supervised 7→ Bandit approach to create controlled bandit feedback datasets from standard multi-label classification data.

In terms of clarity, the paper is exceptionally well-written, with a logical flow from problem identification through theoretical analysis to empirical validation. The authors clearly define all necessary concepts and notation, making the technical content accessible to the NIPS audience. The experimental results are presented with appropriate metrics (Hamming loss) and sufficient detail to assess the significance of the improvements.

Regarding originality, the paper makes a novel contribution by identifying and solving the propensity overfitting problem, which was not previously recognized in the BLBF literature. While the CRM framework and POEM algorithm build on prior work (notably [1]), the authors' specific insight about propensity overfitting and their solution represent a meaningful advancement. The paper adequately references related work in causal inference, off-policy evaluation, and importance sampling, positioning their contribution within the broader context of the field.

The significance of this work is substantial for both theory and practice. BLBF is crucial for applications like ad placement and recommendation systems where historical logs are the primary data source. By addressing a fundamental limitation in current counterfactual learning methods, the authors provide a more robust approach that consistently improves generalization performance. The empirical results demonstrate that Norm-POEM not only avoids the propensity overfitting problem but also achieves performance close to full-information learning, making it a practical improvement for real-world applications.

The paper is well-positioned for NIPS publication, as it meets all four criteria for high-quality work: it is technically sound (quality), clearly written (clarity), makes a novel contribution (originality), and addresses an important problem with demonstrable impact (significance).

One minor suggestion for improvement: While the paper thoroughly explains the theoretical benefits of the self-normalized estimator, it could more explicitly connect the theoretical properties (equivariance, boundedness) to the observed empirical improvements. This would strengthen the narrative by directly linking the theory to the results.

Overall, the paper presents a compelling solution to a previously overlooked problem in counterfactual learning. The theoretical analysis is rigorous, the empirical evaluation is comprehensive, and the proposed method delivers consistent improvements across multiple datasets. The authors have made a clear and valuable contribution to the field of batch learning from logged bandit feedback, and I believe this work deserves publication at NIPS.

Strengths:
- Identifies and resolves a significant problem (propensity overfitting) in BLBF
- Provides rigorous theoretical analysis with clear proofs
- Demonstrates consistent empirical improvements across multiple datasets
- Well-written with clear exposition of concepts
- Builds meaningfully on prior work while making a novel contribution

Weaknesses:
- Could more explicitly connect theoretical properties to empirical results
- Minor room for enhancing the narrative flow between theory and practice

The paper is convincing and well-executed, making a valuable contribution to the field that will be of interest to both theoretical and applied researchers in machine learning.
