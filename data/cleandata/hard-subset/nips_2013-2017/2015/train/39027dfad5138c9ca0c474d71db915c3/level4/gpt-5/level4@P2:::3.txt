Summary
This paper addresses batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF), where only the rewards of actions chosen by a historical policy are observed. Building on the Counterfactual Risk Minimization (CRM) framework of Swaminathan and Joachims (2015), the authors identify a new failure mode—propensity overfitting—arising from the standard inverse propensity scoring (IPS) estimator when used for empirical risk minimization over rich hypothesis classes. They propose replacing IPS with a self-normalized importance sampling estimator (a multiplicative control variate), which is bounded and equivariant to loss translation, and they prove strong consistency. Instantiating CRM with this estimator yields Norm-POEM, a learning algorithm for structured prediction with exponential-family policies. Experiments on several multi-label classification datasets show consistent gains over the prior POEM method, improved robustness to loss translations, and favorable optimization behavior.

Relation to prior work
The paper is firmly grounded in prior work on off-policy evaluation and learning with logged data (e.g., importance sampling, clipping/truncation, control variates, and doubly robust estimators). It extends the CRM principle by swapping the IPS estimator for a self-normalized variant known in the Monte Carlo literature, and connects this choice to concrete pathologies (propensity overfitting) in BLBF that were not previously articulated. The empirical setting and hypothesis class follow [1], enabling direct comparison.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The diagnosis of propensity overfitting is compelling, with a clear illustrative example. The self-normalized estimator is well-motivated; its boundedness and equivariance directly address the anomalies of IPS. The consistency result is standard but appropriate. The variance estimator for the ratio estimator follows established approximations, and clipping is used to control variance.
- Empirics: The experiments are carefully executed in a controlled supervised-to-bandit setup and show consistent improvements in risk and stability across datasets and training set sizes. The analysis of Ŝ(h) supports the proposed mechanism.
- Areas to strengthen: While CRM is motivated by a Bernstein-style generalization bound for IPS, an analogous finite-sample bound or discussion tailored to the self-normalized estimator would strengthen the theory. The experimental baselines omit doubly robust or regression-based estimators, which are relevant competitors. More detail on optimizing the non-convex objective (initialization, stopping criteria, and sensitivity to λ and M) would improve reproducibility.

Clarity
The paper is clearly written and well-organized. The exposition of the pathology, the estimator, and the algorithm is accessible, and the connections to importance sampling diagnostics are helpful. Minor suggestion: expand the optimization section with practical details (e.g., L-BFGS settings, gradient computation, and runtime breakdown).

Originality
The self-normalized estimator itself is not new, but applying it within CRM for BLBF, diagnosing propensity overfitting as a distinct failure mode of IPS-based ERM, and demonstrating substantial empirical benefits constitute a meaningful and thoughtful contribution. The resulting Norm-POEM variant is a natural but impactful modification.

Significance
Learning from logged data is a central problem in many interactive systems. The proposed estimator addresses a concrete weakness of IPS that practitioners routinely face (instability due to propensity weights), and the gains shown are substantial. That said, all experiments are on multi-label classification; it would be valuable to see results on real-world logs or additional domains (e.g., ranking, recommendations) and comparisons to stronger off-policy baselines, to better gauge practical impact.

Pros
- Clear identification and demonstration of propensity overfitting in IPS-based CRM.
- Well-motivated replacement with a self-normalized estimator that is bounded and equivariant.
- Consistent empirical improvements across datasets and training sizes; robustness to loss translations.
- Useful diagnostic via Ŝ(h), tying theory and practice.
- Code availability and reasonable computational footprint.

Cons
- No finite-sample generalization bound tailored to the self-normalized estimator within CRM.
- Experimental scope limited to one domain family; missing comparisons to doubly robust and other stabilized estimators.
- Limited details on optimization and hyperparameter sensitivity (λ, clipping M).
- Real-world applicability is suggested but not demonstrated.

Suggestions for improvement
- Theory: Provide a discussion or bound for CRM with the self-normalized estimator (e.g., via ratio concentration or stabilized importance weights), clarifying the role of clipping and the bias-variance trade-off.
- Baselines: Add doubly robust estimators and regression-control variate methods; include clipped/truncated IPS with tuned thresholds as strong baselines.
- Reporting: Since multiple repetitions are run, report standard errors or confidence intervals on all metrics; include variability of Ŝ(h).
- Ablations: Study sensitivity to λ and M; analyze performance as a function of the quality and stochasticity of the logging policy; examine behavior under extreme propensities.
- Optimization: Provide more details on L-BFGS (initialization, line search, convergence), gradient computation, and per-iteration costs; discuss scalability to large output spaces and handling of partition functions.
- Applications: Include at least one real-world logged dataset (e.g., recommendations or ranking) to substantiate external validity.

Overall assessment
A solid, well-executed paper that identifies a real weakness of IPS in BLBF and proposes a principled, practical fix with strong empirical benefits. With broader baselines, more optimization detail, and evidence from a real-world application, this work would be even more compelling.
