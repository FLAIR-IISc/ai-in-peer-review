- Paper proposes replacing IPS with a self-normalized importance sampling estimator in CRM, yielding Norm-POEM for BLBF.
- Motivates Norm-POEM by propensity overfitting problems with IPS, especially for complex hypothesis classes.
- Conceptual contribution: reframes BLBF variance reduction through multiplicative control variates/self-normalization.
- Practical algorithm: Norm-POEM is a drop-in replacement for POEM with a derived variance proxy for the ratio estimator.
- Empirically, Norm-POEM consistently outperforms POEM on multi-label datasets and is competitive with supervised CRF baselines.
- Norm-POEM is robust to loss translations and shows favorable runtime due to faster convergence despite higher per-iteration cost.
- Main technical critique: the paper’s claim that IPS (Eq. 2) has an “anomaly” due to non-equivariance under loss translation is technically misleading and unnecessary.
- The real practical issue with IPS is sensitivity to small propensities (variance explosion); self-normalization mitigates extreme weights and enforces boundedness.
- Reviewer recommends revising the exposition to disentangle variance/weight-instability arguments from incorrect claims about translation invariance.
- Ŝ(ĥ) analysis supports the claim that Norm-POEM better controls propensity overfitting and avoids degenerate solutions that POEM can select.
- Theoretical aspects (use of self-normalization, variance proxy, consistency claims) are sound and supported by Monte Carlo literature.
- Writing and organization are strong overall, but Example 1 and the “anomaly” framing should be reframed to focus on variance/weight instability.
- Originality: applying self-normalization to CRM/BLBF and deriving Norm-POEM is a meaningful and timely extension.
- Significance: method is simple to adopt, improves stability/generalization, and is likely to be useful to practitioners.
- Recommendation: accept pending revisions to correct the technical narrative about Eq. (2), reframe Example 1, and clarify bias/clipping trade-offs.
