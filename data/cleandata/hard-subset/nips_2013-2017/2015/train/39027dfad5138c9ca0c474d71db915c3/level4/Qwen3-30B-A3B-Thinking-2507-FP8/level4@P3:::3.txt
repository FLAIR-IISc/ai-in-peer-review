

This paper addresses a critical flaw in counterfactual risk minimization (CRM) for batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF), a setting central to improving interactive systems (e.g., ad placement, recommendations) using historical logs. The authors identify a previously overlooked "propensity overfitting" problem in the conventional unbiased risk estimator (Equation 2), where empirical risk minimization (ERM) selects hypotheses that exploit the propensity weights \(h(y|x)/h_0(y|x)\) rather than the loss values. This leads to severely biased risk estimates, as demonstrated in their counterexample (Example 1), where the estimated risk can be arbitrarily lower than the true risk. To resolve this, they propose a self-normalized risk estimator (Equation 7) using multiplicative control variates, which is equivariant under loss translation and bounded within the loss range—properties the conventional estimator lacks. This yields Norm-POEM, a new learning algorithm for structured prediction that consistently outperforms POEM (Swaminathan & Joachims, ICML 2013) across multiple multi-label classification datasets.

**Quality**  
The theoretical contribution is strong and well-justified. The identification of propensity overfitting—distinct from conventional loss overfitting—and its resolution via the self-normalized estimator are technically sound, with rigorous proofs (Theorems 1–2) and empirical validation. The paper effectively connects the estimator’s properties to the practical failure modes observed in experiments (Table 2). However, the optimization analysis is underdeveloped. While the authors note Norm-POEM converges faster than POEM without stochastic optimization (Section 7), they omit critical details: *why* the self-normalized estimator enables faster convergence (e.g., how bounded risk estimates affect gradient stability), and whether the non-convexity of the objective (Section 6) impacts generalization. A brief analysis of the optimization landscape would strengthen the technical contribution. The experiments are thorough but lack variance metrics (e.g., standard deviations in Table 1), which would better assess statistical significance.

**Clarity**  
The paper is exceptionally well-written and logically structured. Key concepts—propensity overfitting, control variates, and the self-normalized estimator—are clearly defined with intuitive examples (e.g., the loss translation experiment in Section 4). The connection to prior work (e.g., importance sampling in [12, 19], control variates in [21]) is appropriately contextualized. Minor improvements could include clarifying the role of thresholding \(M\) in the variance estimator (Equation 8) and explicitly stating why the self-normalized estimator’s lack of unbiasedness is acceptable (Theorem 2 confirms consistency).

**Originality**  
This is a significant extension of Swaminathan & Joachims (2013), which introduced CRM for BLBF. While the hypothesis space (exponential family) and CRM framework follow [1], the core innovation—replacing the unbiased estimator with a self-normalized one to solve propensity overfitting—represents a novel technical advance. The problem is not merely a "follow-up" but a critical fix to a flaw in the foundational CRM approach. The authors properly contextualize this against related work (Section 2), noting that prior variance reduction techniques (e.g., clipping, additive control variates) do not address propensity overfitting.

**Significance**  
The problem of learning from logged bandit feedback is highly relevant to industry applications, and the authors convincingly demonstrate that propensity overfitting is a pervasive issue (Section 4, Table 2). Norm-POEM’s consistent empirical gains (Table 1, Figure 1) suggest tangible improvements for real-world systems. While the experiments use standard benchmark datasets (e.g., Yeast, TMC), the authors note that trends hold under varying logging policy properties (Section 7), implying robustness beyond synthetic setups. To further elevate significance, the authors could briefly discuss *why* propensity overfitting is particularly acute in structured prediction (e.g., due to high-dimensional output spaces) versus simpler bandit settings. The runtime efficiency (Section 7) also enhances practical appeal, as Norm-POEM matches CRF runtime while outperforming POEM.

**Strengths**  
- Clear identification and rigorous resolution of a critical flaw (propensity overfitting) in CRM.  
- Self-normalized estimator is theoretically grounded (equivariance, boundedness) and empirically validated across diverse datasets.  
- Norm-POEM is a direct, implementable algorithm with minimal overhead (same hyperparameters as POEM).  
- Experiments convincingly link theoretical properties to improved generalization (Table 2, Figure 1).

**Weaknesses**  
- Insufficient analysis of the optimization dynamics (Section 6), which is crucial for adoption.  
- Missing variance metrics in experimental results (Table 1), weakening confidence in the reported gains.  
- Limited discussion of *why* propensity overfitting arises in structured prediction (vs. simpler settings), though this is implied by the high-dimensional examples.

**Recommendation**  
The paper makes a strong, technically sound contribution that resolves a fundamental limitation in BLBF. While the optimization analysis and variance reporting could be strengthened, these are minor gaps in an otherwise excellent work. The significance is clear: Norm-POEM provides a reliable, efficient solution to a pervasive problem in real-world learning from logs. I recommend **acceptance**. The authors should address the minor points above in the camera-ready version (e.g., add variance to Table 1, clarify optimization insights), but these do not undermine the paper’s core contribution. This work is a valuable addition to NIPS, advancing both the theory and practice of counterfactual learning.
