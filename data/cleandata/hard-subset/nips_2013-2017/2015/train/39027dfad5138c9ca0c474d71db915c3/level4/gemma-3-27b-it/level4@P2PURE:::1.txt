Due to travel constraints, my review has been limited in scope. However, I found the paper interesting and potentially valuable, and I apologize if I have overlooked or misinterpreted any details.

The paper investigates evaluating a hypothesis given data generated from a different hypothesis. When dealing with non-deterministic hypotheses (e.g., involving conditional distributions), the loss under the new hypothesis can be estimated by reweighting the data, as shown in the equation between (1) and (2). 

The authors address a problem – termed "propensity overfitting" – that is related to, but distinct from, the issue of high variance in the weights. The examples suggest this overfitting arises from insufficient exploration, specifically when limited or no data exists for certain y values corresponding to the evaluated hypothesis x. As a similar explanation accounts for the large variance in weights, a more detailed discussion of the relationship between these two problems would be beneficial.

I have not had sufficient time to fully assess the technical details or novelty of the proposed solution, but the core idea appears reasonable.
