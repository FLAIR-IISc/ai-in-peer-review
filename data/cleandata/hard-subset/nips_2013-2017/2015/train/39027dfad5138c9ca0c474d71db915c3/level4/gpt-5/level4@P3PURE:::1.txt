Problem setting and relation to importance weighting:
- Given data collected under one hypothesis, we often wish to evaluate a different hypothesis. When the hypotheses are non-deterministic (e.g., conditional distributions), one can estimate the loss under the new hypothesis by reweighting (importance weighting) the observed data points; see the equation between (1) and (2).
- The paper appears to address a related but somewhat different issue than the large-variance problem of importance weights. The authors refer to this as “propensity overfitting.” The examples suggest it arises under insufficient exploration: for a given x, we observe very few or even no y consistent with the new hypothesis being evaluated. Because a similar mechanism (scarcity of support) also explains large weight variance, it would be helpful if the paper clarified in more detail how “propensity overfitting” relates to the traditional variance problem. Are these effectively the same phenomenon under different names, overlapping but distinct, or does the proposed method specifically target one but not the other?

I did not have time to check the technical details or verify the novelty claims, but the core idea seems sensible.

Specific questions and comments:
- Line 199: I do not follow the argument. If n is small compared to k, we should see only a few data points with x_i = y_i, correct? In that case, wouldn’t R̂(ĥ) be even smaller than −2? Am I missing something?
- “Unbiased counterfactual risk estimator used in prior works on BLBF [4, 5, 1].” I am not sure this is a fair characterization. I am not familiar with all three papers, but I suspect some use techniques like clipping, which reduce variance at the cost of bias. Please clarify which prior work uses strictly unbiased estimators versus clipped (biased) ones, and ensure the comparisons are framed accordingly.
