This paper addresses a critical issue in batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF), a setting of high practical importance. The authors identify a specific failure mode of the conventional inverse propensity score (IPS) estimator, which they term "propensity overfitting". This occurs when a learning algorithm, particularly over a complex hypothesis space, minimizes the estimated risk not by fitting the losses, but by exploiting the propensity weights to produce arbitrarily low risk estimates. This work builds directly upon the Counterfactual Risk Minimization (CRM) principle introduced in Swaminathan & Joachims (2015), which uses an unbiased IPS estimator. As a remedy, the authors propose replacing the standard IPS estimator with a self-normalized one, framing it as an application of multiplicative control variates. This new estimator gives rise to a novel learning algorithm, Norm-POEM. The empirical evaluation on several multi-label classification tasks demonstrates that Norm-POEM significantly outperforms the previous state-of-the-art.

**Strengths (Arguments for Acceptance):**

1.  **Clear Problem Identification:** The paper provides an insightful analysis of a subtle but severe problem in counterfactual learning. The concept of "propensity overfitting" is well-motivated with a clear illustrative example, distinguishing it from the more familiar "loss overfitting" in supervised learning.
2.  **Principled and Elegant Solution:** The proposed solution—using a self-normalized risk estimator—is theoretically well-founded. It elegantly resolves the identified anomalies of the standard estimator, such as non-equivariance to loss translations and unboundedness. Framing the solution in the language of control variates provides a sound statistical basis for the approach.
3.  **Strong Empirical Results:** The experimental evaluation is convincing. Norm-POEM is shown to consistently and substantially outperform the baseline POEM algorithm across various datasets and training data sizes. Crucially, the experiments directly validate the central hypothesis by showing that Norm-POEM avoids the biased sum of propensity weights (Ŝ(h)) that plagues POEM, and is robust to loss translations that cause the baseline to fail catastrophically.
4.  **High Significance and Impact:** The paper tackles an important problem with direct relevance to real-world applications like recommender systems, ad placement, and search, where learning from historical logs is a key challenge. The demonstrated improvements in robustness and generalization performance make this a significant contribution to the field.

**Weaknesses and Questions for the Authors:**

1.  **Generality of the Problem Formulation:** The setup in Section 3 defines the feedback δ(x,y) as a deterministic function. A more general formulation would treat δ as a realization of a random variable, conditional on x and y. For instance, in a classification problem with noisy labels, the same input x and action y could result in different observed losses in the log. While the proposed estimators are likely still applicable under expectation, framing the risk in Equation (1) to account for stochastic feedback would strengthen the paper's generality.
2.  **Clarity on Loss Scaling and Theoretical Assumptions:** The original CRM framework [1] motivates its variance regularization term for losses rescaled to the [-1, 0] interval. It is unclear if this assumption is still required for Norm-POEM's objective function (Equation 3) and variance estimator (Equation 8). The experiments in Table 2, which use translated losses (δ > 0), seem to operate outside this range. Could the authors clarify whether the theory supports general loss ranges, or if an implicit rescaling is performed during implementation? This is important for understanding the theoretical underpinnings and practical application of the method.

**Minor Comments:**

*   The discussion on the estimator's non-equivariance (lack of linearity under translation) is a key point. It may be worth explicitly noting that this issue was also mentioned in the original POEM paper [1].
*   Please double-check the correctness of argmin/argmax operators in the objective functions. Minimizing a risk or an upper bound on risk should typically use an `argmin`.
*   A final pass for notational consistency would be beneficial (e.g., ensuring all empirical estimates are denoted with hats).
