Due to travel constraints, this review represents a rapid, initial assessment. The paper presents interesting ideas, though I may have overlooked details – I apologize if this is the case.

The work investigates evaluating a hypothesis given data generated from another. When dealing with non-deterministic hypotheses (e.g., conditional distributions), data reweighting, as demonstrated between equations (1) and (2), provides a means to estimate loss under the evaluated hypothesis.

The authors tackle a challenge related to, but distinct from, the issue of high variance in reweighting. They term this "propensity overfitting," which appears to arise from insufficient exploration – specifically, a lack of observed data for outcomes relevant to the hypothesis being evaluated. Given the shared underlying principle with the large variance problem, a more detailed discussion of the connection between these two issues would be valuable. 

I was unable to fully examine the technical details or assess the novelty of the proposed solution; however, the core concept appears reasonable. 

Specific points for clarification:
- l. 199: I find the reasoning unclear. If *n* is small relative to *k*, the number of data points where *x<sub>i</sub> = y<sub>i</sub>* is limited. Shouldn’t  *R̂(ĥ)* then be even lower than -2? I may be missing something.
- The statement regarding an “unbiased counterfactual risk estimator used in prior works on BLBF [4, 5, 1]” warrants scrutiny. While I am not familiar with all cited papers, I suspect many employ variance-reducing techniques like clipping, introducing bias.
- The availability of code is greatly appreciated.

In conclusion, while a thorough technical validation was not possible within the time available, the paper presents compelling ideas worthy of further consideration. I recommend acceptance.
