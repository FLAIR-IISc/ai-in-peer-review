The paper identifies a significant problem of propensity overfitting in the conventional counterfactual risk estimator used in batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF) and proposes a self-normalized estimator to address it. BLBF is presented as a crucial framework for leveraging historical logs from online systems (e.g., ad placement, recommendation), offering a compelling alternative to supervised learning when full-information feedback is unavailable. The core contribution lies in demonstrating that the widely adopted unbiased estimator suffers from instabilities when applied to complex hypothesis spaces, leading to biased risk estimates. The authors propose a self-normalized estimator, implemented within a new algorithm – Normalized Policy Optimizer for Exponential Models (Norm-POEM) – and empirically validate its effectiveness on multi-label classification problems, consistently outperforming the conventional estimator.

The paper begins by contextualizing BLBF within causal inference and relating it to off-policy evaluation in reinforcement learning, drawing connections to established techniques like propensity score matching and importance sampling. It correctly identifies the limitations of existing approaches, particularly the variance issues associated with importance sampling. The introduction of multiplicative control variates and the subsequent derivation of the self-normalized estimator are presented as a principled solution to the identified propensity overfitting problem. The authors thoroughly discuss related work, including classic and recent advancements in counterfactual estimation and variance reduction techniques.

The strengths of this paper are numerous. First, the identified problem of propensity overfitting is insightful and potentially impactful for the BLBF community. The proposed self-normalized estimator offers a theoretically sound and practically effective solution, as demonstrated by the empirical results. The experimental setup, mirroring the supervised-to-bandit methodology from prior work, allows for a fair comparison with existing algorithms like POEM. The consistent performance improvements observed across various datasets provide strong evidence for the efficacy of Norm-POEM.  The analysis of Ŝ(h) and its invariance to loss translation further supports the theoretical claims regarding the self-normalized estimator. The clarity of the presentation and the detailed mathematical derivations are commendable.

However, the paper is not without weaknesses. While the authors claim an issue with the conventional estimator not being equivariant, the technical justification provided contains a subtle but important inaccuracy. The assertion that Equation (2) inherently produces risk estimates outside the range of the loss function is a misinterpretation of expected values and probability measures; the issue lies in the potential for small propensity scores, not a fundamental property of the estimator itself. While the subsequent example illustrates the impact of small propensity weights, the initial framing is technically flawed. Furthermore, while the experiments demonstrate consistent improvement, a more in-depth analysis of the datasets and the specific scenarios where Norm-POEM excels would be beneficial. A discussion of the computational cost of the self-normalized estimator compared to other variance reduction techniques would also strengthen the paper.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Addresses a significant and previously unrecognized problem in BLBF.
*   Presents a theoretically sound and empirically validated solution.
*   Demonstrates consistent performance improvements over existing methods.
*   Clear and well-structured presentation with detailed mathematical derivations.
*   Addresses a practically relevant problem with potential for real-world impact.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Contains a minor but noticeable technical inaccuracy in the framing of the problem.
*   Could benefit from a more in-depth analysis of the experimental results.
*   Lacks a detailed comparison of the computational cost of the proposed estimator.



Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of batch learning from logged bandit feedback. Despite the minor technical inaccuracy, the identified problem, the proposed solution, and the empirical validation are compelling and warrant acceptance. I recommend accepting this paper with minor revisions to clarify the technical justification for the initial claim regarding estimator equivariance.
