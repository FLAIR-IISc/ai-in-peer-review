

The paper identifies a critical flaw in conventional counterfactual risk estimators used for batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF) and proposes a self-normalized estimator that resolves this issue. Building on Swaminathan and Joachims’ 2015 CRM framework, the authors demonstrate that standard inverse propensity weighting suffers from "propensity overfitting" when optimizing complex hypothesis spaces—a phenomenon where risk estimates become arbitrarily biased due to unbounded variance in importance weights. This problem manifests in both negative and positive loss regimes, unlike conventional overfitting in supervised learning. The authors introduce a self-normalized risk estimator (Equation 7) that leverages multiplicative control variates to ensure the estimator is equivariant under loss translation and bounded within the loss range. This leads to their Norm-POEM algorithm, which replaces the conventional estimator in the CRM objective (Equation 3). Empirical results across four multi-label classification datasets show consistent improvements over POEM, with Norm-POEM avoiding propensity overfitting (verified via Ŝ(ĥ) ≈ 1) and achieving faster convergence despite increased per-iteration cost.

**Quality**  
The paper’s technical contribution is strong and well-justified. The authors rigorously characterize propensity overfitting through a concrete counterexample (Example 1) and prove the self-normalized estimator’s consistency (Theorem 2). The experimental design adheres to the Supervised-to-Bandit methodology from prior work [1], with careful hyperparameter calibration and robustness checks. Critically, they validate that Norm-POEM’s improvements are *directly attributable* to mitigating propensity overfitting (Table 2), not merely hyperparameter tuning. The computational analysis (Section 7) effectively addresses scalability concerns, noting that despite a more complex variance estimator (Equation 8), Norm-POEM converges faster than POEM due to better regularization. The only minor gap is the lack of theoretical analysis of the variance estimator’s convergence rate, though this is acceptable given the focus on empirical validation.

**Clarity**  
The paper is exceptionally well-written and logically structured. The problem statement (Section 4) clearly distinguishes propensity overfitting from conventional loss overfitting, and the self-normalized estimator’s advantages (equivariance, boundedness) are demonstrated with minimal technical jargon. Figures 1 and Tables 1–3 effectively visualize the key results. The connection between the control variate technique (Section 5) and the practical Norm-POEM algorithm (Section 6) is seamless. One minor suggestion: the proof sketch for Theorem 2 could be slightly expanded to clarify why the denominator’s convergence (to 1) ensures consistency.

**Originality**  
This work makes a significant original contribution by identifying propensity overfitting—a novel failure mode absent in prior BLBF literature—and solving it via a self-normalized estimator. While self-normalized importance sampling has appeared in finance [17, 19], its application to counterfactual learning for BLBF is new, and the authors convincingly show it outperforms thresholded estimators (e.g., R̂_M) in practice. The Norm-POEM algorithm is a natural extension of POEM [1], but the core innovation (the estimator) is distinct. Crucially, the authors properly contextualize their work against related techniques (Section 2), distinguishing their multiplicative control variates from additive approaches like doubly robust estimators [6].

**Significance**  
The significance is high. BLBF is central to real-world applications (ad placement, recommendation systems), and propensity overfitting represents a previously unaddressed barrier to robust learning from logs. Norm-POEM’s consistent empirical gains across diverse datasets—combined with its computational efficiency (Section 7)—suggest immediate practical impact. The authors demonstrate that their method avoids catastrophic failures (e.g., POEM’s performance on TMC dropping below random guessing), which is critical for deployment. The theoretical guarantee that the estimator resolves all anomalies of the conventional approach (Section 5) provides strong foundational value beyond the specific algorithm. This work should catalyze further research into robust counterfactual estimators.

**Overall Assessment**  
This is a high-quality, impactful paper that addresses a fundamental flaw in BLBF with a clean, theoretically grounded solution. The empirical validation is thorough and directly supports the theoretical claims. The authors’ contributions are clearly novel, well-motivated, and likely to be widely adopted in practice. While the paper could briefly discuss limitations (e.g., scalability to extremely large Y), these are minor. The work is clearly within NIPS scope (causal inference, offline learning) and advances the state-of-the-art in counterfactual learning. I strongly recommend acceptance.
