### Review

**Summary**

This paper addresses the problem of batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF), a setting highly relevant to applications like ad placement and recommendation systems. The authors identify a critical flaw in the conventional unbiased risk estimator used in the Counterfactual Risk Minimization (CRM) framework, which they term "Propensity Overfitting." This issue arises when a model with sufficient capacity learns to minimize the risk estimate not by accurately predicting low-loss actions, but by manipulating the importance weights, leading to degenerate solutions. As a remedy, the authors propose replacing the standard inverse propensity score (IPS) estimator with a self-normalized (ratio) estimator, a technique adapted from the importance sampling literature using multiplicative control variates. This modification leads to a new learning algorithm, Norm-POEM, which is shown to be theoretically more robust (e.g., equivariant to loss translations) and empirically superior to its predecessor (POEM) on several multi-label classification tasks.

**Strengths (Arguments for Acceptance)**

1.  **Clear and Important Problem Identification:** The paper's primary contribution is the identification and lucid explanation of "Propensity Overfitting." The authors use simple, effective examples in Section 4 to illustrate how the standard unbiased estimator can produce arbitrarily poor risk estimates, a subtle but severe problem for learning algorithms based on Empirical Risk Minimization. This is a novel and valuable insight for the BLBF community.

2.  **Principled and Well-Motivated Solution:** The proposed solution—using a self-normalized risk estimator—is not ad-hoc but is well-grounded in the statistical literature on control variates and ratio estimators. The paper clearly explains the theoretical benefits of this estimator, such as being bounded within the range of the loss and being equivariant to constant shifts in the loss, which directly addresses the anomalies of the unbiased estimator.

3.  **Strong Empirical Validation:** The experimental results convincingly demonstrate the practical benefits of the proposed approach. Norm-POEM consistently and significantly outperforms the previous state-of-the-art (POEM) across all datasets and training data sizes (Table 1, Figure 1). The experiments in Table 2 are particularly compelling, as they empirically confirm the theoretical claim that Norm-POEM is robust to loss translation while the baseline method fails catastrophically.

4.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is well-written, clearly structured, and easy to follow. The motivation for the problem is established early, the issue is demonstrated with concrete examples, the solution is presented logically, and the experiments are designed to directly validate the claims made.

**Weaknesses (Arguments against Acceptance) and Suggestions for Improvement**

1.  **Limited Scope of Experiments:** While the experiments are well-controlled, they rely on converting supervised datasets into a bandit setting, following the methodology of prior work [1]. This demonstrates the method's effectiveness in a simulated environment but leaves open the question of its performance on real-world logged bandit data. The paper's impact would be significantly greater if it included results from an application with genuine logged data, where the properties of the logging policy and feedback may differ from the simulation.

2.  **Incremental Nature of the Contribution:** The work is a direct follow-up to [1], adopting the same CRM principle and general learning framework. The core change is the substitution of one risk estimator for another. While the chosen estimator is not new to the statistics community, its application to solve the newly identified "Propensity Overfitting" problem in BLBF is novel. The contribution is thus more of a crucial correction and improvement to an existing framework rather than the proposal of an entirely new one.

3.  **Lack of Detail on Optimization:** The paper notes that Norm-POEM has a more complex objective that is not amenable to stochastic optimization, yet surprisingly converges faster than POEM using L-BFGS. The authors suggest this is because Norm-POEM avoids solutions with large-norm weights. This is an interesting observation that warrants further, even if brief, analysis or discussion. Providing more intuition about the optimization landscape could strengthen the paper.

**Detailed Comments**

*   In Table 1, the performance of POEM and Norm-POEM is reported as an average over 10 runs. Including a measure of variance (e.g., standard deviation) would strengthen the results by showing the stability of the performance improvements.

**Conclusion**

Overall, this is a solid and well-executed paper that makes an important contribution to the field of learning from logged bandit feedback. The identification of the "Propensity Overfitting" problem is a significant finding, and the proposed solution is principled, effective, and convincingly validated through rigorous experiments. While the experimental evaluation could be extended to real-world applications, the work clearly advances the state of the art and corrects a serious flaw in a recently proposed and influential framework. The strengths of the paper convincingly outweigh its weaknesses. I recommend acceptance.
