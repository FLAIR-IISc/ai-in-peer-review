This paper identifies a significant problem—propensity overfitting—in the commonly used counterfactual risk estimator for batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF), and proposes a self-normalized estimator to address it. BLBF is a valuable setting for training online systems (e.g., ad placement, recommendation) using historical data, where full-information feedback is unavailable, and the learner observes feedback only for actions taken by a previous policy. The Counterfactual Risk Minimization (CRM) principle provides a framework for this, but the standard estimator suffers from propensity overfitting when applied to complex hypothesis spaces. The authors demonstrate that their self-normalized estimator avoids this issue and introduce a new learning algorithm, Normalized Policy Optimizer for Exponential Models (Norm-POEM), which consistently outperforms the conventional estimator on multi-label classification problems.

The introduction effectively motivates the problem of learning from logged bandit feedback, positioning it within the broader contexts of causal inference, domain adaptation, and off-policy evaluation. The connection to reinforcement learning and the use of counterfactual estimators are clearly explained. The paper effectively establishes the limitations of existing approaches and highlights the contribution of the self-normalized estimator. The related work section appropriately places the work within the literature, referencing relevant techniques from causal inference, domain adaptation, and importance sampling. The discussion of prior counterfactual estimators and the distinction between Propensity Overfitting and Loss Overfitting are particularly insightful.

The technical presentation is generally strong. The formulation of the problem, the derivation of the self-normalized estimator, and the theoretical justification (Theorem 1 & 2) are well-defined. The explanation of Norm-POEM and its connection to Conditional Random Fields is clear. The experimental setup closely follows established methodologies in the field [1, 2], enabling a fair comparison with existing algorithms. 

However, a more detailed explanation of the relationship between propensity overfitting and the large variance of importance weights would strengthen the paper. While both issues can arise in BLBF, clarifying the nuanced differences and why the self-normalized estimator addresses both simultaneously would be valuable. The example in Section 4 is illustrative but could be made more intuitive. Specifically, the statement “R̂(hoverfit) should be even smaller than -2?” could be clarified to better explain the effect of limited exploration and the resulting overfitted estimate. Also, the authors state “I don't know all three papers [4, 5, 1] but I doubt that they are not using sth. like clipping which leads to biased estimation with reduced variance.” While this is a fair point, explicitly acknowledging existing variance reduction techniques (like clipping) in the related work and discussing their limitations relative to the proposed approach would enhance the paper's completeness.

**Strengths:**

*   **Addresses a critical problem:** Propensity overfitting is a novel and important issue in BLBF that hasn’t been adequately addressed previously.
*   **Sound theoretical foundation:** The self-normalized estimator is well-motivated and theoretically justified.
*   **Empirically validated:** The experiments demonstrate consistent performance improvements over existing methods.
*   **Clear presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and easy to follow, despite the technical complexity.
*   **Code availability:** Providing code enhances reproducibility and encourages further research.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Limited discussion of relationship to variance reduction techniques:** The connection between propensity overfitting and existing variance reduction techniques could be more thoroughly explored.
*   **Illustrative example could be clearer:** The example in Section 4, while helpful, might benefit from further clarification.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper identifies a novel and important problem in a rapidly growing area of research.
*   The proposed solution is theoretically sound and empirically validated.
*   The paper makes a significant contribution to the field of batch learning from logged bandit feedback.
*   The writing is clear and the presentation is well-organized.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   The connection to existing variance reduction techniques could be more thoroughly investigated.
*   The explanation of the illustrative example could be more intuitive.

**Overall Assessment:**

Despite minor areas for improvement, this paper presents a significant contribution to the field. The identified problem of propensity overfitting is important, the proposed solution is well-motivated and empirically validated, and the writing is clear. I recommend acceptance.
