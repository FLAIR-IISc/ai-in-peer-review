### Summary

This paper addresses the problem of batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF), a setting crucial for improving interactive systems using historical data. The authors identify a significant and previously overlooked issue with the conventional unbiased counterfactual risk estimator used in this domain, which they term "propensity overfitting." This form of overfitting occurs when a model with high capacity minimizes the risk estimate not by fitting the losses, but by exploiting the propensity weights, leading to degenerate solutions.

As a remedy, the paper proposes replacing the standard unbiased estimator with a self-normalized risk estimator, which is derived using multiplicative control variates. This alternative estimator is shown to be equivariant to translations in the loss function and is bounded by the range of the observed losses, thus provably avoiding the anomalies that cause propensity overfitting. The authors instantiate this idea within the Counterfactual Risk Minimization (CRM) framework, leading to a new algorithm called Normalized Policy Optimizer for Exponential Models (Norm-POEM). Extensive experiments on multi-label classification tasks demonstrate that Norm-POEM consistently and substantially outperforms its predecessor, POEM, validating the theoretical claims and showing improved generalization performance.

### Strengths

1.  **Originality and Significance of the Problem:** The paper's primary contribution is the identification and clear articulation of "propensity overfitting." This is an insightful analysis of a critical failure mode for counterfactual learning methods that rely on the standard inverse propensity score estimator. Given the growing interest in BLBF for real-world applications, this contribution is both novel and highly significant.

2.  **Principled and Elegant Solution:** The proposed solution—using a self-normalized estimator—is well-motivated and theoretically sound. By leveraging the concept of multiplicative control variates, the authors derive an estimator that naturally resolves the pathologies of the unbiased estimator. The discussion of properties like equivariance and bounded risk provides a strong justification for this choice.

3.  **Strong Empirical Validation:** The experimental results are thorough and convincing. The authors use a well-controlled simulation methodology to show that Norm-POEM not only achieves better generalization performance than the previous state-of-the-art (POEM) but also that this improvement is directly linked to mitigating propensity overfitting. The experiments demonstrating invariance to loss translation (Table 2) are particularly effective in supporting the paper's central claims.

### Weaknesses and Questions for the Authors

While the paper is strong overall, there are several points that could be clarified to improve its quality and completeness.

1.  **Clarity on Loss Scaling Assumption:** The CRM framework introduced in the precursor paper [1] relies on the loss `δ` being scaled to the range `[-1, 0]` for the theoretical bounds to hold. It is unclear whether Norm-POEM inherits this requirement. The experiments in Table 2, which explore the effects of translating the loss to be strictly positive (`δ > 0`), seem to operate outside this range. Could you please clarify if loss scaling is still required for Norm-POEM's objective function (Equation 3 with variance term from Equation 8) to be theoretically sound? If so, how should one interpret the results of the loss translation experiments?

2.  **Generality of the Problem Formulation:** The problem setup in Section 3 treats the feedback `δ(x, y)` as a deterministic function. However, in many practical settings, such as classification with noisy labels, the feedback for a given state-action pair `(x, y)` is a random variable. A more general formulation would define the true risk in Equation (1) with an additional expectation over the stochasticity of `δ`. It would strengthen the paper to discuss whether the proposed framework naturally extends to this more general, stochastic feedback setting.

3.  **Minor Presentation Issues:** The manuscript could benefit from a final proofreading pass to address some minor issues:
    *   In the discussion of control variates (around line 170-171), the correlation is described between `h(y|x)h0(y|x)` and the loss term. This appears to be a typo, as the importance weight is the ratio `h(y|x)/h0(y|x)`, not the product.
    *   While the paper correctly identifies the lack of equivariance in the unbiased estimator as a key motivator, it might be worth mentioning for completeness that this issue was also briefly noted in [1].
    *   Please check for minor notational inconsistencies (e.g., using hats vs. bars for empirical estimates) and potential typos in equations (e.g., `argmin` vs. `argmax` where appropriate).

### Overall Assessment

This is an excellent paper that makes a clear and important contribution to the field of counterfactual learning. The identification of propensity overfitting is a significant finding, and the proposed self-normalized estimator provides an effective and well-justified solution. The paper is well-written, and its claims are strongly supported by a comprehensive set of experiments. The weaknesses identified above are minor points of clarification that do not undermine the core contribution. This work is a valuable follow-up to [1] and is likely to be influential for both researchers and practitioners working with logged bandit feedback. I recommend acceptance.
