### Review

**Summary and Relation to Prior Work**

This paper addresses the problem of Batch Learning from Logged Bandit Feedback (BLBF), a setting of significant practical importance for optimizing interactive systems like ad placement and recommendation engines. The work builds directly upon the Counterfactual Risk Minimization (CRM) principle introduced by Swaminathan and Joachims [1], which uses an unbiased inverse propensity score (IPS) estimator to learn from historical data.

The central contribution of this paper is the identification of a critical failure mode in the conventional unbiased IPS estimator, which the authors term "Propensity Overfitting." They demonstrate, with a clear and insightful example, that a model with sufficient capacity can learn to minimize the estimated risk not by finding a good policy, but by manipulating the propensity weights—for instance, by assigning high probability to training examples with negative loss, or by assigning zero probability to examples with positive loss. This can lead to arbitrarily poor risk estimates that are disconnected from the true risk.

To solve this problem, the authors propose replacing the standard IPS estimator with a self-normalized (or ratio) estimator, derived from the principle of multiplicative control variates. This estimator, while introducing a small bias, is shown to be strongly consistent and possesses several desirable properties: it is equivariant to translations in the loss function and its value is always bounded by the range of the observed losses, thus preventing the pathological overfitting behavior of the unbiased estimator. The authors then instantiate the CRM principle with this new estimator to develop a new learning algorithm, Norm-POEM, for structured output prediction with linear models. The empirical evaluation on several multi-label classification tasks shows that Norm-POEM consistently and substantially outperforms the original POEM algorithm [1], which uses the unbiased estimator.

**Strengths (Arguments for Acceptance)**

1.  **Clear Identification of a Significant Problem:** The paper's primary strength lies in its clear diagnosis of the "Propensity Overfitting" problem. This is a subtle but severe flaw in the standard counterfactual learning framework that has not been well-articulated previously. The illustrative example in Section 4 is highly effective at building intuition for why the unbiased estimator can fail catastrophically.

2.  **Principled and Effective Solution:** The proposed solution—using the self-normalized risk estimator—is not an ad-hoc fix but is well-grounded in the statistical literature on Monte Carlo methods and control variates. The theoretical analysis demonstrates that this estimator elegantly resolves the identified anomalies of the unbiased estimator (e.g., lack of equivariance, unboundedness).

3.  **Strong and Convincing Empirical Results:** The experiments provide compelling evidence for the practical benefits of the proposed approach. Norm-POEM not only achieves better generalization performance than the baseline POEM across multiple datasets and sample sizes but also demonstrates remarkable robustness. The experiments showing POEM's catastrophic failure under a simple loss translation, while Norm-POEM's performance remains unchanged, strongly validate the paper's central claims.

4.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is very well-written and easy to follow. The motivation is clear, the problem is well-defined, the proposed solution is logically derived, and the experiments are designed to directly test the core hypotheses.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement**

1.  **Incremental Nature of the Contribution:** The work is a direct follow-up to [1], modifying one component (the risk estimator) within the existing CRM and POEM framework. While the modification is impactful, the conceptual framework, hypothesis space, and experimental methodology are largely inherited from prior work.

2.  **Limited Scope of Experiments:** The experiments are conducted using the "Supervised-to-Bandit" protocol on standard academic datasets, which is the same setup as in [1]. While this controlled setting is excellent for isolating the effect of the new estimator, it leaves open the question of how these substantial improvements translate to real-world BLBF problems where the logging policy may be more complex, its propensities estimated, or the data generating process more noisy. Demonstrating the method's effectiveness on an additional, more realistic application would have significantly strengthened the paper's claims of practical significance.

3.  **Lack of Detail on Optimization:** The paper notes that the variance estimate for Norm-POEM is more complex and does not lend itself to stochastic optimization, yet it reports that optimization with L-BFGS is surprisingly fast. A more detailed analysis of the optimization would be welcome. For example, a discussion of the properties of the new objective function's landscape or an empirical comparison of convergence behavior (e.g., objective value vs. iterations/wall-clock time) against POEM could provide valuable insights for practitioners.

4.  **Minor Reporting Detail:** The results in Table 1 report the average performance over 10 runs. Including a measure of variance (e.g., standard deviation) would provide a more complete picture of the performance difference and the stability of the results.

**Overall Assessment**

This is a solid and well-executed paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of counterfactual learning. It identifies a critical and previously overlooked problem with the standard unbiased risk estimator and proposes a principled, effective, and empirically validated solution. While the work is incremental and the experiments could be broader in scope, the clarity of the problem identification and the strength of the results make this a noteworthy advance. The insights regarding "Propensity Overfitting" are likely to be influential for researchers and practitioners working on learning from logged data. I recommend acceptance.
