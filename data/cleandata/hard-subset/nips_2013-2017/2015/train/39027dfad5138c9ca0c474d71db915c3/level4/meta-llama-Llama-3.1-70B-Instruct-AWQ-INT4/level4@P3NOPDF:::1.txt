I had limited time to complete this review, and I apologize if I have missed or misunderstood any points. I found the paper to be quite interesting, and I appreciate the opportunity to provide feedback.

The paper addresses the problem of evaluating a different hypothesis based on observed data from a given hypothesis. In the case of non-deterministic hypotheses, such as conditional distributions, reweighting data points can be used to estimate the loss under the new hypothesis (see the equation between (1) and (2)).

The authors focus on a problem related to, but distinct from, the issue of large variance in weights, which they term "propensity overfitting." This problem arises when there is insufficient exploration, resulting in few or no observations of y corresponding to the new hypothesis for a given x. A similar argument explains the large variance of weights, so it would be helpful to provide more detail on the relationship between these two problems.

Although I did not have time to examine the details or assess the novelty of the proposed solution, the idea seems sensible.

I have a few specific comments on the paper:

- Line 199: I am unclear on this point. If n is small compared to k, we would expect to see only a few data points with xi = yi. Shouldn't \hat R(h^) be even smaller than -2? I may be missing something.

- The statement "unbiased counterfactual risk estimator used in prior works on BLBF [4, 5, 1]" seems potentially misleading. While I am not familiar with all three papers, I suspect that they may employ techniques like clipping, which can lead to biased estimation with reduced variance.

I appreciate that the authors have made their code available.

In summary, while I was unable to thoroughly evaluate the technical soundness of the paper, it contains interesting ideas that warrant further consideration. I therefore suggest acceptance.
