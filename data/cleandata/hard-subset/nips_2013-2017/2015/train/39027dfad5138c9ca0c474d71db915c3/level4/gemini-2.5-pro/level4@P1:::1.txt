This paper addresses a key problem in batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF), where a learning algorithm aims to find an optimal policy using historical interaction data. The authors identify a specific failure mode of the standard inverse propensity score (IPS) estimator used for counterfactual risk minimization, which they term "propensity overfitting." This issue arises when a model with high capacity learns to exploit the propensity weights themselves, rather than minimizing the true loss, leading to severely biased risk estimates and poor generalization. To remedy this, the paper proposes replacing the standard IPS estimator with a self-normalized importance sampling estimator. This new estimator is provably robust to the identified issues and forms the basis of a new learning algorithm, Norm-POEM. Empirical results on several multi-label classification tasks demonstrate that Norm-POEM consistently outperforms its predecessor, which uses the standard estimator.

**Strengths:**

*   The paper identifies and clearly illustrates an important and subtle problem with the standard counterfactual risk estimator. The concept of "propensity overfitting" is well-motivated through simple, intuitive examples, showing how a model can achieve an arbitrarily low estimated risk that is disconnected from its true performance. This is a valuable insight for the off-policy evaluation and learning community.
*   The proposed solution—using a self-normalized estimator—is elegant and well-principled. It directly addresses the diagnosed problem by normalizing the importance weights, which makes the estimator equivariant to shifts in the loss function and bounds its value within the range of the observed losses.
*   The experimental evaluation is convincing. The results in Tables 1, 2, and 3 clearly show that the proposed Norm-POEM algorithm not only achieves better generalization performance but is also substantially more robust to the propensity overfitting issue, as evidenced by the sum of weights `Ŝ(h)` remaining close to its expected value of 1.
*   The authors are commended for making their code available, which greatly aids in reproducibility and encourages adoption of the method.

**Weaknesses and Questions for Clarification:**

The paper is well-written and the ideas are interesting. My main suggestions are for clarification to further strengthen the paper's contribution.

1.  **Relation to High Variance:** The paper introduces "propensity overfitting" as a novel problem. However, the conditions that enable it—specifically, a mismatch between the logging policy and the evaluation policy leading to extreme importance weights—are also the root cause of the well-known high-variance problem in importance sampling. It would be beneficial to include a more detailed discussion on the relationship between propensity overfitting and the high-variance issue. Are they two facets of the same underlying problem, or is there a fundamental distinction? Clarifying this would help position the contribution more precisely within the broader literature.

2.  **Characterization of Prior Work:** The paper states that "virtually all existing works on BLBF have focused on a particular unbiased estimator." While the foundational IPS estimator is unbiased, many of the cited works [1, 5, 11] also discuss and employ biased variants, such as clipping or thresholding, precisely to mitigate the effects of high variance. It might be fairer and more accurate to refine this statement to acknowledge that while the unbiased estimator is the starting point, the community has been actively developing biased-but-lower-variance alternatives.

3.  **Clarification on Example 1:** I may have misunderstood a detail in the calculation for Example 1. To arrive at the estimated risk `R̂(hoverfit) <= -k`, does the argument rely on all sampled losses `δi` being -1? The construction of `hoverfit` simply memorizes the sampled `(xi, yi)` pairs, where `yi` is drawn from a uniform logging policy. Could the authors walk through the calculation in a bit more detail to clarify how this bound is derived?

**Summary and Recommendation:**

Overall, this is an interesting and well-executed paper that makes a solid contribution to the field of counterfactual learning. It clearly identifies a significant practical problem with a standard estimator and proposes an effective, principled solution. The empirical results strongly support the claims. While there are minor points that could be clarified regarding the novelty and positioning of the core problem, the ideas are valuable and well-presented. I recommend acceptance.
