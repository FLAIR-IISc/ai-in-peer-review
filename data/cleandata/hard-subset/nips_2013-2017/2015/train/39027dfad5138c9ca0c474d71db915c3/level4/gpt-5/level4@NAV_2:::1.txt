Given observations generated by one hypothesis, we may wish to evaluate a different hypothesis. When these hypotheses are stochastic (e.g., conditional distributions), one can estimate the loss of the target hypothesis by importance reweighting the logged data, as shown in the derivation between Eqs. (1) and (2).

The authors address an issue that is related to—but not identical with—the high variance of importance weights. They term it “propensity overfitting.” The examples suggest this arises under insufficient exploration: for a given x, the log rarely (or never) contains y’s that align with the target hypothesis’s support. Since a similar phenomenon underlies the large-variance problem, it would be helpful if the paper clarified in more depth how propensity overfitting relates to, and differs from, variance blow-up.

I did not have time to verify the technical details or thoroughly check novelty, but the core idea appears reasonable.
