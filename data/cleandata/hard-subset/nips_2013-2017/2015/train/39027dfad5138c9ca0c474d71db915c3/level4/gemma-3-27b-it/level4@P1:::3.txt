The paper studies the problem of batch learning from logged bandit feedback, a highly relevant problem in areas such as ad ranking. The authors identify a “propensity overfitting” problem within the recently proposed Counterfactual Risk Minimization (CRM) principle and propose a solution based on multiplicative control variates, resulting in a self-normalized estimator.

**Quality:**
This is a high-quality and well-motivated work. As a follow-up to [1], one might expect additional experiments and more in-depth analysis of the training objective's optimization – perhaps more detail than is provided in the paragraph starting on line 415. The theoretical analysis appears sound, and the identification of a novel overfitting phenomenon is a significant contribution. The paper demonstrates a careful and honest evaluation of both the strengths and weaknesses of the proposed approach.

**Clarity:**
The presentation is clear and the paper is well-written. The authors effectively explain complex concepts and provide sufficient detail to understand their approach. The organization is logical and facilitates understanding.

**Originality:**
This work builds upon [1] by modifying the risk estimator to be self-normalizing, which appears to yield substantial improvements in the experimental results. The identification of “propensity overfitting” as a distinct phenomenon is novel and insightful. The use of multiplicative control variates in this context, and the demonstration of its effectiveness, is a valuable contribution.

**Significance:**
Learning from logged data is a highly relevant problem. However, it is challenging to assess how the demonstrated experimental improvements will translate to more real-world applications. While the experimental setup closely mirrors prior work ([1]), exploring broader datasets or application scenarios would further strengthen the paper’s impact. 

**Various Remarks:**
Line 365: When running multiple repetitions of an experiment, including a measure of variance in the results would be beneficial. The paper clearly identifies a problem with the recently proposed Counterfactual Risk Minimization (CRM) principle and introduces a compelling solution. Overall, the paper is solid, well-written, and the work is both relevant and novel. I would have appreciated some extended experiments and, particularly, additional applications beyond those presented in [1], including some exploration of actual real-world use cases.

**Arguments Pro Acceptance:**

*   Addresses a significant practical problem (learning from logged data).
*   Identifies a novel and important issue ("propensity overfitting").
*   Proposes a theoretically sound and empirically validated solution.
*   Builds upon and extends existing work in a meaningful way ([1]).
*   Clear writing and well-organized presentation.

**Arguments Con Acceptance:**

*   Experimental evaluation primarily replicates the setting of [1]; more diverse datasets or applications could strengthen the findings.
*   Limited discussion of the optimization process; more detail regarding the training objective would be helpful.
*   While promising, the generalizability of the improvements to real-world scenarios requires further investigation.
