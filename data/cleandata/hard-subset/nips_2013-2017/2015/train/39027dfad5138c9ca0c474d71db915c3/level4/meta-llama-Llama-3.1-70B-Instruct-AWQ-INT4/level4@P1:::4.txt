The paper "The Self-Normalized Estimator for Counterfactual Learning" identifies a significant problem with the conventional counterfactual risk estimator used in batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF) and proposes a novel solution using a self-normalized estimator. The authors demonstrate that the conventional estimator suffers from a propensity overfitting problem, which can lead to severely biased risk estimates. In contrast, the self-normalized estimator provably avoids this issue and provides a more robust estimate of the risk.

The paper is well-organized, and the authors provide a clear explanation of the problem, the proposed solution, and the theoretical justification for the self-normalized estimator. The experimental evaluation is thorough, and the results show a significant improvement over the conventional estimator.

One question to clarify in the rebuttal is regarding the rescaling of the loss function. In Section 4.1 of [1], it is mentioned that the CRM approach only makes sense for delta in [-1,0], and the authors propose to rescale a general loss to [-1,0]. It is unclear whether the same rescaling is used in the experiment in Table 1, particularly when using "delta > 0".

Other comments:

* In the setup for "batch learning from logged bandit feedback" of Section 3, it would be more accurate to consider the feedback as a random variable rather than a deterministic function. This is because the partial feedback may come from a classification problem where the true labels are unknown, and the feedback is generated by trying a prediction and seeing the loss. Additionally, the labeling can be noisy, and there may not be a unique label assigned to each input.
* In Line 170-171, the correct notation should be h(y|x) / h_0(y|x). It is also worth mentioning that the lack of linearity was already pointed out in [1].
* In Line 276, the correct notation should be argmin instead of argmax, as the risk is being minimized.
* In Line 319, the notation should be consistent with the previous convention, using hats instead of bars.

Overall, the paper presents an interesting and significant contribution to the field of counterfactual learning. The analysis of propensity overfitting and the proposal of a self-normalized estimator are well-justified, and the experimental results demonstrate a clear improvement over existing methods.
