The paper studies the problem of batch learning from logged bandit feedback, a very relevant problem in various applications such as ad ranking. The authors identify an "overfitting" issue in the recently proposed Counterfactual Risk Minimization (CRM) principle and propose a solution based on multiplicative control variates, resulting in a biased estimator.

Quality: High-quality and well-motivated. As this is follow-up work on [1], I would have expected additional experiments and some in-depth analysis on the optimization of the training objective, providing more details than presented in the paragraph on line 415.

Clarity: The presentation is clear, and the paper is well-written.

Originality: This is follow-up work on [1], but the authors modify the risk estimator to be self-normalizing, which appears to make a significant difference in the experiments studied.

Significance: The problem of learning from logged data is relevant; however, it is challenging to determine how the substantial experimental improvements translate to more real-world applications.

Various Remarks: On line 365, if the authors have already run several repetitions, it would be beneficial to include some measure of variance. The paper identifies a problem in the recently proposed Counterfactual Risk Minimization (CRM) principle and introduces a solution to this problem. Overall, the paper is solid, well-written, and the work is relevant and novel. However, I would have appreciated some extended experiments and especially additional applications beyond those in [1], including some actual real-world applications.

Arguments For Acceptance:
- The paper tackles a relevant problem in the field of batch learning from logged bandit feedback.
- The authors propose a novel solution to the overfitting issue in the CRM principle.
- The paper presents solid experimental results, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed approach.

Arguments Against Acceptance:
- The paper is follow-up work on [1], and some readers may perceive it as incremental.
- The authors could have provided more in-depth analysis on the optimization of the training objective.
- The paper lacks real-world applications, making it challenging to assess the practical impact of the proposed approach.
