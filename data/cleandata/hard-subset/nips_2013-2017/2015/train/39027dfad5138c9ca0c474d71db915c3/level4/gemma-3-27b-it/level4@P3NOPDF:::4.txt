This paper presents an interesting follow-up to [1], addressing the issue of propensity overfitting in offline policy evaluation. The authors analyze this phenomenon and propose a novel approach using a multiplicative control variate to mitigate it. The results demonstrate a clear improvement over POEM in the context of batch learning from logged bandit feedback, a setting receiving increasing attention. Overall, I believe this is a valuable contribution.

However, I have a few questions and comments regarding the technical details. 

First, I seek clarification regarding the rescaling of delta values. Section 4.1 of [1] indicates that the CRM approach is most effective when delta is within the range [-1, 0], leading them to rescale losses accordingly. It seems reasonable to assume that NORM-POEM would similarly benefit from this rescaling, implying that equations (8) and (3) both utilize delta values within [-1, 0]. If this is the case, I am unclear about the meaning of “delta > 0” in the experiments presented in Table 1. Please clarify this point in the rebuttal.

Second, the setup for “batch learning from logged bandit feedback” (Section 3, and as originally presented in [1]) feels incomplete. The feedback, delta(x, y), is modeled as a deterministic function (line 110), yet in many real-world scenarios, this feedback is inherently stochastic. Consider a classification problem where labels are unknown and inferred through loss calculation following a prediction. While a 'god agent' might label each input x with a y', leading to a loss Delta(y', y), labeling is often noisy. The same input x and action y could yield different delta(x, y) values due to varying labels assigned by the god agent. Consequently, delta(x, y) should arguably be treated as a random variable. In the classification example, the mean of this random variable, for a fixed x and y, would represent the expected loss given the true, potentially noisy, labeling distribution p(y’|x). While this observation may not directly impact the risk estimator (2), the setup in (1) representing the true risk should be updated to include an expectation over the randomness of delta to increase its generality.

Third, on line 170-171, I believe the ratio should be expressed as h(y|x) / h_0(y|x). Additionally, it would be beneficial to acknowledge that the lack of linearity in this context was previously identified in [1]. 

Finally, on line 276, “argmax” should be replaced with “argmin” as the objective is risk minimization. On line 319, maintain consistency with previous notation by replacing bars with hats.

**Update after rebuttal:**

The clarifications provided by the authors were helpful.
