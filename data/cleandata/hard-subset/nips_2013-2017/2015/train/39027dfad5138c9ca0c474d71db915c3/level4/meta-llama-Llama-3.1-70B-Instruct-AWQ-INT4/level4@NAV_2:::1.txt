This paper introduces the concept of "propensity overfitting" in batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF) and proposes a solution using a self-normalized risk estimator. The authors argue that the conventional unbiased risk estimator used in prior works on BLBF suffers from propensity overfitting, which can lead to degenerate risk estimates.

The paper begins by providing a clear summary of the BLBF setting and the counterfactual risk minimization (CRM) principle. The authors then identify the problem of propensity overfitting and provide examples to illustrate its occurrence. They propose using a self-normalized risk estimator, which is shown to avoid the anomalies of the conventional estimator.

The self-normalized risk estimator is derived using control variates, a technique commonly used in importance sampling. The authors provide a thorough explanation of the estimator's properties, including its equivariance and boundedness. They also derive a new learning algorithm, called Norm-POEM, which uses the self-normalized risk estimator and the CRM principle.

The paper includes an extensive empirical evaluation of Norm-POEM on several multi-label classification problems. The results show that Norm-POEM consistently outperforms the conventional estimator, and that it avoids propensity overfitting.

The authors also provide a thorough discussion of related work, including the connection to importance sampling and the use of control variates. They also address potential limitations of their approach, including the need for variance regularization.

Overall, the paper presents a clear and well-motivated solution to the problem of propensity overfitting in BLBF. The authors provide a thorough explanation of the technical details, and the empirical evaluation is convincing.
