- Addresses batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF) and practical applications like ad placement and recommendations.  
- Identifies a previously overlooked problem: propensity overfitting that biases conventional unbiased counterfactual risk estimators.  
- Shows propensity overfitting is severe for complex hypothesis spaces, especially under ERM.  
- Proposes a self-normalized risk estimator using multiplicative control variates to avoid propensity overfitting.  
- Introduces Norm-POEM, a new learning algorithm for structured output prediction based on the self-normalized estimator.  
- Builds on Swaminathan & Joachims (2015) CRM framework and situates the contribution within that theoretical foundation.  
- Connects to related areas: causal inference (propensity score matching), domain adaptation, and off-policy RL evaluation.  
- Provides rigorous theoretical analysis proving the self-normalized estimator resolves propensity overfitting and is strongly consistent.  
- Offers a detailed example showing conventional estimators can produce arbitrarily biased risk estimates.  
- Empirical evaluation across multiple multi-label datasets shows Norm-POEM consistently outperforms POEM for all sample sizes.  
- Analysis of estimated propensity weight sum Ŝ(h) demonstrates Norm-POEM keeps it near 1 while POEM is severely biased.  
- Paper is generally clear and well-organized with consistent notation and progressive theoretical development.  
- Minor clarity gap: needs more explicit connection between theory and practical implementation details (e.g., computational cost at scale).  
- Originality is strong: identifies a new overfitting mode and applies multiplicative control variates in a novel way for BLBF.  
- Significance is substantial: simple, effective method with improved generalization and surprising faster convergence despite higher per-iteration cost.  
- Strengths summarized: identifies/resolves new problem, rigorous theory, strong empirical gains, computational efficiency, solid related-work connections.  
- Weaknesses summarized: limited discussion of failure modes (poor logging policy coverage), incomplete comparison to other variance-reduction methods, and less-detailed theoretical guarantees for structured outputs.  
- Arguments for acceptance: significant, well-supported theoretical and empirical contribution with practical impact and implementation simplicity.  
- Arguments against acceptance: may be seen as a refinement rather than breakthrough and empirical tests are mostly on multi-label classification tasks.  
- Overall recommendation: strong contribution and should be accepted for presentation at NIPS.
