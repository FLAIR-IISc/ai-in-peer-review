This paper presents a novel method for batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF), directly addressing the critical issue of propensity overfitting. The proposed approach builds upon the Policy Optimizer for Exponential Models (POEM) framework from Swaminathan and Joachims (2015) but introduces a significant reformulation that yields substantial improvements.

**Quality and Originality**
The quality of the research is high. The paper's primary contribution is a significant and original reformulation of the POEM algorithm. This new formulation is both elegant and effective, introducing a more sophisticated regularization mechanism to control for model complexity and prevent overfitting to the propensities in the logged data. The extensions result in a demonstrably superior algorithm for training policies in the BLBF setting. The empirical evaluation is thorough and convincingly demonstrates the superiority of the proposed method over the original POEM baseline.

**Significance**
The results presented are significant and impactful. A particularly impressive and compelling finding is that the proposed algorithm is computationally faster than the original POEM, despite the inclusion of additional regularization. This is a non-trivial achievement, as regularization often introduces computational overhead. This gain in efficiency, coupled with improved performance, constitutes a significant practical advantage that could drive widespread adoption of this method in real-world applications like computational advertising, recommendation systems, and personalized medicine. The work successfully advances the state-of-the-art and opens promising avenues for future research.

**Clarity**
The paper is well-written and clearly structured. The authors effectively motivate the problem and present their technical contributions in an accessible manner, making the core concepts and the proposed algorithm easy to follow for readers familiar with the domain.

**Summary**
This is a strong paper that presents a valuable and original contribution to the field of learning from bandit feedback. The proposed algorithm offers a compelling solution to the known problem of propensity overfitting, validated by strong empirical results that demonstrate marked improvements in both policy performance and computational efficiency. The work is significant, and the resulting algorithm is likely to be influential and widely used.
