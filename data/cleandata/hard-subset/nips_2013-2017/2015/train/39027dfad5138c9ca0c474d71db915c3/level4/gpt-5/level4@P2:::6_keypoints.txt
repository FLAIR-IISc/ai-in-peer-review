Summary: Identifies propensity overfitting in IPS-based CRM and proposes a self-normalized importance sampling estimator (Norm-POEM) to mitigate it, with boundedness, equivariance, and strong consistency.  
Estimator properties: Self-normalized estimator is bounded, equivariant to loss translations, strongly consistent, but biased in finite samples.  
Algorithm: Norm-POEM integrates the self-normalized estimator into CRM for structured prediction with an approximate variance estimator and variance regularization.  
Empirical results: Consistently outperforms IPS-based POEM on simulated multi-label datasets, is robust to loss translations, and reduces propensity overfitting diagnostics.  
Relation to prior work: Builds on importance sampling, clipping/truncation, control variates, and complements CRM bounds while focusing on multiplicative control variates less explored in BLBF.  
Strengths: Clear diagnosis and empirical evidence of propensity overfitting; principled self-normalization; practical algorithm with consistent empirical gains and relevant applications.  
Weaknesses/questions: Finite-sample bias not fully characterized; variance estimator is approximate and requires clipping—sensitivity to M and λ unclear; evaluation limited to simulated logs; optimization scalability and runtime need analysis.  
Quality: Technically sound; theoretical claims are standard adaptations and empirical evaluation is careful and aligned with claims.  
Clarity: Well written and organized; suggest clarifying scenarios where self-normalization underperforms, calibration of M and λ, and more implementation details.  
Originality: Novel identification of propensity overfitting in IPS-based CRM and novel use of self-normalization as a learning objective in BLBF.  
Significance: High — addresses a practical failure mode and offers a robust alternative likely to impact counterfactual learning practice.  
Pros: Addresses a real issue, provides a simple principled fix, demonstrates consistent gains, and remains compatible with CRM/exponential-family models.  
Cons: Finite-sample bias and clipping/regularization sensitivity not fully explored; lacks real-world logged data evaluation and broader baseline comparisons (e.g., doubly robust).  
Overall assessment: Strong, timely contribution with recommendation for acceptance; reviewer confidence moderate.
