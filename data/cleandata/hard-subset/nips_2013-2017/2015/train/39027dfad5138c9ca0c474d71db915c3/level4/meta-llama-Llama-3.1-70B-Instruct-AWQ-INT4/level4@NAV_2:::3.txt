The paper "The Self-Normalized Estimator for Counterfactual Learning" studies the problem of batch learning from logged bandit feedback, a very relevant problem in areas such as ad ranking. The authors identify a severe problem with the conventional counterfactual risk estimator used in prior works on batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF), and propose the use of an alternative estimator that avoids this problem. This paper is a follow-up to [1] and modifies the risk estimator to be self-normalizing, which seems to make a big difference in the experiments.

The main idea of the paper is to replace the conventional counterfactual risk estimator with a self-normalized estimator, which is shown to neatly avoid the propensity overfitting problem. The authors propose a new learning algorithm, called Normalized Policy Optimizer for Exponential Models (Norm-POEM), for structured output prediction using linear rules. The algorithm is analogous to POEM [1] in its choice of hypothesis space and its application of the Counterfactual Risk Minimization (CRM) principle, but it replaces the conventional estimator with the self-normalized estimator.

The paper provides a thorough analysis of the propensity overfitting problem and its solution. The authors show that the self-normalized estimator resolves all the problems of the unbiased estimator identified in the paper. They also provide a detailed description of the Norm-POEM algorithm and its components, including the hypothesis space, variance estimator, and training objective.

The experimental evaluation of the paper is thorough and well-designed. The authors compare the performance of Norm-POEM with POEM [1] on several multi-label classification problems and show that Norm-POEM consistently outperforms POEM. They also provide a detailed analysis of the results, including the average Ŝ(ĥ) for the hypothesis ĥ selected by each approach and the prediction error on the test set.

The paper is well-written and clear in its presentation. The authors provide a thorough background on the problem of batch learning from logged bandit feedback and the Counterfactual Risk Minimization (CRM) principle. They also provide a detailed description of the related work and the contributions of the paper.

The quality of the paper is high, and the work is relevant and novel. The authors identify a problem with the conventional counterfactual risk estimator and propose a solution that makes a big difference in the experiments. The paper is well-motivated, and the authors provide a thorough analysis of the problem and its solution.

The originality of the paper is high, as it modifies the risk estimator to be self-normalizing, which is a novel contribution. The significance of the paper is also high, as the problem of learning from logged data is relevant and the authors provide a solution that can be applied to real-world applications.

However, it is difficult to say how the substantial experimental improvements translate to more real-world applications. The authors could have provided more extended experiments and additional applications, including some actual real-world applications. Additionally, the authors could have provided more details on the optimization of the training objective, such as the choice of hyper-parameters and the convergence of the algorithm.

Overall, the paper is solid, well-written, and the work is relevant and novel. The authors provide a thorough analysis of the problem and its solution, and the experimental evaluation is thorough and well-designed. The paper makes a significant contribution to the field of batch learning from logged bandit feedback and is a valuable addition to the literature.
