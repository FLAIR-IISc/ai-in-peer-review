This paper addresses the problem of batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF), a setting highly relevant for improving deployed systems like search engines and ad platforms using historical data. The authors begin by identifying a critical and previously overlooked failure mode in the conventional counterfactual risk estimator used by algorithms based on the Counterfactual Risk Minimization (CRM) principle. They term this issue "Propensity Overfitting," where the learning objective can be minimized not by fitting the true losses, but by finding hypotheses that exploit the propensity weights to produce arbitrarily low risk estimates. This is a novel insight that deepens the community's understanding of the challenges in off-policy learning.

The core proposal of the paper is to replace the standard unbiased inverse propensity scoring (IPS) estimator with a self-normalized variant, derived by using the sum of propensity weights as a multiplicative control variate. The authors show that this self-normalized estimator is equivariant to translations in the loss function and is always bounded by the range of the observed losses, thereby provably avoiding the anomalies that cause propensity overfitting. Building on this, they introduce a new learning algorithm, Norm-POEM, which integrates the self-normalized estimator and an appropriate variance regularizer into the CRM framework. The work is a direct follow-up to Swaminathan & Joachims (2015), extending and correcting a key component of that foundational work.

**Strengths:**

*   **Problem Identification:** The clear articulation and illustration of the "Propensity Overfitting" problem is a significant contribution in itself. Example 1 provides a compelling and intuitive demonstration of how conventional estimators can fail catastrophically in ways distinct from standard supervised learning overfitting.
*   **Principled Solution:** The proposed solution is technically sound and well-motivated. The use of a self-normalized estimator is a classic technique in the importance sampling literature, but its application here to solve the propensity overfitting problem is novel and elegant. The theoretical analysis correctly identifies the desirable properties of the estimator (equivariance, boundedness, and consistency) that resolve the issues with the unbiased estimator.
*   **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and clearly organized. The arguments flow logically from problem identification to solution and empirical validation, making the paper easy to follow and understand.
*   **Strong Empirical Evidence:** The experiments provide convincing evidence that Norm-POEM successfully mitigates propensity overfitting and achieves superior generalization performance compared to its predecessor, POEM. The ablation studies in Tables 2 and 3 effectively support the paper's central claims, showing that Norm-POEM is robust to loss translation (unlike POEM) and that variance regularization remains beneficial.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

*   **Scope of Evaluation:** While the experiments are well-controlled and convincingly demonstrate the method's advantages, they are limited to the same semi-synthetic setup and datasets from the original POEM paper [1]. The paper's impact could be substantially strengthened by evaluating Norm-POEM on a wider variety of benchmarks or, ideally, on a real-world application with genuine logged bandit data. This would provide more robust evidence that the observed improvements generalize to practical scenarios.
*   **Details on Optimization:** The paper states that the non-convex training objective is optimized using L-BFGS. While this is a reasonable choice, the discussion of the optimization process is quite brief. Given that the self-normalized estimator introduces a more complex, non-linear dependency on the model parameters in both the risk and variance terms, a more thorough discussion of the optimization challenges, convergence, or parameter sensitivity would have been valuable.
*   **Reporting of Experimental Variance:** In Table 1, results are presented as averages over 10 runs. To better assess the stability and statistical significance of the improvements, it would be helpful to also report a measure of variance, such as the standard deviation or standard error, for these key results.

In summary, this is a solid and well-executed paper that makes an important contribution to the field of learning from bandit feedback. It identifies a subtle but severe problem with a widely used estimator and proposes an effective, principled solution. The weaknesses are primarily related to the scope of the experimental validation rather than any fundamental flaws in the methodology. The work is a valuable advancement over prior art and is likely to be influential for researchers and practitioners working on off-policy evaluation and learning.
