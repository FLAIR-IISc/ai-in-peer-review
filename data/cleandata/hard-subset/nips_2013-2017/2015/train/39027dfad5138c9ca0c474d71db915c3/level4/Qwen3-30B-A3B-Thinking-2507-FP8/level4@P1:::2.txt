

This paper identifies a significant issue with the conventional counterfactual risk estimator used in batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF), proposing a self-normalized alternative that avoids this problem. The authors demonstrate that the standard unbiased estimator (Equation 2) suffers from "propensity overfitting" when applied to complex hypothesis spaces, where risk estimates can become arbitrarily biased, particularly for negative losses. They introduce a self-normalized estimator (Equation 7) that addresses these anomalies, leading to their proposed Norm-POEM algorithm for structured output prediction.

The paper builds on the Counterfactual Risk Minimization (CRM) principle established in prior work [1], which provides a framework for designing BLBF algorithms. The authors correctly identify a previously overlooked issue with the standard importance sampling estimator used in CRM, showing that it fails to maintain equivariance under translation of the loss function—meaning that adding a constant to the loss does not translate the risk estimate by the same constant, unlike in supervised learning. This theoretical insight is well-motivated and supported by their Example 1, which demonstrates how the conventional estimator can produce arbitrarily low risk estimates for overfitted hypotheses.

The authors' technical contributions are sound and well-justified. They prove that the self-normalized estimator resolves the equivariance issue and bounds the risk estimate within the loss range, making overfitting behavior more predictable. Their theoretical analysis is rigorous, and the empirical evaluation on multi-label classification datasets shows consistent improvements over the baseline POEM algorithm across multiple datasets and training sample sizes. The results demonstrate that Norm-POEM not only avoids propensity overfitting (as evidenced by the normalized propensity weights being close to 1) but also achieves performance close to a full-information CRF baseline.

The paper is well-structured and clearly written, with sufficient technical detail to allow reproduction of the results. The experimental setup follows established methodology from prior work [1], making the comparisons fair and meaningful. The authors also provide a thoughtful discussion of computational considerations, noting that Norm-POEM converges faster than POEM despite the additional normalization step.

I have only two minor concerns. First, the paper's description of the "anomaly" in the conventional estimator (Equation 2) could be clarified to emphasize that the issue is specific to the finite-sample behavior of the estimator, not its theoretical expectation. The estimator is unbiased in expectation but exhibits problematic finite-sample behavior, which is precisely what the authors demonstrate. Second, while the authors reference related work on control variates in importance sampling [12, 19, 22, 23], a more detailed comparison with recent variance-reduction techniques for counterfactual evaluation (e.g., [6, 11, 16]) would strengthen the discussion.

The significance of this work is substantial, as propensity overfitting represents a previously unrecognized problem that can severely undermine the performance of counterfactual learning algorithms. The self-normalized estimator provides a simple yet effective solution that directly addresses this issue while maintaining theoretical guarantees. The empirical results confirm that these theoretical benefits translate to improved generalization performance across multiple datasets.

Overall, this is a strong contribution to the field of counterfactual learning. The paper is technically sound, well-written, and makes a clear, significant advance over prior work. The proposed self-normalized estimator is a simple but powerful modification that should be widely adopted in counterfactual learning applications. The authors have thoroughly addressed the problem and provided compelling evidence for their solution.

Strengths:
- Identifies and correctly diagnoses a previously unrecognized problem (propensity overfitting) in counterfactual learning
- Proposes a theoretically grounded solution that resolves the identified issue
- Demonstrates consistent empirical improvements over the state-of-the-art baseline
- Provides rigorous theoretical analysis with clear proofs
- Well-structured and clearly written with sufficient detail for reproduction

Weaknesses:
- The description of the "anomaly" in Equation (2) could be more precise regarding its finite-sample nature
- Additional comparison with recent variance-reduction techniques for counterfactual evaluation would strengthen the related work section

I recommend acceptance as a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of counterfactual learning. The paper addresses a critical limitation in existing approaches, provides a theoretically sound solution, and demonstrates empirical improvements across multiple datasets. The work is clearly written, technically sound, and makes an important contribution to the state of the art.
