

This paper identifies a critical issue with the conventional counterfactual risk estimator used in batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF), specifically the propensity overfitting problem that arises when applying empirical risk minimization to complex hypothesis spaces. Building on Swaminathan and Joachims' 2015 work on Counterfactual Risk Minimization [1], the authors propose a self-normalized risk estimator that avoids this issue, leading to the Norm-POEM algorithm for structured output prediction. The paper demonstrates that the conventional estimator exhibits severe anomalies in risk estimation, particularly when used with ERM, and proves theoretically that the self-normalized estimator resolves these problems while maintaining consistency.

Quality: The paper is technically sound with rigorous theoretical analysis demonstrating that the self-normalized estimator avoids the propensity overfitting problem. The authors provide a detailed proof of consistency (Theorem 2) and carefully analyze the limitations of the conventional estimator (Section 4). The empirical evaluation on multiple multi-label classification datasets (Section 7) provides strong evidence of improved generalization performance and robustness. The authors also thoroughly address computational considerations, showing that Norm-POEM converges faster than POEM despite its more complex estimator.

Clarity: The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with a logical flow from problem identification to theoretical development to empirical validation. The mathematical notation is consistent and well-defined throughout. The authors provide sufficient detail for reproducibility, including specific experimental setups (Section 7), hyperparameter choices, and implementation details (e.g., use of L-BFGS for optimization). The introduction effectively frames the problem and motivates the contribution, while the related work section properly contextualizes the paper within the literature on BLBF and causal inference.

Originality: The paper makes a significant contribution by identifying and solving the propensity overfitting problem, which was previously unrecognized in the BLBF literature. While the CRM principle and POEM algorithm were established in Swaminathan and Joachims 2015, this work provides a novel solution to a critical limitation of the conventional estimator. The self-normalized estimator represents a meaningful technical advance, as it is not merely a variant of existing approaches but addresses a fundamental issue with the standard estimator. The paper clearly differentiates itself from prior work, particularly in Section 4 where the propensity overfitting phenomenon is rigorously characterized.

Significance: The results have substantial practical implications for applications of BLBF in real-world systems (e.g., ad placement, recommendation systems) where historical logged data is abundant but full-information feedback is unavailable. The demonstrated improvements in generalization performance and robustness (Table 1, Figure 1) suggest that the proposed method could become a standard approach for BLBF tasks. The surprising computational efficiency of Norm-POEM (Section 7) compared to POEM, despite its more complex estimator, further enhances its practical appeal. This work advances the state of the art in BLBF by providing a theoretically grounded solution to a previously unaddressed problem, with clear potential for widespread adoption in industry and academia.

Pro: The paper identifies a previously unrecognized problem in BLBF (propensity overfitting), provides a theoretically sound solution, demonstrates significant empirical improvements, and offers a practical algorithm with better computational properties than the original POEM.

Con: The paper does not explore the theoretical limits of the self-normalized estimator in extremely high-dimensional settings, though this limitation is common to many BLBF approaches and does not detract from the paper's main contribution.
