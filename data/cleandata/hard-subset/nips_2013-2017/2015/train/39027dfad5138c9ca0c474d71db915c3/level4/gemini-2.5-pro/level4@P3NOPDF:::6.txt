### Review

**Summary**

This paper addresses a key challenge in learning from logged bandit data using the Counterfactual Risk Minimization (CRM) principle. The authors identify a specific form of overfitting inherent to standard CRM estimators. They demonstrate that while CRM is consistent, it is susceptible to high variance, which allows a hypothesis to achieve low empirical risk by disproportionately fitting to examples that were logged with a high probability, potentially at the expense of generalization performance. To mitigate this issue, the paper proposes a novel, self-normalized estimator. This is achieved by introducing a multiplicative normalization term that effectively bounds the variance of the estimator while preserving its consistency. The experimental results demonstrate that this normalized approach consistently outperforms the standard, non-normalized CRM estimator.

**Strengths**

1.  **Clear Problem Formulation:** The primary strength of this paper lies in its clear identification and diagnosis of a subtle but significant overfitting problem in counterfactual learning. The insight that a model can "game" the CRM objective by focusing on high-probability examples is important and well-articulated.

2.  **Elegant and Principled Solution:** The proposed self-normalized estimator is an elegant and well-motivated solution. It directly targets the source of the high variance—the inverse propensity scores—without sacrificing the desirable property of consistency, which is crucial for this learning setting.

3.  **Strong Empirical Validation:** The experimental results compellingly support the authors' claims. The comparison against the standard, non-normalized CRM estimator across various settings clearly shows the practical benefits of the proposed technique in improving performance and reducing the effects of overfitting.

**Weaknesses and Questions for the Authors**

While the paper is strong, there are a few areas that could be clarified or expanded upon:

1.  **Comparison to Other Variance Reduction Techniques:** The experiments focus on the comparison between the standard and the normalized CRM. It would be valuable to understand how this approach relates to or compares with other variance reduction techniques from the off-policy evaluation literature (e.g., doubly robust estimators). A brief discussion contextualizing the proposed method within this broader landscape would strengthen the paper.

2.  **Sensitivity and Limitations:** The paper could benefit from a discussion on the potential limitations of the proposed approach. For instance, are there specific data distributions or scenarios (e.g., when propensity scores are extremely small across the board) where the normalization could be less effective or require careful handling?

**Conclusion and Recommendation**

In summary, this is a well-written paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of counterfactual learning from bandit feedback. It successfully identifies a key weakness in the standard CRM framework and proposes a theoretically sound and empirically validated solution. The work is clear, concise, and the results are convincing. The identified weaknesses are minor and can be addressed with additional discussion. I recommend acceptance.
