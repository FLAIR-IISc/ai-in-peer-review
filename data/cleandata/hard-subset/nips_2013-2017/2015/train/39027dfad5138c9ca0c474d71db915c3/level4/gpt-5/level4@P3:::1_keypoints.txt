- Paper studies batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF) under CRM and identifies a learning-time failure of IPS called "propensity overfitting."
- Propensity overfitting: empirical risk can be driven to degenerate values by manipulating importance-weight sums rather than fitting low-loss examples.
- Authors propose using self-normalized importance sampling (SNIS) as a multiplicative control variate to form a bounded, equivariant, strongly consistent risk estimator.
- SNIS is asymptotically unbiased, equivariant to additive loss shifts, bounded within the loss range, and has strong consistency (Theorem 2).
- Building on SNIS, the paper introduces Norm-POEM, a CRM-style learner for exponential-family policies that adds a variance penalty term.
- Empirical results on multi-label datasets show Norm-POEM consistently outperforms POEM, gives better diagnostics (normalized weight sum ≈ 1), and trains competitively with L-BFGS.
- Paper cites relevant off-policy evaluation and Monte Carlo literature; main novelty is framing propensity overfitting and operationalizing SNIS in CRM.
- Technical soundness is good: estimator construction, consistency, boundedness, and variance approximation are standard and correct.
- Theory lacks a CRM-style generalization bound tailored to SNIS (analogue to empirical-Bernstein/CRM analysis).
- Empirical evaluation follows the supervised-to-bandit protocol but uses a narrow baseline set (missing clipped IPS, DR, WIS).
- Variance estimator relies on asymptotic (delta method) approximations without finite-sample guarantees.
- Clarity is generally good; distinction between loss overfitting and propensity overfitting is insightful but relation to weight variance and clipping could be clearer.
- Originality: applying SNIS itself is not novel, but articulating propensity overfitting and integrating SNIS into CRM with diagnostics is original and useful.
- Significance: simple, principled fix for IPS pathology with potential high impact for large-scale interactive systems if validated against stronger baselines.
- Pros: clear identification of pathology, principled SNIS fix with desirable properties, practical Norm-POEM implementation with code, solid empirical evidence and diagnostics, alignment with conference topics.
- Cons: missing theoretical generalization bound, limited baselines (no DR/clipped IPS/WIS), asymptotic variance/bias reliance, and some unclear assumptions (e.g., full support).
- Requests: clarify interaction between propensity overfitting and IPS high variance, and when SNIS helps via variance reduction vs addressing non-equivariance.
- Requests: make Example 1 inequality reasoning explicit to avoid confusion.
- Requests: soften claim about prior unbiased estimators—distinguish IPS vs clipped IPS and discuss how clipping affects propensity overfitting.
- Requests: quantify finite-sample SNIS bias (e.g., O(1/n)) and compare bias–variance trade-offs with clipping and DR, with empirical curves if possible.
- Requests: add DR (Dudík et al.), clipped IPS, and WIS baselines and consider a DR+SNIS variant.
- Requests: report sensitivity to hyperparameters M and λ, fraction of weights clipped, and guidance for choosing them.
- Requests: clarify impact of logging policies missing full support and whether unsupported regions are detected/excluded.
- Minor suggestions: highlight Ŝ(h)=1 diagnostic earlier, empirically show translation invariance, document optimization details and weight norms, and ensure consistent notation.
- Reproducibility: positive—datasets, protocol, and code provided; add scripts for additional baselines to strengthen reproducibility.
- Overall evaluation: well-motivated, practically valuable contribution identifying IPS failure mode and offering a grounded remedy; recommend accept pending broader baselines and minor clarifications.
