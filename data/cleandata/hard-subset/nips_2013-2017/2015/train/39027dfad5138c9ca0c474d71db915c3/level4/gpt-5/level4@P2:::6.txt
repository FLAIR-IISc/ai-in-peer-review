Summary
The paper addresses batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF) under the Counterfactual Risk Minimization (CRM) framework. It identifies a key failure mode of the standard inverse propensity scoring (IPS) estimator used in prior BLBF work: propensity overfitting, where hypotheses exploit variability in logging propensities to achieve spuriously low empirical risk. To mitigate this, the authors propose a self-normalized importance sampling estimator (a multiplicative control variate) that normalizes by the sum of importance weights. This estimator is bounded and equivariant to loss translations, and is strongly consistent. Incorporating it into CRM yields a new algorithm, Norm-POEM, for structured prediction with exponential-family models. Experiments on multi-label classification datasets (using a supervised-to-bandit simulation) show that Norm-POEM consistently outperforms the conventional IPS-based approach (POEM), is more robust to loss translations, and reduces propensity overfitting as measured by the normalized weight diagnostic.

Relation to prior work
The paper builds on importance sampling for off-policy evaluation and learning, including clipping/truncation and control variates, and connects to causal inference, covariate shift, and reinforcement learning. It complements prior CRM generalization bounds by showing how IPS-based learning can overfit via propensities and by proposing self-normalization as a principled fix. The work relates to doubly robust and regression estimators but focuses on multiplicative control variates, which have been less explored in BLBF learning objectives.

Strengths
- Clear diagnosis of propensity overfitting with intuitive examples and empirical evidence.
- Principled use of self-normalization that ensures bounded, equivariant risk estimates and strong consistency.
- Integration into a practical learning algorithm (Norm-POEM) with an approximate variance estimator and variance regularization in the CRM spirit.
- Consistent empirical improvements over POEM across datasets and training sample sizes; robustness to loss translations; better-behaved propensity weight sums.
- Relevant to many counterfactual learning applications (ads, recommendation, search) and likely to influence practice.

Weaknesses and questions
- The self-normalized estimator is biased in finite samples; the paper could better characterize conditions where this bias may hurt, or compare to doubly robust methods.
- The variance estimator for the ratio estimator is approximate and requires clipping; more discussion of sensitivity to M and λ and the resulting bias–variance trade-off would help.
- Experiments rely on supervised-to-bandit simulation with CRF logging policies; results on real logged data or different logging policies (e.g., deterministic with exploration noise) would strengthen claims.
- Optimization uses L-BFGS on a non-convex objective; while convergence is reported as fast, a deeper analysis of scalability and runtime vs. model size would be useful.

Quality
Technically sound and well supported. The theoretical arguments (equivariance, boundedness, strong consistency) are standard for self-normalized IS and appropriately adapted. The empirical evaluation is careful and aligns with the claims, including diagnostics for propensity overfitting.

Clarity
Generally well written and organized. The phenomenon of propensity overfitting is clearly explained, and the proposed fix is motivated via control variates. Minor suggestions: clarify when self-normalization may underperform (e.g., when weight variance is small), detail the calibration protocol for M and λ, and provide more implementation details for reproducibility.

Originality
The identification of propensity overfitting in IPS-based CRM and the use of self-normalization as a learning objective are novel and distinct from prior BLBF work focused on unbiased IPS, clipping, and doubly robust estimators.

Significance
High. The work addresses a practical and theoretically motivated failure mode in counterfactual learning and provides a robust alternative that improves generalization in structured prediction tasks. It should be of broad interest to the NIPS community.

Pros
- Identifies a real and important issue (propensity overfitting) in IPS-based learning.
- Provides a simple, principled fix via self-normalization with strong theoretical properties.
- Demonstrates consistent empirical gains and diagnostic improvements.
- Maintains compatibility with CRM and exponential-family models.

Cons
- Finite-sample bias and sensitivity to clipping/regularization not fully characterized.
- Evaluation limited to simulated bandit data; no real-world logs.
- Less discussion of alternatives (e.g., doubly robust learning objectives) as baselines.

Overall assessment
A strong and timely contribution that improves the robustness of counterfactual learning from logged bandit data. I recommend acceptance. My confidence is moderate.
