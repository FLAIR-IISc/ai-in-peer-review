This paper addresses the problem of learning from logged bandit data, a setting where a learner must use historical data generated by a different policy to train a new one. The standard approach, Counterfactual Risk Minimization (CRM), uses importance weighting to correct for the selection bias in the logged data. While the CRM estimator is consistent, the authors compellingly argue that it is susceptible to a specific form of overfitting. This issue arises because a hypothesis can achieve artificially low empirical risk by fitting well to examples that had a high probability of being recorded under the logging policy, leading to a high-variance estimator that may generalize poorly.

To remedy this, the paper proposes a self-normalized CRM estimator. This technique modifies the standard objective by normalizing the importance-weighted risk by the sum of the importance weights. This normalization acts as a variance reduction mechanism, effectively preventing the estimator from being dominated by a few high-probability examples, while crucially maintaining the estimator's consistency.

The experimental results validate the authors' claims, demonstrating that the proposed normalized counterfactual risk estimator consistently outperforms the standard, non-normalized version.

**Strengths:**

*   **Problem Significance:** The paper identifies and clearly articulates a subtle but important practical problem in off-policy evaluation and learning. The high variance of importance-weighted estimators is a known challenge, and this work provides a sharp analysis of how it manifests as an overfitting problem in the context of risk minimization.
*   **Elegant Solution:** The proposed self-normalized estimator is a simple, intuitive, and effective solution. It provides a practical method to control the estimator's variance without sacrificing the desirable theoretical property of consistency.
*   **Clear Empirical Support:** The claims are well-supported by experimental evidence, which shows a clear and consistent performance improvement for the normalized technique over its non-normalized counterpart.

**Weaknesses:**

*   While the paper establishes the consistency of the proposed estimator, a more detailed theoretical analysis of its variance reduction properties or finite-sample bounds would further strengthen the contribution.

**Recommendation:**

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of counterfactual learning. It successfully identifies a key practical challenge with a standard method and proposes an elegant, effective, and theoretically-grounded solution. The work is well-motivated and the results are convincing. Therefore, I recommend this paper for acceptance.
