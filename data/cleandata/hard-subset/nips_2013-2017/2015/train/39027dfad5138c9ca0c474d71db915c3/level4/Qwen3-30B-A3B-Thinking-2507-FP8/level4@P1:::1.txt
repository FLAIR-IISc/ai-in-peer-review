

The paper identifies a critical flaw in the standard counterfactual risk estimator used for batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF), introducing a novel self-normalized estimator to resolve "propensity overfitting." This problem arises when the conventional unbiased estimator (e.g., inverse propensity scoring with clipping) produces severely biased risk estimates under empirical risk minimization, particularly in complex hypothesis spaces. The authors formally define propensity overfitting as a phenomenon where the estimator minimizes a sum of propensity weights (Ŝ(h)) rather than the true risk, leading to unbounded risk estimates that violate loss equivariance. Their solution—replacing the estimator with a self-normalized variant (R̂<sub>SN</sub>(h))—provides theoretical guarantees (strong consistency, equivariance, boundedness) and enables a new algorithm, Norm-POEM, for structured prediction. Empirical validation across multi-label classification datasets demonstrates consistent outperformance of POEM (the prior state-of-the-art BLBF method) in generalization, robustness, and avoidance of propensity overfitting.

**Strengths:**  
The identification of propensity overfitting is a significant theoretical contribution that clarifies a previously unaddressed vulnerability in BLBF. The authors rigorously distinguish this from variance-related issues (e.g., clipped importance weights), demonstrating through examples that propensity overfitting persists even after loss translation or clipping. The self-normalized estimator (Equation 7) elegantly resolves all identified anomalies: it is equivariant under loss translation, bounded within the loss range, and provably consistent (Theorem 2). Norm-POEM’s empirical results are compelling—consistently outperforming POEM across datasets (Table 1), avoiding propensity overfitting (Table 2), and achieving faster convergence despite higher per-iteration cost (Section 7). The thorough experimental design (Supervised→Bandit methodology, validation on held-out data, and ablation studies) strongly supports the claimed advantages.

**Weaknesses:**  
The paper’s description of prior work requires refinement. The claim that "virtually all existing works on BLBF have focused on a particular unbiased estimator" (Section 1) is misleading; prior BLBF methods (e.g., [1, 5, 6]) explicitly use clipped importance weights (e.g., R̂<sub>M</sub>(h)), which are biased. The authors should clarify that the *unclipped* estimator suffers from propensity overfitting, while *clipped* variants (used in practice) mitigate variance but not this specific problem. Additionally, the example in Section 4 (Example 1) requires careful re-explanation: when n ≪ k, hoverfit’s risk estimate R̂(hoverfit) = −k (not smaller than −2), which is the core of the anomaly. The draft’s confusion ("should be even smaller than −2?") misrepresents the example’s point. The paper also understates the computational cost of Norm-POEM’s variance estimator (Equation 8), though experiments show faster convergence compensates.

**Novelty and Significance:**  
The paper distinguishes itself by identifying a *new* overfitting mechanism (propensity overfitting) beyond standard loss overfitting, with clear implications for BLBF practitioners. This advances the state of the art by resolving a practical vulnerability that undermines prior methods’ reliability. The self-normalized estimator is a well-motivated extension of control variates (citing [19, 22]), and the empirical validation confirms its practical impact. The work aligns with NIPS’ focus on rigorous, impactful contributions to machine learning theory and applications.

**Recommendation:**  
This paper makes a strong contribution to BLBF, with novel theoretical insights, a well-motivated solution, and robust empirical validation. The identified flaw (propensity overfitting) is a critical gap in existing BLBF literature, and the proposed fix is both theoretically sound and practically effective. While minor clarifications are needed on prior work and the example, these do not undermine the core contribution. The significance is high—this work directly enables more reliable offline learning for real-world systems (e.g., ad placement, recommendations) using historical logs. The code availability further strengthens reproducibility. **I recommend acceptance.**
