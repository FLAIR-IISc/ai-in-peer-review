This paper presents an approach for "counterfactual learning," a scenario that can be viewed as a specialization of multi-label learning where only bandit feedback (i.e., the label for a single class) is available for each sample. While this framing provides context, the paper's core contribution is a specific and targeted one: to improve the POEM estimator recently introduced by Swaminathan and Joachims (2015). The proposed method, Norm-POEM, demonstrates strong empirical performance, and overall, the paper is convincing and warrants publication. However, my recommendation is contingent on the authors addressing a significant issue in their theoretical justification.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Strong Empirical Results:** The experimental evaluation is a clear strength of the paper. The proposed Norm-POEM estimator consistently outperforms the original POEM across all datasets. Furthermore, its performance is shown to be close to that of a Conditional Random Field (CRF) trained with full multi-label supervision, which is an impressive result that validates the effectiveness of the proposed approach.

2.  **Clear Practical Motivation:** The paper correctly identifies a key practical weakness in the POEM estimator (Equation 2). The estimator is sensitive to its denominator, the propensity score `pi`, which can be zero or near-zero for samples rarely or never observed in the logging policy's data. This can lead to high variance and instability. The proposed modification in Equation (7) effectively mitigates the influence of small `pi` values through a normalization term, providing a sound practical basis for the new estimator.

**Major Concern:**

My primary criticism relates to the theoretical commentary and justification surrounding the main equation (2). The argument appears to be based on a fundamental misunderstanding of mathematical expectation.

An expectation is defined as a sum (or integral) of a random variable weighted by a probability measure, i.e., `E[f(x)] = sum f(x)p(x)`. In this formulation, `f(x)` is the quantity whose expectation is being taken, and `p(x)` is the probability measure.

In Equation (2), the authors seem to incorrectly identify the loss term as the expected quantity `f(x)`. In fact, the loss is incorporated into the probability measure `p(x)`, while the quantity being averaged is simply a constant (1). Because of this misinterpretation, the authors' subsequent claim that Equation (2) has an "anomaly" due to its lack of invariance to loss translation is technically incorrect. Such invariance is not a required property when the loss function is part of the probability measure itself.

This flawed premise undermines the motivation presented in the following paragraphs and in Example 1, which is constructed to demonstrate a problem that does not exist as described. While the proposed estimator is ultimately effective for different, more practical reasons (as noted above), its theoretical justification as presented is unsound.

**Recommendation:**

This paper introduces a valuable and empirically successful improvement to an existing estimator. The practical motivation is clear, and the experimental results are convincing. However, the flawed theoretical argument is a significant weakness that should be corrected.

I recommend acceptance, but with the strong condition that the authors thoroughly revise their commentary and motivation. The justification for Norm-POEM should be re-framed to focus on the valid practical issue of estimator instability due to small propensity scores, rather than the incorrect argument about loss-translation invariance. If the authors graciously make these modifications, the paper will represent a solid contribution to the field.
