Summary and relation to prior work:
This paper identifies a failure mode—propensity overfitting—of the conventional inverse-propensity (IPS) counterfactual risk estimator commonly used within the Counterfactual Risk Minimization (CRM) framework for batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF). To address this, the authors propose replacing the IPS estimator with a self-normalized (SN) estimator that uses a multiplicative control variate (the sum of importance weights). They argue that the SN estimator is bounded and equivariant under loss translations, thereby avoiding degenerate behaviors of IPS. Building on this, they introduce Norm-POEM, which instantiates CRM for linear-exponential models (CRF-like hypothesis class) using the SN estimator and an approximate variance estimate for regularization. Empirically, Norm-POEM outperforms POEM [1] across several multi-label datasets using the standard Supervised→Bandit setup, with improved robustness to loss translations and better generalization.

Strengths:
- Clear identification and analysis of “propensity overfitting” as distinct from conventional loss overfitting, including illustrative examples.
- A principled use of multiplicative control variates leading to the self-normalized estimator, with desirable properties (boundedness, equivariance, strong consistency).
- A concrete algorithm (Norm-POEM) that integrates SN estimation into CRM and shows consistent empirical gains over POEM.
- Relevance to a growing body of work on BLBF and off-policy evaluation/learning; connects to variance reduction and importance weighting literature.

Weaknesses and clarifications needed:
- Some aspects of the setup and notation could be clarified (see below), especially the treatment of the loss scaling and stochasticity of feedback.

Clarification requested (rebuttal):
- According to Section 4.1 in [1], the CRM approach assumes δ ∈ [−1, 0] (and suggests rescaling a general loss to this range). My understanding is that the same assumption applies here to Norm-POEM—i.e., both the CRM objective in (3) and the variance approximation in (8) use the rescaled δ ∈ [−1, 0]. If so, I do not understand references to “δ > 0” in the experiments (e.g., Table 1). Please clarify whether you apply loss translations after rescaling, how these interact with self-normalization, and exactly which δ the reported numbers correspond to.

Comments on the problem setup:
- In Section 3, δ(x, y) is presented as a deterministic function (line ~110). In many BLBF settings (including classification with noisy labels), the observed feedback is stochastic: for a given x and played y, the realized δ(x, y) can vary across log entries due to latent randomness in the labeling or environment. Conceptually, δ should be modeled as a random variable, with the “true” loss for policy evaluation defined as an expectation over this randomness. In other words, equation (1) is more generally R(h) = E_x E_{y∼h(x)} E[δ | x, y]. This does not change the IPS estimator in (2), but it would make the foundational setup more general and precise.

Minor correctness/notation issues:
- Lines 170–171: It should read h(y | x) / h0(y | x). Also, it may be worth noting explicitly that the lack of equivariance of IPS under additive translations was already pointed out in [1].
- Line 276: Use argmin (you are minimizing risk), not argmax.
- Line 319: Replace overbars with hats to maintain consistency with earlier notation.

This is an interesting and timely follow-up to [1]. The analysis of propensity overfitting is insightful, and the use of a multiplicative control variate to form a self-normalized estimator is well-motivated by the importance sampling literature. Empirically, Norm-POEM shows clear and consistent improvements over POEM on BLBF, a problem of increasing practical and research interest.

Pros for acceptance:
- Identifies and formalizes a previously underappreciated overfitting mode in BLBF with IPS.
- Provides a principled estimator (self-normalized IPS) that addresses equivariance and boundedness issues, with strong consistency.
- Demonstrates consistent empirical gains and robustness to loss translations across datasets.
- Technically grounded in established variance-reduction methods while making a concrete contribution to CRM-based learning.

Cons for acceptance:
- Some clarity issues in the presentation (loss scaling to [−1, 0], interpretation of “δ > 0” in experiments, and the deterministic treatment of feedback).
- Notational slips and minor inaccuracies that could confuse readers.
- The variance approximation for the ratio estimator (8) and its implications for optimization could be discussed in more depth, including conditions for reliable asymptotics.

Overall, with clarifications and minor revisions, I believe this work makes a meaningful contribution to counterfactual learning from logged bandit feedback.
