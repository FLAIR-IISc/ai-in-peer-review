Due to travel constraints, my review has been limited in scope; however, I found the paper quite interesting and potentially valuable. I apologize if I have missed or misinterpreted any details.

The paper addresses the problem of evaluating a hypothesis given data observed from a different hypothesis. When these hypotheses are non-deterministic (e.g., defined by conditional distributions), the loss under the hypothesis being investigated can be estimated by reweighting the data points, as shown in the equation between (1) and (2). 

The authors identify a problem – "propensity overfitting" – that is related to, but distinct from, the issue of large variance in the weights. The examples suggest this overfitting occurs when there is insufficient exploration, specifically when, for a given x, few or no data points are observed corresponding to the outcomes predicted by the hypothesis under evaluation. While a similar rationale explains the large variance of the weights, a more detailed discussion of the relationship between these two problems would be beneficial.

I did not have sufficient time to fully evaluate the technical details or novelty of the proposed solution, but the core idea appears sensible. 

I have a few specific questions and concerns:

- l. 199: I am unclear about this statement. If *n* is small compared to *k*, we observe only a limited number of data points where *xi* = *yi*. Wouldn't this imply that  \hat R(h^) should be even smaller than -2? Am I missing something?
- The statement “unbiased counterfactual risk estimator used in prior works on BLBF [4, 5, 1]” may be inaccurate. While I am not familiar with all three papers, I suspect they likely employ techniques like clipping, which introduce bias in exchange for reduced variance.
- I appreciate the authors’ decision to make their code publicly available.

In summary, while I was unable to thoroughly verify the technical soundness of the paper, it presents interesting ideas that warrant further consideration. Therefore, I recommend acceptance.
