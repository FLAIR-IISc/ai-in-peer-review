

This paper addresses a critical issue in batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF), a setting where algorithms learn from historical data without interactive exploration. The authors identify a problem they term "propensity overfitting" with the conventional counterfactual risk estimator used in prior BLBF algorithms (specifically, the inverse propensity score estimator), and propose a self-normalized estimator to address this issue, leading to their Norm-POEM algorithm for structured output prediction.

The paper builds on the Counterfactual Risk Minimization (CRM) principle introduced in [1], which uses importance sampling to estimate the risk of a new policy based on historical data collected under a logging policy. The authors correctly identify that the conventional estimator has unbounded variance due to small propensity scores, a well-known issue in the field (addressed in prior work through clipping [5, 15]). However, their identification of "propensity overfitting" appears to be based on a misunderstanding of the estimator's properties.

The main technical issue concerns the authors' claim that the conventional estimator R̂(h) (equation 2) is not equivariant to translation of the loss (page 2, second paragraph). This is incorrect. If δ(x,y) → δ(x,y) + C, then R(h) = E[δ(x,y)] → R(h) + C, and the estimator R̂(h) should also shift by C. The authors' Example 1 is based on this incorrect premise, which undermines their analysis of propensity overfitting. The estimator R̂(h) is designed to estimate R(h), not δ(x,y), so it should be equivariant to loss translation. This fundamental misunderstanding affects the entire motivation for the self-normalized estimator.

The self-normalized estimator proposed in equation (7) is indeed a valid alternative that addresses the unbounded variance issue of the conventional estimator. The estimator R̂SN(h) = [∑ δi h(yi|x)/pi] / [∑ h(yi|x)/pi] has the desirable properties of being bounded within the loss range and equivariant to loss translation, as the authors correctly establish in Theorem 2. The authors' empirical results (Section 7) demonstrate that Norm-POEM using this estimator outperforms POEM on multiple datasets, which is a strong practical validation of their approach.

Strengths:
1. The paper identifies a real limitation in current BLBF methods that can lead to poor generalization.
2. The self-normalized estimator is theoretically sound and resolves the identified problem.
3. The empirical results are thorough and demonstrate consistent improvements over the baseline across multiple datasets.
4. The paper is well-structured and clearly written.

Weaknesses:
1. The initial technical error in the motivation section undermines the paper's foundation.
2. The paper does not sufficiently address why prior work (including [1], which is cited as the foundation for their approach) did not identify this issue earlier.
3. The contribution could be better situated relative to other variance reduction techniques in importance sampling, such as additive control variates [6, 16].

Quality: The paper's empirical results are strong and the theoretical analysis of the self-normalized estimator is sound. However, the initial technical error in the motivation weakens the overall quality.

Clarity: The paper is well-written and clearly presents the problem, proposed solution, and empirical results. The technical error in the motivation is the main clarity issue.

Originality: The specific problem of "propensity overfitting" as identified by the authors is novel, though the self-normalized estimator itself is a known technique in importance sampling. The application to BLBF and the empirical validation of the approach are original contributions.

Significance: The paper addresses a practical problem in BLBF that affects real-world applications like ad placement and recommendation systems. The proposed solution leads to consistently better performance in experiments, making this a significant contribution to the field.

The paper's main contribution is the empirical demonstration that the self-normalized estimator improves generalization in BLBF, despite the flawed motivation. The authors should correct the technical error in the motivation section and better contextualize their contribution relative to prior work on variance reduction in importance sampling.

Pro:
- The self-normalized estimator is a valid solution to a real problem in BLBF
- Empirical results consistently show improvements over the baseline across multiple datasets
- The paper is well-structured and clearly written

Con:
- The initial technical error undermines the motivation for the paper
- The contribution is not fully situated relative to existing work on variance reduction in importance sampling

I recommend acceptance with major revisions to address the technical error in the motivation section and to better contextualize the contribution relative to prior work. The authors should clarify why the conventional estimator's behavior (as they describe it) was not identified in prior work, and more thoroughly situate their contribution within the broader literature on variance reduction techniques in importance sampling. If these issues are addressed, this would be a strong contribution to the field of counterfactual learning.
