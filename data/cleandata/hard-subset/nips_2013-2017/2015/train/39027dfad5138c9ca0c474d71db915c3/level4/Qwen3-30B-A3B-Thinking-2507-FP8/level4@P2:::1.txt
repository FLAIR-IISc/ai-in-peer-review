

This paper addresses a critical issue in batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF), a setting where systems learn from historical logs without interactive feedback. The authors identify a previously uncharacterized problem they term "propensity overfitting" in conventional counterfactual risk estimators, which occurs when the unbiased estimator (Equation 2) produces severely biased risk estimates for complex hypothesis spaces. They propose a self-normalized risk estimator (Equation 7) that provably avoids this issue, leading to a new learning algorithm called Norm-POEM for structured output prediction.

The paper makes a significant contribution by revealing a fundamental flaw in the standard approach to counterfactual risk estimation. While previous work (e.g., [1, 5, 6]) has focused on variance reduction through clipping or control variates, the authors demonstrate that the conventional estimator suffers from a more insidious problem: its risk estimates can be arbitrarily biased due to the interplay between hypothesis space capacity and propensity weights. The self-normalized estimator resolves this by ensuring risk estimates remain bounded within the loss range and are equivariant under loss translation—properties lacking in the conventional approach. The theoretical analysis (Theorems 1-2) is sound and provides a clear justification for the proposed solution.

The empirical evaluation across multiple multi-label classification datasets (Table 1, Figure 1) demonstrates consistent improvements of Norm-POEM over POEM (the baseline using the conventional estimator). The authors provide compelling evidence that Norm-POEM avoids propensity overfitting (Table 2), with the sum of propensity weights Ŝ(ĥ) remaining close to its expected value of 1, unlike POEM. The computational efficiency of Norm-POEM (Section 7) is also noteworthy—despite the added normalization step, the algorithm converges faster than POEM due to more stable optimization.

The paper is well-written and clearly organized. The connection to related work (Section 2) is thorough, though the distinction between propensity overfitting and variance issues could be clarified. The authors properly contextualize their contribution relative to prior work on importance sampling, causal inference, and off-policy evaluation.

**Strengths:**
- Identifies and rigorously characterizes a novel problem (propensity overfitting) that affects BLBF algorithms
- Proposes a theoretically sound solution (self-normalized estimator) with clear advantages over conventional approaches
- Provides comprehensive empirical validation across multiple datasets
- The code availability and detailed experimental setup enhance reproducibility
- The paper is well-structured and clearly written

**Weaknesses:**
- The claim that "virtually all existing works on BLBF have focused on a particular unbiased estimator" (Abstract) requires qualification. Many prior works [5, 1] explicitly use clipped estimators (R̂M(h)), which are biased by design, making the "unbiased" characterization misleading. The authors should clarify this distinction.
- The example in Section 4 (l. 199) needs clarification: When n << k, the sample contains few distinct x_i values, meaning the overfit hypothesis hoverfit would assign f(x_i) = y_i for only those few x_i values, not all. Consequently, R̂(hoverfit) = -k/n rather than -k (where n is the number of distinct x_i values in the sample). The current phrasing may mislead readers.
- While the paper demonstrates that the self-normalized estimator avoids propensity overfitting, it could better justify why this approach is superior to other control variate methods (e.g., additive control variates in [6]) for this specific problem.

**Originality:** The paper identifies a new phenomenon (propensity overfitting) that distinguishes this work from prior BLBF research. While the self-normalized estimator has been used in other contexts [12, 19], the authors are the first to recognize its utility in addressing this specific problem in counterfactual learning. The connection to the CRM principle [1] and the derivation of Norm-POEM represent a novel application of this technique.

**Significance:** This work addresses a fundamental limitation in a widely applicable learning paradigm (BLBF), which has direct implications for improving real-world systems like ad placement and recommendation engines. The consistent empirical improvements across multiple datasets demonstrate that the proposed solution has practical value, potentially advancing the state of the art in counterfactual learning.

**Quality:** The theoretical claims are well-supported by theorems and proofs. The empirical methodology is rigorous, with careful experimental design (Supervised 7→ Bandit setup) and thorough analysis. The authors are transparent about limitations (e.g., the need for variance regularization even with the self-normalized estimator, Table 3).

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally clear and well-organized. The problem statement, theoretical development, and empirical results flow logically. The key contributions (propensity overfitting and the self-normalized estimator) are clearly articulated.

**Recommendation:** I recommend acceptance. The paper makes a significant theoretical contribution by identifying and solving a previously unrecognized problem in counterfactual learning. The proposed solution is theoretically sound, empirically validated, and has clear practical implications. While the paper could improve its distinction between propensity overfitting and variance issues, and provide more context about prior work's use of clipped estimators, these are minor points that don't undermine the paper's core contribution.

Pro: Identifies a novel problem (propensity overfitting) with clear theoretical and empirical validation; self-normalized estimator is theoretically sound and practically effective; comprehensive empirical evaluation across multiple datasets; well-written and clear.

Con: Misleading characterization of prior work's use of unbiased estimators; example in Section 4 requires clarification; limited discussion of why multiplicative control variates are superior to other variance reduction techniques for this specific problem.
