This paper addresses the problem of Batch Learning from Logged Bandit Feedback (BLBF), a setting crucial for improving interactive systems using historical data. The authors identify a significant flaw in the conventional unbiased counterfactual risk estimator used in this domain, which they term "propensity overfitting." This issue arises when learning over complex hypothesis spaces, as the estimator can be minimized by manipulating propensity weights rather than by fitting the true loss function, leading to arbitrarily poor risk estimates.

As a remedy, the paper proposes replacing the standard estimator with a self-normalized risk estimator, which is derived using multiplicative control variates. This alternative estimator is shown to be equivariant to translations in the loss function and bounded by the range of the observed losses, thus neatly avoiding the propensity overfitting problem. Building on this improved estimator and the Counterfactual Risk Minimization (CRM) principle from prior work [1], the authors introduce a new learning algorithm, Norm-POEM. The empirical evaluation on several multi-label classification tasks demonstrates that Norm-POEM consistently and significantly outperforms POEM [1], the previous state-of-the-art method which uses the conventional estimator.

**Strengths**

1.  **Novel and Important Problem Identification:** The key contribution of this paper is the identification and clear articulation of the "propensity overfitting" problem. This is a subtle but severe issue with the standard importance-sampling-based estimator that was not previously highlighted. Understanding this failure mode is a significant contribution to the field of counterfactual learning.

2.  **Principled and Elegant Solution:** The proposed solution—using a self-normalized estimator—is well-motivated by the literature on control variates and importance sampling. It directly addresses the identified anomalies of the unbiased estimator (lack of equivariance, unboundedness) in a principled manner.

3.  **Strong Empirical Results:** The experiments provide compelling evidence for the paper's claims. Norm-POEM is shown to achieve substantially better generalization performance than POEM across multiple datasets and training set sizes. The analysis in Table 2, which shows that the sum of propensity weights `Ŝ(ĥ)` for Norm-POEM is stable and close to its expectation of 1, provides direct support for the claim that it mitigates propensity overfitting.

4.  **Significance and Relevance:** BLBF is an increasingly important problem with direct applications in search, recommendation, and computational advertising. By providing a more robust and effective learning algorithm, this work represents a clear advance in a highly relevant research area.

**Weaknesses and Questions for the Authors**

1.  **Clarity on Loss Function Scaling:** A key point requiring clarification is the treatment of the loss function `delta(x,y)`. The original CRM framework [1, Section 4.1] states that the approach is theoretically grounded for losses scaled to the range `[-1, 0]`. It is unclear if the same assumption holds for the CRM objective used by Norm-POEM. If so, it is confusing how the experiments using positive Hamming loss (e.g., Table 2, right, labeled "delta > 0") were conducted. Please clarify the required range for `delta` in your framework and detail how the losses were handled in the experiments.

2.  **Generality of the Problem Formulation:** The problem setup in Section 3 defines the feedback `delta(x, y)` as a deterministic function. This formulation could be more general. In many real-world scenarios, such as classification with noisy labels or stochastic rewards, the feedback for a given pair `(x, y)` is itself a random variable. The presentation would be stronger and more broadly applicable if the risk definition in Equation (1) and the subsequent analysis were presented in this more general setting, for instance, by including an additional expectation over the randomness of `delta`.

3.  **Minor Corrections:** Several minor corrections would improve the manuscript's clarity:
    *   Line 170-171: The expression `δ(x, y) min { M, h(y|x)h0(y|x) }` seems to have a typo. Based on the context of importance sampling, it should likely involve a ratio, such as `h(y|x) / h0(y|x)`.
    *   The paper notes that the unbiased estimator is not equivariant. It might be worth mentioning that this was also pointed out in [1].
    *   Line 319 (in the caption for Figure 1, not numbered): The notation `R̄(h)` with a bar is used for the test set loss. For consistency with the rest of the paper (e.g., `R̂(h)`), it would be clearer to use a hat.

**Summary and Recommendation**

This is an interesting and valuable follow-up to the work on Counterfactual Risk Minimization [1]. The paper makes a strong contribution by identifying the novel problem of propensity overfitting and proposing a well-founded and effective solution. The empirical results clearly demonstrate a significant improvement over the state-of-the-art on a problem of practical importance. While there are minor points of clarification needed regarding the experimental setup and problem formulation, the core ideas are sound and significant. This paper advances our understanding of learning from logged bandit feedback and is a solid contribution to the field.
