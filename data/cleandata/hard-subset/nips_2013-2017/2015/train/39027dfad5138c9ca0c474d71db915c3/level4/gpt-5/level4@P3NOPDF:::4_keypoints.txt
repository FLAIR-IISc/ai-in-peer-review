- Proposal: NORM-POEM introduces a normalized inverse-propensity approach (multiplicative control variate) to mitigate propensity overfitting in batch learning from logged bandit feedback.  
- Empirical claim: NORM-POEM yields clear improvements over POEM across multiple tasks.  

Major questions for rebuttal/clarification:
- Loss scaling: Clarify whether losses were rescaled to [-1,0] in experiments, how rescaling was done, and whether theoretical guarantees hold if unscaled (delta>0) losses were used.  
- Theory assumptions: If delta>0 denotes pre-rescaling loss, state this explicitly and specify replaced boundedness assumptions and their impact on guarantees.  
- Feedback randomness: Model delta(x,y) as possibly random (e.g., label noise); include expectation over feedback in true risk and confirm empirical estimator remains unbiased/consistent, stating any needed independence/SUTVA assumptions.  

Technical and presentation comments:
- Notation fixes: ratio should be h(y|x)/h0(y|x); use argmin at line 276; replace overbars with hats at line 319.  
- Related work: Acknowledge prior note that normalized estimator is non-linear in h (as in [1]) and clarify how analysis extends/refines that.  
- Assumptions & robustness: Spell out boundedness assumptions used in concentration arguments and discuss sensitivity to heavy-tailed losses, extreme propensities, propensity misspecification, and clipping thresholds.  
- Control variate connection: Explicitly relate normalization to standard control variate formulations, stating when variance reduction is guaranteed and when bias may be introduced.  

Strengths:
- Addresses a clear, relevant problem (propensity overfitting in logged bandit learning).  
- Insightful conceptual contribution framing normalization as a multiplicative control variate.  
- Advances theoretical understanding beyond prior work ([1]).  
- Empirically outperforms POEM consistently with reasonable experimental design.  

Weaknesses and suggestions:
- Ambiguity in foundational assumptions (loss range, deterministic vs. stochastic feedback) needs clarification.  
- Lacks systematic discussion of failure modes (extreme propensities, miscalibrated logging models, unbounded losses).  
- Minor notation/typographic issues hinder readability.  

Overall assessment:
- Solid and timely contribution that deepens CRM-style analysis; clarifications on loss scaling and feedback stochasticity would strengthen adoption.
