This paper identifies a significant problem – propensity overfitting – in the commonly used counterfactual risk estimator for batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF), and proposes a self-normalized estimator as a solution. BLBF is a crucial setting for online systems leveraging historical logs, and the Counterfactual Risk Minimization (CRM) principle offers a general framework for learning in this context. The authors demonstrate that the conventional estimator suffers from bias in complex hypothesis spaces and show that their self-normalized estimator avoids this issue, leading to a new learning algorithm, Norm-POEM, which outperforms existing methods on multi-label classification tasks.

The paper is well-motivated, clearly articulating the challenges of BLBF and the limitations of existing approaches. The connection to causal inference, off-policy evaluation, and importance sampling is appropriately drawn, placing the work within a broader research context. The identification of “propensity overfitting” is a novel and insightful contribution, and the proposed solution – the self-normalized estimator – appears theoretically sound and practically effective. The empirical evaluation is thorough, demonstrating consistent improvements over the baseline POEM algorithm across multiple datasets. The discussion of computational efficiency is also valuable. 

However, there are some areas for improvement. The introduction of the problem and the mathematical notation could benefit from greater clarity for readers less familiar with the specific details of BLBF. Specifically, the presentation of the risk in Equation (1) could be enhanced by explicitly acknowledging the potential randomness of the feedback δ(x, y), as discussed in the original review. The authors should address the reviewer's concern about the rescaling of delta in [-1, 0] within the CRM framework and clarify its application in their experiments. While the authors acknowledge the connection to work in [1] regarding the lack of linearity, expanding on this point and clearly highlighting the specific novelty of their contribution beyond simply applying a different control variate would strengthen the paper. 

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The identification of propensity overfitting is a new and important observation in the field of BLBF.
* **Theoretical Soundness:** The proposed self-normalized estimator is well-motivated and appears to address the identified problem effectively.
* **Empirical Validation:** The results demonstrate a consistent improvement over the baseline algorithm on several datasets.
* **Practical Relevance:** BLBF is a highly relevant problem for many real-world applications, making the contribution potentially impactful.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Clarity:** Some sections, particularly the introduction and mathematical derivations, could benefit from clearer explanations.
* **Contextualization:** More explicitly contrasting the contribution with existing work, beyond simply referencing it, is needed.
* **Assumptions:** The dependence on the assumption of a fully supportive historical policy (h0) should be more thoroughly discussed, including potential implications when this assumption is violated.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper addresses a significant practical problem in BLBF with a novel and theoretically justified solution.
* The empirical results provide strong evidence for the effectiveness of the proposed approach.
* The work is well-connected to related research areas and contributes to a growing body of knowledge on learning from logged data.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

* The clarity of the presentation could be improved, potentially hindering the understanding of the contribution.
* While the empirical results are positive, further investigation into the robustness of the method under different conditions might be warranted.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a valuable contribution to the field of batch learning from logged bandit feedback. While there are some areas for improvement, the novelty of the identified problem, the soundness of the proposed solution, and the positive empirical results outweigh the weaknesses. I recommend **acceptance** contingent on the authors addressing the reviewer’s concerns regarding clarity and contextualization in a revised version.
