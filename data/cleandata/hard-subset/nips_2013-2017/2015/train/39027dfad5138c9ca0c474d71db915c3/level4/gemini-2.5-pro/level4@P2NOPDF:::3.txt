### Review

**Summary**

This paper addresses the important problem of learning from logged bandit feedback, a common scenario in applications like computational advertising. The authors identify a key weakness—a form of overfitting—in the recently proposed Counterfactual Risk Minimization (CRM) principle. To address this, they propose a novel self-normalizing risk estimator based on multiplicative control variates. While this estimator is biased, it demonstrates significant empirical improvements over the original CRM approach in the experiments provided.

**Quality and Significance**

The paper is of high quality, presenting a well-motivated and technically sound contribution. The problem of learning effective policies from historical, off-policy data is highly relevant, and improvements in this area have significant practical implications.

**Strengths:**

*   **Problem Relevance:** The paper tackles a well-established and important problem in reinforcement learning and its applications.
*   **Clear Motivation:** The identification of the "overfitting" issue in the standard CRM estimator is a valuable insight and provides a clear motivation for the proposed work.
*   **Novelty:** The proposed self-normalizing estimator is a novel and clever modification. As a follow-up to the work in [1], it offers a distinct and empirically powerful alternative.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and the presentation is clear, making the core arguments accessible and easy to follow.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

1.  **Limited Empirical Scope:** As a follow-up to [1], the experimental evaluation feels somewhat limited. The paper would be significantly strengthened by extending the experiments beyond the applications presented in [1]. Demonstrating the method's effectiveness on new datasets, or ideally a real-world application, would be crucial for establishing the broader significance of the observed improvements and showing how they translate to practice.

2.  **Lack of Variance Reporting:** The experimental results are presented without measures of variance (e.g., standard deviations or confidence intervals). Given that experiments were repeated several times (as mentioned on line 365), including error bars is essential to properly assess the statistical significance of the reported gains and the stability of the proposed method.

3.  **Insufficient Optimization Analysis:** The discussion regarding the optimization of the training objective (line 415) is quite brief. A more detailed analysis of the optimization landscape or convergence properties of the proposed objective would be a valuable addition.

4.  **Discussion of Bias:** While the paper acknowledges that the proposed estimator is biased, a more thorough discussion of the implications of this bias, and the trade-offs involved (e.g., bias-variance), would deepen the paper's theoretical contribution.

**Conclusion**

Overall, this is a solid and well-written paper that presents a relevant and novel contribution to the field of batch learning from bandit feedback. The proposed method is elegant and appears to be effective. However, the paper's impact could be substantially increased by addressing the limitations in the experimental evaluation, particularly by providing a more comprehensive set of results (including variance) and exploring applications beyond those in the prior work. With these additions, the paper would represent a more complete and impactful contribution.
