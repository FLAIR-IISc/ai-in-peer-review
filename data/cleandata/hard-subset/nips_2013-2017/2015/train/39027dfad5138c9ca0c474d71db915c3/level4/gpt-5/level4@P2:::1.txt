Summary
The paper studies batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF) and argues that the inverse-propensity (IPS) estimator commonly used within Counterfactual Risk Minimization (CRM) can behave pathologically when optimized over rich hypothesis classes. The authors identify a distinct failure mode they call propensity overfitting: instead of overfitting to low losses, the learner overfits the importance weights, driving the sum of weights far from its expectation and producing degenerate empirical risks. They propose a self-normalized (SN) counterfactual risk estimator—i.e., an IPS ratio estimator with multiplicative control variates—that is bounded and equivariant to additive shifts in the loss, and strongly consistent. They integrate this estimator into CRM, yielding Norm-POEM, a learning algorithm for structured output prediction with exponential-family models. Experiments on multi-label classification datasets show consistent gains over POEM (which uses standard IPS), improved robustness to loss translations, and competitive runtime; code is provided.

Relation to prior work
The work builds on a large literature on IPS for off-policy evaluation and learning, variance reduction via clipping, and doubly robust/regression estimators. Self-normalized importance sampling is well-known in Monte Carlo and RL, but its integration into CRM for learning (not just evaluation), the explicit diagnosis of propensity overfitting, and the empirical demonstration that SN+CRM improves generalization in BLBF appear to be the paper’s main contributions. It would help to position the approach relative to doubly robust and weighted/self-normalized DR estimators.

Quality
- Theory: The paper proves strong consistency of the SN estimator and argues boundedness and equivariance, which directly address anomalies of IPS. The CRM objective uses an approximate variance for the ratio estimator (delta method/normal approximation). A full generalization bound for the SN-CRM objective would strengthen the theory, but the proposed objective is reasonable and standard in IS practice.
- Empirics: The Supervised→Bandit protocol is appropriate; results are consistent across datasets; diagnostics via Ŝ(h) convincingly illustrate propensity overfitting and its mitigation. Hyperparameter selection and computational comparisons are clearly described. A comparison to (weighted) doubly robust baselines would make the case stronger.
- Reproducibility: Clear setup and available code are strong positives.

Clarity
The paper is generally well written with a helpful motivating example and clear diagnostics. Two points could be clarified:
- The conceptual relation between “variance explosion” in IPS and “propensity overfitting.” The examples suggest overfitting arises when the hypothesis can drive the weight sum away from 1 (limited exploration/support), which is closely tied to high weight variance. A brief, explicit discussion of similarities and differences would aid readers.
- The role of self-normalization vs CRM’s variance regularization: when each helps, and whether either alone suffices.

Originality
While self-normalized IS itself is not new, identifying propensity overfitting as a distinct learning-time failure mode and showing that SN IPS combined with CRM improves learning in BLBF is a novel and useful contribution. The structured-output setting with exponential-family policies is also of practical interest.

Significance
Off-policy learning from logs is important in several applications (ads, recommender systems, search). A simple estimator change that measurably improves robustness and generalization is practically valuable. The diagnostic Ŝ(h) and the demonstration of equivariance and boundedness address real pitfalls practitioners face.

Specific comments and questions
- Example 1 (around l.199): Please clarify the argument about the regime n ≪ k. With uniform logging over y, P(y = x) = 1/k. My reading is that hoverfit achieves empirical risk ≲ −k by matching observed labels, while the true minimizer’s risk is −2. Could you walk through the steps that yield R̂(hoverfit) ≤ −k and contrast this with R̂(h∗), to avoid confusion between the roles of xi = yi events and the learned mappings?
- “Unbiased counterfactual risk estimator used in prior works on BLBF [4,5,1].” Many works rely on clipping/truncation in practice, which introduces bias in exchange for reduced variance. Please soften or qualify this statement and, if possible, include comparisons to clipped IPS and to doubly robust (and weighted/self-normalized DR) baselines.
- Please provide a brief discussion of the bias–variance trade-off of the SN estimator in finite samples, especially under weight clipping, and how this interacts with the CRM variance penalty.
- Assumptions: The theory assumes logging with full support. In structured output spaces this is often violated in practice. Could you comment on robustness when support is imperfect, and whether smoothing/mixtures or exploration scavenging are needed?
- Variance estimator: The delta-method approximation is standard; a short note on conditions for its accuracy (e.g., existence of second moments under clipping and moderate weight dispersion) would be helpful.
- Model/optimization: It would be useful to report the norm of w (as you mention) and training loss traces for both POEM and Norm-POEM to illustrate the different overfitting dynamics.

Pros
- Clear identification and diagnosis of a real failure mode (propensity overfitting).
- Simple, principled fix via self-normalization with strong intuitive and theoretical support.
- Consistent empirical gains and robustness to loss translation; informative diagnostics (Ŝ(h)).
- Practical algorithm (Norm-POEM) with code and competitive runtime.

Cons
- Lack of comparisons to doubly robust/weighted DR baselines.
- Theoretical treatment for CRM with ratio estimators is approximate; a bound or more detailed analysis would strengthen the work.
- Assumes full support; discussion of violations could be expanded.

Recommendation
I find the ideas compelling and the empirical evidence persuasive. While I could not verify all derivations in detail, the contribution is clear, relevant, and practically useful. I recommend acceptance.
