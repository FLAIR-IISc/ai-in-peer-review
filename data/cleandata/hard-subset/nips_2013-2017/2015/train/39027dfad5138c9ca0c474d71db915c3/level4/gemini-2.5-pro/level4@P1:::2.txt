This paper addresses the problem of batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF), a setting where a learner must train a policy using historical data collected by a different, potentially suboptimal policy. The authors identify a significant issue with the conventional inverse propensity score (IPS) estimator used in this domain, which they term "propensity overfitting." This form of overfitting occurs when the learning algorithm minimizes the risk estimate not by fitting the loss, but by exploiting the propensity weights, leading to degenerate solutions.

To resolve this, the paper proposes replacing the standard unbiased IPS estimator with a self-normalized estimator, which is derived using multiplicative control variates. This new estimator is shown to be equivariant to translations in the loss function and bounded by the range of the loss, thus avoiding the anomalies that cause propensity overfitting. Building on this improved estimator, the authors introduce a new learning algorithm, the Normalized Policy Optimizer for Exponential Models (Norm-POEM), which adapts the Counterfactual Risk Minimization (CRM) principle. The paper concludes with a strong set of experiments on multi-label classification tasks, demonstrating that Norm-POEM consistently and significantly outperforms its predecessor, POEM, which uses the standard estimator.

**Strengths**

1.  **Significant Problem:** The paper successfully identifies and formalizes a subtle but severe problem—propensity overfitting—that can plague learning algorithms based on the standard IPS estimator. This is an important contribution to the understanding of off-policy and counterfactual learning.
2.  **Principled Solution:** The proposed solution, using the self-normalized estimator, is well-motivated and technically sound. By drawing on the established literature of control variates and ratio estimators, the authors provide a principled way to mitigate the identified problem. The theoretical analysis, showing the estimator's equivariance and consistency, provides a solid foundation for the approach.
3.  **Strong Empirical Results:** The experimental evaluation is thorough and convincing. On several multi-label classification datasets, the proposed Norm-POEM algorithm not only outperforms the POEM baseline by a wide margin but also approaches the performance of a "skyline" model trained on full (non-bandit) information. The robustness of the method to loss translations, in contrast to the catastrophic failure of the baseline, provides compelling evidence for the paper's central claims.

**Weaknesses and Comments for Authors**

My main concern relates to the paper's central motivation for introducing the self-normalized estimator. The argument hinges on an identified "anomaly" in the conventional estimator (Eq. 2), namely its lack of equivariance with respect to a constant translation of the loss function. I believe this line of reasoning is technically misleading.

In an expectation, `E[f(X)] = Σ f(x)p(x)`, the property of translation equivariance (`E[f(X) + C] = E[f(X)] + C`) holds because the quantity being averaged, `f(x)`, is itself shifted by the constant. However, in the importance-sampled risk estimator of Eq. 2, the quantity being averaged is `δ(x,y) * h(y|x) / h0(y|x)`, not simply the loss `δ(x,y)`. When the loss is translated to `δ(x,y) + C`, the full term becomes `(δ(x,y) + C) * h(y|x) / h0(y|x)`. It is therefore entirely expected, and not an "anomaly," that the resulting estimator does not simply equal the original estimate plus `C`.

Consequently, the justification provided in Section 4 and the subsequent Example 1, which are built upon this premise, appear to be based on a flawed interpretation of the estimator's fundamental properties. While the practical problem of propensity overfitting is real and well-demonstrated, the theoretical explanation for its cause seems imprecise.

Despite this criticism of the exposition, the practical benefits of the proposed self-normalized estimator are undeniable. The estimator in Eq. 7 is better in principle because it effectively normalizes by the sum of importance weights, thereby mitigating the harmful effects of small propensities (`p_i`) in the denominator. The empirical results clearly validate this intuition.

**Recommendation**

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution. It identifies a crucial problem in counterfactual learning and proposes an effective and well-supported solution. The experimental results are excellent and clearly demonstrate the superiority of the proposed approach. While I have reservations about the framing of the theoretical motivation, the method itself is sound and the results are significant. I recommend acceptance. Should the paper be accepted, I strongly encourage the authors to revise the commentary in Section 4 to provide a more precise justification for their method.
