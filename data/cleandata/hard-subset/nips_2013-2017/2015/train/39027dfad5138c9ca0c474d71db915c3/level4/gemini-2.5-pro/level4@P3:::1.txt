### Summary of the Paper

This paper addresses the problem of batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF), a setting crucial for improving interactive systems using historical data. The authors identify a significant flaw in the conventional inverse propensity score (IPS) estimator used within the Counterfactual Risk Minimization (CRM) framework. They term this issue "propensity overfitting," where a model with high capacity can achieve an artificially low empirical risk by manipulating the propensity weights, rather than by accurately modeling the underlying loss function. This can lead to degenerate solutions that are either over-concentrated on training examples (for negative losses) or actively avoid them (for positive losses).

To solve this problem, the authors propose replacing the standard IPS estimator with the self-normalized estimator, a well-known ratio estimator from the importance sampling literature that uses the sum of importance weights as a multiplicative control variate. They show that this self-normalized estimator is equivariant to shifts in the loss function and its value is bounded within the range of the loss, thus provably avoiding the anomalies that cause propensity overfitting. Building on this improved estimator, they introduce a new learning algorithm, the Normalized Policy Optimizer for Exponential Models (Norm-POEM), which integrates the self-normalized estimator and its corresponding variance into the CRM objective. Through extensive experiments on multi-label classification tasks, they demonstrate that Norm-POEM consistently and significantly outperforms the previous state-of-the-art, POEM, which is based on the conventional estimator.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Strengths:**

1.  **Clear Identification of a Novel and Important Problem:** The paper's primary strength is the identification and clear articulation of "propensity overfitting." While the high variance of IPS estimators is a well-known issue, the authors convincingly argue that propensity overfitting is a distinct and pernicious problem related to the estimator's structural properties, particularly its lack of equivariance. The example provided in Section 4 is exceptionally clear and effectively illustrates how a model can "overfit" the propensities to produce an arbitrarily low, and thus misleading, risk estimate. This is a significant contribution to the understanding of counterfactual learning.

2.  **Principled and Effective Solution:** The proposed solution—employing the self-normalized estimator—is not an ad-hoc fix but is well-grounded in the statistical literature on control variates and importance sampling. The authors clearly explain the theoretical benefits, including equivariance, bounded estimates, and strong consistency (Theorem 2). This provides a principled remedy to the identified problem.

3.  **Strong Empirical Validation:** The experimental evaluation is thorough and well-designed. The "Supervised -> Bandit" protocol provides a controlled environment to precisely measure generalization performance. The results are compelling:
    *   Norm-POEM consistently outperforms the POEM baseline across multiple datasets and training data sizes (Table 1, Figure 1).
    *   The experiments directly verify the theoretical claims. Table 2 shows that Norm-POEM is robust to loss translation and that the sum of its importance weights, Ŝ(h), remains close to its expected value of 1, while the baseline POEM is highly sensitive and biased. This provides strong evidence that the performance gains are indeed due to mitigating propensity overfitting.
    *   The authors also provide an ablation study (Table 3) showing that variance regularization within the CRM framework remains beneficial even with the improved estimator.

4.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is very well-written, logically structured, and easy to follow. The motivation is clear, the problem is well-defined, the solution is elegantly presented, and the experiments directly support the claims. The provision of code is also commendable and aids reproducibility.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

1.  **Relation Between Propensity Overfitting and High Variance:** The paper presents propensity overfitting as a distinct problem from the high variance of the IPS estimator. While they are conceptually different, they are not entirely independent; both problems are exacerbated by examples with very small propensities (`p_i`). A more detailed discussion on the interplay between these two issues would be beneficial. For instance, does mitigating propensity overfitting also implicitly help control variance, or vice-versa? Clarifying this relationship would further strengthen the paper's contribution.

2.  **Clarity on Baselines:** The paper critiques the "unbiased counterfactual risk estimator." While this is technically correct for the base IPS estimator (Eq. 2), much of the literature cited, including the POEM baseline the authors compare against, uses clipped (and therefore biased) versions to control variance. It might be clearer to consistently refer to it as the "standard" or "conventional" IPS-based estimator to avoid any potential confusion about whether the critique applies only to the pure, unclipped version.

3.  **Novelty of the Estimator:** It is worth noting that the self-normalized estimator itself is a classic tool in statistics. The novelty here lies in its application to diagnose and solve the specific problem of propensity overfitting within the context of counterfactual risk minimization for BLBF. This is a valid and important contribution, but the framing should continue to emphasize the novelty of the application and problem diagnosis rather than the estimator itself.

### Arguments for Acceptance:

1.  The paper identifies, formalizes, and provides a compelling name for a subtle but critical failure mode in a widely used class of estimators for counterfactual learning.
2.  It proposes a simple, elegant, and theoretically sound solution that draws from established statistical principles.
3.  The empirical results are strong and convincingly demonstrate that the proposed method, Norm-POEM, resolves the identified issue and leads to substantial improvements in generalization performance over the existing state-of-the-art.
4.  The contribution is significant for researchers and practitioners working on learning from logged data, which is a problem of great practical importance (e.g., in recommendation, search, and ad placement).

### Arguments against Acceptance:

1.  The core technical component (the self-normalized estimator) is not new, though its application in this context is.
2.  The discussion could be slightly improved by further clarifying the relationship between propensity overfitting and the classic high-variance problem of importance sampling.

**Conclusion:**

This is a high-quality paper that makes a clear and significant contribution. It identifies a previously overlooked problem in counterfactual learning and provides a well-motivated and empirically validated solution. The strengths far outweigh the minor weaknesses. The paper is well-written, the ideas are interesting, and the results are likely to be impactful for the field. I strongly recommend acceptance.
