Given data generated under one hypothesis, we may want to evaluate a different hypothesis. For non-deterministic hypotheses (e.g., conditional distributions), one can estimate the loss under the target hypothesis by reweighting the data points, see the equation between (1) and (2).

The authors address a problem related to, but not exactly the same as, the high variance of importance weights. They call this issue “propensity overfitting.” The examples suggest it arises when exploration is insufficient; that is, for a given x, we observe very few or no y that align with the new hypothesis to be evaluated. Since a similar scarcity argument also explains the large variance of the weights, it would be helpful to clarify the relationship between these two problems in more detail.

I did not have time to verify the details or assess the novelty of the proposed solution, but the core idea seems reasonable.
