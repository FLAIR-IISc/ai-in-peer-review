By observing data from a specific hypothesis, we may be interested in evaluating another hypothesis. If these hypotheses are unconfirmed (e.g., conditional distributions), we can reweigh the data points and estimate the loss under the hypothesis being investigated, see equations (1) and (2).

The authors have discussed a related but somewhat different problem (see below), which differs from the problem of large variance of weights. The authors call this problem "progenity overfitting." The examples indicate that this problem occurs if there is not enough exploration, that is, for a given x, we see very few or even no y's that would correspond to the new hypothesis to be evaluated. Since a similar argument explains the large variance of weights, it would be better to explain the relationship between these two problems in a little more detail.

I didn't have time to look at the details or novelty of the proposed solution, but the idea seems reasonable.
