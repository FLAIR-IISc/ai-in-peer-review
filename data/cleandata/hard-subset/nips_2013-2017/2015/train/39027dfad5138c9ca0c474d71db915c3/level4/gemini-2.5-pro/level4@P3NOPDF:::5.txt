This paper proposes a novel method for batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF), directly addressing the problem of propensity overfitting. The work builds upon the influential Policy Optimizer for Exponential Models (POEM) framework from Swaminathan and Joachims (2015) and introduces a new formulation that yields significant performance and efficiency improvements.

**Quality and Significance**

The quality of the research is high, and the contributions are significant. The paper effectively identifies a key weakness in the original POEM algorithm and proposes a principled solution. The core contribution—a reformulated objective with an enhanced regularization mechanism—is well-motivated and leads to substantial empirical gains.

One of the most compelling results is the algorithm's efficiency. It is impressive, and somewhat counter-intuitive, that the proposed method converges faster than the original POEM despite the addition of a more complex regularization term. This suggests that the new formulation not only improves policy performance by mitigating overfitting but also creates a more favorable optimization landscape. This dual benefit of improved accuracy and reduced training time makes the work highly significant. Given the prevalence of logged data in real-world applications (e.g., online advertising, recommendation systems), an effective and efficient BLBF algorithm like this has the potential for widespread adoption and practical impact.

**Originality**

The paper's contributions are original. While it operates within the framework established by prior work, it does not present a mere incremental extension. The proposed reformulation of the POEM objective is novel and provides a new perspective on how to regularize policy learning in the batch bandit setting. The insights that lead to this new formulation are valuable and move the field forward.

**Clarity**

The paper is, for the most part, clearly written and well-structured. The authors do an excellent job of situating their work in the context of existing literature, clearly explaining the limitations of the original POEM that they aim to solve. The methodology is presented logically, and the experimental results are communicated effectively, supporting the central claims of the paper.

**Overall Assessment**

This is a strong paper that presents a valuable contribution to the literature on learning from bandit feedback. The proposed algorithm is elegant, effective, and efficient, successfully addressing the critical issue of propensity overfitting while simultaneously accelerating the training process. The results are compelling and likely to be of high interest to the NIPS community. I believe this work will inspire further research in this important area. I recommend acceptance.
