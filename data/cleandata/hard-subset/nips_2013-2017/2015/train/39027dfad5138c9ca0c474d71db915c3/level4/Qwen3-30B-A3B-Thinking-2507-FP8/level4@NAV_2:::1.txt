

This paper identifies a critical issue—propensity overfitting—in the conventional unbiased counterfactual risk estimator used for batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF), proposing a self-normalized estimator to resolve it. The work builds on NIPS 2015 (Swaminathan & Joachims) and subsequent BLBF literature (e.g., Dudík et al., NIPS 2014) but addresses a previously uncharacterized anomaly: the conventional estimator’s lack of equivariance under loss translation and its susceptibility to severe risk overestimation when the hypothesis space is complex. The authors introduce Norm-POEM, a learning algorithm leveraging the self-normalized estimator, and demonstrate its consistent superiority over POEM across multi-label classification tasks.  

**Strengths**  
*Quality*: The theoretical analysis is rigorous. Theorem 2 establishes strong consistency of the self-normalized estimator, and Section 4’s examples compellingly expose propensity overfitting’s mechanism (e.g., Example 1 shows R̂(hoverfit) = −k, which is arbitrarily lower than R(h*) = −2). Empirical validation on standard BLBF benchmarks (Scene, Yeast, TMC, LYRL) is thorough, with consistent outperformance of POEM across all datasets and training sizes (Figure 1, Table 1).  
*Clarity*: The paper is exceptionally well-organized, with clear definitions (Section 3), intuitive examples (Section 4), and a concise derivation of Norm-POEM (Section 6). The code availability further enhances reproducibility.  
*Originality*: Propensity overfitting is a novel problem distinct from known variance issues in importance sampling (e.g., clipped estimators [15]). The self-normalized estimator (via multiplicative control variates) is a clean, principled solution not previously applied to BLBF, differing from prior work on additive control variates [6, 16].  
*Significance*: The problem is highly relevant to real-world applications (ad placement, recommendations), where BLBF is commonly used. Norm-POEM’s robustness (Table 2) and computational efficiency (Section 7) directly address deployment challenges, advancing BLBF beyond theoretical bounds to practical reliability.  

**Weaknesses**  
*Quality*: The claim that prior works use an "unbiased estimator" (line 199) is inaccurate—POEM [1] and others use clipped estimators (R̂_M), which are biased. The paper should clarify that propensity overfitting arises *despite* clipping (Section 4.1). Theorem 2’s proof assumes full support of h₀; this should be explicitly discussed for real-world datasets with sparse exploration.  
*Clarity*: Section 4.1’s Example 1 requires refinement: when n << k, R̂(hoverfit) = −k (not "arbitrarily lower than −2"), which is consistent with the text but could be clarified to avoid confusion. The relationship between propensity overfitting and weight variance (e.g., [15]) needs deeper elaboration in Section 4.  
*Significance*: The paper could strengthen its impact by comparing Norm-POEM’s runtime to recent BLBF methods (e.g., [11]), though the current analysis (Section 7) shows it is competitive with POEM.  

**Pro/Con Acceptance**  
*Pro*: Novel problem identification, rigorous theory, strong empirical results, clear practical value for industrial applications.  
*Con*: Minor inaccuracy in prior work characterization, need for deeper discussion of estimator bias in Section 4.  

This paper makes a significant contribution to BLBF by resolving a critical flaw in existing estimators. The solution is theoretically sound, empirically validated, and directly applicable to high-stakes online systems. I recommend acceptance.
