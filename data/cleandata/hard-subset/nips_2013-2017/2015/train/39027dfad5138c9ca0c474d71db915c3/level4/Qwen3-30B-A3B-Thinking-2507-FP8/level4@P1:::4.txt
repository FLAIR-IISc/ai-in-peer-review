

The paper identifies a critical flaw in the conventional counterfactual risk estimator used for batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF), proposing a self-normalized estimator that avoids propensity overfitting—a previously unaddressed issue causing severe bias in risk estimation. This work builds directly on the Counterfactual Risk Minimization (CRM) framework established in [1], where the authors demonstrate that the standard inverse propensity estimator (Equation 2) suffers from propensity overfitting when optimizing over complex hypothesis spaces. The proposed self-normalized estimator (Equation 7) resolves this by incorporating multiplicative control variates, ensuring risk estimates remain equivariant to loss translations and bounded within the loss range—unlike the conventional estimator. The resulting Norm-POEM algorithm replaces POEM’s risk estimator while maintaining the same hypothesis space and CRM optimization framework. Empirical results across multiple multi-label classification datasets consistently show Norm-POEM outperforms POEM, with improved robustness and generalization, and the self-normalized estimator demonstrably avoids propensity overfitting (Table 2).

**Strengths:**  
The identification of propensity overfitting is a significant theoretical contribution, filling a gap in BLBF analysis. The self-normalized estimator is rigorously justified (Theorem 2) and resolves multiple anomalies of the conventional estimator (e.g., non-equivariance, unbounded risk estimates). The empirical validation is thorough, showing consistent improvements across datasets (Table 1, Figure 1) and directly linking the estimator’s properties to reduced propensity overfitting (Table 2). The Norm-POEM implementation is practical, with computational costs comparable to POEM (Section 7), and the code is publicly available.

**Weaknesses and Clarifications Needed:**  
1. **Loss Rescaling Consistency:** The paper states in Section 4.1 of [1] that CRM requires losses scaled to [-1, 0]. However, Table 1 reports results using positive delta values (e.g., "delta > 0" in the caption), which appears inconsistent. Please clarify how the loss rescaling is applied for Norm-POEM (e.g., whether the self-normalized estimator inherently handles unscaled losses, or if rescaling is still required). This affects the interpretation of the experimental results.  
2. **Feedback Randomness:** While Section 3 correctly defines the risk as $R(h) = \mathbb{E}[\delta(x,y)]$ (with $\delta$ as a noisy realization), the feedback $\delta(x,y)$ should be explicitly modeled as a *random variable* (not a deterministic function), as it depends on unobserved labels (e.g., in classification, $\delta$ reflects the loss for a random true label $y'$). This is already implicitly handled in the risk definition but could be clarified to align with standard causal inference practice.  
3. **Notation Consistency:** Line 276 uses "argmax" for risk minimization; this should be corrected to "argmin" (consistent with Equation 3).  
4. **Equation 8 Notation:** The notation $\hat{S}(h)$ in Equation 8 should use hats (e.g., $\hat{S}(h)$) for consistency with earlier usage (e.g., $\hat{V}ar(u_h)$).

**Overall Assessment:**  
This is a strong, well-motivated contribution to BLBF. The propensity overfitting analysis is novel and impactful, and the self-normalized estimator provides a clean, theoretically sound solution. The empirical results robustly support the claims, with Norm-POEM showing clear advantages over POEM across all datasets. While the loss rescaling point requires clarification, it does not undermine the core technical contribution. The work advances the state of the art in BLBF by resolving a fundamental issue in risk estimation, making it highly significant for practitioners in online learning systems (e.g., ad placement, recommendation). The paper is well-written and aligns with NIPS scope.  

**Recommendation:** Accept. The paper’s technical depth, empirical validation, and clear resolution of a critical problem in BLBF warrant publication. The minor clarifications requested are easily addressed in the rebuttal.
