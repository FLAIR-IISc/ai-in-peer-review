This paper addresses the critical problem of batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF), a scenario highly relevant to applications like ad ranking and recommendation systems. The authors identify a novel “propensity overfitting” issue within the Counterfactual Risk Minimization (CRM) principle, a foundational approach in this field, and propose a solution leveraging multiplicative control variates – resulting in a self-normalized, albeit biased, risk estimator.

The paper is of high quality and well-motivated, building directly upon the authors’ prior work [1]. Given its nature as a follow-up to [1], it would have been beneficial to see more extensive experimentation, and a more detailed analysis of the optimization landscape of the proposed training objective. While the paper touches upon optimization (lines 415-422), expanding on this aspect—perhaps exploring different optimization strategies or providing a deeper characterization of convergence—would strengthen the contribution.

The presentation is clear and the paper is well-written, making the complex concepts accessible. The authors effectively articulate the problem and their proposed solution.

The work demonstrates originality by modifying the risk estimator using a self-normalizing approach, which the experimental results indicate yields substantial improvements. This represents a significant refinement of existing techniques for BLBF. It is clearly differentiated from previous contributions and appropriately references related work. A careful check of recent NIPS proceedings confirms the novelty of this specific approach.

The problem of learning from logged data is undoubtedly relevant. However, assessing the significance – specifically, how the observed experimental improvements translate to tangible gains in real-world applications – remains a challenge. While the results are compelling, demonstrating impact beyond the datasets used (which are similar to those in [1]) would further bolster the significance of the work.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Addresses a crucial problem in BLBF with a well-motivated and technically sound solution.
*   Demonstrates clear improvements over existing methods through rigorous experimentation.
*   Introduces a novel estimator that avoids a previously unrecognized form of overfitting (“propensity overfitting”).
*   The paper is clearly written and logically organized.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The experimental evaluation, while strong, primarily focuses on datasets similar to those used in prior work [1]. Additional, more diverse datasets, or ideally, a demonstration on a real-world application, would strengthen the impact.
*   The analysis of the optimization process could be more detailed.
*   While variance is mentioned, reporting the variance of experimental results across repetitions would provide a more complete picture of the method’s robustness.



Overall, this is a solid and relevant paper with a novel contribution. While some expansion of the experimental results and optimization analysis would be valuable, the work as presented is worthy of consideration.
