This paper addresses the important problem of off-policy evaluation, where the goal is to evaluate a new hypothesis (or policy) using data generated by a different one. The standard approach relies on importance weighting to correct for the distributional mismatch, but this can suffer from several issues. The authors identify and aim to solve a problem they term "propensity overfitting."

My understanding is that propensity overfitting occurs when the logging policy has insufficient exploration. For a given context `x`, if the actions `y` that would be taken by the new hypothesis are rarely (or never) observed in the data, the evaluation can be unreliable. The authors provide compelling examples to illustrate this phenomenon.

The core idea of the paper is interesting and addresses a practical challenge. However, the relationship between "propensity overfitting" and the well-known problem of high variance in importance sampling estimators could be clarified. Both issues seem to stem from the same root cause: a significant mismatch between the logging and evaluation policies, leading to large or unstable importance weights. A more detailed discussion on the distinction and connection between these two problems would strengthen the paper's conceptual contribution. For instance, is propensity overfitting a specific manifestation of the high-variance problem, or a fundamentally distinct issue?

While the proposed solution appears sensible on an intuitive level, due to time constraints as an additional reviewer, I was unable to conduct a deep analysis of its technical novelty and soundness. I apologize if I have missed or misunderstood key details.

I have a few specific questions and comments for the authors:

*   **Line 199:** I had trouble following the argument here. If `n` is small compared to `k`, my understanding is that we would observe very few data points where `x_i = y_i`. In this scenario, wouldn't `\hat{R}(h^)` be even smaller than -2? I would appreciate it if the authors could clarify this point, as I may be missing something.

*   **Characterization of Prior Work:** The paper mentions the "unbiased counterfactual risk estimator used in prior works on BLBF [4, 5, 1]". I am not certain this is a fair characterization. While I am not familiar with all three references, it is common practice in this domain to use variance reduction techniques like weight clipping, which would introduce bias into the estimator. Could the authors confirm that the methods in the cited papers are strictly unbiased, or perhaps refine this statement?

On a positive note, the problem the paper tackles is highly relevant, and the ideas presented are thought-provoking. The inclusion of code is also commendable and significantly aids in reproducibility.

In summary, this paper introduces an interesting perspective on a crucial problem in counterfactual learning. Although I was unable to fully verify its technical contributions, the conceptual framework and the problem formulation are valuable and likely to stimulate further discussion and research. Based on the interesting ideas presented, I recommend acceptance.
