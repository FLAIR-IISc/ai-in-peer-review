Summary
The paper tackles batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF) under the Counterfactual Risk Minimization (CRM) framework. Prior BLBF work typically relies on the inverse propensity (importance sampling) estimator to approximate counterfactual risk, sometimes with clipping to control variance. The authors identify a distinct failure mode of this estimator when used for ERM over rich hypothesis classes: propensity overfitting, where the learned policy manipulates importance weights rather than modeling low loss, leading to severely biased empirical risks and non-equivariance under loss translations. They propose a multiplicative control variate—self-normalization of the importance weights—to construct a self-normalized risk estimator. This estimator is bounded, equivariant, and strongly consistent, and it mitigates propensity overfitting by enforcing the normalization constraint Ŝ(h)≈1. Building on this, they introduce Norm-POEM, which instantiates CRM with the self-normalized estimator for exponential-family (CRF-like) policies, along with an approximate variance estimate to regularize variance across hypotheses. Experiments on multi-label datasets (Scene, Yeast, TMC, LYRL) show consistent gains over POEM, improved stability under loss translations, better diagnostics via Ŝ(h), and competitive runtime.

Relation to prior work
The paper is well positioned with respect to importance weighting for covariate shift, off-policy evaluation, and CRM. It relates to variance reduction via clipping, regression/doubly robust estimators, and to self-normalized importance sampling in Monte Carlo. The key contribution is to articulate and address propensity overfitting—a phenomenon beyond large variance alone—and to integrate self-normalized IS into the CRM learning objective.

Quality
- The main insight (propensity overfitting) is clearly illustrated by examples: ERM on IPS can minimize the sum of weights Ŝ(h) rather than loss, causing degenerate solutions, especially under insufficient exploration.
- The self-normalized estimator is a principled fix: it is bounded, equivariant to loss translations, and strongly consistent. The variance approximation for the ratio estimator is standard.
- I did not have time to check all proofs and derivations in detail, but the approach appears technically sound and in line with established Monte Carlo theory.
- It would help to articulate more explicitly how propensity overfitting relates to (and differs from) the classic large-variance problem. My understanding is that insufficient exploration produces heavy-tailed/unstable weights; IPS-ERM exacerbates this by actively selecting hypotheses that drive Ŝ(h) away from 1 (either inflating or deflating it depending on the sign of losses), which is a distinct ERM-induced bias not fixed by clipping alone. A short formal comparison would improve clarity.

Clarity
The paper is generally well written. The equivalence, bias/variance trade-offs, and diagnostics via Ŝ(h) are explained clearly. The discussion of equivariance and boundedness is helpful. Please consider clarifying the example noted below and briefly summarizing the conditions under which the variance approximation (Equation 8) is reliable.

Originality
Identifying propensity overfitting as an ERM pathologic distinct from variance blow-up, and addressing it via a self-normalized estimator within CRM, is a novel and useful contribution. While self-normalized IS is known, its integration into counterfactual learning objectives and the resulting algorithmic/empirical implications are new.

Significance
The work is practically significant for offline policy learning and structured prediction from logs. The experimental evidence is consistent and suggests the method is robust, especially under loss translations where IPS-based ERM can fail catastrophically. The availability of code is a plus.

Specific comments and questions
- Relation between propensity overfitting and weight variance: The examples show overfitting occurs when exploration is limited and many logged actions mismatch the evaluated policy. Since this is also when weight variance is high, a more explicit discussion of how self-normalization mitigates ERM’s tendency to exploit Ŝ(h) (beyond just variance reduction) would be valuable.
- Line 199 (Example 1): For n small relative to k, we indeed see few cases with xi=yi under uniform logging, so δi is typically −1. Then h(yi|xi)=1 and pi=1/k, yielding contributions around −k; hence R̂(hoverfit) can be much smaller than −2, which is exactly the claimed degeneracy. If I am misunderstanding the bound or the role of cases with yi=xi, please clarify the inequality and the conditions under which −k is attained versus approached.
- “unbiased counterfactual risk estimator used in prior works on BLBF [4,5,1]”: Many practical works use clipping/truncation or doubly robust estimators, which introduce bias to reduce variance. It would be fair to qualify this statement and distinguish between the vanilla IPS estimator and its widely used biased variants.
- Variance estimate (Equation 8): A brief note on conditions (finite second moment, clipping for stability) and when the Normal approximation is accurate would help readers gauge reliability.
- Empirics: The Ŝ(ĥ) diagnostic is compelling. It may help to include distributions or CIs for Ŝ(ĥ) across runs. Also, note explicitly whether hyperparameters M, λ are chosen via counterfactual validation identically for both POEM and Norm-POEM (they appear to be).

Pros
- Clear identification of a real failure mode (propensity overfitting) with concrete examples.
- Principled fix using self-normalization: bounded, equivariant, consistent.
- Solid empirical results showing consistent gains and robustness to loss translation.
- Practical algorithm (Norm-POEM) with code and competitive runtime.

Cons
- Limited theoretical comparison between propensity overfitting and classic variance issues; clipping/DR baselines could be discussed more directly in this context.
- Some derivational details (e.g., variance approximation conditions) could be clarified.
- I could not fully verify the technical details due to time constraints.

Recommendation
Accept. The paper presents a clear and practically important insight with a well-motivated, theoretically grounded solution and compelling empirical evidence. Despite my limited time to check all proofs, the contribution appears both novel and significant for counterfactual learning from logs.
