Summary
This paper addresses batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF), a setting highly relevant to applications such as ad ranking, search, and recommendation. It builds on the Counterfactual Risk Minimization (CRM) principle, which relies on importance-weighted estimators. The authors identify a serious issue—propensity overfitting—when the conventional unbiased inverse propensity estimator is used for learning over complex hypothesis spaces. They propose replacing it with a self-normalized estimator obtained via multiplicative control variates. This estimator is biased but equivariant, bounded within the loss range, and strongly consistent; it avoids the anomalies of the standard estimator that lead to degenerate empirical risk under ERM.

The paper integrates this estimator into a learning algorithm, Norm-POEM, for structured output prediction using exponential-family linear rules (CRF-like models). It derives an approximate variance estimator for the self-normalized risk and applies the CRM principle with variance regularization. Empirically, on several multi-label datasets (Scene, Yeast, TMC, LYRL), Norm-POEM consistently outperforms POEM (the CRM instantiation using the conventional estimator), is robust to translations of the loss, and shows favorable runtime behavior. The authors also provide evidence that variance regularization remains useful with the self-normalized estimator.

Relation to prior work: This contribution follows and extends the CRM framework [1] by replacing the risk estimator. It connects to classic importance sampling, self-normalized IS (e.g., Tukey and Trotter; Hesterberg), variance reduction, and off-policy evaluation. The paper’s central novelty is diagnosing propensity overfitting in learning and showing that a self-normalized estimator mitigates it in both theory and practice.

Quality
The paper is technically solid and well-motivated. The identification of propensity overfitting, the use of a multiplicative control variate, and the theoretical properties of the self-normalized estimator (equivariance, boundedness, strong consistency) are clearly articulated and aligned with the literature. The empirical results are substantial within the chosen setup and convincingly favor Norm-POEM over POEM.

That said, as a follow-up to [1], I expected additional analysis of the optimization procedure beyond the brief discussion (e.g., more detail than the single paragraph around line 415). Practical questions remain about convergence behavior, sensitivity to the clipping parameter M and the variance regularization λ, and the choice of L-BFGS versus stochastic methods. Also, since multiple runs are performed, reporting variability (e.g., standard errors) would strengthen the empirical claims.

Clarity
The presentation is clear and the paper is well written. The exposition of propensity overfitting and the motivation for self-normalization are particularly effective. Minor suggestion: where multiple repetitions are averaged, please include measures of dispersion to help assess stability.

Originality
This is a direct extension of [1], but the shift to a self-normalized risk estimator for learning is a meaningful and well-justified change that appears to make a large empirical difference. While self-normalized IS is known for evaluation, its careful integration into CRM for learning and the focus on propensity overfitting constitute a novel and useful contribution.

Significance
Learning from logged data is an important and timely problem. The demonstrated gains on multi-label classification benchmarks are compelling. However, it remains difficult to assess how these improvements would translate to real-world deployments with complex logging policies and feedback noise. Additional applications beyond those used in [1], ideally including a real-world case study, would help substantiate the broader impact.

Pros
- Identifies and clearly explains propensity overfitting in BLBF with the conventional estimator.
- Proposes a principled self-normalized estimator that is equivariant, bounded, and strongly consistent.
- Integrates the estimator into CRM (Norm-POEM) with an appropriate variance estimate and shows consistent empirical gains.
- Robust to loss translations and exhibits favorable runtime behavior.

Cons
- Limited analysis of the optimization procedure; more detail on convergence and hyperparameter sensitivity would be valuable.
- Experimental scope is narrow (largely mirrors [1]); lacks real-world applications or broader domains.
- Reported results would benefit from variance/error bars across repeated trials.

Detailed comments and suggestions
- Please report standard errors or confidence intervals when averaging over multiple repetitions (e.g., Table 1, Figure 1). This will clarify the statistical significance of improvements.
- Expand the discussion of optimization: practical convergence behavior with L-BFGS on the non-convex objective, typical iteration counts, and sensitivity to initialization. A brief ablation on the roles of M and λ would be informative.
- Consider adding experiments beyond those in [1], ideally including at least one real-world logged dataset, to demonstrate robustness and practical applicability.
- A short diagnostic plot/table of Ŝ(ĥ) distributions across candidates would further illustrate the reduction of propensity overfitting.
- Minor: fix small typos (e.g., “Conterfactual” → “Counterfactual”).

Overall, this is a strong and relevant paper. With expanded experimental scope and more optimization details, it would be even more compelling.
