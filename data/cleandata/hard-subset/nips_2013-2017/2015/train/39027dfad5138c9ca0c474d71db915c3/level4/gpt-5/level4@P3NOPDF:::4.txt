Summary
The paper proposes NORM-POEM, a normalized inverse-propensity approach for batch learning from logged bandit feedback that mitigates “propensity overfitting.” The method can be viewed as introducing a multiplicative control variate to stabilize empirical risk minimization under bandit feedback. The analysis is a thoughtful follow-up to [1], and the empirical results show clear improvements over POEM across several tasks.

Major questions for rebuttal/clarification
- Loss scaling and the meaning of “delta > 0”: Section 4.1 of [1] argues that the CRM approach is well-defined only for losses rescaled to lie in [-1, 0], and they recommend rescaling general losses accordingly. My understanding is that NORM-POEM makes the same assumption: equations (3) and (8) appear to require losses in [-1, 0] after rescaling. However, Table 1 reports results using “delta > 0.” Please clarify:
  - Were the losses in all experiments rescaled to [-1, 0] prior to applying NORM-POEM (and POEM/CRM), and if so, how exactly was rescaling performed?
  - If some experiments used unscaled losses with positive values, does the theory for NORM-POEM (e.g., boundedness assumptions, variance control) still apply unchanged? If yes, please specify the assumptions that replace the [-1, 0] bound and how they impact the guarantees.
  - If “delta > 0” simply denotes the original loss before an affine rescaling to [-1, 0], please state this explicitly in the experimental setup.

- Modeling the feedback as a random variable: In Section 3 (and similarly in [1]), the feedback delta(x, y) is presented as a deterministic function (line 110). In many practical scenarios (e.g., classification with label noise), the observed loss for a fixed x and chosen y is random due to stochastic labels y′ ~ p(y′|x), so delta(x, y) should be modeled as a random variable with mean E[Delta(y′, y) | x, y]. This suggests:
  - The definition of the true risk in (1) should include an expectation over the randomness of delta, not only over x and the logging policy.
  - Please confirm that the empirical risk estimator in (2) remains unbiased/consistent under this more general model (i.e., with an additional expectation over the feedback noise). If further assumptions are required (e.g., conditional independence of feedback noise given x and y, or SUTVA-like assumptions), please state them.

Technical and presentation comments
- Lines 170–171: The ratio should read h(y|x) / h0(y|x). Also, the lack of linearity in h for the normalized estimator was already noted in [1]; it would be helpful to acknowledge this connection and clarify how your analysis extends or refines theirs.
- Line 276: Use argmin rather than argmax since the objective is risk minimization.
- Line 319: Replace overbars with hats to match prior notation for estimates and to avoid confusion.
- Assumptions and stability: Please spell out the boundedness assumptions on losses used in the concentration arguments for NORM-POEM, and discuss how deviations from these assumptions (e.g., heavy-tailed losses, rare but large propensities) affect the method. A brief sensitivity analysis (e.g., to propensity misspecification or clipping thresholds, if any) would strengthen the empirical section.
- Relation to control variates: The interpretation of normalization as a multiplicative control variate is appealing. It would help readers to explicitly connect the estimator to standard control variate formulations, clarifying when variance reduction is guaranteed and when bias could be introduced (if any additional normalization or clipping is used in practice).

Strengths
- Clear and relevant problem: Batch learning from logged bandit feedback is of growing importance, and the paper tackles a central weakness of IPS-based learning—propensity overfitting.
- Conceptual contribution: Framing normalization as a multiplicative control variate is insightful and provides intuition for why and when the approach should help.
- Theoretical development: The analysis of the normalized estimator’s behavior is interesting and advances understanding beyond [1].
- Empirical results: Consistent and meaningful improvements over POEM across multiple datasets, with reasonable experimental design.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Some ambiguity in foundational assumptions (loss range, determinism vs. randomness of feedback) makes it harder to interpret both the theory and experiments. Clarifying these points would significantly improve the paper’s accessibility.
- While the paper shows that normalization helps, a more systematic discussion of failure modes (e.g., extreme propensities, miscalibrated logging models, unbounded losses) would be valuable for practitioners.
- Minor notation and typographic issues (noted above) slightly hinder readability.

Overall assessment
This is a solid and timely contribution that deepens the analysis of CRM-style methods and proposes a practically effective variant. With clarifications on loss scaling and the stochastic nature of feedback, as well as minor fixes to notation and statements, the paper will be stronger and easier to adopt.

Update after rebuttal

The analysis of propensity overfitting and the use of a multiplicative control variate are compelling, and the experimental gains over POEM are clear on an increasingly important problem.
