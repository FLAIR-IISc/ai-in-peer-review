Due to travel commitments, I was only able to perform a cursory review, which is unfortunate as I found this paper to be quite engaging. I apologize in advance if my limited time has led to any misinterpretations or oversights.

The paper operates in the setting where one wishes to evaluate a new hypothesis using data observed from a different one. For non-deterministic hypotheses, such as conditional distributions, the loss under the new hypothesis can be estimated by reweighting the data points, as described in the equation between (1) and (2).

The authors address a problem they term "propensity overfitting," which seems related to, yet distinct from, the well-known issue of high variance in importance weights. The examples suggest this problem occurs when there is insufficient exploration in the data—that is, for a given x, few or no observed y values correspond to the new hypothesis being evaluated. Since a similar mechanism is responsible for high variance in the weights, the paper would benefit from a more detailed explanation of the relationship and distinction between these two problems.

While I did not have sufficient time to investigate the technical details or novelty of the proposed solution, its core idea appears plausible.
