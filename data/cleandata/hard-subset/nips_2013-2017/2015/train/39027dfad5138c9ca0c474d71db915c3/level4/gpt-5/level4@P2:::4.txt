Other comments
- Modeling of feedback in Section 3: The setup (around line 110) presents δ(x, y) as deterministic, but in many logging scenarios the observed feedback is intrinsically stochastic (e.g., noisy labels in classification). Concretely, given x, a historical policy selects y, while the true label y′ is drawn from p(y′|x), and the observed loss is Δ(y′, y). Thus δ(x, y) should be treated as a random variable with mean E[Δ(Y′, y) | x, y] under Y′ ∼ p(·|x). While this does not change the form of the IPS estimator in (2), the definition of risk in (1) would be more general and precise if it included an additional expectation over the randomness of δ (or explicitly stated that δ(x, y) denotes E[δ | x, y]).
- Lines 170–171: The ratio should read h(y|x) / h0(y|x). It may also be worth noting that the lack of equivariance of the standard IPS estimator was already discussed in [1].
- Line 276: Use argmin rather than argmax, since the objective minimizes risk.
- Line 319: Replace bars with hats to keep notation consistent with earlier empirical quantities.

This paper is a compelling follow-up to [1]. The identification and analysis of “propensity overfitting” are insightful, and the use of a multiplicative control variate leading to the self-normalized estimator is a neat and principled idea. Empirically, the self-normalized approach consistently improves over POEM on batch learning from logged bandit feedback, an area of growing importance. Some presentation details (loss scaling, treatment of stochastic feedback, and notation) could be clarified, but overall the contribution is original and significant.
