This paper presents an approach for "counterfactual learning", a learning scenario that is a specialization of multi-label learning. In multi-label learning, the ground-truth annotation for a sample provides a 0/1 label for each of the classes; here, instead, only the label for one class (i.e., bandit feedback) is available. The authors' aim is to improve an estimator (POEM) recently presented by reference [1] at ICML 2015.

The experimental results show that Norm-POEM outperforms POEM on all datasets and is not far from a CRF using full multi-label information. Overall, the paper is convincing and worth publication. However, there are several concerns and suggestions that need to be addressed.

Firstly, the commentary and notations concerning the main equation (2) seem incorrect or at least misleading. An expectation is a quantity of the type ∑ f(x) p(x), where the expected quantity, f(x), is everything else than p(x), the probability measure. In equation (2), the loss is not the expected quantity, which makes the subsequent comment (page 2, second paragraph) that (2) has an "anomaly" because it is not invariant to translation of the loss technically incorrect. Furthermore, the ensuing Example 1 is based on this wrong presumption, which unsurprisingly does not hold.

In practical terms, the estimator in (2) is weak to its denominator, π, which can be 0 if the sample has never been observed in the prior. The modified estimator in (7) seems better in principle because the effects of π are mollified through the double denominator.

I hope the authors will modify their commentary should the paper be accepted. The current explanation may cause confusion and undermine the credibility of the paper.

The authors should also consider providing more insights into the connection between counterfactual learning and multi-label learning. While the paper mentions that counterfactual learning is a specialization of multi-label learning, it would be helpful to discuss the implications of this relationship and how the proposed approach can be applied to more general multi-label learning problems.

Additionally, it would be beneficial to provide more details about the experimental setup, such as the specific datasets used, the number of samples, and the class distribution. This information would help readers better understand the results and the performance of the proposed approach.

In conclusion, while the paper has some minor issues that need to be addressed, it presents a valuable contribution to the field of counterfactual learning. With some revisions to the commentary and additional details about the experimental setup, the paper has the potential to make a significant impact.
