

The paper investigates a critical issue in batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF), a setting with significant relevance to real-world applications like ad ranking and recommendation systems. The authors identify a previously overlooked "propensity overfitting" problem in the conventional counterfactual risk estimator used within the Counterfactual Risk Minimization (CRM) framework [1]. This problem arises when the unbiased estimator (Equation 2) is used for empirical risk minimization in complex hypothesis spaces, leading to severely biased risk estimates that can cause learning algorithms to select hypotheses with arbitrarily low estimated risk. The authors propose replacing the standard estimator with a self-normalized risk estimator (Equation 7), which resolves the identified anomalies and avoids propensity overfitting. This leads to a new learning algorithm called Norm-POEM for structured output prediction, which they demonstrate consistently outperforms the previous approach on multiple multi-label classification datasets.

Quality: The paper makes a technically sound contribution that addresses a genuine problem in the BLBF literature. The identification of propensity overfitting is well-motivated through both theoretical analysis (Section 4) and empirical examples. The authors provide a rigorous theoretical foundation for their self-normalized estimator (Theorem 2), demonstrating its strong consistency and desirable properties (equivariance, boundedness) that the conventional estimator lacks. The experimental evaluation (Section 7) is thorough and well-designed, using the standard Supervised 7→ Bandit methodology to test the limits of counterfactual learning. However, the paper would benefit from deeper analysis of the optimization challenges specific to the Norm-POEM objective. While the authors note that the variance estimator (Equation 8) doesn't admit stochastic optimization, they don't fully explore the implications or provide more detailed analysis of the optimization process beyond mentioning L-BFGS was used. Additional experiments comparing optimization approaches would strengthen the evaluation.

Clarity: The paper is exceptionally clear and well-organized. The problem statement, theoretical analysis, and experimental results are presented in a logical sequence that makes the contribution easy to follow. The authors effectively use examples (Section 4) to illustrate the propensity overfitting problem, making the technical points accessible. The writing is precise and avoids unnecessary jargon, with all technical terms properly defined. The paper would be even more useful for practitioners if the software implementation details were slightly more comprehensive.

Originality: While the paper builds on the CRM framework from [1], it makes a significant and novel contribution by identifying a previously unaddressed problem (propensity overfitting) and proposing a theoretically grounded solution. The self-normalized estimator represents a meaningful modification to the risk estimation approach in BLBF, and the authors convincingly demonstrate that this simple change leads to substantial empirical improvements. The paper distinguishes itself clearly from prior work by addressing a specific technical limitation that was not previously recognized in the BLBF literature.

Significance: The problem of learning from logged bandit feedback is highly significant for real-world applications in online systems, where historical logs provide the only available training data. The authors convincingly demonstrate that their approach (Norm-POEM) consistently outperforms the previous state-of-the-art (POEM) across multiple datasets. The improvements are not marginal but substantial, with Norm-POEM consistently achieving better generalization performance and avoiding the propensity overfitting issues that plague the previous approach. While the experiments are conducted on standard multi-label classification datasets (following the established methodology in [1]), the paper provides strong evidence that the proposed solution addresses a fundamental limitation in the field. The theoretical guarantees combined with the empirical improvements suggest this approach could be valuable for practical applications where logged bandit feedback is the primary data source.

Strengths: The paper makes a clear, important contribution to the BLBF literature by identifying and solving a previously unrecognized problem. The theoretical analysis is rigorous and well-executed, with clear connections between the problem statement and the proposed solution. The empirical results are convincing and consistently demonstrate the advantages of the self-normalized estimator. The paper is well-written and accessible to readers familiar with machine learning fundamentals.

Weaknesses: The paper would be strengthened by more detailed analysis of the optimization process for Norm-POEM, particularly regarding the computational trade-offs. While the authors note that the variance estimator doesn't admit stochastic optimization, they don't provide sufficient justification for why L-BFGS works well in practice or explore alternative optimization strategies. The experimental section could also include more discussion about the sensitivity of the hyperparameters (M and λ) to the choice of dataset or logging policy.

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a meaningful contribution to a relevant area of machine learning. The identification of propensity overfitting and the proposed solution are significant, and the empirical results convincingly demonstrate the practical benefits of the approach. While the paper could benefit from more detailed analysis of the optimization challenges, the core contribution is well-executed and represents a clear advance over previous work in the field. I recommend acceptance.
