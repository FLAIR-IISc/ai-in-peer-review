Due to time constraints while traveling, I was only able to perform a brief review of this paper. I found the work quite interesting, but it is possible that I have missed or misunderstood some points.

The paper addresses the problem of off-policy evaluation, where the goal is to evaluate a target hypothesis using data generated from a different, logging hypothesis. When these hypotheses are non-deterministic (e.g., conditional distributions), the loss under the target hypothesis can be estimated by reweighting data points via importance sampling, as described in the paper.

The authors introduce the concept of "propensity overfitting," which appears to be related to, but distinct from, the well-known problem of high variance in importance sampling estimators. The provided examples suggest that this problem arises from insufficient exploration in the logging data; that is, for a given context x, few or no outcomes y corresponding to the target hypothesis are observed. Since a similar mechanism explains the high variance of importance weights, it would be beneficial for the authors to elaborate on the precise relationship between propensity overfitting and high-variance estimation.

While I did not have time to check the technical details or novelty of the proposed solution, the underlying idea seems sensible.

Specific points:
*   **l. 199:** I was confused by the argument made here. If n is small compared to k, one would observe very few data points where xi = yi. In that case, shouldn't R̂(h^) be even smaller than -2? I may be missing something, and clarification would be helpful.
*   **Characterization of prior work:** The paper refers to the "unbiased counterfactual risk estimator used in prior works on BLBF [4, 5, 1]". This may not be an entirely fair characterization. While I am not familiar with all three cited papers, it is common practice in this domain to use variance reduction techniques like weight clipping, which introduces bias. The authors should confirm whether the prior works they cite truly use strictly unbiased estimators.
*   The availability of code is appreciated.

In summary, I could not perform a thorough check of the paper's technical soundness. However, it contains interesting ideas that are worthy of further consideration. I therefore lean towards acceptance.
