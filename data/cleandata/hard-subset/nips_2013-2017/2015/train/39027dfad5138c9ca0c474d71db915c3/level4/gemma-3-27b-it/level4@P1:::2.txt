This paper identifies a significant problem with the commonly used counterfactual risk estimator in batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF) – a propensity overfitting issue – and proposes a self-normalized estimator to address it. BLBF is a valuable setting for training online systems from historical data, and the Counterfactual Risk Minimization (CRM) principle provides a framework for designing algorithms in this setting. The authors demonstrate that the conventional estimator suffers from biases when learning complex hypothesis spaces, and their proposed self-normalized estimator elegantly avoids this issue. This leads to a new learning algorithm, Normalized Policy Optimizer for Exponential Models (Norm-POEM), which consistently outperforms the conventional estimator on several multi-label classification problems.

**1 Introduction**
The introduction effectively motivates the problem of learning from logged data, highlighting the challenges posed by bandit feedback and the biases inherent in historical logs. The connection to off-policy evaluation in reinforcement learning is clearly articulated, providing context for the use of counterfactual estimators. The authors successfully establish the importance of the problem and position their work within the broader landscape of causal inference and learning from observational data.

**2 Related work**
The related work section demonstrates a strong understanding of the relevant literature, including connections to causal inference, domain adaptation, and off-policy evaluation. The authors appropriately acknowledge existing counterfactual estimators and highlight the novelty of their approach in identifying and addressing the propensity overfitting issue. The discussion of variance reduction techniques is also relevant and well-placed.

**3 Batch learning from logged bandit feedback**
This section provides a concise and clear formalization of the BLBF problem, following the CRM principle established in prior work. The notation is well-defined, and the explanation of counterfactual estimators and importance sampling is accessible. The presentation of Theorem 1 is standard and appropriately references previous work.

**4 The Propensity Overfitting problem**
This is the core contribution of the paper. The authors convincingly demonstrate a previously unrecognized form of overfitting – propensity overfitting – that can severely bias risk estimates. The example provided is insightful and effectively illustrates the problem. The analysis of why the standard risk estimator is not equivariant is a crucial observation, and the explanation of how this leads to overfitting is well-reasoned.

**5 Control variates and the Self-Normalized estimator**
The authors effectively leverage control variate techniques to derive the self-normalized risk estimator. The justification for using S(h) as a control variate is clear, and the explanation of how this estimator addresses the issues identified in Section 4 is compelling. Theorem 2 establishes the consistency of the self-normalized estimator, further strengthening the theoretical foundation of the proposed approach.

**6 Learning method: Norm-POEM**
This section details the Norm-POEM algorithm, building upon the CRM principle and the self-normalized estimator. The description of the hypothesis space and the variance estimator is clear and concise. The discussion of computational efficiency and optimization techniques is also valuable.

**7 Experiments**
The experimental results provide strong evidence for the effectiveness of Norm-POEM. The consistent outperformance of Norm-POEM over POEM on multiple datasets is compelling. The analysis of Ŝ(h) and the demonstration that Norm-POEM is less susceptible to bias from loss translation further support the theoretical claims. The comparison to a full-information CRF provides a useful benchmark.

**Overall Assessment**

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound, with a strong theoretical foundation and convincing empirical results. The authors carefully analyze the limitations of existing methods and propose a novel solution that addresses a significant problem. The work is complete and represents a substantial contribution to the field of batch learning from logged bandit feedback.

**Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The notation is clear, and the explanations are accessible. However, some sections could benefit from further clarification (see Comments to Authors).

**Originality:** The identification of propensity overfitting and the development of the self-normalized estimator are novel contributions. The paper builds upon existing work but significantly advances the state of the art in BLBF.

**Significance:** The results are important and have the potential to be widely used by researchers and practitioners working in online learning, recommendation systems, and other areas where learning from logged data is essential. The paper addresses a difficult problem in a demonstrably better way than previous research.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Addresses a critical, previously unrecognised problem (propensity overfitting) in BLBF.
*   Proposes a theoretically sound and empirically validated solution (self-normalized estimator & Norm-POEM).
*   Consistently outperforms existing methods on multiple datasets.
*   Clear and well-structured presentation of the problem, solution, and experiments.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The initial description of the anomaly in the unbiased estimator is technically imprecise. (addressed in the comments to authors)
*   While the experimental results are strong, further investigation into the scalability of Norm-POEM to very large datasets could be beneficial.




**Recommendation:**

Accept with minor revisions. The paper presents a significant contribution to the field and is worthy of publication in NIPS. I recommend the authors address the minor technical concerns outlined in the "Comments to Authors" section below.
