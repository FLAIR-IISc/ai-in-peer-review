Summary and relation to prior work
This paper addresses batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF), a setting central to training large-scale interactive systems (ads, search, recommendation) from historical logs. Prior work on Counterfactual Risk Minimization (CRM) relies on inverse propensity scoring (IPS)–based risk estimators within an empirical risk minimization framework. The paper identifies a failure mode of the conventional (un/self-normalized) IPS estimator when used for learning over rich hypothesis classes: propensity overfitting. In this phenomenon, the empirical objective can be minimized by hypotheses that systematically inflate or deflate importance weights rather than aligning with low loss, leading to degenerate solutions and estimates that are not bounded by the loss range and are not equivariant to loss shifts.

To remedy this, the authors adopt a multiplicative control variate and propose the self-normalized IPS estimator (SNIPS) for learning, which normalizes by the sum of importance weights. They show SNIPS is bounded within the loss range, equivariant to additive loss shifts, and strongly consistent. Building on this estimator, they develop Norm-POEM, an algorithm analogous to POEM but replacing IPS with SNIPS in both the risk and variance terms of the CRM objective. They provide an approximate variance estimator for the ratio estimator and optimize a non-convex objective using L-BFGS. Empirically, on several multilabel classification datasets (Scene, Yeast, TMC, LYRL), Norm-POEM consistently outperforms POEM under a supervised-to-bandit evaluation protocol. They also diagnose propensity overfitting using the sum of normalized weights Ŝ(h) and show SNIPS avoids these pathologies, including invariance to loss translation.

This contribution connects to a substantial literature on importance weighting and causal inference: IPS and clipping [Rosenbaum & Rubin; Ionides], learning bounds under importance weighting [Cortes et al., NIPS], off-policy evaluation and high-confidence evaluation [Dudík et al.; Thomas et al.], and control variates/self-normalized IS [Hesterberg; Owen; Kong]. Within NIPS/ML, the work relates to learning under covariate shift/domain adaptation, off-policy evaluation in RL, and prior BLBF methods such as the offset tree and learning from logged implicit exploration data (NIPS).

Quality
The paper is technically sound and well-motivated. The diagnosis of propensity overfitting is clear and compelling (with a didactic example), and the shift to SNIPS is well grounded in Monte Carlo theory. The theoretical claims are appropriate: strong consistency of SNIPS and the bounded/equivariant properties; the CRM-style generalization bound leverages prior work and is adapted via an approximate variance for the ratio estimator. The choice to clip weights ensures finite variance, and the discussion of bias/variance trade-offs is sensible.

That said, the optimization aspect could use deeper analysis. The objective is non-convex and non-smooth in places; the paper argues L-BFGS works well in practice, but convergence behavior, initialization, and sensitivity to M and λ would benefit from more detail. The empirical section is carefully set up and shows consistent gains, but additional baselines (e.g., doubly robust objectives/estimators) and uncertainty quantification would strengthen the claims.

Clarity
The presentation is clear and carefully written. The exposition of propensity overfitting, the role of Ŝ(h), and the benefits of self-normalization are particularly clear. Minor issues: there is at least one occurrence of “Conterfactual” that should be corrected to “Counterfactual,” and consistency in naming (Norm-POEM vs. NormPOEM) and citation formatting would help. When introducing bounded loss assumptions (e.g., δ ∈ [−1, 0]) and clipping M, it would help to state these assumptions up front and keep notation uniform.

Originality
While SNIPS is a classical estimator, its targeted use to remedy propensity overfitting within CRM and to construct a learning algorithm appears novel. The identification and formalization of propensity overfitting in BLBF, and the empirical demonstration that normalization stabilizes learning objectives, represent a meaningful and new contribution beyond POEM.

Significance
The work addresses a practical and impactful problem. If CRM objectives are vulnerable to propensity overfitting in realistic settings, a robust alternative is valuable. The empirical improvements over POEM are substantial in the tested benchmarks, and the diagnostic Ŝ(h) analysis is persuasive. The significance would be stronger with: (i) comparisons to doubly robust learning objectives, (ii) experiments beyond those in [1] and/or with real-world logs, and (iii) more guidance on hyperparameter selection and sensitivity analyses. Scalability to very large action spaces and policies with limited support also merits discussion.

Pros (arguments for acceptance)
- Clear identification and analysis of a non-trivial failure mode (propensity overfitting) in a widely used estimator for BLBF/CRM.
- Principled solution via self-normalization with solid theoretical properties (boundedness, equivariance, strong consistency).
- A concrete learning algorithm (Norm-POEM) with consistent empirical gains across multiple datasets and training sizes.
- Useful diagnostics (Ŝ(h)) that align theory and practice; compelling evidence that normalization addresses the pathology.
- Relevance to NIPS community topics: off-policy evaluation, importance weighting, structured prediction, and causal inference.

Cons (arguments against acceptance)
- Experiments largely mirror [1] (same task family and protocol); limited exploration of additional domains or real-world logs.
- Missing baselines: doubly robust estimators/objectives and other variance-reduction schemes would contextualize gains.
- Limited analysis of optimization properties and hyperparameter sensitivity; the variance estimator is approximate.
- Practical constraints (e.g., lack of stochastic optimization) may limit scalability; empirical runtime comparisons could be more systematic.

Suggestions for improvement
- Report uncertainty: add standard deviations/error bars over the multiple repetitions and conduct statistical significance tests.
- Include additional baselines: clipped IPS learning without normalization, doubly robust learning, and offset-tree-style methods where applicable.
- Expand optimization details: initialization strategy, convergence diagnostics, wall-clock vs. iterations, and sensitivity to M and λ; provide guidance for setting M beyond grid search.
- Analyze regimes where SNIPS may underperform IPS (e.g., when loss variance dominates weight variance) and discuss trade-offs.
- Explore robustness to support violations and extreme propensities, and provide ablations for the effect of clipping.
- Add one or two datasets beyond those used in [1], or a small real-world case study if possible.
- Minor edits: fix typos, standardize algorithm naming, and ensure consistent citation formatting and venue/year information.

Overall
A solid and relevant paper that advances BLBF by diagnosing a crucial failure mode and offering a principled, effective fix. With stronger experimental breadth and a bit more algorithmic/optimization analysis, this could be a definitive reference for counterfactual learning with logged bandit feedback. I lean toward acceptance.
