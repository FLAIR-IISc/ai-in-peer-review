When data are generated under one hypothesis but we wish to evaluate another, and the hypotheses are stochastic (e.g., conditional distributions), we can estimate the loss under the target hypothesis by reweighting data points (see the equation between (1) and (2)).

The paper addresses a problem related to—but not identical with—the classic high-variance issue of importance weights. The authors term this “propensity overfitting.” Their examples suggest it arises under insufficient exploration: for a given x, we observe few or no y aligned with the target hypothesis we want to evaluate. Because a similar mechanism also drives the inflation of importance-weight variance, it would be helpful if the paper clarified more explicitly how propensity overfitting relates to, and differs from, the high-variance problem (e.g., whether it is primarily a finite-sample overfitting phenomenon, a misspecification issue, or a consequence of extreme propensities).

I did not have time to verify all technical details or assess novelty thoroughly, but the core idea appears sensible.
