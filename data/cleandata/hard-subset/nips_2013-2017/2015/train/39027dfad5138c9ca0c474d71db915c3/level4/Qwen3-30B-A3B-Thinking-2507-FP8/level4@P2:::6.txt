

This paper identifies a critical flaw in counterfactual risk minimization (CRM) for batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF), where conventional unbiased estimators suffer from "propensity overfitting" when applied to complex hypothesis spaces. The authors propose a self-normalized risk estimator that avoids this problem, demonstrating theoretically and empirically that it leads to better generalization performance. The work builds on the CRM principle established in prior NIPS work (Swaminathan & Joachims, 2015), extending it with a novel estimator that addresses a previously unobserved vulnerability.

The paper makes a strong contribution to the field by identifying a subtle but severe problem that can cause degenerate behavior in existing BLBF methods. The authors show that conventional estimators can produce risk estimates arbitrarily lower than the true risk when the hypothesis space is complex, leading to poor generalization. This is a significant theoretical advance, as the paper provides a clear characterization of this "propensity overfitting" phenomenon, distinguishing it from conventional loss overfitting. The self-normalized estimator they propose resolves all the theoretical anomalies identified in the paper, maintaining equivariance under loss transformations and ensuring bounded risk estimates.

In terms of quality, the paper is technically sound with rigorous theoretical analysis. The authors prove that their estimator is strongly consistent and derive a principled learning algorithm (Norm-POEM) based on the CRM principle. The theoretical results are well-motivated and clearly presented, with theorems properly contextualized within the BLBF literature. The experimental evaluation is comprehensive, comparing Norm-POEM against POEM (the baseline CRM algorithm) across multiple multi-label classification datasets from the LibSVM repository. The results consistently show that Norm-POEM outperforms POEM across datasets, training sizes, and loss transformations, with the self-normalized estimator effectively preventing propensity overfitting as evidenced by the empirical validation of Ŝ(ĥ) being close to its expected value of 1.

The paper excels in originality, addressing a previously overlooked vulnerability in the BLBF literature. While the CRM principle and importance sampling have been well-studied (e.g., in Swaminathan & Joachims, 2015; Dudik et al., 2014), the authors identify a specific failure mode that affects practical implementations. Their solution—using multiplicative control variates to derive a self-normalized estimator—represents a novel and elegant approach that builds upon but significantly extends prior work on variance reduction in importance sampling. The distinction between propensity overfitting and conventional loss overfitting is particularly insightful and clarifies a key conceptual gap in the literature.

The significance of this work is substantial. BLBF is a crucial paradigm for learning from historical data in real-world interactive systems (e.g., ad placement, recommendation systems), where full-information feedback is unavailable. The authors' solution directly addresses a practical vulnerability that could undermine the reliability of existing BLBF methods. The empirical results demonstrate consistent improvements across multiple datasets, suggesting that the proposed estimator could be broadly applicable in real-world settings. The paper also provides a clear implementation (with code available), facilitating adoption by the research community.

The paper is well-written and clearly organized, making the technical contributions accessible. The exposition of the propensity overfitting problem through concrete examples (Section 4) is particularly effective, helping readers understand why the conventional approach fails. The connection between the theoretical results and the experimental findings is strong, with the empirical validation directly supporting the theoretical claims.

A minor weakness is that the paper could more thoroughly discuss the computational cost of the self-normalized estimator. While the authors note that Norm-POEM converges faster than POEM in practice, a more detailed analysis of computational complexity compared to other variants would strengthen the contribution. Additionally, the paper could better contextualize how their approach relates to other variance reduction techniques (e.g., doubly robust estimators), though this is a minor point.

Overall, this is a high-quality contribution that addresses a meaningful gap in the BLBF literature. The paper's theoretical insights are novel and well-supported, and the empirical results confirm the practical benefits of the proposed estimator. The work advances the state of the art in counterfactual learning by resolving a previously unaddressed problem that affects the reliability of existing methods.

Pro arguments for acceptance:
- Identifies and resolves a significant theoretical vulnerability in BLBF methods
- Provides strong theoretical guarantees and empirical validation
- Demonstrates consistent performance improvements across multiple datasets
- Well-motivated, clearly written, and directly addresses a practical problem
- Builds upon and extends established work in the field

Con arguments against acceptance:
- Minor computational complexity analysis could be expanded
- Limited discussion of connections to other variance reduction techniques

This paper makes a clear contribution to the field of counterfactual learning and satisfies all four NIPS criteria. The problem it addresses is important, the solution is novel and rigorously validated, and the results have significant practical implications for real-world applications of BLBF. I strongly recommend acceptance.
