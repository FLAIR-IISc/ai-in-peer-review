Summary
The paper revisits counterfactual risk estimation for batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF). It identifies a failure mode—propensity overfitting—of the standard inverse propensity score (IPS) estimator used within the Counterfactual Risk Minimization (CRM) framework. To address this, the authors propose a self-normalized importance sampling estimator (a multiplicative control variate/ratio estimator) that is bounded and equivariant to affine transformations of the loss, and prove strong consistency. Plugging this estimator into CRM yields a new learning algorithm, Norm-POEM, for structured prediction with exponential-family linear models. The method includes an approximate variance estimate for the ratio estimator, uses clipping to control variance, and is optimized via L-BFGS. Experiments on several multi-label datasets (Scene, Yeast, TMC, LYRL) using a supervised-to-bandit protocol show that Norm-POEM consistently outperforms the original POEM, better controls Ŝ(h) around its expected value of 1, is robust to loss translations, and often converges faster.

Relation to prior work
The paper builds directly on Swaminathan & Joachims (ICML’15) and the CRM principle, connects to importance sampling for off-policy evaluation, and situates the proposal among variance reduction techniques (clipping, control variates) and doubly robust estimators. The use of self-normalized importance sampling is well-established in Monte Carlo and RL literatures, but its analysis and deployment to counterfactual learning for BLBF, especially to mitigate propensity overfitting within learning (not just evaluation), is a valuable and timely contribution.

Evaluation by criteria
- Quality: The argumentation is solid. The self-normalized estimator’s properties (boundedness, equivariance, consistency) are clearly stated, and the experiments are carefully executed and aligned with prior work. The variance estimate is approximate, and clipping remains necessary, but the empirical evidence supports the claims.
- Clarity: The paper is generally well written and easy to follow. The discussion of propensity overfitting is persuasive, with a helpful illustrative example and diagnostics via Ŝ(h).
- Originality: While self-normalization itself is known, its explicit use to counteract propensity overfitting in learning and its integration into CRM to form Norm-POEM are novel and well-motivated.
- Significance: The work addresses a prominent pain point for BLBF and provides a practical, robust alternative with clear gains. Given the prevalence of logged-bandit learning in applications (ads, recommenders, search), the impact could be substantial.

Strengths
- Clear identification and analysis of propensity overfitting with IPS within CRM.
- Principled replacement with a self-normalized estimator that is bounded and equivariant, addressing key anomalies of IPS.
- Strong consistency guarantee and a practical variance estimate enabling a CRM-style objective.
- Consistent empirical gains over POEM across multiple datasets and dataset sizes; robustness to loss translations demonstrated both in Ŝ(h) and test performance.
- Competitive or better runtimes despite additional normalization.
- Good connection to related literatures (importance sampling, OPE, causal inference).

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Comparisons: Experiments mainly compare to POEM and a full-information CRF “skyline.” Including strong baselines such as clipped IPS without CRM, doubly robust estimators, or weighted/defensive mixtures would strengthen the case.
- Theoretical development: While consistency is shown, a generalization bound for the self-normalized estimator within CRM (analogous to the empirical-Bernstein style bound in prior work) would make the learning objective more principled and may guide regularization.
- Practical assumptions: Results rely on known propensities with full support. Discussion and experiments with estimated propensities (and their misspecification) would improve practical relevance.
- Sensitivity studies: More systematic ablations on the clipping threshold M and variance regularization λ, and analysis of the bias-variance trade-off under self-normalization, would be useful.
- Scope of tasks: All evaluations are supervised-to-bandit multi-label classification. Additional domains (e.g., contextual bandits with different action spaces or RL-style off-policy settings) would underscore generality.
- Optimization: The non-convex objective is optimized with L-BFGS; a brief discussion of convergence behavior and potential issues (e.g., local minima) beyond empirical observations would be helpful.

Pros for acceptance
- Addresses a real and underappreciated failure mode in a widely used estimator.
- Simple, principled fix with strong intuition and supporting theory.
- Clear and consistent empirical improvements in accuracy and robustness.
- Practical algorithm likely to be adopted by practitioners.

Cons for acceptance
- Limited baseline comparisons and task diversity.
- Lack of a new generalization bound tailored to the self-normalized CRM objective.
- Dependence on clipping and approximate variance estimation without deeper analysis of induced bias.

Overall assessment
This is a strong and timely paper. It offers a well-argued diagnosis, a principled remedy, and convincing empirical evidence that the remedy improves both stability and performance. With broader comparisons and additional theoretical analysis, it could be even stronger, but as it stands the contribution is clear and significant. I recommend acceptance.
