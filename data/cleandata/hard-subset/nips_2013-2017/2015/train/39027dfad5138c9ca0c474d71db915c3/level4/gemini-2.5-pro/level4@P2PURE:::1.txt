This is a light review, as I had limited time due to travel. I found the paper quite interesting, but I may have missed some details and apologize for any potential misunderstandings.

The paper addresses the problem of off-policy evaluation, where data generated under one hypothesis is used to evaluate a different one. When the hypotheses are non-deterministic, the standard approach is to estimate the loss of the target hypothesis by reweighting data points with importance sampling, as outlined in the introduction.

The authors focus on a problem they term "propensity overfitting," which they frame as distinct from the well-known issue of high variance in importance sampling weights. The examples provided suggest that this problem occurs when there is insufficient exploration in the logging policy—that is, for a given context x, the action y corresponding to the new hypothesis is rarely or never observed. Since a similar lack of support is the canonical explanation for high-variance weights, the paper would be strengthened by a more detailed explanation of the precise relationship and distinction between these two problems.

While I did not have time to vet the technical details or the novelty of the proposed solution, the core idea appears sensible.

Specific points:
*   l. 199: I was unable to follow the reasoning here. If n is small compared to k, we would observe very few data points where x_i = y_i. My understanding is that this would make \hat R(h^) even smaller than -2. Could the authors clarify this point?
*   The characterization of the "unbiased counterfactual risk estimator used in prior works on BLBF [1, 4, 5]" may be imprecise. While I am not familiar with all three papers, it is common for such methods to employ techniques like weight clipping, which reduces variance at the cost of introducing bias.
*   The inclusion of publicly available code is appreciated.

In summary, I could not conduct a full technical verification of the paper's soundness. However, it contains interesting and thought-provoking ideas. On that basis, I recommend acceptance.
