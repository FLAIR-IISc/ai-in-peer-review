This paper identifies a critical failure mode of the conventional counterfactual risk estimator used in batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF), which the authors term "propensity overfitting". The core idea is that when learning over expressive hypothesis spaces, the standard estimator can be minimized not only by finding low-loss actions but also by exploiting the importance weights themselves—for instance, by assigning high probability to actions that happen to have small logging probabilities in the data. To address this, the paper proposes replacing the standard inverse propensity scoring estimator with a self-normalized (ratio) estimator, a technique that uses the sum of importance weights as a multiplicative control variate. This leads to a new learning algorithm, Norm-POEM, built upon the Counterfactual Risk Minimization (CRM) principle. The authors provide strong empirical evidence on several multi-label classification tasks showing that their proposed method is more robust and achieves better generalization performance than its predecessor, POEM.

**Strengths:**

*   **Significance and Originality:** The primary contribution is the identification and clear articulation of propensity overfitting. This is a subtle but important problem that goes beyond the typical discussion of high variance in importance sampling. The illustrative examples in Section 4 are particularly effective at demonstrating how standard estimators can lead to pathological solutions, providing a strong motivation for the work.
*   **Technical Quality:** The proposed solution—using a self-normalized estimator—is well-principled and elegantly resolves the identified anomalies of the standard estimator (e.g., lack of equivariance to loss translations and unboundedness). The connection to control variates from the Monte Carlo literature provides a solid theoretical foundation.
*   **Empirical Evaluation:** The experiments are well-designed and convincing. By using the "Supervised -> Bandit" conversion methodology, the authors can perform a controlled evaluation against a known ground truth. The results in Tables 1-3 and Figure 1 clearly demonstrate that Norm-POEM not only outperforms the baseline but does so precisely because it mitigates the propensity overfitting problem, as shown by the `Ŝ(h)` values in Table 2.

**Weaknesses and Questions for Clarification:**

*   **Relation to High Variance:** The paper presents propensity overfitting as a distinct problem from the well-known high variance of importance sampling. However, both issues seem to stem from the same root cause: training points with small propensities (`p_i`), which can occur with insufficient exploration by the logging policy. The paper would be strengthened by a more detailed discussion of the relationship between these two concepts. Is propensity overfitting a particular manifestation of the high-variance problem that becomes acute during optimization (ERM), as opposed to just evaluation of a fixed policy?
*   **Characterization of Prior Work:** The text states that "virtually all existing works on BLBF have focused on a particular unbiased estimator." This might be an oversimplification, as many key papers in this area (including some cited, like [1] and [5]) explicitly discuss and use techniques like weight clipping/truncation, which introduce bias to control variance. A more nuanced phrasing that acknowledges these biased variants while clarifying how propensity overfitting is a different kind of pathology would be more accurate.
*   **Clarity of Example 1:** The calculation for `R̂(hoverfit)` in Example 1 could be made clearer. Given the definition of `hoverfit`, `h(y_i | x_i) = 1` for each data point `(x_i, y_i)`. The estimated risk is then `(1/n) * sum(delta_i * 1/p_i)`. With `p_i = 1/k`, this becomes `(k/n) * sum(delta_i)`. Since `delta_i` is at most -1, the estimated risk is at most `-k`. While correct, walking the reader through these steps more explicitly would prevent potential confusion.

**Summary of Arguments:**

*   **Pro-acceptance:** The paper addresses a significant and previously under-appreciated problem in counterfactual learning. The proposed solution is simple, theoretically sound, and empirically shown to be highly effective. The work is a clear and valuable contribution to the field. The availability of code is also a plus.
*   **Con-acceptance:** There are minor issues of clarity regarding the relationship to existing concepts (high variance) and the characterization of related work. These points are easily addressable and do not detract from the core contribution.

Overall, this is a strong paper with an interesting insight and compelling results. I recommend acceptance.
