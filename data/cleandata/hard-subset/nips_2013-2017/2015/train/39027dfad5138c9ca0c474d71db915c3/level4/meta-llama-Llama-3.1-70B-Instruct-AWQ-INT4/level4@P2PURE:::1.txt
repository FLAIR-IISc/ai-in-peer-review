I had limited time to complete this review, which is unfortunate given that I found the paper engaging. As a result, I may have missed or misinterpreted certain points, and I apologize if this is the case.

When evaluating data from a given hypothesis, it is often desirable to assess the performance of an alternative hypothesis. For non-deterministic hypotheses, such as conditional distributions, the loss under the new hypothesis can be estimated by reweighting the data points, as shown in the equation between (1) and (2).

The authors appear to address a problem related to, but distinct from, the issue of large variance in the weights. They term this problem "propensity overfitting." The provided examples suggest that this problem arises when there is insufficient exploration, resulting in few or no observations of the outcome variable y corresponding to the new hypothesis being evaluated, given a specific x. Since a similar argument explains the large variance of the weights, it would be beneficial to elaborate on the relationship between these two problems.

Due to time constraints, I was unable to thoroughly examine the details or assess the novelty of the proposed solution. However, the underlying idea seems reasonable.

Some specific comments on the manuscript:

- Line 199: I am unclear about the statement. If n is small compared to k, we would expect to see only a few data points with xi = yi. Consequently, \hat R(h^) should be even smaller than -2. Could you clarify this point?
- The claim that prior works on BLBF [4, 5, 1] use an unbiased counterfactual risk estimator may not be entirely accurate. Although I am not familiar with all three papers, I suspect that they may employ techniques like clipping, which can lead to biased estimation with reduced variance.

I appreciate that the authors have made their code available.

In summary, while I was unable to verify the technical soundness of the paper, it undoubtedly presents interesting ideas worthy of further consideration. Therefore, I suggest accepting the paper.
