## Review of NIPS 2015 Submission: [Paper Title - please fill in]

**Summary:**

This paper addresses the important problem of batch learning from logged bandit feedback, a scenario highly relevant to applications like ad ranking and recommendation systems. The authors identify a significant overfitting issue within the recently proposed Counterfactual Risk Minimization (CRM) principle when applied to this setting. To mitigate this, they introduce a novel solution based on multiplicative control variates, acknowledging that this approach introduces a biased estimator.

**Quality:**

This is a high-quality and well-motivated contribution. As a direct follow-up to [1], I anticipated, and would have benefited from, more extensive experimentation. Specifically, a deeper analysis of the training objective’s optimization – beyond the discussion on lines 415-420 – would strengthen the paper. Investigating the sensitivity of performance to different optimization parameters and strategies would provide valuable insights.  The theoretical justification for the proposed method is sound, and the technical details appear carefully considered.

**Clarity:**

The presentation is exceptionally clear, and the paper is well-written and easy to follow. The authors effectively explain the identified problem and their proposed solution, making the core concepts accessible. The notation is consistent and well-defined.

**Originality:**

While building on the foundation laid in [1], the paper presents a significant modification to the risk estimator – specifically, making it self-normalizing – which demonstrably improves performance across the experimental evaluations. This constitutes a valuable and original contribution to the field.  The use of multiplicative control variates in this context appears novel.

**Significance:**

Learning from logged data is a crucial challenge in many real-world machine learning applications. However, the extent to which the substantial experimental improvements demonstrated in this paper translate directly to more complex, real-world scenarios remains somewhat unclear. While the results on the datasets used are compelling, further investigation into the method’s scalability and performance on larger, more heterogeneous datasets would enhance the assessment of its practical significance. 

**Detailed Remarks:**

*   **Line 365:** When reporting results based on multiple repetitions, please include a measure of variance (e.g., standard deviation or confidence intervals) to provide a more complete understanding of the results' statistical significance.
*   The paper accurately identifies a critical limitation of the CRM principle in the context of logged bandit feedback. The proposed solution, while introducing bias, appears to be a pragmatic and effective approach to address the overfitting issue.
*   Overall, this is a solid and well-written paper addressing a relevant and novel problem. I strongly recommend the authors consider extending the experimental evaluation. Specifically, exploring applications beyond those previously presented in [1] – and including some results from actual real-world deployments – would significantly bolster the paper’s impact. 
*   It would be helpful to briefly discuss the potential impact of the introduced bias on the final learned policy, and whether any methods exist to mitigate this bias further.
