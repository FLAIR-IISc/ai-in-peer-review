### Review

**Summary of the Paper**

This paper addresses the problem of Batch Learning from Logged Bandit Feedback (BLBF), a crucial setting for improving interactive systems like search engines and recommender systems using historical data. The authors build upon the Counterfactual Risk Minimization (CRM) principle, notably the POEM algorithm from Swaminathan and Joachims (2015), which uses an unbiased inverse propensity score (IPS) estimator to learn a new policy.

The central contribution of this work is the identification and formalization of a critical failure mode of this conventional unbiased estimator, which the authors term "Propensity Overfitting". They demonstrate through a compelling example and theoretical argument that when learning over complex hypothesis spaces, the model can achieve an artificially low (or even arbitrarily negative) empirical risk not by fitting the true loss function, but by exploiting the propensity weights. For instance, a model can learn to assign high probability to examples with negative loss already present in the training data, or assign zero probability to examples with positive loss, leading to severely biased risk estimates and poor generalization.

To solve this problem, the authors propose replacing the standard unbiased estimator with a self-normalized risk estimator. This estimator, derived using multiplicative control variates, is a ratio of the weighted sum of losses to the weighted sum of propensities. The paper highlights several desirable properties of this estimator: it is equivariant to translations of the loss function, its value is always bounded by the range of the observed losses, and it is strongly consistent. These properties provably prevent the propensity overfitting anomaly.

Based on this improved estimator, the paper introduces a new learning algorithm, the Normalized Policy Optimizer for Exponential Models (Norm-POEM). An extensive empirical evaluation on several multi-label classification tasks demonstrates that Norm-POEM consistently and significantly outperforms the conventional POEM algorithm, shows greater robustness, and surprisingly, often converges faster in practice.

**Strengths and Weaknesses**

**Quality:** The paper is of high technical quality.

*   **Strengths:** The identification of the "propensity overfitting" problem is a sharp, insightful, and important contribution to the field of counterfactual learning. The theoretical analysis is sound, clearly explaining the anomalies of the unbiased estimator (e.g., lack of equivariance) and the principled advantages of the self-normalized alternative. The claims are strongly supported by a well-designed and comprehensive set of experiments. The "Supervised -> Bandit" evaluation protocol allows for a controlled and convincing demonstration of the problem and the effectiveness of the solution. The experiments directly measure the hypothesized failure modes (e.g., by tracking the sum of propensity weights Ŝ(h)) and confirm the robustness of Norm-POEM.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally clear and well-written.

*   **Strengths:** The organization is logical and easy to follow. The motivation is established powerfully in Section 4 with a simple yet devastating example of propensity overfitting, which makes the subsequent technical contributions easy to appreciate. The distinction between conventional "Loss Overfitting" and the newly identified "Propensity Overfitting" is a useful conceptual contribution. The paper provides sufficient detail for an expert to understand the method and potentially reproduce the results.

**Originality:** The work is highly original.

*   **Strengths:** While self-normalized estimators are an established tool in the broader statistics and Monte Carlo literature, their application to resolve the specific and newly identified problem of propensity overfitting within the CRM framework is novel. This is not an incremental improvement; it diagnoses a fundamental flaw in a standard methodology and provides a principled and effective solution. The paper clearly positions its contribution against previous work and makes a significant conceptual advance.

**Significance:** The results presented in this paper are very significant and likely to have a substantial impact.

*   **Strengths:** BLBF is a problem of immense practical importance, and this paper demonstrates a critical weakness in a state-of-the-art approach. The proposed Norm-POEM algorithm is not just a theoretical construct; it is a practical algorithm that is shown to be more accurate, more robust, and computationally efficient. The observation that it converges faster than POEM despite a more complex per-iteration objective is a compelling practical advantage. Researchers and practitioners working on off-policy evaluation and learning are very likely to adopt these ideas and build upon them. This work raises the bar for what constitutes a robust algorithm in the counterfactual learning setting.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

1.  **Novel Problem Identification:** The paper clearly articulates a previously overlooked but critical failure mode—propensity overfitting—in a widely used class of counterfactual learning algorithms.
2.  **Principled Solution:** It proposes a well-motivated solution by introducing the self-normalized estimator, grounding the choice in solid theoretical properties like equivariance and consistency.
3.  **Strong Empirical Results:** The experimental validation is thorough and convincing, showing that the proposed Norm-POEM algorithm yields substantial improvements in generalization performance and robustness over the existing state-of-the-art (POEM).
4.  **High Practical Impact:** The findings are directly relevant to real-world applications of machine learning. The proposed algorithm is practical, efficient, and a compelling replacement for its predecessor.
5.  **Exceptional Clarity:** The paper is a model of clarity, making a complex topic accessible and the core contributions easy to understand.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

It is difficult to find significant weaknesses in this paper. The use of an approximate variance estimator and the optimization of a non-convex objective are standard in this line of work and are handled appropriately. One might note that the core statistical tool (the self-normalized estimator) is not new, but its novel application and the analysis of its benefits for solving propensity overfitting in a learning context is the key, and highly valuable, contribution.

**Conclusion:**

This is an excellent paper that makes a significant and compelling contribution to the field of learning from bandit feedback. It identifies a crucial problem, offers a theoretically sound and empirically validated solution, and is presented with outstanding clarity. I strongly recommend acceptance.
