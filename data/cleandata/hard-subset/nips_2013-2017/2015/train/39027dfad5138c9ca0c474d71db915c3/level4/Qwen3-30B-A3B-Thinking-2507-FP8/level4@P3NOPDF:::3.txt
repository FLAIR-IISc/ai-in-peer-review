

The paper addresses the critical challenge of batch learning from logged bandit feedback, a problem with significant real-world relevance in applications like ad ranking. It identifies a specific "overfitting" issue in the Counterfactual Risk Minimization (CRM) principle and proposes a solution leveraging multiplicative control variates, resulting in a self-normalizing risk estimator. The core contribution lies in this modification, which demonstrably improves empirical performance in the experiments presented.

The work is high-quality and well-motivated, building logically on the foundation of [1]. As a follow-up, however, it would benefit from more substantial experimental validation and deeper analytical insights. Specifically, the authors should provide a more detailed analysis of the optimization dynamics of their proposed objective (beyond the brief mention on line 415), including, for instance, convergence behavior or sensitivity to hyperparameters. Additionally, while the experiments show clear improvements over CRM in the settings studied, extending the evaluation to include more diverse, real-world applications beyond those in [1] (e.g., beyond synthetic or standard benchmark datasets) would significantly strengthen the case for practical impact. The request for variance measures in the results (line 365) is also essential for assessing the robustness of the reported gains.

The presentation is clear and the paper is well-written, making the technical content accessible. The organization effectively guides the reader through the problem, the identified limitation, the proposed solution, and its evaluation.

The originality is appropriately framed as a follow-up to [1], but the key innovation—the modification to the risk estimator to achieve self-normalization—proves to be highly impactful. This change directly addresses the identified overfitting problem and is the primary reason for the observed empirical improvements, distinguishing this work meaningfully from prior approaches.

The significance of the problem domain is unquestionable, given the prevalence of logged bandit data in industry. However, the paper's claim regarding the "substantial experimental improvements" requires careful interpretation. While the results in the paper's specific settings are compelling, the extent to which these gains translate to broader, more complex real-world scenarios (e.g., large-scale ad systems with high-dimensional contexts or noisy logs) is not sufficiently demonstrated. The authors should more explicitly discuss the potential limitations of their approach in such settings and the steps needed to validate its applicability beyond the current experiments. A stronger case for significance would emerge from including at least one non-synthetic, real-world application case study, or a detailed discussion of the challenges in deploying this method in practice.

Specific Comments:
*   **Line 365:** Please report variance estimates (e.g., standard deviation, confidence intervals) alongside the mean performance metrics across multiple repetitions. This is crucial for assessing the statistical significance and reliability of the observed improvements.
*   **Experiments:** The current experimental scope (primarily synthetic and standard benchmarks) is adequate for demonstrating the core methodological advance. However, the authors should explicitly state the limitations of this scope regarding real-world applicability and outline concrete steps for future validation on more diverse or actual production data. Including one additional, non-synthetic application (even if simulated) would significantly bolster the significance claim.
*   **Analysis:** The discussion of the bias/variance trade-off inherent in the self-normalizing estimator (compared to the unbiased CRM estimator) would be valuable. A brief theoretical or empirical justification for why the bias introduced by the multiplicative control variates is acceptable or even beneficial in the context of the batch learning objective would strengthen the contribution.

Overall, this is a solid, well-executed follow-up paper that makes a clear, useful contribution to the batch learning from logged bandit feedback literature. The core idea of using multiplicative control variates to create a self-normalizing estimator effectively resolves the overfitting issue in CRM. The primary areas for improvement are the depth of the experimental validation (specifically, expanding the application scope and reporting variance) and providing a more nuanced discussion of the real-world implications of the reported gains. Addressing these points would elevate the paper to a stronger contribution for the NIPS audience. The work is relevant, technically sound, and presents a valuable methodological refinement.
