This paper identifies a significant problem – propensity overfitting – in the conventional counterfactual risk estimator used for batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF), and proposes a self-normalized estimator to address it. BLBF is a valuable technique for leveraging historical data from online systems (e.g., ad placement, recommendation) where full-information feedback is unavailable, and the Counterfactual Risk Minimization (CRM) principle provides a framework for designing algorithms in this setting. The authors demonstrate that the standard estimator suffers from biases that can lead to poor generalization, particularly with complex hypothesis spaces. Their proposed self-normalized estimator elegantly avoids this issue, leading to a new learning algorithm, Normalized Policy Optimizer for Exponential Models (Norm-POEM), which consistently outperforms the conventional estimator on multi-label classification tasks.

The paper builds upon prior work in causal inference, domain adaptation, and off-policy evaluation, correctly identifying its place within this landscape. The connection to importance sampling and control variates is well-articulated, with appropriate citations.  The authors clearly relate their work to the previous NIPS paper [1] (Swaminathan & Joachims, 2013), upon which much of the foundation is built. They appropriately acknowledge existing techniques like propensity score matching and importance weighting.

**Strengths:**

*   **Clear Problem Identification:** The paper clearly articulates the problem of propensity overfitting, which appears to be a novel and important issue in BLBF.  The examples provided are insightful and demonstrate the potential for severe bias in the conventional estimator.
*   **Elegant Solution:** The proposed self-normalized estimator provides a theoretically sound and intuitively appealing solution to the identified problem. The use of a multiplicative control variate is well-justified and effectively addresses the limitations of the existing approach.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The experimental results consistently demonstrate the superiority of Norm-POEM over POEM across multiple datasets. The performance gains are convincing and support the theoretical claims.
*   **Thorough Analysis:** The authors provide a comprehensive analysis of the proposed method, including a discussion of its computational efficiency and robustness.
*   **Connection to Related Work:** The paper appropriately positions its contribution within the broader context of causal inference, off-policy evaluation, and importance sampling.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Clarification Needed on Loss Range:** A clarification is needed regarding the assumption of the loss function being within [-1, 0] as described in [1] and its impact on the application of the CRM principle and Norm-POEM. The experiments’ description of "delta > 0" requires further explanation to ensure consistency.
*   **Delta as a Random Variable:** The paper currently treats delta(x, y) as a deterministic function. A more general formulation recognizing delta as a random variable, particularly in scenarios with noisy labels, could strengthen the theoretical foundation. While this doesn't necessarily invalidate the risk estimator (2), acknowledging the inherent randomness in the feedback signal would be beneficial.
*   **Minor Notational Issues:** There are minor notational inconsistencies (e.g., h(y|x) / h\_0(y|x) on line 170-171), and a typographical error (argmin instead of argmax on line 276).
*   **Variance Estimation Complexity**: The computational complexity of the variance estimation in Norm-POEM, while acknowledged to be higher, could benefit from further discussion of practical implications and potential optimization strategies.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** The identification of propensity overfitting and the proposal of the self-normalized estimator represent a novel contribution to the field of BLBF.
*   **Significance:** The problem addressed is important, and the proposed solution has the potential to significantly improve the performance of BLBF algorithms in real-world applications.
*   **Soundness:** The theoretical analysis and empirical validation provide strong evidence for the effectiveness of the proposed method.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the technical details of the proposed approach.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   **Minor Technical Points:** The need for clarification regarding the loss range assumption and the treatment of delta as a random variable, while not critical, raise some concerns about the completeness of the theoretical framework.
*   **Notational Issues:**  The minor notational issues, though easily corrected, detract slightly from the overall clarity of the presentation.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of batch learning from logged bandit feedback. The authors have identified a significant problem, proposed an elegant solution, and provided compelling empirical evidence to support their claims. While a few minor clarifications and corrections are needed, the overall quality of the work is high. I believe this paper is worthy of acceptance to NIPS 2015.
