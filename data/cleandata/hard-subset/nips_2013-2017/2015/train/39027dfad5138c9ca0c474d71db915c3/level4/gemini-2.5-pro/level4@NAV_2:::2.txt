This paper addresses the problem of batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF). It begins by identifying a critical issue with the conventional inverse propensity score (IPS) estimator commonly used within the Counterfactual Risk Minimization (CRM) framework. The authors term this issue "propensity overfitting," where complex models can achieve artificially low empirical risk by manipulating the importance weights rather than by accurately modeling the underlying loss. This is a subtle but important distinction from standard overfitting in supervised learning.

To address this, the paper proposes replacing the standard unbiased IPS estimator with a self-normalized (ratio) estimator. This alternative estimator is motivated by its desirable theoretical properties: it is equivariant to translations of the loss function and its estimates are naturally bounded within the range of the observed losses, thus preventing the degenerate behavior of the standard estimator. The authors incorporate this new estimator into the CRM framework to develop a new learning algorithm, Norm-POEM, for structured output prediction with linear models. The work is situated within the existing literature on off-policy evaluation and counterfactual learning, building directly upon the POEM algorithm [1] by replacing its core risk estimator.

**Strengths (Arguments for acceptance):**

*   **Significance and Originality:** The paper clearly identifies and articulates the problem of "propensity overfitting." This is a novel and valuable insight into a potential failure mode of counterfactual learning methods that rely on the standard IPS estimator, especially when using flexible, high-capacity hypothesis spaces.
*   **Quality of the Proposed Solution:** The proposal to use a self-normalized estimator is principled and well-justified. The paper provides a clear theoretical argument for why this estimator avoids the identified problem, highlighting its equivariance and boundedness properties. The solution is elegant and directly targets the source of the problem.
*   **Strong Empirical Results:** The experimental evaluation is comprehensive and convincing. On several multi-label classification benchmarks, the proposed Norm-POEM algorithm consistently and significantly outperforms its predecessor, POEM. The results show that Norm-POEM is not only more accurate but also more robust to transformations of the loss function. The fact that its performance approaches that of a fully supervised CRF ("skyline") underscores the effectiveness of the approach.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and organized. The "propensity overfitting" problem is effectively illustrated with a simple yet powerful example (Example 1), which makes the motivation for the work very clear.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement (Arguments against acceptance / for revision):**

*   **Clarity of Motivation for Estimator "Anomalies":** The motivation for the proposed estimator rests on identifying "anomalies" in the conventional unbiased estimator (Eq. 2), particularly its lack of equivariance to a constant shift in the loss. While the practical instabilities this causes are real and well-demonstrated, the framing could be refined. In the importance sampling formulation, the expectation is taken over the entire product `δ * h(y|x) / p`, not just the loss `δ`. Therefore, the lack of linearity in a finite-sample estimate when `δ` is shifted is an expected property, not necessarily an "anomaly."
*   **Suggestion:** The authors might consider softening this framing. The key insight is not that the estimator is "technically incorrect," but that this known property can be exploited by a powerful learner in a way that leads to severe overfitting. Emphasizing that the complexity of the hypothesis space `H` allows the learner to minimize the sum of propensity weights (as defined in Eq. 4) instead of fitting the loss is the core, and very strong, contribution. An alternative perspective is that the primary weakness of Eq. 2 is the high variance from small propensities `pi`, and the self-normalized estimator (Eq. 7) elegantly mitigates this by normalizing by the sum of the weights. Clarifying this point would strengthen the paper's theoretical footing without diminishing the practical importance of the proposed method.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a solid and well-executed paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of counterfactual learning. It diagnoses a subtle but important problem with a widely used estimator and proposes a simple, theoretically sound, and empirically superior solution. The experimental results are strong and clearly demonstrate the benefits of the proposed Norm-POEM algorithm. Despite the minor point regarding the framing of the motivation, the core contribution is valuable and convincing. The paper is likely to be of high interest to researchers and practitioners working with logged data in applications like recommendation systems and online advertising. I recommend acceptance.
