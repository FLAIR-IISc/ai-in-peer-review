This paper addresses the problem of batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF). It identifies a critical flaw, which the authors term "propensity overfitting," inherent in the conventional inverse propensity score (IPS) estimator commonly used within the Counterfactual Risk Minimization (CRM) framework. The core idea is that this conventional estimator can be minimized not just by fitting the loss, but also by manipulating the sum of propensity weights, leading to degenerate solutions and poor generalization. To remedy this, the paper proposes replacing the standard IPS estimator with a self-normalized estimator, which uses the sum of propensity weights as a multiplicative control variate. This new estimator is shown to be equivariant to translations in the loss function and bounded within the loss range, thus avoiding the anomalies that cause propensity overfitting. Building on this, the paper introduces a new learning algorithm, Norm-POEM, which integrates the self-normalized estimator and an appropriate variance regularizer into the CRM principle for learning structured prediction models. This work is a direct and significant extension of prior work on the POEM algorithm [1], which is based on the conventional unbiased estimator.

**Strengths:**

*   **Quality:** The paper is technically sound and presents a complete and compelling piece of research. The identification and clear explanation of the propensity overfitting problem (illustrated well in Example 1) is a significant contribution. The proposed solution is principled, drawing on established control variate techniques from the statistics literature. Claims are well-supported by both theoretical analysis (e.g., the consistency proof in Theorem 2) and a thorough empirical evaluation on several benchmark datasets. The experiments are well-designed, following a controlled methodology that convincingly demonstrates the superiority of Norm-POEM over the previous state-of-the-art.
*   **Originality:** While the self-normalized estimator is a known technique in importance sampling, its application to mitigate the specific and newly identified problem of propensity overfitting in counterfactual learning is novel. The conceptual leap to recognize that the standard estimator is susceptible to this form of overfitting, separate from traditional "loss overfitting," is a key original insight. This work clearly distinguishes itself from prior contributions by addressing a fundamental flaw in a widely used method.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow. The motivation for the work is established clearly in the introduction. The core problem is explained with a simple yet powerful illustrative example. The transition from identifying the problem to proposing the self-normalized estimator as a solution is logical and well-argued. The experimental section is clear, and the results are presented in a way that strongly supports the paper's main claims.
*   **Significance:** The results are highly significant for both researchers and practitioners working with logged bandit data, a common scenario in applications like web search, recommendation, and computational advertising. The paper addresses a difficult and subtle problem in a better way than previous research. The proposed Norm-POEM algorithm not only achieves substantially better generalization performance but is also shown to be computationally competitive, sometimes even faster than its predecessor due to better convergence properties. This combination of improved accuracy, robustness, and efficiency makes it highly likely that these ideas will be adopted and built upon.

**Weaknesses / Questions for Authors:**

*   The paper notes that the variance estimator for Norm-POEM does not admit stochastic optimization, necessitating the use of a batch method like L-BFGS. While the empirical results show fast convergence on the tested datasets, a brief discussion on the potential scalability challenges for extremely large-scale problems would be beneficial. Could the variance term be approximated to allow for stochastic optimization, and what might be the trade-offs?
*   The original POEM algorithm was motivated by a finite-sample generalization bound (Theorem 1). Does a similar finite-sample theoretical guarantee exist for the CRM objective when instantiated with the self-normalized estimator? While the estimator is shown to be strongly consistent, a finite-sample analysis would further strengthen the paper's theoretical foundation.
*   The empirical evaluation is strong, but it would be interesting to see how sensitive Norm-POEM is to the quality of the logging policy h0. For instance, how does it perform when h0 is very different from the optimal policy, leading to very high variance in the importance weights?

In summary, this is an excellent paper that identifies a subtle but important problem in counterfactual learning and proposes an elegant, effective, and practical solution. It is well-motivated, clearly presented, and the empirical results are convincing.
