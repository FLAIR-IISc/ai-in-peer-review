This paper addresses the problem of batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF), a crucial setting for improving interactive systems using historical data. Building directly upon the Counterfactual Risk Minimization (CRM) principle introduced by Swaminathan and Joachims (2015), this work identifies a critical and previously overlooked failure mode of the standard inverse propensity score (IPS) estimator used in this context. The authors term this issue "Propensity Overfitting," where a model with sufficient capacity can achieve an artificially low empirical risk not by fitting the true loss landscape, but by exploiting the propensity weights themselves—for instance, by assigning high probability to logged actions with negative loss or zero probability to those with positive loss.

To remedy this, the authors propose replacing the conventional unbiased IPS estimator with a self-normalized (SN) estimator. This is a ratio estimator, motivated by the use of multiplicative control variates, where the empirical risk is normalized by the sum of the importance weights. This simple but powerful modification yields an estimator that is equivariant to shifts in the loss function and is guaranteed to be bounded within the range of the observed losses, thereby resolving the anomalies that enable propensity overfitting. The authors instantiate this new estimator within the CRM framework to develop a new learning algorithm, the Normalized Policy Optimizer for Exponential Models (Norm-POEM). An extensive empirical evaluation on several multi-label classification tasks demonstrates that Norm-POEM consistently and significantly outperforms the original POEM algorithm, validating the paper's central claims.

### Strengths

1.  **Clear Identification of a Novel and Significant Problem:** The primary contribution of this paper is the identification and clear articulation of "Propensity Overfitting." This is a subtle but severe problem in counterfactual learning that can lead to catastrophic generalization failures. The authors provide a simple, intuitive example (Example 1) that perfectly illustrates how the standard IPS estimator can be manipulated, which is highly effective in motivating the need for a new approach. This is a significant conceptual contribution to the field.

2.  **Principled and Elegant Solution:** The proposed solution—using the self-normalized estimator—is well-motivated, elegant, and theoretically sound. By framing the sum of importance weights as a control variate, the authors connect a classic variance reduction technique from the Monte Carlo literature to a specific learning problem in a novel way. They clearly lay out the desirable properties of the SN estimator, such as equivariance and its bounded nature, which directly address the weaknesses of the standard estimator.

3.  **Thorough and Convincing Empirical Validation:** The experimental section is a major strength. The authors do not simply show that their method achieves a lower test error. Instead, they include crucial diagnostic experiments that directly support their core hypothesis. By showing that the sum of weights Ŝ(h) for the learned hypothesis is close to its expected value of 1 for Norm-POEM but severely biased for POEM, they provide direct evidence that they are successfully mitigating propensity overfitting. The experiments demonstrating Norm-POEM's robustness to loss translation further solidify this claim.

4.  **Strong Practical Results and Efficiency:** The paper demonstrates not only statistically significant but also practically meaningful improvements in generalization performance across multiple datasets. A surprising and compelling result is that Norm-POEM, despite having a more complex objective function that precludes stochastic optimization, often converges faster than POEM. The analysis that this is due to finding solutions with smaller norm weights (less extreme probabilities) is insightful and adds to the practical appeal of the method.

### Weaknesses / Questions for the Authors

1.  **Computational Considerations:** The authors note that the variance of the self-normalized estimator does not admit stochastic optimization, necessitating the use of a batch optimizer like L-BFGS. While they show that faster convergence makes up for the higher per-iteration cost on the tested datasets, it would be valuable to discuss the potential scalability limitations. For extremely large-scale datasets where full batch updates are infeasible, would approximations or alternative optimization strategies be required?

2.  **Scope of Empirical Evaluation:** The experiments are well-controlled and follow the established "Supervised-to-Bandit" protocol on multi-label classification datasets. This is a strong and valid methodology. However, demonstrating the method's effectiveness on a problem more natively aligned with the bandit setting, such as a simulation of ad placement or recommendation, could further strengthen the paper's claims of broad applicability.

### Summary of Contributions

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Identifies and clearly explains a novel, important, and subtle problem in counterfactual learning ("Propensity Overfitting").
*   Proposes a simple, principled, and effective solution based on the self-normalized estimator.
*   Provides strong theoretical motivation for the proposed estimator, highlighting its improved properties (equivariance, bounded range).
*   The empirical evaluation is thorough, including diagnostic experiments that convincingly validate the paper's core claims.
*   The resulting algorithm, Norm-POEM, demonstrates significant performance gains over the prior state-of-the-art and exhibits surprisingly efficient convergence.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**
*   The work is an incremental improvement on the existing POEM framework, though the conceptual contribution of identifying propensity overfitting is substantial in its own right.
*   The reliance on batch optimization may raise questions about its scalability to massive datasets where stochastic methods are typically required.

---

This is an excellent paper that makes a clear and significant contribution to the literature on learning from bandit feedback. The identification of propensity overfitting is a key insight, and the proposed solution is both theoretically sound and empirically powerful. The paper is well-written, the arguments are clear, and the experimental results are convincing. It corrects a serious flaw in a widely used class of estimators and provides a practical algorithm that is a clear improvement over its predecessor. I strongly recommend acceptance.
