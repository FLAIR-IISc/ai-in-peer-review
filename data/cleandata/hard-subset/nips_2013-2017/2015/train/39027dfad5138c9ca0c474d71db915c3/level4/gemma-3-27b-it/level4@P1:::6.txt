Counterfactual risk minimization is a technique aimed at learning in a bandit setting from logged data, by correcting for the bias inherent in how those logs were generated. The paper identifies a significant overfitting problem: the conventional counterfactual risk estimator can be susceptible to fitting the propensity scores of the logged data, rather than the true reward function. The authors propose to address this with a self-normalized estimator – employing a multiplicative control variate to bound variance while maintaining consistency. Empirical results demonstrate that this normalized counterfactual technique consistently outperforms the conventional estimator on several multi-label classification problems.

This paper presents a novel technique – a self-normalized estimator – for counterfactual learning in the logged bandit setting. It rigorously analyzes a previously unobserved form of overfitting, where the hypothesis space can overfit by assigning excessive probability to the logged data, rather than learning the underlying reward structure. The proposed solution effectively mitigates this issue. This is a strong paper with clear theoretical motivations and compelling experimental validation, and is a worthy contribution to NIPS.

**Summary of Strengths:**

*   **Identifies a novel and important problem:** The paper highlights a previously unrecognized issue – propensity overfitting – in batch learning from logged bandit feedback.
*   **Provides a theoretically sound solution:** The self-normalized estimator is well-motivated and demonstrably avoids the identified overfitting problem. The theoretical analysis, particularly Theorem 1 and Theorem 2, provides strong justification for the proposed approach.
*   **Strong empirical validation:** The experiments consistently show improvements over the baseline POEM algorithm across multiple datasets, lending credence to the theoretical findings. The ablation study in Section 7 further clarifies the benefits of the approach.
*   **Clear writing and organization:** The paper is generally well-structured and explains complex concepts in a relatively accessible manner.

**Summary of Weaknesses:**

*   **Computational cost:** While the paper notes the comparable runtime to POEM, further discussion of the computational complexity, particularly regarding the variance estimation (Equation 8), would be beneficial.
*   **Hyperparameter sensitivity:** The reliance on parameters like *M* and *λ* and their calibration through a hold-out set is standard, but a brief discussion of their sensitivity and potential for automated tuning could be valuable.
*   **Limited scope of experiments:** The experiments primarily focus on multi-label classification. Exploring the performance on different types of datasets or in different application domains (e.g., recommendation systems) would strengthen the generalizability of the findings.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper addresses a significant and previously unappreciated problem in a rapidly growing field (batch learning from logged bandit feedback).
*   The proposed solution is theoretically well-grounded and empirically validated.
*   The results demonstrate a clear and consistent improvement over existing methods.
*   The paper is well-written and clearly presents its contributions.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The computational complexity, while comparable to existing methods, could be a limiting factor for very large datasets.
*   The experimental evaluation, while strong, could be broadened to assess generalizability across different domains.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a high-quality paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of batch learning from logged bandit feedback. The identified problem of propensity overfitting is novel and important, and the proposed solution is both theoretically sound and empirically effective. Despite minor weaknesses, the strengths of the paper clearly outweigh them. I recommend acceptance.
