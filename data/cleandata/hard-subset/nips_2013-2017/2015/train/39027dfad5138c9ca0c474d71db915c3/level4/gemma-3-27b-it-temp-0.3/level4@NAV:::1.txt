Due to travel constraints, this review is necessarily brief, and I may have overlooked details – I apologize for any resulting misunderstandings. Despite the limited time, I found the paper engaging.

The paper investigates a challenge related to, but distinct from, the well-known issue of high variance in reweighting schemes used when evaluating a hypothesis differing from the data-generating process (as illustrated by the connection between equations (1) and (2)). The authors identify a phenomenon they term “propensity overfitting,” which appears to arise when insufficient exploration of the data leads to limited representation of outcomes relevant to the evaluated hypothesis – specifically, when for a given input *x*, few or no observed outputs *y* align with the new hypothesis. Given the overlap in underlying causes with high-variance weighting, a more detailed discussion of the relationship between these two problems would be beneficial.

While I haven’t had the opportunity to thoroughly examine the technical details or assess the novelty of the proposed solution, the core idea appears reasonable. 

I do have a few specific questions:

- Line 199: I am unclear on the reasoning here. If *n* is small relative to *k*, the number of data points where *x<sub>i</sub>* = *y<sub>i</sub>* will be limited. Wouldn’t this suggest that  *R̂(ĥ)* should be even lower than -2? I may be missing something.
- The statement regarding an “unbiased counterfactual risk estimator used in prior works on BLBF [4, 5, 1]” warrants further scrutiny. While I am not familiar with all cited works, I suspect that techniques like clipping, which introduce bias to reduce variance, may be employed.

The availability of code is greatly appreciated.

In conclusion, I was unable to fully verify the technical soundness of the paper. However, it presents interesting ideas worthy of further consideration. Therefore, I recommend acceptance.
