Summary and relation to prior work
This paper studies batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF) and argues that the inverse propensity scoring (IPS) estimator widely used in counterfactual risk minimization (CRM) can lead to a distinct failure mode the authors call propensity overfitting. They propose replacing IPS with a self-normalized importance sampling estimator (SNIPS), used as a multiplicative control variate, and develop a learning algorithm, Norm-POEM, that instantiates CRM with SNIPS for structured prediction using exponential-family (CRF-like) models. The paper provides intuition, a consistency result for SNIPS, an approximate variance estimate to enable CRM-style variance regularization, and empirical comparisons showing Norm-POEM consistently outperforming POEM (the IPS-based CRM algorithm) across several multi-label datasets created via a supervised-to-bandit protocol.

This line of work builds on BLBF and CRM [Swaminathan & Joachims, ICML 2015], importance weighting under covariate shift [Cortes et al., NIPS 2010], and off-policy evaluation in RL (e.g., truncation/clipping [Ionides], doubly robust estimators [Dudík et al., ICML 2011], high-confidence OPE [Thomas et al., AAAI 2015]). The paper’s novelty lies in diagnosing propensity overfitting as a learning-time pathology of IPS-driven ERM and in advocating SNIPS within CRM, aligning with classical variance reduction via control variates (e.g., Hesterberg; Owen; Kong).

Strengths
- Clear identification of a practically important failure mode: propensity overfitting, in which the learning objective exploits fluctuations in importance weights rather than true loss structure. The thought experiment and the S(h) diagnostic are compelling.
- Principled estimator choice: SNIPS directly uses the known expectation of the sum of normalized weights to reduce sensitivity to propensity-weight variability; it is bounded and translation-equivariant at the finite-sample level, addressing anomalies of IPS-driven objectives.
- Sound theoretical footing: strong consistency for SNIPS in this setting and a reasonable approximate variance formula to enable CRM’s variance regularization.
- Empirical gains: consistent improvements of Norm-POEM over POEM across datasets and training set sizes, and robustness to loss translations, with competitive runtimes.
- Practical relevance: the approach is simple to implement (a plug-in estimator swap) and likely applicable beyond structured multilabel prediction.

Weaknesses and points for clarification
- Finite-sample theory: While consistency is shown, the paper lacks a finite-sample generalization bound for CRM with SNIPS analogous to the earlier IPS-based CRM bounds. Even a partial bound or a discussion of why standard empirical Bernstein arguments are challenging for ratio estimators would strengthen the claims.
- Variance modeling: The variance estimate for SNIPS relies on Normal and delta method approximations. Some empirical calibration (e.g., coverage of confidence intervals) or ablations contrasting different variance estimators would bolster confidence.
- Baselines: Comparisons to alternative estimators such as doubly robust (DR), weighted DR/optimized DR, or clipped-IPS with careful tuning would situate the contribution more fully. Given DR’s popularity in OPE, including it here seems important.
- Assumptions and practice: The methodology assumes full support (nonzero propensities). A discussion of practical logging strategies that ensure exploration (e.g., epsilon-greedy, randomized scoring) and sensitivity to minimal propensity levels would help practitioners.
- Experimental scope: The supervised-to-bandit protocol is standard, but a real logged-bandit dataset would substantiate external validity. Also, results are reported as averages; please add standard deviations or confidence intervals.
- Exposition details: The discussion of IPS “anomalies” would benefit from emphasizing that non-equivariance to loss translations is a finite-sample property of the ERM objective (expectation remains unbiased for any translation). This clarification avoids the impression that the issue is conceptual rather than practical for learning.

Quality
The technical content is largely sound. Using SNIPS to mitigate weight-driven variance and translation non-equivariance in finite samples is well motivated, and the analysis correctly notes the bias-variance trade-off introduced by self-normalization. The reliance on approximate variance formulas is reasonable but would benefit from additional justification or empirical calibration. The experimental methodology is careful and replicates prior BLBF practice.

Clarity
The paper is mostly clear and well organized. Suggestions:
- When introducing the IPS non-equivariance point, explicitly distinguish between expectation-level properties (unbiasedness) and finite-sample properties that matter for ERM.
- Define and consistently use the S(h) diagnostic early, and summarize its expected value and concentration assumptions.
- Proof sketches (or pointers) for variance formulas and asymptotic normality assumptions would aid readability.
- Fix minor typos and stylistic issues (e.g., consistent hyphenation: self-normalized; consistently denote variables; a few misspellings in the experimental section).

Originality
Applying SNIPS as the central estimator for counterfactual learning under CRM, together with the explicit diagnosis of propensity overfitting and its empirical verification, is a novel and useful contribution relative to prior IPS- and DR-based learning objectives.

Significance
The contribution is likely to influence future work on counterfactual learning: SNIPS is simple, robust to loss translations, and improves performance in structured prediction. The ideas should transfer to other tasks and models.

Pros for acceptance
- Addresses a real and underappreciated failure mode of IPS-driven learning.
- Simple, principled estimator swap with strong practical benefits.
- Consistent empirical improvements across datasets and data regimes.
- Solid conceptual connection to control variates and importance sampling practice.

Cons for acceptance
- Missing finite-sample generalization guarantees for CRM with SNIPS.
- Limited baselines (no DR/weighted DR) and absence of real-world logs.
- Variance estimation and hyperparameter sensitivity could be analyzed more thoroughly.

Comments and suggestions to the authors
- Clarify the finite-sample nature of IPS non-equivariance to loss translations and connect it explicitly to the ERM objective; this will make Example 1 even more compelling.
- Include DR (and, if possible, optimized/weighted DR) baselines with careful tuning and report sensitivity to clipping M and regularization λ.
- Report variability (standard deviations or confidence intervals) and, if feasible, results on at least one real logged-bandit dataset.
- Provide diagnostics on S(h) distributions during training (not only at the chosen ĥ) to illustrate how SNIPS mitigates propensity overfitting throughout optimization.
- Discuss the practical requirements for logging policies to ensure full support and how violations (very small propensities) affect SNIPS vs. IPS.
- Consider adding a brief discussion on extending CRM-style generalization bounds to ratio estimators, even if only partial or under additional assumptions.
- Check for anonymity issues in code links if the review is double-blind.

Overall recommendation
Accept. The paper identifies an important issue, proposes a principled and practical remedy, and demonstrates consistent empirical benefits. With the above clarifications and additional baselines, it would be even stronger.
