

This paper addresses a critical issue in batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF), specifically identifying a previously unrecognized problem called "propensity overfitting" that affects the conventional unbiased counterfactual risk estimator used in this setting. The authors build upon Swaminathan and Joachims' 2015 work on Counterfactual Risk Minimization (CRM) for BLBF, which established a general framework for learning from historical bandit feedback without interactive control. While prior work focused on the conventional inverse propensity estimator (Equation 2), the authors demonstrate that this estimator suffers from severe anomalies when used in Empirical Risk Minimization (ERM) for complex hypothesis spaces, leading to biased risk estimates that can be arbitrarily lower than the true risk.

The core contribution is the introduction of a self-normalized risk estimator (Equation 7), which provably avoids these anomalies by leveraging multiplicative control variates. This estimator is equivariant under loss translation and produces risk estimates bounded within the range of the loss function—unlike the conventional estimator. The authors formalize the problem of propensity overfitting through theoretical analysis and compelling examples, showing how it differs fundamentally from traditional loss overfitting in supervised learning. They then derive Norm-POEM, a new learning algorithm that replaces the conventional estimator with their self-normalized approach while maintaining the CRM principle. The paper provides a thorough theoretical analysis, including a proof of strong consistency for the self-normalized estimator (Theorem 2), and demonstrates empirical improvements across multiple multi-label classification datasets.

The paper's strengths are significant. The identification of propensity overfitting represents a substantial theoretical advance, as it reveals a fundamental limitation in prior work that was previously unrecognized. The proposed self-normalized estimator elegantly resolves this issue while maintaining theoretical guarantees, and the empirical results consistently demonstrate superior performance of Norm-POEM compared to POEM across multiple datasets (Scene, Yeast, TMC, LYRL). Notably, Norm-POEM achieves better generalization performance while being computationally more efficient than POEM, which is counterintuitive given its more complex estimator. The authors also provide thorough experimental validation, including ablation studies showing that Norm-POEM maintains consistent performance under loss translation (Table 2) and that variance regularization remains beneficial (Table 3).

The paper is well-written and logically organized, with clear connections between the theoretical analysis and empirical results. The related work section appropriately contextualizes the contribution within causal inference, off-policy evaluation, and importance sampling literature. The experimental setup follows established methodologies from prior BLBF work [1], ensuring fair comparisons. The paper also makes a valuable contribution by providing implementation details and software, enhancing reproducibility.

Potential weaknesses are minimal. While the paper demonstrates significant improvements over POEM, it would benefit from a more detailed discussion of how the self-normalized estimator might perform in settings with extremely sparse logging policies (where h0(y|x) might be very small for many (x,y) pairs). The computational complexity analysis could also be expanded to provide more precise bounds on the per-iteration cost compared to POEM.

In terms of the NIPS evaluation criteria:
- Quality: The paper is technically sound, with rigorous theoretical analysis supported by comprehensive experiments. The claims are well-supported, and the authors are careful to evaluate both strengths and weaknesses.
- Clarity: The paper is clearly written, well-organized, and provides sufficient detail for experts to reproduce the results. The connection between the problem statement and the solution is particularly well articulated.
- Originality: The identification of propensity overfitting as a distinct problem and the development of the self-normalized estimator represent a genuinely novel contribution to the BLBF literature. The paper significantly extends Swaminathan and Joachims' 2015 work.
- Significance: The results are highly significant, demonstrating consistent improvements across multiple datasets and suggesting the potential for widespread adoption in real-world applications like ad placement and recommendation systems. The computational efficiency of Norm-POEM is particularly noteworthy.

Pro arguments for acceptance: The paper identifies and resolves a fundamental limitation in prior BLBF work, provides a theoretically sound solution with strong empirical validation, and demonstrates practical advantages over existing methods. The contribution represents a meaningful advancement in the field.

Con arguments for rejection: None of substantial importance. The paper is well-justified and addresses a clear gap in the literature.

This is a high-quality paper that makes a significant contribution to the BLBF literature. The identification of propensity overfitting, the development of the self-normalized estimator, and the resulting Norm-POEM algorithm collectively represent a substantial advance over prior work. The empirical results are compelling, the theoretical analysis is rigorous, and the paper is well-written. The authors have demonstrated that their approach not only solves a theoretical problem but also delivers practical improvements in both performance and computational efficiency. This paper is an important contribution to the field of machine learning with potential for significant impact in real-world applications.
