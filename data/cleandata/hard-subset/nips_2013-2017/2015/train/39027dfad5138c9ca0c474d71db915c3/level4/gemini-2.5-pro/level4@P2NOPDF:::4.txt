**Summary**

This paper presents an interesting and valuable follow-up to the POEM framework introduced in [1]. The core contributions—the analysis of "propensity overfitting" and the novel use of a multiplicative control variate to mitigate it—are well-motivated and insightful. The proposed NORM-POEM method demonstrates clear empirical improvements over the baseline POEM on the important problem of batch learning from logged bandit feedback, a setting of increasing relevance to the community. While the core ideas are strong, the paper could be improved by clarifying some key details in the experimental setup and problem formulation.

**Major Comments and Questions**

1.  **Clarification on the Loss Range `delta`**: A key point requiring clarification relates to the range of the loss `delta`. In the original CRM paper [1] (Section 4.1), the approach is motivated for losses rescaled to the interval [-1, 0]. It is my understanding that the NORM-POEM method proposed here would inherit this requirement, meaning that the `delta` in Equations (3) and (8) should also be within [-1, 0]. However, the experimental setup described for Table 1 mentions the use of "delta > 0". This seems contradictory. Could the authors please clarify whether the losses were indeed rescaled for the experiments and explain the meaning of "delta > 0" in this context?

2.  **Generality of the Problem Formulation**: The problem formulation in Section 3, following [1], treats the logged feedback `delta(x, y)` as a deterministic function (line 110). I believe this formulation could be strengthened by considering a more general case where the feedback is a random variable. For instance, in a noisy classification setting, the same input `x` and action `y` could result in different observed losses on different occasions, depending on a stochastic true label `y'`. In this scenario, `delta(x, y)` would be a realization of a random variable whose mean (for a fixed `x` and `y`) is the expectation of the true loss `Delta(y', y)` over the true conditional label distribution `p(y'|x)`. While the proposed risk estimator in Equation (2) might remain unchanged, defining the true risk in Equation (1) with an additional expectation over the randomness of `delta` would offer a more robust and general problem setup that better reflects many real-world scenarios.

**Minor Comments and Corrections**

*   **Lines 170-171**: The ratio appears to be inverted; it should likely be `h(y|x) / h_0(y|x)`. It would also be appropriate to cite [1] in this context, as it previously pointed out the lack of linearity.
*   **Line 276**: Since this is a risk minimization problem, the operator should be `argmin`, not `argmax`.
*   **Line 319**: For notational consistency with the rest of the paper, the "bar" notation should be replaced with "hats".
