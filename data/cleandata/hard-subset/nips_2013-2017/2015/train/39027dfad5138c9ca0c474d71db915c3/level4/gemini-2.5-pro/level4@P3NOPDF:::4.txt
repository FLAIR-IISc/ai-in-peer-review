This paper presents an interesting and valuable follow-up to the work on Policy Optimizer for Exponential Models (POEM) from [1]. The authors address the important problem of batch learning from logged bandit feedback, a scenario of growing practical interest. The central contributions are the insightful analysis of "propensity overfitting" in the POEM estimator and the proposal of a novel multiplicative control variate, NORM-POEM, to mitigate this issue. The empirical results demonstrate a clear improvement over the baseline POEM method.

Overall, this is a strong submission with a clear contribution. The following points are intended to help the authors further strengthen the manuscript.

**Detailed Feedback**

1.  **On the Generality of the Problem Formulation:** The problem setup in Section 3, which follows [1], defines the logged feedback `delta(x, y)` as a deterministic function (line 110). This formulation could be generalized to better capture the stochasticity inherent in many real-world applications. For example, consider a classification task where feedback is the loss from a prediction. If the true labels are noisy, the same input `x` and action `y` could yield different loss values on different occasions in the log. In this case, `delta(x, y)` is more accurately modeled as a random variable whose mean for a fixed `x` and `y` is the expectation of the loss over the true noisy labeling distribution. While this generalization may not change the final form of the risk estimator (2), explicitly acknowledging this randomness in the true risk formulation (1) by including an additional expectation would improve the theoretical grounding and generality of the setup.

2.  **Clarification on the Range of the Loss `delta`:** A key point requires clarification regarding the range of the loss function `delta`. In the original CRM paper [1] (Section 4.1), the authors state that their approach is well-motivated for losses `delta` that are rescaled to the interval `[-1, 0]`. It appears that the NORM-POEM method presented here operates under the same assumption, as both the standard POEM estimator (3) and the normalized estimator (8) utilize this rescaled loss. Given this, it is unclear how to interpret the experimental results in Table 1 that involve "delta > 0". Could the authors please clarify how positive loss values are handled by the NORM-POEM framework? Is there a different rescaling procedure involved, or is the method directly applicable to positive losses without modification?

**Minor Comments and Corrections**

*   **Line 170-171:** The importance weight ratio appears to be inverted. It should likely be `h(y|x) / h_0(y|x)`. Additionally, for completeness, it might be worthwhile to mention that the lack of linearity in the original POEM estimator was also pointed out in [1].
*   **Line 276:** As the objective is to minimize the risk, the `argmax` should be replaced with an `argmin`.
*   **Line 319:** For notational consistency with the conventions used earlier in the paper, please consider replacing the bars over the variables with hats (e.g., `\hat{R}` instead of `\bar{R}`).

**Conclusion**

This paper makes a solid contribution to the field of off-policy evaluation and learning. The analysis of propensity overfitting is insightful, and the proposed NORM-POEM estimator is a well-motivated and empirically effective solution. After clarification on the minor points raised, this paper will be a strong addition to the conference proceedings.
