

Due to time constraints, this review is brief. I regret not having sufficient time to thoroughly evaluate the paper, though I found it highly engaging. I may have overlooked or misinterpreted aspects; my apologies if so.

The paper addresses reweighting for hypothesis evaluation under non-deterministic hypotheses (e.g., conditional distributions), as noted between equations (1) and (2). However, the authors' focus on "propensity overfitting" appears related but distinct from the well-known issue of weight variance. Propensity overfitting arises when insufficient exploration leads to sparse observations for specific inputs (e.g., few or no *y* values for a given *x* under the target hypothesis). Since this mirrors the root cause of weight variance, a clearer explanation of the relationship between these two problems would strengthen the paper.

I did not verify the technical details or novelty of the proposed solution, though the approach appears reasonable. Specific concerns:
- *Line 199*: If *n* is small relative to *k*, we observe few (*x_i*, *y_i*) pairs. Shouldn't $\hat{R}(\hat{h})$ then be *more negative* than -2? Am I misunderstanding?
- *"Unbiased counterfactual risk estimator used in prior works [4, 5, 1]"*: This claim seems questionable. I suspect these works employ clipping (introducing bias to reduce variance), rather than strictly unbiased estimation.

I appreciate the availability of code. While I cannot confirm technical soundness due to limited time, the ideas are compelling and warrant further consideration. I recommend acceptance.
