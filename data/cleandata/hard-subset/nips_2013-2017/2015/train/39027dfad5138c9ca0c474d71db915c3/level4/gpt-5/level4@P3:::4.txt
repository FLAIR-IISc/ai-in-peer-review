Summary
This paper addresses propensity overfitting in batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF), a setting where only partial information is logged from a historical policy h0. Prior BLBF algorithms typically use an inverse propensity score (IPS) risk estimator, possibly with clipping, within the Counterfactual Risk Minimization (CRM) framework. The authors show that IPS can lead to severe degeneracies in empirical risk minimization—specifically, “propensity overfitting,” where hypotheses exploit the importance weights instead of fitting the loss signal. They propose replacing IPS with a self-normalized (SN) importance sampling estimator, a multiplicative control variate that normalizes by the sum of weights. This estimator is equivariant to additive shifts of the loss, bounded within the loss range, and (under standard assumptions) strongly consistent. Building on this, they introduce Norm-POEM, which instantiates CRM with the SN estimator and an approximate variance estimate for the ratio estimator. Experiments on multi-label classification datasets using a supervised-to-bandit protocol show consistent improvements over POEM (the IPS-based CRM algorithm), reduced sensitivity to loss translations, and competitive runtime.

Relation to prior work
The work is positioned at the intersection of counterfactual evaluation/learning (e.g., IPS, clipping, doubly robust methods), domain adaptation via importance weighting, and off-policy RL. It builds directly on CRM [1], highlighting a failure mode not previously emphasized there, and draws on classical Monte Carlo ideas (self-normalized importance sampling, control variates). The paper’s analysis and estimator choice are well-connected to known variance-reduction techniques and to related high-confidence off-policy evaluation work.

Strengths
- Clear identification and diagnosis of a genuine failure mode (propensity overfitting) that can arise when optimizing IPS-based estimates over rich hypothesis classes.
- Principled remedy via a self-normalized estimator that has desirable properties (boundedness, equivariance, strong consistency), and an intuitive diagnostic (the sum of weights Ŝ(h) with expectation 1).
- A practical algorithm (Norm-POEM) with a variance-regularized CRM objective tailored to the SN estimator, and a tractable optimization strategy.
- Empirical results consistently improving on POEM across datasets and training set sizes, with evidence that Ŝ(h) stays near 1 and that performance is stable under loss translations.

Weaknesses and questions for authors
- Theoretical gap for CRM with SN: The CRM generalization bound (Theorem 1) is for capped IPS. For the SN estimator, the paper uses an approximate variance formula and argues consistency, but does not provide an analogous finite-sample bound. It would strengthen the contribution to discuss whether a CRM-style bound can be established for the SN estimator (even with clipping), or to clarify limitations.
- Baselines: Since SN-IPS is often compared to clipped IPS and doubly robust (DR) estimators in off-policy evaluation, it would be helpful to include DR (and perhaps defensive mixtures/weighted IS) as baselines for both evaluation and learning, or explain why they are not appropriate for the structured setting considered.
- Bias–variance trade-off: The SN estimator is biased but consistent. More discussion on when SN is expected to dominate (e.g., when weight variance dominates loss variance), and any empirical sensitivity studies with respect to clipping (M) would be valuable.
- Modeling of feedback: In Section 3, δ(x, y) is treated as deterministic. In many applications, feedback is stochastic (e.g., noisy labels or stochastic rewards). It would be clearer to formalize δ as a random variable and write the risk as E_x E_y|h E[δ | x, y], as you note briefly later. This does not change the estimators but improves the generality and reproducibility of the setup.

Specific comments and minor corrections
- Loss scaling and experimental clarity: CRM in [1] assumes δ ∈ [−1, 0] (rescaling if needed). Equations (3) and (8) here appear to inherit this assumption. Please ensure the paper explicitly states whether losses are rescaled to [−1, 0] before optimization. The text and tables mention “δ > 0” in some experiments; after the rebuttal, I understand the issue is resolved, but the final version should make the scaling explicit and consistent with the claims about equivariance and boundedness.
- Nonlinearity/equivariance: The lack of linearity/equivariance of IPS under additive loss shifts was already discussed in [1]; please cite this when you highlight it here.
- Notational fixes:
  - Replace h0(y|x)/h(y|x) by h(y|x)/h0(y|x) where appropriate in the derivation of IPS.
  - Use argmin (not argmax) when minimizing risk in the learning objective.
  - Make hats/bars consistent for empirical quantities (e.g., use hats for sample estimates in Section 6).
- Assumptions: State explicitly that h0 has full support and that clipping is used to ensure finite variance for the SN estimator; note where results rely on these assumptions.

Assessment by criteria
- Quality: Sound problem formulation and solid use of SN importance sampling. Theoretical support includes consistency and an approximate variance estimator; a tighter finite-sample learning bound under CRM for SN would further strengthen the paper. Empirical methodology is reasonable and shows clear gains.
- Clarity: Generally clear, well-organized, and motivated. Please integrate the clarifications about loss scaling, stochastic feedback, and notational consistency.
- Originality: The identification of propensity overfitting in the learning context and the proposed SN-based learning objective are novel and insightful extensions of CRM.
- Significance: BLBF is increasingly important in practice. The proposed estimator and algorithm demonstrably improve robustness and performance over a strong baseline, and the ideas are likely to influence future work on counterfactual learning.

Pros
- Identifies a real and impactful failure mode of IPS-based learning.
- Principled remedy via self-normalization with strong intuitive and empirical support.
- Consistent empirical improvements over POEM; robustness to loss translations.
- Practical algorithm with reasonable computational cost.

Cons
- No CRM-style finite-sample generalization bound for the SN objective.
- Limited comparisons to DR and other advanced off-policy estimators.
- Some presentation inconsistencies (loss scaling, notation) in the current draft.

Update after rebuttal
The authors clarified the loss scaling issue. With that clarification, my view is positive: interesting and useful follow-up to [1], with a compelling analysis of propensity overfitting and an effective estimator/algorithmic remedy.

Scores
- Quality: 6/10
- Clarity: 6/10 (can be improved with edits above)
- Originality: 8/10

Recommendation: Weak Accept.
