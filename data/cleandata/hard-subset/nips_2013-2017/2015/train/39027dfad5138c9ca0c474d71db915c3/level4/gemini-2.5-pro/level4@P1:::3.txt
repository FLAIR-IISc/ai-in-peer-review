### Summary

This paper addresses the problem of batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF), a setting with significant practical applications, such as ad placement and recommendation systems. Building upon the Counterfactual Risk Minimization (CRM) principle introduced in [1], the authors identify a critical issue with the conventional unbiased risk estimator used in this framework, which they term "Propensity Overfitting." This form of overfitting allows the learning algorithm to select hypotheses that achieve an arbitrarily low empirical risk by manipulating importance weights, a pathology not seen in standard supervised learning.

To resolve this, the paper proposes replacing the standard estimator with a self-normalized version derived using multiplicative control variates. While this results in a biased (though consistent) estimator, it is shown to be equivariant to translations of the loss function and always bounded within the range of the loss, thereby avoiding the degeneracies of the unbiased estimator. This principled modification leads to a new algorithm, Norm-POEM, which demonstrates substantial empirical improvements over the original POEM on several multi-label classification tasks.

### Quality

The paper is of high quality, well-motivated, and technically sound. The identification and formalization of the "Propensity Overfitting" problem is a novel and important contribution. The analysis of why the standard unbiased estimator fails is insightful, and the proposed solution is principled and elegant.

My main criticism concerns the depth of the evaluation and analysis. As this is direct follow-up work to [1], the evaluation would be more compelling if it extended beyond the same experimental setup and datasets. Additionally, the discussion of the optimization for the Norm-POEM objective is quite brief. While L-BFGS is a reasonable choice, a more detailed analysis or discussion of the challenges posed by the non-convex objective would be welcome, especially given the claim of faster convergence (line 415).

### Clarity

The presentation is clear and the paper is well-written. The authors do an excellent job of explaining the subtle but severe problem of propensity overfitting with a simple, effective example. The motivation for introducing the self-normalized estimator is well-argued, and the resulting algorithm is described sufficiently for reproduction.

### Originality

The work is a follow-up to [1], but the contribution is significant and original. While the self-normalized estimator is a known technique in the importance sampling literature, its application to mitigate the specific problem of propensity overfitting in counterfactual learning is novel. The modification is not trivial and, as the experiments show, leads to a meaningful difference in performance.

### Significance

The problem of learning robustly from historical logged data is highly relevant to both the research community and industry practitioners. The paper's finding that the standard unbiased estimator is prone to a severe form of overfitting is an important warning. The proposed solution is practical and appears to be a clear improvement. However, it is difficult to fully gauge the real-world impact without seeing how the substantial experimental improvements translate to applications beyond the semi-synthetic multi-label classification tasks presented.

### Detailed Remarks

-   The experimental results are strong and clearly demonstrate the practical benefits of the proposed estimator over the baseline from [1].
-   In Table 1 (referenced around line 365), results are averaged over 10 runs. Please include a measure of variance (e.g., standard deviation) to better assess the stability and significance of the improvements.
-   Overall, this is a solid and relevant paper that identifies a key problem and offers a compelling solution. My primary suggestion for improvement is to broaden the experimental evaluation to include more diverse or real-world applications to further strengthen the claims of practical significance.
