This paper addresses a critical problem in batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF): propensity overfitting in the commonly used counterfactual risk estimator. The authors demonstrate that this estimator can be severely biased, particularly with complex hypothesis spaces, and propose a self-normalized estimator to mitigate this issue. This leads to a new learning algorithm, Norm-POEM, which is empirically shown to outperform existing methods on multi-label classification tasks.

The core idea of identifying and addressing propensity overfitting is a valuable contribution to the BLBF literature, building on the established framework of Counterfactual Risk Minimization (CRM) [1]. The paper clearly articulates the problem, linking it to concepts from causal inference, domain adaptation, and off-policy evaluation. The authors appropriately contextualize their work within the broader landscape of importance sampling techniques [7, 8, 11, 15] and highlight the novelty of their approach. The use of multiplicative control variates, drawing inspiration from financial applications and reinforcement learning [17, 18], is a clever adaptation to address the specific challenges of BLBF. 

The strengths of the paper lie in its theoretical grounding and empirical validation. The formalization of propensity overfitting and the proof of consistency for the self-normalized estimator (Theorem 2) are significant achievements. The experiments, mirroring the setup in [1], provide compelling evidence of Norm-POEM’s improved performance and robustness. The ablation study demonstrating the continued benefit of variance regularization, even with the improved estimator, is also valuable. The inclusion of runtime comparisons is a welcome addition. The paper is well-written and generally clear in its presentation.

However, there are areas for improvement. While the explanation of *why* propensity overfitting occurs is intuitive, a more rigorous mathematical justification for its emergence could strengthen the argument. The connection between the capacity of the hypothesis space and the severity of the overfitting could be further elaborated. The experimental section would benefit from a more thorough comparison with other state-of-the-art BLBF algorithms beyond POEM. While the authors acknowledge the limitations of the experimental setup (reliance on the supervised 7→bandit methodology), discussing potential scenarios where the observed benefits might diminish would add nuance. Finally, the discussion of the computational complexity, while positive, could be more detailed. Specifically, a breakdown of the computational cost for different components of Norm-POEM would be useful.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** The identification and mitigation of propensity overfitting is a novel and important contribution to the field of BLBF.
*   **Theoretical Soundness:** The paper provides a strong theoretical foundation for the proposed self-normalized estimator, including a proof of consistency.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The experimental results consistently demonstrate the superior performance of Norm-POEM compared to existing methods.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and explains complex concepts in a clear and accessible manner.
*   **Significance:** The proposed approach has the potential to significantly improve the performance of learning algorithms in real-world applications where learning from historical logs is essential.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Limited Comparison:** The experimental evaluation primarily focuses on comparison with POEM. A broader comparison with other BLBF algorithms is needed.
*   **Mathematical Rigor:** While intuitive, the explanation of the underlying reasons for propensity overfitting could be more mathematically rigorous.
*   **Generalizability:** A discussion of potential limitations and scenarios where the observed benefits might not hold would be beneficial.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that addresses a significant challenge in batch learning from logged bandit feedback. The authors present a novel and theoretically sound solution, supported by compelling empirical evidence. While there are minor areas for improvement, the strengths of the paper outweigh its weaknesses. I recommend acceptance.
