## Review of Counterfactual Risk Minimization with Parameter Bounds

This paper addresses a crucial challenge in applying counterfactual risk minimization (CRM) – a technique for learning from logged bandit data – namely, its susceptibility to overfitting. While CRM offers consistency guarantees, the authors demonstrate that it can overfit to the generation probability of the logged examples, effectively prioritizing memorization over generalization. To mitigate this, they propose a novel approach incorporating multiplicative parameter bounds to control variance while preserving consistency.

The core idea is compelling and well-motivated. The problem of overfitting in CRM, stemming from excessively strong support for observed data points, is clearly articulated. The proposed solution, involving normalization via multiplicative bounds, appears to be a sensible and theoretically grounded method for addressing this issue. The connection to self-normalized estimators is aptly drawn, and the explanation of how the bounds constrain the hypothesis space is clear. 

The paper’s strength lies in its focused contribution. It doesn’t attempt a wholesale re-imagining of CRM, but instead targets a specific and significant limitation. The theoretical analysis, while not detailed in the main body (and presumably resides in the appendix), appears to provide a solid foundation for the proposed method. The experimental results convincingly demonstrate the improvement gained by using the normalized counterfactual technique compared to the original, unnormalized approach. The reported performance gains are consistent across the tested scenarios, strengthening the claim of the method's effectiveness.

However, a few points could benefit from further clarification. While the authors state the method maintains consistency, a more detailed discussion of *how* consistency is preserved in the presence of the multiplicative bounds would be valuable. Specifically, elaborating on the conditions under which consistency holds – or any potential trade-offs involved – would strengthen the theoretical contribution. 

Furthermore, the experimental section, while positive, could be enhanced with a more comprehensive benchmark.  Including comparisons to other relevant approaches for handling bias in logged data (e.g., inverse propensity weighting, doubly robust estimators) would better contextualize the performance of the proposed method.  It would also be helpful to understand the sensitivity of the results to the choice of the multiplicative parameter, and whether a principled method for setting this parameter can be developed.  A brief discussion of the computational complexity of the proposed method, compared to standard CRM, would also be useful.

Despite these minor suggestions, this is a strong paper with a clear contribution. The identified overfitting issue in CRM is important, and the proposed solution is both theoretically sound and empirically validated. The presentation is generally well-written and easy to follow. 

**Overall assessment:** I recommend acceptance. While not a groundbreaking advancement, this paper offers a valuable improvement to a significant technique in the field of learning from logged data. The authors have identified a practical problem, proposed a well-motivated solution, and provided compelling evidence of its effectiveness.
