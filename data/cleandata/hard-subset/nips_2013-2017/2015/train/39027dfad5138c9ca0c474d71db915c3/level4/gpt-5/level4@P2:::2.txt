Summary and relation to prior work
The paper addresses counterfactual learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF) within the Counterfactual Risk Minimization (CRM) framework. It highlights a pathologic failure mode—propensity overfitting—when the standard inverse propensity scoring (IPS) estimator is used for ERM over rich hypothesis spaces, and proposes a self-normalized importance sampling estimator to mitigate this issue. The approach yields a new learning algorithm, Norm-POEM, for structured output prediction with exponential-family models. The work builds on prior CRM-based learning (POEM; ICML 2015), links to variance control in importance sampling via control variates, and is positioned against existing techniques such as clipping/truncation and empirical Bernstein bounds. Empirically, Norm-POEM outperforms POEM across multi-label datasets and approaches the performance of supervised CRFs.

Main comments on technical exposition
- The discussion around Equation (2) is, in my view, misleading and should be revised for correctness and clarity. An expectation has the form Σ f(x) p(x), where f(x) is the integrand and p(x) the probability measure. In (2), the loss is part of the integrand, not the measure. The claim that the estimator exhibits an “anomaly” because it is not invariant to translations of the loss is presented in a way that blurs this distinction. As written (page 2, second paragraph), the rationale for calling non-invariance an anomaly appears technically incorrect, and Example 1 rests on this premise, which (as framed) does not hold.
- Practically, the IPS estimator in (2) is vulnerable because the denominators pi can be extremely small or zero for actions rarely or never taken by the logging policy, leading to unstable estimates. The self-normalized estimator in (7) reduces this sensitivity by dividing by the sum of weights, thereby dampening the impact of small propensities.
- I strongly encourage the authors to adjust the exposition around (2) and Example 1 to avoid conflating properties of the integrand with those of the probability measure and to carefully articulate what is meant by “anomaly” in finite-sample estimators.

Empirical evaluation
The experiments are well designed within the supervised-to-bandit protocol and demonstrate consistent gains of Norm-POEM over POEM on multi-label datasets. The results also show that Norm-POEM is competitive with a supervised CRF baseline trained with full labels, which underscores the practical impact of the proposed estimator. The runtime observations are useful, and the analysis of Ŝ(ĥ) supports the claim that normalization mitigates propensity overfitting.

Strengths
- Clear problem formulation within BLBF and CRM and a principled use of multiplicative control variates.
- A simple, broadly applicable estimator (self-normalization) with intuitive diagnostics (Ŝ(h) ≈ 1).
- Consistent empirical improvements across datasets; robustness to loss translations as claimed.
- Useful discussion of optimization and variance estimation trade-offs.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- The theoretical exposition around Equation (2), invariance claims, and Example 1 is confusing and risks being technically misleading; revise those sections.
- Clarify assumptions on the logging policy’s support and discuss behavior when pi = 0 (and how clipping/thresholding is handled).
- The variance estimator for the ratio estimator is approximate; a brief discussion of its accuracy and conditions would strengthen the section.
- It would be valuable to position the method empirically against alternative variance-reduction approaches (e.g., stronger clipping baselines) and, if feasible, against doubly robust estimators, to better isolate the benefit of self-normalization.

Evaluation by review criteria
- Quality: The algorithmic idea is sound and well connected to importance sampling literature. The empirical evidence is strong. Exposition around (2) needs correction/clarification to avoid technical confusion.
- Clarity: Generally well written, but key parts of the motivation (Eq. 2, “anomaly,” Example 1) should be rewritten for precision. Minor edits would make the contributions easier to follow.
- Originality: The application of self-normalized importance sampling within CRM for BLBF and its integration into Norm-POEM is a novel and reasonable combination of known tools addressing a real failure mode.
- Significance: High practical relevance for off-policy learning from logs in recommendation/search/ads. The demonstrated performance gains and robustness suggest that practitioners could adopt the method.

Pros for acceptance
- Addresses an important and prevalent issue (propensity overfitting) in BLBF.
- Simple, principled estimator with strong empirical gains.
- Clear path to practical impact and adoption.

Cons for acceptance
- Exposition around the properties of the IPS estimator is misleading and should be corrected.
- Limited comparison to some alternative baselines and limited discussion of the variance approximation’s limitations.

Recommendation
I lean toward acceptance, contingent on revising the commentary around Equation (2) and Example 1 to be technically precise and clearly motivated, and on clarifying the handling of small/zero propensities and the variance approximation. The empirical results and the proposed estimator’s robustness make this a valuable contribution.
