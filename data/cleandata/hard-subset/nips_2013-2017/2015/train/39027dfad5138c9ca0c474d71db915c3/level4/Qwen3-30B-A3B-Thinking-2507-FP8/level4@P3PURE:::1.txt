Nevertheless, I found the paper interesting and believe it merits consideration, though I may have missed or misinterpreted certain aspects—I apologize in advance if this is the case.

The paper addresses the problem of evaluating a new hypothesis using data generated under a different hypothesis, where reweighting (as in the transition between equations (1) and (2)) can estimate the loss under the new hypothesis when hypotheses are non-deterministic (e.g., conditional distributions). However, the authors focus on a problem they term "propensity overfitting," which appears related but distinct from the well-known issue of large weight variance. The examples suggest propensity overfitting arises due to insufficient exploration (e.g., for a given $x$, few or no $y$ observations correspond to the target hypothesis). Given that similar reasoning explains large weight variance, the authors should clarify the precise relationship between these two problems.

I was unable to verify the technical novelty or full correctness of the proposed solution, though the core idea appears sound. Specific concerns include:

- **Line 199**: If $n$ is small relative to $k$, we observe few data points with $x_i = y_i$, implying $\hat{R}(\hat{h})$ should be smaller than $-2$ (not larger). Am I missing something?
- **Citation [4, 5, 1]**: The claim that prior works on BLBF use an "unbiased counterfactual risk estimator" appears misleading. I question whether these works exclusively employ unbiased estimators without variance-reducing techniques (e.g., clipping), which typically induce bias.

I commend the authors for providing code. In summary, while I could not fully validate the technical soundness, the paper presents intriguing ideas worthy of further discussion. I recommend acceptance pending clarification of the relationship between propensity overfitting and weight variance, and the cited methodology.
