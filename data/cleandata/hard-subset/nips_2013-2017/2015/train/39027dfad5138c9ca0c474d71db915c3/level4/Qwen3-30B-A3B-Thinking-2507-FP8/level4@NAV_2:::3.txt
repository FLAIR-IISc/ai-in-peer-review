

Summary  
This paper addresses batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF), a critical problem for improving interactive systems like ad placement and recommendations using historical logs. It identifies a novel "propensity overfitting" issue in the conventional counterfactual risk estimator (R̂(h)) used within the Counterfactual Risk Minimization (CRM) framework [1], which leads to severe risk estimate bias during empirical risk minimization. The authors propose replacing R̂(h) with a self-normalized risk estimator (R̂SN(h)) using multiplicative control variates, resolving key anomalies (non-equivariance, unbounded risk estimates) while maintaining strong consistency. This yields Norm-POEM, a new learning algorithm for structured output prediction, evaluated on multi-label classification datasets. The work extends prior NIPS/ICML contributions [1, 5, 6] by addressing a previously uncharacterized failure mode in BLBF, with empirical results showing consistent outperformance over POEM [1] across datasets.  

Quality  
The paper is technically sound, with rigorous identification of the propensity overfitting problem (Section 4), a clear theoretical resolution via the self-normalized estimator (Theorem 2, Section 5), and thorough empirical validation (Section 7). The derivation of Norm-POEM (Section 6) and its computational analysis (runtime comparisons) are well-motivated. However, the experimental section would benefit from additional variance metrics across the 10 runs (e.g., standard deviations in Table 1) and deeper analysis of the optimization dynamics (e.g., convergence behavior of ∥w∥ in Section 7), particularly given the non-convex nature of the objective.  

Clarity  
The paper is exceptionally well-written and logically structured. Key concepts (e.g., propensity overfitting vs. loss overfitting, self-normalization mechanics) are clearly defined with illustrative examples (Example 1, Section 4). The connection between theoretical guarantees (Theorem 2) and empirical results (Table 2) is particularly effective. Minor improvements could include a brief discussion of the computational cost of the denominator in R̂SN(h) (Section 5) and explicit justification for the choice of L-BFGS over stochastic methods (Section 6).  

Originality  
While building on the CRM framework [1], the paper makes a significant original contribution by identifying and resolving propensity overfitting—a previously overlooked failure mode in BLBF. The self-normalized estimator (R̂SN(h)) is a novel application of multiplicative control variates to counterfactual learning, distinct from prior work on variance reduction (e.g., clipped weights [15], additive control variates [6]). The distinction from related NIPS work (e.g., [5, 11, 13]) is clearly articulated, and the empirical validation on diverse datasets (Table 1, Figure 1) demonstrates a meaningful improvement over [1].  

Significance  
The problem of learning from logged bandit feedback is highly relevant to real-world applications (e.g., ad systems, recommendation engines). The proposed solution directly addresses a critical vulnerability in existing BLBF methods, as evidenced by the consistent performance gains of Norm-POEM over POEM (Table 1, Figure 1). However, the significance could be strengthened by more explicit discussion of how the experimental improvements translate to practical deployments (e.g., sensitivity to logging policy quality [1] or real-world dataset scalability). The work advances the state of the art by providing a theoretically grounded, robust alternative to the conventional estimator, enabling more reliable offline learning from historical logs.  

Pro and Con Acceptance  
**Pro**:  
- Identifies and resolves a fundamental flaw (propensity overfitting) in a widely used BLBF framework.  
- Theoretical guarantees (Theorem 2) and empirical validation (Table 1, Figure 1) are compelling.  
- Norm-POEM is computationally efficient and outperforms POEM across all tested datasets.  
- Clear, well-motivated contribution that advances the field beyond prior NIPS work [1, 5, 6].  

**Con**:  
- Limited experimental scope (only multi-label classification; no real-world applications beyond simulated bandit feedback).  
- Missing variance metrics in experimental results (e.g., standard deviations in Table 1).  
- Insufficient analysis of optimization dynamics (e.g., why Norm-POEM converges faster than POEM despite higher per-iteration cost).  

Overall, this is a strong contribution with clear theoretical and practical impact. The paper meets NIPS quality standards, though minor expansions in experimental reporting would strengthen it further.
