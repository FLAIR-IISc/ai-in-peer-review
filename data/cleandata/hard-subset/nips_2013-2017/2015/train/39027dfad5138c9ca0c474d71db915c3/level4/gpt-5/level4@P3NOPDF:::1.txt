Summary
The paper studies off-policy evaluation and learning when data are logged under one hypothesis (policy/model) but we wish to evaluate or learn another. The standard approach is to reweight examples by inverse propensities (as in the equation between (1) and (2)). The authors identify and target a related but distinct issue they call propensity overfitting: when exploration is insufficient, certain x–y pairs favored by the new hypothesis are extremely rare or unseen in the logged data, and empirical risk minimization with large inverse-propensity weights can exploit noise. The paper proposes a remedy and provides illustrative examples.

Strengths
- Timely and important problem: off-policy evaluation and counterfactual learning are central in bandits and structured prediction.
- The notion of propensity overfitting is intuitive and practically relevant; the examples make the phenomenon clear.
- The proposed solution appears sensible, even though I did not have time to check all derivations and novelty claims in depth.
- Code availability is appreciated and improves reproducibility.

Main concerns and requests for clarification
- Relationship to high-variance importance weighting: Please make the connection between propensity overfitting and the well-known large-variance problem more explicit. To what extent is propensity overfitting simply finite-sample high variance manifested through model selection, versus a qualitatively different failure mode? A discussion that separates (i) the statistical property of the estimator (variance/infinite variance) from (ii) the learning dynamics that overfit to a few high-weighted samples would help, ideally with theory (e.g., conditions on overlap, lower bounds on propensities) and targeted simulations.
- Assumptions and guarantees: Under what conditions on exploration (e.g., minimum propensity, support overlap) is the proposed estimator/controller consistent, and at what rate? How does the method behave when propensities are exactly zero for actions favored by the target hypothesis?
- Comparison to prior work: The paper states “unbiased counterfactual risk estimator used in prior works on BLBF [4, 5, 1].” My understanding is that many practical implementations use clipping, self-normalization, or other variance-control techniques that introduce bias. Please qualify this claim, clearly distinguish between the unbiased IPS estimator and biased-but-lower-variance variants used in practice, and position your method relative to both. Empirical comparisons to IPS, clipped IPS, self-normalized IPS, and doubly robust estimators would be informative.
- Sensitivity and diagnostics: Since the method addresses overfitting due to extreme weights and sparse support, it would be valuable to report:
  - Distributional summaries of weights (e.g., quantiles, max) and the fraction of near-zero propensities.
  - Overlap diagnostics per context, or a measure of coverage of the target hypothesis under the logging policy.
  - Sensitivity to any hyperparameters controlling clipping/regularization and to the exploration rate in synthetic experiments.
- Equation and example around line 199: I am confused by the claim there. If n is small relative to k, we see very few data points with xi = yi, so I would expect the empirical risk R(ĥ) to be even smaller than −2 under the given setup. Could you check the derivation and sign conventions and clarify why this does not occur?

Minor comments
- Please add a brief discussion near the equation between (1) and (2) that highlights the overlap assumption and its practical implications.
- If you use any variance-reduction/device (clipping, self-normalization, control variates), state it prominently and discuss the resulting bias–variance trade-off.
- Where possible, include confidence intervals or standard errors in plots/tables.

Reproducibility
- Thank you for releasing code. Please ensure that all experiments are covered with scripts and seeds, and document any data preprocessing and hyperparameter selection protocols.

Overall assessment
I could not comprehensively verify the technical details or novelty due to time constraints, but I found the problem important, the phenomenon well-motivated, and the proposed idea plausible. With clearer positioning relative to high-variance IPS and prior work (including biased IPS variants), plus the noted clarifications, this paper would make a valuable contribution.
