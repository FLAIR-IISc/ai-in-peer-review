Summary and relation to prior work
The paper addresses batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF), where only the loss for the action chosen by a historic logging policy is observed. Prior BLBF methods largely rely on inverse propensity scoring (IPS) within the Counterfactual Risk Minimization (CRM) framework to correct for logging bias (e.g., Dudík et al. 2011; Strehl et al. 2010; Swaminathan & Joachims 2015). The authors identify a previously underappreciated failure mode of IPS when used for empirical risk minimization over rich hypothesis classes: propensity overfitting. Here, a learner can exploit high-variance importance weights to drive the IPS risk arbitrarily low (or zero after loss translation), independent of true performance. This manifests as non-equivariance of IPS to loss translation and unbounded finite-sample estimates.

To mitigate this, the paper proposes replacing IPS with the self-normalized importance sampling estimator (SNIPS), a classic multiplicative control variate (ratio) estimator known to reduce variance when weights fluctuate (e.g., Hesterberg 1995; Owen 2013; Rubinstein & Kroese 2011). The authors show that SNIPS (i) is bounded within the loss range, (ii) is equivariant to additive translations of the loss, and (iii) is strongly consistent under standard support assumptions. They incorporate SNIPS into a CRM-style training objective with an approximate variance penalty, resulting in a practical learning algorithm, Norm-POEM, for structured output prediction with exponential-family (CRF-like) models. Experiments on multi-label classification datasets from the LibSVM repository demonstrate consistent improvements over POEM (which uses IPS), improved robustness to loss translation, and favorable computational behavior.

Quality
- Theory: The propensity overfitting phenomenon is clearly illustrated via a constructed example and by empirical diagnostics using the known expectation of the sum of normalized weights Ŝ(h). The benefits of SNIPS—boundedness, equivariance, strong consistency—are correctly stated and supported by standard arguments. The variance estimate for SNIPS is based on a normal/delta-method approximation and the use of clipping to ensure finite variance is reasonable.
- Scope and limits: The paper does not provide a generalization bound for SNIPS analogous to the CRM bound for IPS. While the variance-regularized objective is well motivated, the theoretical link between the proposed objective and out-of-sample performance is heuristic. Assumptions (accurate logged propensities, full support) are standard but should be discussed more explicitly for practical settings.
- Experiments: The “Supervised → Bandit” protocol is sound and matches prior work, allowing controlled comparisons. Results consistently favor Norm-POEM across datasets and under loss translations; the diagnostic Ŝ(ĥ) is compelling. It would strengthen the empirical case to include comparisons to clipped-IPS baselines with tuned thresholds, and to doubly robust (DR) estimators/learners (Dudík et al., 2011) which are known variance reducers in off-policy settings.

Clarity
The paper is generally clear and well organized. The exposition of propensity overfitting, the role of the Ŝ(h) diagnostic, and the properties of SNIPS are easy to follow. A few suggestions:
- Clearly separate “estimation” guarantees (consistency, boundedness) from “learning” guarantees (generalization bounds), and discuss what is retained or lost when moving from IPS-based CRM to SNIPS-based training.
- Expand the discussion of when SNIPS helps the most (high variance in weights, modest variance in losses), and when its bias might matter.
- Ensure all references are complete and consistent; a few entries in the draft bibliography are missing publication details.

Originality
Using SNIPS in Monte Carlo estimation is classical; the novelty here is identifying and formalizing propensity overfitting as a learning-time pathology of IPS in BLBF, and integrating SNIPS into a CRM-style objective for structured prediction (Norm-POEM). The diagnostic via Ŝ(h) and the translation-invariance argument are insightful and practically useful. This combination represents a meaningful and original contribution relative to prior BLBF work at NIPS/ICML.

Significance
BLBF is important for modern interactive systems (ads, search, recommendation). A robust estimator that avoids degenerate ERM behavior is practically significant. The gains observed over a strong IPS-based baseline (POEM) across multiple datasets, together with the diagnostic evidence and code availability, suggest the work will influence both practice and follow-up research on counterfactual learning.

Pros
- Clear identification and explanation of propensity overfitting in IPS-based learning.
- Well-motivated use of a self-normalized estimator with desirable properties (boundedness, equivariance, consistency).
- Practical algorithm (Norm-POEM) for structured prediction with variance regularization and tractable optimization.
- Consistent empirical improvements and informative diagnostics (Ŝ(ĥ) near 1, robustness to loss translation).
- Code availability facilitates adoption.

Cons
- Lack of a theoretical generalization bound for SNIPS-based CRM; the objective is motivated but not accompanied by new learning guarantees.
- Empirical comparisons omit DR-based methods; including DR (and clipped IPS with strong tuning) would give a more complete picture.
- Assumptions about accurate propensities and full support are strong; discussion of robustness to misspecification or partial support is limited.
- Variance estimator is approximate; ablations quantifying its impact on performance would be helpful.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide a learning-theoretic analysis for SNIPS-based training (e.g., a bound that explicitly incorporates the ratio estimator’s variance), or at least discuss why existing CRM bounds do or do not carry over.
- Add baselines: clipped IPS with tuned clipping, and DR/doubly robust learning where a reward model is feasible; report statistical significance.
- Analyze sensitivity to hyperparameters M and λ and to logging policy entropy/quality; include confidence intervals.
- Discuss practical issues: handling unknown or noisy propensities, partial support, and diagnostics for when SNIPS may still be unreliable.
- Clarify and complete bibliographic entries; fix minor typos and verify dataset statistics.

Recommendation
I lean toward acceptance. The paper tackles a real and important weakness in IPS-based counterfactual learning, offers a principled and practical remedy with strong empirical evidence, and provides useful diagnostics. Strengthening the theoretical learning guarantees and widening empirical comparisons would further solidify the contribution, but the current version already represents a meaningful advance for BLBF.
