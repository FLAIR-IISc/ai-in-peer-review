This paper identifies a severe problem of propensity overfitting in the counterfactual risk estimator typically used in batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF), and proposes the use of a self-normalized estimator to avoid this problem. In the BLBF setting, the learner does not receive full-information feedback, but observes feedback only for the actions taken by a historical policy. This makes BLBF algorithms particularly attractive for training online systems (e.g., ad placement, web search, recommendation) using their historical logs. The Counterfactual Risk Minimization (CRM) principle [1] offers a general recipe for designing BLBF algorithms, requiring a counterfactual risk estimator. The authors show that the conventional estimator suffers from propensity overfitting when used for learning over complex hypothesis spaces. They propose to replace the risk estimator with a self-normalized estimator, demonstrating that it avoids this problem. This naturally leads to a new learning algorithm – Normalized Policy Optimizer for Exponential Models (Norm-POEM) – for structured output prediction using linear rules. Empirical evaluation on several multi-label classification problems shows that Norm-POEM consistently outperforms the conventional estimator.

1 Introduction
Most interactive systems (e.g., search engines, recommender systems, ad platforms) record large quantities of log data containing valuable information about system performance and user experience. For example, ad-placement system logs record which ad was presented and whether the user clicked on it. While this data should inform system design, it doesn't provide supervised training data in the conventional sense. Instead, it offers bandit feedback – feedback is observed only for the action taken by the system (e.g., the presented ad), and not for other possible actions. Moreover, log entries are biased since actions favored by the system will be over-represented.
Learning from historical logs can be formalized as batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF) [2, 1]. Unlike online learning from bandit feedback [3], this setting doesn’t require interactive control over the system. Learning in this setting is closely related to off-policy evaluation in reinforcement learning [4] – we want to know how well a new system (policy) would perform if it had been used in the past. This motivates the use of counterfactual estimators [5]. Following an approach analogous to Empirical Risk Minimization (ERM), it has been shown that such estimators can be used to design learning algorithms for BLBF [6, 5, 1].
However, the conventional counterfactual risk estimator used in prior BLBF work exhibits anomalies that can lead to degeneracies when used in ERM. Specifically, the estimator exhibits a novel form of Propensity Overfitting that causes severely biased risk estimates. By introducing multiplicative control variates, the authors propose to replace this risk estimator with a Self-Normalized Risk Estimator, provably avoiding these degeneracies. Empirical evaluation confirms that these theoretical properties translate into improved generalization performance and robustness.

2 Related work
Batch learning from logged bandit feedback is an instance of causal inference, making classic inference techniques like propensity score matching [7] relevant. BLBF is also closely related to learning under covariate shift (domain adaptation) [8] and off-policy evaluation in reinforcement learning [4]. Lower bounds for domain adaptation [8] and impossibility results for off-policy evaluation [9] therefore also apply to BLBF.
Several counterfactual estimators have been developed for off-policy evaluation [11, 6, 5], all instances of importance sampling for Monte Carlo approximation and traceable to What-If simulations [12]. Learning bounds have been developed recently [13, 1, 14] showing these estimators can work for BLBF. The authors additionally show that importance sampling can overfit in previously unforeseen ways with the capacity of the hypothesis space, termed "Propensity Overfitting". Classic variance reduction techniques for importance sampling are also useful. For instance, importance weights can be clipped [15] to trade-off bias against variance [5]. Additive control variates give rise to regression estimators [16] and doubly robust estimators [6]; the authors’ proposal uses multiplicative control variates, widely used in financial applications [17] and policy iteration for reinforcement learning [18]. Specifically, they study the self-normalized estimator [12], superior to the vanilla estimator when fluctuations in the weights dominate variance [19], and demonstrate that it addresses propensity overfitting.

3 Batch learning from logged bandit feedback
Following [1], the authors focus on the stochastic, cardinal, contextual bandit setting and recap the CRM principle. Inputs x ∈ X are drawn i.i.d. from a distribution Pr(X). Outputs are y ∈ Y. The hypothesis space H contains stochastic hypotheses h(Y | x) defining a probability distribution over Y. A hypothesis h ∈ H predicts by sampling from the conditional distribution y ∼ h(Y | x). This definition captures both deterministic and stochastic hypotheses. For notational convenience, the distribution h(Y | x) is denoted by h(x), and the probability assigned by h(x) to y as h(y | x). Samples (x, y) ∼ h denote x ∼ Pr(X), y ∼ h(x), and when clear, (x, y) is dropped. Bandit feedback means feedback δ(x, y) is observed only for the predicted y, not for other possible Y \ {y}. The feedback is a number, representing the loss, and smaller numbers are desirable. Generally, the loss is the realization of a stochastic random variable. The expected loss – the risk – of a hypothesis R(h) is:
R(h) = Ex∼Pr(X)Ey∼h(x) [δ(x, y)] = Eh [δ(x, y)] . (1)
The goal of learning is to find a hypothesis h ∈ H with minimum risk.
Counterfactual estimators are used to leverage historical logs for learning. Assuming the historical algorithm h0(x) has full support over Y, the authors use importance sampling to address the distribution mismatch: R(h) = Eh [δ(x, y)] = Eh0 [ δ(x, y) h(y | x) / h0(y | x) ].
With data D = {(x1, y1, δ1, p1), ..., (xn, yn, δn, pn)} where (xi, yi) ∼ h0, δi ≡ δ(xi, yi) and pi ≡ h0(yi | xi), an unbiased estimate of R(h) is:
R̂(h) = 1/n ∑i=1 δi h(yi |xi) / pi . (2)
This estimator has unbounded variance, especially when pi ≈ 0. Thresholding schemes have been proposed to address this [15, 8, 5, 11]. The CRM principle, inspired by ERM, aims to minimize this estimated risk while accounting for variance.
However, the conventional estimator exhibits anomalies leading to degeneracies. Specifically, it exhibits a new form of Propensity Overfitting causing biased risk estimates. The authors propose replacing it with a Self-Normalized Risk Estimator using multiplicative control variates, provably avoiding these degeneracies. Empirical evaluation confirms this leads to improved generalization and robustness.

4 The Propensity Overfitting problem
The CRM objective in Equation (3) penalizes hypotheses "far" from the logging policy h0 based on their empirical variance. This safeguards against overfitting. However, overfitting in BLBF is nuanced. Even with δ(·, ·) ∈ [-1, 0], R̂(h) need not lie in that range. Translating the loss δ(·, ·) by a constant doesn’t translate R̂(h) linearly. These anomalies create vulnerabilities to a unique overfitting form.
Example 1 illustrates this. For inputs X = {1..k} and outputs Y = {1..k}, with δ(x, y) = -2 if y = x-1, otherwise 0, and a hypothesis space H of deterministic functions, an overfitted hypothesis hoverfit assigns f(xi) = yi for all i. The empirical risk R̂(hoverfit) can be arbitrarily lower than the true risk of the best hypothesis, demonstrating severe overfitting unlike in supervised learning where overfitted risk is bounded. The variance regularization of CRM combats both Loss Overfitting and Propensity Overfitting. The authors propose that a multiplicative control variate can directly address Propensity Overfitting.

5 Control variates and the Self-Normalized estimator
To avoid Propensity Overfitting, the authors leverage diagnostic tools from importance sampling. They note that E[Ŝ(h)] = 1, where Ŝ(h) = 1/n ∑i=1 h(yi | xi) pi. Deviations of Ŝ(h) from 1 indicate potential overfitting.
Using S(h) as a control variate for R(h) leads to the Self-Normalized risk estimator:
R̂SN (h) = ∑i=1 δi h(yi|xi) / pi / (∑i=1 h(yi|xi) pi) . (7)
This estimator is more accurate when fluctuations in sampling weights dominate fluctuations in δ(x, y) [19]. It’s also equivariant – translating the loss translates the risk estimate proportionally. Furthermore, R̂SN (h) is bounded within the range of δ. 

6 Learning method: Norm-POEM
The authors derive Norm-POEM, a learning algorithm based on the CRM principle using the self-normalized estimator. The hypothesis space consists of stochastic linear rules hw ∈ Hlin parametrized by w, operating on a joint feature map φ(x, y). The variance estimator utilizes an approximate estimate. The optimization problem is solved using L-BFGS. 

7 Experiments
The authors empirically verify that Norm-POEM avoids propensity overfitting and improves generalization performance. They follow a Supervised → Bandit methodology using multi-label classification datasets. Results demonstrate that Norm-POEM consistently outperforms POEM, with improved generalization and robustness, and faster convergence. Analysis shows that Norm-POEM avoids overfitting by keeping Ŝ(h) close to its expected value.

8 Conclusions
The authors identify and address the problem of propensity overfitting in BLBF. They propose a self-normalized risk estimator and demonstrate its effectiveness through Norm-POEM, achieving improved generalization performance.

**Other comments:**
- There is something I find missing in the setup for "batch learning from logged bandit feedback" of Section 3 (or from [1]): I feel the feedback should also be seen as a random variable, rather than a deterministic function as presented on line 110. Here is my rationale. Suppose that the partial feedback is coming from a classification problem where we just do not know the labels, we only get feedback by trying a prediction and seeing what is the loss. Suppose that there is a true classification loss Delta(y',y), and that the way the feedback is generated is, for a given input x, we make a prediction y using h_0; some god agent labels also the input to y'; and then tells us the loss Delta(y',y) that we incur (so here delta(x,y) is Delta(y',y) for the y' given to this example). On the other hand, often in classification, we suppose that the labeling can be noisy and so there is not necessarily a unique y' assigned to each x. This means that somewhere else in the log, we could have the same x as input, the same y that we played, but with a different delta(x,y) as the god agent just had given a different label for this one... This is why I think that in general delta(x,y) should be seen as a random number (in the classification example above the mean of this random variable for a fix x & played y should be the expectation of Delta(y',y) for y' distributed according to p(y'|x), the true noisy labeling distribution). This perhaps does not change anything about the risk estimator (2); but at least the setup in (1) with the true risk should be presented with an additional expectation over the randomness of delta, to be more general.
