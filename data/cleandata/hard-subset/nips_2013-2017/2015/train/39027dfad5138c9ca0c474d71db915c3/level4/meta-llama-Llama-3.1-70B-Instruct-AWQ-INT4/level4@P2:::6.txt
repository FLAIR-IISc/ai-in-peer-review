This paper presents a technique for learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF) that addresses a severe overfitting problem in the conventional counterfactual risk estimator. The authors identify a new form of overfitting, called Propensity Overfitting, which occurs when the hypothesis class overfits the propensity weights of the logged data. To combat this issue, they propose a self-normalized risk estimator that uses a multiplicative control variate to reduce the variance of the estimator.

The paper is well-motivated, and the problem of Propensity Overfitting is clearly explained and demonstrated through examples. The proposed self-normalized risk estimator is theoretically sound, and the authors provide a proof of its consistency. The experimental results show that the self-normalized estimator outperforms the conventional estimator in several multi-label classification problems.

The paper is well-written, and the authors provide a clear and concise explanation of the proposed technique. The related work section is thorough, and the authors provide a good discussion of the limitations of previous approaches.

The strengths of the paper are:

* The authors identify a significant problem in the conventional counterfactual risk estimator and propose a theoretically sound solution.
* The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed technique in several multi-label classification problems.
* The paper is well-written, and the authors provide a clear and concise explanation of the proposed technique.

The weaknesses of the paper are:

* The paper assumes that the logged data is generated by a stationary policy, which may not always be the case in practice.
* The authors do not provide a detailed analysis of the computational complexity of the proposed technique.
* The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion of the limitations of the proposed technique and potential avenues for future work.
