### Paper Review

**Summary**

This paper identifies a critical and previously overlooked failure mode of the standard unbiased inverse propensity score (IPS) estimator when used for counterfactual risk minimization in the Batch Learning from Logged Bandit Feedback (BLBF) setting. The work introduces and names this problem "Propensity Overfitting," where a learning algorithm with sufficient capacity can achieve an artificially low empirical risk by manipulating the sum of importance weights, rather than by actually modeling the underlying loss function. This can lead to degenerate solutions that either pathologically overfit or avoid the training data, depending on the sign of the losses.

To address this issue, the paper proposes replacing the standard IPS estimator with the self-normalized importance sampling estimator. This estimator, framed as an application of multiplicative control variates, is shown to be equivariant to shifts in the loss function and always produces risk estimates bounded by the range of observed losses, thereby directly mitigating the Propensity Overfitting problem. While the estimator is biased in finite samples, it is strongly consistent.

Building on this improved estimator, the paper develops a new learning algorithm, Norm-POEM, which integrates the self-normalized estimator and an appropriate variance approximation into the Counterfactual Risk Minimization (CRM) framework for learning structured-output models. An extensive empirical evaluation on several multi-label classification tasks demonstrates that Norm-POEM consistently and substantially outperforms its predecessor (POEM), which uses the standard unbiased estimator, and is robust to loss transformations that cause the baseline method to fail catastrophically.

**Strengths and Weaknesses**

**Quality:** The paper is of high technical quality. The central claim—that the standard counterfactual risk estimator is susceptible to a novel form of overfitting—is clearly articulated and convincingly demonstrated with a simple, effective example (Example 1). The proposed solution is theoretically well-grounded, leveraging established techniques from the Monte Carlo literature (control variates, ratio estimators) and correctly analyzing their properties (equivariance, consistency). The experimental methodology is rigorous, following the well-controlled "Supervised-to-Bandit" protocol, and the results provide strong evidence for the paper's claims. The ablation study confirming the continued need for CRM's variance regularization is a valuable inclusion.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow. The motivation is clear, and the logical progression from identifying the problem to proposing and validating the solution is compelling. The use of "Propensity Overfitting" versus "Loss Overfitting" is an insightful distinction that clarifies the nature of the problem. The experimental results are presented clearly in tables and figures, directly supporting the core arguments made in the text. The derivation of the Norm-POEM algorithm is straightforward.

**Originality:** The primary contribution is the identification, formalization, and naming of the "Propensity Overfitting" problem within the context of counterfactual learning. This appears to be a novel and important insight. While the self-normalized estimator is a known tool in statistics, its application as a direct remedy for this specific learning pathology in BLBF is a novel contribution. This work successfully reframes a known estimator as a solution to a newly identified problem in a machine learning context, which is a significant step forward from prior work that focused more generally on variance reduction.

**Significance:** The work is highly significant for the field of counterfactual learning and off-policy evaluation. The BLBF setting is of immense practical importance for improving real-world interactive systems (e.g., search, recommendation, advertising) from logged data. The identified problem is fundamental and likely affects any learning algorithm based on the standard IPS estimator, especially when using expressive hypothesis spaces. The proposed solution is simple, principled, and shown to be highly effective, making it a valuable tool for both researchers and practitioners. The Norm-POEM algorithm provides a concrete, state-of-the-art method that others can readily adopt. This work is likely to influence how counterfactual estimators are chosen and used in learning systems going forward.

**Suggestions for Improvement**
While the paper is strong, a few minor points could enhance it further.
*   The variance estimator for the self-normalized risk is based on an approximation (e.g., via the delta method). A brief comment on the quality of this approximation and its potential impact on the CRM regularizer would be helpful for completeness.
*   The experiments are conducted on multi-label classification datasets. While these are suitable for demonstrating the core ideas, showing the effectiveness of the proposed method on a problem more directly related to the motivating applications, such as ad placement or recommendation, would further strengthen the claims of practical impact. This is a suggestion for future work rather than a required revision.

**Arguments for Acceptance**
1.  **Novel Problem Formulation:** The paper identifies and clearly explains a fundamental and previously unaddressed problem—Propensity Overfitting—in a critical learning setting.
2.  **Principled and Effective Solution:** It proposes an elegant and theoretically justified solution that demonstrably fixes the problem.
3.  **Strong Empirical Validation:** The experiments are thorough and provide compelling evidence that the proposed method is substantially more robust and accurate than the existing state-of-the-art.
4.  **High Impact Potential:** The findings are of immediate relevance to anyone using logged bandit feedback to train machine learning models, a common scenario in industry.

**Arguments against Acceptance**
This is a very strong paper, and it is difficult to find substantial arguments against its acceptance. One could note that the core statistical tool (the self-normalized estimator) is not new, but its application and the problem it solves in this context are novel and significant.

**Overall Recommendation:** This is an excellent paper that makes a clear, important, and well-supported contribution. It should be accepted.
