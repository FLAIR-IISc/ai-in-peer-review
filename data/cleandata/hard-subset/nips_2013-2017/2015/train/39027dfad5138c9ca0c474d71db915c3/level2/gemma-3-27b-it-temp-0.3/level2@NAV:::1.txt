## Review of "The Self-Normalized Estimator for Counterfactual Learning"

This paper addresses a critical issue in Batch Learning from Logged Bandit Feedback (BLBF): propensity overfitting in the commonly used counterfactual risk estimator. The authors convincingly demonstrate that this estimator can lead to severely biased risk estimates, particularly with complex hypothesis spaces. Their proposed solution, a self-normalized estimator, offers a theoretically sound and empirically validated alternative that mitigates this problem. The paper is well-motivated, clearly written, and tackles a significant challenge in applying machine learning to real-world sequential decision-making problems like ad placement and recommendation systems.

The core strength of the paper lies in its insightful analysis of the propensity overfitting phenomenon. The examples provided effectively illustrate how the conventional estimator can be misled, even with simple loss functions. The introduction of the self-normalized estimator, drawing from importance sampling literature, is a clever and effective solution. The theoretical justification, building on existing work in CRM and variance reduction techniques, is solid. The empirical evaluation on multi-label classification problems consistently demonstrates the superiority of Norm-POEM (the algorithm built around the new estimator) over the conventional approach. The ablation study examining the necessity of variance regularization is also valuable.

However, the paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion of the computational cost of the self-normalized estimator. While the authors mention it's comparable to POEM, a more precise analysis, potentially including scaling with dataset size and hypothesis space complexity, would be helpful. Additionally, the connection to existing work on doubly-robust estimators could be more explicitly drawn, highlighting the differences and potential trade-offs. While the paper acknowledges limitations, exploring the scenarios where the self-normalized estimator might *not* be advantageous would strengthen the analysis.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Addresses a significant and practical problem in BLBF.
*   Provides a theoretically sound and well-motivated solution.
*   Demonstrates strong empirical performance across multiple datasets.
*   Clear and well-written presentation.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   Computational cost analysis could be more detailed.
*   Connection to related work (e.g., doubly-robust estimators) could be strengthened.
*   Discussion of potential limitations could be expanded.

**Overall:**

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of learning from logged bandit feedback. The identified problem is important, the proposed solution is elegant and effective, and the empirical results are compelling. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the points mentioned above. The potential impact of this work is significant, and it is likely to be influential in future research on this topic.
