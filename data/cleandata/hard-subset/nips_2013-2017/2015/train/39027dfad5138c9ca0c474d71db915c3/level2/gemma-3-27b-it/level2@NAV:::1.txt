## Review of "The Self-Normalized Estimator for Counterfactual Learning"

This paper addresses a critical issue in Batch Learning from Logged Bandit Feedback (BLBF): propensity overfitting in the commonly used counterfactual risk estimator. The authors convincingly demonstrate that this estimator can lead to severely biased risk estimates, particularly with complex hypothesis spaces. Their proposed solution, a self-normalized estimator, is well-motivated and theoretically sound, offering a compelling alternative that avoids this problem. The introduction clearly sets the stage, connecting BLBF to related fields like causal inference and off-policy evaluation.

The core contribution – the identification and mitigation of propensity overfitting – is significant. The explanation is clear, and the example in Section 4 effectively illustrates the issue. The theoretical justification for the self-normalized estimator is solid, building upon existing work in importance sampling. The proposed Norm-POEM algorithm, built upon this estimator, provides a practical application of the theoretical results. 

Empirically, the results are strong. The experiments on multi-label classification problems consistently show Norm-POEM outperforming POEM, and the analysis in Section 7 convincingly demonstrates the effectiveness of the self-normalized estimator in addressing propensity overfitting. The inclusion of ablation studies (Table 3) further strengthens the claims. The runtime comparison is also valuable, highlighting the practical efficiency of the approach. 

However, the paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion of the limitations. While Theorem 2 guarantees consistency, the rate of convergence isn't discussed. Furthermore, the practical impact of the hyperparameter tuning (M and λ) is briefly mentioned but could be explored further. A more detailed discussion of the computational cost of the variance estimate in Equation (8) would also be beneficial.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Addresses a significant and previously unrecognised problem in BLBF.
*   Provides a theoretically sound and practical solution.
*   Strong empirical results demonstrating improved performance.
*   Well-written and clearly explained.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Limited discussion of the convergence rate of the estimator.
*   Could benefit from a more in-depth analysis of hyperparameter sensitivity.
*   Further elaboration on the computational complexity of the variance estimation.

**Overall:**

This is a high-quality paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of BLBF. While there are some minor areas for improvement, the strength of the theoretical and empirical results strongly supports acceptance. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the limitations outlined above.
