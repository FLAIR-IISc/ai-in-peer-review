

This paper identifies a significant issue with the conventional counterfactual risk estimator used in batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF), introducing the novel concept of "propensity overfitting" and proposing a self-normalized estimator to resolve it. The authors demonstrate theoretically that the standard inverse propensity estimator suffers from severe anomalies leading to biased risk estimates when used in Empirical Risk Minimization (ERM), particularly when learning over complex hypothesis spaces. Their key contribution is the self-normalized risk estimator, which provably avoids these issues and leads to the Norm-POEM algorithm for structured prediction.

The paper excels in theoretical rigor, with clear proofs demonstrating the self-normalized estimator's consistency and its resolution of the propensity overfitting problem. The empirical evaluation on multiple multi-label classification datasets provides strong support for the theoretical claims, showing consistent improvements in generalization performance over the conventional POEM algorithm. The experiments are well-designed, using the Supervised 7→ Bandit methodology to control for variables, and the authors carefully verify that the improved performance stems from better handling of propensity overfitting.

The paper demonstrates strong field knowledge, properly situating itself within the BLBF literature and correctly citing relevant prior work. The novelty is significant - propensity overfitting is a previously uncharacterized problem in BLBF, and the self-normalized estimator represents a meaningful advancement. The significance is high, as BLBF is crucial for real-world applications like ad placement and recommendation systems where full-information feedback is unavailable.

The clarity is excellent, with well-organized exposition and careful definitions. The paper acknowledges limitations, noting that the self-normalized estimator requires more computation per iteration but converges faster overall. A minor weakness is that the authors could provide more intuition about why propensity overfitting occurs in BLBF but not in standard supervised learning.

Overall, this is a high-quality, significant contribution to the field. The paper addresses a genuine problem with a novel solution that is theoretically sound and empirically validated. It advances the state of the art in BLBF by providing a more reliable risk estimator with demonstrably better performance. The work is well-positioned for NIPS, offering both theoretical insights and practical improvements for a problem of growing importance in machine learning applications.
