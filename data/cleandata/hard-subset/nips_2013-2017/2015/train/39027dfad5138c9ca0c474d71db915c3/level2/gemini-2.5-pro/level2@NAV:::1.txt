This paper identifies and addresses a critical failure mode of the conventional unbiased risk estimator used in batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF). The authors introduce the concept of "Propensity Overfitting," where a high-capacity model can achieve an artificially low risk estimate by manipulating importance weights, rather than by actually minimizing the true loss. To solve this, the paper proposes using a self-normalized importance sampling estimator, a technique from the Monte Carlo literature that is novelly applied here to counteract this learning pathology. This principled approach leads to a new algorithm, Norm-POEM, which integrates the self-normalized estimator into the Counterfactual Risk Minimization (CRM) framework, directly improving upon prior work like POEM [1].

The paper’s strengths are numerous and significant.
**Arguments for acceptance:**
*   **Significance and Originality:** The identification and clear articulation of "Propensity Overfitting" is a major contribution. It exposes a subtle but severe vulnerability in a widely used learning paradigm. The application of the self-normalized estimator as a direct remedy is an elegant and effective solution that meaningfully advances the field of off-policy learning.
*   **Quality:** The paper’s claims are rigorously supported. The authors provide an intuitive illustrative example, sound theoretical analysis of the proposed estimator's properties (equivariance and consistency), and thorough empirical validation. The experiments across multiple datasets are convincing, demonstrating that Norm-POEM not only achieves superior generalization but also avoids the catastrophic failures to which the previous method is susceptible.
*   **Clarity:** The manuscript is exceptionally well-written, well-organized, and easy to follow. The problem is clearly motivated, the solution is developed logically, and the experiments are presented with clarity, making the paper's contributions accessible and impactful.

The paper has only minor weaknesses.
**Arguments against acceptance:**
*   The paper could be strengthened by a more explicit discussion of its limitations. While it notes that the new variance estimator is computationally more complex per iteration, it claims faster overall convergence. A more detailed analysis of this computational trade-off would be beneficial for practitioners.
*   The experiments rely on a "Supervised to Bandit" conversion, which is a standard and valuable protocol. However, a brief discussion on potential challenges when applying the method to real-world, non-stationary log data would provide useful context.

In summary, this is an outstanding paper that presents a significant, well-validated contribution. It addresses an important and previously overlooked problem with a principled solution, making it a valuable addition to the conference.
