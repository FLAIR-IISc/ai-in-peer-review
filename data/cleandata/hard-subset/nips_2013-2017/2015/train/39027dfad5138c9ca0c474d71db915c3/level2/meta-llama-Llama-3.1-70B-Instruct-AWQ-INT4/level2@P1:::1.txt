The paper "The Self-Normalized Estimator for Counterfactual Learning" presents a novel approach to addressing the problem of propensity overfitting in batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF). The authors identify a severe issue with the conventional counterfactual risk estimator used in prior works on BLBF, which can lead to degeneracies and severely biased risk estimates. They propose an alternative estimator, called the Self-Normalized Risk Estimator, which provably avoids these degeneracies.

The paper is well-written, and the authors provide a clear and concise explanation of the problem and their proposed solution. The technical content is sound, and the authors provide a thorough analysis of the Self-Normalized Risk Estimator, including its theoretical properties and empirical evaluation.

One of the strengths of the paper is its ability to clearly articulate the problem of propensity overfitting and its impact on BLBF. The authors provide a simple yet effective example to illustrate the issue, making it easy to understand for readers who may not be familiar with the topic. They also provide a thorough review of related work, which helps to situate their contribution within the broader context of BLBF and counterfactual learning.

The empirical evaluation of the Self-Normalized Risk Estimator is also well-done. The authors provide a comprehensive comparison with the conventional estimator, showing that their proposed estimator achieves significantly better generalization performance. They also provide a detailed analysis of the results, including a discussion of the impact of propensity overfitting on the performance of the conventional estimator.

One potential weakness of the paper is that it assumes a relatively high level of background knowledge in BLBF and counterfactual learning. While the authors provide a clear explanation of the problem and their proposed solution, readers who are not familiar with the topic may find some of the technical details challenging to follow. Additionally, the paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion of the implications of the Self-Normalized Risk Estimator for real-world applications.

Overall, the paper is well-written and makes a significant contribution to the field of BLBF and counterfactual learning. The authors' proposed solution to the problem of propensity overfitting is novel and effective, and their empirical evaluation provides strong evidence for its efficacy.
