- Paper identifies "propensity overfitting": IPS risk can be driven to degenerate values due to unstable importance weights and is sensitive to additive loss shifts.  
- Authors propose using the self-normalized IPS (SNIPS/WIS) to replace IPS, arguing it fixes equivariance to loss shifts, bounds finite-sample risk, and is strongly consistent.  
- They introduce Norm-POEM: SNIPS plus an approximate variance regularizer applied to exponential-family policies for structured outputs.  
- Empirical results on supervised→bandit simulations (multi-label) show Norm-POEM consistently outperforms POEM, reduces propensity overfitting (via weight-sum diagnostic), and has competitive training time.  
- Relation to prior work: builds on CRM/POEM and OPE literature; SNIPS/WIS is well-known in Monte Carlo and RL but connections and bias/variance discussion could be clearer.  
- Novelty: SNIPS itself is not new, but diagnosing propensity overfitting and integrating SNIPS into CRM-style learning for structured prediction is a useful, original contribution.  
- Theory quality: diagnosis and SNIPS properties are correct, but paper lacks finite-sample generalization bounds for the SNIPS-CRM objective; variance plug-in is heuristic.  
- Algorithm quality: objectives, gradients, and L-BFGS optimization are reasonable; clipping is still required and trade-offs remain.  
- Experiments quality: controlled simulated protocol is appropriate and shows gains, but baselines are too narrow (no DR/normalized DR, no pure SNIPS baselines) and only simulated logged data is used.  
- Clarity: generally well written; exposition of propensity overfitting and Ŝ(h) is clear, but variance estimator derivation and pseudocode are under-detailed.  
- Significance: practically promising—bounded, translation-invariant estimates that improve performance—but theoretical and empirical limitations temper conclusiveness.  
- Pros: clear identification of propensity overfitting, principled SNIPS fix with strong consistency, practical Norm-POEM integration, consistent empirical gains, and useful diagnostics/analysis.  
- Cons: missing finite-sample learning analysis, limited baseline comparisons, only simulated logged data, incomplete related-work coverage on WIS/weighted IS and normalized DR variants.  
- Suggested improvements: provide finite-sample/rate bounds or ratio concentration results, expand baselines (DR variants, pure SNIPS, ESS diagnostics), test real or adversarial logging, analyze clipping/λ sensitivity, and add pseudocode/implementation details.  
- Recommendation: Weak accept — addresses an important failure mode and offers a principled, effective remedy despite non-novel estimator and limited theoretical/empirical breadth.
