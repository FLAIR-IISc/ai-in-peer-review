

This paper identifies a critical issue—propensity overfitting—in the conventional inverse propensity weighting estimator used for counterfactual risk minimization (CRM) in batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF). The authors demonstrate that this estimator can produce arbitrarily biased risk estimates when optimizing over complex hypothesis spaces, even with bounded loss functions. They propose replacing the standard estimator with a self-normalized version (R̂SN(h)) that leverages multiplicative control variates to maintain equivariance under loss translation and ensure bounded risk estimates. This leads to their Norm-POEM algorithm for structured prediction, which they validate on multi-label classification datasets.

**Strengths:**  
The paper makes a significant theoretical contribution by identifying and rigorously analyzing "propensity overfitting," a previously unrecognized problem that undermines existing BLBF methods. The self-normalized estimator (Equation 7) elegantly resolves the anomalies of the conventional estimator (Equation 2), as proven in Theorem 2. The empirical evaluation is comprehensive and convincing: Norm-POEM consistently outperforms POEM across multiple datasets (Table 1, Figure 1), with clear evidence that it avoids propensity overfitting (Table 2). The authors also demonstrate that the improved estimator remains robust under loss translations and maintains computational feasibility (Section 7). The connection to control variates in importance sampling (Section 5) is well-motivated and aligns with established techniques in financial applications, strengthening the theoretical foundation.

**Weaknesses:**  
While the theoretical analysis is sound, the paper could better contextualize the novelty relative to existing counterfactual estimators. For instance, the self-normalized estimator has been studied in importance sampling (e.g., Hesterberg 1988, Owen 2000), but the paper does not explicitly discuss how their application to BLBF differs from prior uses. A more detailed comparison would clarify the contribution. Additionally, the empirical section could address scalability to larger datasets (e.g., LYRL with 78k test samples) and longer training runs, as runtime comparisons focus on convergence speed rather than absolute scalability. The discussion of variance regularization (Section 7) is insightful, but the paper does not explore whether the self-normalized estimator enables more aggressive regularization strategies.

**Constructive Suggestions:**  
1. **Clarify novelty:** Explicitly contrast the self-normalized estimator’s application to BLBF with its prior use in importance sampling (e.g., "While self-normalized estimators exist in Monte Carlo methods, their use in counterfactual learning for CRM—where propensity weights are learned rather than fixed—is novel").  
2. **Expand empirical scope:** Include a brief analysis of runtime on larger datasets (e.g., LYRL) to address scalability concerns.  
3. **Strengthen related work:** Cite recent NIPS papers on counterfactual learning (e.g., Dudik et al. 2014, Thomas et al. 2015) to better position the contribution.  

**Overall Assessment:**  
The paper addresses a genuine gap in BLBF literature with a theoretically grounded solution that delivers empirical improvements. The propensity overfitting problem is well-justified (Example 1), and the self-normalized estimator’s properties (equivariance, boundedness) directly resolve it. Norm-POEM’s consistent outperformance over POEM (Table 1) and avoidance of propensity overfitting (Table 2) demonstrate clear significance for practitioners in recommendation systems and ad placement. The work advances the state of the art by making counterfactual learning more robust and reliable, particularly for complex hypothesis spaces. While minor clarifications would strengthen the paper, the core contribution is compelling, technically sound, and impactful for the field. I recommend acceptance.
