Summary and relation to prior work: The paper identifies “propensity overfitting” when using the standard inverse propensity scoring (IPS) estimator within counterfactual risk minimization (CRM) for batch learning from logged bandit feedback. It proposes replacing IPS with a self-normalized importance sampling estimator (SNIPS), arguing that SNIPS is bounded, translation-equivariant, and strongly consistent, thereby avoiding degenerate ERM behavior. Building on POEM, the authors instantiate Norm-POEM for structured prediction with exponential-family policies and a CRM-style variance regularizer. Experiments on multi-label classification via supervised→bandit simulation show consistent gains over POEM. The work connects to NIPS literature on importance weighting and domain adaptation (Cortes et al.), off-policy evaluation (Li, Strehl, Thomas), and variance reduction/doubly robust estimators, while highlighting WIS/SNIPS (Kong, Hesterberg) as the central control-variate tool.

Quality: The diagnosis of propensity overfitting is compelling (Example 1) and the use of Ŝ(h) as a diagnostic is sound. The consistency proof for SNIPS and the bounded/equivariant properties are correct and practically important. However, the learning objective relies on an approximate variance for a ratio estimator and lacks a finite-sample generalization bound analogous to the empirical-Bernstein CRM bound used for IPS; this limits the theoretical completeness. Empirically, improvements are consistent but largely on simulated logs; significance tests and error bars are not reported.

Clarity: The paper is clearly written and well organized; the anomaly analysis and translation-invariance discussion are especially lucid. Algorithmic details and optimization choices (L-BFGS) are adequately described.

Originality: While SNIPS is known, applying it as the core estimator within CRM to explicitly eliminate propensity overfitting, and demonstrating its practical effect for structured policies, is a novel and timely contribution. The “propensity overfitting” framing is insightful.

Significance: The estimator swap is simple, robust, and likely to be adopted. If supported with stronger theory and broader baselines, the impact on BLBF practice could be substantial.

Suggestions:
- Provide a finite-sample generalization bound for SNIPS-CRM (e.g., ratio-concentration/Efron–Stein or self-normalized empirical Bernstein).
- Compare against doubly robust learning objectives and clipped-IPS/WIS variants; include DR-CRM baselines.
- Report CIs/significance and sensitivity to M and λ; add ablations on clipping and variance regularization.
- Include at least one real logged dataset with known/estimated propensities; discuss robustness when propensities are misspecified or have limited support.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Clear identification of a real failure mode (propensity overfitting).
- Principled, practical fix with strong intuitive/theoretical support.
- Consistent empirical gains and better diagnostics (Ŝ ≈ 1).
- Good clarity and relevance to NeurIPS audience.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Missing finite-sample theory for the proposed CRM objective.
- Limited empirical scope (simulated logs), no DR baselines, no significance tests.
- Assumes known propensities and full support; limited analysis under misspecification.
