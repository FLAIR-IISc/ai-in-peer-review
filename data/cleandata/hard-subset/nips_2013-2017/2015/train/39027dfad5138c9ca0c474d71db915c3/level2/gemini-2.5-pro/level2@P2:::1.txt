This paper addresses the problem of batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF), a setting of great practical importance for improving interactive systems using historical data. The authors identify a fundamental and previously overlooked problem with the conventional unbiased inverse propensity score (IPS) estimator, which is the cornerstone of most Counterfactual Risk Minimization (CRM) methods. They term this problem "Propensity Overfitting," where a learner with a sufficiently complex hypothesis space can achieve an artificially low estimated risk by manipulating the importance weights, rather than by accurately modeling the underlying loss function. This can lead to degenerate solutions that generalize poorly.

To solve this problem, the paper proposes replacing the standard unbiased estimator with the self-normalized (SN) estimator. This estimator, which normalizes the risk estimate by the sum of the importance weights, is motivated as a form of multiplicative control variate. The authors show that the SN estimator is equivariant to constant shifts in the loss function and that its estimates are always bounded within the range of the observed losses, properties not held by the standard estimator. These properties provably prevent the kind of catastrophic overfitting identified by the authors. Based on this improved estimator, the paper develops a new learning algorithm, Norm-POEM, which integrates the SN estimator and an appropriate variance regularizer into the CRM framework for learning structured prediction models. Extensive experiments on multi-label classification tasks demonstrate that Norm-POEM consistently and significantly outperforms its predecessor (POEM) which uses the standard estimator, and directly confirms that the SN estimator successfully mitigates propensity overfitting.

**Strengths:**

1.  **High Significance and Impact:** The paper's primary contribution is the identification of propensity overfitting. This is a subtle but severe failure mode for a class of methods that is foundational to the field of off-policy learning and evaluation. Given the increasing use of BLBF in industrial applications like recommendation and advertising, this finding is a crucial contribution that serves as an important warning and corrective for the community. The problem is not just a theoretical curiosity; the experiments show it leads to catastrophic failures on real datasets.

2.  **Elegant and Principled Solution:** The proposed solution—using the self-normalized estimator—is simple, well-motivated, and highly effective. By drawing a connection to control variates from the Monte Carlo literature, the authors provide a principled justification for their choice. The demonstration that this estimator resolves the key theoretical anomalies of the standard estimator (lack of equivariance and unboundedness) is compelling.

3.  **Exceptional Clarity and Presentation:** The paper is extremely well-written and easy to follow. The "Propensity Overfitting" problem is introduced with a simple yet powerful illustrative example in Section 4 that makes the core issue immediately and intuitively clear. The logical flow from identifying the problem, to explaining its cause, proposing a solution, and empirically verifying the solution is flawless.

4.  **Strong and Convincing Empirical Evaluation:** The experiments are well-designed and convincingly support all of the paper's claims. By using the standard "Supervised -> Bandit" evaluation protocol, the authors provide a controlled and reproducible comparison. The results not only show that Norm-POEM achieves substantially better generalization performance (Table 1, Figure 1) but also include diagnostic experiments (Table 2) that directly verify the theoretical claims: POEM suffers from biased propensity sums and is sensitive to loss translation, while Norm-POEM is robust to both. This level of thoroughness is commendable.

**Weaknesses:**

This is a very strong paper and it is difficult to find significant weaknesses. The following are minor points for potential improvement:

1.  **Computational Aspects:** The paper notes that the variance estimator for Norm-POEM (Eq. 8) does not admit stochastic optimization and that they use L-BFGS. While they surprisingly find that Norm-POEM converges faster in practice, a slightly more detailed discussion of the optimization landscape or computational trade-offs for very large-scale problems could be beneficial. For instance, is the batch nature of the objective a potential limitation compared to stochastic methods, despite the faster convergence observed here?

2.  **Variance Estimator:** The variance estimator in Eq. 8 is a standard delta-method approximation for a ratio estimator. While perfectly reasonable, it is still an approximation. A brief mention of this or a pointer to literature discussing the quality of this approximation would add a small amount of extra rigor.

**Summary of Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   **Pro:** The paper identifies and names a critical, previously unarticulated failure mode in a widely-used learning setting (BLBF).
*   **Pro:** It proposes an elegant, simple, and principled solution that is shown to be highly effective.
*   **Pro:** The claims are backed by exceptionally clear writing, strong theoretical motivation, and thorough, convincing experiments.
*   **Pro:** The work is of high significance and is likely to have a major impact on how researchers and practitioners approach off-policy and counterfactual learning problems.

*   **Con:** The core statistical tool (the self-normalized estimator) is not new, though its application to solve this specific learning problem is highly novel and insightful.

In conclusion, this is an excellent paper that makes a clear, important, and well-supported contribution. It addresses a fundamental problem in a practical learning setting and provides a robust solution. I strongly recommend acceptance.
