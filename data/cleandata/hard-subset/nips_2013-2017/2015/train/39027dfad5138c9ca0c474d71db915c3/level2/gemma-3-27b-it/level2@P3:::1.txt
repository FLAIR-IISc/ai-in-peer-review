This paper addresses a critical issue in batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF): propensity overfitting in the commonly used counterfactual risk estimator. The authors convincingly demonstrate that this estimator can lead to severely biased risk estimates and propose a self-normalized estimator as a remedy. The theoretical justification and empirical validation on several multi-label classification problems are strong, demonstrating consistent outperformance over the conventional estimator.

The paper is well-structured and clearly written. The introduction provides a compelling motivation for the work, contextualizing BLBF within broader areas like causal inference and off-policy evaluation. The related work section is comprehensive and appropriately cites relevant literature. The presentation of the theoretical analysis, particularly Theorem 1 and 2, is clear and concise. The experimental setup closely mirrors previous work (POEM), allowing for a fair comparison and facilitating reproducibility. The results are thoroughly presented with tables and figures, and the analysis is insightful. The discussion of computational efficiency is also a valuable addition.

A significant strength is the identification and articulation of "Propensity Overfitting" – a novel form of overfitting specific to BLBF that is distinct from traditional overfitting in supervised learning. The example provided (Example 1) beautifully illustrates this phenomenon.  The proposed self-normalized estimator directly addresses this issue by providing an equivariant estimator. The empirical results strongly support the theoretical claims, consistently demonstrating the superiority of Norm-POEM, especially in scenarios where the logging policy is suboptimal. The detailed analysis of Ŝ(h) in Table 2 further corroborates the claim that Norm-POEM mitigates propensity overfitting.

While the paper is strong overall, a few areas could be improved. First, the discussion of the limitations of the self-normalized estimator is somewhat brief. While Theorem 2 establishes consistency, a more detailed discussion of potential scenarios where the estimator might struggle (e.g., extreme weight imbalances, high-dimensional feature spaces) would be beneficial. Second, the explanation of why Norm-POEM converges faster than POEM despite the increased per-iteration cost could be expanded. While the authors mention the shorter ‖w‖, further insight into the optimization landscape would be valuable. Third, the reliance on L-BFGS, while justified, might limit scalability. A brief discussion of alternative optimization strategies, even if preliminary, could strengthen the paper.  Finally, while the choice of datasets is reasonable, exploring datasets with varying degrees of inherent bias in the logging policy could provide further evidence of the robustness of Norm-POEM.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** The identification of propensity overfitting and the proposed self-normalized estimator are novel contributions.
*   **Significance:** Addressing the limitations of counterfactual risk estimation is crucial for practical applications of BLBF in areas like online advertising and recommendation systems.
*   **Technical Soundness:** The theoretical analysis is rigorous, and the empirical results are compelling.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to understand.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The limitations of the self-normalized estimator could be explored in more detail.
*   Scalability concerns related to the optimization algorithm could be addressed.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a high-quality paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of batch learning from logged bandit feedback. The identified problem of propensity overfitting is important, and the proposed solution is both theoretically sound and empirically effective. The paper is well-written, clear, and thoroughly evaluated. Despite a few minor areas for improvement, this work is of high enough quality to be accepted to NIPS.
