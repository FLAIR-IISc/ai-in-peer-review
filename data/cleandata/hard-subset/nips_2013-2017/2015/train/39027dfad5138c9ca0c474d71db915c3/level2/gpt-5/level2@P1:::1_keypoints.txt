- Paper studies batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF) under CRM, identifying propensity overfitting when using IPS for ERM.
- Shows IPS-based ERM can give degenerate/unbounded risk estimates and be non-equivariant to loss shifts, causing learners to exploit weight fluctuations.
- Proposes SN-IPS (self-normalized importance sampling) as a remedy, normalizing by sum of weights to control fluctuations.
- Proves consistency for SN-IPS and highlights boundedness and equivariance properties that mitigate identified degeneracies.
- Instantiates Norm-POEM, a CRM algorithm using SN-IPS and an approximate variance term, with tractable gradients for exponential-family models.
- Empirical results on simulated supervised→bandit multi-label benchmarks show gains over POEM and robustness to loss translation.
- Diagnostics (Ŝ(h)) indicate SN-IPS controls propensity overfitting in experiments.
- Situates work within counterfactual evaluation, importance weighting, and variance reduction literature; notes SN-IPS is well-known in Monte Carlo/OPE.
- Conceptual contribution is articulating propensity overfitting in BLBF and applying SN-IPS within CRM.
- Strength: clear identification and illustrative example of propensity overfitting with a compelling diagnostic.
- Strength: simple principled fix using a classic estimator that directly addresses weight fluctuation and equivariance issues.
- Strength: solid empirical evidence across datasets and training sizes, plus practical algorithmic implementation and competitive runtime.
- Weakness: limited theoretical novelty—SN-IPS is standard and consistency result follows from the law of large numbers.
- Weakness: variance term is an approximation for a ratio estimator; no finite-sample CRM-style bound justifying the exact objective.
- Weakness: baselines are limited—missing comparisons to doubly robust, weighted DR, switch estimators, and stronger clipping/regularization.
- Weakness: experiments use simulated logs from supervised data only, limited loss types (Hamming) and model families (independent-label CRF).
- Weakness: limited analysis of sensitivity to clipping M, regularization λ, and to estimated rather than known propensities.
- Quality: technically sound and pragmatically useful, but main gap is lack of finite-sample bound and stronger baselines.
- Clarity: well written and organized; propensity overfitting example, diagnostic, and methodology are easy to follow.
- Originality: moderate—estimator not new, but framing of propensity overfitting and empirical demonstration are valuable.
- Significance: potentially important for BLBF practice; simple change with empirical benefits, but stronger theory/experiments would increase impact.
- Key questions: request for finite-sample/generalization bounds, comparisons to DR/switch estimators, sensitivity analyses for M and λ, effects of estimated propensities, and combining SN-IPS with DR.
- Pros for acceptance: identifies important failure mode, offers principled remedy with bounded/equivariant properties, consistent empirical gains, and practical relevance.
- Cons against acceptance: limited new theory, missing stronger baselines, simulated-only evaluation, and approximate variance justification.
- Suggestions: add finite-sample or concentration analysis, include DR/switch baselines (and WDR), report standard errors and hyperparameter sensitivity, test estimated propensities and larger structured outputs, and provide Norm-POEM pseudocode and code release.
- Overall recommendation: borderline accept — clear diagnosis and effective remedy, but needs stronger theory and baselines to be unequivocal.
