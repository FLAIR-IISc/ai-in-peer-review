- Identifies “propensity overfitting” in IPS-based CRM and proposes replacing IPS with SNIPS (Norm-POEM) for structured policies with a variance regularizer.  
- Connects to prior work on importance weighting, domain adaptation, off-policy evaluation, and WIS/SNIPS as a control-variate tool.  
- Diagnosis (Example 1) of propensity overfitting is compelling and the Ŝ(h) diagnostic is sound.  
- Consistency proof for SNIPS and its bounded/translation-equivariant properties are correct and practically important.  
- Learning objective relies on an approximate variance for a ratio estimator and lacks a finite-sample generalization bound (limits theoretical completeness).  
- Empirical gains are consistent but mostly on simulated logs; no significance tests or error bars reported.  
- Paper is clearly written, well organized; anomaly analysis and translation-invariance discussion are lucid; algorithmic/optimization details are adequate.  
- Although SNIPS itself is known, using it as the core CRM estimator to eliminate propensity overfitting for structured policies is novel and insightful.  
- The estimator swap is simple, robust, and likely to be adopted; stronger theory and broader baselines would increase impact.  
- Suggestion: provide a finite-sample generalization bound for SNIPS-CRM (e.g., ratio-concentration/Efron–Stein or self-normalized empirical Bernstein).  
- Suggestion: compare against doubly robust objectives and clipped-IPS/WIS variants and include DR-CRM baselines.  
- Suggestion: report confidence intervals/significance and sensitivity to M and λ; add ablations on clipping and variance regularization.  
- Suggestion: include at least one real logged dataset and discuss robustness when propensities are misspecified or have limited support.  
- Arguments for acceptance: clear identification of a real failure mode, principled/practical fix with theoretical intuition, consistent empirical gains, improved diagnostics, and good clarity.  
- Arguments against acceptance: missing finite-sample theory, limited empirical scope (simulated logs), no DR baselines or significance tests, and strong assumptions about known propensities/full support.
