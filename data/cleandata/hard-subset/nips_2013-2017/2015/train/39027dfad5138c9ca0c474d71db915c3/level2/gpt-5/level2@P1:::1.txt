Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper addresses batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF) within the Counterfactual Risk Minimization (CRM) framework. It identifies a failure mode of the standard inverse propensity score (IPS) estimator when used for empirical risk minimization over rich hypothesis spaces: propensity overfitting. The authors show through analysis and examples that IPS-based ERM can produce degenerate risk estimates (unbounded, non-equivariant to loss translations), leading the learner to exploit weight fluctuations rather than genuine low loss. As a remedy, they propose using the self-normalized (weighted) importance sampling estimator (SN-IPS), a classic multiplicative control variate that normalizes by the sum of importance weights. They prove strong consistency and emphasize two desirable properties—boundedness within the loss range and equivariance to additive loss shifts—that directly mitigate the identified degeneracies. They instantiate CRM with SN-IPS and an approximate variance estimate to produce Norm-POEM, a structured prediction algorithm analogous to POEM but with SN-IPS. Experiments on multi-label classification benchmarks using the supervised→bandit protocol show consistent gains over POEM, improved robustness to loss translation, and diagnostics indicating control of propensity overfitting.

The work sits squarely within counterfactual evaluation/learning, importance weighting, and variance reduction (e.g., Dudík et al. doubly robust, Cortes et al. bounds for importance weighting, Thomas et al. high-confidence OPE). The self-normalized estimator (a.k.a. weighted IPS) is well-known in Monte Carlo and OPE (Hesterberg; Kong; Owen), but its use as the core risk estimator within a learning objective in BLBF is less explored. The paper’s conceptual contribution is the identification and analysis of propensity overfitting and the CRM-based instantiation with SN-IPS.

Strengths:
- Clear identification and illustration (Example 1) of propensity overfitting as a distinct degeneracy from conventional loss overfitting. The diagnosis via Ŝ(h) is compelling.
- Simple, principled fix using a classical technique (self-normalization) that directly addresses the root cause: weight fluctuations and lack of equivariance. The bounded/equivariant properties are highly relevant for learning objectives.
- Solid empirical evidence across several datasets and training sizes. The invariance to loss translation and the Ŝ(h) diagnostic strongly support the claims.
- Practical algorithmic instantiation (Norm-POEM) with tractable gradients for exponential-family models and competitive runtime.
- The study is well-situated in prior work on BLBF, importance sampling, and control variates.

Weaknesses:
- Theoretical novelty is limited. SN-IPS is a standard estimator; the paper lacks new finite-sample generalization/error bounds specialized to SN-IPS within CRM. The consistency result is straightforward from the law of large numbers.
- The variance term used in CRM is an approximation for a ratio estimator. The paper does not provide a finite-sample bound that justifies the exact CRM objective with SN-IPS, nor conditions under which the approximation is reliable in learning (beyond OPE settings).
- Baselines are limited. Missing comparisons to doubly robust (DR), weighted/normalized DR, switch estimators, and stronger clipping/regularization strategies that are common in OPE/BLBF. This makes it harder to judge where SN-IPS stands among modern alternatives.
- Experiments are simulated from supervised data; no real logged-bandit datasets. Only Hamming loss and independent-label CRF modeling are considered; no structured dependencies beyond linear CRF-like features.
- Limited analysis of sensitivity to clipping M and regularization λ, and of robustness when propensities must be estimated (common in practice).

Quality:
Technically sound overall, with a compelling identification of a real learning pathology and a pragmatic solution. The lack of a finite-sample CRM-style bound tailored to the SN estimator and absence of stronger baselines is the main gap.

Clarity:
The paper is clearly written and well organized. The propensity overfitting example, Ŝ(h) diagnostic, and experimental methodology are easy to follow. It would help to include a succinct formal definition of propensity overfitting and a brief algorithmic summary of Norm-POEM.

Originality:
Moderate. The estimator itself is not new, but the articulation of propensity overfitting in BLBF/CRM and the empirical demonstration that SN-IPS alleviates it are valuable.

Significance:
Potentially significant for BLBF practice. Many systems rely on IPS-based learning, and the proposed change is simple, principled, and empirically effective. Stronger theoretical and empirical comparisons would further increase impact.

Questions for the authors:
- Can you provide a finite-sample generalization bound for the SN-IPS-based CRM objective, or at least a concentration result that motivates the specific variance penalty used?
- How does Norm-POEM compare to doubly robust and switch-DR estimators, and to clipped IPS with stronger variance regularization, both in accuracy and stability?
- How sensitive is performance to M and λ? Please include sensitivity plots and guidelines for selection.
- How does performance change when propensities are estimated rather than known (e.g., via logged stochastic policies with unknown parameters)?
- Can SN-IPS be combined with DR (i.e., weighted DR), and if so, does it further reduce propensity overfitting?

Pros (arguments for acceptance):
- Identifies an important and previously underappreciated failure mode for IPS-based learning.
- Offers a simple, well-grounded solution with clear theoretical and practical benefits (boundedness, equivariance).
- Consistent empirical gains and informative diagnostics.
- Relevance to real-world BLBF systems and general applicability.

Cons (arguments against acceptance):
- Limited theoretical advances beyond known properties of SN-IPS; no new finite-sample CRM-style bounds.
- Missing comparisons to leading DR/switch estimators.
- Evaluation limited to simulated logs and one family of tasks; no real-world datasets.
- Variance estimator is approximate; justification in the learning context is limited.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Add finite-sample analysis or at least a high-level concentration argument for the SN-IPS CRM objective.
- Include comparisons to DR and switch-DR (and possibly WDR) with/without normalization.
- Report standard errors/confidence intervals and hyperparameter sensitivity.
- Explore scenarios with estimated propensities and larger/structured output spaces.
- Provide implementation details and pseudo-code for Norm-POEM; release code as claimed.

Overall recommendation:
Borderline accept. The paper presents a clear diagnosis and a practical, effective remedy with strong empirical support. Strengthening theory and baselines would make the case unequivocal.
