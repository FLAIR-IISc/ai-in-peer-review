This paper addresses a critical issue in batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF): propensity overfitting in the commonly used counterfactual risk estimator. The authors convincingly argue that this estimator can lead to severely biased risk estimates, particularly with complex hypothesis spaces. They propose a self-normalized estimator as a remedy and demonstrate its effectiveness through both theoretical analysis and empirical evaluation on multi-label classification problems.

**Summary of Strengths:**

* **Important Problem:** The identified problem of propensity overfitting is novel and potentially significant for the BLBF community. Existing methods relying on the conventional estimator could be unknowingly producing unreliable results.
* **Clear Explanation:** The paper clearly explains the issue of propensity overfitting, using an illustrative example to highlight the problem and making the intuition accessible.
* **Sound Theoretical Justification:** The introduction of the self-normalized estimator is motivated by a strong theoretical argument, drawing connections to control variates and importance sampling literature. The consistency proof for the estimator is a valuable addition.
* **Empirical Validation:** The experimental results consistently demonstrate the superiority of Norm-POEM (the algorithm based on the self-normalized estimator) over the baseline POEM algorithm across multiple datasets. The ablation studies regarding the variance regularization and runtime analysis further strengthen the findings.
* **Well-Written and Organized:** The paper is generally well-written and logically organized, making it easy to follow the authors' reasoning and understand their contributions.  The connections to related work are clearly delineated.

**Summary of Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Scope of Experiments:** While the experiments are well-executed, they primarily focus on multi-label classification. It would be beneficial to see results on other types of problems (e.g., regression, ranking) to assess the generalizability of the proposed approach.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper mentions hyperparameter calibration, but provides limited details on the sensitivity of the results to different hyperparameter settings. A more thorough analysis of hyperparameter robustness would be valuable.
* **Computational Complexity:** Although the authors state that Norm-POEM's runtime is comparable to POEM, the discussion of the computational cost of the variance estimation could be more detailed. A more precise analysis of the computational complexity would be helpful.
* **Missing Connection to Existing Work on Robust Estimation:** The paper touches on the use of control variates, but doesn't deeply connect to the broader literature on robust estimation techniques and their application to importance sampling.  Exploring these connections further could enrich the theoretical foundation.



**Detailed Comments:**

* **Introduction:** The introduction does a good job of motivating the problem and setting the stage for the paper. It could be strengthened by briefly mentioning the potential impact of propensity overfitting on real-world applications (e.g., ad placement).
* **Section 3:** The recap of the CRM principle is helpful for readers unfamiliar with the approach.
* **Section 4:** The example illustrating propensity overfitting is excellent. It effectively demonstrates the problem in a concrete setting.
* **Section 5:** The discussion of control variates and the derivation of the self-normalized estimator are well-presented.
* **Section 6:** The description of Norm-POEM is clear and concise.
* **Section 7:** The experimental results are convincing. It would be helpful to include error bars on the performance plots to better visualize the variability of the results.  The table comparing Ŝ(ĥ) is particularly insightful.
* **Related Work:** The related work section is comprehensive.


**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper addresses a significant and previously unrecognized problem in the BLBF literature.
* The proposed solution (self-normalized estimator) is theoretically sound and empirically validated.
* The paper is well-written, clear, and logically organized.
* The experimental results are convincing and demonstrate a clear improvement over existing methods.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

* The experiments are limited in scope and do not cover a wide range of problem domains.
* The analysis of hyperparameter sensitivity and computational complexity could be more thorough.
* While the theoretical contribution is significant, the paper could benefit from a deeper connection to existing work on robust estimation.

**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a significant contribution to the field of batch learning from logged bandit feedback. The identified problem of propensity overfitting is important, and the proposed solution is both theoretically sound and empirically effective. While there are some minor weaknesses, they do not detract from the overall quality of the work. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions, specifically addressing the comments regarding the scope of experiments, hyperparameter sensitivity, and computational complexity.
