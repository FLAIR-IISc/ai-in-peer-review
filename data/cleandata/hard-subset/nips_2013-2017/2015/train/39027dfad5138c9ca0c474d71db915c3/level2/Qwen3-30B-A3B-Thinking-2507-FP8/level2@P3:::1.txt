

The paper identifies a significant issue with the conventional counterfactual risk estimator used in batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF), which the authors term "propensity overfitting." This problem manifests when the standard unbiased importance sampling estimator (Equation 2) is used for empirical risk minimization, leading to degenerate risk estimates that can be arbitrarily lower than the true risk. The paper proposes a self-normalized risk estimator (Equation 7) that resolves these anomalies, showing it is equivariant and bounded within the range of the loss function—properties the conventional estimator lacks. This leads to the Norm-POEM learning algorithm, which replaces the conventional estimator with the self-normalized version while maintaining the CRM principle. The paper provides theoretical guarantees (Theorem 2) showing the self-normalized estimator is strongly consistent, and empirically demonstrates Norm-POEM's consistent outperformance over the conventional approach across multiple multi-label classification datasets.

The work is well-motivated and addresses a genuine gap in the literature. Prior work on BLBF has primarily focused on variance reduction techniques like clipping (R̂M(h)), but the paper reveals a fundamental flaw in the estimator's behavior that these techniques do not address. The identification of propensity overfitting as a distinct phenomenon from traditional loss overfitting is a significant theoretical contribution. The self-normalized estimator builds on control variate methods from importance sampling literature (citing Hesterberg [19]), but the application to BLBF and the demonstration of its superiority in this context is novel.

The theoretical analysis is sound, with clear theorems and proofs. The paper effectively demonstrates how the self-normalized estimator resolves the key anomalies of the conventional estimator through both theoretical arguments and empirical examples (Example 1). The empirical evaluation is thorough, using standard BLBF evaluation methodology from prior work [1] with multiple datasets (Scene, Yeast, TMC, LYRL). The results consistently show Norm-POEM outperforming POEM (the conventional approach) in terms of generalization performance, and the paper provides compelling evidence that this improvement stems from better control of propensity overfitting (Table 2).

The paper's main weakness is that the proposed solution requires a slightly more complex computation than the conventional estimator (Equation 8 for variance estimation), though the authors demonstrate that this is offset by faster convergence in practice. The paper does not fully explore how the self-normalized estimator might interact with other variance reduction techniques, though it mentions thresholding as a complementary approach. The paper's focus on structured output prediction (using CRF-like hypothesis spaces) is reasonable given the context of BLBF applications, but it would be valuable to see if the approach extends to other hypothesis spaces.

The significance of this work is substantial for the practical application of BLBF in real-world systems like ad placement and recommendation engines. The propensity overfitting problem can lead to catastrophic failures in practice (e.g., POEM performing worse than random guessing on TMC, as shown in Table 2), so the proposed solution has clear practical implications. The paper's theoretical contribution to understanding estimator behavior in counterfactual learning is also significant.

The paper is well-written and clearly organized. The connection to related work is appropriate, with careful differentiation from previous approaches to counterfactual estimation. The empirical methodology is standard for the field and properly validated with multiple datasets and runs.

The paper makes a strong contribution to the field of counterfactual learning. It identifies a previously unrecognized problem, provides a theoretically sound solution, and demonstrates empirical improvements across multiple benchmarks. The self-normalized estimator is a simple yet powerful modification that resolves a fundamental issue in BLBF, with clear implications for real-world applications. The work is technically sound, well-justified, and makes a clear contribution to the state of the art.

Arguments for acceptance: The paper identifies a novel problem (propensity overfitting) that has not been previously addressed in BLBF literature, provides a theoretically sound solution, and demonstrates consistent empirical improvements. The problem is significant for real-world applications, and the solution is elegant and practical.

Arguments against acceptance: The paper is somewhat specialized to the BLBF setting, which may limit its broader appeal. However, BLBF is a well-established area with important applications, and the paper makes a clear contribution to this specific domain. The computational cost increase is minimal and offset by faster convergence in practice.

The paper meets all four criteria for quality: it is technically sound with proper theoretical analysis and experimental validation; it is clearly written with good organization; it is original in identifying propensity overfitting and proposing the self-normalized estimator; and it is significant for both theoretical understanding and practical application of counterfactual learning.
