Summary
The paper studies batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF) within the Counterfactual Risk Minimization (CRM) framework. It identifies a failure mode of the conventional inverse propensity scoring (IPS) estimator used in prior CRM work: propensity overfitting, whereby the empirical risk can be driven to pathological values by manipulating the sum of importance weights. The paper proposes replacing IPS with the self-normalized importance sampling estimator (SNIPS), leveraging multiplicative control variates so that the empirical risk becomes a convex combination of observed losses, is equivariant to additive shifts of the loss, and is bounded within the loss range. A strong consistency result is given. Building on this, the paper derives Norm-POEM, a CRM-style learning algorithm over exponential-family policies (CRF-style linear rules), with an approximate variance penalty for the ratio estimator. Experiments on multi-label classification datasets created via supervised-to-bandit simulation show consistent improvements over POEM (the prior IPS-based CRM method), robustness to loss translations, better Ŝ(h) diagnostics, and competitive runtime.

Relation to prior work
The work sits at the intersection of counterfactual learning, domain adaptation via importance weighting, and off-policy evaluation (OPE). It builds directly on CRM/POEM [1] and standard importance weighting bounds [8,13], and connects to OPE estimators including IPS and variance-reduced variants [5,6,11,15]. The proposed estimator is well known as weighted/self-normalized IS [12,19,22,23]; the contribution here is diagnosing “propensity overfitting” in learning and integrating SNIPS into CRM with an explicit variance penalty and empirical study on structured prediction. The work is relevant to recent high-confidence OPE [14] and truncation/clipping [15], and to multiplicative control variates used in RL and finance [17,18].

Strengths
- Clear identification and articulation of a practically important failure mode (propensity overfitting) for IPS-based learning, with intuitive examples showing non-equivariance and unbounded overfitting.
- Use of self-normalization addresses these anomalies: boundedness within loss range, equivariance to additive shifts, and strong consistency (Theorem 2).
- Seamless integration into a CRM objective for exponential-family policies, including a practical approximate variance estimator for the ratio estimator.
- Empirical evidence across multiple datasets that SNIPS-based learning improves generalization, stabilizes Ŝ(h) near 1, and is robust to loss translation; gains persist across dataset sizes and replay counts.
- Useful diagnostic (Ŝ(h)) and qualitative runtime observations indicating fewer optimization pathologies.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Theory: The paper stops short of a finite-sample generalization bound for SNIPS-based CRM analogous to Theorem 1 for IPS/clipped IPS. Since the variance penalty uses an asymptotic approximation for a ratio estimator, a finite-sample concentration result (e.g., via Fieller-type arguments, delta method with empirical Bernstein terms, or ratio concentration in Owen [21]) would strengthen the claims. Recommended as an essential addition if feasible within space.
- Baselines: While IPS/clipped IPS comparisons are thorough, comparisons to doubly robust estimators for learning/evaluation [6] are missing. Including DR and clipped-DR baselines (and, if space allows, a self-normalized or regression-control-variates variant) would provide a stronger empirical case. This is an important but feasible addition.
- Scope of data: Results rely on supervised-to-bandit simulation with stochastic CRF logging. Although common in this area, at least one experiment with a non-uniform logging policy and more extreme propensities (or a semi-synthetic setup controlling support and overlap) would better probe robustness; a small-scale real logged dataset, if available, would further enhance significance. Optional but valuable.
- Optimization and scalability: The objective becomes non-convex and not amenable to stochastic optimization due to the ratio structure. More detail on initialization, convergence criteria, and scaling with |Y| would help. An ablation on sensitivity to λ and M (beyond reporting that Norm-IPS benefits from regularization) would aid reproducibility. Optional clarifications.
- Claims about “resolving all problems” could be tempered. Self-normalization does not eliminate variance blow-up under poor overlap; clipping and careful model selection remain necessary.

Quality
Technical claims are largely sound and appropriately supported. The strong consistency argument is correct. The diagnostic of Ŝ(h) and the discussion of equivariance and boundedness are precise and useful. The main missing piece is a finite-sample bound tailored to the ratio estimator within CRM; absent that, the variance penalty rests on asymptotics.

Clarity
The paper is well written and well organized. The propensity overfitting example is instructive. The variance approximation (Eq. 8) is appropriately cited; a brief derivation sketch or remark on conditions for its reliability (e.g., existence of second moments and weight clipping) would further clarify applicability.

Originality
Self-normalized IS is classical, but its careful diagnosis-driven use in counterfactual learning to avoid propensity overfitting, integrated into a CRM objective for structured prediction, is a novel and meaningful contribution. The identification and analysis of propensity overfitting in learning (as distinct from evaluation) adds conceptual value.

Significance
BLBF is central to recommender systems, ad placement, and search, and CRM-style methods are increasingly deployed. A simple, drop-in change to the estimator that improves robustness and performance is likely to influence practice and subsequent research. The contribution is incremental in estimator novelty but significant in impact and insight.

Arguments for acceptance
- Identifies and addresses a real and underappreciated failure mode in IPS-based learning.
- Provides a principled, simple fix with strong empirical benefits and better behavior under loss translations.
- Fits cleanly into established CRM methodology, making adoption easy.

Arguments against acceptance
- No finite-sample generalization bound for the proposed objective; reliance on asymptotic variance approximations.
- Missing comparisons to DR-style baselines.
- Evaluation limited to simulated bandit feedback.

Recommendations to improve the paper
- Add a finite-sample generalization bound for SNIPS-based CRM (even a conservative one).
- Include DR (and clipped-DR) baselines for both evaluation and learning.
- Provide sensitivity analyses for λ and M, and a brief discussion of initialization and convergence.
- Add experiments with more challenging logging policies (e.g., heavy-tailed propensities) and, if possible, a small real logged dataset.
- Temper absolute claims and highlight remaining limitations (support mismatch, residual variance, bias from normalization and clipping).
