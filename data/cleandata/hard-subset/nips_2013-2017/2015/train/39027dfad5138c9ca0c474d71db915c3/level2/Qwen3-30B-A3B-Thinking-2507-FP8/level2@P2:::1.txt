

This paper identifies a previously overlooked problem in batch learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF) called "propensity overfitting" and proposes a self-normalized risk estimator to address it. The authors demonstrate that the conventional inverse propensity score estimator used in BLBF can produce severely biased risk estimates due to overfitting not related to the loss function but to the propensity weights. They introduce a self-normalized estimator that resolves this issue by making the risk estimate equivariant to loss transformations and bounded within the loss range. The authors derive a new learning algorithm, Norm-POEM, based on this estimator and show through theoretical analysis and empirical evaluation that it outperforms existing methods.

The paper's main strength is its clear identification and rigorous characterization of propensity overfitting, a problem that has been overlooked in the BLBF literature. The authors provide a compelling example demonstrating how the conventional estimator can produce risk estimates arbitrarily lower than the true minimum risk, which is fundamentally different from standard overfitting in supervised learning. The theoretical justification for the self-normalized estimator is sound, with a clear proof of consistency and demonstration of how it avoids the key anomalies of the conventional estimator. The empirical evaluation is thorough, showing consistent improvements across multiple datasets and demonstrating that Norm-POEM maintains good performance even when the loss function is translated.

However, the paper has several weaknesses that need addressing. First, the significance of propensity overfitting in practical settings is not sufficiently established. While the example is theoretically compelling, the authors don't provide evidence that this problem commonly occurs in real-world applications of BLBF. The empirical results show consistent improvements, but it's unclear whether these improvements are primarily due to avoiding propensity overfitting or other factors. The paper would benefit from more detailed analysis of when propensity overfitting occurs and how often it affects learning performance in realistic scenarios.

Second, the connection to existing variance reduction techniques in importance sampling is not sufficiently developed. The paper mentions control variates but doesn't clearly position the self-normalized estimator relative to other approaches like doubly robust estimators or clipped importance sampling. A more comprehensive comparison with these methods would strengthen the paper's contribution and help readers understand the novelty of the proposed approach.

Third, the theoretical analysis of the self-normalized estimator's properties could be more complete. While the paper shows consistency, it doesn't provide a generalization error bound for the Norm-POEM algorithm that would help understand its performance guarantees. The authors mention that the self-normalized estimator is "strongly consistent" but don't provide a rate of convergence or discuss how this affects the sample complexity of learning.

The paper is generally well-written and clearly structured, with the example in Section 4 being particularly effective at illustrating the problem. However, the related work section could be strengthened by more thoroughly discussing how the proposed estimator relates to existing work on variance reduction in importance sampling. The authors cite relevant papers but don't fully engage with how their approach differs from or complements existing techniques.

The significance of the paper is moderate. While propensity overfitting is a genuine problem that affects the reliability of BLBF methods, the proposed solution appears to be a specific application of a well-known technique (self-normalized importance sampling) to a new context. The authors demonstrate that this application leads to improved empirical performance, but the paper doesn't convincingly argue that this is a major advance over existing approaches. The empirical results are promising but don't show dramatic improvements that would suggest this is a breakthrough contribution to the field.

In conclusion, this is a solid paper that identifies a genuine problem in BLBF and proposes a well-motivated solution. The theoretical analysis is sound, and the empirical results are consistent. However, the paper would benefit from a more thorough discussion of the practical significance of propensity overfitting, a clearer connection to existing variance reduction techniques, and a more comprehensive theoretical analysis of the proposed estimator. With these improvements, the paper would make a stronger contribution to the field of counterfactual learning. As it stands, it represents a useful incremental advance that should be accepted for presentation at NIPS, but it does not represent a major breakthrough.
