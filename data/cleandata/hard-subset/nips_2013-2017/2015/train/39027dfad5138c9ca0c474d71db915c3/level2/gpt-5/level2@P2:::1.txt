Summary
The paper addresses learning from logged bandit feedback (BLBF) via the Counterfactual Risk Minimization (CRM) principle. It identifies a fundamental failure mode of the conventional inverse-propensity-scored (IPS) risk estimator used in prior BLBF works: propensity overfitting, whereby, due to instability in importance weights, empirical risk can be driven to degenerate values unrelated to true risk and sensitive to loss translations. The authors propose replacing IPS with the self-normalized IPS (SNIPS) estimator (a multiplicative control variate/ratio estimator). They argue SNIPS eliminates key anomalies (equivariance to additive loss shifts, boundedness of the finite-sample risk) and is strongly consistent. Building on this, they introduce Norm-POEM, which plugs SNIPS and an approximate variance estimator into the CRM objective for exponential-family policies over structured outputs. Experiments on multi-label classification (supervised→bandit simulation) show consistent improvements over POEM, reduced propensity overfitting (measured by the weight-sum diagnostic), and competitive training time.

Relation to prior work
- The work builds on CRM and POEM (Swaminathan & Joachims, ICML/NIPS era) and off-policy evaluation (OPE) literature (e.g., Dudík et al. doubly robust; Thomas et al. high-confidence OPE).
- The proposed estimator is the well-known weighted/self-normalized importance sampling (WIS/SNIPS) from Monte Carlo and RL (e.g., Trotter & Tukey; Kong; Hesterberg; also standard in off-policy RL as weighted IS by Precup, Sutton, and Singh). The paper cites some Monte Carlo sources and TRPO but could more clearly connect to the RL/OPE WIS lineage and its bias/variance properties.
- Using SNIPS in counterfactual evaluation is known; the novelty here is diagnosing propensity overfitting in learning and demonstrating that SNIPS alleviates it within a CRM training objective, together with an empirical variance regularizer and an implementable algorithm for structured prediction.

Quality
- Theory: The diagnosis of propensity overfitting is clear and compelling (Example 1), and the SNIPS properties (boundedness, equivariance, and strong consistency) are correct. However, the paper does not provide a finite-sample generalization bound for the SNIPS-based CRM objective analogous to the earlier CRM bounds for IPS; the variance plug-in (Normal/delta method) is heuristic. Since CRM is motivated by bounds, the lack of a matching bound for the ratio estimator is a gap.
- Algorithms: The objective, gradients, and use of L-BFGS are reasonable. The need for clipping remains, and the paper is honest about trade-offs and computational consequences.
- Experiments: The supervised→bandit protocol is standard and allows controlled comparisons. Results consistently favor Norm-POEM over POEM, including translation invariance tests and diagnostics via the sum of weights. Still, evaluation is limited to simulated bandit feedback; comparison baselines are too narrow (no doubly robust (DR) or clipped/normalized DR, no WIS-without-CRM, no alternative variance regularizers, no ESS diagnostics). Hyperparameter sensitivity is only briefly discussed.

Clarity
- The paper is generally well written. The exposition of propensity overfitting and the role of Ŝ(h) is particularly clear. The derivation of the variance estimator could benefit from more detail or an explicit reference for the exact form used, and a short algorithmic summary (e.g., pseudocode) would help reproducibility.

Originality
- The use of SNIPS itself is not novel; applying it explicitly to counterfactual learning to mitigate propensity overfitting, articulating the pathology, and integrating it into a CRM-style learner for structured prediction is a useful and original contribution. The term “propensity overfitting” and the analysis are compelling.

Significance
- Practical significance is promising: SNIPS yields bounded, translation-invariant risk estimates that are robust in practice, and multi-label experiments show consistent gains. Given the widespread use of IPS-based BLBF, this is likely to influence future work. The absence of a finite-sample bound and stronger baselines tempers the theoretical and empirical conclusiveness, but the contribution is still meaningful.

Pros
- Clear identification and demonstration of propensity overfitting in IPS-based learning.
- Simple, principled fix (SNIPS) with well-understood properties; strong consistency and bounded/equivariant behavior.
- Integration into a practical learner (Norm-POEM) with variance regularization and tractable optimization.
- Consistent empirical improvements over POEM across datasets and robustness to loss translations.
- Useful diagnostics (Ŝ(h)) and insightful training behavior analysis (smaller norms, faster convergence).

Cons
- Missing finite-sample generalization analysis for the SNIPS-based CRM objective; reliance on Normal/delta approximations.
- Limited baselines: no DR/normalized DR, no high-confidence OPE-informed training, no multiple-importance or model-assisted estimators.
- Only simulated BLBF; no real-world logged-policy data; no ablation on clipping thresholds beyond brief mention.
- Incomplete related work on WIS/weighted IS in RL (e.g., Precup et al.) and normalized DR variants.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide a finite-sample learning bound for the SNIPS-CRM objective (e.g., ratio-concentration/empirical Bernstein for ratios), or at least high-probability uniform control of the estimation error to justify the variance regularizer.
- Expand baselines to include:
  - Doubly robust (DR) and clipped/normalized DR.
  - Pure SNIPS without CRM and with alternative regularizers.
  - Effective sample size (ESS) diagnostics; relate ESS to performance.
- Add experiments on real logged data (e.g., recommendations/ads with known propensities), or at least adversarial logging policies in simulation to test heavy-tail regimes.
- Analyze sensitivity to clipping parameter M and λ; report selection stability across seeds.
- Strengthen related work with weighted IS in RL and recent OPE advances; discuss known bias of SNIPS and when it can be problematic.
- Provide pseudocode and implementation details (stopping criteria, initialization, regularization) for reproducibility.

Recommendation
Weak accept. The paper addresses a real and underappreciated failure mode in counterfactual learning and proposes a principled, effective remedy with solid empirical support. While the estimator is not new and the theory could be strengthened with finite-sample guarantees, the problem formulation, analysis, and demonstrated practical impact justify acceptance.
