

# Review of "Faster R-CNN: Towards Real-Time Object Detection with Region Proposal Networks"

This paper introduces Region Proposal Networks (RPNs), a novel approach to object detection that integrates region proposal generation directly into the deep learning pipeline, sharing convolutional features with the detection network. The key insight is that the convolutional feature maps used for object detection can also be used to generate region proposals, eliminating the need for separate, expensive proposal methods like Selective Search. The authors demonstrate that RPNs can be trained end-to-end with Fast R-CNN, achieving state-of-the-art detection accuracy with significantly reduced computational cost (5fps with VGG-16 on a GPU).

**Strengths:**
- The paper presents a clever and elegant solution to a real bottleneck in object detection pipelines.
- The method is technically sound and well-validated on PASCAL VOC benchmarks.
- The experimental results are compelling, showing both improved accuracy (73.2% mAP on VOC 2007) and speed (5fps with VGG-16).
- The idea of sharing convolutional features between proposal and detection networks is clearly articulated and well-motivated.

**Weaknesses (Minor but significant):**

1. **Formatting inconsistencies:** The paper contains several formatting errors that undermine its professional presentation. For example, "de∗Shaoqing Ren" appears in the author note (should be "de" with space or "Shaoqing Ren" without the asterisk). In the abstract, "de∗" appears to be a typo that should be removed.

2. **Technical calculation errors:** The paper claims "the total stride on the last conv layer is 16 pixels on the re-scaled image, and thus is ∼10 pixels on a typical PASCAL image (∼500×375)." This calculation is incorrect. If the stride is 16 pixels on an image with shorter side 600 pixels, the stride on a 500-pixel shorter side image should be 16 × (500/600) ≈ 13.3 pixels, not 10 pixels. This miscalculation raises questions about the paper's attention to detail.

3. **Inconsistent experimental claims:** The paper states "RPN alone only takes 10ms computing the additional layers" but later claims "Fast R-CNN with VGG-16 takes 320ms on 2k SS proposals" and "our system with VGG-16 takes in total 198ms for both proposal and detection." If RPN is 10ms, then Fast R-CNN should be 188ms, but the paper states it takes 320ms with SS proposals. This inconsistency suggests either an error in the reported numbers or a misunderstanding of the computational breakdown.

4. **Unclear loss function normalization:** The paper states "Ncls = 256" but later claims "Nreg ∼ 2,400" without explaining how these numbers were derived. The loss function equation uses Ncls and Nreg as normalizers, but the paper doesn't clarify whether these are batch sizes or anchor counts, creating confusion about how the loss is properly normalized.

5. **Missing technical details:** The paper claims "the effective receptive field on the input image is large (171 and 228 pixels for ZF and VGG, respectively)" but provides no explanation of how these values were calculated, making it difficult to verify this claim.

6. **Inconsistent terminology:** The paper switches between "RPN" and "region proposal network" without consistent formatting, and sometimes uses "region-based CNNs" when "region-based convolutional neural networks" would be more precise.

7. **Unverified speed claims:** The paper states "Our system has a frame-rate of 17 fps with the ZF net" but doesn't specify the hardware used for this measurement, making it difficult to reproduce or compare with other works.

**Conclusion:**

While the Faster R-CNN approach represents a significant improvement in object detection systems and has clear practical value, the paper contains several technical inconsistencies and formatting errors that undermine its quality as a scientific contribution. The errors are not major conceptual flaws but rather reflect a lack of meticulous attention to detail that is expected in top-tier publications. The miscalculation of stride, inconsistent timing measurements, and unclear loss function normalization are particularly problematic for a paper claiming to present a precise, efficient solution.

The paper's significance is substantial, but the minor technical inconsistencies, combined with the paper's lack of precision in reporting experimental details, make it difficult to fully trust the results. A perfect paper would have these minor issues resolved before publication.

**Recommendation:** Reject. While the core idea is strong and the contribution is significant, the paper's technical inconsistencies and lack of precision in reporting detract from its scientific quality. These minor but pervasive issues make it difficult to confidently evaluate the work's claims, and the paper would benefit from a thorough revision to address these concerns.
