Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes Region Proposal Networks (RPNs) that generate class-agnostic object proposals by sliding a small fully-convolutional network over shared convolutional feature maps. Each spatial location predicts k boxes via “anchors” that cover multiple scales and aspect ratios, with both objectness and bounding-box regression learned end-to-end using a multi-task loss. RPNs are trained to share convolutional layers with a Fast R-CNN detector via a simple 4-step alternating scheme, making proposals “nearly free” at test time. On PASCAL VOC, the method improves mAP over strong baselines (e.g., SS+Fast R-CNN), while reducing proposal computation to ~10ms and total inference to 5–17 fps, using VGG-16 or ZF backbones.

This work builds on region-based CNNs (R-CNN, SPPnet, Fast R-CNN) by addressing their main bottleneck: external CPU-based proposal generation (Selective Search, EdgeBoxes). It contrasts with OverFeat (one-stage, class-specific sliding window detection) and MultiBox (class-agnostic proposal prediction with learned anchors) by introducing translation-invariant anchors tied to the shared conv map, a much smaller output layer, and conv weight sharing with the detector. It also follows the trend of shared conv computation in object detection, but provides a new, efficient way to learn proposals jointly with detection.

Quality
Technical soundness: The proposal formulation is clear and well-grounded. The anchor mechanism provides a simple yet effective way to cover scale and aspect ratio variation with a compact predictor that is translation-invariant. The loss and target parameterization follow prior practice and are appropriate. The training procedure (image-centric sampling, smooth L1 regression, normalization, momentum/weight decay) is standard.

Evidence: The experiments support claims with careful ablations:
- Effect of sharing features: shared > unshared.
- Contribution of cls vs reg heads: both matter; removing reg drops mAP substantially; cls ranking enables using as few as 100–300 proposals with mild loss.
- Proposal count and NMS: accuracy robust to NMS and fewer proposals; recall-to-IoU analysis explains behavior.
- Backbone strength: VGG-16 proposals outperform ZF, translating into higher detection mAP.
- One-stage vs two-stage comparison: two-stage (RPN+Fast R-CNN) outperforms dense sliding-window one-stage under similar backbone/implementation.
Speed/accuracy: With VGG-16, proposals cost ~10ms and total detector ~198ms; with ZF, 17 fps is reported. These are compelling and tied to fewer proposals (≈300) and shared convs.

Reservations:
- Joint end-to-end training of the unified network is not explored; the authors adopt an alternating 4-step procedure for stability. While pragmatic, a comparison (even partial) to true joint training would strengthen the case that alternating does not limit performance.
- Evaluation is limited to PASCAL VOC 2007/2012. Given the generality claims, results on a larger dataset (e.g., COCO) would better stress-test small objects and crowded scenes.
- Some design choices (anchor scales/ratios, 16-pixel stride, IoU thresholds of 0.7/0.3, NMS at 0.7) are fixed; sensitivity analysis is limited. The paper notes stride may limit small-object accuracy, but does not quantify it.

Clarity
The paper is clearly written and well-organized. The method is described concretely enough to reproduce, with training hyperparameters, loss definitions, and implementation notes. The distinction to OverFeat and MultiBox is carefully articulated (translation invariance, parameter efficiency). Suggested clarifications:
- Briefly quantify the number of anchors retained after NMS on average vs. maximum top-N to avoid confusion about proposal counts.
- Summarize sensitivity to anchor design and stride; a short table would help practitioners.
- The alternating training procedure could be condensed into a schematic for readability.

Originality
While it builds on Fast R-CNN and prior proposal/detection pipelines, the core contributions are novel and substantial:
- A fully-convolutional, translation-invariant anchor mechanism that predicts class-agnostic proposals at every spatial location with a compact head.
- Sharing conv features between proposal and detection, making proposals effectively free.
- A simple, stable alternating training scheme to learn shared convs.
The differences to MultiBox (global, k-means anchors, large parameterization, no translation invariance) and OverFeat (one-stage, class-specific) are significant in design and practical outcomes.

Significance
The work addresses a major practical bottleneck and demonstrably advances the state of the art on speed-accuracy trade-offs. By making proposals almost costless and reducing proposals needed to 300 while improving mAP (e.g., 69.9% VGG-16 vs SS baseline, up to 73.2% with more training data), it sets a new standard for real-time capable detectors. The approach is generic to backbones, simple to implement, and the code availability enhances impact. It is highly likely to be adopted and extended by the community.

Pros
- Elegant, effective unification of proposals and detection with shared convs; proposals are nearly free.
- Strong empirical results with thorough, relevant ablations; clear speed/accuracy improvements.
- Novel anchor design with translation invariance and compact parameterization.
- Practical training recipe and open-source implementation.

Cons
- No exploration of true end-to-end joint training; alternating scheme may not be optimal.
- Limited dataset scope; small-object performance and generalization beyond VOC not quantified.
- Hyperparameter sensitivity (anchors, stride, NMS) not systematically analyzed.

Questions for authors
- Have you tried joint end-to-end training of the unified network? If so, how does it compare in stability and final accuracy?
- How sensitive are results to anchor scales/ratios and to feature stride (e.g., using dilated convs for stride 8)?
- Do you observe degradation on small objects, and would multi-scale features or FPN-like designs mitigate this within your framework?

Recommendation
Strong accept. The paper clearly meets NeurIPS criteria: it is technically sound, original in key aspects, clearly written, and significant in impact. Minor weaknesses (joint training, broader evaluation) do not detract from its substantial contribution. Confidence: high.
