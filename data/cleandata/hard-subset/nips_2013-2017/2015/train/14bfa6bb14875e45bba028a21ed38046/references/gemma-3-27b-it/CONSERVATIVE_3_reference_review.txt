This paper proposes Region Proposal Networks (RPNs) to accelerate object detection by moving the region proposal stage to the GPU and, crucially, integrating it with the detection network itself to share computations. The core idea – using a convolutional network to generate proposals – is presented as a significant departure from traditional, CPU-bound methods like Selective Search and EdgeBoxes. The authors demonstrate improved performance and speed on PASCAL VOC benchmarks, achieving competitive mAP with a substantial reduction in proposal generation time.

The paper is well-written and clearly presents the motivation, methodology, and results. The organization is logical, building from the limitations of existing region proposal methods to the proposed RPN architecture and training scheme. The ablation studies are particularly helpful in dissecting the contributions of different components (shared convolutions, cls/reg outputs). The inclusion of running time analysis and comparisons to prior work is valuable.

However, despite the generally strong presentation, I have several concerns regarding the novelty and significance of this work, particularly given the NIPS bar. While the idea of using a convolutional network for proposal generation isn’t *entirely* new (references to OverFeat and MultiBox are acknowledged, though perhaps not sufficiently emphasized), the framing of this as a breakthrough feels overstated. MultiBox [20], for instance, already explored a similar concept of directly predicting proposals from a convolutional network. The authors downplay this prior work, focusing instead on the “algorithmic change” of sharing convolutions. This sharing, while certainly effective, feels more like a natural engineering optimization than a fundamental algorithmic innovation. 

My primary concern is the relatively modest gain in mAP (approximately 1-2% over strong baselines like SS+Fast R-CNN) achieved despite the algorithmic complexity introduced by RPNs. While the speed improvement is significant, the improvements in accuracy alone don't yet justify the added architectural sophistication. Furthermore, the experiments feel somewhat limited. The results primarily focus on PASCAL VOC. Demonstrating performance on larger, more challenging datasets (e.g., COCO) would be crucial to establish the generalizability and impact of this method. The ablation studies, while present, don’t fully explore the sensitivity of the method to hyperparameter choices or different network architectures.

The training scheme, alternating between RPN and Fast R-CNN fine-tuning, is described as a key contribution. However, the justification for this specific alternating scheme isn't fully convincing. Why does alternating work better than joint training (acknowledged as a potential future direction)? A more thorough analysis of the convergence behavior and the impact of different fine-tuning schedules would strengthen this argument.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Significant speedup in region proposal generation, enabling near real-time object detection.
*   Clear and well-written presentation of a technically sound method.
*   Demonstrated improvement in mAP on PASCAL VOC.
*   Thoughtful ablation studies providing insights into the method's behavior.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Incremental innovation; the core idea of using a CNN for proposal generation isn't entirely novel.
*   Modest improvement in mAP, not commensurate with the increased complexity.
*   Limited evaluation on PASCAL VOC only.
*   Insufficient justification for the chosen alternating training scheme.
*   Downplaying of relevant prior work (e.g., MultiBox).



**Overall:**

I believe this paper represents a solid engineering contribution and a practical improvement to object detection pipelines. However, I am not convinced that it rises to the level of originality and significance typically expected at NIPS. While the speedup is valuable, the modest accuracy gain and limited scope of evaluation prevent me from strongly recommending acceptance. I would rate this as a borderline paper, leaning towards rejection. Further experimentation on larger datasets and a more thorough comparison to closely related work are needed to strengthen the case for its inclusion in the conference.
