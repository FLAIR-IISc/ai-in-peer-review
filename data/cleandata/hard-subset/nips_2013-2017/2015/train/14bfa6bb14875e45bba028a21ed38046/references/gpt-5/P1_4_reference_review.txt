Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes Region Proposal Networks (RPNs), a fully convolutional module that generates class-agnostic object proposals by sliding a small network over shared convolutional feature maps. At each location, it predicts k=9 anchor boxes spanning multiple scales and aspect ratios via two sibling heads (objectness classification and bounding-box regression), trained with a multi-task loss and simple IoU-based positive/negative labeling. Crucially, the RPN shares convolutional features with a Fast R-CNN detector and is trained via a pragmatic 4-step alternating scheme to yield a unified network whose proposal computation is nearly free given the detector’s backbone.

This work builds directly on R-CNN → SPPnet → Fast R-CNN by addressing the remaining bottleneck: CPU-based region proposals (Selective Search, EdgeBoxes). Compared to OverFeat (one-stage, class-specific sliding windows) and MultiBox (predict many boxes from fixed, non-translation-invariant priors), the RPN uses translation-invariant anchors, a compact output layer, and shares features with the detector. Extensive experiments on PASCAL VOC 2007/2012 demonstrate competitive or superior mAP while reducing runtime to 5–17 fps (including proposals) on a GPU.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The formulation is clear and principled. The anchor-based parameterization, smooth L1 regression, and classification loss follow established practice. The sampling strategy (image-centric mini-batches with balanced positive/negative anchors), NMS, and clipping are standard and appropriate. The alternating training strategy is a reasonable and effective solution to avoid the instability of joint optimization.
- Empirical support: Results cover ablations (shared vs unshared features, role of cls vs reg heads, effect of NMS/top-N, number of proposals) and two backbones (ZF, VGG16). Comparisons to SS and EB are fair from an accuracy standpoint and persuasive for speed/accuracy trade-offs; the one-stage emulation further supports the two-stage design. The recall-to-IoU analysis helps diagnose the proposal quality.
- Reproducibility: Implementation details (input scale, strides, anchor settings, IoU thresholds, learning schedule, normalization, momentum/decay, which layers are fine-tuned) are provided, and code availability is stated.

Clarity
- The paper is well written and organized. The RPN architecture, anchor design, and loss are clearly explained. The alternating 4-step training is described with motivations and practical caveats.
- Minor clarity suggestions:
  - The normalization choices for the loss (Ncls, Nreg) and λ are partly relegated to a footnote; consolidating these details in the main text would aid reproducibility.
  - Explicitly report how many proposals are used at test time per setting (e.g., top-300 after NMS@0.7) in the main results tables for quick reference.
  - Provide per-class AP breakdowns and small/medium/large object analysis (the stride=16 choice may affect small-object performance) to complement recall-to-IoU plots.

Originality
- The key novelty is learning proposals with a compact, translation-invariant, anchor-based fully convolutional network that shares features with the detector, turning proposals into a near-zero-cost byproduct. While anchors and bounding-box regression are known, the specific integration, training scheme, and computational sharing are novel and elegant relative to Fast R-CNN, OverFeat, and MultiBox.
- The alternating training to realize shared features is a pragmatic contribution enabling stable training in practice.

Significance
- The work addresses a major bottleneck in region-based detectors and materially advances state of the art in accuracy-speed trade-offs. It enables practical real-time-like detection with very deep models and reduces system complexity by eliminating a hand-engineered CPU proposal stage.
- The approach is likely to be widely adopted and extended (e.g., to other backbones, datasets, and tasks). The anchor mechanism and proposal sharing are fundamental ideas with broad impact.

Pros
- Elegant, simple architecture for proposals; minimal overhead when sharing features.
- Strong empirical results: better mAP than SS+Fast R-CNN with faster runtime; robust with as few as 300 proposals.
- Thorough ablations clarifying the roles of cls/reg heads, NMS, shared features.
- Clear comparisons to one-stage sliding-window detection and prior proposal methods.
- Implementation details and code availability support reproducibility.

Cons and questions
- Joint end-to-end optimization of proposals and detection is not attempted; the alternating scheme is effective but heuristic. Have the authors tried joint training with careful scheduling or curriculum?
- Sensitivity to anchor design is not deeply explored. How do more/fewer anchors, different scales/aspect ratios, or data-driven anchors affect mAP and recall, especially for small objects?
- Dataset scope is limited to PASCAL VOC; COCO-scale evaluation would strengthen significance claims and test generalization.
- Runtime comparisons partly conflate GPU vs CPU implementations. While the main point is sharing, a discussion of what a GPU-optimized EB/SS might achieve would make the case even stronger.
- Stride=16 may hamper small objects; results with smaller stride (e.g., atrous/dilated conv) or feature pyramid variants would be informative.
- Limited head-to-head comparison with MultiBox in terms of final detection mAP and runtime using similar backbones.

Suggestions for improvement
- Add a brief sensitivity study on anchor configurations and NMS threshold; report small-object performance.
- Discuss attempts (successful or not) at joint training, and whether instability is empirical or principled.
- Include per-class AP tables and error analysis (e.g., localization vs background false positives).
- Provide GPU memory footprint and throughput breakdown per stage (backbone, RPN heads, ROI heads).
- If feasible, include results on a larger benchmark or a subset to demonstrate scalability.

Recommendation
- Accept. This paper presents a substantial and practical advance: a learned, shared, near-free proposal mechanism that improves accuracy and speed over strong baselines. The method is technically sound, clearly described, and well validated. The anticipated impact on the community is high.

Confidence
- High.
