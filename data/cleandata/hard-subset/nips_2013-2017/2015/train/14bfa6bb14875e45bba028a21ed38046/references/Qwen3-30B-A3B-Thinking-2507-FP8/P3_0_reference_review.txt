

# Peer Review: "Faster R-CNN: Towards Real-Time Object Detection with Region Proposal Networks"

## Summary of Main Ideas and Relation to Previous Work

This paper addresses a critical bottleneck in object detection systems: the time-consuming region proposal step. The authors propose Region Proposal Networks (RPNs), which replace traditional methods like Selective Search and EdgeBoxes with a deep learning approach that shares convolutional features with the downstream detection network. The key insight is that convolutional feature maps used by region-based detectors (like Fast R-CNN) can also be used for generating region proposals, making the proposal computation nearly cost-free when combined with the detection network.

The paper builds upon previous work in two key areas: (1) Fast R-CNN [5], which established the paradigm of sharing convolutional features across proposals for detection, and (2) region proposal methods like Selective Search [22] and EdgeBoxes [24]. It extends the concept of shared computation to the proposal stage, which previous work had not addressed. The authors also draw from OverFeat [18] and MultiBox [3, 20], but their approach differs fundamentally by using a translation-invariant anchor scheme and sharing features with the detection network.

The paper's main contributions are: (1) RPNs as a fully convolutional network for region proposal generation, (2) a 4-step training procedure to share convolutional features between RPN and detection networks, and (3) demonstrating that this approach achieves state-of-the-art accuracy with near real-time performance (5-17 fps).

## Strengths

1. **Significant Practical Impact**: The paper solves a real bottleneck in object detection systems that had been overlooked in previous research. The authors correctly identify that region proposal methods (like Selective Search) are an order of magnitude slower than detection networks, and their solution effectively eliminates this bottleneck. This is a major contribution to practical computer vision systems.

2. **Elegant Methodology**: The RPN design is simple yet powerful. The translation-invariant anchor scheme is particularly elegant, avoiding the need for multi-scale feature extraction or sliding windows. The use of 3×3 convolutions with 9 anchors per location (3 scales × 3 aspect ratios) is well-justified and efficient.

3. **Strong Experimental Validation**: The paper provides comprehensive experiments on PASCAL VOC benchmarks with thorough ablation studies. The comparison between RPN+Fast R-CNN and traditional Selective Search shows a clear improvement in both accuracy (73.2% mAP vs. 70.4% for SS) and speed (198ms vs. 1510ms per image). The ablation studies on the RPN components (cls vs. reg layers) provide valuable insights into what makes the proposal method effective.

4. **Practical Implementation Details**: The paper includes detailed implementation specifics (anchor sizes, training procedures, NMS parameters) that make the method highly reproducible. The authors' approach to handling boundary-crossing proposals and the careful treatment of positive/negative anchors during training demonstrates thorough understanding of the practical challenges.

5. **Clear Presentation**: The paper is exceptionally well-written, with clear figures (particularly Fig. 1 showing the anchor design and RPN architecture) and logical organization. The 4-step training procedure is clearly explained and easy to follow.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited Theoretical Justification**: While the paper provides strong empirical results, it lacks theoretical justification for why the RPN design works so well. For instance, why does the translation-invariant anchor scheme outperform non-invariant methods like MultiBox? Why do the cls and reg layers have the specific contributions observed in the ablation studies? This is particularly notable for a NIPS paper where theoretical contributions are highly valued.

2. **Incomplete Comparison to Contemporary Work**: The paper was published in 2015, and while it correctly cites the state-of-the-art at the time, it could have better positioned itself against other recent proposals. For example, it doesn't discuss the relationship to the "R-CNN minus R" approach [27] or the "Deep Neural Networks for Object Detection" paper [28] that were also emerging around the same time. A more comprehensive comparison would strengthen the contribution claim.

3. **Limited Analysis of Generalization**: The paper focuses primarily on PASCAL VOC results. It would be valuable to see how the RPN performs on more challenging datasets (like COCO) or in scenarios with significant domain shifts. The paper mentions using the union of VOC 2007 and 2012 for training, but doesn't provide results on the COCO test set.

4. **Training Procedure Details**: The 4-step training procedure is described, but the authors don't provide evidence that this is superior to a joint training approach (which they mention as a future work direction). It would be helpful to have a comparison of the convergence speed or final performance between the 4-step procedure and a joint training approach.

5. **Lack of Analysis on Proposal Quality vs. Detection Accuracy**: While the paper includes recall-to-IoU analysis (Fig. 2), it doesn't provide a more detailed analysis of how proposal quality directly maps to detection accuracy. For instance, why do RPN proposals with lower IoU still lead to higher mAP than Selective Search? This would help clarify the relationship between the two stages.

## Recommendation

I strongly recommend acceptance of this paper. It presents a significant, practical contribution to object detection that addresses a real bottleneck in the field. The methodology is elegant, the results are compelling, and the paper is well-written and thorough. While it lacks some theoretical depth, this is a common characteristic of highly practical computer vision work, and the empirical results are sufficient to establish the paper's contribution.

The paper has had a profound impact on the field, with "Faster R-CNN" becoming one of the most cited object detection papers in the last decade. It fundamentally changed how object detection systems are designed, and its influence extends far beyond the specific benchmarks presented in the paper.

## Specific Suggestions for Improvement

1. The paper could benefit from a brief discussion of how the RPN design relates to the theoretical properties of convolutional networks (e.g., translation invariance, receptive fields).

2. A comparison to the "R-CNN minus R" approach [27] would help clarify the novelty of the proposal generation versus detection separation.

3. The paper could include a brief discussion of how the RPN design might be extended to other tasks beyond object detection (e.g., semantic segmentation).

4. The authors should consider adding a short section on how their approach fits into the broader context of end-to-end trainable object detection systems, referencing later work that has built upon this foundation.

## Conclusion

"Faster R-CNN" represents a significant step forward in object detection, solving a critical bottleneck in the field with an elegant, practical solution that has become the foundation for many subsequent detection systems. The paper is technically sound, well-executed, and provides clear evidence of its contribution through thorough experiments. While it could benefit from more theoretical justification and broader comparisons, these are minor issues that do not detract from the paper's substantial contribution to the field of computer vision. I believe this paper is a strong candidate for acceptance at NIPS.
