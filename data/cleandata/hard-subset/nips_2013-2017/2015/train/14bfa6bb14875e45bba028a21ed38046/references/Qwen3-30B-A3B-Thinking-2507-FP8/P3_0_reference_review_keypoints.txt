- Main idea: Introduces Region Proposal Networks (RPNs) that generate region proposals by sharing convolutional features with the detection network, making proposal computation nearly cost-free.  
- Builds on prior work: Extends Fast R-CNN’s shared-feature paradigm and contrasts with Selective Search, EdgeBoxes, OverFeat, and MultiBox by using translation-invariant anchors.  
- Key contributions: (1) RPN as a fully convolutional proposal generator, (2) a 4-step training procedure to share conv features between RPN and detector, (3) state-of-the-art accuracy with near real-time speed.  
- Strength — Practical impact: Effectively removes the region-proposal bottleneck, significantly improving detector runtime in real systems.  
- Strength — Elegant design: Simple, efficient anchor scheme (3 scales × 3 aspect ratios) and 3×3 convolutions avoid multi-scale sliding-window overhead.  
- Strength — Strong experiments: Thorough PASCAL VOC benchmarks and ablation studies showing improved mAP and speed vs Selective Search.  
- Strength — Reproducibility: Detailed implementation and training details (anchor sizes, NMS, handling boundary proposals) provided.  
- Strength — Presentation: Clear writing and figures, with a well-explained 4-step training procedure.  
- Weakness — Limited theory: Lacks theoretical justification for why the anchor scheme and RPN design perform so well.  
- Weakness — Incomplete comparisons: Could better position against contemporary alternatives like "R-CNN minus R" and other emerging detection papers.  
- Weakness — Generalization analysis: Evaluation concentrated on PASCAL VOC; lacks results on harder datasets (e.g., COCO) and domain-shift scenarios.  
- Weakness — Training choices: No empirical comparison between the 4-step training and joint end-to-end training regarding convergence or final performance.  
- Weakness — Proposal vs detection link: Insufficient analysis of how proposal quality (IoU/recall) maps to final detection accuracy.  
- Recommendation: Strong accept — practical, well-executed, and influential contribution despite limited theoretical depth.  
- Suggested improvements: Discuss theoretical links to conv-net properties, compare to "R-CNN minus R", explore extensions to other tasks, and situate work within end-to-end detection trends.  
- Conclusion: Faster R-CNN is a significant, foundational advance that addresses a key bottleneck and has had major impact on object detection design.
