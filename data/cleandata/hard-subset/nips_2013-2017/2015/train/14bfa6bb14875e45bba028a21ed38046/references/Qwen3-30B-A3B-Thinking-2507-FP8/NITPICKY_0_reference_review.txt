

# Review of "Faster R-CNN: Towards Real-Time Object Detection with Region Proposal Networks"

This paper introduces Region Proposal Networks (RPNs) as a novel method for generating region proposals in object detection systems, aiming to eliminate the computational bottleneck of traditional region proposal methods like Selective Search. The key insight is that RPNs can share convolutional features with the detection network, making proposal computation nearly cost-free. The paper presents a unified framework (RPN + Fast R-CNN) that achieves state-of-the-art detection accuracy with significantly improved speed.

**Strengths:**
- The core idea of sharing convolutional features between proposal and detection networks is well-motivated and elegantly implemented.
- The paper provides comprehensive ablation studies that clearly demonstrate the contributions of different components (cls vs reg layers, shared vs unshared features).
- The empirical results on PASCAL VOC benchmarks are strong and convincing, showing both accuracy improvements and speed gains.
- The technical details of the RPN architecture, loss function, and training procedure are well-described.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Formatting and consistency errors:**
   - The paper contains inconsistent terminology regarding the 10ms computation time. The introduction states "the marginal cost for computing proposals is small (e.g., 10ms per image)," but later in the implementation section, it says "the RPN alone only takes 10ms computing the additional layers." This creates confusion about what exactly is being measured.
   - In the anchor size description, the paper writes "box areas of 1282, 2562, and 5122 pixels" instead of the intended "128^2, 256^2, and 512^2 pixels," which could be misinterpreted by readers.

2. **Incomplete references:**
   - Several references are incomplete or contain placeholder text. For example, "Object-Proposal Evaluation Protocol is 'Gameable, N. Chavali, H. Agrawal, A. Mahendru, D. Batra, arXiv: 1505.05836, ," lacks proper formatting and missing publication details. Similarly, "Edge boxes: Locating object proposals from edges, C.L. Zitnick, P. Dollár, ECCV, ," is missing the publication year.
   - The reference to "Scalable, C. Szegedy, S. Reed, D. Erhan, D. Anguelov, high-quality object detection. arXiv:1412.1441v2, ," is similarly incomplete.

3. **Inconsistent results reporting:**
   - The paper claims "using the expensive very deep models of [19], our detection method still has a frame rate of 5fps (including all steps) on a GPU" but later states "Our system has a frame-rate of 17 fps with the ZF net." This creates confusion about the actual performance of the VGG-based system versus the ZF-based system.
   - In Table 1, RPN with Fast R-CNN achieves 59.9% mAP with "up to 300 proposals," but in the ablation study, replacing SS with 300 RPN proposals leads to only 56.8% mAP. This discrepancy needs clarification.

4. **Missing technical details:**
   - The paper states "the effective receptive field on the input image is large (171 and 228 pixels for ZF and VGG, respectively)" but fails to explain how these numbers were calculated or why they're relevant to the proposal generation process.
   - The loss function (Equation 1) uses "Ncls" and "Nreg" as normalizations, but these are only defined in a footnote rather than being properly explained in the main text. The footnote states that "Ncls = 256" and "Nreg ∼ 2,400" in their implementation, but this should be part of the main equation description.

5. **Implementation details:**
   - The paper states "We use a learning rate of 0.001 for 60k mini-batches, and 0.0001 for the next 20k mini-batches" but fails to specify the mini-batch size, which is critical for reproducibility.
   - The claim that "the RPN alone only takes 10ms computing the additional layers" lacks hardware specifications, making it impossible to evaluate the significance of this claim.

**Overall Assessment:**

While the paper presents a significant contribution to object detection with a well-motivated technical approach, the numerous minor flaws in presentation, consistency, and detail undermine the paper's quality. The inconsistencies in results reporting (5fps vs. 17fps, 59.9% vs. 56.8% mAP with 300 proposals), incomplete references, and missing implementation details are particularly concerning for a NIPS submission that should set a high standard for scientific rigor.

The paper's technical content is strong, but the presentation issues are too numerous to overlook. A paper of this caliber should have been meticulously proofread for consistency, completeness, and clarity. The reviewers would expect a paper that has been carefully polished for technical and presentation details before submission to NIPS.

**Recommendation:** Reject. The paper's significant contribution is overshadowed by multiple minor but critical issues that indicate a lack of attention to detail, which is particularly problematic for a venue like NIPS that emphasizes technical precision.
