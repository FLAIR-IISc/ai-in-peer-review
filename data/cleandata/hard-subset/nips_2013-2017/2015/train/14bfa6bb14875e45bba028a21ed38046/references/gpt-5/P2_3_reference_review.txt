Summary
The paper introduces Region Proposal Networks (RPNs) that are trained to generate object proposals directly from shared convolutional feature maps and are integrated with Fast R-CNN into a unified, two-stage detection system. Key ideas: (1) anchor boxes at each sliding-window location enable translation-invariant, multi-scale, multi-aspect proposals; (2) a lightweight, fully-convolutional head predicts objectness and box regression for k anchors per location; (3) alternating training shares convolutional layers between the RPN and the Fast R-CNN detector; (4) proposals are nearly “free” when sharing features with the detector. Compared to prior work, RPNs replace hand-crafted or separately engineered CPU proposal methods (Selective Search, EdgeBoxes) and reduce the proposal bottleneck while improving detection accuracy. The paper relates to OverFeat (one-stage detection) and MultiBox (class-agnostic proposals predicted from fixed, non-translation-invariant anchors), and to Fast/Spatial Pyramid Pooling R-CNN for shared feature computation.

Quality
The paper is technically sound and empirically careful.

- Soundness: The anchor formulation is well-defined; losses follow standard multi-task classification/regression with smooth L1; minibatch sampling is justified to balance positives/negatives; boundary-handling and NMS are specified. The translation-invariant anchor design reduces parameters relative to MultiBox and is a plausible explanation for better data efficiency.
- Empirical support: Experiments on PASCAL VOC 2007/2012 show clear improvements: with VGG-16 and shared features, mAP of 69.9% (07), 73.2% with extended training, and 70.4% (12), while achieving near-real-time speed (198 ms per image; 10 ms marginal for proposals). Ablations isolate effects of sharing, number of proposals, removing cls/reg heads, NMS, and switching backbones (ZF vs VGG-16). The one-stage sliding-window baseline supports the two-stage advantage.
- Limitations: Evaluation is limited to VOC; there is no direct empirical comparison to MultiBox on the same splits. The training strategy is alternating rather than fully joint; while pragmatic, convergence and optimality of end-to-end joint training remain unaddressed experimentally. No statistical variation analysis is provided.

Clarity
The paper is clearly written and well-organized. Implementation details (anchor scales/ratios, image resizing, strides, sampling, learning rates, momentum, weight decay) should enable reproduction; code availability strengthens this. Some footnote details on loss normalization and λ could be consolidated in the main text for clarity. A brief schematic of the 4-step alternating training is useful; further clarity on which layers are frozen at each step is provided and sufficient.

Originality
The central novelty lies in:

- Using a fully convolutional, translation-invariant anchor mechanism to generate proposals directly from shared features.
- An integrated, detector-tuned proposal generator trained to share convolutional layers with the detection network via alternating optimization.

While elements (shared conv features, bounding-box regression, sliding windows) are known, their specific combination—particularly the anchor design and feature sharing yielding near-free proposals—constitutes a substantive advance over Fast R-CNN+SS/EB and over MultiBox’s non-translation-invariant, high-parameter output layer. The paper positions itself well relative to OverFeat and MultiBox and explains differences.

Significance
The contribution is practically and scientifically significant:

- Speed/accuracy: Dramatic end-to-end speedup versus SS/EB with improved mAP when using VGG-16, enabling near-real-time detection with strong accuracy.
- Adoption potential: The approach is simple to implement in existing detection pipelines, and code is released. The anchor concept is flexible and likely to influence subsequent designs.
- Generality: Although only VOC is reported, the method is architecture-agnostic (ZF/VGG shown) and likely generalizes.

Strengths
- Elegant, effective proposal mechanism that leverages shared features; proposals cost ~10 ms.
- Clear, well-justified anchor design with translation invariance and low parameter count.
- Strong ablations isolating the roles of cls/reg heads, NMS, number of proposals, feature sharing, and backbone depth.
- Demonstrated improvement over strong SS baseline and over one-stage sliding-window baseline.
- Practical training recipe with enough detail and released code.

Weaknesses and limitations
- Dataset scope limited to VOC; no COCO or larger-scale evidence.
- No head-to-head comparison with MultiBox/other learned proposal methods on the same benchmarks.
- Alternating optimization is a heuristic; no experiments on fully joint end-to-end training or analysis of convergence behavior.
- Fairness of runtime comparisons to CPU SS is discussed but not experimentally controlled by a GPU reimplementation of SS/EB; still, the main advantage is sharing, not raw GPU.
- Sensitivity analysis on anchor choices (more/fewer scales/ratios) is limited; learned anchor distributions reported but no systematic tuning study.

Questions for the authors
- How sensitive are results to anchor design (number/scales/ratios) and stride? Would a smaller stride materially improve mAP with similar cost?
- Did you attempt end-to-end joint training (single graph) and, if so, how did it compare to the 4-step scheme?
- How robust is performance to the IoU thresholds (0.7/0.3) used for positive/negative anchor assignment?
- Can you report results on a larger dataset (e.g., COCO) or provide evidence of generalization?

Suggestions
- Add a direct comparison to MultiBox on VOC with matched backbones and report both detection mAP and proposal recall/AR.
- Include a brief sensitivity study on anchor configurations and NMS threshold.
- Move the loss normalization/λ details from footnotes into the main text and standardize normalization across experiments.

Overall assessment
This is a high-quality, impactful paper that advances the state of the art in object detection by removing the region proposal bottleneck through an end-to-end trained, shared-feature RPN. The technical ideas are simple yet powerful, the empirical results are convincing, and the work is likely to be widely adopted. Despite some limitations in breadth of evaluation and joint training, I strongly recommend acceptance.
