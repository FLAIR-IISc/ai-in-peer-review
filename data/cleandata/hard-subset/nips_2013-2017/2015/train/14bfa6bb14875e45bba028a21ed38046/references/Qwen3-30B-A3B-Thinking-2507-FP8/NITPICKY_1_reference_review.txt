

# Review of "Faster R-CNN: Towards Real-Time Object Detection with Region Proposal Networks"

This paper introduces Region Proposal Networks (RPNs) that share convolutional features with object detection networks, enabling near-cost-free region proposal generation. The authors propose a unified architecture that replaces traditional region proposal methods (like Selective Search) with a deep network that generates proposals while sharing computation with the detection network. This leads to a significant speedup while maintaining competitive accuracy.

## Technical and Methodological Concerns

The paper contains several significant technical inaccuracies that undermine its credibility. The most glaring error appears in the "Implementation Details" section where the authors claim "the total stride on the last conv layer is 16 pixels on the re-scaled image, and thus is ~10 pixels on a typical PASCAL image (~500×375)." This calculation is mathematically incorrect. If the stride is 16 pixels on the rescaled image (with shorter side = 600 pixels), the effective stride on a 500-pixel original image should be (500/600)*16 ≈ 13.3 pixels, not 10 pixels as claimed. This fundamental error suggests the authors may have miscalculated other aspects of their system.

The loss function in Equation (1) contains a formatting error: "λ 1 Nreg" should be "λ 1/Nreg" to match the mathematical notation. This is a minor but unprofessional error in a technical paper.

The paper claims RPNs are "translation invariant" but fails to clearly explain how this property is achieved or why it's important for object detection. The comparison with MultiBox states that "our method requires a (4+2)×9-dimensional output layer" while MultiBox "requires a (4+1)×800-dimensional output layer," but doesn't clarify why the dimensionality difference is meaningful for performance.

The paper states "RPN+VGG has an mAP of 70.4% on VOC 2012" without specifying whether this uses shared features or not, creating ambiguity about the claimed performance.

## Formatting and Clarity Issues

The paper contains several formatting inconsistencies. The author affiliation line contains "de∗Shaoqing Ren" with an incorrectly placed asterisk. The title appears as "Faster R-CNN: Towards Real-Time Object Detection with Region Proposal Networks" in the header but as "Faster R-CNN" in the body text, creating inconsistency.

The paper uses "mAP" without defining it as "mean Average Precision" on first use, which is problematic for readers unfamiliar with the metric.

The statement "the effective running time for proposals is just 10 milliseconds" lacks critical context about the hardware platform used for this measurement, making the claim difficult to verify.

## Experimental Details

The paper claims "Using the expensive very deep models of [19], our detection method still has a frame rate of 5fps (including all steps) on a GPU" but later states "Our system has a frame-rate of 17 fps with the ZF net." This creates confusion about which model achieves which frame rate. The paper should clarify this explicitly.

The claim that "RPN+VGG has an mAP of 70.4% on VOC 2012" is made without specifying whether this is with or without shared features, which is critical information for understanding the contribution.

The paper states "the RPN alone only takes 10ms computing the additional layers" but doesn't explain how this 10ms was measured (e.g., which GPU, batch size, etc.).

## Comparison with Prior Work

The paper's comparison with MultiBox states "Our proposal layers have an order of magnitude fewer parameters (27 million for MultiBox using GoogLeNet [20] vs. 2.4 million for RPN using VGG-16)." However, VGG-16 has approximately 138 million parameters total, so 2.4 million for RPN seems implausibly small. The authors should provide a more accurate parameter count.

The paper claims "the top-ranked 100 proposals at test-time" yield "55.1% mAP" but doesn't explain why this is meaningful given that the main contribution is about reducing the number of proposals from 2000 to 300.

## Overall Assessment

While the paper presents an interesting idea with potential practical value, the numerous technical errors, formatting inconsistencies, and unclear experimental details significantly undermine its credibility. The incorrect stride calculation is particularly troubling as it suggests fundamental misunderstandings of the network architecture. The paper also fails to clearly explain key design choices (like the number of anchors) and makes claims without sufficient supporting detail.

The paper's contribution is significant in the object detection domain, but the methodological and presentation flaws are too severe to overlook. As a perfectionist reviewer, I cannot recommend acceptance given these issues, as they suggest the authors may not have fully understood their own methodology. The paper would benefit from a complete technical revision to address these concerns before publication.

The paper has value in its core idea but presents it with insufficient technical rigor. For a top-tier conference like NIPS, the level of detail and precision required for publication is not met by this submission.
