Summary
The paper proposes Region Proposal Networks (RPNs), a fully convolutional network that generates class-agnostic object proposals by regressing and scoring anchor boxes at each spatial location of a shared convolutional feature map. The RPN shares all convolutional layers with a Fast R-CNN detector, making proposal generation nearly cost-free at test time. The authors introduce a practical 4-step alternating fine-tuning scheme to enable feature sharing, demonstrate strong detection mAP on PASCAL VOC 2007/2012 with VGG-16, and report substantial speed-ups versus Selective Search or EdgeBoxes. Key design choices include translation-equivariant anchors with 3 scales × 3 aspect ratios, a two-branch cls/reg head over 3×3 conv features, Smooth L1 regression with p*-gated losses, and non-maximum suppression at 0.7 IoU.

Relation to prior work
The method builds on R-CNN/SPPnet/Fast R-CNN by moving proposals from engineered or CPU-bound algorithms to a learned, GPU-friendly module that reuses conv features. Relative to OverFeat (one-stage, class-specific sliding window with multi-scale features), this work separates objectness from classification and uses box regression relative to an anchor grid. Compared to MultiBox, which predicts a fixed set of non-translation-equivariant boxes via k-means, RPN uses a dense, translation-equivariant anchor tiling that drastically reduces output dimensionality and parameters while improving data efficiency on smaller datasets like PASCAL. The paper also situates itself among proposal quality analyses (Hosang et al., Chavali et al.) and shared feature pipelines.

Strengths
- Technical contribution and elegance: A simple, general FCN head on shared conv features yields high-quality proposals with negligible overhead. The anchor parameterization and gated multi-task loss are principled and effective.
- Empirical significance: End-to-end system achieves better mAP than SS+Fast R-CNN while running at near real-time rates for PASCAL. Ablations are informative (effects of sharing vs unshared features, cls vs reg, proposal counts, NMS).
- Clear connection to prior art: The contrast with MultiBox and OverFeat is well articulated, highlighting why translation-equivariant anchors reduce parameters and help generalization.
- Practicality: The alternating training schedule is pragmatic, stable, and easy to adopt; the implementation details (sampling 256 anchors with up to 1:1 pos:neg, ignoring border-crossing anchors in training) are reproducible, and code is promised.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Joint training: The paper avoids end-to-end joint optimization of RPN and detector due to stability concerns, yet provides no empirical attempt or analysis. Given the architectural simplicity, even an approximate joint variant would strengthen claims about unification and could yield further gains.
- Dataset scope: Results are limited to PASCAL VOC. Given the claims about proposal quality and speed, evaluation on a more challenging dataset (e.g., MS COCO for small-object regimes) would better demonstrate generality and stress-test the fixed 3-scale/3-ratio anchor design and 16-pixel stride.
- Sensitivity to design choices: Although anchors are central, the paper provides limited analysis on:
  - Impact of anchor scales/ratios beyond {128,256,512} and {1:1, 1:2, 2:1}.
  - Sensitivity to the IoU thresholds (0.7/0.3) for labeling and to NMS threshold (0.7).
  - Effect of stride (16) and receptive field on small-object recall; dilated conv or smaller-stride variants might help.
- “Translation invariance” wording: The learned functions are shared across locations, making the method translation equivariant rather than invariant; boundary handling (ignoring cross-boundary anchors during training) may bias learning near edges.
- Fairness of comparisons: While the paper argues against CPU-bound proposal baselines, a stronger comparison would include GPU-optimized EB/SS or at least a matched wall-clock budget comparison for downstream detection (the authors partly address compute sharing; still, EB at 0.2s vs RPN “free” invites a compute-normalized accuracy-speed plot).
- Limited error analysis: The paper briefly reports recall-vs-IoU curves but offers limited category-wise or size-wise failure analysis (e.g., recall for small vs medium vs large boxes, truncation/occlusion). The claim that NMS “does not harm” mAP is interesting—quantifying false positive types and missed true positives would improve understanding.

Quality
Overall technically sound and well-supported by experiments. The ablations convincingly tie detection mAP to proposal quality and feature sharing. Some implementation choices (e.g., λ setting and normalization) are relegated to footnotes; more explicit guidance would improve reproducibility.

Clarity
Generally well written and organized. The training pipeline and loss are clear. Suggestions:
- Clarify normalization constants (Ncls, Nreg) and the exact λ used in main experiments.
- Explicitly distinguish translation equivariance from invariance.
- Clean up reference formatting and ensure all citations are complete.
- Provide pseudo-code or a schematic for the 4-step alternating optimization.

Originality
The combination of an FCN proposal head over shared features, anchor tiling with aspect ratios and scales, and an integrated training recipe is novel relative to contemporaneous work. While related concepts exist (sliding-window regression, MultiBox), the formulation is distinct and influential.

Significance
High. This work meaningfully advances the speed-accuracy frontier, removes the proposal bottleneck, and provides a widely reusable building block. It is likely to be adopted broadly and to catalyze subsequent research on unified detection.

Requests for additional experiments/analysis
- Joint or approximate joint end-to-end training vs alternating, with stability and accuracy comparison.
- COCO experiments, with small/medium/large recall and AP breakdowns.
- Sensitivity studies on anchor configurations (scales, ratios, k), stride (including dilations), IoU thresholds, and NMS threshold.
- Compute-normalized accuracy-speed trade-offs versus EB/SS and a dense sliding-window one-stage baseline with matched backbone and image pyramid.
- Ablation on ignoring vs including cross-boundary anchors (with proper clipping) to assess border effects.

Minor comments
- Consider reporting proposal recall at higher IoU (e.g., 0.85–0.9) and the proposal count needed to reach fixed recall targets.
- Clarify whether RPN objectness is calibrated across images or requires per-image normalization.
- Fix typos and incomplete references.

Arguments for acceptance
- Elegant, simple, and practical approach with strong empirical gains and clear impact.
- Solid ablations and clear positioning w.r.t. prior work.
- Enables truly end-to-end, GPU-efficient detection.

Arguments against acceptance
- Limited dataset scope and sensitivity analysis given the centrality of anchor design.
- No investigation of joint training.
- Some comparisons could be more rigorous under matched compute.

Overall recommendation: Accept (strong)
Confidence: High
