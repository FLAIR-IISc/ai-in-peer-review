Summary
The paper proposes Region Proposal Networks (RPNs) that generate class-agnostic object proposals by sliding a small fully convolutional subnetwork over shared backbone feature maps. Each spatial location predicts k anchor boxes (multiple scales/aspect ratios) with an objectness score and bounding-box regression. The RPN shares convolutional features with a Fast R-CNN detector, making proposal generation nearly cost-free. Training proceeds with an alternating 4-step scheme to enable feature sharing without joint optimization instability. On PASCAL VOC 2007/2012, the method attains higher mAP than Fast R-CNN with Selective Search/EdgeBoxes while being substantially faster (e.g., ~10ms marginal cost for proposals on GPU; ~5 fps end-to-end with VGG-16). Ablations analyze shared vs. unshared features, the effect of proposal count, cls vs. reg contributions, NMS, and an OverFeat-like one-stage variant.

Relation to prior work
The work builds directly on region-based CNN detectors (R-CNN, SPP-net, Fast R-CNN) and addresses the proposal bottleneck. Compared to OverFeat (sliding-window one-stage) and MultiBox (predicts multiple boxes from FC layers), this paper’s key differences are translation-invariant anchors, fewer parameters per location, and feature sharing with the detector. The approach aligns with trends toward shared convolutional computation (SPP, Fast R-CNN) while replacing hand-crafted CPU proposals (Selective Search, EdgeBoxes) with a learned GPU module.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The formulation (two-branch cls/reg, anchor parameterization, smooth L1 loss, sampling strategy) is standard and well-motivated. The alternating training scheme is pragmatic and converges empirically.
- Empirical support: Results on VOC07/12 include ablations (proposal count, NMS, cls/reg ablations, sharing vs. not, backbone depth) and comparisons to SS/EB. The OverFeat-like comparison demonstrates that two-stage proposal+detector outperforms dense sliding windows.
- Concerns:
  - Evaluation scope is limited to PASCAL VOC; no MS COCO or ILSVRC detection, which would better test generalization and small-object behavior.
  - Comparisons to MultiBox are mostly architectural/parametric; a direct accuracy/speed comparison is absent.
  - Runtime comparisons favor RPN because SS/EB are CPU implementations; a GPU-optimized proposal baseline is not provided.
  - The alternating 4-step training is somewhat ad hoc; no analysis of stability or convergence vs. joint training.
  - Variance over runs and statistical significance are not reported.

Clarity
The paper is generally clear, well-organized, and provides useful implementation details (anchor settings, sampling, thresholds). A few items would benefit from clarification: sensitivity to anchor choices/IoU thresholds, stride effects on small objects, and precise accounting of runtime (including preprocessing, NMS, and proposal counts). References have minor formatting issues.

Originality
Moderate. Using shared convolutional features for proposals and the anchor mechanism represents a thoughtful and effective integration of known components (FCNs, bbox regression, objectness) rather than a fundamentally new detection paradigm. The translation-invariant anchor design and the efficient, shared computation are the main novel aspects relative to MultiBox/OverFeat.

Significance
High practical impact. The method removes the proposal bottleneck, improves mAP over strong baselines, and yields a usable, faster pipeline on commodity GPUs. Given the centrality of proposals in the then-current detection stack, this is likely to influence follow-up work and deployment.

Strengths
- Elegant, unified architecture with effective feature sharing; proposals are nearly free.
- Strong speed/accuracy trade-off; fewer proposals (e.g., 300) suffice without degrading mAP.
- Thorough ablations isolating cls vs. reg, NMS impact, and shared vs. unshared training.
- Clear improvement over widely used hand-crafted proposals (SS/EB) and a fair OverFeat-style one-stage comparison favoring two-stage detectors.

Weaknesses
- Limited datasets; lacks evaluation on larger/more diverse benchmarks where small-object recall is critical.
- No direct empirical comparison to contemporary learned proposal methods (e.g., MultiBox) under matched backbones and hardware.
- Runtime fairness: absence of a GPU-tuned SS/EB baseline leaves some ambiguity about the magnitude of speedup attributable to algorithmic vs. engineering choices.
- Anchor design and hyperparameter sensitivity (scales/ratios, IoU thresholds, NMS threshold) are not systematically explored.
- Alternating training is heuristic; joint end-to-end training feasibility and potential gains are left to future work.

Questions for the authors
1. How sensitive are results to anchor scales/ratios and the positive/negative IoU thresholds (0.7/0.3)? Any guidance for transfer to new domains?
2. What is the effect of using smaller stride (e.g., dilated conv or earlier feature maps) on small-object recall and overall mAP?
3. Can you report proposal recall vs. IoU for small vs. large objects separately?
4. What is the per-image variance in proposal count after NMS for the 300-proposal setting, and how does mAP vary with that?
5. Any experiments on joint training (single-stage end-to-end) and observed issues?

Suggestions for improvement
- Add results on a larger dataset (e.g., COCO) to validate generalization and small-object performance.
- Provide a direct empirical comparison with MultiBox (matched backbone/hardware), and include a GPU-optimized SS/EB baseline for runtime fairness.
- Include sensitivity studies for anchor configurations and thresholds.
- Report training stability/variance and test-time mAP vs. number of proposals in the shared-features setting.
- Clarify runtime breakdown (including NMS and per-proposal FC cost) and standardize the definition of “fps.”

Overall recommendation
Accept. While the novelty is incremental, the method is technically sound, well-validated on standard benchmarks, and offers a substantial, practical advance by eliminating the proposal bottleneck with a learned, shared module. The work is likely to be influential and widely adopted.

Confidence
High.
