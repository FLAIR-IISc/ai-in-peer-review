This paper introduces Region Proposal Networks (RPNs) as a method for generating object proposals within a deep learning framework, aiming to alleviate the computational bottleneck posed by traditional methods like Selective Search. The core idea—sharing convolutional layers between proposal generation and object detection—is presented as a significant efficiency gain. While the authors demonstrate improved speed and competitive accuracy on PASCAL VOC benchmarks, I have several concerns regarding the novelty and thoroughness of the evaluation.

The paper’s strength lies in its practical demonstration of end-to-end trainable region proposals. Integrating proposal generation directly into the detection network is a logical step, and the reported speedup is certainly appealing. The ablation studies, while not exhaustive, provide some insight into the contribution of the different components of the RPN.

However, the presentation relies heavily on framing the work as a substantial algorithmic advancement, which I find overstated. The idea of using a deep network for proposal generation isn’t entirely novel; prior work like MultiBox (referenced in the paper) already explored this direction. The key contribution here appears to be the specific architecture and training procedure for RPNs, alongside the emphasis on shared convolutional layers. While this combination is effective, it’s presented as a breakthrough rather than an incremental improvement.

My primary concern is the limited comparison to existing methods beyond Selective Search and EdgeBoxes. The authors briefly acknowledge OverFeat and MultiBox, but the discussion lacks depth. A more detailed comparison, particularly regarding architectural similarities and differences, would be crucial. Furthermore, the paper doesn’t sufficiently address how this approach differs from and improves upon simply training a fully convolutional network for direct object detection (i.e., eliminating the region proposal step altogether). The justification for maintaining a two-stage process isn’t entirely convincing.

The experimental evaluation, while solid, feels somewhat narrowly focused on PASCAL VOC. Results on a more challenging dataset, such as COCO, would provide a better indication of the method's generalizability. The ablation studies are limited in scope. For instance, a more thorough analysis of the impact of different anchor sizes and aspect ratios would be valuable. The observation that removing the cls layer degrades performance is interesting, but lacks a detailed explanation. Why is accurate ranking of proposals so critical?

The loss function, while standard, could benefit from a more detailed discussion of the weighting parameter λ. The authors mention setting it to 10 empirically, but provide no justification for this choice. A sensitivity analysis would be helpful.

The claim of “nearly cost-free” proposals (10ms) feels somewhat misleading. While a significant improvement over Selective Search, 10ms is not negligible and contributes to the overall processing time. The framing suggests a near-zero computational overhead, which isn’t entirely accurate.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Demonstrates a practical and effective method for accelerating region proposal generation.
*   Achieves competitive accuracy on PASCAL VOC benchmarks.
*   The idea of sharing convolutional layers between proposal generation and detection is a valuable contribution.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The novelty of the approach is overstated. Similar ideas have been explored in prior work.
*   The comparison to related methods is insufficient.
*   The experimental evaluation is limited in scope.
*   The claim of "nearly cost-free" proposals is potentially misleading.
*   The justification for the two-stage approach (RPN + detection) is not fully convincing.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper presents a solid engineering contribution, but falls short of being a groundbreaking advance. The improvements are incremental rather than revolutionary. While the results are encouraging, the lack of a more thorough comparison to existing methods and a more comprehensive experimental evaluation raise concerns. I would recommend **weak acceptance**, contingent upon the authors addressing the weaknesses outlined above, particularly by providing a more detailed comparison to related work (especially MultiBox) and expanding the experimental evaluation to include more challenging datasets. The language used to describe the innovation should be toned down to reflect the incremental nature of the work.
