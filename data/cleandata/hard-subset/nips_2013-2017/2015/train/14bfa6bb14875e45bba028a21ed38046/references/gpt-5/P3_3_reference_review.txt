Summary
The paper proposes Region Proposal Networks (RPNs), a fully convolutional module that predicts class-agnostic object proposals (objectness scores and box regressions) directly from the shared convolutional feature maps used by a Fast R-CNN detector. The key ideas are: (1) anchor boxes at each spatial location with multiple scales and aspect ratios provide translation-invariant reference boxes; (2) a small conv “head” jointly predicts objectness and box deltas for k anchors per location; (3) a multi-task loss and end-to-end training of the proposal module; and (4) an alternating 4-step training scheme to share conv features between the RPN and the detector. On PASCAL VOC 2007/2012, the unified system outperforms Fast R-CNN with Selective Search/EdgeBoxes while reducing proposal time to about 10 ms on GPU and achieving 5–17 fps end-to-end depending on the backbone (VGG-16 vs. ZF).

Relation to prior work
The work is positioned against: (i) region proposal methods using hand-crafted cues (Selective Search, EdgeBoxes); (ii) class-agnostic deep proposal generators (MultiBox) that use large, non-translation-invariant anchor sets; (iii) one-stage sliding-window detectors (OverFeat). Compared with MultiBox, the proposed anchor mechanism reduces parameters and improves translation invariance; compared with OverFeat, the two-stage design maintains efficiency via shared features but gains accuracy by refining class-agnostic proposals with class-specific detection.

Quality
- Methodology: Technically sound and well-motivated. The anchor parameterization, multi-task loss, and smooth L1 regression are appropriate. Sampling strategy (256 anchors/image with up to 1:1 pos:neg) addresses class imbalance. Ignoring cross-boundary anchors stabilizes training.
- Training: The alternating 4-step scheme is pragmatic and converges; the paper acknowledges that joint training is nontrivial and leaves it open. The implementation details (learning rates, layers tuned, input scale) are sufficiently specified for reproduction; code is provided.
- Experiments: Evaluations on VOC07/12 include ablations: shared vs. unshared features; proposal count; roles of cls vs. reg; NMS effects; backbone capacity (ZF vs. VGG). mAP and Recall-vs-IoU analyses are reported. Comparisons to SS/EB are fair in accuracy, and runtime advantages are substantiated on GPU.
- Caveats: Some choices are heuristic (IoU thresholds 0.7/0.3, anchor scales/aspect ratios, NMS at 0.7). Sensitivity analysis for these hyperparameters is limited. The fairness of runtime comparisons to CPU-only SS/EB could be further contextualized, though the argument for shared computation is valid. The dataset scope is VOC; generalization to larger, more diverse datasets is not shown.

Clarity
The paper is clearly written and organized. The architecture, loss, and training scheme are described with enough detail for implementation. Minor issues: normalization constants and λ are explained partly in a footnote; it would help to consolidate this in the main text. Figures/tables (not included here) reportedly illustrate anchor behavior and recall trends; citations contain some formatting issues.

Originality
The central contributions—anchor-based, translation-invariant fully conv proposal generation and feature sharing with a downstream detector—are novel relative to contemporaneous work. While the components (multi-task loss, bounding-box regression, shared conv features) have appeared separately, their combination into a unified proposal-plus-detection architecture with an efficient training recipe is original and impactful. The comparison to MultiBox/OverFeat clarifies distinctions.

Significance
High. The approach removes the proposal bottleneck, enabling near real-time two-stage detection, and improves accuracy. The anchor mechanism and shared-FCN design are likely to influence subsequent detectors and segmentation systems. The work advances the state of the art on standard benchmarks and will be broadly adopted in practice.

Strengths
- Elegant, principled integration of proposal generation into the detection network with shared computation.
- Strong empirical gains in both speed and accuracy; thorough ablations that illuminate where improvements come from (cls vs. reg, proposal counts, sharing).
- Simple, general anchor mechanism that avoids large non-translation-invariant output layers and reduces overfitting risk on small datasets.
- Clear exposition and open-source code, enhancing reproducibility.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Hyperparameter sensitivity: Provide experiments varying the set of anchor scales/aspect ratios, stride, NMS threshold, and the λ/normalization choices, to assess robustness and guide practitioners.
- Border handling: Ignoring cross-boundary anchors during training could bias against near-border objects; quantify its effect (e.g., AP for objects touching image edges).
- Small objects and stride: With a stride of 16, small-object recall may be limited; report per-size recall/AP or experiments with smaller strides/dilations to substantiate the claim that accuracy is “good” despite stride.
- Joint training: While the alternating schedule works, a brief empirical attempt at truly joint end-to-end optimization (e.g., with stabilized proposals or delayed gradients) would strengthen the claim of a unified network.
- Benchmark scope: Results are only on VOC; adding experiments on a larger benchmark (e.g., ILSVRC detection) would better demonstrate scalability and generality.
- Runtime comparisons: Clarify end-to-end latency components and report variance; consider discussing the potential speed of SS/EB if reimplemented on GPU to fully characterize the engineering vs. algorithmic trade-offs.

Questions for authors
- How sensitive are results to the positive/negative IoU thresholds and to the 1:1 pos:neg sampling? Does hard-negative mining further help?
- How many anchors per location are necessary? Do learned anchors (e.g., via k-means per dataset but enforced translation invariance) improve over the hand-picked 3×3 set?
- Does applying NMS during training (e.g., to define top-k positives) affect convergence or final accuracy?
- Can the RPN be class-aware without sacrificing speed, and would that help in crowded scenes?

Pros for acceptance
- Clear, novel contribution that addresses a major bottleneck in detection.
- Strong empirical evidence with careful ablations.
- High potential impact and broad applicability.
- Good clarity and released code.

Cons for acceptance
- Limited analysis of hyperparameter sensitivity and small-object/border cases.
- No results beyond VOC; limited evidence of scalability.
- Joint training left as future work.

Recommendation
Accept. The paper is technically sound, clearly written, and makes a significant, original contribution that substantively advances object detection in both accuracy and efficiency. The weaknesses are addressable and do not detract from the core contribution.
