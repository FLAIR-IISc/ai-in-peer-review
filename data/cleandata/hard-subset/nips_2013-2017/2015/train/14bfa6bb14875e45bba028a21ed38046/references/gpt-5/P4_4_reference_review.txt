Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes Region Proposal Networks (RPNs) that generate class-agnostic object proposals directly from shared convolutional feature maps of a detection network, enabling a unified, near real-time two-stage detector (RPN + Fast R-CNN). The key idea is to slide a small fully convolutional subnetwork over shared conv maps, predicting objectness scores and bounding-box regressions for k “anchors” (multiple scales and aspect ratios) at each spatial location. The approach is translation-invariant with a compact output (2k + 4k channels), and is trained end-to-end with a multi-task loss. A pragmatic 4-step alternating training scheme is used to share features between RPN and Fast R-CNN. On PASCAL VOC 2007/2012, the method yields higher mAP than Selective Search (SS) or EdgeBoxes (EB) as proposals with Fast R-CNN, while reducing proposal time to ~10 ms when sharing conv features; overall system runs at 5–17 fps depending on backbone (VGG-16 or ZF).

Relative to prior art: R-CNN/SS [6,22] pioneered region-based CNN detection; SPP/Fast R-CNN [7,5] shared conv features but still relied on external proposals (SS/EB). OverFeat [18] and MultiBox [3,20] used deep nets for localization and proposals, with MultiBox predicting many boxes from non-translation-invariant priors; the current work differs by (i) translation-invariant anchors tied to conv locations (lower parameter count), (ii) explicit feature sharing and a two-stage cascade with Fast R-CNN, and (iii) efficient end-to-end training. The method builds on FCN ideas [14] and standard bounding box regression [6].

Strengths
- Technical soundness: Clear architectural design (anchor-based FCN head), well-defined multi-task loss, and a reproducible alternating training scheme for feature sharing. Ablations isolate contributions of cls vs reg heads, NMS, number of proposals, feature sharing, and backbone depth.
- Empirical validation: Consistent improvements over SS/EB in mAP when coupled with Fast R-CNN, particularly with VGG-16 (69.9% mAP vs SS baseline, up to 73.2% with extended training data). Proposal recall-to-IoU curves are competitive while using as few as 300 proposals.
- Efficiency: Orders-of-magnitude speed-up of proposal generation through shared conv features (~10 ms), yielding practical end-to-end detection throughput (VGG-16: ~198 ms; ZF: up to 17 fps).
- Novelty/positioning: Conceptual unification of proposal generation and detection via shared convolutional features and translation-invariant anchors; contrasts clearly with MultiBox/OverFeat and sliding-window one-stage emulation.
- Clarity and completeness: Training details, hyperparameters, anchor settings, sampling strategy, and implementation notes are given; code availability is stated.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Training suboptimality: Joint end-to-end training of RPN and detector is not attempted; instead, an alternating scheme fixes shared layers in later steps. While pragmatic, a joint optimization analysis or results would strengthen claims.
- Dataset scope: Evaluations are limited to PASCAL VOC 2007/2012. Broader validation (e.g., COCO), small-object analysis, or robustness across domains would increase significance.
- Design sensitivity: Anchor choices (3 scales, 3 aspect ratios), IoU thresholds for labeling (0.7/0.3), stride (16 px), and NMS threshold (0.7) may affect performance. More sensitivity analyses could help practitioners.
- Bounding box geometry: Proposals are axis-aligned; no exploration of rotated boxes or deformable anchors, which may affect certain categories/aspect ratios.
- Comparison breadth: While SS/EB and one-stage emulation are included, comparisons to GPU-accelerated proposal methods or contemporary learned proposals (e.g., more detailed MultiBox comparisons on same backbones/datasets) are limited.

Quality
The methodology is technically sound and coherently motivated. Loss functions, parameterizations, and training procedures follow established practice, with careful design choices (e.g., sampling to balance positives/negatives, ignoring boundary-crossing anchors in training). Claims are supported with appropriate experiments: end-to-end detector mAP, ablations on proposal counts and components, recall-to-IoU curves, and timing breakdowns. The study is complete for the target setting and demonstrates both accuracy and efficiency.

Clarity
The paper is clearly written and well-organized. The role of anchors, the two sibling heads (cls/reg), and the alternating training procedure are described in sufficient detail. Implementation specifics (scales, aspect ratios, strides, sampling, optimization hyperparameters) are provided and should enable reproduction. Minor clarity suggestions: (i) consolidate and clarify normalization constants (Ncls, Nreg) and lambda selection; (ii) provide a brief sensitivity analysis (even in supplement) for anchor configurations and NMS threshold; (iii) fix formatting issues in references.

Originality
The combination of translation-invariant anchors at every conv location, a lightweight FCN head for proposals, and explicit sharing with a region-based detector is novel relative to SS/EB and to prior deep proposal methods (OverFeat/MultiBox). The alternating training to learn shared features is a practical contribution. While components draw from known techniques (FCNs, bbox regression, Fast R-CNN), the integration and resulting speed/accuracy trade-off are original and impactful.

Significance
High. The work removes the region proposal bottleneck, enabling practical real-time-capable two-stage detectors with state-of-the-art accuracy on VOC. The approach is likely to be widely adopted, adapted to stronger backbones, and extended to other detection tasks. It meaningfully advances the field’s ability to unify proposal and detection within a single deep architecture.

Reproducibility
Good. Architecture, losses, training schedules, sampling, anchors, and preprocessing are specified; code availability is noted. Additional release of trained models and exact evaluation scripts would further help.

Questions for authors
- How sensitive are results to anchor scales/ratios and to the NMS threshold? Are category-wise APs affected differently?
- Can joint end-to-end training (without alternating/fixing layers) converge and improve performance?
- How does performance vary with smaller stride (e.g., using dilated convs) for small objects?
- What is the runtime/memory overhead of RPN without shared features, and how does it compare to optimized GPU implementations of SS/EB?

Arguments for acceptance
- Strong technical contribution with elegant integration of proposals and detection.
- Clear empirical gains in both speed and accuracy; extensive ablations.
- High potential impact and adoption; code available.
- Well written and accessible.

Arguments against acceptance
- Limited dataset scope; no evaluation on larger/more diverse datasets.
- Absence of joint training results; some design heuristics lack sensitivity analysis.
- Comparisons to alternative learned proposal methods could be broadened.
