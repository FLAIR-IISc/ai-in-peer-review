This paper proposes Region Proposal Networks (RPNs) as a means to accelerate object detection by moving the computationally expensive region proposal step onto the GPU and, crucially, sharing computation with the detection network itself. The core idea – leveraging the convolutional features already computed for detection to *also* generate proposals – is intuitively appealing and presented as a significant improvement over existing methods like Selective Search and EdgeBoxes. The authors demonstrate competitive accuracy with faster runtime on PASCAL VOC.

While the idea is promising, and the results presented are solid, I have several reservations regarding the paper’s novelty and the degree to which the presented improvements are truly algorithmic versus engineering. The claim of a near “cost-free” proposal computation is somewhat overstated; while the marginal cost *given* the detection network is low, the entire system still requires substantial computational resources. Moreover, the work builds heavily on established techniques (Fast R-CNN, fully convolutional networks) and, while the combination is effective, the fundamental innovation feels incremental. 

The paper is well-written and clearly organized. The motivation is well-articulated, and the technical details are presented with sufficient clarity. The ablation studies are helpful in understanding the contribution of different components (cls vs. reg layers, shared vs. unshared features). The inclusion of running time comparisons is valuable. However, the presentation of results could be more rigorous; for instance, a more detailed comparison to state-of-the-art detectors *without* relying on Fast R-CNN as the backbone would strengthen the argument for the RPN’s efficacy.  The discussion of related work is adequate but could be more exhaustive, particularly concerning recent advancements in anchor-based object detection.

The originality of the approach is a concern. While elegantly integrating proposal generation into the detection pipeline is valuable, the underlying concepts – fully convolutional networks, anchor boxes, and shared feature representations – are all well-established. The MultiBox method [20], discussed briefly in the related work, already explores generating proposals from a network and shares similarities with the RPN approach. The authors attempt to differentiate their work through the translation-invariant anchor design, which is a worthwhile improvement, but doesn't fundamentally alter the underlying paradigm. The paper feels less like a breakthrough and more like a clever and well-executed engineering optimization of existing components.

Regarding significance, the improved runtime and competitive accuracy are certainly valuable contributions. A practical object detection system running at 5-17 fps is a step forward. However, the reliance on very deep networks (VGG-16) raises questions about scalability and applicability to resource-constrained environments. While the improvements are demonstrable on PASCAL VOC, it remains unclear how well this approach would generalize to more complex datasets or real-time applications with stringent latency requirements.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Demonstrated speed improvements over existing region proposal methods.
*   Competitive object detection accuracy on PASCAL VOC.
*   Clear and well-written presentation.
*   Helpful ablation studies.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Incremental innovation; builds heavily on established techniques.
*   Limited novelty beyond the integration of existing concepts.
*   Reliance on computationally expensive deep networks (VGG-16).
*   Insufficient comparison to state-of-the-art detectors *independent* of Fast R-CNN.
*   The claim of "cost-free" proposal computation is misleading.



Overall, this is a solid technical paper that presents a practical and effective approach to object detection. However, given the incremental nature of the innovation and the reliance on established techniques, I believe its contribution falls short of the exceptionally high bar for acceptance at NIPS. I would lean towards rejection, but would not be strongly opposed to a weaker acceptance if other reviewers find the contributions more significant. I recommend the authors address the concerns regarding novelty and provide a more comprehensive comparison to existing object detection methods to strengthen their claims.
