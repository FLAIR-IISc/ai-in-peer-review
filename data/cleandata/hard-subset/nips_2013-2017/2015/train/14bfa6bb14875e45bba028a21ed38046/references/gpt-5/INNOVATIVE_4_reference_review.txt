Summary
The paper introduces Region Proposal Networks (RPNs), a fully convolutional module that generates class-agnostic object proposals by sliding a small network over shared convolutional feature maps. Each spatial location predicts k proposals via translation-invariant anchors covering multiple scales and aspect ratios, jointly optimized with objectness classification and box regression losses. The RPN shares the backbone with a Fast R-CNN detector, making proposals nearly “free” at test time. A pragmatic 4-step alternating training scheme yields a unified network with shared conv layers. On PASCAL VOC, the approach improves mAP over strong baselines (Selective Search, EdgeBoxes) while dramatically reducing runtime (∼10 ms overhead for proposals; 5–17 fps end-to-end depending on backbone). Ablations dissect the roles of the classification/regression branches, NMS, number of proposals, feature sharing, and one-stage vs two-stage detection. The work is positioned relative to OverFeat (one-stage detection with sliding windows) and MultiBox (dense learned boxes), arguing for translation-invariant anchors and two-stage cascades.

Relation to prior work
- Builds directly on Fast R-CNN and SPP by reusing shared conv features; addresses the dominant runtime bottleneck (CPU-based proposal methods) in region-based detectors.
- Contrasts with MultiBox’s non-translation-invariant anchors and OverFeat’s one-stage sliding windows by introducing simple, parameter-efficient anchors and a two-stage cascade.
- Advances the movement toward fully convolutional, shared-computation detection pipelines.

Strengths
- Novelty and elegance: Translation-invariant anchors + shared conv features for proposals is a simple, powerful idea that neatly removes the long-standing proposal bottleneck while improving accuracy.
- Practical impact: Orders-of-magnitude speedup for proposals with minimal engineering, yielding a fast, accurate, and deployable system; code release further increases impact.
- Solid empirical evidence: Clear ablations (feature sharing, role of cls/reg, NMS, proposal counts), VOC07/12 results, and comparisons to SS/EB; consistent story that 300 proposals suffice with high recall and that two-stage beats one-stage.
- Design insights: Anchor parameterization, smooth L1 loss, sampling strategy, and alternating training form a coherent recipe likely to be widely adopted.

Weaknesses
- Dataset scope: Experiments focus on VOC. Generalization to larger-scale, denser datasets (e.g., COCO) and to small objects is not evaluated; recall/IoU curves help diagnosis but are only a proxy for downstream detection.
- Training scheme: Alternating 4-step training is pragmatic but not fully end-to-end; convergence properties and potential benefits of joint training remain unexplored.
- Hyperparameter sensitivity: Limited analysis of anchor configurations (scales/ratios), feature stride, image scale (s=600), IoU thresholds (0.7/0.3), and NMS threshold; these choices may affect small objects and crowded scenes.
- Fairness of runtime comparisons: While sharing makes proposals “free,” comparisons to GPU-optimized SS/EB or other high-speed proposal methods are not included; memory usage is not reported.
- Boundary handling: Ignoring cross-boundary anchors during training is a practical fix but somewhat ad hoc; impacts near-border object detection are not analyzed.

Quality
Overall technically sound with well-supported claims. The ablations convincingly argue for the design choices, and the speed gains are substantial. Some aspects (hyperparameter sensitivity, broader datasets, joint training) would strengthen the empirical case but, given the novelty and clarity of improvements, the current validation is acceptable.

Clarity
The paper is clearly written and well organized. The training procedure and architecture are explained in sufficient detail for reproduction. Minor suggestions:
- Clarify the normalization/weighting of the loss terms (Ncls, Nreg, λ) in the main text rather than in a footnote.
- Provide a concise diagram of the alternating training schedule.
- Report standard deviations or multiple runs for mAP to assess variability.

Originality
High. While building on Fast R-CNN/SPP, the introduction of translation-invariant anchors within a shared, fully convolutional proposal network, trained to near-zero marginal cost, is a notable conceptual advance over SS/EB and distinguishes itself from MultiBox and OverFeat.

Significance
High. The method removes the key bottleneck in region-based detection and makes accurate detection practical in real time. The anchors + shared conv paradigm is likely to influence subsequent detection architectures and become a standard component.

Questions and suggestions for authors
- Sensitivity to anchor design: How do alternative scales/ratios or learned anchor sets affect mAP/recall, especially for small objects?
- Joint training: Did you attempt simultaneous optimization of RPN and Fast R-CNN with stabilized proposals (e.g., via a proposal buffer)? Any convergence issues?
- Stride and feature level: How does using earlier feature maps (smaller stride) affect proposal quality vs. speed?
- Boundary handling: Can you train with boundary-aware regression targets (e.g., masked losses) instead of ignoring anchors?
- Runtime/memory: Report GPU memory and batch time breakdown; include a comparison to GPU-ported SS/EB to contextualize “free” proposals.
- Generalization: Preliminary results on a larger dataset (or at least small-object recall analysis) would strengthen claims.

Pros for acceptance
- Clear, impactful new concept (RPN with translation-invariant anchors) that unifies proposals and detection.
- Strong speed/accuracy gains with clean engineering and available code.
- Thorough, insightful ablations; compelling argument for two-stage cascades.

Cons against acceptance
- Limited dataset scope and sensitivity studies.
- Alternating training rather than fully joint end-to-end optimization.
- Some heuristic choices (anchors, thresholds, boundary handling) not deeply analyzed.

Recommendation
Accept (strong). The paper introduces a significant and influential idea that advances both the conceptual and practical state of object detection.

Confidence
High.
