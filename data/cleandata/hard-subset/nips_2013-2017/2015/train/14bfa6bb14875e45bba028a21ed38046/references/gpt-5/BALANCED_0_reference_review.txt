Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper proposes Region Proposal Networks (RPNs) that generate class-agnostic object proposals by operating directly on shared convolutional feature maps, enabling end-to-end training and near-free proposal computation when paired with a Fast R-CNN detector. The core idea is to slide a small fully convolutional sub-network over shared conv maps, predicting for each location k anchor boxes (across multiple scales and aspect ratios) with objectness scores and bounding-box regressions. The authors introduce an alternating 4-step training scheme to learn shared features between the RPN and Fast R-CNN. On PASCAL VOC 2007/2012, the joint system outperforms Selective Search (SS) and EdgeBoxes (EB) in mAP while being considerably faster (e.g., ~10ms marginal cost for proposals and 5–17 fps end-to-end depending on backbone).

This work builds directly on R-CNN [6], SPPnet [7], and Fast R-CNN [5] by extending feature sharing to proposal generation, addressing the remaining computational bottleneck. It contrasts with OverFeat [18] (one-stage, class-specific sliding-window detection) and MultiBox [3,20] (class-agnostic proposal prediction from fully connected outputs), offering a translation-invariant, parameter-efficient anchor mechanism and a two-stage cascade that retains the benefits of region-based detectors. The paper appropriately compares to SS/EB and provides ablations to analyze classification (objectness) vs regression contributions and the number of proposals.

Strengths:
- Clear, impactful idea: unify proposal generation and detection via shared conv features. Anchors introduce a simple, translation-invariant parameterization that is efficient and effective.
- Strong empirical results: On VOC 2007/2012, RPN+Fast R-CNN matches or improves mAP over SS/EB with far lower runtime. Useful ablations (shared vs unshared features, role of cls vs reg, effect of proposal counts, Recall-IoU curves) support claims.
- Practical training pipeline: Alternating fine-tuning is simple and achieves stable convergence; code availability increases reproducibility.
- Sound engineering: Choices such as smooth L1 regression loss, sampling strategy to balance positives/negatives, NMS on proposals, and careful handling of cross-boundary anchors are well motivated.
- Significance: Removes the dominant bottleneck in region-based detection and will likely be widely adopted. The approach is general to deeper backbones and potentially other tasks.

Weaknesses / points for improvement:
- Limited dataset scope: Evaluation is primarily on PASCAL VOC. Given the speed/accuracy claims, additional datasets (e.g., a larger-scale benchmark) would strengthen significance and robustness claims.
- Joint training: The paper uses an alternating scheme. While pragmatic, it stops short of demonstrating fully joint end-to-end optimization of proposals and detector, and does not analyze convergence behavior if trained jointly.
- Anchor design sensitivity: Anchors are hand-chosen (three scales, three aspect ratios). The paper could analyze sensitivity to these choices, e.g., more aspect ratios, learning scales/ratios, or per-class anchors.
- Small object performance and stride: With stride 16, small object recall may be challenging. Although recall curves are reported, a more explicit analysis of small/medium/large objects and of stride (or dilated conv) effects would improve clarity.
- Fairness of runtime comparisons: SS/EB are CPU implementations; while the paper argues shared convs make RPN uniquely efficient, a discussion or experiment with a GPU-optimized SS/EB baseline would make the speed comparisons more robust.
- Clarity/details: Normalization constants and λ are partly relegated to footnotes and are a bit confusing; hyperparameter selection (IoU thresholds 0.7/0.3, NMS 0.7, 256 anchors per minibatch) could be justified more systematically.

Quality:
Technically sound with coherent loss formulation and training procedure; ablations thoughtfully isolate key design choices. The work is an integrated system paper rather than theoretical, but the empirical evidence is compelling. Some comparisons to concurrent deep proposal methods (e.g., MultiBox) are discussed conceptually; direct mAP/running-time comparisons would further support originality and effectiveness.

Clarity:
Generally well written and organized. Architecture and training are clearly described; implementation details are sufficient to reproduce. Figures and tables (as described) are informative. Minor suggestions: streamline the loss normalization discussion; add a short algorithmic schematic of the four-step training; explicitly state per-image average number of proposals after NMS.

Originality:
Using shared conv features for proposals and the anchor-based, translation-invariant formulation for a class-agnostic proposal FCN is a substantial and novel contribution relative to SS/EB and prior deep proposal methods. The two-stage cascade with learned proposals is a clean conceptual advance over Fast R-CNN.

Significance:
High. This removes the remaining bottleneck in region-based detection, achieving a new speed/accuracy trade-off that is likely to become standard practice. The method is broadly applicable and easy to integrate with existing detectors.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Evaluate on an additional dataset (e.g., a larger or more diverse benchmark) to test generalization and small-object behavior; report AP by object size.
- Provide sensitivity analyses: number of anchors per location, alternative scales/aspect ratios, and IoU thresholds for positive/negative labels and NMS.
- Explore reducing stride (e.g., via dilated conv) or adding a lightweight feature pyramid to improve small object recall, and quantify the speed/accuracy trade-off.
- Compare directly with a strong MultiBox baseline in terms of proposal recall, mAP, and runtime under identical backbones.
- Investigate joint end-to-end training (single-stage optimization) and report stability and performance relative to alternating training.
- Analyze memory footprint and throughput on different GPUs; clarify the portion of runtime attributable to each stage under different proposal counts.
- Consider hard example mining or curriculum strategies for anchor sampling to further improve objectness classification.

Pros (arguments for acceptance):
- Simple, elegant method with clear impact; strong empirical gains in speed with equal or better accuracy.
- Anchors + shared convs are conceptually novel and practically effective.
- Thorough ablations and code availability; high potential for community adoption.

Cons (arguments against acceptance):
- Evaluation limited primarily to VOC; no direct experimental comparison to leading deep proposal methods like MultiBox.
- Alternating training leaves open the question of fully joint optimization.
- Limited analysis of anchor/stride design choices and small object performance.

Recommendation: Strong accept.

Reviewer confidence: High.
