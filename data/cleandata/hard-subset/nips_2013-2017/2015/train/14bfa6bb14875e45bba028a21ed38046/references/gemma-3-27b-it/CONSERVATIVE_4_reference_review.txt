This paper proposes Region Proposal Networks (RPNs) as a computationally efficient alternative to traditional region proposal methods like Selective Search and EdgeBoxes, aiming to accelerate object detection pipelines. The core idea is to leverage a deep convolutional network to generate proposals, sharing convolutional layers with the detection network itself, thereby reducing overall computational cost. The approach is evaluated on PASCAL VOC datasets, demonstrating competitive accuracy with improved speed compared to existing methods.

The paper is generally well-written and clearly presents the motivation and technical details of the proposed RPNs. The problem addressed – the computational bottleneck of region proposal generation – is indeed a significant one in object detection. The core concept of sharing convolutional features is logical and intuitively appealing. The ablation studies are helpful in dissecting the contributions of different components of the RPN.

However, despite the apparent advantages, I find myself skeptical about the overall significance of this work *as presented*. My primary concern revolves around the incremental nature of the contribution and the comparatively limited validation performed. While the speed improvement is notable, the accuracy gains over a strong baseline like Selective Search + Fast R-CNN are modest, and the improvement comes with the complexity of a new network architecture and training procedure. The authors claim the benefit of “nearly cost-free” proposal computation, but this is relative. The overhead of training the RPN and the complexity of the alternating optimization scheme are not fully explored.

Furthermore, the comparison to other deep learning-based proposal methods is lacking. While related work acknowledges MultiBox, the comparison is somewhat dismissive. A more thorough and quantitative comparison with MultiBox, particularly in terms of computational cost and accuracy, would be crucial. The paper mentions that MultiBox has more parameters, but doesn't translate that into actual training time or memory requirements. The claim about translation invariance is interesting, but its practical impact on performance isn't convincingly demonstrated. The choice of anchor scales and aspect ratios feels somewhat arbitrary and lacks justification beyond common practice.

The experimental evaluation, while comprehensive for PASCAL VOC, doesn’t sufficiently address the potential generalization ability of the approach to more complex datasets (e.g., COCO). The limited ablation studies don’t delve into the sensitivity of the method to hyperparameters or variations in network architecture. It's unclear how well RPN would perform in scenarios with significantly different object scales or aspect ratios.

Specifically, the one-stage vs. two-stage comparison, while illustrating a point about the benefit of cascaded approaches, feels slightly contrived. The implementation of the one-stage system deviates significantly from the main contribution and doesn't offer a compelling reason to dismiss other potential one-stage detection paradigms.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Addresses a relevant and important problem in object detection.
*   Presents a technically sound and logically consistent approach.
*   Demonstrates improved speed compared to traditional region proposal methods.
*   Clear writing and well-organized presentation.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Incremental improvement in accuracy over strong baselines.
*   Limited comparison to other deep learning-based proposal methods.
*   Insufficient validation on more complex datasets.
*   Lack of thorough analysis of hyperparameters and robustness.
*   The “nearly cost-free” claim requires further qualification.



Overall, while this paper presents a reasonable and well-executed technical contribution, I believe it falls short of the novelty and significance typically expected at NIPS. The improvements are incremental, and the validation is insufficient to convince me of the broad applicability and impact of the proposed approach. I recommend rejection, but encourage the authors to address the aforementioned concerns and consider submitting a revised version to a more appropriate venue.
