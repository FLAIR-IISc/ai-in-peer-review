Summary and relation to prior work:
This paper proposes Region Proposal Networks (RPNs), a fully-convolutional network that generates class-agnostic object proposals directly from the convolutional feature maps used by a downstream detector (Fast R-CNN). The core idea is to slide a small conv “head” over shared conv features and, at each spatial location, predict k proposals via translation-invariant “anchors” of multiple scales and aspect ratios, jointly learning objectness (cls) and bounding-box regression (reg). The authors introduce an alternating 4-step training scheme to enable feature sharing between the RPN and the detector, yielding an end-to-end unified system where the marginal cost of proposals is ≈10 ms on GPU.

Relative to prior work: compared to Selective Search and EdgeBoxes (hand-crafted, CPU-bound), the proposal mechanism is learned and GPU-friendly; compared to OverFeat (one-stage, class-specific sliding-window) and MultiBox (predicts many boxes from large crops with non-translation-invariant templates), the RPN uses local, translation-invariant anchors with far fewer parameters, shares features with the detector, and forms a two-stage cascade. The approach builds on R-CNN/SPPnet/Fast R-CNN by removing the proposal bottleneck while retaining high accuracy.

Strengths:
- Novelty and conceptual clarity: A simple, bold unification of proposal generation and detection via shared conv features and anchors. The translation-invariant anchor design is elegant and widely applicable.
- Practical impact: Proposals are nearly cost-free, reducing end-to-end latency to 5–17 fps with VGG/ZF while improving mAP over strong SS/EB baselines. Fewer proposals (≈300) suffice without degrading accuracy.
- Empirical support and analysis: Thorough ablations isolate the roles of cls and reg, sharing vs unshared features, proposal counts/NMS, and backbone strength (ZF vs VGG). Results on VOC07/12 are strong (up to 73.2% mAP on VOC07 union training; 70.4% on VOC12).
- Reproducibility: Many implementation details are provided; code is released.

Weaknesses:
- Scope of evaluation: Experiments are limited to PASCAL VOC. A more diverse benchmark (e.g., COCO) would better stress-test small/medium/large objects and AP at multiple IoUs.
- Training strategy: The alternating 4-step scheme is a pragmatic workaround; the paper does not attempt or analyze joint end-to-end multitask optimization stability (though acknowledged as future work).
- Hyperparameter dependence: Anchor scales/aspect ratios and stride are hand-chosen; limited sensitivity analysis is provided. The approach may be sensitive to small objects given a stride of 16.
- Comparisons and fairness: While comparisons to SS/EB are strong, GPU-optimized baselines (or a controlled engineering comparison) are not provided. Direct, controlled comparisons to MultiBox with shared features and equal backbones would strengthen claims.
- Some ad hoc design choices: Ignoring cross-boundary anchors during training, fixed IoU thresholds (0.7/0.3) for label assignment, and fixed NMS at 0.7 are sensible but could use more justification or sensitivity analysis.

Quality:
Technically sound and carefully implemented. The empirical evidence supports claims about speed, accuracy, and proposal quality. Limitations (e.g., training scheme) are acknowledged. I would have liked joint training results or a stability analysis, and broader benchmarks.

Clarity:
Well written and organized. The method is clearly described (architecture, loss, anchor parameterization, sampling). Training details and ablations are sufficient for reproduction. Minor presentation issues (reference formatting, footnotes) do not impede understanding.

Originality:
High. The anchor-based, translation-invariant, fully-convolutional proposal mechanism that shares features with the detector is a significant conceptual advance over both hand-crafted proposals and prior learned box predictors. The two-stage cascade with shared features is a clean unification.

Significance:
High. This work removes the main bottleneck in region-based detection and provides a practical, accurate, and fast detector. The ideas (anchors, shared features, two-stage training) are likely to be widely adopted and extended.

Questions and suggestions for authors:
- Can the RPN and Fast R-CNN be trained jointly end-to-end without alternating, and if so, does it converge and improve accuracy/speed?
- Sensitivity to anchor design: How do different scale/aspect sets, number of anchors, or learned anchors affect proposal recall and mAP? Any strategies to adapt anchors to dataset statistics?
- Small object performance: Analyze recall/AP by object size; does stride 16 hurt small objects? Would adding a lower-stride feature map help?
- Ablate NMS threshold and number of proposals more extensively; consider soft-NMS.
- Provide results on a larger benchmark (e.g., COCO) and AP at multiple IoUs (0.5:0.95).
- Compare to a GPU-optimized EdgeBoxes or a learned but unshared proposal baseline to better isolate the benefit of feature sharing.

Pros for acceptance:
- Novel, elegant unification of proposals and detection with shared conv features.
- Strong empirical gains in both speed and accuracy; practical system.
- Thorough ablations; clear methodology; released code.
- Likely to catalyze substantial follow-up work.

Cons against acceptance:
- Evaluation limited to VOC; missing broader benchmarks and size-stratified analyses.
- Alternating training instead of fully joint optimization; limited discussion of stability.
- Some hyperparameters/design choices are fixed with limited sensitivity analysis.

Recommendation: Strong accept. The paper introduces a significant new concept and direction with compelling empirical validation and high practical impact. Minor limitations do not detract from its novelty and significance.

Reviewer confidence: High.
