- Proposes Region Proposal Networks (RPNs): a small fully-convolutional net sliding over shared conv feature maps that predicts k anchor boxes per location with objectness and bbox regression learned end-to-end.  
- Shares conv layers with Fast R-CNN via a 4-step alternating training scheme, making proposals nearly free at test time and improving mAP on PASCAL VOC.  
- Addresses the bottleneck of external CPU-based proposal generators and contrasts with OverFeat/MultiBox by using translation-invariant anchors and compact, shared heads.  
- Anchor mechanism is simple, translation-invariant, and effectively covers scale/aspect variation with a compact predictor.  
- Losses, target parameterization, and training practices (image-centric sampling, smooth L1, normalization, momentum/weight decay) are standard and appropriate.  
- Ablation studies support claims: shared convs outperform unshared; both classification and regression heads are necessary; competitive performance with as few as 100–300 proposals.  
- Proposal recall and robustness to NMS/number of proposals are analyzed; stronger backbones (VGG-16) yield better proposals and higher detection mAP.  
- Two-stage RPN+Fast R-CNN exceeds dense sliding-window one-stage alternatives under comparable settings.  
- Speed/accuracy trade-offs are compelling: VGG-16 proposals ~10ms and total ~198ms per image; ZF backbone achieves ~17 fps using ~300 proposals.  
- Reservation: true joint end-to-end training of the unified network is not explored; alternating training choice may limit conclusions about optimal training.  
- Reservation: evaluation is limited to PASCAL VOC; lack of results on larger/difficult datasets (e.g., COCO) leaves small-object and crowded-scene performance untested.  
- Reservation: several design hyperparameters (anchor scales/ratios, 16-pixel stride, IoU thresholds, NMS) are fixed and not systematically sensitivity-analyzed; stride effects on small objects are noted but unquantified.  
- Paper is clearly written and reproducible, with concrete training details; suggested clarifications include average anchors retained after NMS, sensitivity summary for anchors/stride, and a schematic of alternating training.  
- Original contributions: fully-convolutional, translation-invariant anchor mechanism; conv feature sharing between proposal and detection; simple stable alternating training—distinct from MultiBox and OverFeat.  
- Significance: removes a major practical bottleneck, sets a new speed-accuracy baseline, is backbone-agnostic, simple to implement, and likely to be widely adopted; open-source code increases impact.  
- Pros: elegant unification of proposals and detection, strong empirical and ablation results, novel anchor design, practical training recipe and released code.  
- Cons: no exploration of true end-to-end joint training, limited dataset scope and small-object analysis, and insufficient systematic hyperparameter sensitivity studies.  
- Questions for authors: have you tried joint end-to-end training and how does it compare; how sensitive are results to anchor scales/ratios and feature stride (e.g., stride 8 via dilation); do you observe small-object degradation and would multi-scale/FPN designs mitigate it?  
- Recommendation: Strong accept — technically sound, original, clearly written, and significant; minor weaknesses do not undermine the contribution (high confidence).
