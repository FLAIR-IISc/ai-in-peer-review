Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes Region Proposal Networks (RPNs), a fully convolutional network that predicts objectness scores and bounding box regressions for a set of translation-invariant anchors at each spatial location on shared convolutional feature maps. By sharing conv features with a Fast R-CNN detector and using a simple alternating 4-step training scheme, the method yields an end-to-end system in which proposals are nearly “free” given the detection backbone. The authors show strong results on PASCAL VOC 2007/2012 with both ZF and VGG-16 backbones, achieving higher mAP than Fast R-CNN with Selective Search (SS) or EdgeBoxes (EB) while running substantially faster.

This work builds on region-based CNNs (R-CNN, SPPnet, Fast R-CNN) that amortize conv computation across proposals, and on proposal methods (SS, EB). Compared to OverFeat (one-stage sliding window classification + regression) and MultiBox (predicting many boxes via a dense output layer), RPN introduces anchor boxes that are translation-invariant and a lightweight head with far fewer parameters, integrated with a two-stage detector. The paper includes ablations disentangling cls vs reg contributions, proposal counts, NMS, sharing vs not sharing features, and a one-stage vs two-stage comparison, aligning with prior observations that cascaded detection outperforms pure sliding windows.

Strengths
- Clear, elegant formulation of a proposal mechanism that reuses conv features and aligns well with detection architectures; anchors are simple yet effective.
- Strong empirical evidence: competitive or better mAP than SS/EB with far fewer proposals (≈300) and orders-of-magnitude speedup in the proposal stage (≈10 ms).
- Thorough ablations: effect of sharing features, proposal count, NMS, cls vs reg, and backbones (ZF vs VGG-16). The one-stage vs two-stage comparison is especially informative.
- Practical training recipe (alternating 4-step) that is stable and easy to reproduce with standard SGD on ImageNet-pretrained backbones.
- Good discussion of recall vs IoU and why proposal metrics are only loosely tied to detection mAP.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Evaluation scope is limited to VOC; results on larger-scale datasets (e.g., COCO) or across diverse domains would strengthen claims of generality.
- Sensitivity analyses are limited. Important hyperparameters (anchor scales/ratios, IoU thresholds 0.7/0.3, NMS threshold 0.7, λ and normalization choices) are not systematically explored; some choices (normalization footnote) appear ad hoc.
- Comparison to MultiBox is primarily architectural/parameter-count based; a stronger empirical comparison (e.g., MultiBox proposals + Fast R-CNN with same backbone) would clarify relative merits.
- The alternating training scheme is pragmatic, but the paper does not attempt or analyze truly joint end-to-end optimization; it is unclear if joint training would be stable or better.
- Runtime reporting would benefit from more detail (hardware, cuDNN version, batch size) and an explicit breakdown of per-stage times; comparisons to GPU-ported EB/SS (if available) would address fairness concerns.

Quality
- Technical soundness: Good. The loss, parameterizations, and anchor design are standard and well-motivated. The sampling strategy balances positives/negatives to address class imbalance. The sharing scheme is carefully engineered to avoid instability.
- Empirical support: Strong on VOC, with comprehensive ablations that isolate key contributions. However, broader datasets and sensitivity studies would improve the completeness.
- Limitations acknowledged: The authors discuss the proposal metric vs detection metric mismatch and argue for detection mAP as the primary measure. More discussion of failure modes (e.g., small/elongated objects, dense scenes) would help.

Clarity
- Generally well-written and organized. The RPN architecture and training are described clearly, with helpful figures and explicit hyperparameters.
- Suggestions:
  - Consolidate all hyperparameters (anchor sets, IoU thresholds, sampling ratios, λ, normalization constants) into a single table for reproducibility.
  - Clarify the exact handling of cross-boundary anchors at train vs test time and quantify how many anchors are ignored.
  - Provide a brief stability discussion of the alternating training (e.g., learning curves, convergence behavior).

Originality
- The central ideas—translation-invariant anchors used by a fully conv proposal head that shares features with a detector, and the simple alternating training to share features—are novel relative to prior sliding-window and MultiBox approaches. The work is a substantive and nontrivial step beyond Fast R-CNN and prior proposal methods.

Significance
- High. The approach materially reduces detection runtime while improving accuracy, and the formulation is likely to be adopted widely in practice and extended (e.g., different backbones, anchor strategies, multi-scale variants). It advances state-of-the-art speed/accuracy trade-offs and reframes proposals as a learnable, integrated component.

Questions for the authors
- How sensitive are results to the choice of anchor scales/ratios and IoU thresholds for positive/negative labeling? Could you provide a small grid search or analysis?
- What is the impact of varying the NMS threshold and the number of proposals during both training and testing (i.e., training with fewer than 2k proposals)?
- Did you attempt joint end-to-end training (RPN + Fast R-CNN) with proposals updated online? If so, what issues arose?
- How does performance vary with object size (small/medium/large) and aspect ratio? Any notable failure cases?
- Can you report detailed runtime breakdowns and hardware/software configurations?

Minor comments
- Cite matching backbones when comparing to MultiBox if possible, or clearly state the backbone mismatch in the main text where parameters are contrasted.
- A per-class AP table in the appendix would aid analysis.
- Consider reporting proposal recall at multiple IoUs for a fixed N to complement mAP.

Arguments for acceptance
- Elegant, simple method with strong empirical gains in both accuracy and speed.
- Thorough ablations and insightful comparisons (two-stage vs one-stage).
- High potential impact and extensibility; likely to become a standard component.

Arguments against acceptance
- Limited dataset scope; lack of sensitivity analyses on key hyperparameters.
- No empirical head-to-head with MultiBox under matched conditions.
- Alternating training leaves open the question of full end-to-end joint optimization.

Recommendation
Accept with enthusiasm. The paper presents a clear, effective, and influential idea that advances object detection both scientifically and practically.
