Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes Region Proposal Networks (RPNs), a fully convolutional network that generates class-agnostic object proposals by sliding a small network over shared convolutional feature maps, using translation-invariant “anchors” at each spatial location to cover multiple scales and aspect ratios. The RPN outputs objectness scores and bounding-box regressions for k anchors per location, and is trained with a multi-task loss (classification + smooth L1 regression) using IoU-based positive/negative anchor assignment. To integrate with detection, the authors introduce an alternating 4-step training scheme that shares convolutional layers between the RPN and a Fast R-CNN detector, yielding near-cost-free proposals at test time.

This work builds directly on region-based CNNs (R-CNN, SPP-net, Fast R-CNN) that share convolutional features for detection. It is related to OverFeat (sliding-window, class-specific localization/detection) and MultiBox (deep net that predicts a set of bounding boxes), but differs in its use of translation-invariant anchors tiled across the feature map, a compact output head (2k + 4k outputs per location), and tight feature sharing with the detector. Compared with Selective Search and EdgeBoxes, RPNs replace hand-engineered proposal pipelines with learned proposals that leverage the same deep features used by the detector.

Quality
- Methodology: The model and loss are clearly specified. Anchor parameterization and regression targets follow established practice. The anchor tiling strategy (3 scales × 3 aspect ratios) and classification/regression heads are well-motivated. The alternating training procedure to share features is pragmatic and convergent in practice.
- Experiments: Evaluations on PASCAL VOC 2007/2012 show improved or competitive mAP over strong baselines (Selective Search, EdgeBoxes) with far lower proposal cost; overall system achieves 5–17 fps on a GPU. Ablations isolate the effects of sharing features, number of proposals, turning off cls/reg heads, NMS, and sliding-window (one-stage) vs two-stage detectors. Proposal quality is further analyzed via recall vs IoU curves.
- Rigor: The paper reports consistent training/testing setups, compares multiple backbones (ZF, VGG-16), and provides runtime breakdown. Some sensitivity analyses (e.g., anchor choices, IoU thresholds, NMS settings) are limited but partially addressed.

Clarity
The paper is well-organized and generally clear. The RPN architecture, anchor design, loss, and training schedule are described with sufficient detail to reimplement. Minor areas could be clarified: normalization and λ choices in the loss (footnote), hardware details for runtime, and more explicit guidance on cross-boundary anchor handling and its impact on convergence.

Originality
The core contributions are:
- Translation-invariant anchor mechanism integrated into a fully convolutional proposal network.
- Tight feature sharing between proposal generation and detection, making proposals nearly free at inference.
- A simple alternating training scheme to learn shared features.
While bounding-box regression on conv features and proposal generation with deep nets exist (OverFeat, MultiBox), the anchor tiling design, compact head, and integration with Fast R-CNN constitute a novel and effective combination.

Significance
The method substantially reduces the computational bottleneck of proposal generation while improving or maintaining detection accuracy, making high-quality detection practical in near real-time with standard backbones. The approach is likely to influence subsequent research and practice, as it provides a general, learnable, and efficient alternative to hand-crafted proposals and a template for two-stage detectors.

Strengths
- Elegant, simple architecture with clear design choices (anchors, multi-task loss).
- Strong accuracy-speed trade-off: proposals at ~10 ms and end-to-end detection at 5–17 fps.
- Solid ablations demonstrating the roles of cls/reg, feature sharing, proposal counts, and two-stage benefit.
- Competitive or improved mAP on VOC 2007/2012 using standard backbones.
- Code availability and use of standard frameworks facilitate reproducibility.

Weaknesses
- Evaluation scope: limited to VOC and mAP@0.5; no results on larger/more challenging datasets (e.g., COCO) or higher IoU thresholds.
- Comparisons to prior deep proposal methods (MultiBox) are mostly architectural; direct accuracy/speed comparisons are limited.
- Sensitivity analyses for anchor configurations (scales/aspect ratios), positive/negative IoU thresholds, NMS IoU threshold, and stride are not deeply explored; small-object performance is not analyzed.
- The alternating 4-step training is pragmatic but somewhat ad hoc; joint training feasibility and stability are not investigated.
- Speed comparisons note CPU vs GPU disparities for SS/EB, but a GPU-optimized proposal baseline is not provided; memory usage and throughput for high-resolution inputs are not reported.

Suggestions and questions for the authors
- Provide results at stricter IoU thresholds (e.g., AP@0.75) and/or on a larger dataset to assess localization quality and generalization.
- Include a systematic study of anchor configurations (additional scales, aspect ratios, smaller strides/dilations) and their effect on small objects and overall mAP.
- Report sensitivity to positive/negative IoU thresholds used for label assignment and to the NMS threshold; discuss trade-offs between recall and precision for proposals.
- Offer a direct comparison (accuracy and runtime) to MultiBox under matched backbones and hardware, if feasible.
- Explore joint end-to-end training of RPN + detector and discuss stability, convergence, and potential gains.
- Add per-class AP and error analysis (e.g., localization vs classification errors) to better understand where RPN helps.
- Clarify runtime measurement setup (GPU model, batch size, image sizes), and report memory footprint.

Arguments for acceptance
- Clear, well-motivated method that is technically sound and easy to implement.
- Demonstrable, practical impact: significant speedup with equal or better accuracy versus strong baselines.
- Comprehensive ablations supporting design choices.
- Advances the state of the art in proposal generation and integrates cleanly with existing detectors.

Arguments against acceptance
- Limited evaluation breadth (datasets/metrics) and incomplete comparisons to some deep proposal baselines.
- Some design choices (anchors, thresholds, training schedule) lack deeper analysis or theoretical justification.
- Joint training is deferred, leaving open whether a more unified approach could further improve results.

Overall assessment
This is a strong contribution that addresses a key bottleneck in object detection via a principled and effective architectural change. The method is technically sound, clearly presented, novel in its anchor-based RPN design and feature sharing, and significant in practice. Subject to minor clarifications and expanded evaluation, it merits acceptance.
