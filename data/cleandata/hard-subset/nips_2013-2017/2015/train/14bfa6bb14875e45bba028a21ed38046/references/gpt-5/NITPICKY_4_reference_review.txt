Summary
The paper proposes Region Proposal Networks (RPNs), a fully convolutional subnetwork that, when attached to a shared backbone with a Fast R-CNN detector, generates class-agnostic region proposals at negligible marginal cost. RPNs slide a 3×3 conv “mini-network” over shared feature maps and, at each location, predict k=9 anchor-relative boxes and objectness scores across 3 scales×3 aspect ratios. Anchors are translation-tied to the feature map grid. Training uses a multi-task loss (2-class softmax for objectness and smooth L1 for regression) with positive/negative anchor assignment based on IoU thresholds (≥0.7 positive; <0.3 negative; best-IoU anchor per GT also positive). The authors introduce a 4-step alternating fine-tuning scheme to share convolutional layers between RPN and Fast R-CNN. Experiments on PASCAL VOC 2007/2012 show improved mAP over Selective Search (SS) and EdgeBoxes (EB), while reducing test-time proposal cost to ~10ms and achieving ~5 fps end-to-end with VGG-16.

Relation to prior work
This work builds on R-CNN/SSPNet/Fast R-CNN by reusing shared conv features for proposals. It contrasts with OverFeat (one-stage sliding-window detection with scale pyramids) and MultiBox (predicting many non-translation-invariant boxes via a large FC head). The novelty claim rests on translation-invariant anchors and explicit conv sharing between proposal and detection.

Strengths
- Clear architectural idea with strong practical value: proposal generation integrated into the detection net with minimal overhead.
- Solid ablations disentangling effects of shared features, cls vs reg, number of proposals, and NMS influence.
- Competitive to-better mAP than SS/EB with far fewer proposals (e.g., 300) and large speedup.
- Sensible design choices (anchors across scales/aspect ratios; smooth L1; image-centric sampling).
- Alternating training provides a pragmatic route to shared features without joint optimization instability.

Weaknesses and concerns
Technical/methodological:
- “Translation invariance” is imprecise; the architecture is translation equivariant up to sampling/stride/pooling. Please correct the claim.
- Loss normalization and λ: Footnote indicates Ncls=256 (sampled anchors) but Nreg normalized by number of locations (~2400), not by sampled positives. This induces image-dependent scaling and imbalance across mini-batches. Provide a principled normalization (e.g., by number of sampled positives) and show sensitivity to λ.
- Anchor sampling: Up to 1:1 pos:neg strongly upweights rare positives relative to their true frequency; discuss effect on calibration and show sensitivity to sampling ratio.
- Ignoring cross-boundary anchors during training but clipping at test time introduces a train/test mismatch; quantify its impact and justify ignoring rather than masking-regressing with valid interior overlap.
- The claim that proposal computation is “nearly cost-free” is not fully quantified: does the 10ms include sorting, pre/postprocessing, and NMS? Provide a complete per-component breakdown.
- Alternating training vs joint training: the paper defers joint optimization; however, the current training lacks analysis on convergence properties and stability. A comparison to end-to-end joint training (even if negative) would strengthen the work.
- Limited datasets and metrics: No COCO results; Recall-to-IoU curves are only diagnostic. mAP improvements are shown mainly on VOC; significance across broader settings is unclear.
- Fairness/consistency: Some comparisons mix training on SS proposals but testing on RPN (or vice versa), which is acknowledged to hurt. The strongest comparisons should use consistent train/test proposals and matched hyperparameters for all baselines.
- One-stage comparison: The “emulated OverFeat” baseline depends on many design decisions (scales, feature pyramid), with limited detail and no speed breakdown. Comparison to stronger one-stage detectors (even contemporaneous) would be informative.

Clarity/reproducibility:
- Missing implementation details: data augmentation (e.g., flips), preprocessing/normalization, RoI sampling for Fast R-CNN, RPN score thresholds, anchor clipping behavior, batching at test-time, GPU model, CUDNN version, BLAS, and memory footprint.
- Hyperparameters are scattered (learning rates, momentum, decay, steps) without justification or sensitivity analysis.
- Tables and figures (e.g., Table 1–5, Fig. 1–2) are referenced but not fully provided in the text; per-class AP is not shown.
- Code link contains a space and may be invalid as printed.

Formatting and citation issues (numerous):
- Footnotes and symbols appear mid-word (e.g., “de∗tection”, “de∗Shaoqing Ren...”), suggesting typesetting errors.
- Multiple malformed references with scrambled author names, missing years/venues, inconsistent capitalization, and incomplete entries (e.g., “and A, M. Everingham…”, “and B, J. Hosang…”). Ensure all [#] citations map cleanly to complete references.
- Inconsistent notation and typography (e.g., “∼2,400”; stray spaces; inconsistent use of italics/math; tildes with commas).
- Space in the GitHub URL; ensure clickable, exact link.
- Some claims are stated without citations or quantitative evidence (e.g., “training does not converge” when including boundary-crossing anchors).

Requests for clarification
- Provide an ablation for λ and normalization choices (Ncls/Nreg), and report final loss magnitudes for cls/reg.
- Sensitivity to anchor scales/ratios, feature stride (e.g., dilated conv to reduce stride), and NMS threshold; include performance vs. number of proposals.
- Quantify the impact of ignoring cross-boundary anchors vs including them with masked regression targets.
- Full timing breakdown (conv shared time, RPN heads, NMS, sorting, RoI pooling, fc) and hardware details.
- Results on a larger benchmark (e.g., COCO) to validate generality.

Pros
- Conceptually simple, elegant integration of proposals into the detector.
- Strong speed/accuracy trade-off; fewer proposals without mAP loss.
- Clear advances over SS/EB baselines; convincing ablations.

Cons
- Many presentation/formatting/citation issues hinder clarity.
- Important training/normalization choices are ad hoc and not analyzed.
- Limited evaluation scope; incomplete/possibly unfair baselines in places.
- Reproducibility impeded by missing details.

Overall recommendation: Weak Reject
Despite a compelling idea and promising results, the manuscript’s numerous methodological ambiguities, incomplete comparisons, and extensive formatting/citation problems prevent a thorough, reproducible assessment. With clearer presentation, stronger baselines, broader evaluation, and principled analysis of training choices, this could become a strong contribution.

Confidence: High
