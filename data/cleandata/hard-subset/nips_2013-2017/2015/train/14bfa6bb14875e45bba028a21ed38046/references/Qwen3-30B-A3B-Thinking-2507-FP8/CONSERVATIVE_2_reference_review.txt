

# Review of "Faster R-CNN: Towards Real-Time Object Detection with Region Proposal Networks"

This paper introduces Region Proposal Networks (RPNs) that share convolutional features with the detection network to reduce the computational cost of region proposal generation in object detection systems. The authors argue that by training a fully convolutional network to generate proposals, the proposal step becomes nearly cost-free (10ms per image) when sharing features with the detection network, achieving real-time performance (5-17 fps) with state-of-the-art accuracy (73.2% mAP on PASCAL VOC 2007).

The paper builds on established work in region-based CNNs (R-CNN, Fast R-CNN) and proposes RPNs as a novel way to generate region proposals. The key technical contribution is the integration of proposal generation with the detection network through shared convolutional features, eliminating the need for separate proposal computation. The authors provide a thorough ablation study showing the importance of both classification and regression components in the RPN, and compare against established baselines like Selective Search and EdgeBoxes.

**Strengths:**

1. The paper provides a clear technical contribution that addresses a genuine bottleneck in object detection pipelines. The shared convolutional feature approach is logically sound and well-motivated by the observation that the same features used for detection could also be used for proposal generation.

2. The experimental validation is comprehensive, with thorough ablation studies showing how each component of the RPN contributes to performance. The comparison against multiple baselines (Selective Search, EdgeBoxes, OverFeat) is well-executed.

3. The implementation details are clear and the authors provide code, which is valuable for reproducibility.

**Weaknesses:**

1. The claimed "nearly cost-free" proposal computation is misleading. The paper compares CPU-based Selective Search (2s per image) with GPU-based RPN (10ms per image), but the comparison is not apples-to-apples. The paper does not provide a fair comparison of GPU implementations of Selective Search or EdgeBoxes, which would be necessary to validate the claimed speedup. The authors acknowledge this issue but don't properly address it in their experiments.

2. The paper overstates the novelty of the approach. The core idea of sharing convolutional features between proposal and detection networks is not fundamentally new - Fast R-CNN already demonstrated this principle (as cited in the paper). The RPN is essentially an extension of this principle, but the paper doesn't sufficiently distinguish how this extension is novel compared to existing work.

3. The performance improvements over Selective Search (59.9% vs 58.7% mAP with ZF net) are marginal, and the paper doesn't convincingly demonstrate that the RPN-generated proposals are substantially better than Selective Search. The ablation studies show that the RPN's proposal quality is competitive with but not significantly better than Selective Search, which weakens the main motivation for the approach.

4. The paper doesn't sufficiently address whether the RPN approach would scale to more complex detection tasks or datasets beyond PASCAL VOC. The validation is limited to the PASCAL VOC benchmark, which is a relatively small and well-studied dataset.

5. The claim that "our method waives nearly all computational burdens of SS at test-time" is overstated. The paper shows a 10ms RPN time, but doesn't provide a full system comparison of total inference time with a GPU implementation of Selective Search, which would be necessary to validate this claim.

**Overall Assessment:**

While the paper presents a technically sound engineering improvement to object detection pipelines, it falls short of being a significant scientific contribution for NIPS. The approach is largely an incremental extension of existing Fast R-CNN work, with marginal accuracy gains and potentially misleading claims about computational efficiency. The novelty is limited to the specific implementation of the proposal network rather than a fundamental new approach to object detection.

The paper does not sufficiently distinguish itself from previous work in the field, and the experimental validation is not thorough enough to support the claims of substantial performance improvements. The marginal accuracy gains (1.2% mAP improvement over Selective Search with ZF net) do not justify the novelty claim, especially when the proposed method requires significant additional engineering effort.

**Recommendation:**

I recommend rejection. While the paper is well-written and technically sound, it does not present a sufficiently novel or impactful contribution to warrant acceptance at NIPS. The improvements are incremental rather than transformative, and the validation does not fully support the claims made. The field would benefit more from papers that make more significant theoretical or methodological advances rather than engineering optimizations with marginal gains.

The authors would need to substantially strengthen the novelty claim by demonstrating clear theoretical advantages or substantially larger performance gains across multiple benchmarks before this work would be suitable for NIPS.
