Summary
The paper proposes Region Proposal Networks (RPNs) that generate class-agnostic object proposals using the same convolutional (conv) feature maps as a Fast R-CNN detector. A small fully convolutional subnetwork slides over the final shared conv map, mapping 3×3 windows to k=9 “anchors” (3 scales × 3 aspect ratios) per location. For each anchor, the network predicts an objectness score and a 4-d bounding-box regression target. Training uses a multi-task loss (2-class softmax + smooth L1 regression), with anchors labeled positive/negative based on IoU thresholds (≥0.7 positive, <0.3 negative; also highest-IoU per GT). A 4-step alternating training scheme is used to share conv layers between the RPN and Fast R-CNN. With VGG-16 and s=600 single-scale images, the method reports 69.9% mAP (VOC07) with shared features and 73.2% when training on a larger union of VOC trainval sets; runtime is ~198ms per image with ~10ms overhead for proposals.

Relation to prior work
The work builds on Fast R-CNN and SPP-net by sharing conv features across RoIs, and contrasts with OverFeat’s one-stage sliding-window detection and MultiBox’s global proposal prediction (800 non-translation-invariant anchors). The proposed anchors are translation-invariant and tied to each conv location, reducing parameters and aiding generalization on small datasets. The cascade (RPN → Fast R-CNN) is empirically argued to outperform one-stage sliding-window detection with comparable backbones.

Strengths
- Clear, elegant unification of proposal generation and detection with shared features; strong practical impact on speed and accuracy.
- Well-motivated anchor mechanism (scales/aspect ratios) that is simple and effective.
- Solid ablations: shared vs unshared features, effect of cls vs reg heads, proposal counts, and one-stage vs two-stage.
- Significant empirical gains over SS/EB on VOC while drastically reducing proposal cost (~10ms) and proposals (≈300).
- Sensible training heuristics (IoU-based labeling, positive/negative sampling) that yield stable optimization.

Weaknesses and detailed comments
Methodology and analysis
- Joint training is acknowledged but not attempted; the alternating 4-step procedure is ad hoc. At minimum, a small-scale experiment or analysis on why joint end-to-end training was unstable would strengthen the claim and the design choice.
- Hyperparameters are under-explored: only 3 scales and 3 aspect ratios are used; there is no sensitivity analysis to number/choice of anchors, aspect ratios per dataset, or to conv stride (16) vs dilated features for small objects.
- The IoU thresholds for anchor labeling (0.7/0.3) are chosen without justification; no ablation is reported for these thresholds or for class-imbalance handling beyond 1:1 sampling up to 256 anchors.
- Loss normalization and λ are inconsistent. Eqn. (1) states generic Ncls and Nreg, and a footnote describes an “early implementation” with λ=10 and specific normalizations; the exact settings used for all reported results are unclear. This reduces reproducibility.
- Cross-boundary anchors are ignored during training but kept (clipped) at test-time; a short ablation on including them during training (with target clipping) would help, as the current choice introduces a train-test mismatch and potential bias near borders.
- The regression target parameterization follows R-CNN, but there is no discussion of target normalization/standardization (e.g., per-parameter mean/variance) which is known to affect stability.
- NMS IoU is fixed at 0.7 and not ablated; given its central role and the claim that NMS “does not harm mAP,” a sensitivity curve (and per-category analysis) is warranted.
- Some runtime comparisons are not fully equitable: EB/SS timings are CPU while RPN runs on GPU and shares features. While the paper notes this, a GPU EB baseline or a unified timing breakdown (including conv costs) would better support the “nearly cost-free” claim.
- Hardware details are insufficient: GPU model, cuDNN version, and batch size are not specified. Reported per-image times (e.g., 198ms) may vary substantially with hardware.
- Dataset scope is limited to VOC07/12. Given that proposal recall at high IoU is critical on more challenging datasets, COCO results (or at least cross-dataset generalization) would make the conclusions more convincing.
- Small object performance is not analyzed; with stride 16 and limited anchors, a breakdown by object size would be informative.
- The one-stage baseline is constructed with 3 scales × 3 aspect ratios and 5 image scales, but hyperparameters (e.g., score thresholds, sampling) may not be tuned comparably to the two-stage system; further tuning might reduce the reported 4.8% gap.

Clarity, organization, and presentation
- Typographical/formatting issues throughout: e.g., “de∗Shaoqing Ren… de tection,” missing spaces (“sizeW ×H,” “typically∼2,400”), grammar (“they are dominate”), inconsistent hyphenation (“mini-network/mininetwork”), and stray footnote markers (1,2,3) with uneven placement.
- Figures and tables are referenced (Fig. 1, Tables 1–5, Fig. 2) but not provided in the submission text here; ensure they are legible and self-contained.
- References are frequently malformed or incomplete (missing years/venues/authors, stray punctuation, e.g., “and A, M. Everingham…”, “and B, J. Hosang…”). This needs careful cleanup.
- The code URL contains a space (“github.com/ ShaoqingRen/faster_rcnn”) and should be verified; reproducibility depends on accessible code and exact training configs.

Quality
Technically solid with extensive empirical validation on VOC and thoughtful ablations. However, some design choices are insufficiently justified or ablated (label thresholds, NMS, anchor sets, loss normalization), and the training/test mismatches (cross-boundary anchors) could be better addressed.

Originality
The translation-invariant anchor mechanism and tight integration of a proposal network with a detector that shares conv features is a notable and impactful idea distinct from OverFeat and MultiBox. While components are drawn from prior work (Fast R-CNN, SPP, smooth L1), the combination is novel and influential.

Significance
High practical significance: substantial speedups with equal/better accuracy, and a paradigm (two-stage with learnable proposals) likely to be widely adopted. That said, broader validation beyond VOC would strengthen impact claims.

Questions for the authors
- What exact λ, Ncls, and Nreg were used for all reported results? Were regression targets normalized?
- Sensitivity to anchor scales/ratios and to NMS IoU? Any results with additional aspect ratios or smaller strides/dilations?
- How many positives per image on average with 0.7/0.3 thresholds? Does 256 anchors per minibatch suffice for stability?
- Any experiments with joint end-to-end training (RPN+detector) beyond the alternating scheme?
- Hardware details for timing; can you report end-to-end latency including convs vs incremental proposal overhead, and compare to a GPU EB implementation?

Pros
- Elegant, effective unification of proposals and detection with shared computation
- Strong empirical results and ablations; large speedups with few proposals
- Clear contribution relative to OverFeat/MultiBox/SS/EB

Cons
- Incomplete ablations and justifications for key hyperparameters and heuristics
- Limited dataset scope; missing small-object analysis
- Presentation/formatting and reference issues; some reproducibility ambiguities
- Ad hoc alternating training without attempting joint optimization

Recommendation
Weak accept. The contribution is significant and the method is technically sound, but the paper should clean up presentation issues and add key ablations/clarifications to solidify the claims. Confidence: high.
