Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes Region Proposal Networks (RPNs) that generate class-agnostic object proposals by adding a small, fully convolutional subnetwork on top of shared convolutional features used by a Fast R-CNN detector. At each spatial location, the RPN predicts objectness scores and box regressions for k translation-invariant “anchors” across multiple scales and aspect ratios (typically k=9). Training uses a multi-task loss over classification and bounding-box regression with positive/negative labels assigned by IoU thresholds. To share computation with the detector, the authors adopt a pragmatic 4-step alternating training scheme that fine-tunes the RPN and Fast R-CNN in sequence and then ties their convolutional layers.

This work directly addresses the long-standing bottleneck of hand-engineered, CPU-bound proposal methods such as Selective Search (SS) and EdgeBoxes (EB), which have been paired with R-CNN/SSP/Fast R-CNN. It builds on shared-convolution pipelines (SPP, Fast R-CNN) and relates to OverFeat and MultiBox, which also predict boxes with a CNN but differ in key ways: the RPN uses translation-invariant anchors (rather than a large set of learned priors), is trained end-to-end for objectness, and is designed to share features tightly with a downstream detector. The approach is evaluated primarily on PASCAL VOC 2007/2012 with ZF and VGG-16 backbones, showing better mAP than SS+Fast R-CNN at a fraction of the time; the marginal cost of proposals is ~10 ms on a GPU.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The method is well motivated, clearly specified (architectural details, loss, sampling, anchor design), and backed by a range of ablations that isolate the contribution of classification vs regression, proposal counts, NMS, feature sharing, and backbone depth. The alternating optimization is a reasonable workaround to potential instability in joint training, and in practice converges.
- Empirics: On VOC, RPN+Fast R-CNN with VGG-16 achieves 69.9% mAP (single-scale), improving over SS with a substantial speedup; training on 07+12 further boosts to 73.2% mAP. Ablations show that even 300 proposals suffice with minor loss, and that classification scores drive ranking quality while regression is crucial for localization. Runtime breakdowns are provided.
- Caveats: Experiments are limited to PASCAL VOC; evaluation on a larger and more diverse dataset (e.g., COCO) would strengthen the claims, especially for small objects and crowded scenes. Sensitivity analyses for anchor configurations, IoU thresholds (0.7/0.3), NMS thresholds, and stride would improve robustness claims. The decision to ignore cross-boundary anchors during training (while allowing them at test time) introduces a train/test mismatch that could be further investigated.

Clarity
The paper is clearly written and well organized. The design choices (anchors, loss, sampling, training schedule) are explained with intuition and comparisons to OverFeat and MultiBox. Some implementation details are relegated to footnotes or briefly mentioned (e.g., choice of λ and normalization of losses, which differ between text and footnote); consolidating these into a single, reproducible recipe would help practitioners. A brief algorithm summary of the 4-step training would also aid clarity.

Originality
The central contribution—using a fully convolutional, shared-feature RPN with translation-invariant anchors for nearly-free proposals integrated with a Fast R-CNN detector—is novel relative to prior region proposal methods and prior CNN-based detection approaches. While the components (sliding-window conv features, multi-task losses, box regression) are known, their specific combination with anchors and tight sharing delivers a new, effective system.

Significance
High. The work removes a major bottleneck in state-of-the-art detectors, simultaneously improving accuracy and speed and simplifying the pipeline. The approach is likely to be widely adopted and extended. The notion of anchors and shared conv features for proposal generation is an influential design that other methods can build on.

Strengths
- Elegant unification of proposal generation and detection with shared features, yielding near-free proposals.
- Strong accuracy and significant speedup over SS/EB baselines; good trade-off with as few as 300 proposals.
- Clear ablations disentangling effects of ranking vs regression, proposal counts, and feature sharing.
- Practical training protocol that converges and is easy to implement; code availability.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Dataset scope: Include results on a larger benchmark (e.g., COCO) to validate generalization, especially for small object recall and crowded scenes.
- Anchor sensitivity: Report sensitivity to the number, scales, and aspect ratios of anchors; consider data-driven anchor shapes and analyze per-class effects.
- Stride and small objects: With stride 16, small-object performance may be limited. Evaluate smaller stride (e.g., dilated conv) or multi-level features; quantify the trade-off.
- Positive/negative assignment: Analyze sensitivity to IoU thresholds (0.7/0.3) and class imbalance; compare to alternative sampling (e.g., hard-negative mining).
- Boundary handling: Explore training with cross-boundary anchors using clipped regression targets to reduce train/test mismatch; report its effect on stability and accuracy.
- Fair runtime comparisons: Include GPU-optimized SS/EB or discuss end-to-end wall-clock including I/O to ensure fairness; specify GPU model, cuDNN version, batch size.
- Proposal-objectness generalization: Since RPN is trained with labeled categories, examine generalization to unseen categories (hold-out category test) compared to class-agnostic SS.
- Joint training: While alternating training works, a brief experiment or discussion on fully joint end-to-end training stability would be valuable.

Questions for authors
- How sensitive are results to the anchor set and NMS threshold? Any cases where increasing k hurts due to overfitting?
- What is the failure profile (missed small objects vs localization errors)? Can you provide per-class AP and error analysis?
- Does including more conv layers in sharing (for VGG: tuning from conv2_1 or using dilations) improve small-object recall?

Reproducibility
Good. Architecture, loss, sampling scheme, and training schedule are described; code is promised. Please consolidate loss normalization and λ settings and report exact hardware/software details.

Decision
Recommendation: Accept (strong). The paper provides a simple, effective, and influential approach that advances both accuracy and efficiency in object detection.

Confidence
High.
