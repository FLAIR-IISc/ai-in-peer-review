

# Review of "Faster R-CNN: Towards Real-Time Object Detection with Region Proposal Networks"

This paper proposes Region Proposal Networks (RPNs) as a method to integrate region proposal generation with object detection in a unified deep learning framework. The key insight is that convolutional feature maps used for detection can also be used for proposal generation, with RPNs sharing these features with the detection network to make the proposal step nearly cost-free (10ms per image). The authors demonstrate this approach with Fast R-CNN, achieving state-of-the-art detection accuracy (73.2% mAP on PASCAL VOC 2007) with a frame rate of 5fps.

The paper presents a well-structured engineering solution that addresses the computational bottleneck of region proposal methods like Selective Search (which takes 1-2 seconds per image). The authors provide thorough experimental validation on PASCAL VOC benchmarks, comparing against strong baselines (Selective Search and EdgeBoxes) and including ablation studies to analyze the contribution of different components. The training procedure for the unified network is carefully described, and the results show consistent improvements over the baseline.

However, as a skeptical reviewer who prioritizes methodological soundness and established approaches, I have significant concerns about the paper's novelty and theoretical foundation. While the authors claim an "algorithmic change," their approach is fundamentally an engineering optimization of sharing computation rather than a fundamentally new methodological insight. The core idea of using a deep network for proposals is not entirely novel—MultiBox [3, 20] and OverFeat [18] already proposed similar concepts, though the authors don't adequately differentiate their approach from these prior works. The related work section fails to provide a clear contrast between their RPN and these existing methods.

The paper's claim of "nearly cost-free" proposal computation is misleading. As the authors acknowledge, this is achieved through computational sharing with the detection network, but the paper doesn't rigorously address whether the same speed improvement could be achieved with a GPU-optimized implementation of Selective Search, which they dismiss as "an obvious engineering solution." This dismissal is unwarranted without proper comparison.

The theoretical justification for why this approach works better than alternatives is weak. The paper lacks any theoretical analysis or formal proof of why sharing conv layers would produce better proposals than other methods. The ablation studies are thorough but don't address this fundamental question. The authors' claim that "the RPN is actively trained and benefits from better networks" doesn't sufficiently explain why this training process produces better proposals than simply using a more powerful GPU for traditional proposal methods.

Regarding significance, the paper's contribution is primarily practical—reducing the computational bottleneck of object detection systems. While this is valuable for applications, it represents an incremental improvement rather than a significant advance in the field. The accuracy gains over Selective Search (59.9% vs 58.7% mAP) are modest, and the paper doesn't compare with other emerging real-time detection approaches (e.g., SSD, YOLO) that were gaining traction around the time of submission.

The paper is well-written and clearly organized, with sufficient technical detail for reproduction. The experimental results are solid, but they don't provide enough evidence to justify the paper's claim of a fundamental methodological advance. The authors present a clever engineering solution, but it's not sufficiently novel or theoretically grounded to warrant publication at NIPS as a significant contribution to the field.

**Strengths:**
- Thorough experimental validation on standard benchmarks
- Clear description of the unified training procedure
- Strong practical results showing significant speed improvements
- Good ablation studies that help understand the contribution of different components

**Weaknesses:**
- Insufficient differentiation from prior work on deep network-based proposals
- Weak theoretical justification for the approach
- Overstated claims about the novelty and significance of the method
- Failure to properly address the alternative engineering solution (GPU-optimized Selective Search)
- Modest accuracy improvements over strong baselines

**Recommendation:** Reject. While the paper presents a well-executed engineering solution that improves practical object detection systems, it does not offer a sufficiently novel or theoretically sound methodological contribution to warrant publication at NIPS. The core idea is an incremental optimization rather than a fundamental advance, and the paper fails to provide adequate evidence of novelty compared to prior work. The significance claim is overstated given the modest accuracy gains and lack of comparison with other emerging real-time detection approaches.
