Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes Region Proposal Networks (RPNs), a fully convolutional module that generates class-agnostic object proposals directly from shared convolutional feature maps used by a downstream detector (Fast R-CNN). At each spatial location, a small 3×3 conv “head” predicts objectness scores and bounding-box regressions for k anchors (3 scales × 3 aspect ratios). Training uses a multi-task loss with positive/negative anchors defined by IoU thresholds, and the system is trained via a pragmatic 4-step alternating scheme to share convolutional layers between the RPN and Fast R-CNN. With shared features, proposal computation is nearly free (~10 ms/image), enabling 5–17 fps while improving detection accuracy over Selective Search (SS) and EdgeBoxes (EB) on PASCAL VOC 2007/2012. Ablations study the effects of sharing, number of proposals, the roles of classification and regression, NMS, and a one-stage sliding-window baseline similar in spirit to OverFeat.

This work is situated among: OverFeat [18] (one-stage sliding-window localization/detection), MultiBox [3,20] (proposal prediction via a fully connected head on fixed crops), SPP/RCNN/ Fast R-CNN [7,6,5] (region-based detection with shared convs), and engineered proposal methods SS [22] and EB [24]. Key differences: translation-invariant anchors rather than globally learned priors (MultiBox), proposal generation integrated with and trained for the downstream detector, and computational sharing that removes the proposal bottleneck present in Fast R-CNN.

Strengths
- Clear, simple, and effective design: a lightweight conv head on shared features with a principled multi-task loss and anchor parameterization.
- Strong empirical results: higher mAP than SS/EB when paired with Fast R-CNN, with 300 proposals sufficing; VGG-16 + RPN surpasses SS while being much faster.
- Thorough ablations: shared vs unshared features, proposal counts, contributions of cls vs reg, NMS impact, and a one-stage vs two-stage comparison.
- Practical impact: compelling speed–accuracy trade-off and near cost-free proposals on GPU; code availability.
- Good discussion of translation-invariant anchors vs learned global priors (MultiBox), and why the parameter count and overfitting risk are reduced.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Evaluation breadth: limited to PASCAL VOC; no results on larger/modern benchmarks or diverse domains. Even within VOC, more detailed breakdowns (e.g., small vs medium vs large objects, per-class AP) would strengthen the claims.
- Training scheme: the 4-step alternating procedure is ad hoc; joint end-to-end optimization is not attempted. A small study on whether fully joint training converges and how it compares would improve the story.
- Sensitivity to anchor design: only 3 scales × 3 aspect ratios are evaluated. An analysis of sensitivity to (a) anchor scales/ratios, (b) feature stride, and (c) image scale would clarify robustness and guide practitioners.
- Fairness of speed comparisons: SS and EB are CPU implementations while the detector and RPN are GPU. Although the paper acknowledges this, a GPU-optimized SS/EB or a detector-only runtime breakdown and a CPU-only ablation would contextualize the end-to-end gains.
- Limited comparisons to deep proposal methods: MultiBox is discussed, but side-by-side proposal quality and detection comparisons on the same dataset/backbone are not reported.
- Proposal quality metrics: recall–IoU curves are shown, but high-IoU (≥0.85) recall and localization error analysis/failure modes are not explored. This would help diagnose the regression behavior.
- Clarity/details: some normalization constants and the choice of λ are presented partly in a footnote; consolidating the final settings in the main text would improve reproducibility. References contain formatting issues.

Quality
The methodology is technically sound: the FCN-style head with anchors, smooth L1 regression, and IoU-based label assignment are standard choices well supported by ablations. Experimental evidence is convincing for VOC: the RPN proposals yield higher mAP than SS/EB with far fewer proposals and orders-of-magnitude faster proposal generation. The one-stage baseline is a fair internal comparison and supports the two-stage cascade choice. Some aspects (joint training feasibility, anchor sensitivity) are underexplored but do not undermine the main claims.

Clarity
The paper is generally well written and organized. The training pipeline and loss are explained clearly, and the empirical sections are easy to follow. Minor clarity issues:
- Collect all loss normalization and λ choices in one place; the footnote suggests differences between code and paper defaults.
- Explicitly state typical numbers of proposals after NMS, not just maximums.
- Clean up reference formatting and footnote artifacts; a brief algorithm box for the 4-step training would help.

Originality
While building on Fast R-CNN and FCNs, the key novelty is integrating proposal generation into the detection network via shared features and translation-invariant anchors, with an effective alternating training procedure. The anchor idea is related to prior priors (e.g., MultiBox), but the translation-invariant, conv-head formulation with a small output space is a meaningful and practical advance. Overall originality is moderate-to-high, with high pragmatic novelty.

Significance
High. The method removes a major bottleneck in region-based detectors, improves accuracy, and is simple to adopt. The approach is likely to be widely used and extended, influencing both academic research and practical deployments.

Questions and requests for authors
- Can the authors report results with fully joint end-to-end training of RPN + Fast R-CNN? If unstable, what failure modes occur?
- How sensitive are results to the choice of anchor scales/ratios and to the 16-pixel stride? Any gains from adding smaller anchors for small objects?
- How does performance vary with different NMS IoU thresholds or with soft-NMS-like alternatives?
- Could you provide per-class AP and size-based analysis, and a breakdown of false positives (localization vs background vs confusion)?
- Any results with multi-scale testing or smaller input scales to characterize the speed–accuracy trade-off further?

Pros for acceptance
- Strong empirical improvements with substantial speedups; practical impact.
- Elegant, simple architecture with clear ablations.
- Well-motivated anchor design and shared computation.
- Code availability and reproducibility.

Cons for acceptance
- Limited dataset scope; missing comparisons to alternative deep proposal generators on the same backbone.
- Alternating training is somewhat heuristic; no joint optimization results.
- Limited sensitivity analysis of anchor design and image/feature resolutions.

Minor comments and typos
- Several references are incomplete or have formatting issues; please fix.
- Clarify the final λ and normalization choices used in reported results.
- Fix typographical artifacts (e.g., de∗tection, misplaced footnotes).

Recommendation
Accept (strong). This paper offers a significant, well-executed contribution that advances the state of the art in speed and accuracy for object detection, with high likelihood of broad adoption.

Reviewer confidence: High.
