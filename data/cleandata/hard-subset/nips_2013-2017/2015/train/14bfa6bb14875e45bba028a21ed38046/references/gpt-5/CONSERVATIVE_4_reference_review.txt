Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes Region Proposal Networks (RPNs) that generate class-agnostic object proposals by sliding a small fully convolutional subnetwork over shared convolutional feature maps. Each spatial location predicts k anchor boxes (across 3 scales × 3 aspect ratios), outputting objectness scores and box regressions. The RPN shares conv layers with a Fast R-CNN detector and is trained via a pragmatic four-step alternating fine-tuning scheme so that both modules ultimately use the same backbone features. On PASCAL VOC 2007/2012, RPN+Fast R-CNN achieves higher mAP than Fast R-CNN with Selective Search (SS) or EdgeBoxes (EB) while running much faster due to feature sharing and fewer proposals (∼300). The paper positions itself against OverFeat (one-stage, class-specific sliding-window detection) and MultiBox (proposal generation with many non-translation-invariant prototypes), arguing reduced parameters, translation invariance, and the empirical advantage of two-stage cascades.

Strengths
- Clear and well-motivated integration of proposal generation with detection via shared conv features; pragmatic training scheme that works in practice.
- Strong empirical gains in end-to-end runtime with competitive/improved accuracy on PASCAL VOC; proposals effectively “free” relative to detection backbone.
- Sensible anchor design with translation invariance and a compact output head; thorough ablations on proposal count, NMS, and roles of cls vs reg heads.
- Direct comparison with SS/EB and with a one-stage sliding-window baseline; shows the benefit of cascaded proposal + detection.

Weaknesses / concerns
- Evaluation scope is limited to PASCAL VOC; lacks results on larger/more diverse benchmarks (ILSVRC DET, COCO), where small objects and crowded scenes are common.
- Runtime comparisons are not fully controlled: RPN exploits GPU and shared convs while SS/EB are CPU implementations; the “proposals are nearly free” claim would be stronger with GPU-optimized SS/EB or at least conv-sharing baselines that isolate proposal vs detection costs.
- The alternating training heuristic is reasonable but ad hoc; no evidence that joint end-to-end training fails or underperforms. A joint training experiment would strengthen the methodology claim.
- Sensitivity analyses are partial. Anchors (scales/aspect ratios), stride, IoU thresholds for positive/negative labeling (0.7/0.3), NMS threshold (0.7), and image scale (s=600) could materially affect performance; more systematic sweeps and per-size recall/AP analyses (especially for small objects) are missing.
- Comparisons to MultiBox and OverFeat are discussed conceptually, but head-to-head empirical comparisons (e.g., on the same backbone/training data) are limited. MultiBox used many prototypes; still, a controlled variant (translation-invariant prototypes vs learned priors) would clarify where the gains come from (architecture vs training data).
- Generalization of class-agnostic proposals to unseen categories is not evaluated (e.g., held-out classes test).

Quality
The work is technically sound and clearly leverages established components (SPP/Fast R-CNN training, smooth L1 regression, SGD). Claims about speed and accuracy on VOC are supported by experiments and ablations. However, some claims could be better substantiated:
- “Nearly cost-free” proposals: show a breakdown that controls for shared conv compute and compares against GPU baselines where possible.
- Robustness: provide sensitivity to anchor design and IoU thresholds, and failure mode analysis (small/elongated objects, crowded scenes).
- Training design: show whether joint training converges and how it compares to the 4-step schedule.

Clarity
The paper is generally well written and organized. The method is described with sufficient detail to reproduce (loss, sampling, normalizations, initialization). A few places could be clearer:
- The normalization/weighting of the loss terms (λ, Ncls, Nreg) is somewhat confusing, with an “early implementation” note. Please standardize and report sensitivity to λ.
- Explicitly report proposal recall at IoU 0.5/0.7/0.8 for N∈{100,300,1k,2k}, broken down by object size.
- Provide per-class AP tables and small/medium/large AP if available.

Originality
The novelty is incremental but meaningful: leveraging shared conv features for proposals, translation-invariant anchors, and a compact conv head are practical contributions. The anchor formulation is related to classic sliding windows with multiple aspect ratios; the main advance is architectural integration with two-stage detection. Given substantial prior art (OverFeat, MultiBox, SPPnet/Fast R-CNN), the paper is best viewed as a strong systems contribution rather than a fundamentally new theoretical idea.

Significance
High practical significance if the gains generalize beyond VOC: making proposals “almost free” meaningfully reduces end-to-end detection cost and simplifies pipelines. The approach is likely to be widely adopted and extended. That said, the current empirical scope is narrow; confirming results on larger benchmarks and stronger baselines would cement impact.

Questions for the authors
1. Can you report results on ILSVRC DET or COCO, including small-object performance and proposal recall/AP at higher IoUs?
2. How sensitive is mAP to the choice of anchor scales/ratios and the conv stride (e.g., stride 8 vs 16)? Any adaptive/learned anchor variants tested?
3. Have you tried joint end-to-end training of RPN and Fast R-CNN (single graph, shared losses)? Does it converge and how does it compare?
4. Can you provide a more controlled runtime breakdown (e.g., same GPU/CPU setting for all methods, or a GPU-ported EB/SS), to isolate the benefit from sharing vs algorithmic differences?
5. How does performance change with different NMS thresholds, and with Soft-NMS-like variants?
6. What are common failure modes (missed small/thin objects, localization at IoU>0.7/0.8)?

Minor comments
- Clarify treatment of cross-boundary anchors at training vs testing; consider a masking approach to avoid discarding many anchors near borders.
- Include memory usage and batch size constraints for VGG-16; detail which layers are frozen vs fine-tuned.

Pros
- Practical, effective integration of proposal and detection with shared features.
- Strong speedups with maintained/improved accuracy on VOC.
- Clear architectural design and ablations.

Cons
- Limited evaluation scope; missing large-scale benchmarks and stronger baselines.
- Training scheme is heuristic; joint optimization not evaluated.
- Runtime fairness concerns vs CPU proposal methods; limited sensitivity analyses.

Recommendation
Weak accept. The paper is methodologically solid, builds on established components, and presents compelling empirical benefits on VOC. To fully convince a conservative audience, broader evaluation and stronger controls are needed, but the contribution is likely to be impactful and adopted by practitioners.

Confidence
High.
