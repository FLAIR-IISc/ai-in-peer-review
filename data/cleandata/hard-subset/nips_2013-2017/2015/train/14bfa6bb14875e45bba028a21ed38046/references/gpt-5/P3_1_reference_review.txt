Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes Region Proposal Networks (RPNs), a fully convolutional module that predicts class-agnostic object proposals (objectness scores and box regression) directly from shared convolutional features that also feed a Fast R-CNN detector. The key ideas are: (1) sliding a small 3×3 conv “mini-network” over the last shared conv map to produce per-location predictions for k anchor boxes covering multiple scales and aspect ratios; (2) translation-invariant anchors that drastically reduce parameters vs. MultiBox; (3) a multi-task loss coupling objectness and box regression; and (4) an alternating 4-step training scheme to share conv layers between proposal and detection networks. Empirically, coupling RPNs with Fast R-CNN yields higher mAP than Selective Search/EdgeBoxes while reducing proposal cost to ~10 ms on a GPU and enabling near real-time detection.

This work builds directly on SPPnet/Fast R-CNN (shared conv features; RoI pooling) and proposal methods (Selective Search, EdgeBoxes). It contrasts with OverFeat’s single-stage, class-specific sliding-window detection and MultiBox’s non-translation-invariant anchor design predicting many boxes from a large fully connected head. The RPN introduces anchor tiling and a shallow conv head that scales naturally across spatial positions and shares computation with the detector.

Technical quality
Strengths:
- Methodology is sound and well-motivated. The loss, target parameterization, and label assignment are consistent with prior R-CNN-style regression and robust loss (smooth L1).
- The anchor design yields a compact output head ((4+2)×k vs. (4+1)×800 in MultiBox), addressing overfitting and compute.
- The alternating training scheme is pragmatic and converges to a shared feature backbone, improving both proposal quality and downstream detection.
- Experiments are thorough on VOC07/12, with ablations covering shared vs. unshared backbones, proposal count, effect of cls vs. reg heads, NMS, and backbone depth (ZF vs. VGG-16).
- Empirical results support claims: improved mAP over SS with fewer proposals; proposal runtime effectively amortized by shared features; recall vs. IoU curves showing robustness as proposal budget shrinks.

Weaknesses / points to improve:
- Joint end-to-end training of proposal and detection with shared conv layers is deferred to future work. While the authors provide a good alternating scheme, it leaves open whether a single multitask objective could further improve accuracy/simplicity.
- Sensitivity to anchor hyperparameters (scales, aspect ratios, placement stride) is not systematically evaluated. The chosen 3 scales × 3 ratios work well on VOC, but robustness across datasets and small-object performance (given stride 16) deserve deeper analysis.
- Boundary handling: all cross-boundary anchors are ignored during training. This simplifies optimization but throws away a large fraction of anchors and could bias learning near image edges. Alternatives (e.g., valid mask with regression to clipped targets) could be discussed.
- Proposal quality is mostly assessed via VOC mAP and recall-IoU. More localization-focused metrics (AP at higher IoU thresholds) or error diagnosis (e.g., small/occluded object strata) would strengthen the analysis.
- Comparisons to MultiBox and OverFeat are discussed conceptually and via a controlled one-stage baseline, but empirical head-to-head with a strong MultiBox configuration is missing (authors note architectural differences; still, a best-effort comparison would be useful).
- Generalization beyond VOC: evaluation on a larger-scale dataset (e.g., MS COCO) would better support significance claims and expose small-object limitations.

Clarity
The paper is clearly written, with sufficient architectural detail to reproduce results. The description of anchor targets, loss, and sampling is explicit. Implementation details (normalization constants, λ, learning schedule, sampling ratio, NMS threshold) are provided, and code availability is a plus. Two minor clarity suggestions:
- Consolidate and clarify the normalization choices for the loss terms (Ncls, Nreg) and the historical note about λ to avoid confusion for implementers.
- The 4-step training could be presented more compactly with a schematic that highlights which layers are frozen/updated at each step.

Originality
The central conceptual novelty is the anchor-based, translation-invariant, fully convolutional proposal mechanism trained jointly (via shared backbone) with the detector, eliminating the hand-crafted, CPU-bound proposal stage. While prior work predicted boxes with deep nets (MultiBox, OverFeat), this paper’s design (anchors tiled on shared backbone features, shallow conv heads, and alternating feature sharing) is distinct and impactful. The translation-invariant anchor formulation and parameter-efficiency argument are well-articulated.

Significance
High. The method removes the proposal bottleneck in region-based detectors, enabling near real-time detection without sacrificing accuracy. The ideas are likely to be widely adopted (and indeed form a foundation for subsequent detectors), and the release of code further increases impact. The approach provides a clear path forward for unified, learnable proposal + detection systems.

Specific questions and suggestions
- Please report sensitivity to the positive/negative IoU thresholds (0.7/0.3) and to NMS IoU. Does tuning these improve high-IoU localization or small-object recall?
- Analyze performance by object size and aspect ratio; discuss whether smaller strides (e.g., dilated features) or additional smaller-scale anchors help.
- Provide a brief analysis of the runtime/memory breakdown, including NMS cost and proposal pre/post-processing, and specify the GPU used.
- Consider a simple joint training variant (approximate end-to-end with alternating minibatches or a combined loss) to test whether alternating is necessary.
- Evaluate effect of learning anchor shapes (e.g., via k-means initialization or gradient-updated scales/ratios).
- Include per-class AP and qualitative failure modes.

Pros
- Elegant, simple architecture that unifies proposals and detection, with strong gains in speed and accuracy.
- Solid empirical validation and ablations; code available.
- Clear advancement over hand-crafted proposal pipelines and prior deep box predictors.

Cons
- Limited dataset scope (VOC only) and limited analysis of small objects/anchor sensitivity.
- No joint end-to-end training experiment; some design choices justified empirically but not deeply analyzed.

Recommendation
Accept (high). The paper is technically sound, clearly written, and makes a substantial, practical contribution that advances state of the art and will influence future work.
