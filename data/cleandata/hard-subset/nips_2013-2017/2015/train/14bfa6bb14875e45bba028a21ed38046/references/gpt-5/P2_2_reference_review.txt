Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes Region Proposal Networks (RPNs) that generate class-agnostic object proposals by sliding a small fully-convolutional subnetwork over shared convolutional feature maps. Each spatial location predicts k proposals via “anchors” at multiple scales and aspect ratios, outputting objectness scores and bounding-box regressions. The RPN shares convolutional layers with a Fast R-CNN detector, making proposal generation nearly free when combined with detection. Training uses an alternating 4-step scheme to learn shared features.

This work builds directly on region-based CNNs (R-CNN, SPP-net, Fast R-CNN) and addresses the remaining bottleneck: proposal computation (Selective Search, EdgeBoxes). It contrasts with OverFeat (one-stage, class-specific sliding-window detection) and MultiBox (learned proposals from non-translation-invariant priors), highlighting translation-invariance of anchors and much smaller output dimensionality (and hence parameter count and overfitting risk). The method is evaluated on PASCAL VOC 2007/2012, with extensive ablations (proposal count, contributions of cls vs reg, shared vs unshared features, NMS effects).

Quality
- Technical soundness: The model is well specified: architecture, anchor parameterization, loss (multi-task with smooth L1), sampling, optimization details, and the alternating training scheme. The translation-invariant anchor design and per-position k predictions are principled and computationally efficient. The choice to ignore cross-boundary anchors during training and to clip at test time is reasonable.
- Empirical support: Claims are backed by clear experiments:
  - Accuracy: With VGG-16, Fast R-CNN + RPN achieves 69.9% mAP (VOC07) with shared features and 70.4% mAP (VOC12), surpassing SS baselines. Using VOC07+12 union yields 73.2% mAP.
  - Speed: Proposal stage adds ~10 ms on GPU; total per-image time ~198 ms with VGG-16 and ~17 fps with ZF, compared to ~1.5 s for SS proposals plus detection.
  - Ablations: Effects of proposal count (300 vs 2k), NMS, cls vs reg contributions, network backbone (ZF vs VGG), and shared vs unshared features are carefully studied. Notably, turning off regression drops mAP substantially (52.1%), and mAP with only 100 top-ranked proposals remains competitive (55.1% in the fixed-detector setting), showing high-quality ranking.
  - Diagnostics: Recall vs IoU plots show graceful degradation with fewer proposals relative to SS/EB.
- Limitations in evaluation: Experiments are limited to VOC (relatively small, 20 classes). Sensitivity to anchor hyperparameters, stride, and image scale is discussed but not systematically explored. Joint end-to-end training of the unified network is deferred.

Clarity
The paper is clearly written and well organized. The architecture, loss, and training regime are described in enough detail to reproduce the work; hyperparameters, sampling ratios, learning rates, normalization, and implementation specifics (e.g., Caffe, initialization, NMS threshold) are provided. The comparison to related work (OverFeat, MultiBox, SS, EB, SPP/RCNN/FR-CNN) is appropriate and highlights differences. Figures/tables (not included here) reportedly illustrate architecture and recall curves. Minor clarity suggestions:
- Provide a short algorithmic outline for the 4-step alternating training to aid reproducibility.
- Clarify the empirical sensitivity to the NMS IoU threshold and to the chosen 3 scales × 3 ratios.
- Briefly discuss memory footprint and batch sizing constraints when sharing features with VGG-16.

Originality
The core novelty is the unification of proposal generation and detection via shared convolutional features and a fully convolutional anchor-based proposal mechanism. While learned proposals have precedents (MultiBox), the translation-invariant anchor formulation, per-position multi-scale/multi-aspect predictions with a very low-dimensional output head, and practical alternating training for shared features are new in combination and address a key bottleneck. The proposal that proposals are “nearly free” when sharing features is a simple but powerful systems-level insight.

Significance
High. The work removes a major computational bottleneck in state-of-the-art detectors, enabling near real-time performance without sacrificing accuracy, and advances mAP over strong SS baselines. The approach is likely to be widely adopted because:
- It cleanly integrates with existing region-based detectors.
- It is conceptually simple, efficient, and effective.
- It is architecture-agnostic (ZF, VGG) and improves with better backbones.
The diagnostic experiments also clarify why two-stage proposal+detector pipelines outperform one-stage sliding-window detectors in this setting.

Strengths
- Elegant, practical unification of proposals and detection through shared conv features.
- Strong empirical results with thorough ablations; clear speed/accuracy gains.
- Sound anchor-based design with translation invariance and low parameter count.
- Reproducible details and released code.
- Insightful comparisons to one-stage variants and other proposal methods.

Weaknesses
- Evaluation limited to PASCAL; results on larger, more diverse datasets would strengthen generality.
- Anchor choices and feature stride are hand-tuned; no data-driven anchor learning or sensitivity study.
- Training relies on an alternating scheme rather than fully joint optimization; convergence properties of joint training are left open.
- Some components are heuristic (e.g., ignoring cross-boundary anchors, fixed NMS threshold) without sensitivity analysis.

Suggestions and questions for the authors
- Report sensitivity to anchor scales/ratios and NMS threshold; could a small set of data-driven anchors improve further?
- Discuss feasibility and stability of truly joint end-to-end training; any preliminary results?
- Analyze performance on small objects versus large ones given stride 16; could dilated convs or finer strides help?
- Provide breakdown of proposal recall by category and object size, and per-stage timing variance across images.

Arguments for acceptance
- Significant advance in both accuracy and efficiency on a central vision task.
- Clear, novel architectural idea with strong empirical support and broad impact potential.
- Thorough experimentation and ablations; well written and reproducible.

Arguments against acceptance
- Limited dataset scope; some design choices are heuristic with limited sensitivity analysis.
- Alternating training instead of fully joint optimization raises questions about optimality.

Recommendation
Accept. The paper is technically solid, clearly written, and introduces a simple, influential idea that meaningfully advances the state of the art in object detection by eliminating the proposal bottleneck through shared convolutional computation. The empirical evidence is compelling and the method is likely to be widely used and extended.
