Summary
The paper proposes Region Proposal Networks (RPNs) that generate object proposals using a fully convolutional subnetwork sharing features with a detection network (Fast R-CNN). The key ideas are: (1) anchor boxes—translation-invariant reference boxes at each spatial location across multiple scales and aspect ratios—to parameterize proposals; (2) a two-branch prediction head per location to output objectness scores and box regressions for k anchors; and (3) a practical 4-step alternating training scheme to learn shared convolutional features for both proposal generation and detection. Compared to prior pipelines that relied on CPU-based proposal methods (Selective Search, EdgeBoxes), RPNs make proposal computation nearly free when sharing features with the detector, enabling end-to-end deep-learning-based detection that is both accurate and fast.

Relation to prior work
The work builds on region-based detectors (R-CNN, SPPnet, Fast R-CNN) that share convolutional features across proposals. It is related to OverFeat’s sliding-window localization and MultiBox’s neural proposal generation, but differs by using dense, translation-invariant anchors and a compact output head (4+2 outputs per anchor) that shares spatial weights across the image, dramatically reducing parameters versus MultiBox’s image-level anchor set. It also follows the FCN paradigm by using conv prediction layers. The paper carefully contrasts with OverFeat (one-stage vs two-stage) and MultiBox (anchor design, parameter count), and provides ablations that justify the two-stage design.

Strengths
- Clear and effective core contribution: a learnable, convolutional proposal mechanism that shares features with detection, eliminating the proposal bottleneck.
- Elegant anchor design with translation invariance; compact head with k anchors per location supports multiscale/multi-aspect detection without image pyramids.
- Strong empirical results: competitive or better mAP than SS/EB with far fewer proposals; substantial speedup. With VGG-16, 69.9% mAP on VOC07 using shared features; 73.2% with extra training data; 70.4% on VOC12. Proposal computation adds ~10 ms when shared; end-to-end ~5 fps with VGG-16 and up to 17 fps with ZF.
- Thorough ablations: impact of sharing features; proposal count; removing classification or regression branches; effect of NMS; ZF vs VGG; and a controlled comparison against one-stage sliding-window Fast R-CNN showing 4.8% mAP drop for one-stage.
- Practical training recipe and many reproducibility details (losses, anchor settings, sampling, hyperparameters), plus code release.

Weaknesses and questions
- Joint training is not attempted; the paper uses a 4-step alternating scheme. While pragmatic and effective, a discussion of convergence issues or preliminary joint-training results would strengthen the claim of a unified network.
- Evaluation is limited to PASCAL VOC. Given the emphasis on real-time performance, results on a larger, more diverse dataset (e.g., COCO) would better characterize generalization, especially for small objects.
- Sensitivity to anchor design is only lightly explored. The paper fixes 3 scales and 3 aspect ratios; more analysis on anchor choices, strides, and their effect on small/elongated objects would be helpful.
- Stride of 16 pixels may limit small-object recall; the paper notes this but does not quantify it beyond Recall-to-IoU plots. An experiment with reduced stride or feature pyramid variants would clarify limitations.
- The fairness of runtime comparisons is discussed (CPU vs GPU), but there is no GPU implementation comparison of SS/EB; still, the main contribution is algorithmic sharing rather than engineering parity.
- Some referencing/formatting issues in the bibliography; a few implementation details (e.g., exact minibatch composition across images vs per-image, data augmentation) could be clarified.

Quality
Technically sound. The loss, anchor parameterization, sampling, and optimization are standard and well-motivated. Claims are supported by comprehensive experiments and ablations. The analysis is careful (e.g., separating cls/reg roles and training/testing proposal consistency) and includes both accuracy and runtime. Limitations (e.g., not jointly trained) are acknowledged.

Clarity
Generally very clear and well-organized. The method is described with sufficient detail to reproduce, including architecture, loss normalization, sampling strategy, NMS and test-time settings, and training hyperparameters. Figures and tables (not included here) reportedly illustrate the pipeline and ablations. Minor issues: reference formatting and a few implementation clarifications as noted.

Originality
High. While related components existed (FCNs, sliding-window localization, MultiBox anchors), the translation-invariant anchor mechanism coupled with a lightweight, shared proposal head and the unification with Fast R-CNN via feature sharing is a novel and impactful combination. The two-stage argument is empirically substantiated.

Significance
Very high. The work removes a major bottleneck in detection, enabling practical, accurate, near-real-time systems. The approach is likely to be adopted broadly and to influence subsequent designs (anchors, shared heads, two-stage detectors). The demonstrated state-of-the-art mAP and speed represent a substantive advance.

Suggestions for authors
- Provide sensitivity analysis for anchor scales/ratios and stride; report small-object performance explicitly.
- Include preliminary results or discussion on end-to-end joint training stability.
- Add results on a larger dataset to test generalization and speed/accuracy trade-offs under more challenging conditions.
- Clarify a few implementation details (data augmentation, exact minibatch balancing when images vary in positives).

Arguments for acceptance
- Strong, simple idea with elegant implementation.
- Significant empirical gains in both speed and accuracy; extensive ablations.
- Clear advance over prior proposal methods and one-stage baselines.
- High potential impact and broad applicability.

Arguments against acceptance
- Lack of joint end-to-end training results.
- Evaluation limited to VOC; limited analysis of sensitivity to anchor design and small-object performance.

Recommendation
Accept. The paper meets the NeurIPS criteria strongly on quality, clarity, originality, and significance. The contributions are well-justified and experimentally validated, and the work is likely to have substantial and lasting impact.
