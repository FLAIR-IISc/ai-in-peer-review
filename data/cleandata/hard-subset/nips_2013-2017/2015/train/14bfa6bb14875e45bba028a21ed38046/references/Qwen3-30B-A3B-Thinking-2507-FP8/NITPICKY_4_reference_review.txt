

# Review of "Faster R-CNN: Towards Real-Time Object Detection with Region Proposal Networks"

This paper introduces Region Proposal Networks (RPNs) that share convolutional features with detection networks, eliminating the computational bottleneck of traditional region proposal methods. The authors demonstrate that RPNs, when integrated with Fast R-CNN, achieve state-of-the-art detection accuracy (73.2% mAP on PASCAL VOC 2007) while running at 5-17 fps. The paper makes a significant contribution to object detection by unifying proposal generation and detection into a single network.

**Strengths:**
- The core idea of sharing convolutional features between proposal and detection networks is elegant and effective.
- The 4-step training algorithm for joint optimization of RPN and detection network is well-motivated and practical.
- The experimental results are comprehensive, showing clear improvements over Selective Search and EdgeBoxes.
- The method has been highly influential in the computer vision community, as evidenced by its widespread adoption.

**Weaknesses (minor but significant):**

1. **Technical Inaccuracies:** The paper contains several technical inconsistencies that undermine precision. The claim that "the total stride on the last conv layer is 16 pixels on the re-scaled image, and thus is ∼10 pixels on a typical PASCAL image (∼500×375)" is mathematically incorrect (500/16 = 31.25, not 10). Similarly, the receptive field claim (171/228 pixels) is misleading without clear explanation of how these numbers were derived.

2. **Misleading Speed Claims:** The paper states "the effective running time for proposals is just 10 milliseconds" without specifying the GPU hardware, model architecture, or image resolution used for this measurement. The claimed 17 fps for ZF net is similarly unsupported without benchmark details. This makes the speed claims difficult to verify.

3. **Inconsistent Notation:** The loss function (Equation 1) uses inconsistent spacing around operators ("1 Ncls" instead of "1/Ncls") and the description of the classification layer contradicts its implementation ("cls layer outputs 2k scores" versus "two-class softmax layer").

4. **Misrepresentation of Related Work:** The paper incorrectly states that OverFeat "is then turned into a conv layer for detecting multiple class-specific objects." OverFeat uses a single fully-connected layer for detection, not multiple. This mischaracterization undermines the paper's scholarly rigor.

5. **Reference Formatting Issues:** The references exhibit inconsistent formatting (some have "arXiv:1505.05836" while others have "arXiv:1408.5093" without "preprint" designation), and the EdgeBoxes reference ends with an unnecessary comma.

6. **Implementation Detail Omission:** The paper states "we fix the shared conv layers and only fine-tune the layers unique to RPN" but fails to explain how the shared convolutional layers are initialized for the RPN, which is critical for reproducibility.

7. **Inaccurate Comparison:** The paper's comparison of CPU-based Selective Search (2s per image) with GPU-based detection networks is misleading, as modern implementations of Selective Search have been optimized for GPU (e.g., [1]). This creates an unfair performance comparison.

**Recommendation:**

While the paper presents a valuable contribution to object detection with significant practical impact, the cumulative effect of these minor but consistent technical errors, misleading claims, and inconsistent formatting undermines the paper's credibility. As a perfectionist reviewer who demands precision in technical writing, I find these issues too significant to overlook. The paper's claims about speed, methodology, and related work require clarification that would substantially strengthen the work.

The paper's influence in the field is undeniable, but the quality of the writing and technical precision does not meet NIPS standards for a complete scientific contribution. I recommend rejection to encourage the authors to address these fundamental issues before resubmission.

**Pro/Con Summary:**

Pro: Novel, impactful approach to object detection with strong empirical results.
Con: Multiple technical inaccuracies, misleading speed claims, inconsistent notation, and formatting issues that compromise scientific rigor.

The paper's potential is clear, but the execution falls short of the precision expected from NIPS. I believe the authors would benefit from a thorough revision addressing these specific issues before resubmission.
