Summary and relation to prior work
The paper introduces Region Proposal Networks (RPNs) that generate class-agnostic object proposals by adding a lightweight, fully convolutional head on top of shared convolutional features used by a Fast R-CNN detector. Each spatial location predicts k boxes (via translation-invariant “anchors” covering multiple scales/aspect ratios) with objectness scores and box regressions. The key idea is to share feature computation between proposal generation and detection, rendering proposals nearly “free” at test time (~10 ms), and to train the unified system with an alternating 4-step scheme that fine-tunes RPN and Fast R-CNN in turn to share conv layers.

This work builds directly on R-CNN and Fast R-CNN by eliminating the CPU-bound proposal bottleneck (Selective Search, EdgeBoxes). It contrasts with OverFeat (a one-stage, class-specific sliding-window detector) and MultiBox (multiple non-translation-invariant anchors predicted per image/crop) by using translation-invariant anchors and a two-stage cascade: class-agnostic proposals followed by class-specific detection. The paper provides ablations isolating contributions of classification vs. regression within RPN, the impact of feature sharing, and a clean comparison to one-stage sliding-window detection using Fast R-CNN heads.

On PASCAL VOC 2007/2012, RPN+Fast R-CNN improves or matches mAP versus SS/EB while being substantially faster: with VGG-16, 69.9% mAP with shared features (73.2% when trained on VOC07+12 union), and an end-to-end runtime of ~198 ms per image (including detection) vs. 1.5 s for SS proposals alone.

Strengths
- Novelty and conceptual clarity: Turning proposals into a learned, translation-invariant, fully convolutional module that shares features with the detector is a simple, bold idea with large practical impact.
- Elegant engineering: Anchors with multiple scales/aspect ratios and 1×1 sibling heads keep parameter count low and avoid per-proposal convolutions; training uses standard multi-task loss and mini-batching.
- Strong empirical results: Competitive or superior mAP to SS/EB with far fewer proposals (e.g., 300) and orders-of-magnitude speedup. Ablations convincingly show the role of shared features, classification vs regression, NMS, and proposal counts.
- Clear positioning vs. related work: The paper articulates why translation invariance and two-stage cascades help against MultiBox and OverFeat, and provides a fair one-stage baseline within the same framework.
- Practical impact: Reduction of proposal overhead to ~10 ms unlocks near real-time detection with deep backbones, likely to set a new direction for detector design.

Weaknesses and areas for improvement
- Scope of evaluation: Results are limited to VOC; a larger-scale dataset (e.g., COCO) would better characterize generalization, especially for small objects and crowded scenes.
- Training strategy: The alternating 4-step scheme is pragmatic; the paper notes joint training is uncertain. A brief stability analysis or limited experiments with joint end-to-end optimization would strengthen the claim of a unified network.
- Hyperparameter sensitivity: Anchor scales/aspect ratios, NMS IoU (0.7), and mini-batch sampling are fixed; sensitivity analyses are minimal. It would help to show robustness across image scales/strides and alternative anchor sets.
- Fairness of speed comparisons: While the paper discusses CPU vs GPU differences, a more explicit accounting of GPU time for all components and memory usage would aid reproducibility and fairness; a GPU SS/EB baseline (if feasible) would clarify the gap.
- Boundary handling: Ignoring cross-boundary anchors during training is reasonable, but more detail on how this affects recall near image borders would be informative.

Quality
Technically sound and carefully executed. The loss, sampling, and optimization details are clear. Ablations are thoughtful and tie claims to evidence (e.g., top-N proposal behavior, effect of removing cls/reg). Some evaluations that would further bolster the work (COCO, joint training) are missing, but given the magnitude of the contribution, the current experiments suffice to support the main claims.

Clarity
Well-written and well-organized. The method is described in enough detail to reproduce: architecture, loss, anchor parameterization, training schedules, and implementation details are all given. Figures/tables (as described) effectively support the text. Minor suggestions: consolidate footnote-like clarifications in the main text for readability; explicitly list all timing breakdowns and memory footprints.

Originality
High. While proposal networks and sliding-window detection are not new, the translation-invariant anchor mechanism, tight feature sharing with a detector, and the specific FCN-style architecture for class-agnostic proposals constitute a new and influential direction. The comparison to MultiBox and OverFeat highlights the differentiating design choices.

Significance
High. The approach removes a key system bottleneck and provides a unifying architecture that advances both speed and accuracy. It is likely to be adopted broadly and to catalyze further research into end-to-end detection systems.

Questions for the authors
- How sensitive is performance to the choice of anchor scales/aspect ratios and to the stride (e.g., dilated convs to reduce stride)?
- Did you attempt joint end-to-end training (without alternating), and if so, what issues arose?
- How does recall/mAP behave for small objects given the stride-16 backbone?
- Can you report memory and training-time costs for ZF vs VGG variants?

Pros
- Simple, novel, and effective proposal mechanism that shares features with detection
- Strong speed-accuracy trade-off; proposals effectively “free”
- Thorough ablations and fair baselines, including a one-stage comparison
- Clear, reproducible method with strong practical impact

Cons
- Evaluation limited to VOC; larger-scale validation missing
- No joint end-to-end training results
- Limited sensitivity analysis of anchors/NMS/strides
- Speed fairness could be discussed more rigorously

Recommendation
Accept. This is a bold and impactful contribution that introduces a new concept (shared-feature, anchor-based RPNs) and convincingly demonstrates its benefits. Minor methodological gaps are outweighed by the novelty and the clear advance in the state of the art.
