Summary and relation to prior work:
This paper proposes Region Proposal Networks (RPNs) that share convolutional features with a Fast R-CNN detector to produce nearly cost-free region proposals. The RPN is a fully convolutional network that slides over shared feature maps and, at each location, predicts k proposals anchored at multiple scales and aspect ratios via two sibling heads: a classification head (object vs. background) and a box-regression head. Proposals from the RPN feed a Fast R-CNN detector. The authors train the unified system by an alternating scheme that fine-tunes the RPN and Fast R-CNN in turn, encouraging shared convolutional features.

The work builds on region-based CNNs (R-CNN, SPPnet, Fast R-CNN) and tackles the long-standing bottleneck of hand-engineered proposal methods such as Selective Search [22] and EdgeBoxes [24]. Compared to one-stage systems like OverFeat [18], and proposal networks such as MultiBox [3,20], the RPN uses translation-invariant anchors and a lightweight head, reducing parameters and enabling efficient sharing with the detector.

On PASCAL VOC 2007/2012, the unified RPN + Fast R-CNN system achieves strong mAP with as few as 300 proposals per image and runs at several frames per second with VGG-16. The paper includes ablations on sharing features, numbers of proposals, and the roles of the classification vs. regression heads.

Strengths:
- Clear motivation: proposals are the bottleneck for Fast R-CNN; sharing features is a natural and elegant solution.
- Solid empirical results: strong mAP on VOC 2007/2012 with only 300 proposals, and substantial speedups vs. SS/EB-based pipelines due to shared computation.
- Simple, effective architecture: small n×n conv followed by sibling 1×1 conv heads; anchor mechanism is conceptually clean and translation-invariant.
- Pragmatic training procedure that works in practice; ablations help disentangle the contributions (sharing, cls vs. reg, proposal count).
- Good engineering: GPU-friendly, end-to-end training for proposals, and a small marginal runtime (~10 ms) for the RPN when sharing features.

Weaknesses and points needing clarification:
1) Translation invariance and comparison to prior art:
- The paper argues that the anchor mechanism is translation-invariant, unlike MultiBox’s learned priors. Please clarify precisely why your formulation is translation-invariant and explicitly contrast it with MultiBox [20]. The draft text mentions [19] (VGG-16), but the non-invariance discussion seems intended for MultiBox rather than the backbone.
- How does OverFeat [18] fit into this framing? OverFeat also slides over conv maps; in what sense is your anchor-based parameterization different with respect to translation invariance and parameter count?

2) Hyperparameter choices and sensitivity:
- Many design choices are set without analysis, e.g., n = 3 for the conv window and k = 9 (3 scales × 3 aspect ratios). Please provide sensitivity studies or at least a justification: How do mAP and recall vary with n, anchor scales/ratios, and the number of anchors? Are the specific scales (1282, 2562, 5122) and ratios (1:1, 1:2, 2:1) critical? Would a learned or data-driven anchor set (or more/fewer anchors) help or hurt?
- Similarly, please report sensitivity to NMS threshold (0.7), the IoU thresholds for positive/negative labels (0.7/0.3), and the number of proposals at test time.

3) Training procedure and stability:
- The 4-step alternating training is described as a pragmatic solution. How stable is it? Do different random seeds, learning rates, or pretraining choices materially change results? Training curves or variance across runs would help.
- Why not repeat the alternation for more rounds? Does performance saturate after one pass?
- Have you attempted joint end-to-end training (even if it diverges)? Any insights on what prevents convergence and whether partial unfreezing or curriculum schedules help?

Additional comments and suggestions:
- Timing: The claim of “nearly cost-free” proposals is compelling when sharing features; please ensure runtime comparisons are fair (GPU vs. CPU) and report end-to-end timings across backbones at fixed proposal counts. A breakdown (backbone convs, RPN heads, ROI-wise fc) is useful.
- Box regression: The paper argues for k separate regressors (per anchor type) vs. shared regression as in Fast R-CNN. Can you quantify the benefit of per-anchor regressors?
- Cross-boundary anchors: You ignore boundary-crossing anchors in training but allow them at test time (clipped). A brief analysis of how this choice affects recall and mAP would be useful.
- Generalization: Results are limited to PASCAL VOC. If feasible, a brief experiment or discussion on transfer to other datasets or categories would strengthen the significance claim.

Quality:
- The method is technically sound and well-motivated. Experiments are thorough on VOC with helpful ablations. The main gaps are sensitivity analyses and more detailed training stability reporting.

Clarity:
- The paper is generally well-written but some passages are hard to follow. Clarify the translation-invariance argument, distinguish clearly among OverFeat [18], MultiBox [20], and your RPN, and explain design choices (n, k, anchors) and the alternation schedule more explicitly. A figure contrasting anchor-based RPN vs. MultiBox priors would help.

Originality:
- Combining translation-invariant anchors with a lightweight fully convolutional proposal head that shares features with detection is a notable contribution. While related ideas appear in OverFeat and MultiBox, the specific anchor parameterization, tight integration with Fast R-CNN, and alternating training yield a distinct and effective approach.

Significance:
- High. The method substantially reduces the computational bottleneck of proposal generation while improving or matching accuracy. The approach is likely to be widely adopted and extended.

Pros:
- Elegant, efficient integration of proposal generation with detection via shared features.
- Strong accuracy with very few proposals and compelling speed.
- Clear improvements over SS/EB pipelines and thoughtful ablations.

Cons:
- Limited analysis of design choices and training stability.
- Some conceptual comparisons (translation invariance vs. MultiBox/OverFeat) need clearer exposition.
- Results confined to VOC; broader validation would strengthen the paper.

Questions for the authors:
- Please clarify the translation-invariance claim and explicitly compare with MultiBox and OverFeat.
- Provide sensitivity analyses for n, k, anchor scales/ratios, NMS/IoU thresholds, and proposal counts.
- Discuss stability of the alternating training, whether additional alternations help, and any experience with joint training.

Overall, the paper addresses an important bottleneck with a simple, effective, and empirically strong solution. With additional clarity and analysis on the points above, it would make a valuable contribution.
