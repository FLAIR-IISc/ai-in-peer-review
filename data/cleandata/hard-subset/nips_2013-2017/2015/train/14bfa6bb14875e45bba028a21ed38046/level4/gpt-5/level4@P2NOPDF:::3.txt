This paper presents a two-stage CNN-based object detector. Stage one generates region proposals by applying a dense sliding window on the top-layer feature map (RPN). Stage two applies Fast R-CNN on these proposals. By sharing convolutional layers between the RPN and Fast R-CNN and leveraging GPUs, the method achieves near real-time performance (about 5 fps). The approach is a natural progression of CNN-based detection pipelines.

Major concerns:
1) Optimization procedure. The alternating optimization between the RPN and Fast R-CNN appears to run for only a single iteration. If the proposals from the first RPN pass are treated as fixed, this implicitly assumes they are close to optimal, which is unlikely. Please clarify:
   - Do you perform multiple alternations between updating the shared convolutional layers for RPN and for Fast R-CNN?
   - If only one alternation is used, what justifies this choice?
   - How does detection performance change with additional alternations or with end-to-end joint training?

2) Sliding-window stride and localization accuracy. When the pixel-wise sliding window is applied on the top CNN feature map, what is the corresponding stride in the input image? Multiple pooling/striding operations substantially reduce spatial resolution, so a 1-pixel shift on the top feature map may correspond to a large step on the image, potentially harming localization. Please report:
   - The effective stride of the top feature map relative to the input image.
   - How anchors on the feature map are mapped back to image coordinates.
   - Whether you use any mechanisms (e.g., dilated convolutions, deconvolution/upsampling, multi-scale feature aggregation, or finer-stride layers) to mitigate coarse sampling.
   - An ablation showing how different strides or feature-map resolutions affect bounding box localization and overall mAP.

3) Notation clarity. On lines 173–174, h_a is introduced without definition. Please define h_a explicitly and keep notation consistent throughout (e.g., h_a and w_a for anchor height and width, along with their parameterization in box regression).

Overall, the paper addresses an important problem with a practical and well-motivated design. Clarifying the training alternation strategy, detailing the effective stride and its impact on localization, and fixing the notation issues would strengthen the technical presentation and help readers reproduce the results.
