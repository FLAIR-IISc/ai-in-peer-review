This paper introduces a unified, two-stage framework for object detection that integrates region proposal generation and object classification into a single convolutional neural network. The first stage, a Region Proposal Network (RPN), generates high-quality object proposals directly from convolutional feature maps. The second stage utilizes a Fast R-CNN detector to classify these proposals. A key innovation is the sharing of convolutional layers between the RPN and the detector, which enables a highly efficient, end-to-end system. By leveraging GPU acceleration, the proposed method achieves near real-time performance (5 fps) while delivering state-of-the-art accuracy on standard benchmarks.

While the proposed framework is a significant and logical advancement over prior CNN-based detection methods, I have a few concerns and questions for the authors:

1.  **Alternating Optimization Procedure:** The paper describes a training process involving alternating optimization between the RPN and the Fast R-CNN detector. However, the description suggests that only one full cycle of this process is performed (i.e., train RPN, then train Fast R-CNN using those proposals). This approach seems to assume that the initial proposals generated by the first-stage RPN are sufficient for training an optimal detector. Could the authors elaborate on the rationale for not performing additional iterations? It seems plausible that refining the RPN with features fine-tuned by the detector could yield better proposals and, in turn, improve overall detection accuracy. An ablation study on the number of alternating optimization steps would be insightful.

2.  **Effective Stride and Localization Accuracy:** The region proposals are generated by applying a sliding window over the final convolutional feature map. Due to successive pooling layers, this feature map is at a much coarser resolution than the input image. Could the authors clarify the effective stride of these sliding windows when mapped back to the input image coordinates? A large stride could potentially limit the localization precision of the initial anchor boxes, especially for smaller objects. While the bounding box regression step is designed to refine these locations, a more detailed discussion on its ability to compensate for a potentially coarse initial grid would strengthen the paper.

3.  **Clarity on Notation:** In lines 173 and 174, the variable $h_a$ is used without a prior definition. Please clarify its meaning in the text.
