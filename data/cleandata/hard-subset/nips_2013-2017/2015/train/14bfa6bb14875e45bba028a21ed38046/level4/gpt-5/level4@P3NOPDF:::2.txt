Summary
This paper introduces a Region Proposal Network (RPN) that shares convolutional features with Fast R-CNN to generate object proposals efficiently. The RPN is a lightweight network applied on top of the shared feature map and produces objectness scores and bounding-box regressions at each spatial location. The authors integrate RPNs with Fast R-CNN via an alternating training scheme, leading to a unified detection pipeline that achieves state-of-the-art performance on PASCAL VOC 2007 and 2012. A central contribution is the substantial reduction in proposal generation time by reusing the backbone features for both proposal generation and detection.

Strengths
- Clear and impactful idea: Sharing convolutional features between the proposal mechanism and the detector dramatically reduces runtime without sacrificing accuracy.
- Strong empirical results: The method achieves state-of-the-art detection performance on VOC07 and VOC12, and shows favorable trade-offs between speed and accuracy.
- Practical efficiency: Proposal generation becomes extremely fast when placed on top of shared conv features, removing a major bottleneck of earlier R-CNN-style pipelines.
- Well-written and organized: The paper is clear, with strong experimental validation, ablations, and reasonable methodological explanations.

Weaknesses and Concerns
- Training integration is heuristic: The coupling between the RPN and Fast R-CNN relies on alternating optimization. While effective in practice, the procedure is heuristic and lacks convergence analysis or theoretical justification. It would help to see empirical evidence that the process is stable across random seeds, schedules, and datasets, or a discussion of why joint end-to-end training was not adopted or did not perform better at the time.
- Standalone RPN quality: As a proposal method in isolation, it is not clearly established that RPN consistently outperforms strong hand-engineered methods (e.g., Selective Search) across recall/IoU thresholds and proposal budgets. More thorough and nuanced comparisons (e.g., recall at IoU 0.5 vs. 0.7/0.8, AR metrics, small-object recall) would clarify when and why RPN is superior.
- Sensitivity to design choices: Anchor scales, aspect ratios, and NMS thresholds appear to be selected heuristically. The paper would benefit from ablations quantifying sensitivity to these choices and guidance for transferring the method to different datasets or object size distributions.
- Coupling and error propagation: Because the RPN is trained on features that are later fine-tuned by the detector (and vice versa), the interaction between stages could introduce biases (e.g., proposal distributions adapting to detector errors). Additional analysis on how proposals evolve during training and how that impacts final AP would strengthen the story.
- Generalization and robustness: Results are focused on VOC. While understandable for 2015, any evidence of cross-dataset generalization (e.g., to MS COCO or other categories) and robustness to small or crowded objects would further support the method’s claims.

Questions and Requests for Clarification
- Can the authors provide training curves or statistics (e.g., proposal recall and detection AP over alternating steps) to demonstrate stability and convergence behavior?
- How sensitive is performance to the number and shapes of anchors? Are there systematic ways to tune anchor configurations for new domains?
- When evaluating RPN as a standalone proposal generator, how does it compare to Selective Search and EdgeBoxes across a range of IoU thresholds and proposal counts (e.g., 100, 300, 1000 proposals)?
- What is the runtime breakdown with and without feature sharing, including GPU/CPU details, for both training and inference? Is NMS a bottleneck at test time?
- How does the method perform on small objects and in crowded scenes? Do higher-resolution feature maps or feature pyramid variants improve recall without large compute overhead?

Suggestions for Improvement
- Provide a more principled or fully end-to-end joint training procedure, or at least a stronger empirical justification for alternating training (robustness to hyperparameters, schedules, and initializations).
- Expand the analysis of RPN as a proposal method: recall at multiple IoU thresholds, average recall, small-object recall, and per-class recall. Include plots of AP/AR versus number of proposals.
- Add ablations on anchor configurations, NMS thresholds, and positive/negative sampling ratios. Guidance on default settings for new datasets would increase practical impact.
- Include an error analysis (e.g., miss, localization error, confusion) comparing pipelines with Selective Search vs. RPN, to illustrate where improvements arise.
- Discuss memory and compute trade-offs when increasing resolution or using deeper backbones, and the impact on runtime and accuracy.

Overall Assessment
This is an excellent and influential contribution that significantly advances object detection by tightly coupling proposal generation with detection through shared convolutional features. The method delivers both higher accuracy and markedly improved efficiency compared to prior R-CNN pipelines, and the empirical results are compelling. The primary weakness is the heuristic nature of the alternating training scheme and limited analysis of its convergence and robustness, as well as the somewhat mixed picture of RPN as a standalone proposal method versus strong baselines.

Despite these concerns, the paper is well executed, addresses an important problem, and provides a practical solution that is likely to have substantial impact. I recommend acceptance.
