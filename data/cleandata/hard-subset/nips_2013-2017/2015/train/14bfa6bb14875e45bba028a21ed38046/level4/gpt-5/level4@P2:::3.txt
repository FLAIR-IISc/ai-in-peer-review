Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper proposes Faster R-CNN, a two-stage detector that integrates region proposal generation and object detection into a single CNN with shared convolutional features. The key contribution is the Region Proposal Network (RPN), a fully convolutional module that slides over shared feature maps to produce translation-invariant, class-agnostic proposals using a set of anchors at multiple scales and aspect ratios. Proposals (boxes and objectness scores) are trained with a multi-task loss and used by Fast R-CNN for classification and box refinement. A simple alternating training procedure enables the RPN and Fast R-CNN to share convolutional layers. Relative to SPPnet and Fast R-CNN, the main advance is removing the proposal bottleneck; relative to Selective Search and EdgeBoxes, proposals are both faster and better aligned with the detection backbone; relative to OverFeat and MultiBox, the anchor mechanism achieves translation invariance with far fewer parameters. On PASCAL VOC 2007/2012, the method achieves state-of-the-art mAP (73.2%/70.4%) with ~300 proposals per image and an end-to-end runtime of about 5 fps on VGG-16, with proposal generation adding roughly 10 ms.

Strengths:
- Elegant, principled integration of proposal generation and detection via shared conv features; proposal cost is effectively amortized.
- Translation-invariant anchor design (multiple scales/aspect ratios) is simple and effective, and reduces parameter count compared to MultiBox, mitigating overfitting on smaller datasets.
- Clear multi-task formulation and practical training pipeline; ablations isolate the roles of classification vs regression, NMS, proposal counts, and feature sharing.
- Strong empirical results on PASCAL VOC with both ZF and VGG-16 backbones; shows that as few as 300 proposals retain high detection mAP.
- Careful runtime analysis demonstrating substantial speedups over SS/EB while improving accuracy.

Weaknesses and questions:
- Alternating training: The optimization section appears to perform a single 4-step alternation (RPN → detector → RPN with shared conv fixed → detector fc). If so, this assumes proposals from the first RPN are close to optimal for subsequent detector training. Could additional alternations (or joint end-to-end training) further improve convergence and accuracy? Please report whether more cycles were tried, any convergence criteria, and the performance/runtime trade-offs.
- Stride and localization granularity: The RPN operates on top-layer feature maps with a stride of 16 pixels (for ZF/VGG after pooling), which can correspond to relatively coarse steps on the input image. This raises concerns about bounding box localization for small objects and precise alignment. The paper notes good results despite the stride, but it would help to quantify the impact (e.g., AP on small objects, localization error breakdown) and to discuss mitigations (smaller-stride features, dilated convolutions, feature pyramids, or shifted anchors).
- Variable definition: Please clarify the definition of ha in the bounding-box parameterization (lines 173–174). My understanding is that ha denotes the anchor box height (analogously, wa is the anchor width; xa, ya the anchor center).
- Cross-boundary anchors: Training ignores anchors that cross image boundaries, but testing clips them to the image. A brief analysis of how many anchors are discarded during training and whether including them with appropriate targets harms convergence would be informative.
- Fairness of speed comparisons: SS/EB are CPU implementations, whereas the RPN leverages the GPU and shared feature maps. While the argument for shared computation is compelling, it would strengthen the case to include a GPU-accelerated proposal baseline (if available) or to highlight wall-clock end-to-end time comparisons that include feature extraction for all methods.
- Generalization and robustness: Results focus on VOC. Any observations on transfer to datasets with more small objects or crowded scenes (e.g., MS COCO) would contextualize limitations of the current stride/anchor design.

Evaluation:
- Quality: Technically sound with extensive experiments and well-justified design choices. Claims are supported by ablations and state-of-the-art results. A few clarifications (training alternations, variable definitions) would improve completeness.
- Clarity: Generally well written and organized. The paper would benefit from explicitly defining all variables in the box regression equations, stating the exact feature-map stride on the input image, and adding a figure/table mapping anchor centers to input pixels. Also clarify normalization constants (Ncls, Nreg) and the role of the balancing weight λ in the final system.
- Originality: High. While building on R-CNN/SSPnet/Fast R-CNN, the RPN and anchor-based, translation-invariant proposal mechanism with shared features is a substantial, novel contribution relative to OverFeat and MultiBox.
- Significance: High. This work removes a long-standing bottleneck in region-based detectors, provides a practical and accurate system, and has clear potential for broad adoption and extension.

Pros (arguments for acceptance):
- Unifies proposal generation and detection with shared features; strong conceptual and practical impact.
- State-of-the-art accuracy with markedly better speed.
- Thorough ablations that support the design.
- Simple, general mechanism (anchors) with low parameter count and good performance at low proposal counts.

Cons (arguments against acceptance):
- Limited discussion of optimization beyond a single alternation; no demonstration of joint end-to-end training.
- Potential localization limits due to 16-pixel stride; small-object performance not deeply analyzed.
- Minor clarity issues (variable definitions; training normalization and λ; handling of cross-boundary anchors).
- Comparisons to GPU-accelerated proposal baselines are absent.

Overall assessment:
A strong, influential contribution that advances both the speed and accuracy of region-based detection by integrating proposals into the CNN. With minor clarifications on training and variable definitions, and an expanded discussion of stride/localization trade-offs, this paper is well suited for NIPS. I lean toward acceptance.
