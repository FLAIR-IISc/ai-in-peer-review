This work is a natural extension of existing CNN-based object detection. It proposes a two-stage detection algorithm built on convolutional neural networks (CNNs). The first stage performs region proposal using a traditional sliding-window mechanism applied to the top-layer CNN feature map (RPN). The second stage applies a Fast R-CNN to the proposed regions. Because the convolutional layers are shared between the RPN and the R-CNN, and computation is accelerated on a GPU, the method achieves near real-time performance (about 5 fps).

My main concerns are as follows.

1) Alternating optimization between RPN and R-CNN:
- In the optimization section, it appears there is only a single iteration of alternating optimization between the RPN and the R-CNN. If so, this effectively assumes that the proposals generated by the first-pass RPN are optimal, which is clearly not true.
- Please clarify whether multiple alternations were attempted, how many, and with what impact on both accuracy and speed. If only one iteration is used, provide justification or empirical evidence that further alternations do not materially improve results.

2) Effective stride of the top-layer sliding window:
- When applying a pixel-wise sliding window on the top CNN feature map, what is the effective step size in the original image?
- Because the network includes several rounds of max pooling, the top-layer feature map is much lower resolution than the input image. A one-pixel step on the top layer may correspond to a large step on the image, which could lead to inaccurate bounding-box localization.
- Please specify the exact stride (in input pixels) implied by your architecture, and explain how you mitigate potential localization errors (e.g., through bounding-box regression, feature map dilation, or other refinement).

3) Notation clarity:
- What is the definition of h_a in lines 173–174? Please define it explicitly where it first appears.

With these clarifications—particularly regarding the alternation scheme and the effective image-level stride—the paper would be substantially stronger.
