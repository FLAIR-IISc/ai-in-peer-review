This paper proposes a Region Proposal Network (RPN) that shares convolutional features with a Fast R-CNN object detection network, significantly reducing the computational cost of generating region proposals. The RPN is a fully-convolutional network that predicts object bounds and objectness scores at each position, and is combined with Fast R-CNN for object detection. The authors demonstrate state-of-the-art performance on the PASCAL VOC 2007 and 2012 object detection benchmarks, with a frame rate of 5fps on a GPU.

The main idea of sharing convolutional features between the RPN and Fast R-CNN is well-motivated and effectively reduces the running time of generating region proposals. The paper is well-written and provides thorough experimental evaluations, including ablation studies and comparisons to other region proposal methods.

The strengths of the paper include its novelty, with the introduction of a new region proposal method that can be used in conjunction with Fast R-CNN. The authors also provide a thorough evaluation of their method, including comparisons to other state-of-the-art methods. The paper is well-organized and easy to follow, with clear explanations of the proposed method and its components.

However, there are some weaknesses to the paper. One concern is the lack of convergence proof for the alternating optimization method used to train the RPN and Fast R-CNN. While the authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their method through experimental evaluations, a more rigorous analysis of the optimization procedure would strengthen the paper.

Another concern is that the RPN does not seem to outperform Selective Search, a widely-used region proposal method, when used independently. This raises questions about the effectiveness of the RPN as a standalone region proposal method.

In terms of quality, the paper is technically sound and well-executed. The authors provide a clear and detailed explanation of their method, and the experimental evaluations are thorough and well-designed. The paper is well-organized and easy to follow, making it a pleasure to read.

In terms of clarity, the paper is well-written and easy to understand. The authors provide clear explanations of their method and its components, and the experimental evaluations are well-described. The paper includes a number of helpful figures and tables that illustrate the proposed method and its performance.

In terms of originality, the paper introduces a new region proposal method that can be used in conjunction with Fast R-CNN. While the idea of sharing convolutional features between the RPN and Fast R-CNN is not entirely new, the authors provide a novel implementation of this idea that achieves state-of-the-art performance on object detection benchmarks.

In terms of significance, the paper makes a significant contribution to the field of object detection. The proposed method achieves state-of-the-art performance on object detection benchmarks, and the reduction in computational cost makes it a practical solution for real-time object detection applications.

Arguments for acceptance:

* The paper introduces a new region proposal method that achieves state-of-the-art performance on object detection benchmarks.
* The proposed method significantly reduces the computational cost of generating region proposals, making it a practical solution for real-time object detection applications.
* The paper is well-written and provides thorough experimental evaluations.

Arguments against acceptance:

* The lack of convergence proof for the alternating optimization method used to train the RPN and Fast R-CNN is a concern.
* The RPN does not seem to outperform Selective Search when used independently, which raises questions about its effectiveness as a standalone region proposal method.
