## Review of RPN for Object Proposal Generation

This paper presents a novel region proposal method that significantly improves the speed of object detection by sharing convolutional features with the object localization network. The core idea – integrating region proposal generation directly into the CNN pipeline – is compelling and the experimental results demonstrate a substantial speedup (approximately 10x faster than Fast R-CNN) with a slight improvement in accuracy. The paper is well-motivated, tackling a critical bottleneck in object detection systems. However, while the core contributions are strong, the presentation suffers from a lack of clarity in several areas, hindering a complete understanding of the approach and its design choices.

The paper’s goal is clearly stated and the experimental validation is robust, particularly the comparisons against state-of-the-art methods on commonly used datasets. The reported speed and accuracy improvements are impressive. However, several aspects require further clarification and justification.

Specifically, the paper states that the proposed Region Proposal Network (RPN) is translation-invariant, while referencing [19] as not being so. This distinction requires a much more detailed explanation. Why does the architecture presented here achieve translation invariance, and what specific characteristic of the referenced method [19] leads to its lack of it? Furthermore, the connection to Overfeat, a prior work that also explores shared features, is not adequately discussed.  A comparison detailing the differences and advantages of the proposed RPN over Overfeat would strengthen the paper.

A significant concern is the somewhat arbitrary nature of several hyperparameter choices. The values of *n*=3 and *k*=9 are introduced without sufficient justification. While these values may perform well, the paper does not provide any analysis supporting their selection.  Was an ablation study performed to determine the sensitivity of the results to these parameters? A brief discussion on the rationale behind these choices, or evidence of experimentation with alternative values, is crucial to understanding their importance and the robustness of the method.

The training procedure, employing a multi-stage alternative optimization, appears complex and potentially unstable. The description lacks sufficient detail to fully reproduce the process and raises questions about its reliability.  A key question is whether the performance is consistent across multiple training runs. Have the authors performed the training procedure multiple times to assess its stability?  Furthermore, it would be valuable to know if the authors explored other training strategies and, if so, why the current procedure was deemed optimal. Providing a more thorough account of the experimentation process and the reasons for choosing this particular setup would significantly increase confidence in the results.

The novelty lies in the integration of region proposal generation with the object localization CNN, utilizing a classification layer to distinguish objectness and a regression layer to refine bounding boxes. This sharing of convolutional features is the primary contribution and clearly addresses the computational inefficiencies of previous approaches.  The multi-stage training procedure, however, feels somewhat *ad hoc* and detracts from the elegance of the core concept.

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a significant contribution to the field of object detection. The proposed RPN offers a compelling solution to the problem of slow region proposal generation. The results are promising and demonstrate a substantial improvement in speed with minimal loss in accuracy. However, addressing the concerns raised above – specifically clarifying the translation invariance claim, justifying hyperparameter choices, and detailing the training procedure – would substantially strengthen the paper and make it more accessible to the research community. I recommend acceptance contingent upon addressing these points in a revised version.
