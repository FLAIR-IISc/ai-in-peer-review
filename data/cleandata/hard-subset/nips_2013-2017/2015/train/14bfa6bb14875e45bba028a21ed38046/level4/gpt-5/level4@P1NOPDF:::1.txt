The paper’s goal is clear and the experimental results are solid. The authors propose a novel region proposal method that shares CNN features with the object localization module. The region proposal network comprises a classification layer for object vs. non-object and a regression layer for bounding-box prediction. By sharing feature computation with the localization/detection module, the combined system is approximately 10× faster than Fast R-CNN and slightly more accurate. Because the detection CNN assumes region proposals are provided, the authors introduce a multi-stage, alternating training procedure.

However, the paper is not easy to follow, and several parts are confusing:

1) Translation invariance
- The paper claims the RPN is translation-invariant, but [19] is not. Please clarify the basis for this claim. In what sense (theoretical or empirical) is the RPN translation-invariant, and why does [19] fail to have this property despite also using convolutional features?
- How does OverFeat compare on this point? Is OverFeat translation-invariant in the same sense, and if not, what architectural difference breaks invariance?
- Concrete evidence (e.g., controlled experiments with translated inputs or a clear theoretical argument) would make this distinction much clearer.

2) Manually chosen parameters
- Many design choices appear to be set manually without analysis. For example, why choose n=3 and k=9? If n denotes the number of scales and k the number of anchors per location, please justify these specific choices.
- An ablation or sensitivity analysis varying n, the aspect ratios, and the number of anchors would help assess robustness and whether performance is sensitive to these hyperparameters.

3) Training procedure and stability
- The training procedure appears complicated and potentially unstable. Why not apply the same training procedure multiple times (e.g., additional alternating rounds) to check convergence, or adopt a simpler unified training scheme if possible?
- Have the authors tried many other training settings and found the current one to perform best? If so, please report these comparisons. Information on stability across random initializations, the number of alternating iterations, and datasets would be valuable.

Overall, the approach is novel and compelling, and the empirical gains are strong. Improving the clarity around translation invariance, providing justification for manual hyperparameters (e.g., n=3, k=9), and offering a more transparent account of training choices and stability would make the paper much easier to follow and strengthen its contribution.
