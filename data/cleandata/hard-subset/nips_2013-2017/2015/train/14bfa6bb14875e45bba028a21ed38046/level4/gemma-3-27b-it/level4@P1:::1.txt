The paper addresses the bottleneck of region proposal in modern object detection systems. The authors propose a Region Proposal Network (RPN) that shares convolutional features with the detection network, enabling nearly cost-free region proposals. An RPN is a fully-convolutional network that predicts object bounds and objectness scores simultaneously. The RPN and Fast R-CNN are trained in an alternating fashion to share convolutional features, achieving state-of-the-art accuracy on PASCAL VOC with a significant speedup – 5fps including all steps with a VGG-16 model.

This work builds upon prior advances in region-based CNNs [6, 7, 5], which reduced computational cost by sharing convolutions. However, these methods still rely on computationally expensive region proposal algorithms like Selective Search [22] or EdgeBoxes [24]. The key contribution of this paper is to learn region proposals directly within the network, effectively eliminating the need for external proposal methods. This approach is related to other work that utilizes deep networks for bounding box prediction [18, 3, 20], but differs in its focus on sharing computation and a cascaded approach with Fast R-CNN.

**Strengths:**

* **Significant Speedup:** The proposed RPN drastically reduces the time spent on region proposal, resulting in a practical real-time object detection system.
* **Improved Accuracy:**  The method achieves state-of-the-art accuracy on PASCAL VOC 2007 and 2012, demonstrating its effectiveness.
* **Novel Approach:** Learning region proposals directly within a neural network and sharing convolutional features is a novel and effective strategy.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-structured, and the technical details are clearly explained. 
* **Thorough Evaluation:** The ablation studies and comparisons to existing methods are comprehensive and provide strong evidence for the effectiveness of the proposed approach.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Design Choices:** Some parameter choices (e.g., n=3, k=9) appear somewhat arbitrary and lack detailed justification. While the authors may have explored other settings, the rationale behind these specific choices isn't fully explained.
* **Training Procedure Complexity:** The alternating training procedure appears complex and potentially sensitive to hyperparameter settings.  It’s unclear how robust this procedure is and if alternative training schemes were considered. Further discussion of the convergence behavior and sensitivity to the training schedule would be beneficial.
* **Translation Invariance Clarification:** The explanation regarding translation invariance could be clearer. The authors state RPN is translation invariant while referencing [19] as not, but the connection isn't fully elucidated. A more direct comparison with OverFeat [18] regarding this aspect would be helpful.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a significant advance in object detection, addressing a crucial bottleneck in existing systems.
* The experimental results are compelling and demonstrate both improved accuracy and speed.
* The proposed approach is novel and well-motivated.
* The ablation studies provide a strong understanding of the different components and their contributions.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The justification for some design choices could be strengthened.
* The complexity of the training procedure raises concerns about robustness and ease of implementation.
* While not a fatal flaw, the explanation regarding translation invariance could be more precise. 

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that presents a significant contribution to the field of object detection. The proposed RPN effectively addresses the bottleneck of region proposal, leading to a faster and more accurate detection system. While some aspects of the design choices and training procedure could be further clarified, the overall impact of the work is substantial. I recommend acceptance.
