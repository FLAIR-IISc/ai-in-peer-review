Summary and relation to prior work:
This paper introduces Region Proposal Networks (RPNs), fully convolutional networks that generate class-agnostic object proposals and share convolutional features with a downstream detector. The RPN predicts, at each spatial location, k proposals relative to translation-invariant anchors of multiple scales and aspect ratios, outputting both objectness scores and bounding-box regressions. The authors integrate RPNs with Fast R-CNN via an alternating training scheme that yields shared convolutional layers and substantially reduces the computational cost of proposal generation. On PASCAL VOC 2007 and 2012, the combined system achieves state-of-the-art detection accuracy while using as few as 300 proposals per image and running near real-time on a GPU.

This work builds directly on SPPnet and Fast R-CNN, which share convolutional features across proposals to reduce detection cost, but previously relied on external CPU-based proposal methods such as Selective Search or EdgeBoxes. It also contrasts with OverFeat and MultiBox, which use deep nets to predict boxes but either target single objects or use large sets of non-translation-invariant anchors and do not share features with a detector in the same way. The proposed anchor design and shared features result in a more parameter-efficient and faster proposal mechanism.

Strengths:
- Idea: Sharing convolutional features between proposal and detection is elegant and effective, turning proposal generation into a near “free” operation at test time.
- Performance: The integrated RPN + Fast R-CNN system achieves state-of-the-art mAP on VOC07/12 while using a small number of proposals. Ablation studies are thorough and demonstrate the roles of classification vs. regression, the effects of NMS, the impact of deeper backbones (ZF vs. VGG-16), and the importance of feature sharing.
- Practicality: The method is fast in wall-clock terms and reduces the system’s dependence on expensive external proposal methods. Code availability further enhances reproducibility.
- Clarity: The paper is well written and well organized, with clear descriptions of the architecture, training loss, anchor parameterization, and implementation details.

Concerns and weaknesses:
- Training integration: The combination of RPN and Fast R-CNN relies on an alternating optimization procedure that is admittedly heuristic. There is no convergence guarantee or analysis of stability when jointly learning proposals and detections. While the approach works well empirically, a more principled end-to-end training strategy or empirical evidence of robustness across settings would strengthen the contribution.
- Standalone proposal quality: As a proposal method used independently, RPN does not clearly outperform Selective Search in all configurations. In some reported settings (e.g., with ZF), RPN proposals are comparable rather than superior, suggesting that much of the overall gain comes from shared features and integration with the detector rather than from a strictly better proposal distribution per se.

Quality:
The paper is technically sound and careful. The loss design (classification and smooth L1 regression), anchor parameterization, sampling strategy, and training schedule follow established practices and are well justified. Experiments are comprehensive, including ablations on proposal count, NMS, disabling cls/reg branches, and swapping backbones. The primary caveat is the lack of analysis for the alternating training scheme.

Clarity:
Exposition is clear and accessible. The architecture is described succinctly, the role of anchors and translation invariance is well motivated, and implementation details (scales, aspect ratios, strides, normalization, hyperparameters) are sufficient for replication.

Originality:
The key novelty is a proposal mechanism that is fully convolutional, translation-invariant via anchors, and shares features with the detector, yielding near-zero additional cost. The paper positions itself well relative to OverFeat, MultiBox, SPPnet, and Fast R-CNN, and explains parameter and runtime advantages.

Significance:
The method meaningfully advances the state of the art in both accuracy and speed, and is likely to be widely adopted in practice given its simplicity, efficiency, and strong empirical results. Its impact extends beyond PASCAL to general object detection pipelines.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Provide additional evidence (or discussion) on the stability and convergence behavior of the alternating training regime, or a preliminary exploration of joint end-to-end optimization.
- Clarify in which settings RPN proposals outperform or match Selective Search across IoU thresholds and proposal counts, to better characterize when RPN alone is preferable.
- Briefly discuss sensitivity to anchor choices (scales, aspect ratios) and to the IoU thresholds for positive/negative labeling.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Strong conceptual contribution: a unified, efficient proposal + detection framework with shared features.
- Thorough experiments and clear improvements in accuracy and speed.
- Solid engineering with practical impact; code provided.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Integration relies on heuristic alternating optimization without theoretical guarantees.
- As a standalone proposal method, RPN does not clearly and consistently surpass Selective Search in all reported configurations.

Overall assessment:
This is a very strong paper that improves both efficiency and accuracy of region-based detectors by introducing a well-designed, shared-feature proposal mechanism. Despite the heuristic nature of the alternating training and mixed standalone proposal comparisons, the empirical gains and clarity of presentation make this a compelling contribution.
