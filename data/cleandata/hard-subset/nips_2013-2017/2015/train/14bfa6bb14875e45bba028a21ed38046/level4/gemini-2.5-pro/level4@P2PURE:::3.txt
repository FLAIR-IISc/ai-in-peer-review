This paper proposes a two-stage object detection algorithm that builds upon convolutional neural networks (CNNs). The first stage introduces a Region Proposal Network (RPN), which applies a sliding window over the final convolutional feature map to generate object proposals. In the second stage, a Fast R-CNN detector refines these proposals. A key contribution is the sharing of convolutional layers between the RPN and the Fast R-CNN, which, combined with GPU acceleration, enables the system to achieve near real-time performance (5 fps).

While this work is a logical extension of existing CNN-based detection methods, I have the following concerns:

1.  In the section on optimization, the paper appears to describe only a single iteration of the alternating optimization between the RPN and the Fast R-CNN. This approach seems to assume that the proposals generated by the RPN in the first stage are optimal for training the subsequent detector. This assumption is unlikely to hold in practice, as these initial proposals are not guaranteed to be optimal.

2.  The paper applies a sliding window to the final convolutional feature map. Due to the significant downsampling from multiple max-pooling operations, the spatial resolution of this feature map is much coarser than that of the input image. It is unclear what the effective stride of this sliding window is with respect to the original image coordinates. A single-pixel step on the feature map could correspond to a large stride on the input image, which may compromise the localization accuracy of the resulting bounding boxes.

3.  The definition of the term $h_a$ in lines 173 and 174 is unclear and requires clarification.
