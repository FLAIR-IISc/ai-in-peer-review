### Review

**Summary**

This paper introduces the Region Proposal Network (RPN), a novel architecture designed to generate object proposals for use in a region-based CNN detector. The key insight is that the convolutional feature maps computed by an object detection network (like Fast R-CNN) can be reused for the task of generating region proposals, making the proposal step nearly computationally free at test time.

The proposed RPN is a fully-convolutional network that slides over the last shared convolutional feature map. At each spatial location, it simultaneously predicts objectness scores and refines bounding box coordinates for a set of pre-defined anchor boxes of various scales and aspect ratios. The authors present a 4-step alternating optimization scheme to train the RPN and a Fast R-CNN detector to share convolutional layers. This unified system, named Faster R-CNN, significantly accelerates the entire object detection pipeline. Experimental results on PASCAL VOC 2007 and 2012 show that Faster R-CNN not only achieves near real-time performance (5fps with VGG-16) but also improves detection accuracy over the previous state-of-the-art methods that rely on external proposal algorithms like Selective Search.

**Strengths**

1.  **Significance and Novelty:** The paper addresses a critical bottleneck in modern object detection systems: the computational cost of region proposal generation. By integrating the proposal mechanism into the detection network itself, the authors present an elegant and efficient end-to-end solution. This unification of region proposal and detection is a major conceptual advance over the previous multi-stage pipelines.

2.  **Performance and Efficiency:** The method achieves an impressive combination of speed and accuracy. The ability to run the entire VGG-16 based detection pipeline at 5fps, while simultaneously improving the state-of-the-art mAP on PASCAL VOC, is a substantial contribution. The marginal cost of the RPN (10ms) demonstrates the effectiveness of the shared feature computation strategy.

3.  **Thorough Experimental Validation:** The authors provide a comprehensive set of experiments to validate their approach. The ablation studies are particularly insightful, clearly demonstrating the benefits of sharing convolutional layers, the importance of both classification and regression outputs in the RPN, and the superiority of the two-stage approach compared to a one-stage detector baseline.

4.  **Clarity:** The paper is very well-written and organized. The motivation is clearly stated, the proposed method is described in detail, and the training procedure is explained logically. The inclusion of implementation details, such as the handling of anchors and the training sample strategy, adds to the paper's clarity and potential for reproducibility.

**Weaknesses and Questions for the Authors**

1.  **Training Scheme Complexity:** The 4-step alternating training process, while pragmatic and effective, is somewhat complex and feels like a carefully engineered workaround rather than a fully principled solution. It essentially constitutes a single cycle of training the RPN and then the detector. It is not clear if this single cycle is sufficient for convergence.
    *   Have the authors experimented with additional iterations of steps 3 and 4? Does performance continue to improve, or does it saturate after this first cycle?
    *   The paper notes that joint optimization is a direction for future work. A brief discussion of the potential challenges that prevented a simpler, jointly trained model would be beneficial.

2.  **Impact of Large Feature Map Stride:** The sliding window for the RPN operates on the final convolutional feature map, which has a large total stride with respect to the input image (e.g., 16 pixels for VGG-16). While the bounding box regression undoubtedly helps refine object locations, this coarse spatial grid may still pose a limitation for accurately localizing small objects. The paper mentions this provides good results, but a more in-depth analysis of this potential limitation would strengthen the work. For instance, is there a noticeable performance drop for smaller object categories?

**Minor Points and Suggestions**

*   In the description of the loss function (lines 173-174), the variables `h` and `h_a` are used in the parameterization of the bounding box regression targets. While `h_a` is implied to be the height of the anchor box, an explicit definition in the text would improve clarity for the reader.

*   The normalization of the loss function terms `N_cls` and `N_reg` appears slightly inconsistent, with one being the mini-batch size and the other being the total number of anchor locations. The footnote explains this was done to roughly balance the terms, but a little more intuition behind this design choice would be welcome.

**Recommendation**

This is an excellent paper presenting a significant advance in object detection. It solves a well-defined and important problem with a novel and effective solution. The proposed RPN is a powerful concept that fundamentally changed the way object detection systems are designed. The paper is well-written, technically sound, and backed by strong experimental evidence. Despite minor concerns about the complexity of the training scheme, the contributions in terms of performance, speed, and elegance are undeniable.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Presents a novel and highly significant contribution by unifying region proposal and object detection into a single, efficient network.
*   Solves a major computational bottleneck in state-of-the-art detection systems, making high-quality object detection practical for real-time applications.
*   Achieves new state-of-the-art accuracy on standard benchmarks (PASCAL VOC), demonstrating a clear and demonstrable advance for the field.
*   The paper is clearly written, well-motivated, and supported by thorough experiments, including insightful ablation studies.

**Arguments for Rejection:**
*   The 4-step training algorithm is complex and not a fully end-to-end solution, which could be viewed as a limitation of the proposed optimization framework.
*   The justification for stopping after a single cycle of the alternating training could be stronger.
