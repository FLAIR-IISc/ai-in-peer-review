Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes a two-stage object detection pipeline that integrates a Region Proposal Network (RPN) with a Fast R-CNN detector, sharing convolutional features between the two. The RPN is a fully convolutional sliding-window network applied on top-layer feature maps; at each spatial location it predicts objectness scores and regressed bounding boxes relative to translation-invariant anchors spanning multiple scales and aspect ratios. Proposals are then fed to Fast R-CNN for classification and box refinement. By sharing convolutional computation and leveraging the GPU, the system achieves near real-time performance (about 5 fps with VGG-16) while maintaining state-of-the-art accuracy (73.2% mAP on VOC 2007, 70.4% on VOC 2012 with only 300 proposals).

This work builds directly on SPPnet and Fast R-CNN, which reduced per-region computation by sharing conv features, and addresses the remaining bottleneck of CPU-based proposal methods such as Selective Search and EdgeBoxes. Relative to OverFeat (single-stage, class-specific regression/classification on dense windows) and MultiBox (anchor-like, class-agnostic proposals with many learned priors), RPNs use far fewer parameters via translation-invariant anchors and share features tightly with the downstream detector.

Strengths
- Elegant and practical unification of proposal generation and detection via shared conv features, collapsing the proposal bottleneck to roughly 10 ms per image.
- Strong empirical results: competitive or superior mAP to Selective Search with only 300 proposals, plus thorough ablations analyzing the roles of classification vs. regression, NMS, and number of proposals.
- Clear architectural choices (anchors with multiple scales and aspect ratios; small 3×3 conv followed by sibling 1×1 conv heads) that are simple to implement and efficient.
- Sensible training strategy that pragmatically alternates between RPN and Fast R-CNN to enable feature sharing without unstable joint optimization.

Weaknesses and questions
1) Alternating optimization: number of alternations and convergence
- The optimization section appears to perform only a single alternation (RPN fine-tune → Fast R-CNN fine-tune → RPN heads fine-tune with shared conv → Fast R-CNN fc fine-tune with shared conv). If only one such pass is used, it implicitly assumes proposals from the first RPN are close to optimal for training the detector, which may not be true.
- Please clarify: (a) Did you perform multiple alternations? (b) How does detection mAP (and proposal recall) evolve with additional alternations? (c) Is there evidence of convergence or diminishing returns? Reporting these would strengthen the training story and help separate benefits from better proposals vs. better shared features.

2) Sliding-window stride and localization accuracy
- When the pixel-wise sliding window is applied at the top conv layer, what is the effective step size on the input image? Max-pooling reduces spatial resolution, so the top-level stride can be relatively large, potentially impacting localization granularity for small objects and precise box placement.
- Please report the exact image-level stride used by each backbone (e.g., for ZF/VGG), and discuss its effect on localization. How much does bbox regression compensate for coarse stride? Did you experiment with finer strides (e.g., by reducing total stride or using dilated convolutions) and, if so, what was the speed/accuracy trade-off?

3) Notation clarity for bounding-box parameterization
- In the parameterization lines (around 173–174), please define ha explicitly and ensure consistent notation. The paper defines x, y, w, h for box center, width, height, and xa, ya, wa, ha for the anchor box parameters; however, ha (and similarly wa) should be clearly defined alongside xa and ya for completeness.

Quality
The methodology appears sound, with careful design choices (anchors, balanced sampling of positives/negatives, smooth L1 loss) and strong empirical support. The ablations are informative and largely isolate the contributions of the cls and reg heads, proposal counts, and feature sharing. The main missing piece is an analysis of convergence under alternating optimization and a clearer discussion of stride/localization trade-offs.

Clarity
The paper is generally well-written and well-organized. Clarity would improve by: (i) explicitly stating the image-level stride and discussing its implications; (ii) specifying ha (and ensuring all symbols are defined where first used); and (iii) documenting the number of alternations and any observed convergence behavior.

Originality
While grounded in R-CNN/SPPnet/Fast R-CNN, the introduction of a translation-invariant, fully convolutional RPN that shares features with the detector is a notable and influential contribution. The anchor mechanism and lightweight heads are a clean formulation distinct from prior work such as MultiBox and OverFeat.

Significance
High. The method removes a major bottleneck in region-based detection, achieves state-of-the-art accuracy with far fewer proposals, and presents an architecture likely to be widely adopted and extended.

Arguments for acceptance
- Significant practical impact: near cost-free proposals with strong accuracy.
- Simple, general design likely to transfer across backbones and datasets.
- Comprehensive experiments and ablations supporting the main claims.

Arguments against acceptance
- Limited analysis of alternating optimization beyond a single iteration; unclear convergence behavior.
- Insufficient discussion of the effect of conv stride on localization accuracy.
- Minor notation clarity issues (e.g., definition of ha).

Suggestions
- Report results with multiple alternations between RPN and Fast R-CNN.
- Provide explicit image-level stride values per backbone and analyze their impact on small-object detection and box precision.
- Clarify all variables in the bounding-box parameterization to avoid ambiguity.
