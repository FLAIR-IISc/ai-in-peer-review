Summary
The paper proposes a two-stage object detection framework built on convolutional neural networks. A Region Proposal Network (RPN) generates candidate regions by sliding a small network over the top-layer feature map, and a Fast R-CNN detector then classifies and refines these proposals. The convolutional backbone is shared between RPN and detector, enabling efficient inference and a reported throughput of about 5 fps on a GPU.

Strengths
- Clear and practical integration of proposal generation and detection within a shared CNN backbone.
- Conceptually simple extension of CNN-based detection that eliminates expensive external proposal methods.
- Strong potential for speed/accuracy trade-offs, with promising near–real-time performance.

Concerns and questions
1) Optimization/training procedure
- The optimization section appears to describe only a single alternation between training the RPN and the detector. If that is the case, the method effectively assumes that proposals produced by the first RPN pass are sufficiently close to optimal. This is a strong assumption. Please clarify:
  - Do you perform multiple rounds of alternating training (e.g., RPN → detection → RPN fine-tuning → detection fine-tuning)? If so, how many, and what is the convergence criterion?
  - If only one alternation is used, what empirical evidence supports its sufficiency? An ablation varying the number of alternations and reporting mAP would be helpful.
  - Are backbone convolutional layers shared and updated jointly by both losses at any stage, or are they frozen for one head while training the other? How are gradients combined if joint training is used?

2) Sliding window stride and localization accuracy
- When applying a pixel-wise sliding window on the top-layer feature map, what is the corresponding stride on the input image? With multiple max-pooling layers, the effective stride S (often 16 or 32) can make a stride-1 slide on the feature map correspond to S pixels on the image. This coarse sampling may degrade localization, especially at higher IoU thresholds.
  - Please specify the exact feature map stride relative to the input image and the sliding step used on the feature map.
  - How do you mitigate localization error due to coarse stride (e.g., via anchor design, multi-scale features, dilated convolutions, or finer-resolution features)?
  - Provide localization metrics at stricter IoUs (e.g., AP at 0.75 or averaged over [0.5:0.95]) to quantify the impact of stride. An ablation showing performance vs. stride or feature-map level would also strengthen the paper.

3) Notation clarity (h_a)
- Lines 173–174 introduce h_a without definition. Please define all symbols at first use. If h_a denotes the anchor height (analogous to w_a for anchor width) used in the bounding-box regression parameterization, state this explicitly and provide the full equations for the regression targets (e.g., tx, ty, tw, th) and their relation to anchor parameters (x_a, y_a, w_a, h_a) and ground truth.

Additional requests for clarification and analysis
- Runtime breakdown: Report per-stage timings (RPN forward pass, proposal generation/NMS, ROI pooling, classification/regression) to substantiate the 5 fps claim and identify bottlenecks.
- Proposal quality: Report recall of the RPN at multiple IoU thresholds vs. number of proposals, and the effect of NMS thresholds. A precision–recall curve for proposals would contextualize detector performance.
- Sensitivity to the number of proposals: Show mAP as a function of proposals per image to demonstrate efficiency/accuracy trade-offs.
- Anchor design: Specify anchor scales, aspect ratios, and how they were selected. Include an ablation on these choices if possible.
- Sampling and losses: Clarify the positive/negative sampling strategy for RPN and detector minibatches, class-agnostic vs. class-specific bounding-box regression in the second stage, and how losses are weighted when trained jointly.
- Comparison to baselines: Include strong baselines that use external proposals (e.g., Selective Search, EdgeBoxes) under matched backbones and training protocols to isolate the benefit of learned proposals and shared features.

Overall assessment
The paper presents a timely and impactful idea that unifies region proposal and detection within a shared CNN, offering clear computational advantages. The approach is a natural evolution of CNN-based object detection and is likely to influence practice. However, several critical details of the training procedure, stride-to-image mapping, and notation are underspecified. Addressing the questions above—especially around alternating or joint training, localization at higher IoUs, and precise implementation details—would substantially strengthen the paper. Conditional on clarifying these points and adding modest ablations, I would lean toward acceptance.
