- Proposal: Introduces Region Proposal Networks (RPNs) that predict k bounding boxes and objectness scores at each location using translation-invariant anchors on a shared convolutional feature map.
- Integration: RPNs share convolutional layers with Fast R-CNN and are trained with a multitask loss (classification + box regression) using a 4-step alternating optimization.
- Results: Achieves state-of-the-art VOC07/12 mAP with ~300 proposals and ~5 fps end-to-end, with the proposal stage adding ~10 ms.

- Technical strength: Clean, fully convolutional proposal mechanism with translation-invariant anchors and an effective multitask loss that dramatically reduces proposal computation.
- Empirical strength: Strong mAP using far fewer proposals; ablations convincingly isolate roles of classification, regression, NMS, and proposal count.
- Efficiency: Proposal generation is nearly cost-free when sharing features, making the pipeline substantially faster than SS/EB-based methods.
- Reproducibility: Well-organized paper, detailed training schedule, and released code.

- Training integration concern: Uses heuristic alternating optimization to couple RPN and Fast R-CNN without analysis of convergence, stability, or fully joint training.
- Proposal quality in isolation: Lacks thorough detector-agnostic evaluation (e.g., average recall at varied IoUs, cross-dataset tests) to prove RPN proposals are superior independently.
- Sensitivity to design choices: Anchor scales/aspect ratios, IoU labeling thresholds, NMS thresholds, and stride may impact small-object recall and robustness; more systematic analysis is needed.
- Limited evaluation scope: Experiments confined to VOC; harder datasets with small/crowded objects would better test robustness given the 16-pixel stride.

- Method quality: Technically sound with appropriate loss, label assignment, and informative ablations; primary gap is lack of fully joint training and independent proposal-quality metrics.
- Clarity: Clearly written and detailed; suggests adding pseudocode for alternating training, clarifying normalization/λ choices, and summarizing cross-boundary anchor treatment.

- Originality: Novel combination of translation-invariant anchors, fully convolutional proposal prediction, and feature sharing distinct from OverFeat/MultiBox and hand-engineered methods.
- Significance: Removes a major detection bottleneck, delivering substantial speed/accuracy gains likely to influence research and practice; code release increases impact.

- Improve: Explore or discuss end-to-end joint training and report empirical stability/mAP even without formal convergence guarantees.
- Improve: Include detector-agnostic proposal metrics (average recall at IoU 0.5–0.9) and cross-dataset generalization studies.
- Improve: Provide sensitivity analyses for anchor configurations, NMS thresholds, and feature-map stride, focusing on small-object recall.
- Improve: Report distribution of proposal counts after NMS and its effect on runtime, not just the cap.
- Improve: Discuss fairness of runtime comparisons (CPU SS/EB vs GPU RPN) while emphasizing the algorithmic contribution of feature sharing.

- For acceptance: Strong, simple idea with substantial empirical gains in speed and accuracy and clear reproducibility.
- For acceptance: Thorough ablations and code release support broad applicability and practical impact.
- Against acceptance: Relies on heuristic alternating training without end-to-end optimization or analysis.
- Against acceptance: Evaluation limited to VOC and incomplete detector-agnostic proposal assessment.
- Against acceptance: Unclear sensitivity to anchor/stride choices and potential small-object issues.

- Overall: Excellent, impactful paper that meaningfully unifies proposal generation and detection within a shared network; recommended for acceptance despite heuristic training integration.
