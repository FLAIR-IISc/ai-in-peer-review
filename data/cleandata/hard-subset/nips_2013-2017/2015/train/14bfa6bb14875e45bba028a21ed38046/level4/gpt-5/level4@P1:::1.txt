Summary
This paper introduces Region Proposal Networks (RPNs), a fully convolutional network that shares convolutional features with a downstream detector (Fast R-CNN) to generate class-agnostic region proposals. At each location on a shared feature map, the RPN predicts objectness scores and bounding-box regressions for k anchors spanning multiple scales and aspect ratios (k=9 in the default). The authors train the RPN end-to-end using a multi-task loss and adopt an alternating, 4-step fine-tuning scheme to share features with a Fast R-CNN detector. On PASCAL VOC 2007 and 2012, the combined system achieves state-of-the-art mAP (73.2% and 70.4% with VGG-16) while running near real time: proposals add about 10 ms on a GPU, yielding roughly 5 fps with VGG-16 and up to 17 fps with a smaller model. The paper situates the method relative to Selective Search and EdgeBoxes (traditional proposal generators), and to deep learning alternatives such as OverFeat and MultiBox, emphasizing that RPNs exploit translation-invariant anchors and weight sharing to reduce parameters and enable efficient joint training with detection.

Relation to prior work
The work builds on R-CNN, SPPnet, and Fast R-CNN by moving the proposal stage into the network and sharing features, thereby removing the proposal bottleneck that dominates runtime when using SS/EB. Compared to OverFeat (one-stage sliding-window detection with regression) and MultiBox (predicting multiple boxes from a single layer), the RPN formulates proposals as a dense, translation-invariant prediction problem tied to anchors, with far fewer parameters than MultiBox and a two-stage cascade that improves accuracy over pure one-stage sliding windows.

Strengths
- Significant practical impact: proposals become nearly cost-free when sharing features with the detector; the overall system is markedly faster than Fast R-CNN with SS/EB while slightly more accurate.
- Clear architectural idea: anchor-based, fully convolutional proposal generation integrated with Fast R-CNN.
- Solid empirical results: strong mAP on VOC 2007/2012; ablative analyses of proposal counts, NMS, and the contributions of cls vs reg heads.
- Reproducibility: key training details and code are provided.

Weaknesses and points needing clarification
- Translation invariance: The paper argues that RPNs are translation-invariant (due to anchors and shared functions), whereas MultiBox uses non-translation-invariant, k-means-derived anchors. Please clarify the precise sense of this invariance and provide a more explicit comparison with OverFeat’s sliding-window formulation. The current explanation may be confusing, especially given the citation numbering.
- Manual hyperparameter choices: Several design choices are fixed without analysis, notably the n=3 receptive window for the intermediate conv, the choice k=9 (3 scales × 3 aspect ratios), the 0.7/0.3 IoU thresholds for labeling anchors, and the NMS threshold (0.7). Sensitivity studies would strengthen the claims, particularly for n and k, which govern capacity and runtime.
- Training procedure and stability: The 4-step alternating training is more complex than standard end-to-end fine-tuning. The paper states joint training is left for future work, but it would help to report on stability (e.g., variance across runs), whether repeating the alternation beyond one cycle helps, and whether alternative schedules were tried. The current description may suggest fragility or ad hoc choices.
- Clarity: A few sections are dense and could be streamlined—for example, the treatment of cross-boundary anchors (ignored in training, clipped at test time), normalization constants in the loss (Ncls, Nreg) and the choice of λ. A concise rationale for these decisions would aid readability.

Quality
The method is technically sound and well-supported by experiments. The loss, anchor labeling, and sampling strategies are standard and appropriate; the ablations (turning off cls or reg, varying proposal counts, feature sharing) are informative. The runtime accounting is careful, and the comparisons to SS/EB are relevant. The main concerns are the lack of sensitivity analyses for key hyperparameters and limited discussion of training stability.

Clarity
Overall, the paper is readable but could be clearer in the sections on translation invariance vs. prior work, anchor design choices, and the training schedule. A short subsection explaining why n=3 and k=9 were selected, and a table or figure summarizing the alternating training steps, would help.

Originality
Moving proposal generation into a shared, fully convolutional module integrated with Fast R-CNN is a notable and timely contribution. While related to OverFeat and MultiBox, the anchor-based, translation-invariant formulation with feature sharing and a two-stage cascade is distinct and well-motivated.

Significance
The impact is high: proposals cease to be the bottleneck, enabling near real-time detection with strong accuracy. The approach is likely to be widely adopted and extended.

Specific questions and suggestions
- Please clarify the translation-invariance claim: how exactly do the learned functions generalize across spatial shifts, and how does this differ from MultiBox and OverFeat?
- Provide sensitivity analyses for n, k, anchor scales/aspect ratios, and NMS threshold. Do smaller or larger n or different k materially affect recall/mAP vs runtime?
- Training: did you attempt more than one alternation cycle? Does performance improve by iterating steps 3–4? Please report variance across random initializations.
- Loss normalization and λ: justify the chosen normalization constants and weighting, and report whether results are sensitive to these choices.
- Cross-boundary anchors: could you quantify the fraction ignored and whether including them (with clipping) degrades convergence/performance?

Pros for acceptance
- Strong empirical results with substantial speedup (approximately an order-of-magnitude gain over Fast R-CNN with SS) and slight accuracy improvements.
- Clear, effective architectural advance with code available.
- Thorough ablations demonstrating the contributions of key components.

Cons for acceptance
- Insufficient analysis of key hyperparameters (n, k, anchors, thresholds).
- Alternating training procedure seems ad hoc; stability and alternative schedules are not fully explored.
- Some confusion in the discussion of translation invariance relative to prior work.

Overall evaluation
A compelling and impactful paper. Despite some clarity and analysis gaps, the technical contribution and empirical results are strong. I lean towards acceptance, contingent on clarifying the translation-invariance discussion and providing more insight into hyperparameter sensitivity and training stability.
