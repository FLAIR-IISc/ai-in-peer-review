This paper proposes a region proposal method that shares convolutional features with the object detection network. The proposal module includes a binary classifier (object vs. non-object) and a bounding-box regressor. By reusing features for both proposal generation and detection, the integrated system is reported to be roughly 10x faster than Fast R-CNN while achieving slightly higher accuracy. Because the detector is typically trained with precomputed regions, the authors adopt a multi-stage alternating training scheme to couple the proposal network and the detector.

Strengths:
- Clear objective and strong empirical results.
- The idea of sharing convolutional features between proposal generation and detection is compelling and practically impactful.
- The method is novel relative to prior proposal approaches that do not leverage shared deep features to this extent.

Main concerns and questions:
1) Translation invariance: The paper claims that the RPN is translation-invariant, whereas [19] is not. Please clarify the basis for this claim. What architectural or training differences lead to translation invariance in RPN but not in [19]? How does this property compare with OverFeat, which also performs dense predictions over convolutional feature maps? A concise theoretical or empirical explanation (e.g., controlled translations with performance curves) would help.

2) Hand-tuned parameters: Several key parameters appear to be chosen manually without sufficient analysis. For example, why choose n=3 (e.g., scales) and k=9 (e.g., anchors per location)? How sensitive is performance to these choices? Please include ablations varying these parameters (e.g., number of aspect ratios, scales, and anchor tiling) and report trade-offs between accuracy and runtime.

3) Training complexity and stability: The alternating multi-stage training procedure seems complicated and potentially unstable. 
- Why not adopt a single, consistent end-to-end training strategy applied uniformly across experiments? 
- How many alternation cycles are necessary, and does performance saturate? 
- What is the variance across runs with different random seeds? 
- Have the authors tried multiple alternative schedules or optimizer settings and found the current one consistently best? 
- If joint training was attempted and abandoned, please summarize results and failure modes.

Clarity and presentation:
- The core idea is strong, but parts of the exposition are difficult to follow. A schematic that clearly separates the proposal module from the detection head, along with data flow and loss functions, would improve readability.
- Explicitly define all symbols and hyperparameters when first introduced (e.g., n, k) and provide their intuition.
- Summarize training steps in a concise algorithm box, indicating when features are frozen vs. updated, and how proposal-targets are regenerated.
- Provide a short discussion on computational cost: where the 10x speedup arises (e.g., shared backbone, fewer RoIs per image, reduced feature recomputation), and the hardware setup for fair comparison with Fast R-CNN.

Experimental analysis:
- In addition to headline metrics, include per-class AP or error analysis to identify where the proposal sharing helps or hurts.
- Report sensitivity to IoU thresholds for positive/negative samples in both the proposal classifier and the detector.
- Include a small study on NMS thresholds and their impact on recall and final AP, since these interact with the number and design of anchors.
- If possible, compare proposal recall vs. OverFeat-style dense sliding windows to contextualize gains from anchors and feature sharing.

Overall evaluation:
The contribution is significant and timely, with solid empirical improvements and clear practical value. However, the paper would benefit from deeper analysis of design choices, clearer justification of the translation-invariance claim, and a more transparent and reproducible training procedure. Addressing the questions above and adding targeted ablations would substantially strengthen the paper’s clarity and credibility.
