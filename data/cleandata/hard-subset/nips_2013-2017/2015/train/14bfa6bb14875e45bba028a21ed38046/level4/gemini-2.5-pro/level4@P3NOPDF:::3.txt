This paper presents a two-stage object detection algorithm that integrates region proposal generation and object classification into a unified convolutional neural network (CNN) framework. The first stage introduces a novel Region Proposal Network (RPN) that generates object proposals by applying a sliding window over the final convolutional feature map. The second stage consists of a Fast R-CNN detector that processes these proposed regions to perform final classification and bounding box regression. A key contribution of this work is that the RPN and the detection network share the underlying convolutional layers, which enables a highly efficient and nearly end-to-end system. By leveraging GPU acceleration, the proposed method achieves near real-time performance (5 fps) while maintaining high accuracy, marking a significant step forward from previous methods that relied on slow, external proposal algorithms.

While the proposed architecture is elegant and the performance gains are impressive, I have a few concerns and questions that I would like the authors to address.

1.  **Alternating Optimization:** In the optimization section, the authors describe a 4-step alternating training process to learn the shared features. It appears that only one full cycle of this alternation is performed (i.e., train RPN, then train Fast R-CNN using those proposals, then fine-tune RPN, and finally fine-tune Fast R-CNN). This approach seems to assume that the proposals generated by the RPN after its initial training are sufficient for training a high-quality detector. However, these initial proposals are likely suboptimal. Could the authors clarify the rationale for not performing more iterations of this alternating training? It would be insightful to understand if further iterations were explored and whether they led to diminishing returns or introduced any training instabilities.

2.  **Effective Stride and Localization Accuracy:** The RPN operates by sliding a small network over the last convolutional feature map. Due to multiple max-pooling and strided convolution layers in modern CNNs, a single pixel step on this feature map corresponds to a large stride on the input image layer (e.g., 16 pixels for VGG-16). This large effective stride may pose a challenge for precise localization, particularly for smaller objects. While the bounding box regression mechanism is designed to refine the proposal coordinates, its performance can be dependent on the quality of the initial proposal. Could the authors please comment on the impact of this large effective stride on localization accuracy? Specifically, what is the total step size on the input image plane, and how does the system ensure accurate localization despite this coarse sampling?

3.  **Notation Clarification:** For clarity, could the authors please provide the definition for the term $h_a$ used in lines 173 and 174 of the manuscript?

In summary, this work introduces a significant and compelling architecture for fast and accurate object detection. The integration of region proposals into the CNN pipeline is a major contribution. Addressing the points above regarding the training strategy and the implications of the feature map stride would further strengthen this excellent paper.
