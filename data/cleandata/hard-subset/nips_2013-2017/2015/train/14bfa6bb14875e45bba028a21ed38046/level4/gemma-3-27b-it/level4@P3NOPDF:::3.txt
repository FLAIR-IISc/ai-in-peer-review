This work presents a two-stage object detection algorithm leveraging convolutional neural networks (CNNs). The approach builds upon existing CNN-based object detection methods by employing a region proposal network (RPN) – a sliding window approach applied to the top layer feature map of a CNN – followed by a fast R-CNN stage for classification and bounding box regression. The authors report achieving near real-time performance (5fps) due to shared convolutional layers and GPU acceleration.

My primary concerns regarding this submission relate to the optimization procedure and the potential for inaccuracies in bounding box localization stemming from the sliding window approach.

First, the description of the alternating optimization between the RPN and the R-CNN suggests a single iteration. If this is indeed the case, the method implicitly assumes that the initial proposals generated by the RPN are near-optimal. This is a strong assumption, as the RPN requires multiple iterations to refine its proposal generation and achieve a stable solution. A single iteration risks suboptimal proposals being passed to the R-CNN, potentially limiting overall detection performance. Clarification on the number of alternating optimization steps, and justification for this choice, is necessary.

Second, the application of a sliding window to the top layer feature map raises a question about the effective step size on the original image. Given the repeated max-pooling operations within a CNN, the feature map at the top layer represents a significant reduction in spatial resolution compared to the input image. A sliding window with a seemingly small step size at the feature map level may correspond to a much larger step size when projected back to the pixel space of the original image. This coarse granularity could lead to inaccurate bounding box localization, particularly for smaller objects. The authors should address this potential issue by explicitly discussing the relationship between the step size on the feature map and the corresponding step size on the image, and providing analysis or experiments to demonstrate the robustness of the method to this effect.

Finally, the notation $h_a$ used in lines 173 and 174 is undefined. Providing a clear definition for this variable is crucial for understanding the described calculations.

Despite these concerns, the proposed approach represents a logical extension of existing object detection techniques. The use of a shared convolutional backbone is an efficient strategy, and the reported near real-time performance is noteworthy. Addressing the points raised above would significantly strengthen the paper and increase confidence in the validity and robustness of the proposed method. Specifically, elaborating on the optimization process and carefully considering the implications of the sliding window granularity are vital for a more convincing contribution.
