

This paper proposes a two-stage object detection framework leveraging CNNs, where Region Proposal Networks (RPNs) generate regions on the top-layer feature map, followed by Fast R-CNN processing. While the shared convolutional backbone and GPU acceleration enable near real-time performance (5 fps), three critical concerns regarding technical soundness and clarity require resolution before publication.  

**1. Insufficient Alternating Optimization Details**  
The optimization section describes a single iteration of alternating updates between RPN and R-CNN. This implies the initial RPN proposals are assumed optimal, which contradicts empirical evidence that RPNs require iterative refinement to converge toward high-quality proposals. Without multiple optimization steps or justification for a single iteration, the claimed performance gains lack theoretical grounding. The authors must clarify whether the RPN is trained end-to-end with R-CNN or if the single-iteration approach is a simplification. If the latter, the paper should explicitly state this limitation and provide ablation results demonstrating its impact on detection accuracy.  

**2. Unclear Spatial Scaling of Sliding Window Mechanism**  
The RPN employs a pixel-wise sliding window on the top-layer feature map. However, due to multiple max-pooling operations in the CNN backbone, the top layer operates at a significantly reduced spatial resolution (e.g., 1:16 scale relative to input image). The step size of the sliding window on this downsampled feature map is unspecified. If the step size corresponds to, say, 1 pixel on the feature map (e.g., stride=1), it translates to a 16-pixel step on the original image—potentially causing substantial bounding box misalignment or missed detections. The authors must define the exact step size relative to the input image and justify its suitability for accurate localization. Without this, the method’s robustness to object scale and position is questionable.  

**3. Ambiguous Definition of $h_a$**  
The notation $h_a$ appears in lines 173–174 but is never formally defined. Given its role in the proposal scoring function or loss calculation, this omission impedes reproducibility and interpretation. The authors should clarify whether $h_a$ represents a height dimension, a feature vector, or a learned parameter, and explicitly state its dimensionality and derivation. For instance, if $h_a$ denotes anchor height, the paper must specify whether it is a fixed hyperparameter or dynamically computed.  

The paper’s core contribution—applying sliding windows to CNN feature maps for region proposals—builds logically on prior work (e.g., R-CNN, Fast R-CNN). However, the unresolved technical ambiguities undermine confidence in the method’s implementation and generalizability. The authors should address all three points with concrete details, equations, or experimental validation. Until then, the current formulation lacks sufficient rigor for acceptance in NIPS 2015.
