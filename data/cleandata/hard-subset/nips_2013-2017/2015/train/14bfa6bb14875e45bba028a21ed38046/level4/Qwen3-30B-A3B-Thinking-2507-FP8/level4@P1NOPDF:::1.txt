

The paper presents a clear goal and demonstrates solid experimental results. However, the exposition is occasionally difficult to follow due to several unresolved ambiguities. First, the paper states that the Region Proposal Network (RPN) is translation-invariant while referencing [19] as not being so, but does not clarify why this distinction holds. Specifically, how does this differ from OverFeat’s approach, which also handles translation? Second, several key hyperparameters are chosen without justification—such as $n=3$ and $k=9$—leaving readers to speculate on their rationale. Third, the training procedure appears complex and lacks stability; the authors do not address whether the reported results are consistent across multiple runs. Did they test alternative configurations and select the current setup solely because it performed best, or was this the only viable configuration explored?  

The core contribution is a novel region proposal method that shares the CNN features used for object localization, eliminating redundant computation. The proposed method includes a classification layer to distinguish objects from background and a regression layer for bounding box refinement. By reusing the localization CNN’s features, the combined architecture achieves approximately 10× speedup over Fast R-CNN while maintaining marginal accuracy gains. This efficiency stems from avoiding separate feature extraction for region proposals.  

However, the training procedure requires further clarification. The paper describes a multi-stage alternative training approach, motivated by the fact that standard object localization CNNs assume pre-defined regions. Yet, the paper does not explain why this multi-stage process is necessary or how it compares to simpler alternatives. For instance, why not apply the standard training procedure iteratively to refine proposals? The lack of ablation studies on the training protocol weakens the claim of robustness.  

Overall, the technical contribution is promising, but the paper would benefit significantly from addressing these points. Clarifying the translation invariance comparison, justifying hyperparameters, and demonstrating training stability would substantially improve the work’s impact and reproducibility. The speed-accuracy trade-off is compelling, but the current presentation leaves critical implementation details underspecified.
