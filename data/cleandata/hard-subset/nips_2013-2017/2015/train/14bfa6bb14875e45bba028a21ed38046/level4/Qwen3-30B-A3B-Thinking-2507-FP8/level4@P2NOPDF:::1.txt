

The paper presents a novel region proposal method that shares CNN features with the object localization module, resulting in a combined architecture approximately 10x faster than Fast R-CNN with marginally higher accuracy. The core idea and experimental validation are compelling, and the results are well-supported. However, the paper suffers from significant clarity issues that hinder understanding of key technical contributions and implementation details. Below are specific concerns:

1.  **Translation Invariance Clarification:** The paper claims RPN is translation-invariant while [19] is not. This requires explicit justification. Why is RPN translation-invariant? The explanation must address the fundamental difference between the RPN's sliding-window convolutional approach (which inherently shares weights across spatial locations) and the method in [19] (OverFeat). OverFeat, as a fully convolutional network using a sliding window, is *not* translation-invariant in the same way as RPN, but the paper fails to clarify *why* this distinction matters for the proposed method or how [19] specifically violates the requirement. This omission creates confusion.

2.  **Lack of Parameter Justification:** Several critical hyperparameters are chosen arbitrarily without analysis. The most prominent examples are the selection of *n=3* (number of anchor scales) and *k=9* (number of anchor aspect ratios). The authors must provide reasoning for these specific values (e.g., sensitivity analysis, empirical performance on validation sets, comparison with alternatives). Simply stating "we chose n=3, k=9" is insufficient for reproducibility and scientific rigor. Similar justification is needed for other manual choices.

3.  **Training Procedure Stability and Reproducibility:** The proposed multi-stage training procedure appears complex and lacks stability analysis. The authors do not address whether the procedure is sensitive to initialization or hyperparameters. Crucially, they do not state if they ran multiple trials with the same procedure to confirm stability or if they explored alternative training schedules (e.g., single-stage vs. multi-stage, different optimization schedules) before settling on the current approach. The claim that "the current one performs the best" implies extensive experimentation, but the lack of reported ablation studies on the training process makes this assertion unsubstantiated. A brief discussion of training stability and reproducibility checks would significantly strengthen the work.

The paper's core contribution—feature sharing for faster region proposals—is valid and well-motivated. The speed/accuracy trade-off is clearly demonstrated. However, the clarity gaps in the technical exposition (particularly points 1 and 2) and the lack of detail on the training protocol (point 3) prevent the reader from fully grasping the method's nuances or confidently reproducing the results. Addressing these points would substantially improve the paper's quality and impact. The current draft requires significant revision to clarify the key technical distinctions and justify implementation choices before it can be considered for publication.
