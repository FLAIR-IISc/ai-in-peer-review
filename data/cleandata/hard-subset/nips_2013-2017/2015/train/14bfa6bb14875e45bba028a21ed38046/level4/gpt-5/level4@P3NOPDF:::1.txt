Summary
The paper proposes a Region Proposal Network (RPN) that shares convolutional features with an object detection network. The RPN consists of a binary classification head (object vs. background) and a bounding-box regression head applied densely over the feature map via anchors. By sharing computation with the detector, the combined system achieves roughly a 10× speedup over Fast R-CNN while also improving accuracy. Because the detector typically assumes fixed proposals, the authors adopt a multi-stage alternating training scheme to couple the RPN and detector.

Strengths
- Clear goal and strong practical impact: unifying proposal generation and detection within a single CNN substantially improves speed without sacrificing accuracy.
- Conceptual simplicity: a lightweight, sliding-window proposal mechanism on shared features is elegant and easy to deploy.
- Solid empirical results: the method delivers both state-of-the-art accuracy and significant runtime gains.
- Broad relevance: the approach addresses a core bottleneck in detection pipelines and is likely to be widely adopted.

Main Concerns and Requests for Clarification
1) Translation invariance claim
- The paper states that the RPN is translation-invariant, whereas [19] is not. Please define precisely what is meant by “translation-invariant” in this context (e.g., invariance to integer-pixel shifts of the input leading to identical predictions up to a corresponding shift on the output).
- Explain why the RPN enjoys this property. Intuitively, sliding a small, fully convolutional head over a shared feature map can be translation-equivariant, but factors such as feature-map stride, padding, boundary effects, anchor quantization, and non-maximum suppression can break strict invariance/equivariance. Which of these apply here?
- Provide a brief comparison to OverFeat and [19]: Is OverFeat’s sliding-window detector translation-equivariant under the same definition? Why does [19] fail to be invariant? An empirical demonstration (e.g., output consistency under controlled input translations) would make this point convincing.

2) Hyperparameter choices without analysis
- Several key design decisions appear to be set manually without adequate exploration: number of anchor scales/aspect ratios (n=3, k=9), specific scale values and aspect ratios, positive/negative IoU thresholds for RPN training, NMS threshold, number of proposals kept at test time, and the positive/negative minibatch ratio.
- Please include ablation studies showing sensitivity to these choices. For example:
  - Vary the number of anchor scales (e.g., n=2/3/4) and aspect ratios, and report recall vs. IoU for proposals and final mAP.
  - Study the effect of feature stride and anchor scales on small-object performance.
  - Quantify how many proposals are actually needed to reach near-peak accuracy (e.g., 100/300/1k).
  - Report mAP/AR sensitivity to NMS threshold and IoU thresholds used for labeling anchors.
- If these hyperparameters were tuned on a validation set, describe the tuning protocol to avoid overfitting.

3) Training procedure complexity and stability
- The alternating multi-stage training (RPN → Fast R-CNN → RPN → Fast R-CNN) reads as complex. Please justify why multiple alternations are not repeated beyond the reported steps. Does performance saturate? Provide a plot of mAP across alternations.
- Discuss training stability and variance: How sensitive are results to random initialization or minibatch ordering? Reporting mean ± std across multiple runs would increase confidence.
- If an end-to-end joint training variant was attempted, summarize its performance and stability relative to the alternating scheme. If not, explain practical barriers and whether approximate joint training (e.g., sharing RoI pooling gradients) was explored.

4) Empirical reporting details
- Runtime: Provide a clear breakdown of per-image time for RPN, detection, NMS, and data transfer, plus hardware details. Clarify whether the 10× speedup is measured on the same GPU and backbone and whether it includes proposal generation and NMS.
- Proposal quality: Include proposal recall vs. IoU curves (e.g., at 0.5–0.9) and average recall, along with the number of proposals. Compare directly to strong hand-crafted proposal methods and to [19].
- Generalization: If possible, assess robustness across datasets and backbones (e.g., ZF vs. VGG). How do anchors need to change for different datasets or image resolutions?

Minor Comments and Suggestions
- Clarify terminology: refer to the RPN classifier as “objectness” to avoid confusion with downstream multi-class classification.
- Provide a concise algorithmic summary/pseudocode of the training schedule, including loss functions, sampling ratios, and IoU thresholds, so the procedure is reproducible.
- Unify notation for n (scales) and k (anchors per location) and define them at first use. Explicitly list scale values and aspect ratios in the main text or an appendix.
- Discuss failure modes: e.g., small or thin objects relative to feature stride; crowded scenes where NMS may suppress true positives.
- Check for typos and improve figure legends to make the pipeline self-contained.

Overall Evaluation
This is a strong and influential contribution: the idea of a shared-feature RPN is novel, well-motivated, and empirically effective, with clear benefits in speed and accuracy. The paper would be significantly strengthened by clarifying the translation-invariance claim, providing ablations for key hyperparameters (especially anchor design), and offering a more transparent account of training stability and the choice of alternating optimization. Addressing these points would enhance both the paper’s clarity and its reproducibility. I am inclined to support acceptance, contingent on the requested clarifications and ablations.
