This paper proposes a two-stage object detection algorithm based on a Convolutional Neural Network (CNN). The first stage, a Region Proposal Network (RPN), adapts the traditional sliding window method to operate on the final convolutional feature map. In the second stage, a Fast R-CNN detector is applied to the regions proposed by the RPN. As the convolutional layers are shared between the RPN and the Fast R-CNN detector, and computations are accelerated using a GPU, the resulting system achieves near real-time performance (5fps). The proposed method is a logical and effective extension of existing CNN-based object detection frameworks.

I have the following concerns and questions for the authors:

1.  Regarding the optimization procedure, the paper appears to describe only a single iteration of the alternating optimization between the RPN and the Fast R-CNN components. If this is correct, the method assumes that the initial proposals generated by the RPN after the first training stage are optimal for training the final detector. This seems to be a strong assumption, as these initial proposals are unlikely to be optimal. Could the authors comment on why only one iteration is performed and whether additional iterations of joint training were explored?

2.  When the sliding window is applied to the final convolutional feature map, what is its effective stride in the input image space? Due to the multiple max-pooling layers, the spatial resolution of the final feature map is significantly coarser than that of the input image. Consequently, a sliding window on this feature map corresponds to a large stride on the input image, which could potentially lead to inaccurate bounding box localization. Further clarification on how the model achieves precise localization despite this large effective stride would be helpful.

3.  The definition of the term $h_a$ in lines 173 and 174 is unclear. Could the authors please provide a definition for this term?
