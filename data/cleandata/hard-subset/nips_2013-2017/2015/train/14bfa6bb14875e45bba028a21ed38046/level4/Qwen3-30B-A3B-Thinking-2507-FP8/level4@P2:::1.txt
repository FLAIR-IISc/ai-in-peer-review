

This paper introduces Region Proposal Networks (RPNs), a novel approach to object detection that shares convolutional features between region proposal and object detection networks. The authors demonstrate that by training a fully-convolutional network to generate region proposals, they can eliminate the computational bottleneck of traditional region proposal methods like Selective Search and EdgeBoxes. The RPN generates high-quality proposals with minimal additional computation (10ms per image) while sharing convolutional features with the detection network, resulting in a system that runs at 5fps on VGG-16 with state-of-the-art accuracy on PASCAL VOC benchmarks.

The paper builds upon key prior work including SPPnet and Fast R-CNN, which established the importance of shared convolutional features for efficient detection. While previous work (e.g., OverFeat and MultiBox) explored using deep networks for localization, the authors' key insight is that region proposals can be generated by a network that shares features with the detection network, rather than using separate, hand-engineered proposal methods. This represents a significant conceptual advance over prior approaches.

Strengths of the paper include:
- Quality: The experimental results are technically sound and well-supported. The authors provide comprehensive ablation studies showing that the RPN approach outperforms Selective Search and EdgeBoxes in both accuracy and speed. The 73.2% mAP on PASCAL VOC 2007 and 70.4% on 2012 are state-of-the-art results for the time.
- Originality: The core idea of sharing convolutional features between proposal and detection networks is novel and elegantly solves the proposal bottleneck. The translation-invariant anchor design (Section 3.1) is particularly clever and well-motivated.
- Significance: The work has substantial practical significance, making real-time object detection feasible with deep networks. The authors demonstrate a 10x speedup over Fast R-CNN with comparable accuracy.
- Clarity: The paper is generally well-organized and clearly written, with a logical progression from problem statement to solution to evaluation.

Several aspects could be improved to strengthen the paper:
- Clarity: The explanation of why the RPN is translation-invariant while OverFeat is not (Section 3.1) is confusing. The authors should clarify that OverFeat uses a fully-connected layer for prediction, whereas the RPN uses a fully-convolutional architecture that inherently preserves translation invariance. The paper should more explicitly compare the anchor design to OverFeat's approach.
- Quality: The paper states "n=3" without justification (Section 3.1), and similarly uses k=9 without thorough analysis. While the authors mention they tried different configurations, they should provide more detail about why these specific values were chosen, including any ablation studies on these hyperparameters.
- Clarity: The training procedure (Section 3.3) is described as "simple," but the four-step alternating optimization is somewhat complex. The authors should better explain why this approach was necessary rather than joint optimization, and provide evidence that the procedure is stable (e.g., by showing convergence curves or multiple runs).
- Originality: The paper could better differentiate its approach from OverFeat (Section 2). While the authors mention OverFeat briefly, they should explicitly state how their region proposal approach differs from OverFeat's one-stage detection pipeline.

The authors make a compelling case for the practical significance of their work. The demonstration that RPNs can be trained end-to-end to share features with detection networks represents a significant step toward unified, efficient object detection systems. The 5fps frame rate on a GPU with state-of-the-art accuracy makes this approach highly practical for real-world applications.

The paper's main weakness is the lack of sufficient detail on certain design choices. For instance, the anchor configuration (k=9) is used without thorough justification, and the translation-invariance explanation leaves room for confusion. The authors should provide more analysis on why their specific design choices (n=3, k=9) work best, perhaps including ablation studies on these parameters.

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes an important contribution to object detection. The authors have identified a key bottleneck in existing systems and proposed a simple yet effective solution that significantly improves both speed and accuracy. The work is technically sound, well-executed, and has clear practical significance.

I recommend acceptance. The paper addresses a significant problem in object detection with a novel solution that builds elegantly on prior work. The experimental results are thorough and convincing, and the approach has the potential to become the standard for object detection systems. The minor issues with clarity and parameter justification are easily addressable in the final version.
