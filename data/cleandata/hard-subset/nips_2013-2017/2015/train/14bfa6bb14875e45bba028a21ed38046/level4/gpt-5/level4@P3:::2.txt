Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes Region Proposal Networks (RPNs), a fully convolutional architecture that generates class-agnostic object proposals by predicting, at each location on a shared feature map, k bounding boxes and associated objectness scores relative to a set of translation-invariant “anchors” spanning multiple scales and aspect ratios. The RPN shares convolutional layers with a Fast R-CNN detector, making proposal generation nearly cost-free at test time. The authors train RPNs with a multitask loss (objectness classification and box regression) and couple them to Fast R-CNN using a simple alternating optimization scheme that yields shared convolutional features. On PASCAL VOC 2007 and 2012, using VGG-16 and only ~300 proposals per image, the system achieves state-of-the-art detection accuracy (73.2% mAP on VOC07, 70.4% on VOC12) with an end-to-end runtime of about 5 fps on a GPU, while the proposal stage itself adds roughly 10 ms.

The work builds on and unifies two successful lines: region-based detectors that share convolutional computation across regions (SPPnet, Fast R-CNN) and proposal methods (Selective Search, EdgeBoxes). Unlike OverFeat and MultiBox, which predict boxes directly in a one-stage pipeline (class-specific or agnostic) with large fully connected output layers and non-translation-invariant anchor sets, the RPN uses compact, translation-invariant anchors and shares features with the downstream detector. Compared to hand-engineered proposal methods, RPNs are learned end-to-end on detection data and benefit from better backbones.

Strengths
- Technical contribution: A clean, fully convolutional proposal mechanism with translation-invariant anchors and a simple, effective multitask loss. The design dramatically reduces proposal computation by sharing features with the detector.
- Empirical performance: Strong mAP on VOC07/12 with far fewer proposals (~300) and competitive or better accuracy than Selective Search/EdgeBoxes. Ablations convincingly show the roles of classification vs. regression, NMS, and the effect of proposal count.
- Efficiency: Proposal stage adds ~10 ms when features are shared; overall detection is substantially faster than SS/EB-based pipelines.
- Clarity and reproducibility: The paper is well-organized, the training schedule is described in detail, and code is provided.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Training integration: The coupling between RPN and Fast R-CNN relies on a heuristic alternating optimization. While pragmatic and effective, there is no analysis of convergence or stability, and fully joint end-to-end training is not explored.
- Proposal quality in isolation: While the combined system outperforms SS in mAP, the paper suggests mixed results when substituting RPN proposals at test time for a detector trained on SS. More thorough, detector-agnostic evaluation of proposal quality (e.g., average recall at different IoU thresholds, cross-dataset generalization) would strengthen the claim that RPNs are superior proposals independently of the detector.
- Sensitivity to design choices: Anchor scales/aspect ratios, IoU thresholds for assigning labels, NMS thresholds, and stride may affect small-object recall and overall robustness. A more systematic sensitivity analysis would be helpful.
- Scope of evaluation: Results are limited to VOC; broader evaluation (e.g., on a dataset with more small objects or crowded scenes) would test robustness, especially given the 16-pixel stride.

Quality
The method is technically sound and carefully evaluated. The loss, label assignment, and optimization scheme are appropriate and well-motivated. Ablations are informative, including analyses of proposal count, the roles of cls/reg outputs, feature sharing, and one-stage vs two-stage detection. The main gap is the lack of study of fully joint training and a more comprehensive proposal-quality analysis independent of the detector.

Clarity
The paper is clearly written and well-structured. The anchor mechanism, loss, and training schedule are explained with sufficient detail to reproduce results. Minor suggestions:
- Provide a concise pseudocode summary of the 4-step alternating training procedure.
- Clarify the normalization constants Ncls/Nreg and the effective weighting of cls vs reg terms (the λ choice and its impact).
- Summarize the impact of ignoring cross-boundary anchors in training vs clipping at test time.

Originality
The key ideas—translation-invariant anchors, fully convolutional proposal prediction, and feature sharing with the detector—are novel in combination and distinct from OverFeat/MultiBox and hand-engineered proposal methods. The work cleanly integrates proposals and detection within the same convolutional backbone.

Significance
The approach removes a major bottleneck in modern detection pipelines and provides a substantial speed/accuracy improvement, likely to influence subsequent research and practical systems. The code release enhances impact. Demonstrated gains with both ZF and VGG-16 suggest broad applicability.

Suggestions for improvement
- Explore or at least discuss end-to-end joint training of the unified network and report empirical behavior (stability, mAP) even without formal convergence guarantees.
- Include detector-agnostic proposal metrics (e.g., average recall at IoU thresholds 0.5–0.9) and cross-dataset generalization of proposals.
- Provide sensitivity analyses for anchor configurations, NMS thresholds, and feature-map stride, especially regarding small-object recall.
- Report the distribution of the number of proposals after NMS (not just the cap) and its effect on runtime.
- Discuss fairness of runtime comparisons (e.g., SS/EB CPU vs GPU), while acknowledging that the main contribution is algorithmic feature sharing.

Arguments for acceptance
- Strong, simple idea with substantial empirical gains in both speed and accuracy.
- Clear, reproducible method with thorough ablations and code release.
- Meaningful advance over prior proposal mechanisms and one-stage approaches.

Arguments against acceptance
- Heuristic alternating training without end-to-end joint optimization or analysis.
- Limited evaluation beyond VOC and incomplete detector-agnostic proposal assessment.
- Unclear sensitivity to anchor/stride choices and potential issues with small objects.

Overall assessment
This is an excellent paper that makes a significant and timely contribution to object detection by unifying proposal generation and detection within a shared deep network. Despite the heuristic training integration, the method is well validated and impactful. I recommend acceptance.
