The goal of the paper is clear, and the experimental results are solid. However, the paper is not easy to follow, with a few confusing parts. Firstly, the authors claim that their Region Proposal Network (RPN) is translation-invariant, but it is not entirely clear why this is the case, especially in comparison to other methods such as [19] and OverFeat. A more detailed explanation of this property and its significance would be beneficial.

Furthermore, several parameters in the paper are set manually without further analysis or justification. For instance, the choice of n=3 and k=9 is not adequately explained. It would be helpful to provide more insight into the reasoning behind these choices or to explore the impact of varying these parameters on the results.

Additionally, the training procedure outlined in the paper appears complicated and not entirely stable. The authors propose a four-step training algorithm, but it is unclear why this specific procedure was chosen or whether other approaches were explored. It would be useful to provide more information on the stability of the training process and whether the authors tried multiple settings before settling on the current one.

Despite these concerns, the authors propose a novel region proposal method that shares the CNN feature used for object localization. This method consists of a classification layer for recognizing objects versus non-objects and a regression layer for predicting object bounding boxes. By sharing feature computation with the object localization module, the combined new R-CNN is approximately 10 times faster than Fast R-CNN and slightly more accurate.

The paper's main contribution is the introduction of a Region Proposal Network (RPN) that can be trained end-to-end to generate high-quality region proposals. The RPN is a fully-convolutional network that simultaneously predicts object bounds and objectness scores at each position. The authors demonstrate that their method can achieve state-of-the-art object detection accuracy on the PASCAL VOC 2007 and 2012 datasets while reducing the computational burden of region proposal generation.

In terms of quality, the paper is technically sound, and the claims are well-supported by experimental results. The authors provide a thorough evaluation of their method, including comparisons with other region proposal methods and an analysis of the recall-to-IoU metric.

The clarity of the paper could be improved, however. Some sections, such as the explanation of the training procedure, are dense and difficult to follow. The authors could benefit from breaking down complex concepts into simpler, more manageable pieces and providing more visual aids to illustrate key ideas.

The originality of the paper is high, as it introduces a novel approach to region proposal generation that differs significantly from existing methods. The authors provide a clear explanation of how their method differs from previous contributions and adequately reference related work in the field.

The significance of the paper is also high, as it addresses a critical problem in object detection and provides a solution that can be used in a variety of applications. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their method on a challenging dataset and provide insights into the strengths and weaknesses of their approach.

Overall, the paper makes a valuable contribution to the field of object detection, and with some revisions to address the concerns mentioned above, it has the potential to be even stronger.
