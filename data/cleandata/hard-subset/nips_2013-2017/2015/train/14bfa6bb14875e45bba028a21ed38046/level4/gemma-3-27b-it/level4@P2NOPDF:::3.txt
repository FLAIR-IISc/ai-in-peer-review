This work presents a two-stage object detection algorithm built upon convolutional neural networks (CNNs). The first stage generates region proposals using a region proposal network (RPN) that employs a sliding window approach, but operates on the high-level feature maps of a CNN. The second stage refines these proposals using a fast R-CNN architecture. By sharing convolutional layers between the RPN and the R-CNN, and leveraging GPU acceleration, the authors achieve near real-time performance (5fps). This represents a natural progression of existing CNN-based object detection methods.

However, I have several concerns regarding the presented approach. 

First, the optimization procedure described in the paper appears to utilize only a single iteration of alternating optimization between the RPN and the R-CNN. If this is indeed the case, the method effectively assumes that the proposals generated by the initial RPN iteration are optimal. This is a strong, and likely inaccurate, assumption, as the RPN requires multiple iterations to converge and produce high-quality proposals. The impact of this single-iteration optimization on the overall performance should be discussed.

Second, the application of a pixel-wise sliding window to the top layer of the CNN raises a question about the effective step size on the input image. Due to the repeated max-pooling operations within the CNN, the top-level feature map is significantly downsampled compared to the original image. Consequently, a sliding window with a small step size on the feature map corresponds to a much larger step size on the input image. This large step size could lead to inaccuracies in bounding box localization. Clarification regarding the step size and its implications for localization accuracy is needed.

Finally, the definition of $h_a$ in lines 173 and 174 is unclear and requires further explanation. Providing a precise definition would significantly improve the readability of the paper.
