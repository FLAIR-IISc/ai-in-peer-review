

The paper presents a novel region proposal method (RPN) that shares CNN features with the object localization module, resulting in a ~10x speedup over Fast R-CNN while maintaining accuracy. The core contribution is well-motivated and the experimental results are robust, demonstrating clear advantages in efficiency without significant accuracy loss. The proposed staged training procedure to address the dependency between region proposals and object localization is a practical solution to a known challenge in two-stage detectors.

However, the paper suffers from poor flow and insufficient technical justification in critical sections, hindering reproducibility and confidence in the design choices. The following points require clarification:

1.  **Translation Invariance Clarification:** The paper states RPN is translation-invariant while citing [19] as not being so. This claim needs rigorous justification. Specifically, RPN's shared feature maps and sliding window approach inherently produce translation-invariant feature responses, but [19] (likely referring to OverFeat) uses a different architecture (e.g., fully convolutional with stride) that *is* translation-invariant in its feature extraction. The confusion arises because OverFeat *does* use translation-invariant features for classification, but its region proposal mechanism (e.g., sliding window with fixed aspect ratios) is *not* translation-invariant in the *proposal generation* step. The paper should explicitly clarify the distinction between feature translation invariance and proposal mechanism translation invariance, and directly address OverFeat's approach to resolve this ambiguity.

2.  **Lack of Justification for Hyperparameters:** Several key hyperparameters are set manually without analysis. The choice of `n=3` (number of anchor scales) and `k=9` (number of anchor aspect ratios) is pivotal to the RPN's performance but lacks justification. Was this chosen empirically through ablation? Were other combinations (e.g., `n=2, k=6` or `n=4, k=12`) evaluated? The absence of this analysis makes it difficult to assess whether these choices are optimal or arbitrary, and whether the results are sensitive to them.

3.  **Training Procedure Complexity and Stability:** The staged training procedure (first train RPN independently, then fine-tune the full network) is described as necessary but feels ad-hoc. The paper states the procedure "seems complicated and not stable" – this needs elaboration. Why is the procedure unstable? Were multiple training runs with the same setup attempted? If so, what was the variance in results? If not, was stability assessed via repeated experiments? Furthermore, the claim that "the authors have tried many other settings but found the current one performs best" lacks evidence. A brief ablation study comparing the staged procedure to end-to-end training (or alternative staging strategies) would significantly strengthen the argument and address the stability concern.

The core idea of feature sharing between proposal and detection is strong and well-executed in the results. The speedup over Fast R-CNN is a significant practical contribution. However, the lack of clarity on the translation-invariance distinction, the unexplained hyperparameters, and the unresolved questions about training stability undermine the paper's technical rigor. These issues are not merely stylistic; they impact the reader's ability to understand *why* the method works as it does and whether the results are generalizable.

To improve the paper, the authors should:
*   Explicitly clarify the translation-invariance claim, contrasting RPN's proposal mechanism with [19] and OverFeat's approach.
*   Provide a brief ablation study on the anchor configurations (`n`, `k`), demonstrating sensitivity or justification.
*   Include results showing the stability of the staged training procedure (e.g., variance across multiple runs) or a direct comparison to alternative training strategies.

Addressing these points would transform a technically sound contribution into a significantly stronger, more transparent, and reproducible work. The results themselves are compelling, but the presentation currently leaves important questions unanswered for the reader. The paper has strong potential but requires these clarifications to meet the standards expected at NIPS.
