Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes Faster R-CNN, a two-stage detection framework that unifies region proposal generation and object detection within a single convolutional network by sharing features. The key contribution is the Region Proposal Network (RPN), a fully convolutional subnetwork that slides over shared feature maps and predicts, at each location, objectness scores and regressed bounding boxes relative to a set of translation-invariant “anchors” spanning multiple scales and aspect ratios. Proposals from the RPN feed into a Fast R-CNN detector that uses ROI pooling on the same shared feature maps. The authors train the two components via a simple four-step alternating scheme that yields a unified model with shared convolutional layers.

Compared to prior work, Faster R-CNN removes the CPU-bound region proposal bottleneck present in R-CNN, SPPnet, and Fast R-CNN, replacing Selective Search or EdgeBoxes with a learned, GPU-accelerated proposal mechanism. Relative to OverFeat (single-stage, class-specific detection) and MultiBox (learned, class-agnostic proposals), RPNs differ by using translation-invariant anchors, a compact output parameterization, and deep feature sharing with the downstream detector. Empirical results on PASCAL VOC 2007/2012 show state-of-the-art mAP with substantial speedups (≈5 fps with VGG-16, proposals in ≈10 ms).

Strengths
- Clear and elegant unification of proposals and detection via shared conv features; the marginal cost for proposals is effectively negligible.
- Strong empirical results on VOC07/12 with both ZF and VGG-16 backbones; competitive or better mAP than Selective Search or EdgeBoxes with far fewer proposals (≈300).
- Thorough ablations isolating (i) shared vs. unshared features, (ii) the contribution of cls vs. reg heads, (iii) number of proposals and NMS behavior, and (iv) one-stage vs. two-stage detection.
- The anchor-based, translation-invariant design is simple, parameter-efficient, and effective; fewer parameters than MultiBox reduce overfitting risk on small datasets.
- Implementation details and training recipe are provided; code availability further supports reproducibility.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Alternating optimization: The four-step training uses only one alternation (RPN→detector→RPN heads→detector heads), then stops. It would be useful to report whether additional alternations change proposal quality or final mAP, or to provide evidence that performance saturates after one round. While joint end-to-end training is flagged as future work, a brief experiment with 2–3 alternations would strengthen the claim that the simple scheme is sufficient.
- Stride and localization accuracy: The RPN operates on feature maps with a total stride of 16 pixels (on images resized to a shorter side of 600). Although the paper notes good accuracy with this stride, localization errors for small objects may be sensitive to this step size. Please report localization error analysis (e.g., AP_S or error breakdowns) or compare to a reduced stride (e.g., using dilated convolutions) to quantify any trade-offs. Even a brief study would help justify the chosen stride.
- Notational clarity: In the bounding-box parameterization, wa and ha should be explicitly defined as the anchor width and height. While this is standard, making it explicit will remove ambiguity.
- Dataset scope: Results are limited to VOC07/12. Given the claimed speed and proposal quality, an evaluation on a larger, more diverse dataset (e.g., MS COCO) would better test generalization and small-object performance. At minimum, a discussion of expected behavior and limitations on small, crowded objects would be helpful.
- Hyperparameter sensitivity: Anchor scales/aspect ratios, NMS IoU (0.7), λ in the loss, and normalization choices can influence both recall and final mAP. The paper gives defaults (and a footnote for λ), but a brief sensitivity analysis would improve clarity and robustness claims.

Quality
The paper is technically sound and carefully evaluated. The ablations convincingly support the main claims: sharing features improves mAP and speed; the cls head ranks proposals effectively; and regression is critical for high-quality localization. Training details are sufficiently described, and the released code further supports reproducibility. The main missing piece is an exploration of additional alternations or joint training.

Clarity
Overall, the paper is well organized and clearly written. Suggestions:
- Define wa, ha when introducing the parameterization; restate that xa, ya are anchor centers.
- Explicitly state the effective stride on the input image and the corresponding step in pixels after resizing; consolidate these details earlier in the RPN section rather than later in Implementation Details.
- Summarize the normalization constants (Ncls, Nreg) and λ in the main text rather than relying on a footnote.

Originality
The idea of learning class-agnostic proposals with deep networks is not new (e.g., MultiBox), and sliding-window detection on shared conv features has precedent (OverFeat). The novelty here lies in the anchor-based, translation-invariant formulation coupled with feature sharing and a simple training scheme that yields near cost-free proposals in a unified detector. This combination is original and impactful.

Significance
High. Removing the proposal bottleneck while improving accuracy materially advances the practicality of deep object detection. The approach is likely to be widely adopted and extended.

Specific questions for the authors
- Please clarify whether additional alternation rounds were tried and their effect on proposal recall and final mAP.
- Could you report localization error analyses or AP by object size to assess the impact of stride 16, and comment on potential remedies for small objects?
- Please explicitly define ha (and wa) as the anchor height (and width) where the parameterization is introduced.
- How sensitive is performance to the chosen anchor scales/ratios and to the NMS IoU threshold? A brief sensitivity table or plot would be useful.

Pros for acceptance
- Strong technical contribution with elegant design and broad applicability.
- Compelling speed/accuracy improvements over widely used baselines.
- Thorough ablations, clear writing, and released code.

Cons for acceptance
- Limited dataset scope; no large-scale evaluation.
- Training scheme explored only with a single alternation; no joint end-to-end results.
- Minor clarity issues around notation and hyperparameter reporting.

Recommendation
Accept (strong). The paper offers a substantial, well-supported advance that removes a key bottleneck in detection and is likely to influence subsequent research and practice. Addressing the minor clarity issues and providing a short study on additional alternations or stride sensitivity would further strengthen an already strong submission.
