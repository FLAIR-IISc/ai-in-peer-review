This paper presents a compelling approach to object detection, building upon recent advances in CNN-based methods like Fast R-CNN. The core idea – employing a Region Proposal Network (RPN) that shares convolutional features with the detection network – is a natural and effective way to address the bottleneck of region proposal generation. The paper clearly articulates the problem, proposes a novel solution, and demonstrates strong results on PASCAL VOC benchmarks, achieving state-of-the-art accuracy with a significant speed improvement (5fps on VGG-16). The availability of code further enhances the reproducibility and impact of this work.

My primary concerns relate to the details of the training procedure and potential limitations in spatial resolution. Specifically, the description of the alternating optimization between RPN and Fast R-CNN could be clarified. The paper mentions a single iteration of this process, which raises the question of whether the initial RPN proposals are truly optimal, and if further refinement through multiple iterations might yield improvements. It would be valuable to discuss the sensitivity of the performance to this optimization schedule. 

Additionally, the application of a sliding window on the top-most convolutional layer introduces a potential issue with step size at the image level. Given the substantial downsampling caused by multiple max-pooling layers in deep networks (like VGG-16), the step size of the sliding window on the original image could be quite large. This could lead to inaccuracies in bounding box localization and potentially miss smaller or more densely packed objects. A discussion of this effect, and perhaps an analysis of receptive field sizes, would strengthen the paper.

Finally, the definition of $h_a$ in lines 173 and 174 requires clarification. While likely a typographical error, resolving this ambiguity is important for a clear understanding of the bounding box regression formulation. 

In summary, this paper addresses a critical challenge in object detection – the computational cost of region proposal – with an elegant and effective solution. The RPN approach is well-motivated, thoroughly evaluated, and represents a significant contribution to the field. 

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** The RPN is a novel and effective way to generate region proposals, moving beyond traditional methods like Selective Search.
*   **Significance:** The proposed approach achieves state-of-the-art accuracy with a substantial speedup, making real-time object detection more feasible.
*   **Technical Soundness:** The paper presents a clear and well-supported technical approach, with comprehensive experimental results on standard benchmarks.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the proposed method.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Optimization Details:** The single-iteration alternating optimization requires further justification or investigation of more sophisticated schedules.
*   **Spatial Resolution:** The potential impact of large step sizes due to downsampling in deep networks should be discussed.
*   **Minor Typo:** Clarification of the variable $h_a$ is needed.
