This paper introduces a Region Proposal Network (RPN) to address the computational bottleneck of region proposal generation in modern object detection systems like Fast R-CNN. The core idea is to leverage the shared convolutional features of the detection network to perform region proposal in a nearly cost-free manner. The RPN is a fully-convolutional network that predicts object bounds and objectness scores, trained end-to-end with Fast R-CNN using an alternating optimization approach. The authors demonstrate significant speed improvements – achieving 5fps on VGG-16 – alongside state-of-the-art accuracy on PASCAL VOC 2007 and 2012.

This work builds directly on recent advances in object detection, particularly SPPnet [7] and Fast R-CNN [5], which reduced the cost of detection *given* region proposals. This paper logically extends this trend by tackling the proposal generation step itself. The approach also relates to earlier work on fully-convolutional networks for localization, such as OverFeat [18] and MultiBox [3, 20], but differentiates itself by focusing on a cascaded approach – generating proposals with a network that shares features with the detection network, rather than performing end-to-end detection directly from sliding windows. The concept of shared computation is also explored in [18, 2, 5], but this work specifically targets the region proposal stage in a novel way.

**Strengths:**

*   **Significant Speed Improvement:** The reported 5fps frame rate on VGG-16 is a substantial advancement, bringing real-time object detection closer to practical application.
*   **Strong Accuracy:** The reported mAP scores on PASCAL VOC are competitive with, and often exceed, existing methods.
*   **Novelty:** The RPN is a clever and effective approach to address a key bottleneck in object detection. Sharing convolutional features is a well-motivated idea, and its implementation here is compelling.
*   **Clear Problem Statement:** The paper clearly articulates the limitations of existing region proposal methods and positions the RPN as a direct solution.
*   **Availability of Code:**  The publicly available code ([https://github.com/ShaoqingRen/faster_rcnn](https://github.com/ShaoqingRen/faster_rcnn)) is a significant contribution to the research community.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Lack of Justification for Hyperparameter Choices:** Several key parameters, such as *n*=3 and *k*=9, are set without sufficient justification or ablation study. The sensitivity of performance to these choices remains unclear. A more thorough analysis of these settings would strengthen the paper.
*   **Complexity of Training Procedure:** The alternating training scheme appears complex and potentially unstable. While the authors report success with this approach, a more detailed discussion of its robustness and sensitivity to initialization would be valuable. Were other training procedures attempted? What results were obtained?
*   **Translation Invariance Clarification Needed:** The statement regarding translation invariance and its contrast with [19] (and OverFeat) is unclear. A more detailed explanation of why the proposed method is considered translation invariant, and a comparison of this property with other approaches, would improve understanding.
*   **Limited Discussion of Failure Cases:** The paper primarily focuses on overall performance. Providing examples of common failure cases and discussing potential reasons for them would offer valuable insight.




**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and effective solution to a significant problem in object detection.
*   The experimental results are strong and demonstrate a clear improvement over existing methods.
*   The availability of code will facilitate further research in this area.
*   The work has the potential to significantly impact the field of object detection by enabling faster and more accurate systems.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

*   The lack of justification for hyperparameter choices and the complexity of the training procedure raise concerns about the robustness and generalizability of the approach.
*   The unclear explanation of translation invariance hinders understanding of the method’s core principles.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that presents a significant contribution to the field of object detection. While there are some weaknesses related to hyperparameter choices and training procedure clarity, the substantial improvements in speed and accuracy outweigh these concerns. I believe this paper is worthy of acceptance to NIPS 2015, contingent upon the authors addressing the points raised above, particularly providing further justification for key parameter settings.
