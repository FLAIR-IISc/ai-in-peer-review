Summary and relation to prior work
This paper introduces Region Proposal Networks (RPNs), fully convolutional modules that share feature maps with a Fast R-CNN detector to generate class-agnostic proposals at near-zero marginal cost. At each spatial position, a small network with a 3×3 conv (equivalently, an n×n receptive field) followed by two sibling layers predicts objectness and box regressions for k translation-invariant “anchor” boxes spanning multiple scales and aspect ratios. RPNs are trained end-to-end with a multitask loss and combined with Fast R-CNN via an alternating (4-step) optimization that yields shared convolutional layers.

Relative to prior work: R-CNN/SPPnet/Fast R-CNN reduced per-proposal cost by sharing conv features but still relied on external CPU proposal methods (Selective Search, EdgeBoxes). OverFeat used one-stage, class-specific sliding-window detection; MultiBox predicted multiple boxes from fixed, non-translation-invariant anchors. The proposed RPN differs by (i) using translation-invariant anchors of multiple scales/aspect ratios at every location, (ii) sharing conv features with the detector, and (iii) training with a simple alternating scheme to produce a unified, accurate, and fast two-stage detector. On PASCAL VOC 2007/2012, Faster R-CNN achieves state-of-the-art mAP while running at 5 fps with VGG-16 and producing high-quality proposals using only ~300 regions per image.

Strengths
- Technical contribution: Elegant, simple architecture for class-agnostic proposals integrated with a detector; anchor mechanism yields scale/aspect-ratio coverage without image pyramids.
- Efficiency: Proposals are nearly free by sharing conv layers; reported 10 ms for RPN with VGG-16 and 198 ms end-to-end detection (including proposals) versus 1–2 s for Selective Search.
- Accuracy: State-of-the-art on VOC07 (73.2% mAP) and VOC12 (70.4% mAP) with 300 proposals; strong ablations showing proposal count effects, roles of classification vs. regression heads, and feature sharing benefits.
- Thorough experimental evaluation: VOC07/12, ZF vs. VGG backbones, shared vs. unshared features, NMS effects, recall-to-IoU curves, and runtime breakdowns; clear comparisons to SS/EdgeBoxes and discussion of OverFeat/MultiBox.
- Clarity and reproducibility: Well-written, with explicit loss, sampling, anchor settings, and optimization details; code availability.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Training scheme: The joint RPN+detector training uses an alternating heuristic; there is no convergence analysis or guarantee. While practical and empirically stable, a principled joint optimization (or proof of convergence) is missing.
- Proposal quality in isolation: When used standalone (with a detector trained on SS), RPN proposals do not clearly dominate Selective Search; mAP can be similar or slightly worse unless features are shared or the detector is retrained on RPN proposals. This complicates claims of RPN superiority as a drop-in replacement.
- Sensitivity/heuristics: Performance depends on anchor design (scales/aspect ratios), NMS threshold, and sampling ratios. The paper gives one reasonable setting but limited sensitivity analysis.
- Scope of datasets: Results are confined to VOC; generalization to more diverse datasets (e.g., dense small objects) is not extensively evaluated here.

Quality
- Soundness: The model, loss, and training procedure are technically sound, with extensive empirical support. Claims about speed/accuracy and the benefit of feature sharing are well substantiated. The lack of theoretical backing for alternating optimization is the main methodological gap.
- Experimental care: Ablations are thoughtful and illuminate design choices (cls vs. reg, NMS, number of proposals). Comparisons to SS/EB are fair and include runtime. One caveat is that SS/EB run on CPU while the proposed method capitalizes on GPU; the authors note this but the practical takeaway remains compelling.
- Limitations: The paper acknowledges uncertainty about joint training convergence and leaves it as future work.

Clarity
- Writing and organization are excellent. The architecture, anchors, loss, and training pipeline are clearly specified. Figures/tables support the narrative. Minor suggestions: add a brief sensitivity study for anchor scales/ratios and clarify memory usage limits and batch sizes for different backbones.

Originality
- Novel integration of proposal generation with detection via shared conv features and translation-invariant anchors. Related to, but distinct from, MultiBox and OverFeat through anchor design, class-agnostic two-stage pipeline, and feature sharing. The alternating training scheme is pragmatic and new in this context.

Significance
- High impact. The approach removes the region proposal bottleneck while improving accuracy, making two-stage detection practical and likely to be widely adopted. The ideas (anchors, shared features) are foundational and will influence subsequent detection research and systems.

Questions and suggestions for authors
- Can you provide any empirical evidence on stability of the alternating optimization (e.g., sensitivity to initialization, number of alternations)?
- How sensitive is performance to the chosen anchor scales/aspect ratios? Any guidance for new datasets with different object size distributions?
- Would end-to-end joint training without freezing shared layers be stable if proposals are updated with a lag (e.g., EM-like scheme)?
- Any observations on small-object recall and the effect of stride or dilations?

Pros for acceptance
- Strong, simple idea with compelling accuracy and speed.
- Thorough experiments and ablations; code available.
- Clear advancement over SS/EB baselines; state-of-the-art mAP on VOC07/12.
- Well-written and easy to reproduce.

Cons for acceptance
- Alternating optimization is heuristic; no convergence theory.
- RPN as a standalone proposal method does not unambiguously outperform Selective Search without re-training the detector/feature-sharing.
- Limited analysis of sensitivity to anchor/NMS hyperparameters and results beyond VOC.

Recommendation
- Accept (strong). This paper offers a substantial, practical advance in object detection by unifying proposals and detection through shared convolutional features, achieving both higher accuracy and much better efficiency. The main concern—heuristic joint training—does not outweigh the demonstrated empirical benefits. Confidence: high.
