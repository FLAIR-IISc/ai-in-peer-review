Summary and relation to prior work:
This paper proposes Region Proposal Networks (RPNs), a fully-convolutional module that predicts objectness scores and class-agnostic bounding boxes at every spatial location of a shared convolutional feature map. By sharing the backbone conv layers with a Fast R-CNN detector, proposal computation becomes nearly “free,” enabling an end-to-end two-stage detector that runs at 5–17 fps depending on the backbone, while improving accuracy over strong baselines on PASCAL VOC 2007/2012. The method uses a small 3×3 conv (“mini-network”) on top of the shared feature map to produce per-location predictions for k “anchors” (multiple scales and aspect ratios; k=9). A multi-task loss supervises objectness and box regression. Training proceeds via an alternating 4-step schedule so that RPN and Fast R-CNN share conv layers. With VGG-16, the system reaches 73.2% mAP on VOC07 test (trained on 07+12) using only 300 proposals, and ~10 ms marginal time for proposals.

The work builds on region-based CNNs (R-CNN [6], SPPnet [7], Fast R-CNN [5]) by removing the external CPU-bound proposal bottleneck (Selective Search [22], EdgeBoxes [24]). It contrasts with one-stage dense detectors based on sliding windows/regressors (OverFeat [18]) and prior learned proposal networks (MultiBox [3,20]) by introducing translation-invariant anchors tied to feature-map locations and efficient feature sharing with the detector. It also connects to FCNs [14] and uses standard bounding-box regression [6] with a smooth L1 loss [5].

Strengths:
- Clear, practical goal: eliminate the region proposal bottleneck by learning proposals that share features with the detector.
- Strong empirical results with careful ablations: competitive or better mAP than SS/EB on VOC at much higher speed; robust performance with as few as 300 proposals; analyses of the roles of cls vs reg heads and NMS.
- Conceptual simplicity and broad applicability: the anchor formulation, fully-convolutional prediction, and feature sharing are elegant and efficient.
- Public code and use of standard backbones/datasets improve reproducibility and impact.

Weaknesses and points needing clarification:
- Clarity/organization: Several sections are hard to follow, especially the training scheme and the discussion comparing RPNs to prior learned proposal methods. The paper would benefit from a more structured presentation of the 4-step alternating optimization, with a schematic timeline and precise parameter-sharing at each step.
- Translation invariance discussion: The claim that RPN anchors/functions are translation invariant while prior work is not should be clarified and properly attributed. MultiBox [20] uses a fixed set of k-means boxes not tied to spatial locations; OverFeat [18] is a sliding-window method and thus translation-equivariant by design. Please tighten the argument, cite the correct references, and, if possible, provide a small experiment illustrating the claimed advantage (parameter count vs generalization).
- Hyperparameters appear hand-set without justification: n=3 receptive field, k=9 anchors (3 scales × 3 aspect ratios), IoU thresholds (0.7 positive, 0.3 negative), NMS IoU=0.7, mini-batch size/composition, λ=10, learning-rate schedule, stride choices. Sensitivity analyses (e.g., alternative anchor sets, different IoU thresholds, n=1/5, k values) are not reported.
- Training complexity and stability: The need for a multi-stage alternating scheme suggests potential instability for joint training, yet there is no empirical evidence (e.g., failures of naive joint training, convergence plots, or variance across runs). The paper does not report repeated runs or error bars; it is unclear how sensitive final mAP is to random initialization or sampling.
- Scope of evaluation: While VOC is standard, it would help to include more challenging settings (e.g., deeper ablations on small objects vs large, different strides, or cross-dataset generalization). The recall-to-IoU analysis is useful but remains tangential to end detection; connecting it more directly to mAP (e.g., oracle analyses) would strengthen the diagnostic claims.

Quality:
The method is technically sound and well-motivated. The architecture and loss are standard, and the experimental section is extensive for VOC, including relevant ablations (shared vs unshared features, cls/reg/NMS roles, varying numbers of proposals, OverFeat-like one-stage baseline). However, several key design choices are not justified, and the training procedure appears more complex than necessary without evidence that simpler joint training fails. Reporting variance across multiple runs and sensitivity to anchor design would improve rigor.

Clarity:
The high-level ideas are clear, but the manuscript is not easy to follow in parts. Specific suggestions:
- Provide a concise algorithm box for the 4-step training, with explicit which layers are frozen/updated.
- Clarify the translation-invariance argument and correct the reference mapping (MultiBox vs OverFeat).
- Collect hyperparameters and defaults in one place and briefly justify each.
- Explain the “nearly cost-free” claim with a detailed time breakdown, and clarify the baseline (Fast R-CNN with vs without proposal time).

Originality:
The work introduces a notably novel combination: translation-invariant anchors predicted densely by a shared FCN and trained end-to-end, tightly integrated with a region-based detector via feature sharing and alternating training. While related ideas exist (OverFeat, MultiBox, SPPnet/Fast R-CNN), the proposed anchor-based RPN and the way it is integrated with detection represent a substantive advance over prior art.

Significance:
High. Eliminating the CPU proposal bottleneck while improving accuracy is important both scientifically and practically. The approach is likely to be adopted and extended by practitioners and researchers. The speed-accuracy trade-off achieved (few proposals, deep backbone, end-to-end training) advances the state of the art on VOC and provides a foundation for future detectors.

Pros for acceptance:
- Strong results with a clear, widely impactful idea and open-source code.
- Elegant, simple mechanism (anchors + FCN) with compelling speedups and accuracy gains.
- Careful ablations demonstrating where performance comes from.

Cons against acceptance:
- Insufficient justification/sensitivity analysis for key hyperparameters.
- Training scheme feels ad hoc; lack of evidence about stability and variance.
- Some confusing comparisons and clarity issues (translation invariance, reference mapping).

Questions for the authors:
1) Can you report variance across 3–5 runs (different seeds) for the main VGG results and ablations?
2) Did you attempt end-to-end joint training of RPN + Fast R-CNN with proposals updated online? If so, what failed? If not, can you elaborate on why the alternating scheme is preferred?
3) How sensitive are results to anchor designs (scales, aspect ratios, k), n in the mini-network, and IoU thresholds for positives/negatives and NMS?
4) Could you clarify the translation-invariance argument with respect to MultiBox [20] vs OverFeat [18], and provide parameter counts and overfitting analysis supporting your claim?

Minor comments:
- Please consolidate all hyperparameters (k, n, thresholds, sampling ratios, λ) in a table.
- Consider adding a figure illustrating anchor tiling, receptive fields, and the 3×3 mini-network.
- Some reference indices in the discussion appear mismatched; double-check citations when contrasting with prior work.

Overall recommendation:
Accept. Despite clarity and analysis gaps, the paper presents a substantial, well-executed advance in object detection with clear empirical benefits and likely high impact. Addressing the clarity and stability concerns in the final version would further strengthen the contribution.
