- Introduces a Region Proposal Network (RPN) that generates object proposals directly from shared convolutional feature maps, eliminating slow external proposal algorithms.  
- RPN slides a small network over the last conv layer, predicting objectness and regressing box coordinates for multiple anchor scales/aspect ratios.  
- Sharing convolutional features with Fast R-CNN makes proposal generation nearly free at test time (~10ms), enabling Faster R-CNN to run near real-time.  
- Proposes a pragmatic 4-step alternating training scheme to learn shared features between RPN and Fast R-CNN.  
- Experiments on PASCAL VOC 2007/2012 show drastic speedups and improved accuracy (73.2% mAP on VOC 2007) over Selective Search + Fast R-CNN.  
- Ablation studies convincingly justify design choices: shared conv layers, joint classification/regression, and the two-stage approach.  
- Paper is technically sound, well-executed, and supported by extensive experiments and ablations.  
- Writing and organization are clear, with helpful illustrations and sufficient detail for reproducibility; authors promise to release code.  
- Minor clarity issue: bounding-box regression variable definitions could be made clearer (anchor geometric variable notation).  
- Highly original: fully integrated, class-agnostic, anchor-based proposal network sharing expensive layers is novel and elegant.  
- Translation-invariant anchors are a clever design improvement over prior localization methods like MultiBox.  
- Extremely significant: RPN addresses a key bottleneck, enabling fast, accurate detection and influencing many follow-up works (e.g., Mask R-CNN).  
- Question about alternating training: it feels like staged initialization rather than true joint optimization and it's unclear if more cycles of steps 3/4 would help.  
- Concern about feature map stride: operating on a coarse stride (16 px for VGG) may limit localization precision and small-object detection.  
- Arguments for acceptance: novel solution, state-of-the-art speed/accuracy, foundational impact, strong experiments and clarity.  
- Arguments against acceptance: multi-step heuristic training instead of unified end-to-end optimization and unexamined limitations from coarse feature stride.  
- Overall conclusion: excellent, impactful work; strongly recommended for acceptance.
