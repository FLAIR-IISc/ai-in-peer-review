Summary
This paper proposes Faster R-CNN, a two-stage object detection framework that unifies region proposal generation and object detection by sharing convolutional features. The key contribution is the Region Proposal Network (RPN), a small fully convolutional subnetwork that slides over the shared conv feature map to predict, at each spatial position, k anchor-relative bounding boxes and objectness scores. Anchors of multiple scales and aspect ratios (k=9) enable translation-invariant proposal prediction. The RPN is trained end-to-end with a multitask loss (objectness classification + smooth L1 bounding-box regression), using an image-centric minibatch strategy with balanced positive/negative anchors. To share features with the detector, the authors propose a simple 4-step alternating training procedure with Fast R-CNN: train RPN, train detector on RPN proposals, fine-tune RPN heads while freezing shared conv layers from the detector, then fine-tune detector heads while freezing shared conv layers. Using VGG-16 and 300 proposals per image, the system achieves 73.2% mAP on VOC 2007 and 70.4% mAP on VOC 2012, while running at ~5 fps end-to-end on a GPU; the marginal proposal time is ~10 ms.

Relation to prior work
- Builds directly on R-CNN/SPPnet/Fast R-CNN by exploiting shared conv features for region-wise classification and regression; addresses the proposal bottleneck exposed by Fast R-CNN.
- Contrasts with hand-engineered CPU proposal methods (Selective Search, EdgeBoxes), offering much faster, learned proposals integrated with the detector.
- Compared to OverFeat’s one-stage sliding-window detection and MultiBox’s global proposal prediction, RPN introduces translation-invariant anchors with far fewer parameters and couples class-agnostic proposal generation with a second-stage class-specific detector.
- Related to fully convolutional networks (Long et al.) via its sliding-window conv heads and end-to-end training on dense spatial predictions.

Strengths
- Clear and impactful algorithmic idea: anchors + shared conv features delivers high-quality proposals at negligible extra cost.
- Strong empirical results on VOC with VGG-16, including ablations (shared vs unshared features; role of cls vs reg; proposal count; NMS effects).
- Elegant engineering: simple conv heads (3×3 then 1×1), translation-invariant anchors, and pragmatic alternating optimization.
- Public code and training details enable reproduction; careful comparisons to SS/EB and to one-stage baselines.
- Significance: removes the proposal bottleneck, yielding a practical, accurate, near real-time detector.

Weaknesses and questions
- Alternating optimization: As written, only a single alternation is performed. It would be useful to report whether additional alternations change accuracy or converge to a different solution, and whether a joint end-to-end optimization of the unified graph (with proposals treated as latent) is stable and beneficial.
- Stride and small objects: Sliding the RPN heads over the top conv map with stride 16 pixels (VGG/ZF) may be coarse for small objects. While bbox regression and multiple anchors help, please quantify AP for small objects and report results with reduced effective stride (e.g., using a higher-resolution feature map, atrous/dilated conv, or using conv4 instead of conv5) and its speed/accuracy trade-off.
- Notation clarity: In the bounding box parameterization, please explicitly define ha (and wa) as the height (and width) of the anchor box in pixels (or specify units), and clarify that (x, y) are center coordinates. Also clarify whether the parameterization is computed in image coordinates or normalized by image scale after resizing to s=600.
- Hyperparameter normalization: The paper mentions Ncls, Nreg, and λ in the loss and a footnote giving specific normalizations. For reproducibility, move the final choices to the main text and state whether performance is sensitive to λ or to the IoU thresholds (0.7 positive, 0.3 negative).
- Proposal-head capacity: Since the k regressors do not share weights, consider reporting sensitivity to the choice of scales/ratios and to k; an ablation on anchor sets would be helpful.
- Runtime reporting: Please specify the GPU model and batch size for the 5 fps figure, and provide a clear breakdown for conv, RPN heads, ROI pooling, and fc layers (with/without SVD).

Quality
- Sound methodology with well-motivated design choices (anchors, multitask loss, shared features). Experiments are thorough for VOC, with meaningful ablations disentangling proposal quality from detector training. Some aspects (convergence of alternating optimization; small-object analysis; sensitivity to anchor design) could be expanded but do not undermine the main claims.

Clarity
- Generally well-written and well-organized; figures help. Minor notation issues (e.g., explicit definitions of xa, ya, wa, ha; units and coordinate conventions) should be clarified. Training schedule and normalizations could be consolidated in the main text for reproducibility.

Originality
- Novel integration of a class-agnostic proposal FCN with a detector via shared features and anchors; distinct from MultiBox and OverFeat in translation invariance, parameter efficiency, and two-stage design. The alternating training scheme is a practical contribution.

Significance
- High. Eliminates the proposal bottleneck and advances both accuracy and speed. The approach is likely to be widely adopted and extended (different backbones, datasets, and tasks like segmentation/instance detection).

Suggestions for improvement
- Report results with additional alternations and, if possible, preliminary joint training.
- Analyze performance by object size; try smaller stride/dilated conv.
- Add ablations on anchor configurations and IoU thresholds.
- Clarify notation and loss normalization; move footnote hyperparameters to the main text.
- Provide detailed runtime breakdown and hardware specs.

Pros for acceptance
- Strong, simple idea with compelling empirical validation.
- Significant speedup with improved or matched accuracy versus SS/EB.
- Clear path to practical deployment; public code.
- Careful comparisons and ablations; advances state of the art on VOC.

Cons for acceptance
- Limited analysis of convergence and training dynamics of shared-feature learning.
- Potential sensitivity to feature stride not fully explored.
- Minor clarity gaps in notation and hyperparameter specification.

Questions for the authors
- Does more than one alternation improve results, or do you observe convergence after one cycle?
- Have you tried end-to-end joint optimization of the unified graph? If so, how stable is training and what are the gains/losses?
- What is the AP breakdown by object size, and how does reducing the effective stride affect speed/accuracy?
- How sensitive are results to the choice of anchor scales/ratios and to the positive/negative IoU thresholds?
- Please clarify the definitions and units of ha/wa and coordinate conventions in the parameterization equations.

Overall evaluation: Strong accept. Confidence: High.
