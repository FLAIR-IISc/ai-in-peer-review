Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes Faster R-CNN, a two-stage object detector that learns class-agnostic region proposals with a Region Proposal Network (RPN) and then classifies/refines them with Fast R-CNN, while sharing the backbone convolutional layers. The RPN is a fully convolutional head applied on the top-level feature map; at each spatial location it predicts k proposals relative to translation-invariant “anchors” spanning multiple scales and aspect ratios, along with objectness scores. The detector achieves near real-time throughput (about 5 fps with VGG-16), and state-of-the-art mAP on PASCAL VOC 2007/2012 with only ~300 proposals per image. Training uses a pragmatic 4-step alternating schedule to produce shared features without full joint optimization.

Relative to prior work: it builds directly on SPPnet and Fast R-CNN [7,5] to share features, replaces hand-engineered CPU proposal methods like Selective Search [22] and EdgeBoxes [24], and contrasts with one-stage, dense sliding-window CNNs such as OverFeat [18]. Compared to MultiBox [20], it uses translation-invariant anchors (few parameters) instead of global k-means templates (many parameters), reducing overfitting risk and improving efficiency. The method also relates to FCNs [14] through its dense, fully convolutional prediction.

Strengths
- Strong idea-to-practice alignment: by moving proposal generation into the CNN and sharing features, the proposal bottleneck is nearly eliminated with minimal additional compute (~10 ms).
- Elegant, simple RPN design with anchors that cover multiple scales/aspect ratios; good ablations isolating the roles of classification, regression, and sharing.
- Solid empirical results: competitive or superior mAP to SS/EB while using an order of magnitude fewer proposals; thorough recall-to-IoU analysis; clear speed accounting.
- Practical training recipe that converges quickly; public code.

Weaknesses and questions
- Alternating optimization: The 4-step schedule appears to perform essentially one alternation (RPN → detector → RPN heads with conv fixed → detector fc with conv fixed). This implicitly assumes the step-1 RPN proposals and step-2 detector features are close to a fixed point. Please provide evidence that additional alternations do not improve results (e.g., 2–3 cycles with shared convs unfrozen), or a brief experiment showing joint optimization is stable/unstable.
- Coarse top-layer sliding windows: With VGG-16 the effective stride is 16 pixels on the resized image. While regression alleviates discretization, anchor centers remain on a coarse grid, which can hurt small-object localization and high-IoU recall. The paper briefly notes this; it would help to:
  - Report mAP (or recall) broken down by object size and IoU ≥ 0.75, comparing SS/EB vs RPN.
  - Provide an ablation with smaller effective stride (e.g., dilated/à trous conv to get stride 8), denser/shifted anchors, or lightweight image pyramids to quantify the trade-off.
- Clarification of notation: In the bounding-box parameterization (lines 173–174), please explicitly define ha as the anchor box height and wa as the anchor box width. Although inferable, stating this near Eqn. (1) would avoid confusion.
- Runtime comparisons: SS/EB times are CPU-only whereas the detector uses a GPU. The discussion acknowledges this, but a brief GPU-ported EB baseline or a table clearly separating CPU/GPU components would make the speed claim more balanced.

Quality
The method is technically sound and well supported by experiments on VOC 2007/2012. The ablations convincingly argue for the benefit of sharing features and for the roles of cls/reg heads and NMS. The main methodological limitation is the limited exploration of optimization schemes: only one alternation is reported. Additional evidence on convergence and sensitivity to alternations would strengthen the claim that the 4-step schedule “converges quickly.”

Clarity
Overall the paper is clearly written and well organized. Figures and tables convey the design and results effectively. Minor clarity issues include the explicit definition of ha/wa, a reminder of the final feature-map stride, and a short sensitivity analysis for the NMS threshold (fixed at 0.7).

Originality
The core contribution—learning proposals with a small FCN that shares features with the detector and uses translation-invariant anchors—is a significant and novel advance over Fast R-CNN/SPPnet and over prior CNN-based proposal methods (MultiBox). The two-stage formulation contrasts thoughtfully with one-stage OverFeat-like detectors, with an informative empirical comparison.

Significance
High. Eliminating the hand-crafted proposal bottleneck while improving accuracy is impactful; the approach is likely to be widely adopted and extended. The simplicity and speed/accuracy trade-off make it attractive to both researchers and practitioners.

Specific suggestions
- Add experiments with 2–3 alternations and/or a brief joint-training attempt; report mAP changes and training stability.
- Evaluate denser anchor tiling or stride-8 features; include high-IoU (≥0.75) recall and a size-based error analysis.
- Clarify notation: “wa and ha denote the anchor’s width and height; w and h are the predicted box’s width and height.”
- Consider reporting AP75 (if available) in addition to AP50, or include IoU-swept recall curves in the main text.

Pros (arguments for acceptance)
- Conceptually clean and practically important integration of proposal and detection with shared features.
- Strong empirical gains with fewer proposals and clear speedups; state-of-the-art mAP.
- Thorough ablations; public code; likely high impact.

Cons (arguments against acceptance)
- Limited exploration of training optimization (essentially one alternation); no joint end-to-end results here.
- Potential localization/coarse-grid issues not deeply analyzed; limited high-IoU/small-object evaluation.
- Runtime comparisons mix CPU and GPU implementations.

Clarification request
- Please confirm and state explicitly: in the parameterization tx = (x − xa)/wa, ty = (y − ya)/ha, tw = log(w/wa), th = log(h/ha), the symbols wa and ha are the anchor box width and height, respectively. If ha is defined elsewhere, point to that definition to avoid ambiguity.

Overall assessment
An excellent paper that substantially advances two-stage detection by learning proposals jointly with shared features. With minor clarifications and a bit more evidence on the optimization schedule and localization granularity, it is a strong accept.
