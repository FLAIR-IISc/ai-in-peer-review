This paper proposes a Region Proposal Network (RPN) that shares convolutional features with a Fast R-CNN detector to produce nearly cost-free, class-agnostic proposals. Concretely, a small network slides over shared feature maps and, via two sibling heads (classification/objectness and box regression), predicts at each position the bounds and objectness of a set of translation-invariant anchor boxes. The authors couple the RPN and Fast R-CNN through a simple alternating optimization that enables shared convolutional layers without requiring fully joint training. Relative to prior work—R-CNN, SPPnet, and Fast R-CNN for efficient detection; Selective Search and EdgeBoxes for hand-crafted proposals; and deep one-stage/localization methods like OverFeat and MultiBox—this paper’s key novelty is integrating proposal generation into the same deep feature hierarchy as the detector, with anchors providing scale/aspect coverage. The approach achieves state-of-the-art mAP on PASCAL VOC07/12 while reducing proposal time from seconds to about 10 ms on GPU and maintaining strong accuracy with as few as 300 proposals.

Quality: The methodology is technically sound and empirically validated with extensive ablations: effects of feature sharing, number of proposals, roles of classification vs regression, and recall–IoU analysis. Results support claims of speed and accuracy improvements on VOC07/12. The main weakness is that the joint training is achieved via heuristic alternating optimization without a convergence guarantee; end-to-end joint optimization is acknowledged but left as future work. As a standalone proposal method, RPN is competitive but does not unambiguously dominate Selective Search across all metrics/settings.

Clarity: The paper is clearly written and well-organized, with sufficient implementation details (anchors, loss, sampling, NMS) and public code that support reproducibility. A brief discussion of training stability and failure modes would further strengthen clarity.

Originality: The shared-feature RPN with translation-invariant anchors and the pragmatic training scheme represent a novel and impactful combination, distinct from previous proposal pipelines and one-stage detectors.

Significance: High. The work advances the state of the art in both accuracy and efficiency and is likely to be widely adopted and extended.

Pros for acceptance:
- Elegant integration of proposals with detection via shared conv features
- Strong SOTA results on VOC07/12 with large speedups
- Thorough experimental evaluation and open code
- Simple, general anchor-based design

Cons for acceptance:
- Heuristic alternating optimization lacks theoretical backing
- Proposal quality vs Selective Search not decisively superior in isolation
- Evaluation limited to VOC; broader benchmarks would help generality
