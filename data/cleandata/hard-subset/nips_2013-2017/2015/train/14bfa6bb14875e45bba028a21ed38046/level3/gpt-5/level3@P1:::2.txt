This paper proposes Region Proposal Networks (RPNs) that are fully convolutional heads attached to a shared backbone to generate class-agnostic proposals and objectness scores at each spatial location. The key idea is to share full-image convolutional features between proposal generation and detection, eliminating the traditional CPU-bound region proposal bottleneck (Selective Search [22], EdgeBoxes [24]) in region-based detectors. RPNs use translation-invariant “anchors” at each sliding-window position to parameterize boxes across multiple scales and aspect ratios, and learn a multitask loss (objectness classification and box regression) end-to-end. The authors combine RPNs with Fast R-CNN [5] via an alternating 4-step fine-tuning schedule to learn shared conv features. On PASCAL VOC 2007/2012, the approach yields state-of-the-art mAP with dramatically reduced proposal runtime (≈10ms with shared features) and overall 5 fps with VGG-16.

Relation to prior work: This builds directly on SPPnet [7] and Fast R-CNN [5], which share convolution across regions to accelerate detection but still rely on external proposal algorithms. Compared to OverFeat [18], which is a one-stage, class-specific sliding-window detector, the proposed two-stage cascade separates class-agnostic proposals from class-specific classification/regression and shows better accuracy/speed trade-offs. Compared to MultiBox [3,20], which predicts a fixed set of global proposals via k-means-derived anchors, RPN uses translation-invariant anchors at each location, yielding far fewer parameters and better data efficiency on smaller datasets like VOC. The paper also situates results with respect to proposal quality analyses [8,9,1].

Quality: The technical contribution is solid and carefully evaluated. The model, loss, anchor design, training details, and implementation choices are clearly specified (e.g., smooth L1, sampling 256 anchors/image with up to 1:1 positive:negative). Ablations isolate the effects of feature sharing, NMS, cls vs reg heads, number of proposals, and backbone depth (ZF vs VGG-16). The alternating optimization is a pragmatic engineering solution that works in practice, though a joint end-to-end training variant is left as future work. Claims on runtime and accuracy are well supported with detailed breakdowns. A few aspects remain heuristic (anchor scales/ratios, IoU thresholds 0.7/0.3, cross-boundary anchor handling) and sensitivity analyses could be deeper, but the empirical evidence is convincing.

Clarity: The paper is well-written, organized, and easy to follow. The description of anchors, losses, and the 4-step training schedule is clear. Figures and timing tables help. Minor suggestions: (i) quantify sensitivity to anchor configurations and feature stride, especially for small objects; (ii) clarify the “nearly cost-free” phrasing by consistently reporting wall-clock totals alongside per-module times; (iii) provide more guidance on memory/scale choices and when to fine-tune earlier VGG layers.

Originality: The idea of learning proposals via a fully convolutional head that shares features with the detector, coupled with translation-invariant anchors, is a notable conceptual advance over prior deep-proposal methods (MultiBox) and sliding-window detection (OverFeat). While the components (multitask loss, box regression, shared convs) are known, their combination here is novel and impactful.

Significance: High. The work removes the proposal bottleneck, enabling near real-time detection without sacrificing accuracy, and achieves state-of-the-art mAP on VOC with far fewer proposals (≈300). The approach is likely to be widely adopted and extended; code availability increases impact. The RPN design (anchors, shared features) has clear implications beyond VOC and for future detection pipelines.

Weaknesses and suggestions:
- The alternating training scheme is heuristic and lacks convergence guarantees; a joint end-to-end training result (even empirical) would strengthen the paper.
- Comparisons to SS/EB are partly confounded by CPU vs GPU implementations; a GPU-optimized baseline or matched hardware comparison would be fairer.
- Standalone proposal quality vs SS: when used with a detector trained on SS, RPN proposals can underperform due to train/test mismatch; the paper clarifies this, but an apples-to-apples “train on method X, test on method X” comparison is key (and is shown for ZF; adding more backbones would help).
- Limited analysis of sensitivity to anchor choices and stride; a brief sweep over scales/ratios/IoU thresholds would improve reproducibility and guidance.
- The “real-time” claim is borderline with VGG-16 at 5 fps; framing as “towards real-time” (as in the title) is appropriate.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Elegant, principled integration of proposal generation into the detection network via shared conv features.
- Strong empirical results: SOTA mAP on VOC07/12, significant speedups with only ~300 proposals.
- Thorough ablations that illuminate which components matter (cls vs reg, NMS, feature sharing).
- Clear exposition and released code; likely to be broadly influential.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Training is stitched together via a heuristic alternating schedule; no demonstration of stable joint end-to-end optimization.
- Some comparisons to proposal baselines are not fully controlled for hardware, and sensitivity to anchor hyperparameters is limited.
- Evaluation focused on VOC; broader benchmarks (e.g., larger-scale datasets) would further substantiate generality.

Overall assessment: A strong, well-executed contribution that meaningfully advances object detection by eliminating the region proposal bottleneck through a fully convolutional, shared-feature RPN. Despite the heuristic training schedule, the method is technically sound, clearly presented, and highly significant. I recommend acceptance.
