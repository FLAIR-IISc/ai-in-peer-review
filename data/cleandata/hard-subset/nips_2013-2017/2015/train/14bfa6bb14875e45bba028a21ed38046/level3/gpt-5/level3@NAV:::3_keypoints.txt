- Proposes a two-stage detection framework (RPN + Fast R-CNN) that shares convolutional features to make proposals nearly cost-free and enables near real-time detection (~5 fps with VGG-16).
- RPN is a fully convolutional sliding-window module predicting objectness and box regressions relative to translation-invariant anchors of multiple scales/aspect ratios.
- Uses alternating training to share conv layers between RPN and the detector, though only a single outer iteration is reported.
- Empirical results match or exceed Selective Search/EdgeBoxes on PASCAL VOC using as few as 300 proposals (e.g., 73.2% mAP with VGG-16); runtime is carefully measured.
- Novelty: unifies proposal generation and detection, introduces translation-invariant anchors, and contrasts with one-stage detectors like OverFeat and MultiBox.
- Quality: method is technically sound and well-motivated with strong empirical performance.
- Requested experiment: report whether additional alternations or partial joint training improve proposal recall-at-IoU or final mAP.
- Localization concern: sliding-window on top-level feature maps with stride 16 may cause coarse localization—quantify effective input stride and analyze recall for small objects at IoU ≥ 0.7–0.8.
- Suggested mitigations for localization: use smaller-stride features, dilated convolutions, or multi-scale feature maps.
- Clarity: paper is generally clear, but define h_a and w_a explicitly, clarify anchor indexing per spatial location vs. global, and how regression heads are tied across k anchors.
- Significance: high — the efficient unified pipeline removes a key bottleneck and is likely to be widely adopted.
- Pros: elegant unification, strong accuracy-speed trade-off, far fewer proposals needed, and translation-invariant anchors reduce parameters/overfitting risk.
- Cons: limited exploration of alternating/joint training, potential coarse localization from large stride, and minor notation ambiguity (h_a).
