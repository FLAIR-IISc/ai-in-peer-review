

This paper introduces Region Proposal Networks (RPNs) that share convolutional features with Fast R-CNN, creating a unified object detection system where region proposal computation becomes nearly cost-free. The authors propose a fully-convolutional network that simultaneously predicts object bounds and objectness scores at each position, trained end-to-end with Fast R-CNN through alternating optimization. The approach addresses the computational bottleneck of region proposal methods like Selective Search (which takes ~2s per image) by leveraging shared features, achieving 5fps on GPU with state-of-the-art VOC07 (73.2% mAP) and VOC12 (70.4% mAP) performance.

The paper excels in technical quality with thorough experimental validation, clearly demonstrating RPN's superiority over Selective Search and EdgeBoxes in both proposal quality and speed. The writing is clear and well-organized, making the contributions accessible to the community. The approach is novel in its elegant sharing of convolutional features between proposal and detection networks, distinct from previous methods like MultiBox or OverFeat that require separate networks or lack translation invariance.

However, the main weakness lies in the heuristic alternating optimization procedure for joint training, which lacks a convergence proof. While the method achieves strong results, the paper doesn't clearly demonstrate RPN's standalone superiority over Selective Search for proposal generation alone. The ablation studies show RPN proposals are competitive but don't significantly outperform Selective Search in the proposal evaluation metric.

The significance is high: this work enables practical real-time detection systems while advancing the state-of-the-art in accuracy. The method's efficiency gains (10ms proposal time) combined with strong accuracy make it valuable for both research and applications. The authors have made their code publicly available, enhancing reproducibility.

Overall, this is a strong contribution that makes object detection significantly more efficient while maintaining top performance. The heuristic training method is a minor concern that doesn't undermine the paper's substantial technical and practical contributions. I recommend acceptance with confidence.
