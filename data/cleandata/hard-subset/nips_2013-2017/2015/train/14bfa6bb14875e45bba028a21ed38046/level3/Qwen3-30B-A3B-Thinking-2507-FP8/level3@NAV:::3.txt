

This paper introduces Region Proposal Networks (RPNs), a novel two-stage object detection framework that shares convolutional layers between region proposal generation and object detection. The authors propose a fully-convolutional network that simultaneously predicts object bounds and scores at each position on feature maps, enabling nearly cost-free region proposals when shared with Fast R-CNN. The system achieves 5 fps on VGG-16 while setting new state-of-the-art results on PASCAL VOC (73.2% mAP on 2007, 70.4% on 2012). The approach builds on prior work in shared feature computation (e.g., SPPnet, Fast R-CNN) but makes a significant algorithmic contribution by integrating proposal generation directly into the detection network.

Strengths: The paper presents a technically sound solution that addresses a genuine bottleneck in object detection. The experimental results are compelling, with clear demonstration of both speed improvements and accuracy gains over Selective Search and EdgeBoxes. The alternating optimization scheme, while simple, effectively unifies the two networks. The work is clearly written, well-organized, and provides sufficient detail for reproducibility.

Weaknesses: The alternating optimization appears to run only one iteration (step 3 of the 4-step process), implicitly assuming initial RPN proposals are optimal. This should be explicitly justified or addressed. The sliding window on top-level feature maps may map to large steps on the input image due to pooling (e.g., 16-pixel stride on VGG), potentially causing coarse bounding-box locations that could affect accuracy for small objects. The definition of h_a in lines 173-174 is unclear and requires clarification.

Pro: Significant speed-accuracy improvement, elegant integration of proposal generation, strong experimental validation.
Con: Limited justification for alternating optimization, potential issue with coarse bounding boxes, unclear notation.

Overall, this is a high-quality contribution that advances object detection by eliminating a major computational bottleneck. The concerns raised are minor and could be addressed in revision. The work is highly significant, original, and well-executed, making it a strong candidate for acceptance.
