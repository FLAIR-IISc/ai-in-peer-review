This paper introduces a Region Proposal Network (RPN) that shares convolutional features with an object detection network (Fast R-CNN), aiming to accelerate the object detection pipeline. Prior work, like Selective Search and EdgeBoxes, relied on computationally expensive, separate region proposal algorithms. Recent advances in region-based CNNs (R-CNN, Fast R-CNN, SPPnet) reduced detection costs, highlighting region proposal as the bottleneck. The authors elegantly address this by formulating region proposal as a deep learning problem, enabling near cost-free proposals at test time through shared computation. This builds upon the trend of shared computation seen in OverFeat and SPPnet, but distinguishes itself by learning the proposals directly with a dedicated network.

The core idea of RPN is strong and the experimental results are compelling, achieving state-of-the-art accuracy on PASCAL VOC with a significant speedup (5fps with VGG-16). The paper convincingly demonstrates that an algorithmic shift – learning proposals with a deep net – is more effective than simply reimplementing existing methods on GPUs. The improvement in speed, while substantial, is perhaps not the most groundbreaking aspect, but the demonstrated accuracy combined with the speed improvement is a significant contribution.

However, the paper suffers from a lack of clarity in several areas. The explanations regarding translation invariance are particularly confusing. The authors state that RPN is translation-invariant, but contrast this with [19] (likely referring to VGG), without adequately explaining *why* VGG isn’t. The behaviour of OverFeat in this context isn’t fully clarified either; a more detailed comparison of the architectural differences that lead to the observed differences in translation invariance would be valuable.

A significant weakness is the ad-hoc nature of many hyperparameter choices. Parameters like n=3 and k=9 are set without sufficient justification. While some justification is given for k=9 (3 scales x 3 aspect ratios), the choice of n=3 feels arbitrary.  A more thorough exploration of these parameters and their impact on performance would strengthen the work.

The training procedure appears complex and potentially unstable. The alternating optimization scheme, while effective, is described in a somewhat opaque manner. The lack of reported repeated runs or ablation studies exploring alternative training schedules raises concerns about the robustness of the results.  Were other optimization strategies considered? What sensitivity analyses were performed regarding the learning rates and momentum parameters? 

The authors do not adequately address potential overfitting concerns. While the use of ImageNet pre-training helps, the relatively small PASCAL VOC datasets, especially in the context of deep convolutional networks, make overfitting a potential issue. The paper would benefit from discussion of regularization techniques and potentially cross-validation results.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** Introduces a novel and effective method for region proposal generation, integrating it seamlessly with object detection.
*   **Significance:** Significantly speeds up object detection while maintaining or improving accuracy. This addresses a critical bottleneck in existing systems.
*   **Solid Results:** Demonstrates strong performance on standard benchmarks (PASCAL VOC).
*   **Clear Goal:** The paper has a well-defined goal and achieves it successfully.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Clarity:**  The paper is difficult to follow in places, particularly concerning translation invariance.
*   **Hyperparameter Justification:** Lack of justification for key hyperparameter choices.
*   **Training Procedure:** The training procedure appears complex and lacks thorough exploration of alternative settings.
*   **Robustness:** Lack of reported repeated runs and sensitivity analyses.



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite its weaknesses in clarity and justification of choices, this is a strong paper that presents a significant contribution to the field of object detection. The core idea is elegant, the results are compelling, and the potential impact is substantial. The issues raised could be addressed with more detailed explanations, ablation studies, and a more rigorous analysis of the training procedure. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions to improve clarity and address the concerns regarding hyperparameters and training stability. The authors should specifically clarify the point about translation invariance and provide a stronger justification for their hyperparameter choices.
