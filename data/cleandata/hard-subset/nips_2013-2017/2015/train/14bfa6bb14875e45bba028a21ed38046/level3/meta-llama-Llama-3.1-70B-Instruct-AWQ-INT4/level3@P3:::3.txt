This paper proposes a novel Region Proposal Network (RPN) that shares convolutional features with a Fast R-CNN object detection network, achieving near real-time object detection with high accuracy. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach on the PASCAL VOC 2007 and 2012 detection benchmarks, achieving state-of-the-art results with a frame rate of 5fps on a GPU.

The main idea of this paper is to use a deep neural network to generate region proposals, which are then used by a Fast R-CNN object detection network. The RPN is designed to share convolutional features with the detection network, reducing the computational cost of proposal generation. The authors also propose a simple alternating optimization algorithm to train the RPN and Fast R-CNN networks jointly.

The paper is well-written and easy to follow, with clear explanations of the proposed approach and its components. The authors provide a thorough analysis of the results, including ablation studies and comparisons with other state-of-the-art methods.

One of the strengths of this paper is its ability to achieve high accuracy while maintaining a high frame rate. The authors demonstrate that their approach can achieve state-of-the-art results on the PASCAL VOC 2007 and 2012 detection benchmarks, while maintaining a frame rate of 5fps on a GPU. This makes their approach suitable for real-time object detection applications.

However, there are some concerns with the proposed approach. One concern is that the alternating optimization algorithm used to train the RPN and Fast R-CNN networks may not converge to the optimal solution. The authors acknowledge this limitation and propose a pragmatic 4-step training algorithm to learn shared features via alternating optimization. However, it is unclear whether this approach will converge to the optimal solution in all cases.

Another concern is that the sliding window approach used in the RPN may not be suitable for detecting objects at different scales. The authors use a fixed window size of 3x3, which may not be sufficient to detect objects at different scales. Additionally, the authors use a fixed number of proposals (300) for all images, which may not be optimal for images with varying object sizes.

The paper also raises some questions. For example, the definition of h_a in lines 173-174 is unclear and needs clarification. Additionally, the authors do not provide a clear explanation of how the RPN is able to detect objects at different scales, despite using a fixed window size.

In terms of related work, the authors provide a thorough review of existing region proposal methods and object detection networks. However, they do not provide a clear explanation of how their approach differs from other state-of-the-art methods, such as OverFeat and MultiBox.

In terms of originality, the paper proposes a novel approach to region proposal generation using a deep neural network. The authors also propose a simple alternating optimization algorithm to train the RPN and Fast R-CNN networks jointly. However, the idea of using a deep neural network for region proposal generation is not new, and the authors do not provide a clear explanation of how their approach differs from other existing methods.

In terms of significance, the paper demonstrates the effectiveness of the proposed approach on the PASCAL VOC 2007 and 2012 detection benchmarks. The authors also provide a thorough analysis of the results, including ablation studies and comparisons with other state-of-the-art methods. However, the paper does not provide a clear explanation of how the proposed approach can be applied to other object detection tasks or datasets.

Overall, this paper proposes a novel approach to region proposal generation using a deep neural network. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach on the PASCAL VOC 2007 and 2012 detection benchmarks, achieving state-of-the-art results with a frame rate of 5fps on a GPU. However, there are some concerns with the proposed approach, including the potential for non-convergence of the alternating optimization algorithm and the limited ability to detect objects at different scales.
