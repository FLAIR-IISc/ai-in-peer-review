

# Review of "Faster R-CNN: Towards Real-Time Object Detection with Region Proposal Networks"

This paper introduces Region Proposal Networks (RPNs), a novel approach to object detection that shares convolutional features between region proposal generation and object detection. The authors build upon Fast R-CNN [5] and SPPnet [7], addressing the remaining bottleneck in state-of-the-art object detection systems: region proposal computation. By introducing an RPN that shares convolutional layers with the detection network, the authors achieve nearly cost-free region proposals, resulting in a system that runs at 5fps on a GPU while achieving state-of-the-art accuracy on PASCAL VOC.

The paper makes a significant contribution to the field by solving a key practical bottleneck in object detection. The authors demonstrate that RPNs, trained end-to-end to generate high-quality proposals, can be seamlessly integrated with Fast R-CNN, creating a unified network that achieves better accuracy (73.2% mAP on VOC 2007, 70.4% on VOC 2012) than previous methods while running 10× faster than Fast R-CNN with Selective Search (198ms vs 1510ms). The method is technically sound, with thorough experimental validation across multiple datasets and ablation studies.

**Strengths:**
- The goal is clearly defined and the experimental results are compelling and well-documented.
- The paper presents a novel approach that significantly advances the state of the art in object detection.
- The technical contribution is substantial, with the RPN being a well-motivated and effective solution to a major bottleneck.
- The method is practical, with real-world applicability demonstrated by the 5fps frame rate.
- The authors provide code and thorough implementation details.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper is not easy to follow due to confusing explanations, particularly regarding translation invariance and the relationship to OverFeat [18]. The claim that RPNs are translation-invariant while OverFeat is not is unclear and not adequately justified.
- Many hyperparameters (e.g., n=3, k=9) are set manually without justification or exploration of alternatives. The paper lacks ablation studies on these parameters.
- The training procedure is complex and seems unnecessarily complicated. The authors propose a 4-step alternating optimization scheme without adequately justifying why joint optimization isn't feasible, and without reporting whether this procedure is stable or sensitive to initialization.
- The paper does not report repeated runs or exploration of alternative training settings to validate the robustness of their approach. The lack of error bars or statistical significance testing for key results is concerning.
- The explanation of the RPN's relationship to previous work (particularly OverFeat) is inadequate, leaving important conceptual questions unanswered.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
- The method solves a critical bottleneck in object detection pipelines.
- The empirical results are strong and demonstrate both improved speed and accuracy.
- The technical contribution is significant and well-executed.
- The approach is likely to have a major impact on the field of object detection.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
- The paper lacks sufficient clarity on key technical points, making it difficult to fully assess the contribution.
- The justification for key design choices is inadequate.
- The training procedure is unnecessarily complex without clear justification.
- The lack of ablation studies on key hyperparameters and training procedures weakens the empirical validation.

**Recommendation:**
I recommend acceptance with minor revisions. The paper presents a significant contribution to the field of object detection that addresses a critical bottleneck. The core idea is compelling, and the empirical results are strong. However, the authors should address the clarity issues, particularly regarding translation invariance and the relationship to OverFeat, and provide more justification for key design choices and hyperparameters. The training procedure could be simplified if the authors can demonstrate that joint optimization is feasible.

The paper is well-positioned to make a major impact in the field, and with the requested clarifications, it would be a valuable addition to NIPS. The authors have already demonstrated the practical significance of their approach with real-world performance metrics, and the method is likely to be widely adopted by researchers and practitioners. The minor clarity issues do not detract from the substantial technical contribution, which represents a significant step toward practical, real-time object detection systems.
