

This paper introduces Region Proposal Networks (RPNs) that share convolutional features with object detection networks, eliminating the computational bottleneck of region proposal generation in state-of-the-art object detection systems. The authors build upon Fast R-CNN [5] and SPPnet [7], addressing the key observation that region proposal computation has become the dominant cost in modern detectors. The RPN is a fully-convolutional network that predicts object bounds and scores at each position, sharing convolutional features with the detection network. This approach enables nearly cost-free region proposals, achieving 5fps on a GPU while maintaining state-of-the-art accuracy (73.2% mAP on PASCAL VOC 2007).

The paper makes a strong technical contribution with several clear strengths. The core idea of sharing convolutional features between proposal and detection networks is novel and elegantly solves a significant bottleneck in the object detection pipeline. The experimental results are compelling: the combined RPN+Fast R-CNN system is approximately 10× faster than previous approaches (198ms total runtime vs. 1.5s for Selective Search) while achieving slightly better accuracy (73.2% mAP vs. 70.4% for the previous best). The ablation studies thoroughly demonstrate the importance of both the classification and regression components of the RPN, as well as the benefits of sharing convolutional features between tasks. The paper also provides a practical training procedure that alternates between RPN and detection network fine-tuning, which converges quickly and produces a unified network.

The work demonstrates significant significance by addressing a critical bottleneck in object detection systems. The proposed approach is not just faster but also more accurate than previous methods, advancing the state of the art in both speed and accuracy. The results on PASCAL VOC benchmarks are particularly convincing, with the RPN+VGG-16 system achieving superior performance compared to Selective Search and EdgeBoxes. The authors also provide a detailed analysis of recall-to-IoU metrics, showing that RPN maintains good recall even with fewer proposals (300 vs. 2000), which explains the strong detection performance.

While the technical contribution is strong, the paper suffers from several clarity issues that hinder understanding. The explanation of translation-invariance is particularly confusing, as it's unclear why RPN is translation-invariant while the VGG-16 model [19] is not. The paper also makes several key design choices without sufficient justification, including the selection of n=3 for the sliding window size and k=9 for the number of anchors. The training procedure, while practical, is described as "complicated" and the paper does not report repeated runs or exploration of alternative training settings to justify the chosen procedure. This lack of thoroughness in the experimental design weakens the claim of robustness.

The paper also contains some confusing statements regarding related work. The comparison with OverFeat [18] is somewhat misleading, as the authors claim OverFeat is "one-stage" but then describe it as using a "scale pyramid" which contradicts their own one-stage implementation. The paper also fails to adequately explain why the MultiBox method [20] is not translation-invariant, despite claiming this as a key advantage of their approach.

Despite these weaknesses, the paper's core contribution is highly valuable and well-executed. The RPN concept is clearly novel compared to previous region proposal methods, and the implementation details are sufficient for reproduction. The paper makes a clear and significant contribution to the field, advancing both the speed and accuracy of object detection systems. The authors have addressed a genuine bottleneck in the object detection pipeline with a simple yet effective solution.

In summary, this paper presents a strong technical contribution with clear significance, solid experimental results, and meaningful improvements over previous methods. While the writing could be clearer in several places and some design choices lack justification, these do not undermine the core contribution. The work is both novel and impactful, advancing the state of the art in object detection while providing a practical solution for real-time applications.

Strengths: Clear goal, strong experimental results, novel approach to sharing features, significant speed-accuracy improvements over prior work.

Weaknesses: Confusing explanations in several sections, unexplained hyperparameter choices, insufficient exploration of training alternatives, misleading comparison with OverFeat.

Recommendation: Accept. This paper makes a substantial contribution to object detection with a simple, elegant solution to a major bottleneck. The results are convincing, the approach is practical, and the work has clear significance for both researchers and practitioners in the field.
