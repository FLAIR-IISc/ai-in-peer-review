Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper introduces Faster R-CNN, a two-stage object detection framework that unifies region proposal generation and object detection by sharing convolutional features. The key contribution is the Region Proposal Network (RPN), a fully convolutional subnetwork that slides over shared feature maps and predicts objectness scores and box regressions for a set of translation-invariant anchors (multiple scales and aspect ratios) at each location. RPN proposals are then consumed by a Fast R-CNN detector; the two components share convolutional layers and are trained via a simple alternating scheme. Compared to previous region-based detectors (R-CNN, SPPnet, Fast R-CNN), this removes the region proposal bottleneck (Selective Search, EdgeBoxes) and yields near real-time performance with very deep backbones (VGG-16: ~5 fps end-to-end), while improving accuracy on PASCAL VOC 2007/2012. The method is also contrasted with one-stage detection/localization (OverFeat) and proposal nets (MultiBox), arguing for the benefits of translation-invariant anchors and a two-stage cascade.

Strengths:
- Clear algorithmic advance: the RPN shares features with the detector, making proposals essentially “free” at test time (~10 ms), and significantly speeding up the overall pipeline versus SS/EB while improving or matching mAP.
- Principled anchor design with multiple scales/aspect ratios, translation invariance, and smooth L1 regression produce high-quality proposals with as few as 300 regions/image.
- Thorough ablations isolate the effects of sharing features, classification vs. regression heads, proposal counts, and NMS; results convincingly support design choices.
- Strong empirical results: VGG-16 achieves 69.9% mAP on VOC07 (feature sharing), 73.2% with 07+12 union training; 70.4% on VOC12 test. End-to-end runtime measurements are provided.
- Relation to prior art is well articulated, especially versus SPPnet/Fast R-CNN, OverFeat, and MultiBox.

Weaknesses and questions:
- Alternating optimization: The 4-step schedule appears to run a single alternation (RPN → detector → RPN unique layers → detector fc). It implicitly assumes proposals stabilize after one pass. Please clarify whether additional alternations were tried and whether they affect convergence, proposal quality, or final mAP. Reporting mAP vs. number of alternations would strengthen the training story. A brief discussion of why naive joint training was unstable (if attempted) would also be useful.
- Feature-map stride and localization granularity: With VGG-16/ZF, the final shared stride is 16 pixels (on resized images), which can be coarse for small objects. While regression and multi-scale anchors mitigate this, the paper could quantify the effect by: (a) comparing with reduced stride (e.g., removing a pooling layer, dilated convolutions) or (b) using a finer feature map for RPN only. Reporting small-object recall versus IoU would help diagnose residual localization errors attributable to stride.
- Notation clarity (lines 173–174): In the parameterization, tx = (x − xa)/wa, ty = (y − ya)/ha, tw = log(w/wa), th = log(h/ha). It would help to explicitly define xa, ya, wa, ha as the anchor box center (xa, ya) and anchor width/height (wa, ha), and x, y, w, h as the predicted box parameters, with x*, y*, w*, h* as ground truth. Also ensure consistent subscripts/superscripts (e.g., ha vs. h_a) and introduce this notation before the loss.
- Boundary handling: Cross-boundary anchors are ignored during training but not at test time (where proposals are later clipped). An ablation quantifying the impact of (i) including boundary-crossing anchors with appropriate targets or (ii) training-time clipping could reassure readers that the current choice is optimal.
- Sensitivity to λ and normalization: The footnote explains a particular normalization/λ choice that roughly balances cls and reg. Please report sensitivity to λ and alternative normalizations, since stability and performance can depend on this balance.

Quality:
The technical approach is sound and well-supported by experiments. The loss function, training protocol, and proposal ranking/NMS are standard and appropriate. Comparisons are fair and include both runtime and accuracy. Minor gaps (e.g., alternation depth, stride analysis) are addressable and do not undermine the core claims.

Clarity:
The paper is generally well written and organized. Suggested improvements:
- Clarify notation for anchor and box parameterizations as noted above.
- Summarize the 4-step alternating training in a concise algorithm box and specify whether additional alternations were tested.
- Explicitly state the stride of the shared feature map next to each backbone and briefly discuss its implications for localization and small objects.
- Group implementation choices (NMS threshold, top-N proposals at train/test) in a dedicated subsection.

Originality:
The idea of learning proposals with a CNN is related to MultiBox, and sliding-window detection with shared conv features to OverFeat. The novel elements are (1) translation-invariant anchors tied to every location, (2) a minimal, fully convolutional proposal head that shares features with the detector, and (3) a simple alternating scheme to learn shared features. This combination is new and impactful.

Significance:
High. The work removes the principal bottleneck in region-based detection, shows state-of-the-art accuracy with practical speed, and proposes a general mechanism (RPN) that is likely to be adopted widely. The approach is simple, general, and extensible (e.g., to deeper backbones, alternative heads), and it clearly advances the state of the art.

Additional suggestions:
- Report proposal recall at higher IoU thresholds (e.g., 0.75, 0.9) specifically for small objects, and correlate with detector AP by size.
- Evaluate effect of proposal count on detector runtime/accuracy beyond 300 (e.g., 100–1000) to guide practitioners.
- Provide details on random seed variance (std over runs) for key mAP numbers.
- Include a short discussion of memory footprint and batch size constraints for VGG-16.

Pros:
- Elegant, effective unification of proposals and detection via shared features.
- Strong accuracy-speed trade-off; minimal additional overhead for proposals.
- Comprehensive ablations and fair comparisons to SS/EB, OverFeat-like one-stage baselines.
- Clear, broadly useful design (anchors, FCN head, alternating training).

Cons:
- Limited exploration of training alternations and joint training stability.
- Coarse feature-map stride may limit small-object localization; limited analysis provided.
- Some notation and implementation details could be clearer (anchor parameterization, boundary handling, λ sensitivity).

Overall recommendation:
Strong accept. The paper offers a substantial and well-validated advance in object detection, with clear impact on both research and practice.
