This paper proposes Region Proposal Networks (RPNs), a fully convolutional architecture that generates class-agnostic object proposals by sharing convolutional features with a Fast R-CNN detector. Each sliding-window location on the shared feature map predicts k anchor-relative boxes and objectness scores via two sibling 1×1 conv layers, trained end-to-end with a multitask classification/regression loss. A simple alternating 4-step procedure fine-tunes the proposal and detection components to share convolutional layers. On PASCAL VOC 2007/2012, the unified system achieves state-of-the-art mAP (73.2% on VOC07 and 70.4% on VOC12 with VGG-16) while running near real-time (∼5 fps, 10 ms proposal overhead; 198 ms total per image). Compared to traditional CPU-based proposal methods such as Selective Search or EdgeBoxes, RPNs make proposals almost “free” once the shared conv features are computed, and the overall detector becomes about an order of magnitude faster than SS+Fast R-CNN, with slightly higher accuracy. The authors relate the method to SPPnet and Fast R-CNN (shared feature extraction), OverFeat (sliding-window localization), and MultiBox (learned proposals), arguing that their shared, translation-invariant anchor mechanism reduces parameters and overfitting while improving speed/accuracy tradeoffs.

Quality
- Soundness: The architecture and loss closely follow well-understood components (Fast R-CNN’s multitask loss, smooth L1 regression), and the anchor mechanism is technically sound. The ablations isolating the roles of classification vs. regression, NMS, number of proposals, and shared vs. unshared features provide useful evidence. The detection results on VOC07/12 are strong and the reported speed is carefully decomposed (conv sharing, proposal overhead, region-wise cost).
- Concerns: (1) Many design choices appear heuristic and lack sensitivity analysis: n=3 conv window, anchor scales/aspect ratios (3×3, yielding k=9), stride 16, NMS IoU=0.7. The paper would benefit from experiments varying n, k, and the anchor set (e.g., including smaller scales or different ratios) to understand robustness. (2) The 4-step alternating training is pragmatic but adds complexity; no evidence is provided about stability or variance across runs. Repeated trials, joint end-to-end training attempts, or analysis of convergence would strengthen the claims. (3) Some claims about translation invariance are not clearly justified; see clarity below.

Clarity
- The high-level idea is compelling, but the exposition is occasionally hard to follow. The relationship among anchors, translation invariance/equivariance, and parameter count could be more precise. In particular, the argument that RPNs are translation-invariant while MultiBox is not is reasonable (because MultiBox uses a fixed set of k-means prototypes), but the paper interleaves this discussion with VGG [19] (a backbone) and OverFeat [18] (a sliding-window detector), which creates confusion. Please explicitly define invariance vs. equivariance, clarify that VGG [19] is the base conv net (not a proposal method), explain why MultiBox’s anchor prototypes break equivariance, and discuss OverFeat’s behavior (it is sliding-window and thus equivariant). The training description (boundary handling, minibatch sampling, normalization of cls/reg terms, λ) is dense; a summarized algorithm box would help. Finally, justification for the specific hyperparameters should be provided or deferred to an appendix.

Originality
- The core novelty is integrating a learned, fully convolutional proposal mechanism that shares features with the detector and uses translation-equivariant anchors at every spatial location. While prior works used CNNs for proposals (MultiBox) or shared conv features for detection (SPPnet, Fast R-CNN), this paper’s anchor-based proposal generator with shared features and its simple alternating training to unify proposals and detection is a clean and impactful combination. The one-stage vs. two-stage comparison further helps position the contribution.

Significance
- The contribution is substantial. Proposals become nearly cost-free, eliminating the bottleneck that limited Fast R-CNN, and the unified system achieves better accuracy with far higher throughput (∼10× faster than SS+Fast R-CNN while slightly improving mAP). The method is likely to be widely adopted in detection pipelines; code is released. The experiments on VOC07/12 with ZF and VGG backbones and the ablations make a strong case. The work advances both the practical state of the art and the conceptual understanding of proposal generation within deep architectures.

Pros
- Elegant, effective integration of proposals and detection via shared conv features.
- Strong empirical results: 73.2% VOC07 mAP; 70.4% VOC12 mAP; ∼5 fps with VGG-16; ∼10 ms proposal overhead; competitive accuracy with as few as 300 proposals.
- Careful ablations showing the impact of cls vs. reg, NMS, proposal counts, and feature sharing.
- Clear improvement over SS/EB in both speed and accuracy; comparison to one-stage sliding-window baseline is informative.
- Practical training/inference recipe and released code.

Cons
- Limited analysis of sensitivity to anchor design, conv window size n, and NMS threshold; many hyperparameters chosen ad hoc.
- Alternating 4-step training adds complexity; no report of variance across runs or stability under alternative schedules, learning rates, or sampling ratios.
- Exposition issues around translation invariance/equivariance and comparisons to OverFeat/MultiBox can confuse readers.
- No experiments on joint training of RPN and Fast R-CNN, even as a negative result; no analysis on smaller stride or multi-scale features vs. anchors.

Requests for clarification and suggestions
- Provide a clear definition and discussion of translation invariance vs. equivariance in the context of anchors; disentangle the roles of VGG [19], MultiBox [20], and OverFeat [18].
- Add sensitivity Ablations: vary n (e.g., 1, 3, 5), anchor scales/ratios (include smaller scales), k, and NMS IoU. Report proposal recall and detection mAP.
- Report stability across 3–5 independent runs and alternative training schedules; if joint end-to-end training fails, share insights.
- Brief error analysis (per-class AP, localization vs. classification errors) and qualitative failure cases.
- Discuss memory usage and potential impacts of using smaller stride features for improved localization.

Recommendation
- Strong accept. The paper offers a simple, well-supported, and highly impactful idea that removes a central bottleneck in modern detectors and improves accuracy, with code released. Addressing the clarity and hyperparameter sensitivity concerns would further strengthen an already excellent contribution.
