Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes Faster R-CNN, a unified two-stage object detection system that integrates a Region Proposal Network (RPN) with a Fast R-CNN detector to share convolutional features. The RPN is a fully convolutional network that, for each spatial location, predicts k box refinements and objectness scores relative to k “anchor” boxes of multiple scales and aspect ratios. A 4-step alternating training scheme is introduced to enable feature sharing between the RPN and detector while keeping Fast R-CNN’s requirement of fixed proposals during training. On PASCAL VOC 2007/2012, the system achieves state-of-the-art mAP while running at 5 fps with VGG-16 (including proposals and detection), using as few as 300 proposals per image. The approach advances the line of region-based detection (R-CNN → SPPnet → Fast R-CNN) by replacing the CPU-bound, hand-engineered proposal stage (Selective Search/EdgeBoxes) with a trainable, GPU-efficient proposal network. Compared to prior deep proposal/detector systems (OverFeat, MultiBox), the key distinctions are: class-agnostic proposals learned on shared features; translation-invariant anchors; and a two-stage cascade (proposal → detection) rather than a one-stage sliding-window detector.

Strengths
- Clear goal and strong empirical results: The paper convincingly removes the proposal bottleneck while improving accuracy. Using VGG-16, the unified detector is roughly 10× faster than Fast R-CNN with Selective Search (∼0.2s vs. ∼1.8s total) and slightly more accurate.
- Novelty in formulation: The anchor-based, translation-tied proposal mechanism is elegant and parameter-efficient (k small per location), and it fits naturally with fully convolutional feature sharing.
- Solid ablations: The paper disentangles cls vs reg contributions, analyzes NMS, proposal count, sharing vs unshared features, and one-stage vs two-stage detection.
- Practical impact: The approach is simple to deploy, reduces engineering overhead (no external proposal code), and is likely to be widely used, as evidenced by released code.

Weaknesses and points needing clarification
- Clarity/organization: While the high-level idea is simple, the exposition is hard to follow in places (e.g., loss normalization and λ, image-centric sampling, boundary handling, and the 4-step training). Some footnotes and implementation caveats (e.g., different normalizations in code vs paper) are easy to miss and add confusion. Please consolidate these details in the main text with a clear recipe.
- Translation invariance claim: The paper argues RPNs are translation-invariant whereas prior work is not. It would help to clarify precisely:
  - Which prior method is meant—MultiBox uses k-means anchors tied to absolute positions, but OverFeat’s sliding windows on conv maps are, by construction, translation equivariant. Under your definition, would OverFeat count as translation-invariant? Please sharpen the comparison and justify the statement.
  - The citation indexing contributes to confusion (e.g., [19] is VGG-16, not a proposal method). Please ensure references match the claims.
- Hyperparameters without justification: Several key choices feel hand-tuned and lack sensitivity analysis: n = 3 conv window, k = 9 anchors (3 scales × 3 ratios), IoU thresholds (0.7/0.3), NMS threshold (0.7), sampling ratio (1:1, 256 anchors per image), image scale s = 600, and λ/normalizations. How robust is mAP to these settings? For example, do larger n or different anchor sets (more aspect ratios, different scales) help? A brief sweep or rationale would strengthen the paper.
- Training complexity and stability: The authors avoid joint end-to-end training due to convergence concerns and instead propose a 4-step alternating schedule. The procedure involves multiple moving parts (anchor sampling, boundary ignoring, normalization choices). Reported results are single runs; there is no discussion of variance across seeds, stability, or alternative schedules (e.g., simultaneous training, different sharing depths, different positive/negative IoU thresholds). Given the practical importance of training, please:
  - Report variability across repeated runs.
  - Show results for at least one alternative training schedule or a partial joint finetuning attempt.
  - Provide intuition for why alternating converges reliably and under what conditions it fails.
- Reproducibility details: Exact settings for normalization (Ncls/Nreg), λ, learning rate schedule per backbone, and layers frozen/tuned are scattered. Please provide a single, self-contained training recipe matching the final reported numbers, including random seed and hardware.

Quality
Technically sound overall and well-supported by experiments on PASCAL VOC. The ablations are thoughtful, and the speed/accuracy trade-offs are measured carefully. The main gaps are: (1) insufficient sensitivity analyses for the many manual hyperparameters, and (2) lack of repeated runs or exploration of alternative training regimens to justify the chosen alternating scheme.

Clarity
The core idea is simple but the manuscript is not always easy to follow. Consolidating the loss normalization/λ choices, boundary handling, and the 4-step training into a single “Training recipe” subsection, and clarifying the translation-invariance comparison, would substantially improve readability.

Originality
The paper introduces a novel, elegant region proposal mechanism via translation-tied anchors embedded in a fully convolutional network and unifies it with a detector through shared features. While building on known components (FCN, bounding-box regression, Fast R-CNN), the combination and engineering are fresh and impactful.

Significance
High. The method removes a major bottleneck, improves accuracy, and sets a new baseline detector architecture. It is likely to be widely adopted and built upon by both researchers and practitioners.

Questions for the authors
- Please clarify the “translation-invariant” claim relative to MultiBox and OverFeat. Would OverFeat be translation-invariant under your definition?
- How sensitive are results to n and k? What happens with k = 3/6/12 or with additional aspect ratios?
- Can you report repeated runs and standard deviations for key models?
- Did you try partial joint training (e.g., unfreezing shared conv layers late), or alternative positive/negative IoU thresholds?
- How do results change with different NMS thresholds or without NMS at test time but with a fixed top-N cap?

Suggestions
- Provide a consolidated training protocol and hyperparameter checklist.
- Add a brief sensitivity analysis on anchors and IoU thresholds.
- Include a short discussion of failure cases (small/elongated objects) and whether anchor design alleviates them.

Pros for acceptance
- Strong empirical gains and substantial speedup; code available.
- Conceptually simple, elegant design; significant practical impact.
- Solid ablations supporting design choices.

Cons for acceptance
- Exposition has confusing parts; reference/citation mismatches around translation invariance.
- Heavy reliance on manual hyperparameters without sensitivity analysis.
- Training procedure seems ad hoc; no evidence of stability across runs or alternative schedules.

Recommendation
Accept. The contribution is significant and timely, with solid results. Addressing clarity and training robustness would further strengthen the paper.

Reviewer confidence
High.
