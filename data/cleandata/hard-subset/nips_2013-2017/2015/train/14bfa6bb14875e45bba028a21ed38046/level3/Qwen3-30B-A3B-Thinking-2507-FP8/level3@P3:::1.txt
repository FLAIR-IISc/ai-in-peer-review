

# Review of "Faster R-CNN: Towards Real-Time Object Detection with Region Proposal Networks"

This paper introduces Region Proposal Networks (RPNs), a novel approach to object detection that integrates region proposal generation with the object detection network through shared convolutional features. The authors build upon prior work in region-based CNNs (R-CNN, Fast R-CNN) and shared feature computation (SPPnet), addressing the computational bottleneck of region proposal methods like Selective Search. The key innovation is an end-to-end trainable RPN that shares convolutional features with the detection network, enabling nearly cost-free region proposals while maintaining or improving detection accuracy.

The paper makes several significant contributions. First, it proposes a novel region proposal method that shares CNN features with object localization, eliminating redundant computation. Second, it introduces a multi-stage training procedure to jointly optimize RPN and Fast R-CNN, allowing them to share convolutional features. Third, it demonstrates state-of-the-art detection performance (73.2% mAP on PASCAL VOC 2007) at a practical frame rate of 5fps using VGG-16.

The experimental results are robust and well-supported. The authors demonstrate that RPNs with Fast R-CNN outperform Selective Search and EdgeBoxes as proposal methods, while reducing the overall detection time by approximately 10× compared to Fast R-CNN with Selective Search. The ablation studies provide clear evidence that both the classification and regression outputs of RPNs contribute to detection accuracy, and the analysis of recall-to-IoU shows RPNs maintain good proposal quality even with fewer proposals.

However, the paper suffers from several significant weaknesses. The explanation of why RPNs are translation-invariant while OverFeat (reference [19]) is not remains unclear. The paper states RPNs are translation-invariant but does not adequately explain the theoretical basis for this claim or provide a comparison with OverFeat's behavior. The authors mention OverFeat uses a "fully-connected (fc) layer" for localization, but don't clarify how this makes it translation-variant versus RPN's convolutional approach.

The paper also contains numerous hyperparameters set without justification (n=3, k=9, anchor sizes, NMS threshold). While the paper provides some anchor size statistics, it fails to explain why these specific values were chosen or how they might affect performance on other datasets. The training procedure is described as "simple" but involves a 4-step alternating optimization that appears complex and potentially unstable, without any exploration of alternative training schemes or repeated runs to validate the procedure's robustness.

The paper's clarity is hampered by several confusing elements. The explanation of the translation-invariance claim is particularly problematic, as it's central to the method's innovation. The paper also contains some contradictory statements about the relationship between the RPN and OverFeat, which could mislead readers about the novelty of the approach.

The paper's significance is substantial, as it addresses a key bottleneck in object detection systems and enables real-time performance with state-of-the-art accuracy. The proposed method has been highly influential in subsequent object detection work, with many modern detectors (like Mask R-CNN) building upon this framework.

In terms of quality, the paper is technically sound and the experimental results are well-documented. However, the lack of exploration into hyperparameter sensitivity and the absence of repeated runs or ablation studies for the training procedure weakens the claims about the method's robustness.

Regarding originality, the paper presents a novel integration of region proposal and detection networks, but the paper could better clarify how this differs from prior work on shared features (SPPnet, Fast R-CNN) and proposal methods (MultiBox). The related work section could be strengthened by more clearly differentiating the proposed approach from previous work.

For significance, the paper makes a major contribution to practical object detection systems. The 5fps frame rate with state-of-the-art accuracy makes it a compelling solution for real-world applications, and the paper's influence on subsequent work is well-documented.

**Arguments for acceptance:**
- Clear goal addressing a significant bottleneck in object detection
- Solid experimental results demonstrating both speed and accuracy improvements
- Novel integration of region proposal and detection networks
- Practical impact with real-time performance and state-of-the-art accuracy
- Well-documented code and implementation details

**Arguments against acceptance:**
- Poorly explained translation-invariance claim and unclear comparison with OverFeat
- Unjustified hyperparameters (n=3, k=9) without ablation studies
- Complex training procedure with no exploration of alternatives or repeated runs
- Confusing sections that make the paper difficult to follow
- Lack of sufficient justification for the choice of anchor sizes and other design decisions

Overall, this paper makes a valuable contribution to object detection research with significant practical impact. While the technical approach is sound and the results are compelling, the paper would benefit from greater clarity in explaining key concepts, more thorough exploration of hyperparameters, and stronger justification for the design choices. The paper's influence on the field is undeniable, but the current presentation limits its accessibility and reproducibility. I recommend acceptance with the understanding that the paper's impact is substantial, though the presentation could be improved.
