This paper introduces a Region Proposal Network (RPN) to address the bottleneck of region proposal computation in state-of-the-art object detection systems. The core idea is to leverage the convolutional features already computed for detection, sharing them with a new fully-convolutional network—the RPN—to predict object bounds and objectness scores. This shared computation dramatically reduces the cost of generating region proposals. The authors then propose a practical, albeit heuristic, alternating optimization scheme to jointly train the RPN and a Fast R-CNN detector, achieving state-of-the-art results on PASCAL VOC 2007 and 2012 benchmarks.

This work builds upon the significant progress in object detection driven by region-based CNNs (R-CNNs) [6] and their subsequent improvements, SPPnet [7] and Fast R-CNN [5]. These advancements focused on reducing the computational cost of the detection *network* itself, highlighting region proposal as the remaining bottleneck. Previous methods like Selective Search (SS) [22] and EdgeBoxes [24] addressed this, but remained significantly slower than the detection network, particularly when considering GPU-accelerated detection. The authors correctly identify that simply porting these methods to the GPU doesn’t address the fundamental issue of independent computation; sharing computation is key. Related work like OverFeat [18] and MultiBox [3, 20] also explored using deep networks for generating bounding boxes, but these differ from the current approach in their architecture and training strategy. Specifically, OverFeat and MultiBox generate a fixed number of proposals and do not emphasize the shared convolutional feature space as strongly.

The strengths of this paper are numerous. The technical approach is sound and well-motivated. Sharing convolutional features is a natural and effective way to improve efficiency, and the RPN architecture is elegant and well-designed. The extensive experimental evaluation convincingly demonstrates the effectiveness of the proposed method. The results on PASCAL VOC benchmarks are competitive with, and often surpass, existing state-of-the-art methods. The ablation studies are particularly valuable, providing insights into the contribution of different components of the system (shared features, cls/reg outputs). Furthermore, the paper is exceptionally well-written and clearly explains the technical details. The code release is also a significant positive aspect, enabling reproducibility and further research. 

However, the paper also has some weaknesses. The alternating optimization scheme used to train the RPN and Fast R-CNN is a pragmatic solution, but lacks theoretical guarantees of convergence. While the authors demonstrate that it works well in practice, a more rigorous analysis of its convergence properties would be beneficial. Moreover, the evaluation of the RPN as a standalone proposal method doesn't conclusively demonstrate superiority over existing methods like Selective Search; the improvements primarily come from the combined system. Although the authors show comparable performance to SS, a more detailed comparison with updated, highly optimized SS implementations would be valuable.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Significant improvement in object detection speed without sacrificing accuracy.
*   Novel and effective use of shared convolutional features.
*   Strong empirical results on standard benchmarks.
*   Clear and well-written paper with thorough experimental analysis.
*   Publicly available code.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The alternating optimization scheme lacks theoretical justification.
*   The standalone RPN performance is not demonstrably superior to existing methods.
*   The impact of the shared layers on generalization is not deeply investigated.

**Overall:**

This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of object detection. While the alternating optimization scheme could benefit from further theoretical analysis, the practical results and innovative use of shared convolutional features are compelling. The efficiency gains achieved by this method are substantial, and the paper addresses a critical bottleneck in state-of-the-art object detection systems. I recommend acceptance, with a minor request for the authors to discuss the potential limitations of the alternating optimization approach in the final version.
