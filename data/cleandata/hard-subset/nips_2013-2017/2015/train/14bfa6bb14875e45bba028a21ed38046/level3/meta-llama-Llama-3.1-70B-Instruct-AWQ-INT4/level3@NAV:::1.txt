This paper proposes a novel Region Proposal Network (RPN) that shares convolutional features with object localization (classification + regression) to achieve efficient and accurate object detection. The authors demonstrate that their method, Faster R-CNN, is about 10 times faster than Fast R-CNN and slightly more accurate.

The main idea of this paper is to use a fully-convolutional network to predict object bounds and objectness scores at each position, which is then used to generate region proposals. The RPN is trained end-to-end to generate high-quality region proposals, which are used by Fast R-CNN for detection. The authors also propose a simple alternating optimization scheme to train the RPN and Fast R-CNN to share convolutional features.

The paper is well-written and easy to follow, with clear explanations of the proposed method and its advantages over existing approaches. The experimental results are also impressive, with the authors demonstrating state-of-the-art object detection accuracy on the PASCAL VOC 2007 and 2012 benchmarks.

However, there are some unclear points in the paper. For example, it is not clear why the RPN is translation-invariant while the method in [19] is not. Additionally, the authors do not provide a clear justification for the choice of hyperparameters, such as n=3 and k=9. The training procedure also seems complicated and unstable, and the authors do not report repeated runs or exploration of alternative training settings to justify the chosen procedure.

Overall, I would rate the paper as follows:

* Quality: 8/10 (the paper is technically sound, but there are some unclear points and the training procedure seems complicated)
* Clarity: 7/10 (the paper is well-written, but some parts are confusing)
* Originality: 9/10 (the paper proposes a novel method that achieves state-of-the-art results)
* Significance: 9/10 (the paper has significant implications for object detection and computer vision)

Arguments for acceptance:

* The paper proposes a novel and efficient method for object detection that achieves state-of-the-art results.
* The method is well-suited for real-time object detection applications.
* The paper provides a clear and detailed explanation of the proposed method and its advantages over existing approaches.

Arguments against acceptance:

* The paper has some unclear points, such as the translation invariance of the RPN and the choice of hyperparameters.
* The training procedure seems complicated and unstable.
* The paper does not provide a clear justification for the chosen training procedure and hyperparameters.
