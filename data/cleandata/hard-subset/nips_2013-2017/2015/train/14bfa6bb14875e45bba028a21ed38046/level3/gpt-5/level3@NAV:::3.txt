This paper proposes a two-stage detection framework that integrates a Region Proposal Network (RPN) with Fast R-CNN, sharing convolutional features to make proposals nearly cost-free and enabling near real-time detection (~5 fps with VGG-16). The RPN is a fully convolutional sliding-window module that predicts objectness and box regressions relative to translation-invariant anchors (multiple scales/aspect ratios). Alternating training is used to share conv layers between RPN and detector. Empirically, the approach matches or exceeds Selective Search/EdgeBoxes on PASCAL VOC while using as few as 300 proposals. Relative to prior work, this unifies proposal generation and detection (unlike SS/EB), contrasts with OverFeat’s one-stage detection and MultiBox’s non-translation-invariant anchors, and builds on SPPnet/Fast R-CNN’s shared feature compute.

Quality: The method is technically sound and well-motivated. Results on VOC 2007/2012 are strong (e.g., 73.2% mAP using VGG-16 with 300 proposals) and runtime is carefully measured. However, two issues merit attention: (1) The alternating optimization between RPN and Fast R-CNN appears to run only a single outer iteration; this implicitly assumes the step-1 proposals are close to optimal. Please report whether additional alternations (or partial joint training) improve either proposal recall-at-IoU or mAP. (2) The sliding window operates on top-level feature maps with stride 16; mapping back to the image, this can imply large spatial steps and potentially coarse localization (especially for small objects/high IoU). Please quantify the effective stride on input, analyze recall for small objects at IoU ≥ 0.7–0.8, and consider mitigations (smaller-stride features, dilation, or multi-scale features).

Clarity: The paper is generally clear and well-organized. One notation issue: in lines 173–174, please define h_a (and w_a) explicitly as the anchor height (and width), and ensure consistency with x_a, y_a. Also clarify whether anchors are indexed per spatial location or globally, and how regression heads are tied across k anchors.

Originality: The translation-invariant anchor mechanism and shared RPN-detector training distinguish the work from MultiBox/OverFeat and prior region-based methods, representing a novel and well-argued combination.

Significance: High. The unified, efficient pipeline removes a key bottleneck and is likely to be adopted broadly.

Pros:
- Elegant unification of proposals and detection with shared features; strong accuracy-speed trade-off.
- Clear improvements over SS/EB with far fewer proposals.
- Translation-invariant anchors reduce parameters and overfitting risk.

Cons:
- Limited exploration of alternating optimization iterations/joint training.
- Potential localization granularity issues from large feature stride.
- Minor notation ambiguity (h_a).
