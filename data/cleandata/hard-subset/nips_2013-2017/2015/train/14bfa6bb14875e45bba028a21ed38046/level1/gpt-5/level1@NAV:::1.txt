This paper proposes Region Proposal Networks (RPNs) that share convolutional features with a Fast R-CNN detector to produce nearly cost-free, high-quality proposals. An RPN is a fully convolutional mini-network sliding over shared feature maps, predicting objectness scores and bounding-box regressions relative to a set of translation-invariant anchors (3 scales × 3 aspect ratios). Training uses a multitask loss with smooth L1 regression and an alternating 4-step scheme to enable feature sharing between RPN and detector. On PASCAL VOC 2007/2012, the system achieves state-of-the-art mAP with only 300 proposals, and runs at 5 fps with VGG-16 (10 ms marginal cost for proposals). The paper includes ablations (shared vs unshared features, role of cls vs reg heads, NMS effects) and comparisons to Selective Search (SS) and EdgeBoxes (EB). Relative to OverFeat (one-stage) and MultiBox, the method emphasizes parameter-efficient, translation-invariant anchors and two-stage detection with adaptive pooling (SPP/ROI).

Quality: The methodology is technically sound with careful design choices (anchors, loss, sampling) and solid empirical support. The ablations convincingly diagnose where gains come from and justify the two-stage cascade over dense sliding windows. Runtime reporting is thorough and the released code supports reproducibility. Limitations: evaluation is confined to PASCAL; comparisons to stronger baselines (e.g., GPU-optimized proposal methods) and larger datasets are missing. The alternating training is pragmatic but precludes full end-to-end joint optimization; stability and convergence properties are not analyzed.

Clarity: The paper is well organized and clearly written. Architectural details, losses, anchor definitions, and optimization schedules are described sufficiently for reproduction. Minor clarity nit: the normalization/λ choice (footnote) and boundary-anchor handling could be consolidated earlier for readability.

Originality: The key novelty is learning a proposal mechanism that shares features with the detector and uses translation-invariant anchors with a compact output, distinguishing it from MultiBox and classical SS/EB. The analysis contrasting one-stage (OverFeat) vs two-stage is informative.

Significance: High. The approach removes the proposal bottleneck while improving accuracy, enabling practical, high-throughput detection. The ideas are likely to be widely adopted and extended.

Pros for acceptance:
- Elegant integration of proposals and detection with measurable accuracy/speed gains
- Clear, well-executed ablations; open-source implementation
- Novel, parameter-efficient anchor design with strong empirical validation

Cons against acceptance:
- Limited dataset scope (no COCO) and missing small-object analysis
- No fully joint end-to-end training or convergence discussion
- Comparisons to GPU-accelerated proposal baselines are absent
