Summary and relation to prior work:
This paper proposes Region Proposal Networks (RPNs), a fully convolutional subnetwork that predicts objectness scores and bounding-box regression offsets for a small set of translation-invariant anchors (multiple scales and aspect ratios) at each location on shared convolutional feature maps. The key contribution is to share features between a proposal generator and a Fast R-CNN detector, making proposals almost free at test time (≈10 ms), and enabling an efficient two-stage pipeline that achieves state-of-the-art accuracy on PASCAL VOC 2007/2012 with only 300 proposals. Training alternates between optimizing the RPN and Fast R-CNN to obtain shared conv layers.

This work builds on region-based CNNs (R-CNN [6], SPPnet [7], Fast R-CNN [5]) and addresses the proposal bottleneck (Selective Search [22], EdgeBoxes [24]). It relates to OverFeat [18] and MultiBox [3,20], which use CNNs to localize boxes. Compared to MultiBox, which predicts from a large set of global, non-translation-invariant priors, RPN defines a small, translation-invariant anchor set at every spatial position, uses a very lightweight head, and shares backbone features with the detector. Compared to OverFeat’s one-stage detection, the paper shows empirically that the two-stage cascade (class-agnostic proposals followed by class-specific classification/regression) is more accurate at comparable computation.

Strengths:
- Clear, elegant architectural idea with strong empirical validation. Sharing conv features between proposal and detection reduces total test-time to 198 ms with VGG-16 while improving mAP over SS/EB.
- Well-justified anchor design: multiple scales/aspect ratios at each location yields a compact, translation-invariant parameterization and far fewer parameters than MultiBox, helping regularization on modest training sets (VOC).
- Thorough ablations: sharing vs. unshared features; effect of proposal count, NMS, and turning off classification or regression heads; one-stage vs. two-stage comparison.
- Practical training recipe (4-step alternating) that avoids unstable joint training and converges reliably; plausible analysis of why feature sharing improves proposal quality.
- Results are strong and relevant to the NIPS community: state-of-the-art mAP on VOC 2007/2012 at practical speeds; code availability aids reproducibility.

Weaknesses and concerns:
- Joint end-to-end training of RPN + detector is deferred; alternating training is pragmatic but leaves open whether further gains are possible. The paper could at least report an attempt and failure modes.
- Sensitivity to anchor design is not deeply explored. Only 3 scales × 3 aspect ratios are used; it would be useful to quantify performance vs. anchor choices (including more elongated ratios) and per-class recall breakdowns (e.g., small objects).
- The method trains proposals on the same categories used for detection, so “class-agnostic” proposals may be biased toward those categories. Cross-dataset generalization (e.g., training RPN on VOC, testing proposals on ImageNet Detection/COCO, or unseen VOC classes) is not evaluated.
- Runtime comparisons are partly confounded by CPU vs. GPU implementations of baseline proposal methods. While the paper argues for algorithmic sharing, an apples-to-apples comparison (e.g., GPU EdgeBoxes or optimized SS) would strengthen the claim of “nearly free.”
- Clarity: some training details could be better consolidated. The normalization of losses (footnote about λ, Ncls, Nreg) and the treatment of cross-boundary anchors are scattered; a single subsection summarizing final hyperparameters and the exact minibatch composition would help reproduction. The choice of IoU thresholds (0.7 pos, 0.3 neg) merits a brief sensitivity analysis.

Quality:
Technically sound with careful experiments on VOC benchmarks. The loss, sampling strategy, and optimization are standard and appropriate; ablations support the central claims. The proposal-vs-detection disentanglement (testing a detector trained with SS but evaluated with RPN proposals) is particularly informative. Some evaluations that would further solidify the work (anchor sensitivity, generalization, end-to-end joint training) are missing but do not undermine the core contribution.

Clarity:
Generally well written and organized. The method is described precisely, with sensible design choices and justifications. Figures effectively illustrate the architecture and anchor concept. Minor suggestions: collect all training hyperparameters and normalization choices in one place; add a small diagram of the 4-step training schedule; report per-class AP to diagnose gains.

Originality:
Moderately high. Using a CNN to propose boxes is not new per se (OverFeat, MultiBox), but the anchor-based, translation-invariant formulation, its tight integration and feature sharing with Fast R-CNN, and the very lightweight head producing dense proposals are novel and important. The empirical demonstration that a learned, shared proposal mechanism can replace hand-engineered methods at lower cost is a significant conceptual advance.

Significance:
High. The approach addresses a major bottleneck in contemporary detectors and provides a general template (shared backbone + dense anchor-based proposal head) that others can build on. The speed/accuracy trade-off and code release make it likely to be adopted by practitioners. The implications extend beyond VOC: the method should scale to larger datasets and deeper backbones.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Attempt joint end-to-end training of the unified network and report results, even if negative, to inform the community.
- Provide sensitivity analyses: anchor scales/ratios, IoU thresholds for positives/negatives, NMS threshold, λ and loss normalizations, feature stride (e.g., using dilated convolutions to reduce stride for small objects).
- Add cross-dataset or cross-class transfer experiments to test proposal generality.
- Provide per-class AP and recall-at-IoU plots for small/medium/large objects; discuss failure modes (missed small objects due to stride 16).
- Clarify runtime breakdowns and ensure fair baselines (or explicitly state limitations).

Arguments for acceptance:
- Strong, simple idea with substantial empirical gains and practical impact.
- Solid ablations demonstrating why/when it works.
- Clear advancement over prior proposal methods and one-stage CNN localizers.
- Public code and reproducible setup.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Limited analysis of sensitivity to anchor design/hyperparameters.
- No joint end-to-end training result; alternating schedule may not be optimal.
- Proposal generalization beyond training categories not evaluated.
- Runtime comparisons partly confounded by implementation differences for baselines.

Overall: A significant and timely contribution that advances object detection both algorithmically and practically. I recommend acceptance.
