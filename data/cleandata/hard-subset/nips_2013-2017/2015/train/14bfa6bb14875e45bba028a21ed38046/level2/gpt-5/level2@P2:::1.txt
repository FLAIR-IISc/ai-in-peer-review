Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes Region Proposal Networks (RPNs), a fully convolutional module that predicts class-agnostic object proposals (bounding box coordinates and objectness) directly from shared feature maps. By sharing convolutional layers with a Fast R-CNN detector and training via a simple alternating scheme, the method eliminates the hand-crafted, CPU-bound proposal bottleneck (e.g., Selective Search, EdgeBoxes), yielding proposals at ~10 ms per image while improving overall detection accuracy. The core idea uses translation-invariant “anchors” at each sliding-window location across multiple scales and aspect ratios (3×3 convolution + sibling 1×1 conv heads for cls/reg). On PASCAL VOC 2007/2012, using VGG-16 and only 300 proposals per image, the system achieves 73.2%/70.4% mAP with a reported 5 fps end-to-end speed.

The work is positioned against R-CNN/SPPnet/Fast R-CNN, which amortize convolutions over proposals but rely on external proposal methods; and it contrasts with OverFeat and MultiBox, highlighting translation-invariant anchors, parameter efficiency, and learning proposals that explicitly share features with detection. The paper also includes ablations (sharing vs unshared features, number of proposals, turning off cls/reg heads), runtime measurements, Recall–IoU analysis, and a comparison to a one-stage dense sliding window baseline.

Quality
The methodology is technically sound and well-executed. The loss, anchor assignment, sampling, and optimization details are consistent with best practices. The alternating 4-step training is pragmatic and effective, and ablations substantiate the main claims:
- Proposal quality: The cls head meaningfully ranks proposals; the reg head materially improves localization (notably, removing reg reduces mAP from 56.8% to 52.1% in a controlled setup).
- Efficiency: With shared features, RPN adds ~10 ms, and using 300 proposals reduces region-wise compute, delivering net speedups over SS/EB while improving accuracy.
- Generalization across backbones: RPN quality improves with stronger backbones (ZF→VGG).
Comparisons to SS/EB are fair insofar as they reflect typical research implementations; the authors note the CPU/GPU dichotomy. Results are robust across multiple proposal counts, and the one-stage vs two-stage comparison is informative.

Some aspects could be strengthened: evaluation is limited to VOC; proposal recall/error analysis by object scale is discussed qualitatively but could be quantified; and no direct comparison to GPU-optimized proposal baselines or contemporary learned proposal methods (e.g., MultiBox on VOC) under identical backbones is provided. Nonetheless, these do not undermine the core contributions.

Clarity
The paper is clearly written, with sufficient architectural, training, and hyperparameter details to enable reproduction; code availability further helps. The anchor scheme and loss are explained well. Minor clarity improvements:
- Consolidate loss normalization and λ choices (currently partly in a footnote) and report sensitivity.
- Make the 4-step alternating optimization explicit in a concise algorithm box.
- Summarize key hyperparameters (strides, anchor scales/ratios, NMS threshold) in one place.

Originality
Integrating a learned, fully convolutional, translation-invariant proposal network that shares features with the detector is a compelling and novel synthesis. The anchor mechanism and the alternating multi-task training that unifies proposal and detection are substantial contributions beyond prior R-CNN/SPP/OverFeat/MultiBox work. The view of proposals as a dense prediction task on shared feature maps for class-agnostic objectness is elegant and influential.

Significance
High. The approach removes a major bottleneck, pushes state-of-the-art accuracy, and upgrades practicality by improving speed/accuracy trade-offs. The method is likely to be broadly adopted and extended (e.g., to multi-scale features, other backbones, joint training), making it a significant advance for detection systems.

Strengths
- Clear, elegant architecture that unifies proposals and detection via shared convolutional features.
- Strong empirical results: higher mAP with 300 proposals, substantial speedups, comprehensive ablations.
- Translation-invariant anchors offer a parameter-efficient design and good localization.
- Practical training recipe (alternating optimization) that converges quickly; code is provided.
- Thorough runtime analysis and proposal recall diagnostic.

Weaknesses
- Joint end-to-end training of RPN+detector is deferred; alternating training is effective but leaves open whether joint optimization would yield further gains.
- Anchor design is hand-chosen (3 scales × 3 aspect ratios). Sensitivity analysis and/or learning anchor priors are not explored.
- Limited dataset scope (VOC). Small object performance and scale sensitivity are not deeply analyzed; stride 16 may under-serve small objects.
- Comparisons omit GPU-accelerated classic proposal methods and a head-to-head with MultiBox under the same backbone/dataset.
- Some heuristic choices (NMS threshold 0.7, ignoring cross-boundary anchors) could be justified more systematically.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide sensitivity studies for anchor scales/ratios and NMS threshold; consider learning anchor priors or using k-means initialization while retaining translation invariance.
- Report recall and mAP breakdown by object size and aspect ratio; analyze small-object errors and consider reducing effective stride (e.g., dilated conv or using earlier feature maps).
- Include preliminary results on joint end-to-end training (single-stage multi-task loss with proposals feeding the detector) to assess convergence and gains.
- Compare to a strong GPU-optimized EB/SS baseline or to MultiBox under identical conditions.
- Clarify treatment of cross-boundary anchors and explore weighted losses rather than discarding them.
- Discuss memory usage and batch size constraints for VGG-16, and the effect of SVD compression with the proposed system.

Reasons for acceptance
- Addresses a central bottleneck with a principled, effective, and simple approach.
- Demonstrated accuracy and speed improvements with solid ablations and open-source code.
- High potential impact; likely to become a new standard detection pipeline component.

Reasons for rejection
- None critical. The main limitations (dataset scope, lack of joint training) are incremental and do not detract from the core contribution.

Overall evaluation: Strong accept.

Reviewer confidence: High.
