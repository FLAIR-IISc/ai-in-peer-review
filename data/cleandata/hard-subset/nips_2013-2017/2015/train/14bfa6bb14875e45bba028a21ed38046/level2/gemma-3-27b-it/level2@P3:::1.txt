This paper introduces a Region Proposal Network (RPN) to address the bottleneck of region proposal computation in object detection pipelines. The key idea is to share convolutional features between the RPN and the detection network (Fast R-CNN), enabling nearly cost-free region proposals. The RPN is a fully convolutional network that predicts object bounds and objectness scores, and is trained end-to-end with Fast R-CNN through an alternating optimization scheme. The authors demonstrate state-of-the-art accuracy on PASCAL VOC 2007 and 2012, achieving a 5fps frame rate with a VGG-16 model.

The work is well-motivated. Prior work had significantly reduced the cost of detection *given* region proposals, identifying proposal generation as the remaining bottleneck. The proposed solution of a learned, shared-feature proposal network is a natural and effective approach. This aligns with the broader trend in the field of exploiting GPU parallelism and end-to-end learning for performance improvements. The paper clearly articulates the limitations of existing methods like Selective Search, particularly their CPU implementation and lack of shared computation. The introduction of anchor boxes with varying scales and aspect ratios is a crucial component, allowing the RPN to effectively handle objects of different shapes and sizes. The experimental results are thorough and demonstrate significant improvements over existing methods.  The ablation studies are particularly valuable in dissecting the contribution of different components of the proposed system. The inclusion of running time measurements provides a practical assessment of the method’s performance. The code release further strengthens the contribution.

A potential weakness is the relatively limited discussion of the trade-offs between proposal quality and computational cost. While the paper demonstrates a good balance, a more in-depth analysis of how different parameters (e.g., number of anchors, anchor scales and aspect ratios) affect both accuracy and speed would be beneficial. The method relies on an alternating optimization scheme, which, while pragmatic, isn't thoroughly analyzed for convergence properties.  A more rigorous theoretical justification or empirical analysis of the convergence behavior would enhance the work. The comparison to one-stage detection methods like OverFeat is useful, but could be expanded upon; a more detailed discussion of why the two-stage approach remains superior would be insightful. While the paper references related work, it could benefit from a more detailed discussion of other recent proposal generation methods beyond Selective Search and EdgeBoxes. Specifically, mentioning methods that also employ deep learning for proposal generation would strengthen the related work section.  

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* **Novelty:** The RPN is a novel and effective approach to region proposal generation, addressing a key bottleneck in object detection pipelines.
* **Significance:** The results demonstrate state-of-the-art accuracy and a significant speed improvement, making the method practical for real-time applications.
* **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and clearly explains the proposed method, experimental setup, and results.
* **Quality:** The experiments are thorough and well-designed, and the results are convincingly demonstrated. The ablation studies are particularly helpful.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

* Limited analysis of the trade-offs between proposal quality and computational cost.
* Lack of a thorough theoretical analysis of the convergence properties of the alternating optimization scheme.



Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of object detection. The proposed RPN is a clever and effective solution to a critical problem, and the experimental results are compelling. The suggested improvements are relatively minor and do not detract from the overall quality of the work. This paper is highly recommended for acceptance.
