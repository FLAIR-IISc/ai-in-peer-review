Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper addresses the computational bottleneck of region proposals in modern region-based object detectors. Building on SPPnet and Fast R-CNN, the authors introduce Region Proposal Networks (RPNs), a fully convolutional module that shares backbone features with the detector to produce class-agnostic object proposals with objectness scores and bounding-box regressions. A key design is translation-invariant anchors (3 scales × 3 aspect ratios per spatial location) and a small 3×3 conv “head” with two sibling 1×1 conv layers for classification and regression. Training uses a multi-task loss on sampled anchors and an alternating 4-step scheme to enable feature sharing between RPN and Fast R-CNN. On PASCAL VOC 2007/2012, the method achieves state-of-the-art mAP using only 300 proposals while reducing proposal time to ~10 ms when sharing features; the entire pipeline runs at ~5 fps with VGG-16. Ablations analyze sharing vs. unshared features, numbers of proposals, contributions of classification vs. regression in RPN, NMS, and a one-stage sliding-window variant (OverFeat-style), which underperforms the proposed two-stage cascade.

The work relates to: R-CNN/SSPnet/Fast R-CNN (shared conv features, ROI pooling, detection), OverFeat (one-stage sliding window localization/detection), and MultiBox (predict multiple proposals via a dense fc output). The paper argues for translation-invariant anchors with fewer parameters and practical training on smaller datasets than MultiBox, while achieving better speed/quality tradeoffs and tight integration with the detector.

Quality:
Technical approach is sound and clearly justified. The anchor mechanism, loss, sampling, and optimization are well specified, with reasonable hyperparameters and training schedules. Experiments are extensive on VOC with sensible ablations: effect of sharing, number of proposals, turning off cls/reg branches, NMS, backbone depth, and analysis of recall-vs-IoU. The claim of “nearly cost-free” proposals is substantiated by end-to-end timing with shared features. The comparison to one-stage sliding windows further supports the two-stage design choice.

Points that could be strengthened:
- Evaluation breadth: Results are largely on VOC with mAP@0.5. Additional results at stricter IoU thresholds (e.g., mAP@0.7) or on a more diverse dataset (e.g., MS COCO) would better quantify localization quality and robustness to small objects.
- Sensitivity analyses: While the anchor design (3 scales × 3 ratios) is intuitive, an explicit study of sensitivity to anchor scales/ratios/feature stride or learning anchor shapes would strengthen the case that the design is not dataset-specific.
- Training scheme: The alternating 4-step schedule is pragmatic; however, a brief investigation of joint end-to-end training stability (even if negative) or a discussion of when alternating is necessary would be informative.
- Baselines: The discussion contrasts RPN with MultiBox; a head-to-head baseline (proposal recall, mAP, and runtime) on VOC would be valuable if feasible.

Clarity:
The paper is clearly written and well organized. The network architecture, anchor parameterization, labeling policy, loss, sampling, and implementation details (strides, IoU thresholds, NMS, clipping, learning rates, and layer freezing) are described with enough detail to reproduce results. The ablation experiments are easy to follow and informative. Minor editorial issues in the reference formatting aside, the presentation is strong. Providing a schematic of the 4-step training pipeline would further aid clarity.

Originality:
While grounded in Fast R-CNN and FCNs, the paper’s contributions are novel and well-differentiated:
- Using a shared, fully convolutional proposal mechanism with translation-invariant anchors integrated directly atop the detector’s feature maps.
- A simple, effective alternating optimization to produce a unified network.
- A design that avoids large fully connected output layers and dataset-specific anchor clustering, contrasting with MultiBox.
This is an innovative and pragmatic contribution to the detection pipeline.

Significance:
High. The method removes the primary bottleneck in region-based detection while improving accuracy, enabling practical real-time-ish performance with very deep backbones. The design is modular, broadly applicable, and likely to influence both academic and applied detection systems. The careful analysis and released code enhance impact and reproducibility.

Pros:
- Elegant, simple architecture that cleanly integrates proposals with detection via shared features.
- Strong empirical results: state-of-the-art mAP on VOC with 300 proposals; compelling speedups (proposal time ~10 ms; full pipeline ~198 ms per image with VGG-16).
- Thorough ablations that isolate design choices (sharing, classification vs. regression, proposal counts, NMS).
- Clear articulation of differences from OverFeat and MultiBox; convincing evidence for two-stage cascades over one-stage sliding windows in this context.
- Reproducibility aided by detailed implementation notes and code release.

Cons / questions for authors:
- Limited evaluation at stricter localization thresholds and on larger/more diverse datasets. Can you report mAP at IoU≥0.7 and proposal recall at IoU≥0.7/0.8, especially for small objects?
- How sensitive is performance to the choice of anchor scales/ratios and the 16-pixel stride? Have you tried adding smaller anchors or multi-level features to better handle small objects?
- Can you provide results for end-to-end joint training (single-stage backprop) or explain instability observed?
- How do results change with different NMS thresholds, or with Soft-NMS? Is proposal diversity affected?
- A direct quantitative comparison to MultiBox (proposal recall, runtime, detector mAP) on a common setup would strengthen claims.
- Clarify memory usage and training time for the 4-step schedule, and whether the approach scales to deeper backbones without prohibitive memory.

Overall recommendation:
Strong accept. This paper presents a clear, well-motivated, and impactful advance in object detection. It is technically sound, original in its integration of proposals with shared features, and demonstrates substantial practical significance with comprehensive empirical support. With additional analyses on stricter IoU thresholds and broader datasets, the case would be even stronger, but the current evidence already meets a high bar for NIPS.
