This paper introduces Region Proposal Networks (RPNs) that share convolutional features with a Fast R-CNN detector to generate near “free” class-agnostic proposals. The RPN is a fully convolutional module that predicts objectness scores and bounding-box deltas relative to translation-invariant anchors of multiple scales and aspect ratios. An alternating training scheme aligns the proposal and detection networks to share features. On PASCAL VOC 2007/2012, the method achieves state-of-the-art mAP (73.2%/70.4%) using only 300 proposals and runs at ~5 fps with VGG-16; proposal computation adds ~10 ms, removing the traditional bottleneck of Selective Search. Relative to prior work (SPPnet, Fast R-CNN), this replaces CPU proposals with a learned, GPU-friendly, shared module; compared to MultiBox and OverFeat, it emphasizes anchor translation invariance, parameter efficiency, and a two-stage cascade that outperforms a one-stage sliding-window variant.

Quality: The methodology is technically sound. The loss, anchor assignment, sampling, and optimization are well specified; ablations isolate the roles of classification vs regression, proposal counts, NMS, and shared vs unshared features. Runtime measurements are clear. Empirical support is strong on VOC; however, results lack variance estimates/significance tests and broader datasets.

Clarity: The paper is clearly written and organized, with sufficient detail to reproduce, aided by released code. Minor improvements: consolidate the footnote on λ/normalization into the main text; clarify the effect of ignoring cross-boundary anchors during training on recall for large objects.

Originality: While building on Fast R-CNN and FCNs, the integration of a lightweight, anchor-based, translation-invariant RPN that shares features and the pragmatic alternating training are novel and well motivated; the contrast with MultiBox/OverFeat is articulated.

Significance: High. The approach eliminates the proposal bottleneck while improving accuracy and has strong practical impact for detection systems; it is likely to be widely adopted and extended.

Limitations and constructive suggestions: Evaluate on larger/modern benchmarks (e.g., COCO) and report variability across seeds. Explore joint end-to-end optimization, learned/auto-tuned anchor sets, smaller strides/multi-scale features, and analysis of failure cases (small/occluded objects). Provide a fairer speed comparison to GPU-optimized classical proposals and discuss NMS sensitivity.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Strong empirical gains with compelling speed/accuracy trade-off
- Clear, elegant design with thorough ablations and code release
- Novel and impactful integration of proposals with shared features

Arguments against acceptance:
- Evaluation limited to VOC; no statistical robustness
- Comparisons omit GPU-optimized proposal baselines
- Alternating, not fully joint, training; limited analysis of failure modes
