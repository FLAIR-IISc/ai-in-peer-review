Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes Region Proposal Networks (RPNs) that generate class-agnostic object proposals by sharing convolutional features with a Fast R-CNN detector. An RPN is a fully convolutional network operating on shared feature maps; at each spatial location it predicts k proposals (anchors) at multiple scales and aspect ratios via sibling classification (objectness) and regression branches. Anchors are translation-invariant reference boxes, enabling a small output dimensionality relative to prior proposal nets (e.g., MultiBox [20]). Training uses a multi-task loss akin to Fast R-CNN [5] with positive/negative anchor assignment by IoU thresholds. To enable shared features with Fast R-CNN, the paper adopts a pragmatic 4-step alternating training scheme. On PASCAL VOC 2007/2012, the unified system achieves state-of-the-art mAP (73.2%/70.4%) while running at 5 fps with VGG-16; the marginal cost for proposals is ~10 ms on GPU, substantially faster than Selective Search [22] or EdgeBoxes [24]. Ablations analyze the roles of classification vs regression, NMS, number of proposals, and shared vs unshared features. The paper situates the work relative to SPPnet [7], Fast R-CNN [5], OverFeat [18], and MultiBox [3,20], arguing for a two-stage proposal+detector cascade with shared computation.

Quality
The methodology is technically sound and carefully evaluated. The anchor mechanism and multi-task training are well-specified, including IoU thresholds, sampling strategy, loss normalization, and optimization settings. The alternating training procedure is justified pragmatically and shown to converge. Experiments are extensive on VOC, with clear comparisons to SS/EB, analyses of proposal recall vs IoU, and ablations disentangling contributions (e.g., proposal ranking vs regression, NMS effects, shared features). Claims about speed and accuracy are supported by measurements and comparisons on the same backbone (ZF, VGG-16).

Areas where additional evidence would strengthen the paper:
- Generalization beyond VOC (e.g., to larger datasets or more diverse object scales) is not shown; however, this is an optional extension rather than essential for the main claim.
- Sensitivity to anchor configurations (number, scales, aspect ratios) and feature stride is only briefly discussed; a small study would clarify robustness.
- Runtime reporting would benefit from explicit hardware details and a standardized measurement protocol (batching, I/O, NMS timing), to aid reproducibility and fair comparison.

Clarity
The paper is clearly written and well organized. The RPN architecture, anchor parameterization, loss, and training schema are described with sufficient detail to enable reproduction. Figures and ablations are informative. Suggestions for clarity:
- Provide a concise algorithm box for the 4-step alternating training procedure with the data used in each step.
- Summarize all key hyperparameters (IoU thresholds, λ, NMS threshold, proposal counts for training/test) in one place.
- Explicitly state the GPU model and cuDNN/Caffe versions for the reported runtimes.

Originality
The work introduces a novel and effective coupling of region proposal generation with the detection network via shared convolutional features and a translation-invariant anchor mechanism. While prior work explored deep nets for localization (OverFeat [18]) and proposal generation (MultiBox [3,20]), this paper’s anchor design, low-parameter heads, feature sharing with a region-based detector, and practical alternating training constitute a clear advance over existing approaches in 2015. The differences to MultiBox (anchor invariance and parameter efficiency) and to one-stage sliding-window detection are concretely analyzed.

Significance
The contribution is substantial. By making proposals nearly cost-free on top of shared features, the paper removes the dominant runtime bottleneck in region-based detectors and improves accuracy. The approach is likely to influence both research and practice, enabling faster and more accurate detection pipelines and opening paths to end-to-end learning of proposals and detection. The empirical gains (notably with VGG-16) and the practical speedups represent a clear step forward for object detection.

Constructive suggestions
Essential (feasible for camera-ready):
- Clarify runtime measurement setup: GPU model, batch size, inclusion of preprocessing/postprocessing, and whether timings include NMS and proposal transfer.
- Provide a short sensitivity analysis on anchor settings: e.g., varying aspect ratios or adding a smaller scale, and the effect on small-object recall and mAP.
- Report per-class AP tables (possibly in supplementary) and a brief failure analysis (common miss/false positive modes), to guide future use and extensions.

Optional (nice-to-have):
- Explore joint end-to-end training of RPN+Fast R-CNN (even a brief negative or partial result) to complement the alternating scheme.
- Include results with a smaller stride or dilated convs to assess small-object performance.
- Compare to a strong GPU-optimized variant of a hand-crafted proposal method (if available) to further isolate algorithmic advantages from hardware acceleration.

Pros
- Strong, simple idea: proposal generation integrated with and amortized by the detector’s conv features.
- Elegant anchor design with translation-invariant parameterization and low output dimensionality.
- Thorough empirical validation with insightful ablations; improved accuracy and large speed gains.
- Clear and reproducible methodology; released code.

Cons
- Experiments limited to VOC; broader generalization not demonstrated.
- Limited analysis of hyperparameter sensitivity (anchors, NMS threshold, λ).
- Runtimes could be documented more rigorously for standardized comparison.

Overall assessment
A high-quality, original, and impactful contribution that convincingly advances the state of the art in object detection by unifying proposal generation with detection. The work is well executed and clearly presented. With minor clarifications and small additional analyses, the paper would be an excellent fit for NIPS. Recommended for acceptance.
