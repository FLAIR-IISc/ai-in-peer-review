

This paper addresses the problem of retrieving natural sentences that describe a given sequence of images and forming a coherent sequence of sentences. While this problem is significant in computer vision and pattern recognition, it closely relates to generating multiple sentences for video content (e.g., Rohrbach et al., 2014), where a video can be segmented into shots, each described by a key frame. However, the paper fails to cite this line of work.  

The paper is well-structured and clearly written, with a well-defined problem statement and a solution employing solid techniques. The proposed CRCN model effectively captures relationships between image sequences and sentence sequences. Its architecture is technically sound, and it leverages entity-based coherence models to model discourse relationships effectively.  

The sentence generation method, however, is reasonable but notably primitive: each sentence is directly associated with training images similar to the given image, and sequences are concatenated in image order without addressing temporal or contextual dependencies.  

In Section 4.1, the authors state, "We reuse the blog data of Disneyland from the dataset of [11], and newly collect the data of NYC, using the same crawling method with [11]." The dataset has not been publicly released, and the referenced paper [11] does not detail the crawling methodology. This raises concerns about authorship anonymity, as the authors appear to be the same as those in [11].  

Equation (2) may be incorrect, as it implies \(s_t\) depends solely on \(o_t\), suggesting the network is not fully connected. While the model’s architecture is technically sound, the sentence generation approach is ad-hoc. Given the methodological limitations and citation gap, this paper is suitable for acceptance as a poster.
