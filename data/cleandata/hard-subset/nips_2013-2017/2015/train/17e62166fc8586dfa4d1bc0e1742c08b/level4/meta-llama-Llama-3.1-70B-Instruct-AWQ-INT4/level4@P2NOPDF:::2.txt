The paper tackles the challenging problem of generating a sequence of consistent sentences to describe a sequence of images from blog posts. To achieve this, the authors propose a novel approach that first retrieves the K=5 most similar images and associated sentences from the training set for each query image. The primary contribution of the paper lies in defining a method to select the most relevant sentences for the query image sequence, thereby providing a coherent description.

The proposed approach involves embedding sentences in a vector space and modeling the sequence of sentences using a bidirectional LSTM. The output of the bidirectional LSTM is then passed through a ReLU and fully connected layer, followed by a compatibility score calculation between the image and sentence. Additionally, a local coherence model is incorporated to enforce compatibility between sentences.

The paper has several strengths. Firstly, it proposes a novel and effective architecture for retrieving coherent sentences for an image sequence. Secondly, it provides an extensive quantitative, qualitative, and human evaluation, demonstrating the superiority of the proposed approach over several baselines that do not use coherence. The authors also provide an ablation experiment, removing the coherence model, to further validate their approach. Lastly, the authors have committed to releasing the source code and dataset.

However, there are some weaknesses and areas of clarification required. Firstly, the paper claims that there is "no mechanism for the coherence between sentences" in [5]. Although this is not the primary contribution of [5], it does predict an intermediate semantic representation of videos, which is coherent across sentences by modeling the topic of the multi-sentence description. This point requires further clarification.

Secondly, there are some discrepancies in the description and illustration of the model architecture. Specifically, Figure 2b does not seem to correspond to the description in Section 3.3/Equation (2). While Figure 2b implies that the fully connected layer is connected to all sentences, Equation (2) suggests that the parameters are shared across sentences but only connected to the vector representing a single sentence. This inconsistency needs to be addressed.

Thirdly, the use of BLEU as an evaluation metric may not be the most suitable choice, especially when there is only a single reference sentence. The Meteor metric (http://www.cs.cmu.edu/~alavie/METEOR/) may be a better alternative.

Fourthly, the use of two linear functions in Equation (2) (W{f2}, W{f1}) applied in succession is unclear. Given that two linear functions can be reduced to a single linear function, the benefit of this approach is not immediately apparent. An ablation study demonstrating the benefit of these functions would be valuable.

Fifthly, the use of the same parameters for the fully connected layers in the BRNN output (o_t) and the local coherence model q (Equation 2) requires further justification.

Lastly, it is unclear whether the paragraph vector [16] is fine-tuned or kept fixed during training. This point requires clarification.

After reading the rebuttal, I recommend accepting the paper. The authors have successfully addressed issues with the formulation, evaluation, and related work. The proposed changes have improved the paper, and the additional clarification regarding the paragraph vector [16] has strengthened the manuscript. Overall, the paper proposes an interesting new model to retrieve coherent sentences for an image stream, which is convincingly evaluated.
