

This paper addresses the underexplored problem of generating coherent sentence sequences from image streams, rather than single-image descriptions. The authors propose a novel multimodal architecture called Coherent Recurrent Convolutional Networks (CRCN), integrating convolutional neural networks for image representation, bidirectional recurrent networks for sentence modeling, and an entity-based local coherence model to ensure fluent transitions between sentences. The approach leverages large-scale user-generated blog data (20K posts, 140K images) as text-image parallel training resources, avoiding the need for manual annotation. The paper demonstrates superior performance over strong baselines (including extensions of single-sentence generation methods) using both quantitative metrics (BLEU, CIDEr, top-K recall) and user studies via Amazon Mechanical Turk.

**Strengths:**  
*Originality*: This is the first work to explicitly model image streams as input and sentence sequences as output, extending the problem beyond single-image captioning. The paper clearly differentiates itself from related video-captioning work (e.g., [23, 32]) by addressing the unique challenge of abrupt visual changes in image streams requiring explicit coherence modeling. The blog-based dataset creation is also a novel, scalable solution to the data scarcity problem.  
*Significance*: The tourism-focused experiments (NYC/Disneyland) demonstrate practical applicability, and the unsupervised nature of the approach suggests broad domain transferability. The work advances the state of the art by addressing a meaningful gap in multimodal understanding—jointly modeling visual content and linguistic coherence across sequences.  
*Quality*: The CRCN architecture is well-motivated and technically sound. The integration of the coherence model (validated via ablation studies) directly addresses a critical weakness in prior work. Quantitative results consistently show CRCN outperforming baselines, with statistically significant gains in retrieval metrics (e.g., top-5 recall). The user studies (Table 2) further corroborate the qualitative advantage, particularly for longer sequences where coherence is paramount.  
*Clarity*: The paper is well-organized, with clear problem formulation (Fig. 1), thorough methodology (Sections 3.1–3.3), and comprehensive experimental validation. The blog preprocessing pipeline (Section 2) is sufficiently detailed for reproducibility.

**Weaknesses:**  
*Related Work*: The discussion of video-to-sentence work (e.g., [23, 32]) is insufficient. The paper should explicitly contrast CRCN with *Jointly Modeling Deep Video and Compositional Text* (Xu et al., AAAI 2015), which also bridges vision and language for sequential outputs but focuses on video clips rather than user-generated image streams. This omission weakens the positioning of the contribution.  
*Statistical Rigor*: While user study results (Table 2) show CRCN outperforming RCN (the coherence-ablated variant), the paper lacks significance testing (e.g., p-values or confidence intervals) to confirm whether the observed improvements (e.g., 53.2% vs. 49.8% preference in Table 2) are statistically meaningful. This is critical for claims about the coherence model’s impact.  
*Scope Clarification*: The paper’s emphasis on tourism datasets could be broadened to discuss how the unsupervised blog approach generalizes to non-tourism domains (e.g., personal diaries, event documentation), strengthening the significance argument.

**Recommendation:**  
The paper makes a strong, original contribution to multimodal sequence generation with a well-executed methodology and compelling results. The weaknesses are minor and addressable through revisions. I recommend **acceptance** with the following actions: (1) Incorporate Xu et al. (AAAI 2015) into the related work; (2) Add statistical significance tests for user study results; (3) Elaborate on domain generalization potential. These revisions would further solidify the paper’s impact and clarity. The work is technically sound, addresses a meaningful gap, and advances the field beyond incremental improvements in image captioning.
