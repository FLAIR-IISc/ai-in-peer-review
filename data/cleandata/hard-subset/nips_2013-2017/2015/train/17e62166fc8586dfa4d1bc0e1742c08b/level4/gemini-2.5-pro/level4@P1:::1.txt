This paper addresses the problem of retrieving a coherent sequence of natural language sentences to describe an input stream of images. This is presented as an extension of the well-studied single image-to-sentence task, moving to sequence-to-sequence modeling. The authors propose a novel multimodal architecture, the Coherent Recurrent Convolutional Network (CRCN), which learns the mapping from image sequences to sentence sequences. The model integrates three main components: a Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) for image representation, a Bidirectional Recurrent Neural Network (BRNN) to model the flow of text content, and an entity-based local coherence model to ensure smooth and logical transitions between sentences. The model is trained on a large dataset of blog posts, treating them as a source of parallel image-text data. At test time, the system retrieves candidate sentences for each image in a query stream and then uses the learned CRCN model to score and rank the candidate sequences to find the most appropriate description.

**Strengths**

*   **Problem Significance and Novelty:** The paper tackles an important and challenging problem that is a natural next step for the field of image description. To my knowledge, this is one of the first works to formally address the generation of a paragraph-like description for an unstructured stream of images, which has many practical applications.
*   **Technical Soundness:** The proposed CRCN model is technically sound. Its architecture, combining a CNN for visual features, a BRNN for contextual language modeling, and an entity-based coherence model, is a solid and well-reasoned approach. The inclusion of the coherence model to explicitly handle discourse relationships is a particularly strong contribution that appropriately addresses the challenges of multi-sentence generation.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is well-written, clearly organized, and easy to follow. The problem statement, proposed method, and experimental setup are all explained effectively.

**Weaknesses**

*   **Missing Related Work:** The paper fails to discuss the extensive body of work on video description (e.g., Rohrbach et al., ICCV 2013; Venugopalan et al., CVPR 2015). A video can be viewed as a temporally structured sequence of images (or shots, represented by keyframes), making this literature highly relevant to the problem of describing image streams. A discussion of how this work differs from and relates to the video description task is a necessary addition.
*   **Sentence Generation Method:** The method for generating the final sentence sequence is somewhat simplistic. The approach relies on retrieving sentences associated with the nearest-neighbor training images and then re-ranking these retrieved sequences. While this is a reasonable baseline, it is more of a retrieval-and-composition framework than a true generative one. This may limit the system's ability to produce truly novel sentences that are not present in the training corpus.
*   **Reproducibility and Anonymity Concerns:** A significant concern arises from the dataset used. The authors state, "We reuse the blog data of Disneyland from the dataset of [11]," but this dataset is not public. Furthermore, the paper does not describe the data collection methodology in sufficient detail, instead referring to [11], which also appears to lack these specifics. This lack of transparency hinders the reproducibility of the presented results. This close reliance on a specific, non-public dataset from a prior publication also raises concerns about maintaining the anonymity of the double-blind review process.
*   **Potential Error in Formulation:** There may be a minor error or point of confusion in Equation (2). As written, the equation `Wf2Wf1[O|q] = [S|g]` suggests that each output vector `st` (a column in `S`) is derived solely from the corresponding `ot` (a column in `O`). This would imply that the final layers are not fully connected in the way one might expect, as there would be no mixing of information across time steps at this stage. The authors should clarify this formulation.

**Conclusion**

Overall, this paper makes a valuable contribution by introducing and tackling the novel problem of describing image streams with sentence sequences. The proposed CRCN model is well-designed, and the inclusion of a coherence model is a key strength. However, the work is weakened by its omission of relevant literature in video description, a generation method that feels somewhat ad-hoc, and serious concerns regarding reproducibility and anonymity. Despite these issues, the paper's novelty and the soundness of its core model merit its inclusion in the conference. I recommend acceptance as a poster presentation.
