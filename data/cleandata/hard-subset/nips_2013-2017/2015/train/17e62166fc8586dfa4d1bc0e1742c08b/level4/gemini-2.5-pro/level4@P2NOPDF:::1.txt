This paper addresses the problem of generating a coherent sequence of natural language sentences to describe a corresponding sequence of images. This is a significant problem at the intersection of computer vision and natural language processing.

The paper is well-written and easy to follow. The problem is clearly stated, and the proposed solution is built on a set of solid techniques. The core contribution is a neural network model, the Coherent Recurrent Convolutional Network (CRCN), designed to learn the relationships between image sequences and sentence sequences. The model's architecture is technically sound. A key strength is its integration of an entity-based coherence model, which effectively captures discourse relationships between sentences, leading to more natural and story-like descriptions.

Despite these strengths, the paper has several notable weaknesses:

1.  **Missing Related Work:** The problem is closely related to generating multiple sentences that describe video content. A video can be viewed as a sequence of images (e.g., keyframes from video shots), making work in video description highly relevant. However, the authors fail to cite or discuss this entire line of research (e.g., Rohrbach et al., "Coherent Multi-Sentence Video Description with Variable Level of Detail," 2014). This omission is significant, as it prevents a proper assessment of the paper's novelty in the context of existing literature.

2.  **Simplistic Generation Method:** While the CRCN model for scoring image-sentence pairs is sophisticated, the method for generating new sentence sequences is comparatively primitive. For a given test image, a sentence is selected by finding the most similar training image and reusing its associated sentence. The final story is then formed by simply concatenating these retrieved sentences in the order of the input images. This retrieval-and-concatenation approach is rather ad-hoc and does not fully leverage the generative capabilities that a neural model could offer.

3.  **Reproducibility and Anonymity Concerns:** The authors state that they "reuse the blog data of Disneyland from the dataset of [11], and newly collect the data of NYC, using the same crawling method with [11]." There are two issues here. First, the dataset is not made publicly available. Second, the cited paper [11] does not provide sufficient detail on the crawling methodology. This lack of transparency makes it impossible for other researchers to reproduce the experiments or build upon this work. Furthermore, the reliance on a private, vaguely described data collection method from a specific recent paper strongly suggests that the authors of this paper and [11] are the same, which compromises the integrity of the double-blind review process.

4.  **Potential Technical Inaccuracy:** Equation (2) appears to be incorrect. As written, it implies that the state `st` is derived solely from the output `ot`. This would suggest a simple feed-forward connection rather than the intended recurrent structure, where the state at time `t` should also depend on the state at time `t-1`. The authors should clarify or correct this formulation.

In summary, the paper introduces a technically sound model for an interesting and important problem. The use of a coherence model is a commendable strength. However, the work is undermined by a significant failure to acknowledge related literature, a simplistic generation method that feels disconnected from the model's complexity, and serious issues regarding experimental reproducibility and the preservation of anonymity.

Given the solid core contribution but also the notable flaws, I recommend this paper for acceptance as a poster. This recommendation is made with the expectation that the authors will, at a minimum, address the missing related work and clarify the formulation of Equation (2) in the final version.
