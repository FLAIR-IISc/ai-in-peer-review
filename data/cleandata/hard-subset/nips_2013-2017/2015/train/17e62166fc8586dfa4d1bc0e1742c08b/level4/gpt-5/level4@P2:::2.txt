Summary
This paper addresses the task of describing an image stream with a coherent sequence of natural sentences. Unlike most prior work that maps a single image to a single sentence, the authors extend both input and output to sequences. The proposed coherent recurrent convolutional network (CRCN) retrieves candidate sentences by finding K-nearest neighbor images (K=5) for each query image, then scores and selects a sentence sequence by combining: (i) CNN features for images (VGG), (ii) a bidirectional RNN (BRNN) over sentence embeddings (paragraph vectors) to model content flow across sentences, and (iii) an entity-grid-based local coherence model [1] to encourage fluent sentence-to-sentence transitions. The BRNN and coherence signals are passed through ReLU and two fully connected layers, then combined with image features in a max-margin objective that encourages aligned image–sentence sequences to score higher than mismatches. Search over exponentially many candidates is approximated via a divide-and-conquer beam strategy. Experiments on large NYC and Disneyland blog datasets (≈20K posts, 140K images) show improvements over strong single-sentence baselines [9, 12, 30] and global matching [21] using BLEU/CIDEr/METEOR and retrieval metrics, with additional AMT user studies.

Relation to prior work
The paper builds on image captioning methods that align text and images via CNNs and RNNs [5, 8, 9, 12, 19, 26, 30], as well as retrieval-based caption transfer [6, 8, 21, 26]. It is closest to sequence modeling approaches (e.g., LRCN for video description [5]) and to entity-based coherence modeling [1]. Unlike video-captioning works, the method explicitly enforces local coherence across multiple sentences for still-image streams, where visual discontinuities are common. It also differs from [11], which retrieves image sequences for a given paragraph rather than generating paragraphs for image streams.

Strengths
- Novel problem setup and architecture: Extends captioning to image streams and integrates a BRNN with an entity-grid coherence model in a unified retrieval-and-ranking framework.
- Comprehensive evaluation: Strong quantitative results across language and retrieval metrics, ablations isolating the coherence model, and user studies that support the fluency and coherence benefits.
- Practical data source and scale: Clever use of blog posts as parallel text–image sequences, with non-trivial preprocessing (segmentation, summarization, paragraph vectors).
- Reproducibility: Commitment to release code and data increases impact and facilitates follow-up work.

Weaknesses, questions, and clarifications
1) Claim about [5] and coherence (Line 94). The paper states [5] has “no mechanism for coherence between sentences.” While coherence is not the focus of [5], they model intermediate semantic representations and topic structure over multi-sentence descriptions for videos, which can impart cross-sentence consistency. Please qualify this comparison and clarify the distinction (e.g., explicit entity-based coherence versus implicit topic modeling).

2) Figure–equation mismatch (Fig. 2b vs. Sec. 3.3 / Eq. 2). Figure 2b suggests the fully connected layers take inputs from all sentences jointly, whereas Eq. 2 implies shared parameters applied to each sentence representation ot and a single coherence vector q. Please reconcile the diagram with the equations, specifying exactly which tensors are concatenated and how parameters are shared across time steps.

3) Evaluation metrics. METEOR generally correlates better with human judgments than BLEU, particularly with a single reference. The paper’s experimental section mentions BLEU, CIDEr, and METEOR; if METEOR was not used consistently across all comparisons, please include it, and report statistical significance where feasible.

4) Two stacked linear layers (Eq. 2). The paper argues that Wf2Wf1 does not reduce to a single linear mapping “thanks to dropout.” While dropout can regularize training, the test-time mapping remains linear. Please either justify the architectural choice empirically (e.g., ablation showing the benefit of two FC layers versus one) or add a nonlinearity between FC layers and clarify the role of dropout at train vs. test time.

5) Parameter sharing between BRNN outputs and the coherence vector (Eq. 2). The same FC parameters are applied to both ot and q. What motivated this sharing? Have you compared against separate parameterizations for content flow (BRNN) and entity-based coherence? A brief ablation would clarify whether sharing improves generalization.

6) Paragraph vectors [17]. Are the doc2vec embeddings fine-tuned end-to-end within CRCN or kept fixed after pretraining? If fixed, please discuss whether fine-tuning helps or harms performance.

7) Clarity and reproducibility. A few implementation details would improve clarity: the influence of κ in data augmentation, parse/mention resolution quality for the entity grid, sensitivity to K and beam size, and handling of streams of varying length.

Assessment by review criteria
- Quality: The model is sound and trained with a clear max-margin objective. The experiments are thorough, with ablations and user studies. Some architectural choices (stacked linear layers, parameter sharing) would benefit from ablations or clearer justification.
- Clarity: Generally well written, but the Fig. 2b vs. Eq. 2 discrepancy should be fixed. Minor clarifications on preprocessing and training would help reproducibility.
- Originality: The integration of an entity-based coherence model into a neural sequence scoring framework for image streams is novel and timely.
- Significance: Addressing multi-sentence description for image streams is important, and the demonstrated gains over strong baselines suggest practical impact. Released resources would further increase significance.

Pros for acceptance
- Tackles a meaningful, underexplored problem with a principled and effective architecture.
- Strong empirical results across metrics and human judgments.
- Clear contributions beyond single-image captioning, especially coherence modeling.

Cons against acceptance
- Some claims about prior work could be more precise.
- Architectural choices (stacked linear layers, parameter sharing) need clearer justification or ablation.
- Minor clarity issues in figure–equation correspondence.

Post-rebuttal
After reading the rebuttal, I recommend acceptance. The authors addressed concerns regarding formulation, evaluation, and related work. Please incorporate the promised clarifications in the final version and explicitly state whether the paragraph vectors [17] are fine-tuned or kept fixed. Overall, this is a compelling contribution that advances coherent multi-sentence description of image streams.
