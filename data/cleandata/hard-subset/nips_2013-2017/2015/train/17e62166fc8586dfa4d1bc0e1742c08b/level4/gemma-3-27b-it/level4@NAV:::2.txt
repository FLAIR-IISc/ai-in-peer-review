The paper addresses the problem of generating a coherent sequence of sentences to describe a sequence of images, such as those found in blog posts. The core idea is to first retrieve the K=5 most visually similar images and their corresponding sentences from the training data for each input image sequence. The primary contribution of this work is a method for selecting the most relevant sentences from this set to create a fluent and contextually appropriate description. This is achieved by embedding sentences into a vector space and modeling the resulting sequence using a bidirectional LSTM. The LSTM’s output is then processed through ReLU and fully connected layers, and a compatibility score is calculated to assess the alignment between the image and each sentence. Importantly, a local coherence model [1] is incorporated to further ensure consistency *between* the selected sentences.

**Strengths:**

*   The paper presents a novel and effective architecture for retrieving coherent sentences given an image sequence.
*   The authors provide a comprehensive evaluation, including quantitative metrics, qualitative examples, and a human evaluation, demonstrating superior performance compared to baseline approaches that do not explicitly model coherence. An ablation study, specifically removing the coherence model [1], is also included.
*   The authors have committed to releasing both the source code and the dataset used in the experiments.

**Weaknesses / Questions / Unclarities:**

1.  Line 94 states that prior work [5] lacks a mechanism for sentence coherence. While not the central contribution of [5], it *does* predict an intermediate semantic representation of videos that inherently promotes coherence across sentences through topic modeling.
2.  The visualization in Figure 2b appears inconsistent with the description in section 3.3 and Equation (2). The figure suggests fully connected layers connecting to all sentences, whereas Equation (2) implies parameter sharing across sentences, with connections only to the vector representing a single sentence.
3.  Considering the use of a single reference sentence, Meteor (http://www.cs.cmu.edu/~alavie/METEOR/) may be a more appropriate automatic evaluation metric than BLEU.
4.  The use of two sequential linear functions (W{f2}, W{f1}) in Equation (2) is unclear. Since two linear transformations can be collapsed into a single linear transformation, the benefit of this specific design is not immediately apparent. An ablation study exploring the impact of these functions would be valuable.
5.  Why are the parameters of the fully connected layers identical for both the bidirectional RNN output (o\_t) and the local coherence model component (q) in Equation (2)?
6.  Was the paragraph vector [16] fine-tuned during training, or was it kept fixed? 

=== post rebuttal ===

After reviewing the rebuttal, I recommend this paper for acceptance. The authors have effectively addressed the concerns raised regarding the formulation, evaluation methodology, and related work. Additionally, they clarified that the paragraph vector [16] is kept fixed. This paper proposes a compelling new model for coherent sentence retrieval from image streams, and the evaluation convincingly supports its effectiveness. However, a completely polished paper requires these clarifications to be fully convincing.
