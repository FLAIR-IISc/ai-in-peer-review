This work addresses the problem of generating a sequence of natural language sentences from an image stream, extending traditional image captioning which typically focuses on a single image-sentence pair. The authors propose a coherent recurrent convolutional network (CRCN), integrating convolutional neural networks for image representation, bidirectional recurrent neural networks for language modeling, and an entity-based local coherence model to improve fluency. The approach is trained on a large dataset constructed from blog posts containing image-text pairs. The results demonstrate strong performance compared to several baselines, using both quantitative metrics (BLEU, top-K recall) and user studies via Amazon Mechanical Turk.

This paper presents a logical extension of single-image captioning to the more complex task of describing image streams. The motivation – capturing the context of a series of related images, such as those taken during an event – is well-articulated and relevant. The proposed CRCN architecture is a reasonable approach, building upon established techniques in computer vision and natural language processing (e.g., CNNs [13], RNNs [20]) and incorporating a novel coherence model [1] to address the specific challenges of multi-sentence generation. The use of blog posts as a training dataset is a clever solution to the scarcity of image stream-sentence sequence data, and the scale of the dataset (140K images, 20K blog posts) is commendable. The quantitative results reported are compelling, and the inclusion of user studies adds further confidence to the findings. 

Strengths of this work include its novel extension of image captioning to the sequential domain, the well-designed CRCN architecture integrating image and language models with a coherence component, and the effective use of a large, naturally-sourced dataset. The thorough experimental evaluation, including both automatic metrics and user studies, is a significant strength.

Weaknesses include the omission of potentially relevant related work in the area of video captioning (e.g., [23, 32]), which also tackles the problem of sequential data and language generation. While the quantitative results are strong, the user study (Table 2) shows only a marginal preference for the proposed method compared to the RCN variant, particularly when using shorter image streams. A statistical significance test is needed to determine if these observed differences are reliable. Additionally, the paper could benefit from a more detailed ablation study to isolate the contributions of each component of the CRCN, particularly the coherence model.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Addresses a novel and important problem – extending image captioning to image streams.
*   Presents a well-designed and effective architecture (CRCN).
*   Demonstrates strong performance on both quantitative and qualitative evaluations.
*   Utilizes a large and naturally-sourced dataset.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Omission of relevant related work in video captioning.
*   Weak statistical significance in the user study results.
*   Lack of a detailed ablation study.
