

This paper addresses the task of generating a sequence of natural language sentences describing a given sequence of images, a problem with clear relevance to vision-language understanding. While the core problem is significant, the authors fail to adequately contextualize their work within the established literature. Specifically, the task closely parallels video captioning (e.g., Rohrbach et al., 2014), where a video is decomposed into shots, each described by a key frame. The absence of citation to this line of research—particularly work directly addressing sequential image description—weakens the paper's positioning and contribution statement.

The paper is well-written and logically structured, with a clear problem formulation. The proposed CRCN model effectively integrates visual and linguistic features to model relationships between image sequences and sentence sequences, leveraging entity-based coherence models to capture discourse structure. The technical architecture is sound and appropriately justified for the task.

However, the sentence generation method presents a critical limitation. The approach generates each sentence independently based on the most similar training image to the current input frame, then concatenates these sentences in image order. This "naive concatenation" strategy lacks sophistication; it does not model dependencies *between* sentences in the output sequence (e.g., temporal coherence, pronoun resolution), which is essential for fluent, coherent multi-sentence descriptions. The method appears overly simplistic for the stated problem.

The dataset description raises serious concerns. Section 4.1 states the authors "reuse[ed] the blog data of Disneyland from [11] and newly collect[ed] NYC data using the same crawling method." However, the dataset itself is not disclosed, and [11] provides no details on the crawling methodology. This lack of transparency is problematic: the identical data collection approach and reuse of a dataset from another paper suggest the authors may be the same group, yet the paper fails to disclose this connection or provide sufficient methodological detail to replicate the NYC collection. This undermines reproducibility and violates standard practices for dataset disclosure in computer vision.

Equation (2) warrants scrutiny. It defines the hidden state \(s_t\) solely as a function of the observation \(o_t\), implying a network structure where each time step is decoupled. This contradicts the intended use of a recurrent network for sequence modeling, where \(s_t\) should depend on \(s_{t-1}\) and \(o_t\). The equation likely contains an error, as the model description later implies recurrence. This needs clarification or correction.

The paper's strengths lie in its clear problem statement, well-motivated model architecture, and solid technical execution for the core matching task. However, the significant weaknesses—lack of relevant citations, the simplistic sentence generation strategy, the undisclosed dataset methodology, and the potential equation error—prevent it from making a compelling contribution. The core idea (sequence generation via naive concatenation) is not sufficiently novel or advanced for the venue.

While the model's technical soundness is commendable, the overall contribution appears incremental. The paper would benefit substantially from addressing the cited literature, replacing the ad-hoc generation method with a more coherent sequence modeling approach (e.g., using an LSTM or transformer for the sentence sequence), and providing full dataset details. As written, the work lacks the novelty and depth expected for a conference paper of NIPS' stature.

Given the above, the paper is borderline but leans toward rejection. The technical approach is competent, but the lack of novelty, methodological gaps, and insufficient contribution to the field outweigh the strengths. A weak accept as a poster is not justified; the weaknesses are too fundamental to the paper's core claim. The authors would need to substantially revise the method, context, and disclosure to warrant acceptance. I recommend rejection.
