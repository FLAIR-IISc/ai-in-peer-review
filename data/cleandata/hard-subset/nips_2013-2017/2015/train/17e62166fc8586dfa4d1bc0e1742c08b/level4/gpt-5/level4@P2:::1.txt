Summary and relation to prior work
The paper addresses the task of describing an image stream with a sequence of natural sentences. The authors propose CRCN, a multimodal architecture that pairs CNN image features with a bidirectional RNN over sentence representations and augments this with an entity-based local coherence model. Sentences are retrieved from a large corpus of blog posts: for each query image, candidate sentences are drawn from visually similar training images, and a divide-and-conquer beam search selects a coherent sequence under a max-margin compatibility objective. The training data are constructed by segmenting and summarizing blog posts into image–sentence pairs.

The work relates to single-image captioning and retrieval models (e.g., Kiros et al., Karpathy & Fei-Fei, Vinyals et al.) and to video-to-text methods that handle sequential inputs (e.g., Rohrbach et al.; Donahue et al.). It also connects to prior work on image-sequence retrieval from text (Kim et al., CVPR’14). The key claimed novelty is explicitly modeling sentence-to-sentence local coherence (via entity grids in the style of Barzilay & Lapata) within an end-to-end retrieval framework for image streams.

Strengths
- Problem formulation: Extends single-image–single-sentence settings to image-stream–paragraph settings, which is practically important (e.g., photo albums, travelogues).
- Model: Technically sound integration of CNN-based visual features, BRNN for sentence flow, and an entity-based local coherence signal. The coherence component is well-motivated for image streams where adjacent images can be semantically discontinuous.
- Data construction: A scalable pipeline using blog segmentation and LSA-based summarization to obtain image–sentence sequences, with doc2vec sentence embeddings and coreference parsing for coherence features.
- Empirical results: Consistent gains over strong baselines across BLEU/CIDEr/METEOR and retrieval metrics, plus AMT user studies indicating preference for CRCN outputs, especially for longer sequences.
- Clarity: The paper is generally well written and easy to follow.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Candidate generation is retrieval-only. Each sentence is chosen from a small KNN pool per image, and sequences are concatenated/selected. This is simple and often effective, but limits expressivity and can propagate dataset biases and stylistic mismatches. It may also cap performance when the correct sentence is not present in the training pool.
- Limited discussion of relation to multi-sentence video description. Although video-text work is cited, the connection and differences (e.g., paragraph generation, coherence modeling, retrieval vs generation) could be elaborated. A direct comparison to a video-to-paragraph baseline, if feasible, would strengthen the claims.
- Equation (2) and interaction design: As written, Wf2Wf1[O|q]=[S|g] implies st is a function of ot only, while q influences only g; interaction between coherence and per-sentence content occurs via shared parameters and the scoring with images. This seems to underutilize the coherence signal. Please clarify whether you explored conditioning st on both ot and q (e.g., concatenating q to each ot, gating/attention, or residual fusion) and whether this improved ranking.
- Reproducibility and anonymity: The NYC dataset is newly crawled; Disneyland data are reused from [11]. The paper does not provide crawling details sufficient for replication, and [11] reportedly lacks these details as well. Given double-blind constraints, reusing a proprietary dataset associated with prior work may risk de-anonymization. Please ensure the submission remains anonymous and, for camera-ready, release the data or provide a precise crawling recipe and preprocessing code.
- Evaluation metrics: Paragraph-level fluency and coherence are not fully captured by BLEU/CIDEr/METEOR. While AMT helps, automatic coherence metrics (e.g., entity grid likelihoods, entity transition statistics) could provide complementary evidence. Also report variance/confidence intervals where applicable.
- Generality: Experiments focus on travel blogs (NYC/Disneyland). The approach is advertised as domain-agnostic, but some validation on a non-travel domain (or cross-domain transfer) would support this claim.

Quality
The model and training objective are sound, with appropriate optimization details (BPTT, RMSprop, dropout, He initialization). The divide-and-conquer beam search is a reasonable approximation for large candidate spaces. However, more ablations on the fusion design in Eq. (2), the effect of κ in data augmentation, and the contribution of doc2vec vs alternative sentence encoders (e.g., averaged word embeddings, LSTM encoders) would clarify where the gains come from.

Clarity
Clear and well organized. A few places could be improved:
- Precisely define how entity grids are constructed over summarized sentences and how coreference chains are resolved across the entire sequence.
- Provide full doc2vec training details (window size, epochs, negative sampling, training corpus splits) for reproducibility.
- Clarify the divide-and-conquer search: beam size per level, pruning criteria, and complexity.

Originality
Combining an entity-based local coherence model with a multimodal CNN–BRNN architecture for image-stream description is novel and well motivated. The retrieval-based generation per image is not new, but its integration with sentence-to-sentence coherence modeling at paragraph level is a meaningful contribution.

Significance
The task is practically relevant, and the demonstrated improvements over strong baselines suggest others may build on this approach. The impact would be higher with broader domain evaluation and with publicly released data/code.

Pros
- Addresses an important, underexplored setting (image streams to paragraphs).
- Thoughtful integration of coherence modeling improves multi-sentence quality.
- Strong empirical results with user-study support.
- Clear paper and sensible engineering choices.

Cons
- Retrieval-only candidate generation limits expressivity and scalability.
- Coherence–content fusion may be underutilized; Eq. (2) design needs clarification.
- Reproducibility concerns due to dataset availability and anonymization risks.
- Evaluation could better target paragraph-level coherence beyond AMT.

Suggestions for improvement
- Strengthen the fusion between coherence and per-sentence representations (e.g., condition st on q, or use attention over entity transitions).
- Explore hybrid decoding that allows light-weight generation or paraphrasing of retrieved sentences.
- Add automatic coherence evaluations and report statistical significance on AMT.
- Provide detailed, reproducible dataset construction steps and commit to releasing data/code (post-acceptance).

Recommendation
Weak accept (poster). The problem is important, the approach is technically solid and generally well executed, and the empirical results are convincing. Addressing the fusion design, dataset reproducibility, and clearer positioning with respect to video-to-paragraph work would further strengthen the paper.
