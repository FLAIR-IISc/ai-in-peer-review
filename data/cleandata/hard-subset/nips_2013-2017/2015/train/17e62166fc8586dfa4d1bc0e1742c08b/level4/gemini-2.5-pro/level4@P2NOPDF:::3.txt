This paper addresses the task of generating descriptive sentences from a stream of images. To this end, the authors propose a Coherent Recurrent Convolutional Network (CRCN), a model that integrates convolutional neural networks for visual feature extraction, bidirectional recurrent neural networks for sequence modeling, and a novel entity-based local coherence model to ensure narrative consistency across sentences.

The paper tackles an interesting and important problem, and the proposed architecture is well-motivated. The combination of established deep learning components with a specific module for narrative coherence is a valuable contribution. The strong performance reported in the quantitative evaluations suggests that this approach is effective.

However, the paper could be significantly improved in the following areas:

1.  **Positioning within Related Work:** The discussion of related work could be strengthened. While the paper focuses on image streams, the problem is closely related to video-to-text generation. Several key papers in this area appear to be missing, which would help contextualize the contribution. For instance, "Jointly Modeling Deep Video and Compositional Text to Bridge Vision and Language in a Unified Framework" (Yu et al.) is a highly relevant work that should be discussed and potentially compared against. A more comprehensive literature review would better highlight the unique contributions of the CRCN model.

2.  **Validation of User Study Results:** The claims of improvement need more rigorous validation. While the automatic metrics show a clear advantage for the proposed CRCN model, the human evaluation presented in Table 2 suggests that its performance is very similar to the RCN baseline. This discrepancy between automated metrics and human judgment is critical and warrants further investigation. To substantiate the claim that the CRCN is superior in perceived quality, the authors must conduct statistical significance tests on the user study results. Without such analysis, it is difficult to determine whether the observed small improvements are reliable or simply due to random variation.

In summary, this paper presents a promising model for generating coherent sentence descriptions for image streams. The core ideas are sound and the quantitative results are encouraging. However, the work would be substantially more compelling with a more thorough comparison to related literature and a rigorous statistical analysis of the human evaluation results to validate its claimed advantages over strong baselines.
