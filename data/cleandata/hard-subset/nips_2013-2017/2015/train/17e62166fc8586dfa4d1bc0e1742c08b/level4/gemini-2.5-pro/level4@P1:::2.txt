This paper addresses the problem of generating a coherent sequence of sentences to describe a stream of images, such as those found in blog posts. The proposed method first retrieves the K=5 most similar training images and their associated sentences for each image in a query sequence. The main contribution lies in a model that selects the most relevant sentences from this candidate pool to form a coherent description. To achieve this, sentences are embedded as vectors, and the sequence is modeled using a bidirectional recurrent neural network (BRNN). The output of the BRNN, combined with features from a local coherence model [1], is used to compute a compatibility score between the candidate sentence sequence and the query image stream.

**Strengths:**
*   The paper proposes a novel and effective architecture for retrieving coherent sentences to describe an image sequence.
*   The evaluation is extensive, including quantitative metrics, qualitative examples, and a human user study. The results compellingly demonstrate the superiority of the proposed approach over several baselines, particularly those that do not model coherence.
*   The authors include an ablation study that validates the contribution of the coherence model [1].
*   The promise to release the source code and dataset is a valuable contribution to the research community.

**Weaknesses and Questions:**
1.  On line 94, the paper claims that the work of Donahue et al. [5] has "no mechanism for the coherence between sentences". While modeling coherence may not have been the main contribution of [5], their model predicts an intermediate semantic representation for videos that is implicitly coherent by modeling the topic of the multi-sentence description. Could the authors comment on this distinction?
2.  There appears to be a discrepancy between Figure 2b and Equation (2) in Section 3.3. Figure 2b suggests that the fully connected layers take all sentence representations `{o_t}` as a single concatenated input. However, Equation (2) implies a weight-sharing scheme where the layers are applied independently to each sentence vector `o_t`. Please clarify the architecture.
3.  For automatic evaluation, the METEOR metric is generally considered more reliable than BLEU, particularly when evaluating against a single reference sentence. Using this metric would provide a more robust quantitative assessment.
4.  Equation (2) shows two consecutive linear transformations (Wf1, Wf2). Since a composition of linear maps is itself a single linear map, the benefit of using two layers over one is not immediately clear. The paper notes that dropout is applied between them, which prevents them from collapsing into one layer. However, an ablation study demonstrating the advantage of this two-layer design would strengthen the paper.
5.  Why are the same fully connected layer parameters used for both the BRNN outputs (o_t) and the local coherence vector (q) in Equation (2)? What is the intuition behind this parameter sharing?
6.  Are the paragraph vectors [17] fine-tuned during the training of the CRCN model, or are their weights kept fixed after being pre-trained?

**Concluding Remarks:**
This paper proposes an interesting new model for retrieving coherent sentences for an image stream, and it is convincingly evaluated. However, to be a more convincing paper, several clarifications should be made.
