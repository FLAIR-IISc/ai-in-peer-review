This paper addresses the novel and important problem of generating a coherent sequence of natural sentences to describe a stream of images. While most prior work focuses on generating a single sentence for a single image, this work extends both the input and output to sequences, which is a natural and practical next step for the field. The authors propose a multimodal architecture, the Coherent Recurrent Convolutional Network (CRCN), which integrates three components: a Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) to extract image features, a bidirectional Recurrent Neural Network (BRNN) to model the flow of content across sentences, and an entity-based local coherence model to ensure smooth and logical transitions between sentences. The proposed method operates in a retrieval-based framework, first identifying candidate sentences for each image in a query stream and then using the learned CRCN model to rank and select the optimal sequence that is both visually relevant and textually coherent. The model is trained and evaluated on a large-scale dataset created from blog posts, demonstrating its applicability to real-world, user-generated content.

**Strengths**

*   **Novelty and Significance:** The paper tackles a novel problem that is a significant and logical extension of the popular image captioning task. Generating descriptive paragraphs for image collections is a more complex but also more useful capability, and this work is, to my knowledge, one of the first to formally address it.
*   **Sound Technical Approach:** The proposed CRCN architecture is well-motivated and technically sound. The combination of a BRNN for modeling content flow and an explicit coherence model for modeling discourse structure is an effective way to address the challenges of generating multi-sentence descriptions.
*   **Thorough Evaluation:** The experimental evaluation is comprehensive, including extensive quantitative analysis using multiple metrics (BLEU, CIDEr, METEOR, and retrieval metrics), qualitative examples, and a user study on Amazon Mechanical Turk. The results convincingly demonstrate the superiority of the proposed method over several strong baselines that have been adapted for this new task.
*   **Ablation Study:** The authors provide a valuable ablation study by comparing the full CRCN model to a variant without the coherence model (RCN). This comparison effectively validates the contribution of the entity-based coherence component, which is a key element of the proposed architecture.
*   **Reproducibility:** The authors' commitment to releasing the code and dataset is commendable and will be a valuable contribution to the community, facilitating reproducibility and encouraging future research in this direction.

**Weaknesses and Areas for Clarification**

While the paper is strong overall, there are several points that could be clarified to further improve its quality and impact.

1.  **Comparison to Related Work:** The paper claims that the work of Donahue et al. [5] has "no mechanism for the coherence between sentences" (line 94). However, [5] models the topic of a multi-sentence description by predicting an intermediate semantic representation, which provides a form of high-level coherence. Could the authors please elaborate on the distinction between their approach and that of [5] and perhaps refine this claim?
2.  **Clarity of the Model Architecture:** There appears to be a potential discrepancy between the model diagram in Figure 2b and the formulation in Equation (2). The figure seems to imply that the fully connected layers operate on the entire sequence of sentence representations concatenated together, whereas the equation suggests that parameters are shared but applied to each sentence vector `o_t` individually. Please clarify how the sequence of BRNN outputs is processed by the fully connected layers to produce the sequence of scores `s_t`.
3.  **Architectural Design Choices:**
    *   Equation (2) shows two consecutive linear transformations (`W_f1` and `W_f2`). As a composition of two linear maps is itself a single linear map, the benefit of this two-layer design is not immediately obvious. The paper notes that dropout is used, which does break the linearity. It would be helpful to elaborate on this design choice and its benefits over a single, wider fully-connected layer with dropout. An ablation study on this point would be very informative.
    *   The model uses the same parameters for the fully connected layers to process both the sequential BRNN outputs (`o_t`) and the global coherence vector (`q`). Could the authors provide more intuition or justification for this parameter sharing, given that these two features capture different types of information (sequential content flow vs. global discourse structure)?
4.  **Implementation Details:** For full reproducibility, it is important to know whether the paragraph vectors from Le and Mikolov [17] were pre-trained and held fixed during the training of the CRCN, or if they were fine-tuned as part of the end-to-end training process. Please clarify this detail in the final version.

In summary, this paper presents a novel and well-executed study on the task of generating sentence sequences for image streams. The proposed model is interesting, and the convincing experimental results highlight its effectiveness. The paper would be significantly strengthened by addressing the points of clarification raised above, particularly concerning the model architecture and comparisons to prior work. Pending these revisions, I believe this is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field.
