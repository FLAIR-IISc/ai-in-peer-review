The paper proposes a novel approach to describing a sequence of images from blog posts with a sequence of consistent sentences. To achieve this, the authors first retrieve the K=5 most similar images and associated sentences from the training set for each query image. The main contribution of the paper lies in defining a method to select the most relevant sentences for the query image sequence, providing a coherent description. This is accomplished by embedding sentences in a vector and modeling the sequence of sentences using a bidirectional LSTM. The output of the bidirectional LSTM is then fed through a ReLU and fully connected layer, and scored with a compatibility score between image and sentence. Additionally, a local coherence model is included to enforce compatibility between sentences.

The paper has several strengths and positive aspects. Firstly, it proposes a novel and effective architecture to retrieve coherent sentences for an image sequence. Secondly, the authors provide an extensive quantitative, qualitative, and human evaluation, demonstrating the superiority of their approach against several baselines that do not use coherence. They also provide an ablation experiment, removing the coherence model, which further supports their claims. Lastly, the authors have promised to release the source code and dataset, which is a significant contribution to the research community.

However, there are several weaknesses and areas of concern that need to be addressed. Firstly, the paper claims that there is "no mechanism for the coherence between sentences" in [5], which is not entirely accurate. Although not the primary contribution of [5], it predicts an intermediate semantic representation of videos, which is coherent across sentences by modeling the topic of the multi-sentence description. This inaccuracy should be corrected to provide a more accurate representation of related work.

Furthermore, there are some issues with the correctness and clarity of the paper. Specifically, Figure 2b does not seem to correspond to the description in 3.3/Equation (2). While Figure 2b implies that the fully connected layers are connected to all sentences, Equation (2) implies that the parameters are shared across sentences, but only connected to the vector representing a single sentence. This discrepancy should be addressed to ensure that the paper accurately conveys the proposed methodology.

Additionally, the paper uses BLEU as a metric to automatically evaluate the generated sentences. However, a more suitable metric for this task would be METEOR (http://www.cs.cmu.edu/~alavie/METEOR/), especially when there is only a single reference sentence. The authors should consider using this metric in their evaluation to provide a more accurate assessment of their approach.

The paper also raises some questions about the design choices made by the authors. Specifically, the use of two linear functions in Equation (2) (W{f2}, W{f1}) applied behind each other is unclear. Given that two linear functions are equivalent to a single linear function, the benefit of this design choice is not apparent. An ablation study showing the benefit of these functions would be interesting and help to clarify the contribution of this design choice.

Another question concerns the use of the same parameters for the fully connected layers in the BRNN output (o_t) and the local coherence model q (Equation 2). It is unclear why the same parameters are used for these two different components, and an explanation would be helpful in understanding the design decisions made by the authors.

Finally, the paper does not clarify whether the paragraph vector [16] is fine-tuned or kept fixed. This information is essential in understanding the proposed methodology and should be included in the final version of the paper.

After reading the rebuttal, I am pleased to see that the authors have addressed the issues with the formulation, evaluation, and related work. I recommend the paper for acceptance, as it proposes an interesting new model to retrieve coherent sentences for an image stream, which is convincingly evaluated. However, to make the paper more convincing, the authors should address the clarifications mentioned above, particularly the question about the paragraph vector [16].
