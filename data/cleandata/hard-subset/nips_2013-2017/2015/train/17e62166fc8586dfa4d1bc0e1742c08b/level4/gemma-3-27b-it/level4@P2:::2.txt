The paper addresses the novel problem of generating a sequence of coherent sentences to describe a sequence of images, motivated by scenarios like describing a series of photos from a trip. The core idea is to move beyond single image-sentence pairings, leveraging blog posts as a text-image parallel corpus. The authors propose a coherent recurrent convolutional network (CRCN) incorporating convolutional neural networks for image representation, bidirectional recurrent neural networks for language modeling, and a local coherence model to ensure fluency between sentences. The approach retrieves candidate sentences for each image and then selects the most coherent sequence.

The paper builds upon a growing body of work in image captioning (e.g., [5, 8, 9, 12, 14, 15, 26, 21, 30]) by extending the input and output dimensions to handle sequences. It also draws connections to video-sentence tasks (e.g., [23, 32]) but emphasizes the unique challenge of potentially abrupt visual changes between consecutive images in a photo stream, requiring a stronger coherence model. The authors demonstrate improved performance over several baselines on both quantitative metrics (BLEU, top-K recall) and through user studies on Amazon Mechanical Turk.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The problem of sequence-to-sequence image description is relatively unexplored and represents a logical next step in the field.
*   **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The paper provides a thorough evaluation with quantitative metrics, qualitative examples, and user studies, showcasing the effectiveness of the proposed approach.
*   **Ablation Study:** The inclusion of an ablation study, removing the coherence model, helps to isolate the contribution of its key components.
*   **Resource Availability:** The authors’ commitment to releasing code and data is commendable and will facilitate further research.
*   **Strong Baseline Comparison:** Comparing against several established image captioning methods provides a solid benchmark.

**Weaknesses and Questions:**

1.  **Related Work (Line 94):** The assertion that [5] lacks a mechanism for sentence coherence is inaccurate. Their intermediate semantic representation inherently promotes coherence by modeling the overall topic of the video description.
2.  **Figure 2b Clarity:** The diagram in Figure 2b doesn’t fully align with the description in Section 3.3 and Equation (2). The figure suggests a fully connected layer connected to all sentences, while the equation implies parameter sharing across sentences with a connection to a single vector. This needs clarification.
3.  **Evaluation Metric:** While BLEU is a common metric, METEOR ( [16]) offers better correlation with human judgments, particularly with limited reference sentences and could provide a more informative evaluation.
4.  **Equation 2 Redundancy:** The sequential application of two linear functions (W<sub>f2</sub>, W<sub>f1</sub>) in Equation (2) is potentially redundant. An ablation study demonstrating their individual and combined benefits would strengthen the justification for this design choice.
5.  **Parameter Sharing (Equation 2):** The rationale behind using the same parameters in the fully connected layers for both the BRNN output (o<sub>t</sub>) and the local coherence model (q) (Equation 2) warrants further explanation.
6.  **Paragraph Vector Fine-tuning:** It's unclear whether the paragraph vectors [17] were fine-tuned during training or remained fixed. Clarification on this point is needed.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper tackles a challenging and potentially impactful problem in image understanding and generation.
*   The proposed CRCN architecture is well-motivated and demonstrates promising results.
*   The thorough evaluation provides strong evidence for the effectiveness of the approach.
*   The authors have demonstrated a willingness to address reviewer concerns (as evidenced by the rebuttal).

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The inconsistencies between the figure, the equation, and the description in Section 3.3 raise concerns about the clarity and correctness of the model implementation.
*   The potential redundancy in Equation 2 and lack of justification for parameter sharing introduce minor weaknesses in the design.

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of image captioning by extending the task to handle image streams and focusing on coherence. While some minor clarifications are needed, the strengths of the work – its novelty, comprehensive evaluation, and promising results – outweigh the weaknesses. The authors have addressed initial concerns effectively in the rebuttal.

**Recommendation:** Accept.

**Post-Rebuttal Note:** The authors have addressed the primary concerns regarding the figure-equation discrepancy, the justification for design choices, and related work. The remaining question regarding paragraph vector fine-tuning should be clarified in the final version. The paper proposes an interesting and well-evaluated model for coherent sentence sequence generation from image streams.
