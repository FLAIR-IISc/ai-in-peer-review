## Review of CRCN: Learning Discourse-Aware Neural Networks for Image-to-Sentence Retrieval

This paper addresses the problem of retrieving and generating natural language descriptions for a sequence of images. This is a significant problem at the intersection of computer vision and natural language processing. However, the work is closely related to, and builds upon, research in video captioning – the task of generating descriptions for video content. A video can be readily considered a sequence of images (or keyframes representing shots), and existing video captioning approaches (e.g., [Rohrbach et al., 2014]) share considerable overlap with the proposed methodology. A key omission is a discussion of, and comparison to, this related body of work.

The paper is generally well-written and clearly presents its contributions. The problem statement is well-defined and the proposed approach utilizes a solid set of techniques. The authors introduce CRCN, a neural network model designed to capture relationships between image sequences and corresponding sentences, and demonstrate a strong understanding of discourse modeling through the integration of entity-based coherence. 

The core idea of learning associations between images and sentences is sound. However, the sentence generation process, as described, feels somewhat simplistic. Sentences are generated by directly associating each image with similar training sentences and then concatenating these sentences in image order. While functional, this approach lacks the nuance of more sophisticated sequence generation techniques, potentially limiting the coherence and naturalness of the generated descriptions. A more complex decoding strategy could potentially yield better results.

I have concerns regarding the data collection process described in Section 4.1. The authors state they “reuse the blog data of Disneyland from the dataset of [11] and newly collect data of NYC, using the same crawling method.” However, the dataset itself is not publicly available, and the referenced paper [11] lacks sufficient detail regarding the data crawling procedure. This raises a significant issue regarding reproducibility and, given the potential overlap in authorship between this paper and [11], casts doubt on the anonymity of the review process. Clear documentation of the data collection methodology, or ideally, public release of the dataset, is crucial.

Further, I question the correctness of Equation (2). As presented, it suggests that the hidden state *s<sub>t</sub>* is determined solely by the observation *o<sub>t</sub>*, implying a lack of full connectivity within the network. Clarification of this equation, and a confirmation of the network’s connectivity, is necessary. 

Despite these concerns, the proposed architecture is technically sound and demonstrates a promising approach to incorporating discourse relationships into image-to-sentence retrieval. The entity-based coherence modeling is a valuable addition. However, the limited sophistication of the sentence generation method and the reproducibility issues regarding the dataset prevent me from recommending acceptance as a full paper. 

**I recommend this paper be accepted as a poster presentation.** This would allow the authors to receive feedback from the community and further refine their work, specifically addressing the concerns regarding data availability, equation clarity, and the sentence generation strategy.
