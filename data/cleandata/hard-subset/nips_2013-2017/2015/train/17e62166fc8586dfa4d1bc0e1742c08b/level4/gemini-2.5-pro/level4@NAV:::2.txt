This paper addresses the challenge of generating a coherent textual description for a sequence of images sourced from blog posts. The proposed methodology begins by retrieving the K=5 nearest-neighbor images and their corresponding sentences from the training data for every image in the input sequence. The work's primary contribution is a selection mechanism designed to identify the most suitable sentences from this retrieved set, ensuring the final output forms a cohesive narrative for the entire image sequence. To achieve this, sentences are first converted into vector embeddings. These embeddings are then processed by a bidirectional LSTM (Bi-LSTM) to model sequential dependencies. The hidden states from the Bi-LSTM are passed through a ReLU activation and a fully connected layer. The resulting representation is used to compute a compatibility score between the candidate sentence and the target image. In addition, a local coherence model, based on [1], is integrated to ensure compatibility between adjacent sentences.

**Strengths / Positive Aspects:**
-   The authors introduce an innovative and effective architecture for generating coherent sentence descriptions for image sequences.
-   The work is supported by a thorough evaluation, encompassing quantitative metrics, qualitative examples, and human judgments, which demonstrates the proposed model's superiority over several baselines that lack an explicit coherence mechanism. This includes a valuable ablation study that confirms the contribution of the coherence model [1].
-   The commitment to release the source code and dataset is a welcome contribution to the community.

**Weaknesses / Questions / Unclarities:**
1.  On line 94, the manuscript asserts that reference [5] lacks a "mechanism for the coherence between sentences." This may be an overstatement. While coherence was not the primary contribution of [5], that work models the topic of the multi-sentence description by predicting an intermediate semantic representation, which inherently promotes coherence across the generated sentences.
2.  **Correctness/Clarity:** There appears to be a discrepancy between Figure 2b and the description in Section 3.3/Equation (2). Figure 2b suggests that the fully connected layers receive input from all sentence representations simultaneously, whereas Equation (2) implies a parameter-sharing scheme where the layers operate on each sentence vector individually.
3.  The use of the METEOR metric (http://www.cs.cmu.edu/~alavie/METEOR/) would be more appropriate for automatic evaluation than BLEU, particularly in scenarios with a single ground-truth reference sentence.
4.  The rationale behind stacking two linear functions (W{f2}, W{f1}) in Equation (2) is unclear, as their composition is equivalent to a single linear transformation. An ablation study demonstrating the benefit of this design would strengthen the paper.
5.  Could the authors clarify why the same fully connected layer parameters are utilized for both the Bi-RNN output (o_t) and the local coherence model's input (q) in Equation (2)?
6.  It is unclear whether the paragraph vectors from [16] are used as fixed features or are fine-tuned during training.

**=== Post Rebuttal ===**
Following the author rebuttal, I now recommend this paper for acceptance. The authors have convincingly addressed the previously raised concerns regarding the model's formulation, the evaluation methodology, and the discussion of related work.

I encourage the authors to incorporate the promised revisions into the final manuscript. Additionally, one point requires clarification in the camera-ready version:
6.  Are the paragraph vectors [16] kept fixed or fine-tuned during model training?

Overall, the paper introduces a compelling new model for retrieving coherent sentence descriptions for an image sequence, supported by a strong evaluation. With the requested clarifications, the paper will represent a solid contribution to the field.
