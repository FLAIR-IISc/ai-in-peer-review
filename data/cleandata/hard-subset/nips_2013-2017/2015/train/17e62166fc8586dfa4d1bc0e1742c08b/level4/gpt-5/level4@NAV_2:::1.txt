This paper tackles the task of retrieving natural-language sentences that describe a given sequence of images and assembling them into a sentence sequence. The problem is clearly important for computer vision and pattern recognition, and it is closely connected to multi-sentence description of videos (e.g., [Rohrbach+ 2014]): a video can be viewed as a sequence of images, since it can be segmented into shots and each shot summarized by a key frame. However, this line of work is not cited in the paper.

The manuscript is clearly written and easy to follow. The problem is well defined and addressed using a set of solid techniques.

The proposed CRCN model learns relationships between a sequence of images and a sequence of natural sentences. The architecture appears technically sound, and the inclusion of an entity-based coherence component captures discourse relations effectively.

The sentence sequence generation procedure is sensible but somewhat basic: each sentence is directly retrieved using training images similar to each query image, and the resulting sentences are simply concatenated in the order of the corresponding images.

In Section 4.1, the authors state, “We reuse the blog data of Disneyland from the dataset of [11], and newly collect the data of NYC, using the same crawling method with [11].” The dataset is not released, and the referenced paper does not provide details about the crawling process. This strongly suggests substantial author overlap with [11], which undermines anonymity.

Equation (2) may be incorrect, as it suggests that st depends only on ot, implying the network is not fully connected. Overall, the neural architecture is technically sound and models discourse with the entity-based coherence module, but the approach to assembling sentence sequences for an image stream is rather ad hoc. I would support acceptance as a poster in its current form.
