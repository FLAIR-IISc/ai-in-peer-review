This paper addresses the task of retrieving natural sentences to describe a given sequence of images and assembling them into a coherent multi-sentence description. The problem is important in computer vision and pattern recognition. It is also closely related to generating multi-sentence descriptions for video content (e.g., Rohrbach et al., 2014): a single video can be viewed as a sequence of images, since videos can be segmented into shots and each shot can be represented by a key frame. However, this line of work is not cited, which is a notable omission in the related literature.

The paper is well written and easy to follow. The problem is clearly stated and approached with a set of solid techniques. The proposed CRCN neural network learns relationships between a sequence of images and a sequence of natural sentences. The architecture is technically sound and, with the incorporation of entity-based coherence models, captures discourse relationships effectively.

That said, the sentence generation strategy is reasonable but rather primitive: each sentence is retrieved by associating it with training images similar to the given image, and the resulting sentences are simply concatenated in the order of the corresponding images. This per-image retrieval followed by naive concatenation can limit coherence and expressiveness.

There are also concerns about the dataset and anonymity. In Section 4.1, the authors state: “We reuse the blog data of Disneyland from the dataset of [11], and newly collect the data of NYC, using the same crawling method with [11].” The dataset is not disclosed, and [11] does not provide details of the crawling procedure. This strongly suggests that the authors of the current paper are largely the same as those of [11], and thus the submission deliberately lacks anonymity.

A technical issue also needs clarification: Equation (2) may be incorrect, as it appears to imply that s_t is derived solely from o_t, suggesting the network is not fully connected as intended. This should be corrected or explained.

In summary, the CRCN model is technically sound and models discourse relationships well via entity-based coherence, but the sentence sequence generation is rather ad hoc. Given the strengths and the identified issues (missing related work citations, dataset/anonymity concerns, and a potentially incorrect equation), I recommend acceptance as a poster.
