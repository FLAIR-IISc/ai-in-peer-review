This paper addresses the problem of retrieving and generating natural language descriptions for a sequence of images – a task of considerable importance to computer vision and pattern recognition. This work is closely connected to the field of video captioning, where the goal is to generate multiple sentences describing video content (e.g., [Rohrbach+ 2014]). Given that a video can be viewed as a sequence of images (through shot decomposition and keyframe extraction), this connection seems particularly relevant, yet the related literature remains uncited.

The paper is clearly written and logically structured, presenting a well-defined problem and a solution employing sound technical approaches. 

The proposed CRCN model effectively learns relationships between image sequences and corresponding natural language sentences. The model architecture is technically robust and effectively incorporates discourse relationships through the use of entity-based coherence modeling.

However, the sentence generation method appears somewhat simplistic. Sentences are directly linked to similar training images, and sequences are formed by simple concatenation based on image order. 

Regarding data collection, Section 4.1 states, “We reuse the blog data of Disneyland from the dataset of [11], and newly collect the data of NYC, using the same crawling method with [11].” The dataset itself remains undisclosed, and [11] lacks detailed information on the crawling procedure. This raises concerns about potential conflicts of interest, suggesting a strong overlap in authorship between this work and [11], and a deliberate lack of transparency regarding anonymity.

Furthermore, Equation (2) may contain an error, as it suggests *st* is solely dependent on *ot*, implying a lack of full connectivity within the network. While the neural network model is technically sound and leverages entity-based coherence modeling effectively, the sentence sequence generation method feels rather *ad hoc*. 

Overall, I believe this paper is suitable for acceptance as a poster presentation in its current form.
