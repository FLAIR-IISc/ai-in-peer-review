This paper addresses the problem of retrieving a sequence of natural language sentences to describe a corresponding stream of images. The authors motivate this by observing that users often capture series of photos of an event, and a coherent narrative is more descriptive than isolated captions. The proposed method, Coherent Recurrent Convolutional Network (CRCN), integrates three components: a CNN to extract image features, a bidirectional RNN to model the flow of content in the text, and an entity-based local coherence model to ensure smooth transitions between sentences. The model is trained on a large dataset of blog posts and evaluated against several state-of-the-art methods, showing superior performance on both automatic metrics and human evaluations.

The work extends the popular single-image-to-single-sentence paradigm to a sequence-to-sequence setting. However, the claim that this is the first work to do so should be tempered by considering the extensive literature on video description (e.g., Rohrbach et al., ICCV 2013; Donahue et al., CVPR 2015). A video can be viewed as a dense image stream, and generating a descriptive paragraph for a video is conceptually similar to the problem tackled here. The paper would be strengthened by a discussion of this related area, clarifying how describing a sparse, user-curated photo stream differs from describing continuous video frames and how the proposed coherence model is particularly suited for the former, where visual content can change abruptly.

**Strengths:**

*   **Significance and Originality:** The paper tackles a well-motivated and important problem that is a logical next step for the image captioning community. The combination of a BRNN for content flow with an explicit, entity-based coherence model for discourse structure is a novel and technically sound architectural contribution.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is well-written, clearly organized, and easy to follow. The problem statement, proposed method, and experimental setup are all presented effectively.
*   **Quality:** The proposed CRCN model is technically solid, and its components are well-justified. The empirical evaluation is thorough, including comparisons to strong baselines using both automatic metrics (BLEU, CIDEr, R@K) and human studies on AMT. The results convincingly demonstrate the value of modeling sentence-to-sentence transitions.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Related Work:** As mentioned above, the paper overlooks the significant body of work on video description, which deals with a closely related sequence-to-sequence problem. Addressing this would allow for a more precise positioning of the paper's contributions.
*   **Generation Method:** The method for generating sentence sequences is a retrieval-based, two-stage process. First, candidate sentences are retrieved for each image individually, and then these candidates are combined and ranked by the CRCN. While effective, this approach is not fully generative and is constrained by the sentences present in the training corpus. The novelty lies more in the powerful re-ranking of candidate sequences than in the generation of novel text.
*   **Reproducibility:** The experiments rely on blog post datasets, one of which was newly collected for this work. The paper states that the collection method from [11] was used, but the reference itself does not provide sufficient detail, and the dataset is not made publicly available. This makes it difficult for other researchers to reproduce the results or build upon this work.
*   **Technical Clarity:** There is a potential lack of clarity in Equation (2). As written, `Wf2Wf1[O|q] = [S|g]` seems to suggest that the image-space representations `S` are computed solely from the sentence-space representations `O`. A clarification of how image and text information are combined in the fully-connected layers would be beneficial.

**Arguments for acceptance:**
*   The paper addresses a significant and timely extension of the image captioning problem.
*   The proposed CRCN architecture, particularly its integration of a local coherence model, is novel and effective.
*   The comprehensive experiments provide strong evidence that the model outperforms relevant state-of-the-art approaches.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
*   The claims of novelty are somewhat overstated due to the omission of the related video description literature.
*   The approach is based on retrieval and ranking rather than true generation, which limits its scope.
*   The lack of a publicly available dataset and detailed collection methodology raises concerns about reproducibility.

In summary, this paper presents a solid and well-executed piece of work on an interesting problem. The core technical contribution—the CRCN model—is sound and demonstrates the importance of modeling coherence in multi-sentence descriptions. While the concerns regarding the related work and reproducibility are notable, the strengths of the paper make it a valuable contribution. It would be suitable for acceptance, perhaps as a poster, with the recommendation that the authors address the weaknesses above in the final version.
