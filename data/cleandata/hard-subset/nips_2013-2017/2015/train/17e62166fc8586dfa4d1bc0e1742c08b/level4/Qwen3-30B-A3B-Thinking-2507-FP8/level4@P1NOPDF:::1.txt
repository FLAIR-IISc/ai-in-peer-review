

This paper addresses the problem of generating natural language descriptions for sequences of images, a significant topic in computer vision and pattern recognition. However, it fails to cite relevant prior work on video captioning, which directly addresses the same challenge: video content can be decomposed into image sequences (e.g., via key frames per shot), as demonstrated in Rohrbach et al. (2014). The omission of this line of work is notable, as it forms the conceptual foundation for the problem under study.

The paper is well-written and clearly structured, with a well-defined problem statement and a technically sound solution. The proposed CRCN model effectively captures relationships between image sequences and corresponding sentence sequences, leveraging entity-based coherence models to model discourse structure. The architecture is logically designed and appropriately addresses the core challenge.

That said, the sentence generation method is relatively simplistic: each sentence is generated independently based on the nearest-neighbor training image (rather than modeling the entire sequence context), and sentences are concatenated in image order. This approach lacks sophistication compared to sequence-to-sequence methods that model dependencies across the entire output.

The data collection methodology raises concerns. Section 4.1 states that "newly collected NYC data was gathered using the same crawling method as [11]," yet the dataset itself is not publicly available, and the cited reference [11] does not detail the crawling procedure. This makes replication impossible and suggests the authors may be identical to those in [11], undermining submission anonymity.

Equation (2) appears questionable: it implies $s_t$ depends solely on $o_t$, which would prevent the network from utilizing prior hidden states. This contradicts the intended fully connected architecture and requires clarification.

While the technical contributions are sound, the lack of engagement with foundational video captioning work, the simplistic generation strategy, and the unresolved data transparency issues are significant limitations. The paper would be suitable as a poster presentation, but its current form does not meet the bar for a spotlight or oral presentation.
