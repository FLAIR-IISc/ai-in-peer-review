This paper addresses the task of generating a coherent sequence of descriptive sentences for a corresponding sequence of images, such as those found in blog posts. The proposed approach first retrieves the K=5 most similar images and their associated sentences from a training corpus for each image in the query sequence. The core contribution is a model that selects the most relevant and coherent sentence for each image from these candidates.

This selection is achieved by first embedding the candidate sentences into a vector space. A bidirectional LSTM (bi-LSTM) is then used to model the sequential nature of the sentences. The output of the bi-LSTM is passed through a ReLU activation and a fully connected layer, and then scored for compatibility with the corresponding image. To ensure narrative consistency, the model also incorporates a local coherence component, based on the work of [1], to enforce compatibility between adjacent sentences.

**Strengths**

*   The paper introduces a novel and well-motivated architecture for retrieving a coherent sequence of sentences to describe an image stream.
*   The authors conduct a comprehensive evaluation, including quantitative metrics, qualitative examples, and a human study. These experiments convincingly demonstrate the superiority of the proposed approach over several baselines that do not model coherence. The inclusion of an ablation study that removes the coherence model further strengthens the paper's claims.
*   The authors' commitment to releasing their source code and dataset is commendable and will benefit the research community.

**Weaknesses and Questions for Clarification**

While the paper is promising, several points require clarification and could be improved in the final version.

1.  **Comparison to Related Work:** The claim on Line 94 that the work of [5] has "no mechanism for the coherence between sentences" may be too strong. While not its primary contribution, [5] predicts an intermediate semantic representation for videos that implicitly models coherence by focusing on the topic of the multi-sentence description. A more nuanced comparison would be beneficial.

2.  **Clarity of Model Description:** There appears to be a discrepancy between the model diagram in Figure 2b and its description in Section 3.3/Equation (2). Figure 2b suggests that the fully connected layer is connected to the representations of all sentences, whereas Equation (2) implies that the parameters are shared across time steps but are only applied to the representation of a single sentence at a time. Please clarify the exact architecture in the final version.

3.  **Evaluation Metric:** The authors use BLEU for automatic evaluation. While common, METEOR is often considered a more suitable metric for sentence-level evaluation, particularly when only a single reference sentence is available, as it considers synonyms and stemming. The authors might consider reporting METEOR scores as well for a more robust evaluation.

4.  **Model Architecture Rationale:** Equation (2) applies two consecutive linear transformations (W{f1} and W{f2}) to the bi-RNN output. Since a composition of two linear functions is itself a linear function, the benefit of this two-layer design is unclear. An explanation or a small ablation study demonstrating the advantage of this choice over a single linear layer would strengthen the paper.

5.  **Parameter Sharing:** The rationale for using the same fully connected layer parameters for both the bi-RNN output (o_t) and the local coherence model input (q_t) in Equation (2) is not explained. Could the authors elaborate on this design choice? It seems plausible that these two distinct sources of information might benefit from separate transformations.

6.  **Implementation Details:** It is unclear whether the paragraph vector model [16] used for sentence embeddings is fine-tuned during training or if the pre-trained embeddings are kept fixed. Please clarify this important implementation detail.

**Recommendation**

After reading the author rebuttal, I recommend this paper for acceptance. The authors have successfully addressed the main issues raised concerning the model's formulation, evaluation, and relation to prior work.

The paper presents an interesting and novel model for a challenging task, and the approach is convincingly evaluated. To be a strong contribution, however, it is essential that the promised clarifications are incorporated into the camera-ready version. In particular, I urge the authors to clarify the model architecture (point 2) and the status of the paragraph vector model during training (point 6), as these are critical for reproducibility and understanding. Addressing the other points would further enhance the quality and impact of the paper.
