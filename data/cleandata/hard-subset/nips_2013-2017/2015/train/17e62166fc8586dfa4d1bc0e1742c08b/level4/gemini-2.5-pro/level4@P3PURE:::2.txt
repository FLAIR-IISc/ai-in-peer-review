This paper addresses the problem of generating a sequence of coherent sentences to describe a sequence of images, such as those found in blog posts. The proposed method first retrieves the K=5 most similar images and their associated sentences from the training set for each query image. The core contribution is a model that selects and re-ranks these retrieved sentences to form a coherent description for the query image sequence. This is achieved by first embedding sentences into a vector space and then modeling the sentence sequence with a bidirectional LSTM. The output of the LSTM for each sentence is passed through a ReLU activation and a fully connected layer. The final selection is based on a compatibility score between the image and the sentence representation, augmented by a local coherence model [1] that enforces consistency between adjacent sentences.

**Strengths**

*   The paper proposes a novel and effective architecture for retrieving a coherent sequence of sentences to describe an image sequence.
*   The experimental evaluation is extensive, including quantitative, qualitative, and human studies. The results demonstrate the superiority of the proposed approach against several baselines that do not model coherence. An ablation study that removes the local coherence model [1] further strengthens these claims.
*   The authors' promise to release their source code and dataset is a welcome contribution to the community.

**Weaknesses / Questions**

The paper proposes an interesting new model which is convincingly evaluated. However, to be a convincing paper, several clarifications have to be made.

1.  The claim on Line 94 that [5] has "no mechanism for the coherence between sentences" appears to be inaccurate. While sentence coherence may not have been the main contribution of [5], that work models the topic of the multi-sentence description, which enforces a form of coherence across the generated sentences.
2.  There is an apparent inconsistency between Figure 2b and Equation (2). Figure 2b suggests that the fully connected layers receive input from all sentences simultaneously, whereas Equation (2) implies that the parameters are shared across time steps but are applied to the representation of each sentence individually. Please clarify the exact architecture.
3.  The authors use BLEU for automatic evaluation. Have they considered using METEOR? METEOR is often considered a more suitable metric than BLEU for sentence-level evaluation, particularly in settings with a single reference sentence, as it handles synonyms and stemming.
4.  Equation (2) includes two consecutive linear transformations (W_f1 and W_f2). Since the composition of two linear functions is itself a linear function, the motivation for this two-layer design is unclear. An ablation study demonstrating the benefit of this choice over a single linear layer would be helpful.
5.  Equation (2) seems to indicate that the same fully connected layer parameters are applied to both the output of the Bi-RNN (o_t) and the representation from the local coherence model (q_t). Could the authors justify this parameter sharing choice?
6.  Is the paragraph vector model [16] used to generate sentence embeddings kept fixed during training, or is it fine-tuned on the target dataset?

**Post-Rebuttal Recommendation**

After reading the authors' rebuttal, I recommend this paper for acceptance. The authors have successfully addressed the concerns regarding the model's formulation, the evaluation, and the comparison to related work.

I ask that the authors incorporate the promised changes into the final version of the paper. Additionally, please clarify the following point, which remained unanswered:

6. Is the paragraph vector [16] fine-tuned on the target dataset or are its parameters kept fixed during training?
