This paper addresses the problem of retrieving a sequence of natural sentences that describe a given sequence of images, which is a significant problem in the research fields of computer vision and pattern recognition. Although this problem is closely related to generating multiple sentences that describe given video content (e.g., [Rohrbach+ 2014]), the authors do not cite any work in this line of research.

The paper is well-written and easy to follow, with a clear problem statement and a solid technical approach. The proposed neural network model, named Coherent Recurrent Convolutional Networks (CRCN), effectively captures the relationships between a sequence of natural sentences and a sequence of images. The architecture of the proposed model is technically sound, and it successfully describes discourse relationships using entity-based coherence models.

However, the method for generating a sequence of natural sentences is somewhat primitive. The authors associate each natural sentence with training images similar to a given image and then simply concatenate the sequences in the order of the corresponding images.

In Section 4.1, the authors mention reusing the blog data of Disneyland from the dataset of [11] and collecting new data for NYC using the same crawling method. However, the dataset is not publicly available, and the corresponding paper does not provide details on how to crawl the data. This lack of transparency raises concerns about the anonymity of the authors, as it appears that the authors of this paper may be the same as those in [11].

There is a potential issue with Equation (2), as it implies that st can be derived from only ot, suggesting that the network is not fully connected. Nevertheless, the proposed neural network model is technically sound and effectively describes discourse relationships using entity-based coherence models. However, the method for generating a sequence of sentences for a given image stream is somewhat ad-hoc.

In conclusion, while this paper makes a solid contribution to the field, it could be improved by addressing the aforementioned concerns. As it stands, I would recommend accepting this paper as a poster paper.

Arguments for acceptance:

* The paper addresses a significant problem in computer vision and pattern recognition.
* The proposed neural network model is technically sound and effectively captures relationships between sequences of natural sentences and images.
* The paper is well-written and easy to follow.

Arguments against acceptance:

* The paper lacks citations to relevant work on generating multiple sentences that describe given video content.
* The method for generating a sequence of natural sentences is somewhat primitive.
* The dataset used in the paper is not publicly available, and the authors may not be anonymous.
* There is a potential issue with Equation (2) that may indicate a lack of full connectivity in the network.
