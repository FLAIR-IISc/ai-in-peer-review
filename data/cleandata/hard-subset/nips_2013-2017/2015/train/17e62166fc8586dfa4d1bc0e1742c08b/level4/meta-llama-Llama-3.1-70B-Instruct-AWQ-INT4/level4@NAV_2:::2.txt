The paper proposes a novel approach to describing a sequence of images from blog-posts with a sequence of consistent sentences. This is achieved by first retrieving the K=5 most similar images and associated sentences from the training set for each query image. The main contribution of the paper lies in defining a way to select the most relevant sentences for the query image sequence, providing a coherent description. For this, sentences are first embedded in a vector and then the sequence of sentences is modeled with a bidirectional LSTM. The output of the bi-directional LSTM is first fed through a ReLU and fully connected layer and then scored with a compatibility score between image and sentence. Additionally, a local coherence model [1] is included to enforce the compatibility between sentences.

The paper relates to previous work at NIPS and elsewhere, particularly in the areas of image captioning and multimodal learning. The authors extend the existing work by addressing the problem of describing a sequence of images with a sequence of sentences, rather than individual images with individual sentences. They also propose a novel architecture, the Coherent Recurrent Convolutional Network (CRCN), which integrates convolutional networks, bidirectional recurrent networks, and a local coherence model.

The paper provides an extensive quantitative, qualitative, and human evaluation, showing the superiority of their approach against several baselines, not using coherence. They also provide an ablation experiment, removing the coherence model [1]. The authors promise to release source code and dataset.

Strengths of the paper include the proposal of a novel and effective architecture to retrieve coherent sentences for an image sequence, as well as the provision of an extensive evaluation, including human studies. The paper also addresses a novel and challenging problem in the field of multimodal learning.

Weaknesses of the paper include some minor issues with clarity and correctness, such as the discrepancy between Figure 2b and the description in Section 3.3/Equation (2). Additionally, the use of two linear functions in Equation (2) is unclear, and an ablation study showing the benefit of these functions would be interesting. Furthermore, it is unclear why the same parameters of the fully connected layers are used for the BRNN output (o_t) and the local coherence model q (Equation 2).

In terms of quality, the paper is technically sound, and the claims are well-supported by theoretical analysis and experimental results. The paper is also well-written and easy to follow. However, some minor revisions are needed to address the issues mentioned above.

In terms of clarity, the paper is well-organized and easy to follow. However, some minor revisions are needed to address the issues mentioned above, such as clarifying the discrepancy between Figure 2b and the description in Section 3.3/Equation (2).

In terms of originality, the paper proposes a novel approach to describing a sequence of images with a sequence of consistent sentences. The use of a local coherence model to enforce the compatibility between sentences is also a novel contribution.

In terms of significance, the paper addresses a novel and challenging problem in the field of multimodal learning. The proposed approach has the potential to be used in a variety of applications, such as image captioning, video description, and human-computer interaction.
