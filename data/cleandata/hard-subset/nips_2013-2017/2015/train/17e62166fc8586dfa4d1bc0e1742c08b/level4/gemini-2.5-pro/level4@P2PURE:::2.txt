This paper addresses the problem of generating a coherent, multi-sentence description for a sequence of images, such as those found in a blog post. The proposed approach begins by retrieving the K=5 most similar images and their corresponding sentences from the training set for each image in the query sequence. The core contribution is a method for selecting the most relevant sentences from this retrieved set to form a coherent description for the entire image sequence. This is achieved by first embedding the sentences into a vector space. A bidirectional LSTM (Bi-LSTM) is then used to model the sequence of sentence embeddings. The output of the Bi-LSTM for each sentence is passed through a ReLU activation and a fully-connected layer, and then a compatibility score is computed between the resulting sentence representation and its corresponding image. Additionally, a local coherence model from [1] is incorporated to promote compatibility between adjacent sentences.

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a novel and effective architecture for retrieving a coherent sequence of sentences to describe an image sequence.
- The authors conduct extensive quantitative, qualitative, and human evaluations, which demonstrate the superiority of their approach over several baselines that do not model coherence. The paper also includes a valuable ablation study that removes the coherence model [1] to show its impact.
- The authors promise to release their source code and dataset.

**Weaknesses and Questions:**
The paper proposes an interesting new model to retrieve coherent sentences for an image stream, which is convincingly evaluated. However, to be a more convincing paper, several clarifications should be made.

1.  On line 94, the paper claims that reference [5] has "no mechanism for the coherence between sentences." This seems inaccurate. While coherence may not have been the main contribution of [5], that work predicts an intermediate semantic representation for videos which promotes coherence by modeling the topic across the multi-sentence description.
2.  There appears to be a mismatch between Figure 2b and the description in Section 3.3 / Equation (2). Figure 2b depicts the fully-connected layer as being connected to all sentences simultaneously. However, Equation (2) suggests that the layer's parameters are shared across time steps but are applied to the vector representation of only a single sentence at a time. Please clarify this discrepancy.
3.  For automatic evaluation, METEOR (http://www.cs.cmu.edu/~alavie/METEOR/) is often considered a better metric than BLEU, particularly when there is only a single reference sentence. Have the authors considered using it?
4.  In Equation (2), two linear transformations (W{f1} and W{f2}) appear to be applied consecutively. Since the composition of two linear functions is itself a single linear function, the benefit of this two-layer structure is unclear. An ablation study demonstrating the advantage of this design would be helpful.
5.  Could the authors explain the motivation for using the same fully-connected layer parameters for both the Bi-RNN output (o_t) and the local coherence model's output (q_t) in Equation (2)?
6.  Is the paragraph vector model [16] fine-tuned during training, or are its parameters kept fixed?

=== Post-Rebuttal Recommendation ===
I have read the authors' rebuttal and now recommend acceptance for this paper. The authors have successfully addressed my concerns regarding the model's formulation, the evaluation, and the discussion of related work.

I encourage the authors to incorporate the promised changes into the final version. Additionally, please clarify the following point in the camera-ready paper: Is the paragraph vector model [16] fine-tuned during training, or are its parameters kept fixed?
