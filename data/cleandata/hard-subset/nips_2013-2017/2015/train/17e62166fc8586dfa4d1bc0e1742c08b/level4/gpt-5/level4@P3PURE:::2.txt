This paper addresses the problem of describing a sequence of images from blog posts with a sequence of mutually consistent sentences. For each query image, the method first retrieves the K=5 most similar images and their associated sentences from the training set. The main contribution is a mechanism for selecting the most relevant sentences for the entire query image sequence so as to produce a coherent description. Concretely, sentences are embedded into vectors and the sentence sequence is modeled with a bidirectional LSTM (BRNN). The BRNN output is passed through a ReLU and a fully connected layer, then scored via an image–sentence compatibility function. In addition, a local coherence model [1] is used to enforce compatibility across sentences.

Strengths / positive aspects:
- The paper proposes a novel and effective architecture for retrieving coherent sentences for an image sequence.
- The evaluation is extensive—quantitative, qualitative, and human studies—demonstrating superiority over several baselines that do not model coherence. An ablation experiment removing the coherence model [1] is also provided.
- The authors promise to release source code and the dataset.

Weaknesses / questions / clarifications:
1. Line 94: The paper states that [5] has “no mechanism for the coherence between sentences.” While coherence is not the primary contribution of [5], that work predicts an intermediate semantic representation for videos that is coherent across sentences by modeling the topic of the multi-sentence description.
2. Correctness/clarity: Figure 2b does not seem to match Section 3.3/Equation (2). Figure 2b suggests that the fully connected layers are connected to all sentences, whereas Eq. (2) suggests the parameters are shared across sentences but applied to the vector representing a single sentence. Please reconcile the figure and the equation.
3. For automatic evaluation of generated sentences, METEOR (http://www.cs.cmu.edu/~alavie/METEOR/) is generally more appropriate than BLEU, especially when there is only a single reference.
4. Why are two linear functions in Eq. (2) (W_{f2}, W_{f1}) applied sequentially? Since the composition of two linear functions is itself linear, the benefit is unclear. An ablation demonstrating the impact of these layers would be helpful.
5. Why are the same fully connected layer parameters used for both the BRNN output o_t and the local coherence model q (Eq. 2)?
6. Is the paragraph vector [16] fine-tuned or kept fixed?
