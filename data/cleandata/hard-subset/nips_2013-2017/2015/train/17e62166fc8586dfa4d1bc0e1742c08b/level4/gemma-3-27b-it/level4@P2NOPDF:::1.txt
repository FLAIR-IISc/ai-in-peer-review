This paper addresses the task of retrieving and sequencing natural language sentences to describe a given image sequence. This is a valuable problem within computer vision and pattern recognition. However, the work bears a strong connection to video captioning – specifically, generating multiple sentences to describe video content (e.g., [Rohrbach+ 2014]). A video can be readily viewed as a sequence of images (shots and keyframes), and thus related work in video captioning appears highly relevant, yet is not cited.

The paper is generally well-written and clearly presented, with a well-defined problem and a solid technical approach. The proposed CRCN model effectively learns relationships between image sequences and corresponding sentences. The model architecture is technically sound and leverages entity-based coherence modeling to capture discourse relationships effectively. 

However, the sentence generation method feels somewhat simplistic. Each sentence is directly associated with a training image similar to the input image, and the resulting sequence is formed by a straightforward concatenation based on image order. While reasonable, this approach lacks sophistication and could benefit from more complex sequence modeling.

A concern arises regarding dataset transparency. In Section 4.1, the authors state they reused data from [11] (Disneyland blogs) and collected new data from NYC using a similar crawling method. However, the dataset itself is not publicly available, and [11] lacks detailed information about the crawling process. This, combined with potential author overlap between the two papers, raises concerns about a lack of anonymity and reproducibility.

Furthermore, Equation (2) appears potentially flawed. It suggests that the hidden state *st* is solely determined by *ot*, implying a lack of full connectivity within the network. While the model generally exhibits sound technical design, this detail warrants clarification. The overall method for generating sentence sequences, while functional, feels *ad hoc*.

In summary, this paper presents a technically competent approach to image sequence description. However, the lack of engagement with related video captioning work, the concerns surrounding dataset availability and anonymity, and the relatively simple sentence generation strategy prevent a strong recommendation for oral presentation. I believe this work would be suitable for acceptance as a poster.
