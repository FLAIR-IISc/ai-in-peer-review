This paper addresses the problem of retrieving natural sentences that describe a given sequence of images and forming a sequence of sentences, a significant problem in the research fields of computer vision and pattern recognition. Although closely related to generating multiple sentences that describe given video content, such as in [Rohrbach+ 2014], where single video content can be regarded as a sequence of images, the authors do not cite any work in this line of research.

The paper is well-written and easy to follow, with a clearly stated problem and a solid set of techniques used to solve it. The proposed neural network model, CRCN, effectively captures relationships between a sequence of natural sentences and a sequence of images. The architecture of the proposed model is technically sound, and it successfully describes discourse relationships using entity-based coherence models.

However, the method for generating a sequence of natural sentences is somewhat primitive. It relies on directly associating each natural sentence with training images similar to a given image and then concatenating the sequences in the order of the corresponding images.

The authors' data collection process, as described in Section 4.1, raises concerns about anonymity. The statement "We reuse the blog data of Disneyland from the dataset of [11], and newly collect the data of NYC, using the same crawling method with [11]" suggests that the authors of this paper may be the same as those in [11], as the data set and crawling method are not publicly disclosed.

There is a potential issue with Equation (2), which implies that st can be derived from only ot, suggesting that the network may not be fully connected. Nevertheless, the proposed neural network model is technically sound, and it effectively describes discourse relationships with the help of entity-based coherence models. In contrast, the method for generating a sequence of sentences for a given image stream is somewhat ad-hoc. Overall, I believe that this paper can be accepted as a poster paper in its current form.
