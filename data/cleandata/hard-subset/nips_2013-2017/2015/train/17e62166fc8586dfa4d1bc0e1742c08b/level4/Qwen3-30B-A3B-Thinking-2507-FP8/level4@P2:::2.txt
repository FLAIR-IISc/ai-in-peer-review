

This paper addresses the problem of generating coherent natural language descriptions for sequences of images (image streams), rather than single images. The authors propose a novel multimodal architecture called Coherent Recurrent Convolutional Network (CRCN), which extends previous work on image captioning by handling both input (image streams) and output (sentence sequences) as sequences. While existing work has focused on single-image to single-sentence mappings (e.g., [9, 12, 21, 30]), this paper is among the first to address the more complex problem of creating coherent sentence sequences for image streams, which better aligns with how users capture and share experiences (e.g., through blog posts documenting travel experiences).

**Quality**: The paper presents a technically sound approach with thorough evaluation. The authors develop a comprehensive architecture integrating convolutional neural networks (for image representation), bidirectional recurrent neural networks (for sentence modeling), and an entity-based local coherence model (for sentence transitions). They demonstrate the approach's superiority over multiple baselines using both quantitative metrics (BLEU, CIDEr, METEOR, top-K recall) and user studies via Amazon Mechanical Turk. The ablation study effectively demonstrates the contribution of the coherence model. The training procedure is well-justified, with appropriate use of data augmentation, dropout, and optimization techniques.

**Clarity**: The paper is well-written and clearly organized. The problem statement, architecture, and evaluation methodology are thoroughly explained. Figures 1 and 2 effectively illustrate the problem setup and model components. The authors provide sufficient detail to reproduce their results, including dataset collection methods, preprocessing steps, and implementation details. The only minor clarity issue is the relationship between Figure 2b and Equation (2), which could be clarified in the final version.

**Originality**: This work makes a significant contribution by extending the problem of image captioning to sequences of images and sentences, which has been largely unaddressed in previous literature. The authors effectively position their work against related research, noting that while some prior work (e.g., [5]) handles sequential inputs, it focuses on single-sentence outputs rather than coherent sentence sequences. The integration of the entity-based coherence model with multimodal networks represents a novel approach to ensuring fluent transitions between sentences in the generated description.

**Significance**: The problem addressed is meaningful, as it better reflects how users capture and share experiences through image streams rather than isolated images. The approach is demonstrated on large-scale blog datasets (20K+ blog posts with 140K images) and shows clear advantages over baselines. The unsupervised nature of the approach (using blog posts as training data) makes it broadly applicable to any domain with similar blog content. The results are significant enough to advance the state of the art in multimodal language generation.

**Strengths**:
- Novel problem formulation addressing a gap in the literature (image streams to sentence sequences)
- Comprehensive evaluation with quantitative metrics, qualitative examples, and human studies
- Effective ablation study demonstrating the value of the coherence model
- Practical application to real-world blog data without requiring manual annotation
- Clear presentation of the model architecture and training methodology

**Weaknesses**:
1. The claim that [5] lacks "mechanism for coherence between sentences" is questionable, as [5] models topic coherence across sentences through intermediate semantic representations. The authors should clarify how their coherence model differs from [5]'s approach.
2. The relationship between Figure 2b and Equation (2) needs clarification. The figure suggests fully connected layers connect to all sentences, while Equation (2) implies shared parameters across sentences but only connected to a single sentence vector.
3. The rationale for using two sequential linear layers (Wf2Wf1) instead of a single layer is unclear. An ablation study would strengthen this design choice.
4. The paper should clarify whether the paragraph vector [16] is fine-tuned or kept fixed during training, as this affects the model's flexibility and learning capacity.
5. While BLEU is commonly used, METEOR would provide a more nuanced evaluation of sentence coherence, especially when comparing against single-reference sentences.

**Recommendation**: I recommend acceptance. The paper makes a significant contribution to the field by addressing an important gap in image captioning research. The proposed CRCN architecture is well-motivated, technically sound, and thoroughly evaluated. The weaknesses identified are minor and could be addressed in the final version with minor clarifications. The authors have already demonstrated responsiveness to reviewer comments in the rebuttal.

**Pro-acceptance arguments**: Novel problem formulation, comprehensive evaluation, clear contribution of the coherence model, practical application to large-scale blog data, strong empirical results.

**Con-acceptance arguments**: Minor clarification needed regarding the coherence model's relationship to prior work [5], and the rationale for two sequential linear layers.

The authors have addressed all reviewer concerns in the rebuttal, particularly clarifying the relationship between their coherence model and prior work, as well as the design of the fully connected layers. This strengthens the paper's contribution and technical soundness. The work represents a meaningful advancement in multimodal language generation that will be of interest to the NIPS community.
