### **Summary**

This paper addresses the task of generating a coherent sequence of natural language sentences to describe a given stream of images. The authors motivate this problem by observing that users often capture series of photos during events, and describing the entire stream as a whole is more meaningful than describing each image in isolation. To this end, they propose a novel multimodal architecture called the Coherent Recurrent Convolutional Network (CRCN). This model integrates three main components: a Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) to extract visual features from images, a bidirectional Recurrent Neural Network (BRNN) to model the flow of content across sentences, and an entity-based local coherence model to ensure smooth, logical transitions between sentences. The model is trained on a large dataset of blog posts, treating the images and accompanying text as parallel data. The final output is generated by retrieving candidate sentences for each image in the query stream and then using the trained CRCN model to rank and select the best overall sequence. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach through comprehensive experiments on two large blog datasets (NYC and Disneyland), using both automatic metrics (BLEU, CIDEr, etc.) and human evaluations on Amazon Mechanical Turk.

### **Strengths and Weaknesses**

**Strengths (Arguments for acceptance):**

1.  **Significance and Originality:** The paper tackles a novel and important problem. Moving from single image-to-sentence mapping to sequence-to-sequence modeling is a logical and significant step forward for the field. To my knowledge, this is one of the first works to formally address the problem of generating a paragraph-like description for an unordered stream of images, which has many practical applications.

2.  **Technical Soundness:** The proposed CRCN architecture is well-motivated and technically solid. The combination of a CNN for image representation, a BRNN for sequential text modeling, and an explicit coherence model to improve discourse quality is a strong and sensible approach. The integration of the entity-based coherence model is particularly novel and effectively addresses the challenge of making the output text read like a fluent paragraph rather than a disjointed list of captions.

3.  **Thorough Evaluation:** The experimental validation is extensive. The authors evaluate their method on large-scale, real-world data from blog posts, which is appropriate for the task. They compare their model against several relevant and state-of-the-art baselines and use a wide range of quantitative metrics as well as qualitative user studies to demonstrate the superiority of their approach.

4.  **Clarity:** The paper is well-written, clearly organized, and easy to follow. The problem is well-defined, the proposed model is explained in sufficient detail, and the experimental setup is clearly described.

**Weaknesses (Arguments for rejection):**

1.  **Missing Related Work:** The paper fails to connect its work to the extensive literature on video description (e.g., [Rohrbach et al., ICCV 2013], [Donahue et al., CVPR 2015]). A video can be seen as an ordered sequence of image frames, and the task of generating a sentential description for it is conceptually very similar to the problem addressed here. The authors claim their novelty is the coherence model, arguing that image streams have sharper content changes than video, but this argument would be much stronger if it were made in direct comparison to existing video-to-text models. The omission of this entire line of research is a significant weakness.

2.  **Sentence Generation Method:** The method for generating the final sentence sequence is retrieval-based rather than generative. Candidate sentences are sourced from the training data based on nearest-neighbor images, and the CRCN model's role is to rank these pre-existing sentences to form a coherent sequence. While effective, this limits the model's expressive power, as it cannot generate novel sentences tailored to the specific nuances of the input images. The authors should be more explicit about this limitation.

3.  **Reproducibility and Anonymity Concerns:** The authors state they use a dataset from their previous work [11] and collect a new one using the same undisclosed method. The lack of detail about the data collection process and the fact that the dataset is not publicly available raises serious concerns about reproducibility. Furthermore, the heavy reliance on methods and data from a single, recent prior paper ([11]) without providing sufficient new detail could inadvertently compromise the double-blind review process.

4.  **Potential Technical Inaccuracy:** There appears to be a potential issue in Equation (2): `Wf2Wf1[O|q] = [S|g]`. As written, this formulation suggests that `st` (the t-th column of S) is derived only from `ot` (the t-th column of O), and that the global coherence representation `g` is derived only from the coherence feature vector `q`. This seems to prevent the coherence information from influencing the sentence-level representations `s_t`, which undermines the goal of jointly modeling content and coherence. A fully connected layer would typically involve a mapping where each output dimension depends on all input dimensions. The authors should clarify this formulation.

### **Detailed Comments**

The core contribution of this paper—a unified model for generating a coherent paragraph for an image stream—is valuable. The CRCN model is well-designed, and the results are convincing. However, the weaknesses outlined above are significant. The missing discussion of video description literature is a major oversight. The issues surrounding the dataset and potential lack of clarity in the model's technical description also need to be addressed.

Overall, the paper presents a strong idea and a promising direction for research. While the execution has some flaws, the contribution is still noteworthy. I would recommend this paper for acceptance as a poster, with the strong suggestion that the authors address the listed weaknesses in the final version, particularly by properly situating their work with respect to the video description literature and clarifying the technical details of their model.
