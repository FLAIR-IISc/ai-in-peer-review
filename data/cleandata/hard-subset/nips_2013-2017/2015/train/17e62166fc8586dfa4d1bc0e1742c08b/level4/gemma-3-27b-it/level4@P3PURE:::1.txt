This paper addresses the problem of retrieving natural language sentences to describe a sequence of images, and generating a coherent sequence of such sentences. This is a significant problem at the intersection of computer vision and pattern recognition. However, the work is closely related to video captioning – the task of generating multiple sentences describing video content (e.g., [Rohrbach+ 2014]). A video can be viewed as a sequence of images (specifically, keyframes representing shots), and thus existing video captioning approaches are highly relevant. Surprisingly, this paper does not cite work in this area.

The paper is well-written and easy to follow. The problem is clearly stated and addressed with a set of solid techniques. The proposed CRCN model effectively learns relationships between a sequence of natural language sentences and a sequence of images. The model architecture is technically sound and leverages entity-based coherence models to effectively capture discourse relationships.

The method for generating sequences of sentences is reasonable, but relatively simplistic. Each sentence is directly associated with training images similar to the input image, and the resulting sequences are concatenated in the order of the corresponding images. 

A concern arises in Section 4.1, where the authors state, “We reuse the blog data of Disneyland from the dataset of [11], and newly collect the data of NYC, using the same crawling method with [11].” The dataset details are not disclosed, and the referenced paper [11] lacks specifics regarding the data crawling process. This raises the possibility of author overlap between this work and [11], potentially compromising the anonymity of the review process.

Furthermore, Equation (2) appears to be incorrect. As written, it suggests that *st* is derived solely from *ot*, implying a lack of full connectivity within the network. While the CRCN model is technically sound and effectively models discourse relationships via entity-based coherence, the sentence sequence generation method feels somewhat ad-hoc. Overall, I believe this paper is suitable for acceptance as a poster.
