

This paper addresses the problem of generating a sequence of natural language sentences to describe a sequence of images (an "image stream"), extending beyond the typical single-image/single-sentence generation task. The authors propose a multimodal architecture called Coherent Recurrent Convolutional Network (CRCN), which integrates convolutional neural networks for image representation, bidirectional recurrent neural networks for language modeling, and an entity-based local coherence model to ensure fluent transitions between consecutive sentences. The approach leverages blog posts as text-image parallel training data, where each blog post consists of a sequence of images with corresponding text segments.

The paper demonstrates strong technical quality. The CRCN architecture integrates well-established components (CNNs, BRNNs, coherence modeling) in a novel way for the sequence-to-sequence task. The experimental evaluation is comprehensive, using both quantitative metrics (BLEU, CIDEr, METEOR, top-K recall) and user studies via Amazon Mechanical Turk. The authors demonstrate that their approach outperforms several strong baselines, with particularly notable improvements in retrieval metrics. The data collection method using blog posts is well-motivated, though the lack of detailed methodology and dataset availability is a minor weakness.

The paper is well-written and clear overall. The problem statement is well-motivated with a clear example (visiting NYC), and the architecture is described with sufficient technical detail. The figures effectively illustrate key concepts. However, some sections of the methodology (particularly in Sections 3.1-3.3) could benefit from more intuitive explanations to help readers grasp the novel integration of the coherence model with the neural network components.

Regarding originality, the work makes a meaningful contribution by addressing the underexplored problem of sequence-to-sequence image description rather than single-image descriptions. However, the paper does not sufficiently distinguish its approach from related video captioning work. While the authors cite [Rohrbach et al. 2014] on video captioning, they do not clearly articulate how their sequence-of-images problem differs from video captioning tasks, particularly since videos inherently have temporal continuity that image streams may not. The paper should more explicitly address this distinction.

The significance of the work is promising for practical applications like automatic travelogue generation, social media content summarization, and personal photo album captioning. The focus on blog posts as a training resource is particularly relevant for real-world applications where users naturally create image-text sequences. The demonstration of improved performance over baselines suggests the approach advances the state of the art in sequence-to-sequence image description, though the scope is limited to tourism-related blog posts.

The main weaknesses include:
1. Insufficient distinction between the image stream problem and video captioning literature
2. Incomplete description of data collection methodology, with no details on how the NYC dataset was crawled (the paper states they "reuse the blog data of Disneyland from the dataset of [11]" but doesn't explain the crawling method)
3. The user study design uses short image sequences (5 images), which doesn't fully test the coherence model's impact as the authors note that coherence becomes more important with longer sequences

The strengths of the paper include:
1. Well-motivated problem statement addressing a clear gap in the literature
2. Technically sound architecture that integrates multiple established techniques
3. Comprehensive experimental evaluation with both quantitative metrics and user studies
4. Practical application to real-world blog data

The paper's approach to using blog posts as training data is particularly innovative, as it leverages a rich source of naturally occurring image-text sequences. The entity-based coherence model effectively addresses the challenge of ensuring smooth transitions between consecutive sentences, which is critical for generating fluent multi-sentence descriptions.

I believe this paper makes a meaningful contribution to the field of multimodal learning, particularly for sequence-to-sequence image description. The work is technically sound, well-executed, and demonstrates clear improvements over existing methods. The main issue to address would be more clearly situating the work within the video captioning literature and providing more details about the data collection process. With these clarifications, I would recommend acceptance. The paper advances the state of the art in a demonstrable way and offers practical value for real-world applications, making it a strong contribution to the field.
