This work studies how to generate sentences from an image stream. It designs a coherent recurrent convolutional network (CRCN), which consists of convolutional neural networks, bidirectional recurrent neural networks, and an entity-based local coherence model.

The paper addresses the problem of generating a sequence of natural language sentences to describe a sequence of images, extending existing work that typically focuses on single image-single sentence pairs. This is a natural and important next step, motivated by how users often capture experiences as a series of images. The proposed CRCN architecture is well-motivated, integrating established components – CNNs, BRNNs, and a coherence model – in a novel way. The authors leverage a large, readily available dataset of blog posts with associated images for training, which is a pragmatic and effective approach. The quantitative results demonstrate strong performance compared to existing methods, and the user study provides additional support, though with some caveats discussed below.

Several related works addressing video-to-sentence generation are missing from the discussion, such as Xu et al. (2015) ["Jointly modeling deep video and compositional text to bridge vision and language in a unified framework"]. While the authors acknowledge video-sentence work, a more comprehensive review would strengthen the positioning of their contribution. 

Although the quantitative results appear promising, the user study (Table 2) shows only a marginal improvement over the RCN baseline, particularly when using a shorter image stream length. A statistical significance test is necessary to determine whether the observed improvements are reliable and not due to chance. Furthermore, the user study results suggest the coherence model contributes more substantially when dealing with longer image streams, which is a valuable insight.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** The paper tackles a relatively unexplored problem – generating coherent sentence sequences from image streams – extending existing work in a natural direction.
*   **Technical Soundness:** The proposed CRCN architecture is well-designed and integrates established techniques effectively. The use of blog data for training is a practical solution to the data scarcity problem.
*   **Strong Quantitative Results:** The method outperforms state-of-the-art baselines on quantitative evaluation metrics.
*   **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and explains the technical details adequately.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Limited User Study Significance:** The user study shows only a modest advantage over some baselines, and lacks statistical significance testing.
*   **Incomplete Related Work:** The related work section could benefit from a more thorough discussion of relevant video-to-sentence approaches.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a solid contribution with a clear problem statement, a well-motivated approach, and promising results. While the user study results are not overwhelmingly convincing, the strong quantitative performance and the novelty of the work suggest it is worthy of acceptance. Addressing the missing related work and including statistical significance tests in the user study analysis would further strengthen the paper.
