The paper tackles the problem of generating a sequence of consistent sentences to describe a sequence of images from blog posts. To achieve this, the authors propose a novel approach that first retrieves the K=5 most similar images and associated sentences from the training set for each query image. The main contribution of the paper lies in defining a method to select the most relevant sentences for the query image sequence, providing a coherent description. This is accomplished by embedding sentences in a vector and then modeling the sequence of sentences using a bidirectional LSTM. The output of the bidirectional LSTM is first passed through a ReLU and fully connected layer, and then scored with a compatibility score between image and sentence. Additionally, a local coherence model [1] is incorporated to enforce compatibility between sentences.

Strengths/positive aspects:

* The paper proposes a novel and effective architecture for retrieving coherent sentences for an image sequence.
* The authors provide an extensive quantitative, qualitative, and human evaluation, demonstrating the superiority of their approach against several baselines that do not use coherence. They also provide an ablation experiment, removing the coherence model [1].
* The authors plan to release the source code and dataset.

Weaknesses/questions/unclarities:

1. Line 94: The paper claims that there is "no mechanism for the coherence between sentences" in [5]. However, [5] predicts an intermediate semantic representation of videos, which is coherent across sentences by modeling the topic of the multi-sentence description, even though this is not the primary contribution of [5].
2. Correctness/clarity: Figure 2b does not appear to correspond to the description in 3.3/Equation (2). While Figure 2b suggests that the fully connected layers are connected to all sentences, Equation (2) implies that the parameters are shared across sentences but only connected to the vector representing a single sentence.
3. A more suitable metric for automatically evaluating the generated sentences is Meteor (http://www.cs.cmu.edu/~alavie/METEOR/), rather than BLEU, especially when there is only a single reference sentence.
4. The use of two linear functions in Equation (2) (W{f2}, W{f1}) applied in sequence is unclear. Since two linear functions are equivalent to a single linear function, the benefit of this approach is not apparent. An ablation study demonstrating the benefit of these functions would be interesting.
5. It is unclear why the same parameters of the fully connected layers are used for the BRNN output (o_t) and the local coherence model q (Equation 2).
6. Is the paragraph vector [16] fine-tuned or kept fixed?

=== post rebuttal ===

After reading the rebuttal, I recommend the paper for acceptance. The authors have successfully addressed issues with the formulation, evaluation, and related work.

The authors have made the promised changes to the final version, and they have also clarified the point regarding the paragraph vector [16]. The paper proposes an interesting new model for retrieving coherent sentences for an image stream, which is convincingly evaluated. However, to make the paper more convincing, several clarifications need to be made.
