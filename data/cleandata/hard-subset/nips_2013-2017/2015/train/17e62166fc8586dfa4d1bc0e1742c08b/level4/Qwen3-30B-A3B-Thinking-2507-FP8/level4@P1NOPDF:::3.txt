

This paper presents a coherent recurrent convolutional network (CRCN) for generating sentences from image streams, integrating convolutional neural networks, bidirectional recurrent neural networks, and an entity-based local coherence model. Overall, it is a solid contribution to the field of image-to-sentence generation. However, the work could be strengthened in the following areas:  

First, the related work section omits key prior efforts on video-to-sentence generation. For instance, the approach by Chen et al. ("Jointly modeling deep video and compositional text to bridge vision and language in a unified framework") provides a relevant foundation for addressing multimodal alignment, which merits discussion to contextualize the proposed method’s novelty.  

Second, while the quantitative results (e.g., CIDEr scores in Table 1) appear promising, the user study in Table 2 reveals only marginal performance gains over the RCN baseline. Specifically, CRCN achieves similar human-rated fluency and relevance scores as RCN, suggesting the improvement may not be statistically meaningful. A significance test (e.g., paired t-test across multiple samples) would be necessary to validate whether the observed gains are reliable, rather than attributable to random variation in the evaluation data.  

Finally, the paper’s description of the entity-based local coherence model lacks sufficient detail. Clarifying how entity tracking is integrated with the recurrent component—particularly how it resolves temporal inconsistencies in generated sentences—would strengthen the methodological contribution. For example, it is unclear whether entities are dynamically updated during decoding or pre-extracted from the input stream.  

The algorithm itself is well-motivated and addresses a meaningful challenge in multimodal generation. With the above refinements, the work would more effectively demonstrate its advancement over existing approaches. As currently presented, the marginal user study results and missing contextualization of related work slightly undermine the paper’s impact. The authors should address these points to ensure the technical claims are fully supported.
