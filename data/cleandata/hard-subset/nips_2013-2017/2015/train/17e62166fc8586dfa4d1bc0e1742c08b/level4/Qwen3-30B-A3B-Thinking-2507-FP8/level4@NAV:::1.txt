

This paper addresses the problem of retrieving natural sentences describing a sequence of images and forming a corresponding sentence sequence. While significant to computer vision and pattern recognition, the problem is closely related to multi-sentence video captioning (e.g., Rohrbach et al., 2014), where video content can be decomposed into shots described by key frames. However, the paper fails to cite relevant work in this line of research.  

The paper is well-written, clearly states the problem, and employs solid technical approaches. The proposed CRCN model effectively captures relationships between image sequences and sentence sequences, with a technically sound architecture that leverages entity-based coherence models to model discourse structure.  

The sentence generation method, however, is simplistic: each sentence is directly associated with a training image similar to the input image, and sequences are concatenated in image order. In Section 4.1, the authors reuse Disneyland blog data from [11] and collect NYC data "using the same crawling method." The lack of dataset disclosure and insufficient detail on the crawling process in [11] raises concerns about author anonymity, suggesting the authors may be the same as in [11].  

Equation (2) appears questionable, as it implies \(s_t\) depends solely on \(o_t\), potentially indicating an incomplete network connectivity. Despite the model's technical strengths, the sentence generation approach is ad hoc. This paper is acceptable as a poster with minor revisions.
