This paper tackles the task of retrieving natural sentences that describe a given sequence of images and assembling them into a sequence of sentences. The problem is clearly important in computer vision and pattern recognition, but it is closely connected to work on generating multi-sentence descriptions for videos (e.g., [Rohrbach+ 2014]): a single video can be treated as a sequence of images, since videos can be segmented into shots and each shot can be represented by a key frame. However, none of this line of research is cited in the paper.

The paper is clearly written and easy to follow. The problem is well formulated and addressed with a set of solid techniques.

The proposed neural network, CRCN, models the relationships between a sequence of natural sentences and a sequence of images. The architecture is technically sound, and with the aid of entity-based coherence models, it captures discourse relationships effectively.

The approach for generating a sequence of natural sentences is reasonable but fairly rudimentary: each sentence is directly associated with training images similar to a given image, and all sentence sequences are simply concatenated in the order of their corresponding images.

In Section 4.1, “We reuse the blog data of Disneyland from the dataset of [11], and newly collect the data of NYC, using the same crawling method as [11].” The dataset is not released, and the corresponding paper does not provide details on the crawling procedure. This strongly suggests that the authors of this paper are largely the same as those of [11], which compromises anonymity.

Equation (2) may be incorrect, as it implies that s_t is derived solely from o_t, indicating the network is not fully connected. The neural model itself is technically sound and, aided by entity-based coherence models, captures discourse relationships well. Meanwhile, the method for generating a sentence sequence for an image stream is rather ad hoc. I believe this paper can be accepted as a poster.
