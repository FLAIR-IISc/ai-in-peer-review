The paper tackles the problem of describing a sequence of images from blog posts with a sequence of consistent sentences. To achieve this, the authors propose a novel approach that first retrieves the K=5 most similar images and associated sentences from the training set for each query image. The primary contribution of the paper lies in defining a method to select the most relevant sentences for the query image sequence, providing a coherent description.

The proposed approach involves embedding sentences in a vector space and modeling the sequence of sentences using a bidirectional LSTM. The output of the bidirectional LSTM is then passed through a ReLU activation function and a fully connected layer, followed by a compatibility score calculation between the image and sentence. Additionally, a local coherence model is incorporated to enforce compatibility between sentences.

The paper has several strengths. Firstly, it proposes a novel and effective architecture for retrieving coherent sentences for an image sequence. Secondly, it provides an extensive quantitative, qualitative, and human evaluation, demonstrating the superiority of the proposed approach against several baselines that do not utilize coherence. The authors also conduct an ablation experiment, removing the coherence model, to further validate their approach. Furthermore, they plan to release the source code and dataset, which is a significant contribution to the research community.

However, there are some weaknesses and areas of improvement. Firstly, the authors claim that there is "no mechanism for the coherence between sentences" in [5], which is not entirely accurate. Although not the primary contribution of [5], the paper predicts an intermediate semantic representation of videos, which is coherent across sentences by modeling the topic of the multi-sentence description.

Secondly, there is a discrepancy between Figure 2b and the description in Section 3.3/Equation (2). Specifically, Figure 2b suggests that the fully connected layer is connected to all sentences, whereas Equation (2) implies that the parameters are shared across sentences but only connected to the vector representing a single sentence.

Thirdly, the authors may consider using a more suitable metric, such as METEOR (http://www.cs.cmu.edu/~alavie/METEOR/), to automatically evaluate the generated sentences, especially when there is only a single reference sentence. BLEU may not be the most effective metric in this case.

Fourthly, the authors apply two linear functions (W{f2} and W{f1}) in Equation (2) behind each other. However, since two linear functions can be combined into a single linear function, the benefit of using two separate functions is unclear. An ablation study demonstrating the advantage of these functions would be beneficial.

Fifthly, the authors use the same parameters for the fully connected layers for both the BRNN output (o_t) and the local coherence model q (Equation 2). It is unclear why this design choice was made, and an explanation would be helpful.

Lastly, it is not specified whether the paragraph vector [16] is fine-tuned or kept fixed during training. Clarification on this point would be appreciated.

After reading the rebuttal, I recommend accepting the paper. The authors have successfully addressed issues with the formulation, evaluation, and related work. The paper proposes an interesting new model to retrieve coherent sentences for an image stream, which is convincingly evaluated. However, to strengthen the paper, the authors should provide the necessary clarifications, particularly regarding the paragraph vector [16] and the design choices mentioned above.
