This paper addresses the novel and challenging problem of generating a sequence of natural language sentences from a stream of images. The authors propose a multimodal architecture, the Coherent Recurrent Convolutional Network (CRCN), which extends the standard single-image-to-single-sentence paradigm to handle sequences for both input and output. The CRCN model cleverly integrates three main components: a Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) for image representation, a Bidirectional Recurrent Neural Network (BRNN) to model the content flow across sentences, and an entity-based local coherence model to ensure smooth and logical transitions between sentences. To train this model, the authors creatively leverage a large dataset of blog posts, treating them as a source of weakly-aligned image-text sequences. The method is evaluated on two new datasets (NYC and Disneyland) using both quantitative metrics (BLEU, R@K, etc.) and a user study, demonstrating superior performance against several state-of-the-art baselines.

**Strengths:**

*   **Originality and Significance:** The paper tackles an important and logical next step for image description research. Moving from single images to image streams is highly relevant, as users often capture events as a series of photos. To my knowledge, this is one of the first papers to formally define and address the image-stream-to-sentence-sequence generation problem, making it a novel contribution.
*   **Well-motivated Architecture:** The proposed CRCN model is technically sound and well-designed. The combination of a BRNN to capture semantic flow and a classic NLP coherence model (Barzilay and Lapata, 2008) to handle syntactic and referential consistency is a strong and thoughtful approach. Explicitly modeling coherence is a key differentiator from prior work and is well-justified, as disjointed sentences generated independently for each image would likely result in an incoherent paragraph.
*   **Strong Empirical Results:** The quantitative evaluation is thorough and convincing. The proposed method (CRCN) significantly outperforms a range of strong baselines, including several state-of-the-art single-sentence generation models adapted for this task. The ablation study, comparing CRCN against RCN (without the coherence model) and a 1NN baseline, effectively demonstrates the contribution of each component of the proposed architecture.
*   **Creative Use of Data:** The idea of using blog posts as a source of weakly-supervised training data is a practical and scalable solution to the data acquisition challenge inherent in this new problem. The pre-processing pipeline for segmenting and summarizing blog text seems reasonable for this purpose.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

*   **Missing Related Work:** The related work section is generally good but could be strengthened by discussing recent work in the closely related domain of video description, which also deals with sequential visual input. For example, "Jointly Modeling Deep Video and Compositional Text to Bridge Vision and Language in a Unified Framework" (Xu et al., AAAI 2015) is a relevant contemporary work that was missed. While the authors correctly note that image streams can have sharper content changes than video, a more direct comparison and discussion of the similarities and differences in modeling challenges would be beneficial for context.
*   **Statistical Validation of User Study:** While the quantitative results show a clear improvement of CRCN over the RCN baseline, the user study results in Table 2 are less decisive. For shorter sequences (<=5 images), the preference for CRCN over RCN is 53.6% to 46.4%, a relatively small margin. The authors argue that coherence becomes more critical for longer sequences, and the results for longer queries (8-10 images) support this, showing a larger preference margin (61.2% to 38.8%). However, to make this claim more robust, it would be valuable to include statistical significance testing (e.g., a binomial or chi-squared test) on the user study results. This would help verify whether the observed user preference for the full CRCN model is statistically reliable or could be due to chance.
*   **Clarity on the Retrieval Algorithm:** The divide-and-conquer beam search strategy described in Section 3.5 is a pragmatic approach to navigating the exponential search space of candidate sentence sequences. However, the description is quite brief. The performance of such an approximation can be sensitive to hyperparameters like the beam size (M=50). A more detailed analysis or discussion of this search strategy would improve the paper's clarity and reproducibility. For example, how was the beam size M chosen? Is there a risk of pruning the optimal sequence early? An ablation showing the trade-off between search complexity and final performance would strengthen this section.

**Summary of Arguments:**

*   **Pro:** The paper introduces and provides a strong solution for the novel and significant problem of generating descriptive paragraphs for image streams.
*   **Pro:** The CRCN architecture is well-motivated, combining modern deep learning techniques with a classic coherence model in an end-to-end framework.
*   **Pro:** The quantitative experimental results are comprehensive and demonstrate a clear state-of-the-art performance.
*   **Con:** The discussion of related work could be improved by including relevant video-to-text generation literature.
*   **Con:** The user study results, while promising, would be more convincing with the addition of statistical significance tests to validate the claimed improvements of the coherence model.

Overall, this is a high-quality paper presenting a novel problem, a well-designed model, and strong results. The weaknesses identified are minor and addressable. The work represents a solid and interesting contribution to the field of vision and language.
