The paper proposes an approach to describe a sequence of images from blog posts with a sequence of consistent sentences. The authors develop a multimodal architecture, called Coherent Recurrent Convolutional Networks (CRCN), which integrates convolutional networks for image representation, bidirectional recurrent neural networks for sentence sequence modeling, and a local coherence model for ensuring a smooth flow of multiple sentences. The CRCN model is trained on large datasets of unstructured blog posts and evaluated using quantitative measures and user studies via Amazon Mechanical Turk.

The paper's main contribution lies in defining a way to select the most relevant sentences for the query image sequence, providing a coherent description. The authors propose a novel compatibility score between an image stream and a paragraph sequence, which takes into account both the fitness of individual image-to-sentence pairs and the coherence between consecutive sentences.

Strengths of the paper include:

* The proposal of a novel and effective architecture to retrieve coherent sentences for an image sequence.
* The provision of an extensive quantitative, qualitative, and human evaluation, showing the superiority of their approach against several baselines.
* The authors' promise to release the source code and dataset.

Weaknesses and questions include:

1. The paper's claim that there is "no mechanism for the coherence between sentences" in [5] is incorrect. Although not the main contribution of [5], the paper predicts an intermediate semantic representation of videos, which is coherent across sentences by modeling the topic of the multi-sentence description.
2. The correctness and clarity of Figure 2b and Equation (2) need to be improved. While Figure 2b implies that the fully connected layers are connected to all sentences, this is not the case in Equation (2), which implies that the parameters are shared across sentences but only connected to the vector representing a single sentence.
3. The use of BLEU as a metric to automatically evaluate the generated sentences may not be the best choice, especially when there is only a single reference sentence. A better metric, such as METEOR, could be used instead.
4. The application of two linear functions in Equation (2) behind each other is unclear. An ablation study showing the benefit of these functions would be interesting.
5. The use of the same parameters of the fully connected layers for the BRNN output (o_t) and the local coherence model q (Equation 2) needs to be justified.
6. It is unclear whether the paragraph vector [16] is fine-tuned or kept fixed during training.

After reading the rebuttal, I recommend the paper for acceptance, as the authors have successfully addressed the issues with the formulation, evaluation, and related work. However, to make the paper more convincing, the authors should clarify the above points in the final version.

Arguments for acceptance:

* The paper proposes a novel and effective architecture to retrieve coherent sentences for an image sequence.
* The authors provide an extensive evaluation, including quantitative, qualitative, and human studies, showing the superiority of their approach against several baselines.
* The paper addresses a relevant and challenging problem in the field of computer vision and natural language processing.

Arguments against acceptance:

* The paper's claim about the lack of coherence mechanism in [5] is incorrect.
* The clarity and correctness of Figure 2b and Equation (2) need to be improved.
* The use of BLEU as a metric may not be the best choice, and a better metric, such as METEOR, could be used instead.
* The application of two linear functions in Equation (2) behind each other is unclear, and an ablation study would be beneficial.
* The use of the same parameters of the fully connected layers for the BRNN output and the local coherence model q needs to be justified.
* It is unclear whether the paragraph vector [16] is fine-tuned or kept fixed during training.
