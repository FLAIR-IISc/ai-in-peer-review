

This paper addresses the problem of generating natural language descriptions for sequences of images, which is an important task in computer vision and pattern recognition. However, the work closely parallels video captioning research (e.g., Rohrbach et al., 2014), where video content is decomposed into shots and described via keyframes. Crucially, the paper fails to cite relevant prior work in this established line of research, which undermines its novelty claim.  

The paper is well-written and clearly articulates the problem. The proposed CRCN model, which captures relationships between image sequences and sentence sequences, is technically sound. Its use of entity-based coherence models to model discourse relationships is a notable strength.  

The sentence generation method, however, is overly simplistic. It directly associates each sentence with similar training images (without leveraging sequential context) and concatenates sentences in image order, lacking a coherent modeling approach for narrative flow.  

A major concern arises in Section 4.1, where the authors describe collecting NYC data "using the same crawling method" as [11]. The dataset collection methodology is not disclosed, and [11] does not detail their crawling process. This reuse of methodology without proper attribution raises concerns about anonymity compliance, as the authors appear to be the same group referenced in [11].  

Equation (2) may contain an error: it suggests $s_t$ depends solely on $o_t$, implying the network is not fully connected. While the overall model architecture is robust, this inconsistency requires clarification.  

Despite these issues, the technical contributions are valid, and the experimental setup is reasonable. The paper would be acceptable as a poster presentation, though the citation gap and data collection concerns warrant attention in any revised version. The core problem and model design are sound, but the work would benefit from stronger engagement with related video captioning literature and a more rigorous treatment of the generation mechanism.
