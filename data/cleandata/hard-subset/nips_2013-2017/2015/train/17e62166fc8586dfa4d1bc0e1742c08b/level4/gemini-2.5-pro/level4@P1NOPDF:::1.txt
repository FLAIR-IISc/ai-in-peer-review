This paper addresses the problem of retrieving a sequence of natural sentences to describe a corresponding sequence of images. This is a significant problem in computer vision and pattern recognition. The task is closely related to video description (e.g., [Rohrbach+ 2014]), as a video can be treated as a sequence of key frames. However, the authors have not cited any work from this relevant line of research.

The paper is well-written and easy to follow. The problem is clearly stated, and the proposed solution is built on a set of solid techniques.

The proposed neural network, named CRCN, is designed to learn the relationships between image sequences and sentence sequences. The model's architecture is technically sound. A key strength is its effective modeling of discourse relationships, achieved by incorporating an entity-based coherence model.

While the core model is compelling, the method for generating sentences for a given image stream is rather primitive. Each sentence is selected based on its association with training images that are visually similar to a given input image. The final description is then formed by simply concatenating these sentences in the order of the corresponding images. This generation process feels ad-hoc compared to the sophistication of the main model.

There is also a significant concern regarding the dataset and the anonymity of the review process. In Section 4.1, the authors state they "reuse the blog data... from the dataset of [11], and newly collect the data of NYC, using the same crawling method with [11]." The dataset from [11] is not public, and that paper does not detail the crawling method, which hinders the reproducibility of this work. Furthermore, this statement strongly suggests that the authors of this paper and reference [11] are the same, which compromises the integrity of the double-blind review.

Finally, Equation (2) may be incorrect. It implies that the state `s_t` is derived solely from the output `o_t`, which would mean the network is not fully recurrent.

In conclusion, the paper presents a technically sound model for a relevant problem. However, this contribution is weakened by an overly simplistic sentence generation method, a failure to acknowledge related work, and serious issues concerning reproducibility and anonymity. Despite these shortcomings, the core ideas have merit. I recommend this paper for acceptance as a poster.
