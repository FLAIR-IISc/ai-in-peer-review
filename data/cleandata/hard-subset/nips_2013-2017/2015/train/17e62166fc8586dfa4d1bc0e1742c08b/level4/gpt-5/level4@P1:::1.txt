Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper tackles the problem of expressing an image stream with a sequence of natural sentences by retrieving and assembling sentences from a large corpus of blog posts. The proposed architecture, CRCN, integrates CNN-based image features (VGG), a bidirectional RNN over paragraph vectors to model the flow of content across sentences, and an entity-based local coherence model to encourage fluent discourse. Training uses a max-margin alignment objective over paired image–sentence sequences extracted from blogs via segmentation and summarization (LSA), and inference retrieves candidate sentences per image via K-nearest neighbors and assembles them with a divide-and-conquer beam strategy. Experiments on two travel domains (NYC, Disneyland) show improvements over strong single-image captioning baselines (Kiros et al., Karpathy et al., Vinyals et al.) and over ablated variants, in both automatic metrics (BLEU, METEOR, CIDEr, retrieval) and AMT user studies, with larger gains for longer sequences.

This problem is important and closely related to video-to-text work that generates multiple sentences for video content (e.g., Rohrbach et al., 2014). A video can be seen as a sequence of frames or shots; conversely, an image stream can be viewed as a sparse video. However, this line of research is not adequately cited or discussed in the paper.

Strengths:
- Clear problem formulation that extends image captioning from single images to image streams with multi-sentence outputs.
- A technically sound multimodal architecture that combines CNN features, a BRNN for content flow, and an entity-grid coherence model for discourse-level consistency.
- Sensible training objective and practical inference procedure.
- Use of large-scale, weakly supervised blog data to obtain parallel text–image sequences.
- Comprehensive evaluation with both automatic metrics and human judgments; ablations show contributions from BRNN and coherence.

Weaknesses:
- The sequence construction strategy at test time is reasonable but rather primitive: each sentence is directly retrieved from training images nearest to each query image, and the final passage is formed by simple concatenation in image order. This makes the approach more of a retrieval system than a generation model and can limit expressiveness and robustness.
- Prior work on generating multi-sentence descriptions for videos is under-cited, despite the close connection.
- Dataset concerns: Section 4.1 states that the Disneyland data is reused from [11] and NYC is newly collected using the same crawling method, but the dataset is not disclosed and [11] does not detail the crawl. This raises anonymity issues and hinders reproducibility.
- Equation (2) may be incorrect or at least misleading. As written, it implies that s_t is derived only from o_t, which suggests the layer is not fully connected across sentences as claimed; the dimensions and the joint dependence on [O|q] should be clarified.

Quality:
The model components are standard and well-motivated, and the integration is technically sound. The entity-based coherence module is an appropriate choice for local discourse modeling and is implemented cleanly. However, the decoding strategy (nearest-neighbor per image with concatenation) is ad hoc, and stronger sequence-level search/generation or global constraints could materially improve quality. The potential error in Eq. (2) needs correction or clarification.

Clarity:
The paper is generally well written and easy to follow. The system design, training objective, and evaluation are clearly described. Clarity could be improved by:
- Expanding discussion of related multi-sentence video description work and positioning CRCN against it.
- Detailing the dataset crawling and preprocessing (or deferring to a public release) to support reproducibility and preserve anonymity.
- Clarifying Eq. (2), variable dimensions, and how the coherence vector influences all positions.

Originality:
Extending captioning to image streams with explicit local coherence modeling is novel and timely. Using entity-grid coherence within a neural multimodal framework is a nice contribution. The retrieval-based assembly is less novel and could be strengthened.

Significance:
The task is significant, and the empirical results suggest practical gains over strong baselines, especially for longer sequences where coherence matters more. If dataset issues are addressed and decoding strengthened, the approach could be impactful for applications like photo storytelling and blog summarization.

Specific comments and suggestions:
- Please correct or clarify Eq. (2). As written, Wf2Wf1[O|q] = [S|g] implies s_t depends only on o_t, which does not realize a fully connected layer across sentences and the coherence vector; dimensionalities should be explicit.
- Discuss and cite more extensively the multi-sentence video description literature and clarify how CRCN differs (e.g., use of entity coherence, retrieval vs. generation).
- The current inference is constrained by nearest-neighbor retrieval per image. Consider stronger sequence-level decoding that jointly optimizes content coverage and coherence (e.g., global CRF, ILP, or neural sequence generation fine-tuned with coherence features).
- Provide more details on the blog crawling, segmentation, and summarization pipeline, and consider releasing the dataset or sufficient tools to reproduce it. Ensure anonymity in the submission.
- Report sensitivity to K, beam size M, and the number of images per sequence; provide examples where retrieval fails due to visual mismatch.
- Since paragraph vectors are trained separately per domain, discuss cross-domain generalization.

Pros for acceptance:
- Well-motivated task with clear utility.
- Solid architecture that integrates discourse coherence.
- Strong empirical results with human validation.
- Clear writing and thorough experimentation.

Cons for acceptance:
- Decoding strategy is simplistic and primarily retrieval-based.
- Inadequate engagement with closely related video-to-text literature.
- Dataset disclosure and anonymity concerns.
- Potential error/ambiguity in Eq. (2).

Recommendation:
Accept as a poster. The core model is technically sound and the results are promising, but the paper would benefit from stronger sequence-level decoding, fuller related work, clarified equations, and clearer dataset practices.
