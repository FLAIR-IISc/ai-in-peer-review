This paper addresses the problem of retrieving and sequencing natural sentences to describe a given sequence of images. While this is a significant problem in computer vision and pattern recognition, it is closely related to the task of generating descriptions for video content (e.g., [Rohrbach+ 2014]), where a video can be treated as a sequence of key frames from video shots. However, the authors fail to cite this relevant line of research.

The paper is well-written and easy to follow, with a clearly stated problem. The proposed solution is based on a set of solid techniques. The core of the contribution is a neural network model, CRCN, designed to learn the relationship between sequences of images and sentences. The model's architecture is technically sound and effectively captures discourse relationships through the use of an entity-based coherence model.

However, the method for generating the final sentence sequence is rather primitive. For each image in the input sequence, a descriptive sentence is selected from sentences associated with similar images in the training set. The final story is then formed by simply concatenating these retrieved sentences in the order of the input images. This approach seems ad-hoc compared to the sophistication of the CRCN model itself.

There are also concerns regarding experimental methodology and anonymity. In Section 4.1, the authors state, "We reuse the blog data of Disneyland from the dataset of [11], and newly collect the data of NYC, using the same crawling method with [11]." The dataset has not been made public, and the cited paper [11] does not provide sufficient detail on the crawling method to ensure reproducibility. This situation strongly suggests a significant author overlap with reference [11], which compromises the blind review process.

Finally, there appears to be a potential error in Equation (2). The equation implies that the state *s_t* is derived solely from the output *o_t*, which would contradict the claim that the network is fully connected.

In summary, while the proposed neural network model is technically sound and effectively models discourse, the sentence generation method is ad-hoc. Combined with the issues of missing citations and a potential breach of anonymity, my recommendation is for acceptance as a poster paper.
