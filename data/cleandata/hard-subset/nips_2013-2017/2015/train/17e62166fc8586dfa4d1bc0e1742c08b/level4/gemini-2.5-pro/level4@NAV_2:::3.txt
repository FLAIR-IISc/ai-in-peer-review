This paper addresses the novel and important problem of generating a sequence of natural sentences to describe a stream of images. The authors propose a multimodal architecture, the Coherent Recurrent Convolutional Network (CRCN), which integrates three main components: a Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) for image feature extraction, a Bidirectional Recurrent Neural Network (BRNN) to model the flow of text, and an entity-based local coherence model to ensure smooth transitions between sentences. A key aspect of this work is the leveraging of large-scale blog posts as a source of parallel image-text data. The approach is evaluated on two new datasets (NYC and Disneyland) and is shown to outperform several state-of-the-art methods on both retrieval and language-based metrics.

**Strengths:**

*   **Originality and Significance:** The paper tackles a logical and significant next step beyond single image captioning. Moving from a single image/sentence pair to a sequence of images and a corresponding narrative is a novel problem formulation that is highly relevant to how users capture and share experiences. The potential impact is substantial, as it moves towards a more holistic understanding of visual content.
*   **Well-motivated Architecture:** The proposed CRCN model is thoughtfully designed. The combination of a CNN for visual understanding and a BRNN for sequential language modeling is a strong foundation. The key novelty is the explicit integration of an entity-based coherence model, which directly addresses the unique challenge of generating a fluent, multi-sentence paragraph, a problem not faced by single-sentence generation methods.
*   **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The experimental evaluation is thorough. The authors compare their method against multiple strong baselines on large-scale, real-world datasets. The use of both automatic quantitative metrics (BLEU, CIDEr, R@K) and human user studies via Amazon Mechanical Turk provides a convincing and well-rounded assessment of the model's performance.
*   **Resourcefulness in Data Collection:** The approach of mining blog posts to create a text-image parallel corpus is a clever solution to the data acquisition challenge inherent in this new problem, avoiding the need for expensive manual annotation.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

*   **Discussion of Related Work:** While the paper discusses related work in image captioning, the connection to video description could be more thoroughly explored, as video-to-text generation also deals with sequential visual input. For example, works like "Jointly Modeling Deep Video and Compositional Text to Bridge Vision and Language in a Unified Framework" (Xu et al., AAAI 2015) appear relevant. A brief discussion contrasting the challenges of describing a sparsely-sampled image stream versus a dense video stream would help contextualize the contribution more clearly.
*   **Statistical Significance in User Study:** The results of the user study are promising. However, the reported margin between the full CRCN model and the RCN variant (54% vs 46% for shorter sequences) is relatively small. While the authors correctly note that the coherence model's impact grows with sequence length, which is supported by their follow-up experiment, the main claim would be significantly strengthened by including a statistical significance test (e.g., a binomial or chi-squared test) to demonstrate that the user preference for CRCN is statistically reliable and not due to random chance.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   **Pro:** The paper introduces and provides a strong solution to a novel and important problem in the vision-and-language space.
*   **Pro:** The proposed CRCN architecture is technically sound, well-motivated, and effectively combines multiple components to address the core challenges of the task.
*   **Pro:** The empirical results, including both extensive quantitative metrics and user studies, demonstrate a clear and significant improvement over state-of-the-art alternatives.
*   **Con:** The user study results comparing the full model (CRCN) against its main ablation (RCN) show a modest improvement, and would benefit from statistical significance testing to bolster the claim.
*   **Con:** The related work section could be slightly improved by a more detailed comparison to video description literature.

Overall, the strengths of this paper, particularly its novelty, technical contribution, and thorough evaluation, far outweigh the minor weaknesses. This is a complete and high-quality piece of work that advances the field, and I recommend its acceptance.
