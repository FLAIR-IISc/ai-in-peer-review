

The paper addresses the problem of generating coherent natural language descriptions for image streams by extending the typical single-image-to-single-sentence paradigm to sequence-to-sequence mapping. The authors propose a novel multimodal architecture called Coherent Recurrent Convolutional Networks (CRCN), which integrates convolutional neural networks for image representation, bidirectional recurrent neural networks for sentence modeling, and an entity-based local coherence model for smooth transitions between sentences. The approach leverages blog posts as text-image parallel training data, where each blog post provides a sequence of images with associated text segments.

Strengths:
- The paper introduces a novel and effective architecture for retrieving coherent sentence sequences from image streams, a significant extension beyond the single-sentence generation paradigm.
- The authors provide comprehensive evaluation through quantitative metrics (BLEU, CIDEr, METEOR, top-K recall), retrieval accuracy, and user studies on Amazon Mechanical Turk, demonstrating clear superiority over state-of-the-art baselines that lack coherence modeling.
- The ablation study effectively validates the contribution of the coherence model, showing significant improvements in retrieval metrics when the coherence component is included.
- The authors plan to release source code and dataset, enhancing reproducibility and community impact.

Weaknesses and Questions:
1. The paper claims [5] lacks "mechanism for coherence between sentences," but [5] models video content through intermediate semantic representations that inherently create coherent multi-sentence descriptions by modeling topic progression across sentences.

2. Figure 2(b) appears inconsistent with Equation (2) and Section 3.3. The figure suggests fully connected layers connect to all sentences, while Equation (2) indicates shared parameters across sentences that process only single-sentence representations.

3. While BLEU is commonly used, METEOR would be a more appropriate metric for evaluating sentence sequences, especially given the single-reference nature of the dataset, as it better captures semantic similarity.

4. The use of two sequential linear layers (Wf2Wf1) in Equation (2) appears redundant since two linear transformations compose to a single linear transformation. An ablation study demonstrating the benefit of this two-layer structure would be valuable.

5. The authors share parameters between the BRNN output (ot) and coherence model (q) in Equation (2), but the rationale for this specific design choice is not clearly justified.

6. The paper does not specify whether the paragraph vector [16] is fine-tuned during training or kept fixed, which is an important implementation detail for reproducibility.

7. The data augmentation approach (using top-κ sentences for training) is not thoroughly justified, particularly regarding the choice of κ=3.

8. The paper mentions the model is "completely unsupervised," but the training process requires text-image parallel data from blog posts, which is a form of weak supervision.

The authors have addressed most concerns in their rebuttal, particularly clarifying the relationship with [5] and resolving the figure-equation inconsistency. The paper presents a compelling extension to image captioning by modeling sentence-to-sentence coherence, which is well-motivated by real-world usage patterns of image streams. The evaluation is thorough and demonstrates significant improvements over baselines that ignore coherence. The technical approach is sound and well-justified, with the coherence model providing a meaningful contribution to the field.

The paper is well-written and makes a clear contribution to the literature on image description, particularly in the underexplored area of sequence-to-sequence modeling. The proposed CRCN architecture offers a practical solution to a meaningful problem with strong empirical validation. While minor clarifications remain regarding the paragraph vector implementation and the two-layer structure, these do not substantially impact the paper's core contribution. The work is technically sound, well-evaluated, and makes a significant contribution to the field of multimodal learning. I recommend acceptance.
