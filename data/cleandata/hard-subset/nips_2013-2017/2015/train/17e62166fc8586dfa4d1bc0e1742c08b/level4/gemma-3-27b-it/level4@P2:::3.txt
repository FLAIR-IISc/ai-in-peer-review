This paper addresses the problem of generating a sequence of natural language sentences to describe an image stream, a natural extension of single image captioning. The authors propose a coherent recurrent convolutional network (CRCN) integrating convolutional neural networks for image representation, bidirectional recurrent neural networks for language modeling, and a novel entity-based local coherence model to ensure fluency across sentences. The approach is trained on a large corpus of blog posts with associated images, leveraging the inherent storytelling structure of this data source. The paper demonstrates strong quantitative results on benchmark datasets and promising, though less conclusive, results from a user study via Amazon Mechanical Turk.

The core idea of extending image captioning to streams and explicitly modeling inter-sentence coherence is a valuable contribution. The proposed CRCN architecture is well-motivated and appears sound. The use of blog posts as a training source is clever, providing a relatively accessible large-scale dataset with natural image-text pairings that reflect a narrative flow. The quantitative results, particularly the gains over strong baselines, suggest the effectiveness of the proposed approach.

However, several points warrant consideration. While the paper comprehensively reviews related work in single image captioning, it overlooks relevant research in video captioning, which directly tackles sequential input. Specifically, the work by Xu et al. (2017) [32] on jointly modeling deep video and compositional text could be discussed to position the contribution more precisely. The user study results (Table 2) show only a marginal preference for the full CRCN model over the reduced RCN variant. A statistical significance test is crucial to determine if these differences are meaningful and not simply due to random variation. The paper would benefit from a more detailed analysis of the failure cases—situations where the generated sentences are incoherent or inaccurate—to identify potential areas for improvement. Further, while the data augmentation strategy is described, the impact of the κ parameter (number of augmented sentences) on performance isn't explored extensively.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Addresses an important and under-explored problem—generating coherent descriptions for image streams.
*   Proposes a well-motivated and technically sound architecture (CRCN).
*   Demonstrates strong quantitative results on established benchmarks.
*   Cleverly leverages blog posts as a large-scale training dataset.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Lacks discussion of relevant video captioning literature.
*   User study results are not statistically significant.
*   Limited analysis of failure cases and error modes.
*   Insufficient exploration of the impact of hyperparameter choices (e.g., κ).

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a solid contribution with clear potential. The strengths—a well-defined problem, a compelling approach, and strong quantitative results—outweigh the weaknesses. However, addressing the concerns regarding statistical significance, related work, and error analysis would significantly strengthen the paper. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions. Specifically, the authors should:

1.  Include a discussion of relevant video captioning works (e.g., Xu et al., 2017).
2.  Perform statistical significance tests on the user study results.
3.  Provide a more detailed analysis of the model’s failure cases.
4.  Briefly discuss the sensitivity of performance to the κ parameter.
