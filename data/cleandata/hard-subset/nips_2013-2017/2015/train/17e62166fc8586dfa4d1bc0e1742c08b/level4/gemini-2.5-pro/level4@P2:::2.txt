This paper addresses the problem of generating a coherent sequence of natural language sentences to describe a stream of images. This work extends the well-studied task of single-image captioning to a more complex setting, where both the input and output are sequential. The authors propose a novel multimodal architecture, the Coherent Recurrent Convolutional Network (CRCN), which integrates three main components: a Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) for image representation, a Bidirectional Recurrent Neural Network (BRNN) to model the contextual flow of sentence sequences, and an entity-based local coherence model to ensure smooth and logical transitions between sentences. The approach is framed as a retrieval task: for a given query image stream, candidate sentences are first identified for each image from a large training corpus derived from blog posts. The CRCN model is then used to score and rank these candidate sentence sequences, selecting the one that is most visually relevant and textually coherent. The method is evaluated extensively on two large-scale blog datasets using quantitative metrics, qualitative examples, and a user study, demonstrating superior performance over several state-of-the-art baselines.

**Strengths**

*   **Novelty and Significance:** The paper tackles a novel and challenging problem that is a natural and important extension of existing work on image description. The task of describing an image *stream* with a coherent *story* is highly relevant to how users interact with personal photo collections and has significant practical applications.
*   **Technical Contribution:** The proposed CRCN architecture is well-motivated and effectively combines powerful deep learning models for vision (CNN) and language (BRNN) with a classic NLP model for textual coherence. The explicit integration of an entity-based coherence model is a key contribution that directly addresses the unique challenges of generating multi-sentence descriptions for potentially disjointed image streams.
*   **Thorough Evaluation:** The paper provides a comprehensive and convincing evaluation. The authors compare their method against multiple strong baselines on large, real-world datasets. The use of a combination of automatic language metrics, retrieval metrics, and a well-designed Amazon Mechanical Turk user study provides robust evidence for the model's effectiveness.
*   **Ablation Analysis:** The comparison of the full CRCN model against a variant without the coherence component (RCN) clearly demonstrates the value of explicitly modeling sentence-to-sentence transitions, validating a central claim of the paper.
*   **Reproducibility:** The authors' promise to release the source code and dataset is commendable and will be a valuable contribution to the community, enabling further research in this new direction.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement**

The paper is strong, but several points could be clarified to further improve its quality and impact.

1.  **Clarity of Architecture:** There appears to be a potential inconsistency between the depiction of the fully connected (FC) layers in Figure 2b and their formulation in Equation (2). The figure suggests that the FC layers operate on the concatenated outputs of the BRNN and the coherence model for the entire sequence, while the equation implies that the shared weights `Wf1` and `Wf2` are applied to each sentence vector `o_t` individually. Please clarify the precise mechanism and ensure the figure and text are aligned.

2.  **Architectural Justification:**
    *   The paper argues that using two consecutive FC layers (`Wf1`, `Wf2`) is effective because dropout prevents them from collapsing into a single linear map. While plausible, an ablation study comparing this design to a single FC layer (with a comparable number of parameters and dropout) would provide stronger empirical evidence for this choice.
    *   Similarly, the authors state that sharing the FC layer parameters for the BRNN output (`o_t`) and the coherence model output (`q`) empirically outperforms learning them separately. This is an interesting finding. Quantifying this performance difference, perhaps in the supplementary material, would strengthen the paper's claims about the model's design.

3.  **Positioning with Respect to Related Work:** The paper claims that the work by Donahue et al. [5] has "no mechanism for the coherence between sentences." While [5] does not use an entity-based model, their Long-term Recurrent Convolutional Network for video description does learn to generate multi-sentence paragraphs where coherence is implicitly encouraged by the model's recurrent nature. The authors should consider refining this statement to more precisely differentiate their explicit, entity-based coherence modeling from the implicit, topic-level coherence that might be learned by models like [5].

4.  **Missing Implementation Detail:** It is unclear whether the paragraph vectors [17] used to represent sentences were kept as fixed features or were fine-tuned during the training of the CRCN model. Please clarify this detail in the final version.

Overall, this is a high-quality paper that introduces a novel problem and presents a well-designed and thoroughly evaluated solution. The weaknesses identified are primarily requests for clarification that, if addressed, would make an already strong contribution even more convincing.
