

This paper addresses an important extension of image captioning: generating a sequence of natural language sentences to describe a stream of images, rather than a single image. The authors propose a novel multimodal architecture called Coherent Recurrent Convolutional Networks (CRCN), which integrates convolutional neural networks for image representation, bidirectional recurrent neural networks for sentence modeling, and an entity-based local coherence model to ensure smooth transitions between sentences. The key innovation lies in extending the problem from single-image-single-sentence to sequence-to-sequence, which better aligns with how users naturally capture and describe experiences (e.g., through a series of photos during a trip).

The paper makes a strong contribution to the field by addressing a gap in the literature: while most prior work focuses on single-image captioning, the authors recognize that users often capture sequences of images that should be described as a coherent narrative. They leverage blog posts as natural text-image parallel data, where each blog post contains a sequence of images with associated text that follows a narrative structure. The authors demonstrate that their approach significantly outperforms baselines on both quantitative metrics (BLEU, CIDEr, METEOR, top-K recall) and human evaluations on Amazon Mechanical Turk.

The paper excels in several key areas. First, the quality of the work is high: the authors provide a comprehensive evaluation with multiple metrics, ablation studies (showing the contribution of the coherence model), and human evaluations that validate their approach. The method is technically sound and well-justified. Second, the significance is clear: the problem addressed is relevant to real-world applications, and the solution could be extended to other domains with blog-style data. Third, the originality is strong: while there are related works on image captioning and video description, this is the first to explicitly address the sequence-to-sequence problem with coherence modeling for image streams. The authors properly contextualize their contribution against relevant prior work, such as [5] (which focuses on video description but doesn't address sentence coherence) and [11] (which focuses on the opposite problem of image sequence retrieval from text).

The paper's clarity is generally good, though some aspects could be improved. The architecture is well-motivated and the evaluation is thorough. However, there are a few points where the description could be more precise. For example, the relationship between the BRNN output and coherence model in Equation (2) could be clarified, and the role of the paragraph vector (line 103) needs to be specified as either fine-tuned or fixed. The authors should clarify whether the paragraph vector is trained end-to-end with the model or used as a fixed pre-trained representation.

The paper's main strengths include:
- Novel problem formulation (sequence-to-sequence image description)
- Comprehensive evaluation with multiple metrics and human studies
- Clear ablation study demonstrating the contribution of the coherence model
- Practical application with real-world blog data
- Promised release of code and dataset

The main weaknesses to address before publication include:
- Clarifying whether the paragraph vector is fine-tuned or fixed (this affects the model's learning capacity and should be explicitly stated)
- Resolving the discrepancy between Figure 2b and Equation (2) regarding how the fully connected layers connect to the sentence vectors
- Providing justification for using two linear layers (Wf2Wf1) rather than a single linear layer (which would be mathematically equivalent)
- Addressing the claim about [5] not having a coherence mechanism (the paper does model topic coherence across sentences through its intermediate representation)

The authors have addressed these concerns in their rebuttal, and the paper is now substantially stronger. The proposed CRCN model represents a meaningful advancement in the field, moving beyond single-image captioning toward more natural, narrative-based image description. The evaluation is thorough and convincing, with both quantitative metrics and human evaluations showing clear improvements over baselines that don't consider sentence-to-sentence coherence.

The paper is well-positioned to make a significant contribution to NIPS, as it addresses an important gap in the literature with a technically sound and well-evaluated approach. The problem is relevant to practitioners who want to automatically generate narratives from photo collections, and the method could be extended to other domains with sequential image data.

I recommend acceptance. The paper is technically strong, addresses an important gap in the literature, and is well-evaluated. The minor clarifications requested have been addressed in the rebuttal, and the paper now makes a compelling case for its contribution to the field of multimodal learning.
