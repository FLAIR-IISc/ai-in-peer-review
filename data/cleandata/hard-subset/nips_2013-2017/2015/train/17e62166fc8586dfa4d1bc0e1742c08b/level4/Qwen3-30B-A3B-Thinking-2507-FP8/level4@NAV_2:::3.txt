

This paper proposes an approach for generating sentence sequences from image streams, introducing a coherent recurrent convolutional network (CRCN) that integrates convolutional neural networks for image representation, bidirectional recurrent neural networks for language modeling, and an entity-based local coherence model for fluent transitions between sentences. The work extends the typical single-image-to-single-sentence mapping to a more natural sequence-to-sequence problem, motivated by the common practice of capturing image streams during memorable events. The authors leverage blog posts as a natural source of text-image parallel data, which represents a novel approach compared to previous work on image captioning [9, 12, 21].

The paper makes several important contributions: (1) it introduces the novel problem of generating sentence sequences for image streams, (2) it develops the CRCN architecture that integrates multiple complementary components into a unified framework, and (3) it demonstrates the approach on large-scale blog datasets with comprehensive evaluation using both quantitative metrics (BLEU, CIDEr, METEOR, top-K recall) and user studies on Amazon Mechanical Turk.

The paper is technically sound, with a well-structured architecture and thorough evaluation. The problem is original, extending previous work on image captioning to handle sequences rather than single images, and differs from video-to-sentence works [23, 32] by explicitly incorporating a coherence model to handle abrupt visual changes in image streams.

However, two key areas require attention: First, the related work section omits important connections to video-to-sentence generation literature, particularly the work by Xu et al. [26] on "Jointly Modeling Deep Video and Compositional Text to Bridge Vision and Language in a Unified Framework," which has significant overlap with the problem addressed. Second, while the quantitative results show clear improvements over baselines, the user study in Table 2 indicates that CRCN performs similarly to the RCN baseline (which excludes the coherence model), suggesting the coherence model provides only marginal benefits. The paper does not provide statistical significance tests to verify whether the observed improvements are reliable, which is particularly important given the small performance margins observed in the user study.

The paper is well-written and clearly organized, with sufficient detail for reproducibility. The significance of the work is high, as it addresses a natural extension of image captioning with practical applications in summarizing photo collections from events. With minor revisions addressing the related work and statistical significance of the user study results, this would be a strong contribution to NIPS.
