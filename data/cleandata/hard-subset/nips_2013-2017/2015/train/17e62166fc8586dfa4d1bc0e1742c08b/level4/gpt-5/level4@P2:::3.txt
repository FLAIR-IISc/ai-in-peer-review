Summary
The paper addresses the problem of describing an image stream with a coherent sequence of natural sentences. It proposes a multimodal architecture, the coherent recurrent convolutional network (CRCN), which integrates CNN features for images, a bidirectional RNN over sentence embeddings to model content flow, and an entity-based local coherence model (entity grid) to encourage fluent sentence-to-sentence transitions. The system retrieves candidate sentences associated with visually similar training images (from large collections of blog posts), then ranks candidate sequences using the learned compatibility between the image stream and text sequence. Training uses a max-margin objective; evaluation includes automatic metrics (BLEU, CIDEr, METEOR, retrieval R@K/median rank) and AMT user studies. Results show gains over several strong single-sentence captioning baselines and ablations, with the coherence component providing larger benefits on longer sequences.

Relation to prior work
The problem lies at the intersection of image captioning and video description. Unlike single-image–single-sentence models, this work targets image-stream–to–multi-sentence description and explicitly models local coherence. It is related to video-to-sentence methods and multimodal RNN/CNN models, but distinguishes itself by combining BRNNs with an entity-grid coherence model and by framing the task as sequence retrieval from blog corpora.

Strengths
- Problem formulation: Moving from single images to image streams and from single sentences to paragraphs is timely and important.
- Architectural integration: The combination of CNN image features, BRNN language modeling, and an entity-based coherence model is well-motivated and novel in this context.
- Data strategy: Leveraging blog posts as weakly supervised, text–image parallel data is practical and scalable.
- Empirical results: Strong quantitative improvements over several baselines on both language and retrieval metrics; ablations indicate contributions from BRNN and coherence components, especially on longer sequences.
- Implementation details: Clear description of training (BPTT, RMSprop, dropout), negative sampling, and approximate search for sequence retrieval.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Missing related work: The paper should more thoroughly situate itself within video-to-language research. In particular, please discuss “Jointly Modeling Deep Video and Compositional Text to Bridge Vision and Language in a Unified Framework” (Xu et al., AAAI 2015) and other video/paragraph captioning works, clarifying differences (e.g., explicit local coherence modeling, multi-sentence generation vs. single-sentence per clip).
- Statistical significance in user studies: Table 2 shows only a modest preference of CRCN over the BRNN-only variant (RCN), especially for shorter sequences. Please report statistical significance (e.g., binomial tests, bootstrap CIs), inter-annotator agreement, and effect sizes to support the claimed improvements.
- Evaluation focus: Automatic metrics like BLEU/CIDEr/METEOR correlate weakly with discourse coherence. Consider adding coherence-specific evaluations (e.g., entity-grid statistics on outputs, readability/fluency ratings stratified by sequence length).
- Retrieval vs. generation: The approach retrieves sentences rather than generating novel text. Discuss limitations (e.g., stylistic mismatch, redundancy, coverage) and potential diversity mechanisms (e.g., penalizing duplicate n-grams or encouraging sentence variety).
- Preprocessing assumptions: The mapping from images to sentences via paragraph proximity and LSA summarization may introduce alignment noise. Provide validation of segmentation/summarization quality, sensitivity to κ (number of summary sentences), and error analysis of typical failure modes.
- Coherence model details: Zero-padding the 64-d entity-grid vector to 300-d before fusion is ad hoc. Consider a learned projection or MLP and provide sensitivity to the history parameter (length 3) and the impact of coreference/parse errors.
- Search and scalability: Clarify computational costs of retrieval (K-NN on CNN features) and the divide-and-conquer beam search (M=50), including runtime and memory as sequence length grows.
- Reproducibility: Please state whether code, trained models, and processed datasets (or processing scripts) will be released. More details on hyperparameters, negative sampling (100 negatives), and validation criteria would help.

Quality
Technically solid and generally well-executed, but the empirical claims regarding human preference would be stronger with significance testing, agreement measures, and coherence-focused evaluation.

Clarity
The paper is mostly clear and well organized. Some notation could be streamlined (e.g., reusing symbols for augmented sentences), and minor typos should be fixed (e.g., “unied” → “unified”). A brief example illustrating the entity grid over a short sequence would aid readability.

Originality
Integrating an entity-based local coherence model with a CNN-BRNN multimodal framework for multi-sentence description is a meaningful contribution beyond standard image captioning and many video-to-text systems.

Significance
Moderate to high. The task is important, and the approach shows promising gains. Strengthening the user study analysis and broadening related work would increase impact.

Arguments for acceptance
- Addresses a relevant and underexplored problem (image streams to coherent paragraphs).
- Novel, well-motivated integration of coherence modeling with multimodal sequence learning.
- Solid quantitative gains and plausible qualitative examples; ablations support the design.

Arguments against acceptance
- Limited demonstrated advantage over the BRNN-only variant in user studies without significance analysis.
- Incomplete coverage of closely related video-to-text literature.
- Some ad hoc design choices and limited analysis of preprocessing/coherence model sensitivity.

Overall
A strong and promising submission with clear contributions. With improved related work coverage and more rigorous human evaluation (including significance tests and coherence-focused assessments), the paper would be even more compelling.
