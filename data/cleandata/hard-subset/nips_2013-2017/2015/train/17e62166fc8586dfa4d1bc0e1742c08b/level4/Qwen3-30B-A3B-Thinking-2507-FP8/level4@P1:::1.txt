

This paper addresses the problem of generating a sequence of natural language sentences to describe a stream of images, extending the conventional single-image single-sentence description task. The authors propose a novel multimodal architecture called Coherent Recurrent Convolutional Networks (CRCN), which integrates convolutional neural networks for image representation, bidirectional recurrent neural networks for sentence modeling, and an entity-based local coherence model to ensure smooth transitions between sentences. The key innovation lies in extending both input (image stream) and output (sentence sequence) dimensions beyond the standard single-image single-sentence paradigm, which the authors argue better reflects how users naturally capture and describe memorable experiences through photo sequences.

The paper makes several significant contributions. First, it introduces a new problem formulation for describing image streams with sentence sequences, which the authors claim is the first work to address this specific problem. Second, they develop the CRCN architecture that combines CNNs, BRNNs, and a coherence model in an end-to-end manner. Third, they utilize large-scale blog data (20K blog posts with 140K images) as training data, cleverly leveraging the natural text-image parallel structure of blog posts without requiring manual annotation.

The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with a logical flow from problem statement to methodology to evaluation. The authors provide a thorough discussion of related work, particularly distinguishing their approach from video captioning (e.g., Rohrbach et al. 2014) and single-sentence image captioning methods. The technical details of the CRCN architecture are well-explained, with clear descriptions of the BRNN model, coherence model, and their integration.

Strengths:
- The problem formulation is novel and well-motivated by real-world user behavior (capturing photo sequences of memorable moments)
- The use of blog posts as a source of text-image parallel data is innovative and addresses the data scarcity challenge
- The quantitative results (BLEU, CIDEr, METEOR, top-K recall) and user studies via Amazon Mechanical Turk demonstrate clear performance improvements over baselines
- The ablation studies (comparing CRCN vs. RCN vs. 1NN) convincingly show the value of the coherence model
- The paper is technically sound with a solid theoretical foundation

Weaknesses:
- The paper does not adequately cite related work in video captioning, which is directly relevant to the problem (e.g., Rohrbach et al. 2014). While the authors distinguish their work from video captioning (noting that consecutive images in streams may show "sharp changes of visual content"), they should acknowledge and discuss this body of work more thoroughly.
- The dataset collection methodology is somewhat vague. The authors state they "reuse the blog data of Disneyland from [11]" and "newly collect the data of NYC using the same crawling method with [11]," but [11] does not provide sufficient detail on data collection, making reproducibility challenging. The lack of a public dataset release also limits reproducibility.
- The proposed approach for generating sentence sequences is somewhat simplistic: "For each query image, we select K-nearest images from training database using the L2-distance on the CNN VGGNet fc7 features" and then concatenate the associated sentences. This does not fully leverage the coherence model during generation, which appears primarily used for ranking rather than actively constructing the sequence.
- Equation (2) appears to have a notational issue: the right-hand side suggests st can be derived from ot alone, which contradicts the description of the network architecture. The authors should clarify this notation.

The authors make a strong case for the significance of their work by demonstrating that their approach outperforms state-of-the-art baselines on both quantitative metrics and human evaluations. The user studies are particularly convincing, showing that as sequence length increases, the coherence model becomes increasingly important for producing preferred outputs.

Overall, this is a well-executed paper that makes a meaningful contribution to the field. While there are some weaknesses in the presentation of related work and dataset collection, the technical contribution is solid and well-validated. The paper should be accepted as a poster presentation at NIPS.
