Summary
This paper addresses the problem of describing an image stream with a coherent sequence of sentences, moving beyond the common single-image/single-sentence setting. The proposed coherent recurrent convolutional network (CRCN) combines: (i) CNN-based image representations (VGG fc7 features), (ii) a bidirectional RNN over sentence embeddings (paragraph vectors) to model content flow, and (iii) an entity-based local coherence model (Barzilay & Lapata) to encourage fluent sentence-to-sentence transitions. Training uses large, unstructured blog posts (NYC and Disneyland) that are segmented and summarized into image–sentence pairs; paragraph vectors capture sentence content; a max-margin objective learns alignment between image streams and sentence sequences. At test time, the method retrieves K=5 nearest images/sentences per query image and assembles a paragraph via an approximate beam/divide-and-conquer search guided by the learned compatibility score. Quantitative evaluation (BLEU, CIDEr, METEOR; retrieval metrics) and AMT user studies show improvements over strong baselines (Kiros et al., Karpathy et al., Vinyals et al., and a global matching method) and ablations indicate that the coherence component helps, especially for longer sequences.

Strengths
- Novel and well-motivated task: generating coherent multi-sentence descriptions for image streams using web-scale blog data.
- Effective multimodal architecture that integrates CNN features, a bidirectional RNN for sequence modeling, and an entity-grid coherence model.
- Thorough evaluation: quantitative metrics, retrieval metrics, qualitative examples, AMT user studies, and ablations (including removal of coherence).
- Practical contribution: promise to release code and datasets.

Weaknesses, questions, and clarifications
1) Claim about [5]: The paper states there is “no mechanism for the coherence between sentences” in [5]. While [5] does not directly enforce sentence-to-sentence coherence as you do, it predicts an intermediate semantic representation and models video topic across a multi-sentence description, which introduces a degree of cross-sentence consistency. Please qualify this claim to acknowledge that [5] captures topic-level coherence, though not entity-based local coherence.

2) Consistency of Fig. 2(b) with Sec. 3.3 / Eq. (2): Fig. 2(b) suggests the fully connected layers take inputs from all sentences jointly, while Eq. (2) implies shared parameters applied per-sentence outputs ot (and the coherence vector q). Please clarify whether the FC layers operate on each sentence position independently with shared weights, and revise the figure or text so they match.

3) Evaluation metrics: METEOR is often more informative than BLEU, especially with a single reference. You do report METEOR; please emphasize its results and discuss any differences between BLEU, CIDEr, and METEOR to provide a fuller picture.

4) Stacked linear layers in Eq. (2): You use two consecutive linear layers (Wf2, Wf1) without an intervening nonlinearity. Since a composition of linear maps is still linear (dropout aside), the benefit is unclear. You note dropout as the reason they don’t reduce to a single mapping; an ablation disentangling the effects of (a) one vs. two FC layers and (b) dropout rates would strengthen the justification.

5) Parameter sharing between BRNN outputs and the coherence vector: Eq. (2) shares the FC parameters for the sequence content representation O and the coherence vector q. Why is sharing preferable to separate projections? Please provide empirical evidence (e.g., an ablation) or an intuition for why joint parameterization improves ranking.

6) Paragraph vectors [17]: Are paragraph vectors fine-tuned end-to-end within CRCN or kept fixed after pretraining? If fixed, please discuss the trade-offs (stability vs. adaptability) and, if possible, report results with/without fine-tuning.

Assessment against NIPS criteria
- Quality: The model is technically sound; training and inference are clearly specified, and the evaluation is comprehensive. Some architectural choices would benefit from additional ablations (points 4–5).
- Clarity: Generally clear, but Fig. 2(b) vs. Eq. (2) needs reconciliation, and the discussion of [5] should be more precise.
- Originality: The problem setting and integration of an entity-based coherence model with a multimodal RNN/CNN pipeline are novel.
- Significance: The results are strong across metrics and human judgments, and the task/dataset are likely to spur follow-up work.

Pros for acceptance
- Addresses an important, underexplored problem (multi-sentence descriptions of image streams).
- Introduces a coherent, well-engineered architecture with demonstrated gains.
- Solid empirical validation and useful resources (code/data).

Cons for acceptance
- Overstated claim about [5] and a diagram/text mismatch that should be corrected.
- Some architectural choices (stacked linear layers, parameter sharing) need clearer justification via ablation.

Post-rebuttal
After reading the rebuttal, I recommend acceptance. The authors addressed the main concerns regarding formulation, evaluation, and related work. Please incorporate the promised changes and explicitly clarify whether the paragraph vectors [17] are fine-tuned or kept fixed, and the rationale for that choice.
