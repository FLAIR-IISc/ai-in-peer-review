The paper attacks the problem of describing a sequence of images from blog-posts with a sequence of consistent sentences. For this, the paper proposes to first retrieve the K=5 most similar images and associated sentences from the training set for each query image. The main contribution of the paper lies in defining a way to select the most relevant sentences for the query image sequence, providing a coherent description. For this, sentences are first embedded in a vector and then the sequence of sentences is modeled with a bidirectional LSTM. The output of the bi-directional LSTM is first fed through a ReLU and fully connected layer and then scored with a compatibility score between image and sentence. Additionally, a local coherence model [1] is included to enforce the compatibility between sentences.

**Strengths / positive aspects:**
- The paper proposes a novel and effective architecture to retrieve coherent sentences for an image sequence.
- The paper provides an extensive quantitative, qualitative, and human evaluation, showing the superiority of their approach against several baselines that do not utilize coherence modeling. They also provide an ablation experiment, removing the coherence model [1].
- The authors promise to release source code and dataset.

**Weaknesses / Questions / Unclarities:**
1. Line 94: The paper claims that there is "no mechanism for the coherence between sentences" in [5]. Although not the primary contribution of [5], the work predicts an intermediate semantic representation of videos, which fosters coherence across sentences by modeling the overall topic of the multi-sentence description.
2. Correctness/clarity: Figure 2b does not seem to correspond to the description in section 3.3/Equation (2). While Figure 2b implies that the fully connected layers are connected to all sentences, this is not the case in Eq. 2, which implies that the parameters are shared across sentences but only connected to the vector representing a single sentence. 
3. A better metric to automatically evaluate the generated sentences is METEOR [16] instead of BLEU [22], especially when evaluating against a single reference sentence.
4. The use of two linear functions in Eq. 2 (Wf1, Wf2) is unclear. Two linear functions are equivalent to a single linear function, and the benefit of this composition is not immediately apparent. An ablation study demonstrating the advantages of these functions would be valuable.
5. Why are the same parameters used for the fully connected layers for both the BRNN output (ot) and the local coherence model (q) (Equation 2)?
6. Is the paragraph vector [17] fine-tuned during training or kept fixed?

**Arguments for acceptance:** The paper addresses a novel problem – generating coherent sentence sequences from image streams – and proposes a well-motivated and thoughtfully designed architecture (CRCN). The extensive experimental evaluation, including quantitative metrics and user studies, convincingly demonstrates the effectiveness of the proposed approach compared to existing baselines. The ablation studies further validate the contribution of the coherence model.

**Arguments against acceptance:** Some technical details require clarification (points 2, 4, 5, and 6 above). The claim regarding the lack of coherence modeling in [5] is inaccurate.

**Overall Assessment:** Despite the minor issues raised, the paper represents a significant contribution to the field. The proposed approach is innovative, well-evaluated, and addresses an important problem. However, to be a truly convincing paper, several clarifications are necessary.
