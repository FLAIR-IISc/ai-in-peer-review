This paper tackles generating a coherent multi-sentence description for a sequence of blog images. The approach first retrieves the K=5 most similar training images (and their associated sentences) for each query image. The core contribution is a mechanism to select and sequence the most relevant sentences so that the overall narrative is coherent. Sentences are embedded into vectors and modeled across the sequence with a bidirectional LSTM. Each BiLSTM output is passed through a ReLU and a fully connected layer, and then scored via an image–sentence compatibility function. A local coherence model [1] is also incorporated to encourage compatibility between adjacent sentences.

Strengths:
- Proposes a novel, effective architecture for retrieving coherent sentences for image sequences.
- Presents extensive quantitative, qualitative, and human evaluations demonstrating superiority over baselines that do not model coherence, plus an ablation removing the coherence component [1].
- Commits to releasing source code and the dataset.

Weaknesses / Questions / Clarifications:
1. Line 94: The paper claims that [5] has “no mechanism for coherence between sentences.” While coherence is not the primary focus of [5], it predicts an intermediate semantic representation for videos and models a topic across the multi-sentence description, which does induce cross-sentence coherence. Please refine this statement.
2. Correctness/clarity: Figure 2b appears inconsistent with Section 3.3 and Eq. (2). The figure suggests the fully connected layer connects to all sentences, whereas Eq. (2) implies shared parameters applied to each sentence’s vector independently. Please reconcile the figure and equation.
3. Evaluation: With a single reference sentence, METEOR (http://www.cs.cmu.edu/~alavie/METEOR/) is often a more suitable automatic metric than BLEU. Please consider reporting METEOR.
4. Architecture: Eq. (2) applies two linear layers in sequence (W_f2 followed by W_f1). Without an intervening nonlinearity, two linear maps are equivalent to a single linear map. What is the benefit? An ablation examining these layers would be useful.
5. Parameter sharing: Why are the same fully connected parameters used for both the BiRNN output o_t and the local coherence model q in Eq. (2)? Please justify this choice or discuss alternatives without sharing.
6. Training detail: Is the paragraph vector [16] fine-tuned during training or kept fixed? Please specify.

Post-rebuttal:
After reading the rebuttal, I recommend acceptance. The authors have satisfactorily addressed concerns about the formulation, evaluation, and related work. Please implement the promised revisions in the final version and explicitly clarify item (6): whether the paragraph vector [16] is fine-tuned or frozen. Overall, this is an interesting and well-evaluated model for retrieving coherent sentences for image streams, pending the above clarifications.
