

This paper addresses the novel problem of generating coherent sentence sequences from image streams (e.g., a series of photos capturing a travel experience), extending prior work that focuses on single-image/single-sentence generation. The authors propose a multimodal architecture called Coherent Recurrent Convolutional Networks (CRCN), integrating convolutional neural networks (for image features), bidirectional recurrent networks (for sentence modeling), and an entity-based local coherence model (to ensure fluent transitions between sentences). The key innovation lies in treating both input (image sequence) and output (sentence sequence) as structured temporal data, rather than isolated pairs. The approach leverages user-generated blog posts as training data—where text and images are naturally aligned in storytelling contexts—avoiding the need for manual annotation. The paper demonstrates superior performance over baselines (including variants of multimodal RNNs and retrieval models) using BLEU, CIDEr, top-K recall, and user studies on NYC/Disneyland datasets (20K blogs, 140K images).

**Strengths:**  
*Originality & Significance (High):* The problem of sequence-to-sequence image description is genuinely underexplored in NIPS literature (e.g., prior work like [5, 9, 30] focuses on single-image captioning). The authors convincingly argue that users’ photo streams represent a natural input form, and their blog-based dataset is both large and realistic. This addresses a meaningful gap in multimodal understanding, with clear applicability to tourism, social media, and personal memory systems. The CRCN architecture’s integration of coherence modeling (a critical missing piece in prior video-to-sentence work [23, 32]) is a significant technical contribution.  
*Quality (High):* The method is technically sound, with clear rationale for architectural choices (e.g., using sentences as building blocks instead of words to avoid vanishing gradients). The dataset curation from blogs is well-motivated and replicable. Experiments are thorough: quantitative metrics cover both language quality (BLEU, METEOR) and retrieval accuracy (top-K recall), while user studies validate human preference. The ablation study (comparing CRCN vs. RCN) isolates the coherence model’s contribution.  
*Clarity (Good):* The paper is well-organized, with clear diagrams (Figs. 1–2) and concise explanations of the CRCN components. The blog preprocessing pipeline (Section 2) is particularly well-specified. Minor issues: the BRNN equations (Section 3.1) could be more intuitive, and the "entity grid" description (Section 3.2) could benefit from a concrete example.

**Weaknesses:**  
*Significance (Moderate):* While the problem is novel and the method effective, the user study (Table 2) shows only marginal advantages over the RCN baseline (without coherence), especially for shorter sequences (5 images). The paper claims "superior performance" but lacks statistical significance testing (e.g., paired t-tests) to confirm that improvements (e.g., 2.1% higher top-5 recall in Table 1) are meaningful beyond random variation. This weakens the claim of *demonstrable* advancement over state-of-the-art.  
*Clarity (Minor):* The connection between coherence modeling and real-world fluency is implied but not rigorously quantified (e.g., how many "incoherent" sequences does CRCN avoid?). The AMT task design (Section 4.3) could clarify why 5-image queries were used, as the authors note coherence matters more for longer sequences (Table 2, last column).  
*Related Work (Incomplete):* The discussion overlooks key video-to-sentence work, such as Xu et al.’s "Jointly Modeling Deep Video..." (ICCV 2015), which also addresses temporal coherence in visual narratives. This omission undermines the novelty claim, as their problem setup shares conceptual similarities (though their focus is video, not user-generated image streams).

**Pro/Con Acceptance Summary:**  
*Pro:*  
- First work to address sequence-to-sequence image description (addressing a clear gap in NIPS literature).  
- Strong dataset (blog-based, scalable, realistic) and thorough evaluation (quantitative + human).  
- CRCN’s integration of coherence modeling is a meaningful technical advance over prior multimodal networks.  
*Con:*  
- User study lacks statistical significance testing, making the "superior performance" claim less robust.  
- Overlooks relevant video-to-sentence work (e.g., Xu et al.), weakening novelty justification.  
- Marginal gains over RCN in human evaluation for short sequences (5 images) need clearer explanation.

**Recommendation:** Accept. The paper makes a valuable contribution to multimodal learning by tackling a realistic, underexplored problem with a well-motivated, technically sound method. While the significance claim would be strengthened by statistical tests, the core innovation (sequence-level coherence modeling) is significant and the experiments are rigorous. The minor related work gap is easily addressable in revision. This work advances the field beyond single-image captioning and demonstrates clear potential for real-world applications.
