The paper tackles the problem of generating a sequence of consistent sentences to describe a sequence of images from blog posts. To achieve this, the authors propose a method that first retrieves the K=5 most similar images and associated sentences from the training set for each query image. The primary contribution of the paper lies in defining a novel approach to select the most relevant sentences for the query image sequence, providing a coherent description. This is accomplished by embedding sentences in a vector space and modeling the sequence of sentences using a bidirectional LSTM. The output of the bidirectional LSTM is then passed through a ReLU activation function and a fully connected layer, followed by a compatibility score calculation between the image and sentence. Additionally, a local coherence model is incorporated to enforce compatibility between sentences.

Strengths and positive aspects of the paper include:

- The proposal of a novel and effective architecture for retrieving coherent sentences for an image sequence.
- The provision of extensive quantitative, qualitative, and human evaluations, demonstrating the superiority of the proposed approach over several baselines that do not utilize coherence. The authors also provide an ablation experiment, which involves removing the coherence model.
- The commitment to release the source code and dataset.

Weaknesses, questions, and areas of unclarity in the paper include:

1. The claim in Line 94 that there is "no mechanism for the coherence between sentences" in [5] is disputed. Although not the primary contribution of [5], the paper predicts an intermediate semantic representation of videos, which is coherent across sentences by modeling the topic of the multi-sentence description.

2. The correctness and clarity of Figure 2b and its description in Section 3.3/Equation (2) are questionable. Specifically, Figure 2b implies that the fully connected layers are connected to all sentences, whereas Equation (2) suggests that the parameters are shared across sentences but only connected to the vector representing a single sentence.

3. A more suitable metric for automatically evaluating the generated sentences might be Meteor (http://www.cs.cmu.edu/~alavie/METEOR/) instead of BLEU, particularly when there is only a single reference sentence.

4. The application of two linear functions in Equation (2) (W{f2} and W{f1}) behind each other is unclear, as two linear functions can be reduced to a single linear function. An ablation study demonstrating the benefit of these functions would be valuable.

5. The use of the same parameters for the fully connected layers in both the BRNN output (o_t) and the local coherence model q (Equation 2) requires clarification.

6. It is unclear whether the paragraph vector [16] is fine-tuned or kept fixed.

After reading the rebuttal, I recommend the paper for acceptance, as the authors have successfully addressed issues with the formulation, evaluation, and related work. The paper proposes an interesting new model to retrieve coherent sentences for an image stream, which is convincingly evaluated. However, to strengthen the paper, several clarifications must be made. Specifically, the authors should clarify whether the paragraph vector [16] is fine-tuned or kept fixed.
