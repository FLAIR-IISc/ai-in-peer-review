This paper addresses the problem of generating a coherent sequence of sentences to describe a sequence of images, such as those found in blog posts. The proposed method first retrieves the K=5 most similar images and their associated sentences from the training set for each query image. The main contribution is a novel method for selecting the most relevant sentences from this retrieved set to form a coherent description for the entire image sequence. To achieve this, the model first embeds each candidate sentence into a vector representation. Then, a bidirectional LSTM is used to model the sequence of these sentence embeddings. The output of the Bi-LSTM for each sentence is passed through a ReLU activation and a fully connected layer, which is then used to compute a compatibility score between the sentence and its corresponding image. Additionally, a local coherence model based on [1] is incorporated to enforce compatibility between adjacent sentences.

**Strengths:**
*   The paper proposes a novel and effective architecture for selecting a coherent sequence of sentences to describe an image stream.
*   The authors conduct extensive quantitative, qualitative, and human evaluations, which demonstrate the superiority of their approach over several baselines that do not model coherence. The paper also includes a valuable ablation study that shows the contribution of the coherence model from [1].
*   The authors have committed to releasing their source code and dataset, which will benefit the community.

**Weaknesses and Questions:**
1.  **Related Work (Line 94):** The paper claims that the method in [5] has "no mechanism for the coherence between sentences". This seems to be an overstatement. While coherence was not the main contribution of [5], that work does predict an intermediate semantic representation for videos. This representation models the topic of the multi-sentence description, thereby inducing some coherence across the generated sentences.

2.  **Clarity:** There appears to be a discrepancy between Figure 2b and the description in Section 3.3 (specifically, Equation 2). Figure 2b suggests that the fully connected layer is connected to all sentence representations simultaneously. However, Equation 2 implies that the parameters of this layer are shared across sentences, but the layer operates on each sentence representation individually.

3.  **Evaluation Metric:** The authors use BLEU for automatic evaluation. However, METEOR is generally considered a more suitable metric, especially in settings with a single reference sentence, as it better correlates with human judgment.

4.  **Model Architecture:** Equation 2 applies two consecutive linear transformations (W_f1 and W_f2). Since the composition of two linear functions is itself a single linear function, the benefit of this two-layer structure is unclear. An ablation study demonstrating the advantage of this design would strengthen the paper.

5.  **Parameter Sharing:** In Equation 2, why are the same fully connected layer parameters used for both the Bi-RNN output (o_t) and the local coherence term (q)? Could the authors justify this design choice?

6.  **Implementation Details:** Were the paragraph vectors [16] fine-tuned during training, or were they kept fixed?
