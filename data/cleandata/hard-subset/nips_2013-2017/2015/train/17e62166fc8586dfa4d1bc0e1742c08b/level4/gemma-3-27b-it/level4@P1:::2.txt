The paper addresses the problem of describing a sequence of images from blog posts with a sequence of coherent sentences. To this end, the authors propose first retrieving the K=5 most similar images and associated sentences from the training set for each query image. The main contribution of the paper lies in defining a method to select the most relevant sentences for the query image sequence, providing a coherent description. For this, sentences are first embedded into a vector space, and then the sequence of sentences is modeled with a bidirectional LSTM. The output of the bidirectional LSTM is fed through ReLU and fully connected layers, and then scored with a compatibility score between the image and sentence. Additionally, a local coherence model [1] is included to enforce compatibility between sentences.

**Strengths / Positive Aspects:**

*   The paper proposes a novel and effective architecture to retrieve coherent sentences for an image sequence.
*   The paper provides extensive quantitative, qualitative, and human evaluations, demonstrating the superiority of their approach against several baselines that do not utilize coherence modeling. They also present an ablation study removing the coherence model [1].
*   The authors promise to release source code and the dataset.

**Weaknesses / Questions / Unclarities:**

1.  Line 94: The paper claims that “[5] has no mechanism for the coherence between sentences.” However, [5] predicts an intermediate semantic representation of videos, which implicitly provides coherence across sentences by modeling the topic of the multi-sentence description.
2.  Correctness/clarity: Figure 2b does not seem to correspond to the description in section 3.3/Equation (2). While Figure 2b implies that the fully connected layers are connected to all sentences, Equation (2) suggests that the parameters are shared across sentences, connecting them only to the vector representing a single sentence.
3.  A more appropriate metric to automatically evaluate the generated sentences is METEOR ([16]) instead of BLEU, especially given the use of a single reference sentence.
4.  The rationale for applying two linear functions (Wf1, Wf2) in sequence in Equation (2) is unclear. Since two linear functions are equivalent to a single linear function, the benefit of this configuration is not apparent. An ablation study demonstrating the impact of these functions would be valuable.
5.  Why are the same parameters used for the fully connected layers for the BRNN output (ot) and the local coherence model output (q) in Equation (2)?
6.  Is the paragraph vector [17] fine-tuned during training, or is it kept fixed?

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a compelling approach to the challenging task of generating coherent sentence sequences from image streams. The proposed architecture integrates several recent advances in multimodal learning, including CNNs, RNNs, and a local coherence model. The extensive experimental evaluation, including quantitative metrics and user studies, provides strong evidence for the effectiveness of the proposed approach. While some minor clarifications are needed, the paper makes a significant contribution to the field and warrants acceptance. 

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novelty: The problem of generating coherent sentence sequences for image streams is relatively unexplored.
*   Technical Soundness: The proposed architecture is well-motivated and appears to be technically sound.
*   Empirical Validation: The extensive experimental evaluation demonstrates the superiority of the proposed approach.
*   Significance: The ability to generate coherent descriptions of image streams has potential applications in various domains.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   Minor technical clarifications are needed regarding the architecture and experimental setup.
*   The claim regarding the lack of coherence modeling in [5] is inaccurate.
