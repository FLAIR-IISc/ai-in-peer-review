This paper addresses retrieving natural sentences that describe a given sequence of images and composing them into a coherent multi-sentence output. The problem is important in computer vision and pattern recognition, and it is closely related to multi-sentence video description (e.g., Rohrbach et al., 2014): a single video can be viewed as a sequence of images, since videos can be segmented into shots and each shot can be represented by a key frame. However, this line of work is not cited.

The paper is generally well written and easy to follow. The problem is clearly stated and tackled with solid techniques.

The proposed CRCN model learns relationships between a sequence of images and a sequence of natural sentences. The architecture appears technically sound, and, aided by entity-based coherence models, it captures discourse relations effectively.

The procedure for generating a sequence of sentences is reasonable but rather primitive: each sentence is directly associated with training images similar to a given image, and the final output is formed by simply concatenating the selected sentences in the order of the corresponding images. This design seems ad hoc and may limit coherence and diversity.

Data collection raises concerns. In Section 4.1, the authors state: “We reuse the blog data of Disneyland from the dataset of [11], and newly collect the data of NYC, using the same crawling method as [11].” The dataset is not disclosed, and the cited paper [11] does not describe the crawling details. This strongly suggests substantial overlap between the authors of this paper and [11], which undermines the double-blind review process. Moreover, the lack of dataset release and documentation impedes reproducibility.

Equation (2) may be incorrect. As written, it implies that s_t depends only on o_t, which would mean the network is not fully connected as intended. Please clarify whether previous states or other inputs also feed into s_t.

In summary, the CRCN architecture is technically sound and models discourse relationships well with the help of entity-based coherence models, but the sentence generation pipeline is ad hoc and the related-work coverage and dataset/anonymity issues are problematic. I would lean toward acceptance as a poster.
