This paper addresses the problem of retrieving a coherent sequence of natural language sentences to describe a stream of images. This is presented as a logical and important extension to the widely studied problem of single-image captioning. The authors propose a novel multimodal architecture, the Coherent Recurrent Convolutional Network (CRCN), which integrates three main components: a Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) for image feature extraction, a Bidirectional Recurrent Neural Network (BRNN) to model the content flow in the text sequence, and an entity-based local coherence model to ensure smooth transitions between sentences. The model is trained on a large-scale dataset scraped from blog posts. At test time, the system works in a retrieval-based fashion: it first finds candidate sentences for each image in a query stream from the training data and then uses the learned CRCN model to rank the possible combinations of these sentences to form a final, coherent paragraph. The approach is evaluated extensively using both automatic metrics (BLEU, R@K) and human studies on Amazon Mechanical Turk, demonstrating superior performance over several state-of-the-art baselines adapted for this task.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Significance and Originality:** The paper tackles a novel and well-motivated problem. Moving from single image-sentence pairs to image stream-paragraph pairs is a natural and significant step forward for the field. To my knowledge, this is one of the first papers to formally address this specific task.

2.  **Technical Approach:** The proposed CRCN architecture is technically sound and thoughtfully designed. The integration of an explicit coherence model ([1]) to complement the BRNN's modeling of content flow is a key strength. This addresses the unique challenge in image streams where, unlike video, visual content can change abruptly between consecutive images, making textual coherence particularly important.

3.  **Clarity and Presentation:** The paper is well-written, clearly organized, and easy to follow. The problem statement is well-defined, and the proposed architecture is explained in sufficient detail. The experimental setup and results are presented clearly.

4.  **Thorough Evaluation:** The authors conduct a comprehensive evaluation on a large, real-world dataset. The comparison against several strong baselines, including adaptations of recent high-impact models ([9], [12], [30]), is convincing. The inclusion of both automatic metrics and human user studies provides a robust validation of the proposed method's effectiveness.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Relation to Video Description:** The paper could be strengthened by a more thorough discussion of and comparison to related work in video description (e.g., [23, 32], Rohrbach et al. ICCV'13, Venugopalan et al. CVPR'15). The task of describing a sequence of video frames is conceptually very similar to describing a stream of still images. While the authors argue that image streams may have sharper discontinuities, the underlying challenge of generating coherent, sequential text from sequential visual input is a shared one. Acknowledging this connection more directly and discussing how the proposed methods differ from or build upon video description techniques would better contextualize the paper's contribution.

2.  **Retrieval-Based Generation:** The method for generating the final sentence sequence is retrieval-based rather than truly generative. Candidate sentences are drawn from those associated with the K-nearest training images, and the CRCN model's primary role is to re-rank these pre-existing sentences into a coherent sequence. This reliance on a fixed pool of sentences limits the system's expressive power and creativity. The output is constrained to combinations of sentences seen during training, which may be a significant limitation compared to models that generate text word-by-word. This makes the approach feel more incremental and less novel than a fully generative model for this task would be.

3.  **Reproducibility and Anonymity:** There is a potential concern regarding reproducibility and the double-blind review process. The paper states, "We reuse the blog data of Disneyland from the dataset of [11], and newly collect the data of NYC, using the same crawling method with [11]." However, reference [11] does not provide sufficient detail on the crawling method, and the dataset is not made public. This makes the work difficult for others to reproduce or build upon. Furthermore, this specific reference to unpublished methods from a single, recent paper strongly suggests a significant author overlap, which may compromise the integrity of the double-blind review.

4.  **Clarity on Model Architecture:** There appears to be a potential lack of clarity in Equation (2). The formulation `Wf2Wf1[O|q] = [S|g]` seems to imply a column-wise mapping where each sentence score `st` is derived only from its corresponding RNN output `ot`, rather than from the full sequence of RNN outputs. If this is the case, the final layers are not fully connected in the way one might expect, and information from the full sequence is not being integrated at this final scoring stage. A clarification on this point would be beneficial.

**Summary and Recommendation:**

This paper presents a solid contribution by defining a new, important problem and proposing a technically sound model that combines sequence modeling and discourse coherence. The experiments are thorough and demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed CRCN model.

*   **Pros:**
    *   Addresses the novel and significant problem of describing image streams with sentence sequences.
    *   The CRCN architecture, which explicitly models coherence, is well-suited for the task.
    *   The paper is clearly written and well-structured.
    *   Extensive quantitative and qualitative evaluations show strong results.

*   **Cons:**
    *   The retrieval-based sentence generation method is somewhat limited in novelty and expressiveness.
    *   The connection to the highly related field of video description is not sufficiently discussed.
    *   There are concerns regarding the reproducibility of the dataset and a potential breach of reviewer anonymity.

Overall, the paper has more strengths than weaknesses. The problem is interesting, and the proposed solution is elegant and effective. While the generation method is somewhat ad-hoc and not fully generative, the work represents a valuable first step on an important problem. The concerns about reproducibility and anonymity are notable but may be addressable. I recommend acceptance, likely as a poster presentation.
