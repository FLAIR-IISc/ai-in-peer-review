This paper addresses the problem of generating a coherent sequence of sentences to describe a sequence of images, such as those found in blog posts. The core idea is to retrieve the K=5 most similar images and their corresponding sentences from the training set for each input image, and then select the most relevant and coherent sentences to form a complete description. The primary contribution lies in a novel approach to selecting these relevant sentences, achieved by embedding sentences into a vector space and modeling the resulting sequence with a bidirectional LSTM. The LSTM’s output is then processed through ReLU and fully connected layers, followed by a compatibility scoring mechanism that assesses the relationship between each sentence and the input images. To further enforce coherence, the model incorporates a local coherence model, building upon prior work [1].

**Strengths/Positive Aspects:**

*   The paper presents a novel and effective architecture for retrieving coherent sentences given an image sequence.
*   The authors provide a comprehensive evaluation, including quantitative results, qualitative examples, and a human evaluation, demonstrating the superiority of their approach over several baselines that do not explicitly model coherence. An ablation study effectively isolates the contribution of the local coherence model [1].
*   The authors have committed to releasing both the source code and the dataset, which is a valuable contribution to the research community.

**Weaknesses/Questions/Unclarities:**

1.  The paper states (line 94) that [5] lacks a mechanism for coherence between sentences. However, [5] predicts an intermediate semantic representation for videos, which inherently captures topic coherence across sentences within a multi-sentence description, even if that isn’t its primary contribution.
2.  There is a discrepancy between Figure 2b and Equation (2) in Section 3.3. Figure 2b appears to illustrate a fully connected layer connected to all sentences, whereas Equation (2) suggests shared parameters across sentences, with connections limited to the vector representing a single sentence. This needs clarification.
3.  While BLEU is used for automatic evaluation, METEOR (http://www.cs.cmu.edu/~alavie/METEOR/) might be a more appropriate metric, particularly when dealing with single reference sentences.
4.  Equation (2) utilizes two consecutive linear functions (W<sub>f2</sub> and W<sub>f1</sub>). Since applying two linear functions is equivalent to a single linear function, the benefit of this specific design choice is unclear. An ablation study investigating the impact of these functions would be valuable.
5.  Why are the same parameters used for the fully connected layers when processing the bidirectional RNN output (o<sub>t</sub>) and when calculating the local coherence score q (Equation 2)? The rationale behind this parameter sharing should be explained.
6.  The paragraph vector [16] is referenced; is it fine-tuned during training, or is it kept fixed?
