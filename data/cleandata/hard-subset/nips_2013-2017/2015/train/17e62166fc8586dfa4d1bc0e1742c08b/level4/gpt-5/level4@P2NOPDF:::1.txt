This paper addresses the task of retrieving natural sentences to describe a sequence of images and assembling them into a coherent multi-sentence description. The problem is important for computer vision and pattern recognition. It is also closely related to multi-sentence video description (e.g., Rohrbach et al., 2014), since a video can be viewed as a sequence of frames or shots summarized by key frames. However, this line of work is not cited, and the paper would benefit from acknowledging and positioning itself with respect to the video-to-text literature.

The paper is clearly written, and the problem is well formulated. The proposed CRCN model learns relationships between sequences of images and sequences of sentences. The architecture appears technically sound, and the use of entity-based coherence models is an appropriate way to capture discourse-level relations.

That said, the sentence generation strategy is fairly rudimentary. Each sentence is retrieved by matching a given image to similar training images and directly borrowing their associated sentences; the final description is a simple concatenation in image order. This pipeline does not explicitly optimize for global coherence or avoid redundancy across sentences beyond what the coherence model can indirectly enforce. A more integrated generation or re-ranking approach that jointly considers the entire sequence would likely yield stronger discourse structure and content selection.

Section 4.1 raises concerns about dataset availability and anonymity. The authors “reuse the blog data of Disneyland from [11] and newly collect the data of NYC using the same crawling method as [11],” but the dataset is not released and [11] does not provide crawling details. This makes reproducibility difficult. Moreover, the phrasing strongly suggests author overlap with [11], which may compromise double-blind review. The authors should revise the text to preserve anonymity and provide sufficient dataset details (or a plan for release) to enable replication.

Equation (2) appears problematic. As written, it implies s_t depends only on o_t, which would suggest the state is not fully connected to the relevant past inputs or states. Please clarify the intended dependencies and correct the notation if necessary to reflect the full connectivity of the network.

Overall, the model design and learning framework are solid and the paper is easy to follow. The main weaknesses are inadequate engagement with closely related video description literature, a somewhat ad hoc retrieval-and-concatenate generation strategy, and issues around dataset disclosure and anonymity. With these caveats, I believe the paper is worthy of acceptance as a poster.
