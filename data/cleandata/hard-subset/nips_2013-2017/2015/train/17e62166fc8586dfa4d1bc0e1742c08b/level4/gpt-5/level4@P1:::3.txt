Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper tackles the problem of describing an image stream with a sequence of natural sentences, extending prior work that focused on single-image/single-sentence captioning. The proposed coherent recurrent convolutional network (CRCN) integrates three components: (1) a CNN (VGG) for image representation; (2) a bidirectional RNN (BRNN) over sentence representations (doc2vec paragraph vectors) to model textual content flow; and (3) an entity-based local coherence model (entity grid) to encourage coherent sentence-to-sentence transitions. Training data are derived automatically from blog posts by aligning images to text segments via paragraph heuristics and summarizing each segment to key sentences using LSA. At test time, for each image in the query stream the method retrieves K nearest training images and assembles candidate sentence sequences using a divide-and-conquer beam search. The model is trained with a max-margin loss on aligned image/sentence-sequence pairs and evaluated on two tourism domains (NYC, Disneyland) using BLEU/CIDEr/METEOR and retrieval metrics (R@K, median rank), plus AMT user studies. The approach is positioned against retrieval-based captioning (e.g., Socher et al., Karpathy & Fei-Fei, Kiros et al.) and video-to-text methods, with the novelty of explicitly modeling discourse coherence over sentence sequences.

Strengths:
- Problem formulation: Moving from single images to image streams and from single sentences to sentence sequences is a natural and timely extension with practical relevance (e.g., travel blogs, photo albums).
- Model design: Combining a BRNN over sentence embeddings with an entity-based local coherence model is a sensible and novel way to encourage fluent multi-sentence descriptions. The end-to-end training with a structured max-margin objective is clear.
- Data sourcing: Leveraging large-scale user-generated blogs as weakly aligned image–text streams is pragmatic and scalable.
- Quantitative results: On both datasets the method shows strong improvements over competitive baselines (e.g., MLBL variants, CNN+RNN, CNN+LSTM) on language and especially retrieval metrics. Ablations (1NN, RCN vs. CRCN) suggest both the BRNN and the coherence component contribute.

Weaknesses and suggestions:
- Related work coverage: Some closely related video-to-text sequence modeling work is missing or insufficiently discussed, in particular “Jointly Modeling Deep Video and Compositional Text to Bridge Vision and Language in a Unified Framework” (Xu et al., AAAI 2015). Please contrast CRCN with such models that explicitly handle sequential inputs/outputs, clarifying differences in supervision, modeling of temporal/coherence structure, and evaluation.
- Human evaluation: While the automatic metrics favor CRCN, the AMT study in Table 2 shows performance similar to the RCN baseline. A statistical significance analysis (e.g., binomial sign test or bootstrap confidence intervals over pairwise preferences) is needed to establish whether the observed gains are reliable. This is especially important given that the AMT evaluation with up to five images per query appears to attenuate the benefit of the coherence model. The additional study with 8–10 images is a good idea; please report detailed numbers, confidence intervals, and tests there as well.
- Scope and generalization: The datasets are limited to tourism domains (NYC, Disneyland). Although the approach is unsupervised, claims of broad applicability would be stronger with experiments beyond travel blogs or with a cross-domain evaluation demonstrating robustness.
- Preprocessing assumptions: The pipeline relies on heuristic image–text alignment and LSA-based summarization. Some analysis of alignment accuracy and summarization quality (e.g., manual spot checks or sensitivity analysis) would help assess how noise propagates to training and evaluation.
- Clarity and reproducibility: While most components are described, additional details would improve clarity: hyperparameters for doc2vec training, BRNN architectural choices (e.g., why separate input transforms for forward/backward), search complexity and runtime for the divide-and-conquer beam search, and the effect of κ (data augmentation) on performance.

Evaluation against NIPS criteria:
- Quality: Technically sound overall, with a clear architecture and training objective. The quantitative evaluation is thorough across multiple metrics and baselines; however, the human evaluation needs statistical testing to substantiate claims. Some components (entity grid in noisy blog text, heuristic alignment/summarization) would benefit from error/sensitivity analysis.
- Clarity: The paper is generally well organized, and the overall pipeline is understandable. The exposition of the coherence model and its integration is clear. Please improve the related work section to include and contrast missing video-to-text models and add more detail on preprocessing and evaluation statistics.
- Originality: The combination of a BRNN over sentence embeddings with an entity-based coherence model for multi-sentence retrieval is novel, and the focus on image streams is a meaningful extension beyond single-image captioning.
- Significance: The task is important and likely to spur follow-up work. The method’s gains on automatic metrics are promising, but the AMT results currently show only a weak advantage over the RCN baseline; stronger human-study evidence (with significance tests and longer sequences) is needed to establish impact.

Pros:
- Addresses an important and underexplored problem (image streams to coherent sentence sequences).
- Thoughtful integration of CNN, BRNN, and an entity-based coherence model.
- Demonstrated improvements over strong baselines on multiple quantitative metrics.
- Scalable data collection from blogs without manual annotation.

Cons:
- Incomplete discussion of closely related video-to-text sequence models (e.g., Xu et al., AAAI 2015).
- AMT user study shows only marginal advantage over RCN; lacks significance testing.
- Evaluation limited to two tourism domains; generalization not demonstrated.
- Reliance on heuristic alignment/summarization without analysis of noise effects.

Recommendation and actionable feedback:
- Expand the related work section to include and clearly differentiate from recent video-to-text sequence models, especially Xu et al. (AAAI 2015).
- Augment the human evaluation with statistical significance tests and report confidence intervals; include full results for longer sequences where coherence should matter more.
- Provide analysis of the preprocessing quality and its impact on performance, and add more implementation details to improve reproducibility.
- If possible, include an additional domain or a cross-domain test to support claims of broader applicability.

Overall, this is a nice piece of work with a compelling problem and promising results, but it would benefit from a strengthened related work discussion and more rigorous human evaluation to substantiate the claimed gains over RCN.
