- Problem: Generates natural-language descriptions for image streams (sequences) rather than single images, a meaningful extension of captioning.

- Method: Proposes CRCN combining CNN image features, bidirectional RNN sentence modeling, and an entity-based local coherence model.

- Motivation: Users often capture image series during events, so sequence-level descriptions are more natural than single-image captions.

- Contribution 1: Claimed to be the first to explicitly target sentence-sequence generation from image streams.

- Contribution 2: Introduces a novel architecture integrating multimodal representation with a coherence model.

- Contribution 3: Leverages blog posts as parallel text-image training data for image sequences.

- Experiments: Extensive evaluation on NYC and Disneyland blog datasets (>20K posts, 140K images) using BLEU/CIDEr/METEOR/Top-K and AMT user studies.

- Results: CRCN yields consistent quantitative improvements over strong baselines and is favored in user studies, especially for longer sequences.

- Clarity/Reproducibility: Paper is well-written, organized, and detailed enough for reproduction.

- Major concern — related work: Omits important video-to-text literature (e.g., Xu et al., AAAI 2016), weakening novelty claims.

- Major concern — significance: User study margins over RCN are small and lack statistical significance testing.

- Major concern — baselines: Should compare against sequence-specific methods (e.g., Kim et al., CVPR 2014) rather than only single-sentence baselines extended to sequences.

- Major concern — supervision claim: “Completely unsupervised” is overstated because blog selection involved manual curation; practical limitations for non-tourism domains need clarification.

- Weakness: Limited discussion of computational complexity and scalability.

- Overall: Novel and valuable work but requires more comprehensive related work coverage, statistical analysis of user studies, and clarification of data/practical limitations to strengthen the paper.
