Summary and relation to prior work
This paper addresses the task of producing a sequence of natural sentences for a stream of images, moving beyond single-image/single-sentence captioning. The proposed coherent recurrent convolutional network (CRCN) combines: (i) CNN image features (VGG), (ii) a bidirectional RNN over paragraph vectors to model sentence content in context, and (iii) an entity-based local coherence model (entity grid) to encourage fluent, coherent multi-sentence output. Training data are constructed automatically from blog posts by segmenting text around images and summarizing each segment to one sentence (with κ augmentations). At test time, the method retrieves candidate sentences per image from nearest neighbors, then ranks full sequences with the learned compatibility score that mixes image–sentence match and coherence.

The work is related to image captioning by retrieval/embedding (e.g., Kiros et al., Karpathy & Fei-Fei) and to multimodal RNN/LSTM approaches (e.g., m-RNN, Show & Tell, LRCN). It also bears resemblance to video description (e.g., Rohrbach et al., LRCN), which models temporal sequences, though typically generates one sentence per clip. The paper distinguishes itself by explicitly modeling cross-sentence coherence with an entity grid and by retrieving and ranking multi-sentence sequences for image streams. It would be useful to more fully situate the paper with respect to video-to-text methods that learn sequence-to-sequence mappings (e.g., Venugopalan et al., CVPR 2015 S2VT), and to any work that targets paragraph-level outputs.

Quality
Strengths:
- Clear overall architecture and reasonably thorough experimental protocol. Integrating an entity-based coherence model with CNN/BRNN is technically sound and well motivated for multi-sentence outputs.
- The training pipeline that derives paired image–sentence sequences from blogs (segmentation + LSA summarization + doc2vec) is practical and scales to large, noisy data.
- Quantitative evaluations (BLEU, METEOR, CIDEr, retrieval metrics) and AMT user studies on two sizable datasets support the main claims. Ablations (1NN, BRNN-only) help isolate the contributions of coherence.

Weaknesses and points needing clarification:
- Methodologically, the generation strategy is largely retrieval-based (nearest neighbor per image with a combinatorial search over concatenations). While effective, it is relatively primitive and limits novelty: it cannot synthesize content absent from training, risks redundancy, and may copy stylistic idiosyncrasies. Discussing trade-offs vs generative sequence models and showing controls for redundancy would strengthen the work.
- Equation (2) and the description of the two fully connected layers are ambiguous. As written, Wf2Wf1[O|q] = [S|g] suggests shared parameters map the concatenated matrix [O|q] to [S|g], but it is unclear whether each s_t depends on all o_1…o_N (global coupling) or only o_t (per-position mapping). Please clarify dimensions, indexing, and whether the FC layers operate column-wise or across the entire sequence, and explain how dropout prevents a reduction to a single linear map.
- The scoring function Skl = Σ_t (s_k,t · v_l,t + g_k · v_l,t) adds the same coherence-derived term g_k · v_l,t at every time step. This may overweight sequence length and entangle global coherence with per-image matches. Consider normalizing by length or designing a coherence-to-image term that reflects transitions rather than repeating g across t.
- The claim of being “completely unsupervised” is overstated. The approach is trained on paired image–sentence sequences derived using heuristics and supervised NLP components (parsing, coreference); it is more accurate to describe it as weakly supervised/self-supervised from web data.
- Reproducibility and anonymity: the NYC dataset is newly crawled, and no data release is provided; the Disneyland data are “reused” from [11], but [11] (as described here) does not detail crawling. For reproducibility, please either (a) release the processed pairs and code, or (b) provide sufficient crawling, segmentation, and summarization details and statistics to enable reconstruction. With respect to anonymity, citing [11] is appropriate in a double-blind setting; however, avoid language that could reveal authorship (“we reuse”) and ensure no URLs or acknowledgments can deanonymize the submission.

Clarity
- The paper is generally clear and well organized. Figures help. Section 3 would benefit from tighter mathematical exposition: explicitly list tensor shapes for all variables in Eqs. (1)–(4), clarify how κ augmented sentences are handled in training, and describe the beam/divide-and-conquer search complexity, including M, K, N, and runtime.
- Provide more detail on text preprocessing: paragraph tokenizer settings, handling of images with no nearby text, LSA parameters, and error rates of parsing/coreference on noisy blogs (and any robustness measures).
- Specify whether doc2vec is trained per-domain (NYC vs Disneyland) and whether vocabularies are shared.

Originality
- Extending captioning to image streams with explicit discourse coherence modeling is a useful and relatively unexplored direction in 2015. The components (CNN, BRNN, entity grid) are known; the novelty lies in their integration and in framing multi-sentence retrieval with coherence-aware scoring. The work would be stronger with a deeper comparison to sequence-to-sequence video captioning and any paragraph-level captioning contemporaries, and by demonstrating model behavior beyond retrieval.

Significance
- The task is important and aligns well with NIPS topics in vision–language modeling. The proposed architecture improves retrieval metrics and human preference, indicating practical value. If datasets and code are released, the impact could be substantial. The reliance on retrieval may limit generalization to new domains/styles; discussing hybrid retrieval–generation extensions would broaden significance.

Specific suggestions
- Clarify Equation (2) and the parameter sharing; add a schematic showing the dependency of s_t on o_{t-1}, o_t, o_{t+1} and q (if any).
- Normalize or reweight the coherence term to avoid length bias; consider modeling coherence over sentence transitions directly in the score.
- Report runtime and search efficiency; analyze failure cases (e.g., abrupt scene changes, pronoun resolution errors).
- Expand related work to include sequence-to-sequence video captioning (e.g., Venugopalan et al., CVPR 2015) and discuss differences.
- Improve reproducibility: provide enough details to reconstruct the datasets and consider releasing processed pairs.

Pros
- Clear problem formulation and motivation; coherent multimodal architecture
- Strong empirical results with ablations and user studies
- Effective use of entity-based coherence for multi-sentence outputs
- Practical pipeline for constructing training pairs from web blogs

Cons
- Retrieval-based generation is limited and somewhat ad hoc
- Ambiguities in model equations and scoring; potential length bias
- Reproducibility concerns due to undisclosed datasets and limited crawl details
- Related work on sequence-to-sequence video description could be better covered

Recommendation
Weak accept (poster). The paper is technically sound, addresses an important problem with a sensible and effective architecture, and presents convincing evidence. Clarifications on the scoring/FC layers, more thorough related work discussion, and stronger reproducibility would further strengthen the contribution. Confidence: moderate.
