Summary and relation to prior work
This paper tackles the problem of describing an image stream with a coherent sequence of natural sentences. The method retrieves candidate sentences by finding K nearest training images per query image (K=5) and then assembles a paragraph that balances per-image relevance and cross-sentence coherence. The proposed coherent recurrent convolutional network (CRCN) combines: (i) CNN image features (VGGNet fc7), (ii) a bidirectional recurrent network over sentence representations (paragraph vectors) to model content flow, and (iii) an entity-based local coherence model [1] (entity grid) to encourage smooth sentence-to-sentence transitions. The model is trained with a max-margin objective over ordered sentence–image sequence alignments, and at test time uses an approximate divide-and-conquer beam search to rank candidate sequences. The paper evaluates on large blog-derived datasets (NYC, Disneyland) and compares against strong baselines, including log-bilinear multimodal models [12], CNN+RNN [9], CNN+LSTM [30], and a global matching method [21], as well as ablations.

Relative to prior work, CRCN sits between retrieval-based captioning [6, 8, 9, 12, 21, 26] and video-to-sentence models such as LRCN [5]. Unlike single-image/single-sentence models, CRCN jointly considers a sequence of images and explicitly models discourse coherence using [1]. Compared with video description [5, 23, 32], CRCN addresses multi-sentence generation for image streams where visual content may change abruptly, making explicit coherence modeling more critical. It also differs from sequence retrieval from text [11], addressing the opposite direction (images to text).

Strengths
- Clear and timely problem formulation: moving beyond single images to coherent multi-sentence descriptions of image streams.
- Novel architecture that integrates a BRNN for content flow with an entity-based coherence model [1] in a unified scoring framework.
- Construction and use of large blog-based, text–image parallel datasets; practical candidate generation and tractable approximate search.
- Thorough evaluation: automatic metrics (BLEU, CIDEr, METEOR), retrieval metrics (recall@K, median rank), strong baselines, ablations (1NN, without coherence), and AMT user studies. Results consistently favor CRCN, with especially strong gains in retrieval metrics and in human preference for longer sequences.

Weaknesses and questions
1) Related work characterization: The paper states that [5] has “no mechanism for the coherence between sentences.” While [5] does not explicitly model sentence-to-sentence coherence for multi-sentence paragraphs, it predicts an intermediate semantic representation over time and can model topic continuity across sentences. Please refine the discussion to acknowledge this nuance.

2) Clarity of architecture: Figure 2(b) appears inconsistent with Eq. (2)/Section 3.3. The figure suggests FC layers connect to all sentences jointly, whereas Eq. (2) applies shared FC parameters to each sentence’s BRNN output ot and to the coherence vector q. Please revise the figure/caption and text to make the data flow and parameter sharing unambiguous.

3) Evaluation metrics: It is good to report BLEU, CIDEr, and METEOR; METEOR is often more informative with a single reference. Please ensure METEOR is prominently discussed and, if possible, include diversity/novelty or coherence-sensitive metrics, acknowledging their limitations.

4) Two linear layers in Eq. (2): The paper notes that Wf2Wf1 does not reduce to a single linear mapping because of dropout. While true, an ablation (one FC layer vs. two with dropout) would strengthen the claim. If space is tight, a brief justification and a note in the supplement would help.

5) Parameter sharing for BRNN output and coherence vector: Eq. (2) uses the same FC parameters for O (stacked ot) and q. Please clarify the motivation (e.g., to align both sentence-level and coherence-level factors into the same space) and, if available, report whether separate parameters degrade performance.

6) Paragraph vectors: Are the doc2vec paragraph vectors [17] fine-tuned during CRCN training or kept fixed after pretraining? Please clarify and, if fixed, discuss potential gains from fine-tuning.

7) Generality and data assumptions: The approach relies on blogs where images are reasonably aligned with text segments and on accurate parsing/coreference for the entity grid; errors could hurt coherence modeling. Please comment on robustness across domains beyond tourism and on parsing noise.

Quality
The technical approach is sound and well-motivated. Training, optimization, and search details are described, and experimental comparisons are appropriate. Minor clarity issues (Figure 2(b), Eq. (2) ablation) should be addressed. The human studies are persuasive, especially on longer sequences where coherence matters most.

Clarity
Overall, the paper is readable and well-organized. Please tighten the description of Section 3.3 vs. Figure 2(b), refine the related work discussion around [5], and explicitly state whether paragraph vectors are fine-tuned. A brief schematic of the candidate generation and divide-and-conquer search would also aid clarity.

Originality
The problem formulation (image streams to coherent sentence sequences) and the integration of an entity-grid coherence model with CNN/BRNN scoring are original in this context. While components draw on known ideas, their combination and end-to-end training for sequence retrieval are novel.

Significance
The task is practically relevant and understudied. The consistent gains over strong baselines and the positive AMT results suggest others will build on this line of work. The blog datasets and code (promised) will increase impact.

Pros for acceptance
- Addresses an important and underexplored problem; compelling integration of vision, language, and discourse.
- Strong empirical results with comprehensive evaluations and ablations.
- Scalable candidate generation and search; large, realistic datasets.

Cons against acceptance
- Some architectural descriptions need clarification; limited ablation for the FC stack and parameter sharing choices.
- Dependence on retrieval and domain-specific blog structure may limit generalization; coherence relies on noisy parsing/coreference.
- Minor overstatement about prior work [5].

Post-rebuttal and recommendation
After the rebuttal, the authors satisfactorily addressed the formulation, evaluation, and related work issues. I recommend acceptance. Please include in the final version: (i) clarified Figure 2(b)/Section 3.3, (ii) refined discussion of [5], (iii) explicit statement on whether paragraph vectors are fine-tuned or fixed (and any observed impact), and (iv) brief justification or ablation for the two FC layers and parameter sharing in Eq. (2).
