This paper addresses the problem of generating a sequence of natural language sentences to describe a sequence of images, motivated by scenarios where users capture multiple images of a single event or experience. This is a significant problem at the intersection of computer vision and natural language processing, extending single-image captioning to a more complex, sequential setting. However, the paper overlooks closely related work in video captioning, where the input is inherently a sequence of frames (images). For instance, the work of Rohrbach et al. [Rohrbach et al., 2014] is not cited, despite its relevance – a video can be viewed as a sequence of images, and existing video captioning methods address the challenge of generating descriptions for these sequences.

The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the proposed approach. The problem statement is well-defined, and the methodology appears sound. The authors present a coherent recurrent convolutional network (CRCN) architecture, integrating convolutional neural networks for image representation, bidirectional recurrent neural networks for language modeling, and an entity-based local coherence model. This architecture addresses the challenge of maintaining coherence across multiple generated sentences.

A key strength of this work is the technical soundness of the proposed model. The inclusion of an entity-based coherence model is particularly noteworthy, as it directly addresses the problem of generating fluent and logically connected sequences of sentences, something often missing in simpler approaches. The authors also leverage a large dataset of blog posts with associated images, demonstrating a practical approach to obtaining text-image parallel training data.

However, the method for generating sentence sequences feels somewhat simplistic. The approach relies on retrieving sentences associated with similar training images and concatenating them, which may limit the creativity and expressiveness of the generated descriptions. While this strategy is reasonable, it lacks the sophistication of more generative approaches. 

A concerning issue is the potential breach of anonymity in Section 4.1, where the authors state reusing data from [Kim et al., 2011] and using the same crawling method. The lack of detail regarding dataset acquisition and the strong connection to previous work raises questions about potential bias or unfair advantage. The dataset itself is not publicly available, hindering reproducibility.

Equation (2) appears to contain an error, suggesting an incomplete connection within the network. This should be carefully reviewed and corrected.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Technically sound architecture addressing a challenging problem.
*   Novel integration of a coherence model to improve fluency.
*   Demonstrated performance gains over baseline methods through quantitative and qualitative evaluation.
*   Practical approach to dataset creation utilizing readily available blog data.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Omission of relevant work in video captioning.
*   Simplistic sentence generation approach relying heavily on retrieval.
*   Potential breach of anonymity regarding dataset and prior work.
*   Possible error in Equation (2).
*   Lack of publicly available dataset hindering reproducibility.

**Overall Assessment:**

While this paper presents a valuable contribution to the field, the concerns regarding related work, dataset accessibility, and the potential error in Equation (2) prevent a strong recommendation for acceptance as a full paper. The work demonstrates promising results, but the identified weaknesses need to be addressed. I recommend acceptance as a poster, contingent upon the authors addressing the concerns raised regarding related work and the equation.
