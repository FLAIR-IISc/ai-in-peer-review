The paper addresses the problem of generating a coherent sequence of sentences to describe a sequence of images, such as those found in blog posts. To achieve this, the authors propose retrieving the K=5 most similar images and their corresponding sentences from the training set for each query image. The main contribution lies in defining a method to select the most relevant sentences for the image sequence, ensuring a coherent description. This is accomplished by first embedding sentences into a vector space, and then modeling the sequence of sentences using a bidirectional LSTM. The LSTM’s output is then passed through ReLU and fully connected layers, and scored based on its compatibility with the image. Additionally, a local coherence model [1] is incorporated to enforce compatibility *between* sentences.

**Strengths / Positive Aspects:**

*   The paper presents a novel and effective architecture for retrieving coherent sentences for an image sequence.
*   The authors provide a comprehensive quantitative, qualitative, and human evaluation, demonstrating the superiority of their approach compared to several baselines that do not explicitly model coherence. An ablation study, removing the local coherence model [1], is also included.
*   The authors have committed to releasing their source code and dataset.

**Weaknesses / Questions / Unclarities:**

1.  Line 94: The paper claims that [5] lacks a mechanism for coherence between sentences. However, while not its primary contribution, [5] predicts an intermediate semantic representation of videos, which inherently provides coherence across sentences by modeling the topic of the multi-sentence description.
2.  Correctness/Clarity: Figure 2b does not accurately reflect the description in Section 3.3/Equation (2). The figure suggests the fully connected layers are connected to all sentences, whereas Equation (2) implies parameter sharing across sentences, with connections only to the vector representing a single sentence.
3.  A more suitable metric for automatic evaluation of the generated sentences would be METEOR (http://www.cs.cmu.edu/~alavie/METEOR/) instead of BLEU, particularly when only a single reference sentence is available.
4.  The purpose of applying two linear functions (W<sub>f2</sub>, W<sub>f1</sub>) sequentially in Equation (2) is unclear. Since two linear functions can be collapsed into a single linear function, the benefit of this arrangement is not obvious. An ablation study investigating the impact of these functions would be valuable.
5.  Why are the same parameters used for the fully connected layers applied to both the bidirectional RNN output (o<sub>t</sub>) and the local coherence model q (Equation 2)?
6.  Was the paragraph vector [16] fine-tuned during training, or was it kept fixed?

**=== Post Rebuttal ===**

After reviewing the rebuttal, I recommend the paper for acceptance. The authors have successfully addressed concerns regarding the formulation, evaluation, and related work. 

The paper proposes an interesting new model for retrieving coherent sentences for an image stream, and the evaluation convincingly demonstrates its effectiveness. However, to be fully convincing, several clarifications were necessary. Specifically, please clarify whether the paragraph vector [16] was fine-tuned or kept fixed during training.
