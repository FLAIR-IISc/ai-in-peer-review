This paper addresses the task of retrieving natural sentences that describe a given sequence of images and assembling them into a coherent sequence of sentences. The problem is important in computer vision and pattern recognition, but it is closely related to generating multi-sentence descriptions for videos (e.g., Rohrbach et al., 2014). A single video can be treated as a sequence of images: it can be segmented into shots, each well represented by a key frame. However, the paper does not cite prior work along this line.

The paper is well written and easy to follow. The problem is clearly defined and approached with solid techniques.

The proposed neural model, CRCN, learns relationships between a sequence of images and a sequence of natural sentences. The architecture appears technically sound and, with the aid of entity-based coherence models, it captures discourse relationships effectively.

The method used to generate a sequence of natural sentences is reasonable but rather primitive: each sentence is directly associated with training images similar to the given image, and the resulting sentences are simply concatenated in the order of the corresponding images.

In Section 4.1, the authors write: “We reuse the blog data of Disneyland from the dataset of [11], and newly collect the data of NYC, using the same crawling method as [11].” The dataset has not been disclosed, and [11] does not provide details on the crawling procedure. This strongly suggests that the authors of this paper substantially overlap with those of [11], thereby deliberately compromising anonymity.

Equation (2) may be incorrect, as it implies that s_t depends only on o_t, which would mean the network is not fully connected.

Overall, while the neural architecture is technically sound and models discourse relationships well via entity-based coherence, the sentence-sequence generation for a given image stream is rather ad hoc. I believe the paper can be accepted as a poster in its current form.
