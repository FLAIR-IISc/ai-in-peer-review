

This paper addresses the problem of generating natural language descriptions for image streams (sequences of images) rather than single images, which is a meaningful extension of existing image captioning work. The authors propose a coherent recurrent convolutional network (CRCN) that integrates convolutional neural networks for image representation, bidirectional recurrent neural networks for sentence sequence modeling, and an entity-based local coherence model to ensure fluent transitions between sentences. The work is motivated by the observation that users often capture series of images during memorable events (e.g., visiting NYC), making it more natural to generate sentence sequences rather than single sentences.

The paper makes several notable contributions: (1) It is, to the authors' knowledge, the first to explicitly address the problem of generating sentence sequences from image streams; (2) It introduces a novel architecture that integrates multimodal networks with a coherence model; (3) It leverages blog posts as text-image parallel training data, which provides a natural source of image sequences with corresponding text descriptions.

The paper demonstrates the effectiveness of CRCN through extensive experiments on NYC and Disneyland blog datasets (over 20K blog posts with 140K images). The quantitative results (BLEU, CIDEr, METEOR, Top-K recall) show consistent improvements over strong baselines, including variants of the log-bilinear multimodal models, recurrent convolutional models, and global matching approaches. The user studies via Amazon Mechanical Turk further validate the superiority of CRCN over baselines, particularly for longer image sequences where coherence becomes increasingly important.

The paper is well-written, with clear organization and sufficient detail to allow reproduction of the results. The technical approach is sound, and the authors demonstrate appropriate care in evaluating both strengths and weaknesses of their method.

However, there are several important concerns that need to be addressed before acceptance:

1. Missing related work: The paper fails to cite and discuss several relevant works on video-to-sentence generation, such as "Jointly Modeling Deep Video and Compositional Text to Bridge Vision and Language in a Unified Framework" (Xu et al., AAAI 2016). These works are closely related to the problem of generating sequences from visual inputs, and their omission weakens the paper's contribution claim.

2. Statistical significance: The user study results in Table 2 show that CRCN's performance is only marginally better than the RCN baseline (which lacks the coherence model). The paper does not provide statistical significance tests to verify whether these differences are reliable, making it difficult to assess the true impact of the coherence model. Given that the differences in Table 2 are small (e.g., 51.2% vs. 49.8% for 5-image queries), significance testing is essential.

3. Baseline comparison: While the paper compares against several single-sentence generation models extended to sequence generation, it would be stronger to compare against methods specifically designed for sequence generation, such as the work by Kim et al. (CVPR 2014) on "Ranking and Retrieval of Image Sequences from Multiple Paragraph Queries."

4. Practical limitations: The paper claims the approach is "completely unsupervised" but relies on blog posts that require manual selection of travelogue posts. The authors should clarify the practical limitations of this data collection approach for domains beyond tourism.

Strengths:
- Novel problem formulation addressing the gap between single-image captioning and sequence generation
- Well-designed architecture that integrates three complementary components (image representation, language modeling, coherence)
- Comprehensive evaluation with both quantitative metrics and user studies
- Practical application to real-world data (blog posts)

Weaknesses:
- Insufficient discussion of related work in video-to-sentence generation
- Lack of statistical significance testing for user study results
- Overstated claim of being "completely unsupervised" given the manual blog selection process
- Limited discussion of computational complexity and scalability

In terms of the NIPS review criteria:
- Quality: The technical approach is sound with appropriate theoretical grounding and thorough empirical evaluation. However, the lack of significance testing for the user study weakens the quality assessment.
- Clarity: The paper is well-written and organized, with sufficient detail for reproduction.
- Originality: The problem of generating sentence sequences from image streams is novel, though the technical approach builds on existing multimodal networks.
- Significance: The work addresses a meaningful extension of image captioning that could be useful for applications where users capture image sequences (e.g., social media, travel documentation).

The paper makes a valuable contribution to the field by extending image captioning to the more natural sequence generation setting, but requires additional analysis of the statistical significance of the results and more comprehensive discussion of related work to strengthen its contribution claim. With these revisions, the paper would be a strong candidate for NIPS.
