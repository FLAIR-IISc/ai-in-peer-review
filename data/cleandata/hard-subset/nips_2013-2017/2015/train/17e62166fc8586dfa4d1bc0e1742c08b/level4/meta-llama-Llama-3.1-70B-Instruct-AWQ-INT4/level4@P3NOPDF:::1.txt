This paper addresses the problem of retrieving natural sentences that describe a given sequence of images and forming a sequence of sentences. This problem is significant in the research fields of computer vision and pattern recognition, and it is closely related to generating multiple sentences that describe given video content (e.g., [Rohrbach+ 2014]). Since video content can be separated into multiple video shots, with each shot being well described by a key frame, single video content can be regarded as a sequence of images. However, the authors do not cite any research in this line of work.

The paper is well-written and easy to follow, with a clearly stated problem and a solid set of techniques used to solve it. The proposed neural network model, CRCN, effectively captures relationships between a sequence of natural sentences and a sequence of images. The architecture of the proposed model is technically sound, and it successfully describes discourse relationships with the help of entity-based coherence models.

However, the method for generating a sequence of natural sentences is reasonable but somewhat primitive. It involves directly associating each natural sentence with training images similar to a given image, and then concatenating all the sequences in the order of the corresponding images.

In Section 4.1, the authors mention reusing the blog data of Disneyland from the dataset of [11] and newly collecting the data of NYC using the same crawling method as [11]. However, the dataset has not been disclosed, and the corresponding paper does not provide details on how to crawl the dataset. This raises concerns about the anonymity of the authors, as it appears that they may be the same individuals who authored [11].

Furthermore, Equation (2) may be incorrect, as it implies that st can be derived from only ot, suggesting that the network is not fully connected. While the proposed neural network model is technically sound and effectively describes discourse relationships with the help of entity-based coherence models, the method for generating a sequence of sentences for a given image stream is rather ad-hoc.

In conclusion, while this paper has some limitations, it can be considered for acceptance as a poster paper in its current form. The authors demonstrate a good understanding of the problem and propose a solid solution, despite some minor flaws and areas for improvement.
