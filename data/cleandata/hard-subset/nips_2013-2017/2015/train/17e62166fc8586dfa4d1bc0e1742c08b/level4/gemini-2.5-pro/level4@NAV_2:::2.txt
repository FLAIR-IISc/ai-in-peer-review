This paper addresses the task of generating a coherent sequence of natural language sentences to describe a stream of images. The proposed method first retrieves a set of candidate sentences for each image in the input stream from a training corpus of blog posts. The core contribution is a novel architecture, the Coherent Recurrent Convolutional Network (CRCN), designed to select and order these sentences into a fluent paragraph. The CRCN integrates a Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) for image features, a bidirectional Recurrent Neural Network (BRNN) to model the flow of sentence content, and an entity-based local coherence model to ensure smooth transitions between sentences. The final output is determined by ranking candidate sentence sequences based on a compatibility score that jointly considers image-sentence relevance and textual coherence.

**Strengths:**
*   The paper introduces a novel and significant problem: moving from single image-to-sentence generation to a more complex sequence-to-sequence mapping. The proposed architecture is well-motivated for this task.
*   The experimental validation is extensive and convincing. The authors demonstrate the superiority of their method against several relevant baselines using a combination of quantitative metrics, qualitative examples, and a well-designed user study on Amazon Mechanical Turk.
*   The inclusion of an ablation study, which isolates the contribution of the coherence model, effectively demonstrates the value of this key component of the proposed architecture.
*   The authors' commitment to release the source code and dataset is a welcome contribution that will benefit the community and facilitate future research.

**Weaknesses and Questions for Clarification:**
1.  **Relation to Prior Work:** The paper claims that the work of Donahue et al. [5] contains "no mechanism for the coherence between sentences." While not the main focus of [5], their model predicts an intermediate semantic representation for videos that could be seen as enforcing coherence across the generated multi-sentence description. A more nuanced discussion of this comparison would be beneficial.

2.  **Architectural Clarity:** There appears to be a mismatch between the architecture depicted in Figure 2b and the formulation in Equation (2). The figure suggests that the fully connected layers operate on the representations of all sentences in the sequence, while the equation implies that parameters are shared but applied to each sentence vector individually. Please clarify the precise structure of this component.

3.  **Choice of Evaluation Metrics:** For sentence generation tasks, particularly with a single reference, metrics such as METEOR are often considered to correlate better with human judgments than BLEU. Including and discussing results using additional relevant metrics would further strengthen the quantitative evaluation.

4.  **Architectural Justification (FC Layers):** In Equation (2), two consecutive linear transformations (Wf1, Wf2) are applied. As a composition of two linear functions is equivalent to a single linear function, the motivation for this choice is unclear. Could the authors provide an ablation study or further justification for this design over a single fully connected layer?

5.  **Architectural Justification (Parameter Sharing):** The model uses shared parameters in the fully connected layers to process both the BRNN output (o_t) and the local coherence model's output (q). What is the rationale for this design choice over using separate parameters for each input stream?

6.  **Implementation Details:** For the sake of reproducibility, it is important to know if the paragraph vectors [17] used for sentence representation were fine-tuned during the training of the CRCN or kept fixed.

In conclusion, the paper presents a compelling new model for the novel task of describing image streams with coherent sentence sequences, supported by a strong evaluation. The contribution would be significantly strengthened if the authors could address the points of clarification regarding the model architecture, evaluation, and comparison to related work in the final version.
