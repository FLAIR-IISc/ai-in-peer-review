

This paper addresses the problem of generating coherent sequences of natural language sentences to describe image streams, rather than the more common single-image-to-single-sentence task. The authors propose a novel multimodal architecture called Coherent Recurrent Convolutional Networks (CRCN), which integrates convolutional neural networks for image representation, bidirectional recurrent neural networks for sentence modeling, and an entity-based local coherence model to ensure fluent transitions between sentences. The approach leverages blog posts as training data, where each blog typically contains a sequence of images with corresponding text descriptions that naturally form image-text sequences.

The paper makes several notable contributions. First, it identifies an important gap in the literature by addressing the problem of sentence sequence generation for image streams, which extends beyond previous work on single-image captioning (e.g., Karpathy et al., 2015) and video captioning (e.g., Rohrbach et al., 2014). While video captioning is related, the authors correctly note that image streams often contain sharper visual transitions between consecutive images than videos, making coherence modeling particularly important. Second, the CRCN architecture effectively combines established techniques in a novel way to address the coherence problem. Third, the authors demonstrate their approach on two large blog datasets (NYC and Disneyland) with over 20K blog posts and 140K associated images, using both quantitative metrics and user studies via Amazon Mechanical Turk.

The paper excels in quality and clarity. The technical approach is sound and well-motivated, with thorough ablation studies showing the importance of both the bidirectional RNN and coherence model components. The experimental evaluation is comprehensive, employing multiple standard metrics (BLEU, CIDEr, METEOR) alongside user studies. The writing is clear, with well-structured explanations and helpful figures that illustrate the architecture and experimental results. The authors provide sufficient detail for reproducibility, including model parameters and implementation specifics.

The originality of the work is strong, as the paper clearly identifies a gap in the literature and proposes a novel solution. The authors properly contextualize their work against related approaches, particularly distinguishing their method from video captioning work (which typically assumes more temporal continuity) and single-image captioning methods. The problem statement is well-justified by real-world usage patterns (users taking sequences of photos during memorable events).

The significance of the work is evident, particularly for applications in social media content summarization, personal photo album description, and natural human-computer interaction. The approach is completely unsupervised and domain-agnostic, making it broadly applicable to any domain with blog-like content. The user studies via Amazon Mechanical Turk provide compelling evidence of the method's superiority over baselines, with the coherence model becoming increasingly important as sentence sequences lengthen.

However, there are several weaknesses that should be addressed. The method for generating sentence sequences (retrieving K nearest sentences for each image and concatenating them) is somewhat simplistic and could be better justified. While the authors mention vanishing gradient concerns with finer-grained approaches, this claim is not thoroughly supported or discussed. The paper would benefit from a more detailed comparison with alternative sequence generation approaches, such as those using sequence-to-sequence models with attention.

The dataset collection process requires clarification. The authors state they "reused the blog data of Disneyland from the dataset of [11] and newly collected the data of NYC," but do not provide details about their crawling methodology or make the dataset publicly available. This creates reproducibility concerns, as the data collection process could be considered a significant contribution in itself. The draft review's suggestion of "lack of anonymity" is unsubstantiated and unhelpful; a more constructive approach would be to request clarification about the dataset's creation.

Equation (2) in Section 3.3 appears to be incorrectly formatted, potentially implying a non-fully connected architecture. This should be corrected for clarity. Additionally, while the paper focuses on tourism topics, the significance could be strengthened by discussing broader applications beyond this specific domain.

In summary, this paper makes a meaningful contribution to the field by addressing a gap in the literature for coherent sentence sequence generation from image streams. The technical approach is sound, the evaluation is comprehensive, and the problem is significant for real-world applications. The main weaknesses relate to the simplicity of the sentence generation approach, insufficient detail about dataset collection, and minor technical issues in the presentation.

Strengths: Novel problem formulation, technically sound architecture, comprehensive evaluation with both quantitative metrics and user studies, clear writing and organization.

Weaknesses: Simplistic sentence generation approach without sufficient justification, insufficient detail about dataset collection process, minor technical errors in presentation.

The paper is well-suited for NIPS as a poster presentation. It addresses an important problem with a technically sound approach, and the results demonstrate clear improvements over baselines. With minor revisions addressing the dataset collection details and clarifying the sentence generation approach, this work would be an excellent contribution to the field.
