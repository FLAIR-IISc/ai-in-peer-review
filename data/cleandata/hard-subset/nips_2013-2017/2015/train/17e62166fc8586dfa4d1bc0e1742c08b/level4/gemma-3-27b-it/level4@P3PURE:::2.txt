The paper addresses the problem of generating a coherent sequence of sentences to describe a sequence of images, as often found in blog posts. To achieve this, the authors propose retrieving the K=5 most similar images and corresponding sentences from the training set for each query image. The core contribution of the paper lies in defining a method to select the most relevant sentences from these retrieved candidates, ensuring a coherent description for the entire image sequence. This is accomplished by embedding sentences into a vector space and modeling the resulting sequence with a bidirectional LSTM. The LSTM output is then processed through ReLU and fully connected layers, followed by a compatibility scoring mechanism between images and sentences. Furthermore, a local coherence model [1] is integrated to enforce compatibility *between* the selected sentences themselves.

Strengths / Positive Aspects:
- The paper introduces a novel and effective architecture for retrieving coherent sentences for an image sequence.
- The authors provide a comprehensive quantitative, qualitative, and human evaluation, demonstrating the superiority of their approach over several baselines that do not explicitly model coherence. An ablation study, removing the local coherence model [1], is also included.
- The authors commit to releasing the source code and dataset, which is highly valuable for reproducibility and future research.

Weaknesses / Questions / Unclarities:
1. Line 94: The paper claims that [5] lacks a mechanism for coherence between sentences. However, [5] predicts an intermediate semantic representation of videos, which inherently models topic coherence across sentences in a multi-sentence description, even if coherence isn’t the primary contribution.
2. Correctness/Clarity: Figure 2b does not accurately reflect the description in Section 3.3/Equation (2). While the figure suggests fully connected layers connected to all sentences, Equation (2) indicates parameter sharing across sentences, with connections only to the vector representing a single sentence.
3. A more suitable metric for automatically evaluating the generated sentences would be METEOR (http://www.cs.cmu.edu/~alavie/METEOR/) rather than BLEU, particularly given the use of a single reference sentence.
4. The application of two linear functions (W<sub>f2</sub> and W<sub>f1</sub>) sequentially in Equation (2) is unclear. Given that two linear transformations can be collapsed into a single one, the benefit of this design choice is not evident. An ablation study demonstrating the advantage of these functions would be informative.
5. Why are the same parameters used for the fully connected layers processing the BRNN output (o<sub>t</sub>) and the local coherence model q (Equation 2)?
6. Was the paragraph vector [16] fine-tuned during training or kept fixed?

=== post rebuttal ===

After reviewing the rebuttal, I recommend the paper for acceptance. The authors have successfully addressed concerns regarding the formulation, evaluation, and related work. 

The paper proposes an interesting new model to retrieve coherent sentences for an image stream, which is convincingly evaluated. However, to be a truly compelling paper, several clarifications were necessary. Specifically, please clarify whether the paragraph vector [16] was fine-tuned or kept fixed during training.
