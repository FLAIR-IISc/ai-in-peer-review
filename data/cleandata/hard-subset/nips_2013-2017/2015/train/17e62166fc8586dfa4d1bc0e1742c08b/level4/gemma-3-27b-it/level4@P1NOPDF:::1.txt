This paper addresses the problem of retrieving and generating natural language sentences to describe a sequence of images. This is a significant problem within computer vision and pattern recognition, and is closely related to video captioning – the task of generating descriptions for video content (e.g., [Rohrbach+ 2014]). A video can be naturally considered a sequence of images (specifically, keyframes representing video shots), and thus techniques developed for image sequence description have clear relevance to video captioning. However, this paper does not cite related work in this area.

The paper is well-written and easy to follow. The problem is clearly stated and addressed using a set of sound techniques.

The proposed neural network model, CRCN, effectively learns relationships between sequences of sentences and images. The model architecture is technically strong and incorporates entity-based coherence models to effectively capture discourse relationships.

The method for generating sentence sequences is reasonable, but somewhat simplistic. Each sentence is directly linked to training images similar to the input image, and the resulting sequences are concatenated in the order corresponding to the images. 

In Section 4.1, the authors state, “We reuse the blog data of Disneyland from the dataset of [11], and newly collect the data of NYC, using the same crawling method with [11].” However, the dataset itself is not publicly available, and the referenced paper [11] lacks details regarding the data crawling process. This raises concerns about potential conflicts of interest, as it suggests a strong overlap in authorship between this paper and [11], and compromises the anonymity of the review process.

Equation (2) appears to be potentially incorrect, as it suggests that *s<sub>t</sub>* is determined solely by *o<sub>t</sub>*, implying a network that isn't fully connected. While the overall neural network model is technically sound and utilizes entity-based coherence modeling well, the sentence sequence generation approach feels rather *ad hoc*. 

Overall, I believe this paper is suitable for acceptance as a poster.
