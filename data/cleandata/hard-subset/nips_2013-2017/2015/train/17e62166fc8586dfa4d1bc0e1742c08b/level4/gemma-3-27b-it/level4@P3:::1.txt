## Review of “Expressing an Image Stream with a Sequence of Natural Sentences”

This paper addresses the problem of generating a sequence of natural language sentences to describe a sequence of images, motivated by the common scenario of users capturing experiences through photo streams. This is a significant problem at the intersection of computer vision and natural language processing, representing a natural extension of single image captioning. The authors propose a coherent recurrent convolutional network (CRCN) incorporating convolutional networks, bidirectional recurrent neural networks, and an entity-based local coherence model. 

The work builds upon existing research in image captioning (e.g., [5, 8, 9, 12, 14, 15, 26, 21, 30]) and extends it to the sequential domain. However, the paper overlooks a key related area: video captioning. Generating descriptions for image streams shares considerable overlap with the task of describing video content, where a video can be seen as a sequence of images or shots (e.g., [32, 23]). This connection, and relevant prior work, should be discussed. 

The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the motivation, proposed method, and experimental setup. The CRCN architecture appears technically sound, and the inclusion of a local coherence model to ensure fluent sentence transitions is a valuable contribution. The use of blog data as a text-image parallel corpus is a practical approach to addressing the challenges of obtaining training data for sequential image description.

However, the sentence retrieval method, where sentences are directly associated with similar training images and concatenated, feels somewhat simplistic given the complexity of the overall architecture. While effective, exploring more sophisticated methods for sentence selection and ordering could further improve performance. Furthermore, the reliance on pre-trained paragraph vectors introduces a dependency on the quality and domain relevance of the pre-trained model.

A significant concern arises regarding potential breaches of anonymity, as noted in Section 4.1, where the authors state reusing a dataset from [11] and employing the same crawling method. The lack of detailed dataset disclosure and the overlap in authorship between this work and [11] raise questions about maintaining a fair, blind review process. This should be addressed.

Equation (2) appears to contain a potential error; the formulation suggests a limited flow of information, potentially hindering the network's expressiveness. Clarification is needed.

**Strengths:**

*   Technically sound architecture (CRCN) incorporating a coherence model.
*   Addresses a relevant and challenging problem extending single-image captioning.
*   Practical use of blog data for training.
*   Demonstrated improvements over baseline methods through quantitative and user studies.

**Weaknesses:**

*   Oversight of relevant video captioning literature.
*   Simplistic sentence retrieval method.
*   Potential anonymity concerns due to dataset reuse and author overlap.
*   Potential error in Equation (2).
*   Reliance on pre-trained paragraph vectors.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel approach to a challenging problem, extending existing work in a meaningful way.
*   The proposed CRCN architecture is well-designed and incorporates a valuable coherence model.
*   The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the approach compared to established baselines.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The lack of discussion regarding related work in video captioning is a significant omission.
*   The potential anonymity concerns are troubling.
*   The sentence retrieval method feels somewhat ad-hoc and could benefit from further refinement.



**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a solid contribution to the field of image and language understanding. While the concerns regarding related work, anonymity, and the retrieval method are legitimate, the technical soundness of the CRCN architecture and the demonstrated improvements over existing methods suggest that this work has merit. 

**Recommendation:**

I recommend acceptance as a poster paper, contingent on the authors addressing the following:

1.  Include a discussion of related work in video captioning and properly cite relevant publications (e.g., [23, 32]).
2.  Provide a detailed explanation of the dataset creation process, ensuring transparency and addressing the anonymity concerns.
3.  Clarify Equation (2) and ensure it represents a fully connected network as intended.
4.  Consider discussing the limitations of relying on pre-trained paragraph vectors and potential strategies for mitigating their impact.
