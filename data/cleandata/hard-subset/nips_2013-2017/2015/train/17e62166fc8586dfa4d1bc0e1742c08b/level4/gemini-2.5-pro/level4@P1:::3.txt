This paper addresses the novel task of generating a coherent sequence of natural language sentences to describe a stream of images. This work extends the well-studied problem of single image captioning to a more complex, sequential setting, which is motivated by how users typically capture photos of events or experiences. The authors propose a new architecture, the Coherent Recurrent Convolutional Network (CRCN), which integrates three main components: a Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) to extract image features, a Bidirectional Recurrent Neural Network (BRNN) to model the flow of content across sentences, and an entity-based local coherence model to ensure smooth, natural transitions between sentences. To train this model, the authors creatively leverage a large dataset of blog posts, treating them as a source of parallel image-stream and text-sequence data.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty and Significance:** The paper tackles an important and logical next step in image understanding, moving from single images to image streams. This problem formulation is highly relevant and represents a challenging and worthwhile direction for the field.
*   **Technical Approach:** The proposed CRCN model is well-conceived, combining state-of-the-art components in a principled way. The integration of a CNN for visual representation, a BRNN for temporal text modeling, and an explicit coherence model to improve fluency is a strong and sensible approach to this task.
*   **Empirical Results:** The quantitative evaluation is thorough, using multiple established metrics (e.g., BLEU, CIDEr, METEOR, recall). The results presented in Table 1 demonstrate a significant performance improvement for the proposed method over several strong baselines, validating the effectiveness of the overall architecture.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

*   **Discussion of Related Work:** While the paper addresses a new problem, its relationship to existing work on video description (i.e., generating sentences from video clips) could be more thoroughly discussed. For instance, works such as Xu et al. (AAAI 2015, ref [32]) also model sequential visual input. A more detailed comparison would help to better situate the paper's contribution and clarify why the proposed coherence model is uniquely critical for image streams as opposed to videos.
*   **Validation of the Coherence Model:** The primary contribution of the full CRCN model over its RCN variant is the entity-based coherence model. However, the user study results in Table 2 show only a marginal preference for CRCN over RCN (53.8% for sequences of length 5). While the authors provide a supplementary experiment with longer sequences that shows a larger gap, the main result is not entirely convincing on its own. The claim that the coherence model provides a meaningful improvement would be significantly strengthened by performing a statistical significance test to verify that the observed user preference is reliable.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a well-executed paper that introduces a novel and interesting problem. The proposed CRCN algorithm is sound, and the quantitative results are strong. The main weakness lies in the user study, which provides only limited evidence for the advantage of the full model over a key baseline. Nevertheless, the paper makes a solid contribution to the field.
