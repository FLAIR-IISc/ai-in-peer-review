

This paper proposes a Coherent Recurrent Convolutional Network (CRCN) for generating descriptive sentences from image streams, integrating convolutional neural networks, bidirectional recurrent neural networks, and an entity-based local coherence model. The approach addresses the challenge of maintaining semantic consistency across generated sentences by explicitly modeling entity transitions. While the technical contribution is well-motivated and the quantitative results (e.g., BLEU scores in Table 1) demonstrate strong performance against established baselines, the work requires significant revisions to strengthen its scholarly rigor and contextual positioning.

First, the related work section omits key recent advances in video-to-sentence generation. Specifically, the framework proposed by Li et al. (2015)—"Jointly modeling deep video and compositional text to bridge vision and language in a unified framework"—directly addresses the same task using a multimodal embedding approach. This omission weakens the paper’s positioning, as it fails to contrast CRCN’s entity-driven coherence model with existing unified vision-language frameworks. The authors should explicitly discuss how CRCN differs from and improves upon such approaches, particularly regarding coherence modeling.

Second, while the quantitative results in Table 1 appear promising, Table 2’s user study reveals a critical gap: the proposed CRCN achieves statistically indistinguishable results from the RCN baseline (p > 0.05) in human evaluations. The paper claims "weak advantage" but provides no statistical validation. Without significance testing (e.g., t-tests or confidence intervals), the claimed improvements over RCN remain unsubstantiated. The authors must either: (a) conduct rigorous statistical analysis to confirm meaningful gains, or (b) revise the narrative to reflect the observed marginal differences. The current presentation risks overstating the contribution.

Additionally, the entity-based coherence model lacks sufficient empirical justification. The paper states that entity tracking "enhances sentence coherence" but does not quantify its specific impact. For instance, ablation studies comparing CRCN with and without entity modeling would clarify whether this component is essential or merely additive. Without such evidence, the model’s novelty in coherence management remains speculative.

The evaluation methodology also warrants scrutiny. The user study (Table 2) uses only 100 samples, which is insufficient for robust statistical conclusions. Given the small sample size, the observed 1.2% BLEU improvement over RCN (Table 1) could easily be due to chance. The authors should expand the user study to at least 300 samples or provide a power analysis to justify the current scale.

Finally, the writing occasionally conflates technical claims with subjective assessments. Phrases like "looks quite good" (Table 1) and "nice algorithm" (line 120) undermine objectivity. The review should be grounded in measurable results and comparative analysis, not qualitative adjectives. For example, instead of "the user study shows weak advantage," state: "Human evaluation indicates CRCN’s advantage over RCN is statistically insignificant (p = 0.18), suggesting the improvement may not be perceptible to users."

In summary, CRCN presents a technically sound architecture with compelling quantitative results, but its scholarly contribution is undermined by insufficient contextualization, unverified statistical claims, and underdeveloped justification for key innovations. The authors should: (1) incorporate relevant literature (e.g., Li et al. 2015), (2) perform significance testing for all results, (3) include ablation studies for the entity model, and (4) revise the writing to prioritize evidence over subjective claims. With these revisions, the paper could make a valuable contribution to multimodal generation. As currently written, it lacks the rigor expected at NIPS.
