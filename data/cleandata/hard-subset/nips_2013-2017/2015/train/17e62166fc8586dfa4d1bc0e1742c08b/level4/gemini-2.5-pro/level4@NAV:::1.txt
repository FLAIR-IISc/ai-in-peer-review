This paper addresses the significant problem of generating a coherent sequence of sentences to describe a corresponding sequence of images. This task is highly relevant to the fields of computer vision and pattern recognition. However, a major omission is the failure to cite or discuss the closely related body of work on video description (e.g., [Rohrbach+ 2014]), where a video is often treated as a sequence of key frames.

The paper is well-written, clearly structured, and easy to understand. The problem is formulated effectively and approached with a set of robust techniques.

The core contribution, a neural network model named CRCN, is designed to learn the relationships between image sequences and sentence sequences. The architecture is technically sound. A notable strength is its effective use of entity-based coherence models to capture and represent discourse-level relationships within the text.

In contrast, the method for generating the final sentence sequence is reasonable but underdeveloped. The approach involves finding training images visually similar to a given input image, retrieving their associated sentences, and then simply concatenating these sentences in the order of the input images. This generation strategy can be considered rather primitive.

There are serious concerns regarding the dataset and the anonymity of the review process. The authors state in Section 4.1, "We reuse the blog data of Disneyland from the dataset of [11], and newly collect the data of NYC, using the same crawling method with [11]." The dataset has not been made public, and reference [11] does not provide sufficient detail on the crawling methodology. This strongly suggests that the authors of this manuscript are the same as those of reference [11], which constitutes a deliberate violation of anonymity.

Furthermore, Equation (2) appears to be incorrect, as it suggests that `st` is derived only from `ot`, which would mean the network is not fully connected as described. While the proposed model is technically robust and handles discourse relations well, the sentence generation method is ad-hoc. In light of these issues, I recommend that this paper be accepted for a poster presentation.
