This paper addresses the problem of retrieving natural language sentences to describe a sequence of images, forming a coherent paragraph. This is a significant problem within computer vision and natural language processing, representing a logical extension to single-image captioning. However, it shares strong connections with existing work on generating descriptions for video content (e.g., [Rohrbach et al., 2014]), where a video can be viewed as a sequence of images or shots. Surprisingly, this connection and relevant work are not adequately discussed or cited.

The paper is generally well-written and easy to follow. The problem statement is clear, and the proposed solution employs a set of solid techniques.

The authors propose a neural network architecture, the Coherent Recurrent Convolutional Network (CRCN), to model the relationship between image sequences and corresponding sentences. The architecture appears technically sound, and the inclusion of an entity-based coherence model is a notable strength, allowing the model to capture discourse-level relationships.

The method for generating sentence sequences, however, feels somewhat simplistic. It relies on retrieving sentences associated with training images similar to those in the query stream, and then concatenating these sentences based on image order. While reasonable, this approach seems limited in its ability to generate truly novel or nuanced descriptions.

A concern arises in Section 4.1, where the authors state, “We reuse the blog data of Disneyland from the dataset of [11], and newly collect the data of NYC, using the same crawling method with [11].” The dataset itself is not publicly available, and the cited paper [11] lacks details about the crawling process. This raises questions about reproducibility and suggests a potential conflict of interest, as the authors appear to be heavily involved in both datasets.

Equation (2) appears to contain an error. The equation implies that *st* is derived solely from *ot*, which would suggest a less-than-fully connected network. The proposed architecture is technically sound, particularly the inclusion of an entity-based coherence model, but the sentence generation method feels somewhat ad-hoc.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The problem is relevant and represents a natural extension of existing work.
*   The proposed architecture (CRCN) is well-designed and incorporates a valuable coherence model.
*   The experimental results demonstrate performance improvements over several baselines.
*   The user studies on AMT provide supporting evidence for the effectiveness of the approach.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The connection to and lack of discussion of relevant video description work is a significant omission.
*   The sentence generation method is relatively simplistic and may limit the quality of the generated descriptions.
*   Concerns regarding dataset availability, reproducibility, and potential author conflict of interest.
*   A potential error in Equation (2).

I believe this paper could be accepted as a poster presentation, with the caveats mentioned above. The authors should address the missing related work, clarify the dataset details and potential conflicts of interest, and verify the correctness of Equation (2) before a final decision is made.
