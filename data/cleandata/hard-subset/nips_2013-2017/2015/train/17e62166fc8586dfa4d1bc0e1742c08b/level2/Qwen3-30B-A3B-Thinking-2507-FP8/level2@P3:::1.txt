

The paper proposes a novel approach for generating sequences of natural language sentences from image streams, extending the traditional single-image-to-single-sentence task to a sequence-to-sequence problem. The authors introduce a multimodal architecture called Coherent Recurrent Convolutional Network (CRCN), which integrates convolutional neural networks for image representation, bidirectional recurrent neural networks for sentence modeling, and an entity-based local coherence model for ensuring fluent transitions between consecutive sentences. The work leverages blog posts as a source of text-image parallel data, where each blog post contains a sequence of images and associated text, making it suitable for training on image streams.

The paper makes several significant contributions: (1) it addresses a novel problem that extends beyond current single-image captioning work; (2) it develops a unified architecture that integrates multiple components for sequence generation; and (3) it evaluates on large-scale blog datasets with both quantitative metrics and user studies. The authors demonstrate that their approach outperforms several state-of-the-art baselines, particularly in retrieval metrics and user preference for longer sequences.

The quality of the work is strong. The architecture is well-designed and thoroughly evaluated with appropriate baselines, including extensions of existing single-sentence models. The authors provide detailed ablation studies, comparing variants of their method (CRCN vs. RCN) to validate the contribution of the coherence model. The evaluation methodology is sound, using multiple metrics (BLEU, CIDEr, METEOR, top-K recall, median rank) and user studies via Amazon Mechanical Turk. The paper acknowledges limitations of language metrics for evaluating paragraph coherence, which is a thoughtful consideration.

The clarity of the paper is generally good. The problem statement is well-motivated with a clear example (NYC tourism), and the architecture is described with sufficient technical detail. The figures help visualize the approach, and the experimental setup is well-defined. However, the relationship between the coherence model and the BRNN could be explained more explicitly, particularly how the entity grid transitions are integrated with the sentence representations. The paper would benefit from a more detailed explanation of the 64-dimensional transition descriptor and why this specific representation captures coherence effectively.

The originality of the work is notable. While related to video captioning and single-image captioning, the paper clearly distinguishes itself by focusing on image streams with potentially abrupt visual changes and the need for coherence between sentences. The authors cite relevant work (e.g., [11] for image sequence retrieval, [5] for video description) and demonstrate that their approach is distinct in addressing the sequence-to-sequence generation problem with coherence modeling. The blog-based data collection method is also an interesting and practical approach to obtaining text-image parallel sequences.

The significance of the work is substantial. The problem of generating coherent sentence sequences from image streams has practical applications in photo album captioning, travel blogging, and social media content generation. The demonstrated improvements over baselines, particularly for longer sequences, suggest that the coherence model effectively addresses a gap in the literature. The unsupervised nature of the approach (using blog posts as training data) makes it broadly applicable beyond tourism domains.

Several points warrant consideration for improvement. The user studies primarily evaluate short sequences (5 images), with longer sequences (8-10 images) only used to validate the coherence effect. It would strengthen the paper to include more comprehensive evaluation on longer sequences to demonstrate the approach's utility in practical scenarios where photo streams are typically longer. Additionally, while the paper mentions data augmentation with κ=3, the choice of this specific value lacks detailed justification beyond "thorough empirical tests." Providing more insight into how κ was selected would improve the methodological rigor.

The paper would also benefit from a more detailed discussion of computational complexity and training time, which are important considerations for practical deployment. While the authors mention using VGGNet features for all algorithms, it's worth clarifying whether the CRCN model significantly increases training or inference time compared to baselines.

Overall, the paper addresses a meaningful gap in the literature by extending image captioning to sequence-to-sequence generation with coherence modeling. The proposed CRCN architecture is well-motivated, thoroughly evaluated, and demonstrates clear improvements over baselines, particularly for longer text sequences. The use of blog posts as a source of text-image parallel data is a practical and scalable approach. The work advances the state of the art in a demonstrable way, making it a valuable contribution to the field of multimodal learning and image captioning. The paper should be accepted for presentation at NIPS.
