- Task & approach: CRCN retrieves coherent sentence sequences for image streams using VGG CNN features, a bidirectional RNN over doc2vec sentence reps, and an entity-grid local coherence model.  
- Training/data: Uses large-scale weakly aligned image–text sequences mined from blogs via paragraph segmentation, heuristic image–text alignment, and LSA summarization.  
- Inference: Retrieves candidate sentences from KNN images and re-ranks them with CRCN using a divide-and-conquer beam search.  
- Experiments: Evaluated on NYC and Disneyland blogs against single-image captioning/retrieval baselines with BLEU/CIDEr/METEOR, retrieval metrics, and AMT studies; shows consistent improvements, especially for longer sequences.  
- Technical quality: Sound retrieval-and-ranking framework with reasonable ablations and human evaluation, but core components are largely standard.  
- Ad hoc design choices: Some implementation choices (e.g., zero-padding 64-d coherence vector to 300-d; summing g·v_t across time) appear under-motivated and arbitrary.  
- Noisy preprocessing: Blog preprocessing (heuristic alignments, LSA summarization) introduces noise that could confound results; preprocessing accuracy is not reported.  
- Evaluation limitations: Paragraph-level automatic metrics are brittle for this task and human evaluation is limited; richer human protocols are needed.  
- Clarity/reproducibility: Paper is generally clear but omits key reproducibility details (preprocessing hyperparameters, coreference quality, search complexity/runtime, data/code release).  
- Originality: Framing (images→sentences with explicit local coherence) is a meaningful extension beyond single-image captioning; modeling novelty is incremental (integration of known parts).  
- Significance: Promising for album narration and story summarization; practical impact depends on releasing data/code and validation on broader domains.  
- Pros: Clear problem formulation, sensible integration of content flow and coherence, solid ablations and AMT, and large-scale weakly supervised training.  
- Cons: Heavy reliance on noisy preprocessing, suboptimal evaluation metrics and small language-metric gains, occasional ad hoc modeling choices, limited domains, and reproducibility concerns.  
- Suggestions: Quantify preprocessing accuracy, add paragraph-level metrics and error analysis, compare to simple sentence-smoothing baselines, test additional domains, report runtime, release data/code, and analyze sensitivity to entity-grid and coherence components.
