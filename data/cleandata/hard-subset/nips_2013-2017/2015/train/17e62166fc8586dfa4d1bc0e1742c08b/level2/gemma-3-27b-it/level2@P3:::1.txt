This paper addresses the problem of generating a sequence of natural language sentences for a sequence of images, motivated by scenarios like describing a photo stream from a trip. The core contribution is a coherent recurrent convolutional network (CRCN) that combines CNNs for image representation, bidirectional RNNs for language modeling, and a local coherence model to ensure fluency between sentences. The work leverages blog posts with associated images as a training corpus and demonstrates improvements over baseline methods using both quantitative metrics (BLEU, Recall) and user studies via Amazon Mechanical Turk.

The paper is well-motivated and tackles an interesting and challenging problem. Extending image captioning to image *streams* is a natural progression, and the problem of maintaining coherence across multiple sentences is particularly important. The proposed CRCN architecture appears well-designed, and the integration of a local coherence model is a key novelty. The experimental setup, using blog data and a combination of automatic metrics and human evaluation, is commendable. The paper clearly articulates the challenges inherent in this task, such as the difficulty of obtaining suitable training data and the potential for abrupt visual changes between consecutive images. 

However, several areas could benefit from further elaboration. The description of the data preprocessing, particularly the text segmentation and summarization steps, feels somewhat cursory. While references are provided, more detail about the heuristics used for image-to-text block assignment would be helpful. The choice of LSA for text summarization is mentioned, but a justification for this choice compared to other summarization techniques (e.g., more recent neural methods) is missing.

Regarding the architecture, the rationale behind separating the input activations for the forward and backward RNNs (xft and xbt) is not fully explained. While the paper states it “empirically leads a better performance,” a theoretical or intuitive explanation would strengthen the argument.  Similarly, the specifics of the entity grid construction and the 64-dimensional representation for coherence are a bit vague. A more detailed illustration or example would improve clarity. 

The experimental evaluation, while comprehensive, lacks a detailed ablation study. While the paper compares CRCN to RCN (without the coherence model), systematically removing other components (e.g., the BRNN) to assess their individual contributions would provide further insight.  The quantitative results show improvements, but the magnitude of improvement in some cases (e.g., BLEU scores) appears relatively small. A discussion of the limitations of BLEU in evaluating this type of task, as acknowledged in the paper, could be expanded. 

The connection to related work is generally good, but a few relevant papers could be considered. For example, recent work on discourse-aware neural networks for text generation might be relevant to the coherence modeling aspect. 

Overall, this is a solid contribution to the field. The strengths – a well-defined problem, a novel and well-motivated architecture, and a thorough experimental evaluation – outweigh the weaknesses. The suggested revisions, primarily related to providing more detailed explanations and conducting a more comprehensive ablation study, would further enhance the quality and impact of the work.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Addresses a novel and challenging problem (image stream captioning).
*   Proposes a well-designed architecture (CRCN) with a key innovation (local coherence model).
*   Demonstrates improvements over baselines using both quantitative metrics and user studies.
*   Uses a large and relevant dataset (blog posts).

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Some aspects of the data preprocessing and architecture are not fully explained.
*   A more detailed ablation study would strengthen the evaluation.
*   Magnitude of improvements on some metrics is relatively small.
