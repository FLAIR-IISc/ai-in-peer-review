**Summary**

This paper introduces the novel task of generating a coherent sequence of sentences to describe a stream of images. The authors argue that this is a natural extension of the single-image captioning task, motivated by how users capture events with multiple photos. To solve this problem, they propose a Coherent Recurrent Convolutional Network (CRCN). The model has three main components: a Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) to extract features from each image, a Bidirectional Recurrent Neural Network (BRNN) to model the content flow between sentences (represented as paragraph vectors), and an entity-based local coherence model to ensure linguistic fluency and logical transitions between sentences. The overall framework is retrieval-based: for a query image stream, candidate sentences are retrieved for each image via K-nearest neighbors, and the CRCN is used to score and rank the possible composed sentence sequences to find the one that is most visually relevant and coherent. The model is trained on a large dataset collected from blog posts. The authors demonstrate through extensive experiments, including automatic metrics and human studies, that their method significantly outperforms adapted state-of-the-art single-image captioning baselines.

**Strengths**

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The paper tackles a novel, well-motivated, and important problem. Moving from single images to image streams is a logical and significant next step for the vision-and-language community. The work successfully frames a new research direction and provides a strong baseline for it. The results are likely to be of high interest and to inspire future research in story-telling, video description, and multi-image understanding.

2.  **Sound and Novel Technical Approach:** The proposed CRCN architecture is well-designed for the task. The key insight is to model not just the visual content but also the narrative coherence of the output text. The combination of a content-focused model (BRNN) and a separate, linguistically-motivated coherence model is a strong and novel contribution in this context. Explicitly modeling entity transitions is a clever way to enforce a story-like structure that standard RNN language models might fail to capture, especially given the potential for sharp visual changes between consecutive images in a stream.

3.  **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The experimental validation is thorough. The authors compare their model against several strong, albeit adapted, baselines from the image captioning literature. Crucially, they also include ablation studies (comparing CRCN to RCN and 1NN) that clearly demonstrate the contributions of the BRNN and the coherence model. The inclusion of both standard language/retrieval metrics and a human evaluation via Amazon Mechanical Turk provides compelling evidence for the model's effectiveness. The user study insightfully shows that the coherence model's benefit increases with the length of the sequence, which strongly supports the paper's central claims.

4.  **Resource Contribution:** The method for collecting and processing a large-scale, weakly-supervised dataset from blog posts is a practical solution to the data scarcity problem for this new task. While the pre-processing involves heuristics, it provides a valuable methodology for future work in this area.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement**

1.  **Clarity on "Generation" vs. "Retrieval":** The paper's framing could be more precise. The title "Expressing an Image Stream" and terms like "generate" and "author" throughout the text suggest a generative model that creates new sentences. However, the proposed method is entirely retrieval-based—it selects and arranges sentences that already exist in the training corpus. While this is a valid and effective approach, the distinction is important. The authors should clarify this in the introduction and abstract to manage reader expectations. For example, a title like "Retrieving a Coherent Sequence of Natural Sentences for an Image Stream" would be more accurate.

2.  **Inference Scalability:** The inference procedure relies on finding K-nearest neighbors for every image in the query stream across the entire training dataset. This could be computationally prohibitive for very large-scale datasets. The subsequent combinatorial search, even with the proposed divide-and-conquer approximation, could also be slow. A brief discussion of the computational complexity, runtime performance, and potential strategies for scaling (e.g., using approximate nearest neighbor search) would strengthen the paper.

3.  **Potential Noise in Data Pre-processing:** The dataset creation pipeline relies on several heuristics, such as segmenting text based on distance to an image and using LSA for summarization. This process may introduce significant noise, leading to weak or incorrect alignments between images and their "summary" sentences. While the model is evidently robust enough to learn from this data, a brief discussion on the potential impact of this label noise, or an analysis of its severity, would be beneficial for the reader to understand the limitations of the data source.

4.  **Details of Model Combination:** The description of how the BRNN and coherence model outputs are combined in Section 3.3 could be expanded. The equation `Wf2Wf1[O|q] = [S|g]` is very compact. It would be clearer to explicitly state that the per-sentence BRNN outputs `O` and the global coherence vector `q` are concatenated and then projected through two fully-connected layers. A justification for why shared parameters (`Wf1`, `Wf2`) are used for both the sequence content and coherence features would also be helpful beyond the empirical observation that it "mixes well."

**Conclusion**

This paper presents a solid and valuable contribution. It introduces a new and significant research problem, proposes a novel and well-suited architecture, and validates it with a thorough set of experiments. The strengths, particularly the novelty of the problem and the explicit modeling of coherence, far outweigh the weaknesses, which are primarily related to minor points of clarity and potential scalability issues. The work is technically sound, well-executed, and likely to have a high impact on the field. I recommend acceptance.
