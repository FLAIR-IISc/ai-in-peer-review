This paper addresses the problem of generating a sequence of natural language sentences describing a sequence of images, moving beyond the single image-single sentence paradigm prevalent in existing work. The authors propose a Coherent Recurrent Convolutional Network (CRCN) integrating CNNs, BRNNs, and a local coherence model, trained on blog post data. The approach is evaluated on NYC and Disneyland datasets with quantitative metrics (BLEU, Top-K recall) and user studies via AMT.

**Summary:** The paper presents a logical extension of image captioning to image streams. The motivation—describing a series of photos taken during an event—is relatable. The proposed CRCN architecture is reasonably well-designed, building upon existing successful components (CNNs, RNNs) and adding a coherence model to address the unique challenges of sequential description. The experimental setup, including both automatic metrics and human evaluation, is solid.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** Extending image captioning to image *streams* is a valuable and relatively unexplored direction.
*   **Architecture:** The CRCN architecture is well-motivated and appears sound, effectively combining existing techniques with a crucial coherence component. The use of a local coherence model is particularly important for generating fluent, multi-sentence descriptions.
*   **Dataset:** Leveraging blog posts as a source of image-text pairs is a clever solution to the data scarcity problem. The size of the dataset (140K images, 20K posts) is commendable.
*   **Evaluation:** The combination of quantitative metrics and user studies provides a comprehensive evaluation of the approach. The AMT studies are well-designed and lend strong support to the claims.
*   **Clear Writing:** The paper is generally well-written and easy to follow, with clear explanations of the proposed method and experimental setup.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Limited Baselines:** While the paper compares against several existing single-image captioning methods, adapting them to the sequential setting feels somewhat forced. A more direct comparison to approaches that *already* tackle sequential data (e.g., video captioning) would be more informative, even if requiring different datasets. The cited video captioning work ([23, 32]) is acknowledged, but not directly compared.
*   **Coherence Model Detail:** The description of the local coherence model is somewhat brief. While referencing [1], more detail on how the entity grid is constructed and used within the overall architecture would be helpful. The 64-dimensional representation feels somewhat arbitrary.
*   **Data Augmentation:** The data augmentation strategy (top-κ sentences) feels a bit ad-hoc. The choice of κ=3 isn't rigorously justified.
*   **Score Function:** The score function (Equation 3) is presented without a strong intuition behind its components. The weights between CNN and RNN outputs aren't discussed.
*   **Discussion of Failures:** The paper largely focuses on successes. A discussion of common failure cases and potential limitations would strengthen the work. For example, what types of image streams does the approach struggle with?

**Originality:** The work is original in its focus on image stream description. While the individual components (CNNs, RNNs, coherence models) are not novel, their integration in this specific context is a meaningful contribution. The use of blog posts as a data source is also a novel and practical solution.

**Significance:** The results suggest that this approach is a significant step towards more natural and informative descriptions of image sequences. The potential applications are broad, ranging from automatic travelogue generation to assisting visually impaired users in understanding photo collections. The approach is likely to inspire further research in this area.

**Quality:** The paper appears technically sound. The methodology is well-defined, and the results are supported by both quantitative and qualitative evidence. The authors are careful to evaluate both the strengths and weaknesses of their approach, although as noted above, the discussion of limitations could be expanded.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Addresses an important and under-explored problem.
*   Presents a novel and well-motivated architecture.
*   Demonstrates strong performance on relevant datasets.
*   Provides convincing evidence of the approach's effectiveness through both automatic metrics and user studies.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   Limited comparison to direct competitors in the sequential data space.
*   Some aspects of the implementation (e.g., data augmentation, coherence model details) lack rigorous justification.
*   Could benefit from a more thorough discussion of failure cases and limitations.



**Overall Assessment:**  I recommend **Accept** with minor revisions. The paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of image captioning. Addressing the weaknesses outlined above—particularly expanding the baseline comparisons and providing more detail on the coherence model—would further strengthen the work. The originality and significance of the research outweigh the minor shortcomings.
