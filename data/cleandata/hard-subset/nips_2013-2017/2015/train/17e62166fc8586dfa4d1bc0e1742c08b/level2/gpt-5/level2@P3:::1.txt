Summary and relation to prior work
The paper tackles the problem of producing a coherent sequence of natural sentences for a stream of images, extending prevalent single-image–single-sentence captioning to paragraph-like outputs. The proposed coherent recurrent convolutional network (CRCN) combines: (a) CNN image features (VGG fc7), (b) a bidirectional RNN over sentence representations (doc2vec) to model content flow across the sentence sequence, and (c) an entity-grid local coherence model (per Barzilay & Lapata) to encourage fluent sentence-to-sentence transitions. Training uses max-margin ranking over paired image/sentence-sequence streams derived from blogs via segmentation and LSA-based single-sentence summarization; κ-best summaries per segment provide augmentation. At test time, for each image position, K nearest neighbor images are located in CNN space, the associated sentences form a combinatorial candidate set, and an approximate beam-style divide-and-conquer search selects a high-scoring sentence sequence.

The work is positioned relative to retrieval-based captioning [e.g., Kiros et al., Karpathy & Fei-Fei], multimodal RNN/LSTM captioning [e.g., Mao et al., Vinyals et al.], and video description with sequential models [e.g., Donahue et al.], emphasizing that unlike videos, image streams may be discontinuous and thus benefit from explicit local coherence modeling. The closest prior on sequence-level matching is image-sequence retrieval from paragraphs [Kim et al.], but in the reverse direction. Integrating an entity-grid coherence model into a multimodal matching framework for image-stream-to-paragraph retrieval appears novel for the NIPS 2013–2015 literature.

Strengths
- Clear and timely problem formulation: moving from single-image captions to paragraph-level descriptions for image streams.
- Sensible architecture: jointly optimizing image-sentence compatibility and an entity-based local coherence signal; BRNN over sentence embeddings captures contextualized sentence content.
- Pragmatic data strategy: harvesting large-scale text-image streams from blogs; reasonable segmentation and summarization pipeline.
- Diverse evaluation: automatic text similarity metrics, retrieval metrics, and AMT user studies including an analysis showing longer sequences benefit more from coherence modeling.
- Ablations: comparisons among 1NN, BRNN-only (RCN), and full CRCN demonstrate the contribution of the coherence component, especially on retrieval metrics and longer sequences.

Weaknesses
- Retrieval rather than generation: the system stitches together existing sentences, which limits generalization to novel content and domains. Claims of “unsupervised” learning are somewhat overstated; the approach relies on automatically constructed paired supervision from blogs.
- Domain specificity and scope: evaluation is restricted to two tourism datasets (NYC, Disneyland) collected in-house; transfer beyond this domain is untested. Public availability of data is unclear.
- Baseline fairness/details: single-image baselines are adapted by naive concatenation, potentially underestimating sequence-level modeling; training details for baselines on the same blog corpora (tokenization, vocab, stopping criteria) need fuller documentation to ensure parity.
- Coherence modeling granularity: the entity-grid feature is a fixed, coarse 64-d histogram padded to 300 and cannot be trained end-to-end; the way it interacts with images (a single g vector dotted with each image and summed) is heuristic and may not be the most principled alignment between discourse-level signals and visual content.
- Limited analysis: no report of runtime/complexity for the candidate search; sparse sensitivity analysis (e.g., K, M, κ, history length in the entity grid); no error analysis or ablation of the preprocessing steps (segmentation/summarization).

Quality
The technical components are largely sound, leveraging established building blocks. The max-margin training over paired sequences is appropriate, and the candidate search strategy is pragmatic. Some design choices are heuristic (e.g., zero-padding of coherence features; shared FC weights for BRNN outputs and coherence vector; global coherence vector interacting uniformly with each image), and would benefit from deeper justification or alternatives. Experimental evidence (including AMT) supports the main claims, though broader baselines for sequence-level coherence (e.g., sentence-level bigram/NN coherence models) and complexity/runtime reporting would strengthen the case.

Clarity
The paper is generally well written and organized. Model equations and training setup are specified with dimensions and optimization details. Some notational choices (e.g., overloading T with κ-augmented sentences) and aspects of the scoring function could be clarified. A more explicit description of baseline training and search-time computational costs would improve reproducibility.

Originality
The combination of CNN/BRNN with an explicit entity-grid coherence model for image-stream paragraph retrieval is a novel composition at the time. The problem setting and use of blog-derived paired streams are also interesting. The coherence model itself is classical; the novelty lies in its multimodal integration and application.

Significance
If robust, the approach offers a step toward story-like descriptions of photo streams, a use case of practical interest. The demonstrated gains, especially on longer sequences and in user studies, suggest impact. However, the retrieval paradigm and domain-limited evaluation constrain broader significance; demonstrating generalization or releasing the dataset would increase utility.

Suggested improvements
Essential:
- Provide fuller baseline training details and, if possible, include a sequence-aware baseline that incorporates sentence-transition coherence (e.g., bigram over sentence embeddings or a learned sentence-transition model) to isolate the value of the entity-grid integration.
- Report runtime and complexity of the candidate search; include sensitivity analyses for K and beam size M.
- Clarify the “unsupervised” claim; characterize the noise introduced by segmentation/summarization and its effect (e.g., an ablation varying κ and the summarizer).

Nice-to-have:
- Add an ablation on the coherence model (history length, role schema), and compare to simpler coherence proxies (cosine similarity between adjacent sentence embeddings).
- Provide qualitative error analysis and failure modes (e.g., discontinuous streams, sparse entities).
- Consider releasing the datasets and preprocessing code to facilitate replication.

Arguments for acceptance
- Addresses a meaningful extension of image captioning to paragraphs with coherent sequencing.
- Novel integration of a discourse coherence model in a multimodal framework with measurable benefits, especially on longer sequences.
- Solid empirical results and user study support.

Arguments against acceptance
- Retrieval-based approach with limited demonstrated generalization; domain-specific evaluation on non-public datasets.
- Coherence integration is somewhat heuristic and not fully end-to-end; alternative sequence-level baselines not exhaustively covered.
- Some gaps in analysis (runtime, sensitivity, preprocessing ablations).

Overall recommendation
Leaning toward acceptance. The problem is timely, the method is plausible, and evidence shows clear improvements on coherence for image streams. Addressing the essential suggestions would substantially strengthen the contribution.
