This paper tackles the novel and interesting problem of generating a sequence of natural sentences for a corresponding stream of images. This work extends the popular single-image-to-single-sentence paradigm to a more complex sequence-to-sequence setting, which better reflects how users capture and narrate experiences. The authors propose a multimodal architecture, the Coherent Recurrent Convolutional Network (CRCN), which combines a CNN for image representation, a bidirectional RNN for modeling sentence flow, and an entity-based local coherence model to ensure the generated text is fluent and connected. The model is trained on a large-scale dataset of blog posts and evaluated against strong baselines.

This work builds upon the significant progress in visual-semantic models (e.g., Karpathy et al. 2015, Vinyals et al. 2015) but is, to my knowledge, the first to explicitly address the image-stream-to-paragraph task. Its primary originality lies in integrating a classic NLP coherence model (Barzilay & Lapata 2008) into a deep learning framework to improve narrative flow, a critical component when generating multi-sentence descriptions for potentially disjoint images.

**Strengths:**
*   **Significance and Originality:** The paper addresses a significant and logical next step for the image description field. The problem formulation itself is a valuable contribution. The CRCN architecture, particularly the integration of the explicit coherence model, is novel and well-motivated.
*   **Quality:** The technical approach is sound. The evaluation is thorough, employing both automatic metrics (BLEU, CIDEr, retrieval metrics) and human studies on a large, realistic dataset. The ablation study effectively demonstrates the positive contribution of both the BRNN and the coherence model components.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and clearly organized, making the proposed method and experiments easy to follow.

**Weaknesses and Constructive Suggestions:**
*   The method is retrieval-based, meaning it composes paragraphs from sentences in the training set rather than generating novel ones. This limits its expressiveness. Future work could explore a hybrid model that can generate new sentences while being guided by the coherence module.
*   The test-time retrieval process relies on an approximate divide-and-conquer search to manage the exponential complexity. While pragmatic, a brief discussion on the search algorithm's computational cost or its proximity to the optimal solution would be beneficial.

Overall, this is a strong paper that introduces a novel problem, proposes a solid technical solution, and provides a comprehensive evaluation. The work is of high quality and significance, advancing the state-of-the-art in a demonstrable way that will likely inspire future research in multi-image understanding and visual storytelling. I recommend acceptance.
