## Review of "Expressing an Image Stream with a Sequence of Natural Sentences"

This paper tackles the problem of generating coherent natural language descriptions for a sequence of images, moving beyond the single image-single sentence paradigm. The authors propose a Coherent Recurrent Convolutional Network (CRCN) integrating CNNs for image representation, BRNNs for language modeling, and a local coherence model to ensure fluency. The approach leverages a large corpus of blog posts with associated images for training.

The core idea of extending image captioning to streams is a logical and valuable step, addressing a common user scenario. The proposed CRCN architecture appears well-motivated, with each component (CNN, BRNN, coherence model) contributing to a holistic understanding of the visual and linguistic data. The use of blog data as a training resource is clever, offering a readily available source of image-text pairings in a sequential format.  The quantitative results, using BLEU, Recall, and user studies on AMT, demonstrate a clear improvement over the baselines, including established methods like those of Karpathy et al. and Vinyals et al.  The ablation studies (comparing CRCN to BRNN-only) effectively highlight the benefit of the coherence model. 

However, the paper has some weaknesses. While the experimental setup is comprehensive, the baselines are somewhat limited and slightly dated. More recent and sophisticated sequence-to-sequence models incorporating attention mechanisms could provide a stronger comparative point. The details regarding the specific implementation of the local coherence model are a bit sparse; more clarity on how the entity grid is constructed and utilized would be beneficial. Although the authors acknowledge the limitations of BLEU, reliance on this metric remains a concern, given its known shortcomings in evaluating fluency and coherence.  The ablation studies, while helpful, could be expanded to analyze the individual contributions of the CNN and BRNN components more thoroughly.

**Arguments for acceptance:** The paper addresses a novel and important problem, presents a well-motivated architecture, and demonstrates strong empirical results through quantitative and qualitative evaluations. It expands the scope of image captioning and offers a practical solution for describing image streams.

**Arguments against acceptance:** The baseline comparison is not exhaustive. More detail regarding the coherence model is needed. The reliance on BLEU is a weakness. 

**Overall:** This is a solid contribution with clear potential. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions. Specifically, I suggest adding more recent baselines, providing more details on the coherence model, and discussing the limitations of relying heavily on BLEU. Encouraging the authors to release the code would further benefit the community.
