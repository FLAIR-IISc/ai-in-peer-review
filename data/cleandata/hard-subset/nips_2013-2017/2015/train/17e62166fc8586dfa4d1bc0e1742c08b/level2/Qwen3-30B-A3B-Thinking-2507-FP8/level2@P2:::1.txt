

This paper proposes a novel approach for generating a sequence of natural language sentences from an image stream, extending the typical single-image-to-single-sentence task to a more complex sequence-to-sequence problem. The authors introduce a Coherent Recurrent Convolutional Network (CRCN) that integrates convolutional neural networks for image representation, bidirectional recurrent neural networks for sentence modeling, and an entity-based local coherence model for fluent transitions between sentences. The key innovation is addressing both the image stream-to-sentence sequence mapping and ensuring coherence between consecutive sentences, which the authors argue is critical for natural language descriptions of image sequences.

The paper makes a strong case for the problem's relevance by noting that users often capture multiple images during memorable experiences, requiring holistic understanding of the entire image stream. The authors leverage blog posts as training data, which typically contain text-image sequences that naturally form narrative structures. They preprocess blogs to extract image-sentence pairs and use these to train their CRCN model, which is evaluated on NYC and Disneyland blog datasets with over 20K blog posts and 140K images.

Strengths of the paper include:
1. The problem definition is clearly motivated and addresses an important gap in the literature. The authors properly acknowledge related work in video captioning and single-image captioning, making a compelling case for their approach.
2. The CRCN architecture is well-motivated and integrates existing techniques (CNNs, BRNNs) with a novel coherence model. The experimental design is comprehensive, using both quantitative metrics (BLEU, CIDEr, METEOR, top-K recall) and human evaluation via AMT.
3. The results demonstrate clear improvements over baselines (including variants of their own model), particularly in retrieval metrics and human evaluation for longer text sequences. The ablation studies effectively demonstrate the contribution of the coherence model.

However, several weaknesses need addressing:
1. The claim of being "the first" to address this problem requires verification. The paper cites video captioning works but doesn't sufficiently differentiate their task from existing video-to-sentence approaches (e.g., [23, 32] mentioned in the related work). The problem of generating multiple sentences for image sequences has been partially addressed in work like "Generating Image Descriptions from Multiple Images" (Kulkarni et al., 2013) which generates descriptive sentences for multiple images but doesn't explicitly model sentence-to-sentence coherence. The authors should clarify how their work differs from such prior efforts.
2. The blog data preprocessing method (text segmentation and summarization) is somewhat ad-hoc. The paper doesn't provide sufficient justification for the specific heuristics used (e.g., image-to-text block distances) or address potential biases introduced by the text summarization process. The authors claim to use LSA-based summarization, but it's unclear how this affects the quality of the training data compared to manual annotation.
3. The evaluation metrics are somewhat limited. While BLEU, METEOR, and CIDEr are standard for sentence generation, they are not ideal for evaluating coherence across multiple sentences. The paper acknowledges this limitation (noting BLEU's poor performance for paragraph evaluation), but the human evaluation is limited to short image sequences (5 images) where coherence is less critical. The AMT results for longer sequences (8-10 images) show larger improvements for CRCN over RCN, but the paper doesn't provide enough detail on these longer sequences to fully validate the coherence benefit.
4. The paper lacks sufficient discussion of failure cases. Without examples of where the model fails, it's difficult to assess the limitations of the approach and potential directions for future work.

The paper is well-written and technically sound, with clear explanations of the model components and experimental setup. The training and evaluation procedures follow standard practices in the field.

In terms of significance, the problem is meaningful and the proposed approach offers a practical solution for a common user scenario (describing photo collections). The blog-based training data is a clever use of existing resources, and the model's unsupervised nature makes it applicable to various domains. However, the significance could be strengthened by more clearly articulating how this work advances the state-of-the-art beyond simple extensions of existing techniques.

Overall, this is a solid contribution that addresses a meaningful gap in image captioning literature. While the novelty claim could be better substantiated, the technical approach is sound and the results are compelling. I recommend acceptance with the authors addressing the concerns about novelty clarification, data preprocessing justification, and failure case analysis in the camera-ready version. The paper makes a valuable contribution to the field of multimodal learning by explicitly modeling sentence-to-sentence coherence in image stream captioning, which is a critical aspect often overlooked in prior work.
