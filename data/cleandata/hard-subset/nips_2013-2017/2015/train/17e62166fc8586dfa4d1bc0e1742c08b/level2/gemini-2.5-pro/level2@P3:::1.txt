This paper addresses the novel and interesting problem of describing a sequence of images with a corresponding sequence of natural language sentences. This work extends the well-studied task of single image captioning to a sequence-to-sequence setting, motivated by the observation that users often capture events and stories through a stream of photos. To tackle this challenge, the paper proposes a Coherent Recurrent Convolutional Network (CRCN). The approach is retrieval-based: for a given query image stream, candidate sentences are first retrieved for each image from a training corpus. The core of the contribution is a model that scores and ranks entire candidate sentence sequences. This model integrates three main components: a Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) for image feature extraction, a Bidirectional Recurrent Neural Network (BRNN) to model the contextual flow of sentences in a sequence, and an entity-based local coherence model, adapted from prior work in NLP, to ensure smooth and logical transitions between sentences. The model is trained on a large dataset of image-text streams curated from blog posts, using a max-margin objective to rank correctly aligned sequences higher than incorrect ones.

**Quality and Technical Soundness**

The paper is of high technical quality. The proposed CRCN model is a sensible and well-thought-out architecture for this new task. The combination of a strong visual model (CNN), a sequential language model (BRNN), and an explicit discourse model (the local coherence model) is a powerful approach that directly tackles the key challenges of maintaining both local image-relevance and global paragraph-level coherence. The training methodology, based on a max-margin loss, is standard and appropriate for this type of ranking problem. The experimental methodology is rigorous, featuring extensive comparisons, an ablation study, and both automatic and human evaluations. The claims of the paper are well-supported by the empirical results presented.

**Clarity**

The paper is generally well-written and organized. The problem statement is clear, and the motivation is compelling. The overall architecture of the CRCN is explained effectively with the help of Figure 2. However, some areas could benefit from additional clarification:

*   The description of the local coherence model is quite brief. While the paper correctly cites the original work, a bit more intuition about the entity grid and the nature of the 64-dimensional feature vector would improve the self-containedness of the manuscript for readers not familiar with that specific NLP technique.
*   The choice to represent individual sentences with paragraph vectors for input to the BRNN is non-obvious, as paragraph vectors are typically used for document-level representations. A brief justification for this choice over other sentence embedding methods would be beneficial.
*   The paper's framing could more precisely distinguish between sentence generation and sentence retrieval/composition. The method does not generate novel sentences word-by-word but rather retrieves and arranges existing sentences. Clarifying this in the introduction would help to set reader expectations accurately.

**Originality and Significance**

The work is highly original and significant. It is one of the first, if not the first, to formally define and provide a strong baseline for the task of image stream captioning. Moving from single images to sequences is a logical and important next step for the vision-and-language community, and this paper provides a solid foundation for future work. The idea of explicitly modeling sentence-to-sentence coherence, in addition to image-to-sentence relevance, is a key insight and a primary contribution. Furthermore, the resourceful use of blog posts to create a large-scale, albeit noisy, dataset for this new problem is a valuable contribution in itself. This work is likely to be influential and inspire follow-up research on story-telling, video description, and multi-image understanding.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

1.  **Novel Problem Formulation:** The paper introduces a new, challenging, and well-motivated problem that significantly extends the scope of image captioning research.
2.  **Strong Technical Contribution:** The proposed CRCN model is a well-designed and effective architecture that thoughtfully combines components to address the specific challenges of the task.
3.  **Thorough and Convincing Evaluation:** The work is supported by a comprehensive set of experiments, including strong baselines, ablation studies, multiple metrics, and a crucial human evaluation that validates the importance of the coherence model.
4.  **High Potential Impact:** The paper addresses a practical application and opens up a new avenue for research in multimodal AI.

**Suggestions for Improvement (which could be addressed in a final version):**

1.  **Analysis of Inference Approximation:** The divide-and-conquer beam search used at test time is a pragmatic solution to the combinatorial search problem. The paper could be strengthened by a brief analysis of this approximation, for example, by studying the sensitivity of the results to the beam size.
2.  **Discussion on Data Pre-processing:** The data collection relies on heuristics for aligning images and text, and LSA for summarization. A short discussion of the potential limitations or noise introduced by this pipeline would add valuable context.
3.  **Generalizability:** The experiments are focused on tourism datasets. While the approach seems general, a brief discussion on potential challenges when applying the model to more diverse domains would be insightful.

In summary, this is a strong paper that presents a novel problem, a solid technical solution, and a thorough evaluation. The contributions are significant, and the work is a clear advance for the field. The identified weaknesses are minor and do not detract from the overall quality of the work.
