Summary and relation to prior work: The paper tackles “visual storytelling”: given a stream of images, retrieve a coherent sequence of natural sentences. The proposed CRCN combines CNN image features (VGG), a bidirectional RNN over sentence representations (doc2vec) to model content flow, and an entity-grid-based local coherence model (Barzilay & Lapata) to encourage smooth sentence-to-sentence transitions. Training uses weakly aligned image–text sequences mined from blogs via paragraph segmentation, heuristic image–text alignment, and LSA summarization. At test time, candidate sentences are retrieved from KNN images and re-ranked with CRCN using a divide-and-conquer beam search. Experiments on NYC and Disneyland blog datasets compare to single-image captioning/retrieval baselines (e.g., Karpathy & Fei-Fei; Kiros et al.; Vinyals et al.) using BLEU/CIDEr/METEOR, retrieval metrics, and AMT user studies. Results show consistent improvements, with coherence contributing more on longer sequences.

Quality: The system is technically sound as a retrieval-and-ranking approach, with reasonable ablations (1NN, without coherence) and human evaluation. However, core components are standard and some design choices (e.g., zero-padding the 64-d coherence vector to 300d; summing g·v_t across time) feel ad hoc and under-motivated. The blog preprocessing pipeline (heuristic alignment; LSA summarization) introduces noise that could confound evaluation, and its accuracy is not quantified. Paragraph-level automatic metrics are known to be brittle for this setup; the paper partially acknowledges this but does not compensate with richer human protocols.

Clarity: The paper is generally clear and well organized, with equations and architecture details provided (dropout, optimizer, negatives). Some missing details affect reproducibility: exact preprocessing hyperparameters, coreference resolution quality, search complexity/runtime, and whether data/code will be released.

Originality: The framing—sequence-to-sequence (images→sentences) with explicit local coherence—is a meaningful extension beyond single-image captioning and differs from video-to-sentence work (e.g., LRCN) and image-sequence retrieval (Kim et al.). The modeling novelty is incremental (integration of known parts).

Significance: Potentially useful for album narration and story summarization; results and AMT preferences are encouraging, especially on longer sequences. Impact depends on releasing data and on broader-domain validation.

Pros for acceptance:
- Clear problem formulation and relevant task (visual storytelling).
- Sensible integration of content flow and coherence; solid ablations and AMT.
- Large-scale weakly supervised training from blogs.

Cons for acceptance:
- Heavy reliance on noisy preprocessing; limited analysis of its effects.
- Evaluation metrics not ideal for paragraphs; small gains on language metrics.
- Modeling choices occasionally ad hoc; limited domains; reproducibility concerns.

Suggestions: Quantify preprocessing accuracy; add paragraph-suitable metrics and error analysis; compare to simple sentence-smoothing baselines; test on additional domains; report runtime; release data/code; analyze sensitivity to entity-grid history and coherence components. Overall, a promising direction that would benefit from strengthened evaluation and transparency.
