This paper tackles the problem of generating a sequence of natural language sentences to describe an image stream, extending the traditional single image-to-single sentence paradigm. The authors propose a coherent recurrent convolutional network (CRCN) integrating CNNs, BRNNs, and a local coherence model, trained on blog posts as a text-image parallel corpus. The approach is evaluated on NYC and Disneyland datasets, demonstrating superior performance over several baselines using both quantitative metrics (BLEU, top-K recall) and user studies on Amazon Mechanical Turk.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** Extending image captioning to image *streams* is a natural and important step, and this work is among the first to directly address this challenge. The problem setup is well-motivated by real-world scenarios (e.g., vacation photos).
*   **Technical Soundness:** The proposed CRCN architecture seems well-designed, incorporating relevant components like BRNNs for sequence modeling and a coherence model to improve fluency. The use of VGGNet for image representation is standard and reasonable.
*   **Dataset:** Leveraging blog posts as a source of weakly-supervised text-image data is a clever approach to overcome the difficulty of obtaining parallel corpora for image streams. The size of the dataset (140K images, 20K blogs) is substantial.
*   **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The authors provide a thorough evaluation using both automatic metrics (BLEU, top-K recall) *and* human evaluations via AMT. Comparing against several relevant baselines strengthens the results. The inclusion of variations in AMT task (different query stream lengths) is a good point.
*   **Ablation Study:** The comparison of CRCN with its variants (1NN, RCN) helps to demonstrate the contribution of each component, particularly the coherence model.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Coherence Model Details:** While the authors mention a local coherence model based on entity grids and transition frequencies, the explanation feels somewhat superficial. It’s unclear how the transitions are represented and contribute to the final score. More detailed explanation and potentially visualization of the entity grid and transition patterns would be beneficial.
*   **Baseline Selection:**  While the chosen baselines are reasonable, they are primarily single-image captioning models extended to image streams. It’s unclear how difficult it is to adapt more recent sequence-to-sequence models designed for video captioning (e.g., those utilizing attention mechanisms) to this task.  A more direct comparison with these approaches would be valuable.  The authors state these are state-of-the-art *candidate* methods, but the rationale for selecting these specific ones isn't fully clear.
*   **Quantitative Metrics Limitations:** The authors acknowledge the limitations of BLEU and other language similarity metrics for evaluating the fluency and coherence of longer passages. While AMT results provide a more subjective measure, relying solely on these automatic metrics could be misleading. Exploring metrics specifically designed for evaluating coherence (e.g., discourse-based metrics) could be beneficial.
*   **Data Augmentation:** The data augmentation step (selecting top-κ summary sentences) seems somewhat ad-hoc. Justification for choosing κ=3 would be helpful.
*   **Minor Clarity Issues:** Some sentences are a bit dense and could benefit from simplification. For example, the description of the BRNN in Section 3.1 is quite complex.



**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The paper addresses a novel and important problem with a well-motivated approach.
*   The proposed architecture is technically sound and the experimental results are compelling.
*   The comprehensive evaluation, including human studies, strengthens the validity of the findings.
*   The use of blog data as a weakly-supervised resource is a clever solution to a data scarcity problem.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The explanation of the coherence model lacks sufficient detail.
*   The comparison with relevant baselines could be more thorough.
*   The reliance on standard language similarity metrics for evaluating passage coherence is a limitation.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of image and language understanding. The novelty of the problem, the technical soundness of the approach, and the thorough evaluation make it worthy of acceptance. Addressing the weaknesses related to the coherence model explanation, baseline comparison, and evaluation metrics would further strengthen the paper.  I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
