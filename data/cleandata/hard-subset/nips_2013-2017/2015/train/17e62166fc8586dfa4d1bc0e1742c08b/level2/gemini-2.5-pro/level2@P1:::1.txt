This paper addresses the novel and interesting problem of generating a coherent sequence of natural language sentences to describe a stream of images. While most prior work has focused on generating a single sentence for a single image, this work extends both the input and output to sequences, which is a natural and important step forward for the field. The authors propose a multimodal architecture called the Coherent Recurrent Convolutional Network (CRCN), which learns to retrieve an appropriate sentence sequence for a given image stream. The CRCN model integrates three main components: a Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) to extract image features, a Bidirectional Recurrent Neural Network (BRNN) to model the flow of content in the text sequence, and an entity-based local coherence model to ensure the output is fluent and reads like a natural paragraph. To train this model, the authors cleverly leverage a large dataset of blog posts, treating them as a source of weakly-aligned image and text streams. The paper presents a thorough evaluation on two blog datasets (NYC and Disneyland), showing that the proposed method outperforms several adapted state-of-the-art baselines and variants of their own model, according to both automatic metrics and human studies.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Novelty and Significance:** The paper tackles a timely and significant problem. Moving from single-image captioning to describing image streams is a logical and challenging progression that better reflects how humans capture and narrate experiences. The problem formulation is well-motivated and the work is, to my knowledge, the first to address it in this manner. The potential impact is high, with applications in automatic album summarization, storytelling, and assistive technologies.

2.  **Sound Technical Approach:** The proposed CRCN architecture is a thoughtful combination of powerful techniques. The use of a CNN for images and a BRNN for text is a strong baseline, but the key novelty lies in the integration of an explicit, classic NLP coherence model (the entity grid from Barzilay and Lapata). This directly addresses the core challenge of the problem: ensuring that the output is not just a collection of accurate individual captions, but a fluent and coherent paragraph. This is a clever way to inject linguistic structure into a deep learning framework.

3.  **Resourceful Data Collection:** The authors overcome a major hurdle for this new task—the lack of paired image-stream/text-stream data—by creatively using blog posts. The pre-processing pipeline to extract aligned image-sentence sequences is a practical solution that enables training a data-hungry model.

4.  **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The experimental validation is strong. The authors compare their method against several relevant and strong baselines adapted from the single-image captioning literature. The inclusion of an ablation study (CRCN vs. RCN vs. 1NN) clearly demonstrates the contribution of the BRNN and, more importantly, the explicit coherence model. The combination of automatic metrics (BLEU, retrieval metrics) and human evaluation via AMT provides a convincing case for the superiority of their method.

**Weaknesses and Questions for the Authors:**

1.  **Retrieval vs. Generation:** The proposed method is retrieval-based, meaning it composes paragraphs from sentences that exist in the training corpus. However, several of the main baselines (e.g., Vinyals et al. [30], Karpathy et al. [9]) are generative models that can produce novel sentences. This is a fundamental difference. While the retrieval approach has its merits (e.g., ensuring grammatical correctness), it is limited in its expressive power and cannot describe novel scenes or compositions of objects. A discussion of the trade-offs between retrieval and generation for this task would significantly strengthen the paper.

2.  **Clarity of the Scoring Function:** The formulation of the compatibility score in Equation (3) is unclear. The equation is `Skl = sum(s_kt · v_lt + g_k · v_lt)`. Here, `g_k` is the feature vector representing the coherence of the entire sentence sequence `k`. It is unclear why this global coherence vector `g_k` is dotted with *each* individual image feature `v_lt`. This formulation seems to imply that the global coherence of the text must align well with each individual image in the stream, which is not intuitive. A more intuitive formulation might be a single global term, such as `sum(s_kt · v_lt) + g_k · v_global_l`, where `v_global_l` is an aggregated feature for the entire image stream. Could the authors please clarify the motivation behind this specific design choice?

3.  **Scalability of Inference:** The inference step requires searching over an exponentially large space of candidate sequences (K^N). The authors propose a divide-and-conquer strategy with beam search to make this tractable. This is a reasonable approximation, but its quality and computational cost are not discussed. How does the performance (both in terms of accuracy and speed) scale with the length of the image stream, N? The experiments seem limited to relatively short sequences (up to 10 images).

4.  **Dataset Preprocessing Heuristics:** The dataset creation relies on heuristics for segmenting text and associating it with images. This process can be noisy and errors in the alignment could negatively impact the model's performance. It would be beneficial to include a brief analysis of the quality of these alignments or provide some examples of potential failure cases in the pre-processing stage.

5.  **Minor Correction:** In the introduction, the authors state their approach is "completely unsupervised". Given that the model is trained on aligned (albeit weakly) image-text data from blogs, this would be more accurately described as weak supervision.

Overall, this is a strong paper that introduces a novel and important problem with a well-designed and effective solution. The strengths in novelty, technical approach, and evaluation outweigh the weaknesses, which are primarily related to points that could be clarified or explored in future work. I believe this work will be of great interest to the NIPS community and will inspire further research in this direction.
