Summary and relation to prior work:
This paper addresses the task of expressing an image stream with a sequence of natural language sentences. Unlike most image captioning works that map a single image to a single sentence, the authors propose a multimodal architecture, Coherent Recurrent Convolutional Network (CRCN), that (i) retrieves candidate sentences per image, (ii) models sentence flow with a bidirectional RNN over paragraph vectors, and (iii) explicitly models local discourse coherence using an entity-grid model [1]. Training data are automatically constructed from blog posts by segmenting text around images and summarizing each segment to one (plus κ augmented) sentences using LSA; models are trained with a max-margin ranking loss aligning sentence sequences and image streams. At test time, the system performs KNN in image space to construct a large candidate set and applies an approximate beam/divide-and-conquer search guided by the learned compatibility score.

The paper situates itself relative to (a) image captioning and multimodal RNNs/CNNs [5, 9, 12, 19, 21, 26, 30], (b) video description (e.g., LRCN [5]) which models sequential inputs but typically outputs one sentence per clip, and (c) sequence retrieval in the opposite direction [11]. The explicit incorporation of a classic entity-based coherence model [1] into a multimodal architecture appears novel. The use of user-generated blogs as text-image parallel streams is also a distinguishing aspect.

Quality:
- Method: The components are described with reasonable detail: VGG fc7 for images, doc2vec (300d) for sentence representations, a BRNN with ReLU and separate forward/backward input projections, and an entity-grid coherence vector (64 transition features, zero-padded to 300d) combined via two FC layers to 4096d to match image features. The scoring function sums per-position sentence–image dot products and a global coherence vector–image dot product. Training uses a structured max-margin loss with randomly sampled negatives and BPTT/RMSprop. The approximate candidate search is clearly motivated.

- Soundness: The overall approach is plausible and experimentally supported. Some modeling choices would benefit from further justification/analysis:
  • The score S sums over t without explicit length normalization; since test and negative sequences vary in length, this could bias towards longer sequences. An ablation with/without length normalization or with average pooling would help.
  • The global coherence term gk is dotted with each image feature and summed across t, effectively weighting coherence by the aggregate image content; this seems ad hoc and may double-count. A separate term independent of per-image vlt or a learned scalar weight per sequence could be cleaner.
  • Zero-padding the 64-d coherence vector to 300d before FC layers is unusual; a learned linear projection might be preferable (though the subsequent FC likely mitigates this).
  • Using a simple RNN with ReLUs for sentence flow is reasonable at sentence-level time scales, but given the known optimization issues, a BRNN with LSTM/GRU could be stronger; an ablation would strengthen claims.

- Data construction: The blog segmentation/summarization pipeline is practical, but introduces noise (paragraph heuristics, LSA summarization, automatic coreference and parsing on informal text). The paper would be stronger with error analysis or robustness checks (e.g., performance vs. segmentation quality; effect of κ).

- Evaluation: Two tourism domains (NYC, Disneyland) with ~20k blog posts and ~140k images is sizeable. Quantitative evaluation uses BLEU/CIDEr/METEOR against summarized ground-truth sentences and sequence-level retrieval metrics (R@K, median rank), and AMT pairwise preferences. The ablations (1NN vs RCN vs CRCN) isolate the contributions of BRNN and coherence; the coherence term helps more as sequence length grows (AMT with 8–10 images). This is encouraging. Some clarifications would help:
  • Define precisely the sequence-level retrieval setup for R@K and median rank (candidate pool size, whether all training sequences are candidates, handling of length mismatch).
  • Report absolute scores and statistical significance for AMT (e.g., binomial tests); Table 2 is referenced but not fully reproduced here.
  • Discuss generalization beyond tourism topics; even a small third domain would strengthen claims of domain-agnosticism.
  • Runtime/complexity of candidate search and inference.

Clarity:
The paper is generally well organized and readable, with useful figures. Implementation details (optimizers, dropout, K, M) are provided. A few places could be clearer: precise construction of the entity grid across a sequence; how coreference is resolved and with what accuracy; exact splitting and safeguards against leakage; and a more explicit description of the divide-and-conquer/beam search (values for Q, sensitivity to K and M). Minor notation issues (e.g., reuse of indices, overloading Tln) can be cleaned up.

Originality:
Extending image captioning to image streams and outputting coherent multi-sentence descriptions via an entity-based coherence model integrated with a multimodal BRNN is, to my knowledge, novel in the NIPS literature of 2013–2015. Prior work closest in spirit either treats video-to-sentence or performs retrieval in the reverse direction. The idea of training from blogs is pragmatic and relatively new in this context.

Significance:
If robust, the approach advances beyond single-sentence captioning toward story-like descriptions, which is of interest to both CV and NLP communities. While the system retrieves rather than generates sentences, improvements over strong baselines and AMT preferences suggest practical value. The reliance on blog data and heuristic preprocessing may limit immediate applicability, but the framework could catalyze follow-up work on coherent, multi-sentence multimodal description.

Strengths:
- Clear problem formulation: image streams to sentence sequences.
- Novel integration of an entity-based coherence model with a multimodal BRNN/CNN.
- Large, realistic training data sourced from blogs; unsupervised alignment.
- Solid empirical results with both automatic metrics and human evaluation.
- Thoughtful ablations showing increasing benefit of coherence with sequence length.

Weaknesses:
- Some modeling choices appear ad hoc (global coherence term interaction, zero-padding) and lack ablation.
- Potential length bias in the scoring function; sequence-length normalization not discussed.
- Evaluation confined to two related domains; limited discussion of domain transfer.
- Dependence on noisy preprocessing (segmentation, summarization, parsing/coreference) without robustness analysis.
- Retrieval-based formulation restricts linguistic diversity and may inflate n-gram metrics.

Questions for the authors:
- How are R@K and median rank computed at the sequence level? What is the candidate pool per query?
- Did you try LSTM/GRU units for the BRNN? Any differences?
- How sensitive is performance to K (nearest images), M (beam width), and κ (number of augmented sentences)?
- Can you report the effect of length normalization or alternative scoring aggregations (sum vs average)?
- Will the datasets and preprocessed sentence alignments be released?

Suggestions:
- Add ablations on the coherence pathway (no zero-padding; learned projection; alternative history lengths).
- Explore hierarchical RNNs (sentence-level then paragraph-level) and/or LSTM variants.
- Include error analyses illustrating typical failure modes (entity drift, topic shifts).
- Consider a small-scale generalization experiment beyond tourism (e.g., food blogs).
- Provide more detailed AMT protocol and significance tests.

Overall evaluation:
A timely and interesting step toward coherent multi-sentence description of image streams, with a sensible architecture and convincing empirical evidence. Some design and evaluation details need sharpening, but the contribution is, on balance, meaningful.

Recommendation: weak accept. Confidence: medium.
