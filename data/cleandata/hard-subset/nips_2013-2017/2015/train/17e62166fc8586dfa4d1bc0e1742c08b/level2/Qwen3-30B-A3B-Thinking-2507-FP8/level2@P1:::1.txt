

This paper proposes an interesting extension to the image captioning problem by generating sequences of natural sentences for image streams rather than single images. The authors introduce a coherent recurrent convolutional network (CRCN) that integrates convolutional neural networks for image representation, bidirectional recurrent neural networks for sentence modeling, and a local coherence model to ensure fluent transitions between sentences. The work is motivated by the observation that users often capture multiple images during memorable experiences, and the description of these experiences should consider the entire image stream rather than individual images.

The paper makes a clear contribution by addressing a gap in the literature - most prior work focuses on single-image single-sentence generation, while this work extends both input and output dimensions to sequences. The authors demonstrate that their approach outperforms several strong baselines on both quantitative metrics (BLEU, CIDEr, METEOR, top-K recall) and user studies on two blog datasets (NYC and Disneyland) containing over 20K blog posts with 140K images.

Strengths of the paper include:
1. The problem formulation is well-motivated and addresses a genuine gap in the literature. The example of users taking multiple photos during memorable experiences is compelling.
2. The CRCN architecture is well-designed, with a clear integration of CNN for image features, BRNN for sentence modeling, and the coherence model for smooth transitions between sentences.
3. The use of blog posts as training data is creative and leverages abundant user-generated content that naturally contains image sequences and their corresponding descriptions.
4. The experimental evaluation is thorough, using both standard metrics and user studies via AMT, which provides strong evidence of the method's effectiveness.
5. The ablation study (comparing CRCN with RCN and 1NN) effectively demonstrates the contribution of the coherence model.

The paper has several weaknesses that should be addressed:
1. The problem statement could be more clearly distinguished from related work. While the paper mentions video captioning (e.g., [23, 32]), it doesn't sufficiently contrast with recent work on image sequence description. For example, the paper by [11] addresses "retrieval of image sequences for a query paragraph" (the opposite direction of this work), but doesn't discuss how the current work differs from other sequence-to-sequence approaches.
2. The training data collection process is not fully described. The paper mentions using blog posts from Blogspot and WordPress, but doesn't explain how they handled blog posts with text that doesn't correspond to images or how they dealt with image-text misalignments. This is particularly important since the approach relies on text-image parallel data.
3. The paper claims "to the best of our knowledge, this work is the first to address the problem," but doesn't provide a thorough comparison with prior work on sequence generation for images. The related work section could be strengthened by discussing more recent approaches to multi-image description.
4. The quantitative results (Table 1) show that CRCN outperforms baselines, but the improvements are modest (e.g., BLEU score increases of 1-2 points). The paper should better explain why these improvements matter in practical terms.
5. The user study methodology has limitations: they sampled only 100 test streams and restricted queries to 5 images, which may not fully capture the complexity of longer image sequences where coherence becomes more important.

The paper is generally well-written and clear, with good organization. The figures (particularly Fig. 1 and Fig. 2) effectively illustrate the approach. The technical details of the CRCN architecture are well-explained, though the training procedure could benefit from more details about hyperparameter selection.

Regarding the four evaluation criteria:
- Quality: The paper presents a technically sound approach with thorough evaluation. The method is complete and the authors are careful to evaluate both strengths and weaknesses.
- Clarity: The paper is clearly written with good organization. The architecture is well-explained with appropriate figures.
- Originality: The sequence-to-sequence approach is novel compared to the single-image single-sentence generation that dominates the literature. The integration of the coherence model is a significant contribution.
- Significance: The work addresses a meaningful problem with practical applications (e.g., automatically describing travel experiences), and the evaluation demonstrates clear improvements over baselines.

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of image captioning. While there are some weaknesses in the related work comparison and data collection process, the core idea is novel, well-executed, and supported by thorough evaluation. The paper would benefit from addressing the points above, but the current work is sufficiently significant and technically sound to be accepted for NIPS.

The authors should clarify the distinction between their work and prior sequence-based approaches, provide more detail about the blog data collection process, and better explain the practical significance of their modest performance improvements. With these minor revisions, this paper would make an excellent contribution to the conference.
