

This paper proposes a novel approach for generating coherent sentence sequences from image streams, extending the traditional image captioning problem to handle sequences of images and corresponding sequences of sentences. The authors introduce the Coherent Recurrent Convolutional Network (CRCN) architecture, which integrates convolutional neural networks for image representation, bidirectional recurrent neural networks for sentence modeling, and an entity-based local coherence model to ensure smooth transitions between sentences. The key contribution is extending both input and output dimensions from single images/sentences to streams, which the authors convincingly argue is more aligned with real-world user behavior (e.g., capturing experiences through multiple photos).

The paper is well-supported by extensive experiments on two large-scale blog datasets (NYC and Disneyland, totaling 20K blog posts with 140K images). The authors demonstrate superior performance over strong baselines (including Karpathy et al., Kiros et al., and Vinyals et al.) using both quantitative metrics (BLEU, CIDEr, METEOR, top-K recall) and user studies via Amazon Mechanical Turk. The ablation studies clearly show the value of the coherence model, with CRCN outperforming RCN (without coherence) particularly in retrieval metrics, and the user studies confirming that coherence becomes increasingly important as sequence length grows.

The paper demonstrates strong field knowledge, correctly positioning itself against related work in image captioning and video description, while clearly articulating the novelty of handling sequence-to-sequence generation. The use of blog posts as training data is practical and well-justified, leveraging natural text-image parallel data. The method is clearly described, with sufficient detail for reproducibility.

The main limitation is that the paper doesn't thoroughly explore the limitations of the blog-based data, particularly regarding potential biases in blog content or the generalizability to domains outside tourism. While the authors acknowledge that their approach is "completely unsupervised and thus applicable to any domain," they don't provide evidence of this across diverse topics.

Overall, this is a strong contribution that addresses a meaningful gap in the literature. The problem is well-motivated, the solution is novel and well-executed, and the evaluation is comprehensive. The paper makes a clear case for the importance of coherence in multi-sentence image description, which is a significant advancement over previous work. I recommend acceptance.
