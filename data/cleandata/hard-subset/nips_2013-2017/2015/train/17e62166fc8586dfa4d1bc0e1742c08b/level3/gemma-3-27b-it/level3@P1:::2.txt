## Review of "Expressing an Image Stream with a Sequence of Natural Sentences"

This paper proposes a novel approach to generating coherent natural language descriptions for sequences of images, motivated by the common scenario of users capturing a series of photos during an event or trip. The authors extend existing image captioning work, which typically focuses on single image-single sentence pairs, to handle image streams and produce multi-sentence descriptions. They introduce the Coherent Recurrent Convolutional Network (CRCN), integrating CNNs for image representation, bidirectional RNNs for language modeling, and a local coherence model. The method is trained on a large corpus of blog posts and demonstrated to outperform several baselines through quantitative metrics (BLEU, Top-K recall) and user studies via Amazon Mechanical Turk.

This work builds upon a rapidly growing body of research in image captioning, stemming from early works like [5, 8, 9, 12, 14, 15, 26, 21, 30].  The extension to image *streams* is a natural and important next step, especially given the prevalence of multi-image data in real-world applications. The authors correctly identify the challenge of maintaining coherence across sentences when describing a sequence of images, and their proposed coherence model addresses this issue.  The use of blog posts as a training corpus is also a clever solution to the scarcity of large, parallel image-sequence-text datasets.  Related work on video captioning (e.g., [23, 32]) is acknowledged, with a clear distinction made highlighting the explicit inclusion of the coherence model as the key novelty.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The problem of generating coherent descriptions for image streams is relatively unexplored and this paper provides a significant contribution in that direction.
* **Strong Empirical Results:**  The authors provide comprehensive experimental results, demonstrating the superiority of their approach over several baselines using both automatic metrics and human evaluation.  The ablation study effectively highlights the importance of the coherence model.
* **Large-Scale Evaluation:**  Training and evaluation on a sizable dataset of 140K images and 20K blog posts strengthens the validity of the findings.  The promise to release code and data will further facilitate research in this area.
* **Well-Motivated Approach:** The problem setup is clearly motivated by a practical use case – describing user-generated photo streams.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Related Work Oversight:**  The claim that [5] lacks a mechanism for coherence is inaccurate.  [5] models an intermediate semantic representation that implicitly captures coherence between video segments and generated sentences. This oversight suggests a potential lack of familiarity with closely related work.
* **Clarity Issues:** The depiction of the CRCN architecture in Figure 2(b) appears inconsistent with the mathematical formulation in Section 3.3 and Eq. (2). The figure suggests fully connected layers operating on the outputs of all sentences in the sequence, whereas Eq. (2) implies shared parameters applied to each sentence individually. This needs clarification.
* **Evaluation Metric:** The reliance on BLEU as a primary evaluation metric is questionable, particularly with a single reference sentence. Metrics like METEOR [16] are generally more reliable for evaluating text generation quality, especially when capturing semantic similarity.
* **Model Design Justification:** The sequential application of two fully connected layers (Wf2, Wf1) in Eq. (2) seems redundant as their composition results in a single linear transformation. An ablation study demonstrating the benefit of this specific configuration would strengthen the design choice.
* **Parameter Sharing:** The rationale behind using the same fully connected layer parameters for the BRNN outputs (ot) and the local coherence model (q) in Eq. (2) is unclear. This parameter sharing could potentially constrain the model's capacity; an explanation of the benefits or an ablation study would be helpful.


**Specific Questions/Requests for Clarification:**

* **Implementation Detail:** Is the paragraph vector [17] used as a fixed embedding or is it fine-tuned during training?
* **Figure 2(b) & Eq. (2) Consistency:**  Please clarify the architecture depicted in Figure 2(b) and ensure consistency with the mathematical formulation in Section 3.3.
* **Ablation Study:** Conduct an ablation study to demonstrate the benefit of the sequential fully connected layers (Wf2, Wf1) in Eq. (2).
* **Parameter Sharing Rationale:** Provide a justification for sharing the fully connected layer parameters between the BRNN and the coherence model.


**Overall Assessment:**

Despite a few weaknesses, this paper presents a significant contribution to the field of image captioning by tackling the challenging problem of generating coherent descriptions for image streams. The proposed CRCN architecture and the extensive experimental validation demonstrate the potential of this approach. While the related work section could be more thorough and certain aspects of the model design require further justification, the strengths of the paper clearly outweigh its weaknesses.  

**Recommendation (Initial):** Borderline Accept. I recommend acceptance contingent upon addressing the identified weaknesses and providing answers to the questions raised. In particular, clarifying the inconsistencies in the architecture description and providing justification for key design choices are crucial.
