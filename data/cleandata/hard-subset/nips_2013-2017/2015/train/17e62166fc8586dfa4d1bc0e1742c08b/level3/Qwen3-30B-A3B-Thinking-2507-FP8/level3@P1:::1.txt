

This paper proposes a novel approach for generating natural language descriptions from image streams (sequences of images) rather than single images, which is a meaningful extension of the image captioning problem. The authors introduce a Coherent Recurrent Convolutional Network (CRCN) that combines convolutional neural networks for image representation, bidirectional recurrent networks for sentence modeling, and an entity-based local coherence model to ensure fluent transitions between sentences. The work is motivated by the observation that users often take multiple photos of memorable events, requiring a description of the entire sequence rather than individual images.

The paper is well-written and clearly structured, making it easy to follow the problem statement, methodology, and evaluation. The authors effectively relate their work to prior image captioning literature while justifying the extension to image streams and sentence sequences. However, the paper fails to cite important related work in multi-sentence generation and video description, particularly Rohrbach et al.'s "Translating Video Content to Natural Language Descriptions" (ICCV 2014), which directly addresses video-to-text generation with multiple sentences. This omission is significant as it represents a closely related body of work that the authors' approach builds upon but does not acknowledge.

The CRCN architecture is technically sound, with the entity-based coherence model being a particularly valuable contribution to ensure fluent transitions between sentences. The integration of the coherence model with the multimodal network is well-justified, and the authors provide appropriate ablation studies demonstrating its importance. The model's use of blog data as a source of text-image parallel training data is innovative, though the dataset description is insufficient and potentially breaches anonymity (the authors reuse blog data from Kim et al. [11] without sufficient detail about the crawling methodology or ethical considerations).

The paper demonstrates strong quantitative results on two datasets (NYC and Disneyland blogs), with the CRCN model outperforming baselines on BLEU, CIDEr, and retrieval metrics. The user studies via AMT further validate the quality of the generated sequences. However, the method for generating sentence sequences is somewhat primitive: the authors retrieve candidate sentences for each image in the stream and concatenate them, which is an ad-hoc approach that doesn't truly generate new sequences but rather recombines existing ones. This limits the novelty of the generation approach compared to more sophisticated sequence generation methods.

A technical concern arises with Equation (2), which appears incorrect: "Wf2Wf1[O|q] = [S|g]" suggests that st depends only on ot, which would imply a lack of connection between different positions in the sequence. This is implausible for a sequence model, and the authors should clarify the network structure to address this apparent error.

Regarding the four evaluation criteria:
- Quality: The paper presents sound methodology with appropriate ablation studies and comprehensive evaluation, though the dataset description is insufficient.
- Clarity: The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with good explanations of the proposed architecture.
- Originality: The extension to image streams and sentence sequences is novel, but the core architecture (CNN + RNN) is not, and the paper fails to adequately distinguish itself from related video captioning work.
- Significance: The problem is meaningful for real-world applications (e.g., describing travel experiences), and the results demonstrate practical utility, though the methodological approach is somewhat incremental.

The paper's main strengths are its clear problem statement, the coherence model's contribution to fluent multi-sentence generation, and the comprehensive evaluation. The main weaknesses are the insufficient citation of related work, the potentially problematic dataset description, the ad-hoc nature of the sentence generation approach, and the technical error in Equation (2).

I recommend this paper as a poster at NIPS. While the problem is significant and the approach shows promise, the paper would benefit from a more thorough discussion of related work (particularly video captioning), clarification of the technical error, and more detailed description of the dataset collection process. The core idea of incorporating coherence for multi-sentence generation is valuable, but the paper doesn't fully establish how it differs from or improves upon existing video captioning work. The method's reliance on retrieval rather than generation also limits its novelty compared to more recent sequence generation approaches. With the suggested revisions, this could be a strong oral presentation, but as currently written, it's best suited for a poster presentation.
