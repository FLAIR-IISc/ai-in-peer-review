## Review of "Expressing an Image Stream with a Sequence of Natural Sentences"

This paper tackles the problem of generating multi-sentence descriptions for a sequence of images, motivated by scenarios like describing a photo stream from a trip. The authors propose a Coherent Recurrent Convolutional Network (CRCN) integrating CNNs, bidirectional RNNs, and a local coherence model to capture relationships between sentences. The core idea of extending single image-single sentence captioning to image streams and sentence sequences is a logical progression in the field. The writing is clear and the problem statement is well-defined, making the paper easy to follow. The proposed model appears technically sound, aiming to address the crucial aspect of discourse coherence often missing in image captioning.

However, the work feels incremental and heavily relies on prior work in video captioning and image captioning without sufficient acknowledgement. While the authors highlight the novelty of extending the *input and output* dimensions, the fundamental building blocks – CNNs for visual feature extraction, RNNs for sequence modeling, and coherence modeling – are all well-established. Critically, significant prior work in video description (e.g., Rohrbach et al., 2014 and subsequent work building on that) and multi-sentence captioning is not cited, diminishing the perceived novelty of their approach. 

Furthermore, Equation (2) appears flawed. It suggests the sentence-level state *st* depends *solely* on the image feature *ot*, implying a lack of full connectivity within the recurrent network and potentially hindering information flow between sentences. The method for generating sentence sequences—retrieving sentences per image and concatenating them—feels primitive. While a reasonable starting point, it lacks sophistication compared to approaches that directly model sentence generation conditioned on the entire image stream. 

The dataset description is also concerning. Reusing blog data from [11] is acceptable, but the lack of details regarding crawling procedures and potential anonymity issues raises ethical considerations. Further clarification on data privacy and collection methods is necessary.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Clear problem statement and well-written paper.
*   Technically sound model architecture attempting to address coherence.
*   Demonstrated performance gains over simple baselines.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   Incremental contribution, closely mirroring existing video captioning work.
*   Significant prior work is missing from the related work section.
*   Potentially incorrect equation (2).
*   Primitive sentence sequence generation method.
*   Insufficient dataset description and potential privacy concerns.

**Recommendation:** Acceptable as a poster paper. The work demonstrates a reasonable engineering effort and presents a functional model, but lacks the originality and thoroughness expected for a full paper at a competitive conference like NIPS. Addressing the cited issues, particularly the related work and equation, could significantly strengthen the contribution.
