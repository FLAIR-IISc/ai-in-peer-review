This paper addresses the novel task of generating a sequence of natural sentences to describe a stream of images. The authors argue that since users often capture events as a series of photos, generating a coherent narrative is a more suitable goal than describing each image in isolation. To this end, they propose a Coherent Recurrent Convolutional Network (CRCN), a multimodal architecture that combines a Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) for image feature extraction, a bidirectional Recurrent Neural Network (BRNN) to model the flow of sentences, and an entity-based local coherence model to ensure smooth, logical transitions between sentences. The model is trained in an end-to-end fashion on a large dataset of blog posts, which naturally provide parallel image streams and text. The authors demonstrate through quantitative metrics (BLEU, R@K, etc.) and a user study that their approach outperforms several state-of-the-art image captioning baselines adapted for this new task.

This work is a natural and interesting extension of the significant body of recent research on generating sentences for single images (e.g., Karpathy et al. 2015, Vinyals et al. 2015). While most prior work focuses on the single image-to-sentence problem, this paper extends both the input and output to sequences. The core novelty lies in integrating an explicit coherence model, drawing from work in computational linguistics (Barzilay & Lapata, 2008), into a deep neural architecture to tackle the challenge of narrative generation.

**Strengths (Arguments for acceptance):**

*   **Originality and Significance:** The paper introduces and formalizes an important and relatively unexplored problem: generating a coherent story from an image stream. This is a logical next step for the image-and-language community with clear real-world applications, such as automatically creating photo album summaries. The approach of mining blog data for training is also a clever and scalable solution to the data acquisition problem.
*   **Technical Quality:** The proposed CRCN model is technically sound and well-motivated. The architecture thoughtfully combines powerful components: a state-of-the-art CNN for visual understanding, a BRNN for sequential context modeling, and a classic entity-grid model for ensuring textual coherence. The quantitative evaluation is thorough, using multiple standard metrics for both language quality and retrieval accuracy.
*   **Strong Quantitative Results:** As shown in Table 1, the proposed method (CRCN) and its variant (RCN) significantly outperform a range of strong baselines, including recent deep learning models for image captioning. The large margins of improvement on both retrieval and language metrics suggest the approach is highly effective for this task.

**Weaknesses (Arguments against acceptance / Areas for improvement):**

*   **Incomplete Literature Review:** The related work section is missing some highly relevant work on generating descriptions for videos (e.g., "Jointly modeling deep video and compositional text..." by Xu et al., AAAI 2015). While the authors correctly note that image streams can have sharp discontinuities unlike video, the task of mapping a visual sequence to a textual sequence is fundamentally similar. A discussion of these related methods would better contextualize the paper's contribution.
*   **Weak Support for Coherence Model Contribution:** The main novelty claim rests on the integration of the local coherence model. However, the empirical evidence for its benefit is not entirely convincing. In the quantitative results (Table 1), the full CRCN model offers only a slight improvement over the RCN variant (which lacks the coherence model) on language metrics, though the improvement is more pronounced on retrieval metrics. More critically, the user study (Table 2) shows that for sequences of up to 5 images, users preferred CRCN over RCN only slightly (52.8% vs 47.2%), a result that is likely not statistically significant. While the authors present a separate study on longer sequences showing a larger gap, this weakens the overall empirical argument.
*   **Lack of Statistical Significance Testing:** The paper presents numerous quantitative comparisons, but it is unclear whether the reported improvements, particularly the modest gains of CRCN over RCN, are statistically significant. Adding significance tests (e.g., bootstrap or permutation tests) would greatly strengthen the claims and allow readers to more confidently assess the impact of the different model components.

**Clarity:**
The paper is clearly written and well-organized. The problem, motivation, and proposed architecture are explained well. Figure 2 is particularly helpful for understanding the model's structure and the data pre-processing pipeline.

In summary, this is a nice and interesting piece of work that tackles a novel and significant problem with a well-designed model. The strong quantitative results are a major plus. However, the evaluation of the paper's core contribution—the coherence model—could be stronger, as the user study results show only a weak advantage. The paper would also benefit from a more comprehensive literature review and the inclusion of statistical significance tests. Despite these weaknesses, the novelty of the problem and the strength of the overall approach make this a valuable contribution.
