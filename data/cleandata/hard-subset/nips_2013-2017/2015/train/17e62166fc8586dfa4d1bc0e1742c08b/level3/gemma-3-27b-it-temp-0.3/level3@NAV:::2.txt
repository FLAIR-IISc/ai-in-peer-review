## Review of "Expressing an Image Stream with a Sequence of Natural Sentences"

This paper tackles the problem of generating coherent sentence sequences to describe a stream of images, extending beyond the typical single image-single sentence paradigm. The authors propose a Coherent Recurrent Convolutional Network (CRCN) integrating CNNs, BRNNs, and a local coherence model, trained on blog data. This is a natural and important progression in image captioning, moving towards more realistic and contextualized descriptions. The work builds upon existing multimodal approaches like [5, 9, 12] but specifically addresses the coherence challenge inherent in sequential descriptions.

The strengths of this paper are considerable. The extensive quantitative, qualitative, and human evaluations convincingly demonstrate the superiority of CRCN over baselines, including an ablation study highlighting the importance of the coherence model. The promise to release code and the dataset is a significant contribution to the community. The problem is well-motivated, and the proposed architecture appears logically sound. 

However, the paper isn’t without weaknesses. The claim that [5] lacks a coherence mechanism is inaccurate; their intermediate semantic representation *does* contribute to coherence. This oversight suggests a lack of thorough engagement with related work. Furthermore, Figure 2b is inconsistent with Section 3.3 and Eq. (2). The diagram depicts fully connected layers connected to *all* sentences, while the equation suggests shared parameters applied per sentence. This needs clarification. 

Regarding model design, the sequential application of two linear functions (W_f2, W_f1) in Eq. (2) seems redundant given their composition is also linear. An ablation study justifying this design choice would strengthen the paper. Similarly, the parameter sharing between the BRNN outputs and the local coherence model (Eq. 2) is not fully explained – why is this beneficial? Finally, clarification is needed on whether the paragraph vector [16] is fine-tuned or kept fixed during training. While the authors now recommend acceptance after rebuttal, addressing these points will significantly improve the paper's clarity and rigor.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novel approach to a challenging and important problem.
*   Strong empirical results across multiple evaluation metrics.
*   Comprehensive ablation studies.
*   Commitment to code and data release.

**Arguments for Rejection (prior to rebuttal):**

*   Inaccurate claim regarding related work ([5]).
*   Inconsistency between figure and equation.
*   Unjustified model design choices.
