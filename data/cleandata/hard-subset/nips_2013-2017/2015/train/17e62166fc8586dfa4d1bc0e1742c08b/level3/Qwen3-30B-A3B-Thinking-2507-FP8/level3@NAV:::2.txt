

This paper proposes a novel approach to generating coherent sentence sequences for image streams, extending the typical single-image-single-sentence task to a more complex multimodal sequence generation problem. The authors introduce the Coherent Recurrent Convolutional Network (CRCN) architecture, which integrates convolutional neural networks for image representation, bidirectional recurrent networks for sentence modeling, and a local coherence model to ensure smooth transitions between sentences. The key insight is leveraging blog posts as training data, where image sequences naturally correspond to coherent text descriptions.

The paper makes several strong contributions. First, it clearly identifies an underexplored problem domain (image streams to sentence sequences) with significant practical relevance for social media and personal storytelling applications. Second, the CRCN architecture is well-motivated and the authors provide extensive evaluation across quantitative metrics (BLEU, top-K recall), qualitative examples, and human evaluations via AMT. The ablation study removing the coherence model demonstrates its necessity for performance improvement, particularly for longer text sequences. The promise to release code and dataset is a significant plus for reproducibility.

However, several issues require clarification. The claim that [5] lacks coherence mechanisms is inaccurate, as [5] models intermediate semantic representations that inherently provide coherence. Figure 2b appears inconsistent with Equation (2), suggesting FC layers are applied per sentence while the equation implies shared parameters. The authors should use METEOR instead of BLEU for evaluation given their single-reference setup. The sequential application of W_f1 and W_f2 in Equation (2) is redundant (as their composition is linear) and requires ablation to justify. The parameter sharing between BRNN outputs and coherence model (q) in Equation (2) also needs justification. Finally, the status of the paragraph vector (fine-tuned or fixed) must be explicitly clarified.

Despite these concerns, the paper presents a valuable contribution to the field with strong empirical results. The authors have addressed these issues in the rebuttal, making the paper suitable for acceptance. The work advances the state-of-the-art in multimodal sequence generation and demonstrates significant practical value for real-world applications involving image streams. The authors' commitment to releasing code and dataset further enhances the paper's impact.
