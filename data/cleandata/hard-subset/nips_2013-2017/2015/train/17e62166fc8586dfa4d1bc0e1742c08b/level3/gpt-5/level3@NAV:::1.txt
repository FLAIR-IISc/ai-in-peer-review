This paper tackles multi-sentence description of image streams by proposing CRCN, a multimodal architecture that combines CNN image features, a BRNN over sentence embeddings, and an entity-grid coherence model to score and retrieve sequences of sentences. The system uses blog posts as weakly aligned image–text streams, summarizes each segment to sentences, and at test time retrieves per-image candidate sentences and ranks concatenations with the learned compatibility score. The topic is closely related to prior work on video-to-text and paragraph-level description, including deep visual-semantic alignment for images (e.g., Karpathy & Fei-Fei; Kiros et al.; Vinyals et al.) and multi-sentence video description (e.g., Rohrbach et al., ICCV 2013/2014; LRCN, Donahue et al.). The paper’s key novelty is the explicit discourse coherence term via entity grids integrated with a BRNN.

Quality: The CRCN formulation is generally sound and the integration of an entity-based coherence signal is interesting and useful. However, Eq. (2) appears problematic: as written, Wf2Wf1[O|q]=[S|g] suggests each st depends only on its corresponding ot (plus a separate mapping of q to g), with no cross-sentence coupling at this stage. If the intent was to let each st depend on all {ot} and the coherence vector q, the notation and architecture need clarification; otherwise the “fully connected” claim across time is misleading. The decoding strategy (K-NN retrieval per image, then concatenate with beam/divide-and-conquer) is reasonable but primitive and ad hoc relative to structured sequence decoding.

Clarity: The manuscript is well written and easy to follow. The problem setup and high-level system design are clear. Important implementation details (e.g., crawling, anonymization, and data licensing) are insufficiently documented.

Originality: The problem is significant but its framing and many components mirror prior captioning and video description work. Claims of being “first” to generate multi-sentence descriptions for visual streams should be tempered and key prior work on multi-sentence/video descriptions (e.g., Rohrbach et al., 2013/2014; related paragraph-level models) must be cited and contrasted.

Significance: Results show consistent gains (BLEU/CIDEr/METEOR, R@K, AMT), and the coherence component appears beneficial, especially for longer sequences. Generalization is evaluated on two travel domains; broader validation would strengthen impact.

Pros for acceptance:
- Clear problem, solid model, and coherent integration of discourse features
- Empirical improvements with both automatic metrics and human judgments
- Well written and reproducible model components (modulo data issues)

Cons for acceptance:
- Missing key citations and overstated novelty
- Questionable Eq. (2) and limited cross-time interaction in the final layers
- Primitive decoding and limited dataset description; potential anonymity/confidentiality issues

Recommendation: Accept as a poster, contingent on fixing Eq. (2), adding missing related work, clarifying/cleaning the dataset protocol, and discussing more principled decoding.
