This paper addresses the task of generating a coherent sequence of natural language sentences to describe a stream of images. This is presented as an extension of the popular single image-to-sentence task. The authors propose a novel multimodal architecture, the Coherent Recurrent Convolutional Network (CRCN), which integrates a CNN for image features, a bidirectional RNN to model the flow of sentence content, and an entity-based local coherence model to ensure smooth transitions between sentences. The model is trained on a large-scale dataset of blog posts, treating the images and associated text as parallel data. The proposed method works in a retrieval-based fashion: for a query image stream, it first retrieves candidate sentences for each image and then uses the learned CRCN model to score and rank possible sentence sequences, selecting the one that is most compatible and coherent. The approach is evaluated against several state-of-the-art single-sentence generation baselines on two blog post datasets, demonstrating superior performance through quantitative metrics (BLEU, R@K) and human studies on Amazon Mechanical Turk.

### Strengths

1.  **Significance and Problem Formulation:** The paper tackles a significant and logical next step in the image description field, moving from single images to image sequences and from single sentences to coherent paragraphs. This is a challenging problem with clear practical applications, such as summarizing photo albums or creating narratives from visual data.

2.  **Clarity and Presentation:** The paper is well-written, clearly structured, and easy to follow. The motivation for the problem is well-articulated, and the proposed architecture and experimental setup are explained in a comprehensible manner.

3.  **Novelty of the Model:** The core contribution, the CRCN model, is technically sound. The integration of an explicit entity-based coherence model [1] to capture discourse-level relationships is a key strength and a novel idea in this context. While CNNs and RNNs are standard tools, their combination with a model that explicitly scores sentence-to-sentence transitions based on grammatical roles is a valuable contribution that directly addresses the goal of generating coherent multi-sentence descriptions.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Originality and Related Work:** The paper's claim to be "the first to address the problem of expressing image streams with sentence sequences" is too strong and overlooks a significant body of related work. The problem is closely related to video captioning or description, where a sequence of visual frames is described by one or more sentences. Several key papers in this area are not cited or discussed, such as Rohrbach et al. ("Coherent Multi-Sentence Video Description with Variable Level of Detail," 2014) and other related work on multi-sentence generation from visual data. While image streams from blogs may have different properties than video frames (e.g., larger temporal gaps), the conceptual problem is very similar, and the lack of comparison or discussion with this line of research is a major omission.

2.  **Technical Clarity:** There appears to be an issue in Equation (2) that requires clarification. The equation `Wf2Wf1[O|q] = [S|g]` suggests that `st`, the t-th column of `S`, depends only on `ot`, the t-th column of `O`. This seems to imply that the image compatibility score for the t-th image is independent of the overall coherence `q` and the context from other sentences, which contradicts the goal of an integrated model. This may be a notational error, but as written, it describes a model that is not fully connected in the way one would expect.

3.  **Methodological Limitations:** The method for generating the final sentence sequence is somewhat ad-hoc. It relies on retrieving candidate sentences for each image independently and then scoring their concatenations. This is a primitive generation strategy compared to end-to-end generative models. The combinatorial explosion of candidates is handled by an approximate divide-and-conquer search, which is a practical heuristic but highlights the limitations of the retrieval-based framework.

4.  **Dataset and Reproducibility:** The description of the dataset is insufficient. The paper mentions reusing a dataset from [11], which may inadvertently hint at the authors' identity, potentially violating the double-blind review policy. Furthermore, details on the new NYC dataset collection are sparse, which hinders reproducibility. Providing more information on the crawling process, filtering criteria, and preprocessing steps would strengthen the paper.

### Arguments For/Against Acceptance:

**Arguments for:**
*   Addresses an important and challenging problem that extends existing image captioning work.
*   The proposed CRCN model introduces a novel and sensible combination of components, particularly the explicit coherence model for improving discourse quality.
*   The paper is clearly written and the experimental results, including human studies, demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed approach over reasonable baselines.

**Arguments against:**
*   Fails to cite and position the work with respect to highly relevant prior research in video and multi-sentence description.
*   Contains a potentially incorrect or misleading equation (Eq. 2) that needs clarification.
*   The overall sentence generation pipeline is a retrieval-and-ranking approach, which is less sophisticated than end-to-end generation.
*   Insufficient details on the dataset may raise concerns about reproducibility and adherence to the double-blind review process.

### Conclusion and Recommendation

This paper presents an interesting approach to a significant problem. The strengths lie in its clear presentation and the novel integration of an entity-based coherence model. However, the weaknesses, particularly the failure to acknowledge and discuss closely related work in video description, are significant. I would recommend this paper for acceptance as a poster, contingent on the authors thoroughly addressing the concerns about related work and clarifying the technical details of their model (specifically Equation 2) in the final version. A more nuanced discussion of the problem's relation to video captioning and the limitations of the retrieval-based approach would substantially improve the paper's contribution to the field.
