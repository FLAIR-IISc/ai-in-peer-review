This paper proposes a novel approach to generating sequences of natural language sentences describing image streams, extending the traditional single image-to-single sentence mapping. The authors introduce a Coherent Recurrent Convolutional Network (CRCN) which combines CNNs for image representation, BRNNs for language modeling, and a local coherence model to ensure fluent transitions between sentences. The method is trained on a large dataset constructed from blog posts and evaluated on NYC and Disneyland image streams using both quantitative metrics (BLEU, top-K recall) and user studies via Amazon Mechanical Turk.

The problem addressed is a natural extension of existing image captioning work and relevant to real-world scenarios where users capture series of images representing a cohesive experience. The paper is well-written and clearly articulates the motivation, proposed solution, and experimental setup. The CRCN architecture is technically sound and the integration of a coherence model to address the challenges of multi-sentence generation is a key strength. The reported results demonstrate that the proposed approach outperforms several baselines, and the user studies provide further evidence of its effectiveness.

However, the paper lacks a comprehensive review of related work. While the authors mention several papers in image captioning, they fail to adequately acknowledge closely related research in video description and multi-sentence generation. For example, the work of Rohrbach et al. (2014) on translating video content to natural language descriptions and subsequent work building upon this foundation (e.g., Xu et al., 2017) are highly relevant and should be discussed. Similarly, papers addressing coherence and discourse modeling in text generation (beyond the single citation of Barzilay & Lapata, 2008) are missing. This omission weakens the argument for the novelty of the proposed approach. 

A minor technical concern arises in Equation (2). The equation suggests that the sentence-level representation *st* depends solely on the output *ot* of the BRNN, implying a less-than-fully connected network. This seems counterintuitive and warrants clarification – is the CNN feature vector *vlt* directly involved in computing *st*, or is the connection more complex?

The method for generating sentence sequences, while reasonable, appears somewhat primitive. Retrieving a candidate sentence for each image independently and then simply concatenating them lacks a global optimization strategy to ensure the overall coherence and narrative flow of the generated sequence. More sophisticated decoding methods, such as beam search with a coherence penalty, might yield improved results.

The dataset description is also lacking in detail. While the authors mention crawling blog posts, specifics about the crawling process (e.g., keywords used, filtering criteria) are not provided, hindering reproducibility. Furthermore, reusing data from Kim et al. (2011) raises potential concerns regarding data privacy and ethical considerations. The authors should clarify whether appropriate anonymization techniques were employed and whether they obtained consent from the original content creators.

**Strengths:**

*   Clear problem statement and motivation.
*   Technically sound CRCN architecture with a novel coherence model.
*   Strong empirical results on both quantitative metrics and user studies.
*   Well-written and easy to follow.

**Weaknesses:**

*   Insufficiently comprehensive related work, particularly in video description and multi-sentence generation.
*   Minor technical concern regarding Equation (2).
*   Primitive sentence sequence generation method.
*   Incomplete dataset description and potential ethical concerns.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The paper addresses a relevant and challenging problem in multimodal learning.
*   The proposed CRCN architecture demonstrates a promising approach to generating coherent multi-sentence descriptions.
*   The empirical results are convincing and support the claims made by the authors.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The lack of a thorough literature review undermines the novelty argument.
*   The dataset description and potential ethical concerns require further attention.
*   The relatively simple sentence sequence generation method limits the potential of the proposed approach.

**Overall Assessment:**

Despite some weaknesses, this paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of multimodal learning. The proposed CRCN architecture and the integration of a coherence model are significant advancements. Given the strengths of the paper, I recommend accepting it as a **poster paper**. The authors should address the concerns regarding related work and dataset description in a revised version. Addressing the minor technical point regarding Equation (2) would also strengthen the paper.
