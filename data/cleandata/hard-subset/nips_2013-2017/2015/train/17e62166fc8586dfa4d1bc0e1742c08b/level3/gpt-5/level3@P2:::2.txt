Summary and relation to prior work
This paper tackles the problem of producing a coherent sequence of natural sentences for an image stream, motivated by real-world photo streams in blogs. The approach retrieves candidate sentences from training blog posts and selects a coherent sequence conditioned on the test image sequence. The proposed coherent recurrent convolutional network (CRCN) integrates: (i) CNN image features (VGG fc7), (ii) a bidirectional RNN over sentence representations (paragraph vectors) to model content flow, and (iii) an entity-grid local coherence model following Barzilay and Lapata to encourage fluent sentence-to-sentence transitions. Scoring is defined as an ordered, paired compatibility between each image feature and both the BRNN-derived sentence representation and the coherence vector; training uses a max-margin ranking loss with contrastive negatives. At test time, K-nearest images per position (K=5) provide sentence candidates; an approximate divide-and-conquer beam search assembles full sequences. Experiments on two blog datasets (NYC, Disneyland; ~20k posts, ~140k images) show improvements over strong single-image captioning/retrieval baselines (Kiros et al., Karpathy et al., Vinyals et al.), as well as ablations without coherence, using BLEU/CIDEr/METEOR, retrieval metrics, and AMT user studies.

Compared with prior NIPS and related work on image captioning and video-to-text (e.g., m-RNN, LRCN, Show and Tell), this paper extends from single images and single sentences to image streams and sentence sequences, and is distinctive in explicitly integrating a local coherence model into a multimodal framework and using blog-derived text–image streams as training data. The direction is related to sequence retrieval from paragraphs (Kim et al., CVPR’14) but inverts the mapping.

Strengths
- Problem and setting are timely and relevant; moving from single captions to coherent multi-sentence descriptions addresses an important gap between single-image captioning and story-like descriptions.
- Solid architecture design combining BRNN for content flow and an entity-grid coherence model; end-to-end training with a structured ranking loss is appropriate for retrieval.
- Comprehensive evaluation: multiple automatic metrics and retrieval metrics, ablations (1NN, no-coherence RCN vs CRCN), and AMT user studies. The longer-sequence AMT results particularly support the value of the coherence component.
- Uses large, weakly supervised blog data and outlines practical preprocessing (segmentation via proximity heuristics, LSA summarization), which is valuable for scalability.
- Claimed release of code and dataset will enhance impact and reproducibility.

Weaknesses and questions
- Related-work claim: The statement that LRCN [5] has “no mechanism for coherence” is too strong. While [5] does not include an explicit entity-level coherence model, its recurrent decoder does model temporal linguistic dependencies that contribute to coherence at the sentence level. Please revise to “no explicit discourse-level coherence modeling.”
- Figure/model mismatch: Figure 2b seems to suggest fully connected layers aggregating all sentence positions jointly, while Eq. (2) applies shared FC mappings to each BRNN output o_t and a separate mapping to the coherence vector q, then concatenates. Please clarify the exact connectivity and whether any cross-sentence interactions occur beyond the BRNN and the entity-grid summarization.
- Two-layer linear mapping: Eq. (2) stacks Wf2 and Wf1 with ReLU+dropout between them. Although dropout means the training-time mapping isn’t equivalent to a single linear layer, at test time the composition is linear. Please provide an ablation comparing a single FC layer (with comparable dropout) versus the two-layer stack, and discuss the gain attributable to depth vs regularization.
- Parameter sharing: Why do O (per-sentence BRNN outputs) and q (global coherence vector) share the same FC parameters? Intuitively, sentence-local content and global coherence might benefit from different mappings. Please justify with an ablation or provide intuition about shared vs separate projections.
- Representation learning: Are paragraph vectors (doc2vec) trained only on the blog corpus? Are they fine-tuned during CRCN training or kept fixed? Please clarify and, if not fine-tuned, comment on whether joint fine-tuning helps.
- Automatic metrics: With a single (or few) references per test stream and retrieval-based outputs, BLEU can be misleading; METEOR and CIDEr tend to correlate better with human judgments in such settings. The paper already reports METEOR/CIDEr; please de-emphasize BLEU in the discussion and consider reporting SPICE or human judgments as primary evidence.
- Minor: Given the BRNN is a simple ReLU-RNN, did you try LSTM/GRU units for the sentence-sequence model, especially for longer streams where vanishing gradients are a concern? A small comparison would strengthen the design choice.

Quality
Overall technically sound, with appropriate training objective and ablations. The coherence modeling and its contribution are well supported (notably in AMT for longer sequences). Some architectural choices (shared FC parameters; two FC layers justification) warrant clarification and minor ablations, but do not undermine the main claims. Preprocessing (segmentation/summarization) introduces noise; a brief intrinsic check or sensitivity analysis would further strengthen the evaluation.

Clarity
Generally clear and well organized. The noted inconsistency between Fig. 2b and Eq. (2) should be fixed. Please also detail whether paragraph vectors are fine-tuned, and specify training details for doc2vec (window size, negatives, epochs). A high-level algorithm box for the test-time divide-and-conquer beam search would aid reproducibility.

Originality
The move to sequence-level, retrieval-based description with explicit discourse coherence is novel relative to prior single-image captioning and video captioning work. The integration of entity-grid coherence into a multimodal retrieval model is a meaningful contribution.

Significance
Likely to influence subsequent work on visual storytelling, paragraph generation, and retrieval-based captioning. The datasets and code (if released) will be valuable resources. The empirical gains and human preference results suggest practical utility.

Pros for acceptance
- Addresses an important, underexplored problem (image streams to coherent multi-sentence descriptions).
- Clear algorithmic contribution (CRCN) with explicit discourse coherence.
- Strong empirical results, including user studies and ablations.
- Scalable use of weakly supervised blog data; promised release.

Cons against acceptance
- Some overstatements in related work; need to refine positioning.
- A few architectural choices need clarification/ablation (FC stack, parameter sharing, fine-tuning of paragraph vectors).
- Dependence on heuristic preprocessing (segmentation/summarization) not extensively analyzed.

Requested clarifications for the camera-ready
1) Revise the comparison to LRCN [5] to accurately reflect its capabilities. 2) Clarify the connectivity in Fig. 2b vs Eq. (2). 3) Provide an ablation on single vs two FC layers and on shared vs separate mappings for q and o_t. 4) State whether paragraph vectors are fine-tuned; if not, please clarify and consider a brief sensitivity result. 5) De-emphasize BLEU in the narrative.

Overall recommendation
Accept. The paper presents a timely and well-executed contribution with clear novelty and solid evidence, and the authors’ promised clarifications and releases will further improve its impact.
