This paper addresses the task of generating a coherent sequence of natural language sentences to describe a stream of images. This is a logical and important extension of the widely studied single-image-to-single-sentence captioning problem. The proposed method first retrieves a set of K candidate sentences for each image in the query stream from nearest neighbors in a training set of blog posts. It then uses a novel Coherent Recurrent Convolutional Network (CRCN) to score and rank these candidate sentence sequences. The CRCN architecture models the sentence sequence with a bidirectional LSTM and combines this with an entity-based local coherence model to select the final output that is both relevant to the images and internally coherent.

**Strengths:**
1.  **Novelty and Significance:** The paper tackles a novel and challenging problem: moving from single image captioning to describing image streams with coherent paragraphs. This is a significant step towards more practical and human-like visual storytelling. The proposed CRCN architecture, which explicitly integrates a language model (BRNN) with a classic NLP coherence model, is a well-motivated and novel contribution to this problem.
2.  **Thorough Evaluation:** The authors provide a comprehensive evaluation of their method. This includes extensive quantitative experiments using multiple automatic metrics (BLEU, CIDEr, METEOR, and recall metrics), qualitative examples that clearly illustrate the model's capabilities, and a human evaluation via Amazon Mechanical Turk. The inclusion of an ablation study (CRCN vs. RCN) effectively demonstrates the value of the explicit coherence model, particularly for longer sequences.
3.  **Reproducibility:** The authors promise to release their source code and the newly collected dataset of blog posts. This is a commendable practice that will be of great value to the community, encouraging further research on this important problem.

**Weaknesses and Questions for the Authors:**
The paper is generally well-written and presents a solid contribution. However, there are several points that require clarification or revision.

1.  **Related Work:** The claim in the introduction that Donahue et al. [5] has "no mechanism for coherence" seems inaccurate. While their model does not have an *explicit* sentence-level coherence component like the one proposed here, their recurrent architecture models an intermediate semantic representation (or topic) across video frames, which implicitly enforces a form of coherence in the generated sentence. This distinction should be clarified.
2.  **Model Clarity (Figure 2b vs. Eq. 2):** There appears to be a discrepancy between the architecture diagram in Figure 2b and the formulation in Equation (2). The diagram implies that the outputs of the FC layers (`s_t` and `g`) are derived from a combination of *all* BRNN outputs (`o_1`...`o_N`) and the coherence vector `q`. However, Equation (2) suggests that the parameters `W_f2` and `W_f1` are applied independently to each `o_t` to produce a corresponding `s_t`, with `g` being the only output dependent on `q`. Please clarify which interpretation is correct and update the figure or equation accordingly. The latter interpretation (shared parameters applied per-sentence) seems more plausible.
3.  **Model Design (Sequential Linear Layers):** In Equation (2), two linear transformations (`W_f2` and `W_f1`) are applied sequentially. Since the composition of two linear functions is itself a single linear function, the utility of this two-layer structure is not immediately obvious, especially as the only non-linearity (dropout) is applied during training. Could the authors provide justification for this design choice? An ablation study comparing this two-layer design to a single, wider fully-connected layer would be very informative.
4.  **Model Design (Parameter Sharing):** Following on from the clarity issue in point 2, Equation (2) indicates that the same FC layer parameters (`W_f2`, `W_f1`) are used to transform both the per-sentence BRNN outputs (`o_t`) and the global coherence vector (`q`). This seems unusual, as these two vectors represent different types of information (local sentence context vs. global paragraph structure). What is the motivation for sharing parameters here? It would be interesting to see if using separate transformations for the BRNN outputs and the coherence vector affects performance.
5.  **Evaluation Metric:** While the use of multiple metrics is good, BLEU is known to perform poorly on sentence-level evaluation, especially with a single ground-truth reference, a problem that is exacerbated at the paragraph level. I would suggest emphasizing the METEOR score in the results, as it is designed to better handle synonymy and paraphrasing and generally shows a stronger correlation with human judgments.
6.  **Implementation Detail:** It is unclear whether the paragraph vector representations [17] used as input to the BRNN are kept fixed after being pre-trained or if they are fine-tuned during the end-to-end training of the CRCN model. Please clarify this important implementation detail.

**Summary:**
This paper presents a novel and well-executed approach to the important problem of describing image streams. The strengths—a novel architecture, a thorough evaluation, and a commitment to releasing code/data—are significant. The weaknesses are primarily related to clarity in the model description and minor issues in the positioning against related work. These seem addressable in a revision.
