Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper tackles the problem of describing an image stream with a coherent sequence of natural sentences by retrieving and composing sentences from a large corpus of blog posts. Unlike most captioning work that maps a single image to a single sentence, the authors model both image and text as sequences. The proposed coherent recurrent convolutional network (CRCN) has three components: (i) a CNN (VGGNet fc7) to represent each image, (ii) a bidirectional RNN over sentence embeddings (doc2vec paragraph vectors) to model the content flow across sentences, and (iii) an entity-grid based local coherence model [1] to encourage coherent sentence-to-sentence transitions. The BRNN outputs and coherence vector are projected via two fully connected layers and used to produce per-sentence compatibility vectors s_t and a global coherence vector g. A max-margin objective (inspired by [9]) trains the model to score aligned image–sentence sequences higher than misaligned ones. At test time, K-nearest neighbor image–sentence candidates (K=5) are retrieved per image; a divide-and-conquer beam search assembles and ranks candidate sequences.

This extends retrieval-based image captioning [6, 8, 9, 12, 21, 26] to the sequence setting and integrates an explicit coherence model, which is especially pertinent for image streams where adjacent images can vary sharply. It differs from video description (e.g., LRCN [5]) by generating multiple sentences for disjoint images and explicitly modeling discourse coherence. It also complements prior image–paragraph retrieval [11] by addressing the reverse mapping and by unifying CNN, BRNN, and an entity-based coherence signal.

Strengths:
- Problem novelty and practical motivation: moving from single image–sentence to image stream–paragraph generation, with an explicit, interpretable local coherence component.
- Methodological integration: CNN for vision, BRNN for sentence-flow modeling, and entity-grid coherence [1] combined in a trainable scoring function.
- Data and evaluation: large-scale blog-derived parallel data (NYC and Disneyland; ~20k posts, ~140k images); comprehensive evaluation with automatic metrics (BLEU, METEOR, CIDEr, R@K, median rank) and AMT user studies.
- Empirical results: consistent gains over strong baselines ([12], [9], [30], and a global-matching baseline), and ablations showing added value of coherence, especially for longer sequences. Clear qualitative examples.
- Reproducibility: authors state they will release code and data (valuable for this setting).

Weaknesses and points needing clarification:
- Related work claim: The statement that [5] has “no mechanism for coherence” is too strong. While [5] targets single-sentence video description and lacks an explicit entity-based coherence module, its sequence-to-sequence modeling over temporal visual features does capture temporal consistency. Please soften and differentiate by emphasizing multi-sentence discourse coherence versus temporal smoothness in video.
- Architectural clarity: Fig. 2(b) and Sec. 3.3/Eq. (2) could be clearer. The diagram seems to suggest fully connected layers aggregate across all sentences, while Eq. (2) indicates shared projections applied to each sentence embedding and to the coherence vector. Please refine the figure or text to unambiguously indicate the sharing and per-time vs sequence-level pathways.
- Linear layers and dropout: You argue that Wf2Wf1 does not reduce to a single linear map thanks to dropout. A small ablation comparing one vs two FC layers (with/without dropout) would strengthen this design choice.
- Parameter sharing: Using the same FC parameters for BRNN outputs o_t and the coherence vector q is a strong inductive bias. Please justify empirically (e.g., separate-parameters ablation) and conceptually (why should coherence and sentence content live in the same projection space?).
- Scoring function: In Eq. (3), g_k · v_lt is added for every t. This effectively multiplies the coherence–image alignment by sequence length. Consider normalizing or using a true sequence-level visual summary (e.g., mean/attention over v_t) for the coherence term; at minimum, clarify this design and whether length normalization is applied during training/evaluation.
- Sentence representation: Please clarify whether the doc2vec paragraph vectors are fixed or fine-tuned end-to-end with CRCN. If fixed, discuss impact and feasibility of fine-tuning. Also clarify whether the CNN (VGG) is frozen or fine-tuned.
- Evaluation metrics: BLEU with single references is known to be brittle. The inclusion of METEOR and CIDEr is welcome; please foreground these metrics in the main text and consider multi-reference evaluation using the κ augmented summaries where appropriate.
- Preprocessing assumptions: The paragraph-assignment heuristic (distance-based) and LSA summarization may introduce noise. A brief error analysis (e.g., alignment or summarization accuracy, effect on downstream) would be useful. Coreference/parser errors also affect the coherence model; robustness discussion would help.
- Search/scalability: The divide-and-conquer beam search is reasonable; however, with predominantly local dependencies (BRNN, entity transitions), a dynamic-programming formulation could provide exact decoding under a Markov assumption. Please discuss trade-offs.

Quality: Overall technically sound with clear training objective and well-controlled comparisons. The above clarifications would further solidify the contribution.

Clarity: Generally well-written and organized. Improving Figure 2(b)/Eq. (2) consistency and expanding on design choices would help.

Originality: Good. Integrating an entity-based coherence model into a multimodal retrieval framework for image-stream-to-paragraph generation is novel for NIPS 2015.

Significance: Moderate to high. The task is practically relevant, and results indicate measurable gains, especially on longer sequences. Released data/code would increase impact.

Questions for authors:
1) Are doc2vec paragraph vectors and VGG features frozen or fine-tuned? If frozen, did you try fine-tuning? 2) Why share FC parameters between o_t and q? Results for separate projections? 3) Two FC layers vs one: ablation results? 4) How does normalizing the coherence term or using a sequence-level visual summary affect performance? 5) Sensitivity to K (neighbors) and κ (augmented sentences)? 6) Please soften the claim about [5] as noted.

Pros for acceptance:
- Addresses an important, underexplored problem with a coherent, effective architecture.
- Strong empirical performance across automatic and human evaluations.
- Clear evidence that explicit coherence matters for longer sequences.
- Large, practical dataset and commitment to release resources.

Cons against acceptance:
- Some claims about prior work are overstated.
- Architectural details and certain design choices need clearer justification and ablations.
- Heavy reliance on retrieval (limited generative capacity) and noisy preprocessing.

Recommendation:
After rebuttal clarifications, I recommend acceptance. Please incorporate the promised code/data release and explicitly clarify the treatment of paragraph vectors (fine-tuned vs fixed) and the parameter-sharing/FC-layer ablations in the final version.
