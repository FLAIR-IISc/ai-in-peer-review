Summary and relation to prior work
This paper tackles sequence-level description of image streams by retrieving coherent sentences from a large corpus of blog posts. For each query image, the method retrieves K=5 nearest training images and their associated sentences, embeds each candidate sentence using paragraph vectors, models the sentential sequence with a bidirectional RNN, and adds an entity-grid–based local coherence model. The BRNN outputs and the coherence vector are passed through ReLU and two shared fully connected layers to produce sentence-level scores that are combined with CNN features of the images to score image–sentence compatibility for the whole stream. An approximate divide-and-conquer beam search is used to handle the combinatorial space of sequence candidates. The work relates to retrieval-based captioning (e.g., Kiros et al., Karpathy & Fei-Fei), sequence modeling (BRNN/LSTM), and coherence modeling (Barzilay & Lapata). It differs from single-image captioning and from video-to-sentence work by explicitly selecting coherent multi-sentence sequences for image streams and by integrating an entity-based coherence component.

Strengths
- Clear task formulation: retrieve coherent sentence sequences to describe image streams; practical dataset construction from blogs (NYC, Disneyland).
- Novel architecture that combines BRNN content flow with an entity-grid local coherence model for sentence-level transitions.
- Extensive evaluation: quantitative metrics (BLEU, CIDEr, METEOR, recall@K, median rank), qualitative examples, and AMT user studies, plus an ablation (RCN vs CRCN) isolating the coherence contribution; human studies show larger gains for longer streams, aligning with the hypothesis.
- Practical retrieval and search strategy (KNN candidates + beam-style divide-and-conquer).
- Dataset scale and promise to release data/code increase potential impact.

Weaknesses and requests for clarification/correction
- Related work claim: The paper states that [5] (LRCN) has “no mechanism for coherence.” While [5] targets video-to-single-sentence, it does model temporal dynamics via an intermediate semantic representation that encourages temporal consistency. The manuscript should soften/correct this claim and explicitly delineate that the novelty here is multi-sentence selection with explicit sentence-to-sentence coherence modeling, not that prior work lacked any temporal modeling.
- Figure vs. text inconsistency: Figure 2b appears to depict FC layers connected to all sentences jointly, whereas Eq. (2) and the notation Wf2Wf1[O|q] = [S|g] imply shared parameters applied column-wise to each ot and to q. Please revise the figure or clarify explicitly that the same FC stack is applied per sentence vector and to q with shared weights.
- Two linear layers in Eq. (2): The composition of Wf2 and Wf1 is linear. The paper states this does not reduce to a single mapping “thanks to dropout,” but at inference a two-layer linear stack with dropout disabled is equivalent to a single linear map. Please (i) provide an ablation comparing one FC with dropout vs two FCs with dropout, reporting validation metrics and human preference, and (ii) clarify whether any nonlinearity is applied between Wf1 and Wf2 (it appears not).
- Parameter sharing question: Why share the same FC parameters for the BRNN outputs ot and the coherence vector q? These representations are semantically different (contextual sentence embeddings vs global coherence features). Please justify the inductive bias and provide an ablation with separate FC parameters.
- Evaluation metrics: With single references per sentence/stream, BLEU is known to correlate poorly with human judgment; METEOR generally correlates better. You already report METEOR and CIDEr; please de-emphasize BLEU in the main text/tables, prioritize METEOR (and CIDEr), and discuss correlations with AMT outcomes.
- Implementation detail: Are the paragraph vectors (doc2vec) fine-tuned during CRCN training or kept fixed? Please clarify and, if feasible, include a small ablation (frozen vs fine-tuned vs learned sentence encoders) to assess sensitivity.
- Minor: The divide-and-conquer beam search would benefit from reporting search completeness: e.g., oracle upper bound vs found top-1/top-5 to quantify approximation quality.

Quality
- Technically sound overall: the max-margin objective, BRNN modeling, and entity-grid coherence are appropriate choices; training details are reasonable. The main technical questions concern (i) linear-layer stacking rationale, (ii) parameter sharing between content and coherence pathways, and (iii) clarity of the architecture diagram vs Eq. (2). Empirical support is solid, including an ablation showing the value of coherence.

Clarity
- The paper is generally well written and organized. Please fix the Figure 2b/Eq. (2) mismatch and expand the explanation of the FC layers, dropout, and parameter sharing. Add the missing detail about paragraph vector fine-tuning.

Originality
- Addressing image-stream-to-multi-sentence retrieval with an explicit coherence model is novel for NIPS 2015-era literature. The architecture is a thoughtful combination of known components applied to a new task and data source.

Significance
- The problem is relevant and practical; the method outperforms baselines across metrics and in human studies, with larger benefit for longer sequences. The promised release of code and dataset will likely spur follow-on work.

Pros for acceptance
- Novel task/architecture integrating content flow and entity-based coherence.
- Strong quantitative, qualitative, and human evaluation; clear gains over strong baselines.
- Ablation validates coherence component; scalable retrieval framework.
- Public dataset/code commitment increases impact.

Cons against acceptance
- Overstated related-work claim regarding [5]; needs correction.
- Architectural clarity issues (Figure 2b vs Eq. (2)); open questions about linear-layer stacking and parameter sharing.
- Missing clarification on whether paragraph vectors are fine-tuned.

Post-rebuttal decision
After rebuttal and satisfactory responses to (i) correcting the [5] claim, (ii) clarifying Figure 2b/Eq. (2), (iii) justifying or ablating the two-FC design and parameter sharing, and (iv) clarifying paragraph vector fine-tuning (please state explicitly in the camera-ready), I recommend acceptance. Please also prioritize METEOR/CIDEr in the main results, and make good on the promise to release code and the dataset.
