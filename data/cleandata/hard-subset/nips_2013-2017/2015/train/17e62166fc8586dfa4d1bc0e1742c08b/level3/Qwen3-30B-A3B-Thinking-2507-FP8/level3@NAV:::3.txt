

This paper introduces a novel approach for generating coherent sentence sequences from image streams, addressing a significant gap in existing image captioning work that focuses solely on single-image-single-sentence mapping. The proposed Coherent Recurrent Convolutional Network (CRCN) integrates CNNs for image representation, bidirectional RNNs for sentence modeling, and an entity-based local coherence model to ensure fluent transitions between sentences. The authors leverage unstructured blog posts as training data, transforming them into text-image sequence pairs via text segmentation and summarization, and demonstrate strong quantitative results on NYC and Disneyland datasets using BLEU, CIDEr, and retrieval metrics.

The work is well-motivated and technically sound, with a clear architecture that effectively combines multimodal learning with coherence modeling. Quantitative results (Table 1) show consistent superiority over strong baselines (e.g., CNN+RNN, MLBL-F), particularly in retrieval metrics (R@K), validating the value of the coherence component. The problem extension to image streams and sentence sequences represents a meaningful advancement in multimodal generation.

However, several critical gaps limit the paper’s impact. First, the literature review omits key related work, notably Xu et al.’s "Jointly Modeling Deep Video and Compositional Text" (AAAI 2016), which directly addresses video-to-sentence generation with coherence modeling—a closer parallel than the cited video works. Second, the user study results (Table 2) show only a modest advantage of CRCN over RCN (the coherence-free baseline), with no statistical significance testing to confirm whether these differences are meaningful. The weak advantage in user studies (especially for shorter streams) contradicts the quantitative claims, suggesting the coherence model’s contribution may be overstated. Third, while the blog data is cleverly leveraged, the narrow tourism focus limits broader significance claims.

The paper is technically robust and addresses a genuine gap, but the missing literature, unvalidated user study results, and understated coherence benefits require revision. I recommend *acceptance with major revisions* to address these points, particularly adding the Xu et al. reference, conducting significance tests, and clarifying the coherence model’s impact. The core contribution is novel and significant, but its validation needs strengthening.
