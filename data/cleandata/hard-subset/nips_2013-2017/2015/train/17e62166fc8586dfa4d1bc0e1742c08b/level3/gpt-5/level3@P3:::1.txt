Summary
This paper tackles the problem of producing a coherent sequence of sentences for an input image stream. The proposed coherent recurrent convolutional network (CRCN) integrates (i) a CNN (VGG) to represent images, (ii) a bidirectional RNN over sentence representations (doc2vec) to capture intra-paragraph content flow, and (iii) an entity-grid–based local coherence model (Barzilay & Lapata, 2008) to model discourse transitions. Training uses a max-margin ranking objective that scores aligned image–sentence-stream pairs higher than mismatched streams. At test time, the system retrieves K candidate sentences per image and assembles a paragraph via an approximate beam search guided by the CRCN score. Experiments on large blog-derived datasets (NYC, Disneyland) show improvements over single-sentence captioning baselines and are supported by AMT user studies.

Relation to prior work
The task is closely related to video-to-text and multi-sentence description work (e.g., LRCN; Venugopalan et al. S2VT; Rohrbach et al. 2013/2014; Xu et al. 2015). The main novelty here is incorporating an entity-based coherence model within a multimodal ranking framework for image streams. However, several important multi-sentence/video description papers are not discussed in sufficient depth, and some key works appear missing from the related work section (e.g., Rohrbach et al. 2014 ICCV, subsequent video paragraph generation papers).

Strengths
- Clear problem statement and well-written paper; the approach and training procedure are easy to follow.
- CRCN is technically sound and the integration of an entity-based coherence model is well-motivated for multi-sentence output.
- Sensible evaluation using both automatic metrics and AMT user studies; ablations (RCN vs CRCN) show the value of coherence modeling.
- Scalable training with a reasonable ranking loss and approximate decoding.

Weaknesses and detailed comments
Quality
- Equation (2) and connectivity: As written,
  Wf2 Wf1 [O | q] = [S | g]
  implies each s_t is a function of o_t (and shares parameters across t), without any mixing across time indices within these FC layers. If this is intentional, please clarify that temporal interactions are handled exclusively by the BRNN and not by the FC layers. If the intent was a fully connected mapping across the entire sequence, the current formulation does not realize that. A clearer column-wise formulation (e.g., s_t = W2 ReLU(W1 o_t) and g = W2 ReLU(W1 q)) or an explicit temporal mixing (e.g., 1-D conv/attention over O) would resolve the ambiguity.
- Scoring length bias: In Eq. (3) the global coherence term g·v_t is summed over t, effectively scaling the score with sequence length. Unless all candidates have the same length or the score is normalized by N, this introduces a bias in the ranking. Please clarify and/or normalize.
- Generation pipeline is reasonable but primitive: retrieving K nearest images per position and concatenating associated sentences is ad-hoc. There is no explicit mechanism for redundancy avoidance, coverage, or global constraints (e.g., no-duplicate sentences, length control). Stronger structured decoding (e.g., a sentence-level CRF/ILP with coverage/diversity penalties, or a hierarchical decoder with coherence-aware re-ranking) would likely improve results.
- Dataset description is insufficient and raises anonymity/ethics concerns:
  - The paper states the Disneyland set is reused from [11] and that the NYC set was crawled with the same method, but does not disclose crawl queries, filters, or any IRB/ethics/privacy considerations for reusing personal blog content. Reproducibility is limited without URLs, crawl scripts, or a precise description of the de-identification process.
  - The use of manual selection of “travelogue posts” is under-specified (criteria, annotator agreement).
  - The acknowledgments and the reuse statement may compromise double-blind anonymity; please ensure compliance with the conference policy.
- Baselines: Comparing single-image captioners concatenated across time underplays sequence modeling. Consider baselines that include: (i) per-image retrieval + bigram/trigram sentence-level language models for re-ordering/selection; (ii) sequence-level re-ranking with simple redundancy penalties; and (iii) video-to-text systems adapted to image streams.

Clarity
- The paper is clearly written and well-structured. Please clarify:
  - Whether s_t depends only on o_t (as Eq. (2) suggests) and how q interacts with s_t.
  - Details of doc2vec training (“two classes of the blog dataset” is ambiguous).
  - Preprocessing choices: κ for augmentation, LSA parameters, parsing error handling, and how coreference is resolved for the entity grid.

Originality
- The task extension from single image–single sentence to image-stream–multi-sentence is meaningful, but conceptually close to existing video captioning and retrieval+coherence work. The primary novelty is the specific combination of BRNN content flow with an entity-based coherence feature in a joint ranking model.

Significance
- The approach yields improvements and provides a practical recipe for coherent multi-sentence description from weakly aligned web data. However, the retrieval-and-concatenate decoding and limited dataset transparency temper the broader impact. Strengthening decoding and clarifying dataset construction would enhance significance.

Missing/under-emphasized related work
- Multi-sentence/video description: Rohrbach et al. (ICCV 2013/2014), Venugopalan et al. (S2VT, CVPR 2015), video paragraph captioning work, and other multi-sentence image/video description papers should be discussed and contrasted more thoroughly.
- Discourse coherence modeling beyond entity grids (neural coherence models) could be acknowledged, even if post-dating some cited work.

Questions for the authors
1. Do candidates in training/testing always have matched lengths? If not, how is length bias handled in Eq. (3)?
2. Does Eq. (2) intentionally avoid temporal mixing in the FC layers? If not, please correct/clarify.
3. How do you avoid selecting duplicate or near-duplicate sentences in decoding?
4. Will you release the dataset/crawl scripts and provide an ethics statement regarding blog data use?

Pros
- Clear exposition and well-motivated problem.
- Technically sound model; coherent integration of discourse features.
- Solid empirical gains and user study support.
- Simple and scalable training/inference.

Cons
- Equation (2) ambiguity; possible architectural mismatch with claims.
- Decoding is ad-hoc; lacks global constraints and redundancy control.
- Incomplete related work, especially multi-sentence/video captioning.
- Dataset description insufficient; potential anonymity/ethics issues.

Recommendation
Accept as a poster. The paper is well-written, tackles a significant problem, and presents a technically sound model with promising results, but would benefit from clarifying Eq. (2), strengthening the sequence decoding, expanding related work, and improving dataset transparency and anonymity compliance.
