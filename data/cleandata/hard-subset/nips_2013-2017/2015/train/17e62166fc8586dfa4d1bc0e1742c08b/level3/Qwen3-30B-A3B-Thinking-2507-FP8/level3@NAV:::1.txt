

This paper proposes a novel approach for generating coherent sentence sequences from image streams, extending beyond single-image captioning to address the problem of describing sequences of images with natural language. The authors introduce a Coherent Recurrent Convolutional Network (CRCN) that integrates CNNs for image representation, bidirectional RNNs for sentence modeling, and an entity-based coherence model for fluent transitions between sentences. The work leverages blog posts as training data, extracting text-image parallel sequences through text segmentation and summarization.

The paper demonstrates technical soundness in its CRCN architecture and shows competitive results through BLEU scores, top-K recall, and user studies. The clarity of presentation is commendable, with well-organized sections and effective figures explaining the approach. However, the work suffers from significant limitations in originality and evaluation. The core problem closely mirrors prior video captioning research (e.g., Rohrbach et al. 2014), yet these foundational works are not properly cited, weakening the contribution claim. The dataset description is insufficient, reusing blog data from [11] without disclosing crawling details, raising potential anonymity concerns. Equation (2) appears implausible as it suggests st depends only on ot, implying a not fully connected network. The sentence generation method is primitive, using simple K-nearest neighbor retrieval followed by concatenation rather than a more sophisticated sequence generation approach.

Strengths: Well-written, clear problem statement, technically sound CRCN architecture, strong quantitative results. Weaknesses: Insufficient citation of related work, questionable equation, primitive generation method, inadequate dataset description.

Quality: The technical approach is sound but the evaluation lacks depth. Clarity: Excellent. Originality: Limited, as the problem and approach closely parallel prior work. Significance: The extension to multi-sentence generation is modest given the primitive method and lack of substantial innovation.

The paper makes a marginal contribution to the field, primarily offering a slightly improved implementation of existing concepts without addressing key limitations in prior work. While the results are promising, the lack of proper contextualization and methodological shortcomings prevent it from being a strong contribution to the literature. I recommend acceptance as a poster paper due to the clear presentation and reasonable results, but the work does not advance the state-of-the-art significantly enough for a spotlight or oral presentation.
