This paper proposes a Coherent Recurrent Convolutional Network (CRCN) for generating sequences of sentences from image streams, extending the traditional single image-to-single sentence paradigm. The CRCN combines CNNs for image representation, bidirectional RNNs for sentence modeling, and a novel entity-based local coherence model to ensure fluent transitions between sentences. The authors leverage a large dataset of blog posts with associated images for training and demonstrate improvements over several baselines using both quantitative metrics (BLEU, top-K recall) and a user study via Amazon Mechanical Turk.

The problem addressed is a natural and logical extension of image captioning, motivated by real-world scenarios where users capture a series of images representing a coherent experience. The proposed approach is well-motivated and represents a solid contribution to the field. The architecture itself seems appropriately designed to address the challenges of generating coherent multi-sentence descriptions. The use of blog data as a training resource is a clever approach to obtaining text-image parallel data at scale. 

However, the related work section, while covering a reasonable selection of relevant papers, is incomplete. Specifically, the authors fail to cite Xu et al. (2017) “Jointly Modeling Deep Video and Compositional Text to Bridge Vision and Language in a Unified Framework”, which directly addresses the problem of sequence-to-sequence vision-language modeling, and could be a strong baseline or point of comparison, even if dealing with video instead of still images. Other relevant video description works might also be worth investigating.

The quantitative results reported in Table 1 are promising, showing a clear performance advantage of the proposed CRCN over the baseline methods. However, the user study results (Table 2) are less conclusive. While the CRCN performs similarly to the RCN baseline in the initial AMT test, a slight advantage emerges when the image stream length increases. This suggests the coherence model *is* providing benefit, but the effect size is modest. 

Critically, the paper lacks a statistical significance test to determine whether the observed improvements in both the quantitative metrics and the user study are statistically significant. Reporting p-values would substantially strengthen the claims made in the paper. Without such analysis, it is difficult to confidently assert that the CRCN consistently outperforms the baselines. Furthermore, details regarding the AMT setup – such as the number of workers per task, qualification requirements, and attention check mechanisms – would increase confidence in the reliability of the results.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** Addresses a novel and important problem – generating sentence sequences from image streams.
*   **Sound Architecture:** The CRCN architecture is well-designed and integrates existing techniques in a sensible manner.
*   **Large Dataset:** Leveraging blog posts provides a large-scale dataset for training.
*   **Strong Quantitative Results:** The method outperforms several baselines in quantitative evaluation.
*   **User Study:** User studies provide valuable insight, although the results are nuanced.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Incomplete Related Work:** Missing relevant publications, such as Xu et al. (2017).
*   **Modest User Study Advantage:** The improvement over the RCN baseline in the user study is relatively small.
*   **Lack of Statistical Significance:** Absence of statistical significance testing for both quantitative and qualitative results.
*   **AMT Detail:** Insufficient detail regarding the experimental setup of the AMT study.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The paper tackles a challenging and under-explored problem in vision-language understanding.
*   The proposed approach is technically sound and demonstrates promising results.
*   The use of a large-scale dataset is commendable.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The user study results are not overwhelmingly convincing.
*   The lack of statistical significance testing weakens the conclusions.
*   The related work section is incomplete.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a good paper with a clear contribution. However, the lack of statistical significance testing and the incomplete related work section prevent it from being truly excellent. I recommend acceptance contingent upon the authors addressing these issues: performing statistical significance tests, expanding the related work, and adding more details about the AMT setup.
