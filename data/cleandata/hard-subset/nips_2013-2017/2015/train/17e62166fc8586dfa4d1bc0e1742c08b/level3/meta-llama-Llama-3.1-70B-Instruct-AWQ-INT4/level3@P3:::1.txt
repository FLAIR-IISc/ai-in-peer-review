This paper proposes a novel approach for retrieving a sequence of natural sentences for an image stream. The problem is significant and related to video-to-text/multi-sentence generation, but it closely mirrors prior video captioning work. While the authors extend both input and output dimensions to a sequence of images and a sequence of sentences, the work builds upon existing research in multimodal neural networks.

The paper is well-written and easy to follow, with a clear problem statement and solution. The proposed CRCN model is technically sound and captures discourse relationships using entity-based coherence. However, Equation (2) appears incorrect or implausible, as it suggests that the output depends only on the previous output, implying a not fully connected network.

The method for generating sentence sequences is reasonable but primitive and ad-hoc, involving retrieving a sentence per image and simple concatenation. The dataset description is insufficient and may breach anonymity/confidentiality, as the authors reuse blog data from [11] and do not disclose crawling details.

The authors evaluate their approach using quantitative measures (BLEU and top-K recall) and user studies via Amazon Mechanical Turk. The results show that the proposed CRCN approach outperforms other state-of-the-art candidate methods.

However, there are some concerns regarding the related work section. Important prior work, such as Rohrbach et al. (2014) and related multi-sentence/video description papers, is not cited. The authors should have discussed these papers and explained how their work differs from or builds upon existing research.

In terms of quality, the paper is technically sound, but the claims could be better supported by theoretical analysis or experimental results. The authors are careful about evaluating both the strengths and weaknesses of their work, but the paper could benefit from a more in-depth discussion of the limitations and potential future directions.

Regarding clarity, the paper is well-written and easy to follow. The problem statement and solution are clear, and the authors provide sufficient information for the expert reader to reproduce the results.

In terms of originality, the problems or approaches are not entirely new, but the paper presents a novel combination of familiar techniques. The work differs from previous contributions in that it integrates a multimodal architecture with an entity-based coherence model. However, the authors could have better explained how their work differs from existing research and how it advances the state of the art.

Regarding significance, the results are important, and other people (practitioners or researchers) are likely to use these ideas or build upon them. The paper addresses a difficult problem in a better way than previous research, and it provides unique data and conclusions on existing data.

Overall, I recommend this paper as a poster paper. While it has some limitations, the work is technically sound, and the authors present a clear problem statement and solution. The paper could benefit from a more in-depth discussion of related work, limitations, and potential future directions.

Arguments for acceptance:

* The paper proposes a novel approach for retrieving a sequence of natural sentences for an image stream.
* The proposed CRCN model is technically sound and captures discourse relationships using entity-based coherence.
* The authors evaluate their approach using quantitative measures and user studies via Amazon Mechanical Turk.
* The results show that the proposed CRCN approach outperforms other state-of-the-art candidate methods.

Arguments against acceptance:

* The paper closely mirrors prior video captioning work and does not significantly advance the state of the art.
* Equation (2) appears incorrect or implausible, implying a not fully connected network.
* The method for generating sentence sequences is primitive and ad-hoc.
* The dataset description is insufficient and may breach anonymity/confidentiality.
* Important prior work is not cited, and the authors do not adequately explain how their work differs from existing research.
