This paper addresses the task of generating a sequence of natural sentences to describe a stream of images. The authors propose a novel architecture, the Coherent Recurrent Convolutional Network (CRCN), which combines a CNN for image features, a bidirectional RNN to model the sequence of sentences, and an entity-based coherence model to ensure smooth discourse transitions. The model is trained on a large dataset of blog posts and evaluated using both automatic metrics and human studies, demonstrating improvements over several single-image captioning baselines adapted for this task.

This work tackles a significant and interesting problem, extending single-image captioning to the multi-image, multi-sentence domain. The paper's main strengths lie in its clarity and its core technical contribution. The problem statement and proposed solution are presented clearly, making the paper easy to follow. The integration of an explicit entity-based coherence model into a neural framework to improve narrative flow is a novel and well-motivated idea. The proposed CRCN architecture is technically sound in its high-level design.

However, the paper has several weaknesses. A major concern is the lack of engagement with highly relevant prior work. The problem is framed as novel, but it is functionally very similar to video description, where a sequence of frames is described with multiple sentences. The paper fails to cite or discuss key works in this area (e.g., Rohrbach et al., ICCV 2013; Venugopalan et al., CVPR 2015), making the claims of novelty overstated.

On the technical side, there are some concerns. The sentence generation process, which retrieves sentences for each image individually before concatenating and re-ranking them, feels somewhat primitive and less sophisticated than end-to-end generation. Furthermore, Equation (2) appears incorrect, as it implies that the output `st` is only a function of `ot`, which contradicts the description of fully connected layers. This requires clarification. Finally, the dataset description is insufficient for reproducibility and potentially breaches anonymity by reusing data and methods from a prior publication [11] without detailing them.

In summary:
**Pros:**
*   Addresses a significant problem.
*   Clearly written and well-organized.
*   Novelty in incorporating an explicit coherence model for discourse.

**Cons:**
*   Major omission of related work on video and multi-sentence description.
*   Generation method is a simple retrieval and concatenation approach.
*   Potential technical error in Equation (2).
*   Insufficient dataset details, hindering reproducibility.

Given the novel idea of the coherence model but the significant weaknesses in positioning and methodological detail, I recommend this paper be accepted as a poster.
