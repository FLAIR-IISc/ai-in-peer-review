This paper proposes a novel approach for generating coherent sentence sequences to describe image streams, motivated by the common scenario of users capturing a series of related images (e.g., during travel). The authors address the under-explored problem of extending image captioning beyond single image-sentence pairs to sequences, introducing a coherent recurrent convolutional network (CRCN) architecture. The CRCN integrates CNNs for image representation, bidirectional RNNs for language modeling, and a local coherence model to ensure fluency. The method leverages a large corpus of blog posts with associated images for training and demonstrates superior performance to several baselines using both quantitative metrics and user studies.

The core idea of extending image captioning to image streams is a natural and valuable step. The problem is well-motivated, and the proposed approach is reasonably well-structured. The extensive experimental evaluation, including quantitative metrics (BLEU, Top-K Recall) and user studies on Amazon Mechanical Turk, strengthens the claims of improved performance. The authors’ commitment to releasing the source code and dataset is also a positive aspect, promoting reproducibility and further research.

However, there are several weaknesses and points for clarification. The claim that [5] lacks a mechanism for coherence is inaccurate. Long-term recurrent convolutional nets [5] explicitly model relations between sequential inputs and outputs via an intermediate semantic representation, which contributes to coherence. This oversight demonstrates a possible gap in the authors’ familiarity with closely related work. 

Figure 2b, depicting the CRCN architecture, appears inconsistent with Section 3.3 and Equation (2). The diagram suggests fully connected layers operating on the outputs of *all* sentences in the sequence, while Equation (2) implies shared parameters are applied to each sentence individually. This discrepancy requires clarification.

The authors rely heavily on BLEU scores for quantitative evaluation. Given the task involves generating *sequences* of sentences, and given a single reference sequence, metrics like METEOR might provide a more informative assessment of fluency and adequacy.

Several design choices raise questions. Applying two linear functions sequentially in Eq. (2) appears redundant, as their composition is a single linear transformation. An ablation study justifying this design choice would be valuable. Furthermore, the decision to use the *same* fully connected layer parameters for the BRNN outputs (o_t) and the local coherence model representation (q) in Eq. (2) is unclear. An explanation for this parameter sharing would enhance understanding.

Finally, the paper would benefit from clarifying whether the paragraph vectors [17] used to represent text are fine-tuned during training or remain fixed. This detail impacts the reproducibility and interpretation of the results.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Addresses a novel and important problem – extending image captioning to image streams.
*   Demonstrates strong empirical results, outperforming several baselines across both automatic metrics and human evaluation.
*   Provides a well-defined architecture (CRCN) integrating CNNs, RNNs, and a coherence model.
*   Authors commit to releasing code and data.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Inaccurate claim regarding related work [5].
*   Inconsistency between Figure 2b and the mathematical formulation in Section 3.3.
*   Over-reliance on BLEU as an evaluation metric.
*   Unjustified design choices (two sequential linear layers, parameter sharing) in the model.
*   Lack of clarity regarding the paragraph vector training procedure.

**Overall Assessment:**

The paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of image captioning by tackling the challenging problem of generating coherent descriptions for image streams. The experimental results are compelling, and the proposed CRCN architecture appears promising. However, the identified weaknesses and points for clarification require attention.

**Recommendation:**

I initially recommend a **weak reject**. The issues raised are significant enough to warrant revision and re-evaluation. Specifically, the authors need to address the inaccuracies in related work, resolve the inconsistencies in the architectural description, justify their design choices, and clarify the implementation details. A rebuttal addressing these concerns, and a revised manuscript incorporating the necessary changes, would significantly strengthen the paper and potentially lead to acceptance.
