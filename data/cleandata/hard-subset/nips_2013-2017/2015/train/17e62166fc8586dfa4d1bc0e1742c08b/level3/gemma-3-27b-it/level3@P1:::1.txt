## Review of "Expressing an Image Stream with a Sequence of Natural Sentences"

This paper addresses the problem of generating a sequence of natural language sentences to describe a stream of images, extending the more common single-image-to-single-sentence task. The authors propose a coherent recurrent convolutional network (CRCN) integrating CNNs, bidirectional RNNs, and an entity-based local coherence model. The method is trained on a large corpus of blog posts and evaluated on NYC and Disneyland image streams with both quantitative metrics and user studies via AMT.

The problem tackled is a logical and significant extension of existing image captioning work. Capturing the relationships *between* images within a stream, and reflecting this understanding in a coherent text, represents a valuable step towards more comprehensive visual understanding. The proposed CRCN architecture appears technically sound and well-motivated, particularly the inclusion of a local coherence model to address the potential for abrupt transitions in generated text. The paper is also generally well written and clearly explains the problem, the proposed solution, and the experimental setup. 

However, while the work is promising, it suffers from a lack of comprehensive engagement with relevant prior research. The introduction mentions the surge of interest in image captioning, but overlooks several key papers in the related field of video description, which directly address the generation of multi-sentence descriptions from sequential visual data. Notably, the work of Rohrbach et al. (2014) and subsequent publications building on this foundation (e.g., Xu et al., 2017) are not cited or discussed. These papers represent highly relevant prior art and offer a basis for comparison beyond the single-image captioning baselines used.  Failing to acknowledge this body of work weakens the paper's claim of novelty.

A specific technical concern arises with Equation (2), which describes the combination of the CNN, RNN, and coherence model outputs. The equation suggests that the image stream representation *st* is solely dependent on the RNN output *ot*, implying a disconnect between the image stream representation and the coherence model. This seems implausible and potentially limits the model’s ability to fully integrate visual and linguistic coherence. Clarification is needed to explain the full information flow within the model and justify this architectural choice.

The method for retrieving sentence sequences, while reasonable, appears somewhat primitive. Retrieving individual candidate sentences for each image and simply concatenating them lacks a holistic approach to generating a cohesive narrative. While the coherence model attempts to mitigate this issue, a more sophisticated strategy for planning and generating the sentence sequence could yield better results.  

Finally, the dataset description is insufficient in its detail.  The authors mention reusing data from Kim et al. (2011) and collecting new data, but lack specific details about the crawling process, inclusion/exclusion criteria, and potential biases. It is crucial to address these aspects, particularly regarding user privacy and data anonymization.  The reuse of blog data raises ethical considerations and requires careful justification to ensure compliance with privacy regulations.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The problem is a significant and logical extension of existing image captioning work.
*   The proposed CRCN architecture is technically sound and incorporates a valuable component (local coherence model) for improving text fluency.
*   The paper demonstrates improvements over baseline single-image captioning methods.
*   The experimental evaluation, including user studies, provides evidence supporting the approach.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Lack of comprehensive engagement with relevant prior work in video description and multi-sentence generation.
*   Potential technical flaw in Equation (2) impacting the model’s information flow.
*   Primitive sentence sequence generation strategy.
*   Insufficient detail in the dataset description and potential ethical concerns regarding data collection.


**Overall Recommendation:**

I recommend this paper as **acceptable as a poster presentation**. While the work presents a valuable contribution to the field, the weaknesses related to prior work, technical details, and dataset description prevent it from being considered for oral presentation. Addressing these points, particularly through more thorough literature review and clarification of the model architecture, would significantly strengthen the paper.
