This paper addresses the task of generating a sequence of natural sentences to describe a stream of images. This is presented as a novel extension of the popular single image-to-sentence generation problem. The authors propose a multimodal architecture, the Coherent Recurrent Convolutional Network (CRCN), to tackle this problem. The CRCN integrates three main components: a Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) to extract visual features from each image, a Bidirectional Recurrent Neural Network (BRNN) to model the content flow and dependencies within the sentence sequence, and an entity-based local coherence model to ensure smooth, natural transitions between sentences. The model is trained end-to-end on a large dataset of blog posts, which serve as a source of parallel image-stream and text-sequence data. The proposed method is evaluated against several state-of-the-art single-sentence generation baselines (adapted for this task) using both automatic metrics (BLEU, CIDEr, METEOR, R@K) and a user study on Amazon Mechanical Turk.

**Strengths:**
1.  **Originality and Significance:** The problem formulation itself is a key strength. Moving from single image-to-sentence generation to a stream-to-sequence setup is a logical and important next step for the field, with clear applications in areas like photo album summarization and storytelling. To my knowledge, this is one of the first papers to formally address this specific task.
2.  **Technical Approach:** The proposed CRCN architecture is technically sound and well-motivated. The combination of a BRNN to capture the semantic flow of the narrative and a separate, explicit local coherence model to handle entity-based transitions is a strong and sensible design choice. This tackles a potential weakness of standard RNNs which might not effectively capture the fine-grained syntactic and referential consistency required for a multi-sentence paragraph.
3.  **Experimental Results:** The quantitative evaluation presented in Table 1 is comprehensive and demonstrates that the proposed CRCN model, as well as its RCN variant, achieves state-of-the-art performance, outperforming all adapted baselines by a significant margin across both language and retrieval metrics. This provides solid evidence that modeling the sequential nature of the input and output is crucial for this task.

**Weaknesses:**
1.  **Related Work:** The literature review, while covering single-image captioning well, could be strengthened by including more relevant work from video description. The authors briefly mention video-sentence work but dismiss it by noting their novelty lies in the explicit coherence model. However, the connection is stronger than acknowledged. For example, works like "Jointly Modeling Deep Video and Compositional Text to Bridge Vision and Language in a Unified Framework" (Xu et al., AAAI 2015) also deal with mapping sequential visual input to descriptive text. A more thorough discussion of the similarities and differences with such video-to-paragraph methods would better contextualize the paper's contribution.
2.  **Evaluation of the Coherence Model:** A significant weakness lies in the experimental validation of the paper's main novelty: the entity-based coherence model.
    *   The results from the Amazon Mechanical Turk user study (Table 2) show only a marginal preference for the full CRCN model over the RCN baseline (which lacks the coherence model). For shorter sequences of 5 images, the win rate is nearly 50/50, indicating users did not have a strong preference. While the authors correctly note that the margin increases for longer sequences, the evidence presented still suggests a relatively weak advantage for the added complexity of the coherence model.
    *   Similarly, in the quantitative results (Table 1), the improvements of CRCN over RCN are quite small for the language similarity metrics (BLEU, CIDEr, METEOR). The primary gains are seen in the retrieval metrics.
3.  **Lack of Statistical Significance Testing:** Related to the point above, the paper makes claims of superiority based on small numerical differences in performance metrics without providing any statistical significance tests. For instance, the modest improvements of CRCN over RCN in Table 1 are difficult to interpret without knowing if they are statistically significant. The inclusion of such tests is standard practice and would be necessary to confidently conclude that the coherence model provides a reliable improvement over the already strong BRNN baseline.

**Summary of Arguments:**

**Arguments for acceptance:**
*   Addresses a novel and significant problem: generating a paragraph-like description for a sequence of images.
*   The proposed CRCN model is a technically sound and well-designed architecture that combines powerful components for vision, language modeling, and text coherence.
*   The model demonstrates strong quantitative performance, significantly outperforming several state-of-the-art baselines on a large, real-world dataset.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
*   The experimental evidence supporting the main contribution—the entity-based coherence model—is not entirely convincing. The user study shows only a weak preference for the full model over a simpler baseline.
*   The paper lacks statistical significance testing, making it difficult to validate whether the reported improvements over the RCN baseline are reliable.
*   The discussion of related work is incomplete, particularly concerning analogous tasks in video description.

Overall, this is an interesting and well-executed piece of work on an important problem. The primary concern is that the justification for the paper's central technical contribution (the coherence model) is not fully supported by the experiments. If the authors can strengthen their evaluation with significance testing and provide a more compelling analysis of the coherence model's impact, this would be a very strong paper.
