Summary and relation to prior work
The paper addresses the task of producing a sequence of natural sentences for an image stream, motivated by photo streams commonly found in travel blogs. The proposed coherent recurrent convolutional network (CRCN) combines (i) CNN image features (VGG), (ii) a bidirectional RNN over sentence representations (paragraph vectors), and (iii) an entity-grid–based local coherence model to influence sentence-to-sentence transitions. Training uses a max-margin objective over aligned image–sentence-sequence pairs constructed from blog posts, where each blog is segmented and summarized into image–sentence pairs. At test time, candidate sentences are retrieved per image via KNN on CNN features and combined with a divide-and-conquer beam search; the CRCN scores candidates by image–text compatibility and a global coherence term. Experiments on two large blog corpora (NYC and Disneyland) report improvements over strong captioning baselines (LBL variants, CNN+RNN, CNN+LSTM) on BLEU/METEOR/CIDEr as well as retrieval metrics and AMT user studies, with ablations indicating the coherence model helps especially for longer sequences.

The problem setup is closely related to prior work on image captioning [Karpathy & Fei-Fei 2015; Vinyals et al. 2015], multimodal ranking/embedding [Kiros et al. 2014], and video description/sequence modeling [Donahue et al. 2015; Rohrbach et al., ICCV 2013]. It also connects to paragraph-level/video-to-paragraph generation and multi-sentence video description; several such works (e.g., Rohrbach et al., 2014 on movie description and subsequent multi-sentence/video paragraph descriptions) should be discussed to more clearly position the contribution beyond single-sentence captioning and general video-to-text.

Strengths
- Clear motivation: moving from single images/sentences to streams/paragraphs is important and timely.
- Technically sound integration of BRNN for contextualized sentence representations with an entity-grid coherence model from NLP; the ablation suggests tangible benefits, especially for longer sequences.
- Leverages large, naturalistic blog data; practical retrieval+reranking pipeline is scalable.
- Experiments include both automatic metrics and human preferences on AMT; comparisons to multiple strong baselines are provided.
- Writing is clear and easy to follow; the overall architecture and training objective are well presented.

Weaknesses and detailed evaluation

Quality
- Equation (2) and the combination of signals raise concerns. As written, S = [s1|…|sN] depends only on O = [o1|…|oN]; g depends only on q. This implies the per-sentence features st are not influenced by the coherence vector q (beyond possible implicit coupling through shared weights), and the coherence signal g is applied uniformly to every image via g·vlt in Eq. (3). This design seems implausible if the goal is to propagate coherence constraints to individual sentence choices. Please clarify whether there is any temporal cross-talk (e.g., concatenating [ot; q] before FC layers, attention, or gating), and whether Eq. (2) is a notational simplification or a mistake.
- The term ∑t g·vlt in Eq. (3) effectively repeats the same global coherence–to–image dot-product across all positions. The semantics of dotting a text-only coherence vector with per-image CNN features merits justification. Why should the entity-transition statistics be linearly comparable to visual features? Some analysis/ablations (e.g., without the g term, or using a learned scalar weight per t) would help.
- The decoding is largely retrieval-based and somewhat ad hoc: per-image KNN sentence pools, then divide-and-conquer beam search. While practical, this restricts outputs to training sentences and limits modeling of long-range constraints across the whole paragraph during search. A structured decoder (e.g., with global factors or explicit coverage/consistency constraints) could strengthen the approach.
- Several experimental details are under-specified (e.g., exact dataset splits per topic, average sequence lengths, confidence intervals for human studies, inter-annotator agreement, and statistical significance). Claims of “unsupervised” learning are overstated; this is weakly supervised from aligned image–sentence sequences derived from blogs.

Clarity
- Generally well written and organized. However, the dataset description is insufficient for reproducibility: crawling criteria, filtering rules, languages, license/permissions, and whether the datasets and the code will be released are not specified. The anonymity of sources and authors may be compromised (naming platforms and a previously released dataset, plus acknowledgments in the submission); please check compliance with the double-blind policy and move acknowledgments to the camera-ready.
- Notation around Eq. (2) should be clarified; dimensions and the role of dropout “preventing reduction to a single linear mapping” need a more precise explanation.

Originality
- Moving to image streams and multi-sentence outputs with an explicit coherence model is a useful extension; the entity-grid incorporation is a nice connection to NLP discourse modeling. That said, the overall retrieval-and-rerank framework mirrors prior captioning and video-to-text pipelines, and the novelty is primarily in combining known components rather than introducing a fundamentally new learning formulation. Related multi-sentence video/paragraph generation works should be more comprehensively covered.

Significance
- The problem is important, and the reported gains and human preferences indicate potential impact. However, without public release of data and code or broader-domain validation, practical uptake may be limited. The reliance on retrieved sentences also constrains generalization beyond the training corpus style/domain.

Questions for the authors
- Please clarify Eq. (2): does st depend on q in any way, and is there temporal mixing beyond independent columns of O?
- Justify the g·vlt term and report an ablation without it. Does learning a time-dependent g_t help?
- Provide more details on dataset construction, anonymization, licenses, and whether you will release data/code.
- How sensitive are results to K, M, and sequence length? Provide statistics and significance tests for AMT.
- Could you report failure cases and analysis of when the coherence model hurts/helps?

Pros
- Well-motivated problem with clear, readable presentation.
- Sound architecture; meaningful use of entity-based coherence.
- Strong empirical results and user studies; ablations support claims.

Cons
- Combination/scoring equations appear questionable; need clarification and ablations.
- Decoding is primitive and restricted to retrieval; limited modeling of global paragraph structure.
- Related multi-sentence/video description work is incompletely covered.
- Dataset description insufficient; potential anonymity issues.

Recommendation
Accept as a poster. The paper tackles a significant problem with a reasonable and effective approach, is clearly written, and presents solid empirical evidence. Addressing the technical concerns around Eq. (2)/(3), expanding related work, and strengthening dataset/reproducibility details would further improve the contribution.
