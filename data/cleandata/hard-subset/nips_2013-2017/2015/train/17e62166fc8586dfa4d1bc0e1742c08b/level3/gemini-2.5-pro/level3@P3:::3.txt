This paper addresses the problem of generating a sequence of natural language sentences to describe a stream of images. This is a novel and interesting extension of the widely studied single image-to-sentence generation task. The authors propose a Coherent Recurrent Convolutional Network (CRCN) that combines three main components: a Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) to extract features from each image, a bidirectional Recurrent Neural Network (BRNN) to model the flow of content across the sentence sequence, and an entity-based local coherence model to ensure smooth, logical transitions between sentences. The model is trained on a large dataset of blog posts using a max-margin objective. At test time, the task is framed as a retrieval problem where the CRCN is used to score and rank candidate sentence sequences generated from the training data.

This work builds upon recent successes in combining CNNs and RNNs for image description. However, it extends this paradigm by considering sequences for both the input (images) and output (sentences). The main novelty lies in explicitly modeling the coherence of the output paragraph, a crucial aspect when moving beyond single-sentence generation, where the generated text needs to be read as a cohesive whole.

**Strengths**

*   **Novelty and Significance:** The paper tackles a timely and important problem. As users often capture events as a sequence of photos, generating a coherent narrative for an entire photo stream is a logical and significant step forward for the field. The problem formulation itself is a valuable contribution.
*   **Technical Approach:** The proposed CRCN architecture is well-motivated and technically sound. The integration of a BRNN for sequential content modeling with an explicit, entity-based coherence model is an elegant way to capture both semantic flow and syntactic/referential consistency in the generated text.
*   **Quantitative Performance:** The experimental results presented in Table 1 are strong. The CRCN method consistently and significantly outperforms several adapted state-of-the-art baselines on both language similarity (BLEU, CIDEr) and retrieval (R@K, Median Rank) metrics. This provides solid evidence for the effectiveness of the proposed sequence-to-sequence modeling approach.
*   **Dataset:** The use of a large-scale, real-world dataset curated from blog posts is well-suited for the task and adds to the practical relevance of the work.

**Weaknesses**

*   **Incomplete Related Work:** The literature review is missing some relevant work on generating descriptions from visual sequences, particularly in the video domain. While the paper acknowledges video-sentence work, it does not discuss or compare itself with contemporary methods that also bridge visual sequences and language. For example, "Jointly Modeling Deep Video and Compositional Text to Bridge Vision and Language in a Unified Framework" (Xu et al., AAAI 2015) is a highly relevant work that should be discussed to better contextualize the contribution.
*   **Weak Justification for the Coherence Model:** A central contribution of the paper is the integration of the entity-based local coherence model. However, the empirical evidence for its benefit is not entirely convincing. In the user study (Table 2), the full CRCN model performs only marginally better than the RCN baseline (which lacks the coherence model), with a preference score of 52% vs. 48% for shorter sequences. While the authors show this gap widens for longer sequences (58% vs. 42%), the advantage is still modest. This suggests that the coherence component, as implemented, provides only a weak benefit, which somewhat undermines its claimed importance.
*   **Lack of Statistical Significance Testing:** The paper reports numerous results, but provides no statistical significance tests. This is a crucial omission, especially when comparing CRCN to its RCN variant. For instance, the improvements in language metrics (Table 1) and the user study preferences are small. Without significance testing (e.g., a paired t-test or bootstrap test), it is difficult to determine if these improvements are reliable and statistically meaningful or simply due to noise in the evaluation. This is a critical point that should be addressed to strengthen the paper's claims.

**Overall Assessment**

This is an interesting and well-executed paper that introduces a novel problem and a sound technical solution. The strengths—particularly the novelty of the task and the strong quantitative results over baselines—make it a valuable contribution. However, the weaknesses, especially the lack of statistical analysis and the underwhelming empirical support for the key coherence model component, temper the enthusiasm. The paper would be significantly improved by a more thorough comparison to related work and, most importantly, a statistical analysis to validate its claims about the benefits of the full CRCN model. Despite these concerns, the paper is a nice piece of work on a promising research direction.
