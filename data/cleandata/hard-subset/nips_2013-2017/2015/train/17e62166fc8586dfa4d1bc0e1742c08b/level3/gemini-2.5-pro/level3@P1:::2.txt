This paper addresses the novel and interesting task of generating a coherent sequence of natural language sentences to describe a stream of images, such as those found in a blog post. The authors propose a multimodal architecture, the Coherent Recurrent Convolutional Network (CRCN), which tackles this problem as a retrieval and ranking task. The proposed method integrates three key components: a Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) to extract deep features from each image, a bidirectional Recurrent Neural Network (BRNN) to model the contextual flow of the sentence sequence, and an entity-based local coherence model to ensure smooth, logical transitions between sentences. The model is trained on a large dataset of blog posts, which serve as a source of parallel image-text streams. For a new image stream, the system first retrieves a set of candidate sentences for each image from the training data and then uses the learned CRCN to score and rank the possible resulting sentence sequences, selecting the one with the highest compatibility and coherence.

This work is original in that it is one of the first to move beyond the single-image, single-sentence paradigm to the more complex and realistic problem of describing image sequences with coherent paragraphs. It builds upon recent advances in multimodal learning that combine CNNs and RNNs (e.g., [5, 9, 30]) but makes a novel contribution by explicitly incorporating a classic NLP coherence model [1] into an end-to-end deep learning architecture to improve the quality of multi-sentence output. The significance of the work is high, as it addresses a practical and challenging problem with applications in automatic content creation, summarization, and human-computer interaction.

**Strengths:**

*   **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The paper's claims are well-supported by an extensive and convincing set of experiments. The authors evaluate their method against several relevant and state-of-the-art baselines using a suite of quantitative metrics (BLEU, METEOR, CIDEr, recall@K, median rank). Crucially, they supplement this with qualitative examples and a well-designed user study on Amazon Mechanical Turk, which confirms that human evaluators significantly prefer the output of their model.
*   **Ablation Study:** The comparison between the full CRCN model and an ablated version without the coherence model (RCN) effectively demonstrates the value of explicitly modeling discourse-level coherence. The results, particularly the AMT study on longer sequences, provide strong evidence for this specific contribution.
*   **Quality and Significance:** The paper tackles an important and difficult problem, and the proposed method demonstrably advances the state of the art. The idea of leveraging large-scale, unstructured blog data is clever and practical.
*   **Reproducibility:** The authors' promise to release their source code and the newly collected NYC dataset is a significant strength that will facilitate reproducibility and encourage further research on this novel task.

**Weaknesses and Questions for Clarification:**

The paper is technically sound and well-written, but there are several points that could be clarified or improved:

1.  **Related Work Positioning:** The claim in the related work section that the model from Donahue et al. [5] has "no mechanism for the coherence between sentences" is an oversimplification. While it does not use an *explicit* entity-grid model, its LSTM architecture is designed to capture temporal dependencies and topic flow across video frames, which provides an implicit form of coherence. The authors should refine this statement to more accurately distinguish their contribution, which lies in the *explicit* integration of a symbolic coherence model.

2.  **Model Architecture Clarity:** There appears to be a potential inconsistency between the model diagram in Figure 2b and the formulation in Equation (2). The diagram seems to show that the outputs from the BRNN for all sentences are concatenated before being passed through the fully connected layers. However, Equation (2) implies that a shared weight matrix `Wf2Wf1` is applied to each per-sentence output vector `o_t` individually. Please clarify the exact architecture to ensure the method is fully reproducible.

3.  **Model Design Justification:** Equation (2) uses two consecutive linear transformations, `Wf2Wf1`. Since the composition of two linear maps is another linear map, this is typically equivalent to a single fully connected layer. The text notes that dropout is applied between the layers, which breaks the strict linearity and can be seen as a form of regularization. Could the authors explicitly state this as the justification for this design choice? An ablation study comparing this two-layer design to a single FC layer (with an equivalent level of dropout) would further strengthen this non-standard architectural choice.

4.  **Parameter Sharing:** In Equation (2), the same fully connected layer parameters are used to project both the per-sentence BRNN outputs (`o_t`) and the global coherence vector (`q`). What is the motivation for this parameter sharing? These vectors capture very different types of information (local, content-specific vs. global, structural), and it is not immediately obvious why they should be processed by the same transformation. A brief justification would be helpful.

5.  **Implementation Details:** For full clarity and reproducibility, please specify whether the 300-dimensional paragraph vectors [17] used to represent sentences are kept fixed after pre-training, or if they are fine-tuned during the end-to-end training of the CRCN model. This is an important detail for anyone attempting to replicate the results.

6.  **Evaluation Metrics:** The authors use a good range of metrics. Given the known limitations of BLEU for sentence-level evaluation (especially with a single reference), it might be beneficial to give more prominence to the METEOR and CIDEr scores in the discussion, as these are generally considered to correlate better with human judgments of quality for this type of task.

In summary, this is a high-quality paper that introduces a novel problem, proposes a well-motivated and effective architecture, and supports its claims with strong, multi-faceted experiments. The identified weaknesses are primarily minor points of clarification that, if addressed, would improve the paper's overall clarity and impact. The work represents a significant step forward in connecting vision and language. I recommend acceptance.
