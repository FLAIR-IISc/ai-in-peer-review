This paper proposes a novel approach for generating a sequence of natural sentences from an image stream, which is a significant extension of the traditional image-to-sentence generation problem. The authors introduce a multimodal architecture called Coherent Recurrent Convolutional Network (CRCN) that integrates convolutional neural networks (CNNs), bidirectional recurrent neural networks (RNNs), and an entity-based local coherence model. The CRCN model is trained on a large dataset of blog posts with images, where each blog post is transformed into a sequence of image-sentence pairs.

The paper is well-written, and the authors provide a clear explanation of their approach, including the architecture of the CRCN model and the training procedure. The experimental results are also well-presented, with a comparison to several state-of-the-art baseline methods. The authors demonstrate that their approach outperforms the baselines in terms of quantitative metrics, such as BLEU and top-K recall, as well as in a user study using Amazon Mechanical Turk.

One of the strengths of the paper is its novelty. The authors address a problem that has not been well-studied in the past, and their approach is innovative and well-motivated. The use of a local coherence model to capture the relationships between sentences in a sequence is particularly interesting, as it allows the model to generate more fluent and coherent text.

However, there are some weaknesses in the paper. One of the main limitations is the lack of a more thorough literature review. While the authors mention some related work, they do not provide a comprehensive overview of the existing approaches to image-to-sentence generation, and they miss some relevant papers, such as "Jointly modeling deep video and compositional text...".

Another limitation is the evaluation methodology. While the authors provide a user study using Amazon Mechanical Turk, the study is limited to a small number of participants, and the results may not be generalizable to a larger population. Additionally, the authors do not provide a statistical significance test to determine whether the reported improvements are reliable.

In terms of originality, the paper is a novel combination of familiar techniques, and the authors make a significant contribution to the field of image-to-sentence generation. However, the paper is not entirely new, as it builds upon existing work in the area.

The significance of the paper is high, as it addresses a challenging problem in computer vision and natural language processing. The results have the potential to be useful in a variety of applications, such as image captioning, video description, and human-computer interaction.

In conclusion, this is a nice and interesting piece of work that makes a significant contribution to the field of image-to-sentence generation. While there are some limitations, the paper is well-written, and the authors provide a clear explanation of their approach. The results are promising, and the paper has the potential to be influential in the field.
