

# Review for "Expressing an Image Stream with a Sequence of Natural Sentences"

This paper proposes an interesting extension to the image captioning problem by addressing the generation of natural language sentence sequences from image streams, rather than single images. The authors develop a coherent recurrent convolutional network (CRCN) that integrates convolutional neural networks for image representation, bidirectional recurrent neural networks for sentence modeling, and an entity-based local coherence model to ensure fluent transitions between sentences. The key novelty lies in extending both input and output dimensions to sequences, rather than working with single image-sentence pairs as in most prior work.

The paper builds upon several existing techniques: the use of CNNs for image representation (e.g., [13]), bidirectional RNNs for language modeling (e.g., [20]), and the idea of incorporating coherence between sentences (e.g., [1]). However, it is the first to explicitly address the problem of generating coherent sentence sequences from image streams. The authors leverage blog posts as a natural source of text-image parallel data, which is a clever and practical approach to obtaining training data for this novel task.

**Strengths:**

1. **Originality:** The problem of generating sentence sequences from image streams represents a meaningful extension of the single-image captioning problem. This is an important step toward more natural language descriptions of visual experiences, as people often take multiple photos during events rather than single images. The paper effectively distinguishes itself from related work, though it misses some key video-to-sentence papers (e.g., "Jointly modeling deep video and compositional text to bridge vision and language" [26] and "Translating Video Content to Natural Language Descriptions" [23]).

2. **Technical Approach:** The CRCN architecture is well-motivated and integrates three complementary components: image representation, sentence modeling, and coherence modeling. The use of paragraph vectors and entity-based coherence modeling is particularly interesting and addresses a real challenge in sequential text generation.

3. **Evaluation:** The paper uses both quantitative metrics (BLEU, CIDEr, METEOR, top-K recall) and user studies via AMT, which is appropriate for a language generation task. The use of large-scale blog datasets (20K posts, 140K images) provides substantial empirical support.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Impact of Key Contribution:** The user study results (Table 2) show only marginal improvements of CRCN over the RCN baseline (which lacks the coherence model), with the advantage becoming more pronounced only when using longer image sequences. This weakens the argument for the necessity of the coherence model, which is the paper's main theoretical contribution. The statistical significance of these improvements is not tested, making it difficult to assess whether the observed differences are meaningful.

2. **Incomplete Related Work:** The literature review omits several relevant video-to-sentence generation works that directly address sequential visual content, which undermines the paper's claim of novelty in handling sequential input and output. This is particularly problematic given the close relationship between video description and image stream description.

3. **Technical Details:** Some technical aspects of the model, particularly the coherence model's implementation, could be clarified. The description of the entity grid and transition patterns could benefit from more detailed explanation.

**Quality Assessment:**
The paper is technically sound with appropriate experimental design. The approach is well-justified, and the results generally support the claims. However, the lack of statistical significance testing for the reported improvements (especially in the user studies) is a notable weakness in the evaluation.

**Clarity:**
The paper is generally well-written and well-organized, with clear figures illustrating the approach. Some technical details could be more thoroughly explained, particularly regarding the coherence model implementation.

**Originality Assessment:**
The problem of generating sentence sequences from image streams is novel, though the paper's claim of novelty is somewhat weakened by the omission of relevant video-to-sentence literature. The CRCN architecture itself represents a novel integration of existing components for this specific task.

**Significance Assessment:**
The problem addressed is significant in the context of natural language processing for visual content, as it better aligns with how people document experiences. However, the limited improvement shown over the RCN baseline (without coherence) suggests the significance may be somewhat overestimated. The approach is completely unsupervised and thus potentially applicable to many domains, which is a positive.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
- Novel problem formulation extending image captioning to image streams
- Well-motivated architecture combining three complementary components
- Good quantitative results on multiple metrics
- Practical use of blog posts as training data
- Large-scale dataset evaluation

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
- Weak user study results showing only marginal advantage over RCN
- Missing key related work in the literature review
- Lack of statistical significance testing for reported improvements
- The coherence model's contribution appears limited based on the results

Overall, this is a solid technical contribution to a meaningful extension of the image captioning problem. While the results are promising, the marginal improvements over the baseline and the lack of statistical significance testing weaken the paper's impact. With additional analysis and clarification of the coherence model's contribution, this could be a strong paper. As it stands, the paper's significance is somewhat overestimated given the limited improvement shown.

I recommend rejection, but with the understanding that the paper could be significantly strengthened with additional analysis and literature coverage. The work represents an interesting direction for future research, but the current results do not sufficiently demonstrate the necessity of the coherence model, which is the paper's main contribution.
