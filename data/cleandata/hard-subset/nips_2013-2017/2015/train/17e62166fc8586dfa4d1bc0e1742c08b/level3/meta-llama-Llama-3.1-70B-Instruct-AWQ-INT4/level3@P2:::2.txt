This paper proposes an approach for retrieving a sequence of natural sentences for an image stream, which is a novel and challenging task. The authors extend both input and output dimensions to a sequence of images and a sequence of sentences, respectively. They design a multimodal architecture called coherent recurrent convolutional network (CRCN), which integrates convolutional neural networks, bidirectional recurrent neural networks, and an entity-based local coherence model.

The CRCN model is trained on a large dataset of blog posts, which provides a rich source of text-image parallel data. The authors evaluate their approach using both quantitative measures (e.g., BLEU and top-K recall) and user studies via Amazon Mechanical Turk. The results show that their approach outperforms other state-of-the-art candidate methods, including log-bilinear multimodal models and recurrent convolutional models.

The main contribution of this paper is the novel architecture of the CRCN model, which is specifically designed to select coherent sentences for image sequences. The authors also provide an extensive quantitative, qualitative, and human evaluation of their approach, which demonstrates its superiority over baselines and an ablation removing the coherence model.

However, there are some weaknesses and related-work issues in the paper. For example, the authors claim that the long-term recurrent convolutional net [5] has "no mechanism for coherence," which is inaccurate. The paper [5] models an intermediate semantic/topic representation that yields coherence. Additionally, the diagram in Figure 2b appears inconsistent with Section 3.3/Eq. (2), which may cause confusion.

In terms of clarity and correctness, the paper is well-written and easy to follow. However, there are some minor issues, such as the use of BLEU as an evaluation metric, which may not be the best choice, especially with a single reference. The authors may consider using METEOR instead.

There are also some model design questions and implementation details that need to be clarified. For example, the authors use two linear functions (W_f2, W_f1) applied sequentially in Eq. (2), which may be unnecessary. The authors may consider providing an ablation to justify this design choice. Additionally, the authors use the same fully connected layer parameters for BRNN outputs (o_t) and the local coherence model q in Eq. (2), which may not be the best choice. The authors may consider providing a justification for this parameter-sharing scheme.

Finally, the authors promise to release the source code and dataset, which is a great contribution to the research community.

Arguments for acceptance:

* The paper proposes a novel and challenging task of retrieving a sequence of natural sentences for an image stream.
* The authors design a novel architecture called coherent recurrent convolutional network (CRCN), which is specifically designed for this task.
* The paper provides an extensive quantitative, qualitative, and human evaluation of the approach, which demonstrates its superiority over baselines and an ablation removing the coherence model.
* The authors promise to release the source code and dataset, which is a great contribution to the research community.

Arguments against acceptance:

* The paper has some weaknesses and related-work issues, such as the inaccurate claim about the long-term recurrent convolutional net [5].
* The diagram in Figure 2b appears inconsistent with Section 3.3/Eq. (2), which may cause confusion.
* The paper uses BLEU as an evaluation metric, which may not be the best choice, especially with a single reference.

Overall, I recommend acceptance of this paper after the authors address the minor issues and provide clarifications on the model design and implementation details.
