This paper targets paragraph-level description of image streams by retrieving a sequence of natural sentences that jointly match the visual content and maintain discourse flow. The proposed coherent recurrent convolutional network (CRCN) combines: (i) CNN features (VGG fc7) for images, (ii) a bidirectional RNN over paragraph vectors of candidate sentences to model content flow within a sequence, and (iii) an entity-grid–based local coherence model to encourage fluent sentence-to-sentence transitions. Training uses a max-margin objective over aligned blog image–text streams (NYC and Disneyland) harvested from the web via segmentation and summarization. At test time, the system retrieves per-image candidate sentences via KNN in CNN space and assembles the best sequence with an approximate beam/divide-and-conquer search. Compared to single-sentence captioners and simple concatenation baselines, CRCN shows gains on BLEU/METEOR/CIDEr, retrieval recalls, and AMT preferences.

Relation to prior work: The problem extends single-image captioning (e.g., Karpathy & Fei-Fei 2015; Vinyals et al. 2015) and is closely related to video description (e.g., Donahue et al. 2015; Rohrbach et al. 2013), where multi-sentence outputs or paragraph-level coherence become important. The entity-grid coherence modeling (Barzilay & Lapata) is a sensible addition not commonly integrated in multimodal captioning at the time. The coverage of related video-to-text and multi-sentence description work could be strengthened (e.g., more recent video-to-paragraph and sequence-to-sequence approaches from 2014–2015), as the proposed framing mirrors several elements explored there.

Strengths
- Clear problem statement and well-written paper; architecture and training objective are easy to follow.
- Sensible combination of BRNN content modeling and entity-based local coherence; the coherence component is novel in this multimodal setting.
- Large, realistic data source (blogs) and a pragmatic pipeline for creating image–sentence streams; ablations (1NN vs RCN vs CRCN) isolate contributions.
- Empirical improvements over strong baselines on both automatic metrics and AMT user studies; analysis showing the increasing value of coherence for longer sequences is insightful.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Equation (2) and the fusion design: Wf2Wf1[O|q] = [S|g] suggests each s_t depends only on o_t (column-wise mapping), while g is derived from q alone. Despite sharing parameters, this does not explicitly fuse the sequence-level coherence signal q into each s_t. If the intent is to condition per-sentence representations on global coherence, the formulation should concatenate/broadcast q into each column or otherwise enable cross-column interactions. As written, the network is not “fully” connected across sentences and coherence in the way the text claims.
- Scoring function length bias and coupling: In Eq. (3), g_k · v_lt is summed over t, so the coherence vector contributes N times, potentially inducing length bias. It is unclear whether sequences are length-normalized; if not, training may learn degenerate solutions or be sensitive to sequence length. Clarify and, if needed, normalize.
- Retrieval pipeline is primitive: per-image KNN sentence retrieval followed by beam search is a reasonable baseline but ad hoc for paragraph generation. There is no explicit mechanism to prevent redundancy, enforce diversity/coverage, or model higher-order discourse beyond the entity grid. A learned decoder (e.g., sequence-to-sequence with attention or hierarchical RNN) or joint selection with coverage/anti-redundancy constraints could be stronger.
- Dataset construction and reproducibility: The blog crawling, filtering, and summarization procedure is only sketched; important details (queries, domains, filtering criteria, summarization hyperparameters, de-duplication) are needed to ensure reproducibility and to assess potential biases. The reuse of [11] and new crawling raises anonymity and licensing/privacy questions; please clarify release plans and ethical safeguards.
- Evaluation: Automatic metrics at paragraph level (BLEU/METEOR/CIDEr) are known to be noisy; while AMT is helpful, the main study caps sequence length at 5 images (where coherence is less critical). Significance tests and more robust long-sequence human evaluations would strengthen claims. Also, because “ground truth” sentences are LSA summaries of blogs, the metrics assess match to summaries, not full human-authored paragraphs; discuss implications.
- Related work coverage: The paper cites some video description work, but additional contemporaneous multi-sentence/video-to-paragraph approaches (e.g., Venugopalan et al. 2015; hierarchical or seq2seq treatments) should be discussed for a balanced positioning.
- Minor technical clarity: Are CNNs fine-tuned? Are paragraph vectors trained per-domain or jointly? How are duplicate sentences across candidates handled? How is the beam/divide-and-conquer search guaranteed not to prune globally optimal sequences?

Quality
- Generally sound, with a clear objective and sensible training. However, the fusion in Eq. (2) and the length effects in Eq. (3) need clarification or correction. The retrieval/search strategy is heuristic.

Clarity
- Very well written. Please add the missing implementation specifics for data creation, training, and search; expand the related work; and fix/explain Eq. (2)/(3).

Originality
- Moderate. The application to image streams and integration of entity-grid coherence into a multimodal retrieval framework are novel, but the overall approach builds on established CNN+RNN and entity-grid ideas, and parallels developments in video description.

Significance
- Reasonable. The task is important and underexplored; the method shows solid gains and may influence subsequent work on multi-sentence visual description. Broader impact depends on stronger generation models and better datasets.

Pros for acceptance
- Addresses a timely and relevant problem (image-stream to paragraph).
- Integrates discourse coherence into multimodal modeling.
- Clear empirical gains with ablations and human studies.
- Well written; likely to stimulate follow-up work.

Cons for acceptance
- Fusion/scoring equations appear flawed or, at least, insufficiently justified.
- Heuristic retrieval pipeline limits novelty and scalability.
- Dataset construction lacks sufficient detail; potential anonymity/ethics concerns.
- Incomplete related work on multi-sentence/video-to-paragraph methods.

Recommendation: Accept as a poster. With fixes to the fusion/scoring formulation, stronger discussion of related work, and clearer dataset/evaluation details, the contribution would be solid and useful to the community.
