This paper tackles the novel and challenging problem of generating a coherent sequence of natural language sentences to describe a stream of images. While most prior work focuses on generating a single sentence for a single image, this work extends both the input and output to sequences. The authors propose a Coherent Recurrent Convolutional Network (CRCN), a multimodal architecture that combines a CNN for image representation, a bidirectional RNN to model the flow of sentence content, and an entity-based local coherence model to ensure fluent transitions between sentences. The approach is retrieval-based: for a query image stream, candidate sentences are first retrieved from a training set for each image. The CRCN model is then used to score and rank full candidate sequences, selecting the one that is both visually relevant and textually coherent. The model is trained on a large dataset of blog posts, which provide a natural source of parallel image and text sequences.

**Strengths**

1.  **Significance and Originality:** The paper addresses a logical and important next step in the image-and-language domain. Moving from single sentences to coherent paragraphs is a significant challenge, and to my knowledge, this is the first work to formally address the task of generating a sentence sequence for an image stream. The problem formulation and proposed solution are novel.

2.  **Technical Quality:** The proposed CRCN model is technically sound. It thoughtfully integrates three crucial components: a state-of-the-art visual representation (CNN), a sequence model for language context (BRNN), and an explicit discourse model for textual coherence. The end-to-end training framework with a max-margin objective is appropriate for the ranking task.

3.  **Thorough Evaluation:** The experimental evaluation is a major strength. The authors compare their method against several relevant and state-of-the-art baselines on two large-scale datasets collected from blogs. The evaluation is comprehensive, using a suite of quantitative metrics (BLEU, METEOR, CIDEr, and retrieval metrics), qualitative examples, and a user study on Amazon Mechanical Turk. The results consistently demonstrate the superiority of the proposed approach.

4.  **Ablation Study:** The comparison between the full model (CRCN) and a variant without the coherence module (RCN) clearly validates the contribution of the explicit coherence modeling, particularly for longer sequences as shown in the user study.

5.  **Reproducibility:** The authors promise to release their source code and dataset, which is a commendable contribution to the community that will facilitate future research in this new area.

**Weaknesses and Questions for the Authors**

The paper is strong, but there are several points that could be clarified or improved.

1.  **Comparison to Related Work:** The paper claims that the long-term recurrent convolutional network [5] has "no mechanism for the coherence between sentences". This statement is debatable. The model in [5] learns and passes a shared representation (e.g., an activity vector) through time, which implicitly enforces topic continuity and thus provides a form of coherence. A more nuanced discussion of the differences—for instance, contrasting their implicit topic-based coherence with this work's explicit entity-based coherence—would be more accurate.

2.  **Clarity of the Architecture:** There appears to be a mismatch between the diagram in Figure 2b and the formulation in Equation (2). The figure depicts the fully-connected layers being applied after the outputs from all timesteps {o_t} and the coherence vector `q` have been concatenated. However, Equation (2) seems to imply that the weights Wf1 and Wf2 are applied to each `o_t` individually (with shared weights) and to `q`. Could you please clarify the exact mechanism here?

3.  **Model Design Choices:**
    *   Could you provide the motivation for using the same fully-connected layer parameters (Wf1, Wf2) to process both the per-sentence BRNN outputs (`o_t`) and the global sequence-level coherence vector (`q`)? It seems these two features represent different levels of information.
    *   Equation (2) applies two linear transformations, Wf1 and Wf2, sequentially. The paper correctly notes that dropout between them prevents them from collapsing into a single linear map. However, was a single, wider FC layer with dropout also tested? A brief justification for this two-layer design would be helpful.

4.  **Implementation Details:** The paper uses paragraph vectors [17] to represent sentences. Is the paragraph vector model pre-trained on the blog corpus and then held fixed, or are the sentence vectors fine-tuned during the training of the CRCN model? This detail is important for reproducibility.

5.  **Evaluation Metrics:** It is excellent that the authors report METEOR and CIDEr alongside BLEU. The discussion could be slightly strengthened by explicitly acknowledging that n-gram overlap metrics like BLEU are known to correlate less well with human judgments of quality for paragraph-level tasks than for sentence-level tasks. This might help explain why the performance gains on language metrics appear modest compared to the significant improvements seen in retrieval metrics and human evaluations.

Overall, this is a high-quality paper that introduces a novel problem and presents a well-designed and thoroughly evaluated solution. The weaknesses are primarily minor points of clarification that do not detract from the core contribution. I am leaning towards acceptance and look forward to the authors' response to the questions above.
