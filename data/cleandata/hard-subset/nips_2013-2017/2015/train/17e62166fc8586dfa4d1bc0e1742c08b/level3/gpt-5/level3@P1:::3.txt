This paper tackles the underexplored problem of expressing an image stream with a sequence of natural sentences. The proposed coherent recurrent convolutional network (CRCN) integrates: (i) a CNN (VGG fc7) for image representation; (ii) a bidirectional RNN over sentence representations (doc2vec) to capture forward/backward context across a paragraph; and (iii) an entity-based local coherence model (Barzilay & Lapata) to encourage fluent sentence-to-sentence transitions. The system retrieves candidate sentences for each image via KNN in image space, then scores and ranks candidate sequences using a max-margin multimodal compatibility function that blends per-sentence content and a global coherence feature. Training uses weakly aligned text–image sequences derived from blog posts via paragraph-based segmentation and LSA summarization; decoding uses a divide-and-conquer beam-like search to avoid exponential growth. Experiments on two large, noisy travel blog corpora (NYC, Disneyland) show gains over strong single-sentence baselines (e.g., Karpathy et al., Vinyals et al., Kiros et al.) on BLEU/CIDEr/METEOR and retrieval metrics, with ablations indicating added value from coherence, especially for longer streams. AMT pairwise preferences generally favor CRCN over baselines.

Relation to prior work: The paper is well-situated with respect to image captioning via retrieval and multimodal RNNs (Karpathy & Fei-Fei; Vinyals et al.; Kiros et al.) and acknowledges sequence modeling for video description (e.g., Donahue et al. LRCN; Rohrbach et al.). The closest antecedents in sequence-level retrieval (Kim et al., CVPR) operate in the reverse direction (text→images). The central differentiator here is explicit entity-grid coherence modeling combined with a sequence-level retrieval-and-ranking objective for image streams. That said, the related work on video-to-text that jointly models temporal structure (e.g., Xu et al., AAAI “Jointly Modeling Deep Video and Compositional Text…”) should be discussed more explicitly vis-à-vis multi-sentence generation and coherence; clarifying how CRCN differs from hierarchical decoders or sequence-conditioned captioners would strengthen positioning.

Quality:
- Technical soundness: The max-margin alignment objective is standard and appropriate, and the BRNN formulation is reasonable. Integrating an entity-grid feature into a learned fusion is novel and defensible. However, two aspects merit clarification: (1) the scoring term gk·vlt is added for each time step, effectively repeating a sequence-level coherence term N times; this may introduce length bias. Consider normalizing by sequence length or using a single gk·mean_t(vlt) term. (2) The 64-d entity-grid feature is zero-padded to 300-d before fusion; a learned projection or small MLP might be more principled.
- Evaluation: Quantitative improvements are consistent, but the AMT results show only a modest advantage over the RCN ablation (BRNN without coherence), especially for shorter sequences. Statistical significance testing is missing for both automatic metrics and AMT. Please include paired bootstrap resampling for BLEU/CIDEr/METEOR and a sign/binomial test with confidence intervals for AMT preferences, and report inter-annotator agreement.
- Baselines: Current baselines concatenate per-image captions, which handicaps coherence. A stronger sequence-aware baseline (e.g., an LSTM/GRU paragraph decoder that conditions on previous sentence embeddings; an LRCN-style decoder across the image sequence; or a neural coherence discriminator trained end-to-end) would better contextualize gains.
- Analysis: Provide sensitivity studies for K (nearest neighbors), M (beam size), and Q (divide-and-conquer depth), and a runtime/complexity analysis. Ablate κ (number of summary sentences per segment), the doc2vec dimensionality, and the separate forward/backward input mappings in the BRNN. Report length normalization effects on retrieval metrics.

Clarity:
- The paper is generally clear and well-organized, with useful architectural details. Clarify notation around q vs g (coherence vector) and the two FC layers (explicitly state the nonlinearity between Wf1 and Wf2). Fix minor reference-number inconsistencies and explicitly enumerate the video-to-text works considered and those omitted.
- The preprocessing pipeline (paragraph tokenizer, image–text alignment heuristic, LSA summarization) is sensible but potentially noisy. A brief qualitative assessment of summarization quality and error modes would help.

Originality:
- Moderate to high. Moving from single images to image streams and from single sentences to coherent multi-sentence descriptions is an important step. The use of an entity-grid coherence model inside a multimodal retrieval-and-ranking framework is novel in this context.

Significance:
- Promising. The datasets are sizable and the improvements on retrieval metrics are notable; the coherence contribution grows with sequence length, matching intuition. However, the modest human-preference margins and lack of significance testing temper the strength of the claims. Demonstrating cross-domain generalization (e.g., train on Disneyland, test on NYC) or adding a third domain would bolster impact.

Specific suggestions/questions:
- Add statistical significance tests and confidence intervals for all metrics and AMT; report inter-annotator agreement and, if possible, separate AMT questions for relevance, fluency, and coherence.
- Include at least one sequence-aware baseline beyond simple concatenation.
- Normalize scores to control for sequence length; report metrics stratified by length.
- Analyze the approximate search: how close to exact is it in practice? Provide oracle upper bounds and sensitivity to M.
- Discuss and mitigate potential data leakage (near-duplicate images across blogs; author overlap). Consider near-duplicate filtering and report its effect.
- Justify “unsupervised” wording; the approach is better described as weakly supervised using automatically aligned text–image pairs.
- Release data splits and code for reproducibility.

Pros for acceptance:
- Addresses a timely and underexplored problem (image streams → coherent paragraphs).
- Thoughtful multimodal architecture with explicit coherence modeling.
- Solid quantitative gains and ablation evidence, especially on longer sequences.
- Scalable, weakly supervised data construction from blogs.

Cons for acceptance:
- Missing statistical significance; AMT advantages over RCN are modest.
- Baselines do not include strong sequence-aware models; coherence claims could be better substantiated.
- Some design choices (coherence padding/projection, length bias in scoring) feel ad hoc.
- Limited analysis of preprocessing noise and search approximation; generalization beyond travel blogs not demonstrated.

Overall, this is a nice and interesting piece of work with a clear problem, a reasonable and novel solution, and encouraging results. With stronger baselines and significance analysis, it would make a solid contribution.
