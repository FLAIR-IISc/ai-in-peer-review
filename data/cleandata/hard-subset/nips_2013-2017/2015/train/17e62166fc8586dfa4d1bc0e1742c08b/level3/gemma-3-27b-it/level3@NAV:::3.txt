## Review of "Expressing an Image Stream with a Sequence of Natural Sentences"

This paper presents a Coherent Recurrent Convolutional Network (CRCN) designed to generate sequences of sentences from image streams, extending the traditional single-image-to-single-sentence paradigm. The core idea of combining CNNs, bidirectional RNNs, and a novel entity-based local coherence model is a reasonable and interesting approach to tackling the challenges inherent in describing a series of related images. The motivation—modeling how humans naturally describe a sequence of photos taken during an event—is well-articulated and provides a strong foundation for the work. 

The paper builds upon a significant body of work in image captioning and video description, drawing connections to existing multimodal architectures like those employing CNNs and RNNs (e.g., [9, 5, 30]). The authors correctly identify the need for coherence modeling when generating multiple sentences, addressing a gap in previous research. The use of blog posts as a training corpus is a clever solution to acquiring parallel image-text data, and the pre-processing steps are reasonably described. 

The quantitative results (Table 1) demonstrate promising performance, with the CRCN outperforming several baselines. However, the user study (Table 2) reveals a more modest advantage over the RCN baseline, suggesting the coherence model’s impact might be limited in practice, at least with the current experimental setup. A critical weakness is the lack of statistical significance testing on the user study results. While the reported improvements look favorable, it's impossible to determine whether they are statistically reliable without appropriate testing (e.g., paired t-tests, Wilcoxon signed-rank tests). 

Furthermore, the literature review, while covering relevant work, misses some recent and pertinent publications, specifically "Jointly modeling deep video and compositional text..." [Xu et al., AAAI 2017], which tackles a very similar problem of sequence-to-sequence modeling with visual input. 

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novel approach to extending image captioning to image *streams*.
*   The combination of CNNs, RNNs, and a coherence model is a logical and potentially impactful architecture.
*   Strong quantitative results on standard metrics.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   Modest improvement in user studies without statistical significance testing.
*   Missing related work (e.g. [Xu et al., AAAI 2017]).
*   The coherence model’s contribution feels incremental based on the user study results.



**Overall:** This is a solid piece of work with a clear contribution. Addressing the missing related work and adding a statistical significance test to the user study results would significantly strengthen the paper. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
