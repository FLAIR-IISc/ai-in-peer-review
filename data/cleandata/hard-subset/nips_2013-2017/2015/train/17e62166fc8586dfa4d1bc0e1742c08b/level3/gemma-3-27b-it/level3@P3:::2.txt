This paper proposes a novel architecture, the Coherent Recurrent Convolutional Network (CRCN), for retrieving a sequence of sentences to describe a sequence of images. Extending existing work focused on single image-sentence pairs, the authors tackle the more challenging problem of modeling image *streams* with sentence *sequences*. They leverage blog posts as a large-scale, weakly-supervised dataset and demonstrate strong performance via quantitative metrics, user studies, and ablation analysis.

The core idea is to combine CNNs for image representation, BRNNs for sentence sequence modeling, and a local coherence model to ensure fluency. The approach first retrieves candidate sentences for each image, then selects the best sequence considering both individual image-sentence compatibility *and* coherence between consecutive sentences. This builds upon recent trends in multimodal learning and image captioning (e.g., [5, 9, 12, 30]) by explicitly addressing sequential coherence, a crucial aspect often overlooked. The promise of releasing code and the dataset is a significant positive aspect, fostering reproducibility and further research.

However, the paper isn’t without weaknesses. The claim that [5] has “no mechanism for coherence” is inaccurate. While not explicitly modeled in the same way as the authors' local coherence model, [5] learns an intermediate semantic/topic representation that inherently promotes coherence within the generated sentence for a short video clip. This mischaracterization weakens the novelty claim.

A specific clarity concern arises with Figure 2b and its relation to Section 3.3/Eq. (2). The diagram depicts FC layers connected to *all* sentence outputs (o_t) simultaneously, suggesting a parallel application of the FC layers. However, Eq. (2) implies shared parameters applied per sentence, processing each o_t individually before concatenation. This discrepancy should be clarified. 

Regarding evaluation, while the authors correctly move beyond BLEU, the reliance on METEOR with only a single reference sentence is suboptimal. METEOR performs best with multiple references. Consideration of other metrics, or justification for the single reference setup, would strengthen the evaluation.

Several model design choices warrant further justification. Why are two linear functions (W_f2, W_f1) applied sequentially in Eq. (2)? Their composition is a single linear transformation. An ablation study demonstrating the benefit of this sequential application (beyond regularization via dropout) is necessary. Similarly, the parameter sharing between the BRNN outputs (o_t) and the local coherence model (q) in Eq. (2) is unclear. The rationale behind this design choice, and potentially the impact of separate parameters, should be explored. 

Furthermore, the implementation details require clarification. Is the paragraph vector [16] fine-tuned during training, or is it kept fixed? This significantly impacts the learning process and should be explicitly stated.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Strong quantitative and qualitative results, consistently outperforming baselines.
*   Well-designed architecture addressing a novel and important problem.
*   Comprehensive experimental evaluation, including user studies.
*   Promise of code and dataset release.
*   The local coherence model demonstrably improves performance.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   Inaccurate claim regarding related work ([5]).
*   Clarity issue with Figure 2b and Eq. (2).
*   Suboptimal evaluation metric (METEOR with a single reference).
*   Lack of justification for specific model design choices (sequential FC layers, parameter sharing).
*   Unclear implementation detail (paragraph vector fine-tuning).

**Decision (Post-Rebuttal):**

After reviewing the rebuttal and considering the authors’ responses, I now recommend acceptance. The authors acknowledged the inaccuracy regarding [5] and will revise the manuscript accordingly. They clarified the diagram in Figure 2b and addressed the concerns about the sequential FC layers and parameter sharing, promising to include an ablation study in the final version. Finally, they confirmed that the paragraph vector is fixed during training and will explicitly state this in the paper. Addressing these points substantially improves the clarity and rigor of the work. The authors should fulfill their promise of releasing the code and dataset to maximize the impact of their contribution.
