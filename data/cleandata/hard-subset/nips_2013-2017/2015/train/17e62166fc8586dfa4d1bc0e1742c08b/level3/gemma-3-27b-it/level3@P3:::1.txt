This paper addresses the problem of generating a sequence of natural language sentences to describe a stream of images, extending the traditional image captioning task to a multi-sentence, multi-image setting. While the problem is significant and motivated by real-world scenarios like describing vacation photo streams, the approach largely mirrors existing work in video captioning and single-image captioning, and the novelty feels incremental.

The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow. The problem statement and proposed solution – a Coherent Recurrent Convolutional Network (CRCN) – are clearly articulated. The architecture integrating CNNs, BRNNs, and a local coherence model based on entity relationships is technically sound and represents a reasonable approach to capturing discourse relationships. 

However, a critical examination reveals some weaknesses. Equation (2) is particularly concerning. It suggests the sentence representation *st* depends *only* on the image representation *ot*, implying a fully disconnected network concerning previous sentences. This seems implausible and likely a simplification or error, as the BRNN is intended to model sequential dependencies. A more detailed explanation of how the BRNN’s hidden state is incorporated into the overall scoring function is needed.

The method for generating sentence sequences – retrieving candidate sentences per image and simple concatenation – appears somewhat primitive and ad-hoc. While acknowledged as a starting point, a more sophisticated approach, perhaps incorporating beam search or a more nuanced ranking strategy, could yield better results. The reliance on selecting *k*-nearest images limits the diversity of potential sentences.

A significant concern is the dataset description. It's insufficient and potentially problematic. Reusing the blog data from Kim et al. [11] is fine, but the paper lacks detail regarding the crawling process and selection criteria for the NYC data. This lack of transparency raises questions about potential biases in the dataset. Furthermore, the description skirts the line regarding user anonymity. The specifics of how blog posts were collected and any potential identifying information removed aren't adequately addressed. This could be a breach of confidentiality, even if the data is publicly accessible.

Crucially, the paper omits citations to important prior work. Rohrbach et al. [23] and related papers on video description (e.g., Xu et al. [32]) are directly relevant, as they also tackle sequential input and output. Similarly, works extending single-image captioning to multi-sentence descriptions, such as those focusing on discourse coherence, should be acknowledged. The claim of being “the first” to address this problem is therefore inaccurate.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Clear problem statement and well-explained solution.
*   Technically sound architecture that incorporates discourse modeling.
*   Demonstrated improvements over baseline methods through quantitative and qualitative evaluation.
*   Well-written and easy to understand.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Incremental novelty; closely related to existing video captioning work.
*   Omission of key related work.
*   Questionable correctness of Equation (2).
*   Ad-hoc sentence sequence generation method.
*   Insufficient dataset description and potential privacy concerns.

**Overall Recommendation:**

I recommend this paper as an **acceptable poster paper**. While the technical contributions are sound, the lack of novelty and the issues surrounding the dataset and related work prevent a stronger recommendation. Addressing the cited weaknesses, particularly the related work and dataset details, would significantly strengthen the paper. The equation clarification is essential. As a poster, it could spark valuable discussion regarding the challenges of extending image captioning to multi-image streams.
