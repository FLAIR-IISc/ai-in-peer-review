This paper proposes a novel approach to generating coherent sentence sequences from image streams, a natural extension of the single image-to-sentence generation task. The authors introduce the Coherent Recurrent Convolutional Network (CRCN), integrating convolutional neural networks for image representation, bidirectional recurrent neural networks for language modeling, and a local coherence model to ensure fluency and logical flow between sentences. The work leverages a large dataset of blog posts with associated images as training data, and demonstrates strong performance through quantitative metrics (BLEU, top-K recall) and user studies via Amazon Mechanical Turk.

The problem addressed is a relevant and increasingly important one. As users routinely capture image streams representing events or experiences, automatically generating descriptive narratives holds significant value. Extending existing techniques to handle sequential data rather than single images is a logical and worthwhile step. The proposed CRCN architecture appears well-motivated, and the inclusion of a coherence model is particularly noteworthy, as it directly tackles the challenge of generating fluent multi-sentence descriptions. The use of blog data as a training resource is clever, offering a large-scale, naturally occurring text-image parallel corpus.

The strengths of this paper are numerous. The technical approach is sound, building upon established techniques like CNNs and RNNs, but extending them in a meaningful way to address the complexities of sequential image description. The quantitative results in Table 1 demonstrate a clear advantage over the baselines, indicating the effectiveness of the proposed method. The user studies in Table 2, while showing a smaller advantage, still support the preference for the CRCN’s output. The authors clearly articulate the contributions of their work and position it within the broader context of existing research. The detailed explanation of the architecture, training procedure, and experimental setup facilitates reproducibility. 

However, there are also weaknesses that need to be addressed. The related work section, while covering many relevant papers, omits several recent contributions in the area of video description and sequential modeling. For instance, Xu et al. (2015) "Jointly modeling deep video and compositional text to bridge vision and language in a unified framework" presents a related approach and should be discussed. A more comprehensive review of the literature would strengthen the paper.

The user study results, while positive, are somewhat marginal, particularly for the comparison with the RCN baseline. The difference in performance appears relatively small, and the authors do not provide any statistical significance testing to confirm whether the observed improvements are statistically reliable. Performing a statistical significance test (e.g., a paired t-test) on the user study data is crucial to establish the robustness of the findings. 

Additionally, the data augmentation technique described in Section 2.1, while seemingly beneficial, lacks a detailed analysis of its impact. A more rigorous ablation study exploring the effect of different augmentation parameters (e.g., the value of *κ*) would provide further insight into its effectiveness.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Addresses a novel and important problem: generating coherent descriptions for image streams.
*   Proposes a technically sound and well-motivated architecture (CRCN).
*   Demonstrates strong performance through quantitative metrics and user studies.
*   Utilizes a large and naturally occurring dataset for training.
*   The integration of a coherence model is a significant contribution.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The related work section is incomplete, missing relevant publications.
*   The user study results show only a marginal improvement over some baselines.
*   Lack of statistical significance testing for user study results.
*   Limited analysis of the data augmentation technique.

**Overall Assessment:**

Despite the minor weaknesses, this is a well-executed and promising piece of work. The proposed CRCN architecture addresses a challenging problem in a novel and effective manner. The strong quantitative results and positive user study findings suggest that this approach has the potential to significantly advance the field of image and video description. Addressing the concerns regarding the related work section and the statistical significance of the user study results would further strengthen the paper. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions. Specifically, the authors should incorporate the missing related work (e.g., Xu et al., 2015) and provide statistical significance testing for the user study results.
