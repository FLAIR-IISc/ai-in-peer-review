## Review of "Expressing an Image Stream with a Sequence of Natural Sentences"

This paper tackles the problem of generating multi-sentence descriptions for a sequence of images, motivated by the common scenario of users capturing a series of photos during an event. The authors propose a Coherent Recurrent Convolutional Network (CRCN) that integrates CNNs for image representation, BRNNs for language modeling, and a local coherence model to ensure fluency between sentences. The approach is trained on a large dataset of blog posts with associated images.

The problem addressed is significant, extending the well-studied area of image captioning to the more complex task of describing an entire image stream. The proposed CRCN architecture is technically sound, and the inclusion of an entity-based coherence model is a reasonable approach to address the challenges of maintaining discourse relationships across multiple sentences. The paper is well-written and easy to follow, clearly articulating the problem statement and the proposed solution. The experimental results demonstrate improvements over several baselines, both quantitatively and through user studies.

However, the paper suffers from a lack of engagement with relevant prior work. While the authors acknowledge the surge in image captioning research, they omit crucial citations. Specifically, the seminal work of Rohrbach et al. (2014) on video description, and subsequent papers building upon it that address multi-sentence generation and temporal coherence, are not discussed. This omission weakens the positioning of the work within the broader research landscape. Furthermore, the equation (2) appears questionable; defining *st* solely based on *ot* suggests a network lacking full connectivity, a potentially significant limitation. 

The method for generating sentence sequences – retrieving a sentence per image and concatenating them – feels somewhat primitive and ad-hoc, especially given the focus on coherence. A more sophisticated approach to sentence planning or re-ordering might yield better results. Finally, the dataset description is insufficient. The reuse of data from [11] is noted, but details regarding the crawling process for the new NYC data are lacking, raising potential concerns about reproducibility and anonymity. 

**Arguments for acceptance:** The problem is relevant, the proposed method is technically sound, and the experimental results are promising.
**Arguments against acceptance:** Significant omissions in related work, a potentially flawed equation, a simplistic sentence generation method, and a weak dataset description.

**Recommendation:** Accept as a poster paper. The work presents a reasonable approach to a challenging problem, but the limitations outlined above prevent it from being considered for oral presentation at this time. Addressing the missing citations and clarifying equation (2) would significantly strengthen the paper.
