This paper tackles the novel and interesting problem of retrieving a coherent sequence of sentences to describe a stream of images. The proposed method is a retrieval-based approach that first finds candidate sentences for each image from nearest-neighbor examples in a training corpus of blog posts. The core contribution is the Coherent Recurrent Convolutional Network (CRCN), which scores candidate sentence sequences by modeling image-sentence compatibility and sequence-level coherence. This is achieved by combining image features from a CNN with sentence features from a paragraph vector model, which are then processed by a bidirectional LSTM. A key novelty is the explicit integration of an entity-based local coherence model. This work extends the popular single image-to-sentence task to a more complex sequence-to-sequence setting, building upon established techniques in visual-semantic modeling.

The paper has several significant strengths. The primary contribution is a novel architecture for a relatively under-explored problem. The claims are strongly supported by an extensive set of experiments, including quantitative metrics, qualitative examples, and human evaluations via AMT, which convincingly demonstrate the method's superiority over several strong baselines. The ablation study effectively validates the positive impact of the coherence model. Furthermore, the authors’ promise to release their code and dataset is highly commendable and will benefit the research community.

Despite these strengths, there are points that require attention. The claim that prior work [5] has “no mechanism for coherence” is inaccurate, as its intermediate topic representation serves a similar purpose. For clarity, there appears to be an inconsistency between Figure 2b and Equation (2) regarding the fully connected layers; the diagram implies separate connections while the equation suggests shared parameters. Several design choices also warrant justification: Why use two sequential linear layers in Eq. (2) when their composition is linear? A clarifying ablation would be valuable. Similarly, what is the motivation for sharing parameters for the BRNN output and the coherence model? Finally, for better evaluation, METEOR should be used instead of BLEU, and it should be clarified if the paragraph vector model is fine-tuned.

Having reviewed the author's rebuttal, my initial concerns have been addressed. The paper presents a solid contribution with strong empirical results. I now recommend acceptance, on the condition that the authors incorporate their promised clarifications and corrections into the final version, including the explicit detail on whether the paragraph vector model is fine-tuned or fixed.
