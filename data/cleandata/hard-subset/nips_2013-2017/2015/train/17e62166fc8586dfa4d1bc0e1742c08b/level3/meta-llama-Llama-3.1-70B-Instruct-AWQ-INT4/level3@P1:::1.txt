This paper proposes a novel approach to generating natural language descriptions for image streams. The authors extend the traditional single-image-to-single-sentence paradigm to a sequence-of-images-to-sequence-of-sentences framework, which is more suitable for real-world applications where users often take multiple photos to capture a moment or event.

The authors' contributions are threefold. Firstly, they address the problem of expressing image streams with sentence sequences, which is a significant and challenging task. Secondly, they develop a multimodal architecture called Coherent Recurrent Convolutional Networks (CRCN), which integrates convolutional networks for image representation, bidirectional recurrent neural networks for sentence modeling, and an entity-based local coherence model for fluent transitions of sentences. Thirdly, they evaluate their method on large datasets of unstructured blog posts, demonstrating superior performance compared to other state-of-the-art alternatives.

The paper is well-written and easy to follow, with a clear problem statement and solution. The proposed CRCN model is technically sound and captures discourse relationships using entity-based coherence. The use of a bidirectional recurrent neural network to model sentence sequences is a good choice, as it allows the model to consider both previous and next sentences when generating a sentence.

However, there are some limitations and concerns with the paper. Firstly, the authors do not cite important prior work on video-to-text and multi-sentence generation, such as Rohrbach et al. (2014). Secondly, the method for generating sentence sequences is reasonable but primitive and ad-hoc, involving retrieving a sentence per image and simple concatenation. A more sophisticated approach, such as using a sequence-to-sequence model or a graph-based model, may be more effective.

Additionally, the dataset description is insufficient, and the authors may have breached anonymity and confidentiality by reusing blog data from [11] and not disclosing crawling details. The authors should provide more information about the dataset, including how it was collected, preprocessed, and anonymized.

In terms of the Qualitative Evaluation criteria, the paper scores well on Quality, as the proposed CRCN model is technically sound and well-motivated. The paper also scores well on Originality, as the authors address a significant and challenging problem and propose a novel solution. However, the paper scores lower on Clarity, as the method for generating sentence sequences is not well-explained and the dataset description is insufficient. Finally, the paper scores well on Significance, as the proposed approach has the potential to advance the state-of-the-art in image and video captioning.

In conclusion, while the paper has some limitations and concerns, it is a well-written and technically sound contribution to the field of image and video captioning. With some revisions to address the limitations and concerns, the paper has the potential to make a significant impact in the field.

Arguments for acceptance:

* The paper addresses a significant and challenging problem in image and video captioning.

* The paper demonstrates superior performance compared to other state-of-the-art alternatives.

* The paper does not cite important prior work on video-to-text and multi-sentence generation.
