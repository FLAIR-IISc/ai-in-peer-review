

# Review for "Expressing an Image Stream with a Sequence of Natural Sentences"

This paper proposes a novel approach for generating sequences of natural language sentences from image streams, addressing a problem that extends beyond the typical single-image-to-single-sentence generation framework. The authors introduce a Coherent Recurrent Convolutional Network (CRCN) that integrates convolutional neural networks (for image representation), bidirectional recurrent neural networks (for sentence modeling), and an entity-based local coherence model (to ensure fluent transitions between sentences). The key innovation lies in extending both input and output dimensions to handle sequences rather than single elements, with the authors leveraging blog posts as a source of text-image parallel training data.

The paper builds upon existing work in image captioning (e.g., Karpathy et al., 2015) and multimodal neural networks (e.g., Kiros et al., 2014), but makes a significant contribution by explicitly addressing the coherence problem in sentence sequences. Previous work on video captioning (e.g., Rohrbach et al., 2015) does not fully address the coherence between sentences, while the authors' approach specifically targets this gap. However, the literature review misses important related work, particularly "Jointly Modeling Deep Video and Compositional Text to Bridge Vision and Language in a Unified Framework" (Xu et al., AAAI 2015), which addresses similar problems in the video domain.

**Strengths:**
1. The problem formulation is novel and well-motivated by real-world user behavior (taking multiple photos during memorable events).
2. The quantitative results are impressive, with CRCN significantly outperforming baselines in retrieval metrics (R@K and median rank) on both NYC and Disneyland datasets.
3. The use of blog posts as a natural source of text-image parallel data is clever and practical, avoiding the need for expensive manual annotation.
4. The architecture is well-designed, with careful integration of CNN, BRNN, and coherence components, and the authors provide sufficient technical details for reproducibility.

**Weaknesses:**
1. The user study results (Table 2) show only a weak advantage of CRCN over the RCN baseline (which excludes the coherence model), particularly for shorter sequences (5 images). The performance gap widens for longer sequences (8-10 images), but this is not sufficient to demonstrate a strong practical advantage.
2. The paper lacks statistical significance testing for the reported improvements, which is essential for determining whether the observed gains are reliable or merely due to chance.
3. The literature review omits several key related works, particularly Xu et al. (AAAI 2015), which addresses a very similar problem in the video domain. This weakens the paper's claim of novelty.
4. The paper does not adequately address why the coherence model is necessary for image streams but not for videos (the authors claim consecutive images may show "sharp changes" while videos have more continuity, but this isn't rigorously justified).

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound with a well-constructed architecture and thorough experimental evaluation. The quantitative results are strong, though the lack of statistical significance testing is a notable weakness. The authors are careful to acknowledge the limitations of their approach (e.g., BLEU's inadequacy for evaluating paragraph coherence).

**Clarity:** The paper is well-organized and clearly written. The architecture (Fig. 2) and experimental setup are described in sufficient detail. The supplementary material would likely provide additional clarity on implementation details.

**Originality:** The paper makes a genuine contribution by addressing the sequence-to-sequence generation problem for image streams. While the combination of CNNs and RNNs is not novel, the explicit integration of a coherence model for sentence sequences is a meaningful advancement. However, the omission of Xu et al. (2015) in the literature review weakens the novelty claim.

**Significance:** The problem addressed is important and well-motivated. The results demonstrate clear improvements over existing approaches, though the practical significance is somewhat limited by the weak user study results for typical sequence lengths. The approach could be valuable for applications like travel blogging or social media content generation.

**Overall Assessment:**
This is a solid piece of work that makes a meaningful contribution to the field by addressing a gap in the literature (sequence-to-sequence image description). The quantitative results are strong, and the problem formulation is well-motivated. However, the user study results showing only a weak advantage over the baseline, combined with the lack of statistical significance testing, weaken the paper's claims about the necessity of the coherence model. The omission of key related work in the literature review also detracts from the paper's novelty claim.

**Recommendation:** Accept. The paper addresses an important problem with a novel solution that builds on existing techniques in a meaningful way. While the user study results don't show a dramatic advantage, the quantitative metrics demonstrate clear improvements, and the architecture is sound. The missing literature reference is a concern but doesn't undermine the core contribution. The authors should address the statistical significance question and include the missing related work in a revision.
