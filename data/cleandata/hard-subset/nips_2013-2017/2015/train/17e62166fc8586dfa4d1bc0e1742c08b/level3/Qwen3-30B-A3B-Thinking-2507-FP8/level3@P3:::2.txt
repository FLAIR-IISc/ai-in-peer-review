

# Review of "Expressing an Image Stream with a Sequence of Natural Sentences"

This paper proposes a novel approach to generate coherent sentence sequences describing image streams, extending the typical single-image-to-single-sentence problem to handle sequences of images and corresponding sequences of sentences. The authors introduce a "coherent recurrent convolutional network" (CRCN) that integrates convolutional neural networks for image representation, bidirectional recurrent neural networks for sentence modeling, and a local coherence model based on entity transitions to ensure fluent sentence-to-sentence transitions. The work leverages blog posts as training data, which naturally contain image sequences and associated text descriptions, and evaluates on NYC and Disneyland datasets containing over 20K blog posts with 140K images.

The paper makes a clear contribution by addressing a meaningful gap in the literature: most prior work focuses on single-image captioning, while the authors argue that users often take sequences of images to describe memorable moments, requiring a coherent narrative. The CRCN architecture represents a thoughtful integration of several existing techniques (CNNs, BRNNs, coherence modeling) into a unified framework specifically designed for sequence-to-sequence image description.

**Strengths:** The paper demonstrates strong empirical results through extensive quantitative evaluation (BLEU, CIDEr, METEOR, top-K recall) and human evaluation via Amazon Mechanical Turk. The ablation studies clearly show the value of both the BRNN component and the coherence model, with the complete CRCN model outperforming all baselines. The authors' promise to release source code and dataset is a significant plus for reproducibility and community impact. The problem formulation and dataset construction from blog posts are well-motivated and practical.

**Weaknesses:** The paper makes an inaccurate claim in Section 1 that [5] has "no mechanism for coherence" – the long-term recurrent convolutional network model explicitly models semantic topics that inherently provide coherence. This misrepresentation undermines the novelty claim. Figure 2b appears inconsistent with Section 3.3/Eq. (2), as the diagram suggests FC layers connected to all sentences while the equation indicates shared parameters applied per sentence. The paper should use METEOR rather than BLEU as the primary evaluation metric, especially with a single reference, as BLEU is known to be less correlated with human judgment for longer text sequences.

Several architectural questions remain unanswered: Why are two linear functions (W_f2, W_f1) applied sequentially in Eq. (2) when their composition is linear? The authors should provide an ablation study to justify this design choice. Additionally, why are the same fully connected layer parameters used for BRNN outputs (o_t) and the local coherence model q in Eq. (2)? The paper does not clarify whether the paragraph vector [16] is fine-tuned or kept fixed during training, which is a critical implementation detail.

**Recommendation:** The paper presents a meaningful problem extension with a well-motivated approach that shows strong empirical results. The core contribution of addressing coherent sentence sequence generation for image streams is novel and significant. While the paper has some technical inconsistencies and misrepresentations of related work, these can be addressed in the final version. The authors have already addressed several concerns in their rebuttal, particularly clarifying the coherence mechanism in [5] and promising to make the requested implementation clarifications.

The work advances the state of the art in image description by moving beyond single-image captioning to sequence-level description, which has practical applications for social media and personal photo organization. The quantitative and qualitative results, combined with the human evaluation, provide convincing evidence of the method's superiority.

**Final Recommendation:** Accept. The paper makes a valuable contribution to the field with a novel problem formulation, effective architecture, and thorough evaluation. The minor weaknesses identified can be addressed with straightforward revisions, and the authors have demonstrated their willingness to make these improvements. The work aligns well with NIPS' focus on machine learning advances and demonstrates strong technical quality with appropriate evaluation methods.
