

This paper proposes a novel approach for generating coherent sentence sequences to describe image streams, extending beyond the typical single-image to single-sentence generation task. The authors introduce the Coherent Recurrent Convolutional Network (CRCN), which integrates convolutional neural networks for image representation, bidirectional recurrent neural networks for sentence modeling, and an entity-based local coherence model. The work is motivated by the observation that users often capture sequences of images during memorable events (e.g., travel experiences), making it more natural to generate sequence descriptions rather than individual sentence descriptions. The authors leverage blog posts as a natural source of text-image parallel data, preprocessing them into image-sentence sequence pairs for training.

The paper makes several notable contributions. First, it addresses a previously underexplored problem: generating sentence sequences for image streams rather than single images. Second, the CRCN architecture explicitly incorporates a coherence model to ensure fluent transitions between sentences, which is a significant departure from prior work that typically focuses on individual image-sentence pairs. Third, the authors provide extensive evaluation across multiple metrics (BLEU, CIDEr, METEOR, top-K recall) and human evaluations via Amazon Mechanical Turk, demonstrating consistent superiority over strong baselines including multimodal models from [9, 12, 30].

The paper's strengths are substantial. The quantitative results are thorough and convincing, with the CRCN model significantly outperforming all baselines, particularly in retrieval metrics that directly measure how well the generated sequences match ground-truth descriptions. The ablation study removing the coherence model (RCN) shows that this component is critical for performance, especially as sequence length increases, which is validated by the human evaluation results. The authors also demonstrate practical applicability with two tourism datasets (NYC and Disneyland) containing over 20,000 blog posts, and they promise to release their code and dataset, which is highly valuable for reproducibility.

However, the paper contains several weaknesses that need addressing. First, the claim that [5] (Long-term Recurrent Convolutional Networks) "has no mechanism for coherence" is inaccurate. While [5] focuses on video description rather than sentence sequence generation, it models intermediate semantic representations that inherently promote coherence, so this comparison is misleading. Second, there's an inconsistency between Figure 2b and Equation (2): the figure suggests fully connected layers are connected to individual sentence representations, while Equation (2) implies shared parameters applied to the entire sequence. Third, the authors should justify why they use two sequential linear transformations (Wf1 and Wf2) in Equation (2) when their composition is linear—this could be simplified to a single linear transformation. Fourth, they use BLEU as a primary metric despite its known limitations for sentence sequence evaluation; METEOR would provide a more nuanced assessment, especially with a single reference. Finally, the paper doesn't clarify whether the paragraph vectors [16] are fine-tuned or kept fixed, which is important for understanding the model's training dynamics.

The paper makes a meaningful contribution to the field by addressing a natural extension of image captioning to sequence generation with explicit coherence modeling. While the core idea is sound and well-supported by experiments, the inaccuracies in related work comparison and the technical ambiguities in model design weaken the paper's scientific rigor. The authors should clarify these points in the final version.

In terms of quality, the paper is technically sound with comprehensive experiments, though the model design choices require better justification. The clarity is generally good, but the figure-text inconsistency needs correction. The originality is strong for addressing the sequence generation problem with coherence, though the technical components draw from established techniques. The significance is high, as the work opens a new direction in multimodal learning that could benefit applications like social media content generation and personal photo storytelling.

The authors have addressed many of these concerns in their rebuttal, particularly clarifying the paragraph vector implementation and acknowledging the [5] comparison error. With these clarifications and the promised code release, the paper presents a valuable contribution to the field. I recommend acceptance, as the core contribution is significant and well-validated, and the remaining issues are straightforward to address. The work advances the state of the art by providing a practical solution to a meaningful problem, with clear potential for real-world application.
