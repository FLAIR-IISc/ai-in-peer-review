This paper proposes a novel approach to generating coherent natural language descriptions for a sequence of images, extending traditional image captioning which typically focuses on single images and sentences. The authors tackle the challenging problem of understanding and verbalizing an entire image stream, motivated by real-world scenarios like travel photos. The core of their method is the Coherent Recurrent Convolutional Network (CRCN), which integrates convolutional neural networks for image feature extraction, bidirectional recurrent neural networks for language modeling, and a local coherence model to ensure fluent transitions between sentences. The approach leverages a large dataset constructed from blog posts with associated images, framing the task as a retrieval problem where candidate sentences are selected and then refined based on coherence. Overall, this is an interesting and well-motivated piece of work that tackles a complex and under-explored problem.

The paper effectively positions its contribution within the existing literature. It acknowledges the growing body of work on image captioning (citing [5, 8, 9, 12, 14, 15, 26, 21, 30]) and highlights how their work extends the input and output dimensions to sequences. They also correctly identify related video description approaches, however, the discussion of related work could be more comprehensive. Specifically, the authors miss several relevant papers in the video-to-sentence domain, such as Xu et al. [47] (“Jointly modeling deep video and compositional text…”) which presents a unified framework for vision and language in video understanding and could be a useful comparison point. While the authors acknowledge the difference between video and image streams (the potential for abrupt changes in images), a more thorough comparison to existing video methods is warranted.

The strengths of this paper lie in its thoughtful architecture and strong empirical results. The CRCN architecture appears well-designed, integrating crucial components for both content understanding and coherence. The choice of using a blog post corpus as a training dataset is a clever way to obtain image-text pairs that inherently exhibit a narrative structure. The quantitative results (Table 1) are promising, demonstrating that the CRCN consistently outperforms several strong baselines using both language similarity metrics (BLEU, CIDEr, METEOR) and retrieval accuracies (top-K recall, median rank).  The extensive experimentation with different baselines and model variants strengthens the validity of the findings. The ablation study comparing CRCN to RCN (without the coherence model) clearly demonstrates the contribution of the coherence component.

However, the paper also has several weaknesses. The user study results (Table 2) are less convincing. While the CRCN performs slightly better than the RCN baseline, the difference is not substantial, especially considering the added complexity of the coherence model. Crucially, the authors do *not* provide any statistical significance testing to determine whether the observed improvements are statistically significant. Without such analysis, it is difficult to confidently claim that the CRCN is truly superior to the RCN, or to the other baselines based solely on the user study data. This is a critical omission. Furthermore, the discussion of the AMT setup could be more detailed - what were the instructions given to the turkers, and how were their responses validated? More information about the user study methodology is needed.

Another minor issue is the organization and clarity in certain sections. Section 3.4, describing the training process, is dense and could benefit from a more streamlined presentation.  While the equations are present, the reasoning behind the cost function could be explained more intuitively. 

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novel approach to a challenging and under-explored problem (image stream description).
*   Well-designed architecture (CRCN) that integrates CNNs, RNNs, and a coherence model.
*   Strong quantitative results demonstrating superior performance compared to several baselines.
*   Utilizes a clever approach to building a training dataset from blog posts.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   User study results are not statistically significant and show a relatively small improvement.
*   The discussion of related work could be more comprehensive (missing key video-to-sentence papers).
*   Some sections (e.g., 3.4) are dense and could be clarified.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a valuable contribution to the field, despite its weaknesses. The problem addressed is important, the proposed approach is novel and well-motivated, and the quantitative results are promising. However, the lack of statistical significance testing for the user study results is a significant concern. I recommend **acceptance with minor revisions**. The authors should address the following:

1.  **Perform statistical significance testing** on the user study results to determine whether the observed improvements are statistically significant.
2.  **Expand the related work** section to include more relevant papers in the video-to-sentence domain (e.g., [47]).
3.  **Provide more detail** about the user study methodology (instructions, validation procedures).
4.  **Clarify** the training process description in Section 3.4.
