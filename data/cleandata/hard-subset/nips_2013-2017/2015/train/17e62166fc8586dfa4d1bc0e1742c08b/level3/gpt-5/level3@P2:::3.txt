Summary
This paper addresses the underexplored problem of describing an image stream with a coherent sequence of sentences. The proposed coherent recurrent convolutional network (CRCN) combines: (a) a CNN (VGG) to encode each image, (b) a bidirectional RNN operating on sentence-level paragraph vectors to model content flow across the sequence, and (c) an entity-grid–based local coherence model (following Barzilay & Lapata) to encourage fluent sentence-to-sentence transitions. Training uses max-margin ranking over aligned image–sentence sequences mined from blogs, where each blog is preprocessed into image–text pairs via paragraph-based segmentation and LSA summarization. At test time, candidate sentences are retrieved per image via K-NN in CNN feature space and combined with a divide-and-conquer beam search to select a high-scoring sequence under the CRCN. Experiments on two tourism blog datasets (NYC, Disneyland; ~20k posts, ~140k images total) show improvements over several single-image captioning baselines (Kiros et al., Karpathy & Fei-Fei, Vinyals et al.) using BLEU/CIDEr/METEOR and retrieval metrics, plus an AMT user study.

Relation to prior work
The work extends image captioning by moving from single-image/sentence to multi-image/sentence sequence modeling, and differs from video-to-sentence work by explicitly modeling inter-sentence coherence across potentially discontinuous image streams. It is related to retrieval-based captioning (e.g., Ordonez et al.; Socher et al.; Karpathy & Fei-Fei) but ranks sentence sequences using both content compatibility and coherence. The entity-grid coherence component is rooted in classic NLP work (Barzilay & Lapata). The paper mentions video-sentence work but does not fully situate itself with several contemporaneous efforts on sequential visual-to-language modeling, e.g., Venugopalan et al. (“Translating Videos to Natural Language,” ICCV 2015), Yao et al. (temporal attention for video captioning, 2015), and Xu et al. (“Jointly Modeling Deep Video and Compositional Text,” AAAI 2015). A clearer discussion contrasting CRCN with these sequence modeling approaches—especially the role of coherence modeling versus temporal smoothness in video—would help.

Quality
- Soundness: The overall architecture and max-margin training objective are reasonable, and the separation of concerns (image content vs. sentence flow vs. local coherence) is well-motivated. That said, key design choices warrant further validation:
  - The BRNN uses ReLU units rather than (bi)LSTM/GRU; given the long sequences and vanishing gradients, a biLSTM baseline would be appropriate.
  - The coherence feature is a hand-engineered, 64-d entity-grid transition vector zero-padded to 300-d and linearly fused; stronger learned alternatives (e.g., learned embeddings over entity transitions or coreference chains) could be considered or at least compared.
  - The pipeline relies on heuristic segmentation and LSA summarization; robustness to these steps (and to κ=3 augmentation) should be analyzed via ablations (κ=1, alternative segmenters/summarizers).
  - The test-time search uses K=5 neighbors per image and beam size M=50; sensitivity analyses for K and M would strengthen the claims.
- Evaluation: Improvements in automatic metrics are promising, but the AMT results show only a small advantage over the RCN (no coherence) baseline, particularly for shorter sequences; statistical significance tests (paired bootstrap for BLEU/CIDEr/METEOR; binomial/sign test for AMT pairwise wins) are needed to assess reliability.
- Baselines: In addition to single-image captioners concatenated per time step, stronger sequence baselines should be included, e.g., (i) a biLSTM sequence model without the entity-grid, (ii) a simple sentence-level Markov or CRF model over paragraph vectors, (iii) a video-to-text model adapted to image streams (e.g., Venugopalan et al.), and (iv) coherence baselines (e.g., penalizing abrupt changes in sentence embeddings).
- Analysis: Error analysis and qualitative discussion of when the coherence model helps (e.g., longer sequences, entity continuity) would be valuable.

Clarity
The paper is generally well-organized and readable. The architecture and training objective are clearly specified, and implementation details (dropout, RMSprop, negatives) are given. Some points could be clarified:
- How variable-length sequences are handled at training vs. retrieval and whether lengths must match between image and sentence sequences.
- Justification for zero-padding the coherence feature rather than learning a projection.
- More detail on the AMT setup (exact prompts, quality control, inter-annotator agreement) and on whether results are statistically significant.
- Release plans for code and the processed blog datasets to ensure reproducibility.

Originality
Modeling multi-image streams with multi-sentence outputs and explicitly integrating an entity-based coherence model into a multimodal retrieval framework is a novel and timely formulation. While the components (CNNs/RNNs/entity grids) are known, their combination for this task and the use of mined blog data are original. The reliance on retrieval rather than generative decoding is a pragmatic choice, though somewhat limits novelty in language generation.

Significance
The problem is important for applications like photo storytelling. The reported quantitative gains are encouraging, and the coherence modeling is a principled addition likely to matter for longer sequences. However, the practical impact is tempered by (i) relatively modest AMT gains over a strong RCN baseline, (ii) lack of significance testing, and (iii) dependence on preprocessing heuristics and domain-specific data. With stronger baselines, significance analysis, and ablations, the contribution would be more compelling.

Reasons to accept
- Addresses a relevant and underexplored problem (image-stream to sentence-sequence).
- Sensible, modular architecture combining content and coherence.
- Strong quantitative results across two sizeable datasets; consistent improvements over several baselines.
- Coherence modeling is principled and improves retrieval metrics, especially for longer sequences.

Reasons to reject
- Missing or limited comparisons to sequence-capable baselines (biLSTM, video-to-text adapted to image streams).
- Small advantage over RCN in user studies; no statistical significance tests reported.
- Heavy reliance on heuristic segmentation/summarization without robustness analysis.
- Coherence feature engineering and fusion choices seem ad hoc; limited ablations.

Questions for the authors
- Can you report statistical significance for automatic metrics and AMT pairwise preferences?
- How does CRCN compare to a biLSTM-based RNN (and/or GRU) in place of ReLU BRNN?
- Sensitivity to K, M, κ, and sequence length? Any failure modes when coreference parsing is noisy?
- Can you include a baseline that explicitly models sentence-level smoothness without entity grids?
- Will you release code and the processed blog datasets?

Overall
A nice and interesting contribution with a well-motivated architecture and promising results. With stronger experimental comparisons, significance testing, and ablations around the coherence and preprocessing components, this paper would make a solid addition to NIPS.
