

# Review for NIPS 2015

This paper proposes a novel approach to generating natural language descriptions for image streams rather than single images, which is a meaningful extension of the image captioning problem. The authors introduce a Coherent Recurrent Convolutional Network (CRCN) that integrates convolutional neural networks for image representation, bidirectional recurrent neural networks for sentence modeling, and an entity-based local coherence model to ensure smooth transitions between sentences. The key innovation lies in extending both input (image stream) and output (sentence sequence) dimensions, addressing a gap in the literature where most prior work focuses on single-image to single-sentence mapping.

The paper is well-structured and clearly written, making the problem statement and proposed solution accessible. The motivation is sound: users often capture multiple images during memorable events (e.g., visiting NYC), and describing the entire experience with a sequence of sentences would be more natural than individual image captions. The authors leverage blog posts as training data, which provides a natural source of image-text parallel sequences where images and text are carefully curated in a storytelling context.

The CRCN architecture is technically sound and well-justified. The integration of the entity-based coherence model to capture discourse relationships between sentences is a thoughtful addition that addresses a real challenge in multi-sentence generation. The experimental evaluation is comprehensive, including quantitative metrics (BLEU, CIDEr, METEOR, top-K recall) and user studies via Amazon Mechanical Turk, which demonstrates the superiority of their approach over several strong baselines.

However, the paper has several significant weaknesses that prevent it from being a strong main conference paper. First, the novelty claim is overstated: the problem of generating sequences of sentences from sequences of images is closely related to video captioning work (e.g., Rohrbach et al. 2014, "Translating Video Content to Natural Language Descriptions," ICCV 2014), which the authors fail to cite. This is a serious omission in the related work section, as Rohrbach et al. already address the generation of multiple sentences from video sequences, which is conceptually similar to the image stream problem.

Second, Equation (2) in the paper appears incorrect or implausible. The equation suggests that st depends only on ot, which implies that the network is not fully connected as intended. This could undermine the technical soundness of the model.

Third, the method for generating sentence sequences is primitive and ad-hoc: it retrieves one sentence per image and simply concatenates them, without any sophisticated generation mechanism. While the coherence model helps improve the sequence quality, this approach lacks the elegance of end-to-end generation methods common in the literature.

Fourth, the dataset description is insufficient and potentially problematic. The authors reuse blog data from [11] without proper attribution of their specific crawling methodology, and the details of their data collection process are vague. This could potentially breach anonymity or confidentiality requirements, as the paper does not specify how they obtained the blog posts or whether they have permission to use them.

Strengths: 
- Clear problem statement and motivation
- Well-written and well-organized paper
- Comprehensive evaluation with both quantitative metrics and user studies
- Technically sound architecture (aside from Equation 2)
- Practical application to real-world user-generated content

Weaknesses:
- Overstated novelty (fails to properly cite related video captioning work)
- Methodologically primitive sentence sequence generation approach
- Problem statement is closely related to prior video captioning research
- Insufficient dataset description (potential anonymity/confidentiality concerns)
- Equation (2) appears incorrect

The paper makes a modest contribution to the field by extending the image captioning problem to sequence generation and introducing a coherence model to improve fluency. While the results are promising, the paper does not sufficiently differentiate itself from prior work, and the technical contributions are incremental rather than groundbreaking. The dataset issues and questionable novelty claim are particularly concerning for a NIPS paper.

I recommend this paper for poster presentation rather than a main conference paper. It presents a reasonable extension of the image captioning problem with a technically sound approach, but the novelty is limited and the paper would benefit from a more thorough literature review and clearer differentiation from related work. The authors should address the Equation (2) issue and provide more detailed information about their dataset collection methodology before submission to a main conference.

Arguments for acceptance (poster): The paper addresses a meaningful problem extension, provides clear experimental validation, and introduces a coherence model that improves sequence fluency. The blog-based dataset is a practical and interesting source of training data.

Arguments against acceptance (main conference): The novelty claim is overstated, the related work is inadequately cited, the sentence generation method is primitive, and the dataset description is insufficient. The problem is closely related to existing video captioning research that should have been properly addressed.
