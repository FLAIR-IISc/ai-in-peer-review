

# Review of "Expressing an Image Stream with a Sequence of Natural Sentences"

This paper addresses the problem of generating sequences of natural language sentences from image streams, extending the traditional image captioning task (single image → single sentence) to sequences of images → sequences of sentences. The authors propose a Coherent Recurrent Convolutional Network (CRCN) that integrates convolutional neural networks for image representation, bidirectional recurrent neural networks for sentence modeling, and an entity-based local coherence model to ensure fluent transitions between sentences. The approach leverages blog posts as training data, extracting image-sentence pairs through text segmentation and summarization. The authors evaluate their method on NYC and Disneyland blog datasets with 20K+ blog posts and 140K+ images, showing improvements over baselines using both quantitative metrics (BLEU, CIDEr, top-K recall) and user studies via Amazon Mechanical Turk.

**Quality:** The paper presents a technically sound approach that builds upon existing multimodal neural network architectures. The CRCN model integrates several established techniques (CNNs, BRNNs, coherence modeling) in a novel way for sequence generation. However, the paper has several technical weaknesses. Equation (2) appears incorrect as it suggests st depends only on ot, which would imply a not fully connected network, contradicting the paper's description of a joint learning approach. The method for generating sentence sequences (retrieving K nearest sentences per image and simple concatenation) is described as "primitive and ad-hoc" in the review criteria, and the paper does not adequately justify why sentence retrieval is preferable to more direct generation approaches. The dataset description is insufficient, with unclear details about the blog crawling methodology and reuse of blog data from [11], raising potential anonymity/confidentiality concerns. While the evaluation is comprehensive with both quantitative and qualitative metrics, the paper does not adequately address the limitations of their approach or compare with the most relevant prior work.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-written and easy to follow. The problem statement is clear, and the architecture is well-organized with good visualizations. The authors provide sufficient technical detail for reproduction, though the equation (2) issue and insufficient dataset description are notable weaknesses. The writing is professional and accessible to the NIPS audience.

**Originality:** The paper's core contribution is extending image captioning to image streams with sentence sequences. However, the paper fails to adequately cite and distinguish itself from significant prior work. The related work section omits key papers like Rohrbach et al. (2014) on video captioning and other multi-sentence description works. The problem is closely related to existing video captioning research (e.g., Rohrbach et al. 2014, Translating Video Content to Natural Language Descriptions), but the paper does not sufficiently address how their approach differs from or improves upon this prior work. The CRCN architecture combines established components but doesn't present a sufficiently novel integration for a conference paper.

**Significance:** The problem of generating natural language descriptions for image streams is significant and relevant to applications like photo album summarization. However, the paper's approach is limited in that it relies on retrieval rather than generation, and the dataset is restricted to tourism blogs without clear generalizability. The performance gains over baselines are modest (particularly in language metrics), and the paper doesn't convincingly demonstrate that the coherence model provides substantial additional value beyond simple sentence concatenation. The user studies show a clear preference for the CRCN approach, but the effect size is small for short sequences, and the significance of the contribution is somewhat diminished by the lack of clear comparison to more relevant prior work.

**Strengths:** The paper is well-organized and clearly written. The problem statement is compelling, and the CRCN model is technically sound. The evaluation with both quantitative metrics and user studies is comprehensive. The approach to using blog posts as training data is practical and well-justified.

**Weaknesses:** Critical prior work (Rohrbach et al. 2014) is not cited. The equation (2) appears incorrect. The sentence generation method is primitive. The dataset description is insufficient. The paper does not sufficiently differentiate itself from existing video captioning work.

**Recommendation:** Accept as a poster paper. The paper presents a reasonable extension of image captioning to sequence generation with a well-designed model and evaluation. However, the lack of proper citation to key prior work, the technical issue with equation (2), and the limited novelty compared to existing video captioning research prevent it from being accepted as a spotlight or oral presentation. The paper would benefit from a more thorough comparison with related video captioning work and addressing the technical issues before acceptance in a main conference track. As a poster, it would provide a reasonable contribution to the field while highlighting areas for future work.
