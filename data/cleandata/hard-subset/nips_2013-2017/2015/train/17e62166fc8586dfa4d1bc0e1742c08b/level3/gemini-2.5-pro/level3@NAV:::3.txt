This paper introduces an approach for generating a sequence of natural sentences from a stream of images, extending the common single image-to-sentence task. The authors propose a Coherent Recurrent Convolutional Network (CRCN), which integrates three main components: a CNN for image representations, a bidirectional RNN for modeling sentence sequences, and an entity-based local coherence model to ensure fluent textual transitions. The model is trained on data from blog posts, framing the generation problem as a retrieval and ranking task. This work is related to existing research in image captioning and video description, but distinguishes itself by focusing on potentially disjointed image streams where explicit coherence modeling is particularly challenging.

This is an interesting and well-motivated piece of work that addresses a novel and practical problem. The proposed CRCN architecture is a sensible and thoughtful combination of strong components to tackle this task. A key strength of the paper is its quantitative evaluation; the results reported in Table 1 are quite good, showing that the model outperforms several powerful baselines across multiple language and retrieval metrics, which convincingly demonstrates the overall framework's effectiveness.

However, the paper has a few weaknesses that temper its impact. Firstly, the literature review is incomplete, notably missing several related video-to-sentence works (e.g., Xu et al., "Jointly modeling deep video and compositional text...", AAAI 2015) that also map sequential visual input to text. Discussing these would help to better contextualize the paper's novelty. Secondly, the validation for the main contribution—the coherence model—is not fully convincing. The user study results (Table 2) show that the full CRCN model performs quite similarly to the RCN baseline, suggesting that the coherence component provides only a weak advantage in human perception. The claim that this gap widens for longer sequences is interesting but needs more thorough validation. Finally, to support the claims of improvement, a statistical significance test is crucial. Without it, it is difficult to assess whether the reported gains of CRCN over the RCN baseline are reliable or simply a result of experimental variance.

Overall, the paper presents a nice contribution to an important problem, but the claims regarding the novel coherence component need to be supported by more rigorous analysis.
