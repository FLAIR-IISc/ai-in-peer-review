

This paper proposes a novel approach for generating natural language descriptions from image streams (sequences of images) rather than single images, introducing a Coherent Recurrent Convolutional Network (CRCN) that integrates convolutional neural networks for image representation, bidirectional recurrent neural networks for sentence modeling, and an entity-based local coherence model for fluent transitions between sentences. The work extends the line of research on image captioning (e.g., Karpathy et al., 2015; Vinyals et al., 2015) by addressing a more natural scenario where users capture sequences of images during memorable events, rather than isolated shots. The authors leverage blog posts as a source of text-image parallel data, which is a clever and scalable approach to obtaining training data without manual annotation.

The paper demonstrates strong technical quality through comprehensive experiments on two large datasets (NYC and Disneyland with 140K images), showing significant improvements over several state-of-the-art baselines. The quantitative results (Table 1) show clear advantages in retrieval metrics (top-K recall and median rank), with CRCN outperforming baselines by substantial margins (e.g., 25.3% higher R@1 than the next best method). The ablation studies effectively demonstrate the contribution of each component, particularly showing that the coherence model is crucial for retrieval performance.

The work is highly original in its problem formulation, as the paper correctly identifies that most prior work has focused on single-image-single-sentence generation, while users typically capture sequences of images. The CRCN architecture represents a meaningful extension that addresses the challenge of maintaining coherence across multiple sentences describing sequential images. However, the literature review misses several relevant video-to-sentence works that could have better positioned this contribution, particularly "Jointly modeling deep video and compositional text to bridge vision and language" (Xu et al., 2015) and "Translating Video Content to Natural Language Descriptions" (Rohrbach et al., 2015), which address similar sequence generation problems.

While the quantitative results are compelling, the user study results (Table 2) reveal a more nuanced picture. The preference for CRCN over the RCN baseline (which lacks the coherence model) is modest (57.5% vs 54.7%), and the difference becomes significant only when longer image sequences are used (8-10 images). This suggests that the coherence component provides limited improvement for shorter sequences, raising questions about the necessity of the complex coherence model for practical applications. The paper would benefit from statistical significance testing (e.g., t-tests) to determine whether the reported improvements are reliable, as the absence of such analysis weakens the confidence in the results.

The paper is generally well-written and clearly organized, with appropriate figures and a logical flow from problem statement to experiments. The blog preprocessing pipeline is well-justified, and the use of paragraph vectors for text representation is sound. However, the coherence model description could be clearer - it's not entirely evident how the entity grid and transition patterns directly contribute to sentence fluency beyond what the BRNN might capture.

The significance of this work is moderate. While the problem of describing photo streams is practical and the approach demonstrates clear technical advances, the improvement over the RCN baseline is not dramatic for typical use cases (shorter sequences), and the method is limited to domains with sufficient blog data. The unsupervised nature of the approach (applicable to any domain with blog posts) is a strength, but the need for large-scale blog datasets may limit its applicability to other scenarios.

Overall, this is a technically sound and interesting contribution that addresses a meaningful gap in the literature. The CRCN architecture is well-designed, the experiments are comprehensive, and the problem formulation is novel. However, the modest user study results and lack of statistical significance testing for the quantitative improvements slightly reduce the confidence in the claimed advantages. The paper would be strengthened by addressing the missing literature, providing significance tests, and more clearly demonstrating the necessity of the coherence model for practical applications.

Pro: Novel problem formulation addressing the gap between single-image captioning and natural user behavior with image sequences; strong quantitative results on large datasets; clever use of blog posts as training data; well-designed architecture with ablation studies.

Con: User study results show only modest advantage over baseline; missing key related works in literature review; lack of statistical significance testing for improvements; coherence model's contribution appears limited for shorter sequences.
