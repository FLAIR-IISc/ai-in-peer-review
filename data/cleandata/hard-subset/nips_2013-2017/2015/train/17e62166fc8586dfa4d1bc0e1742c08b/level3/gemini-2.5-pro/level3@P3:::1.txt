### Summary

This paper addresses the problem of generating a sequence of natural sentences to describe a stream of images. The authors propose a multimodal architecture called the Coherent Recurrent Convolutional Network (CRCN), which integrates three components: a Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) to extract image features, a bidirectional Recurrent Neural Network (BRNN) to model the flow of content in a sentence sequence, and an entity-based local coherence model to ensure smooth transitions between sentences. The overall approach frames the problem as a retrieval task. For a given query image stream, candidate sentence sequences are constructed by finding nearest-neighbor images in the training set and concatenating their associated sentences. The CRCN is then used to rank these candidates and select the one with the highest compatibility score. The model is trained on a large dataset of blog posts, where image-text pairings are automatically extracted. The authors demonstrate through quantitative metrics (BLEU, R@K) and user studies that their approach outperforms several baseline methods.

The work is a logical extension of single image-to-sentence generation and is closely related to the task of video description, where a sequence of visual inputs (frames) is mapped to a textual output. However, the authors argue that image streams from photo collections can have sharper content changes than video, making an explicit coherence model more critical.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Quality:**

*   **Strengths:** The paper is technically sound for the most part. The proposed CRCN model is a well-designed architecture that sensibly combines established components (CNNs, RNNs) with a more novel element for this task: the explicit modeling of discourse coherence via an entity grid. This is a strong contribution, as it directly addresses the challenge of making the generated text read as a coherent paragraph rather than a list of disjointed captions. The experimental evaluation is thorough, including both automatic metrics and human judgments, and the results convincingly show the benefit of the proposed model over baselines.
*   **Weaknesses:**
    1.  There appears to be a significant error or lack of clarity in Equation (2): `Wf2Wf1[O|q] = [S|g]`. This formulation suggests that each output vector `st` is a linear transformation of only the corresponding input vector `ot` (i.e., `st = Wf2*Wf1*ot`). This implies that the final compatibility score between an image and a sentence at a specific position `t` depends only on the text representation at that same position, without influence from other parts of the sequence at this final mapping stage. This seems to contradict the purpose of using a recurrent model and should be clarified or corrected.
    2.  The method for generating candidate sentence sequences is reasonable but somewhat primitive. By retrieving sentences for each image independently and then simply concatenating them, the approach is constrained to sentences that already exist in the training corpus. A fully generative model might offer more flexibility and novelty.

**Clarity:**

*   **Strengths:** The paper is very well-written and easy to follow. The problem statement is clearly motivated, the proposed architecture is explained with helpful diagrams, and the overall narrative is coherent. The authors do an excellent job of guiding the reader through their methodology and experiments.

**Originality:**

*   **Strengths:** The primary novelty lies in the integration of an explicit, entity-based coherence model into a deep learning framework for multi-sentence description. This successfully bridges ideas from computational linguistics (discourse coherence) with recent trends in vision-and-language models.
*   **Weaknesses:** The paper's claim to be the "first to address the problem of expressing image streams with sentence sequences" is a strong overstatement and overlooks a significant body of highly relevant prior work. The problem is conceptually almost identical to video description, where a sequence of frames is described with sentences. The paper fails to cite or discuss seminal work in this area, such as:
    *   Rohrbach et al., "A Dataset for Movie Description," CoRR abs/1412.4723, 2014.
    *   Guadarrama et al., "YouTube2Text: Recognizing and Describing Arbitrary Activities in Videos," ICCV 2013.
    *   Venugopalan et al., "Translating Videos to Natural Language Using Deep Recurrent Neural Networks," NAACL 2015.
    This omission significantly weakens the paper's positioning and claims of novelty. While the authors mention video-sentence work in passing, they do not engage with the specific models or acknowledge the deep similarities in the task formulation.

**Significance:**

*   **Strengths:** The problem is significant and timely. As automated content generation becomes more sophisticated, moving from single captions to coherent, multi-sentence paragraphs is an important and challenging frontier. The ideas presented here, particularly the use of a coherence model, could be influential for future work in this area and in related fields like video summarization and story generation.
*   **Weaknesses:** The insufficient description of the dataset collection process is a concern for reproducibility. The authors state they "reuse the blog data of Disneyland from the dataset of [11]" and use the "same crawling method" for the NYC data. However, the details of this method are not provided in this paper, making it difficult for others to replicate the work or build upon the dataset. Furthermore, using data from personal blogs without discussing the methods for ensuring anonymity or addressing potential copyright issues is a minor but notable oversight.

### Arguments for Acceptance:

1.  The paper tackles an important and challenging problem that is a natural next step for the vision-and-language community.
2.  The core technical idea of explicitly modeling discourse coherence with an entity-based model within a neural architecture is novel and well-motivated.
3.  The paper is exceptionally clear, well-written, and presents a complete piece of work with solid experimental validation.
4.  The empirical results are strong and demonstrate the value of the proposed CRCN model, especially the coherence component.

### Arguments for Rejection:

1.  The paper makes an unsubstantiated claim of being the first to tackle this problem and fails to cite or adequately discuss highly relevant and established work in video description. This is a major scholarly weakness.
2.  Equation (2) appears to be incorrect or is at least poorly explained, raising questions about a key part of the model's implementation.
3.  The dataset description is insufficient for reproducibility and does not address potential privacy/confidentiality concerns.

### Recommendation:

I recommend this paper be accepted as a poster. The core contribution—integrating an explicit coherence model into a framework for describing image streams—is valuable and the paper is very clearly presented. The strengths outweigh the weaknesses, but the weaknesses are significant enough to preclude a higher recommendation. The authors should be strongly encouraged to revise the paper to (1) properly position their work within the context of video description literature, (2) clarify or correct Equation (2), and (3) provide more detail on their dataset collection methodology.
