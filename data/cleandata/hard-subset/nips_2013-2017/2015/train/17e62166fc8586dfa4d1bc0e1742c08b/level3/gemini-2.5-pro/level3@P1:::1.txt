### Summary

This paper addresses the novel problem of generating a sequence of natural language sentences to describe a stream of images. The authors propose a multimodal architecture called the Coherent Recurrent Convolutional Network (CRCN) to tackle this task. The model integrates three components: a Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) to extract image features, a Bidirectional Recurrent Neural Network (BRNN) to model the flow of content in the sentence sequence, and an entity-based local coherence model to ensure smooth and logical transitions between sentences. The model is trained on a large dataset of blog posts, which serve as a source of parallel image-stream and text-sequence data. The approach works by first retrieving candidate sentences for each image in a query stream and then using the trained CRCN to rank and select the best overall sequence. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their method through quantitative evaluations using metrics like BLEU and R@K, as well as a user study on Amazon Mechanical Turk, showing improvements over several baseline methods adapted from single-image captioning.

The work is a natural extension of the popular single-image-to-sentence task and shares significant conceptual similarities with the task of video description, where a sequence of frames is described by one or more sentences. While the paper positions itself as the first to tackle image streams, its connection to the extensive literature on video captioning and multi-sentence description is highly relevant and warrants more thorough discussion.

### Quality and Clarity

The paper is well-written and clearly structured. The problem statement is well-motivated, and the proposed CRCN architecture is explained in a comprehensible manner. The experiments are thorough, including both automatic metrics and human evaluations, which provides a convincing case for the model's performance.

However, there are some concerns regarding the technical soundness and clarity of the model description:

1.  **Equation (2)** appears problematic. The equation `Wf2*Wf1*[O|q] = [S|g]` implies a column-wise mapping where the `t`-th column of the output, `st`, is computed solely from the `t`-th column of the input, `ot` (i.e., `st = Wf2*Wf1*ot`). This suggests that the feature vector for the `t`-th sentence is independent of all other sentences in the sequence (`oj` for `j != t`) and the global coherence vector `q`. This structure does not seem to represent a fully connected layer where all outputs depend on all inputs, which seems necessary for jointly modeling the sequence. This point requires clarification, as it undermines a key part of the model's architecture.

2.  The description of the **dataset collection and pre-processing** is somewhat brief. Crucial details regarding the blog crawling process and the heuristics for text segmentation are not fully specified, which could hinder reproducibility. More importantly, the paper states that it reuses the Disneyland blog dataset from [11]. If this dataset is not a standard, publicly available benchmark, its use could potentially compromise the double-blind review process, as it may reveal the authors' identity.

### Originality and Significance

The main contributions of the paper are: (1) formulating the problem of describing an image stream with a sentence sequence, (2) proposing the CRCN architecture that explicitly models coherence, and (3) demonstrating its effectiveness on a large-scale blog dataset.

**Strengths:**

*   **Problem Formulation:** The problem is significant and timely, moving beyond single images to more complex, structured visual inputs that better reflect real-world scenarios like photo albums or travelogues.
*   **Coherence Modeling:** The integration of an explicit, entity-based coherence model [1] is a novel and valuable contribution. Most prior work on captioning relies on an RNN's implicit ability to capture sequence structure. Explicitly modeling discourse-level phenomena like entity transitions is a strong and original idea that effectively improves the fluency of the generated paragraph.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Relation to Prior Work:** The paper does not adequately situate itself within the context of video description research. The task of describing an ordered image stream is conceptually almost identical to describing a video (a sequence of frames). Significant prior work on generating single or multiple sentences for videos (e.g., Rohrbach et al., ICCV 2013; Guadarrama et al., ICCV 2013; Rohrbach et al., GCPR 2014; Venugopalan et al., ICCV 2015) is either not cited or not discussed in sufficient detail. This omission tends to overstate the novelty of the problem formulation.
*   **Retrieval-based Approach:** The method is not a generative model in the strictest sense. It retrieves existing sentences from a training corpus and combines them. While ranking these combinations with a sophisticated model is a valid approach, the underlying mechanism of concatenating sentences retrieved for individual images is somewhat primitive and ad-hoc. This limits the model's ability to generate truly novel descriptions tailored to the specific nuances of an image stream.

### Overall Recommendation

This paper addresses an interesting and important problem with a novel architectural component for modeling coherence. The approach is sound in principle, and the experimental results are promising. The paper is also very clearly written.

However, the contribution is diminished by a lack of engagement with highly relevant prior work in video description, a potential technical flaw in a core model equation, and a retrieval-based generation process that feels less ambitious than the problem framing suggests.

Despite these weaknesses, the paper's strengths—particularly the novel problem formulation and the integration of the coherence model—make it a noteworthy contribution. I believe the work is of interest to the NIPS community but requires revision to address the issues raised.

**Arguments for acceptance:**
*   Addresses a novel and significant problem.
*   The CRCN model's use of an explicit entity-based coherence model is an original contribution.
*   The paper is well-written and the empirical evaluation is thorough.

**Arguments for rejection:**
*   Fails to adequately discuss highly relevant prior work in video description.
*   Contains a potentially incorrect formulation in Equation (2) that needs clarification.
*   The sentence generation process is a simple retrieval-and-ranking method, not fully generative.
*   Potential issues with dataset description and adherence to double-blind review protocol.

**Recommendation:** Acceptable as a poster paper, contingent on the authors addressing the technical clarification of Equation (2) and improving the discussion of related work.
