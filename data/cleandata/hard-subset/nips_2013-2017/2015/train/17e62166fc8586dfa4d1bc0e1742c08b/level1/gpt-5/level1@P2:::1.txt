Summary and relation to prior work
The paper tackles the underexplored problem of describing an image stream with a coherent sequence of natural sentences. Unlike most captioning work that maps a single image to a single sentence, the authors extend both input and output to sequences. The proposed coherent recurrent convolutional network (CRCN) combines (i) CNN image features (VGG fc7), (ii) a bidirectional RNN over sentence representations (doc2vec) to model content flow, and (iii) an entity-grid based local coherence model (Barzilay & Lapata, 2008) to favor fluent, entity-consistent transitions across sentences. Training uses a max-margin retrieval objective over image-stream/sentence-sequence pairs harvested from travel blog posts (NYC and Disneyland), where text is aligned to images via paragraph proximity heuristics and summarized via LSA. At test time, the method retrieves candidate sentences per image via K-NN on CNN features, then assembles and ranks sequence candidates using a divide-and-conquer beam search guided by the learned compatibility function. The approach is compared to strong single-image captioning/retrieval baselines (e.g., Karpathy & Fei-Fei 2015; Kiros et al. 2014; Vinyals et al. 2015) adapted by concatenation, as well as ablations, using BLEU/CIDEr/METEOR, retrieval metrics, and AMT preferences.

The idea builds on multimodal retrieval/generation (e.g., [9,12,30]) and video-to-sentence models (e.g., Donahue et al. 2015), but differs by (a) handling image streams and multi-sentence outputs, and (b) explicitly integrating a classical local coherence model [1] with neural content-flow modeling. It is also related to the paragraph-to-image-stream retrieval of Kim et al. (2015), but addresses the opposite direction.

Strengths
- Problem novelty and motivation are clear; handling coherent multi-sentence descriptions for image streams is a natural next step beyond single-image captioning.
- Architectural novelty: a principled integration of BRNN content flow with entity-grid coherence, learned end-to-end for sequence-level scoring. The BRNN encodes context in both directions; the coherence model enforces entity-level transitions known to correlate with discourse quality.
- Data scale and practicality: leveraging large, weakly aligned blog corpora is attractive; the method is domain-agnostic in principle.
- Empirical gains: consistent improvements over adapted single-image baselines on retrieval metrics; AMT studies indicate user preference, especially for longer sequences, supporting the value of coherence modeling.
- Ablations (1NN, RCN vs CRCN) help attribute gains to the coherence component.

Weaknesses
- Data alignment quality is not validated. The text-to-image association (distance heuristics) and LSA summarization can be noisy; no quantitative or human assessment of alignment/summarization accuracy is provided, leaving uncertainty about training signal quality and evaluation ground truth.
- Evaluation protocol needs clearer specification. Since candidates come from the training set and ground-truth summaries are from held-out blogs, standard BLEU/CIDEr/METEOR are known to be brittle for cross-sentence paraphrase at paragraph level. Retrieval metrics (R@K, median rank) are described but it is unclear what constitutes “ground truth” in the ranking when exact sentences are absent in candidates. Please clarify how positives/negatives are formed at test time.
- Modeling detail: the final score Skl sums per-position text-image dot products and adds a global coherence vector dot product with each image feature. The rationale for dotting the same coherence vector with every image feature (thus counted N times) is not well justified; an alternative would be a separate scalar coherence term or a gating that conditions coherence on sequence-level image features.
- Comparisons may favor the proposed method. Baselines are adapted by concatenating single-image sentences without sequence-level modeling. A stronger sequence baseline (e.g., training an LSTM/GRU over sentence embeddings with a sequence-level ranking loss, or a CRF over per-position sentence candidates with pairwise potentials) would isolate the contribution of the entity-grid component more convincingly.
- Generalization is demonstrated only on two tourism datasets. No evidence is shown for other domains (e.g., event albums, instructional photo sets), despite the “unsupervised” claim.

Quality
- Generally sound, with a coherent training objective and reasonable optimization choices. The divide-and-conquer beam search is plausible, but lacks analysis of suboptimality and runtime.
- Evidence supports the claims directionally, but some metrics (BLEU/CIDEr over paragraphs drawn from different corpora) are of limited interpretability. AMT results help, but significance testing and inter-annotator agreement are not reported.

Clarity
- The paper is mostly well organized. The data pipeline, BRNN equations, and entity-grid construction are described clearly.
- Ambiguities: evaluation protocol for retrieval metrics; justification of the coherence-image interaction in Eq. (3); details of candidate search (beam width, halving depth Q) and computational cost.
- Minor notational issues (reusing symbols, shape descriptions) could be tightened.

Originality
- The problem framing (image-stream → sentence-sequence) and the integration of neural content-flow with a classical coherence model are novel relative to contemporaneous work.
- Retrieval-based construction of paragraphs from mined sentences is a pragmatic design distinct from word-level generation.

Significance
- Addresses a meaningful step toward narrative understanding and generation. Gains on user preference suggest practical utility for consumer photo streams.
- Broader impact would be stronger with cleaner evaluation, stronger sequence baselines, and demonstrations beyond tourism blogs.

Pros for acceptance
- Novel task and architecture; principled incorporation of discourse coherence.
- Empirical improvements and user preference, especially on longer sequences.
- Scalable training from weakly aligned web data; comprehensive ablations.

Cons for acceptance
- Unclear/fragile evaluation protocol; language metrics may not reflect quality.
- No validation of alignment/summarization quality; potential noise undermines conclusions.
- Coherence term’s interaction with images is under-justified; stronger sequence-level baselines are missing.
- Limited domain evidence; runtime and search details not analyzed.

Suggestions for improvement
- Clarify test-time retrieval metrics and provide statistical significance for AMT; report inter-annotator agreement.
- Quantify alignment/summarization accuracy on a small annotated subset.
- Compare against a sequence model baseline without entity grids (e.g., LSTM/GRU over sentence embeddings trained with the same structured loss) and a model with pairwise sentence-transition potentials.
- Explore alternative scoring where coherence contributes as a sequence-level term, not repeatedly per image, and ablate the coherence history length.
- Report sensitivity to K, M, Q, and runtime.
- Demonstrate on at least one additional domain to support generality.

Overall assessment
A timely and interesting contribution with a thoughtful integration of discourse coherence into multimodal sequence retrieval. While promising, the paper would benefit from clearer evaluation methodology and stronger baselines. With revisions addressing these concerns, it could make a solid addition to NIPS. I lean borderline accept based on novelty and user-study-backed improvements.
