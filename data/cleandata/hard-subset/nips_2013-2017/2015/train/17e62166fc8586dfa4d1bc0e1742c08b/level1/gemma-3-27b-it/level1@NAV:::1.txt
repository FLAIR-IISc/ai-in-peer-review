## Review of "Expressing an Image Stream with a Sequence of Natural Sentences"

This paper tackles the challenging problem of generating a coherent sequence of natural language sentences to describe a stream of images, extending beyond the typical single image-single sentence captioning task. The authors propose a Coherent Recurrent Convolutional Network (CRCN), integrating CNNs for image representation, BRNNs for language modeling, and a local coherence model to ensure fluency between sentences. They leverage a large corpus of blog posts with associated images as training data and demonstrate improved performance over several baselines, using both quantitative metrics (BLEU, Recall) and user studies on Amazon Mechanical Turk.

The work builds upon a substantial body of research in image captioning (e.g., [5, 8, 9, 12, 14, 15, 26, 21, 30]) and addresses a natural progression: handling sequential visual input.  The extension to sequential *output* (multiple sentences) is a key novelty. The authors correctly situate their work within the existing literature, citing relevant approaches like retrieval-based methods [6, 8, 21, 26], video-sentence models [23, 32], and multimodal architectures [5, 9, 30]. The use of a coherence model is particularly important, addressing a crucial gap in previous work, as highlighted by the authors.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** Extending image captioning to sequences of both images and sentences is a meaningful advancement.
* **Technical Soundness:** The proposed CRCN architecture appears well-designed, integrating established components (CNNs, BRNNs) with a novel coherence model. The training process and loss function are clearly explained.
* **Empirical Validation:**  The experiments on blog data (NYC and Disneyland) are comprehensive, including quantitative evaluations and user studies, providing strong evidence of the method’s effectiveness.
* **Data Source:** Leveraging blog posts is a clever approach to obtaining text-image parallel data suitable for this task. 

**Weaknesses:**

* **Baseline Selection:**  While several baselines are included, their implementation details are limited.  It's difficult to fully assess the comparative advantage of CRCN without knowing specifics of the baselines’ adaptation to the sequential output setting. 
* **Coherence Model Complexity:** The description of the coherence model based on parse trees and entity grids is a bit dense. A more intuitive explanation and potential ablation studies could strengthen the analysis of its contribution.
* **Limited Error Analysis:**  The paper lacks a detailed analysis of failure cases. Understanding *where* the model struggles would be valuable for future research.




**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* Addresses an important and under-explored problem in multimodal understanding.
* Demonstrates a technically sound and novel approach with strong empirical results.
* The use of user studies adds significant value to the evaluation.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The detailed implementation of baselines and error analysis are lacking.
* The coherence model explanation could be improved for clarity.


**Overall:**

I believe this paper represents a significant contribution to the field of multimodal understanding. While there are minor areas for improvement, the novelty, technical soundness, and strong empirical validation make it a worthy candidate for acceptance to NIPS. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions, particularly regarding baseline details and clarification of the coherence model.
