This paper addresses the problem of generating a sequence of sentences to describe an image stream, extending the standard image captioning task from single images/sentences to streams. The proposed approach, CRCN, combines CNNs for image feature extraction, BRNNs for language modeling, and a local coherence model to ensure fluency between sentences. The authors leverage blog posts as a text-image parallel corpus, a practical and relatively unexplored resource for this task.

The paper is well-motivated and tackles a natural extension of existing image captioning work. Extending to sequential data is a logical progression, and the blog post dataset provides a realistic, albeit noisy, source of training data. The CRCN architecture is reasonable, building on established techniques (CNNs, RNNs) and adding a coherence model which is crucial for multi-sentence generation. The quantitative and qualitative results presented demonstrate a clear improvement over baseline methods, and the user studies provide further evidence of the effectiveness of the proposed approach.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** Extending image captioning to image *streams* is a significant step beyond the existing literature. The problem itself is novel and relevant, especially considering the increasing prevalence of image streams captured by users.
* **Technical Soundness:** The proposed CRCN architecture is well-defined and integrates established techniques in a sensible manner. The inclusion of a coherence model is particularly important and a key contribution.
* **Dataset:** Utilizing blog posts as a training source is a clever solution to the lack of large-scale text-image stream datasets. The pre-processing steps described are reasonable.
* **Experimental Evaluation:** The authors provide a comprehensive evaluation, including quantitative metrics (BLEU, Recall) and user studies via AMT. Comparing against several baselines strengthens the validity of the results. The ablation study (comparing CRCN, RCN, and 1NN) is also valuable for understanding the contribution of each component.
* **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The figures (especially Fig. 2) effectively illustrate the architecture and data flow.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Coherence Model Detail:** While the paper introduces a local coherence model, the explanation of *how* coherence is actually measured and incorporated into the scoring function is somewhat lacking. Specifically, how the 64-dimensional coherence representation impacts the final compatibility score (Eq. 3) could be clarified. Is it simply concatenated? Weighted?
* **Data Preprocessing Caveats:** The text summarization step using LSA could introduce significant information loss and bias. While acknowledged as a limitation, the potential impact on performance isn't fully explored. A comparison with other summarization techniques might be beneficial.
* **Baseline Limitations:** The baselines, while reasonable, are somewhat dated or adapted from single-image captioning. More recent and sophisticated sequence-to-sequence models or attention mechanisms specifically designed for longer sequences could provide a stronger comparison. Specifically, transformers would be a useful benchmark.
* **Limited Error Analysis:** While qualitative examples are provided, a more thorough error analysis would be valuable. Identifying common failure cases could provide insights for future improvement. Are errors primarily due to inaccurate image representation, poor coherence modeling, or issues with the training data?
* **Scalability:** The approximate search strategy using beam search is necessary for practical implementation, but its impact on performance isn't thoroughly discussed. A more detailed analysis of the trade-off between search efficiency and retrieval accuracy would be helpful.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper tackles a novel and important problem in image captioning.
* The proposed CRCN architecture is technically sound and well-motivated.
* The experimental results demonstrate a clear improvement over existing methods.
* The use of blog posts as a training source is a creative and practical solution.
* The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the approach.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The description of the coherence model is somewhat lacking in detail.
* The pre-processing steps (especially text summarization) introduce potential biases.
* The baseline comparisons could be stronger with more recent sequence-to-sequence models.
* Limited error analysis prevents a deeper understanding of the model's weaknesses.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper is a strong contribution to the field of image captioning. The problem is novel and relevant, the proposed approach is well-designed, and the experimental results are convincing. While there are some minor weaknesses, they do not detract significantly from the overall quality of the work. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions, focusing on clarifying the coherence model implementation, addressing the potential biases introduced by the text summarization step, and considering the inclusion of stronger baseline comparisons (e.g., using transformer-based models). A more thorough error analysis would also be beneficial.
