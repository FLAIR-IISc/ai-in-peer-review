- Proposes Coherent Recurrent Convolutional Network (CRCN) to generate sentence sequences from image streams, combining CNNs, BRNNs, and a local coherence model.  
- Leverages blog post image-text pairs as a novel training corpus for sequence captioning.  
- Demonstrates improvements over baselines using automatic metrics (BLEU, Recall) and human evaluation (AMT).  
- Experimental setup is reasonably thorough with clear loss function and training details.  
- Technique builds on established components; the combination is novel but each component is well-known (incremental novelty).  
- Limited discussion of limitations such as dataset-induced biases and sensitivity to hyperparameters (e.g., κ).  
- Results presentation is clear but would benefit from more error analysis.  
- Paper is generally well-written and organized, with helpful architecture diagram and appropriate notation.  
- Explanations of some components (coherence model, training specifics) are overly verbose or could be more focused.  
- Section 3.5 (retrieval process) is hand-wavy and needs clearer explanation.  
- Originality: extends image captioning to streams and integrates coherence modeling within a deep framework, addressing an under-explored problem.  
- Insufficient differentiation and comparison to related video-captioning work; the “first to address” claim is overstated.  
- Significance: relevant practical application (photo albums, user experience summarization) with moderate impact and promising transfer of blog-data approach.  
- AMT user-study results strongly support practical usefulness.  
- Arguments for acceptance: novel problem focus, solid CRCN implementation, strong quantitative and qualitative results, clever dataset usage, positive user studies.  
- Arguments against acceptance: incremental novelty, limited limitations discussion, weak comparison to video captioning literature, unclear retrieval description, some sections need concision.  
- Overall recommendation: accept with minor revisions to expand comparisons to video captioning, discuss limitations and biases, clarify retrieval procedure, and add error analysis.
