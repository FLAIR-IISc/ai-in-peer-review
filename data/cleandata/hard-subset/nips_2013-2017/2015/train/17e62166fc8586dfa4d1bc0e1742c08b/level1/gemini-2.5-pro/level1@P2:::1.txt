This paper tackles the novel and interesting problem of generating a sequence of natural language sentences to describe a stream of images. The authors correctly identify this as a logical, albeit challenging, next step beyond the well-studied task of single-image captioning. The motivation is compelling: users often capture experiences as a series of photos, and a coherent narrative is a more natural description than a set of disjointed single-sentence captions.

To address this, the authors propose the Coherent Recurrent Convolutional Network (CRCN), a multimodal architecture that integrates three key components. First, a standard Convolutional Neural Network (CNN, specifically VGGNet) is used to extract visual features for each image. Second, a Bidirectional Recurrent Neural Network (BRNN) models the sequential nature of the text, using paragraph vectors to represent sentences and capture the flow of content. Third, and most notably, the model incorporates an entity-based local coherence model, based on the prior work of Barzilay and Lapata (2008), to explicitly model the smooth transition of discourse entities (e.g., nouns) across sentences, thereby improving fluency. The model is trained on a large dataset ingeniously collected from blog posts, using a max-margin objective to learn a compatibility score between image and sentence sequences. At test time, the task is framed as a retrieval problem, where a near-optimal sentence sequence is found from a combinatorial candidate space using a divide-and-conquer search strategy.

This work builds upon the popular CNN+RNN paradigm for image captioning (e.g., Karpathy et al., 2015; Vinyals et al., 2015) but significantly extends it to handle sequences on both the input and output side. The key novelty lies in identifying the need for and integrating an explicit coherence model to handle the potential discontinuities in image streams, distinguishing it from related work in video description.

### Quality

The paper is of high technical quality. The proposed architecture is sound, and the integration of the three components is well-motivated. The combination of a learned sequence model (BRNN) with a more structured, rule-based NLP model for coherence is a clever design choice that directly addresses a key challenge of the task. The claims are well-supported by a comprehensive set of experiments. The authors create two new, large-scale datasets for this task and evaluate their method using a wide range of automatic metrics (BLEU, CIDEr, METEOR, and retrieval metrics) as well as a human evaluation study on Amazon Mechanical Turk. The ablation study, comparing the full CRCN model to variants without the coherence model (RCN) and without the sequence model (1NN), clearly demonstrates the contribution of each component. The experimental setup is thorough and the results are convincing.

One potential weakness lies in the inference procedure described in Section 3.5. Given an input image stream, the model must search over an exponentially large space of candidate sentence sequences (K^N). The proposed divide-and-conquer beam search is a necessary heuristic, but the claim that it "achieves almost optimal solutions" is strong and lacks rigorous justification. More analysis on the sensitivity of this search to its parameters (M, Q) and its performance relative to the (intractable) optimal solution would strengthen the paper. Additionally, the data pre-processing pipeline relies on heuristics (e.g., assigning text to the nearest image) which may introduce noise into the training data; a brief discussion of the potential impact of this noise would be welcome.

### Clarity

The paper is clearly written and well-organized. The problem is well-defined, and the motivation is presented effectively in the introduction. Figure 2 provides a helpful overview of the data processing pipeline and the model architecture. The individual components of the CRCN are explained in sufficient detail. The training objective and inference process are also laid out logically. The paper provides enough information that an expert reader could likely reproduce the main results.

### Originality

The primary contribution of this work is the formulation of a new and important problem: expressing an image stream with a sentence sequence. To the best of my knowledge, this is the first paper to address this specific task. While the constituent parts of the model (CNNs, BRNNs, entity-grid coherence models) are based on prior work, their combination into a unified framework for this novel task is original. The idea of leveraging blog posts as a source of weakly-supervised training data is also a valuable and practical contribution that enables research on this problem. The work clearly delineates how it differs from and extends previous work in single-image captioning and video description.

### Significance

The paper's contributions are significant. By moving from single images to image streams, it pushes the field towards a more holistic understanding of visual data and its connection to narrative language. This has potential applications in photo album summarization, travelogue generation, and assistive technologies. The proposed model demonstrably advances the state of the art on this new task, outperforming strong baselines adapted from related fields. The datasets collected and made available will likely spur further research in this direction. Other researchers are likely to build upon these ideas, perhaps by exploring end-to-end differentiable coherence mechanisms or generative (rather than retrieval-based) models for this task.

### Summary of Strengths and Weaknesses

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   **Novel Problem Formulation:** Addresses the novel and significant task of generating a coherent story for an image stream.
*   **Sound and Effective Model:** Proposes a well-motivated architecture (CRCN) that intelligently combines deep learning models with a classic NLP coherence model to good effect.
*   **Thorough Evaluation:** The experimental validation is comprehensive, including strong baselines, multiple automatic metrics, human studies, and an ablation analysis on two new large-scale datasets.
*   **Clear Contribution:** The paper clearly demonstrates a significant improvement over state-of-the-art alternatives on this new task.

**Arguments for Rejection:**
*   **Heuristic Inference:** The inference process relies on a complex, approximate search strategy whose optimality is asserted but not proven.
*   **Retrieval-Based Limitation:** As a retrieval-based method, the model cannot generate truly novel sentences and is constrained by the content of its training corpus.
*   **Incremental Novelty of Components:** The novelty lies in the problem and system combination, rather than in a new fundamental machine learning technique.

**Conclusion:**
This is a strong paper that introduces a novel problem and presents a well-executed, high-quality solution. The strengths, particularly the significance of the problem and the thoroughness of the evaluation, far outweigh the minor weaknesses. The paper represents a solid contribution to the field and is likely to inspire follow-up work. I recommend acceptance.
