## Review of "Expressing an Image Stream with a Sequence of Natural Sentences"

This paper tackles the problem of generating coherent natural language descriptions for a sequence of images, moving beyond the single image-single sentence paradigm prevalent in image captioning. The authors propose a Coherent Recurrent Convolutional Network (CRCN) integrating CNNs for image representation, BRNNs for language modeling, and a local coherence model to ensure fluency across sentences. They leverage a large dataset constructed from blog posts, treating image-text pairs within blogs as sequential data. The paper demonstrates improvements over several baselines using both quantitative metrics (BLEU, Recall) and user studies via AMT.

This work builds upon a substantial body of research in image captioning (e.g., [5, 8, 9, 12, 14, 15, 26, 21, 30]).  The extension to image *streams* is a natural progression, addressing a more realistic scenario where users often capture a series of related images. The inclusion of a coherence model is a key contribution, recognizing the importance of inter-sentence relationships, a point also touched upon in video captioning work [23, 32], but explicitly addressed here. The use of blog data as a weakly-supervised source is also a clever approach to overcome the lack of large-scale sequential image-text datasets.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** Extending image captioning to sequential data and explicitly modeling coherence is a valuable contribution.
* **Technical Soundness:** The CRCN architecture appears well-designed, integrating established techniques (CNNs, RNNs) with a novel coherence component.
* **Empirical Validation:**  The paper presents thorough quantitative and qualitative results, including user studies, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed approach.
* **Dataset Creation:** Leveraging blog data provides a large-scale, albeit noisy, dataset for training.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Baseline Comparison:** While several baselines are included, they are largely single-sentence models adapted for sequence generation.  A stronger baseline specifically designed for sequential image description would provide a more compelling comparison.
* **Coherence Model Detail:** The description of the local coherence model, while present, could benefit from further clarification and potentially ablation studies to demonstrate its specific impact.
* **Data Quality:** The reliance on blog data introduces potential noise and biases. A more detailed analysis of the dataset characteristics and their impact on the results would be beneficial.
* **Limited Error Analysis:** The paper lacks a detailed error analysis of the generated sequences, which would provide insights into the model's limitations and areas for improvement.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* Addresses a novel and important problem in image understanding.
* Presents a technically sound and well-motivated approach.
* Demonstrates strong empirical results with both quantitative and qualitative evaluation.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

* Baseline comparisons could be stronger.
* Further detail and analysis of the coherence model and dataset are needed.




**Overall:** I believe this paper represents a solid contribution to the field. The novelty of the problem, the technical soundness of the approach, and the strong empirical results outweigh the minor weaknesses. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions to address the points raised above.
