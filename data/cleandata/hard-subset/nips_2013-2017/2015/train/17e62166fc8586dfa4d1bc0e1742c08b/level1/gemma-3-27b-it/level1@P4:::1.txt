The paper addresses the problem of generating a sequence of natural language sentences for a sequence of images, extending the traditional image captioning task from single image-sentence pairs to image streams and corresponding descriptions. The authors propose a Coherent Recurrent Convolutional Network (CRCN) that combines CNNs for image representation, BRNNs for language modeling, and a local coherence model to ensure fluency between sentences. They train and evaluate their approach on blog post data from NYC and Disneyland, demonstrating improvements over several baselines using both quantitative metrics (BLEU, Top-K recall) and user studies via Amazon Mechanical Turk.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The extension of image captioning to image streams is a natural and valuable progression, addressing a realistic scenario where users capture multiple related images. This moves beyond single-image analysis.
* **Technical Soundness:** The proposed CRCN architecture appears well-motivated and leverages established techniques (CNNs, RNNs) in a reasonable way. The inclusion of a local coherence model is a key contribution, specifically addressing a challenge unique to sequential description generation. The loss function, based on max-margin ranking, is appropriate for this retrieval-based approach.
* **Empirical Validation:** The authors provide a comprehensive set of experiments, including quantitative comparisons with several baselines and user studies to assess the quality of generated descriptions. The results consistently show the superiority of their proposed approach.
* **Dataset Creation:** Leveraging blog posts as a source of text-image parallel data is a clever solution to the data scarcity problem for this task. The pre-processing pipeline, including text segmentation and summarization, seems reasonable.
* **Clear Explanation:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the motivation, approach, and experimental setup. Figures are helpful in illustrating the architecture and experimental results.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Baseline Comparison:** While the authors compare with several baselines, many are single-sentence image captioning models adapted to generate sequences by simply concatenating individual captions. More direct competitors designed for sequential description are lacking. A more robust comparison would include adapting recent sequence-to-sequence models specifically for this task.
* **Data Augmentation Detail:** The description of the data augmentation technique using κ=3 is somewhat superficial. The choice of κ=3 isn't thoroughly justified and a sensitivity analysis isn’t included.
* **Coherence Model Complexity:** While the local coherence model is a key contribution, its reliance on parse trees and the entity grid might introduce complexity and potential limitations. The explanation of how it captures coherence could be more detailed and intuitive.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper doesn't sufficiently discuss the sensitivity of the model to different hyperparameter settings. Understanding how the performance changes with variations in learning rate, dropout rates, or RNN hidden size would strengthen the analysis.
* **Error Analysis:** While the user studies indicate a preference for the generated descriptions, a deeper qualitative error analysis would be valuable. Examining specific failure cases could reveal limitations of the approach and suggest future directions.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper addresses a novel and important problem in image understanding and natural language generation.
* The proposed CRCN architecture is technically sound and well-motivated.
* The experimental results are compelling and demonstrate the effectiveness of the approach.
* The authors provide a thorough evaluation using both quantitative metrics and user studies.
* The dataset creation process is a valuable contribution.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

* The baseline comparison is not comprehensive enough, lacking direct competitors for sequential description.
* Some aspects of the methodology (e.g., data augmentation, coherence model) lack sufficient detail and justification.
* The paper would benefit from a more in-depth analysis of hyperparameter sensitivity and error cases.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of image captioning and natural language generation. The problem addressed is relevant and the proposed approach is technically sound and empirically validated. While there are some minor weaknesses, they do not significantly detract from the overall quality of the work. I believe this paper is suitable for acceptance to NIPS.

**Recommendation:** Accept.
