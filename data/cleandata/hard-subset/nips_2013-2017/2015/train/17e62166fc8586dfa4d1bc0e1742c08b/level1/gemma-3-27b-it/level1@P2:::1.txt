This paper proposes a novel approach for generating sequences of natural language sentences from image streams, a problem extending traditional single-image-to-single-sentence captioning. The core contribution is the Coherent Recurrent Convolutional Network (CRCN), integrating CNNs, BRNNs, and a local coherence model. The authors leverage blog posts as a text-image parallel corpus and demonstrate improvements over baselines using both quantitative metrics and user studies.

**Quality:** The paper presents a technically sound approach, building upon established techniques (CNNs, RNNs) but combining them in a novel way to address a more complex problem. The experimental setup is reasonably thorough, with comparisons to several baselines and validation through both automatic metrics (BLEU, Recall) and human evaluation (AMT). The loss function and training details are clearly described. The use of blog data as a training source is clever, addressing the scarcity of paired image-sequence-sentence datasets. However, the degree of novelty is incremental; while the combination is new, each component individually is well-established. The discussion of limitations is minimal; for example, the reliance on blog data introduces potential biases, and the sensitivity to the choice of hyperparameters (e.g., κ for data augmentation) isn't fully explored. The presentation of results is clear, though could benefit from more error analysis.

**Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The motivation is clear, and the architecture is explained with a helpful diagram (Fig. 2). The mathematical notation is appropriate. However, the detailed explanations of some components (e.g., the coherence model, the specifics of the training process) could be more concise and focused.  Section 3.5, describing the retrieval process, feels somewhat hand-wavy and could benefit from further clarification.  The terminology is consistent throughout. 

**Originality:** The primary novelty lies in extending the image captioning task to image *streams* and sentence *sequences*, incorporating a coherence model for fluent transitions. While extending existing work to a sequential setting isn’t groundbreaking, it addresses an under-explored problem. The integration of the coherence model within a deep learning framework is a valuable contribution. However, the paper does not adequately differentiate itself from related work on video captioning (e.g., [23, 32] cited in the paper) beyond explicitly mentioning the coherence model. More in-depth comparison to these video captioning approaches is warranted. The claim of being "the first to address" the problem is perhaps overstated, as related work exists, even if not directly addressing the same combination of factors.

**Significance:** The problem of generating descriptions for image streams is relevant, particularly in scenarios like automatic summarization of photo albums or understanding user experiences documented through photos. The results demonstrate a significant improvement over baselines, particularly in terms of retrieval accuracy and user preference.  The successful application of blog data as a training source is a valuable contribution that could be extended to other multimodal tasks.  The potential impact of this work is moderate; while not a paradigm shift, it offers a practical solution to a real-world problem and lays the groundwork for future research in multimodal sequence modeling. The AMT results are a strong indicator of practical usefulness.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Addresses a novel and relevant problem: image stream description.
*   Presents a technically sound and well-implemented approach (CRCN).
*   Demonstrates significant improvements over baselines with both quantitative and qualitative evaluations.
*   Successfully leverages a large, readily available dataset (blog posts).
*   Positive results from user studies (AMT).

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   Incremental novelty; the core components are well-established.
*   Limited discussion of limitations and potential biases.
*   Insufficient comparison to related work in video captioning.
*   Some sections could benefit from more concise and focused explanations.
*   The retrieval process description lacks sufficient detail.




**Overall Assessment:**

I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions. The paper presents a solid contribution to the field of multimodal learning, addressing a relevant problem with a technically sound approach. Addressing the concerns outlined above (more thorough comparison to video captioning, discussion of limitations, clarification of the retrieval process) would further strengthen the paper. The positive results and user study findings support its acceptance to NIPS.
