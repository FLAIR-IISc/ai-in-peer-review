This paper introduces the novel problem of generating a coherent sequence of natural sentences to describe a stream of images. To address this, the authors propose the Coherent Recurrent Convolutional Network (CRCN), a multimodal architecture that combines a CNN for image representation, a bidirectional RNN for modeling sentence sequence flow, and an entity-based local coherence model to ensure narrative fluency. This work represents a significant and well-motivated extension of the prevalent single-image-to-sentence captioning task, previously explored at NIPS and other venues (e.g., Karpathy & Fei-Fei, 2015; Vinyals et al., 2015), by shifting the focus from individual captions to cohesive, story-like paragraphs. The model is trained and evaluated on a large, new dataset constructed from blog posts.

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound, presenting a complete and well-executed study. The claims are strongly supported by a comprehensive experimental evaluation that includes multiple automatic metrics (BLEU, R@K, etc.) and a user study on Amazon Mechanical Turk. The inclusion of an ablation study (CRCN vs. RCN) effectively demonstrates the contribution of the coherence model, which is a key component of their proposed architecture.

**Clarity:** The manuscript is clearly written and well-organized. The CRCN architecture is explained effectively, aided by a helpful diagram. The authors provide sufficient detail on their dataset creation and experimental setup, which aids in understanding and potential reproducibility.

**Originality & Significance:** The key contribution is the formulation of this new, important problem and a strong baseline solution for it. The integration of a classic NLP coherence model (Barzilay & Lapata, 2005) into a modern deep learning framework is a novel and effective architectural choice for this task. By moving beyond single images, this work opens up a promising direction for research in vision and language, with potential applications in automated storytelling and photo album summarization. The results are significant, demonstrating a clear improvement over adapted state-of-the-art methods.

One minor weakness is that the inference mechanism is retrieval-based rather than fully generative, composing paragraphs from sentences in the training set. While pragmatic, this limits the production of entirely novel sentences. This could be more explicitly framed as a limitation and an avenue for future work.

**Arguments for acceptance:**
*   Tackles a novel, well-motivated, and significant problem in the vision-and-language space.
*   Proposes an original architecture that intelligently combines deep learning with established NLP techniques.
*   Presents a thorough and convincing evaluation on a large-scale dataset, demonstrating clear state-of-the-art performance on the new task.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
*   The inference method is based on retrieval and ranking, not pure generation, which slightly limits the scope of the contribution.

The strengths of this paper far outweigh its minor weaknesses. It is a high-quality contribution on a compelling new problem that is likely to be of high interest to the NIPS community.
