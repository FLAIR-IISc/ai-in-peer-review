This paper tackles the underexplored problem of describing an image stream with a sequence of natural sentences. The authors propose CRCN, a multimodal retrieval architecture that (i) encodes images with VGGNet, (ii) represents candidate sentences using paragraph vectors and a bidirectional RNN to model sentence-to-sentence context, and (iii) injects discourse coherence via an entity-grid model (Barzilay & Lapata) aggregated over the whole paragraph. Training uses a max-margin objective aligning image and sentence sequences, and inference performs approximate beam-style search over sentences associated with K-nearest images. The authors construct a large text–image parallel corpus from blogs (NYC and Disneyland) by paragraph proximity-based alignment and LSA summarization. Experiments show gains over strong single-image captioning baselines (Kiros et al.; Karpathy & Fei-Fei; Vinyals et al.) on BLEU/CIDEr/METEOR and retrieval metrics, and AMT users prefer CRCN outputs, especially for longer sequences. The work relates to prior NIPS/vision–language literature on image captioning and video-to-text (e.g., LRCN) and retrieval/embedding methods, but is distinct in explicitly modeling local coherence to improve multi-sentence fluency.

Quality: The overall approach is technically sound and well-motivated. The dataset construction pipeline is practical but introduces alignment noise; an analysis of segmentation/summarization accuracy or robustness would strengthen claims. The scoring function Skl adds a global coherence vector gk against each image feature vlt; this duplicates the coherence contribution N times—clarify normalization or justify this coupling with empirical ablations. Baselines are mostly single-sentence systems; adding a hierarchical/paragraph model (e.g., LSTM/GRU variants or a simple bigram sentence-level LM without entity grids) would better isolate the coherence gain. The “unsupervised” claim is overstated; the method is weakly supervised using mined pairs.

Clarity: The paper is generally clear and well-organized. Please specify preprocessing errors, κ sensitivity, K/M sensitivity, runtime, and whether code/data will be released. Minor citation formatting issues remain.

Originality: Integrating an entity-grid coherence model into a multimodal retrieval framework for image streams is novel and timely relative to image captioning/video description work.

Significance: If datasets/code are released, the impact is likely, and the gains (particularly in retrieval metrics and human preference for longer sequences) are meaningful for storytelling and album summarization.

Pros for acceptance:
- Clear, novel problem formulation; cohesive CRCN architecture.
- Strong empirical gains and human studies; coherence matters more with length.
- Scalable dataset creation from real blogs.

Cons against acceptance:
- Noisiness of alignment not quantified; limited ablations on coherence term.
- Baselines may be disadvantaged (single-sentence); missing hierarchical paragraph baselines.
- Ambiguity in scoring formulation and “unsupervised” claim.
