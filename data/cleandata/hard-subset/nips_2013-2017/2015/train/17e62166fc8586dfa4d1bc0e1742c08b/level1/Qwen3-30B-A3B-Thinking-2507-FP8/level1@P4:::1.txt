

# Review of "Expressing an Image Stream with a Sequence of Natural Sentences"

This paper proposes a novel approach to generating sequences of natural language sentences from image streams, extending the traditional image captioning task (single image to single sentence) to sequences (image stream to sentence sequence). The authors introduce a multimodal architecture called Coherent Recurrent Convolutional Network (CRCN) that integrates convolutional neural networks for image representation, bidirectional recurrent neural networks for sentence modeling, and an entity-based local coherence model for smooth transitions between sentences. The approach leverages blog posts as training data, as they naturally contain sequences of images and associated text. The paper evaluates the method on NYC and Disneyland blog datasets (20K blog posts, 140K images) using both quantitative metrics (BLEU, CIDEr, METEOR, top-K recall) and user studies via Amazon Mechanical Turk.

The main contributions are threefold: (1) addressing the novel problem of image stream to sentence sequence generation; (2) developing the CRCN architecture that integrates CNN, BRNN, and coherence model; and (3) demonstrating superior performance on large-scale blog datasets.

Strengths:
The paper makes a compelling case for extending image captioning to sequences, which aligns with how users typically capture experiences (e.g., taking multiple photos of a trip to NYC). The CRCN architecture is well-motivated and integrates existing techniques (CNNs for image representation, BRNNs for language modeling, and entity-based coherence) in a novel way. The use of blog posts as training data is particularly clever, as it provides abundant text-image parallel sequences without requiring manual annotation. The experimental evaluation is comprehensive, with both quantitative metrics and human evaluation. The results consistently show that CRCN outperforms strong baselines, especially in retrieval metrics and for longer sequences. The ablation study (comparing CRCN, RCN, and 1NN) demonstrates that both the BRNN and coherence model contribute to the performance.

Weaknesses:
The paper doesn't sufficiently justify why blog posts constitute good text-image parallel data. While the authors claim blog authors carefully select images to accompany text, they don't provide evidence of alignment quality between images and text in the blog dataset. The coherence model, while novel, appears to be a relatively simple extension of existing work on discourse coherence (citing [1]), and the paper doesn't clearly quantify its contribution versus the BRNN. The user study only evaluates on short sequences (5 images), which contradicts the paper's motivation for longer sequences (e.g., "a series of pictures on their special moments"). The paper also lacks discussion of failure cases or limitations of the approach.

Technical soundness:
The paper presents a technically sound approach with clear mathematical formulations for the BRNN and coherence model. The training procedure using max-margin structured loss is appropriate for the retrieval task. The implementation details are sufficiently clear to reproduce, and the use of standard evaluation metrics (BLEU, CIDEr, METEOR) is appropriate for text generation tasks. The ablation study provides evidence for the contribution of key components.

Novelty:
The paper convincingly argues that it is the first to address the problem of expressing image streams with sentence sequences. Previous work focuses on single-image single-sentence generation (e.g., [9, 12, 14, 15, 26, 21, 30]), while [11] addresses the opposite problem (image sequence retrieval from text). The CRCN architecture extends multimodal neural networks (e.g., [9, 19, 30]) by explicitly integrating a coherence model for sentence transitions, which is a significant contribution to the field.

Significance:
The problem addressed has practical relevance for real-world applications where users capture multiple images of events. The approach could be applied to other domains with blog-like content, making it broadly applicable. The results demonstrate that considering sequence coherence significantly improves performance, particularly for longer passages, which advances the state of the art in multimodal representation learning. The use of user-generated blog content as training data is a valuable contribution to the field.

The paper is well-written and clearly organized. The figures effectively illustrate the problem statement and architecture. The related work section adequately covers previous approaches to image captioning and related tasks, though it could better emphasize why the sequence-to-sequence problem has been underexplored.

Overall, this is a solid contribution to the field of multimodal learning. The problem is novel and well-motivated, the approach is technically sound, and the evaluation demonstrates clear improvements over baselines. The main limitations are the insufficient justification for the blog post data source and the user study being limited to short sequences. However, these do not undermine the overall contribution, which advances the state of the art in multimodal representation learning.

Pro: Novel problem formulation extending image captioning to sequences; strong technical approach with comprehensive evaluation; practical application with real-world relevance.

Con: Insufficient evidence for blog post data quality; user study limited to short sequences; coherence model contribution not fully quantified.

The paper addresses an important gap in the literature and makes a clear contribution to the field. It meets all four criteria (quality, clarity, originality, significance) and should be accepted.
