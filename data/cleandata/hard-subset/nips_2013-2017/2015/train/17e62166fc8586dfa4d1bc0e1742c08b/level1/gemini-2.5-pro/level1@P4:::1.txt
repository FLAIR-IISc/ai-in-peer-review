### Summary

This paper addresses the novel problem of generating a sequence of natural sentences to describe a stream of images. This extends the well-studied problem of single-image captioning to a more complex sequence-to-sequence setting, motivated by real-world scenarios like summarizing photo albums from an event or trip. To solve this, the authors propose a multimodal architecture called the Coherent Recurrent Convolutional Network (CRCN). The model is retrieval-based; for a given query image stream, it first finds candidate sentences for each image from a training set and then selects and arranges them to form the best output paragraph. The CRCN model integrates three key components: 1) a Convolutional Neural Network (VGGNet) to extract features from each image; 2) a Bidirectional Recurrent Neural Network (BRNN) to model the contextual flow of content across the sentence sequence; and 3) an entity-based local coherence model, adapted from prior work in NLP, to explicitly ensure syntactic and referential smoothness between sentences.

To train this model, the authors introduce a practical method for creating a large-scale parallel corpus by processing blog posts, which naturally contain sequences of images accompanied by narrative text. The CRCN is trained end-to-end using a max-margin loss function that learns a compatibility score between image streams and sentence sequences. The paper provides a thorough evaluation on two large blog datasets (NYC and Disneyland), comparing the proposed method against several state-of-the-art single-image captioning methods adapted for this task. Results from both automatic metrics (BLEU, CIDEr, etc.) and human studies on Amazon Mechanical Turk demonstrate the superiority of the proposed approach.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

1.  **Novel and Significant Problem Formulation:** The paper tackles the generation of sentence sequences from image streams, a logical and important next step for the vision-and-language community. This moves beyond single-image analysis to a more holistic understanding of visual narratives, which has numerous practical applications.
2.  **Well-Motivated and Sound Architecture:** The proposed CRCN model is thoughtfully designed. It combines a model for semantic content flow (BRNN) with a model for structural discourse coherence (entity grid). The integration of an explicit coherence model is a key contribution that directly addresses the challenges of generating fluent multi-sentence text, a known difficulty that standard RNNs may not fully capture.
3.  **Innovative Data Collection Strategy:** The use of blog posts to create a large-scale, "in-the-wild" dataset for this new task is a significant strength. The described pre-processing pipeline for converting unstructured blogs into parallel image-sentence sequences is a valuable contribution in itself and provides a template for future work.
4.  **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The experimental evaluation is extensive. The authors compare their model against multiple strong baselines, including several recent deep learning models. The use of both automatic language/retrieval metrics and human user studies provides convincing evidence for the model's effectiveness. The ablation study (comparing CRCN to RCN and 1NN) effectively validates the contribution of the model's key components.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

1.  **Retrieval-based vs. Generative:** The method is fundamentally a retrieval-and-composition approach rather than a truly generative one. It is constrained by the sentences present in the training corpus. This limits its ability to describe novel content or compositions not seen during training. While retrieval is a valid approach, the paper's framing as "expressing" or "verbalizing" an image stream could be seen as slightly overstating its generative capabilities.
2.  **Clarity on the Scoring Function:** The formulation of the compatibility score in Equation (3) could be better justified. Specifically, the coherence term `gk` (a single vector representing the coherence of the entire sentence sequence) is dotted with each individual image feature `vlt` and summed. The intuition for why a global text property should be matched against each local image representation is not immediately clear and warrants further explanation.
3.  **Approximation in Test-Time Search:** To manage the exponential `K^N` search space during retrieval, the paper proposes a divide-and-conquer strategy with beam search. While this is a practical necessity, the paper lacks an analysis of the quality of this approximation. It is unclear how often this heuristic finds the globally optimal sequence according to the model's score, or what types of errors it might introduce.

### Detailed Comments

**Quality:** The paper is of high quality. The technical approach is sound, combining well-established components in a novel way to address a new problem. The claims are well-supported by extensive experiments on large-scale datasets. The ablation studies effectively demonstrate the benefits of the BRNN and the coherence model. The work appears complete and carefully executed.

**Clarity:** The paper is clearly written and well-organized. Figure 2 provides a helpful overview of the data pre-processing pipeline and the CRCN architecture. The motivations and individual components are explained well. Minor points for improvement include a more detailed justification for the design of the scoring function (Eq. 3) and perhaps a more detailed discussion of the computational complexity of the test-time retrieval algorithm.

**Originality:** The paper is highly original. To the best of my knowledge, it is the first to formally define and tackle the problem of generating a sequence of sentences from a stream of images. The CRCN architecture, which unifies image features, a sequence-level language model, and an explicit discourse coherence model in an end-to-end framework, is a novel contribution. The method for curating training data from blogs is also an original and practical solution to the data scarcity problem for this task.

**Significance:** The work is significant and likely to have a substantial impact. It opens up a new and promising research direction in the intersection of computer vision and NLP, moving beyond the now-saturated single-image captioning task. The problem formulation is compelling and relevant to real-world applications. The proposed model and the data collection methodology provide a strong baseline and resource that future research can build upon. Researchers working on visual storytelling, video description, and multi-modal summarization will find this paper highly relevant.
