This paper proposes a novel approach for retrieving a sequence of natural sentences for an image stream. The authors extend both input and output dimensions to a sequence of images and a sequence of sentences, which is a natural next step towards joint understanding of visual content and language descriptions. The proposed multimodal architecture, called Coherent Recurrent Convolutional Network (CRCN), integrates convolutional neural networks, bidirectional recurrent neural networks, and an entity-based local coherence model.

The authors' problem setup is motivated by the fact that general users often take a series of pictures on their memorable moments. They aim to automatically produce a sequence of natural language sentences that best describe the essence of the input image set. The CRCN model is trained on a large dataset of unstructured blog posts, consisting of 20K blog posts with 140K associated images. The authors evaluate their approach using both quantitative measures (e.g., BLEU and Top-K recall) and user studies via Amazon Mechanical Turk (AMT).

The paper is well-organized, and the authors provide a clear explanation of their approach, including the architecture of the CRCN model and the training process. The experimental results demonstrate the superior performance of the proposed approach compared to other state-of-the-art alternatives.

Strengths:

1. Novel problem setup: The authors address a new problem of retrieving a sequence of natural sentences for an image stream, which is a natural extension of the existing image captioning task.
2. Multimodal architecture: The proposed CRCN model integrates convolutional neural networks, bidirectional recurrent neural networks, and an entity-based local coherence model, which allows for a more comprehensive understanding of the visual content and language descriptions.
3. Large-scale dataset: The authors use a large dataset of unstructured blog posts, which provides a more realistic and challenging setting for evaluating the proposed approach.
4. Comprehensive evaluation: The authors use both quantitative measures and user studies to evaluate their approach, which provides a more complete understanding of the performance of the proposed method.

Weaknesses:

1. Complexity of the model: The CRCN model is a complex architecture that requires a large amount of computational resources and training data. This may limit its applicability to smaller-scale datasets or devices with limited computational power.
2. Dependence on pre-trained models: The authors use pre-trained models, such as VGGNet, which may not be optimal for the specific task of retrieving a sequence of natural sentences for an image stream.
3. Limited interpretability: The authors do not provide a detailed analysis of the learned representations and the decision-making process of the CRCN model, which may limit the understanding of the proposed approach.

Arguments pro acceptance:

1. The paper proposes a novel approach to a challenging problem, which is a significant contribution to the field of computer vision and natural language processing.
2. The experimental results demonstrate the superior performance of the proposed approach compared to other state-of-the-art alternatives.
3. The paper provides a clear explanation of the approach, including the architecture of the CRCN model and the training process.

Arguments con acceptance:

1. The complexity of the model may limit its applicability to smaller-scale datasets or devices with limited computational power.
2. The dependence on pre-trained models may not be optimal for the specific task of retrieving a sequence of natural sentences for an image stream.
3. The limited interpretability of the learned representations and the decision-making process of the CRCN model may limit the understanding of the proposed approach.

Overall, the paper proposes a novel approach to a challenging problem and demonstrates its superior performance compared to other state-of-the-art alternatives. However, the complexity of the model and its dependence on pre-trained models may limit its applicability, and the limited interpretability of the learned representations and the decision-making process may limit the understanding of the proposed approach.
