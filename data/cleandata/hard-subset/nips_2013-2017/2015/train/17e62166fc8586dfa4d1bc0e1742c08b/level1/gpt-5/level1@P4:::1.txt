Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper addresses the problem of describing an image stream with a sequence of natural sentences. Unlike most prior image captioning work that maps single images to single sentences (e.g., Karpathy & Fei-Fei 2015; Vinyals et al. 2015; Kiros et al. 2014), this work extends both input and output to sequences and explicitly models sentence-to-sentence coherence. The proposed coherent recurrent convolutional network (CRCN) combines: (i) CNN features (VGG fc7) for images, (ii) a bidirectional RNN over sentence representations (doc2vec) to model content flow across sentences, and (iii) an entity-based local coherence model (Barzilay & Lapata 2008) that encodes discourse entity transition patterns via an entity grid. Training uses weakly aligned text-image streams obtained from blogs (NYC and Disneyland), where text is segmented and summarized (LSA) into one sentence per image. A max-margin ranking loss encourages paired image- and sentence-sequences to score higher than mismatched pairs. At test time, candidate sentence sequences are formed by retrieving sentences from K nearest neighbor images per position, and an approximate divide-and-conquer beam search ranks sequences by the CRCN score. Evaluation includes language similarity metrics (BLEU, METEOR, CIDEr), retrieval metrics (R@K, median rank), and AMT pairwise preferences; the paper reports that CRCN outperforms adapted single-sentence baselines and its own ablations.

Quality:
- Technical soundness: The overall retrieval-and-ranking formulation is reasonable and grounded in prior multimodal embedding work, extending it to sequence-level modeling with a learned combination of BRNN outputs and a coherence descriptor. The entity grid integration for coherence is principled, and the ranking loss is standard. However, several aspects require clarification or raise concerns:
  - Scoring function: Skl = sum_t s_k^t·v_l^t + g_k·v_l^t. The second term applies the same coherence-derived vector g_k to every image v_l^t, effectively scaling with sequence length and tying a text-only coherence signal directly to visual features via a learned mapping. The rationale and empirical impact of this design would benefit from ablation (e.g., length normalization; applying g_k once; or conditioning g_k only on text).
  - Length bias: Scores scale with the number of time steps; there is no explicit normalization by sequence length. This can bias ranking toward longer candidates. A simple normalization or per-time-step averaging would address this.
  - Search: The divide-and-conquer beam search is only sketched. Reproducibility would benefit from pseudocode, complexity analysis, and sensitivity to K and beam size M.
  - Supervision claim: The method is described as “completely unsupervised,” yet the model is trained with aligned image–sentence-sequence pairs derived from blogs via heuristics; this is better characterized as weakly supervised.
  - Preprocessing noise: Paragraph-to-image assignment by index distance and LSA-based summarization are heuristic; potential misalignments are not quantified. An analysis of noise rates and robustness (e.g., training with/without summarization; κ augmentation effects) would strengthen the claims.
  - Coherence model details: The entity grid uses transitions of length 3 and is zero-padded to 300 dimensions before an FC mapping. While pragmatic, this is ad hoc; reporting ablations for history length and padding/mapping choices would be helpful.

- Empirical evaluation: The paper reports consistent gains over strong baselines and its own ablations on both datasets and in user studies. However, the reviewable text does not include exact numeric results, confidence intervals, or significance tests (for AMT). Reporting these, plus inter-annotator agreement, would make the claims more persuasive. Given the departure from standard single-image datasets, releasing the blog datasets and code would be important for reproducibility.

Clarity:
The paper is generally well-organized and detailed, with explicit equations for the BRNN, coherence feature construction, and training objective. Notation is mostly clear. Areas that would benefit from clarification:
- The motivation and interpretation of the g·v term; whether g is applied once or T times; and whether scores are length-normalized.
- The candidate search procedure (provide algorithmic details).
- Doc2vec training details (corpora, window size, negative sampling), handling OOV, and whether embeddings are fine-tuned.
- Dataset preprocessing error analysis and examples illustrating text–image alignment quality.

Originality:
The paper’s main novelty is extending captioning to image streams with multi-sentence outputs and integrating an entity-based coherence model into a multimodal ranking framework. While multimodal CNN–RNN models and retrieval-based captioning are established, explicitly modeling discourse coherence at the sentence-sequence level for image streams appears novel relative to contemporaneous work, and is distinct from video-to-single-sentence models (e.g., Donahue et al. 2015) and paragraph-to-image-sequence retrieval (Kim et al. 2015).

Significance:
The problem is relevant and underexplored for the 2015 timeframe, and the approach is likely to influence subsequent “visual storytelling” research. The ability to produce more coherent multi-sentence descriptions is meaningful for applications. That said, significance is somewhat limited by reliance on retrieval rather than true generation, domain-specific datasets (tourism blogs), and heuristic preprocessing. Demonstrating generalization to other domains or to standard multi-image datasets (if available) would increase impact.

Strengths:
- Clearly motivated problem and novel integration of coherence modeling with multimodal sequence ranking.
- Sensible architecture leveraging BRNN over sentence embeddings and entity-grid coherence.
- Comprehensive evaluation protocol including user studies and ablations (CRCN vs RCN vs 1NN).
- Use of large-scale weakly aligned blog data.

Weaknesses:
- Potential length bias and unclear role of the global coherence term in the scoring function.
- Approximate search details are insufficient for reproducibility.
- Heuristic preprocessing (segmentation/summarization) may introduce substantial noise; robustness analysis is limited.
- “Unsupervised” characterization is misleading; supervision comes from weakly aligned pairs.
- Evaluation is confined to two domains; lack of standard benchmarks; limited reporting detail in the text available.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Normalize scores by sequence length and/or report ablations showing insensitivity to length.
- Provide ablations for: removing the g·v term; applying g once vs per time-step; coherence history length; κ sentence augmentation.
- Detail the beam/divide-and-conquer search with pseudocode and complexity, and analyze sensitivity to K, M, and N.
- Quantify preprocessing alignment accuracy and its effect on performance.
- Report full metrics with confidence intervals; for AMT, include preference percentages, significance tests, and agreement.
- Clarify doc2vec training and whether embeddings are updated end-to-end.
- Release code and datasets to facilitate comparison.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Addresses a timely and underexplored problem (multi-sentence descriptions for image streams).
- Introduces a coherent and principled integration of discourse coherence into multimodal learning.
- Demonstrates improved performance and user preference over reasonable baselines.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Some technical choices (score formulation, length normalization) are insufficiently justified and may bias results.
- Reproducibility concerns around search, preprocessing, and dataset release.
- Evaluation limited to custom domains; limited generalization evidence.

Overall, the paper presents a novel and well-motivated approach with promising results, but would benefit from clearer justification of the scoring design, stronger analysis of preprocessing noise and search, and more thorough reporting.
