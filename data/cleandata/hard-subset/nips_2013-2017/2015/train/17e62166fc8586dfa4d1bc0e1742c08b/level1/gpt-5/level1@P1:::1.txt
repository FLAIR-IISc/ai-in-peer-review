Summary and relation to prior work
This paper tackles the problem of producing a sequence of natural sentences for an image stream, arguing that many real-world use cases involve multiple images describing an event or story. The proposed coherent recurrent convolutional network (CRCN) combines: (i) CNN image features (VGG fc7), (ii) a bidirectional RNN over sentence-level paragraph vectors (doc2vec) to model content flow, and (iii) an entity-based local coherence model (Barzilay & Lapata’s entity grid) to encourage fluent sentence-to-sentence transitions. The system is retrieval-based: for each image in the stream, it gathers K candidate sentences from nearest-neighbor images in the training set (blogs), and then uses a learned compatibility score to select the best sequence via an approximate beam-style search over the combinatorial space. Training uses a max-margin ranking loss over aligned and randomly sampled misaligned image/sentence-sequence pairs. Evaluation on two blog datasets (NYC and Disneyland; ~20K posts, 140K images) uses BLEU/CIDEr/METEOR and retrieval metrics (R@K, median rank), plus AMT user studies.

The work extends single-image/single-sentence captioning [Karpathy & Fei-Fei; Vinyals et al.; Kiros et al.] and is related to video-to-text (e.g., LRCN) but differs by generating multi-sentence descriptions of discrete image streams and by explicitly modeling discourse coherence through entity transitions. It is also connected to image-sequence retrieval from text [Kim et al.], but in the opposite direction.

Strengths
- Problem formulation: Generating coherent multi-sentence descriptions for image streams is important and underexplored relative to single-image captioning and video captioning. Positioning is clear and timely.
- Architectural integration: The combination of a sentence-level BRNN with an explicit entity-based coherence model is novel in multimodal grounding, and empirically useful (CRCN > RCN ablation in retrieval metrics and AMT for longer sequences).
- Data and scalability: Mining large, weakly aligned blog corpora is pragmatic; the pipeline (segmentation + LSA summarization + doc2vec) allows training without manual sentence-level annotations.
- Evaluation: Multiple metrics and AMT preference tests; the finding that coherence gains matter more for longer sequences is insightful and supports the paper’s premise.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Scoring function design: The final sequence score sums over time s_t · v_t + g · v_t, where g (sequence-level coherence vector) is identical across t. This effectively multiplies the coherence contribution by sequence length and ties a text-only coherence vector to each image feature via a dot product. Conceptually, coherence is a property of the sentence sequence, not of image features; operationally, this can (i) introduce length bias (no length normalization is reported), and (ii) encourage the model to learn spurious interactions between g and v_t. A clearer formulation would score coherence separately (e.g., add a length-normalized coherence scalar) or condition g on sentence features rather than image features.
- Retrieval-only generation: The method cannot produce novel sentences; it reuses training sentences. This is reasonable for a first step, but limits expressivity and may bias results if training data is narrow. The models compared that “generate” per image are disadvantaged because they were not designed to optimize cross-sentence coherence; an additional baseline that retrieves per-image and then re-ranks sequences using a textual coherence model would sharpen the isolation of the proposed contribution.
- Preprocessing noise and validation: Alignment relies on simple index-distance heuristics and LSA summarization to select a single key sentence per image. No quantitative validation of segmentation/summarization quality is provided; errors here could influence both training and evaluation. Similarly, using doc2vec trained on the same corpus may entangle topic/domain artifacts.
- Fairness and breadth of baselines: All baselines are adapted to the blog data and concatenated per-image outputs, but (i) stronger retrieval baselines (e.g., sentence-embedding retrieval with sequence-level smoothness constraints), (ii) hierarchical RNNs for paragraph modeling, or (iii) LSTM/GRU-based BRNNs at sentence level would strengthen the comparison. The paper partially addresses this with RCN vs CRCN but not with stronger sequence models.
- Clarity and reproducibility: Important details are deferred to supplementary material (e.g., doc2vec training settings, sequence lengths, optimization schedules). The approximate search procedure (divide-and-conquer + beam, M=50) lacks analysis of suboptimality or sensitivity to K/M. Code/data availability is not discussed; releasing the blog streams and preprocessing scripts would aid impact.
- “Unsupervised” claim: The approach depends on paired image–text streams mined from blogs (weak supervision) and supervised NLP components (parsing, coreference approximations). The claim should be toned down to “weakly supervised.”

Quality
- Technically mostly sound with a clear training objective and ablations. The coherence-to-image interaction and length normalization issues merit attention and possibly ablation to verify they are not driving gains.
- Experiments are positive but somewhat limited in baselines for sequence modeling and in diagnostics (e.g., performance vs. length; error analyses).

Clarity
- Generally well written and organized; the pipeline is understandable. Improve clarity around the score function, length effects, and the intuition for mixing q with image features. Include more implementation specifics in the main text.

Originality
- The problem setting and the incorporation of entity-grid coherence into a multimodal sequence-ranking framework are original within the NIPS literature of the time. The components are known, but their integration and application are novel.

Significance
- Practical and scientifically relevant—multi-sentence, coherent grounding across image streams is an important step beyond single-image captioning. Demonstrated gains and AMT preferences suggest utility. Broader impact would be higher with stronger baselines and additional domains.

Arguments for acceptance
- Addresses a meaningful, underexplored task with a clear, modular solution.
- Introduces an explicit coherence model into multimodal sequence selection and shows improvements, especially for longer sequences.
- Leverages large-scale weak supervision; datasets and results are non-trivial.

Arguments against acceptance
- Conceptual mismatch and potential length bias in the scoring function; coherence scored via dot products with image features is questionable.
- Limited breadth of sequence-level baselines; retrieval-only approach restricts generality.
- Preprocessing heuristics are unvalidated; reproducibility details are sparse.

Suggestions for improvement
- Normalize scores by sequence length and/or separate the coherence term from image-compatibility terms; report ablations.
- Replace or augment the simple BRNN with LSTM/GRU at the sentence level; explore hierarchical paragraph RNNs.
- Compare against a strong retrieval baseline that enforces temporal smoothness or coherence purely in text space.
- Provide quantitative validation of segmentation/summarization quality; include error analyses.
- Test on additional domains; discuss and, if possible, release data and code.

Overall: A solid and timely contribution with a clear idea—bringing discourse coherence to multimodal sequence description. Despite concerns about the scoring formulation and baselines, the work advances the state of the art for this task. I lean toward acceptance if the authors can clarify and ablate the score function and strengthen comparisons.
