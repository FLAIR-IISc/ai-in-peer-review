This paper addresses the novel and interesting problem of generating a coherent sequence of natural language sentences for a given stream of images. The authors correctly point out that this is a natural extension of the well-studied single-image-to-single-sentence captioning task, motivated by the common user behavior of capturing events as a series of photos. The proposed method, Coherent Recurrent Convolutional Network (CRCN), frames this as a retrieval problem. It first finds candidate sentences for each image in the stream from a large corpus and then ranks the possible sentence sequences to find the one that is most appropriate. The ranking model is the core contribution, integrating three components: a CNN (VGGNet) to represent images, a Bidirectional RNN (BRNN) to model the content flow between sentences (represented as Paragraph Vectors), and an entity-based local coherence model (Barzilay & Lapata, 2008) to ensure smooth grammatical and referential transitions. The model is trained end-to-end with a max-margin loss on a large dataset collected from blog posts.

This work builds upon the flurry of recent activity in image captioning that combines CNNs and RNNs (e.g., Karpathy & Fei-Fei, 2015; Vinyals et al., 2015), but makes a significant step forward by considering sequences of both inputs and outputs. It also distinguishes itself from video description work by explicitly modeling coherence to handle the potentially sharp visual discontinuities between images in a stream, which is a key insight. The use of a classic NLP coherence model in a modern deep learning architecture is a novel and effective combination.

**Strengths**

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The paper tackles a new, well-motivated, and challenging problem. Moving from single images to image streams and from single sentences to coherent paragraphs is a significant and logical progression for the vision-and-language community. The results could have a substantial impact on applications like automated photo album narration or summarization.

2.  **Technical Quality:** The proposed CRCN model is technically sound and thoughtfully designed. The integration of a content-based sequence model (BRNN) with a structural coherence model is a key strength. This addresses two different facets of what makes a paragraph readable: semantic flow and linguistic structure. The end-to-end training framework with a structured loss is appropriate for the ranking task.

3.  **Thorough Evaluation:** The experimental validation is comprehensive and convincing. The authors have constructed a new, large-scale dataset for this task. The comparison against several strong, albeit adapted, state-of-the-art baselines is fair. Crucially, the paper includes an ablation study (CRCN vs. RCN) that clearly demonstrates the value of the explicit coherence model. The inclusion of an AMT user study is commendable, as automated metrics like BLEU often fail to capture the nuances of generation quality, especially coherence and fluency, for which human judgment is the gold standard.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Retrieval-based Formulation:** The method is fundamentally based on retrieving and composing existing sentences from the training corpus. While this ensures fluency in individual sentences, it limits the model's expressiveness. The system cannot generate truly novel descriptions tailored to the specifics of the query images; it can only re-purpose sentences seen during training. This is a significant trade-off compared to generative models like Vinyals et al. (2015) or Karpathy & Fei-Fei (2015). A more detailed discussion of the pros and cons of this retrieval-based choice would strengthen the paper.

2.  **Inference Complexity and Approximation:** The search space for the best sentence sequence is exponential in the length of the stream (K^N). The paper proposes a divide-and-conquer beam search strategy to make inference tractable. While this is a reasonable engineering solution, its quality is not analyzed. How sensitive is the performance to the beam size M? How far are the retrieved solutions from the optimal ones found by an exhaustive search on a small example? A more in-depth analysis of this approximation would be valuable.

3.  **Data Pre-processing Pipeline:** The entire framework relies on the quality of the parallel data extracted from blogs. The pipeline involves heuristic text segmentation (associating text blocks with the nearest image) and extractive summarization (LSA). Both steps can be noisy. For instance, a single text block might refer to multiple images, or the LSA summary might miss the most relevant sentence. Some discussion or analysis of the noise level in the training data and the model's robustness to it would be beneficial.

4.  **Clarity of Model Architecture:** The description of how the BRNN outputs and the coherence model output are combined in Section 3.3 could be clearer. The equation `Wf2Wf1[O|q] = [S|g]` is very compact. It seems the single coherence vector `q` is concatenated with the matrix of sentence-level representations `O`, and then passed through two fully connected layers. It is not immediately obvious how this allows the single global coherence score to influence the individual sentence scores `st`. Is the coherence vector `q` broadcast or replicated? A small diagram or a more descriptive walkthrough of this specific combination step would improve clarity.

**Summary of Pros and Cons**

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Addresses a novel and important problem, pushing the boundary beyond standard image captioning.
*   Presents a strong, well-motivated model that cleverly combines deep sequence models with a classic NLP coherence model.
*   Evaluation is extensive, including a new large-scale dataset, strong baselines, an ablation study, and essential human evaluation.
*   The work is a complete and significant contribution to the field.

**Arguments for Rejection:**
*   The retrieval-based approach limits generative capability, a point that should be discussed more thoroughly.
*   The approximate inference search is not well-analyzed.
*   The data pre-processing pipeline could be a significant source of unanalyzed noise.

In conclusion, this is a high-quality paper that introduces a novel problem and provides a strong baseline solution. The strengths, particularly the novelty of the task and the thoroughness of the evaluation, substantially outweigh the weaknesses, which are primarily limitations that could inspire future work. The paper is well-written, the method is sound, and the results are significant. I recommend acceptance.
