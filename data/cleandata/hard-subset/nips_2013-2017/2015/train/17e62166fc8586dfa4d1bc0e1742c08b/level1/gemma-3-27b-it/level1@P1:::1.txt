This paper addresses the problem of generating a sequence of sentences to describe an image stream, extending the traditional single-image-single-sentence captioning task. The authors propose a coherent recurrent convolutional network (CRCN) that combines CNNs for image representation, BRNNs for sentence modeling, and a local coherence model to ensure fluency between sentences. The approach is trained on blog posts with associated images and evaluated on NYC and Disneyland datasets using both quantitative metrics (BLEU, top-K recall) and user studies via AMT.

The paper presents a logical progression, motivated by the observation that users often capture experiences as image streams. The extension from single images to streams is a natural and valuable one. The CRCN architecture appears well-designed, thoughtfully integrating existing components (CNNs, RNNs) with a novel coherence model. The use of blog posts as a training dataset is clever, providing a readily available source of image-text pairs in a sequential format. The quantitative and qualitative (AMT) evaluations are comprehensive and support the claims made by the authors. 

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** Extending image captioning to image streams is a significant and previously under-addressed problem.
*   **Technical Soundness:** The CRCN architecture is well-motivated and leverages established techniques (CNNs, RNNs) in a coherent manner. The inclusion of a local coherence model is particularly valuable for generating fluent sequences.
*   **Dataset & Training:** The use of blog posts as a training corpus is a smart solution to the data scarcity problem for image stream captioning.
*   **Evaluation:** Thorough evaluation with both quantitative metrics and user studies demonstrates the effectiveness of the proposed approach. The AMT studies provide strong evidence of human preference for the generated descriptions.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The figures are helpful in illustrating the architecture and experimental setup.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Limited Comparison to Video Captioning:** While the paper briefly mentions video captioning, a more detailed comparison to existing video description methods (e.g., those leveraging 3D CNNs or LSTMs on video features) would strengthen the contribution.  Image streams and videos share similarities, and acknowledging/differentiating the approaches would be beneficial.
*   **Coherence Model Details:** The description of the local coherence model feels somewhat cursory. While the reference to [1] is provided, more details on how entity grids are constructed and how the transitions are represented would be helpful for reproducibility and deeper understanding. The 64-dimensional representation seems arbitrary; a justification for this dimensionality would be valuable.
*   **Data Augmentation:** The κ=3 value for data augmentation is empirically chosen, but the rationale for choosing this value isn’t strongly articulated. A sensitivity analysis exploring different κ values could provide insights into the optimal augmentation strategy.
*   **Retrieval Stage Approximation:** The divide-and-conquer search strategy introduces an approximation.  While acknowledged, a discussion of the potential impact of this approximation on performance would be useful. How does the beam size (M=50) affect results?
*   **Baselines:** While the selected baselines are relevant, they primarily focus on single image captioning extended to sequences. More specialized approaches to sequence generation might offer stronger comparisons (though these might be less readily available).



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper tackles a novel and important problem.
*   The proposed approach is technically sound and well-motivated.
*   The experimental results are compelling, demonstrating superior performance compared to existing methods.
*   The clarity of the paper and thoroughness of the evaluation contribute to its value.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

*   The discussion of related work, especially in video captioning, could be more comprehensive.
*   The description of the coherence model could be more detailed and provide more justification for design choices.
*   The potential limitations of the retrieval approximation could be better addressed.




**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a significant contribution to the field of image captioning.  The problem is well-defined, the proposed approach is innovative, and the experimental results are convincing.  While there are some minor weaknesses, they do not detract significantly from the overall quality of the work. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions to address the points raised above (particularly providing more detail on the coherence model and elaborating on the connection to video captioning).
