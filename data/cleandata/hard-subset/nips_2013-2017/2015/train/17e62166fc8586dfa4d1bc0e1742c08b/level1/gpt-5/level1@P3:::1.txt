Summary and relation to prior work
The paper addresses generating a coherent sequence of sentences for a stream of images. Unlike most image captioning work [5, 8, 9, 12, 14, 26, 30] that maps a single image to a single sentence, the authors aim to handle image sequences and produce multi-sentence descriptions. The proposed coherent recurrent convolutional network (CRCN) combines: (i) CNN image features (VGG fc7), (ii) a bidirectional RNN over sentence representations (doc2vec) to model content flow, and (iii) an entity-grid based local coherence model [1] to encourage coherent sentence-to-sentence transitions. Training uses blog posts as weakly aligned image–text streams, obtained by paragraph-based image assignment and LSA summarization [4] to one key sentence per image (with κ=3 alternatives for data augmentation). At test time, for each image in the query stream the method retrieves K nearest training images and assembles candidate sentence sequences; a divide-and-conquer beam search selects the sequence maximizing a sequence-level compatibility score learned with a max-margin ranking loss [9]. Experiments on two travel blog corpora (NYC, Disneyland) show improvements over single-image captioning baselines [9, 12, 30] concatenated across time, as measured by BLEU/CIDEr/METEOR, retrieval metrics (R@K, median rank), and AMT preferences.

Strengths
- Problem formulation is timely and relevant: moving from single images to streams and from single sentences to multi-sentence paragraphs is an important step for vision–language research.
- Architectural idea to explicitly integrate an entity-based coherence model [1] with a neural sequence model is novel in this context, and addresses a clear gap (sentence-to-sentence coherence).
- Use of readily available blog data to obtain weakly aligned image–text streams is practical and scalable, and the pipeline is clearly described.
- The sequence-level training objective and sequence selection via beam search are reasonable choices; empirical results show consistent gains over strong single-image captioning baselines.
- Human evaluation corroborates the automatic metrics and further isolates the contribution of the coherence component for longer sequences.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Scoring formulation and length bias: The sequence score Skl sums terms over time and includes gk·vlt at every t (Eq. 3). Since gk is a global coherence vector for the entire sentence sequence, adding gk·vlt for all t double-counts coherence and makes the score scale linearly with the number of images, potentially biasing training towards longer sequences. Moreover, training negatives are sampled with random lengths (Sec. 3.4), which could confound the margin with length effects. A length-normalized or per-sequence coherence term would be more principled.
- Conceptual mismatch in combining coherence and vision: Dotting a text-only coherence descriptor gk with each image feature vlt is semantically questionable. Coherence is a property of the text sequence independent of the specific images; it would be cleaner to (a) use coherence as a separate sequence-level prior, or (b) model pairwise sentence transitions as a language-only factor in a structured objective (e.g., CRF-like).
- “End-to-end” and “unsupervised” claims are overstated. The system contains multiple non-differentiable, hand-engineered components (LSA summarization, Stanford parsing/coref, entity grid), and uses supervised pretraining (ImageNet VGG). The alignment between images and sentences relies on heuristics; no analysis is provided of alignment noise or its impact.
- Baselines are not fully comparable. The proposed method is retrieval-based; baselines include generative models [9, 30] concatenated per image, which are disadvantaged on this task and domain. A stronger retrieval baseline with a structured sequence model (e.g., per-image 1-NN with a learned bigram/entity-grid coherence term or a latent structural SVM akin to [11] adapted to sentence selection) would be informative. The 1NN baseline is too weak; the RCN ablation partially addresses this but still shares much of the proposed machinery.
- Dataset is narrow and noisy. Both domains are travel blogs (NYC, Disneyland), with heavy stylistic and topical bias. The alignment pipeline (paragraph proximity + LSA) is noisy, but there is no quantitative validation (e.g., manual alignment quality, coreference accuracy). Generalization to other genres is asserted but not demonstrated.
- Evaluation: BLEU/CIDEr/METEOR at paragraph level are known to correlate poorly with multi-sentence quality (the authors acknowledge this). AMT details (worker qualification, agreement, statistical significance) are limited; while results are promising, more rigorous reporting would strengthen the claims. Search approximation quality (beam width M, K) is not analyzed.

Quality
Technically sound overall, but with notable issues in the scoring objective (length bias and the way coherence interacts with vision). Training and optimization choices are standard. The experimental protocol is broadly reasonable, yet stronger baselines and deeper analysis (alignment noise, coherence ablations, sensitivity to K/M, length normalization) are needed.

Clarity
Generally clear and well organized. Some notational ambiguities (e.g., Tln as both single summary and κ-augmented set) and missing details (doc2vec training regime; whether CNN is fine-tuned; candidate explosion vs. beam settings across lengths). The “end-to-end” phrasing is misleading.

Originality
Moderate. Integrating an entity-grid coherence model with a neural sequence model for image-stream description appears novel. The rest of the pipeline (CNN features, doc2vec/BRNN, ranking loss, retrieval with beam search) builds on known components.

Significance
Moderate. The problem is important and the results suggest meaningful gains, but the method’s reliance on retrieval and domain-specific blog data, combined with the concerns above, limit immediate impact. With stronger evaluation and a cleaner scoring formulation, the contribution could be impactful.

Suggestions for improvement
- Redefine the sequence score to (i) separate image–sentence compatibility from a sequence-level coherence prior, and (ii) normalize by length. Provide ablations showing the effect of each term.
- Add a strong retrieval baseline with pairwise coherence potentials (entity-grid or learned bigrams) and dynamic programming, and/or adapt [11] for sentence selection.
- Validate the alignment pipeline: report alignment accuracy on a small annotated subset; analyze coreference/parse noise impact.
- Report significance tests for AMT and inter-annotator agreement; include longer sequences more extensively.
- Analyze sensitivity to K and M, and the gap between approximate and exhaustive search on shorter sequences.
- Consider LSTM/GRU for the sequence model, finetuning the CNN, and using learned sentence encoders (e.g., skip-thought-like) rather than doc2vec; or at least justify choices empirically.

Pro arguments for acceptance
- Addresses an important extension of image captioning to coherent multi-sentence descriptions.
- Novel and sensible integration of a coherence model; consistent gains across metrics and human judgments.
- Practical use of large-scale weakly aligned data.

Con arguments for acceptance
- Questionable scoring formulation and potential length bias.
- Incomplete and potentially unfair baseline comparisons; missing stronger retrieval-based structured baselines.
- Limited domain and noisy supervision without validation; overstatement of “end-to-end/unsupervised.”
- Evaluation lacks depth in analysis and significance reporting.
