This paper tackles the novel problem of generating a coherent sequence of natural language sentences to describe a stream of images. The authors argue that this is a natural extension of the well-studied single-image-to-single-sentence captioning task, motivated by how users capture experiences in photo sequences. To address this, they propose a novel architecture, the Coherent Recurrent Convolutional Network (CRCN). This model integrates three key components: a Convolutional Neural Network (CNN, specifically VGGNet) to extract visual features from each image, a Bidirectional Recurrent Neural Network (BRNN) to model the contextual flow of sentences (represented by paragraph vectors), and an entity-based local coherence model (based on Barzilay and Lapata, 2008) to ensure smooth narrative transitions. A key aspect of the work is the practical approach to data acquisition; the authors leverage a large corpus of blog posts, pre-processing them to create weakly-aligned sequences of image-sentence pairs. The task is framed as a retrieval problem at test time: for a query image stream, candidate sentence sequences are constructed from the training data and ranked using a compatibility score learned by the CRCN model. The model is trained using a max-margin loss to rank ground-truth sequences higher than mismatched ones. The paper presents a thorough evaluation on two new blog datasets (NYC and Disneyland), comparing CRCN against adapted state-of-the-art single-image captioning methods and performing ablation studies to validate the contribution of its components.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The paper introduces a new, important, and well-motivated problem. Moving from single images to image streams and from single sentences to coherent paragraphs is a logical and significant step forward for the vision-and-language community. This work is, to my knowledge, the first to formally address this task and provides a strong foundational method. It has the potential to inspire follow-up research in story-telling, video summarization, and multimodal narrative generation.

2.  **Sound and Novel Methodology:** The proposed CRCN architecture is a thoughtful combination of powerful existing techniques. The novelty lies in integrating an explicit, classic NLP coherence model into a modern deep learning framework for this task. While RNNs can implicitly learn sequence structure, explicitly modeling entity-based coherence is a strong and sensible inductive bias for generating narrative text. The justification for using a BRNN to capture both preceding and succeeding context is also sound.

3.  **Thorough and Convincing Evaluation:** The experimental validation is comprehensive and robust.
    *   The authors compare against several strong and relevant baselines adapted from state-of-the-art single-image captioning literature.
    *   The ablation study clearly demonstrates the value of both the BRNN component (CRCN vs. 1NN) and, crucially, the coherence model (CRCN vs. RCN).
    *   The use of both automatic language/retrieval metrics and an Amazon Mechanical Turk (AMT) user study provides a well-rounded picture of performance. The AMT study, in particular, confirms that the model's output is preferred by humans, which is the gold standard for a generation task. The finding that the coherence model's benefit increases with sequence length is an insightful and convincing result.

4.  **Resourcefulness:** The approach of mining blog posts for training data is a clever and practical solution to the lack of curated datasets for this new problem. This demonstrates how to leverage vast, weakly-supervised web data for complex multimodal tasks.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Retrieval vs. Generation:** The paper frames the task as "expressing" an image stream and the abstract mentions "producing" sentences. However, the method is fundamentally retrieval-based—it selects and re-assembles sentences that already exist in the training corpus. This limits its ability to generate truly novel descriptions for unseen object configurations or events. While retrieval is a valid and powerful approach, the paper could be clearer about this distinction and its implications. The contribution is more in ranking and composing sequences coherently than in novel sentence generation.

2.  **Inference Complexity and Heuristics:** The inference process requires searching through an exponentially large space of candidate sequences (K^N). The authors propose a pragmatic divide-and-conquer beam search strategy. While this is a reasonable heuristic, its performance relative to an exhaustive search is not analyzed. The computational cost could still be significant for long streams, and the quality of the approximation is unclear.

3.  **Potential Noise in Data Pre-processing:** The training data quality hinges on a pre-processing pipeline involving heuristic-based text segmentation and LSA-based summarization. Errors from these upstream components (e.g., incorrect image-text alignment, sub-optimal summary sentences) will inevitably propagate as noise into the training of the CRCN model. While the scale of the data may mitigate this, the potential brittleness of this pipeline is a limitation.

4.  **Clarity in Model Combination:** Section 3.3, which describes the fusion of the CNN, RNN, and coherence model outputs, is quite dense. The equation `Wf2Wf1[O|q] = [S|g]` is compact but could benefit from a more detailed explanation. Specifically, it is not immediately obvious how the single coherence vector `q` for the entire sequence is combined with the per-sentence outputs `O` to produce the final per-image scores `S` and the global score `g`. A small diagram or more explicit description of this step would improve the paper's clarity and reproducibility.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Presents a novel and significant problem to the NIPS community.
*   Proposes a technically sound and novel model that intelligently combines deep learning with established NLP concepts.
*   Provides a very strong and thorough empirical evaluation, including human studies, which convincingly demonstrates the effectiveness of the proposed approach over strong baselines.
*   The work is a complete and solid contribution that is likely to be influential and built upon by others.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   The method is retrieval-based, which may be seen as less ambitious than true generation, and this aspect could be made clearer.
*   The inference mechanism relies on a heuristic search whose optimality is not characterized.
*   The data pipeline has potential for noise, which is a minor weakness.

**Overall Recommendation:**
This is a high-quality paper that introduces a new, interesting, and challenging problem and presents a strong, well-evaluated solution. The strengths, particularly the novelty of the problem and the thoroughness of the evaluation, far outweigh the weaknesses. The paper is well-written, clearly motivated, and represents a solid step forward in multimodal research. I strongly recommend acceptance.
