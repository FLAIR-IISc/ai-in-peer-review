

This paper proposes a novel task of generating a sequence of natural language sentences to describe an image stream, rather than the typical single-image-to-single-sentence approach. The authors introduce CRCN (Coherent Recurrent Convolutional Network), which integrates CNNs for image representation, bidirectional RNNs for sentence modeling, and an entity-based local coherence model to ensure fluent transitions between sentences. The work leverages blog posts as training data, where text and images are naturally aligned through storytelling. The approach is evaluated on NYC and Disneyland datasets (20K blog posts, 140K images) using BLEU, top-K recall, and user studies.

The paper makes a clear contribution by extending the problem formulation to sequence-to-sequence generation for image streams. It relates well to prior work like Karpathy et al. (2015) and Vinyals et al. (2015) on image captioning, while addressing the underexplored challenge of coherent multi-sentence output. The coherence model specifically targets a gap in video captioning work (e.g., Rohrbach et al., 2015), which doesn't handle abrupt transitions in image streams.

Strengths: The technical approach is sound with thorough ablation studies showing the coherence model's value. The blog dataset collection method is clever and scalable. The evaluation is comprehensive, using both quantitative metrics and user studies that reveal how coherence becomes more important as sequence length increases.

Weaknesses: The paper doesn't sufficiently address how the approach would handle non-tourism domains or cases where blog posts don't follow the assumed text-image alignment pattern. The AMT studies with short sequences (5 images) may not fully demonstrate the coherence benefits, as the authors note. The architecture is complex without clear justification for all design choices.

Quality: High - well-supported by experiments and ablation studies.  
Clarity: Good - well-organized with clear figures and equations, though some technical details could be more accessible.  
Originality: High - addresses a genuinely new problem extension beyond standard image captioning.  
Significance: Moderate - the application is relatable (tourism blogging), but the unsupervised nature limits immediate practical adoption. The core contribution is methodological rather than application-focused.

Pro: Novel problem formulation, strong evaluation with multiple metrics, clever use of blog data.  
Con: Limited domain applicability, coherence benefits not fully demonstrated on longer sequences, some technical complexity without clear justification.

Overall, this is a technically sound and original contribution to the field that extends the image captioning problem in a meaningful way. While not revolutionary, it makes a clear methodological contribution with practical implications for real-world image description tasks. I recommend acceptance.
