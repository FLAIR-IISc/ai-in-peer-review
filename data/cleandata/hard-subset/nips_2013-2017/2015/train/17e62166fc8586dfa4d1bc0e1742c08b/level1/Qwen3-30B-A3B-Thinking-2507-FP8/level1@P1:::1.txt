

# Peer Review

## Summary
This paper introduces a novel approach for generating natural language descriptions from image streams (sequences of images) rather than single images, addressing a significant gap in the literature where most prior work (e.g., [5, 8, 9, 12, 14, 15, 26, 21, 30]) focuses on single-image single-sentence generation. The authors propose a multimodal architecture called Coherent Recurrent Convolutional Network (CRCN) that integrates convolutional neural networks (CNNs) for image representation, bidirectional recurrent neural networks (BRNNs) for sentence modeling, and an entity-based local coherence model to ensure smooth transitions between sentences. The key innovation lies in extending both input (image stream) and output (sentence sequence) dimensions while addressing the challenge of maintaining coherence across multiple sentences. The authors leverage blog posts as training data, which naturally contain sequences of images and associated text, and evaluate their approach on NYC and Disneyland datasets with 20K+ blog posts and 140K+ images. The paper demonstrates that CRCN outperforms strong baselines on both quantitative metrics (BLEU, CIDEr, METEOR, top-K recall) and user studies via Amazon Mechanical Turk.

## Quality
The paper is technically sound and well-executed. The CRCN architecture is carefully designed with a clear rationale for each component, and the authors provide sufficient detail for reproducibility. The training procedure, optimization strategy, and experimental setup are well-documented. The ablation studies (comparing CRCN to variants without coherence or with simpler models) provide strong evidence for the contribution of each component. The user studies via Amazon Mechanical Turk are a particularly valuable addition to the evaluation, providing human judgment on the quality of generated text sequences. The authors are careful to acknowledge limitations, such as the coherence model's reduced importance for shorter passages, which demonstrates appropriate honesty in evaluation.

## Clarity
The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The problem statement is clearly motivated with a compelling example (visiting NYC), and the architecture is effectively illustrated with Figure 2. The contributions are explicitly stated, and the related work section properly contextualizes the paper within existing literature. The methodology is described with sufficient detail, including mathematical formulations of key components (BRNN and coherence model), making it easy for readers to understand the technical approach. The experimental results are presented clearly with well-organized tables and figures that effectively support the claims.

## Originality
The paper makes a significant contribution by extending the image captioning problem to image streams and sentence sequences. As the authors correctly note, this is "the first to address the problem of expressing image streams with sentence sequences" - a meaningful extension from the current state of the art. While the individual components (CNNs, BRNNs) draw from existing work, the integration of these with a coherence model specifically designed for sentence sequences represents a novel contribution. The use of blog posts as a source of text-image parallel data is also a practical and innovative approach to obtaining large-scale training data for this problem, which the authors justify well.

## Significance
The problem addressed has clear practical significance for applications like photo album description, travel blogging, and social media content generation. The authors convincingly demonstrate that their approach significantly outperforms baselines on both quantitative metrics and human evaluation, suggesting real-world applicability. The method's unsupervised nature and domain independence (beyond tourism) further enhance its potential impact. The paper advances the state of the art in multimodal learning by addressing a more complex and naturalistic problem setup.

## Strengths
1. Novel problem formulation extending from single-image to image-stream generation
2. Effective integration of coherence modeling with multimodal networks
3. Practical use of blog posts as training data source
4. Comprehensive evaluation with both quantitative metrics and user studies
5. Strong ablation studies validating key components
6. Clear, well-organized presentation with effective illustrations

## Weaknesses
1. The paper doesn't sufficiently address scalability concerns for very long image streams (beyond the 5-10 images used in user studies)
2. The coherence model focuses primarily on entity transitions but may not capture all aspects of text coherence (e.g., thematic continuity, emotional flow)
3. The user study design limits queries to 5 images, which might understate the coherence model's benefits for longer sequences
4. The paper doesn't provide concrete examples of failure cases or limitations of the coherence model
5. The blog data may contain domain-specific biases (e.g., travel narratives) that limit generalizability

## Recommendation
This paper presents a significant and well-executed contribution to the field of multimodal learning. The extension from single-image to image-stream generation is both novel and meaningful, and the CRCN architecture effectively addresses the coherence challenges of generating sentence sequences. The thorough evaluation with both quantitative metrics and human judgment provides strong evidence of the approach's superiority. While the paper has minor limitations regarding scalability and coherence modeling scope, these do not detract from the overall quality and significance of the work.

I recommend acceptance of this paper for NIPS. It makes a clear, well-supported contribution to the field that advances the state of the art in multimodal learning and natural language generation from visual content. The paper is technically sound, well-written, and addresses an important problem with practical applications. The authors have demonstrated a significant improvement over existing methods through rigorous evaluation.
