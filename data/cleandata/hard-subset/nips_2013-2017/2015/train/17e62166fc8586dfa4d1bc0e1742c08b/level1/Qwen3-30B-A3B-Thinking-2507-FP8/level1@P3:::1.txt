

# Peer Review: Expressing an Image Stream with a Sequence of Natural Sentences

This paper proposes a novel approach for generating sequences of natural language sentences to describe image streams, rather than single images. The authors introduce a "Coherent Recurrent Convolutional Network" (CRCN) that integrates convolutional neural networks (for image features), bidirectional recurrent neural networks (for sentence modeling), and an entity-based local coherence model (for fluent sentence transitions). The key insight is that users often capture multiple images of memorable events, and a sequence of sentences better captures the narrative than individual sentence-image pairs. The authors leverage blog posts as training data, as they naturally contain image-text sequences that follow a storytelling pattern. The paper evaluates on NYC and Disneyland blog datasets (20K+ posts, 140K images) using BLEU, CIDEr, METEOR, top-K recall, and AMT user studies.

The paper correctly situates itself within the growing literature on image captioning (e.g., Karpathy et al. 2015, Vinyals et al. 2015), acknowledging that most prior work focuses on single-image to single-sentence generation. However, it overlooks several relevant works that have addressed multi-sentence captioning or image sequence description. For instance, Kuznetsova et al. (2014) proposed "TreeTalk" for generating image descriptions using compositional trees, which inherently supports multi-sentence output. Similarly, Rohrbach et al. (2015) addressed "Translating Video Content to Natural Language Descriptions," which directly deals with sequence-to-sequence generation for visual content. The authors should clarify how their approach differs from these works, particularly in terms of coherence modeling.

**Strengths:** The paper makes a compelling case for the importance of sequence-level description, which is well-motivated by real user behavior. The blog-based dataset collection is innovative and practical, leveraging existing user-generated content without manual annotation. The CRCN architecture integrates multiple components cohesively, and the evaluation methodology is strong with both quantitative metrics and human evaluation. The user studies with varying sequence lengths (5 vs 8-10 images) effectively demonstrate the importance of coherence for longer sequences.

**Weaknesses:** The novelty claim is overstated. While the paper correctly identifies the lack of prior work on image *streams* (as opposed to videos), it fails to acknowledge that multi-sentence captioning has been explored. The paper claims to be "the first to address the problem," but this is inaccurate given the existing literature. The coherence model is described but not sufficiently justified—why entity-based transitions (as in Barzilay & Lapata 2008) are superior to other coherence approaches (e.g., lexical chains or topic modeling) is not thoroughly argued. Additionally, the blog dataset may introduce biases, as blog writing often follows specific stylistic conventions not representative of general user descriptions.

The comparison to baselines is problematic. The authors extend single-sentence models by simple concatenation, but these models weren't designed for sequence generation, making the comparison potentially unfair. For example, the "GloMatch" baseline uses weak image representations (GIST and Tiny images), while the proposed method uses VGGNet features, creating an uneven playing field. The paper also doesn't discuss computational complexity or scalability for longer image streams.

**Quality:** The technical approach is sound, with appropriate use of CNNs, BRNNs, and a coherence model. The experiments are well-designed with multiple evaluation metrics. However, the paper lacks a thorough discussion of limitations (e.g., performance on non-tourism domains, handling of abrupt visual transitions) and doesn't provide ablation studies on the coherence model's contribution beyond the RCN vs CRCN comparison.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with good figures illustrating the methodology and results. The technical details of CRCN are sufficiently detailed for reproduction.

**Originality:** The integration of a coherence model into image-to-sequence generation is novel, but the core idea of multi-sentence captioning is not. The paper would be stronger with a more nuanced discussion of related work and a clearer articulation of what's truly new.

**Significance:** The problem is relevant to real-world applications (social media, photo management), and the approach could be useful. However, the significance is somewhat limited by the blog dataset's domain specificity and the lack of evaluation on more diverse or practical applications.

**Arguments for acceptance:** The problem is well-motivated, the dataset is innovative, the evaluation is thorough with human studies, and the coherence model adds value for longer sequences. The paper makes a meaningful contribution to extending image captioning to sequential contexts.

**Arguments against acceptance:** The novelty is overstated, the comparison to baselines is unfair, and the paper fails to adequately address relevant prior work on multi-sentence captioning. The claims of being "the first" to address this problem undermine the paper's credibility.

Overall, this is a solid contribution that advances the field of image captioning by addressing sequence-level description. However, the paper would be significantly stronger with a more accurate assessment of related work, a fairer comparison to baselines, and a deeper justification for the coherence model. With these revisions, it would make a valuable contribution to NIPS. As it stands, the paper's significance is somewhat diminished by the novelty claim, but the core technical contribution remains relevant and well-executed.
