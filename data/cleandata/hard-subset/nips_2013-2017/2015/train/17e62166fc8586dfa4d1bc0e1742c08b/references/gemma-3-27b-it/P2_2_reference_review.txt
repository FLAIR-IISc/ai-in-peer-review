## Review of "Expressing an Image Stream with a Sequence of Natural Sentences"

**Summary:**

This paper tackles the problem of generating a sequence of natural language sentences to describe a sequence of images (an “image stream”), moving beyond the more common single image-single sentence captioning task. The authors propose a novel architecture, the Coherent Recurrent Convolutional Network (CRCN), which combines CNNs for image representation, BRNNs for language modeling, and a local coherence model to ensure fluent transitions between sentences. They leverage a large dataset constructed from blog posts containing image streams and accompanying text, and demonstrate superior performance compared to existing methods through quantitative metrics (BLEU, Top-K recall) and user studies.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The task of generating descriptions for *image streams* is a logical and valuable extension of single-image captioning, and relatively unexplored. The authors correctly identify this gap in the literature.
* **Technical Approach:** The CRCN architecture appears well-motivated and thoughtfully designed. Integrating a coherence model directly into the sequence generation process is a key contribution, addressing a significant challenge with multi-sentence descriptions. The use of BRNNs is also appropriate for capturing context.
* **Dataset:** Leveraging online blog posts as a source of image-text stream data is a clever and practical solution to the data scarcity problem.  The scale of the dataset (140K images, 20K blog posts) is impressive.
* **Evaluation:** The paper provides a comprehensive evaluation, including both automatic metrics and user studies. The AMT studies, with varying stream lengths, provide valuable insights into the effectiveness of the coherence model.
* **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The diagrams (Figs. 1 & 2) are helpful in understanding the approach.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Baselines:** While the authors compare to several relevant single-image captioning baselines, adapting these to the sequence generation task feels somewhat forced.  A more direct comparison to methods designed for video description (e.g., those incorporating temporal modeling) would strengthen the analysis.  The video description literature (mentioned in the related work) provides models specifically designed for sequential data.
* **Coherence Model Detail:** The description of the local coherence model (Section 3.2) feels a bit hand-wavy. While the authors mention entity grids and transition frequencies, the specifics of how this information is incorporated into the overall architecture (beyond the 300-dimensional vector) could be elaborated. The justification for the history parameter of three isn't fully clear.
* **Data Bias:** The use of blog post data introduces potential biases. Blog authors curate their image and text, presenting a particular style of storytelling.  The model might learn to mimic this style rather than generating truly general descriptions. This limitation isn’t sufficiently discussed.
* **Retrieval-Based Nature:** The method fundamentally relies on retrieving sentences from the training data. This limits the model's ability to generate truly novel sentences or describe situations not encountered in the training set. This is acknowledged, but the implications for originality could be discussed further.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a solid paper addressing an important and under-explored problem. The proposed CRCN architecture is novel and well-motivated, and the evaluation is thorough. While some weaknesses exist, particularly regarding the limited comparison to relevant video description methods and the potential for data bias, the paper represents a valuable contribution to the field of image and video understanding. The coherence model is a particularly strong aspect of the work.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* Addresses a novel and important problem.
* Proposes a technically sound and well-motivated architecture.
* Demonstrates strong performance through comprehensive evaluation.
* Uses a large and cleverly constructed dataset.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* Limited comparison to methods designed for sequential data (video description).
* Potential biases inherent in the blog post dataset.
* Relatively limited originality given the reliance on sentence retrieval.




**Qualitative Evaluation:**

* **Quality:**  Technically sound, with claims generally supported by experiments. The experimental setup is well described, and the results are presented clearly.
* **Clarity:**  Well-written and organized. The diagrams and equations are helpful.
* **Originality:**  The task and the integration of a coherence model are novel. The overall approach is a sensible combination of existing techniques.
* **Significance:**  The results are significant and potentially useful for applications involving image streams (e.g., automatic photo album generation, travelogue creation).



**Recommendation:**

**Accept** (with minor revisions).  I recommend accepting this paper, contingent on the authors addressing the concerns regarding the comparison to video description methods and further discussing the potential biases introduced by the dataset. A more detailed explanation of the coherence model would also be beneficial.
