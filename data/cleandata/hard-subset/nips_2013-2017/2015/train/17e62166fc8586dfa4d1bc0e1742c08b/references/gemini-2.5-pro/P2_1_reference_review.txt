### Summary

This paper addresses the novel and interesting problem of generating a coherent sequence of natural language sentences to describe a stream of images, such as a photo album from a trip. The authors propose a new architecture, the Coherent Recurrent Convolutional Network (CRCN), which integrates three main components: a Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) to extract features from individual images, a Bidirectional Recurrent Neural Network (BRNN) to model the semantic flow of the story, and an entity-based local coherence model to ensure grammatical and referential smoothness between sentences. The approach is framed as a retrieval task: for a given image stream, the model retrieves and ranks candidate sentence sequences from a large training corpus. The authors construct a new large-scale dataset from travel blog posts for training and evaluation. Through extensive experiments, including quantitative metrics (BLEU, R@K) and a user study on Amazon Mechanical Turk, they demonstrate that their method outperforms several state-of-the-art single-image captioning models that have been adapted for this sequence-to-sequence task.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Quality:** The paper is of high technical quality.

*   **Strengths:** The experimental evaluation is thorough and convincing. The authors compare their method against multiple strong baselines from recent literature ([9], [12], [30]) and, importantly, include ablation studies (CRCN vs. RCN vs. 1NN) that clearly demonstrate the contribution of their model's key components (the BRNN and the coherence model). The inclusion of both automatic metrics and a well-designed user study provides a robust validation of their claims. The user study's extension to longer sequences to specifically highlight the benefit of the coherence model is a particularly strong piece of evidence. The overall framework is technically sound and the training procedure, using a max-margin loss, is appropriate for the retrieval-based task.

*   **Weaknesses:** The data preprocessing pipeline seems to be a potential weak point. The alignment of images to text segments relies on a distance-based heuristic, and the summarization of text blocks into single sentences using LSA may introduce noise or lose crucial information. While necessary to create the training data, the potential impact of errors in this pipeline on the final model performance is not discussed. Furthermore, the model is not truly generative; it is constrained to re-using sentences from the training set. While this is a reasonable simplification for an initial foray into this problem, it is an important limitation.

**Clarity:** The paper is mostly well-written and organized, but could be improved in a few areas.

*   **Strengths:** The introduction provides excellent motivation for the problem, and Figure 1 clearly illustrates the task. The overall architecture is explained logically, and Figure 2 provides a helpful visual guide. The experimental setup and results are presented clearly.

*   **Weaknesses:** The explanation of how the coherence model's output is integrated into the final score is somewhat opaque. Equation 3 defines the score `Skl = ∑ (s_kt · v_lt + g_k · v_lt)`. Here, `g_k` is a single vector representing the coherence of the entire sentence sequence *k*, yet it is added to the calculation for each image-sentence pair at every timestep *t*. This implies the global coherence score modifies the compatibility at each local step. While this might be a valid modeling choice, the intuition behind it is not explained, making this key part of the model less understandable and harder to reproduce. A more detailed justification for this specific formulation would significantly improve the paper's clarity.

**Originality:** The paper makes several original contributions.

*   **Strengths:** To the best of my knowledge, this is the first work to address the problem of generating a multi-sentence paragraph from an *unordered* stream of images. This is a significant and novel step beyond single-image captioning or video description (where frames are temporally ordered and visually similar). The architectural contribution of combining modern deep learning models (CNN, BRNN) with a classic, symbolic NLP technique (the entity-grid coherence model of Barzilay and Lapata [1]) is also novel in this context. While the individual components are not new, their combination to solve this new problem is creative and effective. Finally, the creation and use of a large dataset from blog posts is a valuable contribution to the community.

**Significance:** The work is highly significant and likely to have an impact.

*   **Strengths:** This paper tackles a difficult problem that represents a natural and important frontier for vision and language research. Moving from describing single images to telling stories about collections of them has many potential applications (e.g., automatic photo album summarization, aiding content creation). The demonstrated success of the CRCN model provides a strong baseline and a clear path for future research in this area. Other researchers are likely to build upon this work, for instance, by exploring end-to-end differentiable coherence models or fully generative frameworks instead of retrieval-based ones. The work demonstrably advances the state of the art on this new, challenging task.

### Arguments for and against acceptance:

**Arguments for acceptance:**
1.  **Novel Problem Formulation:** It introduces and provides a strong solution for the novel and important task of generating sentence sequences from image streams.
2.  **Strong Empirical Results:** The paper presents a comprehensive evaluation with strong baselines, ablation studies, and a user study, all of which convincingly support the effectiveness of the proposed CRCN model.
3.  **Novel Architecture:** The combination of a BRNN for semantic flow and a classic entity-based model for grammatical coherence is an original and effective architectural choice.
4.  **High Potential Impact:** The work opens up a new direction in vision-and-language research and is likely to inspire follow-up work.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
1.  **Limited Clarity on a Key Component:** The paper lacks a clear intuitive explanation for the mathematical formulation used to integrate the coherence score into the final ranking function (Equation 3).
2.  **Retrieval-based Limitation:** The model is not fully generative, which limits its expressive power to sentences seen during training.
3.  **Heuristic-based Data Pipeline:** The training data is generated via a series of heuristics for alignment and summarization, which may introduce noise and could be a confounding factor in the model's performance.

**Conclusion:**
Despite some minor weaknesses in clarity and the inherent limitations of a retrieval-based approach, the strengths of this paper are substantial. It introduces a novel and significant problem, proposes an effective and original solution, and validates it with a thorough and convincing set of experiments. This is a solid piece of work that advances the state of the art and will be of high interest to the NIPS community. I recommend acceptance.
