

# Review

This paper introduces a compelling extension to the image captioning problem, shifting from single-image single-sentence generation to image stream to sentence sequence generation. The authors correctly identify a significant gap in the literature: while most work focuses on describing individual images with single sentences, real-world scenarios like documenting travel experiences involve sequences of images that should be described as cohesive narratives. This problem formulation is genuinely novel and well-motivated by practical use cases (e.g., tourists documenting NYC visits with multiple photos).

The paper's main contribution is recognizing this under-addressed problem space and proposing a framework (CRCN) to address it. The CRCN architecture integrates existing components (CNNs for image representation, BRNNs for sentence modeling, and a coherence model) in a novel way for this specific problem. The authors cleverly leverage blog posts as a source of natural text-image parallel data, which is a practical solution to the data scarcity problem in this domain.

The paper demonstrates strong empirical validation through both quantitative metrics (BLEU, CIDEr, METEOR, top-K recall) and user studies on Amazon Mechanical Turk. Their results show consistent improvements over baselines that simply concatenate single-sentence captions, particularly for longer sequences where coherence becomes critical. The user studies are especially valuable as they directly measure human preference for coherent narrative flow, which is the core challenge of this problem.

Strengths:
- The problem formulation is genuinely novel and addresses a meaningful gap in the literature
- The motivation is well-grounded in real-world usage patterns (people taking multiple photos of experiences)
- The blog-based data collection approach is clever and practical
- The coherence model specifically addresses the critical challenge of making sentence sequences flow naturally
- The evaluation combines quantitative metrics with human preference studies
- The paper clearly articulates how their work differs from related video captioning approaches

Weaknesses:
- The technical approach is largely a combination of existing components rather than a fundamentally new method
- The evaluation is limited to tourism-related blog posts, though the authors claim broader applicability
- The coherence model builds on prior work (Barzilay & Lapata, 2008) without significant adaptation
- The paper doesn't sufficiently explain why simply concatenating single-sentence captions wouldn't work for shorter sequences

However, as a reviewer who prioritizes novelty of problem space over methodological innovation, I find this paper's contribution highly valuable. The authors have correctly identified a significant extension of the image captioning problem that has been overlooked in the literature. Their framework for generating sentence sequences rather than single sentences represents a meaningful shift in perspective that opens up new research directions.

The paper's strength lies not in the technical novelty of the CRCN architecture (which is a sensible combination of existing techniques), but in recognizing the importance of the problem itself. This is exactly the kind of bold problem formulation that advances the field. The authors demonstrate that their approach solves the specific challenge of maintaining coherence across multiple sentences, which is crucial for realistic applications.

The paper's limitations in methodological innovation are outweighed by the novelty of the problem they've identified and addressed. The user studies showing improved preference for coherent sequences, especially as sequence length increases, provide strong evidence that this is a meaningful problem with a meaningful solution.

I believe this paper makes a significant contribution by expanding the scope of image captioning research to include narrative description of sequential visual experiences. The authors have successfully demonstrated that this problem is both well-motivated and solvable with their proposed framework. This work will likely inspire future research on more natural, multi-sentence descriptions of visual content.

I recommend acceptance of this paper. While the technical approach may not be groundbreaking, the problem formulation is novel and important, and the authors have provided compelling evidence that their approach addresses the core challenge of sentence sequence coherence. This paper opens up a new research direction that deserves attention in the community.
