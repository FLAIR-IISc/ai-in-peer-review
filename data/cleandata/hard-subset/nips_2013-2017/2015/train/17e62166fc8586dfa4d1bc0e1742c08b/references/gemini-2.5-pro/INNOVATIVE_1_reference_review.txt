### Review of "Expressing an Image Stream with a Sequence of Natural Sentences"

**Summary**

This paper introduces the novel problem of generating a coherent sequence of natural language sentences to describe a stream of images. This task is a significant and logical extension of the well-studied single-image-to-single-sentence captioning problem. The authors propose a new architecture, the Coherent Recurrent Convolutional Network (CRCN), to tackle this challenge. The model uniquely integrates three components: a standard CNN (VGGNet) for image feature extraction, a Bidirectional RNN to model the semantic flow of the sentence sequence, and, crucially, an explicit entity-based local coherence model inspired by classic NLP work [1] to ensure smooth and logical transitions between sentences. The approach is retrieval-based: for a query image stream, it first finds candidate sentences for each image and then uses the trained CRCN to score and rank possible sentence sequences, selecting the one that is most visually relevant and textually coherent. To train their model, the authors resourcefully curate a large-scale dataset from blog posts. The efficacy of the proposed method is demonstrated through extensive experiments, including automatic metrics and human studies on Amazon Mechanical Turk, showing clear improvements over adapted state-of-the-art single-image captioning baselines.

**Strengths**

1.  **High Novelty and Significance of the Problem:** The primary strength of this paper is its formulation of a new, important, and challenging problem. Moving from single images to image streams and from single sentences to coherent paragraphs is a bold and necessary step towards a more holistic understanding of visual narratives. This work has the potential to open up a new and fruitful subfield in multimodal research, addressing practical applications like summarizing photo albums from trips or events.

2.  **Creative and Bold Modeling Approach:** The proposed CRCN architecture is a compelling fusion of modern deep learning techniques and a classic, explicit model of discourse coherence. While many contemporary approaches might simply rely on a larger neural model (e.g., a Transformer or a more complex RNN) to implicitly learn coherence, the authors make a deliberate and intelligent choice to integrate an entity-grid-based coherence model. This explicit modeling of syntactic and referential structure is a non-obvious and powerful idea that directly targets the core challenge of generating multi-sentence text. This demonstrates a thoughtful approach to model design that I find highly commendable.

3.  **Strong and Convincing Empirical Validation:** The authors provide a thorough evaluation of their method. They compare against multiple strong baselines, which they have fairly adapted to their new task. The results, across both automatic metrics (BLEU, CIDEr, Recall@K) and human judgments, consistently show that the proposed method is superior. Crucially, the ablation study comparing the full CRCN model to an RCN variant (without the coherence model) clearly isolates and validates the contribution of the explicit coherence component, especially for longer sequences as shown in the AMT study.

4.  **Resourceful Data Collection Method:** Tackling a new problem often requires creating new datasets. The authors' strategy of mining blog posts to create a parallel corpus of image streams and associated text is both clever and effective, providing a scalable way to gather data for this task.

**Weaknesses**

While the paper is strong, there are some aspects that could be improved, though in my view, they do not detract from the core contribution.

1.  **Heuristic-driven Data Preprocessing:** The pipeline for processing blog data into aligned image-sentence pairs relies on several heuristics (e.g., text segmentation based on block distance, LSA for summarization). This process may introduce significant noise into the training data, as alignments and summaries may not always be accurate. While a necessary step to enable this research, the methodology is somewhat brittle. A deeper analysis of the noise introduced or an exploration of alternative alignment techniques would have strengthened the paper.

2.  **Limitations of a Retrieval-Based Framework:** The model is fundamentally constrained to composing paragraphs from sentences that already exist in the training corpus. It cannot generate truly novel sentences that might better capture unique details or combinations of objects in the query images. This is a reasonable simplification for a first work on this difficult problem, but it is an inherent limitation worth acknowledging more explicitly.

3.  **Approximate Inference:** The use of a divide-and-conquer beam search for inference is a practical necessity to handle the exponential search space. However, it is an approximate method. The reported performance is therefore a lower bound on what the model might achieve with a more optimal (if computationally infeasible) search. This is a minor point but relevant for context.

**Arguments for/against Acceptance**

*   **Pro:**
    *   Introduces a novel, significant, and challenging problem that pushes the field forward.
    *   Presents a creative and bold architecture that intelligently combines different modeling paradigms.
    *   The core idea of explicitly modeling coherence is well-motivated and empirically validated.
    *   Results are strong and convincingly demonstrate the superiority of the proposed approach.

*   **Con:**
    *   The data preprocessing pipeline is based on heuristics and could be a source of noise.
    *   The model is retrieval-based, limiting its generative capacity.
    *   Inference is based on an approximate search algorithm.

**Recommendation: Accept**

This is an excellent paper that I am enthusiastic to support. It tackles a genuinely new and important problem with a creative, well-designed, and effective solution. The core contribution—identifying the need for and successfully integrating an explicit coherence model for visual storytelling—is a significant one. While the methodology has some limitations, such as the heuristic-based data pipeline and the constraints of a retrieval framework, these are reasonable trade-offs in a paper that is the first to address such an ambitious task. The novelty and potential impact of this work far outweigh these minor imperfections. This paper charts a clear and exciting new direction for research in vision and language, and I expect it to be highly influential.
