Summary
This paper tackles “image stream to sentence sequence” generation: given a sequence of images (e.g., a travel photo stream), produce a coherent multi-sentence description. The proposed coherent recurrent convolutional network (CRCN) combines: (i) CNN image features (VGG fc7), (ii) a bidirectional RNN over sentence representations (doc2vec) to model content flow, and (iii) an entity-grid-based local coherence model (Barzilay & Lapata) built from parses and coreference to capture discourse transitions across sentences. The system is retrieval-based: for each image position, candidate sentences are retrieved from a large blog corpus (NYC and Disneyland), and a divide-and-conquer beam search ranks concatenations using a max-margin-trained compatibility score between the image stream and sentence sequence. Training data are automatically constructed from blogs via paragraph-based text-image alignment and LSA summarization. The paper reports improvements over strong single-image captioning baselines (concatenated per-image) in BLEU/CIDEr/METEOR, retrieval metrics, and AMT preferences, with larger gains for longer sequences.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The components are standard and reasonable: VGG features per image, doc2vec per sentence, BRNN to contextualize sentence embeddings, and an entity-grid coherence descriptor. The ranking objective with negative sampling and BPTT is appropriate. However, there are a few technical questions:
  - Score formulation: Skl = sum_t s_k,t · v_l,t + g_k · v_l,t uses the same coherence vector g_k against every image, effectively g_k · (sum_t v_l,t). This is plausible but deviates from the stated goal of modeling local coherence in relation to temporal alignment; a justification or ablation would help.
  - The coherence descriptor (64-d transition frequencies) is zero-padded to 300-d before FC layers. A learned projection (rather than zero padding) seems strictly better; it would be good to report whether direct projection or normalization matters.
  - The retrieval search (divide-and-conquer beam search) is heuristically justified; an empirical comparison to exhaustive search on shorter sequences would substantiate the “almost optimal” claim.
  - “Completely unsupervised” is overstated. The approach leverages weak supervision from automatically aligned blog image–text pairs; alignment heuristics (paragraph tokenization + distance) and LSA summarization are non-trivial supervision choices. Some analysis of alignment noise and its effect on learning is needed.
- Empirical support: The experimental protocol is sensible and includes human evaluation. However, the paper refers to quantitative tables we cannot see in the excerpt. Assuming the reported trends, the ablations (1NN vs RCN vs CRCN) support the value of BRNN and coherence, particularly for longer sequences, which aligns with the method’s intent. Stronger diagnostics (e.g., effect of sequence length, diversity, and per-component ablations including removing g or replacing entity-grid with simpler bigram coherence) would further strengthen claims.

Clarity
- The paper is generally well organized and readable, with clear problem setup and architecture diagrams. Implementation details (optimizers, dropout, dimensions) are reported, which aids reproducibility.
- Some notational/typographical issues: “ne examples” presumably means “negative examples”; clarity on the dimensions and normalization of g and s_t would help; more detail is needed on candidate generation and pruning (e.g., how duplicates are handled, whether repetition penalties are used).
- The claim of “end-to-end” is somewhat misleading since doc2vec is trained separately and parsing/coref are external; clarifying the training pipeline would improve transparency.

Originality
- Problem formulation—jointly describing an image stream with a multi-sentence paragraph—is timely and distinct from single-image captioning and short-video description. While related to video-to-text, the explicit modeling of coherence is novel in this context.
- Methodologically, CRCN is a thoughtful combination of known components (CNN, BRNN, entity grid, ranking loss) rather than a fundamentally new learning paradigm. The integration of an entity-based coherence model into a multimodal ranking framework is a useful and original design choice.
- Related work is broadly covered; the paper distinguishes itself from video captioning, single-image captioning, and image-sequence retrieval. It would be helpful to discuss paragraph-level retrieval/embedding baselines (e.g., paragraph encoders trained end-to-end) as an alternative to sentence-wise candidate concatenation.

Significance
- Addressing multi-sentence, coherent descriptions is important; many downstream applications (storytelling, summarization, albums) require coherence beyond per-image captions.
- The large-scale weakly-aligned blog corpus is valuable. If released, it could benefit the community.
- The demonstrated gains—especially in human preference for longer sequences—suggest practical impact. That said, the approach is retrieval-based; generalization beyond the training domain and sentence inventory is limited compared to generative models. A discussion of domain transfer (e.g., to non-tourism data) and coverage would contextualize applicability.

Strengths
- Clear, relevant problem and motivation.
- Sensible architecture that explicitly models discourse coherence.
- Large-scale weak supervision from blogs; principled training objective.
- Comprehensive evaluation protocol with human studies and ablations indicating the benefit of coherence for longer texts.

Weaknesses
- Overstatement of “unsupervised” and “end-to-end”; reliance on external doc2vec and parsing.
- Limited analysis of the alignment quality and parsing/coref errors, especially since sentences may come from different sources and coreference chains may not be meaningful across them.
- Heuristic search with limited justification; no analysis of near-optimality.
- Coherence term’s interaction with image features is somewhat ad hoc (global g applied at every timestep); missing ablations on its precise formulation.
- Retrieval-only generation limits novelty/diversity and may copy boilerplate; diversity control and repetition handling are not discussed.

Questions and suggestions for authors
- Provide an ablation on the coherence term: g·sum(v), per-timestep weighting, or learned attention over positions. Does coherence help when computed over the final selected sentences vs. candidate pools?
- Analyze the effect of sequence length and domain shift. How does performance degrade on longer (>10) sequences or on a different domain?
- Include diagnostics on alignment noise and parser/coreference accuracy, and their impact on learning.
- Compare to a paragraph-level retrieval baseline (encode full image stream and entire paragraphs with a learned multimodal embedding).
- Consider learning a projection for the entity-grid vector rather than zero-padding, and report its impact.

Overall assessment
- Quality: good (with caveats about coherence scoring and search analysis)
- Clarity: good
- Originality: moderate to high (problem framing and coherence integration)
- Significance: moderate (practical relevance; retrieval limits generalization)

Arguments pro acceptance
- Addresses an important, under-explored problem and shows coherent, multi-sentence improvements, especially in human evaluation.
- Sound integration of discourse coherence into multimodal modeling.
- Scalable training on weakly aligned data.

Arguments con acceptance
- Method is a combination of known pieces; novelty is incremental.
- Some technical choices (coherence scoring, search) are heuristic and under-analyzed.
- Retrieval-based nature and domain specificity may limit broader impact without released data/code and transfer analysis.

If the quantitative gains are as claimed and tables substantiate them, I lean toward borderline accept; otherwise, this is a strong workshop/near-miss that would benefit from the suggested analyses.
