This paper addresses the novel and interesting problem of generating a sequence of natural language sentences to describe a stream of images. The authors motivate this task by the common user behavior of capturing experiences (e.g., a trip) as a series of photos. To tackle this, they propose a Coherent Recurrent Convolutional Network (CRCN), a multimodal architecture that integrates three primary components: a CNN for image feature extraction, a bidirectional RNN to model the flow of text, and an entity-based local coherence model to ensure smooth transitions between sentences. A key contribution is the use of online blog posts as a source of weakly supervised parallel data, which are pre-processed via text segmentation and summarization. The model is trained using a max-margin objective to rank correct image-text sequence pairs higher than incorrect ones. At test time, the task is framed as a retrieval problem, where candidate sentences are selected for each image, and a beam-search-like strategy is employed to find the best overall sequence. The proposed method is evaluated on two blog datasets (NYC and Disneyland) and shows superior performance over adapted single-image captioning baselines on both automatic metrics and human evaluations.

### Strengths:

1.  **Novel Problem Formulation:** The paper extends the popular image-captioning task from a single image-sentence pair to a sequence-to-sequence problem (image stream to paragraph). This is a logical and important next step for the field, with clear practical applications in summarizing personal photo collections or generating descriptive travelogues.

2.  **Clever Data Sourcing:** The approach of mining blog posts for parallel image-text sequence data is resourceful. It circumvents the need for expensive manual annotation and provides a scalable way to collect large datasets, which is crucial for training deep models.

3.  **Well-motivated Architecture:** The architecture explicitly models different aspects of the problem. The CNN for visual features and the BRNN for textual context are standard but appropriate choices. The key novelty is the integration of an explicit coherence model [1], which is well-justified by the nature of the problem, as disconnected sentences generated for individual images would likely form an incoherent paragraph.

4.  **Thorough Evaluation:** The authors conduct a comprehensive evaluation, including multiple automatic metrics (BLEU, CIDEr, METEOR, R@K) and a carefully designed Amazon Mechanical Turk user study. The study's design, particularly the comparison between short and long sequences to highlight the benefit of the coherence model, is a strong point.

### Weaknesses:

1.  **Clarity and Technical Soundness:**
    *   The description of the final compatibility score in Equation (3) is ambiguous and potentially flawed. The equation `Skl = ∑t(skt · vlt + gk · vlt)` suggests that the global coherence score `gk` is dotted with *each* individual image feature vector `vlt` and then summed. This is structurally questionable. A global property of the text sequence (`gk`) should arguably be compared against a global property of the image sequence (e.g., an aggregation of `vlt` vectors), not repeatedly against each local image feature. This core formulation requires significant clarification.
    *   The claim in Section 1 that the approach is "completely unsupervised" is inaccurate. The model is trained on image-text pairs mined from blogs. While this data is "found" and not manually annotated for this specific task, it represents a form of weak or self-supervision, as it relies on the semantic alignment curated by human blog authors. The term "unsupervised" is misleading.
    *   In Section 3.1, the authors state their modified BRNN "empirically leads a better performance" but provide no ablation study or quantitative evidence in the experiments to support this specific architectural choice.

2.  **Methodological Choices:**
    *   Several components of the pipeline feel dated. Text summarization relies on LSA [4] from 2002, and the coherence model [1] is from 2008. While these are seminal works, more recent neural approaches to summarization and coherence modeling might have provided stronger performance and a more unified, end-to-end architecture.
    *   The model is framed as a generation task but is fundamentally a retrieval and re-ranking system. It is constrained to using sentences that already exist in the training corpus. This limitation is not sufficiently discussed, and the title "Expressing an Image Stream with a Sequence of Natural Sentences" might imply a more generative capability.

3.  **Presentation and Formatting:** The paper suffers from a distracting number of minor formatting and stylistic errors, which collectively reduce its perceived quality.
    *   The citation list is incomplete and poorly formatted. For example, [7], [28], and [29] are listed as "arXiv" without publication venues (where applicable), and [1] and [2] are missing years and publisher details. The formatting is inconsistent across entries.
    *   In-text citations are not sorted numerically (e.g., "[5, 8, 9, 12, 14, 15, 26, 21, 30]").
    *   There are minor grammatical infelicities and awkward phrases, such as "hike of interest" in the introduction.
    *   The "abuse of notation" mentioned in Section 2.1 for the augmented sentences is confusing and could have been easily avoided with clearer definitions.

### Summary of Pros and Cons:

**Pros:**
*   Addresses a novel and well-motivated problem.
*   Proposes a clever, scalable method for data collection.
*   The architecture explicitly models sequence flow and coherence.
*   Evaluation is thorough and includes a well-designed human study.

**Cons:**
*   Major ambiguity in the core scoring function (Eq. 3).
*   Inaccurate claims (e.g., "completely unsupervised").
*   Several methodological components are dated.
*   The model is retrieval-based, not truly generative, a point that is not made clear.
*   The manuscript is marred by numerous formatting and presentation errors, especially in the bibliography.

### Recommendation:

**Reject.**

While the paper presents a novel problem and a promising direction, the combination of a critical lack of clarity in the model's core scoring function and a substantial number of minor flaws in methodology and presentation prevent me from recommending acceptance at this time. The ambiguity in Equation 3 is a significant technical issue that needs to be resolved. Furthermore, the sheer volume of presentation errors, particularly in the citations, falls short of the high standards expected for a NIPS publication. I believe the work has potential, but it requires a major revision to address the technical clarity, refine the methodological claims, and thoroughly polish the manuscript.
