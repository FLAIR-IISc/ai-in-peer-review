### **Summary of the Paper**

This paper addresses the novel and challenging task of generating a sequence of natural language sentences to describe a stream of images. This extends the well-studied problem of single-image captioning to a multi-image, multi-sentence setting, which is motivated by real-world scenarios like describing a photo album from a trip. The authors propose a new model, the Coherent Recurrent Convolutional Network (CRCN), which integrates three main components: a Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) for image feature extraction, a Bidirectional Recurrent Neural Network (BRNN) to model the semantic flow of sentences, and a classic entity-based local coherence model to ensure grammatical and referential smoothness between sentences.

A key contribution is the method for acquiring training data: the authors leverage blog posts, which naturally contain sequences of images accompanied by narrative text. They pre-process these blogs to create a parallel corpus of image streams and corresponding sentence sequences. The proposed model operates in a retrieval-based framework: for a new image stream, it first finds candidate sentences for each image from the training data and then uses the learned CRCN to score and rank all possible combinations of these sentences, selecting the one that is both visually relevant and textually coherent. The method is evaluated on two new blog-post datasets (NYC and Disneyland) using both automated metrics (BLEU, CIDEr, R@K) and human judgments via Amazon Mechanical Turk, demonstrating superior performance over adapted state-of-the-art single-image captioning baselines.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Novel and Significant Problem Formulation:** The paper tackles a well-motivated and logical next step in vision-and-language research. Moving from single images to image streams is a non-trivial extension that better reflects how humans capture and share experiences. This work is among the first to formally define and provide a strong baseline for this problem, which is a significant contribution.

2.  **Sound and Well-Motivated Architecture:** The CRCN model is intelligently designed. The authors rightly recognize that coherence in a narrative has multiple facets. The BRNN is well-suited to capture high-level semantic flow, while the entity-grid model [1] explicitly handles lower-level linguistic phenomena like pronoun references and consistent grammatical roles of entities across sentences. Combining these two distinct views of textual coherence with a strong visual model (VGGNet) is a clear and powerful approach.

3.  **Creative Data Sourcing and Curation:** Creating a dataset for this new task is a major challenge. The idea of using blog posts as a source of weakly supervised, naturally occurring parallel data is clever and scalable. The pre-processing pipeline, while heuristic, provides a practical solution to transform unstructured web content into a usable training corpus.

4.  **Thorough and Convincing Experimental Evaluation:** The evaluation is a major strength. The authors compare their method against several strong, relevant baselines adapted from the single-image captioning literature. The inclusion of both language-generation metrics and retrieval metrics provides a comprehensive view of performance. Furthermore, the ablation study (CRCN vs. RCN vs. 1NN) clearly demonstrates the value of both the BRNN and the coherence model. The user studies on AMT provide crucial validation that the model's output is preferred by humans, and the experiment showing that the coherence model's benefit increases with sequence length is particularly insightful.

### **Weaknesses and Questions**

1.  **Retrieval vs. Generation:** A key aspect that is somewhat understated is that the proposed method is fundamentally retrieval-based, not generative. It composes narratives from sentences that already exist in the training corpus. In contrast, several of the main baselines (e.g., [9, 30]) are generative models that can produce novel sentences. This is a crucial distinction. While retrieval-based methods can guarantee fluency for individual sentences, they are limited in their ability to describe truly novel content or compositions of objects/events not seen in the training data. This makes the comparison to generative models less direct, as they are solving different underlying problems (retrieval vs. generation). The paper would be strengthened by a more explicit discussion of this trade-off.

2.  **Scalability and Optimality of the Test-Time Search:** The number of candidate sentence sequences grows exponentially with the length of the image stream (K^N). The authors propose a divide-and-conquer strategy with beam search to make this tractable. However, this is an approximate search that is not guaranteed to find the optimal sequence according to the model's score. More analysis is needed on how this approximation affects performance. For instance, how often does the beam search fail to find the globally optimal sequence, and how sensitive are the results to the beam size (M)? This seems like a potential bottleneck for longer sequences.

3.  **Clarity of the Scoring Function (Eq. 3):** There is a potential technical issue or lack of clarity in Equation (3). The score `Skl` includes the term `∑t(gk · vlt)`. Here, `gk` represents the coherence vector for the *entire* sentence sequence `k`, yet it is being dotted with the feature vector `vlt` for *each individual* image `t` in the stream `l`. This seems unusual. Why should a global textual coherence score be matched against each image individually? One might expect a global text score to be matched with a global image stream representation (e.g., `mean(vl)`), or for it to be a separate term added to the total score, independent of the per-image matching. This formulation requires clearer justification or may be a typo.

4.  **Potential for Noise in Data Pre-processing:** The data creation pipeline relies on heuristics for text segmentation (distance-based assignment) and summarization (LSA-based). These steps could introduce significant label noise. For example, a paragraph might be wrongly associated with an image, or the LSA summary might not capture the most relevant sentence. The paper does not analyze the model's robustness to this potential noise. How much does a "cleaner" (e.g., hand-aligned) dataset improve performance?

5.  **Misleading Use of "Unsupervised":** The introduction states the approach is "completely unsupervised." This is inaccurate. The model is trained on image-text pairs mined from blogs. While it doesn't require manual annotation in the traditional sense, it is a form of weak or self-supervision, not unsupervised learning. This terminology should be corrected.

### **Conclusion**

Overall, this is a strong paper that introduces a novel and important problem to the community and provides a well-designed and thoroughly evaluated solution. The core ideas of modeling multi-sentence coherence and leveraging blog data are significant contributions. The primary weaknesses relate to the limitations of the retrieval-based approach and some lack of clarity in the model's technical details. Despite these points, the paper's strengths in problem formulation, methodology, and evaluation make it a valuable contribution to the field. It clearly advances the state of the art and will likely inspire future work on narrative generation from visual data.

**Recommendation: Accept**
