Summary and relation to prior work
The paper addresses the problem of turning an image stream into a coherent multi-sentence description. The proposed coherent recurrent convolutional network (CRCN) retrieves sentences (not word-by-word generation) conditioned on an image sequence. The model has three parts: (i) image features from VGG fc7; (ii) a bidirectional RNN over paragraph vectors (doc2vec) that models sentence content in context; (iii) an entity-grid–based local coherence model (Barzilay & Lapata) that encodes sentence-to-sentence discourse transitions. Two fully connected layers map both the BRNN outputs and the coherence descriptor into the same 4096-D space as the image features, and the compatibility score sums dot products between per-sentence text vectors and corresponding image vectors, plus an additional dot product between a global “coherence” vector and each image vector. Training uses a structured max-margin objective over aligned image–sentence sequences; decoding is a KNN-based retrieval with approximate beam search to assemble a sentence sequence.

This builds on: deep visual-semantic alignment and retrieval/generation for single images (e.g., Karpathy & Fei-Fei; Kiros et al.; Vinyals et al.), video-to-text (Donahue et al.; Rohrbach et al.), and entity-grid coherence modeling (Barzilay & Lapata). The main novelty claimed is modeling sequences on both sides (image streams and multi-sentence outputs) with an explicit coherence component.

Strengths
- Problem formulation: Moving from single-image/single-sentence to image streams and multi-sentence outputs is important and underexplored at the time; using blogs as weakly aligned multimodal data is resourceful.
- Architecture: Integrating a discourse entity-grid coherence signal with a context-sensitive sentence-level embedding (BRNN) is interesting and plausibly beneficial for multi-sentence fluency.
- Practical retrieval framing: Using sentence retrieval avoids the brittleness of word-level generation on noisy blog data and leverages naturally written sentences.
- Empirical evaluation: A fairly large-scale dataset (NYC, Disneyland), multiple automatic metrics, and human preferences via AMT. Ablations (1NN, RCN vs CRCN) attempt to isolate components.

Weaknesses and concerns
Quality/technical soundness
- Scoring function and length effects: Skl = sum_t s_k,t·v_l,t + g_k·v_l,t adds the same coherence vector dot-product term g_k·v_l,t for every t. This effectively multiplies a sequence-level scalar by the number of images, conflating length with score unless lengths are controlled. No normalization for sequence length is described. Clarify and, ideally, normalize or justify this choice.
- Coherence-image interaction: The coherence descriptor is computed purely from text; mapping it to the image space and dotting with every image feature is hard to interpret semantically. Why should a discourse pattern align with per-image features repeatedly? An alternative would be to use coherence as a prior/penalty independent of image features or as a factor in transitions between sentences, not a per-image dot product.
- Variable-length negatives: The loss compares pairs of potentially different lengths, yet the score sums over t=1..N without defining how N is chosen when lengths differ (min, max, padding?). This is a critical missing detail for both training and evaluation and can bias learning.
- Recurrent modeling: The BRNN uses vanilla ReLU units rather than gated LSTM/GRU, despite long-range dependencies and known vanishing gradient issues acknowledged by the authors. An ablation with LSTM/GRU (especially since a baseline uses LSTM) would be appropriate.
- Candidate search: The approximate divide-and-conquer beam search is under-specified. What is the exact objective decomposed at subproblems? How are boundary interactions between sub-sequences handled so that local optimality implies global near-optimality? Reproducibility is at risk without details.
- Reliance on noisy alignment: Paragraph-to-image alignment via simple index distance and LSA summarization is likely very noisy on blogs; there is no quantitative analysis of alignment quality or its effect on performance. No comparison to stronger alignment (e.g., learned attention, multimodal similarity) is presented.

Clarity and reproducibility
- Several crucial implementation details are missing or ambiguous: whether CNN is fine-tuned or fixed; how variable lengths are handled; tokenization/sentence limits for doc2vec; optimization schedule and early stopping; precise settings for entity extraction/coreference (coreference resolution on noisy blogs can be unreliable).
- The evaluation protocol for retrieval metrics is unclear. The paper says training and test are disjoint, so “groundtruth retrieval” among training candidates seems impossible; please clarify what “R@K” and median rank refer to in a sequence setting and how ground truth is defined among candidates.

Originality
- Combining an entity-grid coherence model with a neural sequence scoring function for multimodal sequence-to-sequence retrieval is novel and interesting. However, many other components (VGG features, doc2vec, BRNN, max-margin ranking, KNN candidate pool) are standard. The main novelty is in the integration and the task setting.

Significance
- The task is important, but the current evaluation leaves uncertainty about how much the coherence model helps beyond a context-sensitive sentence embedding (BRNN). Reported gains on language metrics are small; larger gains appear mainly on retrieval metrics whose definition is unclear. AMT shows modest but positive preferences that grow with sequence length, which is encouraging.
- Generality is limited to retrieval on domain-specific blog corpora; it is not evident that the approach extends to open-domain story generation or to settings without large in-domain sentence pools.

Suggestions for improvement
- Clarify and, if needed, revise the scoring function: normalize for sequence length; justify the role of the coherence term; consider modeling coherence as transitions between adjacent sentences (e.g., pairwise potentials) rather than a global vector dotted with image features.
- Provide exact handling of variable-length sequences in training and evaluation. Include ablations normalizing scores by length.
- Strengthen the recurrent component with LSTM/GRU and compare to the current BRNN.
- Add ablations: remove entity-grid, replace with simpler pairwise lexical/co-reference features; vary K (nearest neighbors), beam width M, and κ in data augmentation.
- Improve alignment: compare the heuristic alignment to a learned image–sentence alignment or to a stronger summarizer; quantify alignment noise.
- Expand evaluation clarity: define retrieval metrics precisely; report statistical significance and inter-annotator agreement on AMT; include error analyses and qualitative failures.
- If possible, release the dataset and code to improve reproducibility and impact.

Pros
- Addresses an underexplored and relevant multimodal sequence task.
- Interesting integration of discourse coherence with neural sequence scoring.
- Sensible retrieval framing for noisy data; reasonable human evaluation.

Cons
- Ambiguities and potential flaws in scoring and training (length handling, coherence-image coupling).
- Under-specified search and evaluation protocols; reproducibility concerns.
- Limited gains on language metrics; unclear fairness of baselines; no strong ablation isolating the coherence model’s contribution.

Recommendation
Borderline reject. The problem and high-level idea are compelling and potentially impactful, but the paper needs clearer, more rigorous methodology and evaluation (especially around the scoring function, sequence length, and retrieval metrics) to support its claims and ensure reproducibility. With the suggested clarifications and ablations, this work could become a strong contribution.
