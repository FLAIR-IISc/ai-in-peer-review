

# Review of "Expressing an Image Stream with a Sequence of Natural Sentences"

This paper proposes a novel task of generating sentence sequences to describe image streams, rather than single-image captioning. The authors introduce Coherent Recurrent Convolutional Networks (CRCN), which integrates CNNs for image representation, bidirectional RNNs for sentence modeling, and a local coherence model for sentence transitions. The approach leverages blog posts as a source of text-image parallel data.

**Strengths:**
The paper identifies a meaningful gap in the literature (extending from single-image to image-stream description) and provides a well-motivated solution. The CRCN architecture integrates several existing techniques in a novel way, and the blog-based dataset construction is a reasonable approach to the data scarcity problem. The experimental evaluation covers both quantitative metrics and user studies, and the results demonstrate consistent improvements over baselines.

**Weaknesses:**
1. **Technical Inconsistencies:** The paper contains numerous minor but troubling technical inconsistencies. In Section 3.1, the BRNN equations use inconsistent notation (e.g., "xft = f(W f i pt + b f i )" vs. "x f t = f(W f i pt + b f i )"). Section 3.3 states "We drop the bias terms for the fully-connected layers" but earlier in Section 3.1, the BRNN model explicitly includes bias terms. This creates confusion about the model's architecture.

2. **Methodological Ambiguity:** The paper claims to be "completely unsupervised" (Section 1) but uses labeled blog posts with image-text alignments, which is a form of weak supervision. This contradiction needs clarification.

3. **Evaluation Limitations:** The AMT user study (Section 4.3) restricts queries to 5 images maximum, despite the paper's claim that coherence becomes more critical for longer passages. The final results in Table 2 show minimal differences between CRCN and RCN (the coherence model variant) precisely because the test sequences are too short (5 images), contradicting the paper's own claim about coherence's importance for longer sequences.

4. **Data Splitting Ambiguity:** The paper states "We randomly split our dataset into 80% as a training set, 10% as a validation, and the others as a test set" (Section 4.1), but "the others" is unclear. The dataset sizes in Section 4.1 (11,863 NYC + 7,717 Disneyland = 19,580 blog posts) don't match the "more than 20K" claim in Section 1.

5. **Citation Inconsistencies:** The paper cites "Modeling Local Coherence: An Entity-Based Approach, R. Barzilay, M. Lapata, ACL" as [1], but the reference list entry is incomplete (missing year, pages, etc.). The paper also inconsistently formats citations (e.g., [5, 8, 9, 12, 14, 15, 26, 21, 30] vs. [9, 12, 21]).

6. **Technical Detail Omissions:** The paper states "We choose κ = 3 after thorough empirical tests" (Section 2.1) but doesn't provide any details about these tests or the specific metrics used to select this value. Similarly, the "approximate divide-and-conquer strategy" (Section 3.5) is described vaguely without sufficient technical detail.

7. **BLEU Limitation:** The paper acknowledges BLEU's limitations for paragraph evaluation (Section 4.2) but still relies heavily on it for language metrics, which is a significant methodological flaw.

**Minor but Annoying Issues:**
- "hike" instead of "hike" in the first paragraph
- Inconsistent capitalization of technical terms (e.g., "Bidirectional Recurrent Neural Networks" vs. "bidirectional recurrent neural networks")
- The paper refers to "Fig.1" without providing the figure, making it difficult to verify claims
- The paper claims to be the "first to address" this problem, but related work in [11] addresses the inverse problem (retrieving image sequences from text), suggesting the problem space is not entirely novel

**Recommendation:**
This paper has a compelling problem statement and a novel architecture that integrates several existing techniques. However, the numerous technical inconsistencies, ambiguous methodological descriptions, and evaluation limitations significantly undermine the paper's quality. While the core idea is promising, the paper fails to present a rigorous technical contribution due to these minor but critical flaws.

The paper is technically sound in its overall approach but lacks the meticulous attention to detail required for NIPS. The inconsistencies in the model description and evaluation methodology prevent readers from fully understanding and potentially reproducing the work. Given the paper's stated focus on coherence for longer text sequences, the evaluation on very short sequences (5 images) is particularly problematic.

While the work is not completely without merit, the cumulative effect of these minor but pervasive technical flaws means the paper does not meet NIPS's high standards for technical rigor. I recommend rejection. The authors should address these inconsistencies, provide more detailed experimental analysis (including longer sequence evaluations), and clarify the unsupervised claim before resubmitting to a venue with more lenient technical standards.
