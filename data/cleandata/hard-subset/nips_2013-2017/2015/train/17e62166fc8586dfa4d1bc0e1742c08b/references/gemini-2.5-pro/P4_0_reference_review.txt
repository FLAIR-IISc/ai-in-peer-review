**Summary**

This paper addresses the novel problem of generating a coherent sequence of natural language sentences to describe a stream of images. This extends the well-studied task of single-image-to-single-sentence generation. The authors propose a multimodal architecture named Coherent Recurrent Convolutional Networks (CRCN). This model integrates three main components: (1) a Convolutional Neural Network (CNN, specifically VGGNet) to extract features from each image, (2) a Bidirectional Recurrent Neural Network (BRNN) to model the content flow and dependencies across the sequence of sentences (represented by paragraph vectors), and (3) an entity-based local coherence model, adapted from Barzilay and Lapata (2008), which captures the transitions of grammatical roles of discourse entities across sentences to ensure narrative fluency.

The model is trained end-to-end using a max-margin structured loss objective, which encourages aligned image-sentence sequence pairs to score higher than misaligned pairs. Generation at test time is framed as a retrieval problem. For a query image stream, candidate sentences are sourced from the training data associated with K-nearest neighbor images for each position in the stream. An approximate divide-and-conquer search strategy is then used to find the sentence sequence that maximizes the compatibility score produced by the trained CRCN model. To facilitate this research, the authors collect and process a large-scale dataset from blog posts on tourism topics (NYC and Disneyland). The proposed method is evaluated against several state-of-the-art single-image captioning methods adapted for this sequence-to-sequence task, demonstrating superior performance on both automatic metrics and human user studies.

**Quality**

The paper is technically sound. The proposed architecture is a logical and well-motivated combination of established techniques to address the challenges of the new problem. The claims are well-supported by a comprehensive set of experiments. The authors compare their method against multiple strong baselines and include important ablation studies (e.g., comparing CRCN vs. RCN) that clearly demonstrate the contribution of the key components of their model, particularly the coherence model. The use of both automatic metrics (BLEU, CIDEr, retrieval metrics) and human evaluation via Amazon Mechanical Turk provides a robust and convincing assessment of the model's performance. The training procedure, including the max-margin objective and optimization details, is clearly described and follows established practices in the field. The work appears to be a complete and well-executed piece of research.

**Clarity**

The paper is very clearly written and well-organized. The problem statement is introduced with a compelling example in Figure 1. The overall architecture is effectively illustrated in Figure 2, which helps the reader understand the flow of information through the different components. The authors provide sufficient detail on the pre-processing of the blog data, the formulation of each model component (BRNN, coherence model), the training objective, and the inference-time retrieval strategy, which should allow an expert reader to understand and potentially reproduce the work. The related work section effectively positions the paper within the broader context of image-and-language research, highlighting the key differences from single image captioning and video description.

**Originality**

The main originality of this work lies in the formulation of the problem itself: expressing an image stream with a sequence of sentences. To the best of my knowledge, this is one of the first papers to formally address this task, which is a significant and non-trivial extension of single image captioning. While the individual components of the CRCN model (CNNs, BRNNs, entity-grid coherence models) are not new in their respective fields, their integration into a unified framework for this specific task is novel. The explicit modeling of linguistic coherence using a classic NLP model, in conjunction with deep sequence models, is a notable contribution that directly addresses the core challenge of generating a fluent narrative rather than a disjointed list of captions. The paper also contributes a valuable methodology for creating a large-scale dataset for this task from unstructured blog posts.

**Significance**

The results presented are significant. The problem of describing image collections is of high practical importance, with applications in photo album summarization, storytelling, and content accessibility. This paper provides a strong first baseline for this new and challenging task. The demonstrated success of integrating an explicit coherence model suggests a promising direction for future research in long-form text generation conditioned on visual inputs. Other researchers are likely to build upon this work, either by exploring more sophisticated coherence models, developing fully generative (as opposed to retrieval-based) solutions, or applying the framework to different domains. The thorough evaluation and publicly released code (if made available) would be a valuable resource for the community.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   **Novel Problem Formulation:** The paper pioneers a new, important, and challenging task that is a logical next step for the vision-and-language community.
*   **Novel Model Architecture:** The CRCN model is a sensible and novel combination of deep learning and classic NLP techniques, specifically tailored to the proposed task. The integration of an explicit coherence model is a key strength.
*   **Thorough and Convincing Evaluation:** The experimental validation is comprehensive, featuring multiple strong baselines, ablation studies, a wide range of metrics, and crucial human evaluation that supports the main claims.
*   **High Clarity and Quality:** The paper is well-written, well-organized, and technically sound.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   **Retrieval-based Generation:** The method is not truly generative; it retrieves and rearranges sentences from the training set. This limits the novelty and diversity of the generated text. A discussion of the trade-offs of this approach versus a fully generative one would strengthen the paper.
*   **Complexity of Inference:** The inference process involves a K-nearest neighbor search followed by a combinatorial beam search, which may be computationally intensive and is an approximation of the full search.
*   **Dependence on Pre-processing:** The system's performance relies heavily on a multi-stage pre-processing pipeline (segmentation, summarization) which may introduce noise or errors not accounted for in the evaluation.
