

# Review of "Expressing an Image Stream with a Sequence of Natural Sentences"

This paper introduces a novel problem formulation: moving from single-image single-sentence captioning to generating sentence sequences for image streams. The authors correctly identify that while most existing work focuses on single-image captioning, real-world user behavior (e.g., taking multiple photos of a NYC visit) suggests a natural progression to image streams and sentence sequences. They propose a CRCN architecture that integrates CNNs for image representation, bidirectional RNNs for sentence modeling, and a coherence model to ensure smooth transitions between sentences. The authors collect a large dataset from blog posts (20K posts, 140K images) by processing text segmentation and summarization, and evaluate with quantitative metrics and user studies.

The paper's primary strength lies in its problem formulation, which is genuinely novel in the image captioning literature. The authors make a compelling case that this represents an important next step beyond existing work (e.g., [5, 9, 12, 21, 30]) that focuses on single-image single-sentence generation. While the components of CRCN (CNN, BRNN, coherence model) are largely borrowed from existing work, their integration for this specific problem is novel and well-justified. The coherence model, though based on prior work [1], is particularly well-motivated for this problem, as consecutive images in streams often show sharp content changes that disrupt sentence flow.

The paper's significance is substantial. It addresses a practical gap in the literature that aligns with how people actually use cameras (capturing photo streams rather than single images). The authors demonstrate through both quantitative metrics (BLEU, CIDEr, recall) and user studies that coherence matters significantly for sentence sequences, with improvements becoming more pronounced as sequence length increases. The blog-based dataset collection approach is creative and provides a large-scale resource for this new problem.

The paper is well-written and clearly structured. The methodology is sufficiently detailed for reproduction, and the experiments are comprehensive with both quantitative evaluation and user studies. The authors acknowledge limitations (e.g., the coherence model's importance increases with sequence length) and compare against relevant baselines, including extended versions of existing single-sentence models.

While the methodological innovation is modest (a combination of existing components), the problem formulation is genuinely novel and important. As a reviewer who prioritizes novel ideas over methodological perfection, I find this paper's contribution valuable. The authors make a strong case that this is a significant step toward "joint understanding of the visual content of images and language descriptions," addressing a gap that has been overlooked in current literature.

The main weaknesses are:
1. The core architecture doesn't introduce fundamentally new components (though their combination for this problem is novel)
2. The dataset is limited to tourism blogs, though the authors claim domain-agnosticity
3. The coherence model is based on prior work with limited new contribution to coherence modeling

However, these weaknesses are outweighed by the paper's significant novelty in problem formulation and its clear demonstration of the importance of coherence for sentence sequences. The paper establishes a new research direction that other researchers can build upon.

The paper is clearly in scope for NIPS as it addresses a significant problem at the intersection of computer vision and NLP, with a novel approach to a well-motivated problem. The authors have adequately referenced related work, including key NIPS papers like [5, 9, 12, 30].

In summary, this paper makes a strong contribution by identifying and addressing an important gap in the image captioning literature. While the method is a combination of existing techniques, the problem formulation is genuinely novel and the results demonstrate the significance of coherence for sentence sequences. The paper represents a meaningful step beyond the current state of the art in image captioning.

**Recommendation: Accept** (despite modest methodological innovation, the novelty of the problem formulation and its clear significance outweigh the limitations)
