## Peer Review for "Expressing an Image Stream with a Sequence of Natural Sentences"

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper tackles the challenging and novel problem of generating coherent sentence sequences from image streams. The authors propose a Coherent Recurrent Convolutional Network (CRCN) integrating CNNs, RNNs, and a local coherence model. While the problem is interesting and the approach reasonably well-motivated, the paper suffers from a lack of depth in technical detail and analysis, and the empirical evaluation, while thorough, feels somewhat superficial. I believe the paper presents a good starting point for research in this area, but requires significant revisions to reach NIPS standards. My recommendation is **weak reject** with encouragement to resubmit after addressing the concerns outlined below.

**1. Summary and Relation to Prior Work:**

The paper extends image captioning from single images to image streams and single sentences to sentence sequences. This is a natural and potentially impactful direction. The authors correctly identify the challenges of maintaining coherence across generated sentences and propose a CRCN architecture to address this. The related work section is reasonably comprehensive, covering single image captioning, video description, and coherence modeling. However, it lacks critical discussion of recent advances in neural language generation, specifically transformer-based models (e.g., BERT, GPT-2), which are now dominant in the field and could offer strong baselines. The discussion of prior work is descriptive rather than analytical, failing to clearly delineate the specific contributions of this work relative to existing approaches.

**2. Strengths:**

* **Novel Problem Formulation:**  Generating coherent descriptions of image streams is a genuinely new and challenging problem. The motivation based on real-world scenarios (e.g., vacation photos) is relatable and provides clear context.
* **Integration of Coherence Model:** Explicitly incorporating a local coherence model is a valuable contribution.  Addressing sentence-to-sentence transitions is crucial for generating natural-sounding descriptions of longer visual narratives.
* **Large-Scale Dataset:** Utilizing blog posts as a training source is a clever and practical solution to the scarcity of paired image-sentence sequence data. The scale of the dataset (140K images, 20K blogs) is commendable.
* **Thorough Evaluation:** The authors perform both quantitative (BLEU, Recall) and qualitative (AMT user studies) evaluations, demonstrating a commitment to rigorous assessment.

**3. Weaknesses:**

* **Technical Detail & Clarity:** The paper lacks sufficient technical detail. The descriptions of the BRNN and local coherence model are relatively high-level. Crucially, the equations are presented without adequate explanation. For example, Equation (1) is not explained with regard to the dimensionality of the variables. Equation (2) is presented without justification. A more detailed algorithmic description and justification for design choices are necessary.
* **Limited Baselines:** While the authors compare to several baselines, they largely rely on older methods.  The absence of transformer-based language models as baselines is a major oversight.  These models have demonstrated superior performance on language generation tasks and would provide a stronger benchmark.
* **Evaluation Concerns:** The quantitative results, while showing improvements over baselines, are not overwhelmingly convincing. The gains are relatively modest, particularly in the BLEU scores.  The evaluation setup, specifically the use of only 5 images per stream for testing, may not fully capture the benefits of the coherence model. The AMT results are presented, but the experimental details are lacking (e.g., the specific questions asked, number of participants, statistical significance).
* **Data Augmentation:** The use of κ=3 for data augmentation feels arbitrary. A more thorough ablation study exploring the impact of different κ values would strengthen the results.
* **Lack of Ablation Studies:**  Beyond data augmentation, the paper lacks systematic ablation studies to assess the individual contributions of the different components of the CRCN.  How much does the coherence model *actually* contribute to performance?  What is the impact of the BRNN compared to a simpler RNN?
* **Retrieval Strategy:** The approximate divide-and-conquer retrieval strategy is mentioned but not fully explained. Details on how the beam search is implemented and the trade-offs involved are missing.
* **Overclaiming Originality:** While the problem is novel, the claim of being the *first* to address it feels overstated. Related work exists on video captioning, which also deals with sequential data. The core novelty lies in adapting techniques from those areas to the image stream domain and explicitly modeling coherence.

**4. Arguments For and Against Acceptance:**

**For Acceptance:**

* Addresses a novel and important problem.
* Incorporates a valuable coherence model.
* Demonstrates improvement over existing baselines.
* Utilizes a large and readily available dataset.

**Against Acceptance:**

* Insufficient technical detail and clarity.
* Limited and outdated baseline comparisons.
* Modest quantitative gains.
* Lack of ablation studies and analysis.
* Overclaiming originality.




**5. Specific Suggestions:**

* **Expand Related Work:** Include a discussion of recent transformer-based language models.
* **Provide Detailed Algorithm Description:**  Clearly explain the BRNN and coherence model components, including equations and parameter settings.
* **Include Stronger Baselines:**  Compare against transformer-based models (e.g., fine-tuned GPT-2 or BERT).
* **Conduct Ablation Studies:**  Systematically evaluate the contribution of each component of the CRCN.
* **Provide More Evaluation Details:**  Include detailed information about the AMT setup and statistical significance.
* **Justify Design Choices:**  Provide rationale for key hyperparameters and architectural decisions.
* **Clarify Retrieval Strategy:** Provide a detailed explanation of the divide-and-conquer retrieval strategy.




In conclusion, this paper presents a promising initial exploration of a challenging problem. However, significant revisions are needed to address the technical shortcomings and strengthen the empirical evaluation before it would be suitable for acceptance at NIPS.  I encourage the authors to address these concerns and resubmit the paper in the future.
