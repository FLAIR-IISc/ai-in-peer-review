Summary
The paper tackles the problem of generating a sequence of natural sentences for a stream of images, moving beyond single-image/single-sentence captioning. The proposed coherent recurrent convolutional network (CRCN) combines: (i) CNN image features (VGG), (ii) a bidirectional RNN over sentence representations (doc2vec) to capture content flow across sentences, and (iii) an explicit local coherence model based on entity grids (Barzilay & Lapata) to enforce discourse coherence. Training data are obtained from blog posts (NYC and Disneyland): images are aligned to nearby text blocks via heuristics, text segments are summarized via LSA into κ key sentences, and sentences are represented by paragraph vectors. The model learns a ranking-based compatibility between an image stream and a candidate sentence stream using a max-margin loss with negative sampling. At test time, sentences are retrieved by first selecting K-nearest neighbor images per position and then searching over concatenations with a beam-style divide-and-conquer strategy. Evaluation uses BLEU/CIDEr/METEOR, retrieval metrics (R@K, median rank), and AMT user studies. CRCN outperforms baselines (MLBL variants, CNN+RNN/LSTM, global matching) and an ablation (BRNN-only), with human preferences increasing with sequence length.

Quality
- Soundness: The overall retrieval-and-ranking framework is reasonable, and the ablation suggests the coherence component helps for longer sequences. However, several technical choices raise concerns:
  - The scoring function sums dot products over positions without any explicit length normalization. Since negatives are sampled with random lengths, longer sequences may systematically score higher, making the margin constraint easier to satisfy for length-related reasons rather than semantic alignment. At test time candidates share length, but the training objective could still be biased. A simple normalization (average over t or fixed-length negatives) would address this.
  - The coherence contribution g is a single vector derived from the entity-grid features for the entire sentence sequence and is added as g·v_t at every time step. This repeats the same term across positions and effectively encourages the global text coherence vector to align with the sum of image features. It is not obvious why this scalar product should reflect local sentence-to-sentence coherence with respect to the images. A position-sensitive term (e.g., pairwise transitions scored against adjacent image pairs) would better encode coherence.
  - Architecture: Two stacked fully connected layers without a nonlinearity between them reduce to a single linear mapping at test time; dropout does not change that. The claim that Wf2Wf1 does not reduce to a single linear mapping “thanks to dropout” is not correct. If the intended benefit is regularization, that should be clarified.
  - Data construction is noisy (heuristic alignment, LSA summarization, coref/resolution), yet there is little error analysis or robustness discussion. The paper would benefit from reporting the quality of segmentation/summarization and coreference on a small annotated subset.
- Evaluation: The baselines are reasonable for single-image captioning, but fairness and coverage could be improved. It is unclear whether CNN+LSTM and other models were trained on the authors’ blog data with comparable preprocessing/vocabulary. There is no baseline that models sequence-level structure conditioned on images without the explicit entity grid (e.g., an LSTM/GRU over sentences with adjacency terms), nor a simple structured retrieval baseline (e.g., pairwise Markov model on sentence bigrams). Language metrics at paragraph level are known to be weak; the AMT study helps, but more analysis (e.g., coherence judgments or entity-grid-based evaluation) would strengthen claims.

Clarity
The paper is generally readable and well-organized, with a clear motivation and architecture sketch. Some claims and details need clarification:
- “End-to-end” and “unsupervised”: the system relies on fixed doc2vec, external parsers/coref, and heuristic alignment/summarization; the entity-grid is non-differentiable. The claim should be toned down or precisely scoped.
- Training details: clarify how sequence length is handled in the loss; whether negatives are length-matched; whether any normalization is used. Provide details on training the baselines on the same data.
- The rationale for sharing parameters for O and q, and for not using a nonlinearity between FC layers, should be better justified.
- Include sensitivity analyses (K, M, κ) and runtime.

Originality
- Problem formulation (image-stream to sentence-sequence) and explicit incorporation of an entity-grid-based coherence signal into a multimodal ranking model are novel within vision–language work circa prior art cited. Related work is reasonably covered, including LRCN-like video models and paragraph-to-image-sequence retrieval. The method is, however, a novel combination of established components (CNNs, BRNNs, doc2vec, entity grids), rather than a fundamentally new learning principle.

Significance
- The task is important and timely, moving toward paragraph-level description. If robust, such systems could be useful for photo album narration. The datasets are large and realistic, though noisy; releasing them would increase impact. Gains in retrieval metrics and AMT preferences (especially on longer sequences) indicate practical value. On the other hand, the approach is retrieval-based and tied to the training corpus domain; generalization to other topics is asserted but not demonstrated, and the coherence-image interaction is weakly grounded.

Pros
- Addresses a meaningful extension (streams-to-paragraphs) with a principled attempt to model coherence.
- Large-scale, realistic data; both automatic and human evaluation with ablations.
- Practical approximate search strategy; clear engineering.

Cons
- Potentially biased training objective due to length; questionable formulation of the coherence-image interaction (global g reused across t).
- Overstated “end-to-end/unsupervised” claim; entity-grid and doc2vec are fixed, non-differentiable components.
- Baselines may be suboptimal/unfair for this domain; missing structured sequence baselines.
- Some architectural justifications are incorrect (stacked linear layers with dropout).
- Limited error/sensitivity analysis; unclear reproducibility (no dataset/code mention).

Questions for authors
- Do you normalize scores by sequence length during training or sample length-matched negatives?
- How are generative baselines trained on your blog corpus (vocabulary, tokenization, training splits)?
- Why not include a nonlinearity between FC layers or a position-aware coherence term?
- Sensitivity to K, M, and κ, and to coref/parser errors?

Recommendation
Borderline reject. The problem and direction are compelling, and the empirical gains with human validation are encouraging. However, the current formulation of the coherence term, potential length bias in training, overstated claims, and incomplete baseline/evaluation analysis reduce confidence in the technical soundness and generality. With clarifications, stronger baselines, and a better-grounded coherence–image interaction (and length-normalized training), I would lean toward acceptance.
