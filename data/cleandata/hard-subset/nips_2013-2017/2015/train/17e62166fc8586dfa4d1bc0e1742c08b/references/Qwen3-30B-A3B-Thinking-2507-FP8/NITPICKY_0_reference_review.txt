

# Review of "Expressing an Image Stream with a Sequence of Natural Sentences"

This paper proposes a novel approach for generating sentence sequences from image streams using a "coherent recurrent convolutional networks" (CRCN) architecture. The authors extend the traditional image captioning problem from single-image single-sentence generation to sequence-to-sequence generation, motivated by the common practice of taking multiple images during memorable events. They claim this is the first work addressing the problem of expressing image streams with sentence sequences, and present CRCN as a multimodal architecture integrating CNNs, BRNNs, and a local coherence model.

## Strengths
The problem statement is reasonable and addresses a gap in the literature. The motivation for using blog posts as a data source is sound, and the approach of leveraging existing text-image alignments in blogs is practical. The evaluation includes both quantitative metrics and user studies, which is commendable. The architecture integrates several existing components (CNNs, RNNs, coherence modeling) in a novel way for this specific task.

## Weaknesses
Several substantial issues undermine the paper's technical soundness and presentation:

1. **Technical inconsistencies**: The BRNN description (Section 3.1) claims to use "different sets of parameters" for forward and backward input activations, which is non-standard and not well-justified. Standard BRNNs share input parameters but have separate hidden states. The authors provide no empirical evidence that this modification improves performance over standard BRNNs.

2. **Inaccurate claims**: The paper repeatedly claims the approach is "completely unsupervised" (e.g., Section 1, Section 4.1), but the method relies on blog posts with pre-existing text-image alignments, which constitutes weak supervision. This mischaracterization is significant.

3. **Data reporting inconsistencies**: The paper claims "more than 20K blog posts with 140K associated images" but Section 4.1 reports 11,863 + 7,717 = 19,580 blog posts and 78,467 + 60,545 = 139,012 images. This discrepancy should be resolved.

4. **Methodological flaws**: The coherence model (Section 3.2) is poorly explained. The entity grid description lacks clarity on how discourse entities are identified and coreferent entities are resolved. The Stanford CoreNLP reference is insufficient to understand their implementation.

5. **Inadequate evaluation metrics**: The authors use standard single-sentence metrics (BLEU, CIDEr, METEOR) for evaluating sentence sequences, which are not designed to assess coherence between sentences. The paper acknowledges this limitation but fails to address it with appropriate metrics.

6. **User study limitations**: The main user study uses queries with at most 5 images, contradicting the claim that coherence becomes more critical with longer passages. While they test longer passages in supplementary results, this is not reflected in the main user study.

7. **Formatting errors**: Several typos and inconsistent notation exist (e.g., "vlt" vs "v_{l,t}", "Wf2Wf1" without multiplication symbol in Eq 2, inconsistent variable dimensions in Section 3.3).

8. **Reference issues**: The reference list has formatting errors (e.g., "ACL, ," with comma-space before year) that should be corrected.

## Overall Assessment

While the paper addresses a reasonable extension of image captioning to sequence generation, the technical inconsistencies, inaccurate claims, and methodological flaws are substantial enough to undermine the paper's contribution. The claim of "completely unsupervised" approach is particularly problematic as it misrepresents the nature of the training data. The evaluation methodology also fails to properly address the core challenge of sentence sequence coherence, using metrics not designed for this task.

The paper would benefit from:
1. Correcting the unsupervised claim and clarifying the supervision nature
2. Resolving the dataset size discrepancy
3. Providing a clearer explanation of the coherence model
4. Using appropriate metrics for sentence sequence evaluation
5. Conducting user studies with longer passages in the main evaluation

Given the paper's technical inconsistencies, methodological flaws, and presentation issues, I recommend rejection. The authors have a promising idea but have not sufficiently validated or clearly presented it. The issues identified are not minor and significantly impact the paper's technical soundness.
