

# Peer Review: "Expressing an Image Stream with a Sequence of Natural Sentences"

## Summary and Relation to Previous Work

This paper introduces a novel task: generating a sequence of natural language sentences to describe a stream of images (rather than a single image), motivated by the common practice of taking multiple photos during memorable experiences like visiting cities. The authors propose CRCN (Coherent Recurrent Convolutional Networks), a multimodal architecture integrating CNNs for image representation, bidirectional RNNs for sentence modeling, and a local coherence model to ensure smooth transitions between sentences. The key innovation is the explicit integration of a coherence model to handle the discontinuities that may arise when consecutive images in a stream show "sharp changes of visual content."

This work extends the line of research on image captioning (e.g., Karpathy et al., CVPR 2015; Vinyals et al., CVPR 2015) by addressing the sequence-to-sequence generation problem rather than single-image-to-single-sentence. While previous work has explored video captioning (e.g., Rohrbach et al., ICCV 2015), the authors correctly note that their problem is distinct from video captioning due to the potential discontinuities in image streams versus the temporal continuity in videos.

## Strengths

The paper makes several notable contributions. First, it clearly identifies an underexplored problem in multimodal learning: generating coherent sentence sequences for image streams. Second, the dataset construction from blog posts is innovative and practical, leveraging existing online content rather than creating new data. The preprocessing pipeline (text segmentation, summarization, and augmentation) is well-justified and carefully implemented.

The CRCN architecture is well-motivated, with the coherence model addressing a genuine gap in the literature. The authors demonstrate through quantitative results (BLEU, CIDEr, METEOR, top-K recall) and user studies that their approach significantly outperforms baselines that ignore sentence coherence. The ablation study showing the contribution of the coherence model (CRCN vs. RCN) is particularly valuable.

## Weaknesses

Despite these strengths, several critical issues undermine the paper's contribution and evaluation:

1. **Lack of Clear Motivation for the "Sharp Changes" Problem**: The paper claims that image streams often contain "sharp changes" that require a coherence model, but provides no evidence or examples of this phenomenon in their dataset. Without showing that the discontinuities are significant in their data, the necessity of the coherence model is questionable. In fact, their blog dataset likely contains carefully curated sequences where visual continuity is already high.

2. **Insufficient Comparison to Relevant Work**: The paper claims to be the first to address this problem, but overlooks recent work on sequential image captioning (e.g., "Video Captioning with Deep Attention" by Xu et al., 2016) and "Image Sequence Description" by Kim et al. (CVPR 2015), which they cite but do not properly compare against. The paper should have included these as baselines or at least acknowledged their relevance.

3. **Evaluation Metrics Misalignment**: The paper uses standard captioning metrics (BLEU, CIDEr) that focus on n-gram matching rather than coherence. These metrics are poorly suited for evaluating the coherence of multi-sentence sequences, which the paper claims is its key contribution. The user studies are better aligned with the task but are limited to short sequences (5 images), where coherence is less critical.

4. **Data Construction Biases**: The blog dataset may contain significant biases. Bloggers often write for specific audiences, with stylistic choices that may not generalize to other domains. The paper doesn't address how these biases might affect model performance or generalizability.

5. **Methodological Ambiguity**: The paper's description of the coherence model is vague. The "entity grid" approach (Section 3.2) is not well-explained, and it's unclear how the grammatical role transitions are learned or why they are effective for coherence.

## Overall Assessment

The paper addresses an interesting problem and makes a reasonable contribution to the literature on multimodal sequence generation. However, the novelty is somewhat overstated, and the evaluation does not adequately demonstrate the claimed advantage of the coherence model. The dataset construction is sound, but the paper fails to convincingly justify why sentence coherence is a significant challenge for image streams (as opposed to videos).

The paper is technically sound but lacks the depth of analysis expected at NIPS. The results are promising but would be stronger with more rigorous evaluation of coherence (beyond standard captioning metrics) and a more thorough comparison to related work on sequential image description.

## Recommendation

I recommend **weak rejection**. The paper presents an interesting extension to image captioning, but the novelty is limited, and the evaluation does not sufficiently validate the key contribution (the coherence model). The paper would be substantially stronger with:
1. A more compelling argument for why coherence is needed in image streams (with examples from the dataset)
2. Comparison to relevant sequential captioning work
3. Better evaluation metrics for coherence (e.g., human evaluation focusing specifically on coherence)
4. More thorough ablation studies showing the impact of the coherence model on longer sequences

While the dataset and architecture have merit, the paper does not sufficiently advance the state of the art or provide a clear path for future work. The field would benefit more from a paper that thoroughly addresses the coherence challenge rather than a slight extension of existing image captioning methods.
