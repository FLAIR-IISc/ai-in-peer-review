

**Review of "Expressing an Image Stream with a Sequence of Natural Sentences"**

**Summary**  
This paper introduces a novel problem: generating coherent *sequences of natural sentences* from *image streams* (e.g., a sequence of travel photos), extending prior work on single-image single-sentence captioning (e.g., [9, 12, 14, 26, 30]). The authors propose *Coherent Recurrent Convolutional Networks (CRCN)*, integrating convolutional networks (VGGNet) for image features, bidirectional RNNs (BRNNs) for sentence modeling, and a local coherence model based on entity transitions (inspired by [1]) to ensure fluent sentence sequences. Training data is derived from blog posts, which inherently pair image sequences with narrative text. The approach is evaluated on NYC and Disneyland blog datasets (20K+ posts, 140K images) using BLEU, CIDEr, METEOR, top-K recall, and user studies (AMT). The authors claim this is the first work addressing *both* input (image streams) and output (sentence sequences) as sequences, distinct from video captioning [23, 32] or image sequence retrieval [11].

**Strengths**  
1. **Problem Novelty & Motivation**: The shift from single-image to image-stream captioning is well-motivated by real-world use cases (e.g., travel blogging), and the paper clearly articulates the gap in existing literature (e.g., video captioning focuses on *continuous* video, not *discontinuous* image streams).  
2. **Technical Soundness**: CRCN integrates established components (CNN, BRNN) with a novel coherence model (entity-based transitions), validated via ablation studies (CRCN vs. RCN). The training objective (max-margin loss) and data collection from blogs are pragmatic and well-justified.  
3. **Comprehensive Evaluation**: Quantitative results (Table 1) show CRCN outperforms baselines (e.g., CNN+RNN, MLBL-F) in retrieval metrics (top-K recall), while AMT user studies (Table 2) confirm human preference for CRCN’s output, especially for longer sequences (8–10 images).  
4. **Scalability & Practicality**: Leveraging unstructured blogs as training data avoids costly manual annotation, and the method is domain-agnostic (tested on tourism, applicable to any blog-rich domain).  

**Weaknesses**  
1. **Limited Scope of Evaluation**:  
   - User studies only test sequences of ≤10 images, despite the claim that coherence *becomes critical for longer passages*. The paper lacks evaluation on very long streams (e.g., 20+ images), where coherence failures might compound.  
   - Metrics like BLEU/CIDEr are designed for *single-sentence* generation and poorly capture paragraph-level fluency (acknowledged by authors, but not mitigated). No qualitative analysis of *why* CRCN fails (e.g., entity tracking errors in complex narratives).  
2. **Data Quality & Alignment Risks**:  
   - Blog text segmentation (via image-to-text block distances) and LSA summarization may misalign images/sentences in non-ideal blog layouts (e.g., images embedded mid-paragraph). The paper lacks analysis of alignment accuracy or error cases.  
   - Blog data is tourism-focused; the claim of "domain-agnostic" applicability is untested (e.g., news, sports blogs with different narrative structures).  
3. **Coherence Model Over-Simplification**:  
   The entity-based coherence model (Section 3.2) focuses *only* on coreference and grammatical roles, ignoring higher-level coherence (e.g., topic shifts, causal links). This may limit performance in narratives with complex discourse (e.g., "After the rain, we saw the Eiffel Tower shining").  
4. **Baseline Comparison**:  
   Baselines (e.g., CNN+LSTM) are adapted by *concatenating* single-sentence outputs, which is suboptimal for sequence generation. A more direct comparison (e.g., fine-tuning baselines for sequence generation) would strengthen claims.  

**Suggestions for Improvement**  
- **Expand Evaluation**: Test CRCN on longer image streams (15–20 images) and include qualitative failure analysis (e.g., "Where does the coherence model fail in this example?").  
- **Strengthen Data Analysis**: Report alignment accuracy (e.g., % of blog segments with correct image-text pairing) and test on a non-tourism dataset (e.g., food blogs) to validate domain generality.  
- **Refine Coherence Model**: Incorporate higher-level discourse features (e.g., topic modeling) alongside entity transitions, and discuss trade-offs in model complexity.  
- **Address Metrics**: Supplement BLEU/CIDEr with paragraph-level metrics (e.g., [33]) or human judgments focused on coherence (beyond pairwise preference).  

**Conclusion**  
This paper makes a compelling contribution to extending image captioning to *sequence-to-sequence* generation, with a well-designed architecture and robust empirical validation. The core novelty—explicit coherence modeling for sentence sequences—is significant and well-supported. However, limitations in evaluation scope (long sequences, non-tourism domains) and the coherence model’s narrow focus prevent it from fully demonstrating its broader impact. With the suggested improvements, this work could become a strong candidate for NIPS. As-is, it is technically solid and novel but lacks sufficient evidence for *general* significance beyond the evaluated domains.  

**Decision**: *Weak Accept* (stronger if weaknesses addressed in revision). The problem is novel and well-motivated, the methodology is sound, and the results are convincing for the stated scope. However, the evaluation’s narrow focus and untested claims about domain generality require clarification.
