Summary and relation to prior work
This paper tackles a novel and under-explored problem: given a stream of images, produce a coherent sequence of natural sentences. The proposed coherent recurrent convolutional networks (CRCN) integrate (i) CNN image features (VGG), (ii) a bidirectional RNN over sentence representations (doc2vec) to model content flow across the sequence, and (iii) an entity-grid based local coherence model (Barzilay & Lapata) to encourage fluent sentence-to-sentence transitions. The system is trained with a max-margin ranking loss over aligned image-stream/text-stream pairs and, at test time, retrieves sentences from a large blog training set via a KNN candidate pool and a divide-and-conquer beam search. The authors construct sizable tourism blog datasets (NYC, Disneyland) via automatic segmentation/summarization of posts into image–key-sentence pairs and evaluate with language metrics, retrieval metrics, and AMT user studies.

Relative to prior work: most image captioning systems operate at the single image–single sentence level (e.g., Karpathy & Fei-Fei; Vinyals et al.; Kiros et al.). Video-to-sentence has modeled temporal continuity but typically outputs a single sentence per clip (e.g., Donahue et al.; Rohrbach et al.). The closest related directions include image–text retrieval with multimodal embeddings and sequence-level retrieval for the inverse problem (retrieving image sequences from paragraph queries). The explicit incorporation of a discourse-coherence model into a multimodal architecture for image-stream-to-paragraph generation appears novel.

Strengths
- Ambitious, timely problem formulation that goes beyond single-image captioning: image streams to multi-sentence descriptions.
- Clear conceptual contribution: combining sentence-sequence modeling (BRNN) with an entity-grid coherence signal within a unified ranking framework; this is a bold and interesting use of classical discourse modeling in a modern multimodal setup.
- Pragmatic, scalable data strategy leveraging blogs; the constructed datasets are large and likely to be useful.
- Empirical results (BLEU/CIDEr/METEOR, retrieval metrics, and AMT) generally favor CRCN over strong single-sentence baselines and ablations, with particularly persuasive AMT preferences for longer sequences.
- The divide-and-conquer beam-search formulation is practical and seems to work well given the combinatorial candidate space.

Weaknesses
- Evaluation clarity: the paper mixes two test-time stories (retrieving from training vs. ranking among test sentences). It is unclear how retrieval metrics are computed when exact ground-truth sequences are unlikely to be present in the candidate set. Please clarify the evaluation protocol for R@K and median rank in the sequence setting.
- Heavy dependence on noisy preprocessing (heuristic image–text alignment; LSA summarization; coreference parsing on blogs). No robustness analysis for these steps; κ = 3 is chosen empirically without ablation.
- Modeling choices feel ad hoc in places: padding a 64-d coherence vector to 300-d; using the same FC mapping for BRNN outputs and the coherence vector; the coherence term g being dot-producted with every image feature in Eq. (3) (potentially double-counting coherence N times). A more principled integration or ablation on these design choices would strengthen the case.
- Sentence “generation” is retrieval-only, constrained to the training corpus. This limits expressivity and raises concerns about stylistic domain bias; there is no analysis of diversity or factual grounding beyond nearest neighbors.
- Comparisons are primarily to single-image captioners adapted by concatenation. Missing stronger sequence baselines such as hierarchical RNN paragraph models or neural coherence modeling (e.g., LSTM with discourse constraints), although we acknowledge some may be contemporaneous.
- Generalization beyond the tourism/blog domain is asserted but not demonstrated.

Quality
- Method is technically plausible and mostly sound for a ranking/retrieval framework. Some details (Eq. (3) coherence aggregation; training of doc2vec on which splits; fine-tuning of CNN) need clarification. Ablations on key hyperparameters (history length in entity grid, κ, K, beam M) and sensitivity to parsing errors are lacking.
- The AMT design is reasonable and the analysis—especially for longer sequences—supports the claim that coherence matters.

Clarity
- Generally well-written and organized with a thorough related work section. However, the evaluation protocol should be clarified, and more detail on the candidate generation and search would aid reproducibility (e.g., precise candidate pool sizes per t after KNN, handling variable N). Please also specify whether doc2vec is trained only on training text or on the full corpus.

Originality
- High. The problem setup and the explicit fusion of a classic entity-based coherence model with a multimodal neural ranker for image-sequence description are novel and interesting.

Significance
- Moderate to high. The work opens a path toward story-like descriptions of photo streams and demonstrates measurable and perceived gains from coherence modeling. While retrieval-only limits generative power, the approach is likely to influence subsequent work on multimodal narratives, dataset construction from web sources, and discourse-aware captioning.

Questions for authors
- How exactly are retrieval metrics computed for sequence retrieval? What are the candidate sets and how is ground truth defined?
- In Eq. (3), why is the coherence vector g dot-producted with v_t for all t (thus summed N times)? Did you consider pooling images (e.g., average v) for the coherence term?
- Was doc2vec trained only on the training split? Any leakage from test text?
- Sensitivity to parsing errors: how does performance change if the coherence component is computed from noisy parses vs. gold parses (if any)?
- Any results outside tourism blogs to gauge domain transfer?

Pros for acceptance
- Novel problem and principled use of discourse coherence in a multimodal setting.
- Solid empirical evidence including human studies; stronger gains on longer sequences where coherence matters most.
- Scalable dataset creation pipeline; potential community impact.

Cons for acceptance
- Evaluation ambiguities and limited ablations on key design choices.
- Retrieval-only generation and heavy reliance on heuristic preprocessing.
- Missing stronger sequence-level baselines.

Recommendation
- Score: 7 (accept)
- Justification: Despite some evaluation and modeling quirks, the paper proposes a fresh and compelling direction—image streams to coherent multi-sentence descriptions—backed by a thoughtful architecture and supportive human evaluations. The novelty and potential to spur follow-up work outweigh the weaknesses.

Confidence
- 3/5 (reasonable familiarity with multimodal captioning and discourse modeling; some uncertainty due to evaluation clarity).
