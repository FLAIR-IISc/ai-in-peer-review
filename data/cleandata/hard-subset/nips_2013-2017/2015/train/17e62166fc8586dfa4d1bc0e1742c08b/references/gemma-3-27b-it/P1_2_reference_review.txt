## Review of "Expressing an Image Stream with a Sequence of Natural Sentences"

**Summary:**

This paper tackles the problem of generating a coherent sequence of sentences describing a stream of images, moving beyond the single-image-single-sentence paradigm prevalent in image captioning. The authors propose a novel architecture, Coherent Recurrent Convolutional Networks (CRCN), integrating CNNs for image representation, BRNNs for sentence modeling, and a local coherence model for smooth transitions between sentences.  A key contribution is the use of blog posts as a naturally occurring image-text parallel corpus. The authors demonstrate strong performance on datasets built from NYC and Disneyland blog posts, using both quantitative metrics (BLEU, Recall) and user studies (AMT).

**Relation to Prior Work:**

The work builds on the extensive literature in image captioning (e.g., [5, 8, 9, 12, 14, 15, 26, 21, 30]). It extends this area by focusing on sequential data, a challenge also addressed in video description [23, 32], but with a crucial distinction.  Unlike video, image streams can exhibit abrupt visual changes, necessitating a stronger emphasis on textual coherence, which the authors explicitly address via their local coherence model [1]. The approach also draws from related work in retrieval-based captioning [6, 8, 21, 26] but combines it with generative aspects and a specific focus on multi-sentence fluency.  The use of blog data is also novel, sidestepping the difficulty of creating large, manually annotated datasets.

**Strengths:**

* **Novel Problem Formulation:** The paper addresses an important and under-explored problem: describing image *streams* rather than individual images. This is a natural extension of existing work and relevant to real-world applications like automatically summarizing travel photos.
* **Coherent Architecture:** The CRCN architecture is well-motivated. Integrating a coherence model specifically designed for sequential text generation is a significant strength, addressing a key challenge in this task.
* **Creative Dataset Construction:** Leveraging blog posts as a source of weakly supervised image-text pairs is clever and allows for training on a large dataset, overcoming the data scarcity problem.
* **Strong Empirical Results:** The quantitative results demonstrate consistently superior performance compared to established baselines.  The user studies further corroborate these findings, showing a clear preference for the generated sequences.
* **Thorough Evaluation:**  The authors employ a comprehensive evaluation strategy, including both automatic metrics and human evaluation, which adds credibility to the results.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the proposed method and experimental setup.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Complexity & Scalability:** The retrieval-based approach, while effective, may face scalability challenges with even larger image databases. The approximate divide-and-conquer strategy helps, but its limitations under extreme scale aren’t fully discussed.
* **Coherence Model Reliance on Parsing:** The local coherence model’s dependency on accurate parse trees could be a bottleneck. Parsing errors may negatively impact the coherence assessment. A more robust alternative, or at least error analysis related to parsing, would be beneficial.
* **Data Bias:**  The reliance on blog data introduces a potential bias towards narrative and storytelling styles. The generated descriptions might not generalize well to image streams with different characteristics (e.g., scientific data visualizations).  This limitation should be acknowledged more explicitly.
* **Limited Baseline Comparison:** While the chosen baselines are relevant, comparing against more recent and sophisticated sequence-to-sequence models (e.g., those utilizing Transformers) would provide a stronger benchmark.
* **Ablation Study Depth:** While ablation studies are conducted, further dissection of the coherence model’s contribution, perhaps by exploring different coherence metrics, would provide more nuanced insights.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* Addresses a novel and important problem.
* Presents a well-designed and effective architecture.
* Demonstrates strong empirical results and user preference.
* Offers a creative solution to the data scarcity issue.
* Well-written and clearly presented.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* Potential scalability concerns with the retrieval-based approach.
* Dependency on accurate parsing for the coherence model.
* Potential bias introduced by the blog dataset.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of image captioning by extending it to the more challenging problem of describing image streams. The proposed CRCN architecture is innovative, and the results are compelling. While there are some limitations, they are acknowledged and do not detract significantly from the overall quality of the work. I believe this paper is a valuable addition to NIPS and recommend acceptance.



**Rating:** Accept (7/10)

**Further Suggestions for the Authors:**

* Discuss the scalability of the retrieval process and potential optimization strategies.
* Investigate the robustness of the coherence model to parsing errors.
* Acknowledge and discuss the potential bias introduced by the blog dataset.
* Include comparisons to more recent sequence-to-sequence models.
* Provide a more detailed ablation study of the coherence model.
