## Review of "Expressing an Image Stream with a Sequence of Natural Sentences"

**Overall Assessment:** This paper presents a novel and interesting problem – generating coherent multi-sentence descriptions for image streams – and proposes a reasonable approach (CRCN) to tackle it. While the idea is strong and the experimental results are promising, the paper suffers from a lack of depth in analysis, particularly regarding the contributions of individual components and the limitations of the approach. I believe the paper is a solid contribution and leans towards acceptance, but requires significant revision to address the concerns detailed below.

**Summary of Main Ideas:**

The authors address the task of automatically generating a sequence of natural language sentences describing a series of images (a stream), motivated by real-world scenarios like travel photo collections.  They propose CRCN, a multimodal architecture combining CNNs for image representation, BRNNs for sentence modeling, and a local coherence model to ensure fluency between sentences.  A key innovation is leveraging online blog posts as a weakly supervised, naturally aligned image-text dataset. The authors demonstrate strong performance on NYC and Disneyland blog data, outperforming several baselines in both quantitative metrics (BLEU, recall) and user studies.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:**  The problem of multi-sentence image stream description is a natural extension of image captioning and largely unexplored.
* **Practical Dataset:** Leveraging blog posts is a clever and practical way to obtain a large-scale dataset for this task, addressing the challenge of creating parallel image-sentence stream data.
* **Coherence Model:**  Explicitly modeling sentence coherence is a crucial addition given the potential for disjointed narratives when dealing with image streams.  The use of parse trees to capture grammatical relationships is a reasonable approach.
* **Strong Empirical Results:** The quantitative and qualitative results (backed by user studies) demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed approach compared to the baselines. The AMT studies are particularly compelling.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and the architecture is clearly illustrated.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Ablation Study:** While the authors compare variants of their model (1NN, RCN, CRCN), the ablation is not thorough enough.  The impact of the coherence model is only partially demonstrated.  For example, what’s the performance if the coherence model is *removed* and replaced with a simpler transition probability model?  How sensitive is the performance to the `κ` parameter in the data augmentation step?
* **Shallow Analysis of Failure Cases:** The paper lacks a detailed discussion of failure cases.  The qualitative examples in Fig. 3 are helpful but don't provide insight into *why* the model fails in certain scenarios. Analyzing these failures would reveal potential areas for improvement.  Are there specific types of image sequences where the model struggles?
* **Baseline Limitations:** The baselines, while reasonable, are not particularly strong or specifically designed for the sequence generation task.  A more challenging baseline that explicitly models sequence coherence would be beneficial. For instance, one could consider adapting a sequence-to-sequence model with attention directly to this problem.
* **Coherence Model Depth:** The description of the coherence model feels somewhat superficial. The choice of using transitions of grammatical roles feels a bit arbitrary. A more thorough justification, perhaps comparing it to alternative coherence metrics, would be helpful.  How robust is it to parsing errors?
* **Data Bias:** Blog posts are inherently biased – authors select images and write narratives to tell a specific story. The authors acknowledge this, but don't fully address its potential impact on the model's generalization ability. Does the model struggle with image streams that don't fit the typical "blog post" narrative structure?
* **Minor Clarity Issues:** While generally clear, some sections (e.g., the combination of CNN, RNN, and coherence model – Section 3.3) could benefit from more detailed explanations and clearer notation.




**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* Addresses a novel and important problem in multimodal learning.
* Demonstrates strong empirical performance on a realistic dataset.
* Presents a well-designed architecture with a key innovation in the coherence model.
*  User studies validate the subjective quality of the generated descriptions.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* Lacks a thorough ablation study to fully understand the contribution of each component.
* Limited analysis of failure cases and potential biases in the dataset.
*  The baselines are not particularly competitive.
* The coherence model's design and justification could be strengthened.




**Specific Suggestions for Improvement:**

* **Conduct a more extensive ablation study.**  Systematically remove or replace components of the CRCN architecture to quantify their impact on performance.
* **Analyze failure cases.**  Identify specific types of image sequences where the model struggles and discuss potential reasons for the failures.
* **Consider stronger baselines.**  Explore sequence-to-sequence models with attention or other methods specifically designed for sequence generation.
* **Elaborate on the coherence model.**  Provide a more detailed justification for the chosen features and explore alternative coherence metrics.
* **Discuss data bias.**  Acknowledge the limitations of using blog posts and discuss potential strategies to mitigate the impact of bias.




**Overall Score:** 7.5 / 10

**Recommendation:**  Accept with Major Revisions.  The paper presents a valuable contribution to the field, but addressing the weaknesses outlined above will significantly strengthen the work and increase its impact. I believe with these revisions, this paper would be a worthy addition to NIPS.
