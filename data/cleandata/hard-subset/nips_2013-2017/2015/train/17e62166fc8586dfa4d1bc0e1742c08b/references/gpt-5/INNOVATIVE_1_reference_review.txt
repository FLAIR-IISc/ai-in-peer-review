Summary and relation to prior work
The paper tackles a new problem: given a stream of images (e.g., a photo album), produce a sequence of natural sentences describing the sequence as a coherent paragraph. The proposed coherent recurrent convolutional network (CRCN) integrates: (i) CNN features (VGG) for images, (ii) a bidirectional RNN that operates at the sentence level using paragraph vectors (doc2vec) to model content flow across the sequence, and (iii) an entity-grid local coherence model (Barzilay & Lapata) to encourage fluent, coherent sentence-to-sentence transitions. Training data are automatically derived from travel blogs: posts are segmented into image-aligned text blocks and summarized into key sentences via LSA; κ-best summaries per segment are used as augmented text. The model is trained with a max-margin ranking loss over aligned and misaligned image–sentence sequences. At test time, for each query image the method retrieves top-K training images to assemble candidate sentence sequences, and uses an approximate beam/divide-and-conquer search to select the best-scoring sequence under CRCN. Experiments on two sizable blog corpora (NYC, Disneyland; >20k posts, 140k images) report improvements over strong single-image captioning/retrieval baselines (Kiros et al., Karpathy et al., Vinyals et al.) on BLEU/CIDEr/METEOR and retrieval metrics, plus AMT user studies that increasingly favor CRCN as sequence length grows.

Strengths
- Novel problem setting: moving from single image–single sentence to image streams–sentence sequences with explicit modeling of cross-sentence coherence. This is a bold and timely step toward paragraph-level multimodal understanding.
- Integrated architecture that couples sentence-level BRNN context with an entity-based coherence signal; the latter is particularly well-motivated for photo streams where consecutive images can shift abruptly, unlike videos.
- Creative use of large, weakly aligned blog posts as training data; the pipeline (segmentation, summarization, augmentation) is practical and domain-agnostic.
- Comprehensive evaluation: two datasets at scale, a range of automatic metrics, and user studies that probe the importance of coherence as sequence length increases. Ablations (1NN, BRNN-only) support the contribution of the coherence component.
- The approximate search strategy is pragmatic and appears to work well in practice.

Weaknesses and questions
- “End-to-end” and “unsupervised” claims are somewhat overstated. CNN and doc2vec are pretrained; alignment is obtained via heuristics and LSA summarization; the pipeline is modular rather than jointly trained from pixels to text.
- Sentence generation is retrieval-based: the system cannot compose novel sentences, which limits generalization and stylistic diversity. The comparison to generative LSTM baselines may thus be somewhat apples-to-oranges on this dataset derived from blog summaries.
- Scoring function (Eq. 3) adds g·v_t for every t. Since g is a global coherence vector for the whole text, summing g·v_t across t implicitly scales the score with sequence length. Without explicit length normalization, this could bias ranking toward longer sequences. Authors should clarify normalization or length control.
- Entity-grid coherence relies on coreference and parsing on noisy blog text; while the ablation suggests benefit, error analysis and robustness discussion are missing. Padding the 64-d transition vector to 300-d by zeros before FC layers is ad hoc; a learned projection could be more principled.
- Negative sampling uses 100 random negatives; no hard-negative mining is reported. Given the retrieval nature of the task, stronger negatives could matter.
- Fairness and breadth of baselines: all models use VGG fc7, but recent work (even at the time) often fine-tunes CNNs; it’s unclear whether CNN is frozen here. Video-to-text and sequence retrieval works are cited, but a baseline that enforces simple Markov coherence over retrieved sentences (or a paragraph RNN without the entity grid) would strengthen attribution of gains to the coherence model.
- Evaluation metrics on automatically summarized blog sentences are noisy; BLEU/METEOR are limited for paragraph fluency and content coverage. The user study is helpful but small; reporting inter-annotator agreement and runtime would be useful.

Quality
Overall technically sound with a clear objective function, architecture, and training protocol. Some design choices are heuristic (zero-padding coherence vector; length sensitivity in scoring). More details on hyperparameters, fine-tuning, runtime, and error modes would improve reproducibility and understanding.

Clarity
Well-written and organized. Figures help. A few implementation details are terse (e.g., whether CNN is fine-tuned; specifics of doc2vec training; candidate search complexity), but the paper is largely clear.

Originality
High. Explicitly modeling sentence-level coherence in multimodal paragraph generation for image streams is, to my knowledge, new. The combination of BRNN and entity-grid coherence within a unified scoring/training framework is a distinctive contribution.

Significance
Potentially high. Photo-album/stream description is an important and underexplored use case; the coherence modeling is likely to influence follow-up work on paragraph-level vision–language tasks. Results and user preferences suggest meaningful gains.

Suggestions for improvement
- Address length normalization in Eq. 3; consider a separate global coherence term not summed over time, or normalize by N.
- Replace zero-padding of the 64-d coherence vector with a learned projection; explore end-to-end learning from coherence features.
- Add hard-negative mining and/or curriculum.
- Provide error analysis and examples where coherence helps vs. hurts; report coref/parse quality on the blog domain.
- Clarify whether CNN is fine-tuned; report runtimes for inference/search.
- Consider a simple learned coherence baseline (e.g., bigram transitions over sentence embeddings) to isolate the benefit of entity grids.

Recommendation
Weak accept. The paper introduces a compelling new problem and a bold, well-motivated approach that integrates coherence modeling into multimodal paragraph generation. Despite heuristic components and some evaluation limitations, the novelty and demonstrated benefits justify acceptance, and the work is likely to spur further research.

Confidence
Medium.
