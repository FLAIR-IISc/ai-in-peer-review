Summary and relation to prior work
The paper tackles the problem of describing an image stream with a sequence of sentences. The proposed coherent recurrent convolutional network (CRCN) combines: (i) CNN image features (VGG fc7), (ii) a bidirectional RNN over sentence embeddings (doc2vec) to model content flow across the sentence sequence, and (iii) an entity-grid coherence model (Barzilay & Lapata) to capture local discourse transitions. Training uses a max-margin ranking loss to align paired image and sentence sequences; at test time, for each image position, K nearest neighbor images are found, their associated sentences form candidates, and a divide-and-conquer beam search ranks concatenations by the learned compatibility score. The dataset is built automatically from blogs (NYC, Disneyland) by segmenting text around images and summarizing segments with LSA; doc2vec provides sentence vectors; Stanford CoreNLP provides parses/coreference. The paper compares against single-image captioning/ranking baselines [Kiros et al., Karpathy & Fei-Fei, Vinyals et al.], a global matching baseline, and ablations (1NN and CRCN without coherence). Metrics include BLEU/METEOR/CIDEr, retrieval recalls, and AMT preferences; CRCN outperforms alternatives, with the coherence term helping most on longer sequences.

Strengths
- Problem is timely and relevant: moving from single-image, single-sentence to sequence-to-sequence with coherence is valuable, and underexplored relative to video captioning.
- Sensible integration of complementary components: CNN for visual content, BRNN for context-sensitive sentence representations, and an entity-grid for local coherence.
- Large, weakly supervised training data from blogs; pragmatic preprocessing pipeline to align images and sentences without manual annotation.
- Solid empirical effort: multiple baselines, two datasets, automatic metrics and human evaluation; ablations suggest the coherence model adds value, particularly as sequence length grows.

Weaknesses
- Evaluation protocol clarity and validity: It is unclear how retrieval metrics (R@K, median rank) are computed when test ground-truth sequences are not in the training pool; please specify the retrieval target and candidate sets precisely.
- Scoring function design: The coherence vector g (sequence-level) is dotted with each image feature and summed across time, which implicitly scales with sequence length and couples a global text-only coherence signal to each image independently. Length normalization or a single g·(aggregate image) term would be more principled; as written, longer sequences may be favored.
- Claim of “end-to-end” and “unsupervised” is overstated. The approach is a retrieval/re-ranking system; it does not generate novel sentences, and relies on heuristically aligned image–sentence pairs (weak supervision) and multiple external NLP tools.
- Coherence modeling is crude: the 64-d entity-grid histogram is zero-padded to 300-d rather than learned via a projection; no analysis of coreference/parsing errors on blogs or sensitivity to history length; no comparison to simpler coherence baselines (e.g., n-gram/skip-gram paragraph LMs, sentence embedding smoothness penalties).
- Missing baselines/ablations: (i) a structured sequence model (e.g., CRF/HMM) over per-position sentence candidates with pairwise smoothness; (ii) hierarchical RNN paragraph generators or sequence-level LSTMs; (iii) a re-ranker that uses only CNN-sentence similarity plus a generic language/coherence score, to isolate the benefit of entity grids; (iv) varying K and beam size M; (v) length normalization in scoring.
- Dataset and reproducibility: unclear whether code and the blog datasets (with processed alignments) will be released; many hyperparameters and preprocessing choices are not fully specified (e.g., doc2vec training details, coref settings, parser versions, thresholds).
- Use of BLEU/METEOR/CIDEr at paragraph level is known to correlate poorly with human judgments; while AMT helps, more detailed human study (e.g., coherence-specific questions) would be stronger.

Quality
- The model architecture is technically sound and training objective is standard. However, the sequence score design conflating a global coherence vector with per-image dot products is questionable; the evaluation protocol needs clearer definition to assess correctness of reported retrieval metrics. Empirical claims are supported by tables and AMT, but additional ablations and stronger baselines are needed to isolate contributions.

Clarity
- Generally readable and organized. Important ambiguities remain: precise definition of candidate pools and targets for R@K/median rank; sequence length effects and length normalization; details of preprocessing quality (segmentation/summarization accuracy); coreference reliability on blogs. Some notation is inconsistent and a few typos exist. Figures reportedly help, but quantitative tables are referenced without full context here.

Originality
- The problem setting (image stream to sentence sequence) and the integration of an entity-grid coherence model into a multimodal matching framework are reasonably novel relative to single-image captioning and video-captioning work. The approach is still largely a novel combination of known components rather than a fundamentally new algorithmic contribution.

Significance
- If validated with clearer evaluation and stronger baselines, this could influence future sequence-level vision–language work and encourage incorporation of discourse-level features. However, the retrieval-only formulation and evaluation ambiguities limit the impact. Dataset release would increase significance.

Detailed comments and suggestions
1) Clarify evaluation: Define exactly how R@K and median rank are computed; what constitutes ground-truth in the candidate set when train/test are disjoint?
2) Address length bias: normalize Skl by N, or apply a single coherence-image interaction (e.g., g·mean(vt)) and/or an explicit length penalty.
3) Learn a projection for coherence features instead of zero-padding; report ablations with different history sizes and with/without coreference.
4) Add baselines: (a) CRF over per-position candidates with pairwise sentence-similarity; (b) generic paragraph LM/skip-thought coherence scorer; (c) hierarchical LSTM paragraph generator; (d) simple average of per-image CNN–sentence similarities plus a separate coherence score.
5) Provide sensitivity analyses: K, beam size M, κ for data augmentation, doc2vec vs alternatives, and runtime.
6) Report the quality of segmentation/summarization (manual spot-check accuracy), and error analysis where coherence hurts/helps.
7) Release code and processed datasets.

Pros
- Addresses an important, underexplored problem.
- Integrates discourse coherence into multimodal sequence matching.
- Large-scale weakly supervised data; both automatic and human evaluation.
- Coherence appears to help on longer sequences.

Cons
- Ambiguous evaluation of retrieval metrics; potential length bias in scoring.
- Retrieval-only system; novelty mainly from component combination.
- Coherence modeling and ablations are limited; stronger baselines missing.
- Reproducibility concerns; limited analysis of preprocessing reliability.

Recommendation: Weak reject until evaluation is clarified and additional baselines/ablations address the scoring and coherence contributions. Confidence: medium.
