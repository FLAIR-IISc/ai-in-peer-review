## Review of "Expressing an Image Stream with a Sequence of Natural Sentences"

**Overall Assessment:** This paper tackles a challenging and increasingly relevant problem: generating coherent, multi-sentence descriptions of image streams. The authors propose a novel architecture (CRCN) that combines CNNs, RNNs, and a local coherence model. While the idea is strong and the experimental setup is reasonably thorough, the paper suffers from some clarity issues and a lack of truly compelling results. I believe the paper is a solid contribution, but currently leans towards a **weak accept**. Significant revisions addressing the clarity and demonstrating stronger advantages over baselines are needed for a stronger recommendation.

**1. Summary & Related Work:**

The paper addresses the problem of generating a sequence of sentences to describe a sequence of images – moving beyond the single-image-single-sentence paradigm prevalent in existing image captioning research. The motivation – representing user-captured photo streams – is well articulated. The CRCN architecture, integrating CNNs for visual feature extraction, RNNs for sentence modeling, and a coherence model for inter-sentence flow, is a logical extension of previous work.

The related work section is reasonably comprehensive, covering retrieval-based methods, video-sentence generation, and multimodal RNN architectures. The authors effectively position their work within the landscape, highlighting how their approach *explicitly* addresses coherence, a critical aspect often overlooked in previous work. However, it could benefit from a more detailed discussion of discourse modeling techniques from NLP, beyond the cited [1].

**2. Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The problem setting – generating coherent descriptions of image *streams* – is relatively unexplored and represents a natural progression from single-image captioning.
* **Architecture:** The CRCN architecture is a reasonable and well-motivated approach to tackle the problem. The integration of a coherence model is a particularly strong point.
* **Dataset:** Leveraging online blog posts as a weakly supervised source of image-text pairs is clever and addresses the data scarcity issue inherent in this task. The scale of the dataset (140K images, 20K blogs) is commendable.
* **Evaluation:** The paper includes both quantitative metrics (BLEU, Recall) and user studies, providing a more comprehensive evaluation than relying on metrics alone.
* **Thoroughness:** The authors compare to a variety of baselines and conduct ablation studies to demonstrate the contribution of individual components.

**3. Weaknesses:**

* **Clarity:** The paper suffers from a significant lack of clarity in certain sections.  Specifically, the description of the coherence model (Section 3.2) is difficult to follow.  The explanation of how parse trees are used to construct the entity grid and the meaning of the resulting 64-dimensional representation is unclear.  More visual aids and detailed explanations would be beneficial.
* **Limited Improvement over Baselines:** While the CRCN outperforms the baselines, the improvements, particularly in quantitative metrics, are often modest. The difference between CRCN and RCN (without coherence) isn't always substantial, raising questions about the effectiveness of the coherence model in practice.
* **Data Quality:**  Using blog posts as a data source introduces noise and potential biases. The authors acknowledge this, but a more thorough analysis of the dataset quality (e.g., the variability in caption quality, the degree of alignment between images and text) would strengthen the work.
* **Simplistic Baselines:** Some of the baselines (e.g., GloMatch) are quite weak. Comparing to stronger, more recent image captioning models (e.g., those employing attention mechanisms) would provide a more challenging and informative benchmark.
* **Evaluation Metric limitations:** The authors acknowledge BLEU's limitations but still heavily rely on it. Exploring more sophisticated evaluation metrics designed for evaluating longer text passages would be valuable.

**4. Detailed Comments & Questions:**

* **Section 3.2:** The description of the coherence model is very dense and hard to understand.  A diagram illustrating the entity grid construction and the transition representation would be extremely helpful.  What is the significance of using a history parameter of 3?  How sensitive is the performance to this parameter?
* **Section 4.1:** Why was κ=3 chosen for data augmentation? Was any analysis done to determine the optimal value?
* **Table 1:** The reported standard deviations are missing.
* **User Studies:** The AMT setup seems reasonable, but more details about the instructions given to the annotators would be helpful. Did the turkers have access to the entire sequence at once, or were they presented with the image stream and two sentence sequences simultaneously?
* **General:** The paper would benefit from a more intuitive explanation of *why* the coherence model is expected to improve performance. What specific types of coherence issues does it address?




**5. Pro/Con Acceptance:**

**Pro:**

* Addresses a novel and important problem.
* Proposes a technically sound architecture.
* Includes a comprehensive experimental evaluation.
* Demonstrates some improvement over existing baselines.

**Con:**

* Lack of clarity in key sections.
* Modest improvements over baselines.
* Reliance on potentially noisy data.
* Limited discussion of the coherence model's effectiveness.



**Overall Recommendation:** **Weak Accept**.

The paper presents a valuable contribution to the field. However, significant revisions are needed to address the clarity issues and demonstrate stronger advantages over baselines. The authors should focus on providing more detailed explanations of the coherence model, conducting a more thorough analysis of the data quality, and comparing to stronger baselines.  Addressing these concerns would significantly strengthen the paper and increase its impact.
