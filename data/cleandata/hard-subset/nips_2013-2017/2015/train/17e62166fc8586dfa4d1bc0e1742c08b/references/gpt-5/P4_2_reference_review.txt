Summary and relation to prior work
The paper addresses the problem of describing an image stream with a sequence of natural sentences. It proposes a retrieval-based multimodal architecture, coherent recurrent convolutional networks (CRCN), that (i) represents images with VGGNet fc7 features, (ii) models sentence-sequence content with a bidirectional RNN over paragraph vectors (doc2vec), and (iii) explicitly models textual coherence using the entity-grid approach of Barzilay & Lapata (ACL’08). A learned scoring function combines per-sentence content compatibility with images and a global coherence term, and a max-margin loss trains the model to score aligned image–text streams higher than mismatched pairs. At test time, candidate sentences are retrieved from training sentences associated with K nearest neighbor images per position and ranked with a beam/divide-and-conquer search. The approach is trained on weakly aligned text–image streams extracted from blogs (NYC, Disneyland), and evaluated with BLEU/CIDEr/METEOR, retrieval metrics (R@K, median rank), and AMT user studies. Baselines include strong single-image captioning models (Kiros et al. ICML’14; Karpathy & Fei-Fei CVPR’15; Vinyals et al. CVPR’15) concatenated across images, a simple nearest neighbor, and an ablated model without coherence. CRCN reports improvements, especially in retrieval metrics and human preference, with larger gains on longer streams.

This work extends image captioning (e.g., AlexNet/VGG + RNN/LSTM models at NIPS/CVPR/ICML) from single images/sentences to streams/paragraphs. It relates to video description (e.g., Donahue et al. CVPR’15), but differs by explicitly modeling sentence-to-sentence coherence, and to image-sequence retrieval from paragraphs (Kim et al. CVPR’14/15) but tackles the reverse direction. Incorporating entity-grid coherence into a multimodal scoring framework appears novel in this context.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The training objective is a standard bidirectional max-margin ranking over sequence pairs with negative sampling and BPTT, which is coherent. The BRNN over doc2vec representations to produce sentence embeddings s_t and a separate coherence embedding g from entity-grid features, projected to the image space for dot-product scoring, is reasonable.
- Some design choices warrant clarification/justification:
  - Equation (3) uses a global coherence vector g_k whose dot product with each image v_t is summed over t. This term does not depend on sentence position and only encodes text-side coherence; a rationale or empirical analysis of its contribution versus per-position coherence would strengthen the argument.
  - The model is described as “end-to-end,” but the pipeline uses non-differentiable preprocessing (text segmentation/summarization, parser, entity-grid) and a fixed doc2vec encoder; it would be clearer to state which components are learned jointly and which are fixed.
  - Given stated concerns about vanishing gradients, using a simple ReLU BRNN (rather than LSTM/GRU) for sentence sequences needs empirical justification; an ablation comparing BRNN vs LSTM within CRCN would help.
  - The blog alignment heuristics (image-to-text assignment by index distance; LSA summarization) introduce noise; there is no analysis of alignment quality or sensitivity to κ (augmentation), K (neighbors), M (beam width), or the history length in the entity grid.
  - Computational aspects: scoring requires parsing and building an entity grid for each candidate sequence; runtime and scalability of the candidate search are not reported.

- Experiments: The datasets are sizeable and the evaluations include both automatic and human metrics. Improvements over baselines are consistent, with larger gains in retrieval and for longer passages, which supports the value of coherence modeling. However, fairness of baselines is a concern: most baselines generate novel sentences per image without sequence-level modeling, whereas CRCN retrieves from a large sentence bank; including retrieval-style, sequence-aware baselines (e.g., sequence-level embedding/ranking, hierarchical RNNs) would provide a stronger comparison. Statistical significance of AMT results is not reported.

Clarity
The paper is generally well organized and readable, with clear problem setup, system diagram, and training objective. Some notation and dimensionalities (Section 3.3) are dense but mostly consistent. Clarifications suggested:
- Explicitly state whether doc2vec parameters are fine-tuned.
- Provide intuition for the coherence term’s interaction with image features and why a global vector g is appropriate.
- Detail candidate search complexity and parsing overhead at test time.
- Avoid “end-to-end” wording given fixed preprocessing.

Originality
- Problem formulation (image streams → sentence sequences with explicit coherence) is a meaningful extension beyond single-image captioning and distinct from prior video description.
- Integration of entity-grid coherence with multimodal neural scoring appears novel.
- Many components (CNN features, doc2vec, BRNN, max-margin loss) are standard; the contribution lies in their combination for a new task.

Significance
- If datasets and code are released, this could catalyze work on album/stream description and multimodal coherence modeling. Reported gains (especially in retrieval metrics and human preference) suggest practical utility.
- Current evaluation is limited to two travelogue domains; generalization to other domains remains to be shown.
- The approach is retrieval-based, which may limit generative flexibility, but is a reasonable first step for this task.

Pros for acceptance
- Clearly defined new task with practical motivation.
- Principled incorporation of textual coherence into multimodal sequence scoring.
- Solid training objective and ablations showing benefits of coherence.
- Large-scale evaluation with both automatic metrics and AMT; stronger improvements on longer sequences.

Cons for acceptance
- Baseline comparison omits sequence-aware paragraph-generation/retrieval baselines; potential advantage from retrieval vs generation.
- Limited analysis of preprocessing noise, parameter sensitivity, and runtime.
- Claims of “end-to-end” and “unsupervised” are somewhat overstated given fixed NLP components and supervised pairing.
- Coherence term design (global g summed over t) is under-justified; no analysis of alternative coherence formulations.

Suggestions for improvement
- Add baselines that model paragraph structure (e.g., hierarchical RNN/LSTM trained at sequence level; sequence-level joint embedding retrieval).
- Provide sensitivity analyses for K, M, κ, and entity-grid history; report runtime and parsing overhead.
- Evaluate statistical significance for AMT; report inter-annotator agreement.
- Analyze error cases and the contribution of the coherence term (e.g., performance vs sequence length, ablation of entity grid vs simpler n-gram coherence).
- Clarify which components are learned jointly; consider fine-tuning sentence encoders or replacing doc2vec with a trainable encoder.

Overall evaluation
The paper presents a coherent and technically reasonable approach to a new and relevant problem, with evidence that explicit coherence modeling improves sequence description quality. The main limitations are the retrieval-centric setup, baseline coverage, and limited analysis. With clarifications and stronger comparisons, this work would be a solid contribution.
