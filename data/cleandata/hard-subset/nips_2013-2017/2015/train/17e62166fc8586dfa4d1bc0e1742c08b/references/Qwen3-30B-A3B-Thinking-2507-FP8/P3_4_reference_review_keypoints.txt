- Proposes a novel task: generate a sequence of natural sentences for an image stream rather than single-image captions.  
- Introduces CRCN: integrates CNN image features, bidirectional RNN sentence modeling, and an entity-based local coherence model.  
- Emphasizes modeling sentence-to-sentence coherence as the key innovation for long image sequences.  
- Argues image streams differ from video due to abrupt visual changes, motivating a different approach than video captioning.  
- Uses blog posts to construct a creative training dataset with careful preprocessing (segmentation, summarization, augmentation).  
- Evaluates thoroughly with BLEU/CIDEr/METEOR/top-K recall and AMT user studies.  
- Ablation studies (CRCN vs RCN vs 1NN) show BRNN and coherence components improve performance, especially for longer sequences.  
- Practical divide-and-conquer strategy effectively handles exponential candidate sentence search.  
- Fails to clearly distinguish image streams from video sequences and doesn’t fully address fundamental differences from video captioning.  
- Misleading claim of being “completely unsupervised” since dataset construction uses supervised text-image pairings.  
- Coherence model lacks strong theoretical justification for why entity transitions suit image-stream sentence coherence.  
- User studies are limited to short sequences (5–10 images), undercutting evidence that coherence helps more on longer streams.  
- Lacks meaningful empirical comparison to video captioning methods, weakening the novelty claim.  
- Overall assessment: technically sound, well-written, convincing results; recommendation: Accept.  
- Camera-ready suggestions: clarify image-stream vs video distinction, correct unsupervised claim, strengthen coherence-model justification, and run user studies with longer sequences.
