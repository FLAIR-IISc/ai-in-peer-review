This paper attempts to extend image captioning to the realm of image *streams*, generating sequences of sentences corresponding to a series of images, motivated by scenarios like travel photo collections. The authors propose a Coherent Recurrent Convolutional Network (CRCN) combining CNNs for image representation, RNNs for sentence modeling, and a local coherence model to improve fluency. The approach is evaluated on blog data from NYC and Disneyland, using BLEU, recall, and AMT studies.

While the problem setting is a natural extension of single-image captioning, and the motivation is clear, this work presents several concerning weaknesses. The core issue is a lack of rigorous justification for the proposed architecture and a reliance on relatively weak baselines. The claim of novelty – extending to image streams and sentence sequences – feels incremental rather than groundbreaking; it’s a fairly obvious next step and the contribution doesn’t seem commensurate with a NIPS publication. 

The proposed CRCN architecture feels heavily engineered without sufficient theoretical grounding. The concatenation of various components (CNN, BRNN, coherence model) feels ad-hoc, and the justification for specific design choices (e.g., separate input activations for forward/backward BRNN units, the choice of κ=3 for data augmentation) is lacking. The reliance on a max-margin loss function with a fixed margin feels arbitrarily chosen; a more thorough exploration of loss functions would be beneficial.

The experimental evaluation is also problematic. The selected baselines are somewhat dated and/or not state-of-the-art for single-image captioning, let alone adapted for sequential data. Comparing against more recent and robust image captioning models (e.g., those employing attention mechanisms or transformers) is crucial to demonstrate genuine improvement. The quantitative results, while showing improvement over the baselines, are not overwhelmingly convincing, and the gains are often modest. The AMT studies are a positive addition, but the experimental setup is not fully detailed and the impact of image stream length on results requires further investigation. The authors acknowledge the limited impact of coherence improvements at shorter stream lengths but this points to a fundamental limitation of the approach itself.

The data collection process, leveraging blog posts, is also a potential source of noise and bias. The quality of blog posts varies greatly, and the correspondence between images and text might not always be perfect. While the authors attempt to address this with text summarization, the effectiveness of this process is not fully demonstrated.

Furthermore, the paper lacks a detailed ablation study. Disentangling the contributions of the BRNN and the coherence model is important, and while the authors present some results for each component individually, a more systematic analysis would be valuable. A more comprehensive analysis of the coherence model’s performance, examining examples where it significantly improves fluency, is also needed.

**Strengths:**

*   The problem of image stream description is a natural and potentially valuable extension of existing work.
*   The use of blog data provides a large-scale dataset for training.
*   The inclusion of a coherence model is a reasonable attempt to address the challenges of generating fluent sequential text.
*   The AMT studies provide some evidence of user preference for the proposed approach.

**Weaknesses:**

*   The architecture lacks theoretical justification and feels heavily engineered.
*   The baselines are weak and do not adequately represent the state-of-the-art.
*   The experimental evaluation is not sufficiently rigorous.
*   The data collection process is potentially noisy and biased.
*   Ablation studies are limited.
*   The gains over baselines are modest and not always statistically significant.



**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Addresses a relevant and potentially useful problem.
*   Presents a novel (though incremental) approach.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Lacks strong theoretical grounding.
*   Weak baseline comparison.
*   Insufficiently rigorous experimental evaluation.
*   Limited ablation study.
*   Modest improvements over baselines.



**Overall:**

I am leaning towards **reject**. While the paper identifies a reasonable research direction, the technical contributions are not substantial enough to warrant acceptance at NIPS. The lack of theoretical justification, weak baselines, and limited experimental validation raise significant concerns about the robustness and generalizability of the proposed approach. The paper feels more like a preliminary investigation than a complete and polished contribution.  Significant revisions addressing the weaknesses outlined above, including a stronger baseline comparison, a more thorough ablation study, and a more detailed analysis of the data and model, would be required for reconsideration.
