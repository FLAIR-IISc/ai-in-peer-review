Summary and relation to prior work
This paper addresses the problem of describing an image stream with a coherent sequence of natural sentences. The proposed coherent recurrent convolutional network (CRCN) learns to rank paragraph-level sentence sequences against image streams. It combines: (i) CNN features (VGGNet fc7) for images; (ii) a bidirectional RNN over sentence representations (doc2vec paragraph vectors) to model content flow across sentences; and (iii) an entity-based local coherence model (Barzilay & Lapata’s entity grid) to encourage fluent sentence-to-sentence transitions. Training uses a max-margin ranking loss over aligned image–text streams extracted from blogs. At test time, per-image KNN retrieval provides sentence candidates; an approximate divide-and-conquer beam-like procedure assembles and ranks full sequences.

The work is positioned against single-image captioning by retrieval [6,8,21,26] and generation [3,5,9,19,30], and video captioning [5,23,32]. It is also related to Kim et al. [11], which retrieves image sequences for paragraph queries (the reverse direction), and to coherence modeling [1]. The novelty here is extending both input (image streams) and output (multi-sentence sequences) with an explicit coherence component.

Strengths
- Problem formulation: moves beyond single-image/single-sentence to stream-to-paragraph, an important and underexplored setting at the time.
- Architectural idea: integrates a sequence model over sentences (BRNN) with an entity-based coherence signal; the training objective ranks whole sequences rather than independent sentences.
- Data: leverages large, weakly aligned blog corpora (∼20K posts; 140K images) with a reasonable pipeline for segmentation and summarization.
- Evaluation: includes both automatic metrics (BLEU, METEOR, CIDEr; retrieval R@K/MedR) and AMT user studies; ablation (1NN vs RCN vs CRCN) supports the contribution of coherence.
- Practicality: candidate assembly via KNN and approximate beam search is scalable and avoids training sequence generators over very long outputs.

Weaknesses and detailed comments

Quality
- Scoring function design: Skl sums per-position compatibility s_k,t · v_l,t plus a coherence term g_k · v_l,t repeated across t. This mixes a text-only coherence vector with image features via a dot-product in a way that is hard to justify semantically (coherence should be a property of the text sequence, not something that “aligns” with image features at every time step). An alternative would combine g with the textual sequence representations (e.g., as a bias over transitions) or use a sequence-level term independent of images.
- “End-to-end” claim: many components are fixed or precomputed (doc2vec, VGG fc7, entity grids via parsing and coref). The CNN appears frozen; the coherence vector is zero-padded to 300 dims and passed through FC layers. This is better characterized as a learned multimodal ranking model with engineered coherence features than a fully end-to-end system.
- Candidate generation: per-image KNN constrains the output space; coherence and BRNN help score/rank, but the system cannot produce sentences not present in training data and may be brittle when nearest-neighbor captions are off. Some analysis of K, κ, and beam size M would help.
- Coherence modeling details: The entity grid requires coreference chains across sentences. The paper mentions parsing (Stanford CoreNLP) but is unclear about whether and how coreference resolution is applied across the entire post; “concatenate parse trees” is not standard. Please clarify the coref pipeline and report entity grid quality (e.g., number of entities per sequence).
- Evaluation reporting: The main text does not include concrete numbers from Tables 1–2, making it hard to gauge effect sizes and statistical significance. Provide absolute scores, deltas, confidence intervals (for AMT), and significance tests.
- Fairness and breadth of baselines: Concatenating captions from single-image models is a strong but somewhat mismatched baseline for a coherence-aware paragraph task. Consider baselines that explicitly model sequence-to-sequence paragraph structure without entity grids (e.g., a hierarchical RNN or LSTM over sentence vectors), and variants that learn paragraph-level embeddings without KNN candidate restriction. Comparison to video-to-paragraph methods [5] adapted to still-image streams would strengthen claims.
- Objective and optimization: Negative sampling draws random misaligned sequences; harder negatives (e.g., same images shuffled, or same content with permuted order) would directly test the coherence and ordering model. There is no analysis of different negative sets.
- Generalization: Experiments are limited to two tourism domains. Claims of being “applicable to any domain” would benefit from a cross-domain test (e.g., train on NYC, test on Disneyland) or on a different blog topic.

Clarity
- Generally well-written and organized. However:
  - Precisely define the coherence computation, including coref resolution across sentences and the “concatenated parse trees” step.
  - Justify zero-padding the 64-d coherence histogram to 300 dims; a learned projection seems more appropriate.
  - Explain the rationale behind “shared parameters for O and q,” and discuss why g is compared with image features.
  - Provide algorithmic details of the divide-and-conquer beam search (depth Q, complexity, pruning).
  - Include full numeric results and key hyperparameters (doc2vec training, BRNN hidden size, whether CNN is fine-tuned).

Originality
- Framing image-stream-to-paragraph retrieval and integrating an entity-grid coherence model with a BRNN is a novel combination relative to contemporaneous work. That said, each component is known, and the integration is relatively straightforward. The most distinctive element is the explicit coherence feature in a multimodal ranking objective.

Significance
- The problem is important, and a pragmatic retrieval-based solution with coherence can be useful for applications like travelogues. The datasets are sizable and could be a resource for the community (consider releasing them). However, because the system retrieves from in-domain blogs and relies on KNN candidates, broader impact on general image storytelling may be limited unless generalization is demonstrated. The coherence contribution appears meaningful in retrieval metrics and AMT (especially for longer streams), but stronger quantitative evidence and analysis are needed.

Suggestions for improvement
- Redesign the sequence-level term so coherence influences sentence ordering/compatibility directly (e.g., a CRF/LSTM over positions with transition potentials informed by entity transitions), or treat coherence as a separate sequence-level score combined at inference.
- Evaluate with harder negatives (shuffled orders) to directly test ordering and coherence; report results as order-sensitivity.
- Provide ablations on K (per-image neighbors), κ (augmentation), history length in the entity grid, and whether CNN fine-tuning helps.
- Add a hierarchical sequence baseline (LSTM over sentences) and a “no-KNN” generator to contextualize retrieval vs generation.
- Report full metrics, effect sizes, statistical significance, and AMT agreement; release code/data if possible.
- Clarify and justify the coreference pipeline and coherence feature projection.

Arguments for acceptance
- Tackles an important, under-addressed task: coherent paragraph description of image streams.
- Novel and reasonable integration of coherence modeling with multimodal sequence ranking.
- Strong empirical results (as stated) with both automatic metrics and user studies; clear ablation showing coherence helps for longer sequences.
- Uses large, weakly supervised real-world data and a scalable inference scheme.

Arguments against acceptance
- Limited technical depth in the integration; several heuristic choices (zero-padding, coherence–image dot-product) are hard to justify.
- Evaluation lacks full numeric details and stronger baselines; fairness and significance are unclear in the current presentation.
- Generalization beyond two tourism domains is untested; the method is constrained by KNN candidate retrieval.
- “End-to-end” and “unsupervised” claims are overstated given reliance on precomputed features and supervised ranking.
