Summary
The paper tackles multi-sentence “story” generation for image streams. The proposed coherent recurrent convolutional network (CRCN) retrieves sentences from a blog-derived training corpus and assembles them into a paragraph for a query image sequence. CRCN combines: (i) VGGNet fc7 image features; (ii) a BRNN over 300-d paragraph vectors (doc2vec) of candidate sentences to model content flow; and (iii) an entity-grid local coherence model (Barzilay & Lapata) to capture discourse transitions. Two FC layers fuse BRNN outputs and the coherence descriptor; training uses a max-margin loss aligning text and image streams; inference retrieves KNN sentences per image and performs a beam/divide-and-conquer search. Evaluation is on two scraped travel-blog datasets (NYC, Disneyland), with automatic metrics (BLEU/CIDEr/METEOR; recall@K; median rank) and AMT user studies. The authors report that CRCN and an ablation without coherence (RCN) outperform several single-image captioning baselines and that coherence helps more on longer sequences.

Relation to prior work
The work is positioned against image captioning by retrieval/alignment (e.g., Karpathy & Fei-Fei; Kiros et al.), multimodal RNN/LSTM captioners (Vinyals et al., Mao et al., Donahue et al.), and entity-based coherence (Barzilay & Lapata). It also cites image-sequence retrieval from paragraph queries (Kim et al.), which is close in spirit but with the reverse mapping. Integrating entity-grid coherence into vision-language generation is a reasonable and under-explored angle.

Strengths
- Addresses a natural and practically relevant problem: multi-sentence descriptions for image streams.
- Clear formulation of a retrieval-based pipeline; avoids long word-level sequences by operating at the sentence level.
- Integration of a classic entity-grid coherence model with a neural BRNN is conceptually appealing; ablations (CRCN vs. RCN) target its contribution.
- Non-trivial dataset curation (20k blogs, 140k images) and an attempt at both automatic and human evaluation.
- Pragmatic approximate search strategy for combinatorial inference.

Major weaknesses
1) Scoring and modeling inconsistencies (Eq. 3). The score sums s_{k,t}·v_{l,t} plus g_k·v_{l,t} for all t. The coherence vector g_k is text-only yet is dotted against each image feature and repeated across time, effectively overcounting a text-only term and tying it directly to image features without justification. If g_k is intended to be a global text representation, a single interaction with a global image representation (e.g., mean-pool of v_{l,t}) or a separate scalar coherence term would be more principled. As written, the model can be incentivized to learn g_k as an “image-prototype” that correlates with many v_{l,t}, conflating coherence with visual similarity.

2) “End-to-end” claim is overstated. The pipeline relies on non-differentiable pre-processing (heuristic image–text alignment by index distance, LSA summarization, doc2vec, coreference parsing). CNN appears fixed; there is no text–image joint attention or conditioning in the BRNN. The learned part mainly maps text-side features to the visual embedding space.

3) Dataset alignment and supervision. The paper repeatedly claims “completely unsupervised,” yet uses aligned image–sentence streams (albeit noisy) as supervision. Image–text association by nearest index in a blog and LSA summarization (with κ-augmentation labeling multiple sentences per image) introduce substantial label noise. There is no quantitative validation of alignment quality or κ’s effect.

4) Evaluation clarity and fairness. The description of retrieval metrics (R@K, median rank) is unclear in the disjoint train/test setting—if test ground truth sentences are not in the candidate set, how is recall@K defined? Tables with absolute numbers and significance are missing in the submission text (only “Table 1/2 show…”). AMT protocol lacks details (quality control, agreement, statistical tests). Some baselines are disadvantaged (e.g., GloMatch uses weaker features while others use VGG; more appropriate baselines would include a reranker that adds entity-grid coherence on top of sentence-by-sentence retrieval, or a simple n-gram/lexical cohesion model).

5) Approximate search underspecified. The divide-and-conquer/beam strategy interacts nontrivially with coherence, which depends on transitions across the entire sequence. It is unclear how coherence features are computed for partial sequences and whether cross-boundary transitions are accounted for during merging. No complexity analysis or ablation on K and M.

6) Architecture/optimization details: 
- Two FC layers without nonlinearity; the claim that “dropout prevents reduction to a single linear mapping” is not correct at test time (the composition is linear in expectation). If nonlinearity is intended, it should be explicit.
- Biases dropped without justification; no gradient clipping; no training schedule; no report of variance across random seeds.
- The BRNN only consumes text; images are not used to condition sentence representations during training, limiting cross-modal learning.

7) Reproducibility and missing details. Many experimental specifics are absent: exact metrics, hyperparameters, length distributions, data splits per domain, doc2vec training setup (“two classes” presumably NYC/Disneyland), and whether CNN is fine-tuned. Figures/tables referenced but not included; references are incomplete (years missing).

8) Coherence modeling robustness. Entity-grid coherence relies on accurate parsing/coreference, which are brittle on noisy blogs; no error analysis or alternative history lengths; padding a 64-d grid to 300-d with zeros is ad hoc (a learned projection would be preferable).

Quality
Conceptually interesting but technically shaky in key places (score function, search, evaluation). Empirical claims are not sufficiently substantiated in the provided text.

Clarity
Readable at a high level, but many crucial details are missing or ambiguous; some notation errors/typos; references incomplete.

Originality
Moderate. Combining entity-grid coherence with a sentence-retrieval story generation framework is a novel combination, but many components are standard. The “first to image-stream → sentence-sequence” claim should be tempered given prior sequence retrieval work and multi-sentence/video captioning.

Significance
Potentially useful direction, but given methodological issues and unclear evaluation, the demonstrated impact is limited in its current form.

Questions for the authors
- Justify Eq. (3): Why is g_k·v_{l,t} summed over t? Did you try a global image representation or a separate coherence-only term?
- How are coherence features computed in the beam/divide-and-conquer search for partial sequences and at merge boundaries?
- Are CNN features fixed or fine-tuned? Is the model trained jointly across domains, and how is doc2vec trained (“two classes”)?
- How exactly are recall@K and median rank defined when test ground truth sentences are not candidates?
- Do you have any quantitative validation of the blog image–text alignment and the effect of κ augmentation?

Pros (for acceptance)
- Addresses a relevant and challenging problem (story-like descriptions for image streams).
- Incorporates discourse coherence into vision-language, an underexplored aspect.
- Large-scale data curation and attempt at human evaluation.
- Ablations suggest coherence helps on longer sequences.

Cons (for rejection)
- Fundamentally questionable scoring formulation; unclear and potentially flawed interaction between coherence and images.
- Overstated “end-to-end”/“unsupervised” claims; heavy reliance on brittle pre-processing.
- Evaluation clarity and fairness issues; missing quantitative details and statistical rigor.
- Search procedure underspecified relative to coherence; missing complexity/ablation.
- Weak/imbalanced baselines; reproducibility concerns; incomplete references/formatting.

Recommendation
Reject.

Confidence
High for the methodological/evaluation concerns; medium overall (some details may be clarified in a fuller version).
