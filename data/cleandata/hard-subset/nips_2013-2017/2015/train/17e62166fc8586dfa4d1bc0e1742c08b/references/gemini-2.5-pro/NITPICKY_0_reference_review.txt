This paper addresses the novel task of generating a coherent sequence of natural language sentences to describe a stream of images. The authors propose a multimodal architecture, the Coherent Recurrent Convolutional Network (CRCN), which aims to model not only the correspondence between individual images and sentences but also the narrative flow and coherence of the resulting paragraph. The method is retrieval-based: for a query image stream, it first finds candidate sentences for each image and then ranks possible sentence sequences. The ranking score is learned by a model combining a CNN for image features (VGGNet), a bidirectional RNN for modeling sentence content flow (using paragraph vectors), and an explicit, entity-based local coherence model. The model is trained on a large dataset of blog posts, which serve as a source of weakly-aligned image and text streams. The authors demonstrate the model's effectiveness through quantitative evaluation using language and retrieval metrics, and through user studies on Amazon Mechanical Turk.

**Strengths:**
1.  **Novelty and Significance:** The paper tackles a logical and important next step beyond single image captioning. The problem of describing an image collection with a coherent story is challenging and has many potential applications. The work is, to my knowledge, one of the first to formally address this specific task.
2.  **Model Motivation:** The core idea of explicitly modeling coherence in addition to semantic content is well-motivated. The authors correctly point out that unlike video frames, images in a stream can have sharp content changes, making an explicit coherence mechanism crucial for generating fluent text.
3.  **Experimental Rigor:** The paper presents a thorough evaluation. The use of two new, large-scale datasets is commendable. The comparison against several relevant baselines, including variants of the proposed model for ablation, is well-structured. Furthermore, the inclusion of a human study via AMT provides valuable insight into perceptual quality, and the experiment showing that coherence is more critical for longer sequences provides strong support for the central claim.

**Weaknesses:**
Despite the promising direction, the paper is marred by a substantial number of technical, methodological, and presentational flaws that undermine its contribution.

1.  **Misleading Claims:**
    *   The authors claim the approach is "completely unsupervised" (Sec. 1). This is incorrect. The model is trained on aligned image-text pairs derived from blogs, which constitutes weak or noisy supervision, not unsupervised learning.
    *   The architecture is described as a "unified end-to-end architecture" (Sec. 1). This is also misleading. The coherence model is based on extracting features (an entity grid) from pre-computed parse trees using a method from 2005 [1]. This feature vector is then concatenated with the RNN output. This is standard feature fusion, not a deeply integrated, end-to-end differentiable component in the same sense as the CNN and RNN.

2.  **Conceptual Flaw in Model Formulation:**
    *   The scoring function in Equation (3), `Skl = ∑t (skt · vlt + gk · vlt)`, appears to be conceptually flawed. Here, `gk` represents the coherence of the entire sentence sequence `k`, while `vlt` is the feature for a single image `t` in stream `l`. This formulation implies that the global coherence score is evaluated against *each individual image feature* and then summed up. There is no clear justification for why the coherence of a paragraph should be repeatedly matched with every single image in the stream. This fundamental part of the model is poorly explained and seems incorrect, casting serious doubt on the technical soundness of the work.

3.  **Dated Methodological Choices:**
    *   Several key components of the pipeline rely on significantly dated techniques without justification or comparison to modern alternatives. These include LSA-based summarization [4] (2004), paragraph vectors [17] (2014) for sentence representation (when transformer-based embeddings are now standard), and the entity-grid coherence model [1] itself (2005). While using established methods is not inherently a flaw, the complete lack of discussion of more powerful, contemporary alternatives is a weakness.

4.  **Lack of Rigor in Details:**
    *   The pre-processing heuristic for aligning text and images based on "minimum index distance" is crude and likely introduces significant label noise. The potential impact of this is not discussed.
    *   Several architectural choices are justified with phrases like "empirically leads a better performance" (e.g., separating BRNN input activations) but are not supported by any ablation studies or results in the experiments section.
    *   The 64-dimensional coherence vector is transformed into a 300-dimensional vector via "zero-padding." This is a simplistic and unusual choice compared to using a standard, learnable linear projection.
    *   The AMT user study results supporting the importance of coherence for longer sequences (58.7% preference) are presented without any report of statistical significance (e.g., p-values or confidence intervals), making it difficult to assess the reliability of the conclusion.

5.  **Numerous Presentation and Formatting Issues:**
    *   The writing contains informal language ("hike of interest") and awkward phrasing ("coherence model is more demanded").
    *   Citations in the introduction are not numerically ordered (e.g., `[...26, 21, 30]`).
    *   The reference list is incomplete and improperly formatted. Reference [28] is cited in the text ("RMSprop optimizer [28]") but is missing from the reference list provided. There are also inconsistencies in the formatting of venues and years.
    *   There are minor but noticeable typos, such as "longshort term memory nets" and "100 ne examples".

**Summary of Recommendation:**

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Addresses a novel, significant, and challenging problem.
*   The high-level idea of explicitly modeling coherence is well-motivated.
*   Presents extensive experiments on new, large datasets, including a human study.

**Arguments for Rejection:**
*   Major conceptual flaw in the core scoring function (Eq. 3).
*   Misleading claims about the method being "unsupervised" and "end-to-end".
*   Reliance on multiple dated techniques without justification.
*   Lack of supporting evidence for several methodological claims.
*   A substantial number of minor errors in writing, citation, and formatting, which collectively suggest a lack of care and polish.

I recommend **rejection**. While the problem statement is interesting and the high-level motivation is sound, the execution is plagued by significant issues. The questionable formulation of the scoring function is a critical technical flaw. This, combined with misleading claims about the nature of the model and a general lack of rigor in the methodological details and presentation, brings the paper below the high standard expected for NIPS. The work has potential, but it requires a major revision to address these fundamental problems.
