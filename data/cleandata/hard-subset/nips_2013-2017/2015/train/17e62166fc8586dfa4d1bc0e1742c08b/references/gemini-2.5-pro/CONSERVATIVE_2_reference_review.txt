This paper addresses the task of generating a sequence of natural language sentences to describe a stream of images. The authors propose a multimodal architecture, the Coherent Recurrent Convolutional Network (CRCN), which combines a CNN for image feature extraction, a bidirectional RNN to model the flow of text, and an entity-based local coherence model to ensure smooth transitions between sentences. The model is trained on a large dataset scraped from blog posts and is evaluated against several state-of-the-art single-image captioning models adapted for this task.

**Strengths:**
1.  The problem formulation is a logical and interesting extension of the well-established single-image captioning task. Moving from single images and sentences to sequences is a natural progression with clear real-world motivations, such as summarizing photo albums from a trip.
2.  The experimental evaluation is extensive. The authors compare their method against a reasonable set of strong baselines adapted from the single-image domain. The inclusion of an ablation study (CRCN vs. RCN vs. 1NN) is valuable for understanding the contribution of the different model components.
3.  The use of both automatic metrics (BLEU, CIDEr, etc.) and a human evaluation via AMT provides a comprehensive picture of the model's performance.
4.  The construction of a large-scale dataset from blog posts is a significant effort and could be a useful resource for future work in this specific domain.

**Weaknesses:**
1.  **Methodological Soundness and Novelty:** The main novelty claim rests on the integration of a CNN, a BRNN, and a local coherence model. However, the components themselves are all well-established, and their combination feels more like an engineering assembly than a fundamental contribution. The "local coherence model" is taken directly from Barzilay and Lapata (2008), a classic but somewhat dated method from a pre-deep learning era of NLP. While integrating coherence is a good idea, the choice of this specific, non-neural model seems arbitrary and is not well-justified against more modern, end-to-end neural approaches to discourse and coherence. The paper would be much stronger if it explored how coherence could be learned within the neural architecture itself, rather than bolting on an external, feature-based model.

2.  **Dataset Construction and Preprocessing:** The entire training process relies on a noisy, heuristic-driven pipeline for creating the image-text parallel corpus from blogs. The alignment of images to text segments is based on proximity, and text summarization is performed using LSA. These are significant confounding factors. The quality of the learned model is fundamentally capped by the quality of this automatically generated "ground truth." The paper does not provide any analysis of the quality or noise level of the preprocessed data, which is a major methodological omission. How often does the text segmentation misalign text? How effective is the LSA summarization at capturing the essence of the text relevant to the image? Without this, it is difficult to trust the validity of the training data and, by extension, the learned model.

3.  **Nature of the Task (Retrieval vs. Generation):** The proposed method is fundamentally a retrieval-based system. It selects and re-ranks sentences that already exist in the training database. This is a significant limitation compared to the baselines ([9], [30]), which are truly generative models capable of producing novel sentences. While retrieval is a valid approach, the paper frames its contribution in the context of "generating" descriptions and does not adequately discuss the implications of this limitation. The model cannot describe novel objects or scenes not present in its training sentence corpus. This makes the comparison to generative baselines somewhat uneven.

4.  **Baseline Adaptation:** While the choice of baselines is sensible, their adaptation is simplistic. Simply concatenating the outputs of single-image captioning models is a straightforward but potentially weak way to create a sequential baseline. These models are not optimized for sequence-level properties. A more compelling baseline would have been a model from the video description literature (e.g., [5]), which is inherently designed to handle sequential visual input, even if the temporal coherence is different from photo streams.

5.  **Approximate Search:** The paper proposes a divide-and-conquer beam search to manage the exponential search space at test time. The justification that "local fluency and coherence is undoubtedly necessary for the global one" is an intuitive but unsubstantiated claim. There is no analysis of how this approximation affects performance or how close its results are to an optimal search.

**Summary of Arguments:**

*   **Pro-Acceptance:**
    *   Addresses a novel and well-motivated problem.
    *   The proposed model, while a combination of existing parts, is thoughtfully constructed for the task.
    *   The experiments are thorough, including strong baselines, ablation studies, and human evaluation.
    *   The creation of a new, large dataset is a contribution.

*   **Con-Acceptance:**
    *   The core technical novelty is limited; it relies heavily on combining off-the-shelf components, including a dated NLP model for coherence.
    *   The soundness of the work is questionable due to the reliance on a complex and unevaluated heuristic pipeline for data preparation.
    *   The approach is retrieval-based, which limits its expressive power and makes comparisons to generative models less direct.
    *   The paper lacks justification for several key design choices (e.g., the specific coherence model) and provides insufficient analysis of its approximations (e.g., the test-time search).

In conclusion, while the paper tackles an important and interesting problem, the proposed solution feels more like a proof-of-concept system built from established parts rather than a novel methodological contribution suitable for NIPS. The heavy reliance on an unevaluated, heuristic-based data pipeline introduces significant concerns about the reproducibility and validity of the results. The core innovation—adding an entity-grid coherence model—is not well integrated into the deep learning framework and feels like an anachronism. The work is a solid piece of engineering, but it lacks the methodological rigor and fundamental novelty I would expect from a top-tier publication.
