

# Peer Review: "Expressing an Image Stream with a Sequence of Natural Sentences"

## Summary and Relation to Previous Work

This paper introduces a novel task of generating coherent sentence sequences to describe image streams (multiple sequential images), extending beyond the conventional image captioning problem that deals with single images and single sentences. The authors propose a multimodal architecture called Coherent Recurrent Convolutional Networks (CRCN) that integrates convolutional neural networks for image representation, bidirectional recurrent neural networks for sentence modeling, and a local coherence model for smooth transitions between sentences. The key innovation lies in explicitly modeling the coherence between consecutive sentences, which addresses a critical gap in existing work that focuses on single-image captioning or video description.

The paper builds upon recent advances in multimodal networks (e.g., [9, 13, 20, 26, 30]) but extends them to handle sequential text-image relationships. Unlike video captioning work (e.g., [23, 32]), which deals with continuous visual content, the authors correctly identify that image streams often contain sharp visual changes, making coherence modeling particularly important. The paper's most significant contribution is the first systematic exploration of this problem space, with a clear motivation from real-world user behavior (capturing photo streams during memorable experiences).

## Strengths

1. **Novel Problem Formulation**: The paper successfully identifies and formalizes a meaningful extension of the image captioning task that has been largely overlooked in the literature. The motivation from user behavior (e.g., capturing NYC experiences through photo streams) is compelling and well-justified.

2. **Comprehensive Evaluation**: The authors provide a thorough evaluation framework, including standard language metrics (BLEU, CIDEr, METEOR), retrieval metrics (top-K recall, median rank), and user studies via AMT. The ablation studies (comparing CRCN, RCN, and 1NN) effectively demonstrate the contribution of each component.

3. **Practical Data Collection Method**: Using blog posts as a source of natural image-text parallel data is innovative and practical. The preprocessing pipeline (text segmentation, summarization, and data augmentation) is well-justified and replicable.

4. **Strong Empirical Results**: CRCN significantly outperforms all baselines, particularly in retrieval metrics (top-K recall), which validates the importance of coherence modeling. The user studies provide compelling evidence that human preferences align with the quantitative metrics.

5. **Clear Contribution Statement**: The paper clearly articulates its three main contributions and effectively positions them against related work.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited Analysis of Coherence Model**: While the coherence model is a key contribution, its implementation details are somewhat brief. The paper doesn't sufficiently explain why the specific 64-dimensional representation (based on grammatical role transitions) was chosen over other coherence modeling approaches. More discussion on how this model specifically addresses the challenges of abrupt visual changes between consecutive images would strengthen the paper.

2. **Scalability Concerns**: The paper doesn't address how the approach scales with longer image streams. The user studies only use short streams (5 images), and the paper acknowledges that coherence becomes more critical with longer passages. The method's computational complexity and performance degradation with increasing stream length should be investigated.

3. **Comparison with Video Captioning**: While the paper contrasts with video captioning work, it doesn't adequately explain how their approach differs from or improves upon methods that generate multiple sentences for videos (e.g., [23, 32]). The distinction between video captioning (where visual content is continuous) and image stream captioning (where content can change abruptly) is important but could be more thoroughly justified.

4. **Dataset Details**: The dataset collection process (e.g., how they handled duplicate content, blog post quality filtering, or image-text alignment) needs more detail. The paper mentions "manually select[ing] the travelogue posts" but doesn't specify the criteria used for selection.

5. **Limitations Discussion**: The paper lacks a thorough discussion of limitations. For instance, it doesn't address how the approach would handle image streams with highly non-linear visual narratives or how it would perform on domains outside tourism.

## Overall Assessment

This paper presents a compelling extension of the image captioning problem to image streams, with a well-designed architecture and thorough evaluation. The problem formulation is novel and addresses a meaningful gap in the literature. The CRCN architecture integrates several existing techniques in a novel way to address the coherence challenge, and the empirical results demonstrate clear improvements over baselines.

The paper makes a solid contribution to the field, with the potential to inspire future work on multimodal sequence generation. While the coherence model's implementation could be better justified, and the scalability analysis could be expanded, these are relatively minor concerns given the paper's overall quality.

## Recommendation

**Accept** with minor revisions

The paper makes a significant contribution to the field by extending the image captioning problem to image streams and introducing a coherence model that significantly improves the quality of generated sentence sequences. The methodology is sound, the evaluation is comprehensive, and the results are compelling. The minor weaknesses noted above are easily addressable in a revision.

The paper is well-suited for NIPS as it addresses a meaningful problem with a technically sound approach, provides clear empirical validation, and demonstrates significant progress over the state of the art. The work aligns well with NIPS' focus on machine learning and multimodal representation learning.

## Specific Recommendations for Revision

1. Expand the discussion of the coherence model, including why the specific grammatical role transition approach was chosen and how it specifically addresses abrupt visual changes.

2. Add a brief analysis of how the method scales with longer image streams (beyond the 5-10 images used in the AMT tests).

3. Provide more detail on the dataset collection process, particularly the criteria for selecting travelogue blog posts.

4. Strengthen the comparison with video captioning work to clearly articulate the distinction between the two problems.

5. Add a paragraph discussing limitations of the approach, particularly regarding highly non-linear image streams or non-tourism domains.

This paper has the potential to be a valuable contribution to the field, and with these minor revisions, it will be an excellent addition to the NIPS proceedings.
