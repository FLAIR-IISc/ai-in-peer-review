### Review of "Expressing an Image Stream with a Sequence of Natural Sentences"

**Summary:**

This paper addresses the novel problem of generating a coherent sequence of natural language sentences to describe a sequence of images. This extends the well-studied task of single image-to-sentence generation to a more complex, multi-modal sequence-to-sequence problem. The authors propose a new architecture, the Coherent Recurrent Convolutional Network (CRCN), which combines three main components: (1) a Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) for extracting visual features from each image, (2) a Bidirectional Recurrent Neural Network (BRNN) to model the semantic flow and context of the sentence sequence, and (3) an entity-based local coherence model to ensure grammatical and referential consistency across sentences, making the output paragraph more fluent.

The overall approach is framed as a retrieval and ranking problem. For a given input image stream, candidate sentences are first retrieved for each image from a training corpus. Then, the CRCN model is used to score entire candidate sentence sequences, ranking them based on a combination of image-sentence compatibility and overall coherence. The authors cleverly leverage online blog posts as a source of weakly-supervised parallel data. The model is evaluated on two new blog-post datasets (NYC and Disneyland) using both automatic metrics (BLEU, R@K, etc.) and human studies on Amazon Mechanical Turk, demonstrating superior performance over baselines adapted from state-of-the-art single-image captioning methods.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Significance and Originality:** The paper tackles a well-motivated and important problem. Moving from single images to image streams and from single sentences to paragraphs is a natural and significant step towards more holistic scene understanding and description. To my knowledge, this is one of the first papers to formally address this specific task, which clearly distinguishes it from prior work on single image or video captioning. The problem formulation itself is a valuable contribution.

2.  **Technical Soundness:** The proposed CRCN model is technically sound and well-designed for the task. The decision to integrate an explicit coherence model (the entity-grid approach from Barzilay & Lapata) alongside a content-flow model (the BRNN) is a strong one. Standard RNNs are good at capturing semantic sequencing but often fail to model the longer-range syntactic and referential structures that make a paragraph coherent. Explicitly modeling this addresses a key challenge of the problem.

3.  **Thorough Experimental Evaluation:** The evaluation is comprehensive and convincing.
    *   The creation and use of large-scale, real-world datasets from blog posts is commendable and provides a strong testbed for this task.
    *   The authors compare against several relevant and state-of-the-art baselines. Although these baselines needed to be adapted for the sequence-to-sequence task (by concatenating their outputs), this is a reasonable approach given the novelty of the problem.
    *   The ablation study, comparing the full CRCN against a version without the coherence model (RCN) and a simple nearest-neighbor baseline (1NN), effectively demonstrates the contribution of each component of their architecture.
    *   The combination of automatic metrics and human evaluation (AMT) provides a balanced view of performance. The AMT study, in particular, provides strong evidence for the value of the coherence model, especially for longer sequences, which is a key insight.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Clarity of the Model's Scoring Function:** The paper could be clearer about certain architectural and mathematical details. Specifically, the formulation of the final compatibility score in Equation (3) is confusing. The equation is `Skl = Σt (skt · vlt + gk · vlt)`. Here, `skt` is the text representation for sentence `t` in sequence `k`, and `vlt` is the image representation for image `t` in stream `l`. The term `gk` seems to represent a global coherence score for the entire sentence sequence `k`. It is unclear why this single global vector `gk` would be dotted with each individual image vector `vlt` within the summation. This would imply that the coherence score is weighted differently by each image in the stream. A more intuitive formulation might have a global coherence term added *outside* the summation, or a coherence term that models transitions between pairs of sentences. This aspect of the model needs better explanation or justification.

2.  **Novelty of Components:** While the problem formulation is novel, the constituent parts of the model (VGGNet, BRNN, entity-grid coherence model, max-margin loss) are all well-established techniques from their respective fields. The main novelty lies in the specific combination and application of these components to the image-stream-to-paragraph problem. This is more of an engineering and systems contribution than a fundamental advance in machine learning methodology. This is not a major flaw, but it is worth noting the scope of the contribution.

3.  **Limitations of Retrieval-Based Approach:** The method is fundamentally a retrieval-based system, meaning it can only construct paragraphs from sentences that already exist in the training corpus. This limits its creativity and its ability to describe truly novel scenes or combinations of events not seen during training. A discussion of the limitations of this retrieval-based framework compared to a fully generative approach would strengthen the paper.

4.  **Baseline Strength:** Simply concatenating the outputs of single-image captioning models is a reasonable first step for creating baselines, but it could be considered a strawman. These models are not designed or trained to produce coherent sequences. A stronger baseline might involve a hierarchical RNN that is fine-tuned on the sequence data, which could potentially learn some notion of sequence-level consistency without an explicit coherence model.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that introduces a novel and challenging problem to the community and proposes a sensible and effective solution. The strengths—particularly the significance of the problem, the soundness of the proposed model, and the thoroughness of the evaluation—outweigh the weaknesses. The lack of clarity on the scoring function is a correctable flaw, and the other points are more about positioning the work's contribution. The paper makes a clear and demonstrable step forward in bridging vision and language. It is likely to inspire follow-up work on this important topic. The results are solid, and the paper is well-written and organized. I recommend acceptance.
