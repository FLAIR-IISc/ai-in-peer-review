This paper tackles a genuinely interesting and underexplored problem: generating coherent multi-sentence descriptions for image *streams*, moving beyond the single-image-single-sentence paradigm. The motivation – reflecting how humans document experiences with a series of photos – is compelling. The proposed CRCN architecture, integrating CNNs, RNNs, and a local coherence model, represents a bold attempt to address the challenges inherent in this task. The use of blog data as a weakly supervised source is also a clever solution to the data scarcity issue.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** This is the primary strength. Extending image captioning to sequential data with sequential output is a natural, yet surprisingly untouched, progression. The coherence modeling component, specifically, feels like a key innovation.
* **Problem Formulation:** The problem is well-defined and intuitively appealing. The NYC/Disneyland blog use case is relatable and justifies the need for sequence-aware description.
* **Architecture:** The CRCN architecture appears thoughtfully designed, combining established components (CNNs, RNNs) with a novel coherence model.  The attempt to integrate these into a unified end-to-end framework is commendable.
* **Dataset:** Leveraging blog data is resourceful and provides a relatively large-scale dataset for training.
* **Evaluation:** The paper includes both quantitative metrics (BLEU, Recall) *and* user studies, demonstrating a commitment to thorough evaluation.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Baselines:** While the paper compares against existing single-sentence captioning models adapted for sequence generation, these baselines don’t truly capture the spirit of sequential description.  A stronger baseline would involve a sequence-to-sequence model explicitly designed for this task, even if less sophisticated than CRCN.
* **Coherence Model Detail:** The description of the local coherence model, while present, feels somewhat superficial. The reliance on parse trees and entity grids is promising, but more detail on how these are incorporated into the scoring function would be beneficial. The 64-dimensional representation feels somewhat arbitrary.
* **Data Preprocessing:** The LSA-based text summarization feels like a potential bottleneck. Summarization *always* introduces information loss.  The impact of this summarization step on the quality of the generated sequences isn’t fully explored.
* **Qualitative Examples:** The provided qualitative examples (Fig. 3) are helpful, but more diverse examples showcasing failure cases would provide a more balanced view.
* **Minor Technicalities:** The discussion of dropout and optimization feels a bit cursory.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a genuinely novel and important problem.
* The proposed CRCN architecture is a solid and well-motivated attempt to solve this problem.
* The experimental results, while not overwhelming, demonstrate promising performance.
* The combination of quantitative and qualitative evaluation is positive.
* The paper opens up a new research direction in image/video understanding and natural language generation.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

* The baselines are relatively weak.
* The coherence model could benefit from more detailed explanation and analysis.
* The data preprocessing (summarization) step could be a limiting factor.
* The improvements over existing methods, while statistically significant, are not massive.



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite some weaknesses, I strongly lean towards **acceptance**. The novelty and ambition of this work are significant. The problem is well-motivated, the proposed approach is conceptually sound, and the experimental results are encouraging. The authors have taken a bold step toward addressing a challenging and important problem. I believe that this work has the potential to inspire further research in this area.  I recommend the authors address the minor comments regarding baseline strength and coherence model details in a final revision. The potential impact of the summarization step also warrants further discussion.



**Confidence:** 8/10
