

# Peer Review: "Expressing an Image Stream with a Sequence of Natural Sentences"

## Summary

This paper addresses an important extension of image captioning: generating a sequence of natural language sentences to describe a stream of images, rather than a single sentence for a single image. The authors propose a novel architecture called Coherent Recurrent Convolutional Networks (CRCN) that integrates convolutional neural networks for image representation, bidirectional recurrent networks for sentence modeling, and a local coherence model to ensure smooth transitions between sentences. The key innovation lies in recognizing that users often capture multiple images of memorable moments (e.g., travel experiences), making it more appropriate to describe the entire image stream rather than individual images. The authors collect a large dataset (20K blog posts with 140K images) from tourism blogs, preprocess it to create text-image parallel sequences, and evaluate their approach against several strong baselines. The work extends previous image captioning research (e.g., Karpathy et al. [9], Vinyals et al. [30]) by addressing the sentence-to-sentence coherence problem, which is crucial for creating fluent multi-sentence descriptions.

## Strengths

1. **Clear Problem Statement and Motivation**: The paper effectively motivates the problem by highlighting real-world use cases (e.g., tourists capturing multiple images of NYC visits) and demonstrating that current single-sentence approaches are insufficient for image streams. The intuitive Fig. 1 effectively illustrates the problem.

2. **Well-Designed Architecture**: The CRCN architecture integrates three complementary components (CNN, BRNN, coherence model) in a unified end-to-end framework. The inclusion of the coherence model specifically addresses a gap in previous work, as the authors correctly note that consecutive images in streams often show abrupt visual changes that would lead to discontinuous sentences without coherence modeling.

3. **Comprehensive Evaluation**: The authors provide both quantitative metrics (BLEU, CIDEr, METEOR, top-K recall) and qualitative user studies via Amazon Mechanical Turk. The ablation study (comparing CRCN with RCN, which excludes the coherence model) effectively demonstrates the importance of the coherence component.

4. **Practical Dataset Collection**: Using blog posts as a source of text-image parallel data is clever and practical. The preprocessing steps (text segmentation, summarization) are well-described and justified, and the resulting dataset is substantial (20K blog posts with 140K images).

5. **Thorough Baseline Comparison**: The authors appropriately compare against multiple state-of-the-art single-sentence captioning models (Karpathy et al. [9], Vinyals et al. [30], Kiros et al. [12]) and extend them to create sentence sequences for comparison.

## Weaknesses

1. **Incremental Novelty**: While the problem extension is meaningful, the core technical contributions (CNN, BRNN, coherence model) are largely adapted from existing work. The paper would benefit from more clearly articulating what is fundamentally new in the CRCN architecture versus previous multimodal networks (e.g., the long-term recurrent convolutional networks [5] mentioned in related work). The title "Coherent Recurrent Convolutional Networks" suggests novelty, but the coherence model is borrowed from Barzilay & Lapata [1], and the other components are standard.

2. **Limited Analysis of Sequence Length Effects**: The user study shows that coherence becomes more important as sequence length increases, but the paper doesn't provide sufficient analysis of how the model performs with very long sequences (e.g., >10 images). This is a significant limitation for real-world applications where image streams could be quite long.

3. **Insufficient Justification for Sentence Sequences**: While the motivation for sequence generation is good, the paper doesn't provide strong evidence that sentence sequences are better than single sentences for describing image streams. The authors assume that multiple sentences are needed, but don't demonstrate how much better they are compared to, say, concatenating multiple single-sentence descriptions.

4. **Technical Details on Coherence Model**: The coherence model is described somewhat superficially. More detail on how the entity grid is constructed and how transitions are used would help readers understand this key component better. The 64-dimensional transition descriptor could be more clearly explained.

5. **Evaluation Scope**: The evaluation is limited to tourism topics (NYC and Disneyland). While the authors claim their approach is domain-agnostic, they don't test it on other domains to demonstrate this claim.

## Recommendation

This paper presents a well-executed extension of image captioning to image streams with a coherent sentence sequence generation approach. The problem is well-motivated, the architecture is sound, and the evaluation is comprehensive. While the novelty is somewhat incremental (extending existing image captioning work to sequences with coherence modeling), the practical contribution is valuable, and the work is technically sound.

The paper would be significantly strengthened by:
1. More clearly differentiating the technical novelty from previous multimodal networks
2. Providing more thorough analysis of performance across different sequence lengths
3. Offering stronger evidence that sentence sequences are necessary (not just convenient) for image streams
4. Adding more detail on the coherence model implementation

Given the quality of the work and its relevance to the field, I recommend acceptance. The paper makes a meaningful contribution to the image captioning literature by addressing a gap in the literature (sentence sequence generation with coherence) and providing a practical solution with a large, well-curated dataset.

## Pro/Con Acceptance Summary

**Pro:**
- Well-motivated problem with clear real-world application
- Comprehensive evaluation with both quantitative metrics and user studies
- Practical and substantial dataset collection from blog posts
- Clear demonstration of the coherence model's importance through ablation study

**Con:**
- Novelty is somewhat incremental (adapting existing techniques rather than introducing fundamentally new ones)
- Limited analysis of performance with longer image sequences
- Insufficient justification for why sentence sequences are better than single sentences for image streams
- Some technical details (especially on coherence model) are under-explained

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field, and I believe it should be accepted to NIPS.
