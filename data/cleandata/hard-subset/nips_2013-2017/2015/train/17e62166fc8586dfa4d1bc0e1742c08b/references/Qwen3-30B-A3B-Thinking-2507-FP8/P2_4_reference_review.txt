

# Review of "Expressing an Image Stream with a Sequence of Natural Sentences"

## Summary and Relation to Previous Work

This paper addresses a novel problem in multimodal learning: generating a sequence of natural language sentences to describe a stream of images, rather than the typical single-sentence description of a single image. The authors propose a "Coherent Recurrent Convolutional Network" (CRCN) that integrates convolutional neural networks for image representation, bidirectional recurrent networks for sentence modeling, and a local coherence model to ensure fluent transitions between sentences. The key insight is that human-authored blog posts provide natural image-text sequences that can be leveraged as training data, rather than relying on artificially constructed datasets. This work extends the literature on image captioning (e.g., Karpathy et al. 2015, Vinyals et al. 2015) and video captioning (e.g., Rohrbach et al. 2015) by focusing on the sequential nature of both input (image stream) and output (sentence sequence).

## Strengths

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound with well-justified methodology and thorough evaluation. The CRCN architecture effectively combines established techniques (CNNs for images, BRNNs for text, entity-based coherence) in a novel way for the specific problem. The authors provide comprehensive ablation studies demonstrating the value of each component (particularly the coherence model), and they carefully analyze both quantitative metrics and user preferences. The blog dataset collection methodology is well-justified and provides a substantial resource (20K blog posts, 140K images) for this underexplored problem.

**Originality:** The paper makes a clear contribution by formalizing the problem of "image stream to sentence sequence" generation, which is distinct from both single-image captioning and video captioning. The authors correctly identify that existing video captioning work (e.g., Donahue et al. 2015) does not address the generation of multiple sentences, and they provide a compelling justification for why coherence is particularly important for image streams (which can have sharp visual changes between consecutive images). The use of blog posts as a natural source of image-text sequences is a clever and well-justified data collection approach.

**Significance:** The problem addressed has clear practical relevance (e.g., describing travel experiences, photo albums), and the paper demonstrates significant improvements over baselines. The user studies with Amazon Mechanical Turk provide convincing evidence of the qualitative improvement in generated sequences, particularly for longer passages where coherence matters most. The work opens up new research directions in multimodal sequence generation and demonstrates that coherence is a critical factor in generating fluent multi-sentence descriptions.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-organized and clearly written. The problem statement is well-motivated with a compelling example (New York City photo stream), and the methodology is described with sufficient technical detail for reproducibility. The figures (Fig. 1-3) effectively illustrate the key concepts, and the experimental results are presented in a logical and transparent manner.

## Weaknesses

**Limited Dataset Generalizability:** While the authors note their approach is "completely unsupervised and thus applicable to any domain," they only evaluate on tourism-related blog posts (NYC and Disneyland). The paper does not discuss whether the coherence model would generalize to other domains where the discourse structure might differ significantly (e.g., sports events, family gatherings). It would strengthen the paper to include a brief analysis of potential domain limitations.

**Coherence Model Limitations:** The coherence model is based solely on entity transitions (coreferent noun phrases), which captures only a narrow aspect of discourse coherence. The paper does not discuss how this might fail to capture other important coherence aspects (e.g., thematic progression, pragmatic relevance). A brief discussion of these limitations would be valuable for readers.

**User Study Limitations:** The user study is limited to 100 test streams with a maximum of 5 images per query. While the authors note that coherence becomes more important for longer passages, they don't provide sufficient evidence about performance on longer sequences. The additional test with 8-10 images is helpful but still limited in scale.

## Overall Assessment

This is a strong paper that makes a clear and significant contribution to multimodal learning. The problem formulation is novel, the methodology is sound, and the results are convincing. The authors have done an excellent job of distinguishing their work from related video captioning and image captioning literature.

The paper meets all the NIPS review criteria well. It is technically sound (Quality), clearly written (Clarity), makes a novel contribution (Originality), and addresses a meaningful problem with significant implications (Significance). While the weaknesses noted above are important to acknowledge, they don't detract substantially from the overall contribution.

## Recommendation

I recommend acceptance of this paper. It represents a meaningful advance in multimodal sequence generation and demonstrates careful, thorough evaluation. The work is well-positioned to inspire future research in this emerging area of multimodal learning, particularly as the use of sequential visual content (e.g., in social media) continues to grow.

The paper would benefit from a slightly expanded discussion of the coherence model's limitations and potential domain-specific challenges, but these are minor points that don't significantly impact the paper's overall contribution. The authors have clearly addressed the core problem with a well-justified approach and compelling evidence of its effectiveness.
