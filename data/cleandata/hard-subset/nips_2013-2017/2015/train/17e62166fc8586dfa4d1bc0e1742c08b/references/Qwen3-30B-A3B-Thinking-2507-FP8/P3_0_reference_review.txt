

# Peer Review: "Expressing an Image Stream with a Sequence of Natural Sentences"

## Summary
This paper proposes a novel approach for generating sequences of natural language sentences to describe image streams, extending beyond the typical single-image captioning paradigm. The authors introduce Coherent Recurrent Convolutional Networks (CRCN), which integrates convolutional neural networks for image representation, bidirectional recurrent networks for sentence modeling, and a local coherence model based on entity transitions to ensure smooth sentence transitions. The paper leverages blog posts as a source of natural image-text parallel data, pre-processing them to extract image-sentence pairs for training. The authors evaluate their approach on NYC and Disneyland blog datasets (20K posts with 140K images) using both quantitative metrics (BLEU, CIDEr, METEOR, top-K recall) and human evaluations via Amazon Mechanical Turk.

## Strengths

The paper makes a clear and well-motivated extension to the image captioning problem, recognizing that users often capture sequences of images (e.g., during travel) rather than single images. The CRCN architecture effectively combines three key components: image representation (VGGNet), sentence modeling (BRNN), and coherence modeling (entity-based transitions). The data collection method using blog posts is practical and well-justified, as these naturally contain image-text pairs in storytelling format.

The evaluation is comprehensive, with both quantitative metrics and human evaluation via AMT. The ablation studies effectively demonstrate the contribution of the coherence model (with CRCN outperforming RCN in retrieval metrics, particularly for longer sequences). The paper also acknowledges limitations, such as the modest gains in BLEU scores compared to the more significant improvements in retrieval metrics.

## Weaknesses

The main weakness is the limited novelty of the approach. While extending to image streams is reasonable, the paper doesn't sufficiently differentiate from recent video captioning work (e.g., [23, 32] cited in the paper), which also deals with sequences of images. The coherence model is based on Barzilay & Lapata (2008), which has been used in other NLP tasks, and the paper doesn't sufficiently explain why this specific implementation is particularly effective for image captioning.

The performance gains over baselines are modest, particularly in language metrics (BLEU, CIDEr, METEOR), with only small improvements over the RCN variant. The paper acknowledges this limitation but doesn't provide sufficient theoretical justification for why the coherence model should significantly improve language metrics. The human evaluation shows a preference for CRCN over RCN, but the differences are smaller than the quantitative metrics suggest, and the evaluation is limited to short sequences (5 images), where coherence is less critical than for longer sequences.

The paper also doesn't sufficiently address potential limitations of using blog posts as training data. Blog posts may represent a specific style of storytelling that doesn't generalize well to other image streams (e.g., casual social media posts), and the paper lacks analysis of how well the model handles different types of image sequences.

## Technical Assessment

The methodology is technically sound, with a clear description of the CRCN architecture and training procedure. The use of blog posts as a data source is practical, though the paper could have provided more detail on potential biases in the data (e.g., the specific topics covered in NYC and Disneyland blogs). The BRNN and coherence model components are well-explained, and the ablation studies provide valuable insights into the contribution of each component.

However, the paper lacks a deeper theoretical analysis of why the coherence model works better than other approaches. The paper claims that "the coherence model is more demanded to make output passages fluent" due to "sharp changes of visual content" in image streams, but doesn't provide empirical evidence to support this claim beyond the observed performance gains.

## Significance

The problem of generating sequential descriptions for image streams is indeed a natural extension of image captioning, and the paper demonstrates that their approach outperforms single-sentence baselines. However, the significance is limited by the modest performance gains and the fact that the problem is not significantly different from recent video captioning work. The paper doesn't convincingly argue why this specific problem is more important than other extensions to image captioning.

The paper's contribution is incremental rather than transformative. While the CRCN architecture is well-constructed, it primarily combines existing techniques rather than introducing fundamentally new approaches. The paper would have been stronger if it had more clearly established how this differs from and improves upon recent video captioning methods.

## Recommendations

The paper presents a reasonable extension to image captioning, but the novelty is limited, and the performance gains are modest. I would recommend acceptance with the following conditions:

1. The authors should more clearly differentiate their work from recent video captioning literature (e.g., [23, 32] and others) and explain why their approach is particularly suited for image streams rather than video.

2. The paper should provide more detailed analysis of the coherence model's effectiveness, particularly why it improves retrieval metrics more than language metrics.

3. The authors should include a discussion of the limitations of using blog posts as training data, including potential biases and how the model might generalize to other domains.

4. The paper should extend the human evaluation to longer image sequences (8-10 images) to better demonstrate the value of the coherence model, as suggested in their own results.

Overall, the paper makes a modest but reasonable contribution to the image captioning literature, with a well-executed methodology and thorough evaluation. The incremental nature of the work is a limitation for a top-tier venue like NeurIPS, but the practical motivation and comprehensive evaluation make it a solid contribution that would be suitable for publication with the suggested revisions.

## Final Recommendation
Accept with minor revisions (or borderline acceptance, depending on the program committee's threshold for incremental work). The paper's main limitation is the incremental nature of the contribution, but the practical motivation, thorough evaluation, and clear presentation make it a worthwhile addition to the literature.
