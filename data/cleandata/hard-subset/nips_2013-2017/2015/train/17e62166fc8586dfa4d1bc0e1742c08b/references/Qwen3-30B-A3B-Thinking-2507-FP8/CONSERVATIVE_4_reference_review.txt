

# Review of "Expressing an Image Stream with a Sequence of Natural Sentences"

This paper proposes a novel problem setting for image description: generating a sequence of natural language sentences to describe an image stream (a sequence of images), rather than the standard single-image to single-sentence task. The authors introduce CRCN (coherent recurrent convolutional networks), which combines CNN image features, bidirectional RNN for sentence modeling, and a local coherence model to ensure smooth transitions between sentences. They collect blog data (NYC and Disneyland datasets) as training data, where each blog post with multiple images is decomposed into image-sentence pairs.

The paper makes several contributions: (1) addressing the image stream to sentence sequence problem, (2) proposing CRCN as a unified architecture, and (3) evaluating on large blog datasets with both quantitative metrics and user studies.

**Strengths:** The paper is well-written, clearly organized, and presents a substantial experimental evaluation with both quantitative metrics (BLEU, CIDEr, METEOR, top-K recall) and user studies via Amazon Mechanical Turk. The datasets are large (20K blog posts with 140K images), and the approach is thoroughly validated against multiple baselines.

**Weaknesses:** 

1. **Lack of clear novelty:** The paper claims to be "the first to address the problem of expressing image streams with sentence sequences," but this problem is remarkably similar to video captioning, which has been extensively studied (e.g., [23, 32] cited in the paper). Video captioning also generates sentence sequences from image sequences, and the authors fail to adequately distinguish their problem from existing video captioning work. The paper does not engage meaningfully with the video captioning literature, which is a major oversight.

2. **Overstated significance:** The motivation that "general users often take a series of pictures on their memorable moments" is weak justification for a new research direction. Image sequences from blogs are often non-temporal (e.g., multiple images from different days of a trip), making the problem less well-defined than video captioning. The paper doesn't convincingly demonstrate that this problem is more important or challenging than existing video captioning tasks.

3. **Inadequate comparison to relevant work:** The authors compare to single-sentence image captioning models (e.g., [9, 12, 30]) but fail to compare with video captioning methods that generate sentence sequences. This is a critical omission, as video captioning methods would be direct baselines for this problem. The paper's comparison is therefore not sufficiently rigorous.

4. **Methodological concerns:** The coherence model is based on a previous work [1] but the paper doesn't clearly demonstrate how their specific implementation differs or improves upon it. The ablation study (comparing CRCN with RCN) is limited and doesn't show the contribution of each component in depth.

5. **Evaluation limitations:** The user studies are conducted on short image sequences (5 images), but the paper claims coherence becomes more important as sequences get longer. The evaluation doesn't test the method on longer sequences where the coherence model would be most beneficial. The paper also doesn't discuss how the approach would generalize beyond blog data.

6. **Data collection concerns:** The blog data collection method (using LSA-based text summarization) may introduce biases and limitations. The paper doesn't address how the quality of the blog data affects the model's performance.

**Overall assessment:** While the paper presents a clear and well-executed study, it does not make a sufficiently novel contribution to the field. The problem addressed is largely a minor extension of existing video captioning work, and the approach is a combination of established techniques rather than a significant methodological advance. The paper's claims of novelty and significance are overstated, and it fails to adequately engage with the relevant literature (video captioning).

The paper does not advance the state of the art in a demonstrable way. The improvements over baselines are marginal, especially when considering that the baselines are not directly comparable (they generate single sentences rather than sequences). The quantitative results show only small improvements in retrieval metrics, and the user studies show even smaller differences.

This paper would be better suited for a more application-focused venue rather than NIPS, where the bar for methodological novelty and theoretical contribution is high. The work is technically sound but lacks the significant innovation required for NIPS acceptance.

**Recommendation:** Reject. The paper's claims of novelty are unsubstantiated, and it fails to properly distinguish itself from existing video captioning literature. The contribution is incremental rather than innovative, and the significance of the problem is questionable.
