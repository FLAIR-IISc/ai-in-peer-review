

# Review of "Expressing an Image Stream with a Sequence of Natural Sentences"

This paper proposes a method for generating sequences of natural language sentences from image streams, introducing a "Coherent Recurrent Convolutional Networks" (CRCN) architecture that integrates convolutional networks, bidirectional recurrent networks, and a local coherence model. The authors claim this is the first work to address the problem of "expressing image streams with sentence sequences" rather than single images with single sentences, motivated by the common practice of taking multiple photos during memorable events.

The paper's main contributions are: (1) extending image-to-sentence generation to image streams and sentence sequences, (2) proposing CRCN as a unified architecture integrating CNNs, BRNNs, and a coherence model, and (3) evaluating on blog datasets with 20K posts and 140K images. The authors compare against several single-sentence generation baselines (e.g., Karpathy et al., Vinyals et al.) by concatenating their outputs to form sentence sequences.

**Strengths:** The problem setup is reasonable and has practical relevance. The paper demonstrates a clear methodology for creating text-image parallel data from blogs, and the evaluation includes both quantitative metrics (BLEU, CIDEr, METEOR, top-K recall) and user studies. The blog dataset collection approach is well-documented, and the authors provide sufficient implementation details.

**Weaknesses:** The paper suffers from several critical issues that undermine its contribution and validity:

1. **Questionable Novelty**: The core problem (generating sequential text from sequential images) is closely related to video captioning research (e.g., Rohrbach et al. 2015, Donahue et al. 2015), which explicitly addresses "long streams of images and text." The paper fails to adequately distinguish its approach from these works. Video captioning inherently deals with sequential visual inputs, making the "image stream" problem a direct extension of existing work rather than a novel problem.

2. **Marginal Performance Gains**: The quantitative results show only marginal improvements over baselines. For instance, CRCN outperforms the best baseline by 0.8 BLEU points on NYC (Table 1), with even smaller gains on Disneyland. The coherence model's contribution (CRCN vs. RCN) is particularly minimal in language metrics (0.2 BLEU difference), with only retrieval metrics showing significant improvement. This suggests the method may not represent a meaningful advance.

3. **Inadequate Baseline Comparison**: The authors compare against single-sentence generation models (which produce one sentence per image) by concatenating their outputs to form sequences. This is fundamentally different from the proposed approach, which generates sequences with coherence considerations. A fairer comparison would require baselines explicitly designed for sequence generation, which do not exist in the literature as claimed.

4. **Lack of Theoretical Justification**: The paper provides no theoretical analysis of why the coherence model should improve performance, nor does it explain why the specific entity-based coherence model (from Barzilay & Lapata) is optimal for this task. The coherence model appears to be tacked on without sufficient justification.

5. **Questionable Validation Methodology**: The user studies (AMT) show only a small preference for CRCN over RCN (Table 2), with the difference becoming significant only when using longer query sequences (8-10 images). This suggests the coherence model's benefit is limited to longer sequences, which the authors don't fully explore. The study design (pairwise comparison of short sequences) may not capture the full benefits of coherence.

6. **Unjustified Claims**: The paper claims to be "the first to address the problem," but fails to properly acknowledge the extensive video captioning literature that directly addresses sequential visual input. The "unsupervised" claim is misleading since the approach uses supervised learning with blog data.

**Overall Assessment:** While the problem is interesting, the paper does not sufficiently advance the state of the art. The proposed method is a combination of established techniques without compelling evidence that the integration provides significant benefits over existing approaches. The marginal performance gains, inadequate baseline comparison, and questionable novelty undermine the contribution. The paper would benefit from a more thorough comparison with video captioning work, a stronger theoretical justification for the coherence model, and more convincing evidence of the method's superiority.

**Recommendation:** Reject. The paper fails to demonstrate a meaningful contribution to the field, with improvements that are marginal at best and the novelty being questionable. The method does not sufficiently advance the state of the art in image captioning or sequential text generation, and the validation is insufficient to support the claims made. The authors should either substantially revise their method with stronger evidence or reconsider the novelty of their approach before resubmitting.
