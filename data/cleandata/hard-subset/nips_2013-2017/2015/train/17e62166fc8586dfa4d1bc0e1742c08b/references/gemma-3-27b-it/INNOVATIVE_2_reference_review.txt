This paper tackles a compelling problem: generating coherent multi-sentence descriptions from image streams. Moving beyond single image-single sentence pairings is a natural and important step, particularly given the prevalence of photo streams capturing real-world experiences. The authors’ framing of the problem through the lens of travel blogs is insightful and provides a readily available, albeit noisy, dataset. The proposed CRCN architecture, integrating CNNs, RNNs, and a coherence model, is a reasonable approach to address the challenges of sequential image understanding and natural language generation.

The key strength of this work lies in its *novelty*. While the individual components (CNNs, RNNs, coherence modeling) aren't groundbreaking, the *combination* to address this specific problem – sequential image description – is a significant contribution. The authors clearly identify a gap in the literature and offer a concrete, end-to-end solution. The decision to leverage blog data is also clever, offering a practical way to obtain paired image-text streams, circumventing the difficulties of creating such datasets from scratch.

However, the paper exhibits several weaknesses. The experimental validation, while reasonably thorough with quantitative metrics and user studies, feels somewhat superficial. The gains over baselines, particularly in the quantitative results, aren’t overwhelmingly large, and a deeper analysis of *why* CRCN succeeds where others fail is needed. The ablation studies (comparing CRCN to RCN and 1NN) are helpful, but more granular ablation (e.g., removing just the coherence model *without* simplifying to 1NN) would be insightful. The user study design, while sound, relies on pairwise comparisons. Reporting confidence intervals and effect sizes would strengthen the conclusions. 

The paper could also benefit from more discussion of the limitations. The reliance on blog data introduces inherent biases (e.g., writing style, image selection), which could affect the generalizability of the model. Acknowledging these biases and discussing potential mitigation strategies would be valuable. Further, the computational complexity of the retrieval step, even with the approximate search, isn’t fully addressed.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** Addresses an under-explored problem in a creative way.
*   **Soundness:** The proposed architecture is logically sound and builds upon established techniques.
*   **Potential Significance:** Successful sequential image description has broad applications in areas like robotics, assistive technology, and content creation.
*   **Demonstrated Performance:** Shows improvement over baselines, both quantitatively and via user studies.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Incremental Improvement:** The performance gains aren't massive, suggesting the approach, while promising, isn’t a revolutionary leap forward.
*   **Superficial Validation:** The experimental analysis could be more in-depth and nuanced.
*   **Limited Discussion of Limitations:** The impact of data biases and computational complexity isn’t fully explored.

**Overall:**

I lean towards *accepting* this paper. While not perfect, the novelty of the problem and the proposed approach are significant. The authors have presented a well-structured and reasonably well-validated solution to a challenging problem. The weaknesses, while present, are not fatal, and could be addressed in future work. The paper opens up a promising new direction for research in image understanding and natural language generation, and that, to me, is more important than methodological perfection. I would encourage the authors to address the points raised above in a future iteration of this work.
