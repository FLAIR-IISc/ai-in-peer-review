### **Summary and Relation to Previous Work**

This paper addresses the novel task of generating a sequence of natural language sentences (a paragraph or story) from a corresponding sequence of images (an image stream). This is framed as a natural and important extension of the well-studied problem of generating a single sentence for a single image. The authors propose a new model, the Coherent Recurrent Convolutional Network (CRCN), to tackle this challenge. The CRCN architecture integrates three key components: (1) a Convolutional Neural Network (CNN), specifically VGGNet, to extract visual features from each image; (2) a Bidirectional Recurrent Neural Network (BRNN) to model the semantic flow and context of the sentence sequence, operating on paragraph vector representations; and (3) an entity-based local coherence model, adapted from Barzilay and Lapata (2008), to explicitly enforce linguistic and structural fluency between sentences.

The work builds upon the recent trend of combining CNNs and RNNs for visual description tasks (e.g., Karpathy et al., 2015; Vinyals et al., 2015). However, it distinguishes itself by moving beyond the single image/sentence paradigm. While related to video description (e.g., Donahue et al., 2015), the authors compellingly argue that image streams present a unique challenge due to potentially abrupt visual changes between consecutive photos, making an explicit coherence model more critical than for temporally smooth video frames. A significant contribution is also the data collection methodology, which leverages large-scale, unstructured blog posts as a source of parallel image-sentence stream data, providing a practical solution to the data scarcity problem for this new task.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Novel and Significant Problem Formulation:** The paper is, to my knowledge, one of the first to formally define and tackle the problem of generating coherent paragraphs for image streams. This is a well-motivated and significant step beyond single-image captioning, with clear applications in areas like photo album summarization, automated storytelling, and content creation. It moves the field towards a deeper, more contextual understanding of visual media.

2.  **Sound and Comprehensive Methodology:** The proposed CRCN model is a thoughtful combination of powerful and appropriate techniques. The integration of a CNN for vision, a BRNN for high-level semantic flow, and a classic NLP coherence model for low-level linguistic flow is novel and well-justified. The BRNN is a good choice for capturing both preceding and succeeding context, and the explicit inclusion of the entity-grid model to handle syntactic/referential coherence is a key insight that directly addresses the challenges of multi-sentence generation.

3.  **Thorough and Convincing Evaluation:** The experimental validation is a major strength.
    *   The use of two large, real-world datasets collected from blogs demonstrates the method's applicability to "in-the-wild" data.
    *   The evaluation uses a comprehensive suite of metrics, including standard language generation scores (BLEU, CIDEr, METEOR) and retrieval metrics (R@K, Med r), providing a multi-faceted view of performance.
    *   Crucially, the authors include an Amazon Mechanical Turk (AMT) user study, which is essential for a subjective task like story generation. The results strongly favor their method.
    *   The ablation study, comparing CRCN against a 1-NN baseline and a variant without the coherence model (RCN), effectively quantifies the contribution of the different components of their architecture, supporting their central claims.

4.  **Practical Data Sourcing Strategy:** The idea of using blog posts, with pre-processing steps for text segmentation and summarization, is a clever and scalable approach to creating a training corpus for this new task. This provides a valuable blueprint for future work in this area.

### **Weaknesses**

1.  **Retrieval-Based vs. Generative:** The approach is fundamentally retrieval-based at the sentence level. It selects and re-ranks sentences from the training corpus rather than generating new ones word-by-word. While this is a practical choice that ensures grammatical fluency of individual sentences, it limits the model's expressive power. The system can never produce a sentence with a combination of objects or actions not seen in the training data. This limitation, while common in early-stage research on a new problem, should be more explicitly discussed as a trade-off.

2.  **Clarity on Score Combination:** The formulation of the final compatibility score in Equation (3) could be clearer. The coherence term `gk` appears to be a global property of the sentence sequence, yet it is added to the image-sentence match score `s_kt · v_lt` at *each* time step `t`. This seems to imply the global coherence score is added `N` times. A more intuitive formulation might be to have it as a single term added to the sum of local scores. While the model may learn to handle this during training, the justification for this specific formulation is not provided, making it slightly difficult to interpret.

3.  **Approximate Inference:** The use of a divide-and-conquer strategy with beam search to navigate the exponential search space of candidate sentence sequences is a necessary and pragmatic heuristic. However, the paper gives limited analysis of its limitations. How sensitive is the performance to the beam size M? Is there a risk of pruning a globally coherent sequence because its initial sub-sequences are only moderately strong? While the authors' claim that local coherence is a prerequisite for global coherence is reasonable, some empirical justification would strengthen the paper.

### **Recommendation**

I recommend **acceptance**.

**Justification:** This is a strong paper that introduces a novel, important, and challenging problem to the community. The proposed CRCN model is a well-designed and effective solution that thoughtfully combines techniques from vision and NLP. The paper's main claims are convincingly supported by a thorough and multi-faceted experimental evaluation on large, real-world datasets, including crucial human studies. The weaknesses identified are relatively minor and represent reasonable trade-offs or avenues for future work, rather than fundamental flaws in the current contribution. This work clearly advances the state of the art and is likely to inspire significant follow-up research on the intersection of vision, language, and contextual understanding. It is an excellent fit for NIPS.
