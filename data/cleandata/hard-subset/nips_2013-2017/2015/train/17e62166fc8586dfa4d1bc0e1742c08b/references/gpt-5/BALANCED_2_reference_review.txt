Summary
This paper tackles a timely extension of image captioning: given a stream of images, generate a coherent sequence of sentences. The proposed CRCN architecture combines (i) CNN image features (VGG), (ii) a bidirectional RNN over sentences represented by paragraph vectors (doc2vec) to model content flow, and (iii) an entity-grid–based local coherence model (Barzilay & Lapata) to encourage fluent cross-sentence transitions. Training uses max-margin ranking between aligned image–sentence sequences and randomly sampled negatives. At test time, the method retrieves K nearest images per position, forms candidate sentence sequences from associated training sentences, and ranks candidates with the learned model using a beam-style divide-and-conquer search. The authors construct two large blog datasets (NYC, Disneyland), derive sentence–image alignments via paragraph segmentation plus LSA-based summarization, and evaluate with BLEU/CIDEr/METEOR, retrieval metrics (R@K, median rank), and AMT user studies. They report that CRCN outperforms adapted single-image captioning baselines and ablations, with larger gains on longer sequences.

Relation to prior work: The paper is situated among (a) image captioning via CNN-RNN and retrieval-based methods, (b) video-to-sentence work modeling temporal inputs, and (c) text coherence modeling via entity grids. Unlike video captioning that outputs a single sentence per clip, this paper aims to produce multi-sentence paragraphs and explicitly integrates a coherence model. It is also related to earlier work by the authors on retrieving image sequences from paragraphs (inverse direction).

Strengths
- Addresses an underexplored but important problem (multi-sentence description of image streams), arguably the next step beyond single-image captioning.
- Sensible architecture combining BRNN for sentence flow with an explicit coherence signal (entity-grid), with end-to-end training via a structured ranking loss.
- Large-scale, weakly supervised training on real blog data; the pipeline to extract sentence–image alignments is clearly described.
- Comprehensive evaluation: automatic metrics, retrieval metrics, user studies; ablations (1NN, RCN vs CRCN) to isolate the contribution of coherence.
- Practical candidate search scheme for sequence selection; evidence that coherence matters more for longer sequences.

Weaknesses
- Retrieval rather than true generation: outputs are limited to training sentences, which constrains expressivity and generalization; the comparison to generative baselines may be unfairly favorable to retrieval on noisy blog data.
- The text–image alignment is produced by heuristics (distance assignment + LSA summarization) with no quantitative validation of alignment quality; noise here can bias both training and evaluation.
- The coherence component is hand-crafted (entity-grid frequencies padded to 300 dims) and weakly coupled to vision. The scoring term g · v_t added at every time step is not well justified and may over-emphasize sequence length; the mechanism by which textual coherence aligns with image features is unclear.
- Evaluation relies heavily on n-gram metrics not designed for paragraph coherence; while the paper acknowledges this, significance testing and agreement statistics for AMT are not reported. Details of AMT instructions/controls are limited.
- Baselines for multi-sentence structure are limited to concatenations of single-sentence models; missing stronger structured baselines (e.g., sequence models over sentence candidates with bigram potentials/CRF, or hierarchical RNNs over sentences).
- Clarity/notation: some overloading (S, s_t), dimensions, and the role of dropout in the FC stack could be clearer. Complexity of the candidate search (time/memory) and sensitivity to K/M/Q are not analyzed.

Quality
- Generally sound engineering and empirical work; the training objective and ablations support the claims.
- However, the justification for the specific fusion (q→g and repeated g · v_t) is weak, and there is no analysis of coherence feature variants (history length, roles, parsing errors).
- No error analysis or quantitative sanity checks of the blog preprocessing/alignment quality.

Clarity
- The overall pipeline is understandable, and equations are provided. Some notational overload and architectural details (e.g., FC stacking rationale, search parameters and complexity) could be improved. Additional qualitative examples highlighting coherence gains would help.

Originality
- The problem formulation and explicit integration of a coherence model into a vision–language sequence retrieval framework are interesting. That said, the components (CNN features, BRNN over doc2vec, entity grid) are standard, and the method is an incremental combination rather than a fundamentally new model.

Significance
- The task is practically relevant (e.g., photo albums). The datasets are sizable and could be useful if released. The coherence result, especially on longer sequences, is promising. However, limitations from retrieval-only output and noisy supervision temper broader impact.

Suggestions for improvement
- Validate and report the quality of the image–sentence alignments (e.g., human spot checks, agreement, error types).
- Provide statistical significance for automatic metrics and report AMT inter-annotator agreement and quality controls; include representative AMT prompts.
- Strengthen baselines: a structured model over sentence candidates (e.g., bigram/CRF potentials on sentence transitions, diversity penalties) and/or a hierarchical RNN that models sentence sequences end-to-end.
- Analyze the coherence module: compare entity-grid variants (history length, role sets), ablate g · v_t vs a single global term, and explore learned neural coherence encoders (e.g., sentence-level RNNs over coreference chains).
- Add redundancy control and lexical diversity analysis; report qualitative failure cases.
- Discuss computational complexity and sensitivity to K, M, and beam depth; clarify whether CNN was fine-tuned.
- Consider evaluating on a different domain to demonstrate generality, and release code/data.

Questions for the authors
- How accurate are the text–image alignments produced by your preprocessing? Any human validation?
- Why is the coherence vector g dotted with each v_t (summed over t)? Did you try a single global compatibility term?
- Were CNN features fine-tuned on your datasets? If not, does fine-tuning help?
- How sensitive are results to K, M, and the search depth? What is the runtime per test sequence?

Recommendation: Reject (borderline)
Pros: novel task; coherent integration; solid dataset and evaluations; clear gains on longer sequences.
Cons: retrieval-only modeling with heavy heuristics; weakly justified fusion of coherence with vision; limited/mismatched baselines for multi-sentence modeling; lack of alignment validation and rigorous statistical analysis.

I believe the paper could become much stronger with validated supervision, more principled sequence modeling/baselines, clearer justification of the fusion term, and deeper analysis of coherence effects.
