

# Review for "Expressing an Image Stream with a Sequence of Natural Sentences"

## Summary
This paper introduces a novel task: generating a sequence of natural language sentences to describe a stream of images, extending beyond the traditional single-image captioning problem. The authors propose Coherent Recurrent Convolutional Networks (CRCN), which integrates convolutional neural networks (for image representation), bidirectional recurrent neural networks (for sentence modeling), and a local coherence model (for smooth transitions between sentences). The work is motivated by the observation that users often capture memorable moments as image streams rather than single images. The authors collect and preprocess a large dataset from blog posts (NYC and Disneyland, totaling 20K blog posts and 140K images), transforming each blog into a sequence of image-sentence pairs. They evaluate their approach using standard captioning metrics (BLEU, CIDEr, METEOR) and retrieval metrics (top-K recall, median rank), as well as user studies on Amazon Mechanical Turk. The paper clearly differentiates itself from prior work on single-image captioning (e.g., [9, 12, 21, 30]) and video captioning (e.g., [23, 32]), arguing that their coherence model is essential for fluent sentence sequences when consecutive images may show sharp visual changes.

## Quality
The technical approach is sound, with a well-structured architecture that integrates established techniques (CNNs, BRNNs) with a novel coherence model. The authors provide sufficient detail about their implementation (VGGNet for images, LSA-based text summarization, BRNN structure), and their evaluation methodology is comprehensive. The paper includes thorough ablation studies showing the contribution of each component (CRCN vs. RCN vs. 1NN). The dataset collection from blog posts is a practical and innovative solution to the lack of existing image-stream-to-sentence datasets. However, the coherence model implementation details could be more explicit - for example, the paper states they "enumerate the transitions of grammatical roles" but doesn't clarify how the entity grid is constructed from parse trees or how the transition probabilities are used in the model. The authors acknowledge that BLEU is limited for paragraph evaluation, showing awareness of evaluation constraints.

## Clarity
The paper is well-organized and clearly written. The problem statement is well-motivated, and the contributions are explicitly stated. The architecture is explained with a figure (Fig. 2) and sufficient mathematical notation. The experimental setup is detailed enough for reproducibility, including dataset statistics, hyperparameters, and baseline comparisons. The explanation of the blog preprocessing pipeline (text segmentation, summarization, data augmentation) is particularly clear. Minor improvements could include more detailed descriptions of the coherence model's implementation and a more thorough discussion of the limitations of the blog data source (e.g., potential bias in how bloggers describe experiences).

## Originality
The core contribution is novel: the paper identifies and addresses the underexplored problem of generating sentence sequences for image streams. While prior work has focused on single-image captioning (e.g., [5, 8, 9, 14, 15, 26, 21, 30]) or video description (e.g., [23, 32]), this is the first to explicitly address sequential sentence generation for image streams. The authors clearly differentiate their work from related approaches: they extend the text retrieval framework to sequence generation with coherence modeling, unlike [6, 8, 21] which focus on single-sentence generation, and they address a different problem than [11] which retrieves image sequences for text. The integration of the local coherence model (from [1]) with the multimodal architecture is a novel combination for this specific task.

## Significance
The work is significant as it extends the image captioning literature to a more realistic scenario (image streams representing user experiences) and provides a practical solution using existing blog data. The evaluation demonstrates clear improvements over baselines, with the CRCN model outperforming alternatives by significant margins on retrieval metrics (top-K recall, median rank) and showing user preference in AMT studies. The unsupervised nature of the approach (using blog posts without manual annotation) makes it applicable to various domains beyond tourism. The paper advances the state of the art by addressing a gap in the literature, and the dataset and code could serve as a foundation for future research on sequential image description.

## Strengths
- Novel problem formulation that extends the image captioning literature in a meaningful direction
- Comprehensive evaluation with quantitative metrics and user studies
- Practical dataset collection method using blog posts (avoiding manual annotation)
- Clear ablation studies demonstrating the contribution of each component
- Well-structured architecture that effectively combines existing techniques

## Weaknesses
- Limited discussion of the limitations of using blog posts as training data (e.g., potential bias in content, language style, or image selection)
- Coherence model implementation details are insufficiently explained
- User studies focus on short image sequences (5 images), while the paper claims coherence is more critical for longer sequences (the results for longer sequences are limited to a single additional AMT test)
- The paper doesn't sufficiently address how the approach handles image streams with abrupt visual changes (which they acknowledge as a challenge but don't fully demonstrate handling)

## Pro/Con for Acceptance
**Pro:**
- Novel problem formulation that extends the literature
- Comprehensive evaluation with multiple metrics and user studies
- Practical approach using existing blog data without manual annotation
- Clear differentiation from prior work and well-articulated contributions
- Demonstrated significant improvements over baselines

**Con:**
- Some implementation details of the coherence model are missing
- Limited testing of coherence benefits on longer image streams
- Insufficient discussion of blog data limitations
- The paper doesn't fully address how to handle abrupt visual changes between consecutive images

## Overall Assessment
This paper presents a novel and well-motivated extension to the image captioning literature. The CRCN architecture is technically sound, and the evaluation demonstrates clear advantages over baselines. The work addresses an important gap in the literature, and the approach is practical and applicable to other domains. While there are some limitations in the presentation of the coherence model and the scope of the user studies, these do not undermine the core contribution. The paper makes a significant contribution to the field and is suitable for publication at NIPS.
