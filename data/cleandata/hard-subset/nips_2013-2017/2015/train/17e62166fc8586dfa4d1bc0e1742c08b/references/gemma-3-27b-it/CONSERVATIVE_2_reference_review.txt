This paper tackles the problem of generating sequential sentences from image streams, extending the single image-single sentence paradigm. While the motivation – describing memorable moments captured as photo streams – is relatable, the paper presents a largely incremental contribution built upon established techniques without demonstrating sufficient novelty or rigorous validation to merit acceptance at NIPS.

The proposed CRCN architecture combines CNNs for image representation, BRNNs for sequence modeling, and a local coherence model. Each component leverages well-established methods (VGGNet, BRNNs, LSA for summarization, paragraph vectors) and their integration, while conceptually sound, lacks a compelling innovation. The authors correctly identify the challenge of creating parallel image-text data and attempt to address it using blog posts, a pragmatic but not groundbreaking solution. 

**Strengths:**

* **Clear Problem Statement:** The motivation for extending image captioning to streams is well-articulated.
* **Established Methodology:** The use of established components (CNNs, RNNs) provides a foundation of known techniques.
* **Dataset Creation:** The construction of a large-scale dataset from blog posts is a practical contribution, though the pre-processing steps (segmentation, summarization) rely on standard methods.
* **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The paper includes quantitative metrics (BLEU, Recall) *and* user studies, demonstrating an effort to comprehensively evaluate the approach.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Lack of Novelty:** The core contribution lies in *combining* existing techniques. The CRCN architecture doesn't present a fundamentally new learning paradigm or algorithmic breakthrough. The coherence model, while present, builds directly upon prior work [1] without significant adaptation or improvement.
* **Limited Baselines:** While the authors compare against several baselines, these are largely single-sentence generation models extended to sequences. More relevant baselines would include methods specifically designed for sequential data, or alternative approaches to coherence modeling. The choice of baselines doesn't fully expose the weaknesses of the proposed approach.
* **Data Dependence:** The reliance on blog data introduces potential biases. Blog authors curate image-text pairings with a specific narrative in mind, which may not generalize to arbitrary image streams. The pre-processing steps (LSA summarization) also inject specific assumptions about text structure.
* **Overstated Claims:** The claim of being “the first” to address the image stream to sentence sequence problem feels overstated. While perhaps the first to formulate it exactly as presented, related work in video captioning [23, 32] already addresses sequential visual data and natural language generation. The distinction primarily lies in the nature of the input (videos vs. image streams), not the underlying problem.
* **Insufficient Ablation Study:** While the paper compares different variants of the CRCN, the ablation study feels limited. A more detailed analysis of the contribution of each component (CNN, BRNN, coherence model) would be beneficial. How much does each component contribute to overall performance?
* **Qualitative Examples:** While present, the qualitative examples in Fig. 3 aren't compelling enough to demonstrate a significant advantage over simpler approaches.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a relatively complete system, incorporating multiple components and evaluation metrics.
* The dataset creation effort is valuable.
* The user study results, while not overwhelming, provide some evidence of user preference.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The lack of significant novelty and theoretical contribution.
* The reliance on well-established techniques without substantial innovation.
* The potential biases introduced by the data source and pre-processing steps.
* The limited comparison to directly relevant baselines.
* The overstated claims regarding the originality of the work.

**Overall Assessment:**

I find this paper to be a solid engineering effort but lacking the originality and impact expected of a NIPS publication. The combination of existing techniques, while functional, does not represent a significant advance in the field. The reliance on blog data and the limited ablation study further weaken the contribution. While the evaluation is thorough, it doesn't demonstrate a compelling advantage over potentially simpler approaches.

**Recommendation:** Reject. The paper is a reasonable contribution to a more applied conference but does not meet the bar for NIPS. I would encourage the authors to focus on developing more novel components or exploring alternative data sources and evaluation methodologies.
