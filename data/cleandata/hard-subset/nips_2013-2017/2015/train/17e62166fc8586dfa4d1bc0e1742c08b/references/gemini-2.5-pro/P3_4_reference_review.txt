This paper addresses the novel task of generating a coherent sequence of sentences to describe a stream of images. The authors present this as a logical and important extension of the single-image captioning problem, moving towards a more holistic form of visual storytelling. The primary contribution is a multimodal architecture, the Coherent Recurrent Convolutional Network (CRCN), which learns to score the compatibility between an image stream and a text sequence. The CRCN integrates a CNN for image representation, a Bidirectional RNN on sentence vectors to model semantic flow, and an explicit entity-based coherence model to ensure linguistic fluency. Recognizing the lack of suitable datasets, the authors present a practical method for creating a large-scale corpus by mining and processing online blog posts. The model is trained using a max-margin objective function. The effectiveness of the approach is demonstrated through extensive experiments on two new blog-derived datasets, where it outperforms adapted state-of-the-art baselines and ablated versions of itself on both automatic metrics and human evaluations.

### Strengths

1.  **Significance and Originality:** The paper pioneers a new and compelling problem. The shift from single images to image streams is a significant step forward for the vision-and-language community, with clear applications in areas like photo album summarization and automated content creation. To my knowledge, this is one of the first works to formalize and provide a strong baseline for the "image stream to paragraph" problem.
2.  **Well-Motivated Model Architecture:** The proposed CRCN model is technically sound and thoughtfully designed. A key strength is the explicit modeling of both semantic content flow (via the BRNN) and linguistic coherence (via the entity grid). This decomposition acknowledges that a good narrative is more than just a sequence of relevant sentences; it must also be syntactically and referentially smooth. The integration of a classic, linguistically-motivated coherence model [1] into a modern deep learning framework is particularly novel and effective.
3.  **Pragmatic Data Collection Strategy:** The methodology for creating a dataset from raw blog posts is a significant practical contribution. This resourcefulness provides a scalable way to obtain the large-scale parallel data needed for this new task, overcoming a major barrier to entry for research in this direction.
4.  **Thorough and Convincing Evaluation:** The experimental validation is robust. The authors compare against strong baselines adapted from related work and perform crucial ablation studies (CRCN vs. RCN) that clearly isolate and validate the contribution of the coherence model. The inclusion of an Amazon Mechanical Turk user study is critical, as automatic metrics like BLEU are ill-suited for evaluating paragraph-level qualities. The experiment showing that the benefit of the coherence model increases with sequence length is an insightful result that strongly supports the paper's central thesis.

### Weaknesses and Areas for Improvement

1.  **Ambiguity in the Scoring Function:** The formulation of the compatibility score in Equation (3), `Skl = ∑t(skt · vlt + gk · vlt)`, is not well-justified. The term `gk` represents the coherence of the entire sentence sequence `k`, yet it is dotted with each individual image feature `vlt` and then summed. This suggests a local matching of a global text property, which is counter-intuitive. A more natural formulation might match the global coherence score with a global representation of the image stream (e.g., `mean(vlt)`). The authors should clarify the rationale for this design choice or confirm if it is a typo, as it is a point of potential technical confusion.
2.  **Retrieval-Based Nature and Its Limitations:** The paper frames the task as "generating" descriptions, but the method is entirely retrieval-based—it composes paragraphs by selecting existing sentences from the training corpus. This is a valid approach, but it cannot produce truly novel sentences to describe scenes or events not witnessed during training. This is a fundamental difference from the baselines ([9], [30]), which are generative at the word level. The paper would be more complete if it explicitly discussed the limitations inherent in a sentence-retrieval framework and positioned itself more clearly with respect to generative approaches.
3.  **Scalability of Inference:** The inference procedure requires a K-NN search for each image in the query stream across the entire training set, followed by a combinatorial beam search. The computational cost of this process is not discussed but could be prohibitive for very large-scale datasets or long image sequences. A brief analysis of the method's computational complexity or runtime would be a valuable addition.
4.  **Dependence on Pre-processing Heuristics:** The model's performance relies heavily on the quality of the upstream data processing pipeline (text segmentation and LSA-based summarization). Errors or biases from these heuristic-based steps are propagated directly into the training data. While the results are strong, the paper would be strengthened by a discussion of the potential impact of this noise on the learning process.

Despite these weaknesses, the paper presents a novel and important contribution to the field. The problem formulation is timely, the proposed model is elegant, and the experimental results are convincing. The weaknesses primarily concern points of clarification and acknowledging the limitations of the approach, which do not undermine the core contribution. This work opens up a promising new direction for research and is likely to have a significant impact.
