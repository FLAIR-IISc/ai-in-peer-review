

# Review of "Expressing an Image Stream with a Sequence of Natural Sentences"

## Summary
This paper proposes a novel approach for generating natural language descriptions for image streams (sequences of images) rather than single images, which the authors claim is a significant extension of the standard image captioning task. They introduce a multimodal architecture called Coherent Recurrent Convolutional Networks (CRCN) that integrates convolutional neural networks for image representation, bidirectional recurrent neural networks for sentence modeling, and a local coherence model for fluent transitions between sentences. The authors leverage blog posts as training data, using text segmentation and summarization to create image-sentence pairs. They evaluate their method on NYC and Disneyland blog datasets with over 20K blog posts and 140K images, comparing against several baselines.

## Strengths
The problem of generating coherent sentence sequences for image streams is indeed an interesting extension of standard image captioning. The use of blog posts as a source of natural text-image pairs is a clever idea. The authors provide a detailed technical description of their CRCN architecture and demonstrate competitive results on their proposed task. The user studies via Amazon Mechanical Turk provide valuable human evaluation of their method.

## Weaknesses

**1. Incorrect Claim of Unsupervised Learning:** The paper repeatedly claims "our approach is completely unsupervised" (e.g., in Introduction, Section 1, and Conclusion), which is factually incorrect. The method requires text-image pairs for training, making it supervised learning. This mischaracterization undermines the paper's credibility and must be corrected.

**2. Inadequate Comparison with Video Captioning Work:** The authors claim to be the first to address "expressing image streams with sentence sequences," but this is not true. Video captioning (e.g., Rohrbach et al., ICCV 2015; Xu et al., AAAI 2015) directly addresses generating sequences of sentences for sequences of images (video frames). The paper cites these works but fails to properly distinguish their approach from video captioning. The authors should clarify why their method is different from existing video captioning approaches, particularly since consecutive images in streams may have similar challenges as video frames.

**3. Formatting and Notation Inconsistencies:** The paper contains numerous formatting inconsistencies:
- "Fig.1" vs. "Fig. 1" throughout the text
- Inconsistent spacing in mathematical notation (e.g., "xft" vs. "x f t" in Equation 1)
- "K-nearest" vs. "K nearest" in section 3.5
- Inconsistent use of "vlt" (section 3.4) without proper definition
- "L2-distance" written as "`2-distance" in section 3.5

**4. Methodological Ambiguities:**
- The coherence model is described as "entity-based" but the paper fails to explain how it differs from the original Barzilay & Lapata (ACL 2008) model they cite.
- The data augmentation section (2.1) claims they use "top-κ highest ranked summary sentences" but then states "we let T ln to denote both the single summary sentence and κ augmented sentences," creating confusion about the training procedure.
- The paper claims "the coherence model is more demanded" because "consecutive images in the streams may show sharp changes of visual content," but this is equally true for video captioning, which the authors fail to address.

**5. User Study Limitations:** The user study (section 4.3) samples only 5 images per query, which is too short to meaningfully evaluate coherence (the authors acknowledge this limitation themselves). The paper claims coherence becomes more important for longer passages, but the user study doesn't properly test this by using longer queries as they suggest.

**6. Technical Details Omission:** The paper lacks sufficient detail about hyperparameters (e.g., learning rates, batch sizes) and computational requirements, making replication difficult. The claim that "joint learning outperforms learning the two terms with separate parameters" (section 3.3) lacks justification.

**7. Reference Formatting Issues:** The reference list contains multiple formatting errors, including missing publication years (e.g., "K. Papineni, S. Roukos, T. Ward, W.-J. Zhu, ACL, , "), inconsistent conference abbreviations, and missing page numbers.

## Conclusion
While the paper addresses an interesting extension of image captioning, it contains multiple significant technical and methodological flaws that undermine its contribution. The incorrect claim of "unsupervised learning" is particularly problematic, as it misrepresents the core approach. The paper also fails to adequately distinguish its work from existing video captioning research, which directly addresses similar problems. The numerous formatting inconsistencies and technical ambiguities further detract from the paper's quality.

Although the problem is interesting and the approach has some merit, the paper's fundamental errors and lack of proper comparison with relevant work make it unsuitable for publication at NIPS. I recommend rejection. The authors should first correct the supervised vs. unsupervised mischaracterization, properly compare with video captioning work, and address the technical ambiguities before resubmission.
