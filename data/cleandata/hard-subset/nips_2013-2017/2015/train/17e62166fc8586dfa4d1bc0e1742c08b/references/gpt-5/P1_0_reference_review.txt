Summary
This paper tackles the under-explored problem of describing an image stream with a coherent sequence of sentences. The proposed architecture, Coherent Recurrent Convolutional Networks (CRCN), combines: (i) CNN visual features (VGG fc7), (ii) a bidirectional RNN (over 300-d paragraph vectors of sentences) to model sentence-level content flow, and (iii) an entity-grid local coherence model (Barzilay & Lapata) to encourage fluent sentence-to-sentence transitions. Training uses a max-margin alignment objective between image-sequence and sentence-sequence representations. At test time, the system retrieves sentence candidates by nearest-neighbor search over images and uses a beam/divide-and-conquer strategy to select a coherent paragraph. The paper constructs two large blog datasets (NYC and Disneyland) by segmenting blog posts into image–text pairs and summarizing text segments via LSA; doc2vec is used for sentence embeddings. CRCN (and an ablation without coherence, RCN) outperforms single-image captioning baselines (LBL variants, CNN+RNN, CNN+LSTM) on BLEU/CIDEr/METEOR and retrieval metrics, and is preferred in AMT user studies, with a widening gap for longer sequences.

Relation to prior work: The paper is positioned relative to retrieval-based captioning, multimodal RNNs for single images, and video-to-text methods, and to Kim et al. on sequence retrieval from paragraphs. The key novelty asserted is paragraph-level modeling with explicit local coherence on image streams (as opposed to video) and integration of coherence into a multimodal learning framework.

Strengths
- Problem relevance and originality: Extending image captioning to streams and emphasizing multi-sentence coherence is timely and important. The integration of an entity-grid coherence model with BRNN content flow is conceptually appealing.
- Data construction at scale: The blog-derived datasets are sizable, and the segmentation/summarization pipeline is explained.
- Modeling details: Clear description of the BRNN, coherence construction (entity grid, transitions), and joint mapping to match CNN feature space. Reasonable training choices (max-margin, RMSProp, dropout).
- Empirical results: Consistent improvements over baselines on language and retrieval metrics, plus user studies that specifically probe coherence benefits (especially for longer sequences). Ablations (1NN vs RCN vs CRCN) support the value of coherence.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Objective/design clarity: The combined score (Eq. 3) adds a global coherence vector g (mapped to 4096-D) to each per-sentence score via g·v_t at every position. Conceptually, coherence is a property of the sentence sequence; tying it to each image feature repeatedly seems ad hoc and risks acting as a global bias. A more principled coupling (e.g., sequence-level scalar coherence term, or coherence interacting with sentence representations, not directly with each image vector) would be preferable, and the current choice needs justification/ablation.
- Variable-length handling: The max-margin objective (Eq. 4) uses mismatched negative pairs that likely have different lengths, but the score Skl sums over t=1..N without specifying which N is used or how sequences of different length are handled (truncate, pad, or min length). This is a significant ambiguity that could affect both training and evaluation.
- Fairness of baselines: Most baselines are single-image captioning/generation methods concatenated across positions, with no paragraph-level modeling. Stronger sequence-aware baselines (e.g., training an LSTM/BRNN over sequences of image features to generate a paragraph) are not included. For the retrieval setting, comparing to a paragraph-level embedding model over image sequences (e.g., learned through sequence encoders) would be fairer.
- Retrieval vs generation mismatch: CRCN retrieves sentences; some baselines generate. Retrieval often scores better on n-gram metrics; this should be discussed, and a retrieval-based baseline without coherence but with the same candidate set would isolate the effect of coherence more cleanly (partly addressed by 1NN, but that lacks the sequence model).
- Coherence modeling details: The entity grid depends on coreference and syntactic parsing, which are error-prone on noisy blogs. No analysis of parser/coref quality or robustness is provided. Zero-padding the 64-d coherence vector to 300-d to fit the network is arbitrary; alternatives (projection layers) should be considered. No ablation on history length or the contribution of coherence under different sequence lengths beyond AMT is shown.
- Data/process transparency: Important implementation details are missing or ambiguous: whether doc2vec is trained on the full corpus (including test) or only training text; sensitivity to K, M, κ; quality of the LSA summarization (does the “ground truth” reflect what users would write?); constraints to prevent repeated/duplicate sentences; diversity mechanisms; runtime and complexity of the search. No code/data release is indicated.
- Claims of being “unsupervised”: The approach relies on aligned (heuristically) image-sentence pairs and learns supervised alignments; “unsupervised” is misleading.

Quality
Technically plausible overall, but key elements need clarification (length handling; justification of the coherence-to-image dot product; candidate search; doc2vec training regime). Empirical evidence is positive but baseline choices limit the strength of the claims. More rigorous ablations and controls would improve confidence.

Clarity
Generally well organized and readable. Equations and dimensions are mostly clear. However, several important details are underspecified (see above), and some design choices appear ad hoc without justification. The paper would benefit from a more explicit algorithmic description of training with variable-length pairs and the beam/divide-and-conquer search.

Originality
Moderate. The problem formulation is novel relative to single-image captioning, and integrating a classic entity-grid coherence model with deep multimodal learning is interesting. However, components (CNNs, BRNNs, doc2vec, entity grids, max-margin alignment) are standard; the novelty lies in their combination and application to image streams.

Significance
Potentially useful for “visual storytelling” and multi-image summarization. Results suggest gains over naive concatenation baselines and user preferences for longer sequences. Broader impact may depend on releasing the dataset and providing stronger sequence-aware baselines.

Questions for the authors
- How do you handle differing sequence lengths in Eq. (3)/(4) at training and testing?
- Why is coherence g combined via g·v_t at each time step? Did you try combining g with sentence representations only, or a separate sequence-level score?
- Is doc2vec trained only on training text? If not, please justify and report sensitivity.
- Do you enforce sentence diversity (e.g., no repeats) in retrieval? Any penalties for redundancy?
- Sensitivity to K, M, κ and to the history parameter in the entity grid?
- Any analysis of parser/coref errors and their effect on coherence?

Pros for acceptance
- Addresses an important, under-studied task; coherent multi-sentence description of image streams.
- Conceptually interesting integration of entity-based coherence with deep multimodal models.
- Sizable datasets; consistent improvements and supportive human evaluation.
- Clear ablation indicates added value of coherence, especially for longer sequences.

Cons for acceptance
- Ambiguities/concerns in the objective and training with variable-length sequences.
- Ad hoc design choices (coherence scoring, zero-padding) without sufficient justification/ablation.
- Baselines do not include strong sequence-aware alternatives; retrieval vs generation mismatch.
- Limited analysis and transparency of preprocessing components and robustness.

Recommendation
Borderline accept. The problem is important and the idea of marrying coherence modeling with multimodal sequence alignment is promising, with positive empirical evidence. However, the paper should clarify several technical aspects and strengthen baselines/ablations. If addressed, this could be a valuable contribution to sequence-level vision–language research.
