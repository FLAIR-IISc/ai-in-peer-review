This paper addresses the problem of generating a sequence of natural language sentences to describe a stream of images, a natural extension of single-image captioning. The core idea is to leverage blog posts as a noisy parallel corpus of images and text, and employ a coherent recurrent convolutional network (CRCN) architecture incorporating CNNs for image representation, RNNs for sentence modeling, and a local coherence model to ensure fluency. The approach is evaluated on NYC and Disneyland blog datasets with quantitative metrics and user studies.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** Extending image captioning to image *streams* is a natural and valuable direction. The problem itself is well-motivated and addresses a scenario common in real-world usage (e.g., photo albums from trips).
* **Technical Soundness:** The proposed CRCN architecture appears well-designed, integrating existing components (CNNs, RNNs) with a novel coherence model. The use of a local coherence model, given the potential for discontinuity between images in a stream, is a sensible approach.
* **Dataset & Experimental Setup:** Utilizing blog posts as a weakly supervised dataset is clever and practical, sidestepping the difficulty of creating large, perfectly aligned parallel corpora. The experimental setup, including quantitative metrics and user studies, is comprehensive.
* **Results:** The quantitative and qualitative results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed approach, outperforming baselines. The user study results, particularly the observation that coherence becomes more important with longer sequences, are insightful.
* **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The figures effectively illustrate the problem setup and the CRCN architecture. The ablation studies help understand the contribution of each component.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Dataset Quality & Bias:** While using blog posts is ingenious, it's crucial to acknowledge the inherent noise and potential biases. Blog authors select images and write text with specific intentions, which might not generalize well to all image streams.  The paper briefly mentions this, but a more thorough discussion of potential dataset limitations is needed. How does the quality of blog writing impact performance? Are certain topics better represented than others?
* **Coherence Model:** The description of the coherence model (section 3.2) is somewhat dense and lacks sufficient detail. It’s difficult to fully grasp how the entity grid and transition frequencies are calculated and incorporated into the overall scoring function. More intuitive explanations and visualizations would be helpful.  The reliance on parse trees introduces a potential point of failure (parsing errors) and adds computational complexity.
* **Baselines:** The baselines, while appropriate given the novelty of the task, are somewhat limited. Extending existing single-image captioning models to handle sequences isn't a particularly strong comparison. Investigating alternative approaches specifically designed for sequence generation (e.g., hierarchical models, transformers) would strengthen the evaluation.
* **Scalability:** The approximate search strategy described in section 3.5 is necessary given the computational complexity, but the details are a bit vague. How does the beam search parameter *M* impact performance? What is the trade-off between search accuracy and computational cost?
* **Minor Issues:** There are a few instances of awkward phrasing and minor grammatical errors throughout the paper.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper addresses a novel and important problem in multimodal learning.
* The proposed approach is technically sound and delivers strong empirical results.
* The use of a large-scale, weakly supervised dataset is a significant contribution.
* The paper is well-written and clearly explains the methodology and findings.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

* The limitations of the dataset (noise, bias) are not adequately discussed.
* The coherence model is complex and lacks sufficient clarity.
* The comparison to existing work could be stronger.
* Scalability concerns with the search strategy are not fully addressed.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of multimodal learning. The problem is well-motivated, the proposed approach is technically sound, and the results are promising. However, the weaknesses related to dataset quality, the coherence model's clarity, and the baseline comparison prevent me from giving a definitive acceptance.

**Recommendation:**

**Weak Reject.** I recommend rejection in its current form, but strongly encourage the authors to address the weaknesses outlined above. Specifically, a more thorough discussion of the dataset limitations, a clearer explanation of the coherence model, and a more comprehensive comparison to alternative sequence generation approaches would significantly strengthen the paper. If these issues are addressed, the paper would be a strong candidate for acceptance.



**Specific Suggestions:**

* **Dataset Analysis:** Perform a more detailed analysis of the blog dataset, quantifying the quality of writing, the diversity of topics, and potential biases.
* **Coherence Model Visualization:** Include visualizations of the entity grid and transition frequencies to help readers understand how the coherence model works.
* **Baseline Expansion:** Consider incorporating more sophisticated sequence generation models (e.g., transformer-based architectures) as baselines.
* **Scalability Analysis:** Provide a more detailed analysis of the scalability of the search strategy, including the impact of the beam search parameter *M* on performance and computational cost.
* **Address reviewer comments:** Carefully address all reviewer comments and provide detailed responses to any questions raised.
