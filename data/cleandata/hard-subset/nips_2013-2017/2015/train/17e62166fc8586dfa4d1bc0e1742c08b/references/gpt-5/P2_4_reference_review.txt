Summary
The paper tackles the problem of describing an image stream with a sequence of natural sentences. The proposed coherent recurrent convolutional network (CRCN) combines: (i) CNN image features (VGG fc7), (ii) a bidirectional RNN over sentence embeddings (doc2vec paragraph vectors) to model content flow, and (iii) an entity-grid-based local coherence model to encourage fluent sentence transitions. Training data are mined from blogs: posts are segmented and summarized (LSA) into image–sentence sequences; top-κ sentences per segment augment training. The model learns a max-margin ranking that scores compatibility between an image sequence and a sentence sequence. At test time, the method retrieves candidate sentences by nearest neighbor images per position and performs an approximate beam/divide-and-conquer search to select a coherent paragraph. Experiments on NYC and Disneyland blog datasets (20k posts, 140k images) show improvements over single-image captioning baselines and ablations, with both automatic metrics (BLEU/CIDEr/METEOR and retrieval metrics) and AMT user studies.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The overall pipeline is coherent and the ranking-based training objective is standard for multimodal retrieval. The BRNN design (separate forward/backward input transforms) is reasonable, and the entity-grid coherence model is an appropriate, established approach for local coherence.
- Evidence: The paper evaluates on two reasonably large datasets and reports multiple metrics plus user preference studies. The ablations (1NN, BRNN-only vs CRCN) support the contribution of each component, particularly the coherence term for longer sequences.
- Concerns:
  - Scoring function clarity: Skl = sum_t s_k,t · v_l,t + g_k · v_l,t adds the same global coherence vector g_k dotted with every image feature. This duplicates a global term N times and implicitly couples a text-only coherence descriptor to image features at each timestep. It would be cleaner to aggregate image features (e.g., mean-pool v_l,t) and use a single g_k · v̄_l term, or to make the coherence factor independent of image features (since coherence is text-internal). As written, there is a risk of length bias and an unclear semantic grounding of g in vision.
  - Length bias and negatives: Because scores are summed over t, sequences of different lengths may be favored. Authors state negatives are sampled randomly and often differ in length; an explicit normalization or length control would strengthen the claim.
  - Data construction noise: The heuristics for aligning text to images (distance-based assignment) and summarization (LSA) can introduce misalignments. There is no analysis of alignment quality or its impact on performance.
  - Limited baselines on multi-sentence coherence: All strong baselines are single-image captioners concatenated per position. It would help to compare against text-only coherence re-ranking (e.g., an LSTM/biLM or entity-grid re-ranker over concatenations) to isolate the benefit of the entity-grid vs. a learned neural language model of paragraph coherence.
  - Reproducibility gaps: Some implementation details are underspecified (doc2vec training specifics, vocabulary handling, candidate generation timing/complexity, dataset release). No statistical significance tests are reported.

Clarity
- The paper is generally understandable, but several notational and architectural details need tightening:
  - Eqn. (2) and the role of [O|q] → [S|g] could be clarified: why zero-pad the 64-d coherence vector to 300 rather than a learned projection? Why share the same FC parameters for O and q, and what is the intuition?
  - The scoring equation (Eqn. 3) should explicitly justify the g term and address length normalization.
  - “Unsupervised” is overstated; the method is weakly supervised from automatically aligned text–image streams.
  - The retrieval search (beam/divide-and-conquer) description lacks complexity estimates and failure cases; a brief algorithmic outline with M, K, N dependence would help.
- Minor language issues and overloaded notation (e.g., T^l_n used for both a single sentence and a set of κ sentences) could be cleaned up.

Originality
- The task—mapping an image stream to a coherent sentence sequence—is a natural and underexplored extension beyond single-image captioning and video sentence generation at the time. Using an entity-grid coherence model in a multimodal ranking framework is a reasonably novel combination, though each component is standard.
- Relative to prior work: The paper differentiates from video captioning [5, 23, 32] and image-sentence retrieval/generation [6, 8, 9, 12, 21, 26, 30]. The closest is modeling sequences (e.g., Donahue et al.’s LRCN), but CRCN uniquely targets multi-sentence coherence. Citing and contrasting to text-only multi-sentence generation/coherence re-ranking would make the novelty clearer.

Significance
- Practical significance: The paper shows consistent improvements in automatic metrics and AMT preferences, particularly on longer streams, suggesting the coherence modeling matters. Mining large-scale blog data is potentially valuable.
- Limitations: The approach is retrieval-based, so it cannot generate truly novel sentences; its generalization beyond travel blogs is claimed but not demonstrated. Without releasing datasets or code, community impact may be limited.

Pros
- Clear, relevant problem formulation; first steps toward coherent multi-sentence descriptions of image streams.
- Sensible architecture combining visual features, contextual language modeling (BRNN), and explicit local coherence.
- Comprehensive evaluation across two sizable datasets, multiple metrics, ablations, and human studies; coherence benefit increases with sequence length.

Cons
- Scoring/design choices around the global coherence term and length normalization are questionable and under-justified.
- Heavily heuristic data construction without quality analysis; potential noise not quantified.
- Baselines do not include stronger paragraph-level language/coherence models; retrieval-only setup limits generative capability.
- Some clarity and reproducibility issues.

Questions for authors
1) Please justify the g_k · v_l,t term and discuss length normalization and potential biases. Have you tried a single g_k · v̄_l or a text-only coherence prior?
2) How sensitive are results to κ in sentence augmentation, K in candidate images, and beam size M? Provide an ablation.
3) Can you report an alignment quality estimate for the blog preprocessing, or a robustness analysis to misalignment?
4) Did you compare the entity-grid coherence against a neural paragraph LM (e.g., LSTM) re-ranker?

Recommendation: Weak accept. The problem is timely and relevant, the integration of coherence into multimodal sequence retrieval is interesting, and empirical results and user studies are encouraging. Addressing the scoring design and clarity/reproducibility concerns would strengthen the paper.
