Summary
The paper addresses generating a sequence of natural sentences for a stream of images (e.g., travel photo blogs). The proposed coherent recurrent convolutional network (CRCN) combines: (i) CNN (VGG) image features; (ii) a BRNN over sentence embeddings (doc2vec) to model forward/backward textual context; and (iii) an entity-grid-based local coherence model (Barzilay & Lapata) to encourage fluent sentence transitions. Training data are derived automatically from blogs: paragraphs are segmented to images via proximity heuristics and summarized to key sentences via LSA; sentences are embedded with doc2vec; coherence features are computed from parsed trees. The model learns a max-margin compatibility between an image sequence and a candidate sentence sequence; at test time, for each image position the KNN training images’ sentences form a candidate pool, and a beam/divide-and-conquer search selects a high-scoring sequence. Experiments on two travel-blog datasets (NYC, Disneyland; ~20K posts, 140K images) report improvements over adapted single-image captioning baselines (Kiros et al., Karpathy et al., Vinyals et al.) and over model variants, using BLEU/CIDEr/METEOR, retrieval metrics, and AMT user studies.

Strengths
- Problem setting (image stream to multi-sentence text) is interesting and underexplored compared to single-image captioning; leveraging discourse coherence is a reasonable angle.
- Integration of an entity-grid coherence signal with a sequential text encoder is novel within vision-language methods of that era.
- Use of large, in-the-wild blog data is appealing; the pipeline for extracting weakly aligned image–sentence sequences is clearly described.
- Ablations (CRCN vs. BRNN-only vs. nearest-neighbor) and AMT studies attempt to isolate the contribution of coherence, especially for longer sequences.

Weaknesses
- Methodological soundness: the core scoring function Skl = Σt s_k,t·v_l,t + g_k·v_l,t raises concerns. The global coherence vector gk is dotted with every image feature and summed over t, effectively scaling with sequence length and duplicating the same global signal across positions. No length normalization is used, so the margin objective may favor longer sequences. This is a substantial modeling flaw that should be addressed (e.g., average over t, or separate a global term once per sequence).
- “End-to-end” and “unsupervised” claims are overstated. The approach is not end-to-end in the sense of jointly learning image and language encoders with ground-truth supervision; it relies on heuristic segmentation/summarization and fixed VGG features. The supervision is weak but still supervision. Please clarify and temper claims.
- Evaluation design is unclear and potentially unfair:
  - Language metrics (BLEU/CIDEr/METEOR) compare retrieved training sentences to LSA-summarized test sentences; given lexical mismatch and summarization noise, these metrics may poorly reflect coherence/adequacy. The paper itself notes limited sensitivity of these metrics.
  - Retrieval metrics (R@K, median rank): it is not clearly defined what the “ground-truth” is when train/test are disjoint and candidates are drawn from training sentences. If the exact ground-truth sequence is not among candidates, how are recall/ranks computed? This needs precise definition; otherwise the reported retrieval gains are hard to interpret.
  - Baseline fairness: most baselines are generative single-image caption models adapted by concatenation; the proposed method is retrieval-based with candidate reranking. A stronger and fairer comparison would include retrieval-based, sequential reranking baselines (e.g., CRF/HMM over candidate sentences with learned transition potentials, or a simple bigram/co-reference coherence scorer), and a KNN+learned pairwise transition model without the entity grid.
- Coherence modeling details are brittle: the entity grid depends on reliable coreference and syntactic roles in noisy blog text; there is no analysis of coref quality or its impact. The 64-dim transition vector is zero-padded to 300 and ReLUed, then mixed with BRNN outputs via shared FC layers—a rather ad hoc choice. A principled projection or separate parameterization with normalization would be preferable. No ablation on history length, coref on/off, or normalization is provided.
- Search and optimality: the divide-and-conquer beam with M=50 is justified qualitatively (“almost optimal”) without empirical evidence (e.g., exact search on short sequences, sensitivity to K/M). Runtime/complexity is not reported.
- Data construction and reproducibility: heavy reliance on heuristic segmentation and LSA summarization, yet no quantitative assessment of alignment quality. Doc2vec training specifics are sparse; doc2vec is sensitive to hyperparameters. Datasets appear proprietary (and include copyrighted blogs), with no commitment to release code/data. This limits reproducibility and validation.
- Claims of novelty (“first to address”) should be carefully positioned relative to prior work on photo stream summarization and video-to-multisentence description; related work is cited but the distinction could be sharpened.

Quality
Given the above modeling issues (length bias, ad hoc fusion, unclear retrieval metrics) and incomplete ablations/controls, I am not convinced the empirical gains are robust or that the coherence component is properly validated. The paper would benefit from tighter methodology and clearer evaluation.

Clarity
Writing is generally clear, but several critical definitions are ambiguous (retrieval metrics ground-truth; exact search procedure; training/fine-tuning of CNN; doc2vec settings). Some notation abuses (e.g., T_ln for both single and κ sentences) hinder precision.

Originality
Moderate. The combination of BRNN with an entity-grid coherence model for sequence retrieval is a novel synthesis, but each component is known. The search strategy and training loss largely follow prior multimodal retrieval work.

Significance
Potentially useful if made sound and reproducible, as multi-image narrative generation is an important application. As is, the methodological and evaluation weaknesses limit confidence that the approach advances the state of the art.

Suggestions for improvement
- Fix scoring to eliminate length bias; justify the role of the global coherence term and consider using it once per sequence or as a prior on transitions.
- Precisely define retrieval metrics and ensure the ground-truth is in the candidate set or use alternative sequence-level similarity measures; report statistical significance.
- Add strong retrieval-based sequential baselines (e.g., CRF over sentence candidates with simple lexical/semantic transition features).
- Provide ablations: with/without coref, varying history length, effect of K and beam size, exact search on short sequences, and sensitivity to doc2vec settings.
- Evaluate coherence with established coherence metrics and targeted human questions (local ordering, referential consistency), not just overall preference.
- Clarify whether CNN is fine-tuned; if not, adjust claims about end-to-end training; consider modern sentence encoders or LSTM for language modeling to avoid ReLU RNN instability.
- Release code and, if possible, a de-identified dataset and preprocessing scripts.

Recommendation: Reject

Confidence: High
