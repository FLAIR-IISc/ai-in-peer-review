## Review of “Expressing an Image Stream with a Sequence of Natural Sentences”

This paper attempts to extend image captioning to the realm of image *streams*, generating a sequence of sentences to describe a collection of images, motivated by scenarios like travel photos. The authors propose a Coherent Recurrent Convolutional Network (CRCN) integrating CNNs for image representation, BRNNs for language modeling, and a local coherence model to promote fluent transitions between sentences. The approach is trained on a dataset constructed from blog posts, and evaluated using standard metrics (BLEU, Recall) and a user study.

While the motivation – extending captioning to sequences – is reasonable, and the problem itself is potentially interesting, this paper suffers from a lack of compelling novelty and insufficient validation. The proposed CRCN appears largely as a straightforward combination of existing techniques, and the central claim of significant improvement stemming from the “coherence model” is not convincingly demonstrated. 

The core weakness lies in the incremental nature of the contribution. The paper frames its work as "the first to address the problem" which feels disingenuous. Existing video description work (acknowledged in the related work) *already* tackles sequential image understanding and description, and the differences highlighted – the nature of the transitions between frames/images – do not seem to warrant a fundamentally new architecture. The “coherence model” leverages established techniques from discourse analysis, and its integration feels ad-hoc rather than theoretically driven. The authors state the model is "completely unsupervised", however, the use of blog posts as a training corpus *inherently* introduces biases and assumptions about image-text alignment crafted by bloggers - this isn't unsupervised learning in the traditional sense.

Methodologically, the experimental setup is problematic. The chosen datasets – NYC and Disneyland blogs – are highly domain-specific. While the authors claim general applicability, the performance gains might be heavily tied to the storytelling nature of travel blogs. The comparison against baselines is also weak. Extending existing single-image captioning models to handle sequences, as done here, is a reasonable approach, but the implementation details are sparse, making it difficult to assess the fairness of the comparison. The ablation studies (testing CRCN against variants without the coherence model) are informative, but the magnitude of the improvement, while statistically significant, is relatively small and doesn't justify the architectural complexity. The retrieval-based approach (selecting sentences from the training data) is a significant limitation, and the paper doesn't thoroughly address the potential for generating repetitive or unoriginal descriptions. The approximate search strategy introduced to manage computational complexity raises concerns about potential sub-optimality.

The writing is generally clear, but overly verbose. The paper could benefit from more concise explanations and a stronger focus on the key technical contributions. The figures are helpful, but could be improved with clearer labeling and visual highlighting of the key components. 

**Strengths:**

*   The problem is intuitively understandable and potentially relevant.
*   The authors provide a comprehensive experimental evaluation, including quantitative metrics and a user study.
*   The architecture is relatively straightforward to understand.

**Weaknesses:**

*   Lack of substantial novelty; the work primarily combines existing techniques.
*   Limited theoretical justification for the architecture, particularly the coherence model.
*   Domain-specific datasets raise concerns about generalizability.
*   Weak baseline comparisons.
*   Retrieval-based approach limits the creativity and originality of the generated descriptions.
*   Verbose writing.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper addresses a potentially interesting extension of image captioning.
*   The experimental results demonstrate some improvement over baseline methods.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   The technical contributions are incremental and lack significant novelty.
*   The evaluation is not sufficiently rigorous to support the claims of improved performance.
*   The retrieval-based approach is a significant limitation.
*   The domain specificity of the datasets raises concerns about generalizability.



**Overall Assessment:**

Reject. This paper, while technically sound, doesn't present a compelling advance in the field. The proposed approach is largely a combination of existing techniques, and the claimed improvements are modest and potentially tied to the specific datasets used. The paper lacks the originality and significance required for acceptance at NIPS. The authors should consider focusing on a more novel aspect of the problem or conducting a more thorough evaluation with a wider range of datasets and baselines.
