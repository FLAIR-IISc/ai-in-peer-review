## Review of "Expressing an Image Stream with a Sequence of Natural Sentences"

**Summary:**

This paper tackles the novel problem of generating a coherent sequence of sentences to describe a stream of images, moving beyond the more common single-image-single-sentence captioning. The authors propose a Coherent Recurrent Convolutional Network (CRCN) which integrates CNNs for image representation, BRNNs for sentence modeling, and a local coherence model to ensure fluency between sentences. They leverage blog posts with associated images as a training corpus and demonstrate improved performance over several baselines using both quantitative metrics (BLEU, Recall) and user studies.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The problem itself – generating descriptions for *sequences* of images – is a natural extension of image captioning and under-explored.  This is a clear contribution.
* **Technical Approach:** The CRCN architecture is well-motivated. The inclusion of a coherence model is particularly insightful, addressing a key challenge in sequential description generation.  Integrating existing techniques (CNNs, BRNNs) with a novel coherence component is a reasonable and effective strategy.
* **Dataset:** Utilizing blog posts as a large-scale, weakly-supervised dataset is a clever solution to the data scarcity problem that plagues this type of research. The pre-processing steps are reasonably well described.
* **Empirical Evaluation:** The paper provides a thorough empirical evaluation, including comparisons to several baselines, quantitative metrics, and importantly, user studies using AMT.  The user studies are particularly valuable for assessing the quality of generated text, which can be hard to capture with automated metrics alone.
* **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The architecture is clearly explained with a helpful diagram (Fig. 2).

**Weaknesses:**

* **Baseline Limitations:** While several baselines are included, they are all adapted single-image captioning models.  More direct competitors tackling sequence-to-sequence generation (even if from other fields like video captioning) would provide a stronger benchmark. The authors acknowledge this, but it limits the strength of their comparison.
* **Coherence Model Complexity:** The description of the local coherence model is somewhat dense and could benefit from more detailed explanation and intuition.  It's not immediately clear how the entity grid and transition frequencies translate to improved coherence.
* **Data Bias:** The use of blog posts introduces a potential bias towards a specific writing style (travel blogs in this case). The authors acknowledge the applicability to other domains but haven't demonstrated this empirically.  The performance on other datasets would be valuable.
* **Limited Ablation Study:** While they compare with and without the coherence model, a more detailed ablation study exploring the contribution of different components within the CRCN (e.g., BRNN vs. RNN) would be useful.
* **Qualitative Examples:** The qualitative examples (Fig. 3) are helpful, but more examples, and a discussion of failure cases, would strengthen the analysis.



**Addressing the Review Criteria:**

* **Quality:** The paper is technically sound.  The proposed architecture is well-defined, and the experimental results support the claims. The max-margin loss function is a standard approach and appropriate for the task. The experiments demonstrate a statistically significant improvement over the baselines.
* **Clarity:** The paper is generally clear.  However, as mentioned above, the coherence model could be explained in more detail.  The notation is generally consistent, and the figures are helpful.
* **Originality:** The problem statement and the proposed architecture are novel.  The authors adequately cite related work, although a broader search for sequence-to-sequence models outside of image captioning might be beneficial.
* **Significance:** This work has the potential to be significant.  The ability to automatically describe image streams has applications in various areas, including personal photo organization, robotics, and accessibility.  The results suggest a promising direction for future research in this area.



**Arguments for and against acceptance:**

**Pro:**

* Addresses a novel and important problem.
* Proposes a technically sound and well-motivated approach.
* Demonstrates strong empirical results with both quantitative metrics and user studies.
* Leverages a creative solution to the data scarcity problem.

**Con:**

* Baselines are not ideal and limit the strength of the comparison.
* Coherence model description is somewhat complex.
* Potential data bias from using blog posts.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a solid contribution to the field and is **above the acceptance threshold for NIPS**.  The novelty of the problem, the soundness of the technical approach, and the strong empirical evaluation outweigh the weaknesses.  However, I would recommend the authors address the weaknesses raised in this review (particularly providing a more detailed explanation of the coherence model, considering stronger baselines, and performing a more thorough ablation study) before final acceptance.  I would rate this paper as a **Strong Accept** with minor revisions.
