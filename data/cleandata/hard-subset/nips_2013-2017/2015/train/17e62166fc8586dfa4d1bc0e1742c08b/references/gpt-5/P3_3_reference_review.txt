Summary and context
This paper addresses the problem of describing an image stream with a sequence of natural sentences. The proposed coherent recurrent convolutional network (CRCN) combines: (i) CNN (VGG fc7) image embeddings, (ii) a bidirectional RNN over sentence representations (doc2vec paragraph vectors) to model sequence-level textual context, and (iii) an entity-grid based local coherence model (Barzilay & Lapata) to encourage fluent sentence-to-sentence transitions. Training uses weakly aligned image–text sequences obtained from blogs via heuristic paragraph-based segmentation and LSA-based summarization; the model is trained with a max-margin ranking loss to align image streams to sentence sequences. At test time, the system retrieves K candidate sentences per image via nearest neighbor in CNN space and assembles full sequences using a beam/divide-and-conquer strategy, scoring with CRCN. Evaluation on two blog datasets (NYC, Disneyland) uses BLEU/CIDEr/METEOR and retrieval metrics, plus AMT user studies; CRCN outperforms single-image captioning baselines and an ablated BRNN-only model (RCN), with larger gains on longer sequences.

Relative to prior work, this extends single-image captioning/retrieval [Karpathy & Fei-Fei; Vinyals et al.; Kiros et al.] and video description [Donahue et al.] to sequence-to-sequence (images→sentences) with an explicit coherence model. It also relates to Kim et al.’s image-sequence retrieval from paragraph queries but tackles the opposite direction.

Strengths
- Problem formulation: Moving from single-image/single-sentence to image-stream/paragraph is timely and valuable, especially for consumer photo streams. The explicit focus on discourse coherence is novel within vision–language at the time.
- Architecture: Sensible modular integration of CNN embeddings, BRNN context over sentences, and entity-grid coherence; the entity-grid injection is interesting and underexplored in multimodal settings.
- Data: Mining large-scale weakly aligned blog streams is practical and likely to generalize; the preprocessing pipeline is clearly described.
- Empirical evidence: Consistent improvements over strong baselines; user studies indicate perceived gains, particularly on longer sequences—supporting the coherence motivation.
- Ablations: Comparing 1NN, RCN, and CRCN helps isolate the benefit of coherence modeling.

Weaknesses and points needing clarification
- Not truly generative: The method retrieves and assembles existing sentences rather than generating novel language. That is fine if presented as retrieval, but parts of the paper and claims of “authoring” could be clearer. The “completely unsupervised” claim is misleading; the model is trained with weak supervision from aligned blog image–text pairs, plus pre-trained doc2vec and fixed parsers.
- Scoring formulation issues: The sequence score Skl = sum_t s_k_t·v_l_t + g_k·v_l_t adds the same coherence vector g_k against each image v_l_t. This double-counts coherence N times and is independent of sentence position. It likely introduces a length bias (longer sequences accrue larger scores) and conflates global coherence with per-image compatibility. Why not length-normalize (e.g., average over t) and/or use g_k once per sequence? A principled justification or ablation is needed.
- Coherence model integration: The entity grid creation requires reliable coreference and role labeling; the paper mentions parses but does not document how coreference chains are constructed (did you use Stanford CoreNLP coref? mention detection? thresholding?). Zero-padding the 64-d grid to 300-d before an FC stack is ad hoc; why not a learned projection? The “end-to-end” claim is weak given non-differentiable preprocessing (doc2vec trained separately, parsing/coref/grids fixed, CNN likely frozen).
- Evaluation clarity: The retrieval metrics (R@K, median rank) are not clearly defined in this setting. Since test ground-truth sentences are not in training, what is the “ground-truth” item being retrieved? If the task is image-sequence→text-sequence retrieval over a candidate bank of training paragraphs, then the ground-truth test paragraph is not retrievable. Please clarify candidate pools, whether evaluation is within-test or train-test cross retrieval, and guard against trivial near-duplicate leakage across splits. Given the known limitations of BLEU etc. for paragraphs, reporting entity-grid coherence metrics or entity transition distributions would strengthen the case.
- Search and complexity: The divide-and-conquer beam search is only sketched. How is the coherence computed for partial sequences during search? Does the coherence signal span boundaries when merging subproblems? What are the actual candidate sizes, runtimes, and K/M sensitivity?
- Baselines and ablations: Useful baselines are included, but two missing comparisons would help: (i) a sentence-level retrieval baseline with a simple Markov/lexical cohesion model (e.g., bigram over sentence embeddings) to isolate the unique contribution of the entity grid; (ii) a variant with length-normalized scoring. Ablate doc2vec vs averaging GloVe or a word-level BiLSTM encoder to show the choice of paragraph vectors is not a bottleneck.
- Domain specificity: Both datasets are tourism-centric; generalization to other domains is claimed but not demonstrated. At minimum, show cross-domain transfer (train on Disneyland, test on NYC, and vice versa).
- Reporting and statistics: Tables are referenced but not shown here; please include statistical significance for AMT (binomial tests, effect sizes) and confidence intervals for automatic metrics.

Quality
The approach is technically plausible and the components are standard. However, the scoring design (global coherence term added per time step) and the evaluation definitions raise concerns about correctness and interpretability. More rigorous analysis (length normalization, alternative g_k aggregation, sensitivity) is needed. The claim of end-to-end learning should be tempered.

Clarity
Generally well organized with reasonable detail, but crucial parts (coherence pipeline, evaluation protocol, search) need clearer specification. Tighten the description of supervision vs. “unsupervised,” and explicitly state which components are trained/frozen.

Originality
Moderately novel. The problem setting and the integration of an entity-grid coherence model with a multimodal retrieval architecture are original contributions relative to contemporaneous image captioning/video description work.

Significance
Potentially impactful for visual storytelling and summarization. If evaluation and formulation issues are addressed, the paper could spur more research on discourse-level modeling in vision–language.

Pros
- Addresses a meaningful and underexplored sequence-to-sequence vision–language task
- Integrates discourse coherence into multimodal modeling
- Large-scale weakly aligned dataset; practical pipeline
- Gains on human preference, especially for long sequences

Cons
- Retrieval-only, not generative; “unsupervised” claim overstated
- Questionable scoring design with likely length bias
- Coherence pipeline underspecified and not end-to-end
- Evaluation protocol and metrics unclear; limited domains

Questions for authors
1) How exactly are R@K and median rank computed given disjoint train/test? What is the candidate set and the ground-truth item? 2) Did you length-normalize Skl or try using g_k once per sequence? Please report the effect. 3) Do you perform coreference resolution (which system/settings)? How are entities extracted and linked? 4) Are CNN features fixed or fine-tuned? Is doc2vec trained only on training text? 5) What are the average sequence lengths and runtime of the search? Sensitivity to K and M? 6) Can you provide a baseline with a simple cohesion prior (e.g., sentence embedding bigrams) and a domain-transfer test?

Recommendation
Borderline reject. The problem and high-level idea are compelling, but the current formulation and evaluation leave important questions unresolved. With clearer evaluation, stronger analysis of the scoring and coherence integration, and additional ablations, this could become a solid contribution. My confidence is medium.
