

# Review of "Expressing an Image Stream with a Sequence of Natural Sentences"

This paper introduces a novel problem of generating a sequence of natural language sentences from an image stream, extending the conventional image captioning task from single images to sequences of images and from single sentences to sequences of sentences. The authors propose a multimodal architecture called Coherent Recurrent Convolutional Networks (CRCN) that integrates convolutional neural networks for image representation, bidirectional recurrent neural networks for sentence modeling, and a local coherence model based on entity transitions to ensure fluent sentence sequences. The authors leverage blog posts as a source of text-image parallel data, as blogs typically contain sequences of images with associated descriptive text that naturally aligns with the problem setup.

The paper builds upon several key lines of previous work. It extends the image captioning literature (e.g., Karpathy et al., 2015; Vinyals et al., 2015) by addressing sequential output generation rather than single-sentence generation. It also relates to the work on text-image retrieval (e.g., Kiros et al., 2014; Hodosh et al., 2013), but differs by focusing on sequential output generation rather than retrieval. The coherence model draws inspiration from Barzilay and Lapata (2008) but applies it specifically to the problem of sentence sequence generation from image streams. The paper effectively situates its contribution within the existing literature and clearly articulates how it differs from prior work.

**Quality**: The paper demonstrates strong technical quality. The CRCN architecture is well-motivated and properly integrated, with the coherence model explicitly addressing a gap in previous multimodal approaches. The authors provide a thorough experimental evaluation, including both quantitative metrics (BLEU, CIDEr, METEOR, top-K recall) and user studies via Amazon Mechanical Turk. The ablation studies (comparing CRCN with variants like RCN and 1NN) provide compelling evidence for the contribution of each component. The dataset construction process from blog posts is detailed and well-justified, with 20K blog posts and 140K images across two domains (NYC and Disneyland). The paper is technically sound, with appropriate theoretical grounding for the model components.

**Clarity**: The paper is well-organized and clearly written. The problem statement is effectively motivated with a concrete example (NYC visit). The architecture is explained with helpful figures and equations. The experimental setup, including dataset details and baseline comparisons, is described thoroughly. The supplementary material appears to provide sufficient details for reproducibility. The writing is accessible to readers familiar with multimodal learning, though some technical details about the coherence model could be clarified (e.g., why the specific 4^3 transition patterns were chosen).

**Originality**: The paper makes a strong claim of novelty in addressing the image stream to sentence sequence problem, which the authors convincingly argue is underexplored in current literature. The integration of the local coherence model with multimodal networks for sequence generation represents a meaningful extension beyond prior work on single-sentence generation. The use of blog posts as a source of text-image parallel data is also novel for this specific problem setup. While the individual components (CNNs, RNNs) are not new, the combination and application to this specific problem is original.

**Significance**: The problem addressed has practical relevance for applications like automatically generating narratives from photo albums or travelogues. The paper demonstrates significant improvements over baselines (with up to 15% relative improvement in retrieval metrics), and the user studies confirm that human annotators prefer the generated sequences. The unsupervised nature of the approach and its applicability to any domain with blog-style content enhances its potential impact. However, the paper could strengthen its significance argument by discussing broader applications beyond tourism blogs.

**Strengths**:
- Clear problem statement with well-motivated real-world application
- Novel integration of coherence model for sequence generation
- Thorough evaluation with multiple metrics and user studies
- Detailed dataset construction process using blog posts
- Strong ablation studies demonstrating component contributions
- Good comparison with relevant baselines

**Weaknesses**:
- The problem's significance could be better justified beyond tourism examples
- The coherence model's specific design choices (e.g., 4^3 transition patterns) lack deeper justification
- The blog data collection process may introduce domain-specific biases
- The paper doesn't address scalability to very long image sequences
- Baseline comparisons are limited to extending single-sentence models rather than more sophisticated sequence models

**Arguments for acceptance**: The paper presents a novel problem in multimodal learning with a well-designed solution, thorough evaluation, and clear demonstration of advantages over baselines. The technical approach is sound, and the contribution is significant within the context of image captioning research.

**Arguments against acceptance**: While the problem is novel, its practical importance may be limited compared to more established image captioning tasks. The paper could strengthen its significance argument and provide more thorough justification for the coherence model's design.

Overall, this is a well-executed study that makes a meaningful contribution to the field of multimodal learning by extending image captioning to sequence generation with coherence modeling. The paper is technically sound, clearly written, and addresses an underexplored problem with practical relevance. The quality of the evaluation and the novelty of the approach make it a strong candidate for NIPS.
