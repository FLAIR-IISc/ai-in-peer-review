This paper addresses the novel problem of generating a sequence of natural language sentences to describe a stream of images. The authors motivate this as a natural extension of the popular single-image captioning task, reflecting how users often capture experiences in photo series. The proposed method, Coherent Recurrent Convolutional Networks (CRCN), is a multimodal architecture that integrates three key components: a Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) to extract visual features, a Bidirectional Recurrent Neural Network (BRNN) to model the flow of content between sentences, and an entity-based local coherence model to ensure grammatical and referential smoothness in the output paragraph. A significant aspect of this work is its resourceful use of online blog posts as a large-scale, weakly-supervised source of parallel image-stream and text-stream data. The method is evaluated on two new datasets (NYC and Disneyland) derived from blogs, demonstrating superior performance over adapted state-of-the-art single-image captioning methods through both automatic metrics and human studies.

This work builds upon the extensive literature in image captioning, particularly methods combining CNNs and RNNs (e.g., Karpathy et al., 2015; Vinyals et al., 2015). However, it distinguishes itself by moving from a single image-sentence pair to a sequence-to-sequence problem. The key novelty lies in explicitly modeling the coherence between generated sentences, a factor not typically addressed in single-sentence generation or even in video captioning, where visual content is often more continuous. The incorporation of the entity-grid coherence model from Barzilay & Lapata (2008) into a deep learning framework for this task is a central contribution.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Novel and Significant Problem:** The paper tackles a well-motivated and important problem that is a logical and practical next step for the vision-and-language community. Generating coherent, multi-sentence descriptions for photo albums has clear real-world applications and presents a more challenging research frontier than single-image captioning.

2.  **Sound and Well-Motivated Architecture:** The proposed CRCN model is a thoughtful combination of powerful and appropriate components. The use of a CNN for image features and a BRNN for text sequence modeling is standard and effective. The main architectural contribution—the integration of an explicit, entity-based coherence model—directly addresses the core challenge of making the output text read like a fluent paragraph rather than a disjointed list of captions. The justification for needing this model, due to potential sharp visual changes between consecutive photos, is convincing.

3.  **Creative Use of Data:** The strategy of using blog posts as a source of weakly-supervised training data is clever and resourceful. It allows the authors to assemble a large-scale dataset without expensive manual annotation. The described pre-processing pipeline (text segmentation and LSA-based summarization) is a practical approach to extracting aligned image-sentence sequences from unstructured web data.

4.  **Thorough and Convincing Evaluation:** The experimental evaluation is comprehensive. The authors compare their method against several strong baselines adapted from the image captioning literature. The use of a suite of automatic metrics (BLEU, CIDEr, METEOR) and retrieval metrics (R@K, median rank) provides a multi-faceted quantitative assessment. Crucially, the inclusion of an Amazon Mechanical Turk user study provides essential evidence of perceptual quality, which automatic metrics often fail to capture for generative tasks. The ablation study, comparing CRCN with RCN (without coherence) and a 1NN baseline, clearly demonstrates the value of both the BRNN and the coherence model.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

1.  **Retrieval vs. Generation:** The paper's framing could be more precise about the nature of the task being performed. The title "Expressing an Image Stream" and terms like "generate" suggest a generative process. However, the method is fundamentally retrieval-based: it selects and re-ranks pre-existing sentences from the training data. While this is a valid and powerful approach, the distinction is important. True generative models create novel sentences word-by-word. It would strengthen the paper to clarify this in the introduction and contributions, for example, by framing the problem as "retrieving and composing a descriptive paragraph" rather than "generating" one.

2.  **Handling of Combinatorial Search Space:** At test time, the method faces a combinatorial explosion of candidate sentence sequences (K^N). The paper proposes a divide-and-conquer strategy with beam search as an approximate solution. While this is a reasonable heuristic, the description is brief. A more detailed analysis would be beneficial. For instance, how sensitive is the final performance to the beam size (M)? A brief experiment showing performance saturation as M increases would add confidence that this approximation is not significantly harming the results.

3.  **Clarity on "Unsupervised" Nature:** The introduction states the approach is "completely unsupervised." This is slightly misleading. The model is trained in a supervised fashion using a max-margin objective on image-sentence sequence pairs. The "unsupervised" aspect is in the *data collection*, where raw blog posts are used without manual alignment. Using a more precise term like "weakly-supervised" or "trained on web-scraped data" would more accurately reflect the methodology.

4.  **Details of Data Pre-processing:** The blog pre-processing is a critical and non-trivial component of the pipeline. While the paper provides an overview, additional details would improve reproducibility. For example, how robust is the heuristic for associating text blocks with images? Errors in this initial alignment step could introduce significant noise into the training data. A brief discussion of the potential failure modes of this process and their impact would be welcome.

**Summary of Pro and Con Arguments:**

*   **Pro:** The paper introduces a novel and significant problem, proposes a sound and well-motivated architecture, makes clever use of web-scale data, and validates the approach with a thorough and convincing set of experiments.
*   **Con:** The framing could be clearer about the retrieval-based nature of the method. The treatment of the combinatorial search problem is heuristic and could benefit from more analysis. The claim of being "unsupervised" should be revised to "weakly-supervised."

**Decision:**

Overall, this is a high-quality paper that makes a solid contribution. It introduces a new, interesting, and challenging task to the community and proposes a strong baseline method with a compelling evaluation. The weaknesses identified are primarily related to clarity and detail rather than fundamental flaws in the methodology or conclusions. The work is original, technically sound, and its results are significant. I recommend acceptance.
