This paper addresses the novel and important task of generating a sequence of natural language sentences from a stream of images. The authors propose a model, Coherent Recurrent Convolutional Networks (CRCN), that extends upon recent work in single image-to-sentence generation. The core idea is to learn a mapping between an image sequence and a sentence sequence by jointly modeling visual features, sentence content flow, and discourse-level coherence. The model leverages a CNN for image features, a bidirectional RNN to model the sequence of sentences, and, crucially, incorporates an entity-based local coherence model to ensure the resulting paragraph is fluent and reads naturally. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach by creating two large-scale datasets from blog posts and showing superior performance against strong baselines through both automatic metrics and human evaluation.

### Strengths:

1.  **Problem Formulation:** The paper tackles a logical and significant next step in visual-language understanding. Moving from single images to image streams and from single sentences to coherent paragraphs is a challenging but highly relevant problem, with applications in automatic storytelling, trip summarization, and more. The authors' claim that this is the first work to address this specific sequence-to-sequence problem appears credible.

2.  **Novelty of the Approach:** The primary contribution is the integration of an explicit, entity-based coherence model [1] into a modern deep learning architecture for text generation. While CNN-RNN architectures have become standard for captioning, they typically model semantic flow implicitly. By incorporating a classic NLP model that explicitly tracks discourse entities and their grammatical roles, the CRCN architecture directly optimizes for a property (coherence) that is essential for multi-sentence text but often overlooked by purely neural models. This hybrid approach is a key strength.

3.  **Thorough Experimental Validation:** The evaluation is comprehensive and convincing.
    *   The creation of two large-scale datasets from blog posts is a valuable contribution in itself and a clever way to source weakly-supervised data for this task.
    *   The authors compare against a strong suite of state-of-the-art baselines ([9], [12], [30]), appropriately adapting them for the sequence-to-sequence task.
    *   The ablation study (CRCN vs. RCN vs. 1NN) clearly demonstrates the value of both the sequence modeling (RNN) and the explicit coherence model.
    *   The use of both standard language/retrieval metrics and a well-designed AMT user study provides a balanced view of performance. The finding that the coherence model's advantage grows with sequence length in the user study is a particularly strong piece of evidence supporting the paper's central claim.

4.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is well-written and easy to follow. The motivation is clearly laid out, the model is described in sufficient detail, and the experimental setup is sound. Figure 2 provides a helpful overview of the architecture.

### Weaknesses:

1.  **Clarity of the Scoring Function:** There is a significant point of confusion in the model's formulation, specifically in the compatibility score (Equation 3). The score is defined as `Skl = Σ(s_kt · v_lt + g_k · v_lt)`. Here, `g_k` is derived from the coherence model and represents a single vector for the entire sentence sequence `k`. It is unclear why this global coherence score `g_k` is dotted with *each* individual image feature `v_lt` in the stream `l` and then summed. This implies the coherence score is weighted by each image's content, which is not intuitive. A more natural formulation might involve a single dot product with a global representation of the image stream, or simply adding a global coherence term to the total score. This part of the model needs better justification or revision.

2.  **Data Pre-processing Pipeline:** The method for constructing the parallel corpus from blogs relies on a chain of heuristics: text segmentation based on image distance and text summarization using LSA. This pipeline could introduce considerable noise. For instance, LSA summarizes based on semantic content within the text block, but this summary is not guaranteed to be visually grounded in the associated image. The impact of potential misalignments or summarization errors on the learned model is not discussed.

3.  **Retrieval-Based Limitation:** The approach is fundamentally a retrieval-based system, constructing paragraphs by selecting and re-ordering sentences from the training corpus. While effective, this limits its generative power; it cannot produce truly novel sentences or describe scenes, objects, or compositions that are not well-represented in the training data. This is a common trade-off, but it would strengthen the paper to explicitly acknowledge this limitation in the discussion.

### Summary and Recommendation:

The paper presents a novel and well-executed study on generating descriptive paragraphs for image streams. The main strengths are the timely problem formulation, the clever integration of a classic coherence model into a deep neural network, and a thorough, multi-faceted evaluation that clearly demonstrates the proposed model's effectiveness. The weaknesses are primarily centered on a lack of clarity in one key equation of the model and the potential for noise in the data collection pipeline.

Despite these weaknesses, the strengths are significant. The paper makes a solid contribution by being the first to tackle this problem with a compelling model and strong empirical results. The ideas presented are likely to be influential for future work on visual storytelling and long-form multimodal generation.

**Arguments for acceptance:**
*   Addresses a novel and important problem.
*   Presents a novel architecture combining neural sequence models with an explicit discourse coherence model.
*   Evaluation is rigorous, with strong baselines, ablation studies, and human judgments.
*   Results convincingly demonstrate the value of the proposed components.

**Arguments for rejection:**
*   The formulation of the scoring function (Eq. 3) is confusing and not well-justified.
*   The model's performance may be sensitive to the heuristic-based data pre-processing pipeline, a point which is not analyzed.

I recommend **acceptance**. The contributions are significant, and the primary weakness regarding the scoring function is a point of clarification that could likely be addressed in a final version. The paper advances the state of the art in a demonstrable way on a challenging new task.
