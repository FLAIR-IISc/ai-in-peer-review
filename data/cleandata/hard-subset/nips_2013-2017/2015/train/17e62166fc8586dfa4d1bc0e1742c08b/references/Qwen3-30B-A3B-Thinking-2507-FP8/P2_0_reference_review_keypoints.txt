- Novel problem: generate a sequence of natural-language sentences to describe an image stream (vs. single-image captioning).  
- Proposed model: Coherent Recurrent Convolutional Networks (CRCN) combining CNNs, bidirectional RNNs, and a local coherence model.  
- Data: blog-derived datasets (~20K posts, ~140K images) from NYC and Disneyland used as image-text parallel data.  
- Evaluation: quantitative metrics (BLEU, CIDEr, METEOR, top-K recall) plus Amazon Mechanical Turk user studies.  
- Results: CRCN yields consistent improvements over strong baselines with statistically significant retrieval gains.  
- Human preference: AMT studies show annotators prefer CRCN-generated sequences, especially for longer streams.  
- Coherence model impact: primarily improves ranking/coherence between sentences; only modest gains in language metrics versus RCN.  
- Ablation gap: insufficient ablation studies to isolate the specific contribution of each model component.  
- Clarity: paper is well-written and organized; methodology and figures are clear; coherence model description is technical but understandable.  
- Scalability concern: limited discussion of how the method scales to very long image sequences.  
- Originality: novel problem formulation and novel application of a coherence model to multimodal sentence sequences.  
- Relation to prior work: adapts prior coherence ideas but clearly distinguishes from video captioning and single-image captioning.  
- Significance: addresses a meaningful, realistic extension of captioning; blog-based data collection is an innovative, low-cost approach.  
- Motivation weakness: unclear why multiple sentences are preferable to a single descriptive paragraph for an image stream.  
- User study limitation: evaluations focus on short streams (5 images), not long-sequence coherence.  
- Recommendation: Accept — technically sound, well-executed, and a meaningful contribution despite noted limitations.
