Summary and relation to prior work
The paper tackles the problem of describing an image stream with a sequence of natural sentences. Unlike single-image single-sentence captioning, the input and output are both sequences. The proposed coherent recurrent convolutional network (CRCN) combines: (i) CNN (VGG) features for images, (ii) a bidirectional RNN over sentence embeddings (doc2vec) to model the “content flow” of the text stream, and (iii) an entity-grid–based local coherence model (Barzilay & Lapata) to encourage fluent sentence-to-sentence transitions. Training uses a max-margin objective aligning image streams and sentence sequences; at test time they retrieve candidate sentences for each image via KNN and rank concatenations with a beam-like divide-and-conquer search. They build training data by segmenting and summarizing blog posts to produce aligned image–sentence sequences, and evaluate on two travel datasets (NYC, Disneyland) with automatic metrics (BLEU, METEOR, CIDEr, retrieval metrics) and AMT user studies. The work is positioned against single-image captioning (e.g., Karpathy & Fei-Fei, Vinyals et al.), multimodal RNNs, and coherence modeling, and contrasted with video-to-sentence work and image-sequence retrieval for paragraph queries (Kim et al.).

Strengths
- Problem importance and scope: Extends captioning to image streams and multi-sentence outputs, which is practically relevant (photo albums) and under-explored relative to single-image captioning.
- Architectural idea: A principled attempt to combine sentence-level sequence modeling (BRNN over paragraph vectors) with explicit local coherence via the entity grid; end-to-end learned compatibility between text-sequence features and image features.
- Data contribution: A scalable, unsupervised pipeline to mine image–sentence sequences from blogs (large scale, two domains).
- Empirical results: Quantitative gains over strong single-sentence baselines and meaningful AMT preferences; ablation indicates coherence adds value, especially for longer sequences.
- Clear exposition of the training objective and the retrieval/search strategy; reasonable engineering choices (dropout, RMSprop, He init).

Weaknesses and concerns
- Evaluation clarity and fairness:
  - Retrieval metrics are insufficiently specified given that ground-truth sentences are not in the training pool. It is unclear how R@K and median rank are computed in this setting; as written, the “groundtruth retrieval” seems impossible. Precise definition and candidate pools are needed.
  - Baselines are adapted single-image captioners by concatenation, which may disadvantage them on discourse coherence. Stronger sequence-aware text baselines (e.g., a sentence-level LSTM/GRU coherence scorer without entity grids; or a simple bigram/trigram sentence transition model) would clarify the incremental value of the entity-grid component.
  - No comparison to a pure generative paragraph model (e.g., hierarchical RNN over sentences) trained on the same blog text, even if images are used only to retrieve a seed; this would test whether retrieval is the limiting factor.
- Modeling choices:
  - The scoring function sums s_t·v_t plus g·v_t at each t, where g encodes coherence of the entire text sequence. This ties a global text-only coherence vector to each image feature and repeats it across time. The justification for g interacting with each v_t (rather than being a separate additive term or used solely to re-rank sequences) is unclear; it may conflate image compatibility with text-only coherence and unintentionally scale with sequence length.
  - The coherence representation is zero-padded from 64 to 300 and then projected; this seems ad hoc and could be replaced by a learned projection without padding. No sensitivity analysis on history length or alternative coherence models is provided.
  - The BRNN runs over paragraph vectors of candidate sentences, not words; this trades tractability for granularity. The consequences (e.g., inability to rephrase or correct minor mismatches) deserve discussion.
- Data and preprocessing:
  - The text segmentation/summarization pipeline (heuristics + LSA) is noisy; there is no human validation of alignment quality or error analysis. Because the approach depends critically on this pipeline, evidence about its accuracy is important.
  - Potential domain bias: only two travel domains. Generalization to other topics is claimed but not demonstrated.
- Reproducibility: Many implementation details are deferred to the supplement; dataset release/code availability are not stated. The approximate search procedure is practical but its impact vs. exact search is not quantified.

Quality
Overall technically sound, with a coherent objective and ablations that partially support claims. However, the scoring design for the coherence term and the ambiguity around retrieval metrics weaken the empirical support. More rigorous baseline design and clearer metric definitions would strengthen the case.

Clarity
Generally well written and organized. The main pipeline and equations are understandable. Some notation is awkward (padding the 64-d coherence vector; mixing O, S matrices) and several design choices lack justification. The evaluation section needs clearer definitions for retrieval metrics in the “disjoint” setting.

Originality
Moderate. The problem setting (image streams to multi-sentence descriptions) and the integration of entity-grid coherence with multimodal CNN-RNN are novel combinations of known components. Related work is reasonably covered, including video description and sequence retrieval. The core technical novelty lies in the unified scoring/learning with a coherence component rather than a new learning algorithm.

Significance
Moderate. The task is important and the approach yields improvements and user preferences, suggesting practical impact. That said, reliance on sentence retrieval limits generative flexibility and domain-transferability, and the evaluation is confined to two domains. The work is likely to inspire follow-ups on discourse-aware multimodal captioning.

Questions for the authors
- Precisely define R@K and median rank in your setup where ground-truth sentences are not in the training pool. What is the candidate set over which ranks are computed?
- Why is the coherence vector g multiplied against each image feature v_t and then summed over t? Did you try an additive text-only term (independent of v_t) or late re-ranking by coherence?
- How accurate is the blog segmentation/summarization pipeline? Any manual evaluation or error analysis?
- How sensitive are results to κ (number of top LSA sentences), history length in the entity grid, and the choice of sentence embedding (doc2vec vs. averaged word2vec)?
- Will you release code and the processed datasets?

Pros for acceptance
- Addresses an important, under-explored multimodal task; sensible and interpretable incorporation of discourse coherence.
- Large-scale, unsupervised dataset construction; solid user study evidence.
- Clear architectural description and meaningful ablations.

Cons for acceptance
- Ambiguity in retrieval metric definitions; potentially unfair/weak baselines for multi-sentence coherence.
- Some ad hoc design choices (coherence scoring, padding) and limited analysis.
- Domain-limited evaluation; no human validation of alignment quality.

Recommendation
Weak accept (assuming clarifications on evaluation metrics). The problem is timely and the paper offers a reasonable and empirically supported step toward discourse-aware multimodal description. With clearer evaluation and stronger baselines, this could be a solid contribution.

Reviewer confidence: medium.
