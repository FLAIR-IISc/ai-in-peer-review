

# Review of "Expressing an Image Stream with a Sequence of Natural Sentences"

## Summary
This paper addresses a novel problem: generating a sequence of natural language sentences to describe a stream of images (e.g., a series of photos from a trip), rather than the more common single-image-single-sentence captioning task. The authors introduce Coherent Recurrent Convolutional Networks (CRCN), a multimodal architecture that integrates convolutional networks for image representation, bidirectional recurrent networks for sentence modeling, and a local coherence model for smooth sentence transitions. They leverage blog posts as a source of training data since blogs typically contain image-text sequences that tell a story. The paper evaluates their approach on two datasets (NYC and Disneyland) with 20K blog posts and 140K associated images, demonstrating superior performance over baselines that extend single-sentence captioning models to sequences.

## Quality
The paper is technically sound with well-justified methodology. The CRCN architecture builds upon established techniques (VGGNet for images, BRNN for text) while introducing the novel integration of a coherence model specifically for sentence-to-sentence transitions. The evaluation is comprehensive, using both standard NLP metrics (BLEU, CIDEr, METEOR) and retrieval metrics (Top-K recall, median rank), along with user studies via Amazon Mechanical Turk. The authors carefully compare with relevant baselines and provide ablation studies to validate the contribution of each component (e.g., CRCN vs. RCN without coherence model). 

The paper's main technical strength is how it addresses the key challenge of maintaining coherence across multiple sentences, which is particularly important for longer image streams. The coherence model based on entity transitions (following Barzilay & Lapata) is well-integrated into the overall architecture. The empirical results consistently show that CRCN outperforms all baselines, particularly on retrieval metrics that directly measure the ability to rank the correct sentence sequence highly.

## Clarity
The paper is clearly written and well-organized. The problem statement is compelling, with the NYC blog example effectively illustrating the motivation. The architecture description is thorough, with appropriate mathematical formulations and a helpful figure (Fig. 2) that visualizes the CRCN components. The experimental setup is well-explained, including data collection methods, preprocessing steps, and evaluation metrics. 

One minor weakness is the lack of detailed discussion about how the coherence model specifically differs from prior work on text coherence (beyond citing [1]). A more detailed comparison would strengthen the presentation. Additionally, while the paper mentions the use of blog posts as training data, more specific details about the blog crawling process and potential biases in the data would be helpful.

## Originality
The core contribution—addressing the problem of generating sentence sequences for image streams rather than single sentences—is genuinely novel. The authors correctly identify this as an underexplored direction in the literature, with most prior work focusing on single-image captioning. The CRCN architecture's integration of a coherence model for sentence transitions represents a meaningful extension of existing multimodal networks.

The paper adequately differentiates itself from related work, particularly noting that video captioning approaches (e.g., Donahue et al., Rohrbach et al.) don't address the specific challenge of abrupt visual changes between consecutive images in a stream. The authors also properly distinguish their approach from image sequence retrieval (e.g., Kim et al.) which is the opposite direction of their problem.

## Significance
The paper addresses a meaningful gap in the literature with practical applications for tourism, social media, and personal photo organization. The significance is strengthened by the development of a new dataset (blog-based image-text sequences) that could be valuable for future research. The results demonstrate a clear advancement over the state-of-the-art for this specific problem, with consistent improvements across multiple metrics.

The most compelling evidence of significance is the user study showing that human annotators prefer CRCN's output over baselines, particularly for longer sequences where coherence becomes more critical. This validation with human judgment adds substantial weight to the technical results.

## Strengths
1. Identifies and addresses a meaningful gap in the literature (sequence generation rather than single sentence generation)
2. Well-motivated and well-executed integration of coherence modeling
3. Comprehensive evaluation with both quantitative metrics and user studies
4. Practical use of blog posts as training data, providing a scalable source of image-text sequences
5. Clear ablation studies demonstrating the contribution of each component

## Weaknesses
1. The problem, while novel, is a relatively small extension of existing work. The paper could better justify why this specific extension is more significant than other potential extensions to image captioning.
2. The coherence model is based on existing work [1], and the adaptation for this specific task isn't thoroughly explained.
3. The user studies only tested with 5-10 images per query, limiting the ability to fully assess coherence for longer sequences (though the authors do run a supplementary test with longer sequences).
4. The baselines are limited to single-sentence captioning models extended to sequences, without direct comparison to other sequence generation approaches (if they exist).

## Recommendation
This is a strong paper that makes a meaningful contribution to the field of multimodal generation. The problem is well-motivated, the approach is novel and technically sound, and the evaluation is comprehensive. While the problem extension is not revolutionary, it's a significant and well-executed step forward for the field. The paper would benefit from slightly more detailed justification of the significance of this specific extension and a deeper discussion of how the coherence model differs from prior work, but these are minor issues.

I recommend acceptance. The paper meets all NIPS criteria well, particularly in quality and significance. It advances the state of the art in a demonstrable way by addressing a gap that has been underexplored in the literature, and the results provide clear evidence of the approach's superiority.

**Final Score: 8 (Strong Accept)**
