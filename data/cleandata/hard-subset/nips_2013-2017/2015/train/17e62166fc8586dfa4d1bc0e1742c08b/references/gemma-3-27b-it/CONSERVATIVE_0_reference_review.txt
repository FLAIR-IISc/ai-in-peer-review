This paper addresses the problem of generating a sequence of sentences to describe a stream of images, motivated by scenarios like travel photo collections. The authors propose a Coherent Recurrent Convolutional Network (CRCN) integrating CNNs for image representation, RNNs for sentence modeling, and a local coherence model to improve fluency. They evaluate on blog datasets from NYC and Disneyland, comparing against several baselines using BLEU, recall, and user studies.

While the problem is a natural extension of single-image captioning, and the motivation is reasonable, the paper presents a largely incremental contribution lacking sufficient novelty to warrant strong acceptance at NIPS. The core idea of combining image stream processing with a coherence model is not groundbreaking. Existing video description work (explicitly acknowledged, yet downplayed) already addresses sequential data with inter-frame dependencies, and the presented coherence model appears relatively standard. The authors rightly note the difficulty of obtaining parallel image-text stream data, but the reliance on blog posts, while practical, introduces significant noise and biases. Blog posts aren't designed as objective descriptions of image sequences; they are narratives with a subjective viewpoint and often stylistic embellishments. This impacts the validity of the evaluation. 

The methodological rigor, while present, is not exceptionally strong. The ablation studies (RCN vs. CRCN) are helpful, but more comprehensive analysis is needed. For example, the impact of the LSA-based summarization on the quality of the generated sequences isn’t sufficiently discussed. Is the summarization step a bottleneck? How sensitive is the performance to the choice of κ in the data augmentation? Furthermore, the evaluation relies heavily on automatic metrics (BLEU, etc.), known to be imperfect proxies for human judgment, particularly for longer, more nuanced text. The AMT studies are a positive aspect, but the experimental details are sparse (e.g. number of participants, specific instructions), hindering a full assessment of their reliability. The claim of “completely unsupervised” is also slightly misleading, given the use of LSA and parsing tools which require pre-trained models.

The writing quality is generally good, but the paper is verbose and repetitive. The constant reiteration of the “novelty” of extending single-image captioning to image streams feels forced. The description of the architecture (section 3) is overly detailed and can be streamlined. 

**Strengths:**

*   Addresses a potentially useful problem.
*   Presents a relatively complete system with clear components.
*   Includes both quantitative and qualitative (AMT) evaluations.
*   Demonstrates some improvement over baseline methods.

**Weaknesses:**

*   Limited novelty; builds on existing techniques in a fairly straightforward manner.
*   Reliance on noisy and biased blog data.
*   Insufficient analysis of the impact of individual components.
*   Over-reliance on automatic evaluation metrics.
*   Verbose and repetitive writing.
*   Claim of “completely unsupervised” is debatable.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a functional system that addresses a plausible problem.
*   The experimental results, while not dramatic, demonstrate a performance improvement over baselines.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The lack of substantial novelty and methodological depth.
*   The reliance on a problematic dataset.
*   The limited insights gained from the evaluation.
*   The paper feels more like an engineering effort than a significant scientific contribution.



**Overall Assessment:** Reject.

This paper, while technically sound, does not represent a significant advance in the field. The novelty is incremental, the methodological contributions are limited, and the reliance on noisy data weakens the conclusions. It lacks the originality and significance expected of a NIPS publication. The authors should consider submitting to a more specialized venue where the incremental contribution might be more appreciated. I recommend the authors focus on addressing the data quality issues, performing more in-depth analysis of the model components, and demonstrating a more substantial improvement over existing techniques.
