**Summary**

This paper addresses the novel and interesting problem of generating a sequence of natural language sentences to describe a stream of images, moving beyond the well-studied task of single-image-to-single-sentence captioning. The authors propose a new architecture, the Coherent Recurrent Convolutional Network (CRCN), which integrates three main components: a standard CNN (VGGNet) for image feature extraction, a Bidirectional RNN (BRNN) to model the semantic flow of sentence sequences, and an entity-based local coherence model inspired by classic NLP work [1] to enforce grammatical and referential smoothness. The model is trained on a large dataset of blog posts, cleverly repurposed as weakly-supervised parallel data. At inference time, the approach is retrieval-based: for a query image stream, it first finds candidate sentences from the training data via K-NN image search and then uses the trained CRCN model as a scoring function to rank and select the best, most coherent sentence sequence via a beam-search-like strategy. The method is evaluated on two blog datasets (NYC and Disneyland) using both automatic metrics (BLEU, R@K, etc.) and human studies on Amazon Mechanical Turk, demonstrating superior performance over adapted state-of-the-art single-image captioning baselines.

**Strengths (Reasons for Acceptance)**

1.  **Novel and Significant Problem Formulation:** The paper's primary strength is its framing of a new and important problem. Moving from single images to image streams is a logical and necessary step toward more complex AI systems that can understand and narrate events. This extends the scope of vision-and-language research in a meaningful way.

2.  **Novel Integration of Techniques:** The core technical contribution—integrating a classic, explicit NLP coherence model (the entity grid) into a modern deep learning architecture (CNN+RNN)—is highly original. While most contemporary models rely on the RNN itself to implicitly learn coherence, this work shows the value of explicitly modeling discourse-level phenomena. This hybrid approach is a compelling idea that could inspire further research at the intersection of deep learning and symbolic NLP.

3.  **Strong and Thorough Experimental Validation:** The evaluation is comprehensive. The authors compare their method not only against relevant state-of-the-art baselines (adapted for the sequence task) but also against meaningful ablations of their own model (1NN, RCN). This clearly demonstrates the individual contributions of the BRNN and the coherence model. The inclusion of both automatic metrics and human evaluation via AMT provides a robust and convincing case for the method's effectiveness. The final AMT experiment, which shows the coherence model's benefit increasing with sequence length, is a particularly insightful piece of analysis.

4.  **Resourceful Data Collection Method:** The idea of using blog posts as a source of weakly-aligned image and text sequences is clever and practical. It provides a scalable way to collect large amounts of training data for this new task without requiring expensive and laborious manual annotation.

**Weaknesses (Areas for Improvement)**

1.  **Retrieval-based, Not Generative:** A key limitation, which could be made more explicit in the paper, is that the system is fundamentally a retrieval system, not a generative one. It composes paragraphs by selecting and reordering existing sentences from the training corpus. This significantly limits its creative potential and its ability to describe novel object configurations or events not seen during training. The title "Expressing an Image Stream" and terms like "generate" might imply a level of novel text creation that the model does not possess. This is a crucial distinction that affects the work's overall significance.

2.  **Scalability and Efficiency of Inference:** The inference procedure involves a K-NN search for each of the N images in the query stream, followed by a combinatorial search over `K^N` potential sentence sequences. While the authors propose a divide-and-conquer strategy to make this tractable, it remains a potential bottleneck for long sequences (large N) or a desire for a more diverse candidate pool (large K). The performance of this heuristic search compared to an exhaustive one is not quantified.

3.  **Domain Specificity:** The experiments are conducted on two relatively narrow, albeit large, tourism-focused domains. The model's performance relies heavily on having a training corpus that is thematically similar to the query images due to its retrieval-based nature. It is unclear how well this approach would generalize to more open-domain scenarios where the visual content may have no close neighbors in the training set.

**Critique based on Review Criteria**

*   **Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The proposed architecture is well-motivated, and the components are combined logically. The claims are well-supported by extensive experimental results. The work is presented as a complete system, from data processing to evaluation. The authors are honest about the need for an approximate search strategy during inference.

*   **Clarity:** The paper is very clearly written and well-organized. The problem, motivation, and proposed method are explained effectively, aided by useful diagrams (Fig. 2). The experimental setup and results are presented in a straightforward manner, making the paper easy to follow and understand.

*   **Originality:** High. The problem formulation itself is novel in the context of the cited literature. The central idea of combining a modern neural sequence model with a classic entity-based coherence model for this task is a significant and original contribution. It clearly differentiates itself from prior work on single-image captioning and video description.

*   **Significance:** The paper holds considerable significance. It pushes the boundary of image description towards narrative generation, a challenging and important direction. The results convincingly show that explicitly modeling coherence is beneficial, a finding that is valuable to the broader vision-and-language community. However, its ultimate impact is somewhat constrained by the non-generative, retrieval-based nature of the final output. Nevertheless, it advances the state of the art in a demonstrable way on the task it defines and provides a strong foundation for future work.

**Overall Recommendation:** This is a strong paper that introduces a novel problem, proposes an original and effective solution, and backs it up with a thorough evaluation. While the retrieval-based nature presents a limitation, the contributions in problem formulation and model architecture are significant and novel. The paper is well-executed and would be a valuable addition to the NIPS proceedings.
