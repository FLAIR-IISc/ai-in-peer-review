This paper addresses the novel and interesting problem of generating a sequence of natural sentences to describe a stream of images. This extends the well-studied task of single-image-to-single-sentence captioning to a more complex, sequential setting, which is motivated by real-world applications like summarizing photo albums from a trip. The core contribution is a multimodal architecture, the Coherent Recurrent Convolutional Network (CRCN), which combines three main components: a CNN (VGGNet) for image feature extraction, a bidirectional RNN for modeling the semantic flow of sentences (represented by paragraph vectors), and an entity-based local coherence model to ensure linguistic fluency between sentences. The entire system is framed as a retrieval and ranking problem: for a query image stream, candidate sentence sequences are constructed from a training database, and the CRCN is used to score and rank these candidates. The model is trained on a large dataset of blog posts using a max-margin objective. The proposed method is evaluated against several adapted state-of-the-art captioning models on two blog datasets (NYC and Disneyland) using both automatic metrics and human studies, demonstrating superior performance.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Novelty and Significance of the Problem:** The paper is, to my knowledge, one of the first to formally tackle the "image stream to sentence sequence" problem. This is a logical and important next step for the vision-and-language community, moving beyond single images towards more holistic, narrative descriptions. The problem formulation is well-motivated and has clear practical relevance.

2.  **Well-Motivated Architecture:** The CRCN architecture is thoughtfully designed. The combination of a BRNN to capture high-level semantic flow and a classic, explicit entity-based coherence model [1] to capture low-level syntactic and referential transitions is a key strength. This acknowledges that while RNNs are powerful sequence models, they may not explicitly capture the fine-grained linguistic phenomena that make a paragraph coherent. Integrating a well-established NLP technique for this purpose is a clever and effective choice.

3.  **Thorough and Convincing Experiments:** The evaluation is comprehensive.
    *   The use of large-scale, real-world blog data is appropriate for the task.
    *   Baselines are reasonably chosen by adapting strong single-image captioning models, providing a fair point of comparison.
    *   The ablation study (comparing CRCN to RCN and 1NN) clearly demonstrates the value of both the RNN component and the coherence model. The insight that the coherence model significantly improves retrieval metrics more than language metrics is particularly sharp, suggesting its main role is in correctly ranking the ground-truth sequence.
    *   The inclusion of user studies via AMT is crucial for a task where automatic metrics like BLEU are known to be insufficient for evaluating qualities like fluency and coherence. The second AMT study showing that the benefit of the coherence model increases with sequence length is a very strong result that supports the central claims of the paper.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Retrieval-based Limitation:** The method is fundamentally a retrieval and ranking system, not a generative one. It is constrained to using sentences that exist in the training corpus. While this is a pragmatic choice that simplifies the problem, it limits the model's expressiveness. It cannot describe novel object compositions or events not seen in training. This limitation and its implications should be more explicitly discussed. This makes the title "Expressing an Image Stream..." slightly misleading, as it's more "Assembling an Expression for an Image Stream from a Corpus."

2.  **Potential for Noise in Data Preprocessing:** The pipeline for creating aligned image-sentence pairs from raw blog posts relies on a series of heuristics: paragraph segmentation, distance-based image-text alignment, and LSA-based summarization. This process could be quite noisy. For example, a paragraph of text might refer to multiple images, or an image might not have a direct corresponding sentence. The paper does not analyze the robustness of the model to this potential alignment noise, which could be a significant factor.

3.  **Inference Complexity and Approximation:** The search space for the best sentence sequence is exponential in the length of the image stream (K^N). The paper proposes a divide-and-conquer strategy with beam search as an approximation. While this is a practical necessity, the details are brief. The quality of this approximation is asserted based on the intuition that local coherence is necessary for global coherence, but this is not empirically validated. An analysis of the sensitivity to the beam size (M) or a comparison to a random-sampling baseline for the search would strengthen this part.

4.  **Integration of Coherence Model:** The 64-dimensional feature vector from the entity grid is zero-padded to 300 dimensions before being passed through the same FC layers as the BRNN outputs. This seems somewhat ad-hoc. While it appears to work empirically, it's not a very elegant fusion method. It treats a global property of the sequence (coherence) in the same way as a local property (sentence content), which might not be optimal.

**Clarity and Quality:**
The paper is generally well-written, clearly organized, and easy to follow. The figures effectively illustrate the problem and the proposed architecture. The methodology is described with sufficient detail for understanding, though perhaps not full reproduction without access to the exact data processing scripts.

**Summary and Recommendation:**

Overall, this is a strong paper that introduces a novel and significant problem to the community. The proposed CRCN model is a well-designed and effective combination of modern deep learning techniques and classic NLP models. The experimental validation is thorough and provides compelling evidence for the model's effectiveness, particularly through the insightful user studies.

The weaknesses, primarily related to the retrieval-based nature of the approach and the heuristics in data processing and inference, are acceptable for a conference paper introducing a new direction. They represent clear avenues for future work rather than fatal flaws.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Addresses a new, important, and challenging problem.
*   Proposes a novel and well-motivated architecture combining different modeling paradigms.
*   Provides a strong and comprehensive empirical evaluation, including crucial human studies.
*   Likely to inspire follow-up work in story-like visual description.

**Arguments for Rejection:**
*   The system is retrieval-based, which limits its novelty compared to fully generative models.
*   The data preparation and inference steps rely on potentially brittle heuristics.

I believe the strengths substantially outweigh the weaknesses. This is a solid contribution that advances the state of the art in a meaningful way. I recommend acceptance.
