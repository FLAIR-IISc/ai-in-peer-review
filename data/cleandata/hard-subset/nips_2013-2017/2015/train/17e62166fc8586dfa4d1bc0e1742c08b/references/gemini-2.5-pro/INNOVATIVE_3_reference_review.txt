### Review

**Summary:**
This paper addresses the novel and challenging task of generating a coherent sequence of natural language sentences from a stream of images. This is a significant step beyond the now-common task of single-image captioning. The authors propose a new architecture, the Coherent Recurrent Convolutional Network (CRCN), which uniquely integrates three components: a standard CNN for image feature extraction, a bidirectional RNN to model the semantic flow of the sentence sequence, and, crucially, an explicit entity-based local coherence model adapted from prior work in NLP. To train this model, the authors present a clever method for creating a large-scale dataset by scraping and processing blog posts, treating them as weakly-aligned streams of images and text. The proposed model is retrieval-based: for a query image stream, it first identifies candidate sentences for each image and then uses the learned CRCN to score and rank possible sentence sequences to find the one that is both visually grounded and textually coherent. The method is evaluated on two new blog-post datasets (NYC and Disneyland) using both automatic metrics and human studies, demonstrating superior performance over baselines adapted from state-of-the-art single-image captioning models.

**Strengths:**

1.  **High Originality and Significance:** The primary strength of this paper is its boldness in defining and tackling a new, important problem. Moving from single images to image streams and from single sentences to paragraphs is a natural but non-trivial evolution for the vision-and-language community. This work has the potential to open up a new subfield focused on "visual storytelling" and could inspire a great deal of follow-up research. It clearly advances the state of the art by moving beyond simple object description towards narrative generation.

2.  **Novel Architectural Contribution:** The CRCN architecture is a novel and intelligent combination of techniques. While CNNs and RNNs are now standard, the explicit integration of an entity-based coherence model (Barzilay & Lapata, 2008) is a standout idea. Many deep learning approaches would simply use a more powerful RNN or Transformer and hope that coherence emerges implicitly. The authors' decision to incorporate a more symbolic, structured model of linguistic coherence is a creative and effective approach that directly addresses the core challenge of making the output text flow naturally. This fusion of sub-symbolic and symbolic reasoning is a promising research direction.

3.  **Pragmatic and Scalable Data Acquisition:** The authors solve the critical data bottleneck for this new task in a clever way. The idea of using blog posts as a source of weakly-supervised image-stream-to-text-sequence data is highly practical and scalable. The pre-processing pipeline, while heuristic, is well-reasoned and provides a blueprint for others wanting to work on this problem.

4.  **Thorough Evaluation:** The experimental validation is strong. The authors not only compare against adapted state-of-the-art methods but also perform a crucial ablation study (CRCN vs. RCN) that clearly demonstrates the value of their main contribution—the coherence model. The inclusion of both automatic metrics and human evaluations via AMT provides a comprehensive picture of the model's performance. The final user study on longer sequences effectively isolates and validates the importance of the coherence model, which is a nice touch.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Retrieval-Based Limitation:** The current framework is retrieval-based, meaning it combines existing sentences from the training set rather than generating truly novel sentences. This inherently limits the expressive capacity of the system to the vocabulary and sentence structures present in the training corpus. While this is a perfectly valid design choice for a first paper on such a complex problem, it is a limitation worth noting. The paper could be strengthened by a brief discussion of this choice and the potential for future work on fully generative models.

2.  **Approximate Inference at Test Time:** The test-time search for the best sentence sequence is computationally intractable (K^N combinations). The authors propose a practical divide-and-conquer strategy akin to a beam search. While this is a reasonable engineering solution, it is an approximation that is not guaranteed to find the globally optimal sequence according to the model's scoring function. The claim that it "achieves almost optimal solutions" is supported empirically but lacks theoretical grounding. However, given the problem's complexity, this is a minor flaw and an acceptable compromise.

3.  **Dependence on Pre-processing Heuristics:** The quality of the training data is highly dependent on the success of the text segmentation and LSA-based summarization pipeline. Errors or noise from this upstream process will inevitably propagate into the learned model. For instance, LSA summarization might not always capture the most relevant sentence for an image. This is a common issue in weakly supervised learning, but it means the model's performance is tied to the quality of these heuristics.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Introduces a novel and significant problem (visual storytelling) that pushes the vision-and-language field in an exciting new direction.
*   The core architectural idea—integrating an explicit NLP coherence model into a deep network—is highly novel and well-motivated.
*   The paper is a complete piece of work with a novel model, a clever data collection strategy, and a thorough empirical evaluation that validates its claims.
*   The results are strong, and the work is likely to be highly influential and spawn new research.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   The method is retrieval-based, not generative, which limits its novelty from a pure language generation perspective.
*   The inference procedure is an approximation, and the training data relies on heuristics which may introduce noise.

**Recommendation:** Accept

This is precisely the kind of bold, forward-looking paper that NIPS should champion. It identifies and makes the first significant attempt at solving a challenging and important new problem. The central idea of explicitly modeling coherence is creative and shows a thoughtful combination of different AI paradigms. While the methodology is not without its minor imperfections (e.g., retrieval-based approach, approximate search), these are acceptable limitations for a pioneering work in a new domain. The paper's novelty and potential impact on the field far outweigh these weaknesses. It is a well-executed study that will undoubtedly inspire future research in visual storytelling.
