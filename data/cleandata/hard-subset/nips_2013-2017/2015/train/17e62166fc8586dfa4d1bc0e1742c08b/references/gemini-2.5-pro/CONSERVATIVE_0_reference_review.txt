### **Summary of the Paper**

This paper addresses the task of generating a coherent sequence of natural language sentences to describe a stream of images. The authors argue this is a natural extension of the well-studied single image-to-sentence captioning problem. They propose a new model, the Coherent Recurrent Convolutional Network (CRCN), which combines three main components: a standard Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) for image feature extraction (VGGNet), a Bidirectional Recurrent Neural Network (BRNN) to model the semantic flow of the text, and an entity-based local coherence model from prior work (Barzilay and Lapata, 2008) to enforce grammatical and referential consistency. The model is trained on a large dataset of blog posts, which are pre-processed to create weakly aligned image-sentence sequences. The approach is retrieval-based: for a new image stream, candidate sentences are retrieved for each image, and the CRCN is used to score and rank possible sentence sequences, with a beam search-like strategy to manage the combinatorial complexity. The authors evaluate their method against several state-of-the-art single-image captioning models (adapted to this task by concatenation) and demonstrate superior performance on both automatic metrics and human evaluations.

### **Strengths and Weaknesses**

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound in its implementation of established components. The experimental setup is comprehensive, including both quantitative metrics (BLEU, CIDEr, R@K) and a human study via AMT. The authors also perform a useful ablation study, comparing their full model (CRCN) against a version without the coherence model (RCN), which helps to isolate the contribution of this specific component.

However, there are several weaknesses regarding the methodological rigor.
1.  **System Complexity and Dependencies:** The proposed pipeline is not truly end-to-end. It relies heavily on a complex and heuristic-driven pre-processing stage to create the training data from blogs (text segmentation based on block distance, LSA-based summarization). The performance of the final model is likely highly sensitive to the quality of this pre-processing, yet this pipeline is not validated or analyzed.
2.  **Retrieval-based vs. Generative:** The approach is fundamentally a retrieval and re-ranking system, not a generative one. While this simplifies the problem, it limits the model's expressiveness to sentences that already exist in the training corpus. This is a significant limitation compared to true generative models that can produce novel sentences.
3.  **Inference Approximation:** The inference procedure for finding the best sentence sequence is an approximation (divide-and-conquer with beam search). While the authors argue this works well in practice, it is a heuristic necessitated by the combinatorial explosion of their retrieval framework, highlighting a potential brittleness in the approach for longer sequences.

**Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The architecture of the CRCN is clearly explained with a helpful diagram in Figure 2. The training objective and inference process are also described in sufficient detail.

**Originality:** The primary novelty of this work lies in the formulation of the image stream-to-sentence sequence problem and the specific combination of existing techniques to address it. However, the individual components are not new. The use of CNNs for images and RNNs for language is a standard paradigm in this area. The main "novel" element is the integration of the entity-based coherence model from Barzilay and Lapata (2008). While this is a clever integration, it combines a modern deep learning architecture with a classic, symbolic NLP technique. A more innovative approach might have explored a neural or learned coherence model that could be integrated more seamlessly into the backpropagation framework. As such, the work feels more like a strong piece of engineering than a fundamental methodological advance.

**Significance:** The problem itself is significant and represents a valuable direction for research beyond single-image captioning. The proposed method demonstrates that considering inter-sentence coherence improves results, which is an important finding. The paper provides a solid baseline for a new and challenging task.

However, the overall significance is tempered by several factors.
1.  **Reliance on Established Methods:** By building the system from well-known, off-the-shelf components, the long-term impact of the specific CRCN architecture may be limited. The field is moving rapidly towards end-to-end generative models, and this retrieval-based system may represent an incremental, rather than transformative, step.
2.  **Baseline Comparisons:** The baselines are state-of-the-art single-image captioning models. They are adapted to the sequence task by simply concatenating their individual outputs. This is a reasonable first step, but it is a weak baseline for the *sequence* generation aspect. The baselines have no mechanism to model coherence, so it is unsurprising that a model explicitly designed for coherence performs better. A stronger baseline would involve a sequence-to-sequence model trained on the same data but without the explicit, external coherence module.
3.  **Magnitude of Improvement:** While CRCN outperforms RCN (the version without the coherence model), the improvements on automatic language metrics like BLEU are quite modest. The authors correctly note the limitations of these metrics for evaluating paragraph-level quality, but the small quantitative gain slightly undermines the central claim regarding the coherence model's impact. The improvement is more pronounced in retrieval metrics and human studies, which is a stronger point, but the overall evidence is not overwhelming.

### **Arguments for and against acceptance**

**Arguments for acceptance:**
*   Addresses a novel and important problem: extending image captioning to sequences of images and sentences.
*   The proposed system is complete and is shown to work, providing a strong baseline for future work on this task.
*   The evaluation is thorough, including multiple automatic metrics, an ablation study, and a human evaluation that supports the main claims.
*   The use of blog data is a practical solution to the data scarcity problem for this task.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
*   The technical contribution is primarily the combination of existing, well-established methods. The core coherence model is a classic NLP technique from 2008, not a modern, learned component.
*   The approach is retrieval-based, which is less ambitious and powerful than a fully generative model capable of producing novel descriptions.
*   The comparison baselines are adapted from a different task (single-image captioning) and are inherently disadvantaged, potentially inflating the perceived performance of the proposed model.
*   The system's performance is tied to a complex, heuristic-based pre-processing pipeline whose impact and robustness are not analyzed.

### **Conclusion**

This paper presents a solid piece of work on the important problem of describing image streams with coherent text. The proposed CRCN model is sensible, and the experiments demonstrate its effectiveness over reasonable baselines. However, the work's primary weakness is its limited novelty. It is a carefully engineered system built from existing parts, rather than a paper that introduces new fundamental techniques. The reliance on a retrieval framework and a classic, non-neural coherence model makes the approach feel somewhat dated in the context of rapid progress in deep generative models. While the results are promising, the contribution appears more suited for a top-tier application-focused conference than for NIPS, which typically requires a greater degree of methodological innovation.
