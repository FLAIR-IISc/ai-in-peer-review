This paper addresses the problem of generating a sequence of natural sentences to describe a stream of images. This is presented as a natural extension of the well-studied image captioning task. The authors propose a multimodal architecture, the Coherent Recurrent Convolutional Network (CRCN), which combines three main components: a standard Convolutional Neural Network (VGGNet) to extract image features, a Bidirectional Recurrent Neural Network (BRNN) to model the flow of sentence content, and a local coherence model based on the entity-grid method of Barzilay and Lapata (2008). The overall approach is retrieval-based; for a given query image stream, candidate sentences are retrieved for each image, and the CRCN is used to score and rank possible full sentence sequences. The model is trained on a large dataset of blog posts, from which parallel image-sentence sequences are automatically extracted. The authors evaluate their method against several state-of-the-art single-image captioning baselines (adapted for this task) and demonstrate superior performance through both automatic metrics and human studies.

**Strengths:**
1.  **Problem Formulation:** The paper tackles a novel and well-motivated problem. Moving from single image-sentence pairs to image-sentence sequences is a logical and important step towards a deeper integration of vision and language. The motivation of describing photo collections from events like a vacation is practical and compelling.
2.  **Dataset Collection:** The idea of leveraging blog posts to create a large-scale, text-image parallel corpus for this task is resourceful. It provides a practical solution to the data scarcity problem that would otherwise make training such a sequence-to-sequence model difficult.
3.  **Thorough Evaluation:** The experimental evaluation is comprehensive. The authors compare their model against several relevant and strong baselines, including recent deep learning models for image captioning. The use of both automatic metrics (BLEU, CIDEr, etc.) and human evaluation via AMT provides a convincing case for the performance of the proposed method. The ablation study (CRCN vs. RCN) is useful for assessing the contribution of the coherence model.

**Weaknesses:**
1.  **Limited Methodological Novelty:** The primary weakness of this work lies in its limited technical novelty. The proposed CRCN model is largely an assembly of existing, off-the-shelf components. The use of a pre-trained CNN for image features and an RNN/BRNN for language modeling is standard practice in this domain. The main claimed novelty, the integration of a coherence model, involves applying a well-established, non-neural technique from 2008 [1]. The integration itself is rather superficial: a 64-dimensional feature vector is computed from a separate NLP pipeline (parsing, entity grid construction) and then simply concatenated with the BRNN output before being passed through fully connected layers. This falls short of being a "unified end-to-end architecture" as claimed, and it feels more like an engineering pipeline than a novel machine learning model.
2.  **Data Processing Pipeline:** The process for creating the parallel corpus is based on a series of heuristics for text segmentation (distance-based assignment) and summarization (LSA-based). This pipeline is likely to introduce significant noise and weak alignments between images and their associated sentences. The quality of the resulting dataset is a critical factor for the model's performance, yet there is no analysis or validation of the alignment quality. This lack of rigor regarding the training data undermines the confidence in the reported results. The claim that the approach is "completely unsupervised" is also misleading, as the model is trained in a fully supervised manner on the extracted image-sentence pairs.
3.  **Retrieval-Based Approach:** The system does not truly *generate* novel paragraphs but rather retrieves and stitches together existing sentences from the training set. This is a significant limitation that is not emphasized enough. The expressiveness of the model is fundamentally capped by the diversity of sentences in the training corpus. While retrieval is a valid approach, framing the work as sentence *generation* is imprecise.
4.  **Contribution of the Coherence Model:** According to Table 1, the performance gain of the full CRCN model over the RCN variant (without the coherence model) is marginal on language similarity metrics (BLEU, CIDEr, METEOR). The primary improvement is seen in the retrieval metrics (R@K, Med r). This suggests that the coherence model mainly acts as a re-ranking feature, helping to better order a list of already plausible candidates, rather than fundamentally improving the model's understanding of language. A more compelling contribution would have been a novel method to learn coherence implicitly or in an end-to-end fashion within the neural architecture itself, rather than bolting on a classic, feature-engineered model.

**Summary of Arguments:**

*   **For Acceptance:**
    *   Addresses a novel and significant problem.
    *   Presents a complete system that demonstrably outperforms strong baselines on this new task.
    *   Evaluation is extensive, combining automatic metrics and human judgments.

*   **Against Acceptance:**
    *   The proposed model is an incremental combination of existing techniques with limited novelty.
    *   The core "coherence" contribution is based on an old, non-neural method that is not deeply integrated.
    *   The data processing pipeline is heuristic-driven and its quality is unevaluated, casting doubt on the soundness of the experiments.
    *   The approach is retrieval-based, not generative, which limits its overall significance and should be clarified.

In conclusion, while the paper addresses an interesting and timely problem, the proposed solution lacks the methodological novelty and rigor expected for a NIPS publication. The work is more of a systems paper, successfully engineering a solution by combining existing parts, than a fundamental contribution to machine learning. The reliance on a dated coherence model and a questionable data-processing pipeline are significant concerns. Therefore, I cannot recommend acceptance in its current form.
