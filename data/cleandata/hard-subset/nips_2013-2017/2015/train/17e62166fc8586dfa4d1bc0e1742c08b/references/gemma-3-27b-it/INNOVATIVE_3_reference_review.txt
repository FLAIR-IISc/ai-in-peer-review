This paper tackles the interesting and relatively unexplored problem of generating coherent multi-sentence descriptions for image *streams*, moving beyond the single-image, single-sentence paradigm. The core idea – leveraging blog posts as a naturally occurring image-text parallel corpus and incorporating a coherence model – is genuinely novel and represents a worthwhile direction for research. While the methodology isn’t flawless and the experimental validation could be more extensive, the boldness of the approach and the potential impact are significant enough to warrant strong consideration.

**Summary:** The authors introduce CRCN, a multimodal architecture combining CNNs, RNNs, and a local coherence model to generate sequential descriptions from image sequences. They utilize blog posts as a training dataset, addressing the lack of parallel corpora for this task. The experiments on NYC and Disneyland blogs demonstrate promising results, surpassing several baselines via quantitative metrics (BLEU, Recall) and user studies.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** This is the primary strength. Extending image captioning to *streams* is a natural, yet under-addressed, progression. The blog-as-corpus idea is clever and addresses a key bottleneck in this area.
*   **Coherence Modeling:** Explicitly modeling coherence between sentences, crucial for multi-sentence descriptions, is a significant contribution. The use of parse trees and entity grids is a reasonable approach.
*   **Comprehensive Architecture:** The integration of CNNs, RNNs, and the coherence model into a unified end-to-end architecture is well-designed.
*   **Solid Experimental Setup:** The use of two real-world datasets (NYC and Disneyland) and both quantitative metrics and user studies provides a reasonably comprehensive evaluation.
*   **Clear Writing:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the motivation, methodology, and results.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Data Quality/Bias:** Relying solely on blog data introduces potential biases (e.g., writing style, topic focus). The authors acknowledge this but a more detailed analysis of the dataset characteristics would be beneficial.
*   **Coherence Model Limitations:** While the coherence model is a key contribution, its reliance on parse trees may be fragile and sensitive to parsing errors.  The history parameter of three seems arbitrarily chosen; justification or experimentation with other values would strengthen this aspect.
*   **Limited Baseline Comparison:** While the baselines are reasonable, extending them to handle sequential descriptions isn’t fully detailed, potentially understating their performance.  More directly comparable architectures would be useful.
*   **Quantitative Metric Limitations:** As the authors themselves acknowledge, BLEU and similar metrics are imperfect for evaluating the fluency and coherence of longer passages. Relying heavily on these scores may not fully capture the quality of the generated descriptions.
*   **Ablation Study Depth:** The ablation study (comparing CRCN, RCN, and 1NN) could be more granular. Breaking down the contribution of *specific* components within the BRNN or coherence model would be insightful.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The problem addressed is novel and important.
*   The proposed approach (CRCN) is a creative and potentially impactful solution.
*   The experimental results are encouraging and support the claims.
*   The paper is well-written and clearly presents the methodology and results.
*   The potential for future work in this direction is high.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The reliance on blog data introduces potential biases.
*   The quantitative evaluation is limited by the choice of metrics.
*   The experimental validation could be more thorough, particularly with more comprehensive baseline comparisons and ablation studies.
*   The coherence model’s reliance on parsing may be a limitation.

**Overall Assessment:**

Despite some weaknesses, this paper presents a significant contribution to the field. The novelty of the problem, the creativity of the approach, and the promising experimental results outweigh the methodological shortcomings.  I believe this work has the potential to open up a new avenue of research in multimodal understanding and generation. I recommend **acceptance**, with minor revisions addressing the limitations mentioned above (e.g., providing a more detailed dataset analysis and exploring alternative coherence modeling approaches).  I am prioritizing the novelty and potential impact over absolute methodological perfection.
