The paper addresses the problem of generating a sequence of natural language sentences to describe an image stream, extending traditional image captioning from single images/sentences to sequential data. The core idea is to combine convolutional neural networks for image representation, bidirectional recurrent neural networks for language modeling, and a local coherence model to ensure fluency. The authors leverage blog posts with associated images as a training corpus, a pragmatic approach given the scarcity of parallel image-sentence stream datasets.

This work builds upon a significant body of research in image captioning (e.g., [5, 8, 9, 12, 14, 15, 26, 21, 30]) and video description ([23, 32]).  It distinguishes itself by explicitly modeling coherence between sentences, acknowledging the potentially abrupt transitions in image streams compared to videos. The use of blog data is also a novel source of training data for this specific task, sidestepping the need for manually annotated datasets. The paper positions itself well within the existing literature, clearly outlining its contributions and differentiating itself from related work like [5, 11].

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The extension of image captioning to image streams and sequential sentence generation is a valuable contribution. The problem setting is more realistic than single image captioning for many real-world scenarios (e.g., summarizing travel photos).
* **Technical Soundness:** The proposed CRCN architecture appears well-motivated and integrates established techniques (CNNs, RNNs, coherence modeling) in a sensible manner. The combination of these components is technically sound, with clear explanations of each module.
* **Dataset & Experimental Setup:**  Leveraging blog posts as a large-scale, weakly-supervised dataset is a pragmatic and effective approach. The experimental setup, including comparisons to several baselines and user studies, is reasonably thorough.
* **Clear Writing:** The paper is generally well-written and organized, with a clear articulation of the problem, proposed solution, and experimental results.
* **Comprehensive Related Work:** The authors demonstrate a good understanding of the related literature and appropriately position their work within it.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Coherence Model Detail:** While the local coherence model is presented, the specifics of its implementation, particularly the handling of entity grids and transition frequencies, could benefit from more detailed explanation.  The impact of the history parameter (set to three) is not thoroughly investigated.
* **Data Augmentation:** The data augmentation technique (selecting top-κ sentences) feels somewhat arbitrary. A more systematic investigation of different augmentation strategies and their impact on performance would be beneficial.
* **Limited Baseline Comparison:** The selected baselines, while reasonable, are mostly single-sentence captioning models adapted to the sequential setting.  Exploring alternative sequence-to-sequence models or approaches specifically designed for coherent text generation (beyond image captioning) would strengthen the evaluation.
* **Qualitative Examples:** While qualitative examples are provided, they are limited in number and scope. A more extensive set of examples, including failure cases, would provide more insight into the strengths and weaknesses of the approach.
* **BPTT Concerns:** The paper acknowledges the potential difficulties of BPTT with long sequences but doesn't explicitly address how these were mitigated in practice (e.g., truncated BPTT, gradient clipping).



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* Addresses a novel and relevant problem in multimodal learning.
* Presents a technically sound and well-motivated architecture.
* Demonstrates strong performance through quantitative evaluation and user studies.
* Provides a valuable dataset and experimental setup for future research.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

* The coherence model and data augmentation strategies could benefit from more in-depth analysis and justification.
* The baseline comparison is limited, and the lack of comparison with more sophisticated sequence generation models is a concern.
* The paper lacks detailed discussion of practical challenges like handling long sequences during training (BPTT).

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a solid contribution to the field of multimodal learning. The problem of generating coherent sentence sequences from image streams is both novel and practical. While the paper has some weaknesses, particularly regarding the coherence model and baseline comparison, the strengths outweigh the concerns. I believe this paper is suitable for NIPS, and would recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions to address the weaknesses outlined above. Specifically, I recommend adding more detail about the implementation of the coherence model, investigating alternative data augmentation strategies, and including a discussion of the BPTT challenges and their mitigation.
