

# Review of "Expressing an Image Stream with a Sequence of Natural Sentences"

This paper proposes a novel task: generating a sequence of natural language sentences to describe a stream of images, rather than a single image. The authors introduce a "Coherent Recurrent Convolutional Networks" (CRCN) model that combines convolutional neural networks for image representation, bidirectional recurrent neural networks for sentence modeling, and a local coherence model based on entity transitions to ensure smooth sentence transitions. The authors argue this is the first work addressing this problem, using blog posts as training data with image-sentence pairs.

## Strengths
The paper is well-organized, clearly explains the proposed architecture, and provides a thorough description of the dataset construction process. The evaluation includes both quantitative metrics (BLEU, CIDEr, METEOR) and user studies via Amazon Mechanical Turk, which is commendable. The authors also demonstrate careful ablation studies comparing variants of their method.

## Weaknesses

**Lack of Novelty and Justification for the Problem Extension**: The paper's main claim is that extending from single-image captioning to image-stream captioning is a meaningful step. However, the paper fails to convincingly demonstrate why this extension is significantly more challenging or valuable than existing single-image captioning. The problem statement is presented as an "obvious next step," but the authors provide no compelling evidence that this specific problem is a critical gap in the literature or that it addresses a real-world need beyond academic curiosity. The motivation example of "visiting NYC" is weakly supported and doesn't establish the problem's importance.

**Insufficient Validation of the Core Contribution**: The paper's primary claim is that their CRCN model, particularly the coherence component, significantly improves performance. However, the improvements over baselines are modest (e.g., 1-2% gains in BLEU scores), and the paper fails to demonstrate why these small gains are meaningful. More critically, the coherence model is simply a straightforward adaptation of prior work (Barzilay & Lapata, 2008), not a novel contribution. The paper doesn't provide evidence that this specific coherence model is necessary for the problem, nor does it compare to other coherence modeling approaches.

**Inadequate Baseline Comparison**: The authors compare against single-sentence generation models, which are fundamentally different from their task (generating sequences of sentences). This is not a fair comparison, as the baselines aren't designed for the sequence generation task. The paper claims there are no prior works addressing this exact problem, but this claim needs verification against recent literature on multi-sentence image captioning or video captioning. The authors should have compared against the closest possible methods, not just single-sentence models.

**Lack of Theoretical Justification**: The paper provides no theoretical analysis of why their approach should work better than alternatives. The model is presented as a "natural" extension of existing techniques, but without evidence that this specific combination is necessary or optimal. The paper would benefit from a more principled justification for their architectural choices.

**Questionable Significance**: The paper doesn't convincingly establish why this work matters. The improvements are small, the problem is a narrow extension of existing work, and the authors don't demonstrate practical applications where this specific approach would be necessary. The evaluation on blog posts is interesting but limited in scope, and the paper doesn't discuss how their approach would generalize to other domains beyond tourism blogs.

## Overall Assessment

This paper presents a minor extension of existing image captioning methods to handle image streams rather than single images. While the problem is technically interesting, the paper fails to convincingly demonstrate that it's a significant problem requiring new methods, nor does it provide sufficient evidence that their proposed CRCN model offers meaningful advantages over simpler alternatives. The coherence model is not novel, the improvements over baselines are marginal, and the evaluation strategy is not rigorous.

The paper would benefit from stronger justification for why this specific problem is important, a more thorough comparison to relevant prior work (including video captioning approaches), and a clearer demonstration that their coherence model is necessary rather than just helpful. Without these, the paper's contribution appears incremental at best.

## Recommendation

This paper is a marginal extension of existing work without sufficient evidence to justify its novelty or significance. While the authors have conducted thorough experiments, the core contribution lacks sufficient rigor to warrant publication at NIPS. I recommend rejection.

**Pro**: Well-organized, clear technical description, thorough dataset construction, good ablation studies.

**Con**: Weak justification for the problem extension, modest performance gains, coherence model is not novel, inadequate baseline comparison, lacks theoretical justification, limited significance.
