Summary and relation to prior work
The paper tackles the problem of describing an image stream with a sequence of natural sentences. Instead of generating a caption per image, the authors retrieve and assemble sentences into a coherent paragraph. The proposed architecture, coherent recurrent convolutional networks (CRCN), combines: (i) a CNN (VGGNet) for image features, (ii) a bidirectional RNN (on sentence-level paragraph vectors) to model sentence content in context, and (iii) an entity-grid local coherence model (Barzilay & Lapata) to bias toward fluent sentence-to-sentence transitions. Training data are derived from blogs (NYC and Disneyland): posts are segmented via paragraph proximity to images, summarized with LSA into key sentences (top-κ augmentation), and embedded with doc2vec. The model is trained with a max-margin ranking loss over aligned/misaligned image–sentence-sequence pairs. At test time, per-image K-NN over VGG features yields candidate sentences; a beam/divide-and-conquer search assembles the best-scoring sequence. The paper reports gains over retrieval and captioning baselines using BLEU/CIDEr/METEOR, retrieval metrics, and AMT studies.

This work relates to extensive image captioning research (e.g., Karpathy & Fei-Fei; Vinyals et al.; Kiros et al.) and video-to-text (Donahue et al.; Rohrbach et al.). Unlike most single-image captioners, it addresses multi-sentence outputs and explicitly adds a coherence model, echoing classical discourse modeling in NLP. It is also related to sequence-level retrieval (Kim et al., 2015), but in the reverse direction.

Strengths
- Problem framing: Moves beyond single-image/single-sentence to sequence-to-sequence, which is relevant for albums, blogs, and “visual storytelling.”
- Architecture: Sensible integration of per-image retrieval with sequence-level re-ranking using BRNN context and an entity-grid coherence signal.
- Data construction: Clever use of blog posts as weakly aligned image–text streams; the pipeline is clearly described (segmentation, summarization, doc2vec, CoreNLP).
- Empirical evaluations: Multiple metrics and AMT user studies; ablation (RCN vs CRCN) demonstrates the added value of the coherence component, especially for longer sequences.
- Practical search: The approximate divide-and-conquer beam search addresses the combinatorial explosion of candidate sequences.

Weaknesses
Quality/technical soundness
- Scoring function ambiguity: Equation (3) adds ∑t gk·vlt, effectively using the same global “coherence” vector gk against each image and summing across t. This double-counts and seems agnostic to sentence ordering with respect to images (order-sensitivity comes only via s_t from the BRNN). The motivation and effect of gk·vlt are unclear; a sequence-level term (e.g., gk·f({vlt}) with an order-aware aggregation) would be more principled.
- Length bias: The sum over t without explicit normalization or length control can bias scores toward longer sequences. The paper does not discuss length normalization or curriculum for variable lengths.
- “Unsupervised” claim: The method relies on heuristically constructed aligned image–sentence pairs (segmentation and LSA summarization) and supervised doc2vec/BRNN training with positive/negative pairs. This is weak supervision, not fully unsupervised.
- Baseline fairness: It is unclear whether generative baselines (CNN+RNN/LSTM) were retrained on the same blog corpus with adapted vocabularies. Using off-the-shelf captioners trained on MSCOCO/Flickr would unfairly handicap them on blog domains. Also missing are strong retrieval baselines that incorporate a simple language model or coherence re-ranking without the proposed architecture.
- Metric definitions: The retrieval metrics (R@K, median rank) are not fully specified for sequence-level matching when ground-truth sentence sequences are held out. If ground-truth sequences are not in the candidate set (since training/test are disjoint), how is recall defined? Similarly, BLEU/METEOR/CIDEr applied to multi-sentence paragraphs without explicit paragraph-level tokenization and multiple references can be misleading.
- Coreference quality: The entity-grid coherence depends on accurate parsing/coref. Blogs are noisy; the paper lacks an error analysis or robustness study regarding the coherence features.

Clarity
- Several equations and dimensions are terse or confusing (e.g., sharing Wf1/Wf2 for O and q, and why dropout prevents collapsing to a single linear map). The role of g, its dimensionality, and how it captures coherence beyond padding to 300 are under-explained.
- The data processing pipeline is well outlined, but key choices (κ=3 for augmentation, history=3 for grids, K=5, M=50) lack sensitivity analyses.

Originality
- The integration of an entity-grid coherence model with a multimodal retrieval/re-ranking pipeline for multi-sentence description is novel relative to common single-image captioning. However, the core pieces (K-NN retrieval, BRNN over sentence embeddings, entity grids) are standard; the novelty is primarily in their combination and problem instantiation.

Significance
- The problem is important and anticipates later “visual storytelling.” However, domain focus (tourism blogs) and retrieval-only generation limit broader impact. Without comparisons on established datasets or release of the blog dataset, adoption may be limited.
- The improvements, while consistent, are modest on language metrics and stronger on retrieval metrics; it is unclear whether they translate to downstream utility beyond the two domains.

Pros for acceptance
- Addresses an under-explored but relevant problem (sequence-to-sequence image–text with coherence).
- Thoughtful architectural integration; ablation suggests the coherence model adds value.
- User studies indicate perceived improvements in fluency/coherence, especially for longer sequences.

Cons for acceptance
- Ambiguities in the scoring function and potential length bias.
- Baseline setup may not be strictly fair; missing stronger sequence-level retrieval baselines.
- Limited generality (retrieval-only, domain-specific blogs); unclear reproducibility/dataset availability.
- Overstated “unsupervised” claim; lack of rigorous analysis of the coherence component’s reliability on noisy text.
- Clarity issues in technical descriptions and evaluation protocol.

Suggestions for improvement
- Clarify and justify Equation (3): consider a true sequence-level coherence term (e.g., g·aggregate(v1…vN) with order-aware aggregation) and length normalization.
- Ensure baseline fairness: retrain CNN+LSTM/RNN on the same blog corpus; include a K-NN + n-gram/LM coherence re-ranker and a text-only BRNN coherence model as baselines; compare to stronger multi-sentence baselines (e.g., image-paragraph generation if available).
- Provide sensitivity analyses for K, M, κ, entity-grid history; analyze sequence length effects and add length normalization.
- Evaluate on or relate to broader datasets (or release your blog dataset and code) to support reproducibility and impact.
- Add error analysis of coref/parsing and ablate the coherence model to diagnose when it helps or hurts.
- Consider stronger sequence encoders (GRU/LSTM) at the sentence level and explore alternative sentence embeddings beyond doc2vec.
- Refine the “unsupervised” claim to “weakly supervised” and discuss limitations of retrieval-based outputs.

Recommendation
Weak reject. The problem is timely and the idea of adding explicit coherence to multi-sentence image description is appealing, but technical ambiguities, potential evaluation/baseline issues, and limited evidence of generality keep it below the NeurIPS bar in its current form. With clearer methodology, fairer baselines, and stronger analyses, this line of work could be impactful.
