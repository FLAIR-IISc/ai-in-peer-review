

# Review

This paper proposes a novel problem formulation: generating a sequence of natural language sentences to describe a stream of images, rather than the typical single-image single-sentence description task. The authors introduce CRCN (Coherent Recurrent Convolutional Networks), which integrates convolutional networks for image representation, bidirectional recurrent networks for sentence modeling, and a local coherence model to ensure fluent transitions between sentences. The key innovation lies in recognizing that users often capture memorable moments through sequences of images (e.g., photo streams from travel experiences), and that existing approaches fail to handle the coherence between consecutive sentences.

The paper's main novelty is well-motivated and clearly articulated. The authors correctly identify that while prior work has focused on single-image captioning (e.g., [5, 8, 9, 12, 14, 15, 26, 21]), the extension to image streams and sentence sequences represents a meaningful next step. They address the data challenge by leveraging blog posts as a source of natural image-text parallel data, which is both clever and practical. The CRCN architecture effectively integrates existing components (CNNs, BRNNs, coherence modeling) into a unified framework specifically designed for the new problem.

The evaluation is thorough and convincing. The authors create two large datasets (NYC and Disneyland) with 11,863 and 7,717 blog posts respectively (78,467 and 60,545 images), making their approach applicable to real-world scenarios. The quantitative results on BLEU, CIDEr, METEOR, and retrieval metrics (top-K recall, median rank) show clear improvements over baselines. Crucially, the user studies via Amazon Mechanical Turk confirm that human annotators prefer the CRCN-generated sequences, particularly for longer image streams where coherence becomes more important.

The paper's strengths are significant: it identifies a genuinely new problem that extends beyond current literature, proposes a novel architecture specifically designed for this problem, and demonstrates convincing results through both quantitative metrics and human evaluation. The authors make a strong case that their approach is the first to address this specific problem.

Minor weaknesses include some methodological details that could be clarified (e.g., the exact implementation of the entity grid transitions in the coherence model), and the fact that the approach builds on existing components rather than introducing entirely new techniques. However, as a reviewer who prioritizes novel ideas over methodological perfection, these are not deal-breakers.

The paper is well-written and clearly organized, with appropriate references to related work. The authors correctly position their contribution as the first to address image stream to sentence sequence generation, which is a meaningful extension of the field. The problem has clear practical applications (e.g., automatically describing photo albums or travel experiences), and the approach is completely unsupervised, making it broadly applicable.

Compared to other NIPS papers, this work stands out for its bold problem formulation rather than incremental improvements. While many papers in this space focus on marginal improvements to single-image captioning, this paper rethinks the fundamental problem setup. The CRCN architecture is not just a minor tweak to existing approaches but a novel integration specifically designed for the new problem.

In terms of significance, this work opens up a new direction for multimodal representation learning. It addresses an important gap in how we model visual narratives (which are inherently sequential) and has potential applications in areas like social media analysis, travel blogging, and personal memory systems. The paper advances the state of the art by demonstrating that sentence sequence generation requires explicit coherence modeling beyond simple image-sentence matching.

I recommend acceptance. This paper presents a genuinely novel problem and a thoughtful approach to solving it. The authors have made a clear contribution to the field by identifying and addressing an important gap in multimodal representation learning. While the technical components are largely built on existing methods, the integration and problem formulation represent a significant step forward. The evaluation is comprehensive, and the human studies provide strong evidence of the approach's effectiveness.

The paper meets all NIPS criteria: it's technically sound, well-written, significantly novel, and has clear significance for the field. As a reviewer who prioritizes novel ideas over methodological perfection, I believe this paper's contribution to expanding the scope of image captioning research is substantial and worthy of publication at NIPS.
