## Review of "Expressing an Image Stream with a Sequence of Natural Sentences"

**Summary:**

This paper tackles the problem of generating a sequence of natural language sentences to describe a stream of images, moving beyond the single image-single sentence paradigm. The authors propose a Coherent Recurrent Convolutional Network (CRCN) which integrates CNNs for image representation, BRNNs for language modeling, and a local coherence model to ensure fluency between sentences. They leverage blog posts as a text-image parallel corpus and demonstrate superior performance against baselines using quantitative metrics (BLEU, Recall) and user studies.

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a reasonable and well-motivated extension to existing image captioning work. The problem of describing image *streams* is a natural progression, and the proposed CRCN architecture addresses key challenges like maintaining coherence across multiple sentences. The use of blog data is clever given the scarcity of paired image sequence/text data. While the novelty is incremental, the technical execution appears sound and the empirical results are encouraging.  I believe this paper is a solid contribution that warrants acceptance, though some minor clarifications and strengthening of the novelty claims would be beneficial.

**Detailed Evaluation based on NIPS Criteria:**

* **Quality (8/10):**  The technical approach seems sound. The integration of CNNs, RNNs, and a coherence model is logical. The experimental setup with quantitative metrics and user studies strengthens the claims. The authors acknowledge limitations (e.g., the approximate search strategy).  However, the theoretical analysis is minimal; a deeper exploration of *why* the coherence model helps, perhaps through ablation studies focusing specifically on coherence-related loss components, would be valuable. The data augmentation strategy (κ=3) feels a bit arbitrarily chosen and could benefit from further justification.
* **Clarity (7/10):** The paper is generally well-written and organized. Fig. 2 effectively illustrates the CRCN architecture.  However, some sections could be more concise. For example, the lengthy description of related work, while thorough, could be streamlined.  The notation is mostly clear, but defining variables earlier in the equations would improve readability. The description of the coherence model, specifically the "entity grid", could be clearer with a small illustrative example.
* **Originality (6/10):**  While the *problem* of image stream description is relatively unexplored, the individual components of the CRCN (CNNs, RNNs, coherence models) are not novel in themselves. The main contribution lies in the *combination* of these components for this specific task. The authors acknowledge this but perhaps downplay existing work that incorporates coherence (even briefly). A more focused discussion of how CRCN distinguishes itself from, say, video captioning approaches that similarly address temporal coherence, is needed.
* **Significance (7/10):** The ability to automatically describe image streams has potential applications in areas like tourism, event summarization, and assistive technologies. The results suggest the approach is effective, and the user studies lend further credibility.  However, the impact hinges on the generalizability of the approach beyond the specific blog datasets used.  Demonstrating performance on other types of image streams (e.g., from social media, robotics) would significantly boost the significance.



**Strengths:**

*   Addresses a relevant and under-explored problem.
*   CRCN architecture is a reasonable and well-motivated approach.
*   Leverages a readily available dataset (blogs) to overcome data scarcity.
*   Strong empirical evaluation with both quantitative metrics and user studies.
*   Clear writing and good organization overall.

**Weaknesses:**

*   Limited theoretical analysis.
*   Incremental novelty – the individual components are well-established.
*   Justification for some parameter choices (e.g., κ=3) is weak.
*   Generalizability to domains beyond blogs is unclear.
*   Coherence model explanation could be improved with an example.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

*   Addresses a meaningful problem with a practical approach.
*   Solid empirical results demonstrate effectiveness.
*   Well-written and clearly explained.
*   Provides a useful baseline for future research in image stream description.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   Incremental novelty - doesn't represent a major paradigm shift.
*   Limited theoretical grounding.
*   Dependence on a specific data source (blogs) raises questions about generalizability.




**Recommendation:**

**Accept with Minor Revisions.**  I recommend accepting this paper with the following suggestions:

1.  **Strengthen Novelty Claims:**  More clearly articulate how CRCN differs from related work, particularly in video captioning and existing coherence models.
2.  **Expand Ablation Studies:**  Conduct more focused ablation studies to specifically assess the contribution of the coherence model.
3.  **Justify Parameter Choices:**  Provide more justification for parameter settings like κ.
4.  **Discuss Generalizability:**  Explicitly discuss the limitations of the current dataset and potential strategies to improve generalizability.
5.  **Clarify Coherence Model:** Add a small, illustrative example to explain the entity grid concept within the coherence model description.
