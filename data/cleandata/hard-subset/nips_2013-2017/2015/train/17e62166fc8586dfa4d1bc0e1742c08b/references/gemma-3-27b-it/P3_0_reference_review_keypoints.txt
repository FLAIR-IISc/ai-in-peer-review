- Tackles the problem of generating coherent sentence sequences for image streams rather than single-image captioning.  
- Proposes the Coherent Recurrent Convolutional Network (CRCN) combining CNNs, bidirectional RNNs, and a coherence model.  
- Uses blog posts as a weakly supervised source of parallel image-text data for training.  
- Builds on established techniques (CNNs, RNNs, coherence modeling) but unifies them for the sequence-to-sequence task.  
- Claims the coherence model as a key contribution to maintain fluency across multiple sentences.  
- Empirical evaluation uses quantitative metrics (BLEU, Recall) and human studies (AMT).  
- CRCN outperforms several baseline methods on the blog dataset.  
- Problem formulation is a natural and relevant extension of image captioning.  
- Architecture design is sensible and addresses sequence coherence.  
- Dataset creation via blogs is a clever heuristic to obtain image-text pairs.  
- Results are promising and demonstrate effectiveness on a challenging dataset.  
- Paper lacks strong theoretical justification for the chosen architecture and coherence model.  
- Coherence model implementation details are vague and may struggle with informal blog language.  
- Baseline comparisons are not sufficiently strong or fully optimized (e.g., lack of advanced attention baselines).  
- Ablation study is limited; missing analyses like removing bidirectionality or assessing data augmentation impact.  
- Related work discussion is superficial, especially regarding differences with video captioning and prior image-sequence retrieval work.  
- Writing contains awkward phrasing and repetition and needs editing.  
- Originality is moderate: novel combination/application but uses well-known components.  
- Potential significance is high for applications like storytelling and accessibility but is limited by reliance on blog data and generalizability concerns.  
- Arguments for acceptance: relevant problem, reasonable architecture, strong empirical evidence, mixed evaluation methods.  
- Arguments against acceptance: weak theoretical grounding, under-evaluated coherence model, weak baselines, limited ablations, shallow related work, and writing issues.  
- Recommendation: weak reject with major revisions requested.  
- Suggested revisions: strengthen theoretical justification (esp. coherence model), expand ablation studies, include more competitive baselines, provide detailed coherence-model analysis and examples, deepen related work discussion, and improve writing clarity.
