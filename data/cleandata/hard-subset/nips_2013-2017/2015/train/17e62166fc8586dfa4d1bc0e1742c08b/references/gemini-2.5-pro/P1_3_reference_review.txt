**Summary of the Paper**

This paper addresses the novel problem of generating a sequence of natural language sentences to describe a stream of images. This extends the well-studied task of single-image captioning to a sequence-to-sequence setting, which is motivated by real-world scenarios like describing a photo album from a trip. The authors propose a new architecture, the Coherent Recurrent Convolutional Network (CRCN), which integrates three main components: a Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) to extract image features, a Bidirectional Recurrent Neural Network (BRNN) to model the semantic flow of the sentence sequence, and an entity-based local coherence model to ensure smooth, natural transitions between sentences. A key contribution is the use of online blog posts as a source of parallel image-text sequence data, which is cleverly pre-processed through text segmentation and summarization. The approach is retrieval-based: for a query image stream, it first finds candidate sentences for each image and then uses the learned CRCN model to rank and select the best overall sentence sequence that is both visually relevant and textually coherent. The method is evaluated on two large-scale blog datasets (NYC and Disneyland) and shows significant improvements over adapted state-of-the-art single-image captioning baselines, as measured by automatic metrics and human studies on Amazon Mechanical Turk.

**Strengths**

1.  **Novel and Significant Problem:** The paper tackles a logical and important next step in visual-linguistic research. Moving from single image-sentence pairs to image stream-paragraph pairs is a well-motivated problem with clear applications. To my knowledge, this is one of the first papers to formally define and address this task.

2.  **Innovative Model Architecture:** The proposed CRCN model is a clever combination of modern deep learning techniques and a classic, interpretable NLP model. The integration of the entity-based coherence model [1] to explicitly model discourse structure is the main novelty and is well-justified. Unlike end-to-end models that might implicitly learn some coherence, CRCN enforces it with a proven technique, which the experiments show is particularly effective for longer sequences.

3.  **Strong and Thorough Experimental Evaluation:** The evaluation is comprehensive. The authors compare against several strong and relevant baselines adapted for the new task. The inclusion of ablation studies (CRCN vs. RCN vs. 1NN) clearly demonstrates the value of the individual components, particularly the BRNN and the coherence model. The combination of automatic metrics (BLEU, CIDEr, etc.) and human evaluation via AMT provides a convincing case for the model's superiority. The final AMT experiment showing that the coherence model's benefit increases with sequence length is a particularly strong piece of evidence supporting the core claim of the paper.

4.  **Resourceful Data Collection and Processing:** The idea of leveraging blog posts as a source for weakly-supervised training data is highly practical and scalable. While the pre-processing is heuristic-based, it provides a viable pipeline for creating a large-scale dataset for this new task, which is a contribution in itself.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Clarity on Model Combination:** The description of how the different model components are combined in Section 3.3 and Equation (3) could be clearer. The compatibility score is defined as `Skl = ∑t (skt · vlt + gk · vlt)`. This formulation suggests that the coherence-derived vector `gk` (which is global for the sentence sequence `k`) is dotted with *each* image feature vector `vlt`. This effectively adds the contribution of the coherence score N times, once for each image. The intuition behind this specific formulation is not well explained. A more intuitive approach might have been to add a single global coherence score to the sum of local image-sentence scores, i.e., `Skl = (∑t skt · vlt) + score(gk, {vlt})`. A clearer justification for the chosen formula would strengthen the paper's technical quality.

2.  **Limitations of Data Pre-processing:** The paper relies on a pipeline of heuristics for data creation (paragraph tokenization, distance-based image-text alignment, LSA summarization). This process likely introduces noise. For instance, a single text block might refer to multiple images, or the LSA summarization might fail to extract the most relevant sentence. The paper would be more complete if it discussed the potential impact of this noise on the model's performance and the limitations of this data creation strategy.

3.  **Retrieval-based Nature:** The method is framed as "expressing" an image stream, which implies generation, but the underlying mechanism is purely retrieval-based. This means the system can only output sentences (or concatenations of sentences) that exist in its training database. This is a valid and powerful approach, but the paper should be more upfront about this distinction and its implications (e.g., lack of compositional creativity, potential for retrieving near-duplicates). This is not a fundamental flaw but a point of positioning and clarity.

**Arguments for Acceptance (Pros):**
*   Addresses a novel, important, and challenging problem in vision and language.
*   The proposed CRCN architecture is original in its integration of a classic NLP coherence model into a deep learning framework.
*   The experimental results are strong, comprehensive, and convincingly demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method and its components.
*   The work provides a valuable new task definition and a practical method for creating a large-scale dataset.

**Arguments for Rejection (Cons):**
*   The technical description of how the coherence score is integrated with image features lacks clarity and strong justification.
*   The potential limitations and noise introduced by the heuristic-heavy data pre-processing pipeline are not discussed.
*   The paper could be clearer about the distinction between retrieval and generation.

**Overall Assessment**

This is a high-quality paper that introduces a novel and significant problem and proposes an effective and well-evaluated solution. The strengths, particularly the novelty of the task, the cleverness of the hybrid model, and the rigor of the experiments, substantially outweigh the weaknesses. The lack of clarity on one aspect of the scoring function is a minor point that can be easily addressed in a final version. The paper makes a clear and demonstrable advance in the state of the art for multimodal research. It is likely to inspire follow-up work on generating coherent, paragraph-level descriptions for visual data. I recommend acceptance.
