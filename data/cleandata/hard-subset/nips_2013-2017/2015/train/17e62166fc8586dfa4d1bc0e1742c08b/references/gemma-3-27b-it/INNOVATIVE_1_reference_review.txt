This paper tackles a genuinely interesting and relatively unexplored problem: generating coherent multi-sentence descriptions for image *streams*, moving beyond the single image-single sentence paradigm. The core idea – leveraging blog posts as a noisy source of image-text parallelism and explicitly modeling coherence – is a bold and potentially impactful direction. While the implementation isn’t pristine and the validation isn’t exhaustive, the novelty of the problem and the proposed approach outweigh the shortcomings. I find myself very excited by the core concept and believe it has the potential to open up a new research avenue.

The authors effectively motivate the problem, framing it within the common scenario of users capturing experiences as a series of images. The CRCN architecture, integrating CNNs, RNNs, and a local coherence model, is a reasonable approach. The use of blog data, while a necessary workaround for the lack of paired image-sequence-text datasets, is intelligently handled, and the data augmentation strategy appears sensible. 

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The core contribution – extending image captioning to image *streams* with *sequences* of sentences – is genuinely novel. This is a clear step beyond existing work.
*   **Problem Motivation:** The problem is well-motivated by a realistic use case, making the research relevant.
*   **Coherence Modeling:** The explicit inclusion of a coherence model is a key strength. The authors correctly identify that sequential descriptions require more than just individual image-sentence alignment; flow and consistency are crucial.
*   **Resourcefulness:** Leveraging blog data demonstrates resourcefulness in tackling the data scarcity problem.
*   **Solid Baseline Comparison:** Comparisons to several relevant baselines demonstrate the efficacy of the approach.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Evaluation Limitations:** The quantitative evaluation, while thorough, relies on standard captioning metrics (BLEU, CIDEr, METEOR) which may not be ideally suited for evaluating multi-sentence coherence. The user study helps address this, but could be expanded.
*   **Data Quality:** The reliance on blog data introduces noise and potential biases. The impact of these is not fully explored. While acknowledged, a more detailed analysis of the blog data characteristics would be beneficial.
*   **Implementation Details:** The paper lacks some implementation specifics. More details regarding hyperparameter tuning and computational costs would be useful for reproducibility.
*   **Coherence Model Specifics:** The description of the coherence model is somewhat high-level. More detailed explanation of the entity grid creation and transition frequency calculation would enhance understanding.
*   **Ablation Study Limitations:** The ablation study comparing CRCN to RCN is not entirely convincing; the difference in performance is greater with retrieval metrics, and the authors rightly point out the length of the image sequence matters, but a more systematic evaluation across sequence lengths would be valuable.



**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a genuinely novel and potentially significant research direction.
*   The proposed approach (CRCN) is a reasonable and well-motivated solution to a challenging problem.
*   The experimental results, despite some limitations, demonstrate the effectiveness of the approach.
*   The authors acknowledge the limitations of their work and suggest directions for future research.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The evaluation is not entirely comprehensive and relies on metrics not perfectly suited to the task.
*   The reliance on noisy blog data introduces potential biases.
*   Some implementation details are lacking, hindering reproducibility.




**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a valuable contribution to the field. While not perfect, the novelty and potential impact of the work outweigh the identified weaknesses. I am willing to overlook the relatively minor flaws in implementation and validation, given the boldness of the approach and its promise for future research. I recommend **acceptance**.  The authors should be encouraged to address the evaluation limitations and provide more implementation details in future work. I anticipate that this paper will inspire further research in the exciting area of multi-sentence image stream description.
