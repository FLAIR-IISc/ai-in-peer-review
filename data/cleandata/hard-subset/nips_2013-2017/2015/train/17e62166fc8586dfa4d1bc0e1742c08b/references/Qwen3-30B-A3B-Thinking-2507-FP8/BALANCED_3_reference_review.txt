

This paper proposes "coherent recurrent convolutional networks" (CRCN) for generating sentence sequences from image streams, framed as a retrieval problem using blog posts as training data. The core contribution is extending image captioning from single-image/single-sentence to sequence-to-sequence, with a novel coherence model for fluent transitions between sentences. The problem is motivated by tourism blog examples (e.g., NYC photo streams), and the authors collect 20K+ blog posts with 140K images for evaluation.

**Strengths**  
1. **Novel problem formulation**: The extension from single-image captioning to image-stream-to-sentence-sequence is well-motivated and addresses a clear gap (e.g., unlike video captioning [23,32], which generates one sentence per video).  
2. **Robust evaluation**: The large-scale dataset (20K+ blogs) and dual evaluation (quantitative metrics + AMT user studies) are strong. The AMT results convincingly show CRCN’s superiority over RCN (without coherence) for longer sequences (Table 2), validating the coherence model’s impact.  
3. **Technical soundness**: The integration of CNNs, BRNNs, and entity-based coherence (from [1]) is well-executed. The ablation studies (Table 1) demonstrate the necessity of both BRNN and coherence components. The retrieval-based approach (Section 3.5) is pragmatic given the lack of sequence-to-sequence training data.  

**Weaknesses**  
1. **Misrepresentation of contribution**: The paper claims "generating" sentence sequences, but the method is strictly *retrieval* (Section 3.5: "retrieve the best sentence sequence"). This conflates generation with retrieval, misleading readers and reviewers. The title ("Expressing... with... Sentences") and abstract amplify this confusion. *This is a critical flaw*: the work does not *generate* novel text but selects from existing blog sentences.  
2. **Weak baselines**: Baselines like "CNN+RNN" (Section 4.1) are not fair comparisons. They naively concatenate single-sentence outputs, ignoring the sequence nature of the problem. Better baselines would include sequence-to-sequence models (e.g., [3, 19]) or explicit coherence models.  
3. **Limited significance**: The method is unsupervised but domain-specific (tourism blogs). The claim "applicable to any domain with blog posts" is unsubstantiated—no cross-domain tests are shown. The blog-data dependency also limits scalability (e.g., blogs rarely exist for medical or technical image streams).  
4. **Clarity issues**: Section 3.3’s equation (2) claims "Wf2Wf1 does not reduce to a single linear mapping," but with dropout, it remains linear. This is misleading. The coherence model (Section 3.2) also lacks detail on how entity transitions are computed from parse trees.  

**Originality Assessment**  
The problem extension is novel, but the *technical approach* is incremental. The coherence model [1] is reused verbatim, and the core architecture (CNN+RNN) draws directly from [9,30]. The paper does not sufficiently differentiate its "integration" from prior multimodal work (e.g., [3,19]).  

**Significance**  
The work demonstrates a useful pipeline for tourism contexts but does not advance the *state of the art* in generative modeling. The retrieval-based nature means it cannot produce novel descriptions (e.g., "The Eiffel Tower sparkled at sunset"), only recombine existing blog text. This limits its impact on language generation research.  

**Recommendation**  
**Reject**. The misrepresentation of the method as "generation" (vs. retrieval) is a fundamental flaw that undermines the paper’s contribution. The problem is valid, but the solution is not novel *in its approach*, and the significance is overstated. The authors should clarify this upfront and, if they wish to pursue the problem, reframe it as a *retrieval task* (e.g., "Retrieving Coherent Sentence Sequences from Image Streams").  

**Key Suggestions for Improvement**  
1. **Correct the core premise**: Explicitly state that the method retrieves *existing* sentences from a blog corpus (not generates new text). Revise title/abstract/contributions to reflect this.  
2. **Strengthen baselines**: Compare against sequence-to-sequence models (e.g., [3]) and coherence-aware baselines (e.g., [11]’s retrieval framework).  
3. **Expand evaluation**: Test on non-tourism datasets (e.g., sports events, scientific images) to validate domain generality.  
4. **Clarify technical details**: Explain why the coherence model’s 64-dimensional vector is sufficient, and resolve the equation (2) ambiguity.  
5. **Reframe significance**: Focus on the *retrieval* application (e.g., "automated blog summarization for image-heavy content") rather than generative language modeling.  

This work has solid engineering but misaligns with NIPS’ focus on *generative* advances. With the above revisions, it could become a strong contribution to retrieval-based multimodal tasks, but as submitted, it overstates its novelty and impact.
