### Summary of the Paper

This paper addresses the novel problem of generating a coherent sequence of natural language sentences to describe a stream of images. This extends the well-studied task of single-image captioning to a multi-image, multi-sentence setting, motivated by real-world scenarios like summarizing photo albums from a trip. The authors propose a multimodal architecture named Coherent Recurrent Convolutional Networks (CRCN). This model integrates three main components: (1) a Convolutional Neural Network (CNN), specifically VGGNet, to extract visual features from each image in the stream; (2) a Bidirectional Recurrent Neural Network (BRNN) to model the semantic flow and context of the sentence sequence, operating on paragraph vector representations of the sentences; and (3) an entity-based local coherence model, based on the work of Barzilay and Lapata (2008), to ensure smooth and logical transitions between sentences by analyzing the grammatical roles of discourse entities.

To train this model, the authors propose a practical method for collecting large-scale parallel data by leveraging online blog posts. Each blog post, with its associated images, is pre-processed through text segmentation and summarization to create aligned image-sentence stream pairs. The CRCN model is trained using a max-margin structured loss that aims to make the compatibility score of an aligned image-sentence stream pair higher than that of misaligned pairs. At inference time, the task is framed as a retrieval problem. For a given query image stream, candidate sentence sequences are constructed from the sentences associated with the K-nearest training images. The CRCN model then ranks these candidates to find the most suitable descriptive sequence. Due to the combinatorial complexity, an approximate divide-and-conquer search with beam search is employed.

The work is evaluated on two large datasets of blog posts (NYC and Disneyland) collected by the authors. The proposed CRCN model is compared against several state-of-the-art single-sentence generation baselines (adapted for this sequence task) and ablated versions of their own model. Performance is measured using both automatic language metrics (BLEU, CIDEr, METEOR) and retrieval metrics (R@K, median rank), as well as a user study on Amazon Mechanical Turk. The results demonstrate that the proposed method outperforms the baselines, and that the explicit modeling of coherence provides significant benefits, especially for longer sequences.

### Strengths

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The paper introduces a novel and important problem that is a logical and significant advancement over single-image captioning. The task of generating a narrative for an image collection has numerous practical applications, such as automatic photo album summarization and blog post generation. This work is one of the first to formally define and provide a strong baseline for this problem, making it a valuable contribution that is likely to stimulate further research in this area.

2.  **Soundness of the Technical Approach:** The proposed CRCN architecture is well-motivated for the task. It thoughtfully combines established and powerful techniques: a state-of-the-art CNN for image understanding, a BRNN for modeling sequential dependencies in language, and an explicit coherence model to capture discourse-level properties. The integration of an entity-based coherence model is a key element that distinguishes this work from a naive extension of single-image captioning models, as it directly addresses the challenge of making the output text fluent and story-like, which is crucial given that images in a stream can have sharp visual discontinuities.

3.  **Comprehensive and Rigorous Evaluation:** The experimental validation is thorough. The authors compare their method against multiple strong, contemporary baselines from the image captioning literature. The inclusion of ablation studies (comparing CRCN to a variant without the coherence model, RCN, and a simple 1NN baseline) effectively isolates and quantifies the contribution of the key components of their model. The use of both automatic metrics and human evaluation via AMT provides a comprehensive picture of the model's performance. The user study design, which tests sequences of different lengths to specifically highlight the importance of the coherence model, is a particularly strong aspect of the evaluation.

4.  **Practical Data Collection Methodology:** A major challenge for this new task is the lack of established datasets. The authors' approach of mining blog posts for weakly-aligned image-sentence streams is a creative and scalable solution to this problem, making the proposed framework practical for application in various domains without requiring expensive manual annotation.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Clarity of Model Formulation:** Certain aspects of the model's formulation could be clarified for better understanding and reproducibility. In Section 3.3, the mechanism for combining the outputs of the BRNN and the coherence model into the final score is not fully transparent. The score function in Equation (3) includes a term `gk · vlt`, which suggests a compatibility score between a global coherence representation for the whole sentence sequence (`gk`) and the feature vector of *each individual image* (`vlt`). The justification for this design choice and the precise derivation of `gk` from the model's final layers (Equation 2) are not clearly explained.

2.  **Limitations of the Retrieval-Based Framework:** The proposed method is fundamentally a retrieval-based system, as it constructs output sequences by selecting and arranging sentences from the training corpus. The paper's title and descriptive language could be interpreted as suggesting a fully generative model. This retrieval-based nature imposes a significant limitation: the system cannot generate novel descriptions for scenes, objects, or actions not explicitly described in the training sentences. A more explicit discussion of this limitation and its implications would strengthen the paper.

3.  **Dependence on Pre-processing:** The quality of the training data is heavily dependent on the text segmentation and summarization pre-processing steps. The text summarization relies on LSA, a method from 1999. It is unclear how sensitive the final model's performance is to the quality of this summarization step. Errors or suboptimal summaries produced at this stage could introduce considerable noise into the training data, potentially limiting the model's performance.

4.  **Novelty of Components:** While the problem formulation is novel, the constituent parts of the CRCN model—the CNN, BRNN, and the entity grid coherence model—are existing techniques. The main technical novelty lies in the specific architecture that combines these components for this new task. As such, the work is more of an effective and novel application and integration of known methods rather than a fundamental methodological advance.
