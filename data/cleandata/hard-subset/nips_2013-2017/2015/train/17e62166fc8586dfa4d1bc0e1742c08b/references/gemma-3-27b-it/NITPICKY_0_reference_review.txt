This paper addresses the problem of generating sequential sentences for a stream of images, a natural extension of single-image captioning. The authors propose a Coherent Recurrent Convolutional Network (CRCN) integrating CNNs, BRNNs, and a local coherence model. While the problem is relevant and the general approach reasonable, the paper suffers from a multitude of minor, yet distracting, issues that collectively detract from its impact.

The core idea of leveraging blog posts as a weakly supervised dataset is reasonable, given the difficulty of obtaining parallel image-sentence stream data. However, the pre-processing pipeline, while described, lacks sufficient detail regarding hyperparameter selection (e.g., the choice of κ=3 for data augmentation). The LSA-based summarization, while standard, feels somewhat arbitrarily chosen and its impact not rigorously examined. The justification for choosing 300-dimensional paragraph vectors seems ad-hoc. 

The CRCN architecture itself is a complex assembly of existing components. The motivation for separating input activations in the BRNN (xft and xbt) is stated as “empirically leads a better performance” – a weak justification lacking analysis. The local coherence model, based on parse trees and entity grids, introduces significant complexity. While theoretically sound, its implementation details are vague. The 64-dimensional representation based on transition frequencies is not thoroughly explained; the choice of a history parameter of three appears arbitrary.  The combination of the BRNN and coherence model through fully connected layers, while functional, lacks a clear theoretical basis. The dropout rates (0.5 and 0.7) also feel arbitrarily chosen.

The experimental setup includes comparisons to several baselines, which is positive. However, the baseline selection appears somewhat dated. The choice to reimplement rather than directly compare against more recent, sophisticated captioning models is questionable. The quantitative results show improvements, but the margins are not consistently large, raising concerns about practical significance. The AMT studies support the quantitative findings, but the experimental setup (limiting stream length to 5 images) seems designed to favor the coherence model, potentially inflating the results.  The justification for extending the AMT study to longer streams *after* seeing the initial results feels like post-hoc rationalization. 

Numerous stylistic and grammatical errors throughout the text are distracting. For example, “albeit under-addressed in current literature” is awkward phrasing. The repeated use of “Thus” at the beginning of sentences becomes monotonous. The frequent use of parentheses disrupts the flow of reading. Reference formatting is inconsistent (e.g., some references include years, others do not). Equation (2) is poorly written and requires significant effort to decipher.  The terminology is not always consistent (e.g., "image stream" vs. "sequence of images"). Several sentences are overly verbose and could be simplified. The use of abbreviations without prior definition (e.g., AMT before it is introduced) is problematic. The phrase “jointly learning outperforms learning the two terms with separate parameters” is unclear.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Addresses a relevant and under-explored problem.
*   Presents a reasonably well-motivated, albeit complex, architecture.
*   Demonstrates improvements over baseline methods.
*   Includes both quantitative and qualitative evaluation.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The numerous minor flaws in writing, implementation details, and justification diminish the impact of the work.
*   The experimental setup and baseline comparisons are not entirely convincing.
*   The complexity of the CRCN architecture may outweigh its benefits.
*   The contributions, while present, do not represent a substantial advancement in the field.
*   The paper lacks a critical self-assessment of its limitations.



**Overall Assessment:** Reject. While the paper identifies a worthwhile research direction, the numerous imperfections in its execution and presentation prevent it from reaching the standard expected of a NIPS publication. A significant revision addressing the identified issues would be necessary for reconsideration. The level of detail and justification is insufficient for a top-tier conference like NIPS. The authors need to demonstrate a greater level of rigor and attention to detail throughout the paper.
