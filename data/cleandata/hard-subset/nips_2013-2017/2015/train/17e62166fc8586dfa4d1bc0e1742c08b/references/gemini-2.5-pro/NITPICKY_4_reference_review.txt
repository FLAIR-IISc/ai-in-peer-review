This paper addresses the task of generating a sequence of natural language sentences to describe a stream of images, a problem the authors pose as a logical extension to the well-studied single-image captioning task. To this end, they propose a novel architecture, the Coherent Recurrent Convolutional Network (CRCN). This model integrates three components: a Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) for image feature extraction, a Bidirectional Recurrent Neural Network (BRNN) to model the flow of text content, and an entity-based local coherence model to ensure smooth syntactic and referential transitions between sentences. The model is trained and evaluated on a new dataset curated from blog posts, which provide weakly-aligned streams of images and text. The authors demonstrate through automatic metrics and human studies that their approach outperforms several baselines adapted from single-image captioning.

**Strengths:**

1.  The problem formulation is a timely and valuable extension of existing work. Moving from single images to image streams and from single sentences to coherent paragraphs is a challenging and practical next step for the field.
2.  The core idea of explicitly modeling sentence-level coherence in addition to semantic content flow is sound. The authors correctly identify that an RNN alone may not capture the syntactic and discourse-level properties that make a paragraph fluent, especially when visual content changes abruptly between images.
3.  The proposed method of mining blog posts for weakly-supervised training data is a resourceful approach to tackle the data scarcity problem inherent to this new task.

**Weaknesses:**

Despite the promising direction, the paper is undermined by a substantial number of technical inaccuracies, methodological weaknesses, and a general lack of rigor in its presentation and evaluation.

**1. Technical and Methodological Flaws:**

*   **Misunderstanding of Dropout:** In Section 3.3, the authors claim, "the multiplication Wf2Wf1 of the last two FC layers does not reduce to a single linear mapping, thanks to dropout." This statement is incorrect and reveals a fundamental misunderstanding of dropout. At test time, when inference is performed, dropout is inactive (or accounted for by weight scaling), and the composition of two linear layers is mathematically equivalent to a single linear layer. This error calls into question the authors' technical grasp of the models they are using.
*   **Mischaracterization of BRNNs:** In Section 3.1, the paper states, "Unlike other BRNN models, we separate the input activation into forward and backward ones with different sets of parameters W fi and W b i". This is not a novel contribution; having separate sets of parameters for the forward and backward passes is the standard definition of a Bidirectional RNN.
*   **Crude Model Integration:** The integration of the classic coherence model from Barzilay and Lapata (2008) is rudimentary. The 64-dimensional feature vector is projected to 300 dimensions simply by "zero-padding." This is an inefficient and unprincipled way to merge feature spaces. A simple learned linear projection would be a more standard and effective approach.
*   **Dated Components:** The choice of Latent Semantic Analysis (LSA) for text summarization and paragraph vectors for text representation feels dated in the context of a deep learning paper. More contemporary neural summarization and sentence embedding techniques could have been considered.
*   **Unsubstantiated Claims:** The paper makes several claims without providing evidence. In Section 3.5, it is asserted that the approximate beam search "achieves almost optimal solutions," but no experiment is presented to validate this against an exhaustive search on a smaller problem.

**2. Evaluation and Experimental Design:**

*   **Strawman Baselines:** The baselines are adapted by simply concatenating the output of single-image captioning models. This is a weak adaptation that is almost guaranteed to lack coherence. A fairer comparison would involve more sophisticated adaptations, such as using the final state of the RNN from generating the description for image `t-1` to initialize the generation for image `t`. As it stands, the experimental setup is heavily biased in favor of the proposed model.
*   **Inconclusive Quantitative Results:** The authors' primary contribution is the coherence model, yet the quantitative results in Table 1 show that the full CRCN model offers only marginal gains over the RCN variant (without the coherence model) on language metrics like BLEU, METEOR, and CIDEr. The authors themselves concede this, attributing it to the limitations of the metrics. While plausible, it also strongly suggests the tangible impact of the coherence model on the generated text quality is minimal. The main improvement is in retrieval metrics, which indicates better ranking of the ground truth, not necessarily better generated text.
*   **Reporting Errors in User Studies:** The text in Section 4.3 makes a crucial point about coherence becoming more important for longer sequences (8-10 images) and refers the reader to "the last column of Table 2" for these results. However, Table 2 does not contain this column or these results. This is a significant reporting error that leaves a key argument of the paper entirely unsubstantiated.

**3. Clarity and Presentation:**

*   **Overstated Claims:** The paper claims the approach is "completely unsupervised" (Section 1). This is misleading. Using blog posts where humans have intentionally paired images with text is a form of weak supervision, not unsupervised learning.
*   **Formatting and Writing Style:** The paper is replete with minor but distracting errors. The citation list in the introduction `[5, 8, 9, 12, 14, 15, 26, 21, 30]` is not numerically sorted. There are grammatical awkwardnesses ("a hike of interest") and typos ("100 ne examples"). The bibliography is sloppily formatted, with incomplete entries (e.g., [1]). The notation is at times inconsistent or poorly defined (e.g., `Zt` in Fig 2(b) is not defined in the text).

**Summary and Recommendation:**

While the paper introduces an interesting problem and a conceptually sound high-level approach, the execution is critically flawed. The combination of a fundamental technical misunderstanding of dropout, weak and poorly justified methodological choices, an unfair baseline comparison, and unsubstantiated claims makes it impossible to trust the results or the conclusions drawn from them. The numerous presentation errors further detract from the paper's quality.

Although the direction of research is promising, this specific manuscript in its current form falls well below the standard for publication at NIPS. A complete overhaul addressing the technical errors, strengthening the methodology, and conducting a more rigorous and transparent evaluation would be required.

**Recommendation: Reject**
