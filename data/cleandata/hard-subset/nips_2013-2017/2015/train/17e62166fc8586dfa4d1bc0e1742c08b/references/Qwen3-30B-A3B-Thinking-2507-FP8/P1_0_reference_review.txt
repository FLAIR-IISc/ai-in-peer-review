

# Peer Review: "Expressing an Image Stream with a Sequence of Natural Sentences"

## Summary

This paper introduces a novel problem setting and solution for generating a sequence of natural language sentences to describe a stream of images, rather than the typical single-image captioning task. The authors propose Coherent Recurrent Convolutional Networks (CRCN), which integrates convolutional neural networks for image representation, bidirectional recurrent networks for sentence modeling, and a local coherence model to ensure fluent transitions between sentences. The key innovation lies in extending the problem from single-image/single-sentence to image-stream/sentence-sequence, with the authors using blog posts as a natural source of image-text sequences (since blogs often contain a sequence of images with associated text). The paper evaluates on NYC and Disneyland blog datasets (over 20K posts, 140K images) and demonstrates superior performance over baselines including Karpathy et al. (2015), Kiros et al. (2014), and Vinyals et al. (2015).

## Strengths

1. **Original Problem Formulation**: The paper identifies a meaningful gap in the literature—extending image captioning to image streams with sequence of sentences—rather than merely extending existing single-image captioning methods. This is a significant conceptual leap with clear real-world relevance (e.g., summarizing travel photo albums).

2. **Strong Experimental Validation**: The paper provides thorough evaluation with both quantitative metrics (BLEU, CIDEr, METEOR, top-K recall) and user studies via AMT. The ablation studies effectively demonstrate the contribution of each component (especially the coherence model).

3. **Practical Data Source**: Using blog posts as training data is clever and practical, as blogs naturally contain image-text sequences that tell stories. The preprocessing pipeline (text segmentation, summarization) is well-justified.

4. **Technical Soundness**: The architecture integrates well-established components (CNN, BRNN, coherence modeling) in a novel way for the specific problem. The training procedure using max-margin loss is standard but appropriate for this task.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited Theoretical Analysis**: While the experimental results are strong, the paper lacks theoretical analysis explaining *why* their approach works better. For instance, why does the coherence model specifically improve performance beyond just better sentence representation?

2. **Overstated Novelty**: The paper claims to be "the first to address" this problem, but related work (e.g., [11] on "Ranking and Retrieval of Image Sequences from Multiple Paragraph Queries") addresses a similar problem (though in the opposite direction). The paper should better distinguish itself from this related work.

3. **Evaluation Scope**: The experiments are limited to tourism blogs (NYC, Disneyland), which may not generalize to other domains. While the authors claim the approach is "completely unsupervised and thus applicable to any domain," they don't demonstrate this with cross-domain experiments.

4. **Implementation Details**: Some critical implementation details are missing (e.g., exact hyperparameters for the coherence model, computational complexity of the search strategy). The "divide-and-conquer" strategy for candidate retrieval is described but lacks sufficient detail for reproduction.

## Quality and Significance Assessment

**Quality**: The paper is technically sound with a well-designed methodology and thorough evaluation. The claims are well-supported by both quantitative results and user studies. However, the lack of theoretical analysis and some missing implementation details slightly limit the quality.

**Clarity**: The paper is generally well-written and organized. The figures (Fig. 1-3) effectively illustrate the problem and results. Some sections (particularly the coherence model description) could be clearer, but the paper is sufficiently detailed for experts to reproduce the work.

**Originality**: The problem extension to image streams with sentence sequences is genuinely novel, though the specific architecture integrates known components. The paper's main contribution is the problem formulation and the integration of coherence modeling for sequence generation. The authors appropriately distinguish their work from single-image captioning and video description tasks.

**Significance**: The work addresses a meaningful real-world problem with clear applications (e.g., photo album summarization). The results show consistent improvements over baselines, suggesting practical utility. The approach could be extended to other domains with blog-like data, making it potentially impactful for multimodal learning.

## Recommendation

I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions. The paper makes a meaningful contribution to the field by addressing a novel problem (image stream description) with a technically sound approach. While the theoretical analysis could be strengthened, the experimental validation is strong and the problem is clearly significant.

## Specific Suggestions for Improvement

1. Clarify the relationship with [11] (Ranking and Retrieval of Image Sequences) in the related work section to better distinguish the paper's contribution.

2. Include a brief theoretical discussion on why the coherence model is necessary for sequence generation (beyond just "to make output passages fluent").

3. Add a sentence or two about the limitations of the blog-based data (e.g., potential bias toward tourism content).

4. Provide more details about the "divide-and-conquer" candidate retrieval strategy (e.g., the exact recurrence structure).

5. Consider adding a small cross-domain experiment (e.g., using a different blog category) to better demonstrate the generalizability claim.

This paper is a strong contribution to multimodal learning and image captioning, with clear novelty, sound methodology, and significant practical implications. The minor suggestions above would further strengthen an already promising work.
