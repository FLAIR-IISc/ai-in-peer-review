This paper addresses the novel and interesting problem of generating a sequence of natural language sentences to describe a stream of images. The authors propose a new multimodal architecture, the Coherent Recurrent Convolutional Network (CRCN), which integrates three main components: a Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) for image representation, a Bidirectional Recurrent Neural Network (BRNN) to model the semantic flow of the sentence sequence, and an entity-based local coherence model to ensure smooth linguistic transitions between sentences. A significant contribution is the use of online blog posts as a large-scale, weakly-aligned data source for training. The model is trained with a max-margin loss to rank ground-truth image-text sequence pairs higher than mismatched pairs. The authors conduct a thorough evaluation on two large blog datasets (NYC and Disneyland), demonstrating that their approach outperforms several adapted state-of-the-art single-image captioning methods, with results validated by both automatic metrics and human studies.

This work is a natural and important extension of the widely studied single-image-to-single-sentence captioning problem (e.g., Karpathy et al. [9], Vinyals et al. [30]). By moving from single instances to sequences on both the visual and textual sides, it tackles a more complex and practically relevant task, akin to creating a narrative for a photo album. The explicit modeling of coherence between sentences is a key differentiator from prior work that has focused on video-to-sentence generation [5], where visual continuity is often stronger than in a photo stream.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Significance and Originality:** The paper formulates and provides a strong baseline for a new, challenging, and practically useful problem. Generating a coherent story from a collection of photos is a compelling application. To the best of my knowledge, this is one of the first works to tackle this specific task with a dedicated sequence-to-sequence architecture that explicitly models textual coherence.

2.  **Novel Technical Contribution:** The proposed CRCN architecture is a well-motivated and sensible combination of powerful components. The integration of a classic NLP coherence model [1] with modern deep learning components (CNN, BRNN) is particularly novel. This design choice directly addresses the core challenge of the problem: ensuring that the output is not just a collection of accurate but disjointed sentences, but a fluent and coherent paragraph.

3.  **Creative Data Collection:** The methodology for creating a large-scale dataset from unstructured blog posts is a valuable contribution in itself. This approach circumvents the need for expensive manual annotation and provides a scalable way to collect training data for this and similar tasks.

4.  **Thorough and Convincing Evaluation:** The experimental evaluation is comprehensive. The authors compare their method against multiple strong baselines adapted for the task. The ablation study (CRCN vs. RCN vs. 1NN) clearly demonstrates the value of both the BRNN component for content flow and the coherence model for fluency. Furthermore, the use of human evaluation via AMT provides crucial validation that the model's output is preferred by people, and the specific test comparing performance on short vs. long sequences effectively highlights the importance of the coherence model.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

1.  **Retrieval vs. Generation Framing:** The method is fundamentally a retrieval-based system: it selects and re-ranks existing sentences from a training corpus to form a new paragraph. While this is a valid and effective approach, the paper's title ("Expressing an Image Stream...") and framing sometimes imply a generative process. This could be made clearer in the introduction and abstract to manage reader expectations. The comparison with truly generative baselines like [9, 30] is slightly indirect, as their models are adapted by simply concatenating individually generated sentences. A more direct comparison would involve sequence-to-sequence generative models, though this would be a significant undertaking and is likely future work.

2.  **Clarity of Model Architecture:** In Section 3.3, the mechanism for combining the per-sentence BRNN outputs (`O`) with the single sequence-level coherence vector (`q`) is unclear. The equation `Wf2Wf1[O|q] = [S|g]` suggests a concatenation, but the dimensions do not obviously align. Is the coherence vector `q` broadcasted or replicated for each sentence in the sequence before being fed into the fully connected layers? Clarifying this detail would significantly improve the paper's clarity and reproducibility.

3.  **Inference Scalability:** The inference process requires finding K-nearest neighbor images for *each* image in the query stream from the entire training set, which could be computationally expensive for very large datasets. While the use of a beam-search-like strategy to manage the combinatorial explosion of sentence candidates is clever, a brief discussion of the overall runtime complexity and potential scalability issues of the retrieval step would strengthen the paper.

4.  **Coherence Model:** The chosen entity-based coherence model [1] is a well-established and effective technique. However, it is a relatively classic approach. The paper could be strengthened by briefly acknowledging or discussing more recent, potentially neural-based, approaches to discourse and coherence modeling in the related work section. This would better position the work within the current NLP landscape.

**Summary of Arguments:**

*   **Pro-Acceptance:**
    *   Addresses a novel, significant, and challenging problem.
    *   Presents a well-designed architecture that thoughtfully combines deep learning with classic NLP for coherence.
    *   Introduces an innovative method for collecting large-scale training data.
    *   Provides strong empirical evidence of the method's effectiveness through extensive experiments and human studies.

*   **Con-Acceptance:**
    *   The framing as a "generation" task is slightly misleading for a retrieval-based method.
    *   A key detail in the model architecture is unclear, hindering reproducibility.
    *   Potential scalability concerns with the inference method are not addressed.

**Conclusion:**

This is a high-quality paper that makes a strong contribution to the intersection of computer vision and natural language processing. The problem it tackles is novel and important, and the proposed solution is elegant and effective. The experimental validation is thorough and convincing. While there are minor weaknesses related to clarity and positioning, they are addressable. The paper's strengths, particularly its originality and the quality of the technical contribution, far outweigh these weaknesses. I recommend acceptance.
