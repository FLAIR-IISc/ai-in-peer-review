Summary
The paper addresses generating a sequence of natural sentences for a stream of images (e.g., photo blogs). The proposed coherent recurrent convolutional network (CRCN) combines: (i) CNN (VGG fc7) features for images; (ii) a sentence-level bidirectional RNN (BRNN) operating on paragraph vectors (doc2vec) for modeling contextualized sentence content; and (iii) an entity-grid-based local coherence model (Barzilay & Lapata) to encourage fluent sentence-to-sentence transitions. Training uses a max-margin objective over aligned image–sentence sequences built from blog posts. At test time, for each query image the method retrieves K-nearest training images, gathers their associated sentences, and searches over concatenations with a divide-and-conquer beam search, ranking candidates with the learned compatibility score. The paper evaluates on two blog datasets (NYC and Disneyland), reporting that CRCN outperforms adapted single-image captioning baselines on BLEU/CIDEr/METEOR, recall/median rank, and AMT user studies.

Relation to prior work
The setup extends single-image captioning (e.g., Karpathy & Fei-Fei; Vinyals et al.; Kiros et al.) to sequences. It is related to video description (e.g., Donahue et al.; Rohrbach et al.) but differs in that photo streams may be discontinuous and the output is multi-sentence, where coherence modeling matters. The entity-grid coherence model is a classic NLP approach (Barzilay & Lapata). The paper’s novelty is integrating that coherence signal with visual and sentence-sequence modeling, and posing the task as retrieval from blog corpora.

Strengths
- Addresses a sensible and underexplored problem: multi-sentence description of image streams.
- Thoughtful architecture that blends BRNN context with an explicit discourse coherence signal.
- Pragmatic use of large, weakly-aligned blog data and a retrieval-based decoding with approximate search.
- Ablation (RCN vs. CRCN) suggests added value of coherence on longer sequences; AMT study design is reasonable.
- Implementation details (optimizers, dropout, weight init) are reported.

Weaknesses and detailed concerns

Quality
- Scoring function design: Skl = Σt (skt · vlt) + (gk · vlt). The stream-level coherence vector gk contributes additively for every image, effectively acting as a per-stream bias added N times. This double-counts coherence with sequence length and conflates discourse coherence with visual similarity. There is no principled interaction between coherence features and sentence-to-sentence transitions in the score; g is not tied to transitions at position t. This is a central modeling mismatch.
- Length bias: The score sums over t without normalization. Since negatives typically have different lengths, longer candidates will have larger raw scores, potentially making the margin trivial to satisfy. No length normalization, per-token averaging, or margin rescaling is described.
- Training data construction is weakly specified. The “data augmentation” by taking top-κ LSA sentences per segment: are these treated as separate positives for the same image? How is ambiguity resolved in the loss when multiple sentences are associated to one image/time step?
- BRNN details are incomplete. Initial hidden states, sequence padding/packing, and gradient stability (no clipping mentioned) are omitted. Dropout is excluded from BRNN but applied elsewhere; justification is not given.
- Entity-grid reliability: Coherence depends on accurate coreference and parsing on noisy blog text; no diagnostics (e.g., coref quality, entity counts) or robustness analysis is provided.
- Evaluation fairness and leakage risks:
  - The proposed method performs retrieval from training sentences, while baselines (CNN+RNN/LSTM) generate de novo sentences; though retrained, this favors retrieval when reference style matches the training corpus. A retrieval-based baseline that uses only visual similarity at sequence level (e.g., sequence-level nearest neighbor with simple smoothing) is missing.
  - KNN at test time on VGG fc7 risks near-duplicate leakage between train/test unless near-duplicate filtering is applied. This is not discussed.
  - doc2vec training “separately from the two classes” is ambiguous: was doc2vec trained on the full corpus including test text (unsupervised leakage) or only on training text?
- AMT: Only three judgments per item; no inter-annotator agreement, no significance tests, no details on quality control (gold checks, time filters), and no effect sizes reported. The claim of “almost optimal” search with beam size M=50 lacks empirical justification.

Clarity
- Several core aspects are under-specified: exact handling of κ augmented sentences, negative sampling protocol, sequence length distribution, and beam/divide-and-conquer search procedure (recursion depth Q, merging policy).
- Equations/notation issues: “ne examples” presumably “negative”; dimensions are sometimes unclear; Fig. references are not present; many references lack years and are inconsistently formatted.
- The “end-to-end” claim is overstated: the entity-grid features are hand-engineered and not learned jointly from raw text; parsing/coref are external.

Originality
- The problem framing is timely; however, the technical combination is largely a straightforward composition of known components (VGG, BRNN over doc2vec, entity grids). The specific way coherence is injected (single g shared across t) is not standard but also arguably suboptimal.

Significance
- Potential impact is moderate: a useful task and a large blog dataset. However, the methodological issues (score design, length bias, evaluation fairness) weaken the strength of the empirical claims. Lack of dataset release details also limits reproducibility and follow-up.

Specific questions and suggestions
- Normalize scores by sequence length or use a sequence-length-aware margin; report results with and without normalization.
- Redesign the coherence interaction to model transitions explicitly per position (e.g., pairwise factors between adjacent sentences) rather than a global vector added N times.
- Clarify and ablate κ augmentation; report how many sentences per segment are selected at train vs. test.
- Ensure doc2vec is trained on training text only; report sensitivity to doc2vec training corpus and embedding size.
- Add a sequence-level retrieval baseline (e.g., dynamic time warping over image features with per-image sentence retrieval) and a hierarchical neural baseline (e.g., image-sequence encoder + sentence-level decoder without entity grid).
- Provide statistical significance for all metrics and AMT; detail quality control and inter-annotator agreement.
- Perform near-duplicate filtering between train/test images; report its effect.
- Fix references (missing years), typos, notation, and include the missing figures/tables.

Pros
- Addresses an important, underexplored task.
- Sensible integration of sentence context and discourse coherence.
- Large-scale blog data; practical retrieval framework.
- Ablation suggests coherence helps on longer sequences.

Cons
- Core scoring formulation is mismatched and length-biased.
- Ambiguities in data construction and training; possible leakage.
- Evaluation lacks fair retrieval baselines and statistical rigor.
- Overstated “end-to-end” and “unsupervised” claims.
- Formatting and clarity issues impede reproducibility.

Recommendation: Reject (borderline). The idea is promising, but the accumulation of technical and experimental issues (especially the score design and evaluation fairness) undermines confidence in the conclusions. I encourage the authors to address the concerns above; a revised version could be impactful.
