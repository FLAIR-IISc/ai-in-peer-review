

# Review of "Expressing an Image Stream with a Sequence of Natural Sentences"

This paper introduces a novel task: generating a sequence of natural language sentences to describe a stream of images (e.g., a series of photos from a trip), rather than the more common single-image single-sentence generation. The authors propose a multimodal architecture called Coherent Recurrent Convolutional Networks (CRCN) that integrates convolutional neural networks for image representation, bidirectional recurrent neural networks for sentence modeling, and a local coherence model for smooth transitions between sentences. The key innovation is addressing the coherence between sentences in the output sequence, which is essential for natural language generation from image streams but overlooked in existing work.

The paper relates to previous work by distinguishing their approach from existing image captioning methods (e.g., [9, 12, 21]) and video captioning approaches (e.g., [23, 32]). While most prior work focuses on single-image single-sentence generation, the authors correctly identify that image streams (e.g., travel blogs) require multiple sentences with proper coherence. The paper also differs from the video captioning literature by addressing the challenge of abrupt visual changes between consecutive images in streams, which necessitates explicit coherence modeling.

**Strengths:**

1. The paper identifies a meaningful extension to existing image captioning research by addressing the problem of generating coherent sentence sequences for image streams. The motivation is well-justified with real-world examples (e.g., travel blogs).

2. The CRCN architecture is well-motivated and integrates three key components (CNN, BRNN, coherence model) in a coherent way. The ablation studies (Table 1) clearly demonstrate the contribution of each component, particularly the coherence model's impact on retrieval metrics.

3. The paper makes a novel contribution in using blog posts as a data source, which provides natural image-text sequences. The preprocessing pipeline (Section 2) is thorough and well-documented, with data augmentation and text summarization techniques that enhance the dataset quality.

4. The evaluation is comprehensive, employing both quantitative metrics (BLEU, CIDEr, METEOR, top-K recall) and user studies via AMT. The user study design (Section 4.3) is particularly strong, with the authors demonstrating that coherence becomes more important as passage length increases, validating their core contribution.

5. The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with appropriate figures that illustrate the problem and model architecture.

**Weaknesses:**

1. The training procedure for the coherence model is not sufficiently detailed. The paper states that "joint learning outperforms learning the two terms with separate parameters" but doesn't explain how the coherence model is trained or optimized in the end-to-end architecture. The training objective (Equation 4) doesn't explicitly include the coherence model's contribution.

2. The paper claims to be "the first to address the problem," but it would be strengthened by a more thorough comparison with related video captioning work (e.g., [23, 32]), which also deals with sequential image-text generation, even if the specific problem setup differs.

3. The computational complexity and training time are not discussed, which is important for understanding the practical applicability of the approach.

4. The user study design limits query sequences to 5 images (or 8-10 images in the additional test), which may not sufficiently stress the coherence model. The paper acknowledges that coherence becomes more important with longer passages, but the evaluation doesn't fully demonstrate this for longer sequences.

5. The paper doesn't address how the model handles image streams with very different content (e.g., a mix of landscapes and people) where coherence might be particularly challenging.

**Pro and Con Arguments for Acceptance:**

*Pro:* This paper presents a well-motivated extension to the image captioning literature with a clear problem statement and a novel solution. The CRCN architecture effectively integrates coherence modeling, which is a critical gap in existing work. The evaluation is comprehensive with both quantitative and qualitative results, and the paper makes a significant contribution to the field by addressing a meaningful extension of the image captioning task.

*Con:* The paper lacks sufficient detail on how the coherence model is trained and integrated into the overall architecture. The evaluation of coherence's importance is limited to relatively short passages, and the computational aspects of the model are not discussed. While the problem is novel, the technical contribution of the coherence model itself could be more clearly articulated.

**Overall Assessment:**

The paper makes a significant contribution by extending the image captioning task to image streams and introducing a coherence model to generate fluent sentence sequences. The approach is technically sound, with thorough experiments and evaluation. While the training procedure for the coherence model could be more clearly explained, the core contribution is novel and well-validated. The work represents a meaningful advancement in multimodal language generation, with clear potential for applications in social media, travel blogging, and multimedia content summarization. The paper meets all four review criteria (quality, clarity, originality, significance) and makes a strong contribution to the field.
