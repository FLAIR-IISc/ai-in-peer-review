**Review of "Expressing an Image Stream with a Sequence of Natural Sentences"**

**Summary:**
This paper tackles the novel and interesting problem of generating a coherent sequence of sentences to describe a stream of images, moving beyond the well-studied task of single-image-to-single-sentence captioning. The authors propose a multimodal architecture called Coherent Recurrent Convolutional Networks (CRCN). This model is retrieval-based; it constructs a descriptive paragraph by selecting and sequencing the best sentences from a training database. The CRCN architecture integrates three key components: (1) a standard Convolutional Neural Network (VGGNet) to extract image features, (2) a Bidirectional Recurrent Neural Network (BRNN) to model the semantic flow and context of the sentence sequence, and (3) an entity-based local coherence model, based on classic work by Barzilay and Lapata, to ensure grammatical and referential smoothness between sentences.

To train this model, the authors cleverly leverage a large dataset of blog posts, processing them to create a parallel corpus of image streams and associated sentence sequences. The model is trained using a max-margin objective to score matching image-sentence sequences higher than mismatched ones. At test time, a combinatorial search over candidate sentences (retrieved via K-NN for each image) is performed to find the highest-scoring sequence. The paper demonstrates the effectiveness of CRCN through extensive experiments on two blog datasets (NYC and Disneyland), showing superior performance over adapted state-of-the-art single-image captioning methods using both automatic metrics (BLEU, R@K) and human evaluations via Amazon Mechanical Turk.

**Strengths:**
1.  **Problem Formulation and Significance:** The paper addresses a well-motivated and important problem. Moving from single images to image streams is a logical and significant step towards a more holistic understanding of visual content and its relationship with narrative language. This task has clear practical applications, such as automatic album summarization or storytelling, and is likely to inspire future research in this direction.

2.  **Novel Combination of Techniques:** The core contribution, the CRCN architecture, is a novel and thoughtful combination of modern deep learning techniques and a classic NLP model. While CNNs and RNNs are standard for vision-language tasks, the explicit integration of an entity-grid coherence model to handle the fluency of a multi-sentence output is a key insight. This directly addresses the main challenge of this new problem: ensuring the output is not just a list of independent captions but a coherent paragraph.

3.  **Resourceful Data Collection and Processing:** The authors address the lack of suitable training data for this new task by creatively using online blog posts. The methodology for processing unstructured blogs into a parallel corpus of image-sentence sequences, while heuristic-based, is pragmatic and enables research on a problem that would otherwise be stymied by data scarcity.

4.  **Thorough and Convincing Evaluation:** The experimental evaluation is comprehensive and well-executed.
    *   The authors compare against several strong baselines adapted from state-of-the-art single-image captioning.
    *   Crucially, they include ablation studies (1NN, RCN vs. CRCN) that clearly demonstrate the value of both the BRNN component and, more subtly, the coherence model.
    *   The use of both retrieval metrics (R@K, median rank) and language metrics (BLEU, etc.) provides a multi-faceted quantitative assessment.
    *   The inclusion of a user study via AMT is essential for a task where fluency and coherence are paramount, as these qualities are poorly captured by n-gram-based metrics. The final AMT experiment demonstrating the increased importance of the coherence model for longer sequences is a particularly strong piece of evidence supporting their architectural choices.

**Weaknesses:**
1.  **Retrieval-based vs. Generative Nature:** The paper frames the task as generating descriptions, but the method is fundamentally retrieval-based—it selects and reorders existing sentences from the training corpus. This limits the expressive power of the model, as it cannot produce novel sentences or describe object/scene configurations not present in its database. This is a crucial distinction that could be made clearer. While a pragmatic first step, it is less ambitious than a truly generative sequence-to-sequence model.

2.  **Scalability and Complexity of Inference:** The inference procedure appears computationally expensive. It requires a K-NN search over the entire image dataset for each image in the query stream, followed by a combinatorial search over K^N possible sentence sequences. While the authors propose a divide-and-conquer beam search approximation, the scalability of this approach to much larger datasets or longer image sequences (N > 10) is a concern. The paper could benefit from a discussion on the computational complexity and the quality of the approximation.

3.  **Reliance on Heuristic Pre-processing:** The quality of the training data hinges on a series of heuristics for text segmentation (distance-based assignment) and summarization (LSA-based). This pipeline could introduce significant noise; for example, a text block might be incorrectly associated with an image, or the LSA summary might not capture the essence of the text. The paper does not analyze the impact of potential noise from this pre-processing pipeline on the model's performance.

4.  **Integration of the Coherence Model:** While effective, the integration of the classic entity-grid model feels somewhat disjointed from the end-to-end deep learning paradigm. The 64-dimensional feature vector is simply zero-padded to 300 dimensions to match the BRNN output. A more modern, perhaps attention-based or neural, coherence mechanism learned jointly with the rest of the model could be a more elegant and potentially more powerful alternative.

**Overall Assessment:**
This is a high-quality paper that introduces a novel and significant problem to the community. The proposed CRCN model is a well-reasoned and effective solution that combines existing components in a novel way to specifically address the challenge of narrative coherence. The experiments are thorough and provide strong support for the authors' claims. Despite the weaknesses concerning the retrieval-based nature of the method and the reliance on heuristics, the paper represents a complete and solid piece of research that demonstrably advances the state of the art. It successfully frames a new research direction and provides a strong baseline for future work. The contribution is significant and of clear interest to the NIPS community. I recommend acceptance.
