This paper addresses the novel problem of generating a sequence of natural language sentences to describe a stream of images. The authors propose a coherent recurrent convolutional network (CRCN) integrating CNNs for image representation, RNNs for language modeling, and a local coherence model for fluent transitions. They leverage online blog posts as a text-image parallel corpus and demonstrate superior performance compared to state-of-the-art alternatives through quantitative measures (BLEU, Top-K recall) and user studies on NYC and Disneyland datasets.

The paper tackles a challenging and relatively unexplored area, extending image captioning to sequential data. The motivation of describing image streams, particularly from user-generated content like travel blogs, is well-articulated. The proposed CRCN architecture is reasonably well-defined, combining established techniques (CNNs, RNNs) with a novel coherence model. The use of blog data as a training source is a creative solution to the scarcity of parallel image-sentence sequence datasets. The experiments are comprehensive, including quantitative evaluation and user studies, and comparisons to relevant baselines.

However, the paper suffers from several weaknesses. While the authors claim novelty, the individual components (CNN, RNN, coherence model) are not new. The primary contribution is the integration of these components into a single framework for this specific task. The explanation of the coherence model (section 3.2) lacks sufficient detail. The description of the entity grid and transition representation feels superficial, leaving the reader unsure of its practical implementation and effectiveness. The data augmentation technique (κ=3) seems arbitrary and isn't thoroughly justified. The ablation study (RCN vs CRCN) is limited and doesn’t sufficiently dissect the contributions of individual components. The quantitative results, while showing improvement, are not overwhelmingly significant, particularly the BLEU scores which are often low in image captioning tasks. The improvement observed in the user study for longer sequences is mentioned but not thoroughly demonstrated with corresponding results. The writing is sometimes verbose and repetitive. The connection between the coreferent entity grid and the actual coherence improvement is not explicitly clear. While the authors mention the vanishing gradient problem, there isn’t an explanation of how the architecture mitigates it.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Addresses a novel and relevant problem in multimodal learning.
*   Presents a reasonably well-defined architecture (CRCN) integrating existing techniques.
*   Demonstrates improved performance over baselines through quantitative evaluation and user studies.
*   Utilizes a creative data source (blog posts) to overcome the lack of parallel data.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Limited novelty – the components themselves are not new.
*   Insufficient detail in the description of the coherence model.
*   Lack of thorough ablation studies to validate the contribution of each component.
*   Quantitative results are not overwhelmingly significant.
*   Verbose and sometimes repetitive writing.



**Overall Assessment:**

The paper presents a solid contribution to the field of multimodal learning. However, the limited novelty and lack of in-depth analysis prevent it from being a particularly strong submission. The authors successfully adapt existing techniques to a new problem domain, but a more thorough investigation of the coherence model and a more compelling demonstration of its effectiveness would strengthen the paper considerably.  I would rate this as a **weak accept**. Further refinement and elaboration on the coherence model, accompanied by stronger empirical evidence, could elevate this work to a higher level.
