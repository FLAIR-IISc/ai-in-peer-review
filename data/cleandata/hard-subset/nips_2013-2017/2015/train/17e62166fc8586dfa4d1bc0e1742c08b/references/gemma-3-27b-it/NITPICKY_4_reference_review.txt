This paper tackles the problem of generating sequential sentences from image streams, a natural extension of single-image captioning. The authors propose CRCN, a multimodal architecture integrating CNNs, BRNNs, and a local coherence model. While the problem statement is relevant and the proposed architecture conceptually sound, the paper suffers from a multitude of minor flaws, ranging from insufficient detail in methodological choices to inconsistencies in presentation, ultimately hindering a strong assessment of its contributions.

The introduction establishes the motivation well, but the phrasing is often imprecise. For example, stating the problem is “obvious” is dismissive and unhelpful. The claim of being the “first” to address this problem requires stronger justification – a more comprehensive survey of related work is needed, particularly examining work in video captioning and story generation, which heavily overlap. While the authors acknowledge video-sentence work, the distinction drawn feels superficial.  The reliance on blog data is a pragmatic choice, but the rationale for its “unsupervised” nature needs further elaboration – all methods require supervision in the form of image-text pairings.

Section 2, detailing the dataset creation, is particularly problematic. The text segmentation based on paragraph divisions followed by distance heuristics feels ad-hoc and lacks justification. Why is the image-to-text block distance a sensible metric? The LSA-based summarization is mentioned but lacks detail regarding parameter tuning and its impact on the quality of the resulting single-sentence summaries. The data augmentation strategy, selecting top-κ sentences, introduces a hyperparameter (κ) that requires detailed justification. Simply stating it was chosen “after thorough empirical tests” is insufficient.  The paragraph vector extraction using gensim doc2vec is mentioned, but the training details (corpus, parameters) are missing. Finally, the use of Stanford CoreNLP for parse trees is adequate, but the relevance of specific grammatical roles isn't fully explored.

Section 3, describing the CRCN architecture, is overly verbose and lacks mathematical rigor. The explanations of the BRNN and coherence model are convoluted. Equation (1) introduces variables (xft, xbt, hft, hbt, ot) without clear explanation of their dimensionality. The separation of input activations into forward and backward units in the BRNN feels arbitrary and requires stronger empirical support. The coherence model’s reliance on a fixed history parameter of three is unexplained. The explanation of how parse trees are transformed into a 64-dimensional representation is vague.  Equation (2) presents a matrix multiplication without specifying the shapes of the involved matrices, making it difficult to follow. The justification for the dropout rates (0.5 and 0.7) is missing. The loss function in equation (4) is standard, but the choice of 100 negative samples needs justification. Why 100?  The training details, particularly the learning rate and optimization schedule, are missing.

The experiments section lacks crucial details. The validation set is mentioned but not used for hyperparameter tuning (or this isn't stated). The baselines are appropriate, but the implementation details for their adaptation to the sequential setting are omitted.  The presentation of quantitative results in Table 1 is clear, but the differences between the methods are often small, warranting a more in-depth statistical analysis. The qualitative examples in Figure 3 are helpful but lack a systematic comparison – a side-by-side comparison highlighting specific strengths and weaknesses would be more impactful.  The AMT study provides valuable user feedback, but the lack of demographic information about the turkers is a limitation. The rationale for selecting 5 images per query for the initial AMT tests, and then increasing it to 8-10, feels arbitrary.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Addresses a relevant and under-explored problem.
*   Presents a conceptually plausible architecture (CRCN).
*   Demonstrates some performance gains over baseline methods, albeit modest.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   Lack of methodological detail and rigor.
*   Numerous minor flaws in presentation and explanation.
*   Insufficient justification for design choices and hyperparameters.
*   Missing statistical analysis of quantitative results.
*   Limited discussion of limitations and potential future work.
*   The significance of the improvements over baselines is questionable.



Overall, the paper feels rushed and lacks the polish expected of a NIPS submission. While the idea is interesting, the execution is flawed. The authors need to provide significantly more detail, address the identified weaknesses, and demonstrate a more substantial contribution to warrant acceptance. **Recommendation: Reject.** A major revision addressing the detailed points raised in this review would be required for reconsideration.
