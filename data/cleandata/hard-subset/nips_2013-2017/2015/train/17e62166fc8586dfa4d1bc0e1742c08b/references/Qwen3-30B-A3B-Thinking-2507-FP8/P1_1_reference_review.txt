

# Peer Review: "Expressing an Image Stream with a Sequence of Natural Sentences"

## Summary
This paper introduces a novel problem setting: generating a sequence of natural language sentences to describe a stream of images (rather than a single image), motivated by real-world scenarios like documenting travel experiences with multiple photos. The authors propose a multimodal architecture called Coherent Recurrent Convolutional Networks (CRCN), which integrates convolutional neural networks for image representation, bidirectional recurrent networks for sentence modeling, and a local coherence model to ensure fluent transitions between sentences. The authors collect and preprocess blog posts (NYC and Disneyland datasets with 20K+ blog posts and 140K images) to create text-image parallel data. They evaluate their approach against several baselines (including extensions of single-sentence captioning models) using both quantitative metrics (BLEU, CIDEr, METEOR, top-K recall) and user studies via Amazon Mechanical Turk.

## Quality
The paper is technically sound and well-executed. The authors provide a comprehensive methodology that integrates existing techniques (CNN, BRNN, coherence model) in a novel way for the specific problem of image stream description. The CRCN architecture is logically designed, with ablation studies demonstrating the contribution of each component. The dataset construction from blog posts is well-motivated, and the preprocessing pipeline (text segmentation, summarization, augmentation) is carefully described. 

The evaluation is thorough, with both quantitative metrics and human evaluation via AMT. The authors acknowledge limitations of BLEU for paragraph evaluation, which demonstrates careful consideration of evaluation metrics. The comparison against multiple baselines (including variants of their own approach) provides strong evidence of their method's superiority, particularly in coherence-related metrics.

However, the paper could better address the "exponentially many candidates" problem during retrieval (Section 3.5). While the divide-and-conquer strategy is mentioned, the details of how this approximate search achieves near-optimal solutions are insufficient. A more detailed analysis of the search space complexity and the trade-offs between approximation accuracy and computational cost would strengthen the paper.

## Clarity
The paper is generally well-written and logically organized. The problem statement is clearly motivated with a compelling real-world example (Fig. 1). The methodology is described with sufficient detail for reproducibility, including model architectures, training procedures, and evaluation metrics. The figures (Fig. 1-3) effectively illustrate the problem and results.

The paper could improve clarity in a few areas: 
1) The explanation of the divide-and-conquer strategy for candidate retrieval (Section 3.5) is somewhat vague
2) The distinction between the different baseline models could be clearer (e.g., why MLBL-F outperforms other variants of [12])
3) The user study methodology (Section 4.3) could better explain how they handled the shortening of longer queries to 5 images

## Originality
This paper makes a significant contribution by addressing a clear gap in the literature: generating sentence sequences for image streams rather than single-image captions. While some prior work has addressed video captioning (e.g., [23, 32]), the authors convincingly argue that image streams differ from videos in terms of abrupt visual changes, making coherence between sentences more critical. The problem statement is well-motivated and represents a natural extension of the image captioning literature.

The CRCN architecture integrates existing techniques in a novel way for this specific problem, with the coherence model being particularly important for the sentence sequence generation task. The authors properly distinguish their approach from related work, especially in Section 1 (Related Work) where they contrast with video captioning and single-image captioning approaches.

## Significance
The problem of describing image streams has clear practical applications in tourism, social media, and personal photo management. The authors demonstrate that their approach significantly outperforms baselines, particularly in coherence-related metrics, which is crucial for generating natural-sounding descriptions of multi-image sequences. The results from both quantitative metrics and user studies provide convincing evidence of their method's superiority.

The paper advances the state of the art in multimodal learning by addressing a gap that has been underexplored despite the prevalence of image streams in real-world applications. The use of blog posts as a source of natural text-image parallel data is particularly clever and scalable, making the approach applicable to many domains without requiring manual annotation.

## Strengths and Weaknesses Summary

**Strengths:**
- Well-motivated problem statement with compelling real-world examples
- Comprehensive evaluation with both quantitative metrics and human studies
- Novel integration of coherence model with multimodal networks for sequence generation
- Large-scale dataset collection and preprocessing methodology
- Clear ablation studies demonstrating contributions of key components

**Weaknesses:**
- Insufficient detail on the approximate retrieval strategy (Section 3.5)
- Limited discussion of scalability to longer image sequences
- Baseline extensions from single-sentence models could be better justified
- User study size (100 test streams) may not be sufficient for statistical significance

## Recommendation
This is a strong paper that makes a meaningful contribution to the field of multimodal learning. The problem is well-motivated, the methodology is sound, and the results are compelling. While there are some minor weaknesses in the details of the retrieval strategy and evaluation scale, these do not significantly undermine the paper's overall contribution.

The paper represents a significant step beyond the standard image captioning task and addresses a gap that has been underexplored in the literature. The CRCN architecture provides a principled solution to the problem of generating coherent sentence sequences for image streams, and the evaluation demonstrates clear advantages over existing approaches.

I recommend acceptance at NIPS. This paper would be a valuable contribution to the conference, advancing the state of the art in multimodal representation learning with clear practical implications.
