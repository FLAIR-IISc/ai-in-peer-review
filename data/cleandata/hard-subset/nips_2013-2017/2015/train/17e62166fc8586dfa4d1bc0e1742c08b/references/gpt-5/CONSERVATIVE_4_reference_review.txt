Summary
The paper tackles the problem of generating a sequence of natural sentences for a stream of images. The proposed coherent recurrent convolutional network (CRCN) combines: (i) CNN image features (VGG fc7), (ii) a bidirectional RNN over sentence-level paragraph vectors to model content flow, and (iii) an entity-grid–based local coherence model to encourage smooth inter-sentence transitions. Training uses a max-margin ranking loss over paired image-stream / sentence-sequence examples built from blog posts via paragraph-level segmentation and LSA-based summarization (with κ-sentence augmentation). At test time, the method retrieves K candidate sentences per image via nearest neighbors in image space and uses an approximate divide-and-conquer beam search to assemble the best paragraph under the CRCN scoring function. Experiments on NYC and Disneyland blog datasets report improvements over several single-image captioning baselines (LBL variants, CNN+RNN, CNN+LSTM) on BLEU/METEOR/CIDEr and retrieval metrics, plus AMT user preferences; ablations suggest the coherence model helps more on longer sequences.

Strengths
- Addresses a relevant and under-explored setting (image streams to multi-sentence descriptions) beyond single-image captioning and video description.
- Incorporates a well-known coherence model (entity grid) into a vision-language framework; the ablation indicating larger gains for longer sequences is plausible and aligns with prior discourse work.
- Assembles a reasonably large domain-specific dataset via a well-described preprocessing pipeline; pragmatic use of weakly aligned blogs is sensible.
- Clear high-level problem formulation and modular system; training objective and inference strategy are straightforward to implement.

Weaknesses and concerns
Quality/technical soundness
- Not truly “end-to-end”: the coherence signal is hand-engineered (parser, coreference, entity grid), and gradients cannot flow to those components. The claim of a “unified end-to-end architecture” is misleading.
- The method is not “unsupervised”: it trains with paired image-stream / sentence-sequence data constructed via heuristics. Please correct the claim.
- The scoring function Skl includes a global coherence vector g dotted with every image feature vlt, which collapses to g · (sum_t vlt). This ad hoc interaction is not position-sensitive and lacks justification; alternative formulations (e.g., position-dependent coherence, pairwise sentence transition potentials) seem more principled.
- The BRNN operates on 300-d paragraph vectors (doc2vec) rather than word-level representations; using a vanilla ReLU RNN (no gating) is known to struggle with long-range dependencies. There is no comparison to a sentence-level LSTM/GRU over learned sentence embeddings.
- Inference uses an approximate divide-and-conquer beam search without analysis of search error or bounds.

Evaluation and fairness
- The proposed method is retrieval-based, selecting sentences from the training set; many baselines are generative (CNN+LSTM), which are disadvantaged on n-gram metrics and stylistic matching. A fairer comparison would include strong retrieval baselines that (a) retrieve K sentences per image and (b) assemble sequences with a language model or a simple CRF/HMM with bigram transition potentials (with and without entity-grid features).
- Domain restriction: both datasets are travel blogs (NYC/Disneyland). The strong domain regularities (named entities, stereotyped activities) could inflate coherence and retrieval gains. There is no cross-domain transfer or generalization study.
- Critical details are missing or unclear: whether VGG is fine-tuned, whether doc2vec embeddings are updated during training, exact metric values (the text references tables/figures but none are provided here), significance testing, and error analysis (e.g., failure modes due to parsing/coreference on noisy blogs).
- AMT protocol: pairwise preferences are reported, but there is no measure of inter-annotator agreement or significance; for longer sequences, gains are claimed but not quantified in this excerpt.

Originality
- The problem setting (image-stream to sentence sequence) is interesting and relatively new, but the technical components are mostly standard (CNN features, BRNN over sentence embeddings, entity-grid coherence) with a simple linear fusion. The novelty lies in the combination and application; methodological novelty is modest.
- Related work discussion is reasonable, but there is no comparison to simpler structured retrieval models (e.g., latent structural SVM/CRF) that directly model sequence-level coherence without neural BRNNs.

Clarity
- Generally well organized and readable, with a clear system diagram and training/inference description. However, several claims (end-to-end, unsupervised) are inaccurate, and some implementation choices are under-specified (optimization schedules, negative sampling protocol, hyperparameters beyond dropout).

Significance
- The task is meaningful, and the idea of injecting discourse coherence is valuable. However, the current evidence is limited to two narrow domains, with potentially unfair baseline comparisons, and modest methodological novelty. Without stronger, carefully controlled evaluations, it is difficult to assess lasting impact.

Questions and requested clarifications
1) Are CNN features fine-tuned on this task? Are paragraph vectors frozen or updated during training?
2) Provide exact metric numbers, confidence intervals, and significance tests; include inter-annotator agreement for AMT.
3) Report sensitivity to K, M, and sequence length; analyze search errors.
4) Add strong retrieval baselines: nearest-neighbor retrieval plus bigram/trigram LM; a CRF with sentence compatibility and transition potentials; entity-grid scoring without BRNN.
5) Justify or revise the g · vlt term; consider position-dependent or sentence-pair interactions.
6) Release the dataset and preprocessing scripts to enable reproduction; otherwise, reproducibility is limited.

Pros for acceptance
- Addresses a timely, practical task beyond single-image captioning.
- Incorporates discourse coherence into multimodal retrieval with plausible gains on longer sequences.
- Clear system and a reasonable dataset construction pipeline.

Cons for acceptance
- Methodological novelty is limited; key claims (end-to-end, unsupervised) are overstated.
- Retrieval-vs-generation comparison appears unfair; missing strong sequence-level retrieval baselines.
- Questionable scoring design and weak sequence model (ReLU RNN over doc2vec) without comparison to stronger alternatives.
- Limited domains and unclear generalization; reproducibility concerns.

Recommendation
Leaning toward rejection in its current form. The paper proposes an interesting task and a sensible high-level approach, but the technical contribution is incremental, and the evaluation does not convincingly establish superiority over strong, fair baselines. Addressing the above concerns—especially fair retrieval baselines, stronger sequence models, clearer ablations/analysis, and corrected claims—would substantially strengthen the work.
