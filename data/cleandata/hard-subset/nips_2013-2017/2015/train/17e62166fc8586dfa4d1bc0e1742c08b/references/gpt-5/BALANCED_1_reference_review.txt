Summary and relation to prior work
This paper tackles “visual storytelling”: given a stream of images (e.g., from a travel blog), retrieve a coherent sequence of natural sentences. The proposed Coherent Recurrent Convolutional Network (CRCN) combines (i) a CNN (VGG) to encode images, (ii) a bidirectional RNN over sentence vectors (doc2vec) to model sentence content in context, and (iii) an entity-grid local coherence model (Barzilay & Lapata) to encourage smooth sentence-to-sentence transitions. Training uses weakly aligned image–text pairs mined from blog posts via paragraph-based segmentation and LSA summarization; κ-best summary sentences per image are used as augmented training targets. A max-margin objective encourages higher compatibility of aligned image–sentence sequences than unaligned ones. At test time, candidates are built by retrieving K-nearest images per position and concatenating their associated sentences; a divide-and-conquer beam search finds top sequences. Experiments on two large blog datasets (NYC and Disneyland) use BLEU/METEOR/CIDEr, retrieval metrics, and AMT user studies, comparing against adapted single-image captioning models (Kiros et al., Karpathy & Fei-Fei, Vinyals et al.) and simple retrieval baselines. Authors report consistent gains, with the coherence component helping more for longer sequences.

Relative to prior work, the paper goes beyond single-image captioning (Karpathy & Fei-Fei; Vinyals et al.; Mao et al.) and retrieval (Kiros et al.; Ordonez et al.) by modeling multi-sentence outputs and explicitly incorporating local coherence. It differs from video-to-text (Donahue et al.; Rohrbach et al.) by handling streams of independent photos with potentially abrupt changes.

Strengths
- Problem formulation: Addresses a natural and under-explored extension from single images to image streams with multi-sentence outputs.
- Architectural idea: Integrates sentence-level contextualization (bi-RNN) with an entity-based coherence signal; conceptually appealing and grounded in NLP discourse modeling.
- Data: Assembles sizeable weakly aligned multimodal datasets from blogs; the pipeline is practical and broadly applicable.
- Evaluation: Uses both automatic metrics and human preference tests; shows that coherence matters more as sequences lengthen.
- Empirical gains: Ablations (1NN vs. RCN vs. CRCN) suggest each component contributes.

Weaknesses
- Fairness and strength of baselines: Most baselines generate novel sentences, whereas CRCN retrieves human-written sentences from training. This inherently favors readability and fluency and conflates the task (retrieval vs. generation). Stronger retrieval baselines tailored to sequences (e.g., structured models/CRFs over candidate sentences with pairwise potentials, or multi-sentence embedding-and-ranking approaches) are missing.
- Scoring design and conditioning: The final score sums s_t·v_t + g·v_t over t, where g is a single coherence vector per candidate sequence. This implies the coherence term contributes g·sum_t v_t, a global interaction that is only weakly conditioned on per-position compatibility and may bias toward text-side properties irrespective of local image content. No length normalization is discussed, so scores may scale with sequence length.
- “End-to-end” and “unsupervised” claims: The pipeline relies on external preprocessing (LSA summarization, doc2vec trained separately, Stanford CoreNLP parsing/coref). CNN appears to be fixed. The approach is better described as weakly supervised with substantial external tooling, not end-to-end.
- Dataset and evaluation clarity: It is unclear whether doc2vec is trained only on training text or also on test (potential leakage). Retrieval metrics are ambiguously defined: since training and test are disjoint, how can the exact ground-truth test sentences be “retrieved”? Details on the candidate space containing ground-truth or on sequence-level match criteria are missing. AMT details (quality control, significance tests) are sparse.
- Preprocessing noise: The heuristics (index-distance assignment, LSA summarization) can yield noisy alignments, but the impact on performance is not quantified (e.g., ablations on κ, segmentation quality, or parser/coref errors).
- Limited analysis: No sensitivity analysis of K, beam size M, halving depth Q, or the relative weights between BRNN and coherence components. No qualitative analysis of coherence transitions or error modes.
- Generalization: Evaluation is confined to two related tourism domains from blogs; transfer to other domains/datasets is not demonstrated.

Quality
The model is technically plausible and reasonably described. However, aspects of the scoring, conditioning, and evaluation protocol are under-specified. Baseline comparisons do not isolate the contribution of the coherence mechanism against equally strong retrieval-based sequence models.

Clarity
Generally clear, with helpful architectural equations. Some notational overload and missing details:
- Precisely define the retrieval metrics at sequence level.
- Clarify whether CNN and doc2vec are fixed or fine-tuned; training-only data vs. all data.
- Explain length normalization and the role/weight of the coherence term g.
- Provide more details on AMT setup and statistical significance.

Originality
Moderately novel. Combining an entity-grid coherence model with multimodal sequence retrieval is a nice idea, and multi-sentence description for image streams was under-explored at the time. The components themselves are known.

Significance
Potentially useful for visual storytelling and summarization. However, the empirical significance is hard to gauge due to fairness concerns with baselines and limited diversity of datasets. The retrieval-vs-generation mismatch reduces impact for the captioning community.

Pros for acceptance
- Addresses an important and natural extension (image streams, multi-sentence).
- Conceptually interesting integration of discourse coherence with multimodal matching.
- Large-scale dataset construction and promising human preference results.

Cons for acceptance
- Baselines not matched in task (generation vs retrieval) and lack stronger sequence retrieval models.
- Scoring formulation and evaluation protocol have ambiguities; limited analysis and ablations.
- Overstated claims about end-to-end/unsupervised.
- Domain-limited evaluation.

Suggestions for improvement
- Include stronger, fair sequence-retrieval baselines: e.g., chain-CRF over candidate sentences with pairwise potentials (content similarity + lexical cohesion), or a learned bi-encoder with sentence-level and bigram transition scoring.
- Clarify and, if needed, normalize the scoring with respect to sequence length; consider a learned scalar to weight the coherence term or condition it on image-local context (e.g., attention over images to compute g).
- Provide detailed evaluation protocol: ensure no leakage in doc2vec/coref; define the retrieval ground-truth; report statistical significance and inter-annotator agreement on AMT.
- Add ablations: effect of κ, K, M; contribution of coref quality (e.g., with/without coref), alternative coherence models (neural entity-transitions).
- Explore generalization to another domain and release data/code.
- Consider a retrieval-only setting for all methods (including baselines) to equalize comparison.

Recommendation: Weak reject
Confidence: High
