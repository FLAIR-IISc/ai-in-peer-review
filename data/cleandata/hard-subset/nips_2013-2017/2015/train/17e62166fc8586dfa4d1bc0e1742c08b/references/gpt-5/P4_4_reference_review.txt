Summary and relation to prior work
The paper tackles the problem of describing an image stream with a coherent sequence of sentences. Instead of the common single-image/single-sentence setup, the authors propose coherent recurrent convolutional networks (CRCN) that (i) represent images via a CNN (VGG fc7), (ii) model sentence flow with a bidirectional RNN over paragraph vectors (doc2vec) of candidate sentences, and (iii) incorporate a local coherence model based on entity grids (Barzilay & Lapata) to encourage fluent sentence-to-sentence transitions. Training data are constructed automatically from travel blogs (NYC, Disneyland): blog text is segmented by proximity to images and summarized via LSA to a key sentence per image; top-κ summaries are used as augmented training sentences. At inference, the method retrieves a sentence for each query image from the training set (K-NN by image features), then selects a sequence using a learned compatibility score under a beam-style divide-and-conquer search.

This relates to image captioning via retrieval/ranking (e.g., Socher et al., Karpathy & Fei-Fei; Kiros et al.), multimodal CNN-RNN captioning (Karpathy, Vinyals, Mao), and video description (Donahue et al., Rohrbach et al.). It is close to image-sequence retrieval given paragraph queries (Kim et al.) but addresses the reverse direction. The main claimed novelty is integrating an entity-grid coherence model with CNN+BRNN to select coherent multi-sentence paragraphs for image streams.

Strengths
- Problem formulation: moves beyond single-image captioning to multi-sentence descriptions for image streams, a practically relevant setting.
- Architecture: clear integration of three components—image features, sequential language modeling (BRNN), and entity-grid coherence—into a unified ranking objective with a structured max-margin loss.
- Data construction at scale: automatic pipeline from blogs (20k+ posts, 140k images) with text segmentation and summarization; no manual annotations required.
- Inference strategy: pragmatic candidate reduction (K-NN per image) and approximate search with a beam-like divide-and-conquer approach; computationally sensible.
- Evaluation breadth: automatic language metrics, retrieval metrics, and AMT user studies; ablations (1NN, BRNN-only vs. CRCN) to isolate coherence contributions, especially for longer sequences.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Score function design and length bias: The sequence score Skl sums skt·vlt + gk·vlt over t. The coherence term gk is sequence-level yet dotted with each per-image vector and summed across all t, effectively (∑t vlt)·gk. This introduces a length dependency and may encourage degenerate correlations between a global coherence vector and cumulative image features. No normalization by sequence length or analysis of length effects is provided; negatives are sampled with random lengths, which risks the model exploiting length differences rather than semantics.
- Coherence-image coupling: The local coherence representation is text-only; coupling it to images via dot products with image features is not well motivated. Why should a text-only coherence vector align linearly with image features multiple times? A more principled combination (e.g., using coherence only to score sentence transitions or as a prior over sentence adjacency) would be preferable.
- Fairness of baselines: CRCN retrieves sentences from the training set; several baselines generate sentences word-by-word. On a blog-domain dataset with noisy, long-form text, retrieval has an advantage and may better match n-gram metrics. The paper mitigates this by using common CNN features and reporting multiple metrics, but a stronger comparison would include retrieval-based multi-sentence baselines with explicit sequence modeling (e.g., training a paragraph-level RNN/LSTM to generate multiple sentences, or a retrieval baseline that ignores coherence but optimizes the same structured loss). Clarify whether identical sentences appear across train/test blogs, which would further favor retrieval.
- “Unsupervised” claim: The approach uses automatically aligned image–sentence pairs from blogs. While no manual annotations are used, this is still supervised learning from noisy pairings; “weakly supervised” is more accurate.
- Preprocessing noise and robustness: Paragraph tokenization, heuristic image–text alignment by proximity, LSA summarization, and doc2vec all introduce noise. There is no analysis of segmentation/summarization accuracy or its impact on results. Coreference parsing quality on noisy blogs is also a concern for the entity-grid.
- Experimental reporting: The paper states that CRCN outperforms baselines, but numerical results are not shown in the excerpt. Effect sizes, variance, and significance tests are not discussed. It would be useful to show performance vs. sequence length and explicit tests against shuffled sentence orders to validate coherence modeling.
- Reproducibility: Dataset availability, preprocessing code, and trained models are not specified; many implementation choices (e.g., doc2vec training details, coreference settings) could affect replication.

Quality
- The core methodology is plausible and generally sound. The BRNN equations and training objective are specified, and optimization details are given. However, the coherence–image coupling in the score is not fully justified, and potential length bias is unaddressed. Evaluation design needs stronger controls and clearer reporting of results and statistical significance.

Clarity
- The paper is mostly clear and well organized. Some notation choices could be cleaned (e.g., reusing T for both a single and κ sentences; zero-padding the 64-d coherence vector to 300-d is ad hoc and should be explained). The rationale for combining g with each v_t needs clearer motivation.

Originality
- The problem setup (image-stream to multi-sentence text) and the integration of entity-grid coherence with a multimodal CNN+BRNN are reasonably novel within the cited literature. The retrieval framing is not new, but its extension to sequence-level coherence is a contribution.

Significance
- If validated, the approach could influence multi-sentence description for image collections, an important application. However, the reliance on retrieval and the current coherence–image coupling may limit broader impact. A stronger empirical and ablation analysis would enhance significance.

Suggestions for improvement
- Normalize scores by sequence length; compare with a version where coherence scores only sentence transitions (e.g., bigram/trigram transitions over candidate sentences) independent of image features.
- Add baselines that retrieve sentences without coherence but trained with the same structured loss; include a hierarchical RNN/LSTM that generates paragraphs.
- Provide analyses: performance vs. sequence length; shuffled-order robustness; error cases; ablation on K, beam size M; quality of segmentation/summarization.
- Use stronger coherence metrics (e.g., entity-grid scores) in addition to BLEU/CIDEr/METEOR; report statistical significance.
- Clarify data splits, sentence overlap across splits, and release data/preprocessing code.

Arguments for acceptance
- Addresses a relevant and underexplored task.
- Integrates coherence modeling into multimodal sequence selection.
- Scalable, practical pipeline with promising qualitative results and AMT preferences, especially on longer sequences.

Arguments against acceptance
- Questionable score design (coherence–image coupling, potential length bias).
- Limited and potentially unfair baseline comparisons; incomplete quantitative reporting.
- Heavy reliance on noisy preprocessing with little robustness analysis.
- “Unsupervised” claim is overstated.

Overall, the paper presents an interesting direction and a plausible model, but it would benefit from clearer justification of the scoring function, stronger baselines, and more rigorous empirical analysis.
