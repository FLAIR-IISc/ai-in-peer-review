- Proposes a novel task: generate a sequence of natural-language sentences to describe an image stream (multiple images) rather than single-image captions.  
- Introduces CRCN: combines CNN image features, bidirectional RNN sentence-sequence modeling, and a local coherence model for smooth sentence transitions.  
- Trains on blog-post data (NYC and Disneyland datasets: 20K+ posts, 140K+ images) collected from blogs.  
- Shows improvements over strong baselines on BLEU, CIDEr, METEOR, top-K recall, and in user studies.  
- Methodology is technically sound with clear architecture descriptions, equations, and reproducible experimental setup.  
- Ablation study (CRCN vs RCN vs 1NN) effectively demonstrates the contributions of BRNN and the coherence component.  
- Paper is generally well-written and organized, with clear problem statement and illustrative figures.  
- Minor clarity issues: the link to Barzilay & Lapata’s coherence work and the definition/timing of “local coherence model” need clearer exposition.  
- Could better justify why coherence is necessary for image streams but not for videos beyond brief remarks about “sharp changes.”  
- Core contribution (image-stream description) is novel and distinct from video captioning; integration of known components is meaningful for the new task.  
- Use of blog posts as a scalable, practical data source is innovative and valuable for future research.  
- Strengths: novel problem, well-designed CRCN, comprehensive quantitative and user-study evaluation, scalable dataset, and strong ablation evidence.  
- Weaknesses: limited discussion of computational complexity/training time, insufficient failure-case analysis for long/semantically-shifting streams, incomplete connection to prior coherence work, and a literature review that could be more thorough.  
- Recommendation: Accept — significant, well-executed contribution meeting conference standards, with only minor concerns to address.
