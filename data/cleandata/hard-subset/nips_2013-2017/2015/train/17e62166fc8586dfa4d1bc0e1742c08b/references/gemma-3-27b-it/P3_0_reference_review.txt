## Review of "Expressing an Image Stream with a Sequence of Natural Sentences"

**Overall Assessment:** This paper tackles a challenging and increasingly relevant problem: generating coherent descriptions for sequences of images, moving beyond single-image captioning. The proposed CRCN architecture, integrating CNNs, RNNs, and a coherence model, is a reasonable approach, and the experimental results on blog data are promising, demonstrating improvement over several baselines. However, the paper suffers from a lack of depth in theoretical justification and a reliance on empirical validation. While the results are encouraging, the core novelty doesn't feel groundbreaking enough for a strong acceptance at NIPS, and the presentation could be significantly improved. I lean towards **weak reject** with significant revisions requested.

**1. Summary and Relation to Prior Work:**

The paper addresses the problem of generating a sequence of natural language sentences describing a sequence of images, motivated by the common scenario of users capturing experiences through photo streams. The authors propose the Coherent Recurrent Convolutional Network (CRCN) which combines image representation using CNNs, sequential modeling via bidirectional RNNs, and a local coherence model to ensure smooth transitions between sentences.  The authors correctly position their work within the growing field of image captioning, differentiating it from single-image tasks and acknowledging prior work on video description. They rightly identify the challenge of obtaining parallel image-text data for sequences and their solution of leveraging blog posts as a reasonable heuristic.

The work builds on established techniques (CNNs for image features, RNNs for language modeling, coherence modeling) but attempts to unify them for a sequence-to-sequence problem. They correctly identify key related work such as [9, 12, 21, 5] and attempt to differentiate themselves, primarily highlighting the inclusion of a coherence model, which is a legitimate point. However, the discussion of related work feels somewhat superficial and doesn't fully acknowledge the nuances of existing approaches (more on this in weaknesses).

**2. Strengths:**

* **Problem Formulation:**  The problem of describing image streams is a natural extension of single-image captioning and addresses a real-world need.
* **Architecture Design:** The CRCN architecture is a sensible integration of existing components.  The inclusion of a coherence model is a key contribution, addressing the inherent challenge of maintaining fluency across multiple sentences.
* **Dataset Creation:**  Leveraging blog posts as a source of weakly supervised image-text pairs is a clever approach to overcome the lack of large, parallel datasets for image streams.
* **Empirical Evaluation:** The paper provides reasonably thorough empirical evaluation using both quantitative metrics (BLEU, Recall) and user studies (AMT).
* **Positive Results:** The CRCN consistently outperforms the baselines, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed approach.

**3. Weaknesses:**

* **Lack of Theoretical Justification:**  The paper lacks a strong theoretical foundation.  While the architecture is intuitive, there’s limited discussion on *why* this specific combination of components is expected to perform well.  The coherence model, in particular, feels like a somewhat ad-hoc addition without a strong theoretical motivation.
* **Coherence Model Details:** The coherence model is described, but its implementation details are somewhat vague. Specifically, how effective are parse trees at capturing coherence in blog posts that often exhibit informal writing styles?  A more in-depth analysis of the effectiveness of this component is needed.
* **Baseline Comparison:**  The comparison with baselines is somewhat weak.  While the baselines are reasonable, they aren’t pushed to their full potential. For instance, could more sophisticated attention mechanisms within the RNNs improve their performance?  A more competitive set of baselines would strengthen the paper.
* **Limited Ablation Study:** The ablation study (comparing CRCN to RCN and 1NN) is helpful, but could be more extensive.  For example, what's the impact of removing the bidirectional aspect of the RNN? What is the impact of the data augmentation?
* **Superficial Related Work Discussion:** The related work section is somewhat superficial.  For example, it mentions video captioning but doesn’t fully explore the similarities and differences between modeling temporal coherence in video (where frames are highly correlated) versus image streams (where transitions can be abrupt). The discussion of [11] feels particularly brief – their work on retrieving *image sequences* is highly relevant, and a more detailed comparison would be beneficial.
* **Writing Quality:** The writing is generally clear, but contains some awkward phrasing and repetitive statements. The paper could benefit from a thorough editing pass.



**4. Originality and Significance:**

The originality of the work is moderate.  While combining CNNs, RNNs, and a coherence model for image stream description is a novel combination, each individual component is well-established. The main contribution lies in the specific architecture and the application to this novel task. The significance is potentially high, as the ability to automatically describe image streams has applications in various domains, including photo organization, storytelling, and accessibility. However, the impact is limited by the reliance on blog data and the lack of generalizability to other domains without significant adaptation.

**5. Arguments for and Against Acceptance:**

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* Addresses a novel and relevant problem.
* Proposes a reasonable and well-motivated architecture.
* Demonstrates empirically strong results on a challenging dataset.
* Includes both quantitative and qualitative evaluation.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* Lacks strong theoretical justification.
* Coherence model could be more rigorously evaluated.
* Baseline comparison could be more competitive.
* Limited ablation study.
* Related work discussion is superficial.
* Writing quality needs improvement.




**Recommendation:**

**Weak Reject.** While the paper presents a promising approach to a challenging problem, the lack of theoretical depth, the superficial related work discussion, and the need for more extensive experimentation prevent it from reaching the quality threshold for NIPS.  I would recommend the authors significantly revise the paper, addressing the weaknesses outlined above, before resubmitting it to a future conference.  Specifically, I suggest:

1. **Strengthen Theoretical Justification:** Provide a more rigorous analysis of why the CRCN architecture is expected to perform well, particularly regarding the coherence model.
2. **Expand Ablation Study:** Conduct a more comprehensive ablation study to evaluate the contribution of each component.
3. **Improve Baseline Comparison:**  Include more competitive baselines.
4. **Detailed Coherence Model Analysis:** Provide a more in-depth analysis of the coherence model’s effectiveness, perhaps including examples of how it influences sentence selection.
5. **Revise Related Work:** Thoroughly discuss related work, acknowledging nuances and limitations of existing approaches.
6. **Improve Writing Clarity:**  Ensure the paper is clearly written and well-organized.
