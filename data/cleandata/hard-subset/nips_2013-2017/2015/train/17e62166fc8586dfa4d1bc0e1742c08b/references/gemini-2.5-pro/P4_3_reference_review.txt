This paper addresses the novel task of generating a coherent sequence of natural language sentences from an input stream of images. The authors argue this is a natural extension of the well-studied single-image-to-sentence problem, motivated by how users capture experiences (e.g., trips) in photo collections. The core contribution is a new multimodal architecture, the Coherent Recurrent Convolutional Network (CRCN). This model integrates three key components: a Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) for extracting visual features from each image, a Bidirectional Recurrent Neural Network (BRNN) to model the semantic flow of the sentence sequence, and an entity-based local coherence model to ensure smooth, logical transitions between sentences. To train this model, the authors propose a practical method of sourcing parallel data by processing online blog posts, using text segmentation and summarization to align image sequences with sentence sequences. The problem is framed as a retrieval task at test time: for a query image stream, candidate sentence sequences are constructed from the training data and ranked using the learned CRCN compatibility score. The paper presents a comprehensive evaluation on two new blog post datasets (NYC and Disneyland), showing significant improvements over adapted state-of-the-art single-sentence generation methods using both automatic metrics and human studies.

This work builds upon the recent success of combining CNNs and RNNs for single-image captioning (e.g., Karpathy et al., 2015; Vinyals et al., 2015). However, it distinguishes itself by tackling a sequence-to-sequence problem where the output is a multi-sentence paragraph. The key novelty lies in explicitly modeling the coherence of the output paragraph, a dimension not typically addressed in single-sentence or even video-captioning works (e.g., Donahue et al., 2015), where visual content is often more continuous.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The paper introduces and formalizes a new, important, and challenging problem: describing an image stream with a coherent story. This is a logical and significant step beyond single-image captioning, moving the field towards more holistic visual understanding and narrative generation. The potential impact is high, with applications in automatic photo album summarization, travelogue creation, and more.

2.  **Technical Soundness:** The proposed CRCN architecture is well-motivated and technically sound. The combination of a CNN for visual perception, a BRNN for temporal context, and a classic entity-based coherence model (Barzilay & Lapata, 2008) to capture discourse structure is a principled and effective approach to the problem. The integration of these disparate components into a unified, end-to-end trainable model using a max-margin loss is well-executed.

3.  **Novel Data Sourcing:** A major challenge for this new task is the lack of suitable datasets. The authors' solution of mining and pre-processing blog posts to create weakly-supervised parallel data is both clever and practical. This methodology for dataset creation is a valuable contribution in itself and can be adopted by others working on similar problems.

4.  **Thorough Evaluation:** The experimental validation is comprehensive and convincing. The authors compare their method against several strong, recent baselines adapted for the sequence-generation task. The use of multiple automatic metrics (BLEU, CIDEr, METEOR, Recall@K) and a well-designed user study on Amazon Mechanical Turk provides strong evidence for the model's effectiveness. The ablation study, which compares the full CRCN model to a nearest-neighbor baseline (1NN) and a version without the coherence model (RCN), clearly demonstrates the contribution of each component.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Retrieval vs. Generation:** The method is fundamentally retrieval-based, meaning it constructs paragraphs by selecting and arranging sentences that already exist in the training corpus. This limits the expressive power of the system, as it cannot generate truly novel sentences to describe unseen visual compositions. The paper frames this as using "sentences from training database to author a sequence," but the distinction and its implications could be discussed more explicitly. While this is a reasonable first step for this complex problem, it is a significant limitation compared to true generative models.

2.  **Inference Approximation:** The search space for the best sentence sequence at test time is exponential (K^N). The paper proposes a divide-and-conquer strategy with beam search to make this tractable. While the justification that local coherence contributes to global coherence is intuitive, the paper lacks an analysis of this approximation. It is unclear how sensitive the results are to the beam size (M) or how close the found solutions are to the optimal ones under the model. A brief discussion of the computational complexity and the trade-offs of this search strategy would strengthen the paper.

3.  **Dependence on Pre-processing:** The quality of the training data hinges on a multi-step pre-processing pipeline involving heuristic-based text segmentation and LSA-based summarization. This pipeline may introduce noise or errors (e.g., incorrect image-text alignment, suboptimal sentence selection), but the model's robustness to such noise is not analyzed. The quality of the "ground truth" for evaluation is also dependent on this pipeline.

4.  **Coherence Model Integration:** The 64-dimensional feature vector from the entity-grid coherence model is zero-padded to 300 dimensions to match the BRNN output dimensionality. This seems somewhat ad-hoc. While empirically effective, it would be interesting to see if a more principled fusion method or the integration of a more modern, neural coherence model could yield further improvements.

In summary, this paper makes a strong contribution by defining a novel and significant problem and proposing a well-designed, effective model to solve it. The strengths in originality, technical execution, and evaluation outweigh the weaknesses, which are primarily related to the retrieval-based formulation and the lack of analysis of the inference approximation. The work opens up a promising new direction for research in vision and language.
