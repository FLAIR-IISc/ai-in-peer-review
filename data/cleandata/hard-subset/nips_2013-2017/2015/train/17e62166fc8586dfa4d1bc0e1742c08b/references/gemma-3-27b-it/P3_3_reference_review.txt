## Review of "Expressing an Image Stream with a Sequence of Natural Sentences"

**Overall Assessment:** This paper presents a novel and interesting problem – generating coherent textual descriptions for *sequences* of images, motivated by real-world scenarios like travel photo streams. The proposed CRCN architecture, combining CNNs, RNNs, and a local coherence model, is a reasonable approach. However, the paper suffers from a lack of thoroughness in baselines, a somewhat superficial evaluation beyond quantitative metrics, and a presentation that could be more concise. I believe this paper is currently a borderline accept/reject, leaning towards reject. Significant revisions addressing the weaknesses outlined below would be needed for acceptance.

**1. Summary & Relation to Prior Work:**

The paper correctly identifies a gap in current image captioning research, which primarily focuses on single image-single sentence pairs.  The authors propose to extend this to image streams and sentence sequences, drawing motivation from the common human experience of documenting events with a series of photos.  The CRCN architecture is well-motivated – leveraging CNNs for image features, RNNs for sequential language modeling, and crucially, a coherence model to ensure fluency across sentences. The connection to prior work is generally well-established, referencing relevant papers in image captioning ([5, 8, 9, 12, 14, 15, 26, 21, 30]) and coherence modeling [1]. The authors appropriately position their work as building upon existing multimodal RNN architectures.

**2. Strengths:**

* **Novel Problem Formulation:** Addressing image *streams* instead of single images is a worthwhile extension and opens up interesting avenues for research.
* **Coherence Modeling:** Explicitly incorporating a coherence model to address the discontinuity inherent in image streams is a strong point. This is a key contribution and differentiates this work from simply stringing together individual image captions.
* **Leveraging Blog Data:**  Using blog posts as a weakly-supervised data source is a clever way to obtain image-text pairs at scale.  The pre-processing steps (segmentation & summarization) are reasonably well-described.
* **Quantitative and Qualitative Evaluation:** The inclusion of both quantitative metrics (BLEU, Recall) *and* user studies (AMT) is commendable. 
* **Thorough Architecture Description:** The CRCN architecture is presented with sufficient detail, including the mathematical formulation of each component.

**3. Weaknesses:**

* **Weak Baselines:** The chosen baselines are not sufficiently strong or competitive. Simply extending existing *single image* captioning models ( [9, 12, 21, 30]) to handle sequences doesn’t represent a true state-of-the-art comparison. For example, simply concatenating single-image captions, even from advanced models, doesn't address the coherence problem that the authors highlight. More relevant baselines would involve methods that explicitly model sequential dependencies or coherence, even if not specifically designed for image streams (e.g., hierarchical RNNs, models leveraging discourse parsing).  
* **Limited Ablation Study:** While the authors compare variations of their own model (1NN, RCN, CRCN), the ablation is not comprehensive enough. It would be valuable to analyze the contribution of each component (CNN, RNN, coherence model) more rigorously.  What is the performance impact of *different* coherence models? How sensitive is the model to the choice of paragraph vector representation?
* **Evaluation Concerns:** While AMT provides valuable insights, the experimental setup needs more details. How many images were in the query streams used for AMT?  Were the sentence sequences presented in random order? Providing examples of the generated sequences in the paper itself, beyond Fig. 3, would also strengthen the qualitative evaluation.  The claim that “the coherence becomes more important as the passage is longer” based on the second AMT test is reasonable, but requires more statistical rigor.
* **Presentation & Conciseness:**  The paper is quite lengthy and could benefit from more concise writing. Some sections (e.g., the detailed description of the BRNN) feel overly verbose and could be streamlined. The repetition of information across sections makes the reading experience less fluid.
* **Data Augmentation:** The justification for κ=3 in the data augmentation step feels somewhat arbitrary. A more systematic exploration of different κ values would be helpful.
* **Minor Technical Detail:** The claim that the coherence model captures “entity-based” coherence feels weak. The description relies on grammatical roles (S, O, X) which are not necessarily indicative of true coreference.

**4. Arguments for/against Acceptance:**

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* Addresses a novel and important problem in image understanding and generation.
* Proposes a technically sound architecture (CRCN) with a well-motivated coherence model.
* Demonstrates promising results on a large-scale dataset, with both quantitative and qualitative evaluation.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

* Weak baseline comparisons make it difficult to assess the true significance of the contribution.
* Limited ablation study hinders a thorough understanding of the model’s components.
* Evaluation details could be more robust and the presentation more concise.
* The impact of the work remains uncertain given the limitations in the evaluation.




**5. Recommendation:**

**Reject.**  The paper presents an interesting idea and a reasonably well-executed approach. However, the weaknesses, particularly the lack of strong baselines and a comprehensive ablation study, prevent me from recommending acceptance at this time.  I believe the authors have the potential to address these concerns and significantly improve the paper with a major revision. Specifically, I recommend:

1. **Strengthen Baselines:**  Include more sophisticated baseline methods that explicitly address sequential modeling or coherence.
2. **Perform a More Thorough Ablation Study:** Analyze the contribution of each component of the CRCN architecture.
3. **Provide More Detailed Evaluation Details:** Clarify the experimental setup for the AMT studies and include more examples of generated sequences.
4. **Improve Presentation:**  Streamline the writing and remove redundant information.
