## Review of "Expressing an Image Stream with a Sequence of Natural Sentences"

**Summary:**

This paper tackles the problem of generating a sequence of natural language sentences to describe a sequence of images (an “image stream”), extending beyond the typical single image-single sentence captioning task. The authors propose a novel architecture, Coherent Recurrent Convolutional Networks (CRCN), integrating CNNs for image representation, BRNNs for language modeling, and a local coherence model to ensure fluency between sentences. They leverage a dataset constructed from blog posts with associated images and sentences and demonstrate improvements over several baselines using both quantitative metrics (BLEU, Recall) and human evaluation (AMT).

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The problem setting – generating *sequences* of sentences for *sequences* of images – is a natural and important extension of existing work and, as the authors claim, relatively unexplored.  This pushes beyond the limitations of single-image captioning.
* **Technical Approach:** The CRCN architecture is well-motivated. Combining CNNs, RNNs, and a dedicated coherence model seems a reasonable approach to tackle the challenges posed by sequential data.  The inclusion of a coherence model is particularly insightful, as sequential fluency is a critical aspect of the task.
* **Dataset & Experimental Validation:** Utilizing blog posts as a source of weakly-supervised training data is clever and practical. The authors demonstrate performance improvements over several baselines on this dataset using both automatic metrics and human evaluation, bolstering their claims. The AMT experiments, with the longer query length test, are a strong point.
* **Thorough Related Work:** The paper provides a detailed and well-organized review of relevant literature, correctly positioning their work within the existing landscape of image captioning and sequence modeling.
* **Clear Writing:** The paper is generally well-written and logically structured, facilitating understanding of the proposed method and experiments.



**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Baselines:** While the authors compare against several baselines, the chosen baselines are primarily single-sentence models adapted to the sequential setting. A stronger baseline would be a sequence-to-sequence model specifically designed for image stream captioning, even if it doesn't include a coherence model.  This would more rigorously test the contribution of the coherence component.
* **Coherence Model Complexity:**  The description of the coherence model (based on parse trees and entity grids) feels somewhat complex and potentially brittle.  It's not entirely clear how robust this approach is to variations in sentence structure and parsing accuracy.  The ablation study results are helpful, but more analysis into *why* the coherence model helps would be beneficial.
* **Dataset Limitations:** While leveraging blog posts is clever, it's important to acknowledge the inherent biases within this data source. Blog authors curate content with specific narratives and stylistic choices, which might not generalize to other image stream scenarios.  The domain is also heavily focused on tourism, potentially limiting the generalizability of the approach.
* **Evaluation Metrics:** While BLEU and Recall are standard, they may not fully capture the quality of a *coherent* multi-sentence description.  More sophisticated metrics that explicitly evaluate coherence or narrative flow would be valuable (though admittedly, these are difficult to define).



**Quality (8/10):** The paper presents a technically sound approach, supported by experimental results and analysis. The dataset creation and experimental setup are well-defined. While the coherence model is complex, it's justified by the task’s requirements.

**Clarity (8/10):** The paper is generally clear and well-organized, although the description of the coherence model could be simplified. The inclusion of a diagram illustrating the CRCN architecture (Fig. 2) is helpful.

**Originality (7/10):** The combination of components (CNN, RNN, coherence model) is not entirely novel in isolation. However, the application of this combination to the problem of image stream captioning, *and* the focus on coherence, provides a degree of originality.

**Significance (7/10):**  The problem of describing image streams is potentially impactful, with applications in areas like automated story generation from photo albums or summarizing surveillance footage. The demonstrated improvements over baselines suggest the approach has merit, but further research is needed to assess its broader applicability and real-world impact.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* Addresses a novel and important problem.
* Presents a well-motivated and technically sound approach.
* Demonstrates promising results on a relevant dataset.
* Includes a thorough literature review and thoughtful experimental analysis.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* Limited scope of baselines.
* Complexity and potential brittleness of the coherence model.
* Potential biases within the training dataset.




**Overall Recommendation:**

**Accept with Minor Revisions.**  

I recommend accepting this paper, contingent upon the authors addressing the concerns regarding the baselines and potentially providing more analysis of the coherence model.  A discussion of the limitations of the blog post dataset and its potential impact on generalizability would also be valuable.  While not a groundbreaking advance, this paper represents a solid contribution to the field and opens up interesting avenues for future research.  The clear presentation and thorough experimentation make it a worthwhile addition to the NIPS proceedings.
