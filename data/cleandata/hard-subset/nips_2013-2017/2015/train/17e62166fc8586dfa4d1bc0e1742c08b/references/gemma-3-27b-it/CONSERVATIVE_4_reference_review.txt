This paper addresses the problem of generating sequential sentences to describe a stream of images, a natural extension of single-image captioning. The authors propose a Coherent Recurrent Convolutional Network (CRCN) integrating CNNs, RNNs, and a local coherence model. While the problem statement is reasonable and potentially useful, the paper suffers from a lack of substantial novelty and insufficient justification for the complexity of the proposed architecture, particularly given the relatively modest performance gains demonstrated.

The core idea – considering image sequences instead of individual images – feels incremental rather than groundbreaking. The authors claim this is an under-addressed area, but fail to adequately discuss *why* this hasn’t been a focus previously. Is it a data scarcity issue, or are the inherent difficulties insurmountable with existing techniques? The proposed solution feels like a direct application of existing components (CNNs, RNNs, coherence models) to a slightly different input, rather than a fundamentally new approach. The use of blog posts as a data source is clever, but the resulting dataset is inherently noisy and may introduce biases not fully acknowledged. The reliance on LSA for text summarization feels dated, and potentially introduces artifacts that impact downstream performance. 

The technical details are generally sound, and the model is presented with sufficient clarity. However, the complexity of CRCN – particularly the combination of BRNN and the coherence model – feels disproportionate to the reported gains. The motivation for separating forward and backward activations in the BRNN (section 3.1) is weakly supported ("empirically leads a better performance") and lacks deeper explanation. The connection between the parse trees and the coherence model is somewhat vague, and the benefit of this specific coherence mechanism is not convincingly demonstrated. 

Regarding originality, the components are well-established. The combination is not entirely trivial, but the paper doesn’t sufficiently highlight *why* this specific combination is crucial for the problem at hand. Related work is acknowledged, but the discussion feels somewhat superficial.  The authors position their work against video-sentence approaches but don’t adequately address the inherent differences between the temporal coherence in video and the potentially more abrupt transitions in image streams. 

The significance of this work is questionable. While the results are positive, the improvements over baselines are not dramatic, especially considering the increased complexity of the CRCN. The qualitative examples (Fig. 3) are helpful, but not compelling enough to justify the architectural choices. The user studies (Table 2) support the quantitative findings, but don’t reveal any significant user preference that would justify widespread adoption of the proposed method. The AMT results showing increased coherence benefit at longer sequence lengths is sensible, but it begs the question of *why* the baselines struggle with longer sequences, and whether a simpler model could be improved to address this specific issue.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

* Addresses a potentially useful problem – sequence-to-sequence image description.
* Presents a reasonably well-engineered system integrating established techniques.
* Demonstrates positive, albeit modest, improvements over baselines.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

* Lack of substantial novelty. The approach feels like a straightforward application of existing components.
* The complexity of the proposed architecture is not fully justified by the performance gains.
* Reliance on noisy blog data introduces potential biases.
* The significance of the work is questionable; the improvements are not transformative.
* Limited ablation studies to justify the contribution of each component.

**Overall:** I lean towards rejection. The paper is technically sound and well-written, but it lacks the novelty and significance expected of a NIPS publication. The authors have identified a reasonable problem, but their solution, while competent, doesn’t represent a substantial advancement in the field. More thorough ablation studies, a stronger justification for the architectural choices, and a more compelling demonstration of performance improvements would be needed to warrant acceptance. The authors should consider simplifying the model and focusing on a more targeted analysis of the coherence component’s impact.
