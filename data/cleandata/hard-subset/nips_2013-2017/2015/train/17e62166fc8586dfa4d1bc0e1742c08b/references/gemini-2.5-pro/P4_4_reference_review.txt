### Summary

This paper addresses the novel task of generating a sequence of natural language sentences to describe a stream of images. The authors argue this is a natural extension of the widely studied single-image-to-sentence generation problem, motivated by how users capture experiences in photo collections. To solve this, they propose a novel architecture, the Coherent Recurrent Convolutional Network (CRCN). The CRCN is a retrieval-based model that integrates three main components: a Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) for extracting image features, a Bidirectional Recurrent Neural Network (BRNN) to model the semantic flow of sentence sequences, and an entity-based local coherence model to ensure grammatical and referential smoothness between consecutive sentences.

A key contribution is the method for creating a suitable training corpus. The authors leverage online blog posts, proposing a pipeline to pre-process them into parallel streams of image-sentence pairs. This involves segmenting blog text and associating it with images, followed by LSA-based summarization to extract a key sentence for each image. The model is trained using a max-margin objective that encourages aligned image-sentence sequences to score higher than misaligned ones. At inference time, a divide-and-conquer beam search approach is used to efficiently find a high-quality sentence sequence from an exponentially large candidate space.

The approach is evaluated on two large-scale blog datasets (NYC and Disneyland). The authors demonstrate the superiority of their method over several adapted state-of-the-art single-image captioning baselines (e.g., Karpathy et al. [9], Vinyals et al. [30], Kiros et al. [12]) and an ablation of their own model. The evaluation is comprehensive, using both automatic language/retrieval metrics and a user study conducted via Amazon Mechanical Turk.

### Quality and Technical Soundness

The paper is technically sound. The proposed CRCN model is a well-motivated combination of established techniques. The use of a CNN for image features and an RNN for sequence modeling follows a successful pattern in recent multimodal research. The integration of the entity-based coherence model [1] is a core contribution that directly addresses the challenge of generating multi-sentence, coherent text, which is a clear limitation of naively applying single-sentence models. The max-margin training objective (Eq. 4) is appropriate for this ranking/retrieval task.

The experimental methodology is strong. The authors compare against relevant, albeit adapted, state-of-the-art methods. The ablation study, comparing the full CRCN model to a variant without the coherence model (RCN) and a simple nearest-neighbor baseline (1NN), effectively demonstrates the contribution of each component. The combination of automatic metrics and human evaluation provides a robust validation of the results. The claim that the coherence model becomes more important for longer sequences is well-supported by the targeted user study.

However, a few areas could be strengthened:
1.  The inference algorithm described in Section 3.5 is a crucial part of the method for handling the `K^N` search space, but its description is somewhat brief. A more detailed explanation or pseudocode of the "approximate divide-and-conquer strategy" would improve clarity and reproducibility. The claim that this search "achieves almost optimal solutions" is a strong one and would benefit from empirical validation, for instance by comparing its output to an exhaustive search on a small-scale problem.
2.  The data pre-processing pipeline relies on heuristics for text-image alignment and summarization. While the approach is practical, the quality of the resulting parallel corpus is critical to the model's performance. The paper would be more complete with an analysis of the quality of this alignment, perhaps through manual evaluation on a small subset, to assess the noise level in the training data.

### Clarity

The paper is well-written and clearly organized. The motivation is compelling, and the problem statement is well-defined. Figure 2 provides a helpful overview of the data processing pipeline and the CRCN architecture. The individual components of the model are explained logically. The related work section effectively situates the paper within the broader context of image-to-text generation and highlights its unique contributions. The experimental setup and results are presented in a way that is easy to understand. The provided qualitative examples in Figure 3 effectively illustrate the benefits of the proposed approach.

### Originality and Significance

The main contribution of this work lies in defining and providing a strong baseline for a new, challenging, and significant problem: generating coherent narratives for image streams. While prior work has focused on single images or short video clips, this paper is, to the best of my knowledge, the first to tackle generating a sequence of sentences for a sequence of still images. This is a significant step towards more complex, story-like machine understanding of visual content.

The CRCN model itself is a novel combination of existing techniques. While CNN+RNN architectures are common, the explicit integration of a classic, symbolic NLP coherence model [1] into the end-to-end deep learning framework is original. This hybrid approach, which leverages both the content modeling power of RNNs and the structural modeling of entity grids, is a valuable contribution. Furthermore, the practical solution to the data scarcity problem by mining and processing blog posts is a useful methodology for future work in this area.

The results are significant and demonstrate a clear advance over simply concatenating single-image captions. This work is likely to inspire follow-up research on story generation from visual media, exploring alternative coherence models, generative (non-retrieval) approaches, and more sophisticated data mining techniques.

### Arguments for Acceptance

1.  **Novel Problem Formulation:** The paper introduces and formalizes the important and previously unaddressed problem of generating a sentence sequence from an image stream.
2.  **Sound and Novel Model:** The proposed CRCN architecture is a thoughtful and effective combination of deep learning and classic NLP techniques tailored to the problem.
3.  **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The paper includes a thorough empirical validation with strong baselines, ablation studies, multiple metrics, and crucial human evaluation that supports the main claims.
4.  **Significant Results:** The method shows substantial improvements over state-of-the-art baselines, demonstrating the importance of modeling coherence for this task.
5.  **Practical Data Collection Method:** The proposed method for creating a training corpus from blog posts is a valuable contribution in its own right.

### Arguments against Acceptance

1.  **Clarity of Inference:** The description of the beam search algorithm for sentence sequence retrieval is brief and could benefit from more detail to ensure full understanding and reproducibility.
2.  **Heuristic Data Processing:** The reliance on heuristics for text-image alignment in the data creation process introduces an unquantified amount of noise, which may affect the model's performance and the interpretation of the results.
3.  **Adapted Baselines:** The baselines are single-sentence generation models adapted to the sequence task by concatenation. While this is a necessary first step given the novelty of the task, it inherently frames the comparison in a way that favors the proposed sequence-aware model.

Overall, the strengths of this paper—particularly its novel problem formulation, well-designed model, and thorough evaluation—far outweigh its minor weaknesses. It represents a significant and exciting step forward in the vision-and-language field.
