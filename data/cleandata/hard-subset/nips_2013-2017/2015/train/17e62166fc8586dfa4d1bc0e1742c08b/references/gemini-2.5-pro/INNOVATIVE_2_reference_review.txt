### Review of "Expressing an Image Stream with a Sequence of Natural Sentences"

**Summary**

This paper introduces the novel and ambitious task of generating a sequence of coherent natural language sentences to describe a stream of images. This work extends the popular single-image-to-single-sentence captioning problem to a more complex sequence-to-sequence setting, which better reflects how humans capture and narrate experiences. The authors propose a new architecture, the Coherent Recurrent Convolutional Network (CRCN), which combines three main components: a standard CNN for image feature extraction, a Bidirectional RNN to model the flow of content across sentences, and, most notably, an entity-based local coherence model inspired by classic NLP work (Barzilay & Lapata, 2008) to ensure smooth and logical transitions between sentences. The model is trained and evaluated on a large-scale dataset mined from blog posts, a clever and practical approach to obtaining weakly-supervised data for this new task. The authors demonstrate through extensive quantitative metrics and user studies that their proposed model significantly outperforms adapted state-of-the-art single-image captioning methods.

**Strengths**

1.  **High Novelty and Significance:** The primary strength of this paper is its bold vision. It moves beyond the now-crowded space of single-image captioning to tackle a much more challenging and arguably more useful problem. Defining and providing a strong baseline for this new task of "image stream verbalization" is a significant contribution in itself. This is precisely the kind of work that can open up a new and fruitful direction for the vision and language community.

2.  **Creative and Effective Model Architecture:** The proposed CRCN architecture is a novel and thoughtful combination of techniques. While CNNs and RNNs are standard components, the integration of an explicit, entity-based coherence model is a clever move. Many sequence-to-sequence models rely on the RNN to implicitly learn coherence, which can be difficult. By incorporating a proven, external model of discourse coherence, the authors provide a strong inductive bias that, as their experiments show, is highly effective. This blend of modern deep learning with a more symbolic NLP approach is refreshing and well-motivated for this task.

3.  **Thorough and Convincing Evaluation:** The authors have conducted a comprehensive evaluation. They compare against multiple strong baselines adapted from related tasks, and crucially, they perform an ablation study (CRCN vs. RCN) that clearly isolates and validates the contribution of the coherence model. The use of both automatic language/retrieval metrics and an Amazon Mechanical Turk user study provides a robust case for their method's superiority. The user study's finding that the coherence model's benefit increases with sequence length is a particularly insightful result.

**Weaknesses and Opportunities for Improvement**

While this is an exciting paper, there are some aspects that could be viewed as limitations, though in my opinion, they do not detract from the overall contribution and are better seen as avenues for future work.

1.  **Retrieval-Based Approach:** The current method is retrieval-based rather than truly generative; it selects and re-ranks existing sentences from a large training corpus. This is a pragmatic and effective choice for a first paper on a new problem, as it sidesteps the immense difficulty of word-level generation of long, coherent paragraphs. However, this limits the model's expressive power to the sentences seen during training. A fully generative extension of this work would be a natural and exciting next step.

2.  **Looseness of Coherence Model Integration:** The integration of the coherence model, while effective, is somewhat "bolted on." The coherence feature vector is computed over the entire candidate sentence sequence and then combined with the RNN outputs before the final fully connected layers. A tighter, more dynamic integration—perhaps where coherence constraints could influence the state transitions of the RNN at each step—could be a powerful future research direction.

3.  **Heuristic Data Processing and Inference:** The data collection pipeline relies on heuristics for text segmentation and summarization (LSA), which may introduce noise. Similarly, the inference process uses an approximate divide-and-conquer search to navigate the exponential `K^N` search space. While these are necessary and reasonable practical choices, they represent points of approximation in the system. Given the novelty of the problem, these are acceptable compromises.

**Recommendation**

I strongly recommend accepting this paper.

This work tackles a novel, important, and challenging problem with a creative and well-motivated approach. Its core contribution—defining the task of image stream-to-paragraph generation and proposing a coherence-aware model—is a significant step forward for the field. While the methodology has limitations (e.g., being retrieval-based, the somewhat loose integration of the coherence model), these are minor points when weighed against the novelty and ambition of the work. This paper is not an incremental improvement on an existing task; it charts a new course. It is exactly the kind of forward-looking, high-impact research that NIPS should champion. I anticipate that this work will inspire a great deal of follow-up research on generating narrative descriptions for sequential visual data.
