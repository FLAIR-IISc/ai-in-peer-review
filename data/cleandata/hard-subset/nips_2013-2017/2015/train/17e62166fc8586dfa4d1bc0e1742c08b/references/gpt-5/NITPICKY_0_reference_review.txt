Summary and relation to prior work
The paper tackles sequence-to-sequence captioning for image streams: given a sequence of photos (e.g., from a blog), retrieve a coherent sequence of natural sentences. The proposed coherent recurrent convolutional network (CRCN) combines: (i) CNN features (VGG fc7) for images, (ii) a bidirectional RNN over sentence-level paragraph vectors (doc2vec) to model content flow, and (iii) an entity-grid–based local coherence model (Barzilay & Lapata) to encourage fluent multi-sentence output. Training data are mined from blogs via paragraph-based segmentation and LSA summarization to produce one (augmented to κ=3) sentence per image. The model assigns a compatibility score between an image stream and a candidate text sequence and is trained with a max-margin loss using sampled negatives. At test time, candidate sequences are generated by per-image KNN retrieval and pruned via a divide-and-conquer beam search. Experiments on two travel-blog datasets (NYC, Disneyland) report improvements over multimodal baselines (LBL variants, CNN+RNN, CNN+LSTM) on BLEU/CIDEr/METEOR and retrieval metrics, plus AMT preferences.

Strengths
- Addresses a natural and underexplored setting: sequence of images to multi-sentence descriptions, distinct from single-image captioning and video-to-text.
- Integrates entity-grid coherence with a sequence model; the architectural attempt to couple content flow and coherence is interesting.
- Leverages weakly aligned, large-scale blog data; the pipeline is pragmatic and does not require manual sentence-level alignment.
- Provides both automatic metrics and user studies; shows coherence matters more for longer sequences.

Weaknesses and detailed comments
Quality
- Scoring formulation is questionable. The sequence-level coherence vector g (derived from text only) is dotted with each per-image visual feature v_t and summed over t: Skl = Σt s_k,t·v_l,t + g_k·v_l,t. This repeats the same g across all time steps, effectively scaling the coherence signal by sequence length and introducing a length bias; no length normalization is discussed. More fundamentally, g is text-only; its dot with visual features does not encode image–text relevance of coherence, merely allows the model to learn some projection that correlates with images. A clearer formulation would make coherence a prior over sentence transitions independent of images, or condition coherence on image transitions explicitly.
- BRNN choice and justification. The paper avoids gated units (LSTM/GRU), citing vanishing gradients at word level, but still models possibly long sentence sequences with a vanilla BRNN and ReLU. There is no analysis of gradient stability, and baselines show LSTM benefits; authors should compare BiLSTM/GRU at the sentence level.
- Co-reference/coherence details are underspecified. Constructing an entity grid requires reliable coreference resolution across sentences. The paper mentions Stanford CoreNLP and “concatenating parse trees into one large one,” but gives no concrete procedure for entity extraction, head matching, and coreference clustering. Without reliable coref, the entity grid becomes noisy, weakening the central claim.
- Candidate search and efficiency are vague. The divide-and-conquer beam search is only sketched. How are partial scores computed (especially for coherence that is defined on the whole sequence)? What is the runtime as a function of N, K, M? No complexity or wall-clock analysis is provided.
- Dataset construction risks noisy alignment. Assigning text segments to images via minimum index distance and summarizing with LSA may yield weak or incorrect alignments. The paper provides no human validation (e.g., alignment accuracy) or error analysis to bound noise.
- Baseline fairness. Generative single-image captioners trained to produce one sentence are concatenated as-is for sequences, which underutilizes their modeling capacity in the multi-sentence setting. Conversely, the proposed method is retrieval-based on the same domain, which likely benefits from topical redundancy in travel blogs. There is no control for near-duplicate images across splits, which could inflate retrieval scores; deduplication across train/val/test should be reported.
- Missing quantitative details. The paper repeatedly refers to tables and figures and claims “large margins” but provides no numbers in the main text we can inspect. No significance testing on AMT or inter-annotator agreement is reported. No ablations over K, κ, M, BRNN variants, or coherence history length are presented.
- The statement that “Wf2Wf1 does not reduce to a single linear mapping, thanks to dropout” is misleading. At test time, the composition is linear; dropout is a training-time regularizer.
- Loss and sampling details are unclear. Eq. (4) appears to sum over all l; later the text mentions sampling “100 ne examples.” It is not clear whether negatives are within-batch or global, and how length imbalance is handled in the loss.
- Reproducibility: Code/data availability are not mentioned; doc2vec is “trained separately from the two classes,” but hyperparameters, vocabulary handling, and training epochs are omitted.

Clarity
- Numerous writing/formatting issues: incomplete and inconsistent references (many missing years and venues), typos (“ne examples,” “more demanded”), awkward phrasing, figures not available in text. The BRNN is said to have five layers but only four are listed. Notation for sequence indices (k, l, t; N per sequence) is confusing at times.
- Important pieces are deferred to supplementary without sufficient summary (implementation details, more results).

Originality
- The problem formulation (image streams to multi-sentence descriptions) is timely and distinct from single-image captioning. However, related video-to-text works with multi-sentence outputs and temporal modeling (e.g., S2VT; hierarchical RNNs for paragraph generation) are not adequately discussed, nor are multi-sentence or paragraph-level captioning/coherence models beyond [1]. The entity-grid coherence integration is a known technique brought into this setting; the combination is reasonable but incremental.

Significance
- Potentially useful for photo-album narration, but demonstrated only on two travel domains with retrieval-based outputs. Without stronger baselines tailored to multi-sentence coherence, ablations, and clearer gains, the impact is uncertain. Lack of dataset release limits reuse.

Pros for acceptance
- Addresses an underexplored and practical task.
- Sensible architectural idea to couple sentence-level sequence modeling with coherence.
- Large-scale weakly supervised data construction; pragmatic pipeline.
- Human evaluation suggests coherence gains for longer sequences.

Cons for acceptance
- Methodological issues in scoring and coherence–vision interaction; possible length bias.
- Underspecified and potentially weak coreference/coherence construction.
- Baseline comparison may be unfair; no deduplication check; missing ablations and numbers.
- Clarity and presentation problems, incomplete references.
- Limited analysis of efficiency and reproducibility.

Suggestions for improvement
- Normalize scores by sequence length; reconsider how coherence influences scoring (e.g., as a transition potential in a structured model independent of images, or condition coherence on visual transitions).
- Replace BRNN with BiLSTM/GRU at the sentence level; provide ablations.
- Detail and evaluate the coreference pipeline; report entity-grid accuracy and its contribution.
- Ensure fair baselines (e.g., hierarchical LSTM trained for multi-sentence output), deduplicate across splits, and provide comprehensive quantitative tables and significance tests.
- Release code/data or at least detailed dataset statistics; include runtime/complexity.

Recommendation
Reject. The paper tackles an interesting problem and has promising ideas, but the current version has methodological ambiguities, presentation and evaluation gaps, and fairness/reproducibility concerns that prevent confident acceptance. Confidence: high.
