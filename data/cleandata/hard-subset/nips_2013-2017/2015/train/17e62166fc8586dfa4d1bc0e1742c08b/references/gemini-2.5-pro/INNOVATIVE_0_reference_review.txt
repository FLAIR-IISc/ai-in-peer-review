This paper addresses the novel and ambitious problem of generating a sequence of natural sentences to describe a stream of images. While most prior work has focused on the single image-to-sentence mapping, this work extends both the input and output to sequences, tackling the more complex challenge of creating a coherent, multi-sentence narrative for a collection of photos, such as one might find in a travel blog. The authors propose a new architecture, the Coherent Recurrent Convolutional Network (CRCN), which uniquely integrates three components: a CNN for image understanding, a bidirectional RNN for modeling the flow of textual content, and a classic entity-based local coherence model to ensure smooth and logical transitions between sentences. The model is trained and evaluated on a large-scale dataset created from blog posts, which naturally provide the required parallel data of image streams and associated text.

**Strengths:**

1.  **High Novelty and Significance:** The primary strength of this paper lies in its bold problem formulation. Moving the vision-and-language community from single images to image streams and from single sentences to coherent paragraphs is a significant and necessary step towards more human-like AI. This work is, to my knowledge, the first to formally define and provide a strong baseline for this problem. The potential impact is substantial, opening up a new and exciting direction of research that could lead to applications in automatic storytelling, trip summarization, and more. This is exactly the kind of forward-looking research that NIPS should encourage.

2.  **Novel Integration of Techniques:** The proposed CRCN architecture is a clever combination of modern deep learning components and a classic, structured NLP model. While CNNs and RNNs are now standard for this domain, the explicit integration of the entity-grid coherence model by Barzilay and Lapata is a novel and well-motivated choice. The authors correctly identify that unlike video, where frames are often visually similar, image streams can have abrupt visual shifts. Explicitly modeling discourse coherence is therefore not just an addition but a crucial component for solving this task, a fact strongly supported by their ablation studies. This hybrid approach is a refreshing departure from a purely end-to-end deep learning paradigm and demonstrates a thoughtful approach to model design.

3.  **Strong Empirical Validation:** The paper provides a thorough evaluation using both automatic metrics and human studies on Amazon Mechanical Turk. The results compellingly demonstrate that the proposed method significantly outperforms baselines adapted from state-of-the-art single-image captioning models. The ablation study, comparing the full CRCN model to an RNN-only version (RCN) and a nearest-neighbor baseline, effectively isolates and validates the contribution of the coherence model and the sequence modeling. The user study, in particular, confirms that the generated passages are not just relevant but also preferred by human judges for their fluency, which is the core challenge the paper sets out to solve. The finding that the advantage of the coherence model increases with sequence length is a particularly insightful result.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Retrieval vs. Generation:** The proposed method is fundamentally retrieval-based; it selects and re-ranks sequences of *existing* sentences from the training corpus. While this is a practical and effective first approach to a very difficult problem, it is not a truly generative model. This limits the system's expressive power to the vocabulary and sentence structures present in the training data. This limitation should be acknowledged more explicitly, though it does not detract from the novelty of the core problem and the proposed scoring model. I view this as a pragmatic simplification for a pioneering work, rather than a fatal flaw.

2.  **Baseline Comparisons:** The baselines are state-of-the-art models for single-image captioning, and their outputs are simply concatenated to form a sequence. This is a reasonable, if somewhat weak, point of comparison, as these models were not designed for sequence-level coherence. It is therefore not surprising that the proposed model, which is explicitly designed for this task, performs much better. However, given the novelty of the problem, there are no directly comparable prior methods, making this approach understandable.

3.  **Complexity of Data Pre-processing:** The creation of the training dataset relies on a pipeline of heuristics for text segmentation and summarization. The quality of the learned model is dependent on the success of these pre-processing steps, which may introduce noise into the image-sentence alignments. While the use of blog data is very clever, the paper could benefit from a brief analysis of the quality or potential failure modes of this data extraction process.

**Summary and Recommendation:**

This paper introduces a novel and important problem to the community and presents a well-designed and effective model for solving it. The conceptual contribution of shifting the focus from single images to image streams and coherent narratives is highly significant. The architectural novelty of integrating an explicit coherence model with deep networks is both clever and empirically justified.

While the approach has limitations—being retrieval-based rather than fully generative and relying on somewhat weak baselines for comparison—these are acceptable tradeoffs for a paper that is breaking new ground. The core ideas are bold, the direction is promising, and the execution is solid. This is a paper that is likely to be highly cited and to inspire a great deal of follow-up work. I value its novelty and forward-thinking approach highly and strongly recommend acceptance.
