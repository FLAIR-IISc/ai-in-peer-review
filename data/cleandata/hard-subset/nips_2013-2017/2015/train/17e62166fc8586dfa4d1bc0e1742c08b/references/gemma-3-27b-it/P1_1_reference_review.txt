## Peer Review for "Expressing an Image Stream with a Sequence of Natural Sentences"

**Overall Assessment:** This paper presents a novel and interesting problem: generating coherent natural language descriptions for *sequences* of images, moving beyond the single image captioning paradigm. The proposed CRCN architecture, integrating CNNs, RNNs, and a coherence model, is a reasonable approach to tackle this challenge. The experiments on blog datasets are well-conducted, demonstrating promising results compared to established baselines. While the contributions are valuable, the paper suffers from some clarity issues and a lack of in-depth analysis of the coherence model's impact.  I recommend **acceptance with minor revisions**.

**1. Summary of Main Ideas and Relation to Prior Work:**

The paper addresses the problem of translating a stream of images into a coherent sequence of sentences.  This is a natural extension of the popular image captioning task and is motivated by real-world scenarios like describing travel experiences represented by photo streams. The authors propose a CRCN architecture that builds upon recent advances in multimodal learning (CNNs for image features, RNNs for language modeling – specifically BRNNs) and introduces a crucial component: a local coherence model. This model aims to ensure a smooth flow between generated sentences, addressing a key challenge that single-image captioning doesn't face. 

The work is well-situated within the existing literature. The authors correctly identify and differentiate their approach from retrieval-based methods ([6, 8, 21, 26]) and video description models ([23, 32]), highlighting the novelty of their explicit coherence modeling. They also accurately place their work within the broader context of multimodal RNN architectures ([9, 12, 19, 30]). The reference to latent structural SVM for image sequence retrieval [11] provides good context, clearly differentiating the opposite direction of their problem.

**2. Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:**  Addressing sequential image description is a genuinely novel contribution.  The problem setting is well-motivated and likely to attract further research.
*   **Technical Soundness:** The proposed CRCN architecture seems well-designed, effectively combining existing successful components (CNNs, RNNs) with a novel coherence model. The use of BRNNs is justified, and the incorporation of parse trees for the coherence model is a reasonable choice.
*   **Empirical Evaluation:** The experiments on the NYC and Disneyland blog datasets are comprehensive.  The authors compare against several strong baselines and utilize both quantitative metrics (BLEU, Recall) and user studies (AMT).
*   **Data Source:** Leveraging blog posts as a source of weakly supervised image-text data is a clever idea, overcoming the difficulty of obtaining large, parallel corpora for sequential image description.
*   **Reproducibility:** The authors mention providing details of implementation, which is a positive sign for reproducibility (though I haven't verified this).

**3. Weaknesses:**

*   **Clarity:** While the overall idea is clear, some sections are dense and difficult to follow. The notation is occasionally inconsistent, and the explanation of the BRNN and coherence model could be more intuitive.  For example, the explanation of how the coherence model combines entity grid transitions with a 300-dimensional vector feels rushed.
*   **Coherence Model Analysis:** The coherence model is a central component of the approach, yet its contribution isn't thoroughly analyzed. While the quantitative results show an improvement with the CRCN, the paper lacks a more detailed ablation study specifically focusing on the coherence model’s impact.  How does the performance change with different history parameters? What is the impact of different parsing techniques?
*   **Limited Baselines:** The selected baselines, while reasonable, are mostly extensions of single-image captioning approaches. A stronger baseline specifically designed for sequential data (e.g., adapting a video description model to image streams) would strengthen the evaluation.
*   **Qualitative Examples:** While the provided examples are helpful, they are limited. Showing a wider range of examples, including failure cases, would be beneficial.
*   **Loss Function Discussion:** The max-margin loss function is stated but lacks a clear justification for its choice within the context of this specific problem.



**4. Arguments For and Against Acceptance:**

**For Acceptance:**

*   The problem addressed is novel and relevant.
*   The proposed architecture is well-motivated and technically sound.
*   The experimental results demonstrate a clear improvement over strong baselines.
*   The use of blog data is a creative solution to the data scarcity problem.

**Against Acceptance:**

*   The clarity of the paper could be improved.
*   The analysis of the coherence model is insufficient.
*   The baseline comparison could be more rigorous.




**5. Specific Suggestions for Revision:**

*   **Improve Clarity:** Rewrite sections 3.1 and 3.2 with a focus on clarity and intuition. Provide more detailed explanations of the BRNN and coherence model components.
*   **Ablation Study:** Conduct a thorough ablation study focusing on the coherence model.  Experiment with different history parameters, parsing techniques, and consider removing the coherence model entirely to quantify its impact.
*   **Stronger Baselines:** Consider adapting a video description model as a baseline to provide a more challenging comparison.
*   **Expand Qualitative Results:** Include a wider range of qualitative examples, showcasing both successes and failures of the CRCN.
*    **Discuss Loss Function:** Explain the rationale for choosing the max-margin loss function.
*   **Refine Notation:** Ensure consistency in notation throughout the paper.




**Overall, this is a promising paper with a significant contribution to the field. Addressing the weaknesses outlined above, particularly enhancing clarity and providing a more in-depth analysis of the coherence model, will significantly strengthen the paper and warrant a strong acceptance.**
