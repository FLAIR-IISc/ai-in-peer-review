This paper addresses the problem of generating a sequence of natural language sentences to describe a stream of images, framing it as a logical extension to the well-studied single-image captioning task. The authors propose a multimodal architecture, the Coherent Recurrent Convolutional Network (CRCN), which aims to produce descriptive and coherent textual narratives. The method is primarily retrieval-based. For a given input image stream, candidate sentences are first retrieved for each image from a training database. The CRCN model then scores and ranks possible sentence sequences to find the optimal one. The model integrates three established components: a Convolutional Neural Network (VGGNet) to extract image features, a Bidirectional Recurrent Neural Network (BRNN) to model the semantic flow of the sentence sequence (represented by paragraph vectors), and a classic entity-based local coherence model (Barzilay and Lapata, 2008) to capture syntactic and referential consistency. The model is trained using a max-margin loss on a dataset constructed from online blog posts. The authors evaluate their method against several baselines on two blog datasets (NYC and Disneyland), using both automatic metrics and human studies, demonstrating superior performance.

**Strengths:**
1.  **Problem Formulation:** The paper introduces a well-motivated and practical problem. Moving from single images to image streams is a natural and important step for the field, with clear applications in areas like photo album summarization and storytelling.
2.  **Methodological Soundness:** The overall approach of combining visual features, sequential text modeling, and an explicit coherence model is logical and methodologically sound. Rather than proposing an entirely unproven technique, the authors have built a system from well-understood and robust components (CNNs, RNNs, and an established NLP coherence model). The integration of the entity-grid model is a sensible choice for explicitly modeling narrative flow, which is often lacking in standard RNN-based language models.
3.  **Data Curation Strategy:** The use of online blog posts as a source of weakly-aligned image-stream-to-text data is a pragmatic and scalable solution to the challenge of data acquisition for this complex task.
4.  **Experimental Validation:** The paper includes a reasonable set of experiments, including an ablation study (CRCN vs. RCN) that successfully isolates and demonstrates the value of the explicit coherence model. The inclusion of a user study via AMT adds valuable validation to the quantitative results.

**Weaknesses:**
1.  **Weak Baselines:** The primary weakness of this work lies in the choice of baselines. The authors adapt several state-of-the-art single-image captioning models by simply generating a sentence for each image independently and concatenating the results. These models were not designed for sequence-level generation and inherently lack any mechanism for inter-sentence coherence. Consequently, it is not surprising that a model explicitly designed to optimize for coherence outperforms them. A much stronger and more appropriate baseline would involve adapting sequence-to-sequence architectures (e.g., an LSTM encoder over image features decoding into a sequence of sentences, perhaps with a hierarchical structure). Without comparison to a more competitive baseline that attempts to model the sequence, the true significance of the proposed CRCN architecture is difficult to assess.
2.  **Limited Technical Novelty:** The paper's contribution is more in problem formulation than in technical innovation. The CRCN model is a combination of three existing, off-the-shelf techniques. The method of integration is also straightforward, involving the concatenation of feature vectors from the BRNN and the coherence model before feeding them into fully connected layers. While this is a valid engineering approach, the claim of a "unified end-to-end architecture" is an overstatement, as the coherence model relies on a separate, non-differentiable pipeline of parsing and entity grid creation.
3.  **Retrieval-based Limitations:** The approach is fundamentally a retrieval system, not a generative one. It can only recombine sentences that exist in its training database. This significantly limits its expressive power and ability to describe novel scenes or events not well-represented in the training data. The paper fails to discuss this important limitation and its implications. A truly generative model would be a more ambitious and potentially more impactful contribution.
4.  **Fragile Data Pre-processing Pipeline:** The entire system relies on a complex and heuristic-driven pre-processing pipeline to create the training data from raw blog posts. This involves rule-based paragraph tokenization, heuristic assignment of text blocks to images based on proximity, and LSA-based text summarization. The paper provides no evaluation of the quality or robustness of this pipeline. Errors and biases introduced at this stage could propagate and fundamentally limit the performance of the learned model. The claim of the approach being "completely unsupervised" is inaccurate; it is more correctly described as weakly supervised.

**Summary of pros and cons:**

**Pros:**
*   Addresses a novel, well-motivated, and important problem.
*   The architecture is a methodologically sound combination of established techniques.
*   The ablation study clearly shows the benefit of the explicit coherence model.
*   The use of blog data is a practical way to create a large-scale dataset.

**Cons:**
*   Baselines are weak and not designed for the task, making the experimental comparison less convincing.
*   Technical novelty is limited; the work is more of a system integration than a new model.
*   The retrieval-based nature imposes a ceiling on performance and is a key limitation that is not discussed.
*   The reliance on a non-validated, heuristic-heavy data processing pipeline is a point of concern.

In conclusion, while the paper addresses an interesting and timely problem with a sensible approach, the evaluation against weak baselines and the limited technical novelty make it a borderline case. The strength of the paper is in identifying the problem and showing that a combination of existing tools, particularly a classic NLP coherence model, can be effective. However, for a NIPS paper, one would expect either a more novel technical contribution or a more rigorous and convincing evaluation against stronger, sequence-aware baselines.
