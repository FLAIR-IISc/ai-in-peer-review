

# Review of "Expressing an Image Stream with a Sequence of Natural Sentences"

This paper proposes a novel direction in image captioning by extending the problem from generating single sentences for single images to generating sequences of sentences for image streams. The authors argue that current approaches focus on single-image, single-sentence generation, while in reality, people often take multiple sequential images during memorable events (e.g., visiting NYC) and would benefit from a coherent narrative describing the entire experience. They introduce "Coherent Recurrent Convolutional Networks" (CRCN), which integrates convolutional networks for image representation, bidirectional recurrent networks for sentence modeling, and a local coherence model for smooth transitions between sentences.

The paper's most significant contribution is its problem formulation. While image captioning has been extensively studied (e.g., [5, 8, 9, 12, 14, 15, 26, 21, 30]), this work is the first to address the problem of generating sentence sequences for image streams. The authors motivate this direction well by noting that consecutive images in a stream may show abrupt visual changes, making coherence between sentences particularly important—something existing approaches don't address.

The paper leverages blog posts as a source of image-text parallel data, which is clever and practical since blogs naturally contain image sequences with associated text. The preprocessing pipeline for transforming blogs into training data is well-justified, with text segmentation and summarization to create image-sentence pairs. The CRCN architecture is a thoughtful integration of existing components: CNNs for image features, BRNNs for sentence modeling, and a coherence model based on entity transitions (inspired by [1]) to capture discourse flow.

The evaluation is comprehensive, using both quantitative metrics (BLEU, CIDEr, METEOR, top-K recall) and user studies via Amazon Mechanical Turk. The results consistently show that CRCN outperforms baselines, particularly in retrieval metrics and user preference, with the coherence model proving especially valuable for longer image sequences. The ablation studies (comparing CRCN with variants like RCN and 1NN) effectively demonstrate the contribution of each component.

**Strengths:**
- **Novel problem formulation**: The shift from single-image to image-stream captioning is genuinely new and addresses a meaningful gap in the literature.
- **Clear motivation**: The real-world use case (tourism photo streams) is compelling and well-explained.
- **Practical data collection**: Using blogs as a source of training data is clever and avoids the need for expensive manual annotation.
- **Significant results**: The coherence model makes a measurable difference, especially for longer sequences, which aligns with the paper's core hypothesis.
- **Thorough evaluation**: The combination of quantitative metrics and user studies provides strong evidence for the approach's effectiveness.

**Weaknesses:**
- The coherence model's handling of abrupt visual changes (mentioned in the introduction) isn't fully addressed in the results.
- The blog data might not perfectly represent the target application (e.g., blogs may not always pair images with sentences in the way needed for the task).
- The paper doesn't explore how well the model generalizes to non-tourism domains beyond the two datasets tested.

**Overall assessment:**
This paper makes a significant contribution by introducing a novel problem direction in image captioning. While the technical approach builds on existing components (CNNs, RNNs, coherence models), the integration for the new problem is thoughtful and well-motivated. The paper's novelty is substantial—this isn't an incremental improvement but a clear shift in the problem space. The authors provide compelling evidence through both quantitative results and user studies that their approach delivers meaningful improvements, particularly in generating coherent multi-sentence descriptions.

The paper aligns well with NIPS' emphasis on novel ideas and significant contributions to the field. While the methodology isn't perfect (the coherence model's handling of abrupt transitions could be stronger), the novelty and significance of the problem formulation outweigh these minor limitations. The paper clearly articulates how it differs from prior work and makes a strong case for why this direction matters.

In the context of NIPS, this paper represents a bold step forward in multimodal learning, moving beyond the established single-image captioning paradigm to address a more complex, realistic scenario. The authors have demonstrated that their approach not only works but is significantly better than alternatives for the new problem they've defined.

**Recommendation:** Accept. This paper introduces a genuinely new problem and direction in image captioning with strong empirical validation. It's the kind of work that will likely inspire future research in the field, which is exactly what NIPS seeks to publish. The novelty and significance far outweigh the minor methodological limitations.
