This paper addresses the novel and interesting problem of generating a coherent sequence of natural language sentences to describe a stream of images. This extends the well-studied task of single-image captioning to a more complex, multi-image, multi-sentence setting, which is motivated by real-world scenarios like describing a photo album from a trip. The authors propose a new model, the Coherent Recurrent Convolutional Network (CRCN), to tackle this task. The core idea is to treat this as a retrieval problem: for a query image stream, the model retrieves and re-ranks candidate sentence sequences from a training database. The training data itself is cleverly sourced from blog posts, using a pre-processing pipeline to create weakly-aligned image-stream and text-sequence pairs.

The proposed CRCN model integrates three key components:
1.  A Convolutional Neural Network (VGGNet) to extract features from each image.
2.  A Bidirectional Recurrent Neural Network (BRNN) to model the content flow and context of the sentence sequence, operating on paragraph vector representations of the sentences.
3.  An entity-based local coherence model, based on the work of Barzilay and Lapata (2008), which explicitly captures the syntactic and referential consistency across sentences by analyzing transitions in the grammatical roles of discourse entities.

These components are combined in a unified architecture and trained with a max-margin loss to learn a compatibility score between image streams and sentence sequences. At inference time, a beam search-like strategy is used to efficiently find the highest-scoring sentence sequence from a combinatorially large space of candidates. The method is evaluated on two large blog post datasets (NYC and Disneyland) and shows significant improvements over adapted state-of-the-art single-image captioning baselines, according to both automatic metrics and human studies.

### Strengths

*   **Problem Formulation:** The paper tackles a novel, challenging, and significant problem. Moving from single images to image streams and from single sentences to coherent paragraphs is a logical and important step forward for the vision-and-language community. The motivation is clear and compelling.
*   **Methodological Contribution:** The proposed CRCN model is a well-reasoned architecture for this task. The main novelty lies in explicitly integrating a classic NLP coherence model into a modern deep learning framework for multimodal understanding. This is a thoughtful design choice, as coherence is crucial for multi-sentence descriptions, and visual content in an image stream can change more abruptly than in a video, making the BRNN's content-flow modeling potentially insufficient on its own.
*   **Data Sourcing and Experiments:** The use of blog posts as a source of weakly-supervised training data is a pragmatic and effective solution to the data acquisition challenge for this new problem. The experimental evaluation is thorough and convincing. The authors use two large-scale datasets, a comprehensive set of automatic metrics, and, crucially, human evaluation via AMT to validate their results. The ablation study (CRCN vs. RCN) effectively demonstrates the value added by the explicit coherence model.

### Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement

While this is a strong paper, there are a few points that could be clarified or improved.

*   **Generation vs. Retrieval:** The paper's title and introduction frame the task as "expressing" or "generating" sentences. However, the proposed method is fundamentally retrieval-based—it selects and arranges sentences that already exist in the training corpus. This is an important distinction, as the system cannot generate novel sentences to describe unseen object configurations or events. It would be beneficial for the authors to be more explicit about this limitation in the paper, perhaps by framing the task as "composing a narrative" or "retrieving a descriptive paragraph". This does not diminish the contribution but would set clearer expectations.
*   **Clarity of the Scoring Function:** The formulation of the compatibility score in Equation (3) is somewhat unclear. The score is defined as `Skl = Σt (s_kt · v_lt + g_k · v_lt)`. Here, `g_k` is derived from the coherence model and represents a global property of the entire sentence sequence `k`. Adding this global text feature to each local image-text matching score `s_kt · v_lt` is an interesting design choice, but its motivation is not well-explained. It seems to imply that the coherence of a text sequence should be compatible with *each individual image* in the stream, which is not immediately intuitive. A clearer explanation or justification for this specific formulation would strengthen the paper. For instance, is the goal for the global coherence vector `g_k` to act as a contextual bias for each local image-sentence alignment?
*   **Data Pre-processing Pipeline:** The quality of the training data hinges on a multi-step pre-processing pipeline involving text segmentation, alignment heuristics, and LSA-based summarization. While the approach seems reasonable, its robustness is not discussed. How often does the heuristic for aligning images to text blocks fail? How much information is lost during LSA summarization? Providing some qualitative examples of the pre-processing (both successes and failures) or a small-scale human evaluation of the alignment quality would add confidence in the dataset's validity.
*   **Inference Complexity:** The inference process involves a K-NN search for each of the N images in a query stream, followed by a combinatorial search over `K^N` candidates. While the authors propose an effective approximate search strategy, the initial K-NN step over a large training set could still be computationally expensive. A brief discussion of the computational complexity at test time and potential scalability to even larger datasets would be a useful addition.

### Summary of Arguments

**Arguments for acceptance:**
*   Addresses a novel and significant problem that pushes the boundary of image description.
*   Presents a thoughtful model (CRCN) that uniquely combines modern deep learning components with a classic NLP coherence model, which is well-suited to the task.
*   Provides a strong and thorough empirical evaluation on large-scale datasets, including both automatic and human metrics, and a meaningful ablation study.
*   The idea of leveraging blog posts for data is a valuable contribution in itself.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
*   The work is retrieval-based, which limits its expressive power compared to true generative models. This should be made more explicit.
*   The justification for certain design choices, particularly in the scoring function, could be clearer.
*   The dependency on a complex, heuristic-driven pre-processing pipeline introduces potential noise whose impact is not fully analyzed.

Overall, this is a high-quality paper that makes a solid contribution. The strengths, particularly the novelty of the problem and the well-motivated model, significantly outweigh the weaknesses. The paper introduces a new, interesting direction for research and provides a strong baseline for future work. I recommend acceptance.
