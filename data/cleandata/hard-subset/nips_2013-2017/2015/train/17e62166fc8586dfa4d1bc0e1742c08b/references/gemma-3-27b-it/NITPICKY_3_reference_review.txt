## Review of "Expressing an Image Stream with a Sequence of Natural Sentences"

This paper addresses the problem of generating sequential natural language descriptions for image streams, extending the more common single-image-to-single-sentence task. The authors propose a Coherent Recurrent Convolutional Network (CRCN) integrating CNNs, BRNNs, and a local coherence model, trained on blog data. While the problem statement is reasonable and represents a natural progression in image captioning, and the proposed architecture leverages established techniques, the paper suffers from numerous shortcomings in presentation, technical detail, and justification of design choices.

**Summary of Strengths:**

* **Relevant Problem:** Extending image captioning to streams is a logical and potentially useful direction, mirroring real-world scenarios like summarizing photo albums.
* **Integration of Techniques:** The architecture sensibly combines established components (CNNs, RNNs) with a coherence model, attempting a holistic approach.
* **Large-Scale Dataset:** Leveraging blog data provides a reasonable, albeit noisy, source of image-text pairings for training.

**Weaknesses (Numerous and Significant):**

* **Lack of Rigorous Baseline Comparison:**  The chosen baselines are not consistently implemented or directly comparable. Simply extending existing single-image captioning models (CNN+RNN, CNN+LSTM, MLBL-B) to sequential outputs isn't a fair comparison. A naive approach of simply concatenating single-sentence captions from each image would be a strong baseline absent here. The GloMatch baseline is poorly motivated and uncompetitive.
* **Vague Experimental Details:** The experimental setup lacks crucial details. The exact architecture of the CNN (beyond VGGNet) and RNN (number of layers, hidden unit size) are not clearly specified. The choice of κ=3 for data augmentation is justified only by "empirical tests" without presenting the results of those tests. The dropout rates are mentioned, but the impact of different dropout values is not explored.
* **Unclear Coherence Model:** The description of the local coherence model is confusing. The transition description “64 transition descriptions” feels arbitrary and lacks justification. The connection between the entity grid and the final 300-dimensional vector is weak. The claim that this model specifically addresses the discontinuities in image streams, unlike video models, is not substantiated.  Videos *also* exhibit discontinuities, even if of a different nature.
* **Score Function Justification:** The compatibility score function (Equation 3) lacks strong motivation. Why is the CNN feature vector multiplied directly by the BRNN output? What does this product represent?  The cost function (Equation 4) appears standard, but its effectiveness in this context is not thoroughly analyzed.
* **Formatting and Writing Issues:** The paper contains numerous grammatical errors and stylistic inconsistencies.  Several sentences are awkwardly phrased and difficult to understand.  There's inconsistent use of notation (e.g., `Bl` vs. `B`).  The references are not consistently formatted.
* **Overstated Claims of Novelty:** The claim of being “the first” to address this problem is overstated. While perhaps the first *end-to-end* approach, related work on video captioning (discussed by the authors) already deals with sequential data and temporal coherence.
* **AMT Study Issues:** The AMT study setup is unclear regarding the presentation of results to the turkers. The observation that coherence is more crucial with longer sequences is intuitive and doesn’t require an additional AMT experiment to confirm. 
* **Missing Ablation Studies:** No ablation studies are presented to demonstrate the contribution of each component of the CRCN (CNN, BRNN, Coherence Model). Without these, it's impossible to determine which parts are truly essential.



**Pro/Con Acceptance:**

**Arguments for Rejection:**

* Excessive number of minor flaws demonstrating a lack of meticulousness.
* Insufficiently rigorous experimental validation and comparison.
* Unclear and poorly justified design choices.
* Overstated claims and weak novelty.
* Subpar writing quality.

**Arguments for Acceptance (Weak):**

* The problem statement is relevant and potentially important.
* The approach combines existing techniques in a sensible manner.
* The dataset is large and potentially useful.



**Overall Assessment:**

This paper, while addressing a potentially interesting problem, falls short of NIPS standards due to a multitude of issues. The technical details are vague, the experimental validation is weak, and the writing quality is poor. The paper feels incomplete and lacks the rigor expected of a NIPS submission. The numerous flaws overshadow any potential contributions.

**Recommendation: Reject.**  Significant revisions addressing the points outlined above would be necessary for reconsideration. The authors need to provide substantially more detail, perform more thorough experiments, and clarify their contributions to the field.
