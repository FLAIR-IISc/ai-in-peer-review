Summary and relation to prior work
The paper tackles the problem of turning an image stream into a sequence of natural sentences, arguing that most captioning work maps a single image to a single sentence. The proposed architecture, Coherent Recurrent Convolutional Networks (CRCN), combines: (i) CNN image features (VGG fc7), (ii) a bidirectional RNN over sentence representations (doc2vec paragraph embeddings) to model sequence-level content flow, and (iii) an entity-grid based local coherence model (Barzilay & Lapata) to encourage smooth sentence-to-sentence transitions. Training uses a max-margin ranking loss that scores aligned image-stream/sentence-sequence pairs higher than mismatched pairs. At test time, for each image position, the K nearest training images provide K candidate sentences, and a divide-and-conquer beam search assembles a high-scoring sequence.

A key contribution is harvesting weakly aligned image–text sequences from large-scale travel blogs (NYC and Disneyland), using heuristics for image-to-text segmentation (distance-based assignment) and LSA-based summarization to obtain one key sentence per image (plus augmentation via top-κ summaries). Experiments compare CRCN (and a variant without coherence) to single-image captioning baselines (Kiros et al. multimodal LBL variants; Karpathy CNN+RNN; Vinyals CNN+LSTM; a global matching baseline). Metrics include BLEU, CIDEr, METEOR, retrieval recall/median rank, and AMT user preferences. The paper reports gains for CRCN, with larger improvements on longer sequences, attributing this to the coherence model.

This work relates to retrieval-based captioning and multimodal embedding approaches (e.g., Karpathy & Fei-Fei; Kiros et al.), video-to-text models (e.g., LRCN), and entity-grid coherence modeling for discourse. It is also close to Kim et al. on retrieval of image sequences from paragraph queries, but addresses the opposite direction and integrates coherence into a multimodal ranking model.

Strengths
- Problem framing beyond single image–single sentence; addressing paragraph-level coherence is important and underexplored.
- Sensible architecture that marries CNN features, a sequence model over sentence representations, and an established coherence model.
- Practical data construction from blogs; the dataset is large and the task realistic (albums/stories).
- Clear ablation showing added value of coherence over BRNN-only, especially on longer sequences; human studies support the importance of coherence.
- Training objective and retrieval-time beam search are straightforward and reproducible; implementation choices (dropout, RMSprop) are reasonable.

Weaknesses
Quality
- The approach is retrieval-only: output sentences are selected from the training corpus. This limits generalization and novelty of generation compared to contemporary generative models.
- Potential length bias: the score sums s_t·v_t and a global coherence term g_k·v_t over t without length normalization. Longer sequences may be favored; this is neither analyzed nor controlled.
- Evaluation relies on LSA-summarized “ground-truth” sentences as references; such noisy supervision undermines the validity of BLEU/CIDEr/METEOR, especially at paragraph level.
- Baselines are disadvantaged because they generate per-image sentences independently and lack any sequence-level reranking. Missing stronger sequence-aware baselines (e.g., a CRF/HMM over per-image candidates with bigram LM; neural paragraph/Hierarchical RNN; entity-grid-only reranker; skip-thought/transformer-based coherence scoring).
- Limited analysis of the approximate search: no complexity/runtime, no comparison to exact search on short sequences, no sensitivity to K, M, or κ.
- No discussion of domain transfer; both datasets are tourism blogs (NYC/Disneyland). Claims of domain-agnostic “unsupervised” applicability are not substantiated empirically.

Clarity
- Overall readable, but some notation and design choices are under-explained (e.g., why the coherence vector is dot-producted with each image feature at every timestep; effects of zero-padding to 300d; whether scores are normalized by sequence length).
- Important implementation details are relegated to the supplement (numbers in Tables not visible here). Hyperparameters, training time, and parameter counts are sparse.

Originality
- Integrating an entity-grid coherence model with a multimodal BRNN for image-stream-to-paragraph retrieval is a novel combination.
- However, each component is standard, and the overall pipeline (KNN candidates + sequence-level reranking) is a relatively modest extension of prior retrieval frameworks.

Significance
- Practical significance for album/story description is promising, but evidence is constrained to two domains and to retrieval-only outputs.
- Improvements on automatic metrics are reported, but their suitability for paragraph-level evaluation is limited; AMT tests are small-scale (100 queries) with pairwise preferences and no confidence intervals.

Suggestions for improvement
- Normalize the score by sequence length and report length-controlled results; analyze the contribution of the coherence term g_k and its interaction with sequence length.
- Strengthen baselines: (i) a per-position KNN candidate pool with a bigram/trigram LM or entity-grid-only reranker; (ii) a hierarchical RNN/transformer trained to generate multi-sentence captions; (iii) a CRF over candidate indices with learned transition potentials.
- Provide sensitivity analyses for K (nearest neighbors), M (beam width), κ (augmentation), and doc2vec dimension; report runtime.
- Evaluate on additional domains (e.g., general photo albums, news photo streams) and include cross-domain tests.
- Use paragraph-appropriate metrics (ROUGE-L, BERTScore/BLEURT) and report statistical significance; expand AMT with explicit fluency/coherence ratings and inter-annotator agreement.
- Consider finetuning CNNs on the domain and explore LSTM/GRU units in the BRNN; compare to a neural coherence model as an alternative to entity grids.
- Release the dataset, code, and preprocessing scripts to facilitate reproducibility.

Arguments for acceptance
- Addresses an underexplored yet relevant task (image streams to coherent multi-sentence descriptions).
- Coherence modeling clearly helps; human studies corroborate the importance.
- Method is simple, interpretable, and performs well on reported datasets.

Arguments against acceptance
- Limited technical novelty; reliance on retrieval and standard components.
- Evaluation design and baselines do not convincingly establish state-of-the-art at the sequence level; metrics and datasets are noisy/narrow.
- Potential scoring/length biases and search approximations are not rigorously analyzed.

Recommendation
Weak reject. The problem is compelling and the integration of coherence modeling is interesting, but the current empirical evaluation and technical novelty fall short of NeurIPS standards. Strengthening baselines, evaluation, and analysis as suggested could make this a strong submission.
