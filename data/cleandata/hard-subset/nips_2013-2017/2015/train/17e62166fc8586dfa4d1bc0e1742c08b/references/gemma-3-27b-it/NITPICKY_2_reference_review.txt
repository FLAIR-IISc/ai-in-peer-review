This paper attempts to extend image captioning to the domain of image *streams*, generating sequences of sentences to describe a collection of images. While the motivation – modeling experiences captured as photo streams – is reasonable, and the core idea of incorporating a coherence model is conceptually sound, the paper suffers from a multitude of minor flaws and a lack of rigorous evaluation, ultimately hindering its potential contribution.

The introduction is adequate but somewhat verbose. The claim of extending “both input and output dimension” is not particularly insightful; sequentially processing images and generating sequential sentences is a natural progression and hardly a groundbreaking conceptual leap. The reliance on blog posts as a dataset is pragmatic but introduces inherent noise and biases not adequately addressed. Figures 1 and 2 are helpful for intuition, but labeling could be improved for clarity (e.g., what do the arrows in Fig. 2(a) represent?).

The dataset construction (Section 2) is a weak point. The heuristics for associating text blocks with images (“minimum index distance”) feels arbitrary and potentially inaccurate. LSA-based summarization, while standard, is a black box; the authors don't justify its suitability for blog post content or discuss potential limitations. The data augmentation strategy (κ=3) is presented as empirically determined, but lacks statistical justification or ablation study results. The discussion of paragraph vector extraction and parse tree usage feels superficial; the connection to the coherence model is stated but not convincingly demonstrated.

The architectural description (Section 3) is convoluted and contains several instances of imprecise language. The motivation for separate input activations (xf and xb) within the BRNN is weak ("empirically leads a better performance" is insufficient). The ReLU activation choice isn't justified. The description of the compatibility score and the cost function is unnecessarily dense and could benefit from clearer mathematical notation. The dropout rates chosen are mentioned without explanation, and the claim of improved generalization is not backed by experimental evidence.  The divide-and-conquer retrieval strategy is vaguely described, and the justification for the beam search parameters (M=50) is missing. The statement that negative examples are “unlikely” to match positive examples isn’t a substitute for a more thorough analysis of the sampling process.

The experiments (Section 4) are problematic. The baseline selection is reasonable but incomplete – a more recent and competitive model such as AttnGAN [1] should have been included. The quantitative results (Table 1) show marginal improvements over baselines, which are not statistically significant given the lack of error bars. The qualitative examples (Fig. 3) are subjectively chosen and do not provide a comprehensive evaluation. The AMT study (Section 4.3) is better but suffers from the same limitation as the quantitative results: a small sample size and lack of statistical significance testing. The conclusion that coherence becomes more important with longer streams is intuitive but requires more rigorous validation. The lack of discussion about computational cost is a notable omission.

Several minor issues detract from the overall quality: inconsistent use of notation (e.g., using both Bl and B to denote the training set), grammatical errors and awkward phrasing throughout the text, and missing citations (e.g., the LSA method is cited as [4] but a more recent and relevant paper would be beneficial).  The reference list is lengthy but includes older papers where more modern alternatives exist.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

* **Incremental Contribution:** The core idea is not fundamentally novel and the improvements over existing methods are modest.
* **Lack of Rigorous Evaluation:** The experiments lack statistical significance and comprehensive analysis.
* **Numerous Minor Flaws:** The paper is riddled with inconsistencies, imprecise language, and missing details.
* **Superficial Treatment of Key Components:**  The description of dataset construction and architectural details is insufficient.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* **Interesting Problem:** Addressing the problem of image stream description is a worthwhile research direction.
* **Coherence Model:** The integration of a coherence model is a potentially valuable contribution.
* **Large Dataset:** The use of a large-scale blog post dataset is commendable.

**Overall:** Despite a potentially interesting premise, the paper’s numerous flaws, lack of rigorous evaluation, and incremental contribution outweigh its strengths. I recommend **rejection**. The authors need to address the concerns outlined above and conduct more thorough experiments to demonstrate the true value of their approach.
