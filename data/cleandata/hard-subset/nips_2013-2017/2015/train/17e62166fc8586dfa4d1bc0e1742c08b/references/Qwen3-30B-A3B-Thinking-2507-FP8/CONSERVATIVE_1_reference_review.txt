

# Review of "Expressing an Image Stream with a Sequence of Natural Sentences"

This paper proposes a method for generating sequences of natural language sentences to describe sequences of images (e.g., photo streams from a trip), extending the traditional image captioning problem. The authors introduce a "Coherent Recurrent Convolutional Networks" (CRCN) architecture that combines convolutional networks for image representation, bidirectional RNNs for sentence modeling, and a local coherence model to ensure fluent transitions between sentences. They use blog posts as a source of image-text parallel data and evaluate their approach on NYC and Disneyland datasets.

**Strengths:**
The paper is well-written and clearly organized. The authors provide a reasonable justification for their problem setup, noting that users often capture sequences of images during memorable experiences. The use of blog posts as training data is creative and potentially valuable for obtaining natural text-image pairs. The evaluation includes both quantitative metrics (BLEU, CIDEr, METEOR, top-K recall) and user studies via Amazon Mechanical Turk, which is commendable.

**Weaknesses:**

**1. Problem Significance:** The paper fails to convincingly demonstrate why this problem is significantly more important or challenging than standard image captioning. While the "photo stream" concept is intuitive, the paper doesn't establish that existing single-sentence captioning methods can't handle image sequences by simply concatenating outputs. The motivation about "memorable moments" is weak - most photo streams are still typically described with a single caption or a few sentences, not requiring a sequence of natural language sentences.

**2. Methodological Novelty:** The CRCN architecture is essentially a combination of well-established techniques (VGGNet for image features, BRNN for language modeling, and a coherence model based on entity transitions). The paper claims to be "the first" to address this problem, but the methodological contribution appears to be incremental rather than transformative. The coherence model is described as "local coherence" from [1], but the paper doesn't clearly explain how this differs from existing coherence modeling approaches or why it's necessary for this specific problem.

**3. Validation and Evaluation:** The claimed improvements over baselines are marginal (Table 1 shows only small gains in metrics). The baselines are somewhat limited - they only compare with three variants of existing single-sentence models, which were not designed for sequence generation. The user studies (Table 2) show that the coherence model's advantage is only significant for longer sequences (8-10 images), but the paper doesn't explain why this is a fundamental limitation rather than a quirk of their dataset. The paper also doesn't address whether their approach would work on non-tourism domains, despite claiming it's "completely unsupervised and thus applicable to any domain."

**4. Technical Rigor:** The paper lacks sufficient theoretical justification for key design choices. For example, the authors use a 300-dimensional paragraph vector representation without explaining why this dimensionality was chosen or how it compares to other representations. The coherence model's 64-dimensional transition representation is introduced without justification. The paper also doesn't address potential failure cases or limitations of the approach.

**5. Comparison with Recent Work:** The paper doesn't compare with more recent state-of-the-art methods for image captioning, such as transformer-based approaches (e.g., Show, Attend and Tell, or newer vision-language models), which would be more relevant for a fair comparison. The baselines are mostly from 2014-2015, while the field has advanced significantly since then.

**Overall Assessment:**

The paper addresses a potentially interesting problem, but the contribution is not sufficiently novel or significant to warrant acceptance at NIPS. The method is a combination of established techniques rather than a truly novel approach, and the improvements over baselines are marginal. The problem's significance is not convincingly demonstrated, and the evaluation lacks sufficient rigor and comparison with more recent work.

The paper would benefit from:
1) A stronger justification for why the problem is important and different from existing work
2) More comprehensive comparison with recent state-of-the-art methods
3) Deeper analysis of the coherence model's necessity
4) Discussion of limitations and failure cases
5) More convincing evidence that the proposed method is substantially better than simply concatenating single-sentence captions

While the problem is interesting and the paper is well-written, the contribution does not meet NIPS' standards for novelty and significance. The marginal improvements over baselines and lack of clear justification for the coherence model's necessity make it difficult to endorse this as a significant advance in the field.

**Final Recommendation: Reject**
