

# Review for "Expressing an Image Stream with a Sequence of Natural Sentences"

This paper addresses the problem of generating a sequence of natural language sentences to describe a sequence of images (an image stream), rather than the more common single-image captioning task. The authors propose a novel multimodal architecture called Coherent Recurrent Convolutional Networks (CRCN) that integrates convolutional neural networks for image representation, bidirectional recurrent neural networks for sentence modeling, and a local coherence model to ensure smooth transitions between sentences. They collect a large dataset from blog posts (20K+ posts with 140K images) and demonstrate that their approach outperforms several baselines in both quantitative metrics and user studies.

**Strengths:**

1. **Originality**: The problem setup is genuinely novel compared to the vast literature on single-image captioning. The authors clearly identify a gap in the literature regarding image streams rather than single images. The CRCN architecture is a reasonable integration of existing techniques with a novel coherence model.

2. **Significance**: The problem is well-motivated by real-world scenarios where people capture sequences of images (e.g., travel blogs). The approach is unsupervised and potentially applicable to various domains with blog-like content.

3. **Quality**: The paper provides a thorough evaluation with multiple metrics (BLEU, CIDEr, METEOR, top-K recall) and user studies via AMT. The results consistently show that CRCN outperforms baselines, especially when coherence becomes more important in longer sequences. The ablation study (CRCN vs RCN) clearly demonstrates the value of the coherence model.

4. **Clarity**: The paper is well-organized with clear figures (Fig. 2) explaining the architecture and preprocessing steps. The methodology is detailed enough for replication.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Insufficient distinction from video captioning**: The paper mentions video captioning work in the related work section but doesn't adequately differentiate their problem from existing video captioning approaches. Video captioning also deals with sequences of images, often with temporal coherence requirements. The authors should clarify why their approach is fundamentally different from video captioning and why it's more appropriate for image streams (which may have more abrupt content changes than videos).

2. **Lack of dataset analysis**: The blog dataset is interesting, but the paper doesn't discuss potential biases or limitations. Blog posts may have specific stylistic patterns, content focus (e.g., tourism), or image selection criteria that don't generalize well to other image streams. A more thorough analysis of the dataset's characteristics would strengthen the work.

3. **Baseline comparisons**: The baselines are adapted from single-sentence captioning models, but it's not clear if these are the most appropriate baselines for sequence generation. The paper should compare with more relevant sequence-based approaches (e.g., existing video captioning models adapted to their task).

4. **Limited failure analysis**: The paper shows examples of successful generation (Fig. 3) but doesn't discuss cases where the approach fails or where coherence is particularly challenging. Understanding failure modes would help identify limitations.

**Constructive Suggestions:**

1. Strengthen the related work section by more clearly distinguishing this work from video captioning research. Explain why video captioning methods aren't directly applicable to image streams (e.g., image streams may have abrupt content changes, while videos often have smooth transitions).

2. Add a section analyzing the blog dataset's characteristics, including potential biases, content diversity, and how they might affect the model's performance in other domains.

3. Consider including a comparison with a video captioning model (e.g., from [23, 32]) as a more relevant baseline to better demonstrate the value of their approach.

4. Add a failure case analysis to better understand the model's limitations.

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a novel problem setup and a reasonable approach to addressing it. While the core idea is original and the evaluation is thorough, the paper would benefit from a clearer distinction from related work (particularly video captioning) and a more thorough analysis of the dataset. The significance of the work is somewhat limited by the niche problem setup, though it's well-motivated for specific applications.

**Recommendation: Weak Rejection**

The paper has potential but requires substantial revisions to address the gaps in literature context and dataset analysis before it would be suitable for NIPS. The distinction from video captioning is particularly critical, as this is a major related area that the authors don't adequately differentiate from.

**Pro-acceptance arguments:** Novel problem setup, thorough evaluation with multiple metrics and user studies, interesting dataset from blog posts, clear architecture with coherence model integration.

**Con-acceptance arguments:** Insufficient distinction from video captioning work, limited analysis of dataset limitations, baselines could be more relevant to sequence generation task, failure case analysis missing, significance limited to niche application.
