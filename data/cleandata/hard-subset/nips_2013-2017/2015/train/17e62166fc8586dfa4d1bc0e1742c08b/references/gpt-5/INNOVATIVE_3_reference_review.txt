Summary
The paper tackles a novel and important problem: generating a coherent sequence of natural sentences for an image stream. The proposed coherent recurrent convolutional network (CRCN) combines (i) CNN image features (VGG fc7), (ii) a bidirectional RNN over sentence-level paragraph vectors (doc2vec) to model content flow, and (iii) an entity-grid based local coherence model (Barzilay & Lapata) to encourage discourse-level fluency across sentences. Training data are automatically constructed from large-scale travel blogs by segmenting text near images and summarizing each segment to one key sentence (with κ-augmentation). The system retrieves candidate sentences per image via KNN over image features and ranks concatenations using a sequence-level compatibility score learned with a max-margin loss over aligned image–sentence streams. An approximate beam-style divide-and-conquer search makes decoding tractable. Experiments on two sizable datasets (NYC, Disneyland) show gains over adapted single-image captioning baselines on BLEU/CIDEr/METEOR and in AMT user studies; ablations indicate the entity-based coherence helps, especially for longer sequences.

Relation to prior work
The work builds on image captioning (e.g., Karpathy & Fei-Fei; Vinyals et al.), multimodal RNNs, and video-to-text (e.g., LRCN), but moves beyond single sentences and single images. Unlike video captioning, it explicitly addresses abrupt content shifts common in photo streams and integrates a local coherence model from NLP discourse research with multimodal learning. It also relates to sequence retrieval from paragraph queries (Kim et al.) but flips the direction.

Strengths
- Novel task and bold framing: moving from single-image/sentence to stream/paragraph with explicit discourse modeling is timely and impactful.
- Cross-pollination from NLP discourse coherence (entity grids) to vision–language modeling is creative and, to my knowledge, new in this context.
- Pragmatic use of large-scale weakly aligned blog data; the pipeline (segmentation + summarization) is simple and scalable.
- Clear ablations: BRNN vs +coherence; coherence gains grow with sequence length; human evaluations corroborate quantitative metrics.
- The learning objective and search strategy are straightforward and efficient; the approach is likely reproducible with standard components.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Scoring formulation (Eq. 3): adding g·v_t at every t seems to overcount a global coherence term by a factor of N. Is g intended as a global prior over the visual stream? If so, normalization or pairwise (or higher-order) transition potentials would feel more principled, and would also enable dynamic programming/Viterbi decoding. As written, coherence is computed solely from the candidate text and then broadcast to all images; it is not conditioned on visual content beyond the shared embedding.
- “End-to-end” and “unsupervised” claims are somewhat overstated. The system relies on external preprocessing (LSA summarization, parsing/coref), doc2vec training, and KNN retrieval; only the mapping to the joint space is learned with paired supervision. Please soften wording or discuss limits.
- Evaluation fairness and strength of baselines: concatenating single-image captions is a weak paragraph baseline. Missing are sequence-aware baselines such as a hierarchical RNN/LSTM over sentences, or a CRF with pairwise coherence terms over retrieved sentences. Also, since your method is retrieval-based, a retrieval-only baseline with a sequence-aware reranker (e.g., bigram coherence) would be informative.
- Dataset construction quality is not analyzed. The heuristics (paragraph proximity, LSA summarization, κ augmentation) can misalign images and sentences. No statistics on alignment noise, κ sensitivity, or error profiles are provided.
- Metrics: BLEU/CIDEr/METEOR are hard to interpret when references are automatically summarized and retrieved outputs are drawn from training sentences. Please report overlap/diversity statistics (e.g., proportion of tokens/sentences copied verbatim from training), and perhaps semantic similarity measures at the paragraph level.
- Some modeling choices look ad hoc: zero-padding the 64-d entity-grid vector to 300-d before FC; separate input projections Wfi/Wbi without clear analysis; training doc2vec separately per domain may reduce generality.

Questions for the authors
- Can you clarify Eq. 3: why is g·v_t summed over t? Did you try normalizing by N or using pairwise coherence terms between adjacent sentences to better match the notion of local coherence?
- Does the coherence module use visual information anywhere, or could it be conditioned on visual cues (e.g., entities grounded to detected objects)?
- Sensitivity: How do results vary with κ, K (nearest neighbors), beam size M, and the number of halving steps Q?
- What are the typical sequence lengths and how does performance scale with length?
- Any inter-annotator agreement on AMT, and tie rates?

Clarity
The paper is generally well written and organized. Please (i) clarify the scoring and decoding sections with more intuition and a small toy example, (ii) detail preprocessing (coref resolver settings, summarizer parameters) and release code/data, (iii) clean up notation (e.g., Skl indices, dimensions), and (iv) justify the zero-padding of coherence features.

Significance
High. The problem is compelling, the architecture thoughtfully integrates discourse coherence, and the empirical evidence (especially human preferences on longer sequences) suggests real utility. I expect this to spark more work on visual storytelling, hierarchical sequence modeling, and coherence-aware multimodal learning.

Pros for acceptance
- Clear, novel problem definition and dataset construction at scale
- New integration of entity-grid coherence with multimodal sequence modeling
- Strong qualitative and human evaluation gains; ablations support claims
- Simple, reproducible approach with practical impact

Cons against acceptance
- Scoring/coherence integration could be more principled; potential overcounting
- Baselines not fully sequence-aware; limited evaluation analysis
- Heavy reliance on preprocessing; “end-to-end/unsupervised” phrasing misleading

Overall recommendation
Accept (7/10). Despite some methodological imperfections and missing stronger baselines, the paper introduces a significant new direction with a creative and effective architecture. The novelty and potential impact outweigh the weaknesses.
