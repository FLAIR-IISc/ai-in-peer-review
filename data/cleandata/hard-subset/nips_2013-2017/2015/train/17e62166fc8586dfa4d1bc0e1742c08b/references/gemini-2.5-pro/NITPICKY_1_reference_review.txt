### Summary
This paper addresses the novel task of generating a coherent sequence of natural language sentences to describe a stream of images. The authors propose a multimodal architecture, Coherent Recurrent Convolutional Networks (CRCN), which integrates three main components: a CNN for image feature extraction (VGGNet), a bidirectional RNN for modeling the textual content flow of the sentence sequence, and an entity-grid-based local coherence model to ensure smooth transitions between sentences. To train this model, the authors propose a clever strategy of using online blog posts as a source of parallel image-text stream data. The proposed model is trained with a max-margin loss to rank correct image-sentence sequence pairs higher than incorrect ones. At test time, a sentence sequence is constructed by first retrieving candidate sentences for each image in the query stream and then using a beam-search-like strategy to find the most compatible and coherent sequence according to the learned model. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach through quantitative evaluations (BLEU, CIDEr, METEOR, Recall@K) and human studies on Amazon Mechanical Turk, showing improvements over several adapted state-of-the-art single-image captioning methods.

### Strengths
1.  **Novel Problem Formulation:** The paper tackles a novel and important problem that is a logical next step from single-image-to-single-sentence generation. Addressing image streams and sentence sequences is more aligned with how humans capture and narrate experiences, making it a significant research direction.
2.  **Clever Data Sourcing:** The idea of using blog posts to create a large-scale, "in-the-wild" dataset for this task is practical and effective, bypassing the need for expensive manual annotation of image-sentence streams.
3.  **Comprehensive Architecture:** The proposed CRCN model is a sensible combination of powerful, established techniques. Integrating a CNN for vision, a BRNN for sequence context, and an explicit coherence model is a well-motivated approach to this multifaceted problem.

### Weaknesses
Despite the novelty of the problem, the paper suffers from a substantial number of methodological weaknesses, technical inaccuracies, and clarity issues.

**1. Clarity and Presentation:**
*   The writing style is often informal ("hike of interest") and contains grammatical errors ("it would better take the whole photo stream into consideration").
*   The citation format is inconsistent and incorrect. In the introduction, the citations are not numerically sorted (e.g., "[5, 8, 9, 12, 14, 15, 26, 21, 30]"). The reference list itself is poorly formatted, with inconsistent author names (initials vs. full names), missing conference/journal venues, and inconsistent inclusion of page numbers. This lack of attention to detail is concerning.
*   The term "unsupervised" in the introduction is misleading. While the data collection from blogs is unsupervised, the training of the CRCN model itself is supervised on aligned image-text pairs. This distinction should be made clear.
*   The "slight abuse of notation" mentioned in Section 2.1 for `Tln` is an example of imprecise writing that could be easily avoided.

**2. Methodological Concerns:**
*   **Data Pre-processing:** The pipeline for converting blogs into parallel data is based on strong, potentially brittle heuristics. Assigning text blocks to the nearest image by "index distance" (Section 2.1) is a very coarse approximation that could introduce significant label noise. Similarly, using an LSA-based method from 2004 for summarization feels dated; a comparison to more modern summarization techniques would have been warranted.
*   **Design Choice of Retrieval vs. Generation:** The paper justifies its sentence-retrieval approach by citing difficulties with long sequences in RNNs, such as the vanishing gradient problem. This justification is weak. Modern architectures like LSTMs (which one of the baselines uses) and Transformers are specifically designed to handle long-range dependencies and are routinely used to generate long paragraphs of text from scratch. This design choice fundamentally limits the model's expressiveness to the vocabulary of sentences present in the training set, a major limitation that is not adequately discussed.
*   **Coherence Model Integration:** The integration of the 64-dimensional coherence vector is crude. It is simply zero-padded to 300 dimensions to match the BRNN output dimension. A more principled approach, such as using a learned linear projection, would be more appropriate and parameter-efficient.
*   **Retrieval at Test Time:** The divide-and-conquer strategy for search is a necessary heuristic, but its justification that "local fluency and coherence is undoubtedly necessary for the global one" is a hand-wavy assertion. It is plausible that a globally optimal sequence might be composed of locally sub-optimal subsequences. The sensitivity to the beam size `M` is not analyzed.

**3. Technical Inaccuracies:**
*   A significant error exists in Section 3.3. The authors claim "the multiplication `Wf2Wf1` of the last two FC layers does not reduce to a single linear mapping, thanks to dropout." This is incorrect. Dropout is a regularization technique applied during training. At test time, when the model is used for inference, dropout is turned off, and the two consecutive linear transformations `Wf2` and `Wf1` are exactly equivalent to a single linear transformation `W_new = Wf2 * Wf1`. The non-linearity is introduced by the activation functions between layers, not by dropout. This reflects a misunderstanding of a fundamental deep learning concept.
*   In Section 3.1, the paper claims novelty in separating input activations for the forward and backward passes of the BRNN ("Unlike other BRNN models..."). This is a standard formulation; separate parameterizations for forward and backward passes are common. The claim that this "empirically leads a better performance" is unsubstantiated by any ablation study.

**4. Experimental Evaluation:**
*   **Weak Baselines:** The method for adapting single-image captioning models into baselines—by simply generating a sentence for each image and concatenating them—creates a straw man. This approach inherently ignores any notion of coherence, making it unsurprising that the proposed model, which explicitly models coherence, performs better. A stronger baseline would involve adapting the generative models to be sequential, for instance, by conditioning the generation of sentence `t+1` on the final hidden state from the generation of sentence `t`.
*   **Under-analyzed Results:** The paper notes that CRCN is only slightly better than RCN in language metrics but significantly better in retrieval metrics. The explanation provided is vague. A more in-depth analysis is needed. The dismissal of the small gains in language metrics due to "inherent limitation" of BLEU feels defensive.
*   **User Study Design:** The primary user study limited sequences to 5 images. The finding that coherence matters more for longer sequences (8-10 images) was presented as a secondary, follow-up experiment. This critical variable (sequence length) should have been a core component of the main study design, not an addendum.

### Recommendation
Reject.

While the paper introduces a compelling and novel problem, the execution is marred by a multitude of flaws ranging from minor presentational issues to a critical technical misunderstanding of dropout's function. The methodological choices are often based on dated techniques or poorly justified heuristics. Furthermore, the experimental evaluation relies on weakly adapted baselines, which may inflate the perceived performance of the proposed model. The sheer volume of these issues, both large and small, undermines confidence in the paper's contributions and technical soundness. The work has potential, but it requires a major revision to address the identified weaknesses in methodology, technical accuracy, and experimental rigor before it can be considered for publication.
