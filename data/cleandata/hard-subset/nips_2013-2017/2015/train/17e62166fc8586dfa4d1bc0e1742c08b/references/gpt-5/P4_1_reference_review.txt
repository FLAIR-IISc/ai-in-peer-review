Summary and relation to prior work
The paper addresses generating a multi-sentence description for a stream of images, extending the prevalent single-image/single-sentence captioning setup. The proposed coherent recurrent convolutional networks (CRCN) integrate: (i) a CNN (VGG fc7) for image features, (ii) a bidirectional RNN over sentence-level embeddings (doc2vec) to model sequence content flow, and (iii) an entity-grid local coherence model (Barzilay & Lapata) to encourage fluent sentence-to-sentence transitions. Training is formulated as a structured max-margin objective over aligned blog-derived image–text streams, with negative sampling. At test time, the method retrieves candidate sentences per image via KNN on CNN features, then assembles a sequence using an approximate beam/divide-and-conquer search, ranking candidates with the learned compatibility function.

The work connects to retrieval-based captioning and multimodal matching/embedding methods (e.g., Kiros et al., Karpathy & Fei-Fei) but extends them to sequence-to-sequence matching with explicit coherence modeling. It differs from video description (e.g., LRCN, Rohrbach et al.) by handling discrete photo streams (which may be less temporally smooth) and from Kim et al. (image sequence retrieval for paragraph queries) by going in the reverse direction (image→text) and incorporating an entity-based coherence term. The dataset is assembled from travel blogs (NYC, Disneyland) using paragraph-based segmentation and LSA summarization to produce one sentence per image, with top-κ augmentation.

Strengths
- Clear, practically motivated extension from single-caption to multi-sentence stream-level description.
- Novel integration of an entity-grid coherence model with a multimodal CNN–BRNN architecture, and training at the sequence level with a structured loss.
- Scaled weakly supervised training using large blog datasets; methodology for building image–sentence streams is reasonable given limited parallel data.
- Evaluations include both automatic metrics (BLEU, METEOR, CIDEr; retrieval metrics) and AMT user studies; ablations (1NN, BRNN-only vs CRCN) and analysis of sequence length effects support the value of coherence modeling.
- The approximate search over sentence candidates is pragmatic and appears effective in examples.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Scoring function length bias: The sequence score S sums over positions without normalization. This likely favors longer sequences (both the per-sentence term and the global coherence term g are summed over t). The paper argues negatives rarely match lengths, but that does not remove bias; a simple normalization (e.g., average over N or length-penalized objective) would be more principled.
- Coherence feature integration: The 64-dim entity-grid feature is zero-padded to 300-dim before FC layers. Zero-padding is arbitrary and wastes capacity; a learned projection from 64→300 would be better justified. Moreover, the global coherence vector g is added identically at every time step in the score (g·vlt), effectively interacting with the sum of image features rather than modeling transitions; this formulation deserves more justification or redesign (e.g., interact g with an aggregated image representation once).
- Preprocessing/alignment quality is not evaluated. The assignment of text blocks to images via nearest index and LSA summarization introduces noise; without reporting alignment accuracy or human validation, it is hard to gauge data quality and its effect on results.
- Baseline fairness and details: It is unclear whether generative baselines were trained/fine-tuned appropriately on this domain and with comparable CNN features. Using the same fixed VGG fc7 (versus typical fine-tuning for Show-and-Tell/LSTM) may disadvantage them; more training details and tuning parity are needed. Also, retrieval-based evaluation (R@K, median rank) favors retrieval-style methods.
- Limited generalization: Outputs are restricted to sentences present in the training set; while acceptable for retrieval framing, this limits expressivity and applicability compared to generative models.
- Missing implementation details: Coreference resolution settings (necessary for entity grids), parser configurations, doc2vec training specifics (trained separately per domain), runtime/complexity, and candidate search optimality guarantees are not fully described.
- Dataset availability and reproducibility are not discussed; releasing data/code would significantly increase impact.

Quality
Technically competent with a reasonable architecture and training scheme. The entity-grid integration and sequence-level training are sound ideas, supported by ablations and AMT studies. However, the score definition likely has length biases; the coherence interaction is somewhat ad hoc; and several important details (alignment quality, baseline training parity) are missing. Retrieval-based construction is practical but limits generalization.

Clarity
Generally well written and organized. The model description is clear, and the training/inference pipeline is easy to follow. Some components need clarification: how coreference chains are built for the entity grid; whether length normalization is used; how baselines were trained/adapted; and why zero-padding is used rather than a learned projection.

Originality
Moderate. The problem setting (image stream→sentence sequence) and the integration of an entity-grid coherence model with a multimodal CNN–RNN scoring framework appear novel. The other components (CNN features, BRNN over sentence embeddings, structured max-margin learning, KNN candidate retrieval) build on established methods.

Significance
Moderate. Addressing multi-sentence descriptions for image streams is relevant and useful, and the results (including user studies) suggest that explicit coherence modeling matters, especially for longer sequences. Impact is tempered by the retrieval-only nature, dataset specificity, and evaluation limitations.

Suggestions for improvement
- Normalize the sequence score or include an explicit length penalty; analyze sensitivity to sequence length.
- Replace zero-padding with a learned MLP from entity-grid features to the joint space; consider modeling coherence via pairwise/transition potentials between adjacent sentences.
- Provide details and quality measurements for text-image segmentation/summarization and coreference resolution; analyze their errors’ impact.
- Ensure fair, strong baselines (fine-tuned CNNs where appropriate); include a re-ranking baseline that uses only entity-grid coherence on KNN sentence candidates.
- Report search ablations (beam size M, halving depth) and runtime.
- Clarify dataset release plans.

Arguments for acceptance
- Addresses a timely, under-explored multimodal task and demonstrates benefits of explicit coherence modeling.
- Presents a coherent architecture with sequence-level training and shows consistent gains over strong baselines and in human judgments.
- Scales to large weakly supervised data; ablations and length-dependent analysis are informative.

Arguments against acceptance
- Potential length bias and ad hoc aspects of the scoring/coherence integration; missing technical clarifications.
- Retrieval-only approach limits novelty and generalization; evaluation may favor retrieval methods and may not be fully fair to generative baselines.
- Data preprocessing/alignment quality and baseline training details are insufficiently validated; reproducibility is uncertain without dataset release.

Overall, this is a solid step toward coherent multi-sentence descriptions for image streams, with room to strengthen methodological rigor and evaluation.
