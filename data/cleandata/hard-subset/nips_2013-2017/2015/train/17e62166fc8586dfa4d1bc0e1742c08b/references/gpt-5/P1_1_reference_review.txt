Summary and relation to prior work:
This paper addresses the underexplored task of describing an image stream with a sequence of natural sentences. The proposed model, Coherent Recurrent Convolutional Networks (CRCN), combines: (a) CNN image features (VGG fc7), (b) a bidirectional RNN over sentence representations (doc2vec paragraph vectors), and (c) an entity-grid based local coherence model (Barzilay & Lapata, 2008). Training uses a max-margin ranking loss over aligned image-sequence/text-sequence pairs mined from blog posts. At test time, for each query image stream, sentences are retrieved by first finding KNN images per position, then assembling candidate sentence sequences with a beam-like divide-and-conquer search, scored by the learned compatibility function that includes local content flow (BRNN) and sequence-level coherence features. Experiments on two large blog corpora (NYC and Disneyland) show improvements over strong single-image captioning baselines (CNN+RNN/LSTM, Kiros et al.), a global matching baseline, and ablations; AMT studies indicate human preference for CRCN, especially for longer streams.

The work relates to prior image captioning via retrieval/embedding (e.g., Socher et al., Karpathy & Fei-Fei, Kiros et al.), multimodal RNN/LSTM captioning (Mao et al., Vinyals et al.), video-to-text (Donahue et al., Rohrbach et al.), and entity-grid coherence modeling (Barzilay & Lapata). Compared to video captioning, the focus here is on discrete photo streams (with potentially abrupt visual changes), and the explicit incorporation of a coherence model into a multimodal scoring function is novel for this problem.

Strengths:
- Problem formulation is timely and novel: sequence-to-sequence description for image streams.
- Clear architectural idea that integrates sentence-level content flow (BRNN) with entity-based local coherence; good motivation for why coherence matters more for streams than single images/videos with smoother content.
- Large-scale data collection from blogs; practical preprocessing pipeline (segmentation + LSA summarization) and use of weak supervision.
- Comparative evaluation against multiple baselines and meaningful ablations (1NN, RCN vs CRCN). Human studies support the central claim that coherence helps more for longer passages.
- Method is modular and computationally reasonable; the divide-and-conquer search is a sensible approximation for combinatorial candidate spaces.

Weaknesses:
- Evaluation clarity: It is unclear how retrieval metrics (R@K, median rank) are computed at test time given that the final output is constructed from KNN sentences rather than selecting from a fixed gallery of full sequences. Please clarify whether retrieval metrics are computed in a separate setting (ranking ground-truth sequences among all sequences), and if so, whether the same candidate-generation constraint is applied. As written, there is a mismatch between the described inference procedure and retrieval evaluation.
- Length bias: The score S sums over positions; without normalization, longer sequences may receive higher scores. The paper does not discuss length normalization or how negative sampling controls for this. This affects both training and inference.
- Comparisons may be apples-to-oranges: CRCN retrieves human-written sentences from the training corpus, whereas CNN+LSTM/RNN baselines generate novel text. Retrieval often enjoys advantages on n-gram metrics versus generation; conversely, generation baselines are penalized by style/domain mismatch. Include stronger retrieval baselines (e.g., per-position KNN with global reranking by a conventional coherence model alone, or bigram/LM reranking) and report both retrieval and generation variants consistently.
- Coherence term formulation: Mapping a 64-d entity-grid feature to a global vector g and then scoring it via g·v_t at every time step lacks a clear justification. Why should a sequence-level coherence descriptor align (equally) to every image feature? Alternative formulations (e.g., adding a separate scalar coherence score, or modeling sentence-to-sentence transitions directly in the scoring function) might be more principled.
- Dataset and preprocessing: The segmentation/alignment (paragraph tokenizer + nearest-image assignment) and LSA summarization are heuristic and potentially noisy; there is no quantitative assessment of alignment quality or its impact on performance. Coreference parsing and entity grids on noisy blog text can be error-prone; no ablation on coherence history length or parser quality is provided.
- “Completely unsupervised” is an overstatement. The method uses aligned image–sentence pairs mined from blogs; while no manual labeling is added, the learning is supervised from these pairs. Please rephrase.
- Reproducibility details are missing: whether CNN is fine-tuned, whether doc2vec was trained only on training text (using test text for doc2vec training would be transductive), learning rate schedules, runtime, convergence diagnostics, and statistical significance for metric differences are not reported.

Quality:
- Generally sound, but some design choices (coherence scoring and length normalization) need justification and possibly fixes. Negative sampling and BPTT for variable-length sequences are standard; however, the “global coherence applied per time step” is questionable. The experimental section would benefit from clearer protocol descriptions, more exhaustive ablations (K, beam width M, LSA κ, coherence history), and significance tests.

Clarity:
- The paper is readable and well structured. However, several key details are underspecified: exact retrieval metric protocol, length normalization, whether CNN/doc2vec are trained only on training data, and the search algorithm. Some notation is sloppy (e.g., “ne examples”; zero-padding coherence to 300 dims instead of learning a projection). Please add an explicit algorithm box for inference and a diagram for how the coherence vector influences scoring.

Originality:
- The task (image stream → sentence sequence) and the integration of entity-grid coherence with a multimodal neural scoring function are novel and well-motivated. Related work is adequately cited, including sequence retrieval from paragraphs (Kim et al.) and video captioning; differences are clear.

Significance:
- If cleaned up, this work can be impactful: it highlights coherence in multimodal storytelling and offers a scalable training signal from blogs. The datasets could be valuable if released with preprocessing scripts. That said, the retrieval-vs-generation comparison and evaluation ambiguities reduce confidence in the claimed state-of-the-art.

Questions for authors:
1) How are R@K and median rank computed at test time? Are you ranking all test sequences or only constructed candidates? If the former, how does this relate to the KNN-beam inference?
2) Are CNN weights and doc2vec trained solely on the training split? If not, please redo to avoid transductive leakage.
3) Do you normalize S by sequence length? If not, what prevents length bias?
4) Why is the coherence vector applied per time step via v_t? Have you tried adding a separate scalar coherence score or modeling transitions directly?
5) Can you quantify the quality of the blog segmentation/summarization and its effect on performance?

Pros for acceptance:
- Novel problem and integration of coherence modeling with multimodal networks.
- Large-scale datasets and user studies; coherence improvements especially for longer sequences.
- Clear empirical gains over retrieval-style ablations.

Cons for acceptance:
- Evaluation protocol ambiguity and potential length bias.
- Uneven comparison to generative baselines.
- Some technically ad hoc choices (zero-padding coherence; coherence–image dot product).
- Limited ablations and missing reproducibility details.

Suggestions:
- Clarify and possibly revise the scoring to normalize by length; rethink the coherence term’s interaction with images.
- Strengthen baselines and report significance; add ablations on K, M, κ, history length.
- Fine-tune CNN on training data; learn a projection for the coherence vector instead of zero-padding.
- Release data and code; report preprocessing quality; separate domain-specific doc2vec training to training split only.

Overall: Promising and novel direction with encouraging results, but needs clearer evaluation protocol and stronger methodological justifications before it can be confidently accepted.
