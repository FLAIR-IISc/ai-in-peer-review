### Paper Summary
This paper addresses the novel and challenging task of generating a sequence of natural language sentences to describe a stream of images. This extends the well-studied problem of single-image captioning to a sequence-to-sequence setting. The authors propose a new architecture, the Coherent Recurrent Convolutional Network (CRCN), which jointly models visual content, linguistic flow, and inter-sentence coherence. The model integrates three main components: a Convolutional Neural Network (VGGNet) to extract image features, a Bidirectional Recurrent Neural Network (BRNN) to model the contextual flow of sentences represented by paragraph vectors, and a classic entity-based local coherence model [1] to ensure smooth and logical transitions between sentences. To train this model, the authors cleverly leverage blog posts as a source of weakly-aligned image-text sequence data. The proposed method is retrieval-based: given a query image stream, it first finds candidate sentences for each image from the training set and then uses the learned CRCN model to score and rank the candidate sentence sequences to find the one that is most visually relevant and coherent. The approach is evaluated on two new blog-post datasets (NYC and Disneyland) and shows superior performance over adapted state-of-the-art single-image captioning methods through both quantitative metrics and human studies.

### Strengths
1.  **Novel and Important Problem Formulation:** The paper tackles a logical and significant next step in vision-and-language research. Moving from single images to image streams and single sentences to coherent paragraphs is a well-motivated problem that more closely mirrors how humans consume and create narrative content. To my knowledge, this is one of the first papers to formally address this specific task.

2.  **Innovative Integration of Techniques:** The core novelty lies in the architecture's explicit modeling of coherence. While CNNs and RNNs are standard tools in this domain, the integration of an entity-grid coherence model [1] from classic NLP is a clever and effective choice. This directly addresses a key challenge of this task: ensuring the output is not just a collection of accurate but disjointed captions, but a fluent narrative. The joint training of the content (BRNN) and coherence components within a unified framework is a strong contribution.

3.  **Pragmatic Data Collection Strategy:** Acquiring large-scale, parallel data for image-stream-to-paragraph mapping is a major bottleneck. The authors' approach of using blog posts is a practical and scalable solution. The pre-processing pipeline, while heuristic, is sensible and provides a blueprint for creating datasets for this and similar tasks.

4.  **Thorough Experimental Validation:** The evaluation is comprehensive. The authors compare their model against several strong baselines adapted from state-of-the-art single-image captioning. The inclusion of an ablation study (CRCN vs. RCN) effectively demonstrates the value of the coherence model. The combination of automatic metrics (BLEU, CIDEr, retrieval metrics) and human evaluation via AMT provides a convincing case for the model's effectiveness, particularly the AMT study showing the coherence model's increased importance for longer sequences.

### Weaknesses and Questions
1.  **Clarity of Model Combination:** The description of how the BRNN outputs and the coherence model output are combined is somewhat unclear, which could hinder reproducibility.
    *   In Section 3.3, the coherence model produces a single vector `q` for the entire sequence, while the BRNN produces a vector `ot` for each sentence `t`. Equation 2, `Wf2Wf1[O|q] = [S|g]`, is ambiguous. How is the matrix `O` (size 300xN) concatenated with the vector `q` (size 300x1)? Is `q` replicated N times? Or does it operate on `O` and `q` separately to produce `S` and `g` respectively?
    *   Similarly, the scoring function in Equation 3, `Skl = Σt(skt · vlt + gk · vlt)`, is confusing. The global coherence score `gk` seems to be added at every single timestep `t`. This means the coherence term's contribution is scaled by the length of the sequence, N, which seems unintentional and could dominate the image-text alignment term `skt · vlt`. Please clarify the exact formulation here. Is it perhaps intended to be `Skl = Σt(skt · vlt) + gk · vl_global` where `vl_global` is an aggregate feature for the whole image stream?

2.  **Retrieval vs. Generation:** The method is entirely retrieval-based, meaning it can only compose paragraphs from sentences that already exist in the training corpus. This fundamentally limits its expressiveness and ability to describe novel compositions of objects or events. While this is a valid design choice, the paper would be stronger if it explicitly discussed this limitation and the trade-offs compared to purely generative models (e.g., hierarchical RNNs) which could, in principle, be trained for this task.

3.  **Inference Scalability:** The search space for the best sentence sequence is exponential (`K^N`). The paper proposes a divide-and-conquer strategy with beam search, which is a reasonable approximation. However, the claim that it "achieves almost optimal solutions" is unsubstantiated. Some analysis of how close the approximation is to an exhaustive search on short sequences, or how performance degrades with sequence length, would strengthen this claim.

4.  **Simplicity of Coherence Model:** The entity-based coherence model is a classic from 2008. While its inclusion is a key strength, the field of NLP has advanced since then. The model is sensitive to parsing errors and captures a relatively simple form of coherence based on grammatical roles. The paper could briefly acknowledge this and position their work as a first step, opening the door for integrating more modern, perhaps neural, coherence models in the future.

### Overall Assessment
This is a high-quality paper that introduces a novel, significant problem and proposes a well-designed and effective solution. The originality lies in framing the problem and in the thoughtful integration of a classic NLP coherence model into a modern deep learning architecture. The experimental results are strong and convincingly demonstrate the value of the proposed CRCN model, especially its coherence component. While there are some minor weaknesses in the clarity of the model's technical description and inherent limitations of a retrieval-based approach, these do not detract from the overall contribution. The paper is well-written, well-organized, and is likely to inspire significant follow-up work on generating narrative descriptions from sequential visual data.

**Recommendation:** Accept.
