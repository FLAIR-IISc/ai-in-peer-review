

# Review of "Expressing an Image Stream with a Sequence of Natural Sentences"

## Summary and Relation to Previous Work

This paper addresses a novel problem: generating a sequence of natural language sentences to describe a stream of images (rather than a single image), which the authors call "expressing an image stream with a sequence of natural sentences." The authors propose a multimodal architecture named Coherent Recurrent Convolutional Networks (CRCN) that integrates convolutional neural networks for image representation, bidirectional recurrent neural networks for sentence modeling, and a local coherence model for smooth transitions between sentences. The paper is motivated by the observation that users often take sequences of photos during memorable events (e.g., visiting NYC) and would benefit from a description that captures the entire narrative rather than isolated images.

The work builds upon recent advances in image captioning (e.g., Karpathy et al., 2015; Vinyals et al., 2015) and video description (e.g., Rohrbach et al., 2015), but extends these approaches to handle sequences of images and produce sequences of sentences rather than single captions. The authors make a compelling case for the importance of coherence between sentences, which is particularly challenging for image streams that may contain abrupt visual changes.

## Quality

The paper demonstrates strong technical quality. The authors provide a well-structured methodology with clear components, a reasonable dataset collection approach using blog posts (which naturally contain image-text sequences), and a comprehensive evaluation framework. They compare their approach against multiple strong baselines (including variants of the log-bilinear model, CNN+RNN, and CNN+LSTM) using both standard language metrics (BLEU, CIDEr, METEOR) and retrieval metrics (top-K recall, median rank). The ablation studies effectively demonstrate the contribution of the coherence model by comparing CRCN against RCN (which excludes the coherence model).

The experimental design is sound, with proper train/validation/test splits, and the user studies via Amazon Mechanical Turk provide valuable human evaluation. The authors acknowledge limitations (e.g., BLEU's poor suitability for paragraph evaluation), showing appropriate self-critique. The paper provides sufficient detail for reproducibility, though some implementation specifics are relegated to the supplementary material.

## Clarity

The paper is well-organized and clearly written. The problem statement is effectively illustrated with Figure 1, and the architecture is well-explained with Figure 2. The technical details of the BRNN and coherence model are presented with appropriate mathematical formalism. The writing is generally accessible to NIPS readers, though some sections (particularly the BRNN model description) could benefit from slightly more intuitive explanations.

The paper's clarity is further enhanced by the thorough discussion of related work, which properly situates their contribution relative to prior work on image captioning, video description, and text-image retrieval. The authors clearly articulate how their work differs from previous approaches, particularly in addressing the coherence between sentences.

## Originality

The paper makes a strong original contribution in formulating the problem of image stream description as a sequence-to-sequence task rather than a single-image-to-single-sentence task. While the coherence model is based on prior work (Barzilay & Lapata, 2008), the integration of this model into a multimodal architecture for image stream description is novel. The authors successfully position their work as the first to address this specific problem, and their comparison with related work demonstrates this clearly.

The problem formulation is both novel and meaningful, addressing a real-world need (describing photo streams from travel experiences) that hasn't been adequately addressed in previous literature. The use of blog posts as a source for training data is also a practical and novel approach to obtaining image-text sequences.

## Significance

The significance of this work is substantial. The problem addressed has clear practical applications in areas such as social media content summarization, travel blogging, and multimedia search. The authors demonstrate significant improvements over strong baselines in both quantitative metrics and user studies, with the coherence model proving particularly valuable for longer text sequences. The paper's unsupervised nature (using blog posts without manual annotation) makes it broadly applicable to any domain with similar data.

The work advances the state of the art by introducing a more natural representation for image sequences (as sentence sequences rather than single captions) and by addressing the critical challenge of coherence between sentences in the generated output. The authors' evaluation with both automated metrics and human studies provides strong evidence of the approach's effectiveness.

## Strengths

1. Novel problem formulation that extends the image captioning literature to sequences
2. Comprehensive evaluation with multiple metrics and user studies
3. Practical dataset collection method using blog posts
4. Strong empirical results showing significant improvements over baselines
5. Well-structured architecture that effectively integrates multiple components

## Weaknesses

1. The coherence model itself is not novel (it's based on prior work), though its integration into this specific architecture is valuable
2. Limited discussion of computational complexity and scalability to very long image sequences
3. The blog data collection method could introduce selection bias (only certain types of blogs were included)
4. The paper doesn't deeply analyze failure cases or limitations for specific image sequence types

## Recommendation

This paper makes a strong contribution to the field by addressing a meaningful problem that has been underexplored in the literature. The CRCN architecture is well-motivated, well-executed, and demonstrates clear improvements over existing approaches. The work is technically sound, well-evaluated, and has clear practical significance.

While the coherence model component is not entirely novel, the integration into this specific multimodal architecture for image stream description is valuable and well-justified. The paper's clear problem statement, thorough evaluation, and practical dataset collection method make it a strong candidate for NIPS.

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the limitations mentioned above, particularly regarding computational complexity and failure case analysis. The paper makes a significant contribution to the field of multimodal representation learning and has the potential to influence future work on image sequence understanding and description.
