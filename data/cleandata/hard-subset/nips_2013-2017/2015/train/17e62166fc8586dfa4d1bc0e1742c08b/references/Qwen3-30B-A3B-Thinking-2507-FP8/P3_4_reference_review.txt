

# Peer Review: "Expressing an Image Stream with a Sequence of Natural Sentences"

## Summary and Relation to Prior Work

This paper proposes a novel problem setting: generating a sequence of natural language sentences to describe a sequence of images (an "image stream"), rather than the standard single-image, single-sentence captioning task. The authors introduce Coherent Recurrent Convolutional Networks (CRCN), which integrates convolutional networks for image representation, bidirectional recurrent networks for sentence modeling, and a local coherence model based on entity transitions to ensure fluent sentence sequences. The key innovation lies in addressing the coherence between consecutive sentences, which is critical for long image sequences but largely ignored in prior work.

The paper correctly identifies that while video captioning (e.g., [23, 32]) shares some similarities, it differs fundamentally from their problem as image streams often contain abrupt visual changes rather than smooth transitions. The authors also distinguish their approach from single-sentence captioning methods (e.g., [9, 12, 21]) by explicitly modeling sentence-to-sentence coherence.

## Strengths

1. **Novel Problem Formulation**: The paper successfully identifies and formalizes a meaningful extension of the image captioning problem to sequences of images and sentences, addressing a practical need for describing photo albums or travel experiences.

2. **Comprehensive Evaluation**: The authors provide a thorough evaluation using both quantitative metrics (BLEU, CIDEr, METEOR, top-K recall) and user studies via AMT, which is particularly valuable for a natural language generation task.

3. **Creative Dataset Construction**: The use of blog posts as a source for training data is innovative and well-justified. The preprocessing pipeline (text segmentation, summarization, augmentation) is carefully designed and well-described.

4. **Effective Ablation Studies**: The comparison between CRCN, RCN (BRNN-only), and 1NN (simple nearest neighbor) clearly demonstrates the contribution of both the BRNN and coherence components, especially for longer sequences.

5. **Practical Implementation**: The divide-and-conquer strategy for handling the exponential search space of candidate sentence sequences is pragmatic and effective.

## Weaknesses

1. **Problem Definition Ambiguity**: The paper doesn't sufficiently distinguish between image streams and video sequences. While the authors note that image streams may have "sharp changes of visual content," they fail to explicitly address how their approach handles the fundamental differences between sequential images (which may not have temporal continuity) versus video (which has temporal continuity). This is a critical gap since video captioning is a well-established field.

2. **Overstated Unsupervised Claim**: The paper claims to be "completely unsupervised," but the entire dataset creation process relies on supervised text-image pairing from blog posts. This misrepresentation undermines the paper's novelty claim.

3. **Limited Theoretical Justification**: The coherence model is based on prior work (Barzilay & Lapata, 2008), but the paper doesn't adequately explain why this specific entity-based approach is particularly suited to their problem. The connection between entity transitions and sentence coherence for image streams needs stronger justification.

4. **User Study Limitations**: The user studies use short sequences (5-10 images), which limits the ability to demonstrate the coherence model's benefits. As noted in the paper, the coherence effect becomes more pronounced with longer sequences, but the user studies don't test this adequately. The paper could have conducted a more rigorous test with varying sequence lengths.

5. **Comparison to Video Captioning**: The paper makes no meaningful comparison to video captioning approaches (e.g., [23, 32]), which is surprising given the conceptual similarities. A comparison would have strengthened their claim of novelty.

## Overall Assessment

This paper addresses a meaningful extension of the image captioning problem with clear practical applications. The CRCN architecture is well-motivated and the experimental results are convincing, particularly demonstrating that coherence modeling is crucial for longer sentence sequences. The dataset construction from blog posts is a strong point that addresses a key challenge in the field.

However, the paper's claims of novelty are somewhat overstated due to insufficient differentiation from video captioning work, and the "unsupervised" claim is misleading. The theoretical justification for the coherence model could be stronger, and the user study design could better demonstrate the coherence model's benefits for longer sequences.

## Recommendation

**Accept**

This paper makes a valuable contribution to the field by formalizing and solving a meaningful extension of the image captioning problem. While the paper has some weaknesses in its presentation and justification, the core idea is novel, the evaluation is thorough, and the results are convincing. The proposed CRCN architecture represents a significant step toward generating coherent natural language descriptions for sequences of images, with clear practical applications.

The authors should address the following in the camera-ready version:
1. Clarify the distinction between image streams and video sequences
2. Correct the "unsupervised" claim to accurately reflect the supervised dataset construction
3. Strengthen the justification for the coherence model's design choices
4. Conduct user studies with longer image sequences to better demonstrate the coherence model's benefits

The paper is technically sound, well-written, and addresses a meaningful problem that extends the current state of the art in multimodal generation. It represents a significant contribution to the field and would be a valuable addition to NIPS proceedings.
