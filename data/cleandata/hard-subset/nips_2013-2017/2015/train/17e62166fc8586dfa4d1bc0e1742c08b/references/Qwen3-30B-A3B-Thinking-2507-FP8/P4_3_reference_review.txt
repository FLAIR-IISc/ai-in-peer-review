

# Review for "Expressing an Image Stream with a Sequence of Natural Sentences"

This paper proposes a novel approach for generating sequences of natural language sentences to describe image streams (sequences of images), extending the standard image captioning task. The authors argue that existing work focuses on single-image to single-sentence generation, while real-world scenarios (like tourism blogging) often involve sequences of images that should be described as a coherent narrative. They introduce a multimodal architecture called Coherent Recurrent Convolutional Networks (CRCN) that integrates convolutional neural networks for image representation, bidirectional recurrent neural networks for sentence modeling, and a local coherence model for fluent transitions between sentences. The training data is derived from blog posts, which the authors preprocess to create image-sentence sequence pairs. The paper evaluates their approach on NYC and Disneyland blog datasets (20K blog posts, 140K images) against several baselines, including extensions of single-sentence captioning methods.

The paper demonstrates technical soundness through a well-structured methodology. The CRCN architecture is carefully designed to address the specific challenges of sequence generation (image stream to sentence sequence), with a clear separation of components: CNN for image features, BRNN for content flow, and a coherence model based on entity transitions. The authors provide detailed equations for their BRNN and coherence model, and the training procedure using max-margin loss is standard for this domain. The experimental setup is comprehensive, with both quantitative metrics (BLEU, CIDEr, METEOR, top-K recall) and user studies via AMT. The ablation study comparing CRCN with variants (RCN without coherence, 1NN baseline) effectively demonstrates the contribution of each component.

The paper makes a strong claim of novelty. The authors correctly identify that while video captioning (e.g., [23, 32]) exists, it assumes smooth visual transitions, whereas image streams from blogs often contain abrupt visual changes. The related work section appropriately distinguishes their approach from video captioning and from methods that retrieve image sequences for text (e.g., [11]), which is the inverse problem. The use of blog posts as a source of training data for image-text sequences is a reasonable and novel approach to address the data scarcity issue in this domain. The paper also provides a detailed explanation of how they transform blog posts into image-sentence sequences through text segmentation and summarization, which is a necessary and well-motivated step.

The significance of this work is clear. The problem of generating coherent sentence sequences for image streams is a meaningful extension of the image captioning task with practical applications in tourism, social media, and content generation. The authors demonstrate superior performance over baselines on both quantitative metrics and user studies, particularly on retrieval metrics and longer sequences where coherence becomes more critical. The unsupervised nature of their approach (applicable to any domain with blog-like content) broadens its potential impact.

Strengths:
- Clear problem statement with strong real-world motivation
- Well-structured architecture that effectively integrates multiple components
- Comprehensive evaluation with both quantitative and qualitative measures
- Effective ablation studies demonstrating component contributions
- Reasonable data collection methodology using blog posts
- Good comparison against relevant baselines

Weaknesses:
- The blog post preprocessing (text segmentation and summarization) could introduce noise, but the paper doesn't thoroughly analyze error rates or quality of the generated text-image pairs
- The coherence model is based on entity transitions, but it's unclear how it handles cases where entities are not explicitly mentioned (e.g., when the visual content changes dramatically between images)
- The user studies use a limited number of images per query (5-10), which may not fully capture the coherence challenges of longer sequences
- The paper claims novelty but doesn't explicitly compare with any prior work that might have addressed similar problems (though the related work section suggests none exist)

The paper is well-written and clearly organized, with good use of figures to illustrate the problem and architecture. The technical details are sufficient for reproducibility, though the supplementary material would be needed for full implementation details. The authors are careful to acknowledge limitations, particularly noting that BLEU is not ideal for evaluating coherence.

Pro arguments for acceptance:
- Addresses a meaningful gap in the literature (image streams to sentence sequences)
- Proposes a novel, well-motivated architecture with clear technical contributions
- Demonstrates significant improvements over baselines through rigorous evaluation
- Has practical applications in tourism, social media, and content generation
- Provides a useful dataset construction methodology

Con arguments against acceptance:
- The novelty claim is strong, but the paper could better justify why previous work hasn't addressed this specific problem
- The dataset construction from blog posts might introduce quality issues that aren't sufficiently addressed
- The coherence model's limitations with abrupt visual changes aren't thoroughly explored

Overall, this paper makes a significant contribution to the field by addressing a previously unexplored problem (image streams to sentence sequences) with a technically sound approach. The work is well-executed, well-evaluated, and demonstrates clear improvements over baselines. The methodology is novel and the results are convincing. While there are minor limitations in the dataset construction and coherence model implementation, these do not undermine the core contribution. The paper meets all NIPS criteria for quality, clarity, originality, and significance.
