

# Peer Review: "Expressing an Image Stream with a Sequence of Natural Sentences"

## Summary

This paper introduces a novel problem setting: generating a sequence of natural language sentences to describe a stream of images (rather than a single sentence for a single image), motivated by the common practice of capturing memorable moments through multiple photos. The authors propose a novel architecture called Coherent Recurrent Convolutional Networks (CRCN), which integrates convolutional neural networks for image representation, bidirectional recurrent neural networks for sentence modeling, and a local coherence model to ensure smooth transitions between sentences. The authors leverage blog posts as a source for training data, processing them to extract image-sentence sequences, and evaluate their approach on NYC and Disneyland datasets (20K+ blog posts, 140K+ images) against several baselines.

## Strengths

1. **Problem Formulation**: The paper identifies a meaningful gap in the literature—extending from single-image to image-stream description—which is well-motivated by real-world usage patterns (e.g., photo albums of travel experiences). This represents a significant conceptual extension of the image captioning problem.

2. **Technical Approach**: The CRCN architecture is well-motivated and integrates several key components (CNNs, BRNNs, coherence model) in a unified framework. The coherence model specifically addresses the challenge of maintaining fluent transitions between sentences, which is a critical issue for sequence generation that previous work has overlooked.

3. **Evaluation**: The evaluation is thorough, using both standard language metrics (BLEU, CIDEr, METEOR) and retrieval-based metrics (top-K recall, median rank), complemented by human evaluations via AMT. The ablation studies (comparing CRCN with RCN and 1NN variants) effectively demonstrate the contributions of each component.

4. **Data Source**: Using blog posts as a source for training data is innovative and practical, as blogs naturally contain image-text sequences that follow storytelling patterns.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited Scope and Generalization**: While the authors claim their approach is "completely unsupervised and thus applicable to any domain," the experiments are limited to tourism topics (NYC and Disneyland). The paper lacks analysis of whether the approach generalizes to other domains or to different types of image streams (e.g., sports events, daily life).

2. **Data Source Justification**: The paper states that blogs are "carefully selected by authors in a way of storytelling," but it doesn't provide evidence or analysis of the quality of this data. It would be valuable to assess potential biases (e.g., tourism-centric, Western-centric, or gender-skewed content) in the blog datasets.

3. **Comparison with Video Captioning Work**: The paper mentions video-sentence works (e.g., [23, 32]) but doesn't thoroughly contrast their approach with these methods. Video captioning already deals with sequences of images, so a more detailed comparison would strengthen the paper's novelty claim.

4. **Lack of Computational Analysis**: The paper doesn't discuss the computational complexity or scalability of the CRCN model, especially for longer image sequences. This is important for practical applications, as the paper mentions "exponentially many candidates" during retrieval.

5. **Clarity in Coherence Model**: The description of the local coherence model (Section 3.2) is somewhat dense and could benefit from clearer explanation of how the entity grid and transition patterns are used to create the coherence descriptor.

## Quality Assessment

The paper is technically sound with well-supported claims. The proposed CRCN architecture is carefully designed, and the experimental results consistently show improvements over strong baselines. However, the evaluation could be strengthened by:
- Providing more analysis of the blog dataset's characteristics and potential biases
- Including a more comprehensive comparison with video captioning approaches
- Discussing computational requirements for real-world deployment

## Clarity Assessment

The paper is well-organized and generally clearly written, with good use of figures to illustrate key concepts. The methodology is described in sufficient detail for reproducibility. However, the coherence model section could be clarified, and the paper would benefit from a more explicit definition of "image stream" (e.g., typical length, temporal relationships between images).

## Originality Assessment

The paper makes a clear contribution by extending the image captioning problem to sequence generation for image streams. While some aspects (CNNs, RNNs) are built upon existing work, the integration of a coherence model specifically for sentence-to-sentence transitions in this context is novel. The paper adequately distinguishes itself from related work, though the comparison with video captioning could be more thorough.

## Significance Assessment

The problem addressed is significant for applications like automated photo album captioning, travel blogging, and multimedia retrieval. However, the limited scope of the evaluation (tourism blogs only) somewhat limits the perceived significance. The paper would benefit from discussing potential broader applications and how the approach could be adapted to other domains.

## Recommendation

I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions. The paper makes a meaningful contribution to the field by addressing a gap in the literature (sequence generation for image streams) with a well-designed approach. The technical soundness, thorough evaluation, and clear novelty make it a strong candidate for NIPS.

The authors should address the following minor points in revision:
1. Expand the discussion of the blog dataset's characteristics and potential limitations/biases
2. Provide a more detailed comparison with video captioning approaches
3. Clarify the coherence model description in Section 3.2
4. Discuss computational complexity and scalability more explicitly

These revisions would strengthen the paper without requiring major new experiments. The core contribution is novel, well-executed, and represents a meaningful advancement in multimodal sequence generation.
