

# Review: "Expressing an Image Stream with a Sequence of Natural Sentences"

## Summary

This paper proposes a novel problem setting where the goal is to generate a sequence of natural language sentences to describe an image stream (a sequence of images), rather than the standard single-image-to-single-sentence task. The authors introduce Coherent Recurrent Convolutional Networks (CRCN), which integrates convolutional neural networks for image representation, bidirectional recurrent networks for sentence modeling, and a local coherence model for smooth transitions between sentences. The paper leverages blog posts as a source of image-text parallel data, with datasets containing over 20K blog posts and 140K associated images from NYC and Disneyland. The authors evaluate their approach using both quantitative metrics (BLEU, CIDEr, METEOR, top-K recall) and user studies via Amazon Mechanical Turk.

The work is positioned as an extension of existing image captioning literature (e.g., Karpathy et al. [9], Vinyals et al. [30]) but addresses a gap in handling sequential outputs for sequential inputs. The coherence model specifically targets the challenge of maintaining fluent transitions between sentences when consecutive images in a stream show sharp visual changes.

## Quality

The paper is technically sound with a well-structured methodology. The CRCN architecture combines existing techniques (CNNs, BRNNs) with a novel coherence model that explicitly addresses the challenge of sentence-to-sentence transitions. The authors provide sufficient detail about the model architecture, training procedure, and evaluation methodology.

The claims are well-supported by comprehensive experiments. The quantitative results demonstrate consistent improvements over strong baselines (including variants of [9], [12], and [30]), with statistically significant improvements in retrieval metrics. The user studies via AMT further validate that human annotators prefer the CRCN-generated sequences over baselines, particularly for longer image streams.

However, the paper could have provided more thorough ablation studies to isolate the contribution of each component. The comparison between CRCN and RCN (BRNN-only) shows only modest improvements in language metrics, suggesting the coherence model's primary impact is on ranking rather than sentence quality. This is a limitation in the evaluation of the coherence model's specific contribution.

## Clarity

The paper is generally well-written and well-organized. The problem statement is clearly motivated, the methodology is explained with sufficient detail, and the results are presented clearly with helpful examples. The authors effectively distinguish their approach from related work, particularly in addressing the coherence challenge.

The description of the coherence model (Section 3.2) is somewhat technical but understandable for the target audience. The figures (Fig. 1, 2) effectively illustrate the problem setup and architecture. The paper would benefit from a more detailed discussion of the practical limitations of the approach, such as how the method scales to very long image sequences.

## Originality

The paper's main contribution is original in two aspects: (1) the problem formulation of generating sentence sequences for image streams, and (2) the integration of a coherence model into the multimodal architecture. While previous work has addressed image captioning (single image to single sentence) and video captioning (short video to single sentence), this paper is indeed the first to explicitly address the problem of generating coherent sentence sequences for image streams.

The coherence model itself is an adaptation of prior work [1] but is novel in its application to this specific problem. The authors clearly articulate how their approach differs from related work, particularly the distinction from video captioning (which typically deals with smoother transitions) and from simply concatenating single-image captions.

## Significance

The problem addressed is significant as it represents a natural extension of image captioning to more realistic use cases where users capture multiple images of an experience. The blog-based dataset collection method is innovative and leverages existing online content, avoiding the need for expensive manual annotation.

The results demonstrate meaningful improvements over strong baselines, particularly in the coherence of generated sequences. The user studies provide compelling evidence that human preferences align with the quantitative metrics, validating the approach's practical value.

However, the paper doesn't fully establish why a sequence of sentences is superior to a single well-structured sentence for describing an image stream. The motivation for the sequence format is somewhat weak (people take multiple pictures), but it's not clear why multiple sentences are necessary rather than a single descriptive paragraph. This limitation somewhat weakens the significance claim.

## Strengths and Weaknesses Summary

**Strengths:**
- Novel problem formulation addressing a meaningful gap in image captioning literature
- Comprehensive evaluation with both quantitative metrics and user studies
- Well-constructed blog-based datasets for training and evaluation
- Clear distinction from prior work, particularly in addressing coherence challenges

**Weaknesses:**
- Limited discussion of why sequence of sentences is better than single sentence
- Modest improvements in language metrics (BLEU, CIDEr) despite significant retrieval improvements
- User studies limited to short image streams (5 images), not fully testing coherence for longer sequences
- Insufficient ablation studies to isolate the coherence model's specific contribution

## Recommendation

This paper presents a valuable extension of image captioning to the more realistic scenario of image streams. The CRCN architecture with its coherence model addresses a genuine challenge in generating fluent sentence sequences. The evaluation is thorough and the results are compelling.

While the paper has some limitations in fully establishing the significance of the sequence-of-sentences approach and in isolating the coherence model's contribution, the work is technically sound, well-motivated, and represents a meaningful advancement in the field.

**Recommendation: Accept**

The paper makes a clear contribution to the field of multimodal learning and image captioning, with a novel problem formulation and a well-executed solution. The results demonstrate significant improvements over baselines, and the approach has clear potential for real-world applications in social media and travel blogging. The paper meets NIPS standards for quality, clarity, originality, and significance, making it a strong candidate for acceptance.
