

# Review: "Expressing an Image Stream with a Sequence of Natural Sentences"

## Summary
This paper proposes a novel task of generating a sequence of natural language sentences to describe an image stream (a sequence of images), rather than the conventional single-image-to-single-sentence generation. The authors introduce Coherent Recurrent Convolutional Networks (CRCN), which integrates convolutional neural networks for image representation, bidirectional recurrent networks for sentence sequence modeling, and a local coherence model to ensure smooth transitions between sentences. The training data is derived from blog posts, which naturally contain sequences of images with associated text. The authors evaluate their approach on NYC and Disneyland blog datasets (20K+ posts, 140K+ images) and demonstrate improvements over several strong baselines in both quantitative metrics (BLEU, CIDEr, METEOR, top-K recall) and user studies.

## Quality
The paper is technically sound and well-executed. The authors provide a clear methodology, including detailed architecture descriptions with equations, comprehensive experimental setup, and thorough evaluation. The CRCN architecture is a thoughtful integration of existing components (CNN, BRNN, coherence model) specifically designed for the new task. The ablation study (comparing CRCN, RCN, and 1NN) effectively demonstrates the contribution of each component. The dataset collection process from blog posts is well-justified and provides a practical source of training data. The paper acknowledges limitations, particularly noting that BLEU is not ideal for evaluating longer text passages, and the user studies with varying sequence lengths validate the importance of their coherence model.

## Clarity
The paper is generally well-written and well-organized. The problem statement is clearly introduced with a compelling example (Fig. 1), and the architecture is well-explained with figures and equations. The experimental setup is sufficiently detailed for reproducibility. However, there are minor clarity issues: the connection between their coherence model and Barzilay & Lapata's work (cited as [1]) could be better explained, and the paper could more explicitly articulate why coherence is necessary for image streams but not for videos (beyond the brief mention of "sharp changes" in visual content). Some technical terms like "local coherence model" are referenced before being fully defined.

## Originality
The core contribution of addressing image stream description rather than single-image captioning is genuinely novel. The paper makes a strong case that this is an underexplored area compared to single-image captioning, and their task definition is clearly differentiated from related work on video captioning (which typically deals with more continuous visual content). While the individual components (CNN, BRNN, coherence model) are not novel, their specific integration for this new task represents a meaningful contribution. The use of blog posts as a source of training data for this task is also innovative. The paper does a good job of distinguishing their work from related approaches, particularly noting that previous video captioning work (e.g., [23, 32]) doesn't address the same problem due to the more discontinuous nature of image streams.

## Significance
The problem addressed has clear practical relevance, as people commonly take sequences of photos during memorable experiences (e.g., tourism). The paper demonstrates significant improvements over strong baselines in both quantitative metrics and user studies, validating the approach's effectiveness. The CRCN architecture provides a principled way to handle the coherence between sentences, which is crucial for generating fluent multi-sentence descriptions. The paper also provides a valuable new dataset (blog posts with image sequences) that could benefit future research. While the immediate applications are in tourism and social media, the approach could be extended to other domains with sequential visual data.

## Strengths and Weaknesses

**Strengths:**
1. Novel problem formulation that extends the field beyond single-image captioning
2. Well-designed CRCN architecture that effectively integrates image, language, and coherence components
3. Comprehensive evaluation with both quantitative metrics and user studies
4. Practical and scalable approach using blog posts as training data
5. Strong ablation study demonstrating the necessity of both BRNN and coherence components

**Weaknesses:**
1. Limited discussion of computational complexity and training time
2. The paper could better explain why coherence is necessary for image streams but not for videos (beyond the brief mention of "sharp changes")
3. The connection to Barzilay & Lapata's work [1] on local coherence is not sufficiently developed
4. No discussion of failure cases or limitations (e.g., how the model handles very long image streams with large semantic shifts)
5. The paper claims to be the first to address this problem, but a more thorough literature review would strengthen this claim

## Recommendation

This paper makes a significant contribution to the field of multimodal generation by introducing a novel task (image stream description) and a principled approach to address it. The CRCN architecture effectively integrates existing techniques to handle the key challenge of sentence coherence in multi-sentence outputs. The experimental evaluation is thorough and demonstrates clear improvements over strong baselines.

The paper meets NIPS standards in quality, clarity, originality, and significance. The main weakness is the limited discussion of computational aspects and the need for a more detailed explanation of the coherence model's necessity for this specific task. However, these are minor concerns compared to the paper's overall contribution.

I recommend acceptance as a strong contribution to the field. The work is technically sound, the problem is novel and relevant, and the results demonstrate clear improvements over state-of-the-art methods. The paper provides a valuable new direction for multimodal language generation that could inspire future work on sequential visual understanding and description.
