

# Review of "Expressing an Image Stream with a Sequence of Natural Sentences"

This paper introduces a novel problem setup: generating a sequence of natural language sentences to describe a stream of images, rather than the conventional single-image/single-sentence captioning task. The authors propose a multimodal architecture called Coherent Recurrent Convolutional Networks (CRCN) that integrates convolutional networks for image representation, bidirectional recurrent networks for sentence modeling, and a local coherence model specifically designed to ensure smooth transitions between consecutive sentences. The key insight is that users often capture memorable moments as sequences of images (e.g., a trip to NYC), and the resulting text description should reflect this sequential nature rather than treating each image independently.

The paper's main novelty lies in extending the problem space from single-image captioning to image-stream-to-sentence-sequence generation, a direction that has been underexplored despite its practical relevance. The authors cleverly leverage blog posts as a source of naturally occurring image-text parallel data streams, which provides a large-scale unsupervised training corpus. This is a significant methodological shift from previous work that relies on manually annotated image-sentence pairs.

The CRCN architecture represents a meaningful integration of existing components (CNNs, BRNNs) with a coherence model specifically designed for sequential text generation. The coherence model, which tracks discourse entities and grammatical role transitions across sentences, is the paper's most novel technical contribution. The authors demonstrate through quantitative metrics (BLEU, CIDEr, METEOR, top-K recall) and user studies that their approach outperforms baselines that treat each image independently or generate sentences without explicit coherence modeling.

The paper makes a compelling case for the importance of coherence in sentence sequences. The user study results showing increased preference for CRCN outputs as query length increases provide strong empirical support for their coherence model's value. The authors also address a practical challenge in their domain by developing a divide-and-conquer strategy to handle the exponential search space of candidate sentence sequences.

While the paper's core idea is novel and well-motivated, there are some methodological limitations worth noting. The coherence model implementation seems somewhat ad-hoc, and the paper doesn't deeply analyze why specific transition patterns (e.g., SO-, OOX) are most important. The evaluation is limited to two tourism-related datasets, and it's not clear how well the approach generalizes to other domains. The baseline comparisons could also be strengthened by including more recent work on sequential text generation.

However, as a reviewer who prioritizes novelty and bold new directions over methodological perfection, I find this paper highly compelling. It identifies a meaningful gap in the literature (the lack of work on image streams → sentence sequences), proposes a clear and novel solution, and provides strong evidence that their approach is better than existing alternatives. The paper represents a genuine step forward in multimodal understanding, moving beyond the single-image paradigm that has dominated the field.

The problem statement is clearly articulated with a strong real-world motivation (users capturing memorable moments as photo streams), and the authors provide a convincing demonstration of their method's effectiveness. The blog data collection approach is particularly clever and scalable, making the work applicable to many domains without requiring manual annotation.

This paper makes a significant contribution to the field by establishing a new research direction in multimodal learning. While not every detail is perfect, the core idea is sufficiently novel and impactful to warrant acceptance. The authors have opened up a new area of research that others will build upon, which is exactly what NIPS aims to encourage.

**Strengths:** Novel problem formulation, clear real-world motivation, clever use of blog data, well-designed coherence model, strong empirical validation, and significant extension beyond current image captioning paradigms.

**Weaknesses:** Some ad-hoc design choices in coherence modeling, limited domain evaluation, and baseline comparisons could be more comprehensive.

**Recommendation:** Accept. This paper introduces a genuinely novel direction that addresses a meaningful gap in multimodal learning, with strong empirical evidence supporting its approach. The novelty and potential impact on the field outweigh the methodological limitations.
