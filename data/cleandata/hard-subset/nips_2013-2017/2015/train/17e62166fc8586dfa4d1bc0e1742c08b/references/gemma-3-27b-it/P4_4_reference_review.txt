This paper addresses the problem of generating a sequence of natural language sentences describing a sequence of images, motivated by scenarios like travel photo streams. The authors propose a Coherent Recurrent Convolutional Network (CRCN) integrating CNNs for image representation, BRNNs for sentence modeling, and a local coherence model for fluent transitions. They leverage blog posts as a text-image parallel corpus and evaluate on NYC and Disneyland datasets using both quantitative metrics (BLEU, top-K recall) and user studies.

The paper builds upon a large body of work in image captioning ([5, 8, 9, 12, 14, 15, 26, 21, 30]) and extends it to the sequence-to-sequence domain. It also acknowledges related work in video description ([23, 32]) but distinguishes itself by explicitly modeling coherence, which is deemed more critical for image streams due to potentially abrupt visual changes.  The approach of using blog posts as a readily available weakly-supervised dataset is sensible and practical, drawing parallels to work in utilizing web data for vision tasks ([6, 21, 26]). The inclusion of a coherence model is a key contribution, distinguishing the work from simply concatenating individual image captions.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The problem of expressing an *image stream* with a *sequence of sentences* is a natural and important extension of single-image captioning, and this work is among the first to tackle it directly.
*   **Technical Soundness:** The proposed CRCN architecture seems well-motivated and logically constructed, integrating established components (CNNs, BRNNs) with a novel coherence model. The training procedure with a max-margin loss is standard and appropriate.
*   **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The paper includes both quantitative metrics commonly used in the field and user studies via AMT, providing a relatively thorough evaluation of the approach. The user studies with different image stream lengths are insightful.
*   **Practical Data Source:** Leveraging blog posts as a training corpus is a smart way to overcome the scarcity of parallel image-sentence sequence data.
*   **Clear Problem Definition:** The motivation and problem statement are clearly articulated and relatable.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Limited Baselines:** While the authors compare to several image captioning models ([9, 12, 21, 30]), these are adapted to the sequence-to-sequence setting. A more direct comparison to sequence-to-sequence models, even outside of the vision domain, would be beneficial.  It's unclear how much the performance gain is due to the specific architecture versus simply extending existing single-image captioning techniques to sequences.
*   **Coherence Model Details:** The description of the coherence model, while present, lacks depth. The entity grid and transition representations are mentioned but not fully explained.  Understanding *how* the coherence model guides sentence selection is crucial, and more details are needed.
*   **Data Preprocessing:** The text segmentation and summarization processes (LSA-based summarization) used to create the blog dataset are briefly described. The impact of these preprocessing steps on the quality of the training data and, consequently, the performance of the model, isn't adequately discussed.
*   **Ablation Study Limitations:** While the ablation study includes removing the coherence model, more granular ablation studies could reveal the contribution of individual components within the BRNN and CNN.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The problem is novel and important, representing a natural progression in image understanding and generation.
*   The proposed approach is technically sound and well-motivated.
*   The evaluation is relatively comprehensive and demonstrates promising results.
*   The use of blog data is a clever solution to the data scarcity issue.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

*   The baseline comparisons are not strong enough to conclusively demonstrate the superiority of the proposed approach.
*   The coherence model, a key component, lacks detailed explanation and analysis.
*   The impact of data preprocessing on performance isn’t adequately addressed.



**Overall Assessment:**

The paper presents a solid contribution to the field of image understanding and generation. While there are some weaknesses, particularly regarding baseline comparisons and the coherence model’s details, the novelty and overall technical soundness of the approach are significant. I believe the paper is **acceptably strong** and would be a valuable addition to NIPS, but addressing the weaknesses in a revision would substantially improve its quality. Specifically, strengthening the baselines and expanding the explanation of the coherence model are crucial.
