### Summary of the Paper

This paper introduces the novel task of generating a coherent sequence of natural language sentences from a stream of images. The authors argue that this is a natural extension of the well-studied single image-to-sentence generation problem, motivated by real-world scenarios like describing a photo album from a trip. To tackle this task, they propose a new multimodal architecture, the Coherent Recurrent Convolutional Network (CRCN). The CRCN integrates three main components: (1) a Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) to extract visual features from each image, (2) a Bidirectional Recurrent Neural Network (BRNN) to model the semantic flow and context of the sentence sequence, and (3) an entity-based local coherence model, adapted from prior NLP work [1], to ensure linguistic fluency and logical transitions between sentences.

The proposed method operates within a retrieval framework. For a given query image stream, candidate sentences are first sourced from the training data by finding nearest-neighbor images. The CRCN is then used to score and rank all possible sentence sequences, finding the one that is most compatible with the image stream in terms of both visual content and textual coherence. The model is trained using a max-margin objective on a large-scale dataset of image-text streams automatically curated from travel blogs about New York City and Disneyland. The paper demonstrates the superiority of the CRCN model through extensive experiments, including quantitative metrics (BLEU, CIDEr, etc.) and human evaluations via Amazon Mechanical Turk, outperforming several adapted state-of-the-art single-image captioning methods.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Quality:** The paper is technically solid for the most part. The proposed architecture is well-motivated, and the integration of components is thoughtful. The experimental evaluation is a major strength: the authors have not only proposed a new task but also curated a large-scale dataset for it. The inclusion of multiple strong baselines, an ablation study (CRCN vs. RCN without the coherence model), and both automatic and human evaluation provides a comprehensive and convincing validation of the proposed method. The results clearly demonstrate that modeling sequence-level properties (flow and coherence) is crucial and leads to significant improvements over methods that treat images independently.

However, there is a lack of clarity in a key technical detail. The final compatibility score in Equation (3) is defined as S_kl = Σ_t (s_kt · v_lt + g_k · v_lt). Here, g_k is a global coherence score for the entire sentence sequence k, while s_kt is the score for the sentence at position t. It is unclear why the global coherence score g_k is added at *each* time step, scaled by the image feature vector v_lt. This formulation seems to imply that the contribution of global coherence is dependent on the visual content of each individual image, which is not intuitive. A more standard approach would be to have the coherence score as a separate term added to the sum of local image-sentence scores. This aspect of the model needs better justification or revision.

**Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written, clearly motivated, and easy to follow. Figure 2 provides an excellent overview of the data processing pipeline and the model architecture. The problem statement and contributions are laid out clearly. The experimental setup and results are presented in a straightforward manner. The only major point of confusion, as mentioned above, is the formulation and justification of Equation (3). Additionally, the claim in the introduction that the "approach is completely unsupervised" is slightly inaccurate. While the data collection from blogs is automatic, the CRCN model itself is trained in a supervised fashion on aligned image-sentence pairs. This should be rephrased for precision.

**Originality:** The primary contribution of this work is the formulation of a new and important problem: describing image streams with sentence sequences. While building on established components like CNNs and RNNs, the paper's novelty lies in architecturally addressing the sequential and coherent nature of the task. The integration of a classic, entity-based coherence model [1] from NLP into a modern deep learning framework for a vision-and-language task is a novel and clever contribution. This distinguishes the work from prior image captioning and video description literature, which often relies solely on RNNs to implicitly capture temporal structure.

**Significance:** The paper makes a significant contribution by moving the field beyond single-image-sentence pairs towards more complex, structured inputs and outputs. The problem itself has clear practical applications in areas like automatic photo album summarization and storytelling. The proposed model and the created dataset are valuable resources that are likely to inspire follow-up research. The demonstration that explicitly modeling linguistic coherence yields substantial gains provides an important insight for future work on generating longer forms of text (paragraphs, stories) from visual data. The main limitation to its broader significance is that the framework is retrieval-based, meaning it can only combine existing sentences from its training corpus and cannot generate truly novel sentences.

### Arguments for and against acceptance

**Arguments for acceptance:**
1.  **Novel and Important Problem:** The paper introduces and formalizes the task of generating descriptions for image streams, a logical and significant step forward for the vision-and-language community.
2.  **Strong Empirical Results:** The proposed CRCN model convincingly outperforms strong, adapted baselines on a new, large-scale dataset, validated by both automatic metrics and human studies.
3.  **Novel Integration of Ideas:** The architecture thoughtfully combines deep visual and language models with a classic NLP coherence model, demonstrating the value of explicit coherence modeling.
4.  **Thorough Evaluation:** The experimental design is comprehensive, including an ablation study that clearly isolates and validates the contribution of the key components of the model.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
1.  **Technical Clarity Issue:** The formulation of the final scoring function (Equation 3), which combines local and global scores, is counter-intuitive and inadequately justified, potentially indicating a minor technical flaw.
2.  **Retrieval-based Limitation:** The approach is fundamentally limited to retrieving and rearranging existing sentences, lacking the generative capacity of end-to-end models. This limitation should be more prominently discussed.
3.  **Heuristic-driven Pipeline:** The data preprocessing and test-time search both rely on multi-step heuristics whose potential impact (e.g., noise in labels, sub-optimal search results) is not discussed.

### Suggestions for Improvement

Overall, this is a strong paper addressing an interesting new problem. My recommendation leans towards acceptance, provided the authors can address the following points:

1.  **Clarify Equation (3):** Please provide a detailed justification for the specific formulation of the compatibility score. Why is the global coherence vector `g` added inside the summation over time steps? Have you explored alternative formulations, such as `Skl = (Σt skt · vlt) + α * score(gk, {vlt})`, where the coherence score is a single global term? Clarifying this is crucial for the paper's technical soundness.
2.  **Acknowledge Limitations:** Please add a paragraph in the discussion or conclusion that explicitly addresses the limitations of the current retrieval-based framework (i.e., inability to generate novel sentences) and contrasts it with potential fully generative approaches. Similarly, a brief mention of the heuristic nature of the test-time search would add to the paper's transparency.
3.  **Rephrase "Unsupervised" Claim:** For accuracy, please revise the claim of the method being "completely unsupervised." It would be more precise to state that the data is collected from an unsupervised source (blogs) but the model training is supervised.
4.  **Discuss Data Preprocessing:** A brief discussion on the potential for noise introduced by the heuristic-based text segmentation and summarization pipeline would make the work more complete. While the approach is pragmatic, acknowledging its potential impact would be valuable.
