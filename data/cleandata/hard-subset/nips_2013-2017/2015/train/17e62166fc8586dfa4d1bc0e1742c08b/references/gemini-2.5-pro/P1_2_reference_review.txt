**Summary**

This paper addresses the novel problem of generating a sequence of natural language sentences to describe a stream of images. This is a logical and interesting extension of the well-studied task of single-image captioning. The authors propose a multimodal architecture, Coherent Recurrent Convolutional Networks (CRCN), which integrates three main components: (1) a Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) for image feature extraction, (2) a Bidirectional Recurrent Neural Network (BRNN) to model the content flow and semantic dependencies within the sentence sequence, and (3) a classic entity-based local coherence model to ensure grammatical and referential smoothness between consecutive sentences. To train this model, the authors resourcefully curate a large-scale dataset from blog posts, developing a pre-processing pipeline to align image sequences with corresponding text. The final system operates in a retrieval-based framework: for a query image stream, it finds candidate sentences from the training data and then uses the learned CRCN model to score and rank possible sentence sequences, selecting the one that is most compatible and coherent. The approach is evaluated on two new blog datasets (NYC and Disneyland) and shows significant improvements over adapted state-of-the-art single-image captioning baselines, as measured by both automatic metrics and human studies.

**Strengths**

1.  **Novel and Significant Problem Formulation:** The paper tackles a new and compelling problem. Moving from single image-sentence pairs to image-sentence sequences is a natural and important step towards more holistic scene understanding and storytelling. The motivation of describing a user's photo stream (e.g., from a trip) is practical and relatable.

2.  **Sound and Creative Model Architecture:** The proposed CRCN model is well-designed. The combination of a modern deep learning approach (CNN+BRNN) for semantic content with a classic, rule-based NLP technique (entity-grid coherence model) is a key strength. It acknowledges that end-to-end deep models may not perfectly capture all linguistic nuances like entity coherence, and explicitly modeling this aspect is a clever and effective design choice.

3.  **Thorough and Convincing Experimental Evaluation:** The evaluation is comprehensive.
    *   The authors compare against several strong, relevant baselines from the single-image captioning literature, adapting them for this new task.
    *   The inclusion of an ablation study (comparing CRCN, RCN, and 1NN) clearly demonstrates the value of both the BRNN component and, critically, the explicit coherence model.
    *   The use of both automatic metrics (BLEU, CIDEr, etc.) and human evaluation via AMT provides a balanced and convincing case for the model's superiority. The user study's finding that the coherence model's benefit increases with sequence length is particularly insightful.
    *   The creation and use of a large, real-world dataset from blog posts is a valuable contribution in itself.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Retrieval vs. Generation:** The paper's title and some of its language ("generating natural language descriptions") suggest a generative model, but the proposed method is entirely retrieval-based. It re-assembles sequences from sentences that already exist in the training corpus. This is a significant distinction. The model cannot produce novel sentences or describe objects/scenes not present in the training data's text. This limitation should be made more explicit and discussed, perhaps by framing the task as "retrieving and composing a descriptive paragraph" rather than "generating" one.

2.  **Scalability and Inference Approximation:** The inference process involves a combinatorial search over `K^N` candidate sequences, which is addressed with a divide-and-conquer beam search. The paper asserts this "achieves almost optimal solutions," but this claim is not substantiated with empirical evidence. It would be beneficial to include analysis on how performance varies with the beam size (M) or to compare the approximate search result with a brute-force search on a small example. The computational cost of this inference step is not discussed but could be a practical limitation for long image sequences.

3.  **Potential Noise in Data Pre-processing:** The pipeline for creating the image-sentence parallel corpus relies on heuristics, such as assigning a text block to the spatially closest image in the blog layout. While reasonable, this could introduce considerable label noise, as the visual content of an image might be discussed in a non-proximal paragraph. The paper does not analyze the potential impact of this noise on the model's performance.

4.  **Clarity of Model Combination:** Section 3.3, which describes the combination of the BRNN and coherence model outputs, is somewhat dense. The equation `Wf2Wf1[O|q] = [S|g]` and the roles of the resulting `S` and `g` matrices could be explained more intuitively. A clearer explanation of how these outputs are mapped to the final compatibility score in Equation 3 would improve the paper's clarity for readers trying to reimplement the model.

**Detailed Comments and Questions**

*   In the training cost function (Eq. 4), negative examples are sampled randomly. Given that the training data is from specific domains (NYC, Disneyland), could a more sophisticated negative sampling strategy (e.g., hard negatives with similar but incorrect content) lead to a more discriminative model?
*   The entity-based coherence model by Barzilay and Lapata is a well-established technique but is over a decade old. Have the authors considered or experimented with more recent, perhaps neural, approaches to modeling coherence? While the chosen model works well, a brief discussion of alternatives would strengthen the paper.

**Recommendation: Accept**

This is a strong paper that introduces a novel, interesting, and significant problem to the community. The proposed CRCN model is a thoughtful combination of modern and classic techniques that is shown to be highly effective through a rigorous and multi-faceted evaluation. The weaknesses, primarily related to the retrieval-based nature of the method and the lack of analysis on the inference approximation, are outweighed by the strengths. They represent avenues for future work rather than fundamental flaws in the current contribution. This work is likely to inspire follow-up research on story-like visual description and is a solid contribution to the field.
