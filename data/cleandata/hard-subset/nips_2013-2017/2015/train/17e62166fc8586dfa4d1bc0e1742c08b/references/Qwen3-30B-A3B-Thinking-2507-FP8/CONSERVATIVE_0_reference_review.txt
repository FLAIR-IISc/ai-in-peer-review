

# Review of "Expressing an Image Stream with a Sequence of Natural Sentences"

This paper proposes a novel task of generating a sequence of natural language sentences to describe a stream of images (instead of the standard single-image captioning), and introduces a "Coherent Recurrent Convolutional Networks" (CRCN) architecture to address this problem. The authors claim this is the first work to address "expressing image streams with sentence sequences," and propose using blog posts as a source of image-text sequences for training.

The paper's core contribution is the integration of three components: (1) CNN for image representation, (2) BRNN for sentence modeling, and (3) a local coherence model based on entity transitions to ensure smooth transitions between sentences. The authors evaluate their approach on two blog datasets (NYC and Disneyland, totaling ~20K posts with ~140K images) using standard captioning metrics (BLEU, CIDEr, METEOR) and Amazon Mechanical Turk studies.

**Strengths:**
- The task of generating multi-sentence descriptions for image streams is a logical extension of existing image captioning research and could have practical applications for summarizing photo collections.
- The paper is well-organized and presents a clear methodology for transforming blog posts into training data.
- The authors provide thorough ablation studies, showing that both the BRNN and coherence components contribute to performance.

**Weaknesses:**
1. **Questionable novelty:** The paper claims to be "the first" to address this problem, but this is misleading. While the specific task of generating *multiple* sentences for *image streams* is not standard, the approach is essentially a straightforward extension of existing single-sentence captioning methods. The authors simply concatenate generated sentences from individual images (as done in the baselines), with the addition of a coherence model. This is not a fundamentally new approach, but rather an incremental extension of existing techniques.

2. **Inadequate baseline comparison:** The baselines are all single-sentence captioning models that are extended to handle sequences by simple concatenation. This is not a fair comparison, as the authors' method is specifically designed to handle sequences, while the baselines are not. For example, the paper does not compare against methods explicitly designed for multi-sentence generation (e.g., [11] which retrieves image sequences for text, or [23, 32] for video captioning), nor do they compare against methods that explicitly model sentence-level coherence.

3. **Lack of theoretical justification for coherence model:** The coherence model is based on entity transitions, but the paper provides no clear theoretical or empirical justification for why this specific approach is necessary or superior to simpler alternatives. The paper claims the coherence model "is more demanded" due to "sharp changes of visual content" in image streams, but fails to provide evidence that the coherence model actually addresses this specific problem.

4. **Questionable evaluation methodology:** The human evaluation is limited to sequences of 5 images (or 8-10 in a secondary test), which is too short to properly evaluate the coherence model's effectiveness. The paper states that "the coherence becomes more critical as the passage is longer," yet the main human evaluation is conducted on short sequences where coherence is less important. This undermines the main claim of the coherence model's contribution.

5. **Insufficient validation against established baselines:** The paper compares against methods like [9, 12, 21], but these are designed for single-sentence generation. The authors do not provide a convincing argument that their approach is fundamentally different from simply applying existing single-sentence methods to each image and concatenating results. The paper's strongest metric (top-K recall) shows only modest improvements over the concatenation baseline, suggesting the coherence model provides limited additional value.

6. **Lack of analysis on dataset limitations:** The blog post dataset has significant biases (e.g., travel blogs are not representative of general image streams), and the paper does not analyze how this might affect model performance or generalizability.

**Arguments for acceptance:**
- The task is a logical extension of image captioning research.
- The paper provides a clear methodology for the new task.
- The ablation studies show that both components of the model contribute to performance.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
- The novelty is overstated; the approach is a straightforward extension of existing methods rather than a truly novel contribution.
- The baseline comparison is inadequate and misleading.
- The evaluation does not properly test the coherence model's contribution, as the main human evaluation is on short sequences.
- The paper fails to demonstrate that the proposed approach significantly advances the state of the art in a meaningful way.
- The claimed "first" for this problem is inaccurate given related work on video captioning and multi-sentence generation.

**Overall assessment:**
This paper proposes an interesting extension to the image captioning task, but the contribution is incremental rather than novel. The method is a straightforward combination of existing components without sufficient justification for why this specific combination is necessary or superior to simpler alternatives. The evaluation does not properly validate the coherence model's contribution, and the baseline comparison is inadequate. The paper claims to be "the first" to address this problem, but this is misleading given the existence of related work on video captioning and multi-sentence generation.

While the problem is interesting, the paper fails to demonstrate a significant advance over existing methods. The approach appears to be a minor adaptation of established techniques rather than a substantial contribution to the field. The paper would benefit from a more rigorous comparison against relevant prior work on multi-sentence generation and a more thorough evaluation of the coherence model's impact on longer sequences.

I recommend rejection as the contribution does not meet NIPS's standards for novelty and significance. The paper presents an incremental extension of existing methods without sufficient justification for its novelty or the necessity of the proposed components. The claims of being "the first" to address this problem are unsubstantiated, and the evaluation does not convincingly demonstrate that the proposed method provides meaningful improvements over simpler alternatives.
