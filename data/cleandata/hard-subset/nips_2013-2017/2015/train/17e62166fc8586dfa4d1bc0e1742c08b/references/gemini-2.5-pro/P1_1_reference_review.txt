### Paper Summary

This paper addresses the novel and challenging problem of generating a coherent sequence of natural language sentences from a stream of images. This extends the well-studied task of single-image captioning to a multi-image, multi-sentence setting, motivated by real-world scenarios like describing a trip from a photo album. The authors propose a novel architecture, the Coherent Recurrent Convolutional Network (CRCN), which is a retrieval-based system. For a given input image stream, CRCN first identifies candidate sentences for each image from a training database. It then selects the best sequence of sentences by optimizing a score that considers three factors: (1) visual-semantic matching between individual images and sentences, modeled by a CNN; (2) the flow of content between sentences, modeled by a Bidirectional RNN; and (3) the linguistic coherence across sentences, modeled explicitly using an entity-based local coherence model. The model is trained on a large-scale dataset created by crawling and processing blog posts, treating them as naturally aligned image-text sequences. The authors demonstrate through extensive experiments, including automatic metrics and human studies, that their method significantly outperforms adapted state-of-the-art single-image captioning baselines.

### Strengths

1.  **Novel and Significant Problem Formulation:** The paper is among the first to formally tackle the "image stream to story" problem. This is a logical and important next step for the vision-and-language community, moving beyond single, isolated images towards understanding visual narratives. The motivation is clear and compelling, with direct real-world applications.

2.  **Sound and Well-Motivated Technical Approach:** The proposed CRCN architecture is a thoughtful combination of powerful techniques. Integrating a CNN for image features and a BRNN for temporal text modeling is standard, but the key novelty lies in explicitly incorporating an entity-based local coherence model [1]. This is a very sensible addition, as simply concatenating the best captions for individual images often leads to disjointed and unnatural paragraphs. Explicitly modeling entity transitions is a proven technique in NLP for ensuring text fluency and coherence, and its application here is both novel and effective.

3.  **Thorough and Convincing Experimental Evaluation:** The evaluation is comprehensive and provides strong support for the paper's claims.
    *   **Dataset:** The use of large-scale, in-the-wild blog data is a practical and effective way to obtain training data for this complex task.
    *   **Baselines:** The authors compare against several strong, relevant baselines from the single-image captioning literature, adapting them for the sequence generation task.
    *   **Ablation Studies:** The comparison between the full CRCN model and its variants (1NN, RCN) clearly demonstrates the incremental benefits of the BRNN and, crucially, the coherence model.
    *   **Human Evaluation:** The inclusion of an AMT user study is essential for a generation task where automatic metrics can be misleading. The study design, which specifically tests longer sequences to highlight the importance of coherence, is particularly insightful and strengthens the conclusions.

4.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is well-written, clearly structured, and easy to follow. The methodology is explained in detail, and Figure 2 provides an excellent visual summary of the data processing and model architecture.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Retrieval-based vs. Generative:** The method is fundamentally a retrieval-based system that composes paragraphs from sentences already present in the training data. This limits its expressiveness; it cannot generate truly novel sentences to describe unique scenes or unseen object combinations. While effective, this is an important distinction from true "generation" models (e.g., [9, 30]) that compose sentences from a vocabulary. The title "Expressing an Image Stream" might be slightly misaligned with the retrieval-based nature of the work.

2.  **Dependence on a Complex and Heuristic Data Processing Pipeline:** The quality of the training data is critical, yet its creation relies on a multi-stage pipeline with several heuristics (paragraph tokenization, distance-based image-text alignment, LSA-based summarization). The performance of the final model is heavily contingent on the success of these pre-processing steps. It would be beneficial to include some analysis or discussion on the potential for error propagation from this pipeline (e.g., how accurate is the image-to-text-segment association?).

3.  **Inaccurate Claim of being "Unsupervised":** The introduction states, "our approach is completely unsupervised". This is incorrect. While the *data collection* from blogs is unsupervised, the CRCN model itself is trained in a *supervised* fashion using a max-margin loss on aligned image-stream/sentence-sequence pairs. This should be rephrased for clarity and accuracy.

4.  **Inference Complexity:** The inference procedure for finding the best sentence sequence is non-trivial. It involves a K-NN search followed by an approximate divide-and-conquer beam search to manage the exponential (K^N) search space. While pragmatic, this process is computationally intensive and provides no guarantee of finding the optimal sequence. A brief discussion on the computational cost and the trade-offs of the approximation strategy would be welcome.

### Conclusion and Justification

Overall, this is a strong paper that introduces a novel problem and presents a well-designed, effective solution. The core idea of integrating an explicit coherence model to generate fluent textual narratives for image sequences is a significant contribution. The thorough experimental validation, including strong quantitative results and a well-designed user study, convincingly demonstrates the superiority of the proposed approach over sensible baselines.

While the method has limitations—being retrieval-based and relying on a heuristic-driven data pipeline—these do not undermine the core contribution. The weaknesses are more about the scope and potential future directions rather than fundamental flaws in the presented work. The paper is well-executed, clearly presented, and addresses a problem of high interest and significance to the NIPS community. I recommend acceptance.
