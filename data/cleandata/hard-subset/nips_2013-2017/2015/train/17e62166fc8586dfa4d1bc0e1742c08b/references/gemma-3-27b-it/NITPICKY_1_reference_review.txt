## Review of "Expressing an Image Stream with a Sequence of Natural Sentences"

This paper tackles the problem of generating a sequence of natural language sentences to describe a stream of images, extending the standard image captioning task to a temporal dimension. The authors propose a Coherent Recurrent Convolutional Network (CRCN) integrating CNNs, BRNNs, and a local coherence model. They evaluate their approach on blog datasets of NYC and Disneyland, demonstrating superior performance compared to several baselines using both quantitative metrics and user studies.

While the problem addressed is a logical and potentially valuable extension of existing work, and the proposed architecture incorporates several reasonable components, the paper suffers from a multitude of issues, ranging from imprecise language and insufficient detail to methodological concerns and superficial evaluation. These flaws, cumulatively, lead me to recommend rejection.

**Strengths:**

*   **Problem Formulation:** Extending single-image captioning to image streams is a natural progression and addresses a realistic scenario.
*   **Architecture Integration:** The combination of CNNs, RNNs, and a coherence model is conceptually sound and attempts to address key challenges of the task.
*   **Dataset Utilization:** Leveraging blog data as a weakly-supervised source for training is a resourceful idea.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The inclusion of both quantitative metrics (BLEU, Recall) and user studies (AMT) demonstrates an attempt at thorough evaluation.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Lack of Precision & Detail:** The paper is riddled with imprecise language. For example, "a sequence of informative text" is vague. The description of the data augmentation process is confusing; why select top-κ *ranked* sentences for training *all* sentences in the sequence? The statement "we separate the input activation into forward and backward ones with different sets of parameters" is not well-justified.
*   **Insufficient Methodological Detail:** Critical implementation details are missing. What are the exact dimensions of the fully connected layers in Section 3.3? How is the dropout applied? What is the learning rate schedule used for RMSprop? The description of the coherence model is hand-wavy. "We enumerate the transitions of the grammatical roles of entities" is not specific enough. What constitutes a ‘transition’?
*   **Coherence Model Concerns:** The local coherence model relies on parse trees, which are notoriously noisy and may introduce errors. The limited history parameter (3) seems arbitrary and potentially insufficient to capture long-range dependencies. The claim that the coherence model addresses discontinuity in image streams seems overstated given the local nature of its design.
*   **Baseline Comparison:** The choice of baselines is not optimal. While the mentioned approaches are relevant, the comparisons lack nuance. For example, simply “extending” baselines doesn't clarify *how* they were extended to handle sequences. Were the RNNs modified to handle variable-length inputs of sentences? This needs clarification.
*   **Evaluation Limitations:** The AMT experiments are not thoroughly described. How many AMT workers were used? What were the quality control measures? The claims about the effect of image stream length on coherence model performance (last column of Table 2) feel speculative without further analysis. The evaluation relies heavily on BLEU, which is known to be a flawed metric for evaluating natural language generation, especially regarding fluency and coherence.
*   **Redundancy & Repetition:** The paper contains considerable redundancy. The contribution list repeats information already presented in the introduction. The discussions of related work are repetitive across sections.
*   **Formatting & Grammar:** Numerous minor grammatical errors and inconsistencies detract from readability (e.g., inconsistent use of “e.g.” vs. “i.e.”, awkward phrasing).

**Pro & Con Acceptance:**

**Pro:**

*   Addresses a relevant and challenging problem.
*   Presents a conceptually interesting architecture.
*   Includes both quantitative and qualitative evaluation.

**Con:**

*   Lack of methodological detail and precision.
*   Insufficiently justified design choices.
*   Weak baseline comparisons.
*   Limited evaluation scope and reliance on flawed metrics.
*   Numerous minor errors and redundancies.

**Overall:**

The paper presents an interesting idea but falls short of NIPS standards due to its numerous flaws. While the authors attempt to address a significant problem, the lack of rigor in methodology, presentation, and evaluation weakens the contribution substantially. The paper requires significant revisions to address the aforementioned concerns before it can be considered for acceptance. The combination of superficiality and a large number of minor issues creates a negative overall impression. I strongly recommend rejection in its current form.
