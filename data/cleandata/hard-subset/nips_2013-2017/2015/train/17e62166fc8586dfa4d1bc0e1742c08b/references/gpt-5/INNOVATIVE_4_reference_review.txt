Summary and relation to prior work
The paper tackles the underexplored problem of describing an image stream with a sequence of sentences. The proposed coherent recurrent convolutional network (CRCN) integrates: (i) CNN image features (VGG fc7), (ii) a bidirectional RNN over sentence-level paragraph vectors to model content flow, and (iii) an entity-grid based local coherence model (Barzilay & Lapata) to encourage fluent sentence-to-sentence transitions. Training uses a max-margin ranking objective between paired image and sentence sequences constructed from large collections of travel blogs; test-time prediction retrieves a sentence sequence by selecting candidate sentences per image (nearest-neighbor on CNN features) and ranking sequences with the learned compatibility.

This work goes beyond single-image captioning and multi-modal embeddings [Karpathy & Fei-Fei; Kiros et al.; Vinyals et al.] by explicitly modeling sequence-level coherence across sentences and tackling image streams rather than single images. It differs from video captioning (e.g., LRCN) in that image streams can have abrupt content changes, making coherence modeling more critical, and from Kim et al. (CVPR’14/15) which retrieve image sequences given text, by solving the reverse problem and integrating a coherence model within the scoring function.

Strengths
- Novel problem formulation: image-stream to sentence-sequence “storytelling,” with an explicit coherence component. This is a meaningful and ambitious step beyond single-image captioning/retrieval.
- Methodological novelty in the integration: combining BRNN sentence-flow features with an entity-grid coherence signal and image features in a unified ranking objective. To my knowledge, the explicit entity-based coherence model has not been coupled with multimodal neural models for this task.
- Pragmatic data strategy: constructing a large-scale, weakly supervised parallel corpus from blogs, with reasonable segmentation/summarization heuristics and doc2vec sentence representations.
- Empirical evidence: improvements over strong baselines (CNN+LSTM, multimodal LBL variants) on two sizable domains, and user studies (AMT) that especially support gains on longer sequences.
- Scalable inference: approximate divide-and-conquer plus beam search over combinatorial sentence-sequence candidates is sensible and appears effective.

Weaknesses
- Retrieval rather than generation: The approach assembles existing sentences, which limits novelty/diversity and domain transfer. This is fine for some applications but should be more clearly positioned versus generative models; also evaluate potential redundancy or copying artifacts.
- “End-to-end” claim: It appears the CNN is fixed (no fine-tuning) and the pipeline includes external components (LSA summarization, parsing, doc2vec). Please clarify what is learned jointly; “end-to-end” seems overstated.
- Dataset construction/ground truth: Alignment relies on simple proximity heuristics and LSA summarization; test ground-truth is derived via the same summarizer. This risks reinforcing summarizer biases and may not reflect human-authored multi-sentence ground truth. More analysis of alignment noise and its impact would strengthen the paper.
- Baselines and fairness: Single-image captioning models concatenated across positions are disadvantaged for coherence. Consider stronger sequence-level baselines: (a) post-hoc re-ranking of concatenated captions with an entity grid; (b) an LSTM language model over candidate sentences (without entity grid); (c) a simple Markov coherence model; (d) a learned CRF/HMM over sentence candidates.
- Metric mismatch: BLEU/METEOR/CIDEr are not ideal for multi-sentence coherence; while you note this, add sequence-level metrics (e.g., entity transition distributions, sentence order classification) or human ratings specifically on coherence, redundancy, and global consistency.
- Limited ablations/analysis: More granular ablations would help: effect of κ in augmentation; doc2vec vs word-level LSTM encoders; with/without backward RNN; impact of coherence history length; sensitivity to K and beam width M; effect of CNN fine-tuning.
- Generalization: Results are on two tourism domains. It would help to show cross-domain robustness or at least a discussion of domain shift (e.g., training on Disneyland, testing on NYC).
- Reproducibility: Important details are missing/unclear (e.g., whether CNN is fine-tuned, training epochs, runtime, parser/coref specifics, whether data/code will be released).

Quality
Technically sound overall; the ranking objective and architecture are reasonable, and the approximate search is well-motivated. Claims are mostly supported by quantitative and user studies, though the lack of sequence-level baselines and the dataset construction concerns temper conclusions.

Clarity
Generally clear and well-organized. Figures help. Please clarify “end-to-end,” detail training setup (fine-tuning, epochs, optimizer settings per module), and describe the AMT protocol and significance testing. A brief error analysis would be valuable.

Originality
Good. The problem framing and the explicit integration of entity-based coherence with multimodal neural scoring are novel and timely. Prior related work is appropriately cited.

Significance
Moderate to high. The paper opens a direction—story-like description of image streams—with a concrete model that improves human preference. This is likely to stimulate follow-up work, especially on coherence-aware multimodal generation.

Questions for authors
- Is the CNN fine-tuned on your blog data? If not, why not, and how does fine-tuning affect results?
- What fraction of text-image alignments appear incorrect under your heuristics? Any robustness analysis to alignment noise?
- Can you report inter-annotator agreement and statistical significance for AMT studies?
- How does CRCN perform if the entity grid is replaced by a neural coref/coherence module, or if coreference errors are injected?
- Any analysis on redundancy or contradictions in the retrieved sequences?
- Will you release the datasets, code, and preprocessing scripts?

Pros for acceptance
- Clear, novel problem and bold integration of coherence modeling with multimodal ranking
- Strong human preference gains, especially on longer sequences
- Scalable, practical framework with large weakly supervised data

Cons against acceptance
- Retrieval-only; limited generative capability and domain flexibility
- Evaluation/baseline gaps for sequence-level coherence
- Overstated “end-to-end” claim and potential dataset construction biases

Recommendation
Accept (weak accept). The conceptual step toward coherent multi-sentence description of image streams and the integration of entity-based coherence outweigh the methodological imperfections. With clearer positioning and stronger sequence-level baselines/analysis, this could be a solid contribution.

Confidence
Medium.
