Summary and relation to prior work
The paper tackles a timely extension of image captioning: given a stream of images, retrieve a coherent sequence of natural sentences. The proposed coherent recurrent convolutional network (CRCN) combines: (i) CNN (VGG) features for images, (ii) a bidirectional RNN over sentence embeddings (doc2vec) to model content flow, and (iii) an entity-grid local coherence model (Barzilay & Lapata) to encourage fluent sentence-to-sentence transitions. Training uses max-margin ranking over paired image-stream/sentence-sequence examples harvested from blogs. Blogs are preprocessed via paragraph-based text segmentation and LSA summarization; the top-κ summary sentences per image segment serve as training candidates. At test time, for each image position, the method retrieves K nearest neighbor images to form a candidate set of sentences and uses an approximate beam/divide-and-conquer search guided by the CRCN score to select a final sequence. Experiments on two blog corpora (NYC, Disneyland; ~20K posts, 140K images) report improvements over baselines adapted from single-image captioning/ranking (Kiros et al., Karpathy & Fei-Fei, Vinyals et al.) using BLEU/CIDEr/METEOR, retrieval metrics, and AMT user studies. The work is related to retrieval-based captioning (e.g., Socher et al., Karpathy & Fei-Fei), multimodal RNNs/LRCN (Donahue et al.), and video description (Rohrbach et al.), but differs in integrating an explicit coherence model and operating on image streams. It is also complementary to Kim et al. (CVPR’15) on image-sequence retrieval from text, by addressing the reverse mapping and by modeling coherence.

Strengths
- Problem formulation is interesting and relevant: moving from single images to multi-sentence descriptions for image streams.
- Architecture is a thoughtful combination of components: BRNN over sentence embeddings to capture bidirectional context plus an explicit entity-grid coherence signal.
- Use of weakly aligned blog data is pragmatic; the pipeline for segmentation/summarization is clearly described.
- Extensive experiments including ablations (1NN, BRNN-only vs. CRCN) and AMT user studies; the claim that coherence helps more for longer sequences is supported.
- Implementation details (optimizer, dropout, sampling strategy) are reasonably documented.

Weaknesses
- Methodology is largely retrieval-based and heavily heuristic. Despite “end-to-end” phrasing, the system depends on multiple external components (LSA summarization, doc2vec, parsing/coref) and does not fine-tune the CNN. The “unsupervised” claim is overstated given reliance on heuristic image–sentence alignments.
- The coherence integration is not fully convincing. The entity-grid yields a global 64-d vector padded and transformed to g, which is then matched to each image via a sum of g·v_t across positions. This makes coherence influence a per-image linear match independent of sentence position, and the modeling intuition and ablation beyond “RCN vs. CRCN” are limited. More principled sequence-level conditioning or explicit entity tracking within the RNN would be stronger.
- Candidate generation constrains each position to K nearest training images, limiting expressivity and potentially biasing evaluation in favor of retrieval methods. No analysis of sensitivity to K/M or search errors is provided.
- Evaluation choices raise concerns:
  - Paragraph-level BLEU/CIDEr/METEOR are known to correlate weakly with human judgments for multi-sentence text; no coherence-focused automatic metric (e.g., entity-grid coherence scores) is reported.
  - Baseline fairness: single-image generative models are asked to produce sentences independently and concatenated; they are disadvantaged relative to a method explicitly optimizing sequence-level compatibility. A stronger sequence-level baseline (e.g., sequence scoring without coherence; CRF/HMM over candidate sentences; hierarchical RNN trained to generate paragraphs) is missing.
  - Retrieval metrics are not clearly defined for this setup; it is unclear what constitutes “ground truth” in candidate pools formed from KNN per position when train/test are disjoint. Please clarify the exact retrieval protocol and whether the true sequence is ever in the candidate set.
  - Statistical significance and inter-annotator agreement for AMT are not reported.
- Limited analysis and generalization: no cross-domain transfer (train on one domain, test on the other), limited error analysis, and no discussion of failure modes. Dataset/code release is not specified, impacting reproducibility.

Quality
The system is competently engineered and ablations suggest each component helps, but the theoretical justification for the coherence-image interaction is weak, and the evaluation leaves open questions about fairness and metric suitability. Claims of “end-to-end” and “unsupervised” should be tempered.

Clarity
Generally well written and organized. However, the scoring function merits clearer intuition, and the retrieval evaluation protocol needs unambiguous specification. Please also clarify whether CNN/doc2vec are fixed or fine-tuned, and provide more details on parsing/coref quality.

Originality
Moderate. The problem (image stream → sentence sequence) and using entity-grid coherence in a multimodal setting are novel in this context, but the core components (CNN features, BRNN over sentence vectors, max-margin ranking, KNN candidate retrieval) are standard.

Significance
Moderate. The problem is important and under-explored, but the retrieval/heuristic nature, limits in evaluation rigor, and reliance on domain-specific blogs reduce potential impact. A more principled, generative, and broadly validated approach would increase significance.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide a stronger and fairer set of baselines: sequence-level scoring without coherence; CRF/HMM over candidate sentences; hierarchical RNN that conditions on previous sentences and images.
- Clarify and strengthen evaluation: define retrieval protocols precisely; report statistical tests and agreement; include coherence-specific automatic metrics; add error analyses and sensitivity to K, M, κ.
- Analyze the coherence term: ablate entity resolution, history length, random/shuffled grids; report the standalone coherence model’s ability to distinguish coherent vs. scrambled sequences.
- Explore cross-domain generalization and longer sequences; report transfer from NYC→Disneyland and vice versa.
- Temper “unsupervised/end-to-end” claims; discuss limitations of blog-based alignments and parsing noise; consider fine-tuning CNN and learning sentence encoders jointly.
- Consider more integrated entity-aware sequence models (e.g., entity-tracking within RNN) to replace the post-hoc dot-product with g.

Decision: Reject (borderline)
While the problem is compelling and the results are promising, concerns about methodological novelty, evaluation rigor, and the coherence modeling’s integration with vision prevent recommendation for NeurIPS at this time.
