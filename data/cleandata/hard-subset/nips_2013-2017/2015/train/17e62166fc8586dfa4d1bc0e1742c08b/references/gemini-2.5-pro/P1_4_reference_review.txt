### Summary of the Paper

This paper addresses the novel problem of generating a sequence of natural language sentences to describe a stream of images. The authors argue that this is a natural extension of the well-studied single image-to-sentence generation task, motivated by real-world scenarios like describing a vacation photo album. To tackle this, they propose a new architecture called Coherent Recurrent Convolutional Networks (CRCN). This model integrates three main components: a Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) to extract visual features from each image, a Bidirectional Recurrent Neural Network (BRNN) to model the contextual flow of the associated sentences, and a classic entity-based local coherence model to ensure smooth and logical transitions between sentences.

A key contribution is their method for creating a parallel corpus. They cleverly leverage online blog posts, which naturally contain sequences of images accompanied by narrative text. The paper details a pre-processing pipeline to segment blog text, associate it with images, and summarize text blocks into key sentences. The model is trained using a max-margin objective to rank correctly aligned image-sentence sequences higher than misaligned ones. At test time, the task is framed as a retrieval problem: for a query image stream, candidate sentence sequences are generated from the training data and ranked using the learned CRCN model. The authors conduct extensive experiments on two large blog datasets (NYC and Disneyland), demonstrating that their approach outperforms several adapted state-of-the-art baselines on both automated metrics and human evaluations.

### Strengths

1.  **Novel and Significant Problem Formulation:** The paper tackles a new and important problem. Moving from single image captioning to describing image streams is a significant and logical step towards more holistic scene understanding and storytelling. The motivation is well-grounded in how people capture and share experiences.

2.  **Sound and Well-motivated Architecture:** The proposed CRCN model is thoughtfully designed. The combination of a CNN for vision, a BRNN for contextual language modeling, and a separate coherence model directly addresses the core challenges of the task: grounding text in images, maintaining semantic flow, and ensuring grammatical/referential fluency. The integration of a classic NLP coherence model [1] with modern deep learning components is a notable and effective idea.

3.  **Creative Data Collection and Processing:** The use of blog posts as a source for parallel data is a clever and practical solution to the data scarcity problem for this new task. While the pre-processing has its own challenges, it provides a scalable way to build large, "in-the-wild" datasets.

4.  **Thorough Experimental Evaluation:** The evaluation is comprehensive. The authors compare their method against several relevant and strong baselines adapted from the single-image captioning literature. The inclusion of ablation studies (CRCN vs. RCN vs. 1NN) clearly demonstrates the value of both the BRNN component and, crucially, the coherence model. The combination of quantitative metrics (BLEU, R@K, etc.) and a user study via Amazon Mechanical Turk provides a convincing case for the model's effectiveness, especially the finding that the coherence model's benefit increases with sequence length.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Retrieval-based, not Generative:** The approach is fundamentally retrieval-based. It selects and re-assembles existing sentences from the training corpus rather than generating truly novel sentences. This limits the expressive power and diversity of the output, as it cannot produce descriptions with vocabulary or syntactic structures not seen during training. This is a significant limitation compared to modern generative models and should be more explicitly discussed.

2.  **Complexity and Scalability of Inference:** The inference process requires searching through an exponentially large space of candidate sequences (K^N). While the authors propose a practical divide-and-conquer beam search, the scalability of this approach for very long sequences (large N) or very large databases (requiring a large K for good coverage) is a concern that is not addressed.

3.  **Dependence on Pre-processing Quality:** The entire model's performance relies heavily on the initial blog pre-processing pipeline. The text segmentation is heuristic, and the LSA-based summarization [4] is a relatively dated technique. Errors or suboptimal summaries produced in this stage will propagate through the system, creating a ceiling on the achievable performance. A discussion of the potential noise introduced by this pipeline would strengthen the paper.

4.  **Clarity of Model Combination:** Section 3.3, which describes the combination of the different model components, is quite dense. The role of the two fully-connected layers and the specific formulation in Equation 2 could be explained more clearly. For instance, it's not immediately obvious how the outputs `S` and `g` map to the score function terms `s_kt` and `g_k` in Equation 3 without careful inspection. A more detailed walkthrough of the data flow at this stage would improve clarity.

### Arguments for/against acceptance

**Arguments for acceptance:**
*   Addresses a novel, well-motivated, and significant problem in vision and language.
*   The proposed CRCN model is a sensible and effective combination of modern deep learning and classic NLP techniques.
*   The paper is supported by a strong and thorough experimental evaluation on large, real-world datasets, including compelling user studies.
*   The work represents a solid step forward and opens up a new direction for research in multimodal AI.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
*   The method's novelty lies in the combination of existing components and application to a new problem, rather than a fundamental algorithmic innovation.
*   The retrieval-based nature of the sentence generation is a strong limitation that could be discussed more prominently.
*   The inference procedure has potential scalability issues that are not fully explored.

### Conclusion

Overall, this is a strong paper that introduces a new and interesting problem and provides a solid baseline solution. The strengths—particularly the novelty of the task, the well-designed model, and the comprehensive evaluation—outweigh the weaknesses. The paper is well-written, the experiments are convincing, and the work is likely to inspire follow-up research in this area. It is a valuable contribution to the field.
