- Paper proposes CRCN: retrieve multi-sentence descriptions for image streams using VGG features, a BRNN over doc2vec sentence vectors, and an entity-grid coherence descriptor.  
- Training uses a structured max-margin objective; decoding is KNN-based retrieval with approximate beam search.

- Strength: Tackles the important and underexplored problem of multi-sentence description for image streams using weakly aligned blog data.  
- Strength: Integrates a discourse entity-grid coherence signal with context-sensitive sentence embeddings, which is an interesting architectural contribution.  
- Strength: Retrieval framing avoids brittle word-level generation and leverages naturally written sentences.  
- Strength: Empirical evaluation uses a reasonably large dataset, automatic metrics, AMT human judgments, and some ablations.

- Major concern: Scoring function adds the same coherence-image dot product per time step, conflating sequence length with score and lacking normalization.  
- Major concern: Coherence descriptor is text-only yet dotted with per-image features, making its semantic role and repeated alignment unclear.  
- Major concern: Loss compares variable-length sequences without specifying how differing lengths are handled (padding/min/max), a critical missing detail.  
- Major concern: BRNN uses vanilla ReLU units instead of gated recurrent units (LSTM/GRU), risking vanishing gradients and weaker long-range modeling.  
- Major concern: Candidate search/beam search is under-specified—objective decomposition and boundary handling between subproblems are not described, hurting reproducibility.  
- Major concern: Paragraph-to-image alignment is noisy (index distance + LSA) with no quantitative analysis or comparison to stronger alignment methods.

- Reproducibility issues: Missing details on CNN fine-tuning, handling variable lengths, doc2vec tokenization/limits, optimization/early stopping, and entity extraction/coreference settings.  
- Evaluation ambiguity: Retrieval metrics (R@K, median rank) and the definition of ground truth among candidates are unclear given disjoint train/test splits.

- Originality: The fusion of entity-grid coherence with neural sequence scoring for multimodal retrieval is novel, but many other components are standard.  
- Significance: Task is relevant, but gains from the coherence model are small on language metrics and unclear on retrieval metrics; generality beyond blog domains is uncertain.

- Suggested fix: Normalize or otherwise revise the scoring function to avoid length bias and reconsider coherence as transition/pairwise factors rather than repeated per-image dot-products.  
- Suggested fix: Specify and ablate handling of variable-length sequences and report length-normalized scores.  
- Suggested fix: Replace or compare BRNN with LSTM/GRU and report the impact.  
- Suggested fix: Provide full details of the beam search decomposition and candidate assembly to ensure reproducibility.  
- Suggested fix: Improve and quantify alignment (compare heuristic to learned alignment), and evaluate alignment noise impact.  
- Suggested fix: Clarify retrieval evaluation protocol, report statistical significance, inter-annotator agreement, and include error analyses and qualitative examples.  
- Suggested fix: Release dataset and code to aid reproducibility and impact.

- Pros summary: Addresses an underexplored multimodal sequence task, interestingly integrates discourse coherence, and uses sensible retrieval and human evaluation.  
- Cons summary: Ambiguous/possibly flawed scoring and training, under-specified search and evaluation, limited metric gains, and unclear isolation of the coherence contribution.

- Recommendation: Borderline reject pending clearer methodology, corrected scoring/length handling, stronger ablations, and improved evaluation and reproducibility.
