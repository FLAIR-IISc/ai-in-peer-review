

# Review of "Expressing an Image Stream with a Sequence of Natural Sentences"

## Summary
This paper proposes a novel approach for generating natural language descriptions for sequences of images (image streams), extending the traditional image captioning task from single images to sequences. The authors introduce "Coherent Recurrent Convolutional Networks" (CRCN), which integrates convolutional neural networks for image representation, bidirectional recurrent networks for sentence modeling, and a local coherence model to ensure smooth transitions between sentences. The authors leverage blog posts as a source of image-text parallel data, where each blog post contains a sequence of images and associated text. They evaluate their approach on NYC and Disneyland blog datasets with over 20K posts and 140K images, demonstrating improvements over single-sentence generation baselines using both quantitative metrics and human evaluations.

## Strengths
The paper makes a meaningful extension to the image captioning literature by addressing the problem of generating sentence sequences for image streams, which is a natural progression from existing work. The motivation is well-justified through real-world examples (e.g., tourists documenting NYC visits with multiple photos). The CRCN architecture is well-designed, integrating three key components (image representation, sentence modeling, and coherence) in a unified end-to-end framework. The use of blog posts as a data source for training is clever and well-motivated, as blog authors typically create semantically meaningful image-text sequences. The evaluation is comprehensive, employing both standard NLP metrics (BLEU, CIDEr, METEOR) and human evaluations via AMT, with results clearly showing improvements over strong baselines. The paper also provides a thorough comparison of different components (e.g., with and without the coherence model), demonstrating the value of each contribution.

## Weaknesses
While the paper claims to be the first to address this problem, it would strengthen the contribution by more thoroughly reviewing related work on video captioning (e.g., [23, 32] cited in the paper) to better distinguish the novelty of handling image streams (which may have abrupt visual changes) versus video (which has temporal continuity). The data collection methodology from blog posts lacks sufficient detail about how they ensure proper text-image alignment beyond the heuristics described in Section 2.1. The claim that the approach is "completely unsupervised" is misleading since they rely on blog posts with manually curated image-text pairs, which represents a form of weak supervision. The coherence model based on entity transitions (Section 3.2) is interesting but not sufficiently validated for domains beyond tourism; the paper lacks discussion of how well this generalizes to other types of image streams. The paper also doesn't address the computational complexity of handling very long image sequences, which could be a practical limitation for real-world applications.

## Recommendation
I recommend acceptance of this paper with minor revisions. The work represents a meaningful extension to the image captioning literature with a well-motivated problem, sound methodology, and strong experimental validation. The contributions are significant and well-justified.

## Constructive Suggestions for Improvement
1. Clarify the "unsupervised" claim by acknowledging the weak supervision inherent in using blog posts as training data, and explicitly discuss the limitations of this approach.

2. Provide more details about the blog data collection process, particularly how they verify text-image alignment for each blog post (e.g., how they handle cases where text doesn't correspond to images).

3. Expand the discussion of the coherence model's limitations and generalizability beyond tourism. Consider adding experiments on a different domain (e.g., food blogs, sports events) to better demonstrate the approach's versatility.

4. Include a brief discussion of computational complexity, particularly for long image streams, to help readers understand practical limitations.

5. Strengthen the related work section by more thoroughly comparing with video captioning approaches (e.g., [23, 32]) to better highlight the novelty of handling image streams with potential abrupt visual changes.

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of multimodal learning. With the suggested revisions, it would be an excellent addition to NIPS.
