Summary and relation to prior work
This paper tackles the under-explored problem of describing an image stream with a sequence of natural sentences. The proposed coherent recurrent convolutional network (CRCN) combines: (i) CNN image features (VGG fc7), (ii) a bidirectional RNN over sentence representations (Doc2Vec) to model content flow across sentences, and (iii) an entity-grid based local coherence model (Barzilay & Lapata) to encourage fluent sentence-to-sentence transitions. Training data are automatically constructed from travel blogs by aligning images to nearby paragraph segments and summarizing each segment to a key sentence (with top-κ LSA summaries for augmentation). The model is trained with a max-margin ranking loss over sequence-level image–text compatibility, and inference retrieves sentence sequences from a candidate pool formed by KNN images and a beam-style divide-and-conquer search. Evaluation on NYC and Disneyland blog corpora uses BLEU/CIDEr/METEOR, retrieval metrics, and AMT user studies, showing CRCN > baselines including LBL variants, CNN+RNN, CNN+LSTM, and simple global matching. The work situates among image captioning [Karpathy & Fei-Fei, Vinyals et al.], video description [Donahue et al.], sequence retrieval from paragraphs [Kim et al.], and entity-based coherence modeling [Barzilay & Lapata]. The explicit integration of a coherence model into a multimodal sequence-level embedding is novel for image streams.

Strengths
- Problem: Moves beyond single-image/single-sentence to sequence-to-sequence for photo streams, which is timely and practically relevant.
- Architecture: Simple and interpretable combination of sentence-sequence representation (BRNN over Doc2Vec) with an entity-grid coherence signal; end-to-end training of the multimodal scoring (except external parsers).
- Data: Scalable, weakly supervised construction of image–sentence sequence pairs from blogs; reasonably large corpora.
- Evaluation: Multiple metrics and user studies; ablation (RCN vs CRCN) suggests coherence matters more for longer sequences.
- Clarity: Overall readable; components and training procedure are described at a level that an informed reader can reproduce most of it.

Weaknesses and technical concerns
- Scoring/formulation: Equation (3) is problematic. The sequence-level coherence vector gk is dot-multiplied with every image vector vlt and summed over t, effectively acting as a repeated bias term: Skl = Σt (skt + gk)·vlt. This ties a global textual coherence descriptor to each image independently, which is hard to justify mathematically (coherence is a property of the sentence sequence; its interaction with every individual image feature equally is questionable). A more principled form would relate gk to an aggregate of the image sequence (e.g., gk·(Σt vlt)/N) or to sentence–sentence transitions independent of images.
- Variable-length handling: The loss samples negative sequences “with random length,” but the paper does not specify how Skl is computed when lengths differ (alignment, truncation, padding, or normalization by length). This is a critical missing detail that affects both training stability and metric comparability.
- Text modeling choices: Using fixed Doc2Vec sentence embeddings and a vanilla BRNN (ReLU, ungated) is weaker than contemporary word-level LSTMs/GRUs for capturing discourse phenomena and long-range dependencies. The justification that word-level models are hard to train due to vanishing gradients is unconvincing given the LSTM baseline and established practice in 2014–2015.
- Coherence modeling: The entity-grid features depend on accurate parsing and coreference in noisy blog text; no analysis is provided on parser/coreference errors or how they affect performance. The 64-d transition vector is zero-padded to 300 dimensions (a hack) and then linearly projected to image space. The claimed “unified end-to-end” training does not backpropagate through parsing/coreference, so the integration is limited to the final projection layers.
- Retrieval pipeline and search: The candidate set is constrained by KNN images per position; errors in visual retrieval or misaligned blog images can irrevocably exclude good sentences. The approximate divide-and-conquer/beam approach is plausible, but there is no analysis of beam sensitivity (K, M) nor comparison to exact search on small N to support the “almost optimal” claim.
- Dataset construction and evaluation: Alignment via distance heuristics and LSA summarization is likely noisy; there is no human validation of alignment quality or summarization faithfulness. Automatic metrics (BLEU/CIDEr/METEOR) are ill-suited for paragraphs and for retrieval-from-training setups; retrieval metrics against a single “ground-truth” summary per post may be misleading given paraphrastic variability. AMT studies are pairwise preferences but lack inter-annotator agreement and significance testing details. Generalization is claimed but only tourism blogs are evaluated.
- Reproducibility: Critical choices (Doc2Vec training, whether embeddings are fine-tuned, length normalization, negative sampling details, gradient clipping, beam/search parameters, κ sensitivity) are not fully specified; datasets/code availability is not stated.

Questions for the authors
1. How do you handle length mismatches in Skl during training and evaluation? Is there normalization by N or alignment/truncation?
2. Why apply the global coherence vector gk to every image vector vlt? Did you try aggregating image features (e.g., mean-pooled) or modeling sentence–sentence transition potentials independent of images?
3. Are Doc2Vec embeddings fixed or fine-tuned during CRCN training? What is the impact?
4. Can you report sensitivity to K (nearest neighbors), M (beam size), κ (augmentation), and entity-grid history length?
5. Any human validation of the image–text alignment and summarization quality on a subset?
6. Do you release the NYC dataset and preprocessing scripts?

Suggestions for improvement
- Revisit the scoring function to separate global coherence from per-image compatibility (e.g., hierarchical model with sentence-image compatibilities plus a sentence-level transition term; or CRF with transition potentials; or gk·mean(vlt)).
- Strengthen the text encoder (e.g., BiLSTM over words with attention) and consider learning sentence embeddings jointly.
- Provide ablations and sensitivity analyses for K, M, κ, and entity-grid parameters; include exact-search comparison for small N.
- Add human alignment quality checks and targeted coherence evaluations (e.g., sentence ordering tests, entity transition plausibility).
- Report statistical significance for AMT and automatic metrics; include inter-annotator agreement.
- Clarify all training details (normalization, clipping, Doc2Vec training, fine-tuning) and consider releasing code/data.

Assessment
- Originality: Moderate. The problem setting (photo stream to multi-sentence description) and explicit incorporation of an NLP coherence model are interesting. The technical integration, however, is a relatively straightforward combination of known components with a debatable scoring design.
- Quality: Mixed. The approach is plausible and empirically promising, but key technical details are missing, some modeling choices are weak, and evaluation has limitations.
- Clarity: Generally clear, but important ambiguities remain (score definition, length handling, training specifics).
- Significance: Potentially impactful for visual storytelling; current form is constrained by retrieval and dataset-specific preprocessing; broader adoption would benefit from stronger modeling and evaluation.

Arguments for acceptance
- Timely, novel problem formulation for image streams.
- Simple, interpretable architecture that improves over baselines and highlights the value of coherence.
- Scalable weakly supervised dataset construction; promising user study results.

Arguments against acceptance
- Questionable scoring formulation and unclear handling of variable lengths.
- Limited modeling strength on the text side; shallow integration of coherence.
- Evaluation and dataset pipeline lack validation and ablation; reproducibility concerns.

Recommendation: Weak reject. The paper addresses an important problem with an interesting angle (entity-based coherence), but the current modeling/exposition gaps and evaluation limitations undermine confidence. With clarifications and a revised scoring/model, this could become a strong contribution.
