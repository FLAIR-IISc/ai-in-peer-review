Summary
This paper tackles the underexplored problem of describing an image stream with a sequence of sentences. The proposed coherent recurrent convolutional network (CRCN) combines: (i) CNN image features (VGG fc7); (ii) a BRNN over sentence-level embeddings (doc2vec) to capture context-sensitive sentence representations across the sequence; and (iii) an entity-grid based local coherence model (Barzilay & Lapata) to encourage fluent sentence-to-sentence transitions. The model is trained with a max-margin retrieval objective on automatically constructed image–sentence sequence pairs mined from travel blogs (NYC and Disneyland). At test time, the method retrieves per-image candidate sentences from the K nearest training images, then uses a divide-and-conquer beam search to select the best sequence under the CRCN score. Experiments report improvements over several single-image captioning baselines (LBL/MLBL, CNN+RNN/LSTM) on BLEU/CIDEr/METEOR and retrieval metrics, plus AMT user preferences.

Strengths
- Interesting and timely problem formulation: moving beyond single-image/single-sentence to streams and multi-sentence outputs.
- Use of entity-grid coherence modeling for multi-sentence fluency is novel in the vision–language setting.
- A coherent architecture tying a context-aware textual BRNN with a coherence signal and image features; the per-sentence text vectors are conditioned on both past and future context.
- Practical training data strategy using blogs; large-scale datasets assembled for two domains; inclusion of human studies.
- Ablations (1NN vs RCN vs CRCN) support the utility of the BRNN and coherence components.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Retrieval vs generation mismatch: The method retrieves and stitches sentences rather than generating novel text. Many baselines compared are generative captioners adapted by concatenation, which may put them at a disadvantage on n-gram metrics and stylistic match to blog text. A retrieval-based baseline with a coherence-aware reranker (without neural BRNN) would be a fairer comparator.
- Scoring function design: The final score sums s_t · v_t (context-aware sentence embedding aligned to the corresponding image) plus g · v_t for every t, where g is a single vector derived from the entire sequence’s coherence features. This implies the “coherence term” is text-only and interacts with the image stream only via a repeated dot product with each image feature (equivalently g · sum_t v_t). This does not directly model sentence-to-sentence visual transitions, and it is unclear why alignment of a text coherence vector with the sum of CNN features should reflect multi-sentence fluency grounded in the images. This design choice deserves stronger justification or a more principled formulation (e.g., pairwise/triadic compatibility potentials over adjacent (image, sentence) pairs).
- Evaluation protocol clarity: The paper states train/test splits are disjoint and ground-truth sentences in test do not appear in training. Then the retrieval metrics (R@K, median rank) are described as “groundtruth retrieval given top K candidates,” which seems ill-defined when the true test paragraph is not in the candidate pool. Please clarify exactly what constitutes “ground truth” for retrieval metrics and how those metrics are computed for generative baselines that produce a single output.
- Dataset construction noise: Segmenting blogs to image–text pairs via simple distance heuristics and summarizing with LSA introduces substantial noise. It is unclear how robust the model is to these errors or how sensitive results are to κ (top-3 summaries). No inter-annotator/quality checks are reported, and no analysis of alignment errors is provided.
- Limited generalization claims: The approach is evaluated only on two tourism domains. Doc2vec models are trained separately per domain, and images are embedded with VGG without fine-tuning. The claim of domain-agnostic, unsupervised applicability would be stronger with cross-domain tests or at least a third, distinct domain.
- Missing/bare details: Training choices (learning rate schedule, epochs, early stopping), whether the CNN is fine-tuned, sentence/sequence length statistics, and runtime/complexity of the beam search are not reported. The padding of the 64-d coherence vector to 300 by zeros is ad hoc; better to learn a proper projection.

Quality
Technically, the main components are standard and sound individually (doc2vec, BRNN, entity grid), but their integration in the scoring function is questionable, especially the role of the coherence vector g in Eq. 3. The divide-and-conquer beam search is reasonable given the combinatorics, but there is no analysis of optimality gaps or sensitivity to K and M. Experimental results appear positive, but the evaluation protocol for retrieval metrics is unclear, and the baseline suite lacks a strong retrieval+coherence reranker. The user study is helpful, but details on agreement and statistical significance are missing.

Clarity
The paper is generally readable and well-organized. However:
- Clarify the retrieval metrics computation and candidate pools for each method.
- Provide training details (optimizer hyperparameters, epochs), whether CNN is frozen, and complexity/runtime of test-time search.
- Explain the rationale for the g · v_t term; consider a schematic to illustrate what this term is intended to capture.

Originality
The specific combination of a sentence-level BRNN with an entity-grid coherence model for image-stream description is novel. However, the approach is largely a combination of known components, and the output is retrieval-based rather than true generation. Related work is mostly well covered, though comparison to alternative coherence-aware reranking frameworks (e.g., structured ILP/CRF over sentence selections) would contextualize the contribution better.

Significance
The problem is important and underexplored, and the results indicate potential. If the approach were strengthened with a more principled grounding of coherence to the visual stream and clearer evaluation protocol, it could influence subsequent multi-sentence captioning work. As is, the significance is moderate.

Suggestions for improvement
- Redesign the scoring to explicitly model adjacent transitions, e.g., add pairwise terms coupling (s_t, v_t, s_{t+1}, v_{t+1}) or a CRF over the sentence sequence with coherence potentials.
- Add a retrieval baseline with: per-image sentence retrieval, plus an entity-grid coherence reranker over concatenations, and another with only the BRNN-based context-aware text embedding, to isolate the value of neural vs symbolic coherence.
- Clarify and, if needed, revise the retrieval metrics; report statistical significance and inter-annotator agreement in AMT.
- Provide ablations on K, beam width M, κ, and sequence length; report runtime.
- Consider fine-tuning the CNN and learning a proper projection for the 64-d coherence features.

Recommendation
Borderline reject. The paper addresses an interesting problem and proposes a promising architecture, with encouraging results. However, concerns about the coherence–vision coupling, evaluation clarity, and baseline fairness limit confidence in the current claims. With a more principled scoring formulation and clearer evaluation, this work could be much stronger.
