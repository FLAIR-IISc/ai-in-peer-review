Summary
The paper tackles the underexplored problem of generating a coherent sequence of natural sentences for a sequence of images (photo streams). The proposed coherent recurrent convolutional network (CRCN) combines: (i) CNN image features (VGG fc7), (ii) a bidirectional RNN over paragraph vectors (doc2vec) to model content flow across sentences, and (iii) an entity-grid local coherence model to encourage fluent sentence-to-sentence transitions. The system is retrieval-based: for each query image, sentences associated with visually nearest training images are combined into sequence candidates, and CRCN scores sequence–stream compatibility via a max-margin objective that aligns ordered sentence and image streams and incorporates the coherence signal. Training data are mined automatically from travel blogs (NYC, Disneyland): blog text is segmented to align with images by proximity, summarized per image with LSA, and augmented with top-κ (κ=3) sentences. Evaluation uses BLEU/CIDEr/METEOR, retrieval metrics (R@K, median rank), and AMT user studies. CRCN outperforms several single-image captioning/retrieval baselines and its own ablations, with larger perceived gains on longer sequences.

Relation to prior work
The work is positioned between image captioning and video description. It extends image-sentence embedding/ranking approaches (e.g., Kiros et al.; Karpathy & Fei-Fei) to sequence-to-sequence alignment and integrates a classical discourse coherence model (entity grids). It is conceptually related to video-to-text (e.g., Donahue et al., Rohrbach et al.), but differs by handling unstructured photo streams where abrupt visual changes make coherence modeling more salient. The direction is opposite to Kim et al. (retrieving image sequences for paragraphs). The combination of a BRNN for sequence-level content modeling with an entity-grid-based coherence term is the main methodological novelty.

Quality
- Soundness of methodology: The overall architecture and training objective are reasonable extensions of multimodal alignment to sequences. Using entity grids is a principled way to inject discourse signals.
- Some technical choices need clarification/justification:
  - Score definition (Eq. 3): Skl = sum_t s_k,t ⋅ v_l,t + g_k ⋅ v_l,t. The second term ties a single global coherence vector g_k to each image vector and sums across t. This design is non-standard for modeling coherence (which concerns sentence-to-sentence transitions) and its interaction with images is unclear. Why should g_k, independent of t, align in image feature space? A more direct pairwise coherence potential over sentence transitions (and length-normalized scoring) seems warranted.
  - Length effects: Since scores sum over t, comparing sequences of different length may bias toward longer streams. Is length normalized or are all sequences fixed length during training/evaluation?
  - Data alignment quality: Image–text alignment via nearest text block and LSA summarization is noisy. The impact of this noise on training is not quantified. An analysis of alignment error and robustness would strengthen the claims.
  - Negative sampling: 100 negatives per positive may be small for sequence retrieval; sensitivity analysis would help.
  - Entity-grid integration: The 64-D transition histogram zero-padded to 300-D and then mapped to 4096-D seems ad hoc. Are gradients backpropagated through this path? (The grid is non-differentiable; so this is effectively a learned linear map from fixed coherence features.)
- Evaluation: The comparisons to single-image generative captioners are informative but arguably unfair in this retrieval setting and domain (blogs). Retrieval-based baselines (e.g., a sentence-level Markov/LM coherence baseline, or sequence-level CRF/HMM over retrieved sentences) would be more appropriate alternatives. Metrics are described, but exact computation of retrieval metrics for sequence-level ground truth (given disjoint train/test) is unclear—please specify what constitutes a “ground-truth retrieval” and whether lexical overlap is required.
- Results: Reported qualitative and AMT results support the value of coherence, especially for longer sequences. Quantitative tables are summarized in text; full numbers and statistical significance on AMT would help.

Clarity
The paper is generally understandable and well organized. Some notation is overloaded (e.g., Tn used for both a single sentence and κ augmented sentences), and a few modeling choices are insufficiently motivated (see above). Figures are referenced but (presumably) needed to follow architecture details. Reproducibility would benefit from release of the blog datasets and preprocessing scripts, and from more specifics: doc2vec training corpus and split policy (to avoid leakage), parser settings, sequence length distributions, and candidate search complexity/runtime.

Originality
- Problem formulation (image-sequence to sentence-sequence with explicit coherence) is timely and distinct from single-image captioning and video description.
- Methodologically, CRCN is a novel combination of known building blocks (CNN+BRNN alignment plus entity-grid coherence). The integration and end-to-end training of these components for sequence retrieval are original relative to prior work cited.

Significance
The task is impactful (visual storytelling), and the approach shows consistent gains, especially in human preference. Mining weakly supervised blog data is practical and potentially generalizable. That said, the approach is retrieval-based and depends heavily on domain-specific corpora and preprocessing heuristics; broader significance would be strengthened by cross-domain tests and comparisons to stronger sequence retrieval baselines.

Pros
- Addresses an important and underexplored task (image streams → coherent multi-sentence descriptions).
- Principled inclusion of discourse coherence (entity grids) into a multimodal sequence alignment model.
- Large-scale weak supervision from blogs; practical pipeline.
- Consistent improvements over baselines; human studies show coherence helps more with longer sequences.
- Clear ablations (CRCN vs RCN vs 1NN) isolating contributions.

Cons
- Coherence-image interaction term is unintuitive; unclear whether it truly models coherence conditioned on visuals.
- Potential length bias in scoring; unclear normalization.
- Noisy data alignment and summarization; limited analysis of noise robustness.
- Baselines skewed toward single-image generative models; missing sequence-retrieval baselines with simple coherence (e.g., n-gram/LM, CRF over sentence candidates).
- Reproducibility concerns (dataset release, preprocessing details); evaluation protocol for retrieval metrics under-specified.

Suggestions and questions for authors
- Clarify Eq. 3 design and provide an ablation where the coherence term influences only sentence transitions (e.g., pairwise potentials) or operates as a reranker independent of image features.
- Address sequence length normalization in training and scoring.
- Add stronger retrieval baselines: (a) KNN with bigram/trigram LM over sentences; (b) CRF/HMM over sentence candidates with transition features (entity-grid or lexical cohesion).
- Provide robustness analysis to alignment/summarization noise and κ.
- Specify doc2vec training (train/val/test separation), and report candidate search runtime and scalability.
- Consider reporting an automatic coherence metric (entity-grid discrimination) on outputs.

Overall
A solid, well-motivated paper with a novel, coherent integration and promising results on an important task. Some modeling choices and evaluations need clarification and stronger baselines. If addressed, this would be a valuable contribution. I lean toward acceptance.
