### Summary and Relation to Prior Work

This paper addresses the novel task of generating a sequence of natural language sentences to describe a stream of images. This is presented as a logical and important extension to the widely studied problem of single-image-to-single-sentence generation, which has seen significant interest at NIPS and other top conferences in recent years (e.g., Karpathy et al., 2015; Vinyals et al., 2015). The authors motivate the problem by observing that users often capture events as a sequence of photos, and a coherent narrative is more descriptive than a set of disjoint captions.

To tackle this, the paper proposes a new architecture, the Coherent Recurrent Convolutional Network (CRCN). This model integrates three main components: (1) a standard CNN (VGGNet) for image feature extraction, (2) a Bidirectional RNN to model the semantic flow of the text sequence using paragraph vectors, and (3) an entity-based local coherence model, based on the classic work of Barzilay & Lapata (2008), to ensure smooth syntactic and referential transitions between sentences. The overall approach is framed as a retrieval task: for a query image stream, candidate sentence sequences are constructed from the training data, and the CRCN is used to score and rank these candidates to find the best match. A key contribution is the resourceful use of blog posts as a source of weakly-aligned image stream and text data, which is crucial for training a model for this new task. The method is evaluated extensively on two new blog datasets, outperforming several state-of-the-art single-image captioning baselines that have been adapted for this sequence-to-sequence task.

### Strengths

1.  **Novel and Significant Problem Formulation:** The paper successfully defines and motivates a new, challenging, and practically relevant problem. Moving from single image/sentence pairs to streams/paragraphs is a significant step towards a more holistic understanding of visual narratives. This work could inspire a new line of research in this direction.
2.  **Thoughtful Model Architecture:** The proposed CRCN model is a well-designed combination of powerful components. The key novelty lies in explicitly incorporating a classic NLP coherence model into a modern deep learning framework for vision-and-language. This directly addresses the core challenge of the problem—generating a fluent and coherent paragraph, rather than just a collection of sentences. The ablation study in Table 1 effectively demonstrates the value of both the BRNN (RCN) and the coherence model (CRCN).
3.  **Pragmatic Data Sourcing and Pre-processing:** Acquiring a large-scale dataset for this new task is a major challenge. The authors' idea of leveraging blog posts is clever and effective. The described pre-processing pipeline (segmentation, summarization) provides a reasonable method for creating a parallel corpus from this "in-the-wild" data source.
4.  **Thorough and Convincing Evaluation:** The experimental evaluation is a major strength. The authors compare against multiple strong and relevant baselines, including several recent deep learning models. The use of a comprehensive set of metrics, covering both language similarity (BLEU, CIDEr) and retrieval accuracy (R@K, median rank), provides a multi-faceted view of performance. Crucially, the quantitative results are supported by a well-designed Amazon Mechanical Turk user study, which confirms that humans also prefer the output of the proposed model.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Retrieval-based vs. Generative:** The method is fundamentally a retrieval-based system at the sentence level. This means it can only rearrange sentences that exist in the training corpus and cannot generate truly novel descriptions tailored to the nuances of a new image stream. While this is a valid approach, it limits the expressive power compared to generative models. The paper compares against generative baselines (e.g., CNN+LSTM) but should more explicitly discuss this limitation in the main text.
2.  **Inference Scalability and Approximation:** The inference process requires searching through an exponentially large space of candidate sequences (`K^N`). The paper proposes a divide-and-conquer strategy with beam search to make this tractable. However, the details of this approximation are brief. A more thorough analysis of its computational cost and how close its solutions are to the optimal would strengthen the paper. It is unclear how well this would scale to very long image streams (e.g., `N > 10`).
3.  **Loose Coupling of Coherence Model:** The entity-based coherence model produces a single 64-dimensional feature vector for the entire sequence. This vector is then concatenated with the BRNN outputs before the final fully-connected layers. This is a rather loose coupling. The coherence score acts more as a global re-ranking feature for the entire paragraph rather than dynamically influencing the sentence selection process at each step. This might limit its effectiveness compared to a more tightly integrated model.
4.  **Potential for Noise in Data Pipeline:** The data pre-processing pipeline relies on heuristics for text segmentation (associating text with the nearest image) and summarization (LSA-based). This process could introduce significant noise, such as misalignment between images and their assigned sentences. While the results suggest the model is robust to this, the paper lacks an analysis of the quality of the processed training data or the potential impact of this noise.

### Suggestions for Improvement

*   **Error Analysis:** The paper would benefit from a qualitative error analysis. Showing some failure cases would provide deeper insight into the model's limitations. For example, does it fail due to poor image feature matching, incoherent sentence transitions that the model misses, or artifacts from the data pre-processing?
*   **Explore Hybrid Models:** For future work, a hybrid retrieval-and-generation approach could be very promising. For instance, the model could retrieve a template sentence or paragraph structure and then use a generative model to fill in or modify the details based on the specific visual content.
*   **Tighter Integration of Coherence:** The authors could explore alternative ways to integrate the coherence model more deeply. For example, could a coherence score be computed incrementally as the sequence is built, influencing the selection of the next sentence in a beam search decoding framework?
*   **Clarification on "Unsupervised":** The introduction claims the approach is "completely unsupervised". This is slightly misleading. While the data is sourced from blogs without manual annotation, the model itself is trained in a supervised manner on the processed (image stream, sentence stream) pairs, using a max-margin objective. It would be more accurate to describe the data collection as "weakly supervised".

### Final Recommendation

This is a strong paper that introduces a novel and important problem, proposes a sensible and effective model, and supports its claims with a thorough and convincing set of experiments. The strengths—particularly the novelty of the task and the thoughtful integration of a coherence model—outweigh the weaknesses. While the retrieval-based nature of the method is a limitation, this work represents a solid first step on a challenging problem and is likely to inspire follow-up research. I recommend acceptance.
