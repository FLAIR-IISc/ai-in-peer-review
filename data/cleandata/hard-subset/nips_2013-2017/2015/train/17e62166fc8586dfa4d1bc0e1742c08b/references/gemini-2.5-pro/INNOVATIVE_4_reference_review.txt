**Summary:**
This paper tackles the novel problem of generating a sequence of natural language sentences to describe a stream of images. Moving beyond the well-studied task of single-image captioning, the authors propose that a coherent narrative is a more natural description for a sequence of photos, such as those from a trip or event. They introduce a new model, the Coherent Recurrent Convolutional Network (CRCN), which synthesizes three distinct components: a CNN for image feature extraction, a bidirectional RNN to model the flow of content across sentences, and a classic entity-based local coherence model to ensure smooth, logical transitions between sentences. The approach is framed as a retrieval task: for a query image stream, the model scores and ranks candidate sentence sequences drawn from a large training corpus. To facilitate this, the authors curate a new, large-scale dataset from blog posts. The method is evaluated against adapted state-of-the-art single-image captioning techniques, demonstrating superior performance on both automatic metrics and human evaluations.

**Strengths:**
The primary strength of this paper is its originality and ambition. It defines and provides a strong baseline for a new, compelling, and logical next step in visual-language research. While much of the community focuses on incremental improvements in single-image captioning, this work takes a bold step towards a more complex and practical problem: storytelling. This is precisely the kind of forward-looking research that NIPS should encourage.

The proposed CRCN architecture is another major strength. The decision to combine modern deep networks (CNN, BRNN) with a classic, symbolic NLP technique (the entity-based coherence model) is a novel and thoughtful choice. The BRNN captures semantic flow, but the authors correctly identify that this may not be sufficient to ensure grammatical and referential coherence, especially when visual content changes abruptly between images. Explicitly modeling discourse structure via entity grids is a creative solution that proves highly effective, as validated by the human studies which show its benefits become more pronounced for longer sequences.

Further contributions include the creation of a large-scale, "in-the-wild" dataset from blog posts, which is a valuable resource in its own right for this new task. The experimental evaluation is thorough, comparing against multiple strong baselines and employing both quantitative metrics and human studies via AMT. The results are convincing and clearly demonstrate the superiority of the proposed approach.

**Weaknesses:**
The work is not without its limitations, though in my view, they are acceptable for a paper breaking new ground.

1.  **Retrieval-based Formulation:** The system retrieves and re-ranks existing sentences rather than generating novel ones from a vocabulary. This limits its expressive power to the content of the training corpus. While a fully generative model for paragraphs is a much harder problem (due to challenges like maintaining long-term consistency), it represents the ultimate goal. However, the authors' choice is a pragmatic and effective simplification that allows them to focus on the core challenges of sequence-to-sequence matching and coherence.

2.  **Model Integration:** The fusion of the entity-grid coherence model with the neural components is somewhat direct. The 64-dimensional coherence feature vector is simply zero-padded and projected through the same fully connected layers as the BRNN outputs. While empirically effective, this integration feels slightly ad-hoc. A more deeply integrated, end-to-end differentiable coherence mechanism could be a powerful direction for future work, but the current approach serves as an excellent proof-of-concept.

3.  **Inference Approximation:** The use of a divide-and-conquer beam search to manage the exponential search space at test time is an approximation. The paper's justification that local coherence is a prerequisite for global coherence is intuitive, but the method lacks formal guarantees. Nevertheless, this is a practical necessity for the proposed framework.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   **High Originality and Significance:** The paper introduces a novel and important problem setting that moves the field beyond single-image captioning towards narrative generation. It is likely to inspire significant follow-up research.
*   **Novel Architecture:** The CRCN's synthesis of deep learning models with a classic NLP coherence model is a bold and effective architectural choice.
*   **Strong Empirical Validation:** The paper provides compelling quantitative and qualitative results, including human studies, that convincingly demonstrate the value of the proposed model, especially its coherence component.
*   **New Dataset:** The creation of a large-scale dataset for this new task is a valuable contribution to the community.

**Arguments for Rejection:**
*   The approach is not fully generative, which could be seen as a limitation compared to some state-of-the-art captioning models.
*   The integration of the coherence model is not deeply embedded in an end-to-end fashion.

**Conclusion:**
This paper presents a bold and novel approach to a significant new problem. While one could point to the retrieval-based framework or the ad-hoc integration of the coherence model as methodological imperfections, these are minor compared to the overall contribution. The authors have clearly defined a new research direction, proposed a creative and effective initial solution, and validated it thoroughly. This work prioritizes a significant new idea over perfecting an old one, and its potential impact is high. I strongly recommend acceptance.
