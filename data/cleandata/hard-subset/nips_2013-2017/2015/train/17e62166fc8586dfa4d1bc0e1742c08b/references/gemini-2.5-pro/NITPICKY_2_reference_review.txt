**Summary**
This paper addresses the novel problem of generating a coherent sequence of sentences to describe a stream of images, extending the popular task of single-image captioning. The authors propose a multimodal architecture named Coherent Recurrent Convolutional Networks (CRCN), which integrates three main components: a CNN (VGGNet) for image feature extraction, a bidirectional RNN for modeling the textual content flow, and an entity-grid-based local coherence model to ensure smooth transitions between sentences. To train this model, the authors propose a clever, though heuristic, method for creating a parallel corpus from blog posts by segmenting and summarizing text associated with images. The model is trained using a max-margin loss to rank correct image-sentence sequence pairs higher than incorrect ones. At test time, a sentence sequence is generated by retrieving candidate sentences for each image from the training set and then using a divide-and-conquer beam search to find the most compatible sequence according to the learned model. Experiments on two blog post datasets (NYC and Disneyland) show that the proposed method outperforms several adapted state-of-the-art single-image captioning baselines on both automatic metrics and human evaluations.

**Quality**
The paper is technically ambitious, but several aspects of its quality are questionable upon close inspection.

*   **Methodological Soundness:** The data creation pipeline is a significant source of concern. The text segmentation, based on "minimum index distance" between images and text blocks, is a crude heuristic that could easily lead to incorrect image-text pairings. The use of LSA for summarization is a dated choice; more modern techniques could yield higher quality sentences. The model's scoring function in Equation (3) is particularly problematic. It computes a global coherence score `g_k` for the text sequence and then adds its dot product with *each* individual image feature `v_lt` to the final score. This is conceptually flawed; a global property of the text sequence should not be matched against each local image representation. This design choice is not justified and undermines the model's theoretical grounding. Furthermore, the claim that the approximate search at inference "achieves almost optimal solutions" is unsubstantiated and supported only by a weak, intuitive argument.

*   **Clarity and Presentation:** The paper suffers from a lack of polish, which detracts from its overall quality.
    *   The writing contains colloquialisms (e.g., "hike of interest") and awkward phrasing.
    *   There are numerous formatting inconsistencies. For instance, the citation list in the introduction `[5, 8, 9, 12, 14, 15, 26, 21, 30]` is unsorted, which signals a lack of careful preparation.
    *   The mathematical notation is imprecise. Equation (1) has inconsistent subscripting (`h f t−1` instead of `h_{t-1}^f`). The authors admit to a "slight abuse of notation" regarding augmented sentences, which could have been easily avoided with clearer symbols. Equation (2) uses unprofessional ellipses (`..`).
    *   The explanation of the model's final layers is ambiguous. The statement that `Wf2Wf1` does not reduce to a single linear map "thanks to dropout" is a common misunderstanding; the non-linearity is due to an activation function, which is not explicitly shown or discussed in this part of the architecture.

*   **Experimental Rigor:** The experimental setup weakens the paper's claims. The baselines are essentially strawmen; single-image captioning models are adapted by simply concatenating their outputs. This approach inherently lacks any mechanism for sentence-to-sentence coherence, making the comparison unfairly tilted in favor of the proposed sequence-aware model. A stronger baseline would involve post-processing the concatenated outputs with a separate sequence model.

**Originality**
The problem formulation—generating sentence sequences for image streams—is novel and timely. The idea of explicitly modeling coherence is also well-motivated. However, the solution is a combination of existing and, in some cases, dated techniques. The CNN and BRNN components are standard, and the local coherence model is from a 2008 paper by Barzilay and Lapata. While combining existing components to solve a new problem is a valid contribution, the integration here feels somewhat ad-hoc, particularly in the design of the final scoring function. The most original part is arguably the use of blogs for data creation, but the method itself is highly heuristic.

**Significance**
The paper tackles an important and challenging problem that is a natural evolution of image captioning. Advancing from single images to streams is a significant step towards a more holistic understanding of visual narratives. The work could inspire future research in this direction. However, the methodological weaknesses and questionable experimental design limit the immediate impact of the results. Other researchers may be hesitant to build upon a model with a flawed scoring function or to trust results obtained against weak baselines. The reported performance gains from the coherence model (CRCN vs. RCN) are marginal on automatic metrics, which somewhat dampens the significance of that specific contribution.

**Pros:**
*   Addresses a novel and well-motivated problem: generating narrative descriptions for image streams.
*   The idea of incorporating an explicit coherence model is sound and addresses a key challenge of the task.
*   The approach outperforms baselines in both automatic and human evaluations.

**Cons:**
*   The paper suffers from numerous minor flaws in writing, formatting, and notation, indicating a lack of thoroughness.
*   The data processing pipeline relies on crude heuristics, casting doubt on the quality of the dataset.
*   The model's scoring function (Eq. 3) is conceptually flawed in how it combines the global coherence score with local image features.
*   The experimental comparison is weakened by the use of strawman baselines that are not designed for sequence-level phenomena.
*   The novelty is limited to a combination of existing components, with the coherence model being over a decade old.

**Recommendation:**
While the paper addresses an interesting and important problem, the execution is marred by a substantial number of flaws, ranging from minor presentational issues to a conceptually questionable model design and weak experimental comparisons. The accumulation of these issues undermines confidence in the work's technical soundness and the validity of its conclusions. Therefore, I recommend **rejection**. The authors have a promising research direction, but the paper requires significant revision to its methodology, experimental setup, and overall presentation to meet the standards of NIPS.
