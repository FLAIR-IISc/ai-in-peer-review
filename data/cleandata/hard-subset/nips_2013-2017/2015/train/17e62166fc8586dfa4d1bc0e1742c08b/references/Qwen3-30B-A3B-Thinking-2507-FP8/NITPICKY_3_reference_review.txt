

# Review of "Expressing an Image Stream with a Sequence of Natural Sentences"

This paper proposes a novel approach to generate sequences of natural language sentences for image streams, introducing a "coherent recurrent convolutional networks" (CRCN) architecture. The authors claim to be the first to address the problem of expressing image streams with sentence sequences, extending the typical image-to-sentence generation task to sequences of images and sequences of sentences. They leverage blog posts as training data, where each blog post contains a sequence of images with associated text segments.

**Major Weaknesses:**

1. **Inconsistent Dataset Description:** The abstract claims "20K blog posts with 140K associated images," but section 4.1 states NYC has 11,863 posts (78,467 images) and Disneyland has 7,717 posts (60,545 images), totaling 19,580 posts and 139,012 images. This discrepancy of ~420 posts and ~9,000 images is significant and undermines the paper's credibility.

2. **Ambiguous Methodology:** The local coherence model description in section 3.2 is contradictory: "Grammatical role are expressed by three categories and one for absent (i.e. not referenced in the sentence): S (subjects), O (objects), X (other than subject or object) and −(absent)." This implies four categories (S, O, X, -), but the phrasing suggests three categories plus an absent category, creating confusion about the model's design.

3. **Critical Error in BRNN Formulation:** Section 3.1 presents equations (1) that define "xft = f(Wfi pt + bi)" and "hft = f(xft + Wfhft-1 + bf)", but later states "f is the Rectified Linear Unit (ReLU)". The use of 'f' as both the activation function and a parameter (in Wfi) is confusing and technically incorrect. This is a fundamental error in the model description that would prevent replication.

4. **Lack of Justification for Key Design Choices:** The paper arbitrarily selects κ=3 for data augmentation without proper justification beyond "thorough empirical tests," which is insufficient for a scientific paper. Similarly, the choice of 3 for the history parameter in the coherence model lacks explanation.

5. **Misleading Claims:** The paper claims to be "completely unsupervised" but the training process requires careful extraction of image-text pairs from blog posts, which is not unsupervised. This misrepresentation of the methodology is significant.

6. **Inconsistent Terminology:** The paper uses "image stream" and "image sequence" interchangeably but never clearly defines these terms, creating confusion about the input format.

**Minor Weaknesses:**

1. The paper claims to be the first to address this problem, but it fails to adequately distinguish its approach from the work of Kim et al. (2011) which addresses "retrieval of image sequences for a query paragraph" - a closely related problem.

2. The cost function in equation (4) uses 'l' without defining it in the context of the summation, creating ambiguity in the mathematical formulation.

3. The paper references "Fig.1.(a)" in the text but the figure reference format is inconsistent with other figure references ("Fig.1", "Fig.2").

4. The paper claims "we demonstrate the superior performance" but the results in Table 1 show only marginal improvements (e.g., 0.5 BLEU points) over baselines, which is insufficient to justify the complexity of the proposed model.

**Strengths:**

1. The problem formulation is interesting and addresses a gap in the literature regarding sequential image description.

2. The use of blog posts as a source of natural image-text sequences is a reasonable approach to obtaining training data.

3. The coherence model is a plausible addition to the existing image captioning frameworks.

**Overall Assessment:**

While the problem addressed is interesting and the general approach has merit, the paper contains multiple critical methodological errors and inconsistencies that undermine the credibility of the work. The technical errors in the BRNN formulation (using 'f' ambiguously) and the contradictory description of the coherence model are particularly problematic as they would prevent replication. The inconsistent dataset description further damages the paper's credibility.

The marginal improvements over baselines (0.5 BLEU points) do not justify the complexity of the proposed model, especially given the unresolved technical issues. The paper fails to demonstrate that the coherence model provides a substantial improvement over simpler baselines, as evidenced by the minimal differences in Table 1.

The paper is not technically sound due to the fundamental errors in the model description, and the claims made are not fully supported by the evidence provided. The authors have not adequately addressed how their approach differs from previous work, particularly Kim et al. (2011) which addresses a closely related problem.

**Recommendation: Reject**

The paper presents an interesting idea but contains critical technical flaws that prevent it from being considered a solid scientific contribution. The inconsistencies in the dataset description, the ambiguous and contradictory model descriptions, and the fundamental error in the BRNN formulation are serious issues that would prevent replication and validation of the results. The marginal performance gains do not justify the complexity of the proposed approach given these technical shortcomings.
