This paper addresses the problem of generating a sequence of natural language sentences to describe a stream of images, a natural extension of single-image captioning. The authors propose a coherent recurrent convolutional network (CRCN) that combines CNNs for image representation, RNNs for sentence modeling, and a local coherence model to ensure fluency between sentences. They leverage blog posts as a weakly-supervised dataset for training and demonstrate improvements over baselines using both quantitative metrics and user studies.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The problem of describing an *image stream* with a *sequence of sentences* is a logical and valuable extension of existing work. This moves beyond isolated image-sentence pairs to a more realistic scenario.
* **Technical Soundness:** The proposed CRCN architecture appears well-motivated and reasonably designed. The integration of a coherence model is a key contribution, addressing a critical issue in sequential text generation. The use of existing, well-established components (CNNs, RNNs) provides a solid foundation.
* **Dataset Creation:**  Leveraging blog posts as a source of weakly-supervised data is a clever solution to the scarcity of parallel image-sentence stream data. The pre-processing steps (text segmentation, summarization, augmentation) are clearly described.
* **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The paper includes a thorough evaluation with both automatic metrics (BLEU, Top-K recall) and user studies (AMT).  The inclusion of both quantitative and qualitative results strengthens the claims. The ablation studies (comparing CRCN to its components) are valuable.
* **Clear Writing:**  The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the proposed approach.  Figures and equations are helpful.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Baseline Comparison:** While baselines are included, they are largely extensions of single-image captioning models. A more direct comparison to methods specifically designed for sequential data (e.g., video captioning approaches, hierarchical text generation models) would be beneficial.  The chosen baselines don't fully capture the complexity of the proposed task.
* **Coherence Model Detail:**  The description of the local coherence model is somewhat concise.  More detail on how the entity grid is constructed, the transition frequencies are calculated, and the 64-dimensional representation is used would be helpful. A visual illustration of the entity grid might also aid understanding.
* **Data Bias:** Blog posts, while readily available, are a specific type of data with potential biases (e.g., authors intentionally create narratives). The paper acknowledges this to some extent, but further discussion of how these biases might affect the results would be valuable.  Could the model perform differently on other types of image streams (e.g., security camera footage)?
* **Quantitative Metric Limitations:** The paper acknowledges the limitations of BLEU and other n-gram based metrics. Exploring more sophisticated metrics that capture semantic similarity and coherence (e.g., BERTScore, MoverScore) might provide a more nuanced evaluation.
* **Retrieval Details:** The approximate divide-and-conquer search strategy is briefly described. More detail regarding the parameters (e.g., beam size) and its impact on performance is warranted.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* Addresses a novel and important problem.
* Proposes a technically sound and well-motivated architecture.
* Demonstrates improvements over relevant baselines through comprehensive evaluation.
* Offers a practical approach to training with a readily available dataset.
* The work has the potential to impact areas like visual storytelling and image stream understanding.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

* The baseline comparison is not strong enough to fully demonstrate the superiority of the approach.
* Limited detail in the description of the coherence model.
* Potential data bias in the use of blog posts.
* Reliance on traditional evaluation metrics that might not fully capture the nuances of the task.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a good paper that addresses an interesting and challenging problem. The proposed CRCN architecture is a promising approach, and the experimental results are encouraging. However, the weaknesses regarding baseline comparison, coherence model detail, and data bias prevent a strong recommendation for acceptance in its current form. 

**Recommendation:**

**Reject with encouragement to revise.** The authors should strengthen the baseline comparison by including methods specifically designed for sequential data, provide more details on the coherence model, address potential data biases, and consider using more sophisticated evaluation metrics.  Addressing these points would significantly improve the quality and impact of the paper.
