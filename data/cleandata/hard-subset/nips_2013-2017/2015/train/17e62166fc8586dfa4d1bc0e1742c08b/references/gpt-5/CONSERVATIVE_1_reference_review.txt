Summary
The paper targets generating a sequence of natural sentences for an image stream (e.g., travel photos). The proposed CRCN combines: (i) CNN image features (VGG fc7), (ii) a bidirectional RNN over sentence representations (doc2vec) to model content flow, and (iii) an entity-grid-based local coherence model (Barzilay & Lapata) to encourage coherent sentence transitions. Training uses blog posts: images are aligned to text segments via paragraph heuristics and sentences are reduced via LSA summarization; a margin-based loss is optimized over ordered image–sentence sequences. At test time, for each query image t, K nearest training images are retrieved; candidate sentence sequences are assembled via a divide-and-conquer beam search and ranked by the CRCN score. Experiments on two crawled blog datasets (NYC and Disneyland) report improvements over baselines (LBL variants, CNN+RNN/LSTM, global matching) in BLEU/CIDEr/METEOR, retrieval metrics, and AMT user preference.

Relation to prior work
The problem extends single-image captioning (e.g., Karpathy & Fei-Fei; Vinyals et al.) and relates to video description (e.g., Donahue et al.) but focuses on image streams and paragraph-level output. The use of entity grid for coherence follows Barzilay & Lapata. The approach is largely retrieval-based (akin to Im2Text and image–sentence embedding work) but applied at sequence level with coherence.

Strengths
- Addresses a timely and underexplored task: sequence-level “storytelling” for image streams.
- Incorporates sentence-to-sentence coherence explicitly (entity grid) in addition to content modeling via BRNN.
- Uses sizable weakly aligned web data; proposes a practical pipeline (alignment, summarization, doc2vec, parsing).
- Evaluates with both automatic metrics and AMT; includes ablations (1NN, BRNN-only).

Weaknesses
- Evaluation data quality: The “ground truth” sentences are produced by LSA summarization of the same blogs used to train doc2vec and coherence models. This introduces circularity and noise, limiting the validity of BLEU/CIDEr/METEOR as proxies for human quality. There is no human-authored reference for the sequence task, and it is unclear whether the dataset will be released to allow reproducibility.
- Fairness of baselines: The proposed method is retrieval-based, while several baselines are generation models trained for a different objective. Concatenating per-image generated captions is not a strong sequence baseline. Missing stronger retrieval sequence baselines such as KNN + dynamic programming with bigram/LM smoothness, CRF/HMM over sentence candidates, or sequence-level embedding methods. The “GloMatch” baseline uses weaker image features, further skewing comparisons.
- Ambiguity in modeling and “end-to-end” claim: The entity-grid coherence is computed from parsed text (potentially with noisy coreference) and zero-padded, then combined with BRNN outputs through FC layers; this is not truly end-to-end learning across modalities. The score S sums a global coherence vector dot each image feature (g·v_t), which is ad hoc: a global text coherence descriptor is applied identically to every image position. No justification or ablation is provided for this coupling.
- Dataset construction and potential leakage: Image–text alignment relies on simple paragraph proximity; sentence selection via LSA; coreference resolution details are unclear. There is no analysis of duplicate or near-duplicate images across train/test streams, which is critical for retrieval-based systems using VGG fc7 nearest neighbors.
- Insufficient analysis: No sensitivity to K (nearest neighbors) and M (beam width), no ablation of BRNN directionality, doc2vec choice (vs averaging word2vec), κ augmentation, or coherence history size. The search procedure is described heuristically without complexity/runtime or approximation guarantees. No statistical significance tests for automatic metrics; AMT uses only 100 queries with 3 annotators per comparison and reports no confidence intervals.
- Missing details: Whether Stanford CoreNLP coreference is used (beyond parsing) and how entity mentions are extracted is not specified; the coherence model’s contribution is only indirectly assessed. Several references are incomplete, and some notation and architectural descriptions are imprecise.

Quality
The approach is technically plausible but largely a combination of established components with heuristic glue. The experimental methodology raises concerns (noisy ground truth, potential mismatched baselines, lack of significance and sensitivity analyses). Without numbers in the main text and stronger controls, the evidence for superiority remains limited.

Clarity
The paper is generally readable and organized. However, some critical components (entity extraction/coreference, candidate search, coupling of coherence and vision features) are under-specified. References need proper formatting.

Originality
Moderate. The task (image-stream to sentence sequence) is a natural extension, and the integration of BRNN with an entity-grid coherence signal is reasonably novel for this application. However, each component is standard, and the integration seems ad hoc rather than conceptually new.

Significance
Potentially useful direction for visual storytelling, but given the limitations of the dataset, evaluation, and baselines, it is hard to conclude that this advances the state of the art in a robust, generalizable way.

Questions for authors
- How do you ensure no near-duplicate images across train/test? Can you quantify overlap?
- Did you use coreference resolution or only parse trees to build the entity grid? How accurate are entity extractions on blog text?
- Why does the scoring term apply the same global coherence vector to each image (g·v_t)? Have you tried sequence-level normalization or aligning coherence with sentence transitions explicitly?
- Can you report sensitivity to K and M, and ablate BRNN directionality, κ, and doc2vec choice?
- Provide stronger retrieval baselines (e.g., KNN + Viterbi over per-position candidates with bigram LM) and report statistical significance.

Pros for acceptance
- Addresses an interesting, underexplored task.
- Explicit modeling of coherence beyond content.
- Uses large-scale web data; promising qualitative examples.
- Human evaluation suggests preference on longer sequences.

Cons for acceptance
- Weak and noisy evaluation protocol; fairness concerns with baselines.
- Limited novelty; components are standard and integration is ad hoc.
- Insufficient analysis and ablation; key design choices not justified.
- Clarity gaps and incomplete references.

Recommendation
Reject. The problem is interesting and the direction promising, but the current submission does not meet NIPS standards for rigor and evidence. Strengthening the dataset and evaluation, adding stronger baselines and ablations, and clarifying the modeling would substantially improve the work.

Confidence
High.
