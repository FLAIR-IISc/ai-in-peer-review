Summary
This paper tackles generating multi-sentence descriptions for image streams. The proposed coherent recurrent convolutional network (CRCN) combines: (i) CNN (VGG) image features, (ii) a bidirectional RNN over paragraph vectors of sentences to model content flow, and (iii) an entity-grid-based local coherence feature. Training uses a max-margin objective aligning ordered image and sentence sequences. At test time, for each image in a stream, K nearest training images provide sentence candidates; a beam-style divide-and-conquer search selects the best sequence under the learned compatibility. The authors construct two blog datasets (NYC, Disneyland) by segmenting and summarizing posts to create image–sentence streams, and report improvements over single-image captioning baselines (log-bilinear, CNN+RNN, CNN+LSTM) on BLEU/CIDEr/METEOR, retrieval metrics, and AMT pairwise preferences. Ablations (1NN, BRNN-only) suggest benefits from coherence modeling.

Relation to prior work
The work extends image captioning (e.g., Karpathy & Fei-Fei; Vinyals et al.) to sequences, and borrows local coherence modeling via entity grids (Barzilay & Lapata). It is related to video description (e.g., Donahue et al.; Rohrbach et al.) but focuses on still-image streams where visual discontinuities motivate explicit coherence. It also relates to sequence retrieval from text (Kim et al. 2014) but addresses the opposite mapping.

Strengths
- Addresses an under-explored, practically relevant task: multi-sentence description of image streams.
- Sensible modularization: retrieval-based sentence selection combined with sequence-level modeling and an entity-grid coherence feature.
- Uses relatively large, real-world data and evaluates on two domains.
- Includes some ablations and human evaluation indicating the importance of coherence, especially for longer sequences.

Weaknesses and points needing clarification
- Evaluation protocol for retrieval metrics is unclear and potentially flawed. The paper states training/test are disjoint and that test “groundtruth” sentences are not in the training pool; yet R@K/median rank typically require the groundtruth to be in the candidate set. What exactly is the pool over which ranks are computed? If the groundtruth sequence is not in the candidate set, R@K is ill-defined. Please specify whether you rank test texts among test texts (as in bi-directional retrieval) or something else, and ensure no leakage.
- Language metrics (BLEU/CIDEr/METEOR) are of limited validity here because the system retrieves sentences from a different corpus than the groundtruth summaries (paraphrases penalized). This further heightens the need for an unambiguous retrieval protocol and stronger human evaluation focused on coherence and relevance with significance testing.
- Heavy reliance on heuristic preprocessing (paragraph-to-image assignment by index distance; LSA summarization). There is no evaluation of segmentation/summarization quality or sensitivity analyses (e.g., different κ, alternative summarizers). The claim “κ=3 after thorough empirical tests” lacks evidence.
- Model design choices appear ad hoc and under-justified: zero-padding a 64-D entity-grid feature to 300-D; sharing FC mappings for O and q; a global g term added at every time step in the score (effectively repeating the same dot-product across t). No analysis of why this design is preferable to learning a projection/MLP on coherence features, or to a more principled structured model (e.g., CRF with pairwise sentence transitions).
- “End-to-end” characterization is misleading: CNN features, doc2vec, and parser outputs are fixed; no indication of CNN fine-tuning. The method depends on a coreference/parse pipeline whose performance on noisy blogs is unknown. Please report whether coreference is used and with what quality, given it is central to the entity grid.
- Baselines are not fully convincing for the sequence task. Comparing a retrieval-based sequence model to single-image caption generators (CNN+LSTM) that produce words is not apples-to-apples. Missing baselines include: (i) a simple structured re-ranker over per-position top-K sentences with bigram/trigram language model and pairwise adjacent-sentence coherence; (ii) adapting Kim et al. (2014) latent structural SVM to sentence selection; (iii) an HMM/CRF over candidate sentences with image compatibility as unary and coherence as pairwise; (iv) K>1 NN concatenation with LM smoothing. These would better isolate the contribution of the coherence model and sequence training.
- Search procedure (divide-and-conquer beam) lacks analysis: no complexity/runtime, no comparison to dynamic programming with local coherence, no ablation on beam size M or K. “Almost optimal” is asserted without evidence.
- Reproducibility: important training details are missing (learning rates, epochs, early stopping, gradient clipping, whether VGG is fine-tuned), and dataset release is not discussed. Given the nontrivial preprocessing, releasing code/data is essential.
- Clarity issues: Some notation is confusing (e.g., the score definition where g is applied per t), figures/tables are critical but not sufficiently described in text, and several hyperparameters are justified only anecdotally.

Quality
Moderate. The approach is plausible, but several design choices are unmotivated, and the evaluation has ambiguities that undermine the strength of the claims. Ablations are limited; missing stronger structured baselines and significance testing.

Clarity
Fair. The high-level idea is understandable, but important details of the evaluation protocol, preprocessing quality, and training are missing or vague. Some terminology (“end-to-end,” “unsupervised”) is used loosely.

Originality
Moderate. The task extension is interesting. The integration of an entity-grid coherence feature into a multimodal sequence retrieval model is a reasonable, if incremental, combination of known techniques.

Significance
Unclear due to evaluation concerns. If validated with stronger baselines and clearer protocols, the idea could be useful to practitioners working on photo album narration. As presented, the evidence does not convincingly establish state-of-the-art improvements.

Pros for acceptance
- Addresses a timely, practical problem (image streams).
- Incorporates discourse coherence into vision–language modeling.
- Uses large real-world data and includes some human evaluation.

Cons for acceptance
- Ambiguous/possibly invalid retrieval evaluation; language metrics ill-suited here.
- Heavy heuristic preprocessing without validation or sensitivity analysis.
- Missing stronger and fairer baselines; limited ablations.
- Ad hoc design choices with little justification; not really end-to-end.
- Reproducibility and clarity gaps.

Recommendation
Score: 4 (reject)
Confidence: 0.8

Suggestions for improvement
- Precisely define and fix the evaluation protocol; add significance tests. Provide a coherence-focused human study with longer sequences and clear instructions.
- Validate the preprocessing pipeline (segmentation/summarization) and report its impact.
- Add structured sequence baselines and ablations (K, M, κ, history length, learned projection for coherence).
- Clarify model details and training; consider fine-tuning CNN and learning a projection for the entity-grid feature.
- Release code and the processed dataset to support reproducibility.
