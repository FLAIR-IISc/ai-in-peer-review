Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes the task of describing an image stream (photo sequence) with a sequence of natural sentences, extending single-image/single-sentence captioning to multi-image/multi-sentence. The method, coherent recurrent convolutional networks (CRCN), is a retrieval-and-ranking system: for each image in the query stream, the method retrieves K candidate sentences from training data (nearest-neighbor images via VGGNet features). It then scores entire sentence sequences using (i) a BRNN over sentence representations (doc2vec) to model content flow, (ii) an entity-grid coherence model (Barzilay & Lapata) to encourage locally coherent sentence transitions, and (iii) image features (VGG) via dot products with the text-side features. Training uses a max-margin objective over aligned/misaligned image–sentence-sequence pairs constructed from blog posts that are automatically segmented (paragraph tokenizer + proximity heuristics) and summarized into one sentence per image (LSA). At test time, a divide-and-conquer beam-like search finds high-scoring sequences. Evaluation is on two large blog datasets (NYC, Disneyland) using BLEU/CIDEr/METEOR, retrieval metrics, and AMT user studies. Ablations show the value of BRNN and coherence components.

This builds on and contrasts with: retrieval-based captioning and multimodal embeddings (e.g., Kiros et al., Karpathy & Fei-Fei), mRNN/LSTM captioning, video-to-text (e.g., LRCN), and image-sequence retrieval from text (Kim et al.). The main novelty is combining a sentence-sequence model and an entity-based coherence model to rank multi-sentence outputs for image streams.

Quality
Strengths:
- Technically sound combination of known components in a new setup: CNN features for images, doc2vec + BRNN for sentence-sequence context, and entity-grid coherence, trained with a max-margin ranking loss. The scoring function is well-specified, and the training procedure (RMSProp, dropout) is standard.
- Sensible ablations (1NN vs BRNN-only vs BRNN+coherence) show incremental gains, especially in retrieval metrics and on longer sequences, consistent with the claim that coherence matters more for longer passages.
- Human evaluation corroborates quantitative trends and is designed as pairwise comparisons to reduce task complexity.

Weaknesses/concerns:
- Evaluation clarity: It is not fully clear how retrieval metrics (R@K, median rank) are computed when the system retrieves from training sentences but ground-truth sentences are from test posts. Are “ground-truth” sequences ever in the candidate set? If not, how are R@K and median rank defined? Similarly, for generative baselines (CNN+LSTM), how are retrieval metrics computed? This needs precise definition to ensure fair, interpretable comparisons.
- Potential unfairness across baselines: The proposed system is retrieval-based and benefits from selecting natural, human-authored sentences; the generative baselines produce novel sentences. BLEU/CIDEr/METEOR may be biased by lexical overlap patterns in the blog domain and by the LSA-based summaries used to define ground-truth. The paper tries to mitigate by keeping features consistent (VGG fc7), but more discussion on the tradeoffs between retrieval vs generation would be valuable.
- “Unsupervised” characterization is misleading: While no manual annotations are used, the model is trained with aligned image–sentence pairs produced by deterministic heuristics and LSA summarization. This is better described as weakly supervised/self-supervised using noisy alignments, not fully unsupervised.
- Model design questions: The global coherence vector g (4096-D) is added as g·v_t at every time step and summed over t, effectively g·(sum_t v_t). This design conflates a global text-side coherence descriptor with an aggregate of image features; justification and alternative formulations (e.g., using a single global image summary vector) would help.
- Dependence on noisy preprocessing: Accuracy of image-to-text segmentation and coreference parsing on informal blogs can be low. A brief error analysis or robustness study (varying segmenter/summarizer quality) would strengthen claims.
- Reproducibility: Important details (e.g., whether CNN is fine-tuned; doc2vec training settings beyond dimension; beam/divide-and-conquer parameters beyond K and M; dataset release) are insufficient for full replication. Clarifying these and releasing code/data would help.

Clarity
- Generally well-organized with an intuitive high-level picture and equations. However, some notational choices are confusing (e.g., reuse of T^l_n both as single and κ sentences; the padded 64-D coherence vector to 300-D; the shared FC mapping for O and q). A more explicit description of the final score (explaining the role of g across t) and a schematic of the search procedure would improve readability.
- The evaluation section should precisely define each metric in this setting and the candidate space used per metric and per baseline.

Originality
- The problem formulation (image stream → sentence sequence) is a timely and interesting extension of single-image captioning. The explicit use of entity-grid coherence within a multimodal ranking model for multi-sentence outputs appears novel and well-motivated, especially given sharper content changes across consecutive photos vs videos.
- Methodologically, it is a thoughtful integration of known components rather than a new learning architecture per se, but the combination and the task are sufficiently original.

Significance
- Practical relevance: photo album narration is useful and likely to spur follow-up work. The datasets are large and realistic, albeit noisy.
- Empirical gains are consistent and supported by user studies, suggesting the approach can improve multi-sentence fluency over strong single-sentence baselines when extended naively to sequences.
- The reliance on retrieval limits generalization to out-of-domain content, and the absence of comparisons to more recent paragraph-level generation (not widely available at the time) tempers impact. Still, the coherence modeling insight is valuable.

Pros
- Addresses an under-explored but important task; clear motivation.
- Integrates sentence-sequence modeling and entity-based coherence into multimodal ranking; ablations support contributions.
- Large-scale, weakly supervised training from blogs; human studies back up metrics.

Cons
- Ambiguities in evaluation protocol and potential apples-to-oranges baseline comparisons.
- Overstatement of “unsupervised”; reliance on noisy preprocessing not analyzed.
- Some design choices (global coherence interaction) and search procedure need more justification and analysis; limited reproducibility details.

Questions for authors
- Precisely define R@K/median rank in your setting and how they are computed for generative vs retrieval baselines.
- Do you fine-tune the CNN? How sensitive are results to K (nearest neighbors) and M (beam size)?
- How robust is performance to errors in segmentation/summarization and to coreference/parser errors?
- Can you provide an ablation where coherence is computed with simpler proxies (e.g., bigram sentence transitions) to isolate the benefit of the entity grid?

Recommendation
- Overall: Weak accept. The problem is worthwhile, the approach is sensible with demonstrated benefits, and the coherence component is a meaningful contribution. Clarifying evaluation and strengthening reproducibility would make this a solid contribution. Confidence: medium.
