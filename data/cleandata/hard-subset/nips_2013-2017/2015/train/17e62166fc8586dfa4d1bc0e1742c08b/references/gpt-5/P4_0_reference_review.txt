Summary and relation to prior work
The paper addresses sequence-to-sequence grounding between an image stream and a sequence of sentences. The proposed coherent recurrent convolutional network (CRCN) combines: (i) a CNN (VGGNet fc7) to represent each image, (ii) a bidirectional RNN over sentence embeddings (doc2vec paragraph vectors) to model content flow across sentences, and (iii) an entity-grid–based local coherence model (Barzilay & Lapata) to encourage coherent transitions between sentences. Training data are obtained automatically from travel blogs (NYC, Disneyland): images are aligned with nearby text via index-distance heuristics, segments are summarized to one key sentence via LSA (augmented with top-κ sentences), sentences are embedded with doc2vec, and entity grids are extracted with CoreNLP. The model learns a max-margin ranking loss over paired image/sentence sequences. At test time, candidate sentences are retrieved per image from nearest-neighbor images, and an approximate beam/divide-and-conquer search composes a best-scoring sentence sequence. Evaluation uses automatic metrics (BLEU, METEOR, CIDEr) and retrieval metrics (R@K, median rank), plus AMT pairwise preferences, against baselines extended from single-image captioning models (e.g., CNN+RNN, CNN+LSTM, log-bilinear models) and retrieval methods.

This topic extends a large body of work on single-image captioning (e.g., Karpathy & Fei-Fei; Vinyals et al.; Mao et al.) and video description (e.g., Donahue et al.), and is closely related to sequence retrieval across modalities (Kim et al.), as well as NLP coherence modeling (Barzilay & Lapata). The explicit integration of an entity-grid coherence term with a neural multimodal alignment is the key claimed novelty.

Strengths
- Problem formulation is timely and relevant: moving beyond single images to image streams and multi-sentence outputs is a natural and important step.
- Use of large-scale, weakly aligned blogs to construct parallel data is practical and scalable, avoiding manual annotation.
- Architectural integration of BRNN sentence-flow representations with an entity-grid coherence signal is well-motivated for mitigating abrupt content changes in image streams.
- Empirical results on two sizable datasets show consistent improvements over several established baselines on retrieval metrics and on AMT preference studies; ablations (CRCN vs. RCN vs. 1NN) support the value of the coherence component.

Weaknesses
- Evaluation fairness: several baselines are generative (word-level) models concatenated per image, whereas the proposed method retrieves sentences from the training corpus and re-ranks with learned cross-modal scoring. This favors lexical overlap with reference summaries and may penalize generative outputs under BLEU/CIDEr; the paper partly mitigates this with AMT but stronger retrieval-focused baselines (e.g., nearest-neighbor with strong vision features plus a standard coherence re-ranker) would be informative.
- Visual baseline disparity: the GloMatch baseline uses weak image features (GIST/Tiny). A version with VGG features would provide a fairer comparison.
- Score function design: the coherence vector gk is added to every position in Skl = Σt skt·vlt + gk·vlt, implicitly scaling coherence by sequence length and potentially confounding rank by favoring longer sequences. No length normalization or justification is provided; length-variable negatives in the margin loss may bias training.
- Modeling choices are partly ad hoc: the coherence descriptor is zero-padded to 300 and passed through the same FC stack as BRNN outputs; two stacked linear layers without interleaved nonlinearity reduce to a single linear map at test time (dropout acts as regularizer, not a nonlinearity). The claim that Wf2Wf1 “does not reduce to a single linear mapping, thanks to dropout” is inaccurate.
- Data alignment noise: image-to-text alignment via nearest index distance and LSA summarization likely introduce substantial noise; the paper does not quantify alignment quality or its effect on performance.
- Limited analysis: little error analysis of coherence (e.g., improvements on specific entity transition patterns), sensitivity to K, M, κ, or robustness to parsing/coreference errors in informal blog text. Search procedure (divide-and-conquer with beam) lacks ablation vs. standard beam or Viterbi-style DP on Markovized models.

Quality
The approach is technically plausible and produces gains on several metrics and in human studies. However, the scoring formulation’s length sensitivity, the linear stacking claim, and the lack of stronger retrieval baselines reduce confidence in the strength of the conclusions. The training relies on noisy preprocessing without quantifying noise impact. Optimization details are reasonable, but there is no discussion of convergence, validation curves, or early stopping. Overall, sound but with several methodological and evaluation caveats.

Clarity
The paper is generally clear and well-organized, with explicit equations and architectural descriptions. Some notation and design choices need clarification: (i) exact role and normalization of gk across positions; (ii) justification for sharing FC parameters between O and q; (iii) the statement regarding stacked FC layers and dropout; (iv) more precise description of the approximate search. Providing pseudo-code for candidate composition and a schematic for entity-grid construction would help.

Originality
The combination of an entity-grid coherence model with a neural multimodal ranking framework for image-stream-to-paragraph alignment appears novel relative to contemporaneous video captioning and image-paragraph retrieval work. The problem setting (retrieving sentence sequences for image streams from blogs) is a useful and distinct extension of single-image captioning. Some components (doc2vec, BRNN over sentence embeddings, CNN features) are standard; the novelty lies in their integration and objective.

Significance
The problem is important and likely to spur follow-up on multi-sentence, multi-image grounding. The reported gains and AMT preferences suggest practical utility, especially for consumer photo streams. However, the domain is limited to travel blogs, and the reliance on retrieval restricts generalization beyond seen language. Without stronger, fairer baselines and tighter analysis, the demonstrated advance is incremental rather than definitive.

Pros for acceptance
- Addresses an under-explored and relevant problem (image streams → multi-sentence descriptions).
- Integrates discourse coherence into multimodal alignment, with empirical gains and supportive ablations.
- Scalable, weakly supervised dataset construction; thorough evaluation across two datasets and with human judgments.

Cons for acceptance
- Potentially unfair baseline comparisons and weak visual features in one baseline; missing stronger retrieval re-rankers.
- Questionable aspects of the scoring function (length bias) and inaccurate claim about linear layers with dropout.
- Limited analysis of coherence contributions and sensitivity; unquantified noise in alignment/summarization.
- Retrieval-based method versus generative baselines complicates interpretation of automatic metrics.

Suggestions for improvement
- Normalize or otherwise justify the coherence term’s contribution across sequence lengths; report results stratified by length.
- Add baselines: VGG-powered nearest-neighbor with bigram/LM coherence re-ranking; CRF/Markov models over sentence candidates; stronger retrieval systems using modern features.
- Provide ablations on K, M, κ; sensitivity to parsing/coref errors; error analysis of entity-transition distributions.
- Clarify the FC-layer claim; consider adding a nonlinearity between FC layers; explore end-to-end fine-tuning of sentence embeddings and CNN.
- Report alignment quality (manual spot checks) and its effect on performance.
