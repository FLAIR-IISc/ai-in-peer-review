Summary
The paper addresses generating a sequence of natural sentences for a sequence of images (“image streams”). The proposed coherent recurrent convolutional network (CRCN) combines: (i) CNN image features (VGG fc7), (ii) a bidirectional RNN over sentence-level paragraph vectors (doc2vec) to capture content flow, and (iii) an entity-grid based local coherence model to encourage coherent sentence-to-sentence transitions. Training uses weakly aligned text–image streams mined from blogs (NYC, Disneyland), where posts are segmented by proximity heuristics and summarized via LSA to obtain a sentence per image. A max-margin objective aligns ordered image and sentence sequences. At test time, candidate sentence sequences are constructed by retrieving sentences from K-nearest images per position, and a beam-like divide-and-conquer search selects the top sequence under the learned compatibility. The paper reports improvements over extensions of single-image captioning baselines (LBL/MLBL, CNN+RNN, CNN+LSTM), a global matching model, and two ablations (1NN, RCN without coherence), using BLEU/CIDEr/METEOR, retrieval metrics, and AMT pairwise preference.

Strengths
- Tackles the underexplored setting of sequence-to-sequence vision–language beyond single images.
- Integrates a known discourse coherence model (entity grid) into a multimodal scoring framework; the ablation suggests it helps ranking.
- Uses relatively large-scale weakly aligned data without manual annotations; the idea of leveraging blogs is pragmatic.
- The search procedure is carefully engineered to keep combinatorics manageable.
- The paper situates the work among retrieval-based captioning and video/sentence models and provides both automatic and human evaluation.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Novelty is largely a combination of established components (VGG features, doc2vec, BRNN, entity grid) with a retrieval-based inference scheme. The contribution feels incremental.
- “End-to-end” and “unsupervised” claims are overstated: CNN and doc2vec are fixed or pre-trained, entity grid is a non-differentiable feature, and training uses paired image–sentence streams mined with heuristic segmentation/summarization.
- Evaluation design is problematic:
  • Automatic language metrics (BLEU/CIDEr/METEOR) are ill-suited for multi-sentence paragraphs; the paper acknowledges limitations but still relies on them heavily.
  • Retrieval metrics are unclear given the train/test split: since test “ground-truth” sentences are not in the training pool, how is R@K defined? This needs precise specification. As stated, recalling an exact ground-truth paragraph seems impossible.
  • AMT uses pairwise preference without decomposing relevance, coherence, and fluency, and without reporting agreement or statistical significance; results with 5 images may not stress coherence enough.
- Fairness/strength of baselines: concatenating single-image captions is a weak strawman for paragraph generation. Missing stronger sequential baselines such as: an encoder–decoder LSTM over image streams (e.g., LRCN-style or seq2seq with attention) that conditions each sentence on previous sentences; a sentence-level HMM/CRF or simple bigram sentence transition model; or a pure entity-grid reranker without BRNN to isolate the coherence contribution.
- Methodological clarity:
  • The scoring Skl sums s_t·v_t and adds g·v_t at every time step; g is constant for the sequence, so this term scales with length. Are sequences length-normalized? How are variable lengths handled in training and testing?
  • The alignment strictly enforces position-wise matching of images and sentences; no allowance for insertions/deletions or local reorderings, which are common in blogs.
  • No analysis of the impact of noisy preprocessing (paragraph tokenization, LSA summarization, coreference resolution). Coreference on informal blogs can be brittle; the paper provides no error analysis or robustness study.
  • Hyperparameters (doc2vec training details, BRNN depth/hidden sizes, optimization schedules, beam settings) and runtime are under-specified; reproducibility is questionable. It is not stated whether the CNN is fine-tuned.
- Domain generality is claimed but only two related tourism domains are used. No cross-domain test demonstrates transferability.

Quality
Technically sound at a high level, but several aspects are underspecified or ad hoc (evaluation protocol, handling of variable lengths, normalization). The reliance on noisy preprocessing without analysis is concerning. The learning objective closely mirrors prior work; the main addition is the entity-grid feature.

Clarity
Generally readable, but important details are missing (evaluation definitions, normalization, data processing accuracy, hyperparameters). Some reference years and formatting are incomplete. Figures/tables are referenced but not all details are provided.

Originality
Moderate. The problem setting is a reasonable extension; the architecture is a straightforward combination of known ingredients. The coherence component is borrowed from classic NLP work; its integration is useful but not conceptually novel.

Significance
Potentially useful for “visual storytelling,” but the current validation is limited and the improvements over strong sequential baselines are not established. Use of a non-standard private dataset hinders adoption.

Questions for authors
1) Precisely define R@K and median rank in this setup. What constitutes a ground-truth item if test sentences are not in the candidate pool?
2) Do you length-normalize Skl? How do you handle different sequence lengths and missing alignments?
3) Are CNN features and doc2vec embeddings fixed or fine-tuned? If fixed, please remove “end-to-end” claims.
4) What is the accuracy of segmentation/summarization and coreference on your data? How sensitive is CRCN to these steps? Provide ablations (e.g., bag-of-words vs doc2vec; no coreference; different history lengths in the entity grid).
5) Please report AMT inter-annotator agreement, significance tests, and separate ratings for relevance/coherence/fluency.
6) Runtime and search: what is the complexity and wall-clock time per test sequence for K=5, M=50? How close is the approximate search to the exact optimum?

Pros
- Addresses a timely extension of captioning to sequences.
- Sensible integration of content flow and discourse coherence.
- Uses large weakly supervised data; ablation suggests coherence helps.

Cons
- Modest novelty; reliance on combining known techniques.
- Evaluation and baselines are not fully convincing; unclear retrieval metrics.
- Overstated “end-to-end/unsupervised” claims; key details missing.
- Limited analysis of noisy preprocessing and robustness; private dataset.

Recommendation
Lean toward rejection. The idea is interesting and the direction is worthwhile, but the current empirical validation, clarity of evaluation, and strength of baselines do not meet the bar. Strengthening the evaluation protocol (clear metrics, stronger sequential baselines, robustness analyses), clarifying the methodology (length handling, normalization, preprocessing quality), and releasing data/code would substantially improve the paper.
