- Paper proposes a real-time control algorithm where a neural network is trained offline to reproduce Contact-Invariant Optimization (CIO) outputs, with alternating joint training between CIO and the NN.  
- Method also relies on noise injection via data augmentation and additive noise in hidden layers to improve robustness.  
- Experiments show the network produces realistic and stable control policies on diverse character models.  
- Main contributions are the alternating joint training and noise injection, which improve generalization beyond a straightforward supervised fit to CIO.  
- Experimental evaluation is limited by a small training set (100–200 trials with 5 branched segments), weakening claims about generalization and overfitting baselines.  
- Comparative evaluation is weak: only a brief MPC comparison is given (no citation) and systematic comparisons to other methods are missing.  
- Mathematical presentation is mostly clear but some parts (notably the CIO step and Eq. 6) lack definitions and detail, requiring readers to consult external references.  
- Section 3.2 is confusing: notational issues (dropped time index t) make the optimal-action-for-noisy-inputs procedure unclear.  
- Providing code would help reproducibility and adoption.  
- Runtime/time measurements and scaling with model complexity are missing but important for real-time applications (e.g., video games with limited CPU).  
- Clarify whether the target includes orientation (facing angle) as well as position, and whether adding a target angle is straightforward.  
- Terminology critique: the reviewer suggests distinguishing “data augmentation” from “noise injection” and questions the denoising-autoencoder analogy.  
- Questions about algorithmic choices: Are initial X’s computed using random initial weights in Alg. 1, why use the same hyperparameter eta in both steps, and what justifies keeping eta identical?  
- Many minor writing/notation issues and typos (e.g., “unity” → “unify”, missing articles, misplaced stars, acronym order, incorrect references to variables and equations) that need fixing.  
- The asynchronous joint optimization in Sec. 6 differs substantially from the alternating scheme in Alg. 1 and should be discussed earlier or connected more clearly.  
- Experimental ablations could be more granular (e.g., separate “no data augmentation” and “no hidden noise injection” variants).  
- Interesting open question: whether the CIO regularization alpha can be annealed to zero (curriculum vs. permanent constraint) and how that affects learning.  
- Overall assessment: promising and well-executed application of NNs to character control, but practical benefits, broader comparisons, clarifications, and additional experiments are needed.
