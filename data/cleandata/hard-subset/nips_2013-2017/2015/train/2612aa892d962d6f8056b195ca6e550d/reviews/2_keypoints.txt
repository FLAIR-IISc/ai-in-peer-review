- Paper demonstrates a small, fast neural network can represent an interactive control policy for characters in a physics simulator.
- Training uses trajectory optimization to learn the neural policy; claim: same architecture works across diverse cyclic behaviors and body types.
- Reviewer views this as an application of deep learning to existing trajectory-optimization-based control techniques rather than a fundamentally new method.
- Prior work has already established trajectory optimization can produce realistic control signals; the authors need to better position their contribution relative to that literature.
- Using neural networks to approximate step-by-step policy actions has been developed before (e.g., [9]); the novelty relative to such prior work is unclear.
- The closest related line is work by Abbeel and Levine; authors should explicitly compare metrics like learning speed, supported body variety, runtime computation cost, and robustness to environmental variability.
- Clarify what is meant by "very large and ill-conditioned feedback gains" and why this is problematic.
- Move the discussion of why LQG was not used to after the presentation of the proposed strategy for clearer exposition.
- Notation around lines 226–240 is confusing (s(X)=\bar s and cost defined via s(X)-\bar s); visually similar symbols (bars and squiggles) should be disambiguated.
- Explain the claim that "our approach produces softer feedback gains according to parameter λ": define "softer" relative to what, and justify why changing the initial state is beneficial.
- Overall assessment: sound experimental work but the paper must better clarify its contribution relative to recent related work.
