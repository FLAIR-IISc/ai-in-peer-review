

This paper presents a novel method for training neural networks to act as near-optimal feedback controllers for diverse complex character behaviors across multiple locomotion tasks (swimming, flying, bipedal, quadrupedal) in 3D. The authors claim to achieve interactive control without motion capture or task-specific features, using a unified neural network architecture across different character morphologies. The core innovation lies in interleaving supervised learning with trajectory optimization (Contact-Invariant Optimization), injecting noise during training, and using trajectory optimization to obtain optimal feedback gains.

The paper provides strong empirical support for its claims. The authors demonstrate successful locomotion across multiple character types (swimming, flying, bipedal, quadrupedal) with a single network architecture, significantly outperforming baseline approaches (naive neural network training on static trajectories by 2 orders of magnitude). The ablation studies (removing noise injection or joint optimization) convincingly show the necessity of their key innovations. The method's ability to produce cyclical, stable behaviors (unlike MPC's non-cyclical solutions) demonstrates a significant advance in the field.

The paper makes a strong contribution to the field of neural network-based control. The method's ability to generate realistic, stable behaviors across diverse character types without task-specific engineering addresses a major limitation of previous approaches. The authors correctly situate their work in relation to prior trajectory optimization methods [12] and neural control approaches [9, 11], showing how their method extends previous work to solve substantially harder 3D problems.

The paper is technically sound with clear methodology (Algorithm 1) and sufficient detail for reproducibility. The evaluation is comprehensive, covering multiple character types and ablation studies. However, the paper would benefit from more discussion of scalability to even more complex behaviors and potential real-world robotics applications. The computational requirements (2.5 hours of training) might limit practical deployment in some contexts.

Strengths: Novel unified approach to diverse character control, strong empirical validation across multiple domains, clear ablation studies demonstrating key innovations, well-motivated noise injection strategy.

Weaknesses: Limited discussion of scalability to more complex behaviors, minimal discussion of potential real-world robotics applications beyond simulation, no comparison to more recent reinforcement learning approaches.

Overall, this is a high-quality contribution that makes a significant advancement in neural network-based control for complex character animation. The method's ability to generate diverse, stable behaviors with a single architecture across multiple character types represents a meaningful step toward the goal of automatic controller synthesis. The paper is well-written and technically sound, making a compelling case for its approach. I recommend acceptance.
