## Review of "Interactive Control of Diverse Complex Characters with Neural Networks"

This paper presents a method for training recurrent neural networks to act as feedback controllers for a variety of dynamic systems, specifically focusing on character locomotion. The core idea is to combine supervised learning with trajectory optimization (CIO) to generate stable and realistic behaviors across diverse morphologies (swimming, flying, bipedal/quadrupedal walking) without relying on motion capture or hand-designed state machines. The authors demonstrate the ability to learn controllers for complex 3D locomotion, a feat previously challenging for related work.

The paper’s strength lies in its successful application of this combined approach to a challenging problem. Achieving stable 3D locomotion with a single network architecture across multiple character types is a significant accomplishment. The inclusion of noise injection and the use of optimal feedback gains, derived from the trajectory optimizer, are key technical contributions that contribute to robustness and generalization. The distributed training architecture is also well-described and necessary for the scale of the problem. The ablation studies (Figure 2) clearly demonstrate the importance of the joint optimization and noise injection techniques. 

However, the paper suffers from some weaknesses. While the results are impressive visually (and the accompanying video is crucial), the quantitative evaluation is limited. The comparison to other methods (e.g., direct methods for training neural network controllers) is largely impressionistic ("somewhat chaotic behaviors"). A more rigorous comparison, perhaps using standardized locomotion metrics, would strengthen the claims. The discussion of the cost functions used in trajectory optimization is brief; more detail would be helpful for reproducibility. Additionally, the paper could benefit from a more thorough discussion of the limitations of the approach, such as sensitivity to hyperparameter tuning or potential difficulties scaling to even more complex characters or environments.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novel combination of trajectory optimization and neural network training yields impressive results in complex 3D locomotion.
*   Demonstrated generality across diverse character morphologies.
*   Technical contributions (noise injection, feedback gains) improve robustness and performance.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   Limited quantitative evaluation and comparison to alternative methods.
*   Lack of detailed discussion of cost function choices.
*   Insufficient discussion of limitations and potential failure cases.

**Overall:**

This is a strong paper with a compelling approach and impressive results. While the quantitative evaluation could be improved, the demonstrated ability to learn robust controllers for diverse characters is a significant contribution. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions to address the points raised above, particularly regarding quantitative evaluation and discussion of limitations. The authors should be encouraged to expand on the quantitative analysis and provide more details regarding the cost function choices to facilitate reproducibility.
