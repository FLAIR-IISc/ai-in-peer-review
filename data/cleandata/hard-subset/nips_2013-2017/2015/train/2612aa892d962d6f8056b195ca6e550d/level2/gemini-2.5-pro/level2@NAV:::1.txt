This paper presents a method for training a recurrent neural network to act as a real-time, interactive controller for a wide variety of physically simulated characters. The core contribution is a training algorithm that alternates between offline trajectory optimization (using Contact-Invariant Optimization) to generate high-quality motion data, and supervised learning for the network to mimic this data online. This work builds upon recent approaches combining trajectory optimization and policy learning but makes a significant leap by successfully applying the paradigm to complex 3D characters with diverse morphologies and locomotion styles (swimming, flying, bipedal/quadrupedal walking), a substantial advance over prior 2D demonstrations.

**Strengths (Arguments for Acceptance):**
*   **Significance and Originality:** The paper's main strength is its demonstration of a unified framework that produces high-quality, stable controllers for an impressively diverse set of characters without task-specific engineering. The emergent, natural-looking gaits (e.g., trotting) are compelling evidence of the method's effectiveness and represent a significant step towards general-purpose automated controller design.
*   **Technical Quality:** The approach is technically sound and well-motivated. The authors introduce several key improvements, including a principled method for injecting noise and a novel technique for extracting optimal feedback gains from a direct trajectory optimizer, which is crucial for training a robust policy. The ablation study effectively demonstrates the necessity of these components for successful generalization.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and clearly articulates its methodology, especially the overall alternating optimization algorithm.

**Weaknesses (Constructive Suggestions):**
*   **Comparative Evaluation:** The evaluation is primarily qualitative and internal. While the ablation study is valuable, the paper lacks a quantitative comparison against contemporary state-of-the-art methods, particularly from the deep reinforcement learning literature (e.g., TRPO, which is cited). Claims that the resulting behaviors are more "purposeful" would be better supported by quantitative metrics like task success rate, energy efficiency, or stability on a common benchmark.
*   **Reproducibility:** While the method is described well, critical details regarding the cost function formulations for the trajectory optimization are omitted. Given that the quality of the final motion is highly dependent on these costs, providing their explicit form and weightings would substantially improve the paper's reproducibility.

In summary, this is a strong paper presenting a significant contribution to character animation and control. The results are impressive and of high interest to the community. Despite the lack of quantitative comparison to external baselines, the technical novelty and demonstrated generality of the approach are substantial. I recommend acceptance.
