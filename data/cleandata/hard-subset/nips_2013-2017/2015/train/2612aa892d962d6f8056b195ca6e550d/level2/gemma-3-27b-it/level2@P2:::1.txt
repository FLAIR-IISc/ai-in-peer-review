This paper presents a method for training recurrent neural networks to act as feedback controllers for diverse dynamical systems, including swimming, flying, and walking characters. The core idea is to combine supervised learning with trajectory optimization (CIO), leveraging the optimizer to generate training data and learn optimal feedback gains. The authors demonstrate successful control of complex characters in 3D space, a significant advancement over prior work which largely focused on 2D characters.

The paper is well-motivated, clearly articulating the challenges of automating controller design and the limitations of traditional methods. The combination of trajectory optimization and neural network learning is a compelling approach. The experimental results, particularly the successful control of diverse 3D characters with a single network architecture, are impressive and convincingly demonstrate the method's effectiveness. The ablation studies clearly highlight the importance of both the joint optimization process and noise injection. The inclusion of a video is extremely helpful in visualizing the results.

However, the paper suffers from a few weaknesses. While the authors mention comparisons to other direct methods ([3, 6, 14]), they remain largely impressionistic ("networks trained with direct methods give rise to successful yet somewhat chaotic behaviors"). A more systematic comparison, even with limited scope, would strengthen the claim of superiority. It is not entirely clear why the proposed method leads to “more realistic and purposeful behaviors” – a more in-depth analysis of the learned policies would be beneficial. 

The description of the trajectory optimization process itself is somewhat terse. While the authors cite [12], readers unfamiliar with CIO may struggle to fully grasp the details and appreciate the computational challenges involved. Providing more intuition about how CIO works and why it's particularly well-suited for this task would be valuable. The justification for the specific values of the algorithmic constants (σε, σγ, α, λ, η) is lacking; a brief discussion of how these parameters were chosen or tuned would be helpful.

The discussion of the distributed training architecture feels somewhat superficial. While the authors highlight the importance of cloud computing, they don't provide enough details about the implementation or the challenges encountered in scaling the system. 

Finally, while the authors acknowledge the limitations of the current work (e.g., simple sensory representation), they don't offer concrete plans for addressing them. A more detailed discussion of future research directions would be appreciated.

**Strengths:**

*   **Strong empirical results:** Successful control of diverse 3D characters is a significant achievement.
*   **Novel approach:** Combining trajectory optimization and neural network learning is a clever and effective strategy.
*   **Clear motivation and problem statement:** The paper effectively articulates the challenges and limitations of existing methods.
*   **Ablation studies:** Demonstrates the importance of key components of the proposed method.
*   **Video demonstration:**  Crucially helpful for understanding the quality of the learned behaviors.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Limited comparative analysis:** Impressionistic comparison to other direct methods.
*   **Insufficient explanation of CIO:**  The description of the trajectory optimization process is too brief.
*   **Lack of justification for parameter choices:** No discussion of how algorithmic constants were selected.
*   **Superficial description of distributed training:** Insufficient details about the implementation and challenges.
*   **Limited discussion of future work:**  Lack of concrete plans for addressing current limitations.



**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The paper addresses a challenging problem in control and animation.
*   The proposed method achieves state-of-the-art results on a diverse set of tasks.
*   The experimental results are compelling and well-supported.
*   The paper is clearly written and well-organized (despite minor areas for improvement).

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The lack of a systematic comparative analysis weakens the claim of superiority.
*   The limited explanation of key components (CIO, distributed training) hinders understanding.
*   The paper could benefit from a more thorough discussion of limitations and future work.

**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a significant contribution to the field. While there are some areas for improvement, the strong empirical results and novel approach outweigh the weaknesses. I recommend acceptance, with minor revisions to address the points raised above. Specifically, a more detailed comparison to other methods, a clearer explanation of the CIO process, and a discussion of the parameter tuning process would significantly strengthen the paper.
