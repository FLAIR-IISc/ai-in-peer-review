## Review of “Interactive Control of Diverse Complex Characters with Neural Networks”

This paper presents a method for training recurrent neural networks as feedback controllers for a range of dynamical systems, notably complex character locomotion (swimming, flying, bipedal/quadrupedal walking). The core idea is to combine supervised learning with trajectory optimization (specifically, Contact-Invariant Optimization - CIO) to learn a control policy that’s robust and generalizable across different morphologies and tasks. The method generates stable and realistic behaviors without relying on motion capture or hand-crafted state machines.

The paper convincingly argues for a gap in the literature: achieving stable, high-dimensional control with learned policies. While reinforcement learning is mentioned, the authors highlight the difficulty of scaling to these complex scenarios. The proposed approach cleverly leverages trajectory optimization to *generate* training data, which is then used to train a neural network that can execute the control policy in real-time. Key contributions include the use of noise injection during training, training for unexpected task changes, and using the trajectory optimizer to obtain optimal feedback gains.

The strengths of this work are significant. The breadth of characters successfully controlled – from swimmers to walkers – is impressive and demonstrates the generality of the approach. The experimental results, supported by the accompanying video, are compelling and visually demonstrate realistic and stable behaviors.  The method's ability to synthesize controllers without motion capture or task-specific features is a significant step toward automation. The technical improvements discussed, like the optimal feedback gain calculation, appear crucial for stability and performance.

However, the paper also has weaknesses. While the method is shown to work, a more detailed comparative analysis with *direct* methods (mentioned but not rigorously compared in Section 1) would strengthen the claims.  The hyperparameter choices (α, σε, σγ, η) seem somewhat arbitrarily chosen – a sensitivity analysis or justification for these values would be beneficial.  The description of the distributed training architecture, while present, lacks detail on the synchronization and communication overhead. Furthermore, while the authors acknowledge limitations, a discussion on computational cost beyond “hours” would be helpful – the reliance on cloud computing is a potential barrier to wider adoption. 

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Demonstrates a significant advance in automated control policy generation.
*   Achieves impressive results on diverse and complex character locomotion tasks.
*   Offers a compelling combination of trajectory optimization and neural network learning.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   Lack of rigorous comparison to direct learning methods.
*   Limited discussion of hyperparameter sensitivity and computational cost.
*   Details on the distributed architecture could be improved.



**Overall:** This is a strong paper with a novel and effective approach to a challenging problem. Despite the minor weaknesses, the significant advancements and compelling results warrant acceptance, with a recommendation for the authors to address the comparative analysis and discuss computational aspects in more detail in a revised version.
