

This paper presents a compelling approach to training neural network controllers for diverse physical character behaviors using a combination of trajectory optimization and stochastic neural network training. The core innovation lies in interleaving supervised learning with trajectory optimization (specifically Contact-Invariant Optimization [12]), injecting noise during training, and leveraging optimal feedback gains from the trajectory optimizer to enable robust real-time control. The authors demonstrate this method across a wide range of 3D locomotion tasks (swimming, flying, bipedal, quadrupedal) using a single neural network architecture without task-specific features, state machines, or motion capture—addressing a significant gap in the field.

**Strengths:**  
The paper's most significant contribution is its ability to unify control across diverse character morphologies and tasks (Fig. 1, Section 8). Previous work [9,11] was limited to 2D locomotion, while this method achieves stable 3D behaviors (e.g., bird flapping, quadruped trotting) with a single architecture. The technical approach is well-justified: the noise injection strategy (Section 2.1) and distributed optimization framework (Algorithm 1) effectively address overfitting and scalability, as evidenced by the ablation studies (Fig. 2, Table 1). The authors convincingly show that joint optimization with noise injection yields controllers that generalize 100× better than naive training (no joint optimization). The clarity of the trajectory optimization details (Section 3) and the practical implementation (Section 6, AWS/GPU setup) are notable strengths. The significance is high—this work enables interactive character control for animation, robotics, and biomechanics, advancing beyond prior methods that required hand-designed controllers for each task [21,18,13].

**Weaknesses:**  
While the method is robust, the paper could better contextualize its relationship to direct reinforcement learning (RL) approaches. The authors note that RL-based controllers [3,6,14] produce "chaotic behaviors" but provide limited comparative analysis (e.g., no quantitative comparison of task success rates or stability metrics against state-of-the-art RL). A brief experiment comparing their method to a modern RL baseline (e.g., PPO [14]) on the same biped task would strengthen the claims. Additionally, the computational cost (2.5 hours training on 30 nodes) is substantial for real-world robotics applications, though the authors acknowledge this as an offline step. The paper could briefly discuss potential hardware optimizations (e.g., model pruning) to reduce inference latency.

**Originality and Significance:**  
The paper’s originality is clear: it synthesizes trajectory optimization with neural networks in a way that achieves *interactive* control for complex 3D behaviors—a problem previously unsolved. This extends Mordatch et al.’s Contact-Invariant Optimization [12] by adding neural network generalization and robustness. The significance is amplified by the demonstrated versatility: the same network controls swimming, flying, bipedal, and quadrupedal characters without architectural changes. This directly addresses a key challenge in the field (Section 1: "unity these disparate approaches"), and the results (supplementary video) convincingly demonstrate realistic, stable motion.

**Clarity and Quality:**  
The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. Key concepts (e.g., sensory state definition in Section 2, optimal feedback gains in Section 3.2) are clearly explained with sufficient mathematical detail. The ablation studies and hyperparameter analysis (Table 1) provide strong evidence for the method’s design choices. Minor improvements could include a clearer definition of "contact-invariant optimization" early on and a more explicit comparison to [9,11] in terms of task complexity (e.g., "previous work achieved 2D locomotion; this achieves 3D with X, Y, Z dynamics"). The implementation details (Section 6) are highly reproducible.

**Recommendation:**  
This paper makes a strong contribution to the field of neural control for physical systems. It is technically sound, well-motivated, and addresses a meaningful open problem with broad applicability. While the comparison to direct RL methods could be expanded, the current evidence is sufficient to support the claims. The method’s ability to unify control across diverse character types using a single architecture is particularly compelling. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions (e.g., clarifying the RL comparison and computational cost trade-offs).

**Pro/Con Summary:**  
- *Pro:* Unified controller for diverse 3D locomotion, robust noise injection, strong ablation studies, practical implementation.  
- *Con:* Limited RL comparison, substantial training cost (offset by offline nature), minimal discussion of scalability to larger tasks.
