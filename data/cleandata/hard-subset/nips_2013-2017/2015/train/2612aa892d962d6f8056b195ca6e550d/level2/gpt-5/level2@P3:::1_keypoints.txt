- Proposes alternating CIO trajectory optimization and supervised recurrent-policy regression to train RNNs as near-optimal feedback controllers for complex 3D characters.  
- Injects sensory noise and uses locally optimal feedback gains (reusing CIO Gauss-Newton Hessian) to adjust targets during training.  
- Policy outputs desired velocities, recurrent updates, and contact forces; execution uses a T=1 implicit optimization to enforce dynamics.  
- Trained asynchronously on a cluster and demonstrated in real time on diverse 3D tasks (swimming, flying, bipedal, quadrupedal); ablations show joint optimization and noise matter.  
- Builds on CIO and guided policy-search style methods, arguing improved stability and sample efficiency versus model-free RL; offers a practical alternative to LQG for feedback gains.  
- Strength: single architecture generalizes across disparate 3D morphologies producing stable, plausible gaits.  
- Strength: principled robustness from sensory-noise injection and CIO-based feedback-gain computation.  
- Strength: practical asynchronous distributed training with proximal regularization that converges in practice.  
- Strength: clear real-time runtime interface via an implicit T=1 step, enabling interactive control without storing trajectories.  
- Strength: ablation studies explore importance of joint optimization and network architectures.  
- Weakness: limited quantitative evaluation—few metrics like success rates, energy/torque, robustness, or runtime numbers are reported.  
- Weakness: insufficient baselines—no head-to-head with GPS-style methods or modern policy gradients (e.g., TRPO) on shared tasks.  
- Weakness: blurred policy vs MPC boundary due to T=1 optimization and soft constraints; root forces and artifacts not quantified.  
- Weakness: recurrence not analyzed—role, size, and content of recurrent state r are not evaluated.  
- Weakness: limited sensitivity/stability analysis for hyperparameters and gain conditioning.  
- Quality: technically sound alternating optimization and efficient feedback-gain derivation; execution approach is practical but needs stronger empirical validation.  
- Clarity: generally clear, but notation for states/actions and distinction between policy outputs and runtime controls could be clearer; pseudo-code and shapes would help reproducibility.  
- Originality: moderate—combines known ideas but introduces novel CIO-based gain extraction, denoising-style target correction, and robust training across morphologies.  
- Significance: high for physics-based animation and model-based control, moderate for general RL; demonstrations on diverse 3D systems are compelling.  
- Essential recommendation: add quantitative evaluations (success rates, costs, energy, contact realism, disturbance recovery, runtime).  
- Essential recommendation: include strong baselines (GPS/TRPO) on at least one shared task to compare stability, cost, and sample/compute needs.  
- Essential recommendation: quantify physical fidelity (root forces) and test variants with harder dynamics constraints.  
- Essential recommendation: ablate recurrent units and report impacts on performance and stability.  
- Optional recommendation: evaluate terrain and external-push robustness, analyze sensitivity to λ, and release task specs and costs.  
- Pros for acceptance: strong diverse 3D demos, elegant CIO-derived gains with noise injection, real-time control without motion capture, scalable asynchronous training.  
- Cons for acceptance: missing direct comparisons to closest prior work, sparse quantitative metrics and runtime measurements, reliance on soft dynamics constraints complicates pure-policy claims.  
- Overall: solid, well-motivated contribution with promising technical ideas and demos, but needs stronger quantitative evaluation and baselines to fully substantiate claims.
