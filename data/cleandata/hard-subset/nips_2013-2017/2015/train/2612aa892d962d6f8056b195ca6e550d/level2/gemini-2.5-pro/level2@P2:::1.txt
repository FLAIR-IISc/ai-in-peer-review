### Paper Review

**Summary**
This paper presents a general framework for training a recurrent neural network to act as a real-time feedback controller for complex, physically-simulated 3D characters. The core idea is to combine the strengths of offline trajectory optimization, which can produce high-quality, physically realistic motions, with the speed of a neural network policy suitable for interactive control. The authors formulate this as a large-scale, joint optimization problem over both the policy parameters and a dataset of optimal trajectories. This problem is solved via an alternating, distributed optimization scheme where a trajectory optimizer (Contact-Invariant Optimization) generates and refines motion data, and a neural network then learns a policy from this data. The process is iterative, with the policy influencing subsequent trajectory optimizations.

Key technical contributions that enable this method to succeed on challenging 3D locomotion tasks (swimming, flying, bipedal and quadrupedal walking) include a principled method for injecting noise into the training process to improve robustness, and a novel technique for efficiently calculating optimal feedback gains directly from the trajectory optimizer. These gains are used to generate correct target actions for the noisy sensory inputs, which is crucial for learning a stable closed-loop policy. The authors demonstrate that their single, unified method can produce a diverse range of stable, realistic, and cyclic gaits that emerge naturally from the optimization without task-specific engineering.

This work builds upon recent efforts to combine trajectory optimization with neural network function approximation (e.g., Levine & Koltun, 2014; Mordatch & Todorov, 2014) but represents a substantial step forward by successfully tackling a variety of complex 3D characters, whereas previous work was largely confined to 2D.

**Strengths**

1.  **High Significance and Impressive Results:** The paper addresses the very challenging and important problem of creating general-purpose, interactive controllers for physically simulated characters. The ability to use a single algorithm to generate stable and natural-looking locomotion for such a diverse set of morphologies (swimmer, bird, biped, quadruped) is a significant achievement and a clear advance for the state of the art in both computer animation and control. The emergent cyclic gaits are particularly compelling.

2.  **Strong Technical Contributions:** The paper's novelty is not just in scaling up previous ideas, but in introducing key technical innovations that enable this success. The method for extracting well-conditioned optimal feedback gains directly from the CIO solver (Section 3.2) is a clever and valuable contribution in its own right, providing an efficient way to stabilize the policy learning process. This, combined with the principled injection of noise during training, appears to be the critical ingredient for achieving robustness in a closed-loop setting, as strongly supported by the ablation studies.

3.  **Sound Methodology and Evaluation:** The proposed alternating optimization framework is well-motivated and clearly described. The authors provide convincing empirical evidence for the importance of their main contributions. The ablation study in Figure 2 demonstrates that both the joint optimization (vs. training on a static dataset) and noise injection are essential for good performance, with the naive approaches failing dramatically. The qualitative comparison with MPC (Figure 3) also provides an interesting insight, suggesting the framework acts as a regularizer that discovers more natural, cyclic solutions.

4.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is well-written and structured. It begins with a clear overview of the problem formulation, then systematically details each component of the algorithm. The authors are transparent about the parameters used, which aids in understanding the work.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Unclear Policy Execution Step:** Section 7, which describes how the policy is executed in real-time, is confusing. It states that the next physical state is found by solving a T=1 optimization problem at each time step to match the desired velocities from the network while satisfying physics constraints. This seems to contradict the goal of having a simple, fast, feed-forward network for real-time control. If an optimization is being run at every frame, the controller is more of a model-predictive controller that uses the network to propose a target, rather than a direct policy. This critical detail needs significant clarification. Is the per-frame optimization very cheap? Why is it necessary instead of directly using the network's output to advance the state with a standard physics integrator?

2.  **Vague Phrasing in Abstract:** The abstract claims "the network is not learning a control policy, but rather the dynamics under an implicit policy." This is confusing. The network maps state to action, which is the definition of a policy. This phrasing should be clarified or revised, as it may mislead the reader about the network's function.

3.  **Lack of Discussion on Recurrent State:** While the use of recurrent units is mentioned, there is no analysis or discussion of what role the learned memory state (`r`) plays in the final controllers. For cyclic behaviors like walking, memory is crucial. It would strengthen the paper to provide some insight into what information the network learns to store.

**Constructive Suggestions**
*   Please substantially revise Section 7 to clarify the runtime execution model. An explicit algorithm or diagram explaining the flow of information from the policy network output to the final state update at each time step would be very helpful. Explain the computational cost of this step and justify why it is preferred over direct integration.
*   Consider rephrasing the confusing statement in the abstract about learning "dynamics under an implicit policy." Perhaps something clearer like "learning to generate reference velocities for a low-level physics-enforcing optimizer" would be more accurate if the description in Section 7 is correct.
*   If possible, adding a brief analysis of the learned recurrent state `r` for one of the walking characters would add significant value. For instance, a visualization of `r` over a gait cycle could reveal if it has learned to encode phase information.

In summary, this is an excellent paper presenting a powerful and general method with impressive results and strong technical contributions. The main weaknesses relate to a lack of clarity in a few key areas, which can be addressed in a revision. The work is highly significant and should be of great interest to the NIPS community.
