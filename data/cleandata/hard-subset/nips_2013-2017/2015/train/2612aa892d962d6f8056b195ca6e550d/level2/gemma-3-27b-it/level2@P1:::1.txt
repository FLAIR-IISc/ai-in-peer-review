This paper presents a compelling method for training recurrent neural networks to act as controllers for a diverse range of dynamical systems, including swimming, flying, and walking characters. The core idea – interleaving supervised learning with trajectory optimization, injecting noise, and optimizing for task changes – is well-motivated and addresses significant challenges in applying deep learning to complex control problems. The work builds upon prior efforts in trajectory optimization (CIO) and recent approaches combining trajectory optimization with neural networks, but crucially extends these to 3D locomotion and a wider variety of morphologies.

**Strengths:**

* **Impressive Results:** The ability to learn stable and realistic behaviors across such diverse systems is a significant achievement. The accompanying video (which I viewed) demonstrates convincingly the effectiveness of the method.
* **Novel Combination:**  The paper thoughtfully combines existing techniques – trajectory optimization and neural networks – in a novel way that overcomes limitations of each when used in isolation. The use of trajectory optimization to *learn* a neural network controller, rather than simply generating training data, is a key innovation.
* **Technical Depth:** The method includes several crucial technical improvements, such as noise injection for robustness and optimization of feedback gains, which contribute to its success. The explanation of these improvements is clear and well-supported.
* **Scalability:**  The authors address the computational challenges of this approach through a distributed training architecture, demonstrating its scalability.
* **Thorough Evaluation:**  The ablation studies (Section 8.1) provide valuable insights into the importance of different components of the method. The comparison with naive neural network training highlights the benefits of the proposed approach.
* **Clear Writing:** The paper is generally well-written and organized, making it relatively easy to follow the complex ideas.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** While the paper mentions the constants used, a more detailed exploration of hyperparameter sensitivity would be beneficial. How do the results change with different noise levels, learning rates, or network architectures?
* **Limited Comparison to Alternative Methods:** The comparison to “direct methods” (Section 1) is largely impressionistic. A more systematic comparison with methods like DDPG or TRPO would strengthen the paper. The claim that direct methods yield “somewhat chaotic behaviors” needs stronger justification.
* **Computational Cost:** While the authors acknowledge the computational cost, a more detailed breakdown of the training time for each character and task would be helpful. The cost of generating the data is emphasized, but the cost of *training* the network, even if smaller, is still significant.
* **Task Complexity:** The tasks presented primarily focus on locomotion. Extending the method to more complex, goal-directed behaviors (e.g., manipulation, reaching) would further demonstrate its generality.
* **Feedback Gain Calculation:** The explanation of how feedback gains are calculated (Section 3.2) is somewhat dense. A more intuitive explanation or diagram could improve clarity.



**Quality:** The paper is technically sound and presents a complete piece of work. The claims are generally well-supported by experimental results. The authors are careful to address potential limitations and discuss future work.

**Clarity:** The paper is generally clear, but some sections (e.g., 3.2) could benefit from further explanation. The organization is logical, and the figures are helpful.

**Originality:** The combination of trajectory optimization and neural networks is not entirely novel, but the specific approach presented here – particularly the optimization of feedback gains and the application to 3D locomotion – is a significant contribution.

**Significance:** The results are important and have the potential to impact a variety of fields, including robotics, animation, and biomechanics. The ability to learn versatile controllers automatically could simplify the development of complex systems.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* Achieves state-of-the-art results on a challenging set of control problems.
* Presents a novel and effective combination of existing techniques.
* Demonstrates scalability and robustness.
* Includes thorough experimental evaluation.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* Limited comparison to alternative reinforcement learning methods.
* Lack of detailed hyperparameter analysis.
* Computational cost may be a barrier to wider adoption.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of control. While there are a few minor weaknesses, the strengths of the work – particularly the impressive results and the novel approach – outweigh them. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions, primarily addressing the points raised regarding hyperparameter sensitivity and comparison to alternative methods. I suggest the authors add a table comparing performance with other reinforcement learning algorithms on the same tasks, if feasible.
