This paper presents a method for learning neural network-based feedback controllers for a diverse range of complex, physically-simulated 3D characters. The core idea is to combine the strengths of offline trajectory optimization, which can produce high-quality, physically realistic motions, with the real-time, reactive capabilities of a neural network policy. The authors formulate this as a large, joint optimization problem over both a collection of optimal trajectories and the parameters of the neural network. This problem is solved using a block-alternating scheme that iterates between optimizing trajectories to satisfy task goals and match the current policy, and performing supervised regression to update the policy to match the optimized trajectories.

The main contributions that enable this method to succeed on challenging 3D locomotion tasks (swimming, flying, bipedal and quadrupedal walking) are a set of key technical improvements. First, the method introduces a principled way to inject noise during training to improve robustness. Noise is added to the sensory inputs, and the network is trained to predict the optimal action for this perturbed state. This requires computing optimal feedback gains, and the paper presents a novel and efficient technique to extract these gains directly from the contact-invariant trajectory optimizer. Second, the framework utilizes a data generation scheme where new trajectory segments are branched from existing ones to create a dataset that reflects interactive goal changes. Finally, a distributed architecture is used to manage the significant computational cost of trajectory optimization. This work builds upon recent approaches that combine trajectory optimization and policy learning (e.g., Levine & Koltun, 2014; Mordatch & Todorov, 2014), but demonstrates successful application to a much wider and more difficult set of 3D characters than previously shown.

**Strengths:**

*   **Significance and Impact:** The results shown are highly impressive and significant. The ability to generate stable, realistic, and interactive controllers for morphologically diverse characters—from a swimmer to a biped to a spider-like quadruped—using a single, unified algorithm is a substantial advance. This work pushes the state of the art in automated controller synthesis and has high potential for impact in fields like character animation, game development, and robotics. The emergent gaits (e.g., bipedal walking, quadrupedal trotting) are particularly compelling as they arise from general principles rather than task-specific engineering.

*   **Quality and Technical Soundness:** The proposed method is technically strong. The joint optimization framework is well-motivated. The paper’s main technical novelty, the method for extracting optimal feedback gains from a direct trajectory optimizer (Section 3.2), is clever and appears crucial for the success of the stochastic training procedure. The ablation study in Section 8.1 provides clear and convincing evidence for the benefits of the joint optimization approach over static training and for the necessity of noise injection to prevent overfitting and divergence.

*   **Originality:** While the high-level concept of guiding policy search with trajectory optimization exists, this paper’s specific formulation and application represent a significant original contribution. The method for computing feedback gains and integrating them with sensory noise injection is novel. The demonstration of a single method working "out of the box" on such a varied set of complex 3D locomotion problems is a novel and powerful result that significantly extends prior work, which was largely limited to 2D.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

*   **Clarity and Reproducibility:** While the paper is generally well-written, several key details that would be necessary for reproduction are omitted. The cost function `C(X)` is central to the method, but its components (task costs, realism costs, etc.) and their relative weightings are not specified. Similarly, the state `s` and action `a` representations include contact forces `f`, which is a non-standard choice. A more detailed explanation of the intuition behind the policy predicting desired forces, and how this interacts with the physics simulation during execution, would be beneficial.

*   **Evaluation:** The comparison against the ablations of the proposed method is excellent. The qualitative comparison with Model Predictive Control (MPC) in Figure 3 is very insightful, suggesting that the learned policy acts as a regularizer that encourages more natural, cyclic behaviors. This is a strong argument for the approach, and the paper would be improved by a more detailed discussion of this phenomenon. A quantitative comparison, while difficult, could further strengthen this point.

*   **Role of Recurrence:** The controller is introduced as a recurrent neural network, and a recurrent memory state `r` is included in the formalism. However, the contribution of the recurrent units is never analyzed or discussed. It is unclear how important they are for the final performance. An ablation study removing the recurrent connections would be valuable to assess their impact, or alternatively, a discussion of what temporal dependencies they might be capturing would strengthen the paper.

*   **Execution Details:** Policy execution involves solving a single-timestep optimization problem (Section 7). It is stated that this can be solved at real-time rates, but no performance figures are given. Providing the typical computation time for this step would help substantiate the claim of real-time interactive control.

**Summary of Pros and Cons:**

**Pros:**
*   Highly significant results, demonstrating a single general method for controlling a wide variety of complex 3D characters.
*   Technically sound, with a novel and well-motivated method for extracting feedback gains to enable robust training.
*   Strong empirical evidence, including an effective ablation study, supporting the key components of the algorithm.
*   High potential impact for animation, gaming, and robotics.

**Cons:**
*   Lacks some key details for reproducibility, particularly regarding cost function design.
*   The role and contribution of the recurrent components of the network are not analyzed.
*   The discussion comparing the method to alternatives like MPC could be expanded.

Overall, this is a strong paper presenting a significant step forward in automated character control. The strengths, particularly the impressive and general results, far outweigh the weaknesses, which are largely related to clarity and could be addressed in a final version. I recommend acceptance.
