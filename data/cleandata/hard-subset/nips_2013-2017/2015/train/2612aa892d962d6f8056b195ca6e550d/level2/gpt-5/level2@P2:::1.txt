Summary
This paper presents a method to train recurrent neural network policies for real-time interactive control of diverse, physically simulated characters (swimming, flying, bipeds, quadrupeds). The core idea is to alternate between trajectory optimization (via Contact-Invariant Optimization, CIO) and supervised policy regression, with noise injected into sensory inputs and hidden activations to improve robustness. A notable contribution is the use of locally optimal feedback gains to construct noisy training targets: for a small input perturbation ε, the target action is a + A_s ε, where A_s is derived efficiently from the Gauss-Newton structure of the CIO subproblem via a perturbation method that reuses the already computed inverse Hessian CXX. The policy outputs desired velocities, recurrent-state increments, and contact forces; at execution, the next state is obtained by solving a 1-step optimization that matches the policy outputs while softly enforcing the equations of motion and contact constraints. The method is implemented in a distributed asynchronous architecture and demonstrated on multiple 3D characters. Ablations indicate that joint alternation and noise injection are important for test-time stability, and that deeper nets (3×250) offer a favorable trade-off.

Relation to prior work
This builds directly on prior work combining trajectory optimization with supervised policy learning (e.g., Levine & Koltun 2014, Mordatch & Todorov 2014). Compared to guided policy search/iLQG-based pipelines, this paper uses CIO (contact-rich, direct trajectory optimization) and proposes a new way to compute stabilizing gains consistent with the direct method. Compared to model-free RL (e.g., TRPO), the approach is model-based and aims for more “purposeful” behaviors typical in graphics. The empirical emphasis is on 3D characters and interactive goal changes, which goes beyond many earlier 2D demonstrations.

Strengths
- Conceptually neat and practical alternation between CIO and supervised learning, with an effective treatment of robustness via input and hidden noise.
- Efficient and principled derivation of local feedback gains that reuse the Gauss-Newton system, avoiding a separate LQR pass and allowing gain “softness” control via λ.
- Demonstrations across substantially different morphologies (swimmer, bird, multiple bipeds, quadrupeds) with a single network architecture and broadly shared training protocol.
- Simple and scalable distributed implementation; training completes in hours with modest resources.
- Clear ablations showing the necessity of joint optimization and noise injection for test-time stability.

Weaknesses
- Claims of “near-optimal feedback controllers” are not quantitatively substantiated. There are no task metrics (success rates, energy, tracking error, disturbance-rejection) nor comparisons to strong baselines (e.g., LQR/iLQG-based GPS, TRPO, MPC with comparable horizons).
- The execution-time dynamics step uses soft constraints and allows root forces, which undermines physical realism and weakens claims relative to strict feasibility. The effect of root-force penalties on behavior and stability is not quantified.
- The recurrent component is emphasized conceptually but underspecified experimentally (number of recurrent units, their necessity, and ablation vs. purely feedforward policies).
- The feedback-gain contribution is interesting but evaluated only implicitly. There is no head-to-head comparison vs. an LQR/LQG gain computation on the same trajectories.
- Clarity gaps: exact task cost terms are referred to but not fully enumerated; the definition and dimensionality of the sensory vector and action (especially contact forces) and the recurrent state are only partially specified; convergence plots are qualitative.

Quality
Technically sound overall; the CIO and Gauss-Newton machinery are standard and used appropriately; the perturbation-based gain derivation is plausible and well motivated. However, the experimental section is largely qualitative; stronger empirical validation is needed to support the central claims (optimality, stability, generalization, and superiority to alternatives). The 1-step execution optimization is a pragmatic choice but blurs the line between pure policy execution and MPC; its cost/benefit and the role of soft constraints deserve more rigorous analysis.

Clarity
The paper is generally well organized and readable, with a coherent narrative from problem to implementation. Important details are missing: explicit cost terms and weights; precise definitions and sizes of s, a, r; the number of recurrent units; exact training schedules; quantitative performance metrics; and the computational budget at test time. Figures and the promised video help, but the paper should be self-contained enough for reproduction by an expert.

Originality
Moderate. Alternating between trajectory optimization and supervised policy learning is established; the use of CIO for 3D contact-rich skills and the efficient way to obtain feedback gains from a direct optimizer are the main novel aspects. The framing of the action as desired velocities/forces under an implicit one-step optimizer is pragmatic but not conceptually new.

Significance
Potentially high for graphics/control practitioners seeking robust, interactive controllers without hand-crafted state machines or motion capture. The cross-morphology demonstrations are compelling. For the ML/control community, the significance would be much higher with rigorous quantitative evaluation and comparisons to dominant baselines (GPS/iLQG, TRPO, MPC).

Constructive suggestions
- Provide quantitative metrics: success rate under randomized goals and disturbances, cost-to-go, energy/torque usage, and tracking error. Include push-recovery tests and sensitivity to sensor/model noise.
- Compare to: (a) GPS with LQG/iLQG gains; (b) TRPO (or another strong model-free baseline) on a subset of tasks; (c) MPC at test time with the same cost, horizon, and computation.
- Evaluate the proposed gain computation against an LQG gain pass: conditioning, magnitude, and resulting policy robustness.
- Clarify the recurrent architecture: number of recurrent units, recurrence topology, and ablation against purely feedforward policies; explain how r is initialized and updated in execution.
- Detail the cost function and all weights; report the penalty on root forces and analyze outcomes with stricter (harder) contact constraints.
- Report test-time compute (ms per step) for the 1-step optimization; discuss scalability and failure cases.
- Discuss the feasibility of required sensory inputs (previous contact forces) in real robots, and whether they can be estimated from proprioception.

Arguments for acceptance
- Practical, scalable method combining strong trajectory optimization with robust policy learning.
- Novel and efficient computation of stabilizing feedback gains within a direct trajectory optimization framework.
- Diverse and visually compelling 3D results with a single architecture; interactive goal changes.

Arguments against acceptance
- Lack of rigorous, quantitative evaluation and comparison to established baselines; overreliance on qualitative video.
- Use of soft dynamics constraints and root forces at execution weakens physical realism claims.
- Insufficient ablation and clarity on the role of recurrence and feedback gains.
