This paper proposes an approach for training recurrent neural networks as near-optimal feedback controllers for diverse 3D characters (swimmer, bird, bipeds, quadrupeds), without motion capture or hand-crafted state machines. The key idea is to alternate trajectory optimization (Contact-Invariant Optimization, CIO) with supervised regression to a neural policy that outputs desired state velocities, recurrent-state updates, and contact forces. The method injects sensory and hidden-unit noise during training and uses a principled first-order correction a + As ε, where As are optimal local feedback gains computed efficiently from the trajectory optimizer’s Gauss-Newton system via a perturbation method. The optimization is performed asynchronously across a cluster; at test time, the policy runs in real time, with a one-step constrained optimization enforcing dynamics consistency. The work is related to trajectory-centric RL and guided policy search (e.g., Levine & Koltun, 2014) and model-based control with MPC, but targets harder 3D contact-rich behaviors and emphasizes stability and realism.

Strengths
- Addresses a challenging and relevant problem (real-time control of complex 3D characters) with a clear synthesis of trajectory optimization and neural network policies.
- Technical novelty in incorporating local feedback gains into the regression targets, derived efficiently from the same linear systems used by CIO; this directly combats covariate shift and improves closed-loop stability.
- Careful treatment of robustness via noise injection at sensory inputs and within the network; ablations show both joint optimization and noise matter.
- Demonstrates a single training framework that works across multiple morphologies and task variants, producing plausible, cyclic gaits without motion capture or gait primitives.
- Practical distributed system design and runtime controller that achieves interactive rates.
- Empirical comparison to an MPC baseline (albeit on a subset) showing qualitative differences in regularity of behavior.

Weaknesses and questions
- Runtime control uses a one-step optimization with soft dynamics/contacts (allowing root forces). This may mask difficulties in torque-limited, strictly physically consistent settings. Please quantify the magnitude of residual/root forces during execution and their effect on realism across tasks. Are comparable results achievable with hard dynamics or stricter penalties?
- The policy outputs velocities and contact forces rather than torques. This blurs the line between policy and dynamics and relies on the inner optimizer at execution time. How sensitive is performance to this representation? Please add an ablation comparing action parameterizations (e.g., torques, desired joint targets) and clarify the trade-offs.
- The feedback-gain construction is a central contribution. Provide quantitative ablations isolating its effect: full method vs. no As term in the targets; As from LQG vs. your perturbation approach; sensitivity to λ and to the conditioning of CXX. Present metrics on stability (time to failure) and tracking error for each.
- While the paper claims generality across morphologies, it appears each character is trained separately with the same architecture, not a single policy that spans morphologies. Please clarify. If trained separately, discuss sample efficiency and whether training times scale adversely with morphology complexity.
- Limited quantitative evaluation of robustness and generalization. The training protocol includes goal changes and noise, but tests largely use similar conditions. Please report: success rates over randomized initial states; recovery from external pushes; sensitivity to model errors (mass, friction); terrain variation for legged characters.
- Baselines: Beyond MPC, the closest NeurIPS/ICML-era alternatives include guided policy search (ICML’14; NIPS’13 variants), TRPO (2015), and PILCO (2011). Even if full 3D comparisons are difficult, smaller instances (2D biped or swimmer) would strengthen claims. Alternatively, compare to standard physics-based controllers (e.g., SIMBICON) on comparable characters and metrics (energy, foot slip, falls).
- Clarity gaps: the number and role of recurrent units r are not fully specified; training horizon and BPTT details are unclear; the dataset scale (timesteps per trajectory/segment) is not reported; and convergence diagnostics for the alternating optimization are brief. Adding these details would aid reproducibility.

Quality
- The approach is technically sound, with a well-motivated alternating procedure and a clear derivation for obtaining local feedback gains from the trajectory optimizer. Experimental evidence supports the importance of joint optimization and noise. However, more quantitative metrics (success/fall rates, contact realism, actuation effort) and stronger baselines would better substantiate near-optimality and robustness claims.

Clarity
- The paper is generally well-organized and readable. Please clarify: (1) the size/structure of r and whether recurrence uses BPTT; (2) horizons, dataset sizes, and training/validation splits; (3) execution-time complexity and measured latency; (4) precise cost terms and weights used in CIO for all tasks; (5) the magnitude of soft constraint violations at test time.

Originality
- The high-level idea (trajectory optimization + policy regression) is aligned with guided policy search, but the contributions are distinct: feedback-gain-informed targets derived efficiently from CIO’s linear systems; an alternating stochastic procedure with proximal regularization; and application to diverse 3D, contact-rich behaviors without motion capture or contact schedules. This is a meaningful extension of prior work.

Significance
- If validated with stronger quantitative results and stricter dynamics enforcement, this could be a significant contribution for both character animation and model-based RL/control, offering a practical path to real-time, robust controllers for complex systems. The technique to extract stabilizing gains from direct trajectory optimization is of independent interest.

Suggestions for improvement
- Add quantitative robustness metrics and stricter-dynamics experiments (reduced or no root forces).
- Provide ablations on action parameterization and recurrence.
- Include comparisons to GPS/TRPO/PILCO on at least simplified domains; or to established animation controllers on 3D characters with standardized metrics.
- Report sensitivity to λ, σ parameters, and dataset size; plot performance vs. number of trajectories/segments.
- Release more implementation details (optimizer tolerances, finite-difference steps for φX, step sizes for SGD) to aid reproducibility.

Arguments for acceptance
- Addresses an important, difficult problem; compelling demonstrations across several morphologies.
- Technically novel and practical method for integrating feedback gains into policy learning from CIO.
- Clear evidence that joint optimization and noise injection materially improve closed-loop behavior.
- Real-time execution with a simple, reusable architecture.

Arguments against acceptance
- Heavy reliance on soft dynamics/contacts at execution time; limited quantification of physical realism.
- Limited quantitative evaluation and baselines; unclear generalization beyond trained regimes.
- Some methodological details (recurrence, data scale, convergence) need clarification and ablation.

Overall, this is a promising and well-executed direction that could have substantial impact with the requested additional evaluations and clarifications.
