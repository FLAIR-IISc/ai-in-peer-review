This paper presents a method for training recurrent neural networks to act as feedback controllers for a variety of dynamical systems, including swimming, flying, and legged locomotion. The core idea is to combine supervised learning with trajectory optimization (specifically, Contact-Invariant Optimization or CIO) to generate training data for the neural network. The method aims to address the challenges of learning controllers in high-dimensional continuous spaces, where traditional methods are often time-consuming and manual. This work builds upon previous efforts in reinforcement learning and optimal control, but seeks to scale to more complex problems than previously achieved, notably in 3D locomotion.

The paper is well-motivated, addressing a significant challenge in robotics, animation, and biomechanics. The combination of trajectory optimization and neural network learning is a compelling approach, and the demonstrated ability to control diverse characters with a single network architecture is a significant achievement. The inclusion of noise injection and optimization for feedback gains are thoughtful additions that contribute to the robustness and generalization ability of the learned controllers. The empirical results, particularly the demonstration of stable 3D locomotion, are convincing and support the claims made in the paper. The comparison to naive neural network training highlights the benefit of the proposed joint optimization approach.

However, several areas could be improved. While the paper mentions a systematic comparison to “more direct methods” (e.g., reinforcement learning), it does not present such a comparison. A more thorough evaluation against state-of-the-art reinforcement learning algorithms (e.g., TRPO, PPO) would strengthen the paper and provide valuable insights into the strengths and weaknesses of the proposed approach. Currently, the statement that direct methods yield "somewhat chaotic behaviors" feels unsupported without a quantitative comparison. 

The description of the distributed training architecture, while informative, lacks detail regarding the communication overhead and synchronization mechanisms. A more detailed discussion of the practical considerations for scaling the training process would be beneficial. The statement that “we can increase our training performance linearly for the size of cluster we are using” seems optimistic and would benefit from supporting data, perhaps a plot of training time versus the number of nodes.

While the ablation study demonstrates the importance of joint optimization and noise injection, it would be helpful to explore the sensitivity of the method to the choice of hyperparameters (e.g., noise levels, regularization weights). A more detailed analysis of the impact of these parameters on performance would provide practical guidance for applying the method to new problems. The choice of cost functions is mentioned as impacting behavior, but not explored.

The paper references several related works, but some citations are dated. Including more recent relevant publications (e.g., advances in model-based reinforcement learning) would demonstrate a more comprehensive understanding of the state-of-the-art.  Specifically, the work on Differentiable Physics engines could be relevant.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel combination of trajectory optimization and neural networks enables control of diverse complex characters.
*   Demonstrated success in challenging 3D locomotion tasks.
*   Method is relatively data-efficient compared to traditional reinforcement learning approaches.
*   Clear and well-written paper with convincing empirical results.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Lack of a thorough quantitative comparison to state-of-the-art reinforcement learning algorithms.
*   Limited discussion of the practical considerations for scaling the training process.
*   Insufficient analysis of the sensitivity to hyperparameters.
*   Dated citations.



**Overall assessment:**

This is a strong paper that presents a novel and effective approach to learning controllers for complex dynamical systems. While there are some areas for improvement, the significant achievements and potential impact of the work outweigh the weaknesses. The paper is of high quality and makes a valuable contribution to the field. A revision addressing the concerns raised above, particularly the inclusion of a more comprehensive comparison to reinforcement learning baselines, would significantly strengthen the paper.



Recommendation: **Accept with minor revisions.**
