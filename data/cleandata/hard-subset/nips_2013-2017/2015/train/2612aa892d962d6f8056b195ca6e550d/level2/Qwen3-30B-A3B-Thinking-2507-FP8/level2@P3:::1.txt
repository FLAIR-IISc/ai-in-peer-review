

The paper introduces a method for training neural network controllers that combine trajectory optimization (specifically Contact-Invariant Optimization) with stochastic neural network regression to enable interactive, real-time control of diverse 3D character locomotion (swimming, flying, bipedal, quadrupedal). The core innovation lies in a distributed optimization framework that jointly learns a policy and optimal feedback gains while injecting noise during training. The method achieves stable, realistic behaviors across multiple character morphologies without task-specific features or state machines, using a single network architecture. Key technical contributions include: (1) a principled noise injection scheme that leverages trajectory optimization to compute feedback gains for robustness, (2) a distributed training algorithm enabling efficient scaling, and (3) empirical validation across four distinct locomotion tasks in 3D.

Strengths are significant. The technical approach is well-motivated and rigorously evaluated. The ablation studies (Fig. 2, Table 1) convincingly demonstrate the necessity of joint optimization and noise injection, showing orders-of-magnitude performance gains over naive baselines. The method's generality—handling swimming, flying, bipedal, and quadrupedal locomotion with identical architecture—represents a substantial advancement over prior work that required task-specific controllers (e.g., inverted pendulum for bipeds, cyclic encodings for flying). The theoretical derivation of feedback gains via perturbation (Section 3.2) is novel and efficiently reuses trajectory optimization computations. The implementation details (Section 6) provide sufficient clarity for reproducibility, including resource usage (2.5 hours on 30 nodes) and hyperparameters. The results (Section 8) are compelling, with the supplementary video strongly supporting the claims of realistic, diverse behaviors.

Weaknesses are minor but addressable. The significance argument would benefit from a more explicit comparison to model-based MPC, which shares similar cost functions but produces non-cyclical, less "natural" trajectories (Fig. 3). Quantifying the real-time performance advantage of the neural network policy over MPC (e.g., inference latency vs. MPC horizon) would strengthen the practical impact claim. While the noise parameter σ_ε = 10⁻² is used consistently, the paper lacks discussion on how this value was tuned or whether it generalizes across tasks—this is a minor gap in the methodology section. Additionally, the claim that the network "learns dynamics under an implicit policy" (Abstract) could be clarified; the action is velocity, but the learned mapping implicitly encodes dynamics via the trajectory optimization constraints, not explicitly.

The paper meets all NIPS quality criteria. It is technically sound: the optimization framework is non-trivial, the ablations are comprehensive, and the implementation is robust (e.g., proximal regularization preventing divergence). Clarity is generally high, though Section 3.2's mathematical derivation could be more accessible. Originality is clear: while trajectory optimization + NN control has been explored (citing [9,11]), this work extends it to challenging 3D locomotion with noise-injected feedback gains, addressing a gap in prior 2D-focused approaches. Significance is strong—this method enables a unified controller for diverse characters, reducing manual design effort in animation and robotics. The related work section adequately references prior control architectures (e.g., state machines for bipeds [21,18]), though a brief mention of concurrent RL work [3,6] would further contextualize the comparison.

Recommendations for revision: 
1. Add a brief comparison of inference speed vs. MPC (e.g., "The neural network policy executes at X Hz, while MPC requires Y Hz for equivalent horizon") to clarify real-time advantage. 
2. Expand Section 2.1 to discuss noise parameter selection (e.g., "σ_ε was tuned to balance robustness and fidelity across tasks; values below 10⁻³ caused instability"). 
3. Clarify the "implicit policy" phrasing in the Abstract to emphasize the velocity-action mapping's role in implicitly learning dynamics via trajectory constraints.

The paper makes a clear, well-executed contribution to interactive control. The technical depth, broad applicability, and rigorous ablation studies outweigh the minor weaknesses. It advances the state of the art in neural control for complex dynamics and addresses a practical problem in animation/robotics with demonstrable results. The proposed revisions are straightforward and essential for maximizing impact.
