Summary
The paper proposes a method to train recurrent neural networks as near-optimal feedback controllers for complex, high-DOF characters. The approach alternates between direct trajectory optimization via Contact-Invariant Optimization (CIO) [12] and supervised neural policy regression. A key idea is to inject sensory noise during training and to adjust targets using locally optimal feedback gains as computed from a perturbation analysis that reuses the Gauss-Newton Hessian from CIO. The policy outputs desired velocities, recurrent-state updates, and contact forces; at execution, a short-horizon (T=1) optimization enforces dynamics and matches the policy’s desired velocities and forces. The system is trained asynchronously on a compute cluster and demonstrated in real time on diverse 3D tasks: swimming, flying, bipedal and quadrupedal locomotion. Ablations indicate joint optimization and noise are important. The approach is related to Guided Policy Search and trajectory-optimization–based policy learning [9, 11], but emphasizes 3D locomotion, CIO-based feedback-gain computation, and a unified architecture across morphologies.

Relation to prior work
The work builds on CIO for generating complex motions [12], and on the idea of combining trajectory optimization with supervised learning of policies [9, 11]. Compared to model-free RL such as DQN [10] and TRPO [14], this method leverages optimizer-generated data and local feedback to improve stability and sample efficiency. Prior neural control of dynamical systems and recurrent policies [3, 6] are acknowledged, but the presented results target harder 3D settings and multiple morphologies. The feedback-gain derivation via a soft-constraint perturbation approach provides a practical alternative to LQG passes.

Strengths
- Demonstrated generality: a single architecture and training recipe produce stable, cyclic gaits and plausible behaviors across disparate 3D morphologies (swimmers, flyers, bipeds, quadrupeds), which is significantly more challenging than 2D.
- Principled robustness: noise injection in sensory inputs together with matching of locally linearized feedback (a + asε) is well-motivated and empirically important; the CIO-based gain computation is elegant and efficient.
- Practical training system: asynchronous, distributed alternation with proximal regularization is sensible and converges in practice.
- Clear runtime interface: the controller runs in real time using a T=1 implicit step that enforces dynamics constraints, enabling interactive control without storing trajectories.
- Ablation studies: evidence that joint optimization and noise are critical; exploration of network depth/width.

Weaknesses and points for improvement
- Quantitative evaluation is limited. Claims of “near-optimal” control rely primarily on CIO costs; there is little reporting of success rates, energy/torque metrics, robustness to perturbations, or timing performance (fps, per-step solve time, CPU/GPU utilization).
- Baselines are insufficient. Direct comparison to Guided Policy Search-style methods [9, 11] and modern policy gradients (e.g., TRPO [14]) on at least one shared task would strengthen the case for the proposed technical differences (feedback-gain matching, action parameterization, distributed optimization).
- Action parameterization and execution: the policy outputs desired velocities/contact forces, and a T=1 optimization enforces dynamics. This blurs the boundary between “pure” policy and MPC, and the reliance on soft constraints may introduce root forces; the magnitude and frequency of such artifacts are not quantified.
- Limited analysis of recurrence. The role, size, and learned content of the recurrent state r are not evaluated; it remains unclear how much memory contributes beyond physical state.
- Sensitivity and stability. Hyperparameter sensitivity (σε, σγ, α, λ, η) and the conditioning of the computed feedback gains are not thoroughly analyzed; stability and robustness are shown qualitatively but not systematically.

Quality
Overall technically sound. The alternating optimization with proximal terms is reasonable; the feedback-gain derivation via Woodbury reusing C−1XX is a solid contribution. Execution via implicit one-step optimization is practical. However, stronger empirical validation (quantitative metrics and competitive baselines) is needed to fully support the claims.

Clarity
Generally clear and well-organized. The notation for state/action and the distinction between policy outputs (desired velocities/forces) and actual controls solved at runtime could be clarified further. Providing pseudo-code with units and shapes of s, a, and r would aid reproducibility.

Originality
Moderate but meaningful. Combining CIO with supervised policy learning is known [9, 11], yet the specific contributions—CIO-based feedback-gain extraction via soft-constraint perturbations, denoising-style target correction, and robust training for diverse 3D characters—appear novel and practically impactful.

Significance
High for physics-based animation and model-based control; moderate for general RL. Demonstrations on diverse 3D systems are compelling. With stronger baselines and quantitative analyses, this could influence how trajectory optimization is coupled to neural policies in continuous control.

Essential recommendations
- Add quantitative evaluations: success rate over test goals, trajectory cost, energy/torque, contact realism, and disturbance-recovery metrics. Report real-time performance numbers.
- Include strong baselines: GPS-style policy learning [9, 11] and TRPO [14] on at least the biped task; compare stability, cost, and sample/compute requirements.
- Quantify physical fidelity: report the magnitude/frequency of root forces; consider a variant with harder dynamics constraints or stronger penalties to assess impact on learned policies.
- Clarify recurrence: ablate recurrent units and report performance and stability impacts.

Optional recommendations
- Evaluate robustness to terrain variations and external pushes.
- Analyze sensitivity to λ in the gain computation and provide guidelines.
- Release task specifications and cost terms for reproducibility.

Pros for acceptance
- Strong, diverse 3D demonstrations with a unified method.
- Practical and elegant use of CIO-derived feedback gains with noise injection.
- Real-time interactive control without motion capture or state machines.
- Asynchronous distributed training that scales.

Cons for acceptance
- Missing head-to-head comparison with closest prior methods (GPS/TRPO).
- Sparse quantitative metrics; robustness and runtime performance not rigorously measured.
- Reliance on soft dynamics constraints and a T=1 solve complicates claims of purely learned feedback control.

Overall assessment
A solid, well-motivated contribution at the intersection of trajectory optimization and neural control with convincing demos across complex 3D systems. With stronger quantitative evaluation and baselines, the impact would be clear-cut. As it stands, significance and practicality are promising, and the technical ideas—especially the feedback-gain matching—are likely to be useful beyond animation.
