- Core idea: alternate trajectory optimization (CIO) and supervised policy regression with input/hidden noise for robust recurrent policies.
- Novel feedback-gain method: compute local gains a + A_s ε by reusing the CIO Gauss-Newton inverse Hessian (CXX) via a perturbation trick.
- Policy outputs desired velocities, recurrent-state increments, and contact forces; execution uses a 1-step optimizer enforcing dynamics and contacts softly.
- Implementation: distributed asynchronous training, single network arch across multiple 3D morphologies; training completes in hours.
- Ablations: joint alternation and noise injection are crucial for test-time stability; deeper nets (3×250) perform well.
- Builds on prior guided policy search and trajectory-optimization work, but uses CIO for contact-rich direct optimization and a new gain extraction approach.
- Contrasted with model-free RL (e.g., TRPO): method is model-based and targets purposeful, graphics-style behaviors, emphasizing 3D interactive tasks.
- Strength: conceptually neat and practical alternation between CIO and supervised learning with effective robustness treatment.
- Strength: principled, efficient derivation of stabilizing gains without a separate LQR pass, with a tunable softness parameter λ.
- Strength: demonstrations across diverse morphologies using a shared training protocol, showing generality.
- Strength: simple, scalable implementation and clear ablations supporting key design choices.
- Weakness: “near-optimal feedback controller” claims lack quantitative support and comparisons to strong baselines or metrics.
- Weakness: execution-time soft constraints and allowed root forces reduce physical realism; effects of root-force penalties are unquantified.
- Weakness: recurrent component underspecified experimentally (unit counts, necessity, ablation vs feedforward).
- Weakness: feedback-gain idea evaluated only implicitly; no direct comparison to LQR/LQG gains on the same trajectories.
- Weakness: clarity gaps—missing explicit cost terms, full definitions/dimensions of sensory vector, action, recurrent state, and training schedules.
- Quality: technically sound use of CIO and Gauss-Newton, but experiments are largely qualitative and need stronger empirical validation.
- Quality: 1-step execution optimization is pragmatic but blurs distinction between pure policy execution and MPC; cost/benefit unclear.
- Clarity: paper is organized and readable but omits reproduction-critical details and quantitative performance metrics.
- Originality: moderate—alternation and policy learning are established; novelty lies in CIO for contact-rich skills and efficient gain extraction.
- Significance: potentially high for graphics/control practitioners seeking robust interactive controllers; less impactful for ML/control without rigorous comparisons.
- Suggestion: provide quantitative metrics (success rates, energy, tracking error, disturbance recovery) and push-recovery tests.
- Suggestion: compare to GPS/LQG/iLQG, TRPO (or other model-free), and MPC baselines on representative tasks.
- Suggestion: evaluate proposed gain computation directly against an LQG/LQR pass (conditioning, magnitude, robustness).
- Suggestion: clarify recurrent architecture, initializations, ablations vs feedforward, full cost function weights, root-force penalties, and test-time computation (ms/step).
