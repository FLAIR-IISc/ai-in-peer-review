

This paper presents a method for training recurrent neural networks to serve as near-optimal feedback controllers for diverse dynamic systems (swimming, flying, bipedal, and quadrupedal locomotion) in 3D environments. The authors combine supervised learning with trajectory optimization (specifically Contact-Invariant Optimization), using a neural network that learns state-action mappings while incorporating a small number of recurrent units for memory. The key innovation lies in interleaving supervised learning with trajectory optimization, injecting noise during training, and using trajectory optimization to obtain optimal feedback gains. The authors demonstrate that their method produces stable, realistic behaviors across multiple character types without task-specific features or motion capture.

Strengths:
1. The paper addresses an important problem in character control: automating the synthesis of interactive controllers for diverse physical systems. The ability to use a single architecture across multiple character types (swimming, flying, bipedal, quadrupedal) is a significant contribution.

2. The method is technically sound, with a clear algorithm (Algorithm 1) that properly interleaves trajectory optimization and neural network training. The ablation study (section 8.1) convincingly shows the necessity of their key components (joint optimization, noise injection).

3. The empirical results are impressive, demonstrating stable 3D locomotion where previous work was limited to 2D. The supplementary video (as mentioned in the abstract) would likely provide strong visual evidence of the method's success.

4. The paper provides a clear theoretical justification for their approach, particularly regarding the optimal feedback gains calculation (section 3.2), which is a non-trivial contribution.

Weaknesses:
1. The paper claims to solve "substantially harder problems" than previous work but lacks sufficient quantitative comparison. While they note previous work was limited to 2D, they don't provide a clear metric showing the degree of improvement in 3D (e.g., how much more stable, efficient, or diverse the behaviors are compared to prior methods).

2. The paper doesn't adequately address the scalability of their approach. Training takes "on the order of hours" using cloud resources (section 6), but they don't discuss how this scales to more complex systems or larger character morphologies. The computational requirements for training would be a major factor for practical applications.

3. The paper doesn't sufficiently compare their method to contemporary reinforcement learning approaches that also aim to learn control policies without task-specific features. For example, methods like PPO (Schulman et al., 2017) or DDPG (Lillicrap et al., 2015) have been used for similar locomotion tasks. A comparison would strengthen their claims of superiority.

4. The paper mentions "learning both optimal controls and a model of dynamics simultaneously" but doesn't clearly differentiate this from other approaches that learn dynamics models (e.g., [14]). The distinction is important for understanding the novelty.

5. The ablation study in section 8.1 shows the importance of noise injection, but the paper doesn't explain why their specific noise injection method (sensory noise with optimal feedback gains) works better than standard dropout or other regularization techniques.

Significance:
The method has clear potential applications in animation, robotics, and biomechanics. The ability to generate diverse, stable locomotion behaviors with a single architecture across character types represents a meaningful advance over task-specific controllers. The paper's claim that this "unifies disparate approaches" (section 1) is well-supported by their results.

Overall, this is a strong paper with a clear technical contribution that advances the state of the art in neural network-based control. The method is well-motivated, technically sound, and demonstrates impressive empirical results. The main weaknesses relate to insufficient comparison to contemporary approaches and inadequate discussion of computational scalability. With these points addressed, the paper would be an excellent contribution to NIPS.

Recommendation: Accept. The paper presents a significant technical advance with strong empirical results that would be valuable to the NIPS community. The minor weaknesses noted can be addressed in the camera-ready version.
