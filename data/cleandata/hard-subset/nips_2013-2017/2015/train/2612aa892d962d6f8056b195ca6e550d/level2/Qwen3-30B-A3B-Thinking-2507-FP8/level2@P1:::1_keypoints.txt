- Presents a method that interleaves trajectory optimization (Contact-Invariant Optimization) with stochastic neural network training, noise injection, and optimal feedback gains for robust real-time controllers.  
- Demonstrates a single neural network architecture producing diverse 3D locomotion behaviors (swimming, flying, bipedal, quadrupedal) without task-specific features or motion capture.  
- Major strength: unifies control across diverse morphologies and tasks, achieving stable 3D behaviors where prior work was limited to 2D.  
- Noise injection strategy and distributed optimization effectively reduce overfitting and improve scalability, supported by ablation studies.  
- Joint optimization with noise yields controllers that generalize ~100× better than naive training.  
- Trajectory optimization details and practical implementation (AWS/GPU) are clearly explained and reproducible.  
- High significance: enables interactive character control for animation, robotics, and biomechanics, reducing need for hand-designed controllers.  
- Weakness: limited contextualization and quantitative comparison to modern direct RL baselines (e.g., PPO), with no task success/stability metrics versus RL.  
- Weakness: substantial computational cost (2.5 hours on 30 nodes) may limit real-world adoption; authors note it is offline but could discuss inference/hardware optimizations.  
- Originality: novel synthesis of trajectory optimization and neural networks to achieve interactive 3D control, extending prior Contact-Invariant Optimization work.  
- Clarity: well-written with clear mathematical and implementation details; ablation and hyperparameter analyses are strong.  
- Minor clarity improvements suggested: define contact-invariant optimization earlier and explicitly compare task complexity versus prior 2D work.  
- Recommendation: accept with minor revisions to expand RL comparison and discuss computational trade-offs.  
- Pro/Con summary: Pro—unified 3D controller, robust noise injection, strong ablations, practical implementation; Con—limited RL comparison, high training cost, limited discussion of scaling to larger tasks.
