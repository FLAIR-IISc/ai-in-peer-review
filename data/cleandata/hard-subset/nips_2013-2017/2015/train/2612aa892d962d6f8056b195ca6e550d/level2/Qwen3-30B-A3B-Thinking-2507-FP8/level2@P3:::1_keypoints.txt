- Introduces a method combining contact-invariant trajectory optimization with stochastic neural network regression for interactive 3D character locomotion.  
- Core innovation: a distributed optimization framework that jointly learns a policy and optimal feedback gains while injecting noise during training.  
- Demonstrates stable, realistic behaviors for swimming, flying, bipedal, and quadrupedal characters using a single network architecture without task-specific features.  
- Technical contribution 1: principled noise-injection scheme that leverages trajectory optimization to compute robustness-providing feedback gains.  
- Technical contribution 2: distributed training algorithm enabling efficient scaling.  
- Technical contribution 3: empirical validation across four distinct 3D locomotion tasks.  
- Strength: well-motivated approach with rigorous evaluation and comprehensive ablation studies showing orders-of-magnitude gains from joint optimization and noise injection.  
- Strength: strong generality—handles diverse morphologies with identical architecture, improving over prior task-specific controllers.  
- Strength: novel theoretical derivation of feedback gains via perturbation that reuses trajectory optimization computations.  
- Strength: implementation details (runtime, hyperparameters) support reproducibility; supplementary video corroborates realism.  
- Weakness: lacks explicit comparison to model-based MPC and demonstration of why resulting trajectories are more "natural."  
- Weakness: does not quantify the real-time performance advantage (e.g., inference latency vs. MPC horizon).  
- Weakness: missing discussion on tuning and generalization of the noise parameter σ_ε = 1e-2.  
- Weakness: ambiguous phrasing that the network "learns dynamics under an implicit policy"—should clarify velocity-action mapping and implicit encoding via trajectory constraints.  
- Recommendation: add inference speed vs. MPC comparison, expand Section 2.1 on noise parameter selection, and clarify the "implicit policy" wording in the Abstract.
