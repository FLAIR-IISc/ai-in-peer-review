This paper presents a unified method for training neural network-based feedback controllers for a wide range of complex, physically-simulated characters. The core idea is to leverage an offline trajectory optimizer to generate training data for a recurrent neural network (RNN) policy. The training is framed as a large-scale, joint optimization problem over both the trajectories and the network parameters. This problem is solved using a distributed, block-alternating optimization scheme that alternates between refining the trajectories and performing supervised learning updates on the network.

Key contributions of the work include a principled method for injecting noise during training to improve robustness, which involves augmenting the training targets using optimal feedback gains. A novel and efficient method for extracting these feedback gains directly from the direct trajectory optimizer is also introduced. The approach is demonstrated on an impressive variety of 3D characters, including swimming, flying, bipedal, and quadrupedal morphologies, successfully generating stable and interactive locomotion policies from a single, general-purpose architecture without task-specific engineering. This work builds upon recent efforts to combine trajectory optimization with policy learning (e.g., Levine & Koltun, 2014; Mordatch & Todorov, 2014), but makes a significant step forward by successfully tackling more complex, unstable 3D locomotion problems and introducing several technical innovations that appear crucial for this success.

**Strengths (Arguments for acceptance):**

*   **Significance and Generality:** The paper addresses a central and challenging problem in control, robotics, and animation. The ability to automatically synthesize high-quality, interactive controllers for such a diverse set of morphologies using a single, unified algorithm is a substantial achievement. It represents a significant advance towards the goal of creating "universal" methods for motor control. The emergent behaviors, such as natural walking and trotting gaits, highlight the power of the approach.
*   **Technical Novelty and Quality:** The proposed framework is technically sound and contains several novel contributions. The method for computing optimal feedback gains (Section 3.2) from a direct trajectory optimization method is an elegant and broadly useful contribution. Furthermore, using these gains to create appropriate targets for noise-perturbed inputs is a principled approach to robust policy learning that is more sophisticated than simply adding noise to actions. The ablation studies in Section 8.1 provide compelling evidence that both the joint optimization and the noise injection schemes are critical to the method's success.
*   **Clarity and Empirical Results:** The paper is well-written and the methodology is clearly explained. The results, particularly as demonstrated in the supplementary video, are highly convincing and showcase the quality and stability of the learned controllers. The comparison with Model-Predictive Control (MPC) in Section 8.1 provides an interesting insight, suggesting that the policy learning component acts as a valuable regularizer, encouraging the discovery of more periodic and natural-looking solutions.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

The work is very strong, and the following points are primarily suggestions for clarification and strengthening the presentation, which could likely be addressed in a final version.

*   **Clarity on State-Action Representation:** The formulation of the state `s_t` and action `a_t` in Section 2 could be clarified. The action `a_t = [ q̇t, ṙt, f t ]` seems to define the velocity and forces at the current timestep `t`, which are outcomes of the dynamics rather than control inputs. Section 7 clarifies that the network output is a *desired* velocity, which is then tracked by a one-step optimization. Clarifying this "inner loop" tracking controller versus the "outer loop" policy earlier in the paper would improve readability. The statement that the network learns "optimal controls and a model of dynamics simultaneously" is intriguing and could be expanded upon.
*   **Details for Reproducibility:** While the paper provides a good overview, additional details would benefit reproducibility. The exact form of the cost functions `C(X)` used in trajectory optimization (e.g., weighting of task vs. effort terms) would be valuable. Similarly, more detail on the training data generation strategy (Section 5), such as the distribution from which new goals are sampled when a trajectory is "branched," would be helpful.
*   **Discussion of RNN State Update:** The action vector includes `ṙt`, the time derivative of the recurrent state. This suggests an explicit integration scheme for the recurrent state, which is a non-standard treatment. Typically, recurrent states are updated via a learned transition function within the network. A brief clarification of how `r` is updated based on the network's output `ṙt` would be beneficial.
*   **Context with Respect to Model-Free RL:** The paper rightly positions itself relative to other trajectory optimization-based methods. A slightly more detailed discussion on the potential trade-offs compared to concurrent model-free reinforcement learning approaches (e.g., TRPO) could further strengthen the paper's positioning. For example, discussing the respective roles of a powerful dynamics model and optimizer versus the sample-efficiency challenges of model-free methods would provide valuable context for the reader.

Overall, this is an excellent paper presenting a powerful and general method for synthesizing controllers for complex systems. The technical contributions are significant and the empirical results are impressive. The weaknesses are minor and relate mostly to points of clarification. The work is a clear and valuable contribution to the field.
