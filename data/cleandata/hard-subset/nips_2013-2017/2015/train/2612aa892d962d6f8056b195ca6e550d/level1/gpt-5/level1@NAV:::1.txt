This paper proposes an alternating optimization framework that trains recurrent neural network controllers to act as near-optimal feedback policies by interleaving direct trajectory optimization (Contact-Invariant Optimization, CIO) with supervised regression on noisy state-action pairs. The action space is chosen as desired velocities and contact forces, with a small recurrent state providing memory. A key contribution is a principled way to inject sensory noise during training while adjusting targets using locally optimal feedback gains computed efficiently from the direct trajectory optimizer via a perturbation/Woodbury trick. A distributed, asynchronous training architecture is described. Results show real-time, stable behaviors for swimming, flying, and 3D bipedal/quadrupedal locomotion without motion capture, hand-crafted state machines, or contact schedules. The work builds on and extends trajectory-optimization-guided policy learning (e.g., Levine & Koltun ICML’14; Mordatch & Todorov RSS’14), relates to model-free policy search (e.g., TRPO, Schulman et al.), and contrasts with MPC and graphics controllers (e.g., SIMBICON).

Quality: The method is technically sound and well-motivated. The derivation of feedback gains for a direct optimizer is a useful advance and appears to be implemented carefully. Ablations (no joint optimization, no noise) support design choices. However, quantitative evaluation is limited: there is little reporting of success rates, energy usage, disturbance rejection, or generalization beyond target changes; the comparison to MPC is qualitative; and stability guarantees are absent. The execution-time “T=1” optimization with soft dynamics (allowing root forces) raises questions about physical fidelity and transfer.

Clarity: The paper is generally clear and well-organized, with a precise problem statement and algorithm. Some details are missing: dimensions of recurrent state, exact network hyperparameters beyond width/depth, feedback gain regularization (λ) sensitivity, and timing/inference rates. Minor typos exist.

Originality: While conceptually aligned with guided policy search and CIO, the specific integration, especially the efficient computation of local gains for noisy supervision and application to diverse 3D morphologies with a single architecture, is novel.

Significance: Demonstrations are compelling for animation and model-based control. If strengthened with quantitative benchmarks and stricter dynamics enforcement, the approach could influence robust control for complex systems. Real-world robotics is speculative due to model reliance.

Pros for acceptance:
- Elegant, practical integration of CIO with NN policy learning and a novel feedback-gain computation.
- Strong, diverse 3D demonstrations with ablations showing necessity of components.
- Clear potential impact in graphics and control.

Cons for acceptance:
- Limited quantitative evaluation and weak baselines versus state-of-the-art model-free methods and MPC.
- Soft dynamics and action-as-velocity may reduce physical realism and transferability.
- Missing details affecting reproducibility.

Overall: Leaning toward accept, contingent on adding quantitative metrics, stronger baselines, and clarifying execution-time dynamics.
