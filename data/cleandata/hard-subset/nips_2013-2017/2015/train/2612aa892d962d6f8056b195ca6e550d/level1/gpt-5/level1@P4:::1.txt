Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper presents a method to train recurrent neural networks (RNNs) to act as near-optimal feedback controllers for complex, continuous-control characters (swimming, flying, bipedal and quadrupedal locomotion) in 3D. The key idea is to alternate between (i) direct trajectory optimization via Contact-Invariant Optimization (CIO) to generate optimal trajectories, actions, and local feedback gains; and (ii) supervised regression to fit a neural policy to (noisy) sensory inputs and corresponding (gain-adjusted) actions. Noise is injected in the sensory inputs and hidden activations to improve robustness. A distributed, asynchronous architecture supports large-scale data generation and learning. At execution time, the learned policy outputs desired velocities, contact forces, and recurrent updates; the next state is obtained by solving a one-step constrained optimization enforcing dynamics softly.

This work builds on and extends “learning from trajectory optimization” approaches such as Guided Policy Search and related work [Levine & Koltun ‘14; Mordatch & Todorov ‘14], but targets harder 3D control tasks with significant contact and underactuation, uses CIO to synthesize diverse behaviors without motion capture, and introduces a perturbation-based method to compute local feedback gains directly from the trajectory optimization quantities. Compared to model-predictive control (MPC), the learned policy aims to regularize toward predictable, cyclic behaviors while remaining interactive.

Quality:
- Technical soundness: The alternating optimization is well-motivated: CIO produces trajectories and local feedback gains; supervised learning regresses a stochastic policy with noise-consistent targets a + As ε. The derivation of feedback gains via a soft-constrained perturbation (using the Woodbury identity to reuse the Gauss-Newton inverse) is elegant and computationally efficient. The distributed asynchronous scheme is stabilized via proximal terms. Execution via a T=1 optimization step is consistent with the training representation (action as desired velocity and forces), and likely contributes to stability.
- Empirical support: The paper demonstrates a range of 3D characters and behaviors; ablations show the importance of joint optimization and noise injection; a small architecture study is provided. However, quantitative evaluation is limited: metrics (e.g., success rates, stability margins, energy usage, tracking errors, robustness to perturbations) are sparse, and comparisons to alternative policy-learning methods (e.g., GPS, TRPO) are not reported beyond qualitative discussion and a single MPC comparison.
- Limitations: Dynamics constraints at execution are enforced softly, which can introduce artificial root forces and weaken claims of physical realism. The policy targets are “desired velocities/forces” rather than torques; the controller thus combines a learned policy with an online constrained step, blurring attribution between learned control and online optimization. The recurrent state design is under-specified (size, role, and ablation). Hyperparameter selection (e.g., λ for gain softness, noise scales) and sensitivity are not analyzed. Real-time performance is claimed but not quantified.

Clarity:
The paper is generally clear and well-organized. The alternating optimization objective and algorithm are described precisely. The feedback gain computation is a useful and clearly presented contribution. Some aspects could be clarified:
- Explicit definitions of the state features φ and task costs (now deferred to section 8) would aid reproducibility.
- More detail on the recurrent state (dimension, initialization, learned dynamics) and its effect on performance.
- Clear, consistent reporting of training/test metrics (what constitutes “two orders of magnitude better”?).
- Execution-time runtime per control step and latency across all character types.

Originality:
Moderate to strong. Combining CIO with policy learning is known (e.g., GPS), but the paper contributes:
- A direct method to extract stabilizing feedback gains from a direct trajectory optimizer without a separate LQG pass.
- An alternating, asynchronous, proximal regularized optimization that scales across many characters and tasks.
- Demonstrations of diverse, stable 3D behaviors without motion capture or task-specific state machines.
The “action as desired velocities and forces” formulation coupled with a T=1 optimization is a specific and practically effective design choice in graphics/control contexts.

Significance:
High for physics-based character control and potentially relevant to robotics (with caveats about soft constraints and root forces). The work demonstrates that trajectory-optimization-informed neural policies can achieve robust, interactive control across varied morphologies and contact-rich dynamics, advancing beyond prior 2D or more constrained demonstrations. The feedback-gain extraction method is of independent interest for practitioners using direct trajectory optimizers.

Strengths:
- Unified framework producing interactive controllers for diverse 3D characters without motion capture or state machines.
- Principled integration of noise with local feedback targets improves robustness.
- Efficient, reusable computation of local feedback gains from CIO.
- Scalable distributed training; practical engineering enabling a nontrivial dataset.
- Clear ablation evidence that joint optimization and noise matter.

Weaknesses:
- Limited quantitative evaluation and baseline comparisons to contemporary policy learning (e.g., GPS/TRPO).
- Reliance on soft dynamics constraints and root forces undermines claims of strict physical realism.
- Under-specified recurrent component and limited analysis of its contribution.
- Lack of robustness tests to disturbances, model error, and environment variation.
- Execution-time computational costs and latency not reported.
- Some conceptual ambiguity: the “policy” outputs desired velocities/forces while a separate optimization enforces dynamics.

Questions and suggestions:
- Provide quantitative metrics across tasks: success rate, fall rate, distance-to-goal, energy/torque costs, and response to external perturbations.
- Compare to GPS/TRPO on at least one task with matched observation/action spaces and costs.
- Report execution-time performance (ms per control step) and GPU/CPU usage.
- Analyze sensitivity to λ, σε, σγ; include ablations on recurrent unit count and effect of removing r.
- Quantify the impact of soft dynamics enforcement and root forces; if possible, present a variant with stricter constraints.
- Detail network architecture per task, recurrent size, and training dataset sizes.
- Clarify the evaluation metric used in Figure 2 and Table 1.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Demonstrates robust, interactive 3D control across diverse morphologies without task-specific engineering.
- Introduces a practical and general method to extract stabilizing gains from direct trajectory optimization.
- Sound, scalable training procedure with clear ablation support.
- Advances the state of the art in neural control for complex physics-based characters.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Insufficient quantitative comparisons to strong baselines (GPS/TRPO) and lack of standardized metrics.
- Use of soft constraints and root forces raises concerns about physical fidelity and transferability to real robots.
- Under-documented recurrent component and hyperparameter sensitivity.
- Execution-time performance and robustness not rigorously evaluated.

Overall, the paper offers a coherent and practically valuable approach that meaningfully extends trajectory-optimization-guided policy learning to challenging 3D character control, though a stronger quantitative evaluation and baseline comparison would further solidify the contribution.
