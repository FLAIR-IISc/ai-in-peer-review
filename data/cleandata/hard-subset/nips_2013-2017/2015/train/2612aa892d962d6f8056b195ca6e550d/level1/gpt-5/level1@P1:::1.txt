Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper proposes a method to train recurrent neural networks (RNNs) as near-optimal feedback controllers for diverse, high-DOF dynamical systems (swimming, flying, biped and quadruped locomotion) with a single architecture and training pipeline. The key idea is to interleave direct trajectory optimization (Contact-Invariant Optimization, CIO) with supervised learning of a stochastic policy: the network observes noisy sensory inputs and is trained to output both nominal actions and local linear feedback corrections a + As ε, where As are optimal feedback gains computed efficiently from the trajectory optimizer via a perturbation method that reuses the Gauss-Newton inverse Hessian. The action is chosen to be desired velocities/forces and recurrent-state rates, and real-time execution solves a horizon-1 constrained optimization (implicit integration) to satisfy dynamics while tracking the network’s outputs. Training uses alternating block optimization with proximal regularizers, input/hidden noise for robustness, and asynchronous distributed execution (many CPU nodes for CIO, one GPU for NN).

This work builds on “trajectory optimization + function approximation”/guided policy search lines (e.g., Levine & Koltun ICML’14; Mordatch & Todorov RSS’14) by (i) handling full 3D locomotion with multiple morphologies, (ii) introducing principled input noise with corresponding optimal corrective targets using gains extracted from a direct trajectory optimizer (rather than an LQG pass), and (iii) an asynchronous proximal alternating scheme. It contrasts with model-free deep RL (e.g., TRPO, DQN) and prior graphics controllers relying on hand-crafted state machines or simplified models. The execution strategy resembles feature-based MPC but with a learned policy regularizing the solution toward cyclic, stable gaits.

Quality:
- Technical soundness: The alternating optimization objective is clearly specified, with a quadratic penalty tying trajectories to the policy and proximal terms for stability. The perturbation-based derivation of feedback gains leveraging the Gauss-Newton system and Woodbury identity is a useful contribution and appears correct; importantly it avoids ill-conditioned gains sometimes seen in LQG passes on different objectives. The execution scheme (T=1 optimization under dynamics) is sensible and practically stable with implicit enforcement.
- Empirical support: Results are primarily qualitative but compelling (multiple 3D morphologies; stable gaits emerge without hand-engineered contact schedules). Ablations show that (a) joint optimization versus static datasets and (b) noise injection both matter for test-time stability; a small architecture study is included. The comparison to MPC is interesting, showing the learned policy regularizes solutions toward more predictable, cyclic motions.
- Limitations: Quantitative evaluation is sparse. There are no metrics on success rate, robustness to perturbations, energy cost, speed, or user-interaction latency; comparisons to strong model-free baselines (e.g., TRPO) or to guided policy search variants are missing. The method allows soft dynamics (including root forces), which can compromise physical realism. The recurrent state dimension and its role are not analyzed. Generalization beyond the training distribution (e.g., to large goal changes or novel terrains) is not quantified.

Clarity:
The paper is generally well organized and readable. The algorithm, objective, and execution pipeline are clearly described. However, several clarifications would improve reproducibility:
- Precisely specify the recurrent state dimension, its initialization, and ablations with/without recurrence.
- Detail task costs (weights for torque, contact force penalties, contact-invariant terms) and trajectory lengths.
- Report the exact state/action vector sizes, and whether reference frames are root-relative/goal-relative for all features.
- Provide runtime numbers (policy forward pass and T=1 solve) per control step across characters.
- Expand on stability of the asynchronous alternating scheme (e.g., sensitivity to proximal weight η, noise σ).

Originality:
The overall paradigm (learn policies from trajectory optimization) is not new, but the paper offers several novel and practically important elements:
- A general, scalable pipeline that demonstrably handles 3D contact-rich locomotion across morphologies without task-specific state machines or mocap.
- A principled way to obtain stabilizing feedback gains directly from a direct optimizer’s linearization, avoiding a mismatched LQG post-pass.
- Stochastic supervised targets (a + Asε) tied to injected sensory noise, aligning data augmentation with local optimal feedback.
- An asynchronous, proximal alternating scheme suitable for distributed training.

These advances are incremental relative to GPS-style methods but collectively enable a qualitatively more challenging set of behaviors than prior 2D demonstrations.

Significance:
For graphics/animation, this is a valuable step toward automatic, interactive controllers without hand-engineered gait generators. For control/RL, it strengthens the case for combining trajectory optimization and deep networks, and the feedback-gain extraction is of independent interest. Transfer to real robots is less clear due to reliance on accurate models, soft contact constraints, and action definitions (desired velocities/forces with an inner optimization loop). Nonetheless, the approach is likely to influence subsequent work on robust policy learning from model-based supervision.

Strengths:
- Unified controller training across diverse 3D characters; no state machines or mocap.
- Elegant integration of stochastic supervision with optimal feedback gains computed from direct trajectory optimization.
- Practical, asynchronous distributed system; training completes in hours.
- Clear ablations supporting key design choices; meaningful MPC comparison.

Weaknesses and questions:
- Limited quantitative evaluation and baseline comparisons; no robustness metrics.
- Use of soft dynamics (root forces) may reduce physical fidelity; discuss magnitude and impact.
- Execution still relies on an optimization at every step; analyze failure modes and cost if constraints are hardened or removed.
- Action as desired velocity/force vs. torque: discuss implications for real hardware and whether torque-level policies are feasible with this pipeline.
- Clarify recurrent state usage and necessity; provide ablations and interpretations.
- Sensitivity analyses for λ (feedback softness), σ parameters, and proximal weight η are missing.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Demonstrates a practically impactful, general solution to a challenging class of control problems, with convincing qualitative breadth.
- Introduces a neat and reusable technique for extracting stabilizing gains from direct trajectory optimizers.
- Advances the state of practice in combining trajectory optimization and deep nets; likely to be adopted by both graphics and RL/control communities.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Insufficient quantitative evaluation and lack of comparisons to contemporary policy learning (e.g., TRPO/GPS variants).
- Some reliance on soft physics and a per-step optimization at execution limits claims of pure “policy control” and physical realism.
- Novelty is partly incremental relative to guided policy search; contributions could be better isolated and measured.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Add quantitative metrics (success rate, average cost, disturbance recovery) and comparisons to TRPO/GPS on at least one morphology.
- Report the magnitude of root forces and results with harder dynamics constraints.
- Provide ablations on recurrent state, λ, σ, and η; include sensitivity plots.
- Discuss feasibility of torque-level execution and hardware transfer.

Overall: Strong, well-executed paper with solid methodological ideas and impressive demonstrations; I lean toward acceptance contingent on strengthening quantitative evaluation and clarifying execution fidelity.
