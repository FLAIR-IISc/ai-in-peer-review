This paper proposes a novel method for training recurrent neural networks to act as near-optimal feedback controllers for a range of dynamical systems and tasks. The authors present a framework that combines supervised learning with trajectory optimization, specifically Contact-Invariant Optimization (CIO), to generate stable and realistic behaviors for complex characters. The method does not require motion capture or task-specific features or state machines, making it a significant contribution to the field.

The paper is well-written, and the authors provide a clear and concise overview of their approach. The use of figures and equations helps to illustrate the methodology and make it easier to understand. The authors also provide a detailed description of their experiments, including the characters and tasks used, and the results are presented in a clear and organized manner.

One of the strengths of the paper is its ability to generate realistic and stable behaviors for complex characters, including swimming, flying, biped, and quadruped walking. The authors demonstrate that their method can learn successful controllers for these characters, even in the presence of noise and perturbations. The use of noise injection during training is a key aspect of the method, and the authors provide a clear explanation of how this helps to improve the robustness of the controllers.

The authors also provide a detailed comparison of their method with other approaches, including naive neural network training on a static optimal trajectory dataset and model-predictive control (MPC). The results show that their method performs significantly better than these approaches, particularly in terms of stability and realism.

However, there are some limitations to the paper. One of the main limitations is the reliance on a specific trajectory optimization method (CIO), which may not be suitable for all types of characters or tasks. Additionally, the authors do not provide a detailed analysis of the computational cost of their method, which could be a significant factor in its practical application.

In terms of originality, the paper presents a novel combination of supervised learning and trajectory optimization, which is a significant contribution to the field. The authors also provide a new method for generating optimal feedback gains, which is an important aspect of their approach.

The significance of the paper is high, as it presents a new approach to generating realistic and stable behaviors for complex characters. The method has the potential to be applied to a wide range of fields, including robotics, animation, and gaming.

Overall, the paper is well-written, and the authors provide a clear and concise overview of their approach. The method is significant, and the results are impressive. However, there are some limitations to the paper, and the authors could provide more detail on the computational cost and the applicability of their method to different types of characters and tasks.
