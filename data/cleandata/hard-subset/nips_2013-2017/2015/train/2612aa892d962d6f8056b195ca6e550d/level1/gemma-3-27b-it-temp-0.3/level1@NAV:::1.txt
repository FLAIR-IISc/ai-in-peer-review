## Review of "Interactive Control of Diverse Complex Characters with Neural Networks"

This paper presents a method for training recurrent neural networks to act as feedback controllers for a variety of dynamic systems, specifically focusing on character locomotion. The core idea is to combine supervised learning with trajectory optimization (CIO) to learn a control policy that is both stable and realistic. The authors demonstrate successful control of swimming, flying, bipedal, and quadrupedal characters using a single network architecture, a significant achievement given the challenges of controlling complex, 3D dynamics. This work builds upon previous efforts in reinforcement learning and trajectory optimization (e.g., Levine & Koltun, 2014; Mordatch et al., 2012), but distinguishes itself by tackling more challenging, high-dimensional problems and achieving interactive control. The approach addresses the difficulty of obtaining training data for controllers by leveraging trajectory optimization to generate it, and mitigates overfitting through noise injection and proximal regularization.

**Strengths:** The paper’s primary strength lies in its impressive empirical results. Achieving stable and realistic locomotion across such a diverse range of character morphologies with a single controller is a significant accomplishment. The combination of trajectory optimization and neural network learning is a clever approach, effectively leveraging the strengths of both techniques. The inclusion of noise injection and optimal feedback gain calculation further enhances the robustness and performance of the learned policies. The authors clearly articulate the challenges of learning controllers compared to other deep learning tasks and provide a well-motivated solution. The distributed training architecture is also a practical consideration for scaling to these complex problems.

**Weaknesses:** While the results are compelling, the paper lacks a more thorough comparison to existing direct methods for neural network controller training (e.g., those cited in the introduction). The authors state their impression that direct methods yield “somewhat chaotic behaviors,” but a quantitative comparison would strengthen this claim. The ablation studies (Figure 2) are helpful, but could be expanded to include variations in network architecture or training parameters. The explanation of the mathematical formulation, while present, could benefit from further clarification, particularly regarding the interplay between trajectory optimization and neural network regression. The reliance on MuJoCo as the physics simulator is a limitation, and the transferability of the learned policies to other simulators or real-world robots is not addressed.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Demonstrates a novel and effective approach to learning complex controllers.
*   Achieves impressive results on a challenging set of problems.
*   Combines existing techniques in a clever and impactful way.
*   Addresses a significant problem in robotics, animation, and biomechanics.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   Lacks a comprehensive comparison to alternative methods.
*   Mathematical formulation could be more clearly explained.
*   Limited discussion of transferability to other environments.



**Overall:** This is a strong paper with significant potential. The impressive empirical results and novel combination of techniques outweigh the minor weaknesses. I recommend acceptance, with a request for the authors to consider expanding the comparative evaluation and clarifying the mathematical details in a revised version.
