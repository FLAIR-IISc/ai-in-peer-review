**Summary**

This paper presents a method for training a single recurrent neural network (RNN) architecture to act as a real-time, interactive feedback controller for a diverse set of complex, physically-simulated characters. The tasks include swimming, flying, bipedal walking, and quadrupedal locomotion. The core contribution is a training paradigm that jointly optimizes a set of motion trajectories and the parameters of the neural network controller. Specifically, the method alternates between two steps: 1) using a powerful offline trajectory optimizer (Contact-Invariant Optimization, CIO) to generate physically realistic and task-oriented trajectories, and 2) training the neural network via supervised regression to mimic the state-action pairs from these trajectories.

The work builds upon prior efforts that combine trajectory optimization with neural network learning [9, 11], but extends them to significantly more challenging 3D locomotion problems. Key to its success are several technical innovations: a principled method for injecting noise into the network's sensory inputs during training to promote robustness; a novel technique to extract optimal feedback gains directly from the CIO optimizer, which are used to compute the correct target actions for the noisy inputs; and a distributed, asynchronous training architecture to manage the high computational load. The result is a unified framework capable of generating stable, realistic, and controllable behaviors for various morphologies without motion capture data or task-specific state machines.

**Quality**

The paper is of high technical quality. The proposed framework is well-reasoned and presents a complete and functional system. The claims of generating stable and realistic control for diverse characters are strongly supported by the experimental results, which are best appreciated in the supplementary video.

The technical components are sound. The formulation of the problem as a joint optimization (Eq. 4) that is solved via alternating minimization is a principled approach to a difficult problem. The method for extracting optimal feedback gains from a direct trajectory optimization method (Section 3.2) is particularly clever. Rather than running a separate LQR pass, it reuses the Hessian computed during the main optimization, framing the problem as finding the optimal response to a soft-constrained perturbation. This is an elegant and efficient solution.

The authors are careful in their evaluation. The ablation studies presented in Figure 2 are crucial and convincing. They clearly demonstrate that both the joint optimization (vs. training on a static dataset) and the noise injection are essential for success, preventing the policy from quickly diverging during execution. This provides strong evidence that the proposed components are not just incidental but are fundamental to the method's performance.

One minor point of ambiguity is in the policy execution step (Section 7). The paper states that evolving the physical state involves solving a single-step optimization problem at each frame to match the network's desired velocity output. While this is a valid technique reminiscent of feature-based control, it is more complex than a simple forward simulation based on network outputs (e.g., torques). This adds a non-trivial computational step at runtime and means the network is not the sole component of the final controller. While the authors state this can be solved at real-time rates, more detail on its complexity and performance would be beneficial.

**Clarity**

The paper is very well-written and organized. The introduction clearly motivates the problem and situates the work within the existing literature. The "Overview" section provides an excellent high-level summary of the entire method before diving into the details of each component, which greatly aids reader comprehension. The algorithm is presented clearly, and the mathematical notation is consistent. The figures and tables effectively support the text, especially the comparative evaluation in Figure 2.

While the overall method is clear, reproducing this work would be extremely difficult from the paper alone. The success of the method hinges on complex underlying components like the MuJoCo simulator and the CIO trajectory optimizer, coupled with a custom distributed training framework. More details on the specific cost functions used in trajectory optimization would also be helpful for those wishing to build on this work.

**Originality**

The paper demonstrates a high degree of originality. While the high-level concept of using trajectory optimization to generate training data for a neural network controller existed [9, 11], this work makes several novel and significant contributions:

1.  **Generality and Scale:** The most original contribution is the successful application of a *single, unified* framework to a wide and challenging array of 3D locomotion tasks. Prior work was largely confined to 2D characters. Demonstrating that this approach scales to 3D bipeds, quadrupeds, swimmers, and fliers is a major advance.
2.  **Principled Denoising:** The combination of injecting sensory noise and using analytically derived optimal feedback gains to compute the "correct" noisy action is a novel and principled way to train a robust policy. It is a smart adaptation of ideas from denoising autoencoders to the closed-loop control setting.
3.  **Feedback from Direct Optimization:** The method for extracting feedback gains from a direct trajectory optimizer is, to my knowledge, novel and a valuable contribution in its own right, with potential applications beyond this specific paper.

The work clearly distinguishes itself from prior contributions and adequately references related literature in both character animation and reinforcement learning.

**Significance**

The significance of this paper is very high. It represents a substantial step forward in the automatic synthesis of controllers for complex physical systems. By replacing hand-crafted state machines and motion capture data with a general-purpose optimization framework, it points the way toward a more automated and scalable future for creating physically-based interactive characters in animation, games, and robotics.

The results are important and impressive. The emergence of natural, cyclic gaits (e.g., walking, trotting) from general-purpose cost functions is a powerful demonstration of the method's capabilities. Researchers and practitioners in computer graphics and robotics are very likely to use and build upon these ideas. The work directly addresses the difficult problem of generating stable, high-DOF continuous control and advances the state of the art in a demonstrable way.

The main weakness in its claims is the comparison to reinforcement learning. The paper suggests its behaviors are "more realistic and purposeful" than those from "direct methods" (RL), but provides no direct comparison. While this was likely true for the state of RL in 2015, the claim is left unsubstantiated. Nevertheless, this does not detract from the paper's core contributions.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   **Pro:** Presents a powerful, general, and automated method for a very difficult problem: interactive control of diverse 3D characters.
*   **Pro:** The quality of the resulting motions is state-of-the-art, demonstrating stable and realistic gaits that emerge naturally from optimization.
*   **Pro:** Contains several novel and significant technical contributions, particularly the method for extracting feedback gains and the principled use of noise for robustification.
*   **Pro:** Claims are well-supported by strong empirical evidence, including thorough ablation studies.
*   **Con:** Claims regarding superiority over reinforcement learning methods are not experimentally verified.
*   **Con:** The computational cost of training is very high, potentially limiting practical adoption.
*   **Con:** The real-time execution step involves more than just a network forward pass, which could have been explained in more detail.

**Conclusion:**

This is an excellent paper presenting a significant advance in control synthesis for physics-based animation. The strengths in originality, quality, and significance far outweigh the minor weaknesses. It tackles a grand challenge problem with an elegant and effective solution, supported by impressive results and solid technical innovations. I strongly recommend acceptance.
