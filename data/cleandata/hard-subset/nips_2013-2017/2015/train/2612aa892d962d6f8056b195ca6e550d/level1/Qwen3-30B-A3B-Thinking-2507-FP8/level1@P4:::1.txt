

# Review of "Interactive Control of Diverse Complex Characters with Neural Networks"

This paper presents a method for training recurrent neural networks to act as near-optimal feedback controllers for diverse dynamical systems, including swimming, flying, biped, and quadruped locomotion in 3D. The authors combine supervised learning with trajectory optimization (specifically Contact-Invariant Optimization), introducing novel techniques including interleaved optimization, noise injection, and the calculation of optimal feedback gains as a byproduct of trajectory optimization. Unlike previous work that focused on 2D characters [9, 11], this method successfully achieves stable 3D locomotion across multiple character morphologies without motion capture or task-specific features.

The paper builds on prior trajectory optimization work [12] and recent neural network approaches to control [9, 11], but makes significant technical improvements that enable application to more complex 3D problems. The authors demonstrate that their method produces more realistic behaviors than direct neural network training approaches [3, 6, 14], which often yield "somewhat chaotic behaviors."

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound with well-justified methodology. The authors provide a clear mathematical formulation of their approach, including the constrained optimization problem (1) and its relaxed version (4). The ablation studies in Section 8.1 convincingly demonstrate the necessity of their key innovations (joint optimization and noise injection), showing that removing these components results in policies that quickly diverge. The trajectory optimization and feedback gain calculation methods (Sections 3.1-3.2) are well-motivated and theoretically grounded. The implementation details (Section 6) provide sufficient information for reproducibility. The paper acknowledges limitations, such as the soft enforcement of dynamics constraints in execution (Section 7).

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The main ideas are presented logically with clear explanations of the technical innovations. The mathematical formulations are precise and well-explained. The figures (especially Figure 1) effectively illustrate the range of behaviors achieved. The paper provides sufficient detail for an expert reader to understand and potentially reproduce the results. The authors clearly distinguish their approach from previous work and explain the rationale for their design choices.

**Originality:** While the combination of trajectory optimization and neural networks has been explored [9, 11], this paper makes several significant technical contributions that enable application to substantially harder problems. The method for calculating optimal feedback gains as a byproduct of trajectory optimization (Section 3.2) is novel and solves a key problem in adapting direct trajectory optimization methods to neural network training. The noise injection strategy (Section 2.1) and distributed training architecture (Section 6) are also significant contributions. The demonstration of a single network architecture controlling diverse 3D character behaviors (Section 8) represents a notable advancement over previous work limited to 2D.

**Significance:** The work addresses a fundamental challenge in character animation and robotics: creating controllers that work across diverse character morphologies without specialized hand-crafted controllers. The demonstrated behaviors (swimming, flying, bipedal, quadrupedal locomotion) are realistic and stable, with the same method applied across all character types. The paper convincingly shows that the method outperforms naive neural network training (Section 8.1) and produces more predictable behaviors than MPC (Section 8.1). The potential applications in robotics (Section 9) and animation make this work broadly significant. The unification of disparate control approaches (Section 1) represents a major conceptual contribution.

**Strengths:**
- Demonstrates successful 3D locomotion across diverse character types with a single architecture
- Provides rigorous ablation studies showing necessity of key innovations
- Introduces novel techniques for calculating feedback gains and injecting noise
- Well-motivated and technically sound approach
- Comprehensive experimental validation

**Weaknesses:**
- Limited discussion of computational requirements (training time, hardware) beyond stating "hours of training"
- No direct comparison with state-of-the-art reinforcement learning approaches (e.g., [14, 15])
- Noise parameters (σε = 10^-2, σγ = 10^-2) lack thorough justification
- Limited discussion of generalization to novel character morphologies not seen during training

**Recommendation:**
This paper makes a significant contribution to the field of neural network-based control for physical systems. The technical innovations are well-motivated and effectively demonstrated across a diverse set of challenging 3D locomotion tasks. The method represents a substantial advancement over previous work in both the complexity of problems solved and the generality of the approach. While the computational requirements are notable, the results demonstrate clear practical value for animation and robotics applications. The paper is technically sound, well-written, and makes a compelling case for the novelty and significance of its contributions.

The paper meets all four NIPS criteria: it is technically sound with strong experimental validation (Quality), clearly written with sufficient detail (Clarity), presents novel technical contributions that advance the state of the art (Originality), and addresses a significant problem with broad applications (Significance). The work is clearly different from previous NIPS papers, particularly in its successful application to 3D locomotion across multiple character types.

I recommend acceptance.
