### Review of "Interactive Control of Diverse Complex Characters with Neural Networks"

**Summary of the Paper**

This paper presents a method for training a recurrent neural network to serve as a real-time feedback controller for complex, physically-simulated characters. The core idea is to combine the strengths of offline trajectory optimization with supervised learning. The authors propose an iterative, alternating optimization scheme where: 1) a powerful offline trajectory optimizer (Contact-Invariant Optimization, CIO) generates optimal state-action trajectories for a variety of tasks (e.g., walking to a goal), and 2) a neural network is trained via regression to mimic the optimizer's behavior.

Crucially, this is not a simple two-step "generate then train" process. The two components are coupled: the trajectory optimizer's cost function includes a term that penalizes deviation from the current neural network policy, and the network is trained on data that is continuously refined by the optimizer. This joint optimization is designed to find solutions that are both optimal and learnable by a compact policy.

Key technical contributions that enable this method to work on complex 3D characters include:
1.  A principled method for injecting noise into the network's sensory inputs during training to improve robustness. This requires computing optimal feedback gains, and the authors derive a novel and efficient way to extract these gains from a direct trajectory optimization method like CIO.
2.  A training data generation scheme that simulates interactive use by branching trajectories towards new, randomly chosen goals.
3.  The use of a distributed, asynchronous architecture to manage the high computational cost.

This work builds upon previous efforts to combine trajectory optimization and policy learning, such as Levine & Koltun (2014) and Mordatch & Todorov (2014). However, the authors claim their technical improvements allow them to solve substantially harder problems, demonstrating successful control for a diverse set of 3D characters—including bipeds, quadrupeds, swimmers, and flyers—using a single, general-purpose algorithm.

**Strengths**

1.  **Significance and Impressive Results:** The primary strength of this paper is the high quality and diversity of the results. Successfully generating stable, realistic, and interactive gaits for 3D bipeds, quadrupeds, a swimmer, and a flyer from a single, unified framework is a significant achievement. This convincingly demonstrates the power and generality of the proposed approach, advancing the state of the art in physics-based character animation and control. The emergent behaviors (e.g., cyclic gaits, trotting, flapping) are compelling evidence that the method discovers effective control strategies without task-specific engineering.

2.  **Technical Quality and Novelty:** The paper is technically sound. The formulation of the joint optimization problem is clear, and the alternating minimization scheme is a sensible way to approach this difficult problem. The method for deriving well-conditioned feedback gains from the direct trajectory optimizer (Section 3.2) is a clever and valuable contribution in its own right. This technique is crucial for their noise injection strategy and could be useful for other applications that require stabilizing optimal trajectories.

3.  **Thorough Experimental Validation:** The authors provide a strong empirical case for their design choices. The ablation study presented in Figure 2 is particularly convincing. It clearly shows that both the joint optimization ("no joint") and the noise injection ("no noise") are essential for success; without them, the resulting policy is unstable and performs poorly. This rigorous analysis elevates the paper from a demonstration of a working system to a well-supported scientific contribution. The qualitative comparison with Model Predictive Control (MPC) also provides interesting insight into how the neural network policy acts as a regularizer, favoring more predictable, cyclic behaviors.

4.  **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and well-organized. The overview in Section 2 provides a clear, high-level picture of the optimization problem, and the subsequent sections successfully elaborate on the key components. The algorithm is presented logically, and the motivations for the main technical ideas are well-explained.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Limited Comparison to Alternative Methods:** While the comparison to a static training regime and an MPC baseline is useful, the paper would be stronger with a quantitative comparison to contemporary reinforcement learning (RL) methods. The introduction mentions that RL methods can produce "somewhat chaotic behaviors," but this claim is not substantiated with a direct comparison. By 2015, methods like Trust Region Policy Optimization (TRPO, cited as [14]) were emerging as powerful techniques for continuous control. A comparison would help to better situate the trade-offs between this "optimization-as-supervision" approach and more direct policy search methods.

2.  **Reproducibility Details:** While the overall method is described well, some of the finer details required for reproduction are omitted. Specifically, the exact formulation of the cost functions `c(φ)` used for trajectory optimization is described only at a high level ("minimizing torque usage and contact force magnitudes"). Given the sensitivity of trajectory optimization to cost function design, providing the precise terms and weights (perhaps in an appendix) would greatly improve the paper's value to other researchers.

3.  **Analysis of Recurrent Units:** The controller is described as a *recurrent* neural network, and the state includes a recurrent memory component `r`. However, the role and importance of this recurrence are not analyzed. Do the learned behaviors actually leverage this memory? An analysis of the recurrent state, or an ablation study comparing to a purely feed-forward (MLP) architecture, would be necessary to justify the "recurrent" aspect of the model and understand what it contributes. For the locomotion tasks shown, it is not immediately obvious that memory beyond the instantaneous physical state is required.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   **Pro:** The paper successfully tackles an extremely difficult problem in control and graphics: generating robust, interactive controllers for diverse 3D characters.
*   **Pro:** The results are state-of-the-art and visually compelling, representing a significant advance over prior work that was largely limited to 2D.
*   **Pro:** The methodology contains a novel and technically sound contribution for extracting feedback gains from direct trajectory optimization.
*   **Pro:** The claims are well-supported by strong ablation studies that demonstrate the importance of the key components of the algorithm (joint optimization and noise injection).

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   **Con:** The quantitative comparison to other state-of-the-art controller synthesis methods, particularly from the reinforcement learning literature, is missing.
*   **Con:** The paper lacks some details (e.g., precise cost functions) that would be needed for full reproducibility.
*   **Con:** The contribution and necessity of the recurrent components of the network are not evaluated or discussed.

**Recommendation**

Overall, the strengths of this paper far outweigh its weaknesses. It presents a powerful, general framework for a challenging and important problem, supported by impressive results and solid technical contributions. This work is highly significant and is likely to influence future research in character animation, robotics, and reinforcement learning. I strongly recommend acceptance.
