### **Summary of Main Ideas**

This paper presents a unified framework for training a recurrent neural network (RNN) to act as a real-time, interactive feedback controller for a wide variety of complex, physically simulated characters. The method successfully generates stable and realistic locomotion for swimmers, flyers, bipeds, and quadrupeds using the same network architecture and training algorithm, without requiring motion capture data or task-specific engineering.

The core of the method is an iterative, alternating optimization scheme that combines the strengths of offline trajectory optimization and supervised learning. In one phase, a powerful trajectory optimizer, Contact-Invariant Optimization (CIO), generates a set of optimal state-action trajectories for various locomotion tasks. In the other phase, an RNN policy is trained via regression to mimic the optimizer's behavior. Crucially, this is not a simple two-stage process. The policy and the trajectories are optimized jointly: the optimizer is regularized to produce trajectories that the current policy can easily replicate, and the policy is then updated on this new data.

The authors introduce several key technical contributions that enable this approach to succeed on complex 3D characters where previous work was limited to 2D. First, to ensure the resulting policy is robust to perturbations and does not diverge in a closed loop, they inject noise into the sensory inputs during training. Second, and most notably, they propose a principled way to determine the optimal action for these noisy inputs. Instead of just using the original action, they use a first-order approximation involving optimal feedback gains. They derive a novel and efficient method to compute these gains directly from the CIO optimizer, a significant contribution as direct optimization methods typically do not provide such gains. Finally, the entire system is implemented in a distributed architecture to make the substantial computational cost of trajectory optimization manageable.

This work sits within the domain of guided policy search and builds upon recent work like Levine & Koltun (2014) and Mordatch & Todorov (2014). However, it advances the state of the art by demonstrating success on a much more challenging and diverse set of 3D locomotion problems, enabled by the aforementioned technical improvements. It offers a compelling alternative to traditional animation methods (e.g., state machines, motion capture) and direct reinforcement learning approaches, which the authors argue often produce less realistic or "chaotic" behaviors.

### **Strengths and Weaknesses**

#### **Quality**

The paper is of high technical quality. The proposed alternating optimization framework is well-motivated, and the claims are strongly supported by impressive experimental results. The ablation study in Figure 2 provides convincing evidence for the necessity of both the joint optimization and the noise injection scheme; without them, the policy fails dramatically. The method for extracting optimal feedback gains from a direct trajectory optimizer (Section 3.2) is a clever and sound technical contribution that addresses a key challenge in making this approach robust. The work is presented as a complete system, including details on the distributed training architecture, which speaks to its maturity.

#### **Clarity**

The paper is very well-written and organized. The overview in Section 2 provides an excellent high-level summary of the optimization problem before diving into the details. The algorithm is presented clearly, and the motivations behind key design decisions (e.g., noise injection, feedback gains) are well explained.

A minor point of confusion is the state and action representation. The action `a_t = [ q̇_t, ṙ_t, f_t ]` contains the velocity for the current timestep, which seems more like a part of the state transition than a control input like torque. The authors state this means the network learns "optimal controls and a model of dynamics simultaneously," which is an interesting perspective. Section 7 clarifies that at runtime, this network output is used as a target for a one-step optimization to find the actual controls. This is an elegant solution, but this relationship could be foreshadowed more clearly in Section 2 to avoid potential confusion for the reader.

#### **Originality**

While the paper builds on the existing paradigm of combining trajectory optimization with policy learning, it makes several novel contributions. The primary one is the method for computing well-conditioned feedback gains from a direct optimizer like CIO and incorporating them into a principled noise-robust training scheme. This is a significant step beyond prior work and appears to be a key enabler for the success in 3D. The demonstration of a single, unified framework that can generate such a diverse set of complex, emergent behaviors (e.g., gaits for walking, flapping for flying) without behavior-specific priors is highly original and impactful.

#### **Significance**

The significance of this work is very high. It represents a substantial advancement in automated controller synthesis for physically-based animation and robotics. The ability to generate high-quality, interactive controllers for arbitrary character morphologies from only a physical model and a high-level task description is a long-standing goal in the field. The results, as shown in the supplementary video, are state-of-the-art for the time and demonstrate a level of generality and quality that is likely to inspire a great deal of follow-up work. The techniques, especially the feedback gain extraction and robust training procedure, are applicable to a broader range of problems in optimal control and reinforcement learning.

One potential weakness is the lack of direct comparison to contemporary pure reinforcement learning methods (e.g., policy gradient methods). The authors claim that such methods produce "chaotic" behaviors, which is a plausible hypothesis, but an empirical comparison would have made the paper's claims even stronger. Furthermore, the method relies on a powerful but computationally expensive trajectory optimizer; the paper could benefit from a brief discussion on the limitations of this dependency, such as sensitivity to the optimizer's local minima or its computational cost (2.5 hours on a ~30-node cluster).

### **Arguments For and Against Acceptance**

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
1.  **High Significance and Impact:** The paper addresses a major problem in control and animation and presents a general, powerful solution with state-of-the-art results.
2.  **Novel Technical Contributions:** The method for computing and utilizing optimal feedback gains for robust policy training is a key innovation.
3.  **Strong Empirical Validation:** The diversity of the controlled characters and the thorough ablation study provide compelling evidence for the method's effectiveness and the importance of its components.
4.  **Clarity and Completeness:** The paper is well-written, the method is clearly explained, and the work is presented as a complete, functional system.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**
1.  **High Computational Cost:** The training process requires a substantial distributed computing setup, which may limit its accessibility.
2.  **Limited Baselines:** The paper would be strengthened by a direct quantitative comparison to a contemporary reinforcement learning algorithm on at least one of the tasks.

Overall, the strengths of this paper far outweigh its minor weaknesses. It is a landmark paper in the field of character animation and guided policy search.
