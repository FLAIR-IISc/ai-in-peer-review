- Summary: Proposes training RNN policies as near-optimal feedback controllers by alternating direct trajectory optimization (CIO) with supervised NN regression, injecting noise for robustness.
- Summary: Uses a distributed asynchronous loop with proximal penalties; actions are desired velocities/contact forces executed via a T=1 inverse-dynamics-like step for stability.
- Summary: Positions itself relative to GPS/trajectory-optimization and LQG/MPC methods; claims novelties in gain extraction, action formulation, and asynchronous scheme.

- Technical soundness: Alternating optimization and regression is standard and well-motivated; the Gauss–Newton reuse for feedback gains is a neat, practical contribution.
- Empirics: Diverse visual demonstrations and some ablations are compelling, but quantitative evaluation is limited and many claims (e.g., “near-optimal”) lack metrics.
- Assumptions/realism: Defining actions as desired velocities/contact forces with soft dynamics and potential root forces raises realism and sim-to-real transfer concerns.
- Recurrent state: The role, dimensionality, and benefit of the recurrent memory r are undercharacterized.

- Clarity: Paper is generally well written; gain-extraction derivation is clear.
- Clarify: semantics and dimensionality of r and how memory helps beyond the physical state.
- Clarify: exact sensory features, normalization, and goal encoding.
- Clarify: magnitude of soft constraint violations at execution (root forces, penetrations).
- Clarify: proximal-term details and their effect on asynchronous convergence beyond empirical notes.

- Originality: Interleaving trajectory optimization and policy fitting is not new; novel pieces are (i) feedback gain extraction from a direct optimizer with tunable softness, (ii) learning desired velocities/contact forces with T=1 execution, and (iii) unified application across diverse 3D morphologies.
- Significance: Valuable for graphics/character animation as a single pipeline for diverse behaviors; less clear for robotics due to model accuracy and contact softening.

- Strength: Clear, practical algorithm combining CIO and NN regression with scalable distributed implementation.
- Strength: Elegant, efficient method for extracting local feedback gains reusing trajectory Hessian approximations.
- Strength: Robustness via principled input and hidden-unit noise injection.
- Strength: Diverse 3D results across morphologies without motion capture or hand-designed state machines.
- Strength: Ablations demonstrate importance of joint optimization and noise.

- Weakness: Limited quantitative evaluation and sparse baseline comparisons; overreliance on videos and one ablation task.
- Weakness: Action/execution relies on soft dynamics constraints and may permit non-physical aids, undermining realism and “near-optimality.”
- Weakness: Sparse comparisons to strong baselines (GPS, TRPO, iLQG/MPC) and lack of matched numerical MPC baselines.
- Weakness: Insufficient analysis of recurrence and necessity/role of memory state r.
- Weakness: Generalization claims are per-character; no cross-morphology transfer shown.
- Weakness: No convergence analysis for the asynchronous alternating optimization beyond empirical statements.

- Suggestion: Add quantitative metrics—cost-to-go gaps, success rates, energy/torque, tracking error, time-to-failure under disturbances, and model-mismatch robustness.
- Suggestion: Report hard constraint violations at execution and ablate with strictly enforced dynamics if possible.
- Suggestion: Compare quantitatively against GPS, TRPO, and an MPC baseline on at least one shared task.
- Suggestion: Analyze effect of λ on gain magnitudes/performance and compare to LQG gains for the same trajectories.
- Suggestion: Ablate recurrence: vary size of r, test without recurrence, and include tasks that require memory/partial observability.
- Suggestion: Clarify sensory representation, normalization, goal encoding, and generalization to unseen goals.
- Suggestion: Report computational cost and sample efficiency (nodes, wall-clock, number of trajectories).

- Argument for acceptance: Well-engineered integration that yields impressive, diverse 3D behaviors with a single pipeline and a likely reusable gain-extraction technique.
- Argument for acceptance: Addresses an important practical problem in interactive control/graphics with convincing methodology.
- Argument against acceptance: Insufficient quantitative evidence and baseline comparisons; “near-optimal feedback” claims are not substantiated.
- Argument against acceptance: Reliance on soft dynamics and non-physical action abstractions weakens realism and broader applicability.
- Argument against acceptance: Novelty is incremental relative to prior trajectory-optimization-guided policy learning.
