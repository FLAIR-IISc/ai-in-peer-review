- Presents a method alternating Contact-Invariant Optimization (CIO) trajectory optimization with supervised regression to train RNN policies for 3D contact-rich character control.
- Learns policies that output desired velocities, contact forces, and recurrent updates; execution uses a one-step constrained optimization enforcing dynamics softly.
- Builds on guided policy search and related works but targets harder 3D, contact-rich tasks and uses CIO to synthesize diverse behaviors without motion capture.
- Introduces a perturbation-based, computationally efficient method to extract local feedback gains from the trajectory optimizer using the Woodbury identity.
- Uses noise injection in sensory inputs and hidden activations and noise-consistent targets (a + A s ε) to improve policy robustness.
- Employs a distributed, asynchronous training architecture stabilized via proximal regularization for large-scale data generation and learning.
- Alternating optimization design and T=1 execution step are well-motivated and consistent with the training representation.
- Empirical results show a variety of 3D characters and ablations that emphasize joint optimization and noise importance, with a small architecture study.
- Quantitative evaluation is limited: sparse metrics (success rates, energy, tracking errors, robustness) and few baseline comparisons (no GPS/TRPO) beyond one MPC comparison.
- Soft enforcement of dynamics at execution can introduce artificial root forces, weakening physical realism and transfer claims.
- The policy’s action space (desired velocities/forces) plus online constrained optimization blurs attribution between learned policy and online solver.
- Recurrent state design is under-specified: missing details on size, role, initialization, learned dynamics, and ablation results.
- Hyperparameter selection and sensitivity (e.g., λ for gain softness, noise scales) are not analyzed.
- Real-time performance claims lack quantified runtime/latency measurements across characters.
- Paper is generally clear and well-organized; feedback-gain computation and alternating objective are precisely described.
- Additional clarity needed on state features φ, task cost definitions, recurrent state details, and consistent reporting of training/test metrics.
- Original contributions include direct feedback-gain extraction from CIO, scalable asynchronous alternating optimization, and diverse 3D demonstrations without motion capture.
- The action-as-desired-velocity/force formulation coupled with a T=1 optimizer is a practical, domain-specific design choice.
- Significant for physics-based character control and potentially robotics, though caveated by soft constraints and root forces.
- Strengths: unified framework for diverse characters, principled noise+feedback integration, efficient gain computation, scalable training, and supportive ablations.
- Weaknesses: limited quantitative baselines, reliance on soft constraints, under-specified recurrent component, lack of robustness tests, and missing execution-time costs.
- Suggestions: report quantitative metrics (success/fall rates, energy, perturbation response), compare to GPS/TRPO on at least one task, and provide runtime/GPU-CPU usage.
- Additional suggested analyses: sensitivity to λ, σε, σγ; ablations on recurrent unit count and removing recurrent state r; and evaluate stricter dynamics enforcement variants.
- Arguments for acceptance: demonstrates robust interactive 3D control across morphologies, provides a practical gain-extraction method, and offers a sound, scalable training pipeline.
- Arguments against acceptance: insufficient quantitative baselines and standardized metrics, physical-fidelity concerns from soft constraints, under-documentation of recurrent/hyperparameter aspects, and unmeasured execution robustness.
