This paper presents a method for training recurrent neural networks (RNNs) to act as feedback controllers for diverse dynamical systems, including swimming, flying, and walking characters. The core idea is to combine supervised learning with trajectory optimization (specifically Contact-Invariant Optimization – CIO), using the optimizer to generate training data for the network. The network learns both optimal controls and a model of dynamics, and noise injection and distributed stochastic optimization are key components of the training process. The authors demonstrate the ability to control characters with varying morphologies using a single network architecture.

This work builds upon a rich history of neural network control, particularly recent efforts to combine trajectory optimization and neural networks (e.g., [9, 11]). However, it distinguishes itself by tackling substantially harder problems – specifically, stable and realistic 3D locomotion – where previous work was limited to 2D characters. It also addresses the challenges of learning in continuous, high-dimensional spaces and the need for robust controllers that can handle noise and perturbations. The method directly addresses the limitations of traditional control approaches that are often manual and task-specific, and contrasts with direct methods for training neural network controllers ([3, 6, 14]) which the authors suggest yield more chaotic behaviors.

**Strengths:**

* **Technical Soundness:** The method appears technically sound, building on established techniques (CIO, RNNs, stochastic optimization). The description of the algorithm, particularly the combination of trajectory optimization and neural network training, is clear and well-motivated. The use of optimal feedback gains to improve robustness is a clever addition.
* **Novelty:**  Extending the combination of trajectory optimization and neural networks to 3D locomotion is a significant contribution. Successfully controlling a diverse set of characters with a single network architecture is also noteworthy. The details of the stochastic optimization and the method for computing feedback gains appear to be novel.
* **Significance:** The potential applications of this work are broad, including robotics, animation, and biomechanics.  The ability to generate stable, realistic, and adaptable controllers automatically is a valuable advance.
* **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The authors provide sufficient detail to understand the core ideas, and the supplementary video is a strong asset. The connections to related work are clearly established.
* **Empirical Validation:** The results demonstrate the method’s ability to control a range of characters. The ablation studies (Figure 2) provide insight into the importance of the different components of the algorithm.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper doesn't fully explore the sensitivity of the algorithm to hyperparameters. While some values are given, a more systematic investigation of their impact would strengthen the claims.
* **Computational Cost:** The reliance on cloud computing and hours of training is a limitation. While acknowledged, the barrier to entry for researchers without access to substantial computational resources is significant.
* **Limited Comparison to Direct Methods:** The authors state an impression that direct methods yield chaotic behaviors, but a systematic comparison is lacking. A more quantitative comparison would be helpful.
* **Detailed Dynamics Modeling:** While the method works without requiring task-specific features, the quality of the underlying physics simulation (MuJoCo) is critical.  The authors do not discuss the sensitivity of the results to the fidelity of the dynamic model.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* Solves a challenging problem (3D locomotion) with a novel and effective approach.
* Demonstrates strong empirical results across a diverse set of characters.
* Combines established techniques in a creative and synergistic way.
* Has the potential for significant impact in multiple fields.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*  Hyperparameter sensitivity isn't fully explored.
*  Computational cost is high.
*  Lack of thorough quantitative comparison to alternative approaches.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that presents a significant contribution to the field of neural network control. The combination of trajectory optimization and RNNs is a promising approach, and the authors have successfully demonstrated its ability to generate stable and realistic controllers for diverse characters. While there are some limitations, the strengths of the work outweigh the weaknesses.  I recommend acceptance.
