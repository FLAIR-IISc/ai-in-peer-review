- Presents a method to train a single RNN policy as an interactive, real-time feedback controller for diverse simulated characters (bipeds, quadrupeds, swimmers, flyers).  
- Core idea: use offline trajectory optimization to generate expert motions and distill them into a neural network policy via joint optimization.  
- Formulates a joint, non-convex optimization over trajectories and network parameters solved with a distributed, block-alternating scheme.  
- Trajectory phase: multiple contact-invariant optimization processes refine trajectories to satisfy physics and match the current policy.  
- Policy phase: network regression trains to mimic state-action pairs from the updated trajectories.  
- Key technical improvements enable scaling to complex 3D locomotion, notably noise injection into sensory inputs for robustness.  
- Computes first-order corrections to optimal actions via optimal feedback gains to provide valid targets for noisy inputs.  
- Novel, efficient method to extract well-conditioned feedback gains directly from the trajectory optimizer (claimed superior to LQR-based gains).  
- Empirical results: single unified algorithm produces stable, realistic locomotion across varied morphologies; video evidence is compelling.  
- Ablation study shows joint optimization and noise+feedback gains are essential; naive generate-then-train fails.  
- Strength: clear writing and organization, with precise problem formulation and explained distributed training procedure.  
- Weakness: convergence of the distributed, block-alternating optimization lacks theoretical analysis and is justified empirically only.  
- Weakness: role of predicted contact forces in actions and their treatment during execution-time optimization is unclear, hurting reproducibility.  
- Weakness: comparisons to direct RL and MPC are qualitative; lacks quantitative benchmarks against alternative controllers.  
- Arguments for acceptance: significant advance in generality and performance, novel feedback-gain extraction, strong experimental validation, likely high impact.  
- Arguments against acceptance: builds on prior ideas with novelty concentrated in technical improvements, missing formal convergence analysis, and anecdotal comparisons to alternatives.
