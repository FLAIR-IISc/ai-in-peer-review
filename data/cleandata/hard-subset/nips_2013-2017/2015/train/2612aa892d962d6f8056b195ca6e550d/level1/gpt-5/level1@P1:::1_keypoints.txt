- Proposes training RNNs as near-optimal feedback controllers for diverse high-DOF systems by interleaving direct trajectory optimization (CIO) with supervised learning of a stochastic policy that outputs nominal actions plus linear feedback corrections a + As ε.  
- Feedback gains As are computed efficiently from the trajectory optimizer via a perturbation method that reuses the Gauss-Newton inverse Hessian.  
- Actions are desired velocities/forces and recurrent-state rates; execution solves a horizon-1 constrained optimization (implicit integration) each control step to satisfy dynamics while tracking network outputs.  
- Training uses alternating block optimization with proximal regularizers, input/hidden noise for robustness, and asynchronous distributed execution (many CPU CIO nodes, one GPU NN).  
- Builds on guided policy search / trajectory-optimization + function-approximation lines but extends to full 3D locomotion across morphologies, principled input noise with matching corrective targets from direct optimizer, and an asynchronous proximal alternating scheme.  
- Alternating optimization objective is clearly specified; perturbation-based derivation of gains using Gauss-Newton and Woodbury identity is a useful, correct contribution that avoids ill-conditioned LQG gains.  
- The T=1 implicit execution scheme is sensible and practically stable.  
- Empirical results are compelling and qualitative: multiple 3D morphologies and emergent stable gaits without hand-engineered contact schedules.  
- Ablations demonstrate joint optimization and noise injection both matter for test-time stability; a small architecture study is included.  
- Comparison to MPC shows the learned policy regularizes solutions toward more predictable, cyclic motions.  
- Quantitative evaluation is sparse: missing metrics for success rate, robustness to perturbations, energy cost, speed, and latency, and lacking comparisons to strong baselines (e.g., TRPO, GPS variants).  
- Use of soft dynamics (including root forces) may compromise physical realism; magnitudes and impact are not reported.  
- The recurrent state dimension and its role are not analyzed; generalization beyond training distribution (large goal changes or novel terrains) is unquantified.  
- Paper is generally well organized and readable; algorithm, objective, and pipeline are clearly described.  
- Reproducibility needs more detail: recurrent state size/initialization, task cost weights and trajectory lengths, exact state/action vector sizes, and reference-frame conventions.  
- Runtime numbers per control step (policy forward pass and T=1 solve) across characters should be reported.  
- Need analysis of stability/sensitivity of the asynchronous alternating scheme to proximal weight η and noise σ.  
- Original contributions: scalable pipeline for 3D contact-rich locomotion, principled extraction of stabilizing gains from direct optimizer linearization, stochastic supervised targets tied to injected sensory noise, and an asynchronous proximal alternating training scheme.  
- Contributions are incremental relative to GPS but collectively enable more challenging behaviors than prior 2D demonstrations.  
- Significance: valuable for graphics/animation and control/RL; gain-extraction technique is of independent interest, though transfer to real robots is uncertain due to model reliance and action definitions.  
- Strengths: unified controller across diverse 3D characters without state machines/mocap, elegant stochastic supervision with optimal feedback gains, practical distributed system with hours-scale training, and meaningful ablations/MPC comparison.  
- Weaknesses/questions: limited quantitative evaluation, potential reduction in physical fidelity from soft dynamics, reliance on per-step optimization at execution, unclear implications of desired-velocity/force actions for hardware, unclear necessity/interpretation of recurrent state, and missing sensitivity analyses for λ, σ, and η.  
- Arguments for acceptance: practically impactful, general solution with impressive demonstrations; reusable technique for extracting stabilizing gains; advances the practice of combining trajectory optimization and deep nets.  
- Arguments against acceptance: insufficient quantitative evaluation and baseline comparisons; reliance on soft physics and per-step optimization weakens claims of pure policy control and physical realism; novelty is partly incremental.  
- Suggested improvements: add quantitative metrics and comparisons to TRPO/GPS on at least one morphology, report root-force magnitudes and experiments with harder dynamics, provide ablations and sensitivity plots for recurrent state, λ, σ, and η, and discuss feasibility of torque-level execution and hardware transfer.  
- Overall assessment: strong, well-executed paper with solid methodological ideas and impressive demonstrations; leans toward acceptance contingent on strengthening quantitative evaluation and clarifying execution fidelity.
