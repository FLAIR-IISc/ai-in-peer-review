This paper presents a compelling approach to learning controllers for diverse dynamical systems, combining trajectory optimization (specifically CIO) with neural network regression. The core idea – using trajectory optimization to generate training data for a neural network that can then execute the control policy in real-time – is well-motivated and effectively addresses the challenges of learning control policies directly in complex, high-dimensional spaces. The ability to achieve stable and realistic locomotion across a variety of morphologies (swimming, flying, bipedal/quadrupedal walking) with a single network architecture is a significant accomplishment.

The paper is well-structured and clearly explains the methodology. The interleaving of supervised learning with trajectory optimization, the injection of noise during training, and the use of the optimizer to obtain feedback gains are all insightful and contribute to the robustness and generalization ability of the learned controllers. The inclusion of details regarding the distributed training architecture and the comparative evaluation (showing the benefit of joint optimization and noise injection) strengthens the contribution. It builds nicely on previous work in both trajectory optimization (e.g., [12]) and neural network control [3, 6, 9, 11], clearly articulating how it advances the state-of-the-art, particularly in enabling 3D locomotion where prior neural network-based methods struggled.  The paper also acknowledges the trade-offs and limitations – the computational cost of generating training data, the need for careful tuning of hyperparameters – demonstrating intellectual honesty.

However, there are some weaknesses. While the authors demonstrate impressive results across different morphologies, the level of *detail* regarding the task specifics and cost function design for each morphology is somewhat limited.  A more detailed breakdown of these costs (beyond mentioning minimization of torque usage and contact force magnitudes) would be beneficial for reproducibility and understanding how the same algorithm yields such diverse behaviors. For example, how were the costs weighted differently for the swimming, flying, and walking tasks?  The paper references a supplementary video, which helps, but specifics within the paper would be more valuable.

The claim that the method yields “more realistic and purposeful behaviors” than direct methods [3, 6, 14] feels somewhat unsubstantiated. While the authors state their “impression” is that direct methods lead to “chaotic behaviors,” a more rigorous quantitative comparison would be needed to support this claim. The figure illustrating MPC versus the learned behavior (Fig. 3) is helpful, but a more direct comparison with other neural network control methods (e.g., DDPG, TRPO) would strengthen the argument. It would be useful to see metrics beyond visual inspection of the motions.

The discussion of the optimal feedback gains is also slightly opaque. While the perturbation method described is clever, the connection to standard control theory (e.g., linear quadratic regulator) isn't fully fleshed out. Further clarifying how the calculated gains relate to stability margins and robustness would be valuable. The rationale for choosing λ in Section 3.2 is not very clearly explained. 

Finally, while the scaling of computational resources is discussed, a more detailed breakdown of the cost (e.g., hours of GPU time per character) would be useful, especially for researchers considering adopting the approach.


**Arguments for acceptance:**

* **Strong technical contribution:** The combination of trajectory optimization and neural network learning is novel and effective.
* **Impressive results:** Demonstrating stable and realistic control across diverse morphologies is a significant achievement.
* **Clear and well-written:** The paper is easy to follow and clearly explains the methodology.
* **Addresses a challenging problem:** Learning controllers for complex dynamical systems is a notoriously difficult task.
* **Thorough experimental evaluation:** The comparative evaluation provides some evidence of the method’s effectiveness.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

* **Lack of detailed task/cost breakdown:** Insufficient information on task specifics for each morphology.
* **Unsubstantiated claims:** The assertion of superior realism compared to direct methods requires more rigorous evidence.
* **Limited discussion of feedback gains:** The connection to control theory could be further clarified.
* **Limited computational cost details:** The cost of training is not fully quantified.



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite the minor weaknesses, this is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of neural network control. The results are impressive, and the methodology is well-motivated and clearly explained. I believe the paper is worthy of acceptance to NIPS, with the caveat that the authors address the points raised above in a revision. Specifically, providing more details on task-specific costs, strengthening the comparative evaluation, and clarifying the discussion of feedback gains would significantly enhance the paper's impact.

**Rating:** Accept with minor revisions.
