- Core idea: Use trajectory optimization to generate training data for a neural-network policy to learn controllers for diverse dynamical systems.
- Empirical effectiveness: Demonstrated strong results across swimming, flying, and biped/quadruped walking tasks with high-quality motions (video reinforces this).
- Independence from hand-design: Method avoids reliance on motion capture or hand-designed state machines, improving generality.
- Novelty: Well-executed integration of trajectory optimization and neural networks, including using the optimizer to compute feedback gains.
- Builds on prior work: Clearly positions itself relative to existing trajectory optimization and neural-control literature.
- Addresses DL challenges: Tackles precise real-valued outputs, closed-loop control, and limited data via noise injection, stochastic policy optimization, and optimal feedback gains.
- Practical engineering: Uses a distributed training architecture to handle computational demands.
- 3D locomotion claim: Achieves stable 3D locomotion, extending beyond prior 2D-only results.
- Cost observation: Computational cost is weighted toward data generation by the optimizer rather than network training.
- Strong empirical evidence: Visual and quantitative results convincingly show learned controllers work for varied morphologies.
- Generality advantage: Single network architecture and parameter set handle multiple body types and tasks.
- Clarity: Paper is well-written and organized, making the methodology and results easy to follow.
- Ablation studies: Section 8.1 provides useful comparative evaluations highlighting component contributions.
- Hyperparameter sensitivity: Lacks comprehensive analysis of robustness to changes in learning rate, noise, and network architecture.
- Computational cost detail: Needs a more detailed breakdown of computational cost and scaling with system complexity.
- Limited MPC comparison: Insufficient quantitative comparison to Model Predictive Control, which is a natural benchmark.
- Interpretability: Learned policies are a “black box”; paper lacks insight into learned representations or network activations.
- Local optima risk: Alternating optimization may be susceptible to local minima; proximal regularization is used but convergence concerns remain under-discussed.
- Task specification: Insufficient detail on task goals, constraints, and reward formulations used in optimization.
- Acceptance arguments: Significant advance, well-motivated, addresses key DL-for-control challenges, and shows strong empirical support.
- Rejection arguments: High computational cost and unclear scalability, limited hyperparameter/MPC analysis, and limited interpretability.
- Overall verdict: Strong contribution recommended for acceptance, with suggested future work to address scalability, hyperparameter robustness, MPC comparisons, and interpretability.
