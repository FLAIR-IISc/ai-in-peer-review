This paper presents a method for training a single recurrent neural network architecture to act as a real-time feedback controller for a diverse set of physically simulated 3D characters, including swimmers, flyers, bipeds, and quadrupeds. The core idea is to leverage an offline trajectory optimizer (Contact-Invariant Optimization) to generate expert data, which is then used to train the neural network policy. The key contribution lies in a joint, alternating optimization framework where trajectories and the network are optimized together. This work extends a promising line of research that combines trajectory optimization with policy learning (e.g., Levine & Koltun, 2014; Mordatch et al., 2014), but demonstrates success on a significantly more challenging and varied set of 3D locomotion problems.

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound, with claims well-supported by experimental results. The proposed method for deriving optimal feedback gains from the direct optimizer to create richer training targets under noise is particularly clever and well-justified. The ablation study in Figure 2 compellingly demonstrates the necessity of the core components—joint optimization and noise injection—for achieving robust performance, highlighting the authors' careful evaluation.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-written and organized. However, the policy execution mechanism in Section 7 could be elaborated upon. The policy outputs a desired velocity which then serves as a target for a single-timestep optimization. This is a non-standard execution model, and a more detailed explanation of this "implicit integrator" would improve clarity and reproducibility.

**Originality:** While the high-level concept of distilling an optimizer into a policy is not entirely new, the specific formulation and its successful application represent a novel contribution. The method for extracting feedback gains, the principled use of stochasticity during training, and the distributed architecture constitute a novel combination of techniques that pushes the boundary of what is achievable with this approach.

**Significance:** The results are highly significant. The ability to control such a wide variety of morphologies with a single, general-purpose algorithm is a substantial advance for interactive character animation and has clear implications for robotics. The emergence of natural gaits without task-specific priors is particularly impressive and suggests the method is discovering fundamental principles of locomotion.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Addresses a challenging and important problem in control and animation.
*   Demonstrates state-of-the-art results across an impressive diversity of characters.
*   Presents a novel and effective combination of techniques, with strong empirical validation.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   The comparison to Model-Predictive Control is brief and primarily qualitative.
*   Clarity on the real-time policy execution step could be improved.

Overall, this is an excellent paper with significant contributions and compelling results. The strengths far outweigh the minor weaknesses.
