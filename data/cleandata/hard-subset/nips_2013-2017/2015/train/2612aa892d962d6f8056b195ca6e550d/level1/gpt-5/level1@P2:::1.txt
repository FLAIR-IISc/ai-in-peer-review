Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper proposes an alternating optimization framework that couples direct trajectory optimization (Contact-Invariant Optimization, CIO) with supervised learning of a recurrent neural network policy for real-time control of complex characters (swimmer, bird, bipeds, quadrupeds). The policy outputs desired state velocities, recurrent-state updates, and contact forces; execution performs a one-step constrained optimization to track these outputs subject to soft dynamics constraints. Key components include: (i) learning on noisy sensory inputs with first-order corrected targets using locally optimal feedback gains; (ii) a novel, efficient way to compute these gains within direct trajectory optimization via a soft-constrained perturbation and reuse of the Gauss-Newton Hessian inverse; (iii) distributed, asynchronous alternation between trajectory updates and NN regression with proximal regularization; and (iv) training on branched trajectory segments to emulate interactive goal changes.

This sits squarely in the line of work on “learning from an optimizer,” closely related to guided policy search (GPS) and iLQG-based policy fitting (e.g., Levine & Koltun, ICML’14), and to prior Mordatch & Todorov work combining function approximation with trajectory optimization (RSS’14). It contrasts with model-free deep RL (e.g., DQN) and policy gradient/TRPO (Schulman et al.), and with classical animation controllers (SIMBICON, ZMP/capture point). The contribution claims to scale to diverse 3D characters where prior learning-from-trajectory approaches often focused on 2D or simpler tasks.

Quality:
- Technical soundness: The alternating scheme is reasonable and well-motivated; the proximal terms and noise freezing make the asynchrony practical. The derivation of feedback gains via a soft constraint and Woodbury identity is neat and leverages quantities already computed by CIO. The first-order target correction a(s+ε) ≈ a + Asε is consistent with local linearization.
- Empirical support: The paper demonstrates diverse 3D behaviors and ablations showing the necessity of joint optimization and noise. However, quantitative evaluation is limited. The main quantitative evidence appears to be training/test errors for the policy fit; there are no task-level metrics (success rate, distance/orientation error, energy/torque, perturbation recovery) or comparisons to strong baselines such as GPS/iLQG, TRPO, or MPC beyond a qualitative comparison to MPC on a bird. Robustness claims (noise injection, unexpected goal changes) would be stronger with perturbation tests.
- Execution-time control relies on a T=1 optimization under soft dynamics constraints, which may include root forces; this weakens claims of purely learned feedback and physical realism, and makes it harder to attribute success to the learned policy versus the online optimizer.
- Some implementation specifics are underspecified for reproducibility (e.g., dimension and role of the recurrent state r, exact cost features/weights, rollout success criteria, contact model details at execution).

Clarity:
The paper is generally well organized and readable. The problem setup, alternating algorithm, and gain computation are clearly described. The distributed training architecture is explained at a useful level. Points needing clarification:
- Exact definition and dimensionality of r, and whether/how the RNN recurrency materially improves over feedforward; ablation of recurrency would help.
- Full list of cost terms and weights, and how they differ between training and execution (soft constraints, root forces).
- Quantitative measures of convergence for the alternating scheme and sensitivity to α, λ, η.
- Details of the action parameterization (why output contact forces directly versus torques) and implications for physical plausibility.

Originality:
Combining trajectory optimization with supervised policy learning is not new (GPS/iLQG). The paper’s most original elements are: (i) the principled use of local feedback gains from a direct optimizer to define noise-corrected supervision targets; (ii) the efficient gain computation within CIO via soft perturbations and Gauss-Newton reuse, yielding well-conditioned gains without an extra LQG pass; and (iii) the practical, asynchronous alternating scheme with noise injection at both sensory inputs and hidden activations. The specific policy/action parameterization and the uniform application to multiple morphologies are also useful. Overall, the novelty is incremental but meaningful.

Significance:
For character animation and model-based control, the ability to train a single architecture to produce stable, interactive 3D locomotion across diverse bodies is impactful. The gain computation method may be of independent interest to practitioners using direct trajectory optimization. For the broader ML/RL community, significance is moderate: the method advances a practical flavor of guided policy learning but lacks head-to-head comparisons and quantitative evidence of superior performance or sample efficiency. The dependence on online T=1 optimization and soft dynamics at execution further limits immediate robotic applicability.

Pros:
- Clear, practical algorithm that scales to diverse 3D characters and yields realistic behaviors.
- Elegant, efficient method for obtaining stabilizing feedback gains from a direct optimizer.
- Thoughtful use of noise (sensory and hidden) to improve robustness and generalization.
- Distributed training architecture with reasonable wall-clock performance.
- Ablations that demonstrate the importance of joint optimization and noise.

Cons:
- Limited quantitative evaluation; no comparisons to GPS/iLQG, TRPO, or stronger MPC baselines beyond a qualitative example.
- Execution relies on a one-step optimization with soft dynamics (including potential root forces), muddying claims of a purely learned controller and physical realism.
- Insufficient detail on recurrent state and costs; reproducibility would benefit from more specifics and code/data.
- No robustness tests to external perturbations; generalization claims rest primarily on videos.
- Action definition includes contact forces, which may be difficult to realize on physical systems.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Add quantitative task metrics (success rate, time-to-goal, energy, stability margins) and perturbation tests; report across seeds and initial conditions.
- Compare against GPS/iLQG and TRPO (and an MPC baseline with matched horizon and costs) on identical tasks.
- Clarify the role and dimensionality of r; include ablations of recurrency versus purely feedforward policies.
- Report sensitivity to λ (gain softness), α, η, and to noise scales; show how gains affect rollout stability.
- Tighten physical realism at execution (harder constraint enforcement, removal of root forces) or clearly delimit the animation focus.
- Provide implementation details and, ideally, release code and videos.

Recommendation: Leaning toward accept for the graphics/control community value and the gain-computation contribution, contingent on strengthening quantitative evaluation and clarifying execution-time realism. For NeurIPS, the lack of rigorous comparisons and metrics reduces confidence; addressing these would significantly improve the case.
