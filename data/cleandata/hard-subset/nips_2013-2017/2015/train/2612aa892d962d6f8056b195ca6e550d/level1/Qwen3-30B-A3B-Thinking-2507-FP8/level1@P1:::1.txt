

# Peer Review

## Summary

This paper introduces a novel method for training recurrent neural networks to serve as near-optimal feedback controllers for diverse complex dynamical systems, including swimming, flying, bipedal, and quadrupedal locomotion in three dimensions. The core innovation lies in a joint optimization framework that interleaves supervised learning with trajectory optimization (specifically Contact-Invariant Optimization [12]), enabling the neural network to learn state-action mappings that generate stable, realistic behaviors without motion capture or task-specific features. The approach injects noise during training, trains for unexpected task changes, and leverages trajectory optimization to obtain optimal feedback gains. The authors demonstrate their method across multiple character morphologies using a single network architecture, achieving results previously limited to 2D environments.

This work builds on recent NIPS/ICML contributions such as Levine & Koltun's "Learning complex neural network policies with trajectory optimization" (ICML 2014) and Mordatch & Todorov's "Combining the benefits of function approximation and trajectory optimization" (RSS 2012), but significantly extends them by solving substantially harder 3D locomotion problems without the need for specialized controllers for different character types. The paper also relates to work on neural network control [3, 6, 10, 14], but addresses the key challenge of limited training data for control tasks through their noise injection and trajectory optimization approach.

## Strengths

1. **Technical Soundness (Quality)**: The paper presents a well-motivated, technically rigorous approach. The derivation of optimal feedback gains as a byproduct of trajectory optimization (Section 3.2) is particularly elegant and addresses a key limitation in prior work. The ablation studies (Section 8.1) convincingly demonstrate the necessity of their key components (joint optimization, noise injection).

2. **Novelty and Scope (Originality)**: The paper makes a clear contribution by extending previous trajectory-optimization-based methods to 3D locomotion across diverse character morphologies, where prior work was limited to 2D. The unified controller architecture for different locomotion types (biped, quadruped, swimming, flying) represents a significant conceptual advance over task-specific state machines.

3. **Experimental Rigor**: The results are comprehensive, showing successful locomotion across multiple character types in 3D, with ablation studies confirming the importance of each component. The comparative evaluation against naive neural network training and the demonstration of MPC's non-cyclical behavior (Figure 3) provide strong evidence of their method's advantages.

4. **Clarity and Presentation**: The paper is exceptionally well-organized and clearly written. The methodology is explained with sufficient detail for replication, and the figures effectively illustrate the approach and results. The supplementary video (mentioned in the abstract) would further enhance understanding.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited Comparison to Other Deep RL Methods**: While the paper contrasts with "direct methods" (Section 1), it doesn't adequately compare with recent deep reinforcement learning approaches (e.g., PPO [14], SAC [15]) that could potentially achieve similar results. The claim that "networks trained with direct methods give rise to successful yet somewhat chaotic behaviors" needs more substantial evidence.

2. **Computational Complexity**: The paper mentions training takes "on the order of hours" with cloud computing, but doesn't provide a clear comparison to the computational cost of alternative approaches. This is a significant practical consideration for real-world applications.

3. **Theoretical Limitations**: The paper doesn't address theoretical guarantees about the convergence of their alternating optimization procedure (Algorithm 1) or the generalization properties of their learned policies beyond the specific tasks demonstrated.

4. **Lack of Robustness Testing**: While noise injection is used during training, the paper doesn't systematically test how robust the learned controllers are to external perturbations, modeling errors, or sensor noise during execution.

## Arguments for and Against Acceptance

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
- Solves a significant problem in character control (3D locomotion across diverse morphologies) where prior work was limited to 2D.
- Provides a unified controller architecture that eliminates the need for task-specific state machines.
- Demonstrates clear technical advances over previous trajectory-optimization-based methods (Section 8.1).
- Well-written, well-structured paper with comprehensive experiments and ablation studies.
- Potential for significant impact in robotics, animation, and biomechanics.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
- Limited comparison to recent deep reinforcement learning methods that could solve similar problems.
- Insufficient discussion of computational requirements for real-world deployment.
- Theoretical analysis of the alternating optimization procedure is lacking.
- No systematic testing of robustness to external perturbations during execution.

## Overall Assessment

This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of neural network control for complex dynamical systems. The method's ability to solve 3D locomotion problems across diverse character morphologies using a single controller architecture represents a notable advancement over previous work. While the paper could strengthen its position by more thoroughly comparing with recent deep RL approaches and providing more analysis of computational requirements, its technical soundness, novelty, and experimental validation are compelling.

The paper addresses a meaningful problem with a well-executed solution, and the results are impressive. The method has clear potential for impact across robotics, animation, and biomechanics. The minor weaknesses noted do not substantially undermine the paper's contributions, which are significant and well-supported.

**Recommendation: Accept**
