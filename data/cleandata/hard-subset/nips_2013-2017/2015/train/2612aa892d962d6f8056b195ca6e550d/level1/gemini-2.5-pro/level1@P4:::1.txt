This paper presents a method for training a single recurrent neural network architecture to serve as an interactive, real-time feedback controller for a diverse set of physically-simulated characters, including bipeds, quadrupeds, swimmers, and flyers. The core idea is to leverage the power of offline trajectory optimization to generate high-quality "expert" motion data, and then distill this knowledge into a neural network policy.

The proposed framework formulates this as a joint optimization problem over both a set of optimal trajectories and the neural network's parameters. This large, non-convex problem is solved using a distributed, block-alternating optimization scheme. In one phase, multiple trajectory optimization processes (based on Contact-Invariant Optimization [12]) refine motion trajectories to satisfy task goals and physics, while also trying to match the output of the current neural network policy. In the other phase, the neural network is trained via regression to mimic the state-action pairs from the updated trajectories.

Crucially, the authors introduce several technical improvements that enable this approach to succeed on complex 3D locomotion tasks where prior similar work [9, 11] was limited to 2D. To improve robustness and generalization, Gaussian noise is injected into the network's sensory inputs during training. To provide a valid learning target for these noisy inputs, the system computes the first-order correction to the optimal action using optimal feedback gains. The paper presents a novel and efficient method to extract these feedback gains directly from the trajectory optimizer, which they claim produces better-conditioned gains than standard LQR-based approaches.

**Strengths:**

*   **Quality & Significance:** The paper's primary strength lies in its impressive and significant results. The ability to use a single, unified algorithm to generate stable, interactive, and visually plausible locomotion for such a wide variety of character morphologies (bipeds, quadrupeds, swimmers, flyers) represents a substantial advance in character animation and control. The accompanying video provides compelling evidence of the method's success. The work is technically sound, and the ablation study in Section 8.1 convincingly demonstrates that the key components—joint optimization and noise injection with feedback gains—are essential for good performance. This distinguishes the method from a naive "generate-then-train" approach, which is shown to fail.

*   **Originality:** While the high-level concept of distilling a trajectory optimizer into a policy network follows from recent work [9, 11], this paper makes several novel contributions that allow the method to scale to much harder problems. The most significant technical innovation is the method for computing optimal feedback gains from a direct trajectory optimization method (Section 3.2). This is a general technique that could be valuable in other contexts where feedback control is derived from direct optimization methods. The successful application of a single framework to such a diverse set of 3D characters is also a novel achievement.

*   **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and clearly organized. It begins with a high-level overview of the joint optimization problem, then details each of the core components: trajectory optimization, feedback gain computation, and neural network regression. The distributed architecture and training procedure are also explained well. The problem formulation is precise, and the algorithm is presented clearly.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Technical Soundness:** The convergence of the proposed distributed, block-alternating optimization algorithm is asserted based on empirical observation but is not supported by theoretical analysis. Given the stochastic nature and asynchronous updates, a discussion of the conditions under which convergence is expected would strengthen the paper. Additionally, the comparisons to other controller types, such as direct Reinforcement Learning and Model Predictive Control (MPC), are qualitative and brief. While the authors suggest their method produces more "realistic" or "less chaotic" behaviors, quantitative metrics to support these claims would make the comparison more rigorous.

*   **Clarity:** The role of the predicted contact forces `f` in the action vector `a` could be clarified. Section 2 defines the action as `a_t = [q̇_t, ṙ_t, f_t]`, suggesting the network directly outputs forces. However, the policy execution step in Section 7 describes a one-step optimization that finds the next state `x_1` to best match the desired `q̇_des`, `ṙ_des`, and `f_des` while satisfying physical constraints. It is unclear if the predicted `f` is treated as a hard target, a soft target, or a warm start for this inner optimization. A more detailed explanation of this execution-time optimization step would improve reproducibility.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
1.  The paper demonstrates a significant leap in performance and generality for learning-based character controllers, successfully tackling a diverse range of challenging 3D locomotion tasks.
2.  It introduces a novel and technically valuable method for extracting well-conditioned optimal feedback gains from direct trajectory optimizers.
3.  The experimental results are compelling and well-supported by an ablation study that validates the key design choices.
4.  The work is likely to be highly influential in computer animation, robotics, and reinforcement learning.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
1.  The overall framework is an extension of previously proposed ideas [9, 11], with novelty concentrated in the technical improvements that enable scaling.
2.  The convergence of the optimization algorithm is not formally analyzed.
3.  Comparisons to alternative methods like direct RL are anecdotal rather than based on quantitative experiments.

In summary, this paper presents a high-quality, technically sound, and significant piece of work. The impressive generality and robustness of the resulting controllers, enabled by a novel combination of techniques, represent a clear and important contribution to the field. Despite the minor weaknesses regarding theoretical analysis and quantitative comparisons, the strengths of the paper are substantial.
