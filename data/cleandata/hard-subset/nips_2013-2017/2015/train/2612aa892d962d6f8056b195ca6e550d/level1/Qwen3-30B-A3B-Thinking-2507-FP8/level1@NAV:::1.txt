

This paper presents a novel method for training neural networks to act as near-optimal feedback controllers for diverse complex characters (swimming, flying, bipedal and quadrupedal locomotion). The key innovation lies in combining supervised learning with trajectory optimization (specifically Contact-Invariant Optimization), where the neural network learns both optimal controls and a model of dynamics simultaneously. The authors introduce several technical improvements: interleaving supervised learning with trajectory optimization, injecting noise during training, training for unexpected task changes, and using trajectory optimization to obtain optimal feedback gains. Their approach differs from prior work [9, 11] by successfully achieving stable 3D locomotion where previous methods were limited to 2D.

The paper demonstrates strong technical quality with comprehensive experimental validation across multiple character types and locomotion tasks. The ablation studies (Figure 2, Table 1) convincingly show that their joint optimization approach outperforms naive neural network training by two orders of magnitude, and that noise injection is critical for preventing overfitting. The method's significance is evident in its ability to generate diverse, realistic behaviors for various character morphologies using a single architecture without motion capture or task-specific features.

The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with sufficient detail for reproducibility. The technical contributions are properly contextualized against related work in trajectory optimization [12] and neural network control [3, 6, 14], though the discussion of the "unified" approach to disparate control architectures could be strengthened with more explicit comparison to recent work on character control.

Strengths: Novel combination of trajectory optimization and neural networks; impressive empirical results across diverse 3D locomotion tasks; thorough ablation studies; clear technical contributions to feedback gain calculation.

Weaknesses: The theoretical justification for the noise injection and feedback gain approach could be more rigorous; the paper doesn't fully address computational complexity trade-offs compared to alternative methods like MPC (though Figure 3 provides some insight).

This work represents a significant advancement in neural network-based character control, demonstrating both technical soundness and broad applicability. The method produces stable, realistic behaviors without task-specific engineering, advancing the state of the art in interactive character control. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to strengthen the theoretical discussion and comparative analysis.
