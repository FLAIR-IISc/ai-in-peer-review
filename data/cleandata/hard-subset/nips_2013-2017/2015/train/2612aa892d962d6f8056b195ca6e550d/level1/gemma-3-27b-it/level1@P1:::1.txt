This paper presents a compelling approach to learning controllers for diverse dynamical systems using a combination of trajectory optimization and neural networks. The core idea – leveraging trajectory optimization to generate training data for a neural network policy – is well-motivated and demonstrably effective across a range of tasks (swimming, flying, biped/quadruped walking). The method avoids reliance on motion capture or hand-designed state machines, representing a significant step towards generalizable control solutions. The accompanying video is crucial for appreciating the quality of the learned behaviors.

The work builds upon existing research in trajectory optimization (CIO [12]) and neural network control [3, 6, 9, 11, 14], and clearly articulates its contributions. The authors correctly identify the challenges of applying deep learning to control problems – the need for precise real-valued outputs, closed-loop operation, and limited data – and propose thoughtful solutions, including noise injection, stochastic policy optimization, and the calculation of optimal feedback gains. The distributed training architecture is also a practical consideration given the computational demands of the approach.  Notably, the claim of achieving stable 3D locomotion, exceeding the capabilities of previous work [9, 11] limited to 2D characters, is a strong point. The emphasis on the computational cost being weighted towards data generation via the optimizer is an interesting observation.

**Strengths:**

* **Strong Empirical Results:** The presented results are impressive, demonstrating the ability to learn effective controllers for a diverse set of characters and tasks. The visual quality of the resulting motions (as showcased in the video) is high.
* **Novel Combination of Techniques:**  The integration of trajectory optimization and neural networks is well-executed. The use of trajectory optimization not just for data generation, but also for calculating feedback gains, is a particularly insightful contribution.
* **Generality:** The method’s ability to handle different body morphologies and tasks with a single network architecture and parameter set is a significant advantage. This speaks to the method’s potential for widespread applicability.
* **Addressing Key Challenges:** The authors directly address the core challenges of applying deep learning to control problems, offering solutions tailored to the specific requirements of the task.
* **Clear and Well-Written:** The paper is generally clearly written and well-organized, making it relatively easy to follow the key ideas.
* **Thorough Ablation Studies:** The comparative evaluation in section 8.1 provides valuable insights into the contribution of different components of the proposed method.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** While the ablation studies are useful, the paper lacks a comprehensive analysis of hyperparameter sensitivity.  How robust is the method to changes in learning rate, noise levels, or network architecture? The chosen values seem relatively fixed and are not justified in depth.
* **Computational Cost:** The authors acknowledge the high computational cost, but a more detailed breakdown of the cost for different characters and tasks would be valuable.  What is the scaling behavior with dimensionality and complexity?
* **Limited Comparison to MPC:** While the authors briefly mention Model Predictive Control (MPC), a more in-depth comparison, including quantitative results, would strengthen the paper.  MPC is a natural benchmark for real-time control of dynamical systems.
* **Black Box Nature:** As with many deep learning approaches, the learned policies are somewhat of a "black box."  It would be beneficial to provide some insights into what features the network is learning and how it is representing the control problem.  Visualization of the network activations or weights could be informative.
* **Potential for Local Optima:** The alternating optimization process (trajectory optimization followed by neural network regression) could be susceptible to getting stuck in local optima.  The authors address this with proximal regularization, but a more thorough discussion of the potential for instability and convergence issues would be helpful.
* **Lack of Detailed Task Description:** While the broad task of locomotion is defined, there is little information regarding the goals and constraints involved in the optimization. Specifying the task reward is critical.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a significant advance in the field of neural network control, demonstrating the ability to learn complex behaviors for a diverse range of dynamical systems.
* The proposed method is novel, well-motivated, and addresses key challenges in applying deep learning to control problems.
* The empirical results are strong and support the claims made by the authors.
* The paper is clearly written and well-organized.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The computational cost is high, and the scalability of the method is not fully explored.
* The lack of a comprehensive hyperparameter analysis and a more in-depth comparison to MPC weakens the paper.
* The "black box" nature of the learned policies limits interpretability.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper is a strong contribution and recommend acceptance. While there are some weaknesses, the strengths of the work – particularly the impressive empirical results and the novel combination of techniques – outweigh the concerns. The work represents a significant step towards automating the process of designing controllers for complex dynamical systems, and has the potential to impact a range of applications, including robotics, animation, and biomechanics.  Addressing the weaknesses identified above in a future version would further strengthen the paper.
