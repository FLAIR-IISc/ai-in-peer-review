Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper proposes an approach to train recurrent neural networks as near-optimal feedback controllers for complex physics-based characters (swimming, flying, biped/quadruped locomotion) without motion capture or hand-designed state machines. The core idea is to interleave direct trajectory optimization (Contact-Invariant Optimization, CIO [12]) with supervised policy learning: the optimizer produces state-action pairs and local feedback gains; the policy regresses from noisy sensory inputs to actions that are locally corrected by the gains. Noise is injected both in policy inputs and in hidden activations to improve robustness. A distributed, asynchronous implementation alternates between (i) optimizing trajectories with a proximal penalty to keep them close to the current policy predictions and (ii) NN regression. A practical twist is to define “action” as desired velocity/contact forces, with a T=1 inverse-dynamics-like optimization enforcing dynamics at execution time, yielding stable real-time control.

This positions the work close to Guided Policy Search/trajectory-optimization-based policy learning [9, 11], iterative LQG/MPC-style approaches, and graphics controllers (e.g., SIMBICON [21]); the novelty claimed over [9, 11] includes: (a) application to 3D locomotion/flying/swimming with a single architecture, (b) a perturbation-based method to extract well-conditioned local feedback gains directly from a Gauss–Newtonized direct trajectory optimizer (reusing CXX−1), and (c) a distributed asynchronous scheme with proximal regularization. The use of noise-injected targets a(s+ε)=a+Asε aligns with ideas from denoising and stabilizing RNNs [4,5,17,19].

Quality:
- Technical soundness: The alternating optimization and regression are standard and well-motivated. The perturbation method for feedback gains is a neat contribution: it reuses the trajectory Hessian approximation and avoids ill-conditioned LQG passes. The T=1 implicit execution step is practical and stabilizing.
- Empirics: The demonstrations are compelling and diverse; ablations (no joint optimization, no noise) show large performance drops on one biped task, and a coarse network-size sweep is provided. However, quantitative evaluation is limited. Claims such as “near-optimal feedback” are not supported by metrics (e.g., cost-to-go gaps, robustness bounds, disturbance rejection), and the MPC comparison is qualitative.
- Assumptions and realism: Defining actions as desired velocities and contact forces, and enforcing dynamics with soft constraints (including potential root forces) raises concerns for physical realism and robotics transfer. The method leans heavily on accurate models and on CIO’s ability to find good trajectories; the role of the recurrent state is undercharacterized.

Clarity:
The paper is generally well written and organized. The optimization objectives and algorithm are clearly stated. The gain-extraction derivation (Woodbury reuse of CXX−1) is a nice, clear piece. Some parts would benefit from clarification:
- The semantics and dimensionality of the recurrent state r, and how memory improves performance beyond the physical state.
- The exact sensory features and normalization.
- The magnitude of soft constraint violations at execution time (e.g., how large are root forces?).
- The details of the proximal terms and their effect on convergence in the asynchronous setting (beyond empirical observations).

Originality:
The interleaving of direct trajectory optimization with supervised policy fitting is not new [9,11], and input/activation noise are standard regularizers. The primary novel elements are: (i) the specific gain extraction from a direct optimizer with tunable softness via λ, (ii) the decision to learn desired velocities/contact forces and execute with a T=1 constrained update, and (iii) a unified application across diverse 3D morphologies without task-specific state machines. The overall system-level integration is a useful contribution for the graphics/control community.

Significance:
For character animation and interactive control, the ability to produce stable, realistic behaviors across bodies with a single training pipeline is valuable. The method may influence how trajectory optimization and NN policies are combined, and the gain-extraction trick is likely reusable. For robotics, significance is less clear due to reliance on accurate models, contact softening, and commanding contact forces; further work would be needed to bridge the sim-to-real gap.

Strengths:
- Clear, practical algorithm combining CIO with NN regression; scalable distributed implementation.
- Elegant, efficient method for extracting local feedback gains from a direct trajectory optimizer.
- Robustness via principled noise injection in inputs and hidden units.
- Diverse 3D results with a single architecture; interactive control without motion capture or state machines.
- Sensible ablations showing the need for joint optimization and noise.

Weaknesses:
- Limited quantitative evaluation; heavy reliance on video and one task for ablations.
- Action space and execution step rely on soft dynamics constraints and may allow non-physical aids (root forces); implications for “near-optimality” and realism are unclear.
- Sparse comparison to strong baselines (e.g., GPS [9], TRPO [14], iLQG/MPC beyond a qualitative plot).
- Insufficient analysis of the role/necessity of recurrence and memory state r.
- Generalization claims across morphologies are per-character training; cross-character transfer is not shown.
- Convergence properties of the asynchronous alternating scheme are not analyzed beyond empirical statements.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Provide quantitative metrics: success rates, average cost-to-go, energy/torque usage, tracking error, mean time to failure under disturbances, and model mismatch robustness.
- Report hard constraint violations at execution (penetration, complementarity residuals, root forces), and an ablation with strictly enforced dynamics if feasible.
- Compare against GPS [9] and TRPO [14] on at least one shared task; include an MPC baseline with matched costs and horizons and numerical metrics.
- Analyze the effect of λ on gain magnitudes/performance; compare to an LQG gain pass for the same trajectories.
- Ablate recurrence: size of r, performance without recurrence, and tasks requiring memory (e.g., partial observability).
- Clarify the sensory representation and normalization; describe how goals are encoded and whether policies generalize to unseen goal distributions.
- Discuss computational cost more precisely (nodes, wall-clock, trajectories), and sample efficiency (trajectories required per behavior).

Arguments for acceptance:
- Solid, well-engineered integration yielding impressive, diverse 3D behaviors with a single pipeline.
- Useful and likely reusable technique for extracting feedback gains from direct trajectory optimization.
- Addresses an important practical problem in interactive control/graphics with a convincing methodology.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Insufficient quantitative evaluation and limited baseline comparisons; claims of near-optimal feedback not substantiated.
- Reliance on soft dynamics and non-physical action abstractions weakens realism and applicability beyond simulation.
- Novelty is incremental relative to prior trajectory-optimization-guided policy learning.
