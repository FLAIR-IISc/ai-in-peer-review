## Review of "Interactive Control of Diverse Complex Characters with Neural Networks"

This paper presents a method for training recurrent neural networks (RNNs) to act as feedback controllers for a variety of dynamical systems, including swimming, flying, and legged locomotion. The core idea is to combine trajectory optimization (specifically, Contact-Invariant Optimization or CIO) with supervised learning of a neural network policy. The trajectory optimizer generates high-quality trajectories, which are then used to train a neural network to mimic the optimal control actions, enabling real-time interactive control. This approach aims to overcome the limitations of traditional control methods, which are often manual and task-specific, and to scale to high-dimensional control problems where reinforcement learning struggles.

The work builds upon existing research in both trajectory optimization (e.g., [12]) and neural network control (e.g., [3, 6, 14]). It distinguishes itself by successfully applying this combined approach to complex 3D locomotion tasks – an area where previous work was largely limited to 2D scenarios. The authors further introduce techniques like noise injection and a distributed training architecture to improve robustness and scalability, demonstrating successful control of diverse character morphologies with a single network architecture.

**Strengths:**

*   **Technical Soundness:** The methodology appears well-motivated and technically sound, effectively integrating trajectory optimization and neural network learning. The inclusion of optimal feedback gains is a clever addition for improving robustness.
*   **Impressive Results:** The ability to control diverse characters (swimming, flying, bipedal, quadrupedal) with a single network is a significant achievement, as demonstrated in the accompanying video.
*   **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and organized, clearly outlining the method and experimental setup.
*   **Scalability:** The distributed training architecture allows the method to scale to computationally demanding tasks.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Limited Comparative Analysis:** While the ablation studies (section 8.1) provide some insights, a more systematic comparison to alternative control methods (e.g., direct neural network training, MPC) across all character types would strengthen the claims.
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper doesn't thoroughly address the sensitivity of the method to the various hyperparameters involved (e.g., noise levels, regularization weights).
*   **Computational Cost:** While the distributed architecture improves scalability, the overall computational cost (hours of training, relying heavily on trajectory optimization) remains a significant barrier.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novel combination of techniques addressing a challenging problem in control.
*   Demonstrated success in controlling a diverse range of complex characters.
*   Potential for applications in robotics, animation, and biomechanics.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   Limited comparative analysis to establish a clear advantage over existing methods.
*   High computational cost may limit practical applicability.


**Overall:**

I believe this paper represents a valuable contribution to the field of control and character animation. The combination of trajectory optimization and neural networks is a promising approach, and the results presented are impressive. While further comparative analysis and investigation into hyperparameter sensitivity would be beneficial, the strengths of the work outweigh its weaknesses. **I recommend acceptance with minor revisions** to address the points raised above.
