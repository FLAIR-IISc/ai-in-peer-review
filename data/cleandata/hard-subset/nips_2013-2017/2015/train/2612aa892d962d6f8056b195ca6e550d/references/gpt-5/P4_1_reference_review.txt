Summary and relation to prior work
The paper tackles interactive, real-time control of diverse, complex 3D characters (flying, swimming, biped and quadruped locomotion) by learning neural policies from trajectory optimization. The core idea is to alternate between: (1) Contact-Invariant Optimization (CIO) to produce physically plausible, task-completing trajectories, and (2) supervised neural network regression to fit a policy that maps sensory inputs to actions. The coupling is enforced by a quadratic penalty that encourages trajectories to be consistent with the current policy under noisy sensory inputs. The authors inject Gaussian noise into inputs during training and use a first-order correction to target actions via local feedback gains that they derive from the direct trajectory optimization using a soft-constraint perturbation and the Gauss-Newton Hessian inverse. Training is performed asynchronously on a distributed cluster with proximal regularization to stabilize updates. At execution, the learned policy proposes desired velocities and contact forces, and the next state is computed by solving a 1-step constrained optimization, akin to an implicit integrator.

This approach is closely related to trajectory-centric reinforcement learning and guided policy search (e.g., Levine & Koltun, 2014; Mordatch & Todorov, 2014/2015), which also couple trajectory optimization with supervised policy learning. The paper differs in (i) deriving feedback gains from direct trajectory optimization via a soft constraint rather than an LQG pass, (ii) an asynchronous, distributed stochastic optimization with proximal terms, (iii) systematic noise injection in inputs and hidden layers to improve robustness, and (iv) demonstrating diverse 3D behaviors. It also situates within broader continuous-control work (e.g., TRPO/Schulman et al., 2015; Neuroanimator; Simbicon; model-based control with MuJoCo).

Strengths
- Addresses a challenging and relevant problem: interactive, real-time control for high-DOF, contact-rich 3D systems across morphologies with a single procedure.
- Solid formulation that reduces policy search to alternating CIO and supervised regression, leveraging well-established components.
- Practical and seemingly novel method to compute stabilizing feedback gains from direct trajectory optimization using the existing Gauss-Newton Hessian inverse; avoids brittle LQG post-processing.
- Careful engineering choices (input/hidden noise, proximal terms, asynchronous distributed setup) and ablations showing the importance of joint optimization and noise.
- Demonstrated breadth: swimmer, bird, multiple bipeds and quadrupeds with cyclic gaits emerging without task-specific encodings.

Weaknesses
- Quantitative evaluation is limited. Aside from a qualitative MPC comparison and an ablation plot/table, there are no standardized metrics (e.g., goal tracking error, success rate, energy/torque, falls) reported across seeds and perturbations.
- Lack of comparisons to strong baselines in continuous control (e.g., GPS variants, TRPO, iLQG/LQR-MPC) under identical physics and tasks reduces clarity on state-of-the-art advancement.
- Execution-time dynamics are enforced via soft constraints and may include root forces; this compromises physical fidelity and makes it hard to assess true controller quality versus optimizer assistance.
- Some technical aspects are under-specified: the definition and dimensionality of the recurrent state r, the feature vector φ and cost terms c(·), the precise error metric in Fig. 2 and Table 1, and sensitivity to key hyperparameters (λ, η, σε, σγ).
- Reproducibility is challenging: reliance on CIO, MuJoCo, and a sizable distributed setup; derivative computation by finite differences could be numerically sensitive.
- References are incomplete and contain formatting errors.

Quality
The method is technically plausible and internally consistent. The feedback-gain derivation via a soft-constraint perturbation that reuses CXX−1 is a useful engineering contribution and is clearly derived. The alternating scheme with proximal terms is reasonable for asynchronous updates, and empirical convergence is noted. However, the paper does not provide theoretical guarantees, and evaluation lacks rigorous, quantitative comparisons to alternative methods. The use of soft dynamics and possible root forces at execution raises concerns about the strictness of physical consistency.

Clarity
The paper is generally well organized and readable. The optimization setup, noise modeling, and distributed training are described clearly. However, important details are missing or only sketched (exact cost features, recurrent state design, metrics), and there are typos and incomplete references. Algorithm 1 could be typeset more clearly.

Originality
Relative to guided policy search and trajectory-centric RL, the overall framework is incremental. The specific feedback-gain computation for direct trajectory optimization, the particular noise-injection scheme with gain-adjusted targets, and the asynchronous proximal training are novel or at least under-explored in this context. Demonstration in diverse 3D settings is a notable application contribution.

Significance
If robust and quantitatively validated, the approach would be valuable for graphics/animation and potentially robotics, enabling fast, interactive policies across morphologies without hand-crafted gait/state machines. The current lack of strong baselines and standardized metrics limits the impact assessment for the ML community.

Suggestions and questions for the authors
- Provide quantitative results: tracking error to moving goals, success rates under disturbances, energy/torque usage, and fall frequency, with statistics over multiple seeds.
- Compare to GPS variants (e.g., ADMM-based), TRPO, and MPC under identical tasks and physics. Include runtime and compute cost comparisons.
- Ablate the feedback gains: no gains, LQG-based gains, and the proposed gains; report stability and performance differences; analyze sensitivity to λ.
- Clarify the recurrent state r: dimensionality, initialization, and whether the policy is effectively a feedforward network with explicit state update ṙ or a true RNN.
- Report whether root forces are present at execution; if so, provide experiments with stricter constraints and discuss trade-offs.
- Detail φ and c(·) (features, weights) to enable reproduction; report finite-difference step sizes and discuss numerical stability.
- Add sensitivity analyses for σε, σγ, η, network size, and timestep Δt.
- Improve reference formatting and fix typographical errors.

Arguments for acceptance
- Addresses an important problem with a practical, scalable method.
- Introduces a useful way to extract feedback gains from direct trajectory optimization.
- Demonstrates generality across diverse 3D characters with a single architecture.
- Provides ablation evidence for key design choices.

Arguments against acceptance
- Insufficient quantitative evaluation and lack of comparisons to strong baselines.
- Physical fidelity at execution is weakened by soft constraints/root forces.
- Some important technical details and metrics are missing, affecting reproducibility and assessment.
- Overall novelty relative to guided policy search is moderate.
