### Review of "Interactive Control of Diverse Complex Characters with Neural Networks"

**Summary**

This paper presents a method for automatically synthesizing interactive, real-time controllers for physically simulated 3D characters with diverse morphologies. The core idea is to leverage an offline trajectory optimization method, Contact-Invariant Optimization (CIO), to generate a dataset of optimal behaviors, and then train a neural network policy via supervised learning to mimic the optimizer's outputs. This allows the computationally expensive optimization to be performed offline, resulting in a compact policy that can run in real-time. The authors propose several technical improvements over prior work, most notably a method for injecting noise during training in a dynamics-aware fashion. This is achieved by deriving optimal feedback gains directly from the CIO process and using them to compute the corresponding optimal actions for noise-perturbed states. The paper demonstrates the framework's ability to generate locomotion controllers for various characters, including bipeds, quadrupeds, a swimmer, and a flyer, from a single unified algorithm.

**Strengths**

1.  **Ambitious and Significant Goal:** The problem of creating general-purpose, interactive controllers for complex physical characters is highly relevant to machine learning, robotics, and computer graphics. The paper's aim to "unify these disparate approaches" is commendable.
2.  **Impressive Qualitative Results:** The range of characters controlled by the single proposed framework is broad. The emergence of naturalistic gaits (walking, trotting) without explicit encoding is a strong demonstration of the method's potential. (This assessment relies on the description and the assumption that the supplementary video is compelling).
3.  **Novel Technical Contribution in Training:** The proposed method for data augmentation via noise injection (Section 2.1) combined with the derivation of optimal feedback gains (Section 3.2) is a clever and non-trivial contribution. Using the first-order expansion `a(s + ε) ≈ a + a_s ε` to generate training targets is a principled way to teach the network local robustness, which is critical for closed-loop stability. Deriving these gains from a direct optimization method like CIO is a useful technique that could be applied more broadly.

**Weaknesses**

The paper, while conceptually interesting, is marred by a significant number of flaws in its presentation, evaluation, and methodological rigor, which preclude a recommendation for acceptance in its current form.

1.  **Critical Presentation Flaws:**
    *   **Missing Figures and Tables:** The paper repeatedly refers to Figure 2, Figure 3, and Table 1. These are essential for understanding the quantitative results of the ablation study, the network architecture comparison, and the qualitative comparison to MPC. Without them, the core claims of the evaluation section are entirely unsubstantiated.
    *   **Incomplete and Malformed References:** The bibliography is in a state of disarray. Reference [1], for example, is just a title fragment. Several other references are similarly incomplete. This signals a profound lack of care in the manuscript's preparation.
    *   **Lack of Clarity and Precision:** The writing contains numerous ambiguities and imprecise statements. For instance:
        *   In Section 2, the state representation `s_t = [ q_t r_t q̇_{t-1} f_{t-1} ]` and action `a_t = [ q̇_t ṙ_t f_t ]` are confusing. Having the contact force `f` appear in both the action and the subsequent state is unusual and not clearly explained. Is `f_t` in the action a command and `f_{t-1}` a sensed value?
        *   The policy execution step (Section 7) is described as a single-timestep optimization. This is a crucial implementation detail that significantly differs from a standard feed-forward policy. Its implications for real-time performance and stability are not discussed in sufficient depth.
        *   The mathematical notation is sometimes non-standard (e.g., `s 7→ a`, `J>`) and contains apparent typos (e.g., `q̇t , qt+1 − qt` appears to be a list rather than a definition).

2.  **Insufficient Quantitative Evaluation:**
    *   The paper's evaluation is almost entirely qualitative, relying on the reader to view a video. While videos are valuable, they are no substitute for rigorous, reproducible quantitative metrics. There are no metrics reported for motion quality, stability margins, energy consumption, or task success rates.
    *   The comparison with Model Predictive Control (MPC) in Section 8.1 is anecdotal. The claim that the proposed method's trajectories are "less chaotic" is subjective and requires quantitative backing (e.g., via spectral analysis, jerk metrics, or trajectory variance over multiple runs).
    *   The ablation study, while well-conceived, is rendered unverifiable by the missing Figure 2. The claim of a "two orders of magnitude" improvement is strong but requires evidence.

3.  **Methodological Gaps:**
    *   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** A substantial number of crucial hyperparameters (`σ_ε`, `σ_γ`, `α`, `λ`, `η`) are listed in Section 8 without any justification for their chosen values or analysis of the system's sensitivity to them. The strong claim that a "single network architecture and parameters" were used for all creatures seems improbable without further evidence, given the vast differences in their dynamics.
    *   **Unsubstantiated Claims:** The paper claims its method for deriving feedback gains (Section 3.2) produces "better-conditioned gains" than a standard LQG pass. This claim is not supported by any theoretical analysis or empirical comparison.
    *   **Distributed Architecture:** The description of the distributed training setup (Section 6) feels more like an engineering report than a scientific contribution. The choice of a networked file system with file locking for communication is unconventional and its performance characteristics and potential bottlenecks are not analyzed.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   The paper tackles a challenging and important problem.
*   The core idea of combining trajectory optimization with a learned policy and the specific method for noise-aware training are technically interesting.
*   The demonstrated breadth of controllable characters suggests the method is powerful and general.

**Arguments for Rejection:**
*   The paper is incomplete, with missing figures and tables that are critical to the evaluation.
*   The manuscript is poorly prepared, with a malformed bibliography and numerous clarity issues.
*   The evaluation is almost entirely qualitative and lacks the quantitative rigor expected at NIPS.
*   Several key methodological claims are not substantiated with evidence.
*   There is no discussion or analysis of hyperparameter selection and sensitivity.

**Recommendation: Reject**

While the research direction is promising and the ideas have merit, the manuscript in its current state falls far short of the standards for publication at NIPS. The combination of critical missing information (figures, tables), a general lack of rigor in the evaluation, and carelessness in the manuscript's preparation makes it impossible to properly assess the work's contributions. The authors should be encouraged to conduct a thorough quantitative evaluation, address the methodological gaps, and completely revise the manuscript before resubmission to any venue.
