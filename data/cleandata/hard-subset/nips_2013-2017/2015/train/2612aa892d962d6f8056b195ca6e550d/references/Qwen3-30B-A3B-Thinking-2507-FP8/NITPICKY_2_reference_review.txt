

# Review of "Interactive Control of Diverse Complex Characters with Neural Networks"

This paper proposes a method for learning neural network controllers that can generate interactive, real-time control of complex physical characters in 3D environments. The approach combines trajectory optimization (specifically Contact-Invariant Optimization) with neural network training, using a distributed stochastic optimization framework to learn controllers that can handle dynamic changes in task objectives. The authors claim to achieve stable locomotion in 3D for diverse character types (bipedal, quadrupedal, swimming, flying) where previous work was limited to 2D.

## Quality
The paper presents an interesting approach that combines trajectory optimization with neural networks for real-time control. However, the technical quality is undermined by several significant issues. The paper makes strong claims about solving "substantially harder problems" than prior work (e.g., 3D vs 2D locomotion), but provides no quantitative evidence to support these claims. The comparison with prior work [9, 11] lacks specific metrics showing the performance gap.

The paper's core contribution appears to be a distributed stochastic optimization framework for joint trajectory optimization and neural network training. However, the paper fails to provide sufficient detail about how the optimal feedback gains (section 3.2) are computed, making it impossible to evaluate this key component. The claim that "computing optimal feedback gains comes at very little additional cost" is unsupported by any empirical evidence.

The paper states "our full method performs two orders of magnitude better than static training" (section 8.1), but fails to specify what metric this refers to. Without quantitative results to back up this claim, the significance of the approach remains unclear.

The paper also has multiple technical inconsistencies: inconsistent notation (q̇t defined as qt+1 - qt but using standard derivative notation), typos ("changed changed" in section 8), and incomplete references (e.g., "P. Chen, SIAM J. Numerical Analysis" with no year or page numbers).

## Clarity
The paper is generally well-organized, but suffers from several clarity issues. The sensory state representation (section 2) is described as "simple and general-purpose" but without justification for why this particular representation is optimal. The paper claims "our network learns both optimal controls and a model of dynamics simultaneously" but fails to explain how this is achieved or provide evidence for the dynamics model learning.

The distributed training architecture (section 6) is described in sufficient detail, but the paper lacks performance metrics showing how the distributed approach scales with the number of nodes. The statement "we can increase our training performance linearly for the size of cluster" is unsubstantiated.

The paper's most significant clarity issue is the lack of quantitative results. The paper states "our method manages to learn successful controllers" but provides no metrics to support this claim. The supplementary video is insufficient to evaluate the work scientifically.

## Originality
The paper combines trajectory optimization with neural networks for control, which is related to prior work [9, 11]. However, the paper fails to clearly articulate what makes this approach novel compared to previous work. The claim that this "solves substantially harder problems" than [9, 11] is unsubstantiated.

The paper's approach to optimal feedback gains (section 3.2) appears to be the most novel component, but the description is insufficient to understand the innovation. The paper claims this method "produces softer feedback gains" without providing evidence or comparison to alternative methods.

## Significance
The significance of the work is difficult to assess due to the lack of quantitative results. While the ability to control diverse 3D characters is potentially significant, the paper fails to demonstrate that this approach provides meaningful improvements over existing methods. The comparison to MPC (section 8.1) is particularly weak, as it only mentions that trajectories are "different" without providing any metrics.

The paper's claim that "the same algorithm and controller architecture can provide interactive control for multiple creature morphologies" is promising, but the evidence provided (a few video examples) is insufficient to support this claim in a scientific paper.

## Strengths
- The paper addresses an important problem in character animation and robotics.
- The distributed training approach is well-motivated and implemented in a practical way.
- The integration of trajectory optimization with neural network training is conceptually appealing.

## Weaknesses
- Lack of quantitative results to support key claims (e.g., 2 orders of magnitude better, successful 3D locomotion).
- Insufficient detail to reproduce the work (e.g., exact hyperparameters, network architecture details).
- Inconsistent and incomplete technical presentation (notation, references, typos).
- Overstated claims without sufficient evidence (e.g., "substantially harder problems").
- Reliance on supplementary video rather than quantitative metrics.

## Recommendation
I recommend rejection. While the paper addresses an important problem and presents a potentially interesting approach, the lack of quantitative results to support the key claims, the insufficient technical detail, and the presence of multiple technical inconsistencies significantly undermine the paper's scientific contribution. The paper makes strong claims about solving harder problems and achieving better performance without providing the necessary evidence to evaluate these claims. The quality of the paper as a scientific contribution is compromised by these issues.

The paper has promising ideas but fails to meet the standards expected for publication at NeurIPS due to insufficient evidence to support its claims and inadequate technical detail. The authors should provide quantitative results comparing their method to prior work and address the technical inconsistencies before resubmission.
