Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes a hybrid method for learning real-time neural network controllers for diverse physically-simulated characters (bipeds, quadrupeds, swimmer, bird). The core idea is to alternate between (i) direct trajectory optimization via Contact-Invariant Optimization (CIO) to produce locally optimal trajectories and local feedback around them, and (ii) supervised regression to fit a neural policy that maps noisy sensory inputs to actions. To make the policy robust, the authors inject Gaussian noise into sensory inputs and hidden activations, and train the policy to match not only the nominal optimal action but also the first-order action perturbation induced by the noise using locally computed feedback gains. They propose a perturbation-based method to extract these gains efficiently from the same derivatives computed by CIO (via a soft constraint and Woodbury identity), avoiding a separate LQG pass. The overall optimization is a block-alternating, proximal, stochastic procedure distributed asynchronously over many machines; at execution, the learned policy outputs desired state velocity and contact forces, and the next state is obtained by solving a T=1 trajectory optimization problem (an implicit dynamics step).

This work is closely related to guided policy search (GPS) and trajectory-optimization-based policy learning (e.g., Levine & Koltun, ICML 2014; Mordatch & Todorov, RSS 2014), but pushes to 3D characters and emphasizes CIO-based trajectory generation and the reuse of its derivatives to compute feedback gains. It also relates to model-predictive control (MPC), motion optimization in graphics, and recent deep RL in continuous control (e.g., TRPO), though direct empirical comparisons are limited.

Strengths
- Addresses a difficult, high-dimensional, contact-rich control setting and demonstrates real-time controllers for diverse 3D morphologies using a single policy architecture.
- Sound and practical alternating optimization framework that leverages trajectory optimization to generate high-quality supervision and a neural net to amortize control for interactive use.
- Principled noise-injection scheme: training targets include first-order action corrections a + As ε, where As are feedback gains derived efficiently from CIO derivatives via a perturbation method. This is a nice contribution that may be useful beyond this application.
- Asynchronous distributed implementation with proximal regularization is pragmatic and seems to converge in practice; training times and resource usage are reported.
- Ablations indicate the benefits of joint alternating optimization and noise injection.

Weaknesses
- Limited quantitative evaluation. Results rely heavily on videos; reported “two orders of magnitude” improvements lack clear metrics, scales, and statistical confidence. Success rates, stability to perturbations, and generalization to unseen goals are not quantified.
- Comparison to prior art is insufficient. GPS-style methods (Levine & Koltun 2014), TRPO (Schulman et al.), and MPC baselines deserve systematic quantitative comparisons (task cost, stability, sample efficiency, runtime), especially since the approach is incremental to GPS.
- Physics during training and execution use soft constraints and allow root forces; this calls into question physical realism and transfer to robotics. The mismatch between training (long-horizon CIO with soft dynamics) and execution (T=1 optimization) is not analyzed.
- The recurrent state r is central to the formulation but under-specified: dimensionality, initialization, whether the network is truly recurrent vs. using r as a learned memory state, and its effect on performance/robustness.
- Theoretical aspects (convergence of the alternating procedure, stability under rollout) are not addressed; empirical stability is shown informally.
- References are incomplete/irregular; some technical details (exact features φ, cost weights, linearization domains) are deferred or omitted.

Quality
Technically, the approach is coherent and well-motivated. The perturbation-based gain computation that reuses CXX−1 from CIO is elegant and likely more numerically stable than a separate LQG pass. The block-alternating penalized objective is standard and appropriate. However, important aspects are not rigorously evaluated: (i) closed-loop robustness to disturbances and model errors, (ii) sensitivity to λ (feedback softness), α and η (penalties), and noise scales; (iii) generalization beyond the training distribution of branched segments; and (iv) the impact of the T=1 implicit step vs. standard integrators. Empirical comparisons against MPC are qualitative; comparisons against GPS/TRPO are missing.

Clarity
The paper is generally readable and structured. Algorithm 1 is helpful. Still, several clarifications would improve reproducibility:
- Specify the sensory features and action parameterization precisely (dimensions, normalization), and the dimension and role of r.
- Detail the cost function c(·) and weights (including contact penalties), and the training data statistics (trajectory lengths T, total frames).
- Define evaluation metrics in Sec. 8.1 and present quantitative results (means/std across seeds/initial conditions).
- Provide more detail on the T=1 execution solver (objective weights, runtime per step).
References should be completed and standardized.

Originality
The work is an incremental but meaningful advance: it unifies known ingredients (trajectory optimization, supervised policy learning with noise, distributed training) and contributes a practical method for extracting feedback gains from a direct trajectory optimizer. Applying this to diverse 3D characters without hand-designed controllers is noteworthy in the graphics/control space. Relative to GPS, the main novelties are CIO as the generator, the gain computation, and the particular asynchronous proximal training regime.

Significance
If substantiated with stronger quantitative evidence and comparisons, the approach could be influential for learning amortized controllers from trajectory optimizers and for interactive character control. The gain extraction trick is of independent interest. Claims about robotics applicability should be tempered until hard-contact, torque-limited dynamics without root forces are demonstrated.

Suggestions for improvement and questions
- Provide quantitative metrics: task completion cost, tracking error, energy/force usage, perturbation recovery rates, and success rates across random initial states/goals; include statistical variation.
- Compare against GPS-style methods (e.g., linear-Gaussian local controllers + supervised policy), TRPO (or contemporary model-free methods), and an MPC baseline with the same horizon and costs.
- Analyze sensitivity to λ, α, η, σε, σγ; report conditioning and magnitudes of As; compare to an LQG-derived As for at least one task.
- Clarify the recurrent state r: dimension, role, and ablation without r. Is the network recurrent (e.g., RNN) or purely feedforward with r carrying memory?
- Provide execution-time performance (ms per step), stability under external pushes/noise, and generalization to goals beyond the training distribution.
- Discuss the effect of soft contact/root forces and feasibility of torque-limited, constraint-satisfying execution.
- Clean up and complete references.

Pros for acceptance
- Practical, scalable framework; impressive qualitative results across diverse 3D characters.
- Elegant and useful method to extract stabilizing feedback gains from CIO.
- Clear benefit of joint alternating optimization and noise-injection strategy.
- Relevance to both ML (policy learning) and graphics/control communities.

Cons against acceptance
- Insufficient quantitative evaluation and limited baselines.
- Ambiguity around recurrence, execution dynamics, and robustness.
- Reliance on soft dynamics and root forces undermines realism/transfer.
- Novelty relative to GPS-style methods is moderate.

Overall, this is a promising and well-engineered approach with an interesting technical component (feedback gain extraction), but it needs stronger empirical evaluation and clearer positioning relative to prior work to merit acceptance at NIPS.
