**Review of "Interactive Control of Diverse Complex Characters with Neural Networks"**

**Summary of the Paper**

This paper presents a method for automatically synthesizing interactive, real-time controllers for a diverse range of complex, physically-simulated 3D characters. The core idea is to combine the strengths of offline trajectory optimization with the real-time performance of a neural network policy. The authors propose a joint, alternating optimization scheme where: 1) A set of optimal trajectories are generated using Contact-Invariant Optimization (CIO), which aims to satisfy task goals and physical constraints while also staying close to the current neural network's predictions. 2) A neural network policy is then trained via supervised regression to mimic the state-action pairs from these optimized trajectories. This process is iterated, allowing the policy and the trajectory data to co-adapt.

Key technical contributions include a principled method for injecting noise during training to improve robustness, which involves using optimal feedback gains to compute the target action for a perturbed state. The paper also introduces a novel technique to extract these feedback gains from a direct trajectory optimization method like CIO, which is a non-trivial contribution. The entire system is implemented in a distributed, asynchronous architecture to make the computationally intensive training process feasible. The approach is demonstrated on a variety of challenging 3D characters, including bipeds, quadrupeds, a swimmer, and a flyer, all learning stable and natural-looking locomotion gaits from a unified framework without task-specific engineering.

This work builds upon a line of research that uses trajectory optimization to guide policy learning (e.g., Levine & Koltun, 2014; Mordatch & Todorov, 2014). However, it makes significant advances by successfully scaling these ideas to complex 3D characters with contacts, whereas previous work was largely limited to 2D, and by introducing the novel feedback-aware noise injection technique for improved robustness.

**Strengths (Reasons for Acceptance)**

1.  **High Significance and Impact:** The paper tackles a long-standing and difficult problem in control, animation, and robotics: the automatic creation of controllers for complex, high-dimensional systems. The ability to apply a single, general-purpose algorithm to generate controllers for bipeds, quadrupeds, swimmers, and flyers without morphology-specific tuning is a significant step forward. This unified approach could have a substantial impact on character animation in games, robotics, and even biomechanics research, moving the field away from manually designed, brittle state-machine controllers.

2.  **Strong Technical Quality and Novelty:** The proposed method is technically sound and contains several clever components.
    *   The joint optimization of trajectories and the policy is a principled approach to mitigating the compounding errors that plague naive behavioral cloning in control settings.
    *   The method for injecting noise (Section 2.1) is particularly novel. Instead of simply adding noise to inputs and targets, the authors use optimal feedback gains (derived from the optimizer) to calculate the *correct* optimal action for the perturbed state. This provides the network with richer, locally-correct information about how to recover from small deviations, which is crucial for closed-loop stability.
    *   The derivation for extracting these feedback gains from a direct trajectory optimizer (Section 3.2) is a valuable contribution in its own right, as these gains are not typically available from such methods.

3.  **Impressive and Well-Validated Results:** The results are the most compelling part of the paper. The fact that stable, cyclical, and natural-looking gaits (e.g., walking for bipeds, trotting for quadrupeds, flapping for the bird) emerge automatically is a powerful demonstration of the method's effectiveness. The ablation studies in Section 8.1 are crucial and clearly demonstrate the necessity of both the joint optimization framework (vs. training on a static dataset) and the noise injection techniques. The comparison against Model-Predictive Control (MPC) also provides interesting insight, suggesting the policy-regularized optimization finds more structured and less "chaotic" solutions.

**Weaknesses (Areas for Improvement)**

1.  **Clarity on Key Details:** While the paper is generally well-written, some details are sparse, which could hinder reproducibility.
    *   The exact formulation of the cost functions `c(φt(X))` in Section 3 is described only at a high level ("reach goal... minimizing torque"). Providing more specific details on the cost terms and their relative weights would strengthen the paper.
    *   The policy execution step (Section 7) is more complex than a simple forward pass; it involves a single-timestep optimization to satisfy dynamics constraints. This is an important and interesting detail, but its real-time performance implications are not discussed. How fast is this per-step optimization, and does it represent a bottleneck?
    *   The sensitivity to certain hyperparameters, like `λ` which controls the "softness" of the feedback gains, is not analyzed.

2.  **Relation to Guided Policy Search (GPS):** The alternating optimization scheme is conceptually very similar to Guided Policy Search algorithms. The authors cite relevant prior work but could be more explicit in differentiating their approach. They mention that adapting ADMM or dual gradient descent from prior work is "non-trivial" for their asynchronous, stochastic setting, but a more detailed discussion of the trade-offs between their block-alternating optimization with proximal terms and these other constrained optimization techniques would better position their contribution.

3.  **Limited Scope of "Interaction":** The paper emphasizes "interactive" control. However, the interaction demonstrated during training and execution is limited to changing the goal location. The method's robustness to other common forms of interaction, such as unexpected external perturbations (e.g., pushes) or dynamic obstacles, is not evaluated. While the learned policies may possess some robustness due to the noise injection, explicitly testing this would make the claims of interactivity more robust.

**Recommendation**

Overall, this is an excellent paper that presents a significant advance in automated controller synthesis for complex characters. The strengths—a powerful, generalizable method, novel technical contributions, and compelling, well-validated results—far outweigh the weaknesses, which are primarily related to clarifying details rather than fundamental flaws. The work is of high quality and is likely to be highly influential in the fields of reinforcement learning, robotics, and computer graphics.

I strongly recommend **acceptance**.
