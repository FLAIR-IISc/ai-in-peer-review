This paper presents a framework for automatically synthesizing neural network controllers for interactive, real-time control of physically-simulated characters. The core idea is to leverage the power of offline trajectory optimization to generate data for training a neural network policy. The work distinguishes itself from prior art by proposing a joint optimization procedure where the trajectories and the policy are optimized concurrently in an alternating fashion. This is in contrast to a simpler two-stage approach of first generating a static dataset and then training a policy.

A key technical contribution is a principled method for injecting noise to improve policy robustness. Instead of just training on noisy state-action pairs, the authors use the trajectory optimizer to compute optimal feedback gains. These gains are then used to generate corrected target actions for perturbed sensory inputs, effectively teaching the network not just what to do, but how to react to small deviations. The paper also introduces an efficient method for calculating these feedback gains as a byproduct of the direct trajectory optimization, avoiding a separate and potentially ill-conditioned LQR pass. The entire system is implemented in a distributed, asynchronous architecture to handle the significant computational load. The method is shown to be effective across a diverse set of 3D characters, including bipeds, quadrupeds, swimmers, and flyers, successfully learning stable and natural-looking locomotion gaits without task-specific engineering.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Significance and Impressive Results:** The paper tackles a very challenging problem: generating a single, general-purpose controller that can produce high-quality, interactive 3D locomotion for characters with vastly different morphologies and dynamics (walking, trotting, swimming, flying). The ability to generate these complex and natural-looking behaviors, as evidenced by the supplementary video, from a unified framework is a significant achievement and a substantial advance for the field of character animation and control.

2.  **Novel and Principled Methodology:** The proposed joint optimization framework is a clear step up from prior work that learns from static, pre-computed trajectory datasets. The core technical idea of using optimal feedback gains `a_s` to define the learning target `a + a_sε` for a perturbed state `s + ε` is particularly novel and elegant. It provides a principled way to transfer more information from the optimizer to the policy—specifically, the local linearization of the optimal control law—which is crucial for achieving the reported robustness. The derivation of these gains from the direct optimizer's Hessian (Section 3.2) is a clever and valuable contribution in its own right.

3.  **Thorough Empirical Validation:** The authors provide strong empirical support for their design choices. The ablation study in Figure 2 is particularly compelling, clearly demonstrating that both the joint optimization ("full method" vs. "no joint") and the principled noise injection ("full method" vs. "no noise") are essential for success. The poor test-time performance in the ablation cases validates the authors' claims about the importance of these components for generalization and preventing policy divergence.

4.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is well-written and logically structured. It starts with a clear high-level problem formulation (Eq. 1) and systematically breaks it down into the constituent parts: the alternating optimization algorithm, the trajectory optimization details, and the neural network regression. This makes the complex methodology relatively easy to follow.

### **Weaknesses**

1.  **Reproducibility Details:** While the overall method is described clearly, some crucial details for reproducing the results are missing. The cost function `C(X)` is the heart of the trajectory optimization, defining the desired behavior. The paper only provides a high-level description ("reach goal position and orientation while minimizing torque usage and contact force magnitudes"). The precise formulation of these cost terms and their relative weights, which can be difficult to tune, are not provided. Including these details, perhaps in an appendix, would significantly increase the value and reproducibility of the work.

2.  **Limited Scope of "Interaction":** The interaction model is limited to the user changing the goal position and orientation for the character. While this is sufficient for demonstrating locomotion, it is a fairly simple form of interaction. The paper does not address how the framework would scale to more complex, reactive behaviors, such as avoiding dynamic obstacles or physically interacting with objects. The sensory input `s_t` does not appear to include any information about the external environment beyond the character's own state, which would be a prerequisite for such tasks.

3.  **Qualitative Comparisons:** The comparisons to alternative methods like Deep Reinforcement Learning and Model Predictive Control (MPC) are largely qualitative. The paper suggests that direct RL methods produce "somewhat chaotic behaviors," but a quantitative comparison (e.g., on trajectory smoothness, energy efficiency, or task success rate) against a contemporary baseline like TRPO [14] would have made this claim more concrete. Similarly, the comparison to MPC in Figure 3 is illustrative but would be strengthened by quantitative metrics that capture the difference in trajectory "cyclicality" or "predictability."

4.  **Computational Cost:** The method is computationally intensive, requiring a cluster of "about 30 optimization nodes per GPU machine" and several hours of training. While the quality of the results may justify this cost, it represents a significant barrier to adoption and experimentation. A more explicit discussion of these computational requirements and trade-offs would be beneficial.

### **Constructive Suggestions for Improvement**

*   Please consider adding an appendix detailing the exact cost functions, including terms and weights, used for the different characters. This would be invaluable for other researchers wishing to build upon this work.
*   In the discussion, it would be helpful to more explicitly state the limitations of the current sensory inputs and interaction model and to speculate on how the framework could be extended to handle richer environmental perception (e.g., using heightfields or depth information) for more complex tasks.
*   For future work, a direct quantitative comparison with a strong deep RL baseline on one of the simpler characters (e.g., the biped) would be a powerful way to substantiate the claimed benefits of the trajectory optimization-based approach in terms of motion quality and stability.
*   The method for calculating feedback gains (Section 3.2) is very interesting and potentially applicable beyond this paper's scope. It might be worth highlighting this contribution more explicitly as a general technique for extracting local feedback policies from direct trajectory optimizers.

In summary, this paper presents a high-quality, significant, and novel contribution to the problem of interactive character control. The impressive results across a wide range of characters are well-supported by a principled methodology and solid empirical evaluation. While there are minor weaknesses regarding reproducibility details and the scope of comparisons, they do not detract from the importance of the core contribution. This work is a clear advance in the state of the art and is very likely to inspire future research in control, robotics, and animation.
