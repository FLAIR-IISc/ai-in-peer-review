### Paper Summary

This paper presents a method for automatically synthesizing neural network controllers for a diverse range of complex, 3D physically-simulated characters. The goal is to create interactive, real-time policies that produce stable and realistic locomotion (walking, flying, swimming). The core idea is to combine offline trajectory optimization with supervised learning in a joint optimization framework. The method iterates between two main steps: 1) using a powerful trajectory optimizer, Contact-Invariant Optimization (CIO), to generate a dataset of optimal motion trajectories that fulfill various tasks (e.g., moving to a goal), and 2) training a neural network policy to mimic the state-action pairs from these trajectories.

This work builds upon recent efforts to distill slow, offline optimizers into fast, online neural network policies (e.g., Levine & Koltun, 2014; Mordatch & Todorov, 2014). The key contributions that enable this method to tackle more complex 3D characters include: a principled approach to noise injection for robust learning, where not only the sensory inputs are perturbed but the target actions are also adjusted using optimal feedback gains; a novel method for extracting these feedback gains efficiently from a direct trajectory optimization method; and a distributed, asynchronous training architecture to handle the computational scale of the problem. The authors demonstrate the versatility of their single, unified framework by successfully training controllers for bipeds, quadrupeds, a swimming creature, and a flying creature, all of which exhibit plausible, emergent gaits without task-specific engineering.

### Strengths

1.  **Significant and Impressive Results:** The primary strength of this paper is the successful application of a single, general-purpose algorithm to create high-quality controllers for a wide variety of 3D characters with different morphologies and physics (walking, swimming, flying). This is a substantial step forward from prior work that was largely limited to 2D characters. The emergence of natural-looking gaits (bipedal walk, quadrupedal trot, flapping/gliding) from a general task objective is a powerful demonstration of the method's effectiveness.

2.  **Novel and Sound Methodology:** The paper's technical contributions are solid and well-motivated.
    *   The joint optimization framework that alternates between refining trajectories and the policy is elegant. The use of proximal regularization terms is a sensible way to stabilize this alternating process in an asynchronous setting.
    *   The noise injection technique is particularly clever. By computing the optimal action for a *perturbed state* using optimal feedback gains (Eq. 2), the network learns a locally linear model of the optimal policy around the trajectory. This is a much more powerful learning signal than simply training on state-action pairs, and the ablation study convincingly shows its importance for achieving stable policies.
    *   The method for extracting feedback gains from the direct CIO optimizer (Section 3.2) is a valuable technical contribution in its own right. It avoids a costly, separate LQG pass and produces better-conditioned gains.

3.  **Strong Empirical Evaluation:** The comparative evaluation in Section 8.1 is excellent. The ablation study clearly isolates the contributions of the key components of their system: joint optimization ("no joint" case) and noise injection ("no noise" case). The results, showing that the full method outperforms these ablations by orders of magnitude and is the only one to produce stable rollouts, provide strong evidence for the authors' claims. The comparison with MPC, showing that their method produces more regular, cyclic motions, is also insightful.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Limited Comparison to Direct Reinforcement Learning:** The paper positions itself as an alternative to direct policy search methods (like TRPO, etc.), suggesting its resulting behaviors are "more realistic and purposeful" while others are "somewhat chaotic." This is an interesting and plausible claim, but it is stated as an "impression" and is not substantiated with a direct experimental comparison. While such a comparison would be computationally expensive, including results from even one character trained with a state-of-the-art RL algorithm would make the paper's claims about motion quality much more concrete.

2.  **Clarity of the Policy Execution Step:** The policy execution method described in Section 7 is slightly unusual and could be explained more clearly. At each time step, instead of just using the network's output `q_dot` to step the simulation forward, the authors solve a T=1 optimization problem to find the next state that best matches the network's output while satisfying the physics constraints. While this is a valid way to enforce physical plausibility and stability (acting like an implicit integrator), it feels like more than simply "executing the policy." The authors should clarify why this is necessary. Does a simple forward integration of the policy's output lead to instability? A brief discussion of this design choice and its computational implications for real-time performance would be beneficial.

3.  **Density of Technical Sections:** Section 3.2, which describes the extraction of optimal feedback gains, is technically dense. While the derivation appears correct, a bit more intuition behind the steps, particularly the use of the Woodbury identity, could improve readability and help readers appreciate this novel contribution more fully.

### Overall Assessment

This is a high-quality paper that presents a significant advance in automated character control. The authors combine existing techniques in a novel framework and introduce several clever technical improvements that collectively enable a breakthrough in capability—scaling from 2D to a diverse range of complex 3D characters. The results are impressive, the methodology is sound, and the experimental validation is strong. While the comparison to alternative RL methods could be stronger and some technical details could be clarified, these are minor weaknesses. The work is highly significant and is likely to influence future research in computer animation, robotics, and reinforcement learning. It clearly advances the state of the art in a demonstrable way.

**Arguments for acceptance:**
*   Presents a novel and effective framework for a difficult, important problem.
*   Achieves state-of-the-art results on a diverse set of challenging 3D control tasks.
*   Contains strong technical contributions, particularly the principled noise injection and feedback gain extraction methods.
*   Includes a thorough ablation study that validates the key components of the proposed method.
*   High potential for impact in multiple fields.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
*   The claims regarding superiority over direct RL methods are not backed by direct empirical comparisons.
*   Some implementation details (e.g., the policy execution step) could be motivated and explained more clearly.

**Recommendation:** Strong Accept.
