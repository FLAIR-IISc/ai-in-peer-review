This paper presents a method for training a neural network policy to control diverse, physically simulated 3D characters in real-time. The core idea is to leverage an offline trajectory optimization method, Contact-Invariant Optimization (CIO), to generate a dataset of near-optimal state-action trajectories for various tasks (e.g., locomotion to a goal). A neural network is then trained to mimic the behavior prescribed by these trajectories. The authors introduce a joint, alternating optimization procedure where the trajectories and the network policy are iteratively refined together. Key technical contributions include a principled method for injecting noise during training by using optimal feedback gains to generate corrected action targets, and a novel technique for extracting these feedback gains directly from the CIO planner. The method is demonstrated on a range of challenging 3D characters, including bipeds, quadrupeds, a swimmer, and a flyer, producing stable and visually plausible locomotion.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Impressive Qualitative Results:** The primary strength of the paper is its demonstrated ability to generate stable and plausible controllers for a wide variety of complex 3D character morphologies using a single, unified framework. Achieving robust locomotion for bipeds, quadrupeds, swimmers, and fliers without morphology-specific controller structures is a significant achievement.
2.  **Principled Approach to Robustness:** The method for improving policy robustness via noise injection (Section 2.1) is well-motivated. Instead of simply training the network to denoise states, the authors use optimal feedback gains (`as`) to compute the corresponding optimal action for the perturbed state (`a + asε`). This provides the network with direct information about the local dynamics of the optimal controller, which is a more effective learning signal than what is used in simpler imitation learning setups.
3.  **Strong Ablation Study:** The comparative evaluation in Section 8.1 provides convincing evidence for the authors' main methodological claims. The "no joint" case clearly demonstrates that a static dataset is insufficient and that the proposed iterative refinement is critical for success. Similarly, the "no noise" case highlights the importance of their stochastic training procedure in preventing overfitting and achieving policies that are stable when rolled out in a closed loop.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Lack of Comparison to Established Baselines:** The most significant weakness of this work is the omission of a quantitative comparison to standard, state-of-the-art reinforcement learning (RL) algorithms. The paper operates in the domain of continuous control, where methods like Trust Region Policy Optimization (TRPO), Proximal Policy Optimization (PPO), or Soft Actor-Critic (SAC) are considered standard baselines. The authors dismiss "direct methods" in the introduction with the unsubstantiated claim that they produce "somewhat chaotic behaviors." This is insufficient justification. Without a rigorous comparison on quantitative metrics (e.g., task success rate, energy efficiency, stability over long horizons, sample complexity), it is impossible to assess whether this complex, two-stage optimization process offers any tangible benefit over modern RL techniques. The qualitative comparison to Model Predictive Control (MPC) is interesting but lacks rigor and fails to address the most relevant competing methods.
2.  **Incremental Originality of the Overall Framework:** The general approach of using trajectory optimization to guide or train a neural network policy is not new. It is the core idea behind Guided Policy Search and related methods, and the authors cite similar prior work (Levine & Koltun, 2014; Mordatch & Todorov, 2014). The main contribution here is the successful application to more complex 3D characters. The optimization algorithm itself (Algorithm 1) is a straightforward alternating minimization with proximal regularization. The authors acknowledge that this may have a slower convergence rate than the ADMM or dual methods used in prior work, which positions it as more of an engineering simplification than a principled algorithmic advance.
3.  **Insufficient Analysis of Proposed Feedback Gain Method:** The proposed method for extracting feedback gains from a direct trajectory optimizer (Section 3.2) is a novel technical point. However, its properties are not sufficiently analyzed. The claim that it produces "softer" or "better-conditioned" gains compared to a standard LQR pass is asserted based on intuition but not supported by empirical evidence. A direct comparison of the gain matrices, their condition numbers, or the resulting closed-loop stability for both methods would be needed to substantiate this claim and validate the contribution.
4.  **Reproducibility Concerns:** Key details needed for reproducibility are sparse. The specific cost functions used in the trajectory optimization (`C(X)`) are central to the results but are only described in general terms ("minimizing torque usage and contact force magnitudes"). The success of any trajectory optimization method is highly sensitive to the formulation and weighting of these costs. Without more detail, it would be difficult for another researcher to reproduce the behaviors shown.

**Summary of Strengths and Weaknesses for Acceptance:**

**Pros:**
*   Demonstrates impressive, stable control for a diverse set of complex 3D characters from a single framework.
*   The noise injection technique, using optimal feedback gains, is a methodologically sound way to improve policy robustness.
*   The ablation studies provide strong internal validation for the key components of the proposed method (joint optimization and noise).

**Cons:**
*   A critical lack of comparison to standard reinforcement learning baselines makes it impossible to evaluate the significance of the results in the context of the wider control literature.
*   The overall framework is an incremental improvement on established methods (trajectory optimization guiding policy learning).
*   The novelty of the proposed optimization algorithm and feedback gain calculation is modest and not rigorously validated against alternatives.

**Recommendation:**

While the paper presents a well-engineered system that produces impressive visual results, it falls short of the scientific rigor expected at NIPS, primarily due to the complete absence of comparisons to established state-of-the-art reinforcement learning methods. The contributions are more heavily weighted towards demonstrating what can be done by combining existing techniques, rather than presenting and validating a fundamentally new and superior approach. The claims of novelty in the optimization and feedback gain calculations are not sufficiently supported by analysis or experiments. Therefore, I lean towards rejection. A revised version that includes a thorough quantitative comparison against modern RL baselines would be much more compelling.
