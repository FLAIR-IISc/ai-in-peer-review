Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes a method to learn real-time, interactive controllers for diverse 3D characters by combining direct trajectory optimization (Contact-Invariant Optimization, CIO) with neural network policy regression. The policy maps a sensory state to actions consisting of desired generalized velocities, recurrent memory updates, and contact forces. Training alternates between (i) optimizing trajectories that minimize task and physics-consistency costs plus a penalty for deviating from the current policy, and (ii) regressing a neural policy to match noisy sensory inputs and corresponding “corrected” actions. A key idea is to inject input noise and adjust target actions using locally optimal feedback gains (computed efficiently from the CIO Gauss-Newton system via a soft-constraint perturbation derivation), to promote robust closed-loop behavior. Training is distributed and asynchronous across many CPU nodes for trajectory optimization and a single GPU for NN training, with proximal regularization to stabilize alternating updates. At test time, policy outputs are enforced via a 1-step optimization that respects dynamics and contacts. The method is demonstrated on swimming, flying, biped and quadruped locomotion tasks without task-specific architectures.

The work builds on prior “learning from trajectory optimization” approaches, notably Guided Policy Search (Levine & Koltun, 2014) and Mordatch & Todorov (RSS’14), as well as CIO for synthesizing contact-rich motions (Mordatch et al., TOG’12). Compared to earlier 2D demonstrations, the paper claims robust 3D locomotion across morphologies, and introduces a specific mechanism to compute stabilizing feedback gains from a direct optimizer. The comparison to model-predictive control (MPC) highlights that the joint optimization biases toward stereotyped, predictable gaits.

Strengths
- Addresses a hard, impactful problem: real-time control of high-DOF characters with contacts, unifying locomotion across morphologies without hand-crafted state machines or motion capture.
- Methodologically coherent combination of CIO with policy regression and dataset aggregation via branch-and-continue trajectory generation.
- The perturbation-based derivation of feedback gains from the CIO Gauss-Newton system is neat, computationally efficient, and practically relevant for stabilizing learned policies under input noise.
- Well-engineered distributed system; the asynchronous alternating optimization with proximal terms is sensible and appears to converge in practice.
- Ablations (no joint optimization, no noise) provide evidence that both components are important; qualitative results show plausible 3D gaits (swim/flap/walk/trot) that emerge without cycle priors.

Weaknesses and detailed comments
Quality
- Quantitative evaluation is limited. The paper mentions “two orders of magnitude” improvements and shows figures (not in the text), but provides few concrete metrics (e.g., time-to-fall, goal-tracking error, energy/torque, foot-slip/contact violation, success rate across random goals). Robustness to perturbations (external pushes, terrain variations) is not assessed.
- The comparison to alternative learning methods (TRPO, model-free RL; other GPS variants; DAgger-style dataset aggregation) is missing experimentally. Given the positioning relative to [9,11,14], a head-to-head on a common task would substantiate claims of “more realistic and purposeful behaviors.”
- Real-time execution still solves a T=1 optimization enforcing dynamics and contact constraints; this is more than a pure feedforward policy and akin to a short-horizon MPC. The runtime and scalability of this step (with constraint softness, contact complementarity) should be reported; how many Newton steps per frame, and what is the latency on the reported hardware?
- Physical realism: the dynamics are enforced via soft constraints and “may include some root forces.” This undermines claims about stability/realism, especially for bipeds. Please quantify the magnitude/frequency of non-physical residuals during execution.
- The policy outputs contact forces directly. This can aid the 1-step projection but raises concerns about physical consistency when run without the projection. A discussion or experiment on using torques-only actions (or separating force targets from control torques) would clarify generality to robotics.

Clarity
- Overall well-written, but several important details are underspecified:
  - Exact feature set φ and cost terms (weights) used in CIO; without this, reproduction is difficult and fairness of comparisons is unclear.
  - Dimensionality and semantics of the recurrent state r; why not a standard RNN? How is r initialized across tasks and how large is it?
  - Precise training dataset sizes per character (frames, segments, goals), and how the goal is encoded in s.
  - Details of the 1-step execution optimization (solver tolerances, iterations, line search, time per frame).
- Some notation is inconsistent or imprecise (e.g., argmin∗, C̃ definitions). There are typos (“unity” vs “unify,” duplicated words).

Originality
- The central paradigm—distilling a policy from trajectory optimization with noisy inputs and local linear feedback targets—closely resembles Guided Policy Search. The novelty is primarily: (i) using CIO for rich contact behaviors across 3D morphologies, (ii) an efficient perturbation-based computation of feedback gains reusing the CIO Gauss-Newton inverse, and (iii) an asynchronous alternating training scheme with proximal regularization. These are incremental but practically meaningful contributions.
- Related work coverage could cite dataset aggregation (DAgger) and more recent GPS/TRPO variants to better position the approach.

Significance
- If substantiated with stronger quantitative results and comparisons, the approach could be influential in character animation and potentially in robotics simulation, offering a general recipe for learning robust controllers from powerful trajectory optimizers.
- The reliance on soft dynamics and an online projection step may limit adoption for real hardware unless adapted.

Questions for the authors
- Please provide quantitative metrics (tracking error, time-to-fall, torque/energy, contact violations) across tasks, with variance across random seeds/goals.
- What are the sizes of r and s? How sensitive are results to the choice of recurrent state and its noise variance?
- What is the runtime of the T=1 execution step per frame for each character? Can it be ablated (e.g., pure Euler integration) to show its necessity?
- How large are the residual root forces during execution? Can they be driven to near-zero with harder constraints?
- Can you compare against a GPS variant (LQR-FLM/BADMM) and a model-free baseline (TRPO) on the biped task?

Pros
- Demonstrates diverse 3D behaviors with one algorithm.
- Clever, efficient feedback-gain computation from CIO; principled noise-injection scheme.
- Practical distributed training with evidence of convergence.

Cons
- Weak quantitative evaluation and limited baselines.
- Soft dynamics and online optimization at test time complicate claims of realism and “pure policy” deployment.
- Some missing details hinder reproducibility.

Overall assessment
A solid and promising systems paper that pushes policy learning from trajectory optimization to diverse 3D locomotion, with a useful technical contribution on extracting stabilizing gains from a direct optimizer. To merit acceptance at NIPS, the paper should strengthen quantitative evaluation and experimental comparisons, and clarify execution-time realism and efficiency. I lean borderline accept if the authors can address these concerns; otherwise, this is a strong submission for graphics/robotics venues.
