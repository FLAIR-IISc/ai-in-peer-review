### Paper Review

**Summary of the Paper**

This paper presents a method for training neural network control policies for interactive, real-time control of diverse and complex 3D physically-simulated characters. The core contribution is a framework that jointly optimizes a collection of motion trajectories and the parameters of a neural network policy. The method alternates between two main steps: 1) refining a dataset of trajectories using a direct trajectory optimization method (Contact-Invariant Optimization, or CIO), and 2) training a neural network via regression to mimic the state-action pairs from these trajectories.

To enhance the robustness and generalization of the learned policy, the authors introduce a principled method for injecting noise during training. Gaussian noise is added to the sensory inputs of the network. Crucially, the corresponding target actions are adjusted using optimal feedback gains, which reflect how the optimal action should change in response to state perturbations. The paper introduces a novel and efficient technique to compute these feedback gains as a byproduct of the direct trajectory optimization process, a capability not typically associated with such methods. The entire training process is implemented in a distributed, asynchronous architecture to handle the computational load. The authors demonstrate the versatility of their unified approach by successfully synthesizing controllers for a wide range of 3D characters, including bipeds, quadrupeds, a swimmer, and a flyer, all using the same algorithm and network architecture.

This work builds upon prior research that combines trajectory optimization with policy learning, such as Levine & Koltun (2014) and Mordatch & Todorov (2014). The key distinction and advancement claimed by this paper is its successful application to substantially more complex control problems in 3D, whereas previous work was largely confined to 2D characters. This is attributed to the technical improvements in the joint optimization and noise-handling process.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Quality:**

The paper is of high technical quality. The proposed framework is sound, and the claims are well-supported by experimental evidence.

*   **Strengths:**
    *   The derivation of the method for computing optimal feedback gains from a direct trajectory optimization method (Section 3.2) is a significant technical contribution. It provides a principled way to generate corrected training targets for noisy inputs, which is more rigorous than simply training a policy to be robust to noise without adjusting the action.
    *   The experimental results are compelling. The ability to generate stable, interactive locomotion for a diverse set of 3D morphologies (bipeds, quadrupeds, swimmers, flyers) with a single, unified algorithm demonstrates the power and generality of the approach.
    *   The ablation study presented in Figure 2 provides clear empirical evidence for the necessity of the two key components of the method: the joint optimization of trajectories and policy, and the injection of noise. The results show that naively training on a static dataset or training without noise leads to policies that perform orders of magnitude worse and fail to produce stable behavior.

*   **Weaknesses:**
    *   The convergence properties of the overall alternating optimization scheme (Algorithm 1) are discussed only empirically. While the use of proximal regularization terms is sensible, the paper lacks a formal analysis of the algorithm's convergence, relying instead on the observation that it works in practice.
    *   The policy execution step (Section 7) requires solving a small optimization problem at each time step. While the authors state this can be done at real-time rates, details on the computational cost and complexity of this step are sparse. Further clarification would strengthen the claim of real-time interactivity.

**Clarity:**

The paper is generally well-written and clearly structured.

*   **Strengths:**
    *   The problem is clearly formulated, and the overall algorithm is well-described, particularly in the overview (Section 2).
    *   The motivation for each component of the method, such as the use of feedback gains and noise, is well-explained.
    *   The description of the distributed training architecture provides valuable practical insight into implementing such a computationally intensive method.

*   **Weaknesses:**
    *   Reproducibility could be improved with more detail. For instance, the exact composition of the cost function `C(X)` and its feature vector `φt(X)` are not fully specified. While general components are mentioned (e.g., minimizing torque, reaching a goal), the precise formulation and weightings are omitted.
    *   The comparison to Model Predictive Control (MPC) in Section 8.1 is insightful but brief. The claim that the joint optimization acts as a regularizer that encourages more predictable, cyclic behaviors is interesting, but would be stronger with a more quantitative analysis beyond the single illustrative figure.

**Originality:**

The paper makes several novel contributions to the field.

*   **Strengths:**
    *   While the general concept of learning policies from trajectory optimization exists, this work presents a novel and more advanced instantiation. The joint, simultaneous optimization of the policy and the training trajectories is a key improvement over methods that learn from a fixed dataset.
    *   The technique for extracting and utilizing optimal feedback gains from direct trajectory optimization to enable principled noise injection is, to our knowledge, novel. This is a clever adaptation that extends the capabilities of direct optimization methods.
    *   The successful application of a single, unified framework to learn controllers for such a diverse set of complex 3D characters represents a significant and original advance over prior art, which was largely limited to simpler, 2D domains.

**Significance:**

The results presented in this paper are highly significant and likely to have a substantial impact.

*   **Strengths:**
    *   The work makes tangible progress on the long-standing problem of automatic controller synthesis for complex physical systems. The ability to generate robust, interactive controllers from only a character model and a high-level task description is a major step forward.
    *   The methods developed, particularly the feedback gain computation for direct optimization methods, are likely to be influential and applicable to other problems in control, robotics, and reinforcement learning where policies are learned from expert demonstrations or optimal controllers.
    *   The paper demonstrably advances the state of the art in physics-based character animation and control. The emergent, natural-looking gaits (e.g., bipedal walking, quadrupedal trotting) are impressive and showcase the potential of optimization-based approaches to discover complex behaviors without manual engineering. Practitioners in gaming, animation, and robotics are likely to find these ideas useful and build upon them.

---

### Arguments for Acceptance

*   The paper successfully tackles a very challenging problem: learning robust, interactive 3D locomotion controllers for a wide variety of character morphologies using a single, unified method.
*   It introduces a novel and technically sound method for computing and using optimal feedback gains within a direct trajectory optimization framework, which is a significant contribution to the field of optimal control and policy learning.
*   The empirical results are strong and convincing, supported by a proper ablation study that validates the core components of the proposed algorithm.
*   The work is of high significance and is likely to inspire future research in machine learning, computer graphics, and robotics.

### Arguments Against Acceptance

*   The convergence of the main algorithm is not theoretically proven, which is a minor theoretical weakness.
*   Certain implementation details, such as the precise cost function formulation, are not fully specified, potentially hindering exact reproducibility.

Overall, the strengths of this paper far outweigh its weaknesses. It presents a novel, high-quality, and significant contribution to the field.
