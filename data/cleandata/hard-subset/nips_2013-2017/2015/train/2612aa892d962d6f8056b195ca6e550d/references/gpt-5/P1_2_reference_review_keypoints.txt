- Proposes a hybrid method alternating CIO-based trajectory optimization with supervised neural policy learning, using noise-injection and locally computed feedback gains for robustness.  
- Reuses CIO derivatives via a perturbation/Woodbury trick to efficiently extract first-order feedback gains without a separate LQG pass.  
- Executes learned policy by outputting desired state velocity and contact forces, then solving a T=1 implicit dynamics optimization at each step.  
- Related to GPS and trajectory-optimization-based policy learning, but emphasizes CIO for trajectory generation and gain extraction; also connected to MPC and deep RL.  
- Demonstrates real-time controllers for diverse 3D morphologies (bipeds, quadrupeds, swimmer, bird) with a single policy architecture.  
- Presents a pragmatic asynchronous distributed training scheme with proximal regularization and reported training times/resources.  
- Principled noise-injection scheme: targets include first-order action corrections a + As ε derived from CIO, which is a useful technical contribution.  
- Ablation studies suggest benefits of joint alternating optimization and noise injection.  
- Quantitative evaluation is limited: heavy reliance on videos, unclear metrics, no statistical confidence or detailed success rates/stability measures.  
- Lacks systematic comparisons to prior methods (GPS, TRPO, MPC); empirical baselines and sample-efficiency/runtime comparisons are missing.  
- Use of soft contact constraints and root forces during training/execution raises concerns about physical realism and transfer to robotics.  
- Mismatch between long-horizon CIO training (soft dynamics) and T=1 execution is not analyzed.  
- The recurrent state r is under-specified: dimensionality, initialization, whether it’s a true recurrence, and its impact on performance are unclear.  
- Theoretical guarantees (convergence of alternating procedure, closed-loop stability) are not addressed.  
- Important empirical aspects are under-evaluated: robustness to disturbances/model errors, sensitivity to penalty/noise hyperparameters, generalization beyond training distribution, and impact of T=1 step vs. standard integrators.  
- Paper is generally readable and structured, but several reproducibility details are missing (features φ, action parameterization, cost weights, trajectory statistics).  
- Algorithm 1 is helpful, but evaluation metrics and quantitative results (means/std across seeds/initial conditions) should be provided.  
- T=1 execution solver details (weights, per-step runtime) and more complete references are needed for clarity and reproducibility.  
- Novelty is incremental relative to GPS but meaningful: main contributions are CIO usage, gain extraction trick, and asynchronous proximal training regime.  
- The gain-extraction method is elegant and of independent interest; applying the pipeline to diverse 3D characters is notable.  
- If strengthened with quantitative evidence and baselines, the approach could be influential for amortized controllers from trajectory optimizers.  
- Claims about robotics applicability should be tempered until torque-limited, hard-contact dynamics without root forces are demonstrated.  
- Suggested improvements: add quantitative metrics (tracking/error/energy/success rates), compare to GPS/TRPO/MPC, sensitivity analyses, ablate r, report execution-time/stability, and examine contact realism.  
- Pros for acceptance: scalable framework, impressive qualitative results, useful gain extraction, clear benefit of alternating optimization and noise injection.  
- Cons against acceptance: insufficient quantitative evaluation and baselines, ambiguity about recurrence and execution dynamics, reliance on soft dynamics/root forces, moderate novelty vs. GPS.
