Summary
The paper proposes an approach for interactive control of diverse, physically simulated 3D characters by combining direct trajectory optimization (contact-invariant optimization, CIO) with supervised learning of a neural network policy. The key idea is to alternate between (i) optimizing trajectories under physics and task costs and (ii) fitting a neural network to map noisy sensory inputs to actions that reconstruct the optimized trajectories. Noise is injected into sensory inputs and hidden activations; first-order corrections to the action under noisy inputs are provided via locally optimal feedback gains derived from CIO using a perturbation/soft-constraint method. The training is executed asynchronously across a cluster (many CPU workers for CIO, one GPU for NN), with proximal regularization to stabilize alternation. At test time, the learned policy outputs desired velocities, recurrence updates, and contact forces, and a short-horizon (T=1) optimization step advances the physical state subject to constraints. The paper reports qualitative results for swimmers, flyers, bipeds, and quadrupeds, and ablations showing that joint optimization and noise are important.

Relation to prior work: The approach is closely related to guided policy search methods that combine trajectory optimization with supervised policy learning (e.g., Levine & Koltun 2014; Mordatch & Todorov 2014). Compared to those, the paper emphasizes 3D characters, an asynchronous distributed implementation, noise-injection with linearized action targets, and a specific procedure to compute feedback gains from a direct trajectory optimizer. It contrasts qualitatively with MPC and cites classic control/animation approaches (Simbicon, CPGs, ZMP) and modern RL (TRPO, Atari DQN); however, quantitative comparisons are limited.

Strengths
- Pragmatic integration of a strong trajectory optimizer (CIO) with policy learning yields real-time controllers and diverse behaviors without hand-tuned state machines or mocap.
- The perturbation-based method to extract local feedback gains from the Gauss–Newton machinery of CIO is technically interesting and potentially useful beyond this paper.
- Sensory and network noise are used in a principled way to improve robustness; the ablations suggest they matter.
- Implementation details (distributed training, GPU SGD, proximal regularization) are practical and relevant.
- Qualitative demonstrations across several morphologies and 3D locomotion are impressive for interactive control.

Weaknesses
- Evaluation is largely qualitative; heavy reliance on a video. There are no standardized quantitative metrics: success rate, tracking error, falls, energy/torque usage, foot slip, contact violations, robustness to disturbances, generalization to unseen goals, etc.
- Baselines are minimal. There is no systematic comparison to guided policy search variants (e.g., ADMM/dual, TRPO-guided), model-based MPC/iLQG with learned policies, or modern continuous-control RL (e.g., TRPO, PILCO). The single MPC comparison is anecdotal and does not report performance metrics.
- Novelty vs prior guided policy search work is incremental. The overall formulation (alternating trajectory optimization and supervised policy fitting with noise) is well established. The main new components (gain extraction via soft constraints, asynchronous proximal alternation, specific noise usage) are not rigorously analyzed.
- Test-time dynamics enforcement is softened, allowing root forces; this undermines physical realism and may inflate performance. The T=1 optimization horizon effectively performs an MPC-like correction; it is unclear how much credit belongs to the learned policy versus this local optimizer.
- Important design details are underspecified (e.g., dimension and role of recurrent state r, action/state normalization, cost weights, horizon lengths, dataset sizes per character, contact/friction parameters). Reproducibility is limited.
- The “two orders of magnitude” improvement claim lacks clear metrics and confidence intervals. Figures/tables referenced are essential but not described quantitatively in the text.
- Writing and references need polish (typos, duplicated words, inconsistent citation formatting; some references are incomplete).

Quality
Technically, the approach is plausible and leverages solid components (CIO, Gauss–Newton, supervised learning). However, claims about “stable and realistic locomotion” and “substantially harder problems” are not backed by rigorous, quantitative evidence. The feedback-gain derivation is sound at a high level but lacks analysis (conditions on λ, conditioning, stability guarantees). The evaluation does not convincingly show superiority over established baselines.

Clarity
The paper is generally readable but contains notation issues and typos. Several crucial details are missing for replication. Figures/tables are referenced but not described quantitatively. The role of the recurrent state is not made clear.

Originality
The high-level method is close to prior guided policy search frameworks. The feedback-gain extraction via soft-constraint perturbation and the particular asynchronous proximal setup are modest contributions but not deeply analyzed.

Significance
Potentially useful for character animation and model-based control communities; less compelling for NeurIPS without thorough empirical validation and clear advances over established baselines. The lack of quantitative evaluation diminishes impact.

Pros for acceptance
- Practical system demonstrating diverse 3D interactive behaviors from a unified pipeline.
- Neat, low-overhead method to obtain local feedback gains from a direct optimizer.
- Clear evidence that joint alternation and noise are important, with a scalable implementation.

Cons for acceptance
- Insufficient quantitative evaluation and ablations; heavy reliance on qualitative videos.
- Weak and incomplete baseline comparisons to closely related guided policy search and modern RL/MPC methods.
- Incremental novelty relative to prior GPS-style work; limited theoretical or empirical rigor.
- Softened dynamics (root forces) and T=1 online optimization confound the contribution of the learned policy.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide rigorous metrics across tasks: success/fall rates, energy/torque, contact violations, foot slip, path tracking error, robustness to pushes/noise, latency/throughput.
- Compare against: (i) GPS with ADMM/dual; (ii) TRPO or related policy gradient methods; (iii) MPC/iLQG with comparable costs; (iv) LQG-based gain computation vs the proposed method, with conditioning/stability analyses.
- Ablate λ, σε, σγ, α, η; report sensitivity and stability. Clarify the role and dimension of r.
- Remove root forces during evaluation or report results both with and without; quantify the contribution of the T=1 optimization by testing pure open-loop policy rollout and longer-horizon MPC.
- Improve clarity and references; include sufficient details for reproduction.

Recommendation
Lean reject. The paper presents a promising system and some interesting engineering, but lacks the rigor and comparative evaluation needed to support its claims over established methods at NeurIPS.
