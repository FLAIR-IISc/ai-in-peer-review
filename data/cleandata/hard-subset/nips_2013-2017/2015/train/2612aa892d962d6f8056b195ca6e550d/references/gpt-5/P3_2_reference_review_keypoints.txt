- Proposes learning real-time interactive controllers for diverse 3D characters by combining Contact-Invariant Optimization (CIO) trajectory optimization with neural policy regression.  
- Policy maps sensory state to desired generalized velocities, recurrent memory updates, and contact forces; training alternates trajectory optimization with policy regression using noisy inputs and locally optimal feedback targets.  
- Key technical idea: compute stabilizing feedback gains efficiently from the CIO Gauss-Newton system via a perturbation-based derivation to improve robustness.  
- Training is distributed and asynchronous across CPUs for trajectory optimization and a GPU for neural network training, with proximal regularization to stabilize updates.  
- At test time a 1-step optimization enforces dynamics and contacts, producing robust behaviors for swimming, flying, biped and quadruped locomotion without task-specific architectures.  
- Builds on prior work: Guided Policy Search, Mordatch & Todorov, and CIO; claims novelty in applying CIO to rich 3D morphologies and reusing Gauss-Newton structure for feedback gains.  
- Strength: tackles a hard, impactful problem and unifies locomotion across morphologies without hand-crafted controllers or motion capture.  
- Strength: methodologically coherent integration of CIO, policy regression, and dataset aggregation with branch-and-continue trajectory generation.  
- Strength: perturbation-based feedback-gain computation is neat, efficient, and practically relevant for stabilizing learned policies.  
- Strength: well-engineered distributed system with sensible asynchronous alternating optimization and apparent empirical convergence.  
- Strength: ablations show importance of joint optimization and input noise; qualitative 3D gaits emerge without cycle priors.  
- Weakness: limited quantitative evaluation—few concrete metrics (time-to-fall, tracking error, energy, contact violations, success rates) and no robustness tests.  
- Weakness: missing experimental comparisons to alternative learning methods (TRPO, GPS variants, DAgger-style aggregation) to substantiate claims.  
- Weakness: the T=1 execution step is effectively short-horizon MPC; runtime, iterations, and latency on reported hardware are not reported.  
- Weakness: physical realism concerns due to soft dynamics constraints and residual root forces; magnitudes/frequency of non-physical residuals are unquantified.  
- Weakness: policy outputs contact forces directly, raising questions about physical consistency without the 1-step projection; torque-only alternatives are not explored.  
- Clarity issues: underspecified feature set φ, cost weights, recurrent state dimensionality/semantics, dataset sizes, goal encoding, and 1-step solver details; some notation/typos.  
- Originality: approach is close to Guided Policy Search; claimed novelties are incremental but practically meaningful (CIO for 3D, feedback-gain reuse, async proximal training).  
- Suggestion: cite dataset aggregation (DAgger) and recent GPS/TRPO variants for broader context.  
- Significance: potentially influential for animation and simulation if stronger quantitative results and comparisons are provided; reliance on soft dynamics and online projection may limit real-hardware adoption.  
- Questions for authors: provide metrics (tracking, time-to-fall, energy, contact violations), sizes of r and s and sensitivity, runtime of T=1 step and ablation, residual root force magnitudes and possibility to reduce them, and comparisons to GPS/TRPO baselines.  
- Pros: demonstrates diverse 3D behaviors, efficient feedback-gain computation, and practical distributed training.  
- Cons: weak quantitative evaluation and baselines, soft dynamics and online optimization complicate realism, and missing details hinder reproducibility.  
- Overall: solid and promising systems paper with a useful technical contribution; borderline accept if quantitative evaluation, comparisons, and execution-time realism/efficiency are substantially strengthened.
