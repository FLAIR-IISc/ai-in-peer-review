Summary
The paper proposes an approach to learn interactive, real-time controllers for complex 3D characters by combining contact-invariant trajectory optimization (CIO) with supervised learning of a neural network policy. The method alternates between (i) generating/optimizing task-specific trajectories with CIO and (ii) fitting a neural policy to match actions along those trajectories under injected sensory noise. A key ingredient is a way to compute local feedback gains for direct trajectory optimization (reusing Gauss–Newton structure) to produce better targets under noisy inputs. Training is distributed asynchronously (multiple CIO workers plus one GPU for policy fitting). At test time, the learned policy outputs desired velocities, recurrent-state rates, and contact forces; the next state is obtained by solving a 1-step constrained optimization (an implicit, T=1 “MPC-like” update). The paper demonstrates qualitative results on swimming, flying, bipedal, and quadrupedal locomotion, and provides ablations showing the benefit of joint optimization and noise injection.

Relation to prior work
This work is closely related to guided policy search (GPS) and trajectory-optimization-guided imitation learning (e.g., Levine & Koltun, ICML’14; Mordatch & Todorov, RSS’14), as well as to classic trajectory optimization and LQG/MPC controllers. The alternating optimization with proximal terms and supervised regression onto noisy local linearizations is in the GPS family, though the paper emphasizes CIO and proposes a specific way to extract feedback gains from a direct optimizer. It also relates to denoising/regularization (dropout-like noise) and to standard physics-based animation controllers. More recent policy optimization baselines (e.g., TRPO) are cited but not compared.

Strengths
- Addresses a challenging setting: diverse 3D morphologies and interactive control.
- Pragmatic, scalable training pipeline, with clear engineering to make large-scale data generation and policy fitting work in hours.
- Sensible use of noise in inputs and within the network to reduce overfitting and improve closed-loop robustness.
- Technical contribution on extracting feedback gains from a direct trajectory optimizer by reusing Gauss–Newton structure (Woodbury identity) is neat and potentially useful beyond this paper.
- Ablations indicate that joint optimization and noise matter, and architecture trade-offs are explored.

Weaknesses
- Lack of rigorous quantitative evaluation and baselines. No head-to-head comparisons against GPS, TRPO, model-predictive control (beyond a qualitative bird example), or simple state-machine controllers. No metrics such as success rate, fall rate, tracking error, energy/torque cost, or robustness to perturbations.
- Claims of physical realism are weakened by soft enforcement of dynamics and explicit allowance of root forces during execution. Outputting desired velocities and contact forces, then solving a T=1 optimization with soft constraints, risks nonphysical behaviors and complicates interpretation relative to standard torque-control formulations.
- Novelty is incremental relative to established GPS-style methods. The main conceptual pieces (alternating between trajectory optimization and supervised learning with noise, proximal updates) align with prior work; the feedback-gain extraction method is the primary distinct technical element but is evaluated only indirectly.
- Methodological details are under-specified for reproduction: the recurrent memory r is introduced but its dimensionality, initialization, and role relative to hidden activations are unclear; the cost terms used in CIO are not fully enumerated; scaling/normalization of states and noise magnitudes are unspecified.
- The data-generation protocol (branching trajectories at random timestamps) partially addresses distribution shift but is not an on-policy aggregation scheme (e.g., DAgger). There is no evaluation of robustness to disturbances or to goal changes outside the training distribution.
- No stability guarantees; convergence of the asynchronous alternating scheme is asserted empirically without systematic study or sensitivity analysis (e.g., to λ, α, η).
- Heavy reliance on video for assessment; figures suggest improvements but numerical results are sparse.

Quality
The approach is technically plausible and builds on well-established components. However, the empirical support is largely qualitative, with insufficient quantitative comparisons to established baselines and limited reporting of objective metrics. The physics execution step allowing root forces is a significant caveat for claims of realism. The feedback-gain derivation is sound at a high level but evaluated only through downstream behavior; sensitivity to λ and to noise scale is not reported.

Clarity
Generally readable and organized, but important details are missing: full specification of costs, exact network/memory architecture, state/action normalization, and runtime performance (FPS, solver iterations per step). Several references are incomplete. A clearer positioning versus GPS and DAgger would help.

Originality
The combination is close to existing GPS frameworks; the main new technical idea is the efficient feedback-gain computation for direct trajectory optimization. The distributed training architecture is practical but not novel.

Significance
If robust and quantitatively validated, such controllers would be valuable for animation and possibly robotics. As presented, the lack of strong baselines and reliance on soft dynamics constrain the impact. The feedback-gain trick may be of independent interest.

Questions for the authors
- Please quantify performance: success/fall rates, trajectory tracking error, energy/torque, average speed, robustness to external pushes, and generalization to unseen goals.
- Provide head-to-head comparisons with GPS (Levine & Koltun, 2014), TRPO, and MPC under identical costs/dynamics.
- What is the dimension and role of r? Is the policy recurrent beyond this explicit state? How is r initialized and does it materially improve performance?
- How large are the root forces at execution? Can the method run with hard dynamics constraints (no root forces)?
- Sensitivity of performance to λ (feedback softness), α (penalty), η (proximal), and noise scales.
- Runtime details: control rate, number of Newton steps per frame, wall-clock on test hardware.

Pros
- Addresses a difficult and relevant problem.
- Solid engineering and a potentially useful method to obtain feedback gains from CIO.
- Demonstrates breadth across morphologies.

Cons
- Insufficient quantitative evaluation and no strong baselines.
- Physical realism compromised by soft constraints and root forces.
- Incremental novelty relative to GPS-style methods.
- Missing methodological details hinder reproducibility.

Recommendation: Weak reject
Confidence: High
