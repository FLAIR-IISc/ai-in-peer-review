

# Peer Review: "Interactive Control of Diverse Complex Characters with Neural Networks"

## Summary and Relation to Previous Work

This paper presents a method for learning neural network controllers that enable interactive, real-time control of diverse physical characters (bipedal, quadrupedal, swimming, flying) in 3D environments. The authors combine trajectory optimization (specifically Contact-Invariant Optimization, CIO) with neural network training, where the network learns to reproduce the behaviors generated by the optimizer. The key innovation is a distributed stochastic optimization approach that alternates between optimizing trajectories and training the neural network, with noise injection to improve robustness. This builds on prior work by Mordatch et al. (CIO) and Levine & Koltun (learning policies with trajectory optimization), but extends it to 3D and demonstrates success across diverse character types without task-specific hand-crafted controllers.

## Quality

The paper presents a technically sound approach that is well-justified and carefully implemented. The authors provide a detailed mathematical formulation of the problem and their solution, including the necessary derivations for optimal feedback gains. The experimental evaluation is thorough, with ablation studies showing the importance of key components (noise injection, joint optimization) and demonstrations across multiple character types in 3D. The results convincingly show that their method outperforms naive neural network training on static datasets by two orders of magnitude. The authors are careful to acknowledge limitations, such as the need for trajectory optimization to generate training data and the computational requirements (hours of training on cloud infrastructure). The paper is complete and well-supported by experiments, though it would benefit from a more comprehensive comparison to state-of-the-art reinforcement learning methods for continuous control.

## Clarity

The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with a logical flow from problem statement to solution to evaluation. The mathematical formulations are detailed and precise, though some sections (particularly the derivation of optimal feedback gains) could benefit from more intuitive explanations. The experimental setup and results are clearly described, and the supplementary video is a valuable resource for understanding the results. The paper adequately informs the reader about the method and results, though some technical details (e.g., the exact implementation of the trajectory optimizer) would benefit from more detail for reproducibility.

## Originality

The paper makes a significant contribution by demonstrating the first successful application of trajectory-optimization-based neural network control to diverse 3D character types, extending beyond the 2D results of prior work. The distributed stochastic optimization approach is novel, providing a practical way to combine trajectory optimization with neural network training. The noise injection technique for improving robustness is a useful extension of existing methods. While the components (trajectory optimization, neural networks) are familiar, the specific combination and implementation for interactive character control in 3D is novel and well-motivated. The paper properly cites relevant work (CIO, trajectory optimization, neural network control), though it could better contextualize the relationship to recent deep reinforcement learning methods for continuous control.

## Significance

The significance of this work is substantial. It addresses a major challenge in character animation and robotics: developing controllers that can generate complex, stable, and realistic movements across diverse character types without task-specific hand-crafted controllers. The ability to learn controllers for diverse 3D characters (biped, quadruped, swimming, flying) with a single network architecture is a significant advancement over previous work that was limited to 2D or required specialized controllers for each character type. The method has clear applications in animation, robotics, and biomechanics, and the results demonstrate a practical approach to interactive character control that could be adopted by practitioners. The paper convincingly shows that this approach yields more realistic and purposeful behaviors than direct reinforcement learning methods.

## Strengths and Weaknesses

**Strengths:**
- Demonstrates successful interactive control across diverse 3D character types (bipedal, quadrupedal, swimming, flying)
- Provides a thorough ablation study showing the importance of key components
- Introduces a practical distributed optimization approach for combining trajectory optimization with neural network training
- Shows significant improvement over naive neural network training (two orders of magnitude)
- Presents a unified approach that eliminates the need for task-specific controllers

**Weaknesses:**
- Limited comparison to state-of-the-art reinforcement learning methods for continuous control (e.g., SAC, DDPG)
- Does not thoroughly discuss the limitations of the approach (e.g., handling large perturbations, extreme environments)
- Computational requirements are high (hours of training on cloud infrastructure), which may limit practical adoption
- Does not address the applicability to real-world robots (only simulated characters)

## Recommendation

This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of neural network control for physical systems. The method is technically sound, the results are compelling, and the approach has clear practical significance. The paper advances the state of the art in interactive character control by extending the successful trajectory-optimization-based methods to 3D and diverse character types. While the comparison to recent deep reinforcement learning methods could be strengthened, the paper's contribution is substantial and well-supported.

The paper meets all the criteria for acceptance at NIPS. It is technically sound, clearly written, original in its combination of techniques for a challenging problem, and significant in its potential impact on character animation, robotics, and biomechanics.

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions (primarily to add a more thorough comparison to recent deep RL methods and to discuss limitations more fully). The paper demonstrates a valuable approach to a difficult problem that would be of interest to the NIPS community and has the potential to influence future work in neural network control for physical systems.
