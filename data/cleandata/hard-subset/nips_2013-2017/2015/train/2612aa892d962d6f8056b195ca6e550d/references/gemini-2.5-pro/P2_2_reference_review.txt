**Review of "Interactive Control of Diverse Complex Characters with Neural Networks"**

**Summary**

This paper presents a novel method for automatically synthesizing real-time, interactive neural network controllers for complex, physically-simulated 3D characters. The core idea is a joint optimization framework that combines the strengths of offline trajectory optimization (specifically, Contact-Invariant Optimization or CIO) with the real-time performance of neural network policies. The system iteratively alternates between two steps: 1) optimizing a set of motion trajectories to satisfy task goals and physical constraints, while also staying close to the current neural network's predictions; and 2) training the neural network policy to regress onto the actions produced by the optimized trajectories.

Key technical contributions include a principled method for injecting noise during training to improve robustness, which involves calculating and using optimal feedback gains to generate appropriate target actions for perturbed states. The authors also propose a novel technique for extracting these feedback gains from a direct trajectory optimization method (CIO), which typically does not provide them as a byproduct. The method is demonstrated on a diverse set of challenging 3D characters, including bipeds, quadrupeds, a swimmer, and a flyer, successfully learning stable and realistic locomotion gaits for all of them using a single, unified algorithm.

**Strengths**

1.  **Impressive and General Results:** The most significant strength of this paper is the breadth and quality of the results. The ability to apply a single, general-purpose algorithm to synthesize controllers for such a diverse range of morphologies (bipedal, quadrupedal, swimming, flying) in 3D is a substantial achievement. The emergence of natural-looking, cyclic gaits (e.g., walking, trotting, flapping) without being explicitly programmed is a powerful demonstration of the method's capabilities. This significantly advances the state-of-the-art from prior work which was largely limited to 2D characters.

2.  **Novel and Sound Technical Approach:** The joint optimization framework is elegant. The key insight is not just to use trajectory optimization to generate a static dataset, but to have the policy and the trajectory data co-adapt. The ablation study (Fig 2) convincingly shows that this joint approach is critical for success, outperforming a naive "optimize then regress" pipeline by orders of magnitude.

3.  **Principled Robustness Training:** The method for improving policy robustness is a major contribution. Instead of just adding noise to inputs and outputs as a form of data augmentation, the authors use optimal feedback gains (`as`) to calculate the *correct* optimal action for a perturbed state (`a + asε`). This provides the network with explicit information on how to recover from small deviations, which is crucial for stable closed-loop control. The proposed method for extracting these gains from a direct optimization method is also a valuable and non-obvious contribution.

4.  **System Scalability:** The authors have clearly considered the practical challenges of this computationally intensive method by designing a distributed training architecture. This demonstrates a complete piece of work that goes beyond a proof-of-concept to a scalable solution.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Insufficient Comparison to Reinforcement Learning:** The paper dismisses reinforcement learning methods with the brief comment that they produce "somewhat chaotic behaviors." At the time of this work, policy gradient methods like TRPO were emerging as powerful tools for continuous control. A quantitative experimental comparison to a state-of-the-art RL baseline on at least one of the characters would have made the paper's claims of superiority much stronger. Without it, it's unclear if the "realism" of the motion is an inherent advantage of this method or a result of the carefully shaped cost functions within the CIO component.

2.  **Reproducibility Concerns:** The overall system is highly complex, integrating a physics engine, a sophisticated trajectory optimizer, and a distributed neural network training pipeline. While the paper provides a good overview, crucial details for reproducibility are missing. Specifically, the exact formulation and weighting of the various terms in the cost function `C(X)` are not provided. The authors state these are the "same for all our experiments," but greater detail is needed for others to build upon this work.

3.  **Ambiguous Real-time Claims:** The policy execution step at runtime (Section 7) involves solving a small optimization problem at each timestep to enforce physics constraints. The paper claims this can be solved at "real-time rates" but provides no supporting data (e.g., milliseconds per step, or achievable FPS on their hardware). This is a critical claim for a paper focused on *interactive* control and requires quantitative validation.

**Detailed Evaluation**

*   **Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The methodology is well-motivated, and the claims are strongly supported by an excellent ablation study and impressive qualitative results across multiple domains. The main weakness in its quality is the lack of a strong quantitative baseline comparison, particularly from the RL literature.

*   **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The overview in Section 2 effectively communicates the core idea. Algorithm 1 is clear. However, as noted above, clarity could be improved by providing more detail on the cost functions and the performance of the real-time policy execution step.

*   **Originality:** The work is highly original. While it builds upon prior work combining trajectory optimization and policy learning (e.g., Levine & Koltun, 2014), it makes several novel and significant contributions. The extension to complex 3D characters, the method for extracting and using feedback gains for robust training, and the demonstration of a single framework for such diverse behaviors are all highly novel.

*   **Significance:** The significance of this work is very high. It presents a powerful and general framework for a very difficult problem: automated synthesis of controllers for complex articulated bodies. The ideas presented, particularly the co-adaptation of an expert optimizer and a student policy, have been highly influential. Researchers and practitioners in computer graphics, robotics, and biomechanics are very likely to use and build upon these ideas. This paper demonstrates a substantial advance in the state of the art.

**Recommendation: Strong Accept**

Despite some weaknesses regarding baseline comparisons and missing implementation details, the strengths of this paper are overwhelming. The novelty of the technical approach, combined with the truly impressive and general results on a set of very challenging 3D control problems, make this a landmark contribution to the field. It addresses a difficult and important problem with an elegant and effective solution, and its impact is likely to be substantial.
