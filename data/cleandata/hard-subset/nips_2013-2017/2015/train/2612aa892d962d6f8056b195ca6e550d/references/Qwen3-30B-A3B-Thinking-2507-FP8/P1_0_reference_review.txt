

# Peer Review: "Interactive Control of Diverse Complex Characters with Neural Networks"

## Summary
This paper presents a novel approach for training neural network controllers capable of generating complex, stable, and realistic movements for diverse physical characters in real-time. The authors combine trajectory optimization (specifically Contact-Invariant Optimization) with neural network policy learning to create controllers that can handle arbitrary behaviors, body morphologies, and online task changes. Unlike previous work that focused on 2D characters or simple controllers, this method successfully achieves 3D locomotion for bipeds, quadrupeds, swimmers, and flyers using a single network architecture. The approach injects noise during training to improve robustness and uses distributed optimization to scale to complex problems. This work builds on previous trajectory optimization methods like CIO [12] and neural network control approaches [9, 11], but extends them to handle much more complex 3D scenarios with diverse character morphologies.

## Strengths

**Quality**: The paper demonstrates strong technical rigor. The authors carefully analyze the optimization problem, develop a principled method for computing optimal feedback gains during trajectory optimization, and provide a detailed distributed training algorithm. The experimental results are comprehensive, showing successful control across multiple character types in 3D space. The ablation studies (Section 8.1) effectively demonstrate the necessity of their key innovations: joint optimization with trajectory generation and noise injection. The authors are appropriately cautious about limitations, noting that direct RL methods produce "somewhat chaotic behaviors" compared to their approach.

**Clarity**: The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The mathematical formulations are precise, the algorithm descriptions are clear, and the results are presented with appropriate visualizations. The authors effectively explain the technical challenges of neural network control in physics-based settings, and their solution is logically developed from problem statement to implementation. The supplementary video is a particularly valuable addition that conveys the quality of results more effectively than static figures alone.

**Originality**: While the paper combines known techniques (trajectory optimization and neural networks), the specific integration and improvements (particularly the noise injection strategy, distributed optimization, and feedback gain computation) represent a significant advance. The authors clearly articulate how their approach differs from prior work [9, 11] that was limited to 2D scenarios. The demonstration of a single controller architecture working across diverse character morphologies (biped, quadruped, swimmer, flyer) is particularly novel.

**Significance**: The work addresses a fundamental challenge in character animation and robotics: creating controllers that can handle diverse behaviors and morphologies without extensive manual tuning. The method's ability to learn stable 3D locomotion for multiple character types with a single network architecture represents a meaningful advance over existing approaches that require specialized controllers for each character type. The potential applications in animation, gaming, robotics, and biomechanics are substantial.

## Weaknesses

**Theoretical Foundation**: While the experimental results are compelling, the paper lacks deeper theoretical analysis of why the joint optimization approach works better than alternatives. The authors mention that direct RL methods produce "chaotic behaviors," but don't provide a rigorous explanation for this phenomenon or formal guarantees about the stability of their approach. More theoretical grounding would strengthen the paper.

**Comparison to State-of-the-Art**: The comparison to MPC (Section 8.1) is useful but limited. The paper doesn't compare to more recent neural network control approaches like those in [3, 6, 14], which might provide a more relevant benchmark. A more comprehensive comparison to contemporary methods would better establish the paper's position in the literature.

**Computational Cost**: The paper mentions that "we invest more computing resources in generating the data than in learning from it" and that training takes "on the order of hours." While the distributed architecture is well-described, the paper doesn't adequately address whether this approach is practical for real-world applications where training time might be prohibitive. The authors should clarify the trade-offs between training time and deployment performance.

## Overall Assessment

This is a high-quality, well-executed paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of neural network control for physics-based characters. The method successfully demonstrates a key capability that has been challenging for previous approaches: generating stable, realistic 3D locomotion for diverse character types using a single neural network architecture without specialized tuning. The technical innovations around noise injection, feedback gain computation, and distributed optimization are well-motivated and rigorously evaluated.

The paper's strengths significantly outweigh its weaknesses. The work is clearly original within the context of NIPS, makes a substantial contribution to the field of character control, and is technically sound. While the theoretical analysis could be strengthened, the empirical results are compelling and the approach is clearly described with sufficient detail for reproducibility.

## Recommendation

**Accept**

This paper presents a novel and effective approach to neural network control for diverse physical characters that advances the state of the art in several important ways. The combination of trajectory optimization with neural network learning, along with the careful attention to noise injection and distributed training, produces results that significantly outperform alternative approaches. The paper's contribution is substantial, well-executed, and addresses a meaningful challenge in the field. The results across multiple character types demonstrate the generality of the approach in a way that previous work has not achieved.

The authors have made a clear and compelling case for the significance of their work, and the paper is written with exceptional clarity. While the theoretical grounding could be expanded, the empirical evidence is robust and the methodology is sound. This work has the potential to influence both the animation industry and robotics research, making it a strong candidate for NIPS.

## Specific Suggestions for Improvement

1. Add a brief theoretical analysis of why the joint optimization approach produces more stable policies than direct neural network training
2. Include a comparison to more recent neural network control methods (e.g., [3, 6, 14]) in the evaluation
3. Provide more concrete discussion of the computational trade-offs between training time and deployment performance
4. Consider adding a brief discussion of how this approach might extend to more complex tasks beyond locomotion (e.g., manipulation, interaction with objects)

These suggestions are minor improvements to an already strong paper that would make it even more valuable to the NIPS community.
