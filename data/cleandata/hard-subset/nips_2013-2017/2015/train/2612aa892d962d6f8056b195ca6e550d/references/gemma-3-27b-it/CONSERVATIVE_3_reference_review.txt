This paper proposes a method for learning neural network controllers for complex characters by combining trajectory optimization (specifically, Contact-Invariant Optimization) with supervised learning. The core idea is to use the trajectory optimizer to generate training data and then train a neural network to mimic the optimizer's behavior, enabling real-time control. The authors address the challenges of scaling to high-dimensional spaces and ensuring stability through noise injection and a distributed optimization scheme. They demonstrate the approach on a variety of characters – swimmers, fliers, bipeds, and quadrupeds – showcasing the ability to learn diverse locomotion behaviors.

The paper is clearly written and well-organized. The authors articulate the motivation and challenges effectively, and the method is presented in a logical manner. The connection to prior work in trajectory optimization, reinforcement learning, and neural network control is reasonably well-established. However, the clarity is somewhat undermined by the lack of detail in some methodological choices – for example, the exact structure of the neural network and the details of the task costs used in the trajectory optimization are relegated to the appendix/supplementary material. Reproducibility, while mentioned as a goal, feels hampered by this lack of transparency in the main body of the paper.

The main weakness of this work lies in its evaluation. While the results *look* impressive in the supplementary video, the paper lacks a rigorous comparison to established control methods. The authors dismiss more "direct" methods (e.g., reinforcement learning, supervised learning directly from motion capture) with a statement that they produce "somewhat chaotic behaviors," but this is not backed up by quantitative comparisons. Showing performance metrics (e.g., speed, stability, energy efficiency) against these baselines is crucial. Further, while ablation studies are presented, they focus on components *within* the proposed method. A comparison against a strong, well-tuned trajectory optimization-based controller (without the neural network layer) would be highly valuable. The claim of unifying disparate approaches isn’t convincingly demonstrated; simply applying the same network architecture to different character morphologies doesn't constitute unification. The "soft constraints" used in trajectory optimization are not deeply investigated. A discussion of the sensitivity of the results to these constraints would be beneficial.

The originality of the work is moderate. Combining trajectory optimization with neural network learning is not a novel concept in itself (references [9, 11] are acknowledged). The contribution appears to be in the successful scaling of this approach to more complex 3D characters and the specific techniques used to achieve this (noise injection, distributed optimization, and the feedback gain calculation). However, the novelty feels incremental.

The significance of the work is potentially high. A truly universal control framework for diverse characters would be a significant advance. However, the lack of rigorous comparison makes it difficult to assess the actual impact. While the authors demonstrate that their method *can* work, they don’t convincingly show that it works *better* than existing methods. The claim of real-time control is qualified by the fact that the initial data generation is offline and computationally expensive.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

* Demonstrates the feasibility of learning complex locomotion behaviors for diverse characters.
* Presents a potentially useful approach for bridging the gap between offline trajectory optimization and real-time control.
* The paper is well-written and clearly explains the method.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

* Lacks a rigorous comparison to established control methods (reinforcement learning, supervised learning, and a strong trajectory optimization baseline).
* The claim of unifying disparate approaches is not convincingly demonstrated.
* The contribution feels incremental; the core idea is not entirely novel.
* Limited discussion of the sensitivity to hyperparameters and the impact of the soft constraints used in trajectory optimization.



**Overall Assessment:** Reject.

While this paper presents a technically sound and well-written method, the lack of a comprehensive comparative evaluation prevents me from recommending acceptance to NIPS. The authors need to demonstrate that their approach provides a significant improvement over existing techniques before it can be considered a major contribution to the field. The current work feels like a promising step, but further experimentation and analysis are needed to establish its true value. I encourage the authors to address the concerns raised above and resubmit the paper in the future.
