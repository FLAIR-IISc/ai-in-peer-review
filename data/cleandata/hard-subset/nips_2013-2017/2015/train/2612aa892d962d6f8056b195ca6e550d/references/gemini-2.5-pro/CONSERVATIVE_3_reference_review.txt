**Summary**

This paper proposes a method for training neural network policies to control complex, physically-simulated 3D characters in real-time. The core idea is to leverage a powerful offline trajectory optimization method, Contact-Invariant Optimization (CIO), to generate a dataset of expert trajectories. A neural network is then trained via supervised learning to mimic the behavior of the optimizer. The key contribution lies in framing this as a joint optimization problem, solved with a block-alternating scheme, where the trajectory optimizer is regularized to produce trajectories consistent with the current neural network policy, and the network is in turn updated on this evolving dataset. The authors introduce several technical components to make this work, including a method for extracting stabilizing feedback gains directly from the CIO solver and the systematic use of noise injection during training to improve robustness and generalization. The method is demonstrated on a diverse set of characters, including bipeds, quadrupeds, swimmers, and flyers, showing that it can produce stable and seemingly natural locomotion gaits without task-specific architectural design.

**Strengths**

1.  **Impressive Empirical Results:** The primary strength of the paper is the quality and diversity of the generated behaviors. Successfully creating controllers for stable locomotion in 3D for bipeds, quadrupeds, swimmers, and flyers from a single, unified framework is a significant achievement. The emergence of cyclic gaits (walking, trotting, flapping) without being explicitly programmed is a compelling demonstration of the method's potential.
2.  **Sound Methodological Motivation:** The general approach of using a powerful, non-real-time optimizer to bootstrap and guide the training of a reactive, real-time policy is well-motivated. It combines the global planning capabilities of trajectory optimization with the speed and generalization of neural networks.
3.  **Clever Technical Contribution:** The method proposed in Section 3.2 for deriving optimal feedback gains from a direct trajectory optimization method is a valuable and non-obvious contribution. It elegantly reuses computations already performed (the Gauss-Newton Hessian approximation) to produce stabilizing gains without resorting to a full, separate LQR pass, which the authors note can be inefficient and produce ill-conditioned results.
4.  **Effective Ablation Study:** The comparative evaluation in Section 8.1 clearly demonstrates the necessity of the core components of the proposed algorithm. The failures of training on a static dataset ("no joint") and training without noise ("no noise") provide strong evidence for the benefits of the joint optimization framework and the stochastic training procedure.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Lack of Comparison to State-of-the-Art Baselines:** The most significant weakness of this work is the insufficient comparison to alternative, established methods for continuous control. The introduction explicitly mentions reinforcement learning (RL) as sharing the same ambitious goal, and numerous recent works in the field (e.g., TRPO, DDPG, PPO) have demonstrated success on challenging continuous control tasks. The authors dismiss a comparison by stating that their "impression is that networks trained with direct methods give rise to successful yet somewhat chaotic behaviors." This subjective impression is not a substitute for rigorous, quantitative empirical evaluation. To substantiate the claim that this trajectory-optimization-based approach is superior, the paper must include direct comparisons to strong, state-of-the-art RL baselines on the same set of tasks, using metrics such as task success rate, energy efficiency, and stability. Without this, the significance of the contribution relative to the broader control community is difficult to assess.
2.  **Questionable Novelty of the Core Idea:** While the application to 3D characters is novel and the results are impressive, the fundamental concept of training a policy by imitating an "expert" planner is well-established (e.g., imitation learning, DAgger). The authors cite closely related work [9, 11] that combines trajectory optimization and function approximation. The primary contribution over this prior work appears to be an extension to 3D and a number of "technical improvements." While this is a valuable engineering accomplishment, the conceptual novelty seems incremental rather than foundational.
3.  **Absence of Convergence Guarantees:** The proposed "Distributed Stochastic Optimization" (Algorithm 1) is a custom alternating optimization scheme. The authors acknowledge that adapting standard convergent algorithms like ADMM is "non-trivial" and that their method may have a slower convergence rate. However, they provide no theoretical analysis of their algorithm's properties and simply state that they "observe convergence." For a methodology paper, this is insufficient. A more rigorous characterization of the optimization procedure is needed to understand its stability, convergence properties, and potential failure modes.
4.  **Heavy Dependence on a Specific Optimizer:** The success of the entire pipeline is predicated on the availability of a powerful and robust trajectory optimizer, namely CIO. The paper itself notes, "the heavy lifting is done by the trajectory optimizer." This raises questions about the generality of the framework. It functions more as a method for distilling a CIO-generated controller into a real-time policy than as a general-purpose control algorithm. The work would be stronger if it demonstrated that the framework is robust to the choice of the underlying optimizer.

**Overall Assessment**

This paper presents a well-engineered system that produces visually impressive results on a set of difficult 3D character control problems. The approach of combining offline optimization with a learned policy is sound, and the specific technical contributions, particularly the extraction of feedback gains, are clever.

However, the work is let down by a significant lack of rigorous comparison to established baselines in the reinforcement learning literature. The claims of superiority are based on qualitative impressions rather than quantitative evidence, which is a major flaw. Furthermore, the core optimization algorithm lacks theoretical justification, and the overall conceptual novelty is limited. While the results are compelling, the paper in its current form reads more like a demonstration of a powerful, specialized system rather than a generalizable scientific contribution with well-supported claims. For these reasons, I am hesitant to recommend acceptance. The paper would need to be substantially revised with a thorough empirical comparison against state-of-the-art RL methods to be suitable for this conference.
