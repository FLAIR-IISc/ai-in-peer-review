Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes an approach for learning interactive, real-time controllers for complex 3D characters by alternating between trajectory optimization (Contact-Invariant Optimization, CIO) and supervised neural network policy regression. The key ideas are: (i) jointly optimizing trajectories and a policy via a quadratic-penalized coupling term; (ii) injecting sensory and hidden-unit noise to improve robustness and mitigate overfitting; (iii) using a perturbation method to extract local linear feedback gains from the trajectory optimizer at little additional cost (re-using the Gauss-Newton system); (iv) a distributed asynchronous training architecture with proximal regularization; and (v) real-time execution via a per-step (T=1) optimization enforcing (soft) dynamics constraints.

This line is closely related to Guided Policy Search and trajectory-optimization-based policy learning (e.g., Levine & Koltun ICML’14; Mordatch & Todorov RSS’14). The authors argue differences: 3D characters with diverse morphologies, CIO rather than LQG/DDP for trajectory generation, a new way to compute feedback gains directly from the direct-optimization KKT system, and an asynchronous alternating scheme.

Strengths
- Ambitious scope: a unified pipeline trained without mocap, producing plausible 3D locomotion, swimming, and flying across diverse morphologies.
- Sensible alternating optimization formulation with proximal terms; practical distributed implementation.
- The feedback-gain extraction via a perturbation/soft constraint trick using the Woodbury identity is a nice technical contribution with potential reuse beyond this paper.
- Noise-injection rationale is well motivated; ablations (albeit limited) suggest necessity of joint optimization and noise.
- Execution via implicit one-step optimization is elegant and avoids explicit integrator instabilities, enabling 50 ms steps.

Weaknesses and technical concerns
Quality
- Evaluation is largely qualitative and relies on a supplementary video. There are no standard quantitative metrics (success rate, distance/orientation tracking error, fall rate, control effort, perturbation recovery, contact violations/slip, energy cost) across seeds and terrains. The claims of “two orders of magnitude” improvement lack clear definition of the metric and units (Figure 2 not described precisely).
- Baselines are limited. No comparison to strong concurrent methods (e.g., Guided Policy Search variants; TRPO on torque control; SIMBICON/ZMP for bipeds; MPC with identical costs/horizons/compute budgets). The MPC comparison is qualitative and under-specified (horizon, warm starts, constraint handling).
- The dynamics constraints are enforced softly and may include “root forces” at execution. This can compromise physical realism; the extent of constraint violation and root-force magnitudes are not reported.
- Unclear whether a single shared policy controls multiple morphologies, or if each character has a separate policy with shared architecture/hyperparameters. The text seems to imply the latter; if so, the “unified” claim needs qualification.
- Important training details are missing: exact cost function components and weights c(φ) (Section 8 is too vague), trajectory horizons T, branching times distribution, batch sizes, learning rate schedules, optimizer details, dimensionality of recurrent memory r, initialization of r, and the exact spectral radius initialization procedure.
- The “recurrent memory” is not clearly specified: the policy is described as feedforward with r as part of state and ṙ as part of action. How is this different from an RNN? What is the benefit versus simply using an RNN? Stability/observability implications are not analyzed.
- The sensory input uses q̇t−1 while the action outputs q̇t. This off-by-one could be justified by timing/availability, but needs explanation.
- Claims of real-time performance lack timing breakdowns. Solving a constrained optimization each 50 ms could be brittle; worst-case timings and sensitivity to problem size are not given.

Clarity
- Notation inconsistencies and ambiguities: a_s vs a_s̄; η vs η2; “argmin∗”; Algorithm 1 step numbering; whether noise is resampled before both subproblems or only trajectory updates is unclear.
- Parameter formatting is ambiguous: σε = 10 −2, λ = 102, η = 10−2 (likely 10^-2, 10^2, 10^-2).
- Several typos/grammar: “aim to unity” → unify; “changed changed”; awkward phrasing in several places.
- The references are highly incomplete and inconsistent (many missing years/venues/pages; e.g., the SIAM reference, Ijspeert, multiple entries). Important related work (e.g., GPS/Levine & Abbeel 2013–2015) is under-cited.

Originality
- The overall framework is an incremental variant of trajectory-optimization-guided policy learning. The most original piece is the efficient extraction of stabilizing feedback gains from the Gauss-Newton system via soft constraints and Woodbury—this is novel and practical. The asynchronous proximal alternating scheme is a reasonable engineering choice but not conceptually new.

Significance
- If substantiated, robust 3D controllers across diverse morphologies without mocap would be impactful for RL/control-in-graphics. However, the lack of rigorous quantitative evaluation, limited baselines, and reliance on soft constraints/root forces weaken the evidence. The gain-extraction technique is a potentially useful contribution on its own.

Questions and suggestions
- Provide complete definition and weights for c(φ). Report constraint violations, root-force magnitudes, and contact complementarity residuals during execution.
- Clarify whether a single policy handles all morphologies; if not, rephrase claims.
- Quantitatively evaluate robustness (push perturbations), energy/torque costs, goal-tracking accuracy, and success rates. Include comparisons to GPS/TRPO torque policies and to hand-engineered baselines (e.g., SIMBICON) under matched conditions.
- Analyze sensitivity to λ (feedback softness), α (coupling), η (proximal), σε, σγ, and network capacity. Report runtime per frame and per outer iteration.
- Clarify the “recurrent memory” r design (dimension, role), and justify the off-by-one velocity sensing.
- Clean up notation, algorithm steps, and references.

Pros for acceptance
- Practical and potentially general pipeline with promising qualitative results in 3D.
- Neat and low-overhead method to extract feedback gains from direct trajectory optimization.
- Clear demonstration that noise and joint optimization help.

Cons for acceptance
- Insufficient quantitative evaluation and weak baselines.
- Ambiguities in method (recurrent state, sensing, execution realism), and reliance on soft constraints/root forces.
- Clarity/notation issues and incomplete references suggest the paper is not yet camera-ready.

Recommendation
Reject. The ideas are promising and potentially impactful, but the paper needs significantly stronger empirical evaluation, clearer exposition of methodological details, and complete references. With these addressed, and with rigorous comparisons to established baselines, this work could merit acceptance in a future iteration.

Confidence
High.
