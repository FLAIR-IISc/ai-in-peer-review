Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes a framework for learning real-time, interactive controllers for complex 3D characters by combining direct trajectory optimization (Contact-Invariant Optimization, CIO) with supervised neural network policy learning in an alternating, distributed optimization loop. Key ingredients include: (i) learning from optimizer-generated trajectories while injecting additive sensory noise; (ii) using locally optimal feedback gains to provide targets for noisy inputs (a(s+ε) ≈ a + asε), with a novel, efficient way to extract well-conditioned gains from direct trajectory optimization via a soft-constraint perturbation and Woodbury identity; (iii) proximal regularization for asynchronous, stochastic, block-coordinate updates across many machines; and (iv) real-time execution via T=1 horizon optimization that matches policy outputs while softly enforcing dynamics constraints.

The approach is closely related to Guided Policy Search (GPS) and other works that couple trajectory optimization with supervised function approximation (e.g., Levine & Koltun ’14; Mordatch & Todorov ’14). Compared to prior work, the paper emphasizes: fully 3D characters with contact-rich dynamics; a principled noisy-input training target using gains derived directly from CIO (rather than a separate LQG pass); and a practical distributed training architecture. The method yields stable, cyclic locomotion and flight/swimming behaviors across diverse morphologies without task-specific architectures.

Strengths
- Novel and pragmatic synthesis: The integration of CIO with neural policy learning, plus the feedback-gain-based supervision under sensory noise, is a compelling recipe that addresses the closed-loop instability often seen when learning from limited optimizer data.
- Technical contribution on gains: The perturbation-based derivation of feedback gains from a direct trajectory optimizer, reusing CXX−1 and controlled by λ, is both elegant and practical, and addresses conditioning issues of a post-hoc LQG pass.
- Broad scope and ambition: Demonstrations across 3D bipeds, quadrupeds, a swimmer, and a flier with a single architecture, and interactive control via moving goals, show meaningful generality.
- Joint optimization and noise robustness: The alternating optimization with proximal terms and injection of both sensory and hidden-unit noise appears crucial; ablations indicate large improvements over static-data training and no-noise variants.
- Engineering for scale: Asynchronous distributed data generation + GPU training is well thought out and likely of interest to practitioners.

Weaknesses
- Limited quantitative evaluation: The paper relies largely on qualitative videos and narrative. There is little reporting of metrics (success rates, robustness to perturbations, energy/torque usage, goal-tracking error, step frequency, real-time FPS/latency).
- Comparisons to baselines are insufficient: While GPS/TRPO/MPC are discussed, there is no controlled empirical comparison on the same 3D tasks. The claim of “substantially harder problems” than prior work would be much stronger with side-by-side results.
- Realism caveat at execution: The T=1 step “implicit” evolution enforces dynamics softly and may include root forces. This undermines claims of physical realism, especially for contact-rich 3D locomotion. A discussion/ablation of the soft-constraint weight and resultant ground-truth physical plausibility is needed.
- Clarity gaps: The role and training of the recurrent state r is under-specified (is there BPTT? is r simply integrated by the T=1 optimizer?). The sensory/action definitions, network IO normalization, and exact observation space per character could be clearer to aid reproducibility.
- References/formatting issues: Some citations are incomplete; a few typos.

Quality
Technically sound overall, with a solid optimization backbone and a thoughtful supervision signal under noisy inputs. The gain derivation is a nice contribution. However, empirical claims outpace quantitative validation, and the relaxed dynamics at execution raise concerns about physical fidelity.

Clarity
Generally readable and organized. Important implementation details (choice of λ, α, η; compute cost per step; policy latency; precise sensory features; how r is handled across time) should be expanded. Figures referenced for key results are not included in the text here.

Originality
Moderate to strong. The overall paradigm aligns with GPS, but the specific way of deriving feedback gains from CIO, the noise-injected target construction, and the breadth of 3D contact-rich demonstrations are notable. The asynchronous proximal training variant is a pragmatic twist.

Significance
High for communities at the ML–control–graphics interface. If substantiated with stronger quantitative evaluation and baselines, the work could help standardize a pathway from offline trajectory optimization to robust, interactive policies for complex morphologies.

Questions and suggestions for the authors
1) Please provide quantitative metrics across tasks (success rate, distance/orientation error, energy/torque, perturbation robustness), and real-time performance stats (Hz, latency, optimization iterations per step).
2) Clarify the recurrent state r: dimensionality, initialization, training (is there BPTT or is supervision only one-step?), and how r contributes to robustness/generalization.
3) Expand on the choice/sensitivity of λ in gain computation. How do gains and rollout stability vary with λ? Any safeguards against ill-conditioning?
4) Provide controlled comparisons with GPS/TRPO/MPC on at least one 3D locomotion task.
5) Discuss the impact of soft dynamics at execution (root forces). Can hard constraints be enforced or penalties strengthened without losing real-time performance?
6) Report generalization to goal states outside the training distribution, and robustness to exogenous pushes/slips.

Pros
- Elegant gain derivation and principled noisy-target supervision.
- Demonstrates interactive control for diverse 3D characters with a single policy architecture.
- Clear ablation evidence that joint alternating optimization and noise are essential.
- Practical, scalable training system.

Cons
- Sparse quantitative evaluation; limited baselines.
- Execution-time dynamics softened; potential physical implausibility.
- Some clarity/reproducibility gaps (r handling, features, hyperparameter sensitivity).

Recommendation: Weak Accept
Justification: The paper advances a bold and timely direction—learning robust, interactive policies for complex 3D control by tightly coupling trajectory optimization and neural networks. The feedback-gain supervision and CIO-based gain extraction are novel and valuable. Despite empirical gaps and some realism caveats, the conceptual contribution and demonstrated scope are significant enough to merit acceptance, provided the authors strengthen quantitative evaluation and clarify execution-time dynamics and recurrent-state training. Confidence: medium.
