This paper presents a compelling approach to learning neural network controllers for complex characters by bridging trajectory optimization and neural network regression. The core idea – leveraging CIO to generate training data and then distilling that knowledge into a neural network for real-time control – is genuinely novel and addresses a significant challenge in the field: scaling control policies to high-dimensional, physically realistic systems. The authors correctly identify the limitations of both purely reinforcement learning-based approaches and traditional hand-crafted controllers, and position their work as a potentially powerful synthesis. The fact that they achieve stable 3D locomotion, surpassing previous 2D results, is a strong indicator of the method's potential. The inclusion of noise injection as a regularization technique and the distributed training architecture are sensible and well-motivated choices.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The combination of trajectory optimization (CIO) with neural network learning for interactive control is a fresh and promising approach, particularly in the context of complex, 3D character control.
*   **Significance:** Solving the problem of creating universal controllers applicable to diverse morphologies and tasks is highly significant. Achieving stable 3D locomotion is a notable achievement.
*   **Technical Soundness:** The proposed method appears well-grounded in existing techniques (CIO, neural networks) and the authors demonstrate a clear understanding of the challenges involved (data generation, overfitting, real-time performance).
*   **Practicality:** The focus on making the approach scalable through distributed training and the emphasis on efficient data generation (investing more compute in optimization than learning) are valuable considerations.
*   **Good Experimental Validation:** The results on diverse characters (swimming, flying, bipedal, quadrupedal) demonstrate a degree of generality that is encouraging. The ablation studies provide useful insights into the importance of the different components of the method.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Limited Comparative Analysis:** While the ablation studies are helpful, a more thorough comparison with other recent methods for neural network controller learning (e.g., those utilizing direct reinforcement learning or imitation learning) would strengthen the paper. The claim that direct methods yield "somewhat chaotic behaviors" is asserted but not rigorously demonstrated.
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The choice of hyperparameters (noise levels, regularization weights) is likely crucial for performance, but the paper lacks a detailed discussion of how these parameters were tuned and their impact on results.
*   **Task Specificity in Data Generation:** Although the approach aims for generality, the data generation process (branching trajectories based on goal changes) is somewhat task-specific to locomotion. It is not immediately clear how easily this data generation scheme could be adapted to other types of tasks.
*   **Implicit Integration:** Relying on optimization as an implicit integrator for execution could be a potential source of instability or sensitivity to simulation parameters. A more detailed analysis of this aspect would be beneficial.
*   **Complexity of Implementation**: The distributed training architecture, while effective, adds complexity. A more accessible implementation or a clearer explanation of the engineering challenges would be useful for reproducibility.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper tackles a fundamentally difficult problem with a genuinely novel and potentially impactful approach.
*   The results demonstrate a significant advance in the state of the art for 3D character control.
*   The technical details are sound and the paper is generally well-written.
*   The authors clearly articulate the limitations of their work and suggest directions for future research.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The lack of a more comprehensive comparative analysis weakens the claim of superiority over existing methods.
*   The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion of hyperparameter tuning and the robustness of the approach.
*   The task specificity of the data generation scheme raises questions about the generality of the method.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that presents a significant contribution to the field of neural network control. While there are some weaknesses, the novelty, significance, and technical soundness of the work outweigh these concerns. I believe that this paper is a valuable addition to the NIPS program and I recommend acceptance, despite the minor points I raised. The work is bold and addresses a challenging problem with a creative solution. I am excited to see how this line of research develops.
