

# Review: Interactive Control of Diverse Complex Characters with Neural Networks

This paper proposes a method for generating neural network controllers for physically-based character animation using a combination of trajectory optimization (Contact-Invariant Optimization) and neural network training. The authors frame their work as an advancement over previous trajectory-optimization-based approaches by enabling interactive 3D locomotion for diverse character types (bipedal, quadrupedal, swimming, flying), which they claim previous work could not achieve.

The core contribution is a distributed stochastic optimization approach that alternates between trajectory optimization and neural network regression, with noise injection to improve robustness. The method is evaluated on several character types, showing successful locomotion in 3D space, which represents an advancement over prior 2D work. The authors provide ablation studies demonstrating the importance of their joint optimization framework and noise injection.

**Strengths:**
1. The paper presents a technically sound approach that successfully solves a challenging problem in character control.
2. The ablation studies (comparing joint optimization to static training, and with/without noise) provide reasonable evidence for the necessity of their key components.
3. The implementation details are sufficiently clear for replication, and the results are visually compelling (as suggested by the supplementary video).
4. The method demonstrates a clear capability gap over previous work by achieving 3D locomotion for diverse character types.

**Weaknesses:**
1. The method represents a combination of established techniques rather than a fundamentally novel contribution. The authors acknowledge this ("we combine supervised learning with trajectory optimization... which has given rise to some of the most elaborate motor behaviors"), but the paper doesn't sufficiently distinguish this combination from previous work [9, 11] that also used trajectory optimization for neural control.

2. The paper makes unsubstantiated claims about the superiority of their method without rigorous comparison to established alternatives. The authors state "our impression is that networks trained with direct methods give rise to successful yet somewhat chaotic behaviors," but provide no empirical evidence or quantitative comparison to direct RL methods (e.g., [6, 14]) that they cite as alternatives.

3. The paper lacks a clear theoretical justification for why their approach works better than simpler alternatives. The distributed stochastic optimization approach is presented as a "technical improvement" without clear explanation of why it's necessary or how it fundamentally differs from existing methods.

4. The computational cost is extremely high (2.5 hours of training time, requiring cloud computing), which significantly limits the practical applicability of the method. The authors note "we invest more computing resources in generating the data than in learning from it," which raises questions about scalability.

5. The paper doesn't adequately address how this method advances the field of machine learning beyond the specific application of character control. The contribution seems primarily to be an application of existing techniques to a new domain rather than a methodological advancement for ML practitioners.

**Recommendation:**
While the paper demonstrates a technically sound approach to a challenging problem in character animation, the contribution appears to be incremental rather than transformative. The paper fails to provide sufficient evidence that their approach represents a meaningful advance over established methods in machine learning, which is the primary focus of NIPS. The claims about "more realistic and purposeful behaviors" are subjective without clear metrics or comparisons.

The paper would be strengthened by:
1. Providing a more thorough comparison to established direct RL methods (not just mentioning them)
2. Including quantitative metrics for "realism" and "stability" rather than relying on visual assessment
3. Explaining more clearly how their method represents a novel contribution to machine learning rather than just an application
4. Addressing the high computational cost and discussing potential pathways to reduce it

The paper is well-written and presents a technically sound solution to a specific problem, but it doesn't advance the state of the art in machine learning in a way that would make it suitable for NIPS. The work is more appropriate for a graphics or robotics venue where the application is the primary contribution.

**Summary of Arguments:**

*Pro:*
- Demonstrates a capability (3D locomotion for diverse character types) that previous work couldn't achieve
- Provides reasonable ablation studies for key components
- Well-written and technically sound implementation

*Con:*
- Incremental contribution that combines existing techniques without clear theoretical justification
- Lacks rigorous comparison to established baselines (direct RL methods)
- Subjective claims about "realism" without quantitative metrics
- High computational cost limits practical applicability
- Fails to demonstrate advancement in machine learning methodology

I recommend rejection as the paper doesn't sufficiently advance the field of machine learning, which is the primary focus of NIPS. The work is better suited for a graphics or robotics venue where the application is the primary contribution.
