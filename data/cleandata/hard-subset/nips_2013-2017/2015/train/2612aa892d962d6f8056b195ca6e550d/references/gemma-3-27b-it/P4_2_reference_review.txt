This paper presents a method for learning interactive controllers for complex characters by combining trajectory optimization (specifically Contact-Invariant Optimization, CIO) with neural network training. The core idea is to use CIO to generate training data (trajectories) and then train a neural network to mimic the behavior learned by the optimizer, enabling real-time control. The authors address challenges of applying deep learning to control tasks, such as the need for precise outputs, closed-loop operation, and limited data, through techniques like noise injection and distributed stochastic optimization.

The paper is well-motivated, framing the problem within the context of both robotics and animation, and highlighting the limitations of traditional control methods. The connection to prior work is reasonably thorough, referencing relevant work in reinforcement learning, trajectory optimization, and neural network control. The authors clearly articulate the challenges of scaling deep learning to high-dimensional control problems.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novel Combination:** The combination of CIO and neural networks for interactive control is a compelling approach. While similar ideas have been explored (references [9, 11]), this work extends them to more complex, 3D characters and addresses several technical hurdles.
*   **Scalability:** The distributed training architecture and GPU implementation are crucial for handling the computational demands of the problem, demonstrating a practical consideration for real-world application.
*   **Robustness:** The noise injection technique is a sensible way to improve the generalization and robustness of the learned policy, addressing the closed-loop instability issues inherent in control tasks.
*   **Comprehensive Experiments:** The application to diverse character types (swimming, flying, bipeds, quadrupeds) demonstrates the generality of the approach. The ablation studies (Fig. 2) provide valuable insight into the contribution of the key components.
*   **Clear Explanation of Technical Details:** The paper provides a relatively detailed description of the algorithms, optimization procedures, and network architecture, facilitating reproducibility.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Limited Comparative Evaluation:** While ablation studies are present, a more direct comparison to other neural network control methods (e.g., those cited in the introduction [3, 6, 14]) would strengthen the paper. The authors’ impression of those methods being “chaotic” is subjective and lacks quantitative backing.
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The many hyperparameters (noise levels, regularization weights, network size) are not extensively explored. A sensitivity analysis or justification for the chosen values would be beneficial.
*   **CIO Dependency:** The method heavily relies on the quality of the initial trajectories generated by CIO. Any limitations or biases in CIO will likely propagate to the learned policy. This dependency is acknowledged but could be further discussed.
*   **Complexity:** The overall system is complex, with multiple interacting components (CIO, neural network training, distributed optimization). This complexity may make it difficult for others to implement and adapt the method.
*   **Lack of User Study/Interactive Demonstration:** While the supplementary video is mentioned, a more formal user study or interactive demonstration showcasing the real-time control capabilities would be valuable.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and technically sound approach to a challenging problem in control.
*   The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the method for a variety of character types.
*   The authors address key challenges of applying deep learning to control tasks.
*   The paper is well-written and provides a clear explanation of the technical details.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The comparative evaluation is limited, and the authors' claims about other methods are not fully substantiated.
*   The method is complex and potentially sensitive to hyperparameters.
*   The reliance on CIO as a data generator could be a limitation.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with significant potential. The combination of trajectory optimization and neural networks is a promising approach for learning interactive controllers for complex characters. The authors have addressed several important challenges and demonstrated the effectiveness of their method through comprehensive experiments. The weaknesses are not fatal, and the benefits of the work outweigh the drawbacks. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions, primarily focused on providing a more thorough comparative evaluation and discussing the limitations of the CIO dependency. The authors should also consider providing more insight into the sensitivity of the method to hyperparameter choices.
