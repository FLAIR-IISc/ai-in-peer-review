Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes an approach for training interactive, real-time controllers for complex 3D characters by alternating between trajectory optimization (Contact-Invariant Optimization, CIO) and supervised learning of a neural network policy. The key ingredients are: (i) learning a policy that outputs desired state velocities, recurrent-state updates, and contact forces from compact sensory inputs; (ii) injecting Gaussian noise into sensory inputs and hidden activations to promote robustness; (iii) using a principled first-order expansion that maps sensory perturbations to action perturbations, requiring estimates of local feedback gains; (iv) deriving those feedback gains from the trajectory optimizer via a soft-constraint perturbation method that reuses the Gauss-Newton structure; and (v) a distributed asynchronous system with proximal regularization that alternates between re-optimizing trajectories and regressing the policy. At execution, the policy’s desired signals are enforced by a one-step optimization subject to (soft) dynamics and contact constraints, enabling real-time control.

This line of work builds directly on CIO [Mordatch et al., TOG 2012] and on learning policies from trajectory optimization [Levine & Koltun, ICML 2014; Mordatch & Todorov, RSS 2014], and is partly complementary to model-free deep RL in continuous control [e.g., Schulman et al., TRPO]. Compared to those, the paper claims contributions in (a) scaling to diverse 3D characters and locomotion tasks; (b) a new way to extract well-conditioned feedback gains from a direct optimizer for use in training with noisy inputs; and (c) an asynchronous alternating optimization framework with proximal terms.

Strengths
- Tackles a challenging and practically relevant problem: online control for diverse 3D morphologies with contacts, producing plausible locomotion without hand-coded gaits/contact schedules.
- The perturbation-based derivation of local feedback gains that reuses the Gauss-Newton inverse is a neat, computationally efficient idea; softer gains controlled by λ are well-motivated and likely more numerically stable than naïve LQG passes.
- Noise-injection strategy is principled: sensory noise paired with action corrections via gains addresses the covariate shift/closed-loop instability common in policy learning; hidden-unit noise further regularizes.
- Clear algorithmic decomposition: a simple alternating scheme that leverages off-the-shelf components (CIO, SGD on GPU), plus an implementation that scales asynchronously across a small cluster.
- Empirical diversity: swimmers, flyers, bipeds, and quadrupeds in 3D with a single architecture and generic objectives; emergence of gait patterns without explicit encoding is compelling.
- Ablations (no joint alternating, no noise) show large gaps, supporting the need for the joint scheme and noise.

Weaknesses
- Quantitative evaluation is limited. Most claims rely on videos and qualitative description. There are few standardized metrics (e.g., task success, tracking error, energy/torque costs, fall rate, robustness to perturbations), and limited statistical summaries across seeds or initial conditions.
- Baseline comparisons are weak. There is no quantitative comparison to model-based MPC with identical costs/horizons (only a qualitative remark), nor to model-free policy gradient methods (TRPO, DDPG/NAF) or to prior trajectory-optimization–guided policy learning [Levine & Koltun; Mordatch & Todorov 2014]. The claim of substantially harder problems (full 3D) would benefit from head-to-head baselines.
- Execution depends on a per-step optimization with soft dynamics/contact constraints and potential “root forces,” which can mask stability issues and reduce physical realism; this undermines claims of readiness for robotics where hard constraints and strict feasibility matter. It is unclear how often constraints are violated and how much root force is used.
- Some design choices are under-specified or justified only heuristically: the exact cost features φ and weights; dimension and role of the recurrent state r; choice of action parameterization to include contact forces; sensitivity to λ, α, η, σ parameters; convergence properties beyond a single plot.
- Reproducibility is hampered by reliance on MuJoCo and a nontrivial distributed setup; while many constants are given, crucial details (DOFs per character, dataset sizes per character, training curves per task, ablation of λ on gains) are missing.

Quality
Technically, the alternating optimization and the feedback-gain derivation are sound under the stated approximations (Gauss-Newton Hessian; local linearization). The inclusion of proximal terms is reasonable for asynchronous updates. The experimental design demonstrates feasibility across tasks, and ablations support two core claims (need for joint optimization and noise). However, the empirical evidence is insufficiently rigorous: lack of standardized metrics, variance estimates, and strong baselines limits confidence in generality and relative advantage. The soft enforcement of dynamics at test time raises concerns about physical validity.

Clarity
The paper is generally well-written and organized. The high-level algorithm and its components are clearly described, and implementation details (cluster, GPU training) are helpful. Some notation could be tightened (e.g., clarify s_t components vs. available measurements; specify r dimensionality and role; detail φ and c(φ); make explicit the degree of soft constraint violations at test time). Including more precise pseudo-code for the gain computation and a concise description of the cost terms would improve reproducibility.

Originality
The overall approach—learning policies by supervising on optimizer-generated trajectories—is not new, but the paper contributes a novel and practically useful method for extracting feedback gains from direct trajectory optimization and shows a credible scaling to diverse 3D characters. The alternating asynchronous training with proximal regularization is a reasonable engineering refinement. Novelty is moderate but meaningful.

Significance
If substantiated with stronger quantitative results and baselines, this could influence both graphics and control communities by offering a practical route to interactive controllers with realistic motion from generic objectives. As it stands, the significance is limited by the evaluation gaps and by reliance on soft constraint enforcement at test time, which weakens the robotics relevance.

Questions and suggestions
- Provide quantitative metrics: success rate to target, path/time optimality, energy/torque, gait stability, falls per minute, sensitivity to external pushes and parameter noise. Report means/variances over multiple seeds and initial states.
- Compare to MPC with identical costs/horizons and to a strong model-free baseline (e.g., TRPO) on at least one character.
- Report the magnitude/frequency of dynamics/constraint violations and root forces at test time.
- Clarify the recurrent state: dimensionality, initialization, learned role; show ablations with/without r.
- Analyze sensitivity to λ (gain softness), α (penalty), η (proximal), and noise levels.
- Discuss whether execution can avoid per-step optimization (e.g., by predicting torques and simulating) and what happens to stability.

Arguments for acceptance
- Practical, scalable method that unifies trajectory optimization and policy learning for diverse 3D characters.
- Clever, lightweight feedback-gain extraction that reuses optimizer computations.
- Demonstrated feasibility with compelling qualitative results; ablations support core ideas.

Arguments against acceptance
- Insufficient quantitative evaluation and lack of strong baselines.
- Reliance on soft dynamics enforcement and root forces at test time questions physical plausibility.
- Some critical design choices and costs are under-specified, limiting reproducibility and scientific rigor.

Overall recommendation: borderline reject. The ideas are promising and likely valuable, but the paper needs stronger quantitative evaluation, clearer baselines, and more transparency on physical validity at execution to meet NeurIPS standards.
