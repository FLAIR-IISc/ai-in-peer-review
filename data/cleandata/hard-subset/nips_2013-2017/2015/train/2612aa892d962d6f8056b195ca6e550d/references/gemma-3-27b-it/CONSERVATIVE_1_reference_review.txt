This paper proposes a method for learning neural network controllers for complex characters by combining trajectory optimization (specifically Contact-Invariant Optimization) with supervised learning. The core idea is to use trajectory optimization to generate training data, and then train a neural network to mimic the optimized behavior, enabling real-time control. The authors address the challenges of scaling to high-dimensional spaces and maintaining stability through noise injection and a distributed optimization scheme. While the presented results are impressive visually, demonstrating locomotion in 3D for diverse characters, I find the paper falls short of convincingly establishing a significant advance over existing techniques and suffers from a lack of thorough comparative evaluation.

The paper’s strengths lie in the ambition of its goal – automating controller design for complex characters – and the successful demonstration of stable 3D locomotion. The use of CIO to generate training data is a reasonable approach, and the noise injection strategy appears promising for improving robustness. The distributed training architecture is a practical consideration for tackling the computational demands. However, the presentation lacks the rigor expected of a NIPS submission.

My primary concern is the limited comparative analysis. While the authors briefly dismiss “direct methods” (RL, etc.) as yielding “somewhat chaotic behaviors,” this assertion is not supported by quantitative comparisons. Simply stating an impression is insufficient. A systematic comparison against established reinforcement learning algorithms (e.g., PPO, SAC) or model-predictive control techniques trained on similar data would be crucial to demonstrate the superiority of the proposed approach. The ablation study presented (Figure 2) only compares against a naive neural network regression on a static dataset and disabling noise – it doesn’t address the performance relative to alternative *learning* paradigms. The claim that their method yields “more realistic and purposeful behaviors” requires concrete justification beyond visual observation.

The methodology also raises several questions. The choice of sensory input features (position, orientation, joint angles, velocities, forces) seems somewhat arbitrary. While the authors state it is a “general-purpose representation,” there’s no discussion of alternative feature spaces or their potential impact on performance. Similarly, the specific cost functions used in trajectory optimization are not fully detailed, and their influence on the learned behaviors isn’t explored. The sensitivity of the method to the various hyperparameters (noise levels, penalty weights, learning rates) is also not adequately addressed. The reliance on a physics simulator (MuJoCo) is a potential limitation, as the learned policies may not generalize well to real-world systems or different simulators.

The authors acknowledge the computational cost, noting that more resources are spent generating data than learning from it. This is a valid point, but it raises concerns about scalability. If generating high-quality training data remains the bottleneck, the method may not be practical for even more complex characters or environments. The rationale for freezing the noise during neural network optimization is also not entirely convincing.

The description of the optimal feedback gains calculation feels somewhat hand-wavy and reliant on intuition. While the perturbation method described is a clever way to reuse computations, the resulting gains may still be sensitive to the chosen perturbation scale and the initial trajectory.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Demonstrates successful application to complex 3D locomotion tasks.
*   Combines trajectory optimization and supervised learning in a potentially effective manner.
*   Addresses the challenges of high-dimensional control and stability.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Lack of thorough comparative evaluation against established methods.
*   Insufficient justification for design choices (feature selection, cost functions, hyperparameters).
*   Limited discussion of the method's limitations and potential for generalization.
*   Methodology requires significant computational resources for data generation.
*   Insufficient detail on the optimal feedback gain computation.



Overall, while the paper presents an interesting idea and promising results, the lack of rigorous evaluation and detailed analysis prevents me from recommending acceptance to NIPS at this time. I believe the authors need to address the concerns raised above, particularly by providing a comprehensive comparison against alternative control methods, before their work can be considered a significant contribution to the field. I would be open to reviewing a revised version of this paper that addresses these shortcomings.
