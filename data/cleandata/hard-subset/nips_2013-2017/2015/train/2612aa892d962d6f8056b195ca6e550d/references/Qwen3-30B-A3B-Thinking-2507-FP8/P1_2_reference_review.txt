

# Peer Review: "Interactive Control of Diverse Complex Characters with Neural Networks"

## Summary
This paper proposes a method for generating neural network controllers that enable interactive, real-time control of diverse 3D character locomotion. The authors combine Contact-Invariant Optimization (CIO) for trajectory generation with neural network policy learning, using a joint optimization framework to train controllers that can handle various character morphologies (bipedal, quadrupedal, swimming, flying) in 3D. Unlike previous work that was limited to 2D characters, this approach demonstrates stable locomotion in complex 3D environments. The method uses trajectory optimization to generate training data, then trains a neural network to map sensory states to actions with injected noise to improve robustness. The authors demonstrate that their approach yields more realistic behaviors than direct neural network training methods.

## Strengths

**Quality**: The paper presents a technically sound approach that properly combines trajectory optimization and neural networks. The distributed stochastic optimization framework (Algorithm 1) is well-motivated and properly implemented. The ablation studies (Section 8.1) convincingly demonstrate the importance of key components: joint optimization (vs. static training) and noise injection (vs. no noise). The experimental results across multiple character types in 3D are robust and well-documented, with clear comparisons to baseline approaches.

**Originality**: The paper makes a significant contribution by extending trajectory optimization-based control to 3D locomotion where previous work was limited to 2D. The combination of CIO with neural network policy learning, particularly with the noise injection approach for robustness, represents a novel application of known techniques. The demonstration that the same network architecture can control diverse character morphologies without specialized initialization is a strong original contribution.

**Significance**: The work addresses a practical challenge in character animation and robotics with real-world implications. The ability to generate controllers for diverse 3D characters with a single network architecture is a major advancement over previous character-specific control methods. The method's ability to produce stable, realistic locomotion in complex 3D environments represents a meaningful step forward from prior work.

**Clarity**: The paper is well-organized with clear mathematical formulations. The technical details of the optimization framework are well-explained, and the experimental results are presented effectively. The supplementary video (mentioned in Section 8) would be particularly valuable for understanding the results.

## Weaknesses

**Comparison to Recent Deep RL Methods**: While the paper mentions deep reinforcement learning (DRL) in the introduction, it fails to properly compare with state-of-the-art DRL approaches for continuous control (e.g., SAC, PPO, or Soft Actor-Critic). These methods have made significant advances in continuous control problems with complex dynamics. The paper claims "previous work was applied to only two-dimensional characters," but doesn't acknowledge that recent DRL methods have also achieved 3D locomotion (e.g., [14] cited in the paper).

**Computational Efficiency**: The paper states that "the heavy lifting is done by the trajectory optimizer, and yet the neural network complements it," but doesn't provide adequate analysis of computational trade-offs. It would be valuable to compare the total training time (hours) with the time required for DRL training on similar problems, as this is a critical factor for practical adoption.

**Generalization to Novel Morphologies**: The paper claims the same network can control multiple character morphologies, but doesn't provide evidence of generalization to completely novel character types not seen during training. The experiments show success across different character types in the training set, but don't demonstrate true out-of-distribution generalization.

**Limited Ablation Across Character Types**: The ablation studies (Section 8.1) are limited to biped walking. A more comprehensive analysis across different character types would strengthen the claims about the method's robustness and generalizability.

## Recommendation

This is a strong paper that makes a meaningful contribution to the field of character control and neural network-based control. The core method is technically sound, the results are impressive, and the demonstration of 3D locomotion across diverse character types represents a significant advancement over previous work. The paper makes a compelling case for the value of combining trajectory optimization with neural network learning for control.

However, the paper would be significantly improved by:
1. Adding a more thorough comparison with recent DRL methods
2. Providing better analysis of computational costs
3. Including ablation studies across multiple character types
4. More clearly demonstrating generalization to novel character morphologies

Despite these weaknesses, the paper makes a substantial contribution to the field. The core method is novel in its application to 3D locomotion and the demonstration of diverse character control with a single network architecture is compelling.

## Final Recommendation

**Accept** - This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field. While the paper has some weaknesses in comparison to recent DRL work, the core contribution of enabling interactive 3D locomotion control across diverse character morphologies is substantial and represents a meaningful advancement over previous trajectory optimization approaches. The method addresses a practical problem with clear applications in animation and robotics, and the results are well-supported by thorough experimentation. With the suggested improvements (particularly better comparison to recent DRL methods), this paper would be even stronger, but as it stands, it makes a valuable contribution to the literature.
