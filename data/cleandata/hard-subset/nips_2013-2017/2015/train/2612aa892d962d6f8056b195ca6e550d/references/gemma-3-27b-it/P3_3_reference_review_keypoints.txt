- Overall: Technically sound, compelling approach bridging offline trajectory optimization and online control; recommends acceptance.  
- Summary: Uses Contact-Invariant Optimization (CIO) to generate trajectories and trains neural networks to mimic the optimizer for real-time interactive control.  
- Positioning: Clearly situates work relative to neural network, RL, and biomechanics approaches; argues superiority over prior 2D/limited-complexity methods.  
- Technical innovation: Novel integration of CIO with neural regression and noise injection guided by optimal feedback gains to improve stability and generalization.  
- Scalability: Demonstrates generalization across diverse 3D morphologies (swimming, flying, bipedal, quadrupedal).  
- Empirical results: Impressive, realistic motions with strong ablation studies and supportive supplementary video.  
- Practicality: Describes distributed training and computational costs, showing feasibility with current resources.  
- Problem definition: Clearly articulates challenges of interactive control in high-dimensional continuous spaces.  
- Presentation weakness: Some sections (e.g., optimal feedback gains, perturbation–LQG link) are dense and need clearer explanations or examples.  
- Hyperparameter sensitivity: Paper reports constants but lacks analysis of sensitivity and effect on performance.  
- Comparative evaluation: Limited systematic comparison to alternative methods like RL; quantitative comparisons (stability, realism, learning speed) are missing.  
- Engine dependence: Reliance on MuJoCo raises transferability concerns that should be acknowledged.  
- Originality: Genuine contribution in combining CIO with neural learning and a novel, gain-guided noise injection strategy.  
- Significance: High impact potential for character animation, robotics, and biomechanics/neuroscience modeling.  
- Acceptance arguments: Tackles important problem with innovative, well-validated solution and broad applicability.  
- Rejection arguments: Needs clearer presentation, more systematic comparisons, and exploration of hyperparameter robustness.
