

# Review of "Interactive Control of Diverse Complex Characters with Neural Networks"

This paper proposes a method for generating neural network controllers for interactive character control using a combination of trajectory optimization and neural network training. The authors claim to extend previous work [9, 11] to 3D locomotion tasks, which they argue is substantially harder than previous 2D approaches. Their method involves generating training data through Contact-Invariant Optimization (CIO) and then training a neural network policy using a distributed stochastic optimization approach that injects noise to improve robustness.

**Strengths:**
The paper presents a well-structured approach to a challenging problem in character control. The demonstration of successful locomotion for diverse 3D characters (swimmers, fliers, bipeds, quadrupeds) is impressive and addresses a meaningful gap in the literature. The method of generating training data through trajectory optimization with branching segments to simulate interaction is clever and well-motivated. The technical details of the distributed optimization approach and noise injection are reasonably well-described.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Lack of Quantitative Evaluation:** The paper makes strong claims about performance ("two orders of magnitude better than static training") but provides no quantitative metrics to support these claims. The comparison in Figure 2 is qualitative, showing trajectories rather than performance numbers. The paper should include metrics like success rate, time to goal, energy efficiency, or stability duration to properly evaluate performance.

2. **Incomplete Technical Details:** The paper claims to "invest more computing resources in generating the data than in learning from it" but provides no concrete numbers to support this assertion. Similarly, the claim of "real-time interactive control" lacks latency measurements or frame rate data to define what "real-time" means in this context.

3. **Mathematical Inconsistencies:** In section 3.1, the paper writes "q̇t , qt+1 − qt" which appears to be a typo (should be "q̇t = qt+1 − qt" or with time step specification). The notation for CXX is inconsistent throughout, with "t" used as both an index and subscript in the same equation. The paper also references "CIO" without proper definition on first use.

4. **Missing Experimental Details:** The paper states they use "MuJoCo physics simulator" but fails to specify version, physics parameters, or simulation settings. The lack of details makes results difficult to reproduce. Similarly, the paper mentions "the supplementary video" multiple times but doesn't provide a link or description of what's shown.

5. **Overstated Claims:** The paper claims to "unify disparate approaches" for different character types but fails to clearly articulate how their method specifically unifies these approaches. The claim that their method "yields more realistic and purposeful behaviors" compared to direct RL methods is presented without quantitative evidence.

6. **Methodological Ambiguities:** The noise injection method (section 2.1) is described as similar to denoising autoencoders but without sufficient justification for why this specific approach is better. The paper claims to "inject different forms of noise during training" but only describes Gaussian noise, without explaining why other forms weren't considered.

7. **Formatting and Presentation Issues:** The reference list has inconsistent formatting (some entries have "pp. 1-20," others don't; some have extra commas). The paper states they use "3 hidden layer neural networks with 250 hidden units" but doesn't explain why this architecture was chosen over alternatives. The algorithm description in section 2.2 is incomplete, listing steps without explanation.

8. **Insufficient Comparison:** The comparison to MPC (section 8.1) is vague. The paper states MPC creates "effective trajectories that are not cyclical" but doesn't explain why cyclical behavior is better for locomotion or how this comparison is fair (e.g., MPC would likely use different cost functions).

**Conclusion:**

While the paper presents an interesting approach to interactive character control with some promising qualitative results, it suffers from significant technical shortcomings that undermine its scientific contribution. The lack of quantitative evaluation metrics, incomplete methodological details, mathematical inconsistencies, and overstated claims prevent the paper from making a strong contribution to the field. The paper would benefit from more rigorous evaluation, better documentation of experimental parameters, and clearer articulation of how this work differs from and improves upon previous approaches.

Although the problem is important and the results are visually compelling, the paper fails to meet NIPS's standards for technical soundness and completeness. The minor flaws identified above accumulate to a substantial methodological weakness that would prevent the paper from being accepted at NIPS. The authors should address these issues before considering resubmission to a venue that values rigorous experimental validation and methodological completeness.
