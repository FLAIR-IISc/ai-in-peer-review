This paper presents a method for synthesizing interactive, real-time controllers for complex, physically-simulated 3D characters. The core idea is to combine the strengths of offline trajectory optimization with the real-time performance of neural network policies. Specifically, the authors use Contact-Invariant Optimization (CIO) to generate physically plausible and task-oriented motion trajectories. These trajectories then serve as training data for a recurrent neural network controller. The primary contribution lies in framing this as a joint optimization problem, where the trajectories and the network policy are iteratively refined together, rather than as a simple two-stage process. This approach is related to prior work that learns policies from trajectory optimizers (e.g., Levine & Koltun, 2014; Mordatch & Todorov, 2014), but extends these ideas to more complex, 3D characters and introduces several technical improvements, notably a novel method for deriving feedback gains and a principled approach to noise injection during training.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Significance and Quality of Results:** The most compelling strength of this work is the quality and diversity of the generated behaviors. The ability of a single, unified framework to produce stable and plausible locomotion for characters as different as bipeds, quadrupeds, swimmers, and flyers is a significant achievement. As noted in the paper and supplementary video, the emergent gaits (e.g., bipedal walking, quadrupedal trotting, cyclical wing flapping) are realistic and appear natural, despite not being explicitly programmed. This convincingly demonstrates the power and generality of the proposed approach and represents a clear advance in the state of the art for character animation and control.

2.  **Novelty of the Joint Optimization Framework:** The paper's formulation as a joint, alternating optimization problem is elegant and effective. Instead of a static "generate-then-learn" pipeline, the trajectories are continuously updated to remain optimal with respect to the task while also being reproducible by the current neural network policy. This feedback loop appears crucial for regularizing the solution space of the trajectory optimizer, guiding it towards more predictable and cyclically stable behaviors that are amenable to being learned by a compact policy.

3.  **Principled Robustness Training:** The method for injecting noise is technically sound and well-motivated. Rather than simply adding noise to sensory inputs (s) and training the network to produce the original action (a), the authors compute the optimal feedback gains (as) to find the corrected optimal action (a + asε) for the perturbed state. This effectively teaches the network a linearized local policy around the reference trajectory, which is a much more powerful way to learn robustness. The proposed method for efficiently extracting these gains from a direct trajectory optimization method by relaxing the state perturbation constraint is a clever and valuable contribution in its own right.

4.  **Strong Empirical Validation:** The ablation study presented in Figure 2 provides strong evidence for the authors' core claims. The comparisons against training on a static dataset ("no joint") and training without noise ("no noise") clearly demonstrate that both the joint optimization procedure and the stochastic training are critical for achieving good performance at test time. This experimental rigor adds significant weight to the paper's conclusions.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

1.  **Clarity and Reproducibility of Cost Functions:** The paper's primary weakness is a lack of detail regarding the cost functions used in the trajectory optimization. The text mentions "minimizing torque usage and contact force magnitudes" and ensuring the task is satisfied, but the exact formulation, terms, and relative weightings are not provided. Since the cost function is fundamental to the behavior produced, its omission makes the work difficult to reproduce and fully understand. **Suggestion:** The authors should include a detailed description of all cost function terms and their associated weights, perhaps in an appendix, for each category of character (e.g., walker, swimmer).

2.  **Superficial Comparison to MPC:** The comparison to Model Predictive Control (MPC) in Section 8.1 feels incomplete. It is presented as a qualitative observation that MPC produces "less chaotic" but non-cyclical behaviors. While this is an interesting point, a more rigorous comparison would be beneficial. For example, how do the methods compare in terms of computational cost at runtime? Is there a quantitative difference in task success rates or energy efficiency over long horizons? **Suggestion:** To strengthen this comparison, the authors could provide quantitative metrics comparing their final policy to a real-time MPC controller on a specific task. If this is not feasible, the claims should be framed more cautiously as qualitative observations about the regularizing effect of their joint optimization approach.

3.  **Scope of "Interaction":** The term "interactive" in the title may set expectations that are not fully met. The interaction demonstrated consists of changing the target goal position for the character. It is unclear how the learned policies would handle other forms of interaction, such as unexpected external perturbations (e.g., a strong push) or dynamic obstacles. **Suggestion:** The authors could clarify the scope of interaction tested and briefly discuss how their framework might be extended or adapted to handle a wider range of real-time perturbations as part of the future work section.

**Summary of Arguments:**

*   **Pro-acceptance:**
    *   Demonstrates highly impressive and general results on a diverse set of challenging 3D control problems.
    *   Presents a novel and well-motivated joint optimization framework that effectively combines trajectory optimization and policy learning.
    *   Introduces a principled method for noise injection using optimal feedback gains, which is a strong technical contribution.
    *   Provides convincing ablation studies that validate the key components of the proposed algorithm.

*   **Con-acceptance:**
    *   Lacks crucial details on the cost functions, hindering reproducibility.
    *   The comparison with the strong baseline of MPC is primarily qualitative and could be more thorough.
    *   The scope of interactivity is limited, which could be made clearer.

**Decision:**
Overall, this is a high-quality paper that presents a significant advance in automated character control. The strengths, particularly the impressive results and the novelty of the joint optimization and noise injection schemes, substantially outweigh the weaknesses. The lack of detail on cost functions is the most serious concern, but it is addressable. The paper is well-written, the method is technically sound, and the results are likely to be influential for researchers in computer graphics, robotics, and reinforcement learning. I recommend acceptance.
