Summary
The paper proposes a scalable method to train real-time neural network controllers for complex, contact-rich characters by alternating trajectory optimization and supervised policy learning. The key idea is to generate optimal trajectories with Contact-Invariant Optimization (CIO) and fit a policy that maps a sensory state to desired velocities, contact forces, and a recurrent memory update. To improve robustness and reduce sample needs, the authors inject Gaussian noise in sensory inputs and train the policy to match not only nominal actions but also the locally optimal feedback response a(s+ε) ≈ a + A_s ε, where A_s is computed efficiently from the trajectory optimizer via a perturbation method that reuses the Gauss–Newton structure. Training proceeds in an asynchronous, distributed fashion with proximal regularization, alternating between re-optimizing trajectories (with noise and policy penalties) and regressing the policy on a GPU. At test time, the policy runs in real time and the next state is computed by solving a one-step constrained optimization that softly enforces dynamics. The method is demonstrated on diverse 3D characters (bipeds, quadrupeds, swimmer, bird), achieving stable cyclic locomotion without hand-designed controllers. Ablations indicate that joint optimization and noise injection are important; simple MPC produces effective but non-cyclic trajectories.

Relation to prior work
This work builds on CIO [Mordatch et al. 2012], and is closely related to Guided Policy Search and trajectory-optimization-supervised policy learning [Levine & Koltun 2014; Mordatch & Todorov 2014], as well as policy search in continuous control (e.g., TRPO [Schulman et al. 2015]). Compared to these, the paper emphasizes (i) a principled way to inject input noise and train against linearized optimal feedback derived from a direct trajectory optimizer (without an extra LQG pass), (ii) asynchronous distributed alternating optimization with proximal regularization, and (iii) a unified demonstration in full 3D across very different morphologies. Earlier graphics/control work (e.g., Neuroanimator) and model-free deep RL are acknowledged; the authors argue their approach yields more stable, purposeful motion than model-free RL.

Strengths
- Bold and broad scope: a single pipeline and network architecture yield interactive control across swimmers, flyers, bipeds, and quadrupeds in 3D, without task-specific state machines or mocap.
- Technical novelty: the perturbation-based derivation of local feedback gains directly from a Gauss–Newton approximation in CIO is elegant and practical, avoiding ill-conditioned LQG passes and reusing existing computations (Woodbury identity trick).
- Noise-injection scheme is principled: training targets incorporate the first-order action response to sensory noise, making the learned policy locally feedback-consistent and robust.
- Distributed, asynchronous training with proximal regularization is well-motivated and seems to work in practice at non-trivial scale.
- Real-time execution via a one-step optimization that softly enforces dynamics is a pragmatic and effective design, enabling stable large time steps.
- Clear qualitative impact: stable cyclic gaits and plausible swimming/flapping emerge without explicit cyclic priors.

Weaknesses
- Limited quantitative evaluation and baselines. The only ablations are “no joint” and “no noise,” plus small architecture sweeps. There is no comparison to GPS/iLQG-based policy learning, TRPO (or other modern policy gradients), or model-predictive control with learned dynamics. The MPC comparison is qualitative and on a single domain.
- Metrics are sparse. Success rates, tracking error, energy/torque use, robustness to disturbances, and generalization to unseen goals or perturbations are not systematically reported.
- Execution uses soft dynamics constraints and allows root forces, which undermines claims of physical realism (particularly for robotics). This is acceptable for animation, but the paper gestures toward robotics applications.
- Action space includes contact forces directly, which are typically not directly actuated on real systems; the implications for transfer to hardware are not discussed.
- The recurrent memory state r is central but under-specified. How is r initialized/used at test time, what is its dimensionality, and does the network have explicit recurrence beyond treating r as part of the state-action?
- Sensitivity and conditioning of the feedback gain computation are not thoroughly analyzed. Choice of λ and σ_ε, and their effect on stability/conditioning, deserves more empirical backing.
- Data generation cost is significant and depends on CIO quality; scalability to more complex tasks or longer horizons is unclear.

Quality
Methodologically sound overall, with a clever leverage of trajectory optimization structure. The derivations are reasonable (given Gauss–Newton) and the distributed training design is coherent. However, the experimental section prioritizes demos over rigorous evaluation, which weakens claims about superiority over alternatives.

Clarity
Generally clear and well-organized, but several important details are missing:
- Precise definition and dimensionality of r, and the recurrence mechanism.
- Exact dynamics/constraint formulations used at test time, including penalty weights and whether contact complementarity is treated consistently.
- More details on network initialization (spectral radius “just above 1”) and its relation to stability.
There are scattered typos; tightening notation (e.g., shapes of Jacobians/Hessians, consistent use of bars and dots) would help reproducibility.

Originality
Moderate to good. The overall framework aligns with guided policy search ideas, but the specific contribution of extracting stabilizing gains from direct trajectory optimization and using them to shape supervised noisy targets is novel and useful. The breadth of 3D demonstrations without hand-crafted controllers is also noteworthy.

Significance
High for animation/control communities and relevant to NeurIPS audiences interested in bridging optimization and deep policies. If substantiated with stronger comparisons and metrics, this could influence how we learn robust policies from trajectory optimizers at scale.

Questions for authors
- Please detail the design and dimensionality of r, and how recurrence is implemented and initialized at test time.
- How sensitive are results to λ and σ_ε? Can you report the conditioning of A_s and stability of rollouts as these vary?
- Can you provide quantitative robustness tests (e.g., push disturbances) and success rates across random goals?
- How would the method adapt if contact forces are not directly controllable (e.g., torque control only)?
- Can you compare against GPS/iLQG-based policy learning on at least one domain?

Pros for acceptance
- Bold, unifying approach; compelling 3D demonstrations.
- Neat and practical technical ideas (feedback gains from CIO; noise-linearization targets).
- Scalable, real-time system with clear engineering value.

Cons against acceptance
- Weak quantitative evaluation and limited baselines.
- Some ambiguity around recurrence, dynamics enforcement, and action space realism.

Recommendation
Weak accept. The paper introduces a compelling direction with nontrivial technical contributions and impressive scope. While the evaluation could be stronger, the novelty and potential impact justify acceptance, provided the authors add quantitative metrics, clarify recurrence/dynamics details, and include at least one stronger baseline comparison.

Confidence
Medium.
