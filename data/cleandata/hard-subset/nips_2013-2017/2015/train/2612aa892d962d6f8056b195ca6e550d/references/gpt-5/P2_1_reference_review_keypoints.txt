- Paper proposes alternating trajectory optimization (CIO) with supervised neural policy learning for real-time interactive control of complex 3D characters.
- Key components: policy outputs desired state velocities, recurrent updates, and contact forces from compact sensory inputs.
- Key components: inject Gaussian noise into sensory inputs and hidden activations to improve robustness.
- Key components: use a first-order expansion mapping sensory perturbations to action perturbations requiring local feedback gains.
- Key components: derive feedback gains from the trajectory optimizer via a soft-constraint perturbation method reusing Gauss-Newton structure.
- Key components: distributed asynchronous alternating optimization with proximal regularization between trajectory re-optimization and policy regression.
- Execution enforces policy outputs via a one-step optimization with soft dynamics and contact constraints for real-time control.
- Builds directly on CIO and prior trajectory-optimization–guided policy learning, and is complementary to model-free deep RL (e.g., TRPO).
- Claimed contributions: scaling to diverse 3D characters and locomotion tasks with a single architecture.
- Claimed contributions: novel, well-conditioned feedback gain extraction from a direct optimizer.
- Claimed contributions: asynchronous alternating optimization framework with proximal terms for scalability.
- Strength: tackles a challenging, practically relevant problem and produces plausible locomotion without hand-coded gaits.
- Strength: feedback-gain derivation is computationally efficient and numerically stable via Gauss-Newton reuse and λ-controlled softness.
- Strength: principled noise-injection strategy that pairs sensory noise with action corrections and hidden-unit regularization.
- Strength: clear algorithmic decomposition and scalable implementation using off-the-shelf components and cluster-based training.
- Strength: empirical diversity across swimmers, flyers, bipeds, and quadrupeds with emergent gait patterns and supporting ablations.
- Weakness: quantitative evaluation is limited—few standardized metrics, statistical summaries, or robustness statistics.
- Weakness: weak baseline comparisons—no quantitative MPC or strong model-free RL baselines included.
- Weakness: per-step soft optimization and root forces at execution may mask stability issues and reduce physical realism for robotics.
- Weakness: some design choices are under-specified (cost features, recurrent state role, action parameterization, hyperparameter sensitivity).
- Weakness: reproducibility concerns due to reliance on MuJoCo, distributed setup, and missing dataset/training details.
- Quality: alternating optimization and gain derivation are technically sound, but empirical evidence lacks rigor and strong baselines.
- Clarity: generally well-written, but notation and some implementation details (s_t components, r dimensionality, cost terms, gain pseudo-code) need tightening.
- Originality: method of supervising policies from optimizers is not new, but the gain-extraction and scaling to diverse 3D characters are meaningful contributions.
- Significance: potentially influential if stronger quantitative validation and demonstration of physical validity are provided.
- Suggestions: add quantitative metrics, baselines (MPC, TRPO), constraint violation/root force statistics, recurrent-state ablations, and sensitivity analyses for λ, α, η, σ.
- Acceptance arguments: practical scalable method, clever gain extraction, and compelling qualitative results with supportive ablations.
- Rejection arguments: insufficient quantitative evaluation, soft constraint reliance reducing physical plausibility, and under-specified design choices.
- Overall recommendation: borderline reject pending stronger quantitative results, clearer baselines, and more transparency on execution physical validity.
