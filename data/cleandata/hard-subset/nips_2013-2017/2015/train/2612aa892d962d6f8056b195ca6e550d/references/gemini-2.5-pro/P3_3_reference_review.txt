### Summary and Relation to Prior Work

This paper presents a method for training neural network policies to control a diverse set of complex, 3D physically-simulated characters for interactive locomotion tasks. The core idea is to leverage the power of an offline, high-quality trajectory optimizer (Contact-Invariant Optimization, CIO) to generate expert data, and then train a neural network to mimic this expert in an online fashion. Crucially, this is not a simple two-stage process. The authors propose a joint optimization framework where trajectory optimization and neural network training are performed in an alternating, iterative fashion. This allows the trajectories to adapt to the current capabilities of the network policy, while the network is continuously updated with new, relevant data.

This work sits within the "guided policy search" and "imitation learning from an expert" paradigms. It builds directly upon recent work like Levine & Koltun (2014) [9] and Mordatch & Todorov (2014) [11], which also combined trajectory optimization with function approximation. The key contribution and distinction of this paper is the successful application of these ideas to substantially more complex, 3D characters (bipeds, quadrupeds, swimmers, flyers), where previous work was limited to 2D. This leap in complexity is enabled by several technical innovations, most notably a principled method for noise injection during training. To teach the network robustness, the authors perturb sensory inputs and use optimal feedback gains—derived from the trajectory optimizer—to compute the corresponding optimal actions. This effectively teaches the network the local dynamics of the optimal policy. The paper also contrasts its approach with direct reinforcement learning methods (e.g., [14]), arguing that their "teacher-student" framework results in more structured, realistic, and less chaotic behaviors.

### Strengths

1.  **Significant Results and Impact:** The primary strength of this work lies in its impressive empirical results. Demonstrating a single, unified algorithm that can generate stable, interactive controllers for such a diverse range of 3D morphologies (walking, trotting, swimming, flying) is a significant achievement in character animation and control. The emergent, natural-looking gaits (e.g., bipedal walking, quadrupedal trotting) without explicit encoding are a strong validation of the approach. This work substantially advances the state of the art in automated controller synthesis.

2.  **Sound and Novel Methodology:** The joint optimization framework is well-motivated and directly addresses the critical challenge of covariate shift that plagues naive imitation learning. By regularizing the trajectory optimization to stay close to what the current network can produce (via the penalty term `R`), the method avoids generating expert demonstrations that are impossible for the student network to follow.

3.  **Principled Robustness Training:** The technique for injecting noise and calculating target actions using optimal feedback gains (Sec 2.1 and 3.2) is a key technical contribution. It moves beyond simple data augmentation by providing the network with physically meaningful supervision on how to react to perturbations. The method derived for extracting these feedback gains from a direct trajectory optimization method like CIO is itself a valuable contribution that could be useful in other contexts.

4.  **Thorough Ablation Study:** The comparative evaluation in Section 8.1 is convincing. The stark performance difference between the full method and the ablations ("no joint" optimization and "no noise") clearly demonstrates the necessity of both the iterative training scheme and the noise injection strategy. The comparison with MPC, highlighting the regularizing effect of the learned policy, provides valuable insight into why the resulting motions appear more natural and cyclic.

### Weaknesses and Areas for Improvement

1.  **Clarity of the Policy Execution Step:** Section 7, "Policy Execution," describes a process that is more complex than a simple forward pass of the neural network. At each timestep, an optimization problem (Eq. 7 with T=1) is solved to find the next state that best matches the network's output `q̇des` while satisfying physical constraints. This is a crucial detail. It is essentially a form of model-predictive control where the neural network provides the reference target. This blurs the lines between a pure feedforward policy and an online optimization method. The paper should be more explicit about this. What is the computational cost of this per-step optimization? Does it truly run in "real-time"? How does this compare to simply using the network's output to compute torques and stepping forward with a standard integrator? This aspect needs significant clarification and justification.

2.  **Comparison to Direct Reinforcement Learning:** The authors state that their "impression is that networks trained with direct methods give rise to successful yet somewhat chaotic behaviors." While this is a widely held view, the paper would be much stronger if this was substantiated with an empirical comparison, even on one of the simpler characters. Given the citation of recent policy gradient methods like TRPO [14], a quantitative or even qualitative comparison against such a baseline would more rigorously position the paper's contribution relative to the mainstream deep RL community.

3.  **Details on Feedback Gain Derivation and Parameters:** The derivation of the optimal feedback gains in Section 3.2 is very condensed. While elegant, it would benefit from more explanation. Specifically, the role and sensitivity of the parameter `λ` is not discussed. It's introduced as a soft constraint weight but also described as a parameter for producing "softer" gains. How is this value chosen? Is the performance of the final policy sensitive to it? A sensitivity analysis or at least a discussion of its tuning would improve reproducibility.

4.  **Computational Cost:** The method requires significant computational resources (hours on a ~30 node cluster). While the distributed architecture is a sound engineering solution, this high cost is a practical limitation. A more detailed breakdown of where the time is spent (e.g., trajectory optimization vs. NN training, finite differencing for Hessians) and how it scales with character complexity would be beneficial for future work aiming to build upon these ideas.

### Final Assessment

Overall, this is a high-quality paper that presents a significant advance in the automatic creation of controllers for complex characters. The results are compelling, the methodology is novel and technically sound, and the core contributions are well-supported by ablation studies. The main weaknesses relate to a lack of clarity in the real-time execution model and the absence of a direct comparison to state-of-the-art RL methods. Despite these points, the paper's strengths in originality, quality, and significance are substantial. It addresses a difficult, important problem and presents a solution that is likely to be highly influential in both computer graphics and robotics. I strongly recommend acceptance, with the suggestion that the authors address the clarity issues, particularly regarding the policy execution step, in the final version.
