This paper presents a method for learning interactive control policies for complex 3D characters by combining trajectory optimization (specifically, Contact-Invariant Optimization - CIO) with neural network regression. The core idea is to use CIO to generate a dataset of optimal trajectories, and then train a neural network to mimic the resulting control policy. The authors address several challenges, including the need for noise injection to prevent overfitting and the computational cost of generating the training data. They demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach on a variety of characters, including swimming, flying, bipeds, and quadrupeds.

The paper is well-motivated, addressing a difficult problem in character control. The combination of trajectory optimization and neural networks is a sensible approach, leveraging the strengths of both techniques. Prior work in reinforcement learning and optimal control is acknowledged, and the authors clearly articulate how their method differs. Specifically, they emphasize the ability to handle high-dimensional spaces and achieve stable locomotion in 3D, exceeding the capabilities of previous methods [9, 11]. The clear explanation of the distributed training architecture and the analysis of the contributions of different components (noise injection, network architecture) are strengths.  The use of CIO, a well-established technique [12], provides a strong foundation for data generation. The results, particularly the demonstration across diverse character morphologies, are compelling.  The authors appropriately reference related work in areas like biomechanics [21, 18] and MPC [implicitly, through discussion of cost functions].

However, the paper lacks some depth in critical areas. While the authors state their method yields “more realistic and purposeful behaviors” than direct methods [3, 6, 14], there is no quantitative comparison. A direct comparison with state-of-the-art reinforcement learning algorithms, even on a limited set of tasks, would strengthen the claims. The ablation study (Section 8.1) is helpful, but limited to network architecture and noise injection.  A comparison against simpler control methods (e.g., PID control) would establish a clearer baseline. The explanation of the noise injection strategy, while present, could be more mathematically rigorous. The connection between the optimal feedback gains (as) and the injected noise (ε) is stated but not fully elaborated. The rationale for the specific choices of algorithmic constants (σε, σγ, α, λ, η) is not provided, and a sensitivity analysis would be beneficial. The description of the task setup (Section 5) is somewhat vague. More details on the range of goal positions and the distribution of initial conditions would be helpful.  The implementation details, although mentioning cloud computing, do not quantify the computational cost beyond “hours.” Reporting training times and hardware specifications more precisely would increase reproducibility. The statement that the optimization problem is nonconvex is a general observation; a discussion of the challenges specific to this problem and how the algorithm addresses them would be valuable.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel combination of trajectory optimization and neural networks addresses a challenging problem in character control.
*   Demonstrated success on diverse character morphologies, showcasing the generality of the approach.
*   Clear explanation of the method and its components.
*   Addresses practical challenges like overfitting and computational cost.
*   Strong connection to prior work in trajectory optimization and control.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Lack of quantitative comparison with alternative methods (e.g., reinforcement learning, simpler control schemes).
*   Limited ablation study.
*   Insufficient detail regarding noise injection strategy and parameter selection.
*   Vague task setup description.
*   Limited discussion of computational cost and reproducibility.



**Overall assessment:**  This is a solid contribution with the potential to significantly impact the field of character control. However, the lack of quantitative comparisons and the limited ablation study prevent it from being a truly outstanding submission. A strong recommendation for acceptance contingent upon addressing the weaknesses outlined above, particularly the inclusion of a comparative evaluation. I rate it as a **strong accept**, but with the caveat that authors should address reviewer comments.
