Summary
This paper learns interactive, real-time controllers for complex 3D characters by alternating trajectory optimization (Contact-Invariant Optimization, CIO) with supervised neural network training. Key ideas: (1) formulate joint optimization over trajectories and a policy with a relaxed penalty tying policy outputs to optimal actions; (2) inject sensor noise and train on noise-adjusted targets using locally optimal feedback gains; (3) derive those gains efficiently from a direct trajectory optimizer via a perturbation method (soft constraints + Woodbury identity), avoiding a separate LQG pass; (4) an asynchronous, distributed training scheme with proximal regularization; and (5) a T=1 optimization-based execution step that acts like an implicit integrator. The method yields robust, cyclic locomotion for varied 3D morphologies (swimmer, bird, bipeds, quadrupeds) without task-specific architectures.

Relation to prior work
The approach is closely related to learning policies from trajectory optimization (e.g., Levine & Koltun ICML’14; Mordatch & Todorov RSS’14) and model-free RL in control (TRPO; Atari DQN; continuous control works). Compared to these, contributions include: handling 3D multi-contact characters with stable gaits; noise-aware supervision with matched target corrections using gains; an efficient gain derivation within direct trajectory optimization; and a distributed, asynchronous alternating scheme with proximal stabilization. The paper also frames differences to MPC in terms of regularizing toward predictable, cyclic behaviors.

Strengths
- Novelty/direction: The feedback-gain derivation for direct trajectory optimization is elegant and broadly useful; the noise-injection with first-order target correction is principled and addresses covariate shift; the alternating, distributed training is practical and scalable. The unification across very different morphologies with a single policy architecture is compelling.
- Significance: Demonstrating robust, interactive 3D locomotion across swimmers, flyers, bipeds, and quadrupeds from the same pipeline is impactful for graphics/robotics. The approach bridges powerful offline trajectory methods with real-time control and offers a recipe others can build on.
- Quality: The objective, algorithm, and the gain derivation are well-motivated. Ablations (no joint optimization, no noise) show large degradations, underscoring the importance of the joint/robust training.
- Practicality: The distributed training architecture and the implicit-integration-like execution are concrete, with reported training times and cluster setup.

Weaknesses and questions
- Limited quantitative evaluation: Results rely mostly on qualitative demos. There is little task success quantification (e.g., distance/orientation errors, energy, stability metrics, recovery from perturbations), no sample-efficiency analysis, and no timing/compute breakdown beyond rough throughput. For acceptance, at least basic metrics across characters and success rates over random goals would strengthen claims.
- Baselines: No systematic comparison to modern model-free methods (e.g., TRPO) or guided policy search variants on comparable 3D tasks, and only a qualitative contrast to MPC. Even if exact apples-to-apples is hard, some baselines on simplified variants (e.g., 2D or reduced DoF) would help.
- Action realism: The policy outputs desired velocities and contact forces and relies on soft constraint enforcement (including allowable root forces). This limits faithfulness to real hardware and may bias behaviors. Clarify how much root force is used at execution and whether results persist under stricter contact models.
- Generalization/robustness: The training procedure samples goal switches and noise, but robustness to larger disturbances, untrained terrain variations, and sensor/model errors is not quantified. How does performance degrade as noise increases? How far can goal switches deviate?
- Reproducibility/details: Some crucial choices are underspecified: exact cost terms/weights; state features beyond [q,r,q̇−1,f−1]; memory state dimensionality; λ, α, η sensitivity; and the influence of the proximal terms on convergence. References are incomplete and contain placeholders.
- The T=1 execution optimization couples learned policy with an optimization layer; it is fast and stable, but blurs the line between “pure policy” and “policy + online optimization.” An ablation that executes via standard integration would help disentangle contributions.

Quality
Technically sound overall with a creative and well-justified gain derivation. The experimental validation is promising but light on quantitative rigor and baselines. The claimed convergence in the asynchronous alternating scheme is empirical; that’s acceptable here but should be documented better.

Clarity
Generally clear, with a helpful high-level algorithm. However, some derivations could be tightened (assumptions for first-order expansion, range of λ), and many practical details are missing. Reference formatting needs fixing. Adding a concise appendix with cost definitions and hyperparameter sensitivity would improve reproducibility.

Originality
Good. While the overall framing builds on prior CIO + policy learning, the feedback gain computation within direct trajectory optimization, the denoising-with-corrected-targets training, and the practical distributed alternating setup are novel and nontrivial. The breadth of 3D demonstrations is also a notable step beyond prior 2D results.

Significance
High for ML-for-control in graphics/robotics. The approach offers a concrete path to scalable, generalist controllers, and the techniques (gain derivation, robust supervised policy learning from trajectories) are likely to be reused.

Suggestions for improvement
- Add quantitative metrics (task error, energy, gait stability, perturbation recovery) and success rates across characters.
- Include baselines on at least a subset (MPC with tuned horizons; TRPO or a guided policy search variant where feasible).
- Report the magnitude of root forces and effects of enforcing harder contacts.
- Provide sensitivity analyses for λ, α, η, and ablate the recurrent state.
- Clarify cost terms/weights and state/action dimensions; consider releasing code or detailed pseudo-code.
- Discuss feasibility for hardware: mapping forces to torques, actuator limits, and sim-to-real considerations.

Decision
Weak accept. Despite some empirical limitations, the paper introduces fresh, useful ideas and convincingly pushes toward general, interactive control of complex 3D characters. The novelty and potential impact justify acceptance if the authors strengthen quantitative evaluation and clarify details.

Confidence
Medium-high.
