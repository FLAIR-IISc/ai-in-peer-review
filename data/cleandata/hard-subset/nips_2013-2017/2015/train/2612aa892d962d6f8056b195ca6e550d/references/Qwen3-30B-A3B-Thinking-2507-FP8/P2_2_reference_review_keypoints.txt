- Proposes combining Contact-Invariant Optimization (CIO) with distributed stochastic optimization to train neural controllers for diverse 3D characters for interactive real-time control.  
- Extends prior 2D trajectory-optimization based control to challenging 3D locomotion (swimming, flying, bipedal, quadrupedal).  
- Key techniques: noise injection during training and a novel perturbation method for feedback gains to improve stability and realism.  
- Distributed stochastic optimization framework (Algorithm 1) is well-motivated and implemented.  
- Experiments are comprehensive across multiple character types and clearly demonstrate advantages over direct neural network training.  
- Ablation studies show joint trajectory optimization and noise injection are critical; full method outperforms static training by ~100x.  
- Provides sufficient implementation details (network architecture, noise parameters, training procedure) aiding reproducibility.  
- Lacks deeper theoretical analysis explaining why noise injection and the perturbation feedback-gain method improve results.  
- Comparison with modern RL methods (e.g., PPO, SAC) is incomplete and would strengthen the paper.  
- Writing and organization are clear, but some technical sections (notably the feedback-gains perturbation) need more explanation or visual aids.  
- Would benefit from additional visualizations of the network architecture and trajectory optimization; supplementary video is recommended.  
- Original contribution: novel extension to 3D control and a perturbation method that improves over LQG without changing the cost function.  
- Related work is adequately cited, but positioning against recent RL advances could be improved.  
- Significance: important practical advance for animation, robotics, and biomechanics; single NN architecture for diverse characters is notable.  
- Practical impact and specific real-world use cases (e.g., game engines or robotics) are underdeveloped in the discussion.  
- Overall assessment: strong technical contribution with compelling empirical results; recommended for acceptance.  
- Strengths: 3D extension, robust realistic locomotion, thorough ablations, scalable distributed training framework.  
- Weaknesses: limited theoretical grounding, incomplete RL comparisons, underdeveloped robotics discussion, and some unclear technical sections.
