### **Summary and Relation to Previous Work**

This paper presents a method for creating real-time, interactive neural network controllers for a diverse range of complex, physically-simulated 3D characters. The core idea is to combine the power of offline trajectory optimization (specifically, Contact-Invariant Optimization or CIO) with the real-time performance of neural network policies. The authors propose a joint optimization framework where a dataset of optimal trajectories and the neural network parameters are optimized in an alternating fashion. This is not a simple one-shot "distill the optimizer" approach; instead, the network's current state influences the subsequent trajectory optimization, and the newly optimized trajectories provide updated training data for the network.

Key technical contributions that enable this to work on complex 3D characters include:
1.  A novel method for extracting stable, local optimal feedback gains from a *direct* trajectory optimization method (CIO). This is crucial for generating appropriate training targets when noise is injected into the system's state.
2.  The principled use of stochasticity, injecting Gaussian noise into both the sensory inputs during training data generation and into the hidden layer activations during network regression. This is shown to be vital for robustness and generalization.
3.  A distributed, asynchronous training architecture that makes this computationally demanding process practical.

This work sits at the intersection of trajectory optimization, reinforcement learning, and computer animation. It builds directly upon recent work that also learns neural network policies from trajectory optimizers, such as Levine & Koltun (2014) and Mordatch et al. (2014). The primary advance claimed over this prior work is the successful application to significantly more complex and unstable 3D characters (bipeds, quadrupeds, swimmers, flyers), whereas previous efforts were largely confined to 2D. The paper contrasts its approach with direct policy search/RL methods (e.g., Schulman et al., 2015), suggesting that its optimizer-guided method produces more realistic and purposeful motions compared to the often chaotic behaviors learned through direct exploration.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Impressive and Significant Results:** The most compelling aspect of this paper is its demonstrated success on a wide variety of 3D characters with diverse morphologies and locomotion styles (walking, trotting, flying, swimming). The ability to use a single, unified algorithm to generate stable and plausible gaits without task-specific engineering (e.g., hand-designed state machines or contact schedules) is a significant achievement and a clear step forward for the field. The claim that stereotyped gaits (e.g., bipedal walking, quadrupedal trotting) emerge naturally from the optimization is powerful.

2.  **Novel Technical Contribution in Feedback Gain Extraction:** The method for deriving feedback gains from a direct trajectory optimizer (Section 3.2) is a clever and important contribution. Direct methods like CIO do not naturally produce these gains in the same way that indirect methods (like iLQR/DDP) do. The proposed perturbation method, which reuses the computed Hessian, is efficient and provides a way to regularize the gains (via the parameter λ), making them "softer" and better conditioned. This is a generally useful technique that could be adopted by others working with direct trajectory optimization.

3.  **Strong Empirical Validation of Design Choices:** The comparative evaluation in Section 8.1 is excellent. The ablation studies clearly demonstrate the necessity of the two core components of the method: joint optimization and noise injection. The "no joint" case shows that a static dataset is insufficient, leading to policies that fail to generalize, while the "no noise" case highlights the critical role of stochasticity in preventing overfitting and creating robust controllers. This provides strong evidence for the authors' claims.

4.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is well-written and logically structured. It starts with a clear motivation, provides a high-level overview of the algorithm, and then delves into the specifics of each component (trajectory optimization, feedback gains, network regression). The problem formulation (Eq. 1-4) is clear and effectively frames the work.

### **Weaknesses**

1.  **Ambiguity in Real-Time Policy Execution:** Section 7, which describes the policy execution at runtime, is brief and raises questions about real-time feasibility. It states that evolving the state to the next timestep involves solving a T=1 optimization problem subject to physics constraints (Eq. 6). While this is certainly faster than optimizing a long trajectory, it is not immediately obvious that this per-step optimization can be solved at "real-time rates" (e.g., 20Hz, as implied by the 50ms timestep) for complex characters. More details on the computational cost of this step and how it is implemented are needed for reproducibility and to fully substantiate the "real-time" claim.

2.  **Underdeveloped Comparison to MPC:** The comparison to Model Predictive Control (MPC) in Section 8.1 is interesting but feels anecdotal. The paper claims its method produces more "predictable and less chaotic" behaviors, which is a subjective assessment based on a single figure. A more quantitative comparison would be much stronger. For instance, could metrics for gait periodicity, trajectory smoothness, or energy efficiency be used to support this claim? As written, the comparison lacks rigor.

3.  **Limited Scope of "Interaction":** The paper emphasizes "interactive control," but the interaction demonstrated is limited to changing the target goal position. It is unclear how the learned policy would respond to other forms of real-time interaction, such as unexpected external perturbations (e.g., a push). While the noise injection during training should confer some robustness, this is not explicitly tested or discussed.

### **Arguments Pro and Con Acceptance**

**Arguments for acceptance:**
*   Presents a significant advance by successfully applying a policy-learning-from-optimization framework to a diverse set of complex 3D characters, a problem substantially harder than prior 2D work.
*   Includes a novel and valuable technical contribution for extracting well-conditioned feedback gains from direct trajectory optimization methods.
*   Provides strong empirical evidence, including thorough ablation studies, that validate the key components of the proposed method (joint optimization and noise injection).
*   The work addresses an important problem in control, robotics, and animation, and the results are likely to inspire and influence future research.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
*   The description of the real-time policy execution step is unclear and lacks detail, making it difficult to assess the claim of real-time performance.
*   The comparison with the key baseline of MPC is weak and largely qualitative.
*   The scope of "interactivity" demonstrated is narrow, potentially overstating the interactive capabilities of the resulting controllers.

### **Final Recommendation**

I recommend **acceptance** for this paper. The weaknesses, primarily related to clarity in the execution step and the depth of one baseline comparison, are outweighed by the paper's significant strengths. The successful application of a unified framework to generate high-quality locomotion for such a diverse set of 3D characters is a substantial contribution and advances the state of the art. The novel method for feedback gain extraction is also a noteworthy technical result. This work addresses a challenging problem with an effective and well-validated approach that will be of high interest to the NIPS community. The identified weaknesses can be addressed in a final version by expanding Sections 7 and 8.1.
