## NIPS Review: Interactive Control of Diverse Complex Characters with Neural Networks

**Overall Assessment:** This paper presents a compelling approach to learning robust and versatile control policies for complex articulated characters, bridging the gap between offline trajectory optimization and real-time interactive control. The combination of Contact-Invariant Optimization (CIO) for data generation with a neural network policy trained with noise injection is effective, demonstrating stable locomotion in 3D – a significant advance over prior work limited to 2D. While the paper is strong, particularly in its technical details and experimental results, a deeper discussion of limitations and a more thorough comparison to directly-trained neural network controllers would strengthen the contribution. I recommend **Accept with Minor Revisions**.

**1. Summary and Relation to Prior Work:**

The paper tackles a long-standing problem in robotics and animation: creating universal controllers for complex characters capable of handling diverse tasks and environments. Traditional methods rely heavily on manual design or task-specific solutions. The authors propose a novel pipeline where CIO is used to generate a diverse dataset of optimal trajectories, which are then used to train a neural network policy capable of real-time control. This builds upon recent work leveraging trajectory optimization for neural network training [9, 11], but importantly extends it to significantly more challenging 3D locomotion tasks. The authors correctly position their work within the broader landscape of control techniques, acknowledging the historical use of neural networks in control, the successes of Reinforcement Learning, and the limitations of traditional physics-based controllers relying on simplified models.  The emphasis on learning from optimized trajectories rather than direct RL or imitation learning is a key differentiator.

**2. Strengths:**

* **Technical Soundness:** The method is technically rigorous. The formulation of the constrained optimization problem, the use of stochastic policy training with noise injection (drawing inspiration from denoising autoencoders and robust control), and the distributed optimization architecture are all well-justified and clearly explained. The inclusion of optimal feedback gains calculated via a perturbation method is a clever addition for improving robustness.
* **Significant Results:**  Achieving stable and realistic 3D locomotion across diverse character morphologies is a substantial accomplishment.  Demonstrating the same pipeline’s applicability to swimming and flying characters further highlights its generality. The supplementary video convincingly showcases the quality of the learned behaviors.
* **Clarity of Explanation:** The paper is, for the most part, clearly written and well-organized. The mathematical notation is generally accessible, and the explanations of the key techniques (CIO, noise injection, distributed optimization) are concise and informative. 
* **Novelty:**  The combination of CIO for data generation, noise-based training, and a specific neural network architecture for simultaneous control and dynamics learning is a novel approach. Scaling this methodology to 3D locomotion represents a significant step forward.
* **Practical Implementation:** The paper clearly details the computational resources used (cloud computing, GPU implementation) and provides sufficient information for others to reproduce the results.

**3. Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Comparison to Direct Methods:**  While the authors acknowledge and briefly dismiss direct training methods (e.g., using RL), a more systematic and quantitative comparison would be valuable. Providing results from a baseline trained with a standard RL algorithm (e.g., PPO) on the same tasks would significantly strengthen the argument that the trajectory optimization-based approach yields more realistic and purposeful behaviors. The claim of "somewhat chaotic" behaviors from direct methods needs to be substantiated with data.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper lists the algorithmic constants used, which is good, but doesn't discuss the sensitivity of the method to these parameters.  A brief sensitivity analysis or a discussion of how these parameters were chosen would be helpful.
* **Generalization beyond Locomotion:** The experiments primarily focus on locomotion. While the authors mention other behaviors (getting up, climbing), these are not demonstrated. Expanding the experimental evaluation to include these other actions would further demonstrate the versatility of the approach.
* **Potential for Bias in Trajectory Generation:** The quality of the learned policy is inherently limited by the diversity and quality of the trajectories generated by CIO.  The paper could benefit from a discussion of potential biases in the generated data and how these biases might affect the resulting policy. 
* **Computational Cost:** While the authors acknowledge the high computational cost of trajectory generation, a more detailed breakdown of the cost (e.g., time per trajectory, total compute hours) would be helpful for readers considering applying this method.



**4. Arguments Pro and Con Acceptance:**

**Pro:**

* Solves a challenging problem with a novel and effective approach.
* Demonstrates significant results in 3D locomotion and generalizes to other character types.
* Technically sound and well-explained.
* Has the potential to impact the fields of robotics, animation, and biomechanics.

**Con:**

* Lacks a thorough comparative evaluation against direct training methods.
* Limited discussion of hyperparameter sensitivity and potential biases in data generation.
* Focuses primarily on locomotion, limiting the demonstration of versatility.




**5. Specific Suggestions/Questions for Revision:**

* **Direct Method Comparison:** Include a comparison with a standard RL algorithm (e.g., PPO) trained on the same locomotion tasks.  Report quantitative metrics (e.g., distance traveled, stability) and provide qualitative comparisons of the resulting behaviors.
* **Hyperparameter Analysis:**  Provide a brief discussion of the sensitivity of the method to the key hyperparameters (e.g., noise levels, regularization weights).
* **Expand Evaluation:** Include experiments demonstrating the learned policy’s ability to perform other behaviors beyond locomotion (e.g., getting up, climbing).
* **Data Diversity Discussion:** Discuss potential biases in the generated trajectories and how these might affect the resulting policy.
* **Computational Cost Details:**  Provide a more detailed breakdown of the computational cost associated with trajectory generation.




In conclusion, this is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of character control. Addressing the minor weaknesses outlined above would further strengthen the paper and solidify its position as a significant advance in the field.
