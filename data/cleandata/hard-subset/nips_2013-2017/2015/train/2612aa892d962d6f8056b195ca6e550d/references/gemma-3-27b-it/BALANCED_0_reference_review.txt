This paper presents a compelling approach to learning interactive controllers for complex characters by combining trajectory optimization (specifically, Contact-Invariant Optimization - CIO) with neural network policy learning. The core idea – leveraging the strengths of offline trajectory optimization to generate training data for a neural network capable of real-time control – is well-motivated and addresses a significant challenge in the field of character control. The authors successfully demonstrate this approach on a diverse range of characters, including swimming, flying, bipedal, and quadrupedal creatures, showcasing the generality of their method.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty and Significance:** The combination of CIO and neural networks for interactive control is a significant contribution.  Prior work often tackles either offline trajectory generation *or* online control with neural networks, but rarely both in a synergistic way, especially not at the scale and complexity presented here (3D characters). The demonstrated ability to create stable locomotion in 3D is a clear advance over prior 2D work [9, 11].
* **Technical Soundness:** The proposed method appears technically sound, with a clear articulation of the optimization problem and the use of established techniques like Newton's method for trajectory optimization and stochastic gradient descent for neural network training.  The inclusion of optimal feedback gains and noise injection are thoughtful additions that address common challenges in reinforcement learning and control.
* **Experimental Validation:** The authors provide a comprehensive experimental evaluation on a diverse set of characters. The results, supported by a supplementary video, convincingly demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach. The ablation studies (Figure 2) clearly show the benefits of joint optimization and noise injection.
* **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The introduction clearly outlines the problem and the proposed solution. The mathematical notation is reasonable, and the algorithm is presented in a concise and understandable manner.
* **Practicality:** The authors address the computational challenges of this approach by leveraging distributed computing and a GPU implementation. The observation that more resources are spent on data generation than network learning is insightful.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Comparative Analysis:** While the ablation studies are helpful, a more thorough comparison to other state-of-the-art methods (e.g., direct neural network training with more advanced techniques like PPO [17], or model-predictive control with different optimization strategies) is needed.  The authors mention an impression that direct methods yield chaotic behaviors (paragraph 4), but this needs to be substantiated with quantitative comparisons.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:**  The paper mentions specific values for algorithmic constants (σε, σγ, α, λ, η). However, the sensitivity of the method to these hyperparameters is not thoroughly investigated. A more robust analysis of hyperparameter tuning and its impact on performance would strengthen the paper.
* **Complexity of Implementation:** While the authors mention a custom GPU implementation, the complexity of setting up and running the distributed training architecture could be a barrier to adoption.  More details on the implementation aspects and potential tools for simplifying the process would be valuable.
* **Task Specificity of Costs:** The cost functions used in the trajectory optimization are relatively simple (reaching a goal and minimizing torque/force).  The impact of different cost functions on the resulting behaviors is not explored. A more systematic investigation of cost function design could lead to more nuanced and expressive control.
* **Limited Generalization Discussion:** While the method is shown to work across different morphologies, a discussion of the limits of generalization and potential failure cases would be beneficial.  For instance, how well would the learned policy transfer to a character with significantly different mass distribution or joint limits?



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper addresses a challenging problem with a novel and well-motivated approach.
* The experimental results are compelling and demonstrate the effectiveness of the method.
* The paper is well-written and organized.
* The work has the potential to significantly impact the field of character control and robotics.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The lack of a comprehensive comparison to state-of-the-art methods.
* The limited discussion of hyperparameter sensitivity and implementation complexity.
* The reliance on relatively simple cost functions.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of character control. The combination of trajectory optimization and neural networks is a promising approach that enables the creation of realistic and interactive controllers. While some areas could be strengthened with further investigation (particularly comparative analysis and hyperparameter sensitivity), the overall quality of the work justifies acceptance.

**Recommendation:**

**Accept** (with minor revisions addressing the weaknesses mentioned above. Specifically, adding a more detailed comparison to relevant baselines and providing more insight into hyperparameter tuning would be valuable.)
