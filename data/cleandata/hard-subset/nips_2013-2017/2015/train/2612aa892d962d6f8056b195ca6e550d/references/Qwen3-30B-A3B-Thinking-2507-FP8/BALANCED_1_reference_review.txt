

**Review of "Interactive Control of Diverse Complex Characters with Neural Networks"**  

**Summary**  
This paper proposes a novel method for generating neural network controllers that enable interactive, real-time control of diverse 3D characters (swimmers, flyers, bipeds, quadrupeds) via a joint optimization of trajectory generation and policy learning. It extends prior work on Contact-Invariant Optimization (CIO) [12] and trajectory-optimization-based neural control [9, 11] by introducing stochastic noise injection, distributed optimization, and a perturbation method for feedback gains. Unlike prior 2D locomotion work [9, 11], the approach achieves stable 3D behaviors without task-specific handcrafting, addressing a key challenge in character animation and robotics. The method contrasts with RL-based approaches [3, 6, 10] by leveraging offline trajectory optimization to generate high-quality training data, mitigating data scarcity and instability issues.  

**Strengths**  
1. **Technical Quality & Innovation**: The core contribution—joint optimization of trajectories and neural policies with principled noise injection—solves a critical gap in real-time character control. The perturbation method for feedback gains (Section 3.2) is a clever, efficient adaptation of CIO that avoids LQG’s pitfalls, validated via ablation studies (Section 8.1). The method achieves stable 3D locomotion across diverse morphologies (swimming, flying, bipedal, quadrupedal) with a single network architecture, a significant advancement over prior work [9, 11, 12].  
2. **Rigorous Evaluation**: Ablation studies convincingly demonstrate the necessity of joint optimization (vs. static training) and noise injection (vs. no noise), showing two orders of magnitude better performance. The comparison to MPC (Section 8.1) highlights how the method regularizes trajectory optimization toward cyclic, stable behaviors.  
3. **Clarity & Reproducibility**: The algorithm (Algorithm 1) and experimental setup are well-described. The distributed training architecture (Section 6) and implementation details (e.g., MuJoCo, AWS) provide sufficient clarity for replication. The results (Section 8) are compelling, with diverse behaviors demonstrated in the supplementary video.  

**Weaknesses**  
1. **Limited Baseline Comparison**: The paper lacks comparison to modern RL baselines (e.g., SAC [14], PPO [15]) or state-of-the-art trajectory-optimization methods (e.g., [22]). While RL is noted as a "more direct" alternative (Section 1), the claim that "networks trained with direct methods give chaotic behaviors" is unsubstantiated without empirical comparison. This weakens the significance argument.  
2. **Real-Time Performance Ambiguity**: The paper claims "real-time" control (Section 7) but does not specify frame rates or computational overhead (e.g., 50ms timesteps imply 20Hz, which is slow for interactive applications). A quantitative comparison of inference speed vs. MPC or RL methods would strengthen this claim.  
3. **Scalability and Failure Modes**: The method requires 2.5 hours of cloud training per character (Section 6), limiting accessibility. No analysis of failure cases (e.g., character falling during rollout) or robustness to large perturbations is provided, despite the paper’s focus on stability.  
4. **Theoretical Gaps**: The noise injection mechanism (Section 2.1) relies on linearized feedback gains, but the stability guarantees for the full closed-loop system are not formally proven. The perturbation method (Section 3.2) is intuitive but lacks convergence analysis.  

**Significance and Originality**  
The work is highly original in applying CIO to 3D interactive control with neural networks, moving beyond 2D limitations [9, 11]. It bridges trajectory optimization and neural policy learning in a way that enables diverse behaviors without task-specific tuning—a significant step toward unified character control. However, the significance is partially offset by the absence of RL comparisons, which are now standard for continuous control problems. The approach’s applicability to robotics (Section 9) is promising but remains speculative without hardware validation.  

**Recommendation**  
**Reject**. While the technical contribution is strong and the results impressive, the lack of comparison to modern RL baselines (a critical omission for NIPS) and unresolved scalability/real-time performance questions prevent acceptance. The paper makes a valuable incremental advance but fails to position itself against the current state of the art in continuous control.  

**Constructive Suggestions for Improvement**  
1. **Add RL Baselines**: Compare against SAC or PPO on the same locomotion tasks to validate claims about "chaotic behaviors" in direct RL methods. Quantify real-time performance (e.g., inference FPS) and training time vs. RL.  
2. **Clarify Real-Time Claims**: Report frame rates during execution and discuss trade-offs between timestep size (50ms) and control quality.  
3. **Address Failure Modes**: Include analysis of edge cases (e.g., character falling) and robustness to large sensor noise or model mismatch.  
4. **Strengthen Theoretical Justification**: Provide a stability proof for the closed-loop system or discuss limitations of the linearized feedback assumption.  
5. **Improve Accessibility**: Discuss strategies to reduce training time (e.g., transfer learning across similar characters) or explore smaller network architectures for edge deployment.  

**Conclusion**  
This work presents a technically sound and innovative approach to neural control for diverse 3D characters, with compelling empirical results. However, the absence of key comparisons to modern RL methods and unresolved questions about scalability/performance prevent it from meeting NIPS’ bar for significance. With the suggested revisions, particularly adding RL baselines and clarifying real-time capabilities, the paper would be a strong candidate for acceptance. As-is, it represents a solid incremental contribution but does not advance the state of the art sufficiently for NIPS.
