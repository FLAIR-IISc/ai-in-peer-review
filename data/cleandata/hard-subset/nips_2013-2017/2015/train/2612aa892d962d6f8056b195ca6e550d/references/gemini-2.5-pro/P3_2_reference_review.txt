### **Summary**

This paper presents a method for creating interactive, real-time neural network controllers for complex, physically-simulated 3D characters. The core idea is to leverage the power of an offline, non-linear trajectory optimizer (Contact-Invariant Optimization, CIO) to generate a rich dataset of expert behaviors, and then train a neural network policy to mimic the optimizer's outputs in real-time. The key contribution is a joint, alternating optimization framework where the trajectories and the policy are iteratively refined together. This co-adaptation, combined with a novel method for extracting local feedback gains from the direct trajectory optimizer and principled noise injection, enables the learning of robust policies that can control a wide variety of morphologies (bipeds, quadrupeds, swimmers, flyers) using a single, unified algorithm. The work builds upon previous research combining trajectory optimization and policy learning [9, 11], but demonstrates a significant step forward by successfully applying these ideas to complex, unstable 3D locomotion tasks, which are substantially more challenging than the 2D examples shown in prior work.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Impressive and General Results (Significance):** The most compelling aspect of this paper is the sheer quality and diversity of the resulting behaviors, as demonstrated in the supplementary video. Successfully training stable locomotion for 3D bipeds, quadrupeds, swimmers, and flyers with the same underlying algorithm is a major achievement. It strongly supports the authors' claim of developing a more "universal synthesis method" that moves beyond character-specific, hand-engineered controllers. The emergence of natural gaits (e.g., walking, trotting) without explicit encoding is a powerful demonstration of the method's effectiveness.

2.  **Principled Technical Approach (Quality):** The methodology is technically sound and combines several clever ideas.
    *   The joint optimization framework (Algorithm 1) is a significant improvement over a naive two-stage approach (optimize trajectories, then train network). The ablation study in Figure 2 ("no joint") provides clear empirical evidence that this co-adaptation is crucial for achieving good test-time performance.
    *   The method for extracting optimal feedback gains from a direct trajectory optimizer (Section 3.2) is a novel and valuable contribution. This allows the system to generate corrective targets for noisy inputs (`a + a_sε`), which is critical for learning a robust feedback policy, not just an open-loop trajectory follower. The use of the Woodbury identity to efficiently compute these gains is elegant.
    *   The use of noise at both the sensory input and hidden unit levels is well-motivated by prior work and shown to be essential for avoiding overfitting and divergence (Figure 2, "no noise").

3.  **Thorough Empirical Validation:** The authors provide strong evidence for their design choices through a series of ablation studies. The comparisons against training on a static dataset ("no joint") and training without noise ("no noise") clearly isolate and justify the importance of the core components of their proposed algorithm. The comparison of different network architectures (Table 1) is also helpful.

### **Weaknesses and Questions for Authors**

1.  **Clarity on Key Details (Clarity & Reproducibility):** While the high-level overview is clear, several crucial details of the implementation are either ambiguous or omitted, which hinders reproducibility.
    *   **Recurrent State `r`:** The recurrent state `r` is introduced as part of the system state and the network's output `ṙ_t`, but its role, dimensionality, and update mechanism are never explained. Is it simply the hidden state of a recurrent neural network? If so, the network architecture described (3 hidden layers) seems to be a standard feedforward MLP. If not, how is this memory state integrated and what purpose does it serve?
    *   **Action Space `a_t`:** The action `a_t = [q̇_t, ṙ_t, f_t]` contains both desired velocities (`q̇_t`) and desired contact forces (`f_t`). In the real-time execution step (Section 7), the system optimizes for the next state `x_1` to match these targets, subject to physics constraints. This seems potentially over-determined. How are conflicts between matching a desired velocity and applying a desired force resolved? A more detailed explanation of the optimization in Section 7 is needed.
    *   **Cost Function `C(X)`:** The success of trajectory optimization is highly dependent on the cost function design. The paper vaguely states it involves reaching a goal, minimizing torques, and minimizing contact forces. More specific details on the formulation and relative weighting of these terms would be invaluable for understanding the results and for others seeking to build on this work.

2.  **Comparison to Related Work:** The connection to the Guided Policy Search (GPS) family of algorithms [e.g., Levine & Abbeel, NIPS 2014] could be made more explicit. The presented algorithm can be viewed as a sophisticated instance of GPS, using CIO as the trajectory optimizer. Situating the work more clearly within this context would help readers understand its specific contributions (e.g., the feedback gain extraction method, application to 3D contact-rich dynamics) relative to the broader GPS framework.

3.  **Limited Scope of "Interaction":** The interaction is currently limited to updating the target goal position. This is a valid and useful form of interaction, but it would be beneficial to discuss the potential limitations. How would the learned policy respond to significant external perturbations (e.g., a large push) that might knock the character into a state region far from any of the training trajectories? Does the policy generalize to recovery behaviors, or is it brittle outside its training distribution?

### **Evaluation against NIPS Criteria**

*   **Quality:** High. The paper is technically solid, and its claims are well-supported by strong empirical results and insightful ablation studies. The work appears complete and carefully executed.
*   **Clarity:** Good, but with room for improvement. The overall method is well-described, but as noted above, key details necessary for full understanding and reproducibility are missing. The paper is well-organized.
*   **Originality:** Good. While it builds on a known paradigm (learning from an optimizer), it introduces novel components (feedback gain extraction from direct optimization) and combines existing ideas in a new way. The primary originality lies in successfully scaling this approach to a much more difficult and previously unsolved problem domain (diverse 3D locomotion).
*   **Significance:** High. This work represents a demonstrable advance in the state of the art for automated character controller synthesis. The ability to generate high-quality, real-time controllers for a diverse set of complex characters from a unified framework is a significant result that is likely to influence future work in computer animation, robotics, and reinforcement learning.

### **Final Recommendation**

This is a strong paper with impressive results and solid technical contributions. It successfully tackles a very challenging problem in a general way. The weaknesses are primarily related to clarity and could be addressed in a revised version. I recommend acceptance.
