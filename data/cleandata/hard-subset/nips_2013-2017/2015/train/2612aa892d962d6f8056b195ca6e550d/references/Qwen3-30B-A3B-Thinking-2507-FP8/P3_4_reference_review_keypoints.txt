- Presents a joint optimization of trajectory generation and neural-network policy learning for complex 3D character motion using Contact-Invariant Optimization and distributed stochastic optimization with noise injection.  
- Demonstrates controllers for diverse 3D morphologies (biped, quadruped, swimmer, flyer) and claims substantial advance over prior 2D work.

- Strong technical contribution: extends trajectory-optimization + neural network approach to challenging 3D locomotion with a well-designed distributed stochastic optimization framework.  
- Thorough evaluation: comprehensive ablation studies convincingly show the necessity of joint optimization and noise injection.  
- Generalizability: single network architecture handles diverse character morphologies with visually compelling results.  
- Theoretical contribution: provides a solid derivation for optimal feedback gains addressing prior limitations.

- Limited novelty: core idea (trajectory optimization to generate training data) is not new and paper doesn’t clearly distinguish innovations from prior work.  
- Missing comparisons to modern RL methods (e.g., SAC, PPO), which could achieve similar results with different trade-offs.  
- High computational cost: requires hours of cloud compute for data generation, limiting practical applicability and robotics transfer.  
- Narrow task scope: experiments focus only on locomotion despite broader claims of handling arbitrary behaviors.  
- Lacks detailed quantitative metrics (e.g., success rate, energy efficiency, stability) to objectively evaluate performance.

- Methodology and math are sound; noise injection and distributed optimization are well-motivated.  
- Ablations show superiority over naive training but do not fully explain why joint optimization outperforms static-trajectory training.  
- Sensory representation is simple (st = [qt rt q̇t-1 ft-1]) but not justified or compared to alternatives.

- Significant for animation/character control but limited impact for broader ML due to reliance on trajectory optimization rather than end-to-end learning.  
- Paper does not convincingly argue why this approach is superior to modern reinforcement learning for control.

- Overall recommendation: Accept with minor revisions / borderline accept.  
- Key revisions requested: compare to recent RL methods (SAC/PPO), add quantitative performance metrics, justify sensory representation, and expand discussion of limitations (computational cost and robotics applicability).
