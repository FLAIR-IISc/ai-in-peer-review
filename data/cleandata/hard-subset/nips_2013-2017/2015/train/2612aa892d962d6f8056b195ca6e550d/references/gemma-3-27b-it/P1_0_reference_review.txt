## NIPS Review: Interactive Control of Diverse Complex Characters with Neural Networks

**Summary:**

This paper presents a method for learning neural network controllers for complex, physically simulated characters. The core idea is to combine trajectory optimization (specifically Contact-Invariant Optimization - CIO) with supervised learning.  CIO is used to generate training data consisting of optimal trajectories, and a neural network is then trained to mimic the control policies derived from these trajectories. Crucially, the authors address the challenges of training in high-dimensional, continuous spaces by injecting noise into the sensory inputs and using a distributed optimization scheme. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach on a diverse range of characters – swimming, flying, bipedal, and quadrupedal – achieving stable and realistic locomotion, a feat previously difficult to attain in 3D with similar techniques. The work aims to unify disparate control architectures used for different character types (biped vs. quadruped, etc.).

**Relation to Previous Work:**

The paper correctly positions itself within the broader landscape of control and reinforcement learning. It acknowledges the historical use of neural networks for control and the recent advances in deep learning, but emphasizes the difficulty of applying these to complex physical systems. The authors appropriately cite relevant work in trajectory optimization [12], neural network control [3, 6, 10, 11, 14], and related areas like optimal control and robust control [5, 6, 20]. They also acknowledge and differentiate their work from recent approaches that combine trajectory optimization and neural networks [9, 11], highlighting their ability to handle more complex 3D locomotion problems. The acknowledgement of the need to create training data, as opposed to relying on large datasets, is also important.

**Strengths:**

* **Scalability to 3D:** This is arguably the most significant contribution. Previous work in this area largely focused on 2D characters. The authors successfully demonstrate stable and realistic 3D locomotion, a significant step forward.
* **Unified Approach:** The goal of a single control architecture for diverse character morphologies is ambitious and potentially very valuable. The initial results, showing the same network architecture working for swimming, flying, and walking characters, are promising.
* **Principled Noise Injection:** The use of noise injection, guided by optimal feedback gains calculated during trajectory optimization, is a clever and effective way to improve robustness and generalization.  It directly addresses the challenges of closed-loop control.
* **Distributed Optimization:**  The distributed training architecture is well-justified and allows for efficient scaling of the computationally intensive training process.
* **Thorough Ablation Study:** The inclusion of an ablation study (section 8.1) comparing the full method to naive approaches and noise-free training is strong evidence for the effectiveness of the proposed techniques.
* **Clear Writing & Organization:**  The paper is generally well-written and logically organized, making it relatively easy to follow the complex methodology.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Comparative Evaluation:** While the ablation study is valuable, a more systematic comparison with other control methods (e.g., direct reinforcement learning methods like DDPG or PPO) would strengthen the paper. The claim that direct methods yield "somewhat chaotic behaviors" needs to be substantiated with quantitative comparisons.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper mentions algorithmic constants used in the experiments, but a discussion of sensitivity to these parameters would be beneficial. How robust is the method to changes in noise levels (σe, σγ) or the penalty weight (α)?
* **Computational Cost:** The paper acknowledges the significant computational cost of trajectory optimization, noting it outweighs the cost of neural network training. This is a practical limitation that should be discussed more thoroughly. While the distributed architecture mitigates this, it may still be prohibitive for some users.
* **Lack of Generalization Beyond Simulated Characters:** The results are demonstrated in simulation.  While the physics-based approach is a strength, the question of transferring these policies to real robots or characters remains open.



**Arguments For/Against Acceptance:**

**For Acceptance:**

* Solves a challenging problem in character control, achieving significant results in 3D locomotion.
* Presents a novel and effective combination of trajectory optimization and neural networks.
* Demonstrates a promising unified control architecture for diverse character morphologies.
* Includes a strong ablation study supporting the effectiveness of the proposed techniques.

**Against Acceptance:**

* Limited comparative evaluation against other control methods.
* Lack of discussion regarding hyperparameter sensitivity.
*  High computational cost could limit practical applicability.
* Simulation-based results; generalization to real-world scenarios is unclear.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that presents a significant contribution to the field of character control. The authors tackle a challenging problem and propose a novel and effective solution.  The successful application to diverse character types and the scalability to 3D are particularly impressive. While there are some areas for improvement, particularly regarding comparative evaluation and computational cost, the strengths of the paper outweigh the weaknesses.

**Recommendation:**

**Accept** –  I recommend acceptance with minor revisions. I suggest the authors address the weaknesses mentioned above by including a more comprehensive comparison to other control methods, discussing hyperparameter sensitivity, and elaborating on the computational cost and potential mitigation strategies.
