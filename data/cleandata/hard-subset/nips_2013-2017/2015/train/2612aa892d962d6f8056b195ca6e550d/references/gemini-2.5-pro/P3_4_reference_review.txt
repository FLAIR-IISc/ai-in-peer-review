**Summary and Relation to Prior Work**

This paper presents a method for creating interactive, real-time controllers for a diverse range of complex, physically-simulated 3D characters. The core idea is to combine the strengths of offline trajectory optimization, which can produce high-quality, physically realistic motions, with the speed of a neural network policy, which can run in real-time. The authors formulate this as a joint optimization problem where a set of optimal trajectories and the neural network policy parameters are optimized concurrently. This extends a line of research that learns neural network policies from trajectory optimizers (e.g., Levine & Koltun, 2014; Mordatch & Todorov, 2014), which themselves can be seen as a form of imitation learning or policy distillation where the "expert" is an optimizer rather than a human demonstrator.

The key contributions that distinguish this work are its successful application to challenging 3D locomotion tasks (walking, swimming, flying) and the specific techniques enabling this scalability. These include: 1) a principled method for injecting noise into the learning process by training the network to predict the optimally-corrected action for a perturbed state, which requires computing optimal feedback gains from the direct trajectory optimizer; 2) a novel method for extracting these feedback gains from the Contact-Invariant Optimization (CIO) framework; and 3) a distributed, asynchronous optimization scheme that makes the computationally intensive training process tractable.

**Strengths**

1.  **Significance and Impressive Results:** The primary strength of this paper lies in its results. The ability to use a single, unified algorithm to generate stable, interactive controllers for creatures as diverse as a biped, a quadruped, a swimmer, and a bird is a significant achievement. The emergent gaits (e.g., cyclic walking, trotting) are natural-looking and task-oriented without being explicitly programmed, demonstrating the power of the framework. As noted by the authors, scaling such methods to 3D is a substantial leap in complexity over prior 2D work, and the success here advances the state of the art in character animation and control.

2.  **Novelty and Technical Quality:** The methodology contains several clever and well-motivated components.
    *   The joint optimization process (Algorithm 1) is more powerful than a simple two-stage "generate data, then regress" approach. By iteratively refining the trajectories to be both optimal for the task *and* learnable by the current policy, the system avoids generating expert behaviors that are too complex or chaotic for the network to capture.
    *   The stochastic training procedure in Section 2.1 is particularly elegant. Instead of simply training on noisy inputs `s+ε` with the original output `a`, the network is trained to predict the linearly-corrected optimal action `a + a_sε`. This explicitly teaches the policy local robustness and how to recover from perturbations, which is critical for closed-loop stability.
    *   The proposed method for extracting feedback gains `a_s` from a direct trajectory optimizer (Section 3.2) is a valuable technical contribution in its own right. It cleverly reuses the computed Hessian `CXX` and provides an intuitive tuning parameter `λ` to control the "softness" of the feedback, which is a common issue with standard LQR-based approaches.

3.  **Thorough Experimental Validation:** The ablation studies in Section 8.1 provide strong evidence for the authors' design choices. The "no joint" experiment demonstrates that the iterative co-adaptation of trajectories and the policy is crucial, and the "no noise" experiment confirms that the principled noise injection is vital for generalization and preventing catastrophic overfitting. The comparison with MPC, showing that the proposed method encourages more natural, cyclic behaviors, is also insightful and highlights a key advantage of regularizing trajectories through a learnable policy.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement**

1.  **Clarity on Policy Execution:** Section 7, "Policy Execution," is somewhat confusing and potentially contradicts the motivation for using a fast neural network policy. The paper states that at each timestep, they solve a T=1 optimization problem subject to the full dynamics constraints (6) to match the desired velocities from the policy. This sounds like model-predictive control with a horizon of one, where the NN provides the cost function target. If this is the case, it is not simply "querying the policy." This is a critical detail that needs clarification. Is the real-time rate achieved because this T=1 optimization is extremely fast? Or does it undermine the claim that the NN is the sole real-time controller? This should be explained more clearly, as it has significant implications for the method's practical application and comparison to other approaches.

2.  **Lack of Comparison to Modern RL Baselines:** The introduction dismisses direct reinforcement learning methods by stating an "impression" that they produce "somewhat chaotic behaviors." While this may have been true of older methods, recent deep RL algorithms like Trust Region Policy Optimization (TRPO) or Proximal Policy Optimization (PPO) have demonstrated remarkable success in continuous control for 3D locomotion. A significant weakness of this paper is the absence of any quantitative or even qualitative comparison to a state-of-the-art RL baseline on at least one of the characters (e.g., the biped). Such a comparison would be essential to substantiate the claims about superior realism and truly position this work within the current landscape of control literature.

3.  **Details on Trajectory Generation:** The trajectory generation process in Section 5, while clever, could be detailed further. How is the random timestep `t` for branching chosen? How is the new goal location `gk,1` selected to ensure good coverage of the state space without being destructively difficult? Understanding the heuristics here is important for reproducibility and for assessing how much manual tuning is required to generate a good training dataset for a new character or task.

**Overall Assessment**

This is a strong paper presenting a well-engineered and effective system for a challenging problem. The combination of ideas—joint optimization, principled stochastic training using feedback gains, and a practical method for extracting those gains—is novel and leads to impressive, state-of-the-art results in interactive character control. The weaknesses, particularly the lack of comparison to modern RL baselines and the ambiguity in the policy execution step, are notable but do not fundamentally undermine the paper's core contributions. The work is technically sound, significant, and likely to be influential in both the graphics and machine learning communities.

**Recommendation:** Accept. I strongly encourage the authors to clarify the policy execution mechanism and to acknowledge, if not include, a more direct comparison to contemporary deep RL methods in the final version.
