This paper tackles a long-standing challenge in robotics and animation: creating universal controllers for complex characters that are both stable and reactive. The core idea – leveraging trajectory optimization to *teach* a neural network a control policy – is compelling and, frankly, quite bold. While previous work has explored similar avenues, this paper demonstrably scales to 3D locomotion, a significant hurdle. The authors convincingly argue for the benefits of this approach over direct neural network training, suggesting it produces more “realistic and purposeful” behaviors. The distributed training architecture is a practical necessity for this scale of problem and well-described.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The combination of trajectory optimization and neural network learning, while conceptually related to existing work, achieves a notable performance leap in 3D character control. The ability to generate controllers for diverse morphologies (swimming, flying, bipedal, quadrupedal) with a single architecture is a significant achievement.
*   **Significance:** A universal control framework would be transformative for robotics, animation, and biomechanics. While this work is not a complete solution, it represents a substantial step towards that goal. The demonstration of stable locomotion in complex 3D environments is impressive.
*   **Technical Soundness:** The methodology – CIO for trajectory generation, careful noise injection, and distributed training – appears well-reasoned and implemented. The ablation studies provide support for the key design choices.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and the core concepts are explained reasonably clearly. The organization is logical, progressing from motivation to methodology to results.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Limited Comparative Analysis:** While the ablation studies demonstrate the importance of joint optimization and noise injection, a more comprehensive comparison to other control methods (e.g., MPC, purely data-driven RL) would strengthen the paper. The statement that direct methods yield "chaotic" behaviors is somewhat vague and could benefit from quantitative support.
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper lists the hyperparameters used, but doesn't discuss sensitivity to these parameters. It’s unclear how robust the approach is to changes in noise levels, regularization weights, or network architecture.
*   **Cost Function Engineering:** The success of the method relies heavily on the design of the cost function in trajectory optimization. The paper doesn’t delve deeply into the challenges of cost function engineering for different character types or tasks.
*   **Generalization beyond Locomotion:** The results primarily focus on locomotion. While the authors demonstrate control over diverse morphologies, it's unclear how well the approach generalizes to more complex behaviors (e.g., manipulation, interaction with objects).



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

*   The paper addresses a fundamentally important problem with a novel and promising approach.
*   The results demonstrate a significant advance in 3D character control, exceeding the capabilities of previous methods.
*   The methodology is technically sound and well-motivated.
*   The potential impact of this work is high.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The comparative analysis is limited, making it difficult to fully assess the advantages of the proposed method.
*   The paper lacks a thorough exploration of hyperparameter sensitivity.
*   The generality of the approach beyond locomotion is unclear.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with a compelling idea and impressive results. While not perfect, the novelty and potential impact of the work outweigh the minor weaknesses. The authors have successfully tackled a challenging problem and made a significant contribution to the field. I believe this paper is worthy of acceptance, despite some areas for further investigation. The leap to 3D locomotion is substantial and justifies overlooking some incomplete validations. I would encourage the authors to address the comparative analysis and hyperparameter sensitivity in future work.



**Recommendation:** Accept.
