- Proposes an alternating distributed framework combining Contact-Invariant Optimization (CIO) trajectory optimization with supervised neural policy learning for real-time 3D controllers.
- Uses optimizer-generated trajectories and feedback gains to train a noise-perturbed policy with first-order corrected targets a + As ε.
- Computes feedback gains efficiently from the CIO Gauss-Newton system via a soft-constraint perturbation and Woodbury reuse.
- Trains in a distributed fashion (many CPU nodes for optimization, single GPU for regression) with proximal terms to stabilize asynchronous updates.
- At runtime the policy outputs desired velocities and contact forces, with a short-horizon (T=1) inverse-dynamics-like optimization step for stable 50 ms control.
- Builds on guided policy search and trajectory-optimization policy learning, contrasting with model-free RL approaches.
- Novel contributions: robust multi-morphology 3D locomotion without hand-designed controllers, principled noise-injection with first-order targets, efficient feedback-gain computation for direct methods, and an asynchronous proximal alternating scheme.
- Algorithm design is sensible and well-motivated; feedback-gain derivation is a neat, practical contribution.
- Empirical demonstrations show diverse 3D behaviors and useful ablations (static vs alternation, noise vs no-noise, network size).
- Quantitative evaluation is limited: heavy reliance on qualitative videos and few under-described plots.
- Lacks rigorous comparisons to strong baselines (MPC, GPS/TRPO/DDPG) and missing metrics like success rate, energy, contact satisfaction, and robustness.
- Important implementation details are under-specified: exact cost weights, use of memory r, contact-force regularization, and goal-branching distribution.
- Soft dynamics constraints and allowed root forces raise concerns about physical realism and should be measured (penetrations/root-force magnitudes).
- Distributed training and reported wall-clock times (~2.5 hours on ~30 nodes) are pragmatic and convincing for scalability.
- Writing and organization are generally clear; notation consistency and clearer exposition of memory r are needed.
- Originality is moderate: paradigm is known, but feedback-gain computation and first-order noise-corrected targets are novel and valuable.
- High potential impact for animation and model-based control if empirical claims are substantiated and reproducible.
- Strengths: elegant CIO–NN integration, practical systems choices, diverse emergent behaviors without mocap, and efficient feedback-gain method.
- Weaknesses: limited quantitative evidence, weak baselines, physical-fidelity ambiguities, insufficient ablations and sensitivity analysis, and no convergence guarantees for alternating scheme.
- Suggested improvements: add quantitative metrics (success, energy, constraint violations), compare to strong baselines (MPC, GPS/TRPO), test robustness to unseen perturbations, and ablate memory r and key hyperparameters.
- Also suggested: clarify execution-time force feasibility, report root-force/penetration statistics, and provide full cost/weight details for reproducibility.
- Acceptance case: conceptually clean, practical, and potentially influential with a novel feedback-gain result and broad 3D demonstrations.
- Rejection case: insufficient quantitative validation, unclear physical fidelity, and key methodological/evaluation details missing.
- Reviewer recommendation: borderline to weak accept; stronger quantitative evaluation and clearer analysis needed to fully justify acceptance.
