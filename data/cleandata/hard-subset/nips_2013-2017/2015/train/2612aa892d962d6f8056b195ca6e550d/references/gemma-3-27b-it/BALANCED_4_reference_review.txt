This paper presents a novel approach to learning interactive controllers for complex characters by combining trajectory optimization with neural networks. The core idea is to use trajectory optimization (specifically Contact-Invariant Optimization) to generate training data, and then train a neural network to mimic the resulting control policy. This allows for real-time control, a significant advantage over purely trajectory optimization-based methods. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach on a variety of characters, including swimming, flying, bipedal, and quadrupedal creatures, showing impressive results in terms of stability and realistic locomotion.

**Strengths:**

* **Addresses an Important Problem:** Interactive control of complex characters is a challenging problem with applications in robotics, animation, and biomechanics. This work directly tackles this challenge with a well-motivated approach.
* **Novel Combination of Techniques:** The combination of trajectory optimization and neural networks is a compelling idea that leverages the strengths of both approaches. The authors effectively bridge the gap between offline planning and online control.
* **Strong Empirical Results:** The results presented are impressive, demonstrating the ability to learn stable and realistic locomotion for a diverse range of characters in 3D environments. This is a significant achievement, particularly compared to prior work that was limited to 2D.  The supplementary video is crucial for appreciating the quality of the learned behaviors.
* **Technical Soundness:** The proposed algorithm is well-defined and appears to be technically sound. The use of noise injection and the careful handling of optimal feedback gains are insightful details.  The distributed optimization architecture is practical and well-explained.
* **Thorough Ablation Studies:** The inclusion of ablation studies (Section 8.1) provides valuable insight into the contribution of different components of the proposed method.
* **Clear Writing:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the key concepts and technical details.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Comparison to Direct Methods:** While the authors acknowledge and briefly discuss direct methods for training neural network controllers (e.g., reinforcement learning), a more systematic comparison would strengthen the paper. A quantitative comparison, even on a limited set of tasks, would be valuable.  The claim that direct methods yield "somewhat chaotic behaviors" feels unsubstantiated without a more rigorous evaluation.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The number of algorithmic constants presented (σε, σγ, α, λ, η) suggests potential sensitivity to hyperparameters. A discussion of how these parameters were tuned and their impact on performance is missing. The reliance on specific values could hinder reproducibility.
* **Computational Cost:** While the authors acknowledge the significant computational cost (hours of training, more resources spent on data generation), a more detailed breakdown of the computational resources required would be helpful. This is particularly relevant for researchers who may not have access to cloud computing infrastructure.
* **Cost Function Details:** The specific form of the task costs mentioned in section 8 could be expanded upon. While they state these costs are the same for all experiments, providing more detail would assist in reproducibility and understanding the driving forces behind the observed behaviors.
* **Lack of Generalization Analysis:** The paper demonstrates success on a variety of characters, but it doesn’t explicitly address the generalization ability of the learned policies to unseen characters or environments. Testing the robustness of the controllers to perturbations or changes in physical parameters would be valuable.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and effective approach to interactive control of complex characters.
* The empirical results are strong and demonstrate a significant advancement over prior work.
* The technical details are well-explained, and the algorithm is sound.
* The ablation studies provide valuable insights into the importance of different components of the method.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

* The lack of a thorough comparison to direct methods weakens the claim of superiority.
* The potential hyperparameter sensitivity and computational cost could be barriers to adoption.
* Limited analysis of generalization ability.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of character control. The combination of trajectory optimization and neural networks is a clever idea, and the empirical results are compelling. While the paper has some minor weaknesses, the strengths outweigh them. 

**Recommendation:**

**Accept with minor revisions.** I recommend accepting the paper, contingent on the authors addressing the following:

1. **Provide a more quantitative comparison to direct methods for training neural network controllers.** Include results on a common benchmark task, even if it’s a simplified version of the problems presented in the paper.
2. **Discuss the hyperparameter tuning process and provide some guidance on choosing appropriate values for the algorithmic constants.**
3. **Expand on the details of the task costs used in the trajectory optimization.**
4. **Include a brief discussion of the limitations of the approach and potential avenues for future research, particularly regarding generalization ability.**
