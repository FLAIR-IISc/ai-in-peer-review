**Summary**

This paper presents a method for training neural network control policies for complex, physically-simulated 3D characters. The core idea is to leverage an offline trajectory optimization method, Contact-Invariant Optimization (CIO), to generate a dataset of expert trajectories. This data is then used to train a neural network policy in a joint optimization framework, where the trajectories and the network are refined together. The authors introduce several technical contributions to make this feasible for challenging 3D locomotion tasks, including a method for injecting noise during training by approximating optimal feedback gains from the direct trajectory optimizer, and a distributed, asynchronous training architecture. The method is demonstrated on a diverse set of characters, including bipeds, quadrupeds, a swimmer, and a flier, successfully generating stable and plausible gaits without task-specific engineering. This work builds upon previous research combining trajectory optimization and function approximation (e.g., Levine & Koltun, 2014; Mordatch & Todorov, 2014) but extends it to more complex 3D characters and introduces a more integrated, stochastic optimization procedure.

**Strengths**

1.  **Significance and Ambition:** The paper tackles a highly challenging and important problem: the automatic synthesis of general-purpose, real-time controllers for physically-based characters. The goal of a unified method that works across disparate morphologies (walking, flying, swimming) is ambitious and, if successful, would be a significant contribution to control, robotics, and animation.

2.  **Impressive Empirical Results:** The primary strength of the paper lies in its results. Demonstrating that a single algorithmic framework can produce stable, cyclic gaits for bipeds, a trot for quadrupeds, and plausible flapping/paddling motions for flying/swimming characters is a powerful proof of concept. The emergence of these natural behaviors without being explicitly programmed is a notable outcome. The supplemental video effectively showcases the quality and diversity of the learned controllers.

3.  **Novel Technical Components:** The proposed method for computing "softer" optimal feedback gains (Section 3.2) from a direct trajectory optimization method is a clever and potentially useful technique in its own right. The joint optimization framework, which alternates between refining trajectories and the policy, is well-motivated, and the ablation study (Figure 2) convincingly demonstrates its superiority over a naive "train-on-static-data" approach.

**Weaknesses**

Despite the promising results, the paper is marred by a substantial number of issues related to clarity, methodological rigor, and presentation, which collectively undermine its quality.

1.  **Lack of Clarity and Precision:** The manuscript suffers from numerous notational inconsistencies and ambiguities that make it difficult to fully understand and reproduce the method.
    *   In Section 2, the policy mapping is written as "s 7→ a" instead of "s → a".
    *   The definition of action `at` includes `f t`, while the state `st` includes `f t-1`. However, the state definition is `[q f r]`, which seems to refer to a single time step. This is confusing. The text should clarify if `f` in the overall state definition refers to `f_t` or `f_{t-1}`.
    *   The use of `d(q)>f = 0` (Section 3) is unconventional; standard notation would be `d(q)ᵀf = 0`.
    *   The Newton update in Eq. (8) is confusingly written as `X∗ = X∗ − ...`. This should be an iterative update, e.g., `X_{k+1} = X_k − ...`. Using `X*` to denote the variable being updated is misleading.
    *   Algorithm 1 is presented with incorrect notation for the proximal terms. The update for `X̄i` includes `∥X− X̄i∥²`, where `X̄i` is the variable being optimized. The proximal term should regularize towards the value from the *previous* iteration, e.g., `∥X− X̄i_prev∥²`. This is a critical error in the description of the algorithm.
    *   The description of the goal representation in Section 5 is convoluted: "Our character’s goal position was always set to be the origin, which encodes the characters state position in the goal position’s coordinate frame." This could be stated much more simply as "All character states are represented in a goal-relative coordinate frame."

2.  **Insufficient Methodological Detail and Justification:** Key parts of the methodology lack sufficient detail or empirical support.
    *   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The method relies on several crucial hyperparameters (σε, σγ, α, λ, η). These are listed at the end of Section 8, but there is no analysis of the sensitivity of the results to their values, nor justification for the specific choices made. This is a significant omission for a complex algorithm.
    *   **Feedback Gain Calculation:** The paper claims the proposed method for calculating feedback gains (Section 3.2) produces "better-conditioned gains" than an LQG-based approach. This is an empirical claim that is not supported by any evidence (e.g., a comparison of condition numbers).
    *   **Real-time Execution:** The policy execution step (Section 7) requires solving a non-linear optimization problem at each time step (50ms). The paper asserts this "can be solved at real-time rates" but provides no supporting data. What is the computational cost of this step? How many iterations are required for convergence? Without this information, the "real-time" claim is unsubstantiated.
    *   **Convergence:** The paper claims that the algorithm converges, citing Figure 2. However, this figure shows the evolution of training/test cost, not the convergence of the overall objective in Eq. (4) or the algorithm's iterates. For a non-standard, asynchronous, block-alternating optimization scheme, a more careful discussion of convergence properties is warranted.

3.  **Superficial Comparisons:** The comparison to Model Predictive Control (MPC) in Section 8.1 is cursory and purely qualitative. It consists of showing two trajectories and making the subjective claim that the proposed method's behavior is "less chaotic". A quantitative comparison based on metrics like task cost, control effort, or trajectory smoothness would be far more compelling.

4.  **Sloppy Presentation:** The manuscript shows a general lack of polish. Beyond the notational issues, there are grammatical errors ("unity these disparate approaches", "cyclical patter", "changed changed") and, most notably, the reference list is incomplete and poorly formatted. Several references are missing essential information like the conference or journal name, year, and page numbers (e.g., [1], [4], [10], [14], [17]). This level of carelessness detracts from the overall quality of the submission.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   The paper addresses a significant problem with an ambitious and general approach.
*   The empirical results are strong and visually impressive, demonstrating successful control for a wide variety of 3D characters.
*   The core ideas, such as the joint optimization framework and the method for noise injection, are technically interesting.

**Arguments for Rejection:**
*   The paper is rife with notational errors, ambiguities, and typos, indicating a lack of careful preparation.
*   Key methodological details are missing, particularly regarding the real-time performance of the execution step and hyperparameter sensitivity, hindering reproducibility.
*   Important technical claims (e.g., "better-conditioned gains," "real-time rates," "convergence") are not adequately supported with evidence.
*   The comparison with relevant baselines like MPC is superficial.

**Recommendation: Reject**

While the project's goals are commendable and the results are visually compelling, the paper in its current form does not meet the high standards for publication at NIPS. The large number of minor flaws, combined with more significant omissions in methodological detail and rigorous evaluation, makes the work difficult to trust and build upon. The paper has a strong foundation, but it requires a major revision to address the issues of clarity, precision, and unsubstantiated claims before it can be considered a solid scientific contribution.
