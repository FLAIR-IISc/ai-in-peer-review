

# Review of "Interactive Control of Diverse Complex Characters with Neural Networks"

This paper proposes a method for generating neural network controllers for complex character locomotion by combining trajectory optimization with neural network training. The authors claim to achieve interactive real-time control for diverse 3D characters (bipedal, quadrupedal, swimming, flying) without specialized controllers for each character type, which they argue is an advancement over previous work limited to 2D characters.

The core idea is to use Contact-Invariant Optimization (CIO) to generate training trajectories, then train a neural network to reproduce the optimal actions from these trajectories. The authors introduce noise injection during training to improve robustness and present an alternating optimization procedure to jointly optimize trajectories and neural network parameters. The method is evaluated on several character types, with results showing successful locomotion in 3D environments.

**Strengths:**
- The paper presents a clear, well-structured approach to combining trajectory optimization with neural network learning.
- The ablation studies (section 8.1) demonstrate the importance of their key technical contributions (joint optimization and noise injection) compared to simpler variants.
- The results show the method can handle diverse character morphologies, which is an interesting demonstration of the approach's flexibility.

**Weaknesses:**
The paper suffers from significant shortcomings that prevent it from meeting NIPS standards for methodological rigor and scientific contribution.

First, the paper lacks meaningful comparison with established baselines. The authors claim their method "solves substantially harder problems" than previous work but provide no rigorous comparison with state-of-the-art reinforcement learning methods (e.g., PPO, SAC, or other model-based control approaches) that are commonly used for these tasks. The ablation studies only compare to naive variants of their own method, not to standard approaches in the literature. This is a critical omission - the paper makes bold claims about solving harder problems but fails to demonstrate how their method compares to the standard tools researchers would actually use.

Second, the paper overstates the novelty of its approach. The authors acknowledge "closely related recent work along these lines [9, 11]" but don't clearly articulate how their method differs from these prior works in a significant way. The core technique (learning from trajectory optimization) is not new, and the paper doesn't convincingly argue why their specific implementation is a substantial advancement over existing methods.

Third, the paper provides insufficient evidence for its central claim that this approach "yields stable and realistic locomotion in three-dimensional space, where previous work was applied to only two-dimensional characters." The paper doesn't cite specific prior work that was limited to 2D, nor does it provide a clear comparison with 3D methods that might have already achieved similar results. The supplementary video is mentioned as "strongly recommended," but this is not a substitute for rigorous quantitative comparison in the paper.

Fourth, the computational requirements are substantial (hours of cloud computing for training) without sufficient justification for why this level of computation is necessary or worthwhile compared to simpler alternatives. The paper states "we invest more computing resources in generating the data than in learning from it," which raises questions about the practical utility of the approach.

Fifth, the paper fails to adequately address limitations and potential failure cases. For example, it doesn't discuss how the method would handle more complex tasks beyond basic locomotion, how it would scale to even more complex character morphologies, or how it would perform under significant perturbations or in unstructured environments.

The paper also contains several technical weaknesses. The trajectory optimization procedure (section 3) appears to be a standard implementation of CIO without sufficient explanation of why this particular approach is necessary. The neural network architecture (section 4) is standard with no clear justification for the specific choices made (e.g., 3 hidden layers, 250 units each).

**Conclusion:**
This paper presents an interesting application of combining trajectory optimization with neural networks for character control, but it falls short of NIPS standards due to insufficient validation against established baselines, overstated novelty claims, and lack of rigorous comparative evaluation. The method appears to be a natural extension of existing work rather than a significant advancement, and the paper doesn't provide compelling evidence that it outperforms simpler, more standard approaches. The authors would need to substantially strengthen their evaluation by comparing against standard reinforcement learning baselines, providing more detailed analysis of computational requirements, and clearly articulating how their approach differs from and improves upon prior work.

While the idea has potential, the current paper does not provide sufficient evidence to support its claims as a significant contribution to the field. The work would benefit from more thorough comparisons with standard approaches in the literature, a clearer articulation of its novelty, and more detailed analysis of its limitations and practical utility. Without these improvements, the paper does not meet NIPS's standards for quality, significance, and methodological rigor.
