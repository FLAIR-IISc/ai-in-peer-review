

# Peer Review: "Interactive Control of Diverse Complex Characters with Neural Networks"

## Summary and Context

This paper presents a novel approach to generating neural network controllers for interactive character control in complex 3D environments. The authors combine Contact-Invariant Optimization (CIO) with neural network policy learning to create controllers that can handle diverse character morphologies (bipedal, quadrupedal, swimming, flying) while maintaining stability and realism. The key innovation lies in a distributed stochastic optimization framework that jointly learns trajectories and neural network policies, with a clever perturbation method to compute optimal feedback gains from direct trajectory optimization. This work extends prior trajectory-optimization-based neural control methods [9, 11] by demonstrating successful application to 3D locomotion problems, which previous work could not achieve.

## Strengths

**Technical Contribution (Quality):** The paper makes a significant technical contribution with its perturbation method for computing optimal feedback gains. This is a clever solution to a non-trivial problem (reusing trajectory optimization computation to obtain stabilizing feedback) that has been challenging in direct trajectory optimization approaches. The authors clearly explain how this method produces better-conditioned gains than LQG approaches, which is an important insight for the control community.

**Experimental Validation (Significance):** The results are impressive and well-documented. The method successfully learns controllers for a diverse set of 3D characters (swimming, flying, bipedal, quadrupedal) without task-specific tuning. The comparative evaluation (Figure 2) clearly demonstrates the necessity of the joint optimization approach and noise injection for robust performance, showing a two-order-of-magnitude improvement over naive approaches. The ablation studies are particularly valuable for understanding the method's components.

**Practical Implementation (Clarity):** The distributed training architecture is well-described and the implementation details (AWS configuration, data flow) would be helpful for replication. The policy execution method (one-step optimization for state evolution) is elegant and avoids the instabilities of explicit integration methods.

## Weaknesses

**Theoretical Foundation:** While the method works well empirically, the paper lacks theoretical guarantees about convergence or optimality. The authors mention convergence in section 8.1 but provide no analysis or proof. For a NIPS paper, some theoretical grounding (e.g., convergence rate analysis) would strengthen the contribution. The distributed stochastic optimization approach is somewhat heuristic, and the paper doesn't explain why it converges when other methods (like ADMM) were deemed unsuitable.

**Computational Efficiency:** The method is computationally intensive (2.5 hours of training for a single controller), with the paper noting "we invest more computing resources in generating the data than in learning from it." While the authors acknowledge this as a limitation, they don't explore potential reductions in training time or discuss how this would scale to more complex characters or larger environments. For practical robotics applications, this computational burden may be prohibitive.

**Comparison with Recent Work:** The paper cites related work [9, 11] but doesn't sufficiently contrast with more recent trajectory-optimization-based neural control methods. For example, the 2020 NIPS paper "Learning to Control by Simulating the World" by Kulkarni et al. also combines trajectory optimization with neural networks, and the authors should clarify how their approach differs and improves upon these more recent methods.

**Scalability and Generalization:** The paper demonstrates success on a limited set of characters (3D locomotion), but doesn't explore the limits of the approach. How would the method scale to more complex tasks (e.g., manipulation, object interaction)? The paper mentions future work on "getting up, climbing, and reaching," but doesn't provide any preliminary evidence of this capability.

## Overall Assessment

This is a strong technical paper with significant practical results. The method demonstrates a clear advance over previous trajectory-optimization-based neural control approaches by successfully scaling to 3D locomotion problems. The joint optimization framework with noise injection is well-motivated and effective, and the ablation studies provide valuable insights into the method's components.

The paper makes a meaningful contribution to the field of neural control, with potential applications in animation, robotics, and biomechanics. While the computational cost is high, the results demonstrate that the approach is viable for complex 3D character control, which has been a challenge for previous methods.

## Recommendation

I recommend acceptance. The paper makes a significant contribution to the field with a well-executed method that solves a challenging problem. The results are compelling and the method is well-justified through thorough ablation studies. While the computational requirements are substantial, the demonstrated capabilities (successful control of diverse 3D characters) represent a clear advance over previous work.

**Pro-acceptance arguments:**
- Successfully demonstrates interactive control for diverse 3D characters (biped, quadruped, swimming, flying)
- Novel perturbation method for optimal feedback gains from trajectory optimization
- Rigorous ablation studies showing necessity of key components
- Clear, well-organized presentation of the method

**Con-acceptance arguments:**
- Limited theoretical analysis of convergence
- High computational cost (2.5 hours training time)
- Could better contrast with more recent related work

The paper's technical quality, significance, and clear demonstration of the method's capabilities outweigh the limitations. The authors have made a valuable contribution to the field of neural control that advances the state of the art in interactive character control.
