### **Review of "Interactive Control of Diverse Complex Characters with Neural Networks"**

**Summary**

This paper presents a method for training a single neural network policy to produce real-time, interactive control for a diverse range of physically simulated 3D characters, including bipeds, quadrupeds, swimmers, and fliers. The core idea is to combine the strengths of offline trajectory optimization, which can produce high-quality, physically plausible motions, with a neural network, which can execute in real-time. The authors propose a joint optimization framework where a dataset of optimal trajectories and the neural network policy are optimized in an alternating fashion. Key technical contributions include a principled method for injecting noise during training to improve robustness, a novel technique to extract optimal feedback gains from a direct trajectory optimization method (CIO), and a distributed architecture to make the computationally expensive training process feasible. The method successfully learns stable, cyclic gaits and motions for various morphologies without character-specific engineering.

The work is situated within the broader context of learning controllers for high-dimensional continuous systems. It builds upon previous work that also distills policies from trajectory optimizers (e.g., Levine & Koltun, 2014; Mordatch & Todorov, 2014), but extends it to significantly more complex 3D characters and introduces several technical improvements to achieve this.

---

### **Strengths and Weaknesses**

**Quality:** The paper is of high technical quality.

*   **Sound Methodology:** The proposed joint optimization algorithm (Algorithm 1) is a well-reasoned approach to the core challenge of closed-loop control: errors from the policy can accumulate, leading to states outside the training distribution and subsequent failure. By iteratively re-optimizing trajectories based on the current policy, the method effectively forces the optimizer and the policy to converge to a mutually consistent solution. This is a more robust approach than simply training on a static dataset of optimized trajectories.
*   **Novel Technical Contribution (Feedback Gains):** The method for deriving optimal feedback gains from the Contact-Invariant Optimization (CIO) framework (Section 3.2) is a clever and valuable contribution. Direct trajectory optimization methods do not typically produce these gains as a byproduct (unlike LQR/iLQR). The proposed perturbation method, which reuses the computed Hessian, is efficient and provides a principled way to generate training targets for noisy sensory inputs, which is crucial for learning a robust policy.
*   **Strong Experimental Validation:** The ablation study (Figure 2) is compelling. It clearly demonstrates that both the joint optimization ("no joint" case) and noise injection ("no noise" case) are critical for success. The fact that the ablations fail catastrophically at test time provides strong evidence for the necessity of the proposed components. The application of a single, unified algorithm to a wide variety of morphologies (biped, quadruped, swimmer, flier) without manual tuning is a powerful demonstration of the method's generality.

**Clarity:** The paper is mostly well-written and organized, but some areas could be improved.

*   **Well-Structured:** The paper follows a logical flow, starting with a high-level overview, then detailing the trajectory optimization and neural network regression components, and concluding with implementation details and results. Algorithm 1 clearly outlines the overall process.
*   **Areas for Clarification:**
    *   The description of the policy execution (Section 7) is brief. It states that evolving the state is done via a single-timestep optimization. While this approach ensures physical constraints are met, its real-time performance implications and comparison to simply using the network's output (`q̇_des`) in a standard physics integrator could be discussed in more detail.
    *   The training trajectory generation process (Section 5) is slightly confusing, particularly the explanation of the goal being at the origin. It could be stated more plainly that all kinematics are represented in a goal-relative coordinate frame.
    *   The comparison to Model-Predictive Control (MPC) mentions Figure 3, which is not included in the provided text. This makes the argument that the proposed method produces more "predictable and less chaotic behaviors" difficult to evaluate and purely qualitative.

**Originality:** The work represents a novel and significant extension of existing ideas.

*   The high-level concept of using trajectory optimization to train a neural network policy is not entirely new. It is closely related to the work of Levine & Koltun (2014) and can be seen as a form of guided policy search or imitation learning, with similarities to algorithms like DAgger.
*   However, the originality lies in the specific formulation and the successful application to a much harder class of problems. The key novel elements are:
    1.  The specific alternating optimization scheme with proximal terms.
    2.  The method for extracting well-conditioned feedback gains from CIO to enable effective training with sensory noise.
    3.  The successful demonstration on complex, unstable 3D characters, which prior work had not achieved. This leap from 2D to 3D is a non-trivial step that required the technical innovations presented.

**Significance:** The significance of this work is very high.

*   **Advances the State of the Art:** This paper demonstrably advances the state of the art in automated character control. Generating stable, natural-looking locomotion for diverse 3D characters from scratch is a long-standing grand challenge in both computer graphics and robotics. This work presents one of the most successful and general-purpose solutions to date.
*   **Broad Impact:** The ideas are likely to be influential. The framework is applicable to robotics (for generating robust controllers for high-DOF robots), animation (for creating interactive digital characters), and even biomechanics. The core idea of jointly optimizing a "teacher" (the optimizer) and a "student" (the network) is a powerful paradigm for tackling complex control problems where generating optimal data is expensive but possible.
*   **Unifying Approach:** The paper's goal to "unify these disparate approaches" for controlling different character types (biped, quadruped, etc.) is ambitious and largely successful. The ability to use the same architecture and algorithm for such varied morphologies is a major strength and points toward a more general science of motor control.

### **Arguments for and against Acceptance**

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
1.  **Impressive Results:** The method successfully solves a very difficult, high-dimensional control problem for a diverse set of 3D characters, a significant step beyond prior work.
2.  **Strong Technical Contributions:** The joint optimization framework and the method for extracting feedback gains from direct trajectory optimization are technically sound and novel.
3.  **Convincing Ablation Study:** The experiments clearly demonstrate the necessity of the core components of the proposed method (joint optimization and noise injection).
4.  **High Potential Impact:** The work is likely to be influential in control, robotics, and computer graphics, providing a powerful tool and paradigm for controller synthesis.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
1.  **Limited Comparison to Baselines:** The paper acknowledges that a systematic comparison to modern reinforcement learning methods (e.g., TRPO, which they cite) has not been done. While the authors' "impression" is that RL yields more chaotic behaviors, a quantitative comparison would make the paper stronger.
2.  **Minor Clarity Issues:** Some implementation details, particularly around policy execution and the MPC comparison, could be explained more thoroughly to improve reproducibility and fully substantiate the claims.

---

### **Overall Recommendation**

This is an excellent paper presenting a significant advance in learning-based control. The strengths—a novel and sound methodology, impressive and general results on challenging 3D control problems, and high potential impact—far outweigh the minor weaknesses related to baseline comparisons and clarity. The work is a complete and well-executed piece of research. I strongly recommend acceptance.
