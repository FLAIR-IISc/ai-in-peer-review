This paper proposes a method for learning neural network controllers for complex characters by combining trajectory optimization (specifically, Contact-Invariant Optimization) with supervised learning. The core idea is to use trajectory optimization to generate training data, then train a neural network to mimic the optimized trajectories, enabling real-time control. The authors address challenges like high dimensionality, closed-loop control, and limited data by incorporating noise injection and a distributed optimization scheme. They demonstrate the method on several 3D characters – swimmers, fliers, bipeds and quadrupeds – achieving stable and realistic locomotion.

While the results appear promising, particularly the extension to fully 3D characters where previous work was limited to 2D, I find the paper lacks sufficient rigor and comparative analysis to warrant strong endorsement. The approach builds on established techniques – trajectory optimization and supervised learning – but the novelty lies primarily in the combination and scaling to more complex problems. However, the claims of substantial improvement over existing methods are not convincingly substantiated. 

The authors rightly point out the difficulty of obtaining data for neural network controllers, and leveraging trajectory optimization is a reasonable approach. However, the paper lacks a thorough comparison against other “direct” methods for training neural network controllers (as acknowledged in the introduction – [3, 6, 14]). Simply stating an “impression” that these methods yield “somewhat chaotic behaviors” is insufficient. A quantitative comparison, even on a limited set of tasks, is crucial to demonstrate the benefits of their approach.  Furthermore, the comparison to MPC (mentioned in the conclusions) is also qualitative and doesn't provide a clear understanding of the trade-offs.

The stochastic optimization framework, while well-described, appears complex. The rationale for specific choices, like the quadratic penalty (Equation 3) and the proximal regularization, is not fully explained. Why was this particular formulation chosen over alternatives?  The assertion that the asynchronous, distributed architecture is necessary for scalability is plausible, but the paper doesn’t provide sufficient details regarding the computational costs – for example, a clear breakdown of time spent in trajectory optimization versus neural network training.  While the authors mention spending more computing resources on data generation, quantifying this is important.

The noise injection strategy is interesting and intuitively makes sense, and the derivation of optimal feedback gains is a clever addition. However, the sensitivity of the method to the noise parameters (σ<sub>ε</sub>, σ<sub>γ</sub>, λ, η) is not adequately explored.  Are these parameters easily tunable, or do they require significant experimentation for each character and task?

The experimental results are presented primarily through qualitative observations and a single ablation study. While the video demonstrations are helpful, they are not a substitute for rigorous quantitative evaluation. The ablation study (Figure 2) is limited to a single task and demonstrates the importance of joint optimization and noise injection, but it doesn’t assess the performance against other control strategies. The table comparing network architectures is informative, but doesn't address the core question of whether this method outperforms alternative neural network training approaches.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Addresses a challenging problem in character control and robotics.
*   Demonstrates successful application to complex 3D characters.
*   Combines established techniques in a potentially effective manner.
*   Presents a potentially useful approach for generating training data.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Lacks a thorough comparative analysis against alternative methods.
*   Claims of superiority are not convincingly supported by quantitative results.
*   Limited exploration of parameter sensitivity.
*   Complexity of the optimization framework without sufficient justification for design choices.
*   The reliance on CIO as the sole data generation method is a potential bottleneck – what if CIO fails to find a solution?



**Overall Assessment:**

I rate this paper as a **weak reject**. While the ideas are interesting and the results are encouraging, the paper falls short of the NIPS standard for rigor and thorough evaluation. The authors need to provide a more compelling demonstration of the benefits of their approach through comprehensive comparisons with existing methods and a more detailed analysis of the system’s performance and sensitivity. The paper reads more like a proof-of-concept than a fully developed contribution.
