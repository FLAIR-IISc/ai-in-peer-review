### Paper Review

**Summary:**
This paper presents a method for training a single neural network policy to produce interactive, real-time control for a diverse set of physically simulated 3D characters. The core idea is to combine the strengths of offline trajectory optimization, which can produce high-quality, physically realistic motions, with the real-time performance of a neural network controller. The authors formulate this as a joint optimization problem, seeking to find both a set of optimal trajectories and the parameters of a neural network policy that can reproduce them.

The key technical contributions are twofold. First, to improve the robustness of the learned policy and avoid the compounding errors typical of simple imitation learning, the authors train the network on noisy sensory inputs. Crucially, the target action for a noisy input is not the original action, but a new optimal action derived from a first-order approximation using optimal feedback gains. This teaches the policy to actively correct for perturbations. Second, since these feedback gains are not a natural byproduct of the direct trajectory optimization method used (Contact-Invariant Optimization - CIO), the authors propose a novel and efficient "perturbation method" to compute well-conditioned gains by reusing the Hessian inverse already computed during trajectory optimization.

The complex joint optimization problem is solved using a block-alternating scheme, iteratively optimizing trajectories (distributed across multiple machines) and then regressing the neural network policy on the resulting data (on a GPU). The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their universal approach by successfully training controllers for a wide range of morphologies, including bipeds, quadrupeds, a swimmer, and a flyer, all using the same algorithm and network architecture. Ablation studies convincingly show that both the joint optimization and the noise injection with feedback gains are critical to the method's success.

This work builds upon prior research combining trajectory optimization and function approximation, such as Levine & Koltun (2014) and Mordatch & Todorov (2014). However, it makes significant advances by tackling substantially more complex 3D characters, and more importantly, by introducing the principled method of using optimal feedback gains to train a robust policy capable of stabilizing these complex dynamics in real-time.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Significance and Impact:** The paper addresses a very challenging and important problem in control, robotics, and computer graphics: the automatic synthesis of robust, real-time controllers for complex, high-DoF systems. The ability to generate stable, interactive locomotion for such a diverse set of characters (walking, trotting, swimming, flying) from a single, unified framework is a significant achievement and a clear advance for the state of the art. The resulting behaviors shown in the supplementary video are highly compelling.

2.  **Originality and Technical Quality:** The primary technical contribution—using optimal feedback gains to generate training targets for noisy inputs—is both novel and insightful. It provides a principled way to move beyond simple behavioral cloning towards learning a true feedback policy that can handle perturbations. The proposed method for efficiently extracting these gains from a direct trajectory optimization method (Section 3.2) is a clever and valuable contribution in its own right, potentially applicable to other problems.

3.  **Thorough Experimental Validation:** The experiments are extensive and well-designed. The demonstration on multiple characters with fundamentally different dynamics convincingly supports the claim of a universal method. The ablation study (Figure 2) is particularly strong, clearly isolating and quantifying the benefits of the two main components of the approach: joint optimization (vs. static imitation) and noise injection (vs. deterministic training). This provides strong evidence for the authors' claims.

4.  **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and the overall methodology is explained clearly. The motivation is well-established, and the decomposition of the complex problem into an alternating optimization scheme (Algorithm 1) is easy to follow.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Comparison to Reinforcement Learning:** The paper acknowledges contemporary deep reinforcement learning methods but dismisses them with a brief, qualitative statement ("our impression is that networks trained with direct methods give rise to successful yet somewhat chaotic behaviors"). While the approach presented is different, a more rigorous, quantitative comparison to a state-of-the-art model-free RL algorithm (e.g., TRPO, which was concurrent) on at least one of the tasks would have substantially strengthened the paper. This would help delineate the precise advantages and disadvantages (e.g., sample efficiency, final policy quality, stability) of this "optimizer-in-the-loop" approach versus direct policy search.

2.  **Reproducibility and Details:** While the high-level method is clear, some crucial details are missing, hindering reproducibility. The exact structure of the cost functions `C(X)` used in trajectory optimization is only vaguely described. Providing the precise mathematical form of these costs (e.g., in an appendix) is essential. Similarly, the definition of the recurrent state `r` is introduced in Section 2 but never elaborated upon. Its role, dimensionality, and how its dynamics (`ṙ`) are learned and used are unclear, yet it seems to be an important part of the state representation.

3.  **Computational Cost:** The method requires a significant amount of computation (hours on a distributed cluster with ~30 nodes and a GPU). While the authors are upfront about this, a discussion of the method's scalability with respect to character complexity (degrees of freedom) or task horizon would be valuable. This is a practical limitation that should be acknowledged and analyzed more deeply.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Addresses a major, long-standing problem with a novel and effective solution.
*   The results are state-of-the-art, demonstrating unprecedented versatility across diverse 3D characters.
*   The core technical idea of using learned optimal feedback gains for robust policy training is a significant conceptual contribution.
*   The ablation studies provide strong, convincing evidence for the method's design choices.

**Arguments for Rejection:**
*   The comparison to alternative methods (specifically deep RL) is weak and purely qualitative.
*   The paper lacks some key details (e.g., precise cost functions, role of recurrent state) necessary for full understanding and reproducibility.
*   The high computational cost may limit its practical applicability, and this limitation is not fully explored.

**Recommendation:** Accept.

Despite the weaknesses, the strengths of this paper are overwhelming. The significance of the problem, the novelty of the core technical ideas, and the impressive, well-validated results represent a major contribution to the field. The paper clearly advances the state of the art in learning-based control for complex characters. The weaknesses, particularly regarding missing details and comparison to RL, can be addressed in a future version and do not undermine the core value of the work presented.
