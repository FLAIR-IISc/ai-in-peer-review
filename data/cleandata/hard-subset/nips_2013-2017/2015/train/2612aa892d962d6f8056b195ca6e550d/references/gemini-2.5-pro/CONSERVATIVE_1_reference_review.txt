**Summary**

This paper presents a method for synthesizing interactive controllers for complex, physically-simulated 3D characters. The core idea is to couple offline trajectory optimization with supervised learning of a neural network policy. The authors propose a joint optimization framework where a dataset of optimal trajectories is generated using Contact-Invariant Optimization (CIO), and a neural network is trained to mimic the optimizer's state-action mappings. The process is iterative: the network parameters and the trajectories are refined together in an alternating fashion. Key technical contributions include a principled method for injecting noise during training to improve robustness and a novel technique for extracting local feedback gains from the direct trajectory optimizer to supervise the network on how to react to perturbations. The method is demonstrated on a variety of characters, including bipeds, quadrupeds, a swimmer, and a flyer, successfully producing stable locomotion behaviors.

**Strengths**

1.  **Impressive Qualitative Results:** The primary strength of the paper lies in the demonstrated results. The ability to generate stable, plausible gaits for a diverse set of 3D characters with different morphologies (walking, flying, swimming) using a single, unified framework is a notable achievement. This suggests the approach is general and powerful.

2.  **Novel Combination of Methods:** The paper effectively combines the strengths of trajectory optimization (ability to find high-quality, physically realistic solutions) with neural networks (ability to provide fast, real-time inference for interactive control). The alternating optimization scheme that refines both the data and the policy is a sensible approach to this challenging problem.

3.  **Technical Contribution in Feedback Generation:** The method for extracting optimal feedback gains from the CIO optimizer (Section 3.2) is a clever and useful contribution. It avoids the need for a separate, potentially inefficient LQR pass and provides a principled way to generate training data that teaches the network how to react to small perturbations, which is crucial for closed-loop stability. The ablation study effectively demonstrates the importance of this noise-injection scheme.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Insufficient Comparison to Established Baselines:** The most significant weakness of this work is the lack of empirical comparison to established methods in the field of continuous control. The authors compare their full algorithm only against ablations of itself ("no joint optimization," "no noise"). While these ablations are informative, they do not situate the work within the broader context of control and reinforcement learning. The introduction mentions direct RL methods (e.g., [3, 6, 14]), but explicitly states that a "systematic comparison... remains to be done." For a NIPS paper, this is a critical omission. Without comparisons to state-of-the-art policy gradient or actor-critic methods (e.g., TRPO [14], which was contemporary), it is impossible to evaluate the proposed method's performance in terms of sample efficiency, final policy quality, or computational cost. The authors' impression that direct methods yield "somewhat chaotic behaviors" is not a substitute for rigorous empirical evidence.

2.  **Ambiguity in Real-Time Policy Execution:** Section 7, "Policy Execution," is unclear and potentially contradicts the central claim of learning a direct neural network controller. It states that at each timestep, an optimization problem is solved to find the next state that matches the network's desired velocity `q̇_des`, subject to physics constraints. This sounds less like a direct feed-forward policy and more like a Model Predictive Control (MPC) scheme with a horizon of one, where the neural network serves to provide targets. If optimization is still required at runtime, the claim that the network *is* the real-time controller is diluted. This also complicates the comparison against MPC in Section 8.1, as the proposed execution method appears to be a form of MPC itself. The paper needs to clarify precisely what is computed at runtime and what the computational cost is.

3.  **Heuristic Optimization Framework:** The proposed "Distributed Stochastic Optimization" (Algorithm 1) is a block-alternating scheme applied to a non-convex problem. While the authors report that it converges in practice, the method is heuristic and lacks any formal analysis or convergence guarantees. The stability of such a procedure can be sensitive to hyperparameters (e.g., `η`, `α`).

4.  **High Computational Cost and Scalability:** The training process requires a substantial distributed computing setup ("up to 30 optimization nodes per GPU machine") and takes several hours. The authors note that more resources are spent on trajectory optimization (data generation) than on network training. This raises questions about the practicality and scalability of the method. An analysis of how performance scales with computation, and a comparison to the computational requirements of established RL methods, would be necessary to assess its efficiency.

**Overall Assessment**

The paper tackles an important and difficult problem and achieves impressive visual results. The core idea of jointly optimizing trajectories and a policy is promising, and the technical contribution for deriving feedback gains is sound. However, the work in its current form falls short of the rigorous standards expected for NIPS. The lack of comparison to any established reinforcement learning baselines is a major flaw that prevents a fair assessment of the method's contribution to the state of the art. Furthermore, the ambiguity in the policy execution step raises questions about the nature of the final controller. While the ideas presented are interesting, the claims of superiority are not substantiated by the experiments. I would encourage the authors to perform a thorough comparison against modern continuous control RL algorithms and to clarify the runtime execution model before resubmission.

**Recommendation:** Reject
