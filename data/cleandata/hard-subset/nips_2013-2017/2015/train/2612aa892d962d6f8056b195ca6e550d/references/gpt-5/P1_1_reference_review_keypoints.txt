- Proposes supervised policy learning for real-time control by alternating trajectory optimization (CIO) and neural policy regression with a quadratic coupling penalty.
- Introduces perturbation-based extraction of stabilizing feedback gains from the trajectory optimizer using Gauss-Newton and Woodbury computations.
- Uses input and hidden-unit Gaussian noise together with feedback gains to create noisy action targets (a + As ε) to improve closed-loop robustness.
- Trains asynchronously and distributedly: many workers run prox-regularized trajectory optimization while a GPU performs batched SGD on the policy.
- Runtime uses the learned policy to output desired velocities, recurrent updates, and contact forces, then enforces dynamics via a T=1 constrained optimization for stable implicit integration at 20 Hz.
- Demonstrated on diverse 3D agents (swimmer, bird, multiple bipeds, quadrupeds) with ablations showing benefits of joint optimization and noise.
- Situates work within guided policy search / supervised policy search and relates to LQG/MPC, PILCO, and deep RL methods like TRPO/DDPG.
- Claims contributions: scaling to diverse 3D locomotion with contacts, practical gain computation from direct optimization, noise-consistent targets for robustness, and asynchronous/proximal training.
- Strength: practical, well-motivated combination of CIO and neural regression yielding interactive controllers without hand-designed gaits.
- Strength: technically interesting and efficient reuse of trajectory-optimizer computations to obtain stabilizing gains.
- Strength: sensible robustness design choices (noise, proximal regularization, branched segments) and clear engineering for scalability.
- Strength: simple runtime T=1 implicit optimization bridging learned policy and simulation.
- Ablations indicate joint alternating optimization and noise are important for test-time stability.
- Weakness: limited quantitative evaluation—missing metrics like success rate, tracking error, energy use, GRF realism, fall frequency, and perturbation robustness.
- Weakness: weak baselines and no quantitative comparisons to GPS-style methods, TRPO, PILCO, or model-based MPC with learned policies.
- Weakness: soft enforcement of dynamics may allow nonphysical root forces; impact on realism and hardware transfer is not analyzed.
- Weakness: unclear training and role of the recurrent state r—no BPTT or temporal credit assignment appears described.
- Weakness: no sensitivity analysis for key hyperparameters (λ, α, η, σϵ, σγ), raising reproducibility concerns.
- Clarity issues: missing specifics on feature vector φ, CIO cost terms/weights, exact sensory inputs/normalization, dimensionalities, and runtime/per-step timings.
- Presentation gaps: referenced figures/tables are missing or insufficiently described; several citations incomplete or missing related work (e.g., PILCO, broader GPS literature).
- Originality: incremental over guided policy search but novel in gain extraction technique, noisy-target integration, and asynchronous/proximal loop.
- Significance: potentially impactful if claims hold—robust controllers across morphologies and reusable gain computation technique—but limited by missing quantitative analysis.
- Questions for authors: how recurrent states are trained/used, sensitivity to key hyperparameters, exact CIO cost terms/weights, per-step runtime scaling, and quantitative robustness to perturbations.
- Pros for acceptance: well-engineered approach across challenging tasks, practical gain extraction, and demonstrated importance of joint/noisy training.
- Cons for acceptance: limited quantitative evaluation and baselines, potential realism issues from soft dynamics, and missing details harming reproducibility.
- Recommendation: lean accept conditional on stronger empirical evaluation, clearer comparisons to GPS/MPC baselines, and clarification of r and hyperparameter sensitivity.
