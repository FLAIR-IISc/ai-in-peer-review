Summary
The paper proposes a hybrid learning-and-optimization framework to synthesize interactive, real-time controllers for complex characters (bipeds, quadrupeds, swimmer, bird) in 3D. The core idea is to generate locally optimal trajectories via Contact-Invariant Optimization (CIO) and train a neural policy to imitate them in closed loop. To address compounding error and robustness, the authors (i) inject sensory noise during training, (ii) correct the target actions using locally optimal feedback gains around the trajectory, and (iii) alternate between re-optimizing trajectories under a quadratic penalty that encourages consistency with the current policy and updating the policy by regression. They also propose a perturbation-based method to compute feedback gains from direct trajectory optimization by reusing the Gauss-Newton Hessian and Jacobians, and present an asynchronous distributed training architecture. At runtime, the policy outputs desired state velocities and contact forces, and a one-step constrained optimization (horizon T=1) enforces dynamics and contacts.

Relation to prior work
This is closely related to guided policy search (GPS) and “trajectory-optimization-to-policy” methods (e.g., Levine & Koltun 2014; Mordatch & Todorov 2014), as well as DAGGER-like dataset aggregation for closed-loop robustness. Compared to prior 2D demonstrations, the paper claims stable 3D locomotion and diverse morphologies, and introduces a way to compute feedback gains from direct trajectory optimization that avoids a separate LQG/iLQR pass. The training-time noise with first-order target correction is reminiscent of fitting to a local linear-Gaussian teacher in GPS, but derived within CIO with a tunable softness (λ). The runtime one-step implicit solve resembles model predictive control and feature-based control used in graphics and robotics.

Strengths
- Addresses a challenging regime: high-DOF 3D characters, multiple morphologies, interactive goal changes, and contact-rich dynamics.
- Methodologically coherent: alternating optimization with proximal regularization; noise-injected supervised learning with corrected targets; reuse of trajectory optimizer derivatives for feedback gain computation.
- The feedback-gain derivation via a soft constraint and Woodbury identity is neat and practically useful for direct methods where gains are not readily available.
- Distributed asynchronous implementation with practical training times; clear engineering contribution.
- Emergent cyclic gaits across very different bodies without hand-crafted gait encodings is compelling.

Weaknesses
- Limited quantitative evaluation. Most results appear qualitative; there is no systematic comparison to strong baselines (e.g., GPS with iLQG/iLQR, TRPO, MPC) on identical tasks with metrics such as success rate, energy, foot slip, disturbance rejection, or policy rollout stability.
- The approach still relies on a T=1 optimization at execution time. The extent to which the learned policy alone ensures stability vs. the implicit integrator/soft constraints is unclear. Latency and compute budget at runtime are not reported.
- Physical fidelity: soft enforcement of dynamics/contact and allowance of root forces can produce unphysical motions; transfer to robots is therefore speculative.
- The recurrent state r is under-specified (dimensionality, initialization, role). The policy is not obviously recurrent in the usual sense; r is treated as an integrator, but its benefits are not ablated.
- The ablation studies (“no joint,” “no noise”) are underspecified (exact metrics, plots, statistical significance). The claim of “two orders of magnitude” improvement needs concrete numbers.
- Related work coverage is incomplete. DAGGER (Ross et al., 2011), iLQG/iterative LQR, PILCO, and more recent policy-gradient methods (beyond TRPO citation) should be discussed, especially GPS variants that fit linear-Gaussian teachers and alternate with supervised policy learning.
- Clarity and references: several citations lack bibliographic details; some notation (e.g., dimensions of s, a, r, f) and hyperparameter choices (α, λ, η) are not motivated; convergence claims refer to a figure not present in the text provided.

Quality
Technically sound overall, with careful integration of known components and a novel, well-motivated method to obtain feedback gains from direct trajectory optimization. However, the empirical evaluation is insufficiently rigorous to substantiate several claims (robustness, stability, “substantially harder problems” than prior work). More careful baselines and metrics are needed.

Clarity
The paper is generally readable, but several derivations are terse and some implementation details are missing (r structure, network input/output normalization, horizon and cost terms at runtime). References are incomplete. A clearer distinction between what is learned vs. what is enforced by the runtime optimizer would improve clarity.

Originality
Moderate. The overall template (trajectory optimization + supervised policy learning with alternating updates) is known. The contribution of computing feedback gains within CIO using a soft constraint formulation and reusing the Gauss-Newton structure is novel and useful. The distributed asynchronous training setup is practical but not conceptually new.

Significance
Potentially impactful for interactive character animation and possibly for model-based control pipelines, primarily as an engineering recipe. Without stronger quantitative comparisons and physical realism, the impact on robotics/control is limited. Demonstrations across diverse 3D morphologies are promising.

Questions for authors
- What is the dimensionality and role of the recurrent state r? Is the policy actually recurrent at test time, and how critical is r (ablation)?
- What are the exact runtime costs (Hz, CPU/GPU usage) for the T=1 optimization per frame for each character?
- Quantify robustness: how does performance degrade under sensor noise, pushes, or model mismatch?
- How sensitive are results to α, λ, η, σε, σγ? How is λ selected to avoid ill-conditioned gains?
- Can you report metrics (distance-to-goal error, energy, contact consistency, fall rate) and compare to GPS/iLQG-based policies and MPC on the same tasks?

Pros
- Addresses a challenging problem with a coherent hybrid method.
- Novel, practical feedback-gain computation for direct trajectory optimization.
- Diverse 3D demonstrations; simple, unified architecture.

Cons
- Weak quantitative evaluation and limited comparisons to strong baselines.
- Reliance on soft dynamics and runtime optimization complicates claims of learned, physically plausible control.
- Some clarity and citation issues.

Recommendation
Weak reject. The paper is promising and well-engineered, with a nice idea for extracting feedback gains from CIO, but it needs stronger, quantitative evidence and clearer positioning versus GPS/MPC/TRPO to meet NeurIPS standards. Strengthening the empirical section and clarifying the role of r and runtime costs could move this to accept.
