This paper presents a compelling approach to learning complex locomotion controllers for diverse character morphologies by combining trajectory optimization (CIO) with neural network regression. The core idea – leveraging the power of offline trajectory optimization to generate a rich dataset for training a real-time, interactive controller – is highly novel and addresses a significant challenge in the field: scaling control to high-dimensional, physically realistic systems. The ability to achieve stable 3D locomotion, where prior work was largely limited to 2D, is a particularly strong result and demonstrates the potential of the method. The insights into the importance of noise injection for robustness and generalization, along with the clever reuse of trajectory optimization computations for feedback gain calculation, are also noteworthy.

The paper is generally well-written and logically organized. The authors clearly articulate the motivations, challenges, and contributions of their work. The descriptions of the algorithms and training architecture are sufficiently detailed to allow for replication. The inclusion of comparative evaluation, highlighting the benefits of joint optimization and noise injection, strengthens the claims. The distributed training architecture is practical and relevant given the computational demands of the approach.

However, while the results are promising, several aspects could be improved. The ablation studies, while present, feel somewhat limited. More extensive evaluation comparing different neural network architectures and trajectory optimization parameters would provide a deeper understanding of the method’s sensitivity and optimal configuration. The claim of "more realistic and purposeful behaviors" compared to direct methods is asserted without a robust quantitative comparison—a more thorough comparison of behaviors, perhaps using metrics related to gait efficiency or task completion time, would bolster this assertion. While the video provides a qualitative impression of realistic movement, it is insufficient to make definitive claims. Additionally, while the authors acknowledge the potential for overfitting, the regularization techniques seem somewhat ad-hoc; exploring more principled approaches to regularization could be beneficial. The choice of α and η feels somewhat arbitrary and could benefit from justification or sensitivity analysis. 

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** The combination of trajectory optimization and neural network learning for interactive control is a genuinely novel approach.
*   **Significance:** The ability to generate stable 3D locomotion controllers for diverse characters represents a significant step forward in the field. This has implications for robotics, animation, and biomechanics.
*   **Technical Soundness:** The method appears technically sound, and the authors provide sufficient detail for replication. The reuse of trajectory optimization computations for feedback gain calculation is particularly clever.
*   **Potential Impact:** The technique promises to automate the creation of complex controllers, reducing the need for manual tuning and expertise.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Limited Comparative Evaluation:** The qualitative comparison to other methods and the ablation studies could be more comprehensive.
*   **Ad-hoc Regularization:** The regularization techniques are not fully justified and could be improved.
*   **Lack of Quantifiable Metrics:** The claim of “more realistic” behavior lacks quantitative support.
*   **Computational Cost:** While addressed by distributed training, the reliance on extensive trajectory optimization still represents a significant computational burden.



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite the minor weaknesses, I believe this paper represents a significant contribution to the field of interactive control. The novelty of the approach, the impressive results in 3D locomotion, and the potential impact of the work outweigh the shortcomings. I am willing to overlook the incomplete validations and minor flaws given the boldness of the approach and the potential for future research. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions to address the points raised above, particularly strengthening the comparative evaluation and providing more quantitative metrics for assessing behavior realism. The authors should consider expanding the ablation studies and providing a more detailed justification for the choice of hyperparameters.
