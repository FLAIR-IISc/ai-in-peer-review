### NIPS Review

**Summary of the Paper:**

This paper presents a novel method for training a single neural network policy to control a diverse range of complex 3D characters for interactive locomotion tasks. The core contribution is a joint optimization framework that alternates between optimizing a set of expert trajectories using a powerful offline method (Contact-Invariant Optimization, CIO) and regressing a neural network policy to mimic the behavior demonstrated in these trajectories. Critically, these two processes are coupled: the trajectory optimizer is regularized to produce behaviors that the current neural network policy can easily learn, and the neural network is trained on data that is constantly being refined. A key technical innovation is a principled method for injecting noise during training. Instead of just adding noise to the network's sensory inputs, the authors also compute the optimal feedback gains from the trajectory optimizer to determine the correct compensatory action for that noise. This teaches the network local robustness. The authors demonstrate the power and generality of their approach by applying the exact same algorithm to control bipeds, quadrupeds, swimmers, and flying creatures in 3D, all of which learn stable and plausible gaits. This work builds upon prior efforts that combined trajectory optimization and policy learning (e.g., Levine & Koltun, 2014; Mordatch & Todorov, 2014) but makes a significant leap by tackling much more complex 3D characters and introducing a more tightly-coupled, stochastic optimization scheme.

**Strengths (Reasons for Acceptance):**

1.  **Significant Conceptual Novelty:** The primary strength of this paper is its bold and elegant approach. Moving beyond a simple two-stage "generate data, then imitate" pipeline, the authors propose a true joint optimization. The idea that the expert trajectories and the student policy are co-adapted is powerful. This allows the system to find solutions that are not only physically optimal but also learnable by a compact neural network representation, effectively regularizing the vast solution space of the trajectory optimizer towards more predictable and cyclically stable behaviors (as suggested by the MPC comparison). This is a significant conceptual leap in how we can leverage powerful but slow optimizers to train fast, reactive agents.

2.  **Impressive Generality and Ambition:** The paper tackles a grand challenge in control: creating a universal controller synthesis method. The demonstration on a diverse set of morphologies—biped, quadruped, swimmer, and flyer—using a single, unchanged algorithm is a powerful testament to the method's generality. Achieving stable 3D locomotion, which is notoriously difficult, for all these characters is a state-of-the-art result and a substantial advance over prior work that was largely confined to 2D.

3.  **Elegant Technical Contribution in Stochastic Training:** The method for injecting noise (Section 2.1 and 3.2) is particularly clever. Rather than simply using a denoising autoencoder objective, the authors derive the optimal feedback gains from the trajectory optimizer. This provides the network with highly structured and informative training signals about how to react to perturbations. This is a far more principled approach to learning robust policies than ad-hoc noise injection and is a valuable contribution in its own right.

4.  **High Potential for Impact and Significance:** The results, if the supplementary video is representative, are highly compelling. This work has the potential to significantly impact fields like computer animation (for interactive characters), robotics (for controller synthesis), and even computational neuroscience (as a model of motor learning). It presents a clear path to automating the creation of sophisticated controllers for complex systems, a long-standing goal in AI and control theory.

**Weaknesses (Areas for Improvement):**

1.  **Limited Quantitative Comparison to Alternatives:** While the ablation studies convincingly demonstrate the importance of the joint optimization and noise injection, the paper lacks a direct quantitative comparison to other state-of-the-art methods, particularly from the deep reinforcement learning literature (e.g., TRPO, PPO). The authors acknowledge this, but a benchmark against at least one direct RL method on one of their characters would have strengthened the claims about producing "less chaotic" and "more purposeful" behaviors. The comparison with MPC is qualitative and illustrative but not a rigorous benchmark.

2.  **Lack of Theoretical Convergence Analysis:** The proposed distributed, asynchronous, block-alternating optimization algorithm is complex. While the authors empirically show that it works well, the paper provides no theoretical justification or analysis of its convergence properties. Given the stochastic nature and asynchronous updates, it's not immediately obvious that this procedure is stable.

3.  **High System Complexity and Reproducibility:** The method relies on a sophisticated trajectory optimizer (CIO) and a custom distributed training architecture. This makes the entire system quite complex and potentially difficult for other researchers to reproduce without access to the authors' specific codebase and infrastructure.

**Recommendation:**

I strongly recommend acceptance.

This paper is an excellent example of the kind of ambitious, conceptually novel, and high-impact research that NIPS should highlight. While it is not without its flaws—most notably the lack of a rigorous convergence analysis and limited comparison to direct RL methods—these are minor in the face of its significant contributions. The core idea of jointly optimizing expert trajectories and a neural policy is a powerful new paradigm that moves beyond simple imitation learning. The results, demonstrating a single algorithm's ability to create stable 3D locomotion controllers for an array of diverse creatures, represent a major advance in the state of the art. This is a bold paper that pushes the boundaries of control and learning, and I am confident it will inspire a great deal of follow-up work.
