Summary and relation to prior work
This paper presents an approach for training interactive, real-time controllers for complex 3D characters by alternating trajectory optimization (Contact-Invariant Optimization, CIO) with supervised learning of a neural network policy. The key ideas are: (i) use CIO to generate trajectories across different tasks and morphologies; (ii) inject sensory noise during data generation and, crucially, use first-order action linearization a(s+ε) ≈ a + a_s ε, where a_s are locally optimal feedback gains computed efficiently from the Gauss-Newton Hessian and Jacobians already available in CIO; (iii) train a multi-layer feedforward network with injected hidden noise to regress from noisy sensory inputs s+ε to the linearized target actions; (iv) perform block coordinate descent with proximal regularization over trajectories and network parameters in an asynchronous distributed system; and (v) at run time, execute the learned policy and solve a 1-step constrained optimization to realize the desired q̇, ṙ, and contact forces.

The approach follows the “trajectory optimization + supervised learning” paradigm popularized by Guided Policy Search (GPS) and related methods (e.g., Levine & Koltun, ICML’14; Mordatch & Todorov, RSS’14), but departs from ADMM-based coupling and proposes a simpler quadratic penalty plus proximal regularization. Compared to prior work chiefly demonstrated in 2D or with simpler tasks, this paper reports interactive 3D locomotion across diverse morphologies (bipeds, quadrupeds, swimmer, bird) trained with a unified architecture. The paper also contrasts qualitatively with MPC and argues that the joint optimization regularizes toward predictable cyclic behaviors.

Strengths
- Ambitious goal: a single learning pipeline producing interactive controllers for a variety of 3D characters without hand-crafted state machines or motion capture.
- A practical and elegant mechanism to compute stabilizing feedback gains from direct trajectory optimization via a perturbation/soft-constraint formulation and Woodbury identity; this is a useful contribution beyond this specific application.
- Sensible noise-injection scheme matching the linearized action, addressing distribution shift and closed-loop instability; aligns conceptually with denoising autoencoders and local LQR/GPS ideas.
- Asynchronous distributed training with proximal regularization is simple, practical, and appears to converge empirically; training times are reasonable (hours).
- Clear system-level story and strong qualitative results; the variety of characters and emergent gaits are compelling for graphics/control applications.

Weaknesses
- Limited quantitative evaluation. The paper lacks standardized metrics (success rate, distance/orientation tracking error, falls per minute, energy/torque costs) and does not benchmark against strong baselines (e.g., GPS/ADMM variants, TRPO, modern model-free continuous control) beyond a qualitative MPC comparison and ablations.
- Physical realism caveat: dynamics constraints are enforced softly at run time and may admit “root forces.” This undermines claims of physical plausibility and limits relevance to robotics. The magnitude/frequency of such violations should be reported.
- The recurrent memory state r is under-specified. There is no ablation to establish necessity, size, or function of r; the network is feedforward with a learned integrator on r, but the role of memory vs. feedforward features remains unclear.
- Convergence and stability are empirical. The alternating stochastic optimization has no analysis, and the choice of penalty/regularization weights (α, η, λ, σε, σγ) is not justified or sensitivity-tested.
- Dataset size, coverage, and “interactive” generalization are not rigorously characterized. The branching strategy is reasonable, but robustness to perturbations, large goal changes, or sensor noise is not quantified.
- Several references are incomplete; related work on guided policy search and model-free continuous control could be better positioned.

Quality
Technically solid overall, with a clever contribution for extracting feedback gains from direct trajectory optimization. The training and execution pipelines are coherent and reproducible at a high level. However, the evaluation is not up to NeurIPS standards: no head-to-head quantitative baselines, limited metrics, and unreported run-time performance beyond a 50 ms step (no wall-clock latency, solver iteration limits, etc.). The soft-contact/run-time “root forces” are a notable gap.

Clarity
The paper is generally well-written and easy to follow, with a helpful overview and algorithmic description. Some missing details (dimensions of r, exact loss/metrics in Figure/Table, compute budgets at run time, magnitude of constraint violations) and incomplete bibliographic entries should be fixed. Positioning versus GPS could be clearer, including what is gained or lost by the proposed penalty/prox approach.

Originality
Moderate. The overall framework is close to GPS-style methods. Novel aspects include the action linearization paired with noise injection and the efficient computation of feedback gains from CIO’s Gauss-Newton system, plus the distributed asynchronous training recipe. The empirical leap to diverse 3D characters with one architecture is noteworthy.

Significance
Potentially impactful for graphics, animation, and model-based control communities. For NeurIPS, significance would be strengthened by rigorous quantitative comparisons and demonstrations of robustness. As presented, the contributions are promising but feel engineering-heavy with underdeveloped evaluation.

Pros for acceptance
- Practical, general pipeline with impressive qualitative demos across diverse 3D characters
- Useful technique to compute feedback gains from direct trajectory optimization
- Sensible noise-injection and data branching that improve stability/generalization
- Simple distributed implementation with reasonable training time

Cons for acceptance
- Insufficient quantitative evaluation and lack of strong baselines
- Soft dynamics at run time with possible non-physical forces
- Under-specified memory component and ablations
- No theoretical guarantees; limited sensitivity analyses

Suggestions for improvement
- Add quantitative benchmarks: success rates, tracking errors, fall frequency, torque/energy, and wall-clock/control latency. Compare to GPS (BADMM/dual variants), TRPO (and/or other modern continuous-control RL), and MPC, on identical tasks.
- Report contact model fidelity at run time (constraint violation magnitudes, friction cone satisfaction). If possible, harden constraints or show a version without root forces.
- Ablate recurrent state r (dimension, necessity) and the effect of outputting contact forces vs. e.g., desired joint torques/accelerations.
- Provide sensitivity analysis for α, η, λ, σε, σγ and network size; clarify the metric used in Section 8.1 and include test-time rollouts under disturbances.
- Clarify positioning relative to guided policy search: discuss trade-offs of quadratic penalties/prox vs. ADMM/BADMM; include a quantitative comparison if feasible.
- Improve references (complete citations) and fix minor typos.

Recommendation
Weak reject. The system is promising and the technical trick for feedback gains is neat, but the paper needs stronger quantitative evaluation and clearer positioning against established baselines to meet NeurIPS standards. With the suggested additions, this would be a compelling submission.
