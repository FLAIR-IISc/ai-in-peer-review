This paper presents a method for learning neural network controllers for complex characters through a combination of trajectory optimization and supervised learning. The core idea is to use Contact-Invariant Optimization (CIO) to generate training data, then train a neural network to mimic the optimized trajectories. The key contribution lies in successfully scaling this approach to 3D locomotion, which previous work struggled with, attributing this success to several technical improvements including noise injection and a distributed stochastic optimization scheme.

The paper is well-structured and clearly presents a complex approach. It begins with a strong motivation, outlining the difficulties of traditional control methods and the potential of learning-based approaches. The overview section provides a concise mathematical formulation of the problem, and the subsequent sections detail the technical components – trajectory optimization, stochastic policy, and distributed optimization – with sufficient clarity. The authors relate their work to existing literature, acknowledging related work in reinforcement learning, optimal control, and neural network controllers. The inclusion of ablation studies and comparison to a naive approach strengthens the evaluation.

**Strengths:**

* **Addresses a Challenging Problem:** Learning controllers for complex, high-dimensional characters is a difficult problem, and this work demonstrates a promising approach.
* **Scalability to 3D:** Successfully extending the trajectory optimization + neural network learning paradigm to 3D locomotion is a significant advancement over prior work, which largely focused on 2D scenarios.
* **Clear Technical Presentation:** The paper is well-written and the technical details are presented in a clear and logical manner. The mathematical formulation is concise and helpful.
* **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The authors provide ablation studies and comparisons to baseline methods, demonstrating the effectiveness of their proposed approach. The inclusion of a supplementary video is invaluable for visualizing the results.
* **Practical Implementation Details:** The description of the distributed training architecture and implementation choices (e.g., using MuJoCo) provides useful insights for reproducibility.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Novelty in Components:** While the *combination* of techniques is effective, many individual components (CIO, neural networks, noise injection, stochastic optimization) are not novel in themselves. The paper acknowledges this but doesn’t sufficiently highlight the unique synergy achieved.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper mentions specific values for algorithmic constants (e.g., noise levels, regularization weights). A more thorough investigation into the sensitivity of the method to these hyperparameters would be beneficial.
* **Lack of Comparison to State-of-the-Art Reinforcement Learning:** The paper briefly mentions reinforcement learning but does not provide a direct comparison to current state-of-the-art RL algorithms for continuous control (e.g., PPO, SAC). This would provide valuable context for evaluating the performance of the proposed method.
* **Computational Cost:** While the paper acknowledges the high computational cost of trajectory optimization, a more detailed analysis of the trade-offs between data generation and network training would be useful.
* **Limited Generalization Discussion:** While noise injection is used to improve generalization, a more rigorous evaluation of the controller's performance on unseen environments or tasks would be beneficial.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a well-engineered and technically sound approach to a challenging problem in robotics and animation.
* The successful extension of the method to 3D locomotion represents a significant advance over previous work.
* The comprehensive evaluation, including ablation studies and comparisons to baseline methods, demonstrates the effectiveness of the proposed approach.
* The paper is clearly written and provides sufficient details for reproducibility.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The individual components of the method are not particularly novel.
* The lack of comparison to state-of-the-art reinforcement learning algorithms limits the ability to assess the method's performance relative to the current state of the art.
* The computational cost of trajectory optimization could be a barrier to wider adoption.



**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a solid contribution to the field of interactive control. While the individual components are not groundbreaking, the combination of techniques and the successful application to 3D locomotion are noteworthy achievements. The clarity of the presentation, the comprehensive evaluation, and the practical implementation details make this a valuable addition to the NIPS proceedings. Despite minor shortcomings, the strengths of the paper outweigh its weaknesses, and I recommend acceptance.
