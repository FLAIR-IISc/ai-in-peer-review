

# Review of "Interactive Control of Diverse Complex Characters with Neural Networks"

This paper proposes a method for training neural network controllers for complex character animation by combining trajectory optimization (Contact-Invariant Optimization) with neural network regression. The key insight is to use trajectory optimization to generate training data, then train a neural network to imitate the optimal behaviors, with special attention to noise injection for robustness. The authors claim to achieve interactive, real-time control for diverse 3D characters including bipeds, quadrupeds, swimmers, and flyers using a single network architecture.

## Strengths

The paper presents a novel integration of trajectory optimization and neural networks for character control, addressing a significant challenge in the field. The approach is well-motivated, and the results demonstrate successful locomotion across diverse character morphologies in 3D space. The authors provide a detailed technical description of their method, including the distributed training architecture and the use of noise injection for robustness. The results section effectively demonstrates the method's capabilities through diverse character examples.

## Weaknesses

Despite the promising results, the paper contains several technical inconsistencies and minor flaws that undermine its scientific rigor:

1. **Reference formatting issues**: Multiple references in the bibliography are incomplete (e.g., missing years, page numbers, and conference details), which is unprofessional for a scientific publication. For example, "Hessian matrix vs. gauss-newton hessian matrix, P. Chen, SIAM J. Numerical Analysis,, , ," lacks crucial publication details.

2. **Mathematical notation errors**: In section 2.1, the equation "a(s + ε) = a + asε" is ambiguously written and should be "a(s + ε) = a + (a_s)ε" to clarify the matrix-vector product. In equation (3), the norm should be squared with proper parentheses: "‖(a + a_sε) − π_θ(s + ε)‖^2".

3. **Mischaracterization of prior work**: The paper states "Wager et al. [19] observed this noise model makes hidden units tend toward saturated regions" - this misrepresents the dropout paper, which actually suggests dropout prevents co-adaptation, not promotes saturation.

4. **Contradictory claims**: The paper states "we use a single network architecture and parameters to create all controllers" but then in section 8.1, "we have compared the performance of different policy network architectures," creating confusion about the scope of the approach.

5. **Inconsistent notation**: The paper uses "f t−1" in the sensory state definition but "f t" in the action definition, creating confusion. The statement "q̇t , qt+1 − qt" incorrectly places a comma between the definition and the equation.

6. **Missing technical details**: The paper claims "our full method performs two orders of magnitude better than static training" without providing the actual metrics or referencing the figure with this data. The paper also fails to clarify whether the 100-200 trials are per character or total.

7. **Formatting errors**: The EC2 instance type is incorrectly written as "3.8xlarge" instead of "c3.8xlarge." The MuJoCo reference is cited without proper attribution to the IROS'12 paper.

## Significance and Originality

The paper addresses an important challenge in character animation, but the combination of trajectory optimization with neural networks is not entirely novel (citing [9, 11]). The paper's main contribution is scaling this approach to 3D complex characters, but the technical presentation of this advancement is undermined by the aforementioned flaws.

## Overall Assessment

While the paper presents an interesting approach with promising results, the numerous technical inconsistencies, formatting errors, and minor mathematical ambiguities significantly detract from the paper's scientific quality. The paper would benefit from careful proofreading and correction of these technical details before publication. As a NIPS reviewer, I must note that these are not just minor typos but rather inconsistencies that affect the paper's credibility and reproducibility.

The paper's main contribution is interesting, but the technical execution is insufficient for acceptance at NIPS. The authors have a good idea but need to address these fundamental issues before the paper can be considered a high-quality scientific contribution. The minor flaws identified here collectively constitute a significant enough problem to warrant rejection, as they undermine the paper's technical soundness.

I recommend rejection due to insufficient technical rigor and presentation quality, despite the potentially interesting contribution. The authors should address these issues before resubmitting to a venue that values precision in scientific writing.
