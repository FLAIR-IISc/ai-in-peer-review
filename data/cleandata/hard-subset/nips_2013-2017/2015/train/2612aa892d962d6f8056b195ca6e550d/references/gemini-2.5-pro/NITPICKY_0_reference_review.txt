### Summary

This paper presents a framework for training neural network control policies for complex, physically-simulated 3D characters. The core contribution is a method that jointly optimizes a set of motion trajectories and the parameters of a neural network. Trajectories are generated using Contact-Invariant Optimization (CIO) to satisfy task goals and physical constraints. The neural network is trained via supervised regression to reproduce these trajectories. This process is structured as an alternating, distributed optimization. To improve robustness and generalization, the authors introduce a stochastic training scheme where noise is added to the network's sensory inputs. Critically, the corresponding target actions are adjusted using optimal feedback gains, which are derived from the trajectory optimizer through a novel perturbation method. The authors demonstrate the versatility of their approach by training a single network architecture to produce stable locomotion for a diverse set of characters, including bipeds, quadrupeds, a swimmer, and a flyer. This work builds upon prior research combining trajectory optimization and policy learning (e.g., [9, 11]), with the primary claimed advance being its successful application to more complex 3D characters and the introduction of a more principled method for stochastic training.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Quality:**

The paper is technically ambitious, and the empirical results (assuming the supplementary video is illustrative) appear impressive. The core concept of using a joint optimization to prevent the compounding errors common in imitation learning is sound. The proposed method for calculating optimal feedback gains from a direct trajectory optimizer (Section 3.2) is a novel technical contribution.

However, the paper suffers from a significant number of technical and methodological shortcomings that undermine its quality:

1.  **Unsubstantiated Claims and Vague Comparisons:** The paper makes several unsubstantiated statements. For instance, the authors state their "impression is that networks trained with direct methods give rise to successful yet somewhat chaotic behaviors." This is a strong claim made without a single quantitative comparison or citation to support it. A rigorous comparison against contemporary policy gradient or actor-critic methods is conspicuously absent.

2.  **Policy Execution is Unclear and Potentially Misleading:** Section 7 describes the policy execution phase. It states that to evolve the state, an optimization problem (Eq. 7 with T=1) is solved at *every timestep*. This is effectively a model-predictive control (MPC) scheme where the neural network only provides a reference target (`q̇des`). This critically reframes the contribution: the system is not a pure neural network controller but a hybrid system that relies on a per-step optimizer at runtime. The paper fails to provide any details on the computational cost of this step, how it is solved at "real-time rates," or how this impacts the overall claim of learning a standalone policy. This is a major omission that borders on misrepresenting the final controller's nature.

3.  **Lack of Rigor in Optimization:** The paper presents a complex, non-convex, block-alternating optimization scheme (Algorithm 1). The authors claim they "still observe convergence as shown in section 8.1," but Figure 2 shows task performance, not the convergence of the objective function (4). Without any empirical or theoretical evidence of convergence, the stability and reliability of the training algorithm are in question.

4.  **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The method relies on at least five critical, manually-tuned hyperparameters (`σε`, `σγ`, `α`, `λ`, `η`). The paper simply lists the values used without any sensitivity analysis or justification for their selection. For example, the parameter `λ` is introduced in Section 3.2 to produce "softer feedback gains," but this is an ad-hoc modification whose effect is not rigorously analyzed.

**Clarity:**

The paper is generally readable, but lacks the precision expected in a top-tier publication.

1.  **Notational Inconsistencies:** The notation is occasionally imprecise. For example, velocity is defined as "q̇t , qt+1 − qt", conflating a continuous derivative with its finite-difference approximation. The mapping in Section 2 is written as "s 7→ a" instead of the standard "s ↦ a".

2.  **Typos and Formatting:** There are several typographical errors (e.g., "cyclical patter" instead of "pattern," "changed changed" in Section 8). The complementarity constraint in Eq. (6) appears to contain a typo ("d(q)>f = 0" should likely be a dot product `d(q)ᵀf = 0`). Several bibliographic entries are incomplete (e.g., [1], [4], [10]).

3.  **Explanations Lack Depth:** The derivation of optimal feedback gains in Section 3.2 is presented in a dense, purely mathematical fashion without sufficient intuition, particularly around the use of the Woodbury identity and the final expression for the gains.

**Originality:**

The work is an incremental, though substantial, step beyond prior art [9, 11]. The primary novelty lies in the specific combination of CIO with a stochastic neural network policy and the method for deriving feedback gains. The application to a diverse set of 3D characters is more of an engineering achievement and demonstration of scalability than a fundamental algorithmic innovation.

**Significance:**

The demonstrated ability to create controllers for a wide range of morphologies from a single, unified pipeline is significant for fields like computer animation and robotics. If the method is as general as claimed, it could reduce the extensive manual effort typically required for such tasks.

However, the significance is tempered by the weaknesses outlined above. The reliance on a complex, difficult-to-tune optimization pipeline and the unclear nature of the real-time execution step may limit its practical adoption by other researchers. The lack of comparison to modern deep reinforcement learning methods also makes it difficult to assess where this work stands in the broader landscape of control.

### Arguments for Acceptance:
1.  The paper tackles a challenging and important problem: automatic synthesis of controllers for complex 3D characters.
2.  The empirical results demonstrate a high degree of generality across diverse character morphologies, which is a notable achievement.
3.  The proposed method for integrating feedback gains into the learning of a stochastic policy is clever and potentially applicable elsewhere.

### Arguments for Rejection:
1.  The description of the policy execution at runtime (Section 7) is critically underdeveloped and suggests the final controller is a hybrid MPC system, not the standalone neural network policy implied throughout the paper.
2.  The paper lacks rigorous experimental validation, including a proper analysis of the algorithm's convergence, sensitivity to numerous hyperparameters, and quantitative comparison to relevant baselines (e.g., deep RL methods).
3.  The manuscript suffers from a lack of precision, including vague claims, notational sloppiness, and numerous small errors, which collectively detract from its scientific quality.

### Recommendation

I recommend **rejection**. While the paper presents an interesting direction and visually impressive results, the accumulation of minor and major flaws is substantial. The lack of clarity surrounding the real-time execution model is a primary concern, as it calls into question the fundamental nature of the learned controller. Furthermore, the absence of rigorous analysis regarding convergence, hyperparameter sensitivity, and comparison to alternative methods prevents this work, in its current form, from meeting the high standards of a NIPS publication. The paper would need a major revision to address these critical issues of technical soundness and clarity.
