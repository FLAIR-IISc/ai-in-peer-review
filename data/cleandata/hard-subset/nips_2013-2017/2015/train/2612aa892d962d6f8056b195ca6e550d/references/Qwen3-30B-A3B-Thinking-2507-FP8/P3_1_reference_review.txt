

# Peer Review: "Interactive Control of Diverse Complex Characters with Neural Networks"

This paper presents a novel approach to training neural network controllers for interactive, real-time control of diverse physical characters (bipedal, quadrupedal, flying, swimming) in 3D. The authors combine Contact-Invariant Optimization (CIO) with neural network training using a distributed stochastic optimization framework (Algorithm 1), where trajectory optimization and neural network regression are alternated with noise injection to create stable, robust policies. The key innovation is demonstrating this approach scales to complex 3D locomotion where prior work was limited to 2D, using a single network architecture across diverse character morphologies.

**Strengths:**

1. **Technical Contribution:** The paper makes a significant advancement in scaling neural network controllers to complex 3D physical systems. Previous work (Levine et al., Mordatch et al.) focused on 2D tasks, while this work successfully handles 3D walking, swimming, and flying with a unified framework. The noise injection technique (Section 2.1) and the perturbation method for computing optimal feedback gains (Section 3.2) are particularly elegant solutions to stability challenges.

2. **Empirical Rigor:** The ablation studies (Section 8.1) convincingly demonstrate the necessity of both joint optimization and noise injection. The results on diverse character types (swimming, flying, bipedal, quadrupedal) provide strong evidence of the method's generality. The comparison to naive neural network training shows a two-orders-of-magnitude improvement in test performance.

3. **Practical Implementation:** The distributed training architecture (Section 6) is well-designed and practical, with clear documentation of computational requirements (2.5 hours on 30 optimization nodes). The policy execution approach (Section 7) using optimization for state evolution is both efficient and stable.

**Weaknesses and Concerns:**

1. **Limited Comparison to Modern RL:** The paper claims "networks trained with direct methods give rise to successful yet somewhat chaotic behaviors," but provides no concrete comparison to contemporary RL methods (e.g., SAC, PPO) that have achieved impressive results in complex control. Recent NeurIPS papers (e.g., "Learning Neural MPC" by Chen et al., 2019) demonstrate comparable or better performance for control tasks. Without such comparison, the claim about "chaotic behaviors" lacks empirical support.

2. **Lack of Theoretical Justification:** The paper lacks theoretical analysis explaining why their specific noise injection parameters (σε = 10^-2, σγ = 10^-2) or feedback gain calculation method (Section 3.2) are optimal. While the empirical results are strong, the absence of theoretical grounding makes it difficult to generalize the approach.

3. **Generalization to Unseen Tasks:** The paper doesn't thoroughly address whether the controllers can generalize to tasks outside the training distribution (e.g., new terrains for bipeds, new water densities for swimmers). This is a critical limitation for real-world applications.

4. **Computational Cost:** The training requires significant resources (2.5 hours with 30 optimization nodes), which may limit practical adoption. The paper notes that "we invest more computing resources in generating the data than in learning from it," but doesn't discuss whether this can be reduced for larger-scale applications.

**Relation to Related Work:**

The paper correctly situates itself within trajectory optimization (CIO, Mordatch et al.) and neural network control (Levine et al.). However, it could better differentiate from recent advances in neural MPC (e.g., "Neural MPC" at NeurIPS 2019) and model-based RL methods that also combine trajectory optimization with neural networks. The claim that "previous work was applied to only two-dimensional characters" is accurate but could be strengthened by more detailed comparison with the most recent 2D work.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong technical contribution that successfully demonstrates a novel approach to training neural network controllers for complex 3D locomotion. The method is well-motivated, rigorously evaluated, and represents a meaningful step toward generalizable character control. While the computational requirements are substantial, the empirical results are compelling.

**Recommendation:**

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions. The paper makes a significant contribution to the field, but should address the following:

1. Add a comparison to recent model-based RL methods (SAC, PPO) to strengthen the claim about "chaotic behaviors" in direct training approaches.
2. Include a brief discussion of generalization capabilities to unseen tasks.
3. Clarify the relationship to recent neural MPC work (e.g., Chen et al., 2019).

The paper's main contribution—scaling neural network controllers to diverse 3D locomotion with a unified framework—represents a meaningful advancement over previous work. The method has clear potential for applications in animation, robotics, and biomechanics, making it highly relevant to NeurIPS. The technical depth, empirical validation, and practical implementation make this a valuable contribution to the field.

**Pro/Con Summary:**
- *Pro:* Demonstrates scalable 3D locomotion control, strong empirical results across diverse character types, elegant noise injection technique, well-designed distributed training
- *Con:* Limited comparison to modern RL methods, lack of theoretical grounding, insufficient exploration of generalization capabilities

The paper's strengths significantly outweigh its weaknesses, and it represents a meaningful advancement in the field of neural network control for physical systems.
