**Summary of the Paper**

This paper presents a method for training a single neural network policy to produce interactive, real-time control for a diverse range of physically-simulated 3D characters. The core idea is to combine the strengths of offline trajectory optimization and supervised learning. The authors propose a joint optimization framework that alternates between optimizing a set of motion trajectories using Contact-Invariant Optimization (CIO) and training a neural network to regress onto these trajectories. The key technical contributions that enable this method to work on complex 3D characters include: 1) A principled method for injecting noise into the training process to improve robustness, where the target actions for noisy states are computed using a first-order approximation based on optimal feedback gains. 2) A novel and efficient technique to compute these feedback gains directly from the CIO trajectory optimizer, which typically does not produce them. 3) A distributed, asynchronous optimization algorithm to make this computationally demanding process feasible. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness and generality of their approach by successfully training controllers for various morphologies, including bipedal, quadrupedal, swimming, and flying characters, showing that complex and natural-looking gaits emerge without task-specific engineering. This work builds upon prior research that combines trajectory optimization and policy learning (e.g., Levine & Koltun, 2014; Mordatch & Todorov, 2014), but extends it to significantly more complex 3D characters and behaviors, which represents a substantial step forward.

**Strengths**

1.  **Significance and Impact:** The results presented are highly impressive and significant. The ability to use a single, unified algorithm to generate stable, interactive controllers for such a wide variety of character morphologies (bipeds, quadrupeds, swimmers, fliers) is a major achievement. The emergent behaviors, such as natural walking and trotting gaits, demonstrate the power of the optimization framework and its potential for high-impact applications in computer animation, game development, and robotics.

2.  **Originality and Technical Quality:** The paper contains several novel and well-motivated technical contributions. The method of using optimal feedback gains (`as`) to generate targets for noisy sensory inputs (`s + ε`) is a clever way to train a policy that is inherently robust to perturbations. Furthermore, the proposed perturbation-based method for extracting these feedback gains from a direct trajectory optimizer like CIO is a valuable contribution in its own right and could be useful to other researchers using similar optimization tools. The formulation of the joint optimization problem and its distributed implementation is sound and well-executed.

3.  **Thorough Experimental Validation:** The ablation studies in Section 8.1 are convincing and clearly demonstrate the necessity of the core components of the proposed method. The "no joint" experiment effectively shows that iteratively refining the trajectories and the policy together is far superior to a one-shot "generate then train" approach. Similarly, the "no noise" experiment highlights the critical role of stochasticity in preventing overfitting and achieving robust controllers that do not diverge at runtime. The diversity of characters tested serves as a strong testament to the generality of the algorithm.

**Weaknesses and Constructive Suggestions**

1.  **Comparison to State-of-the-Art:** While the paper provides a compelling self-contained evaluation, the comparison to alternative methods could be stronger. The comparison to MPC is only qualitative. More importantly, the paper contrasts its approach with model-free reinforcement learning (e.g., TRPO, DDPG) by suggesting that RL produces "somewhat chaotic behaviors." This is a strong claim that is not substantiated with evidence. Including a direct quantitative and qualitative comparison to a state-of-the-art deep RL algorithm on at least one of the characters (e.g., the biped) would significantly strengthen the paper. Such a comparison could evaluate metrics like task success rate, energy efficiency, and stability, providing a more concrete assessment of where this method stands relative to the broader control literature.

2.  **Reproducibility:** The paper is well-written, but some details crucial for reproducibility are sparse. The cost functions `C(X)` are only described at a high level ("minimizing torque usage and contact force magnitudes"). Providing the exact mathematical form of the costs, including the weights for each term, would be invaluable for others looking to build on this work. This could be included in an appendix. Additionally, the policy execution step (Section 7), where a `T=1` optimization is solved in real-time, could be elaborated on. For example, how many optimization steps are taken per frame, and what is the typical wall-clock time?

3.  **Scope of Tasks:** The framework is demonstrated on locomotion tasks where the goal is to reach a target position and orientation. The data generation scheme in Section 5 is specifically designed for this. While the conclusion mentions extending the work to behaviors like getting up or climbing, it is not clear how the current trajectory generation strategy would apply to such non-locomotor or more sequential tasks. A brief discussion of the potential challenges and necessary modifications to handle a wider repertoire of interactive behaviors would be a valuable addition.

4.  **Minor Clarity:** The notation for the state and action in Section 2 could be slightly confusing. The state `s_t` includes `f_{t-1}`, while the action `a_t` includes `f_t`. This suggests the policy predicts the contact forces for the current timestep, which are then used as an input for the next. This is likely resolved by the policy execution model in Section 7 (where `f_des` is a target for an optimization), but clarifying the flow of information in Section 2 would improve readability.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   **Pro:** The paper demonstrates a significant leap forward in automated character control, showcasing a single algorithm that works for a remarkable diversity of complex 3D characters.
*   **Pro:** The core technical ideas—particularly the principled use of noise via learned feedback gains and the method for computing them—are novel, insightful, and well-validated.
*   **Pro:** The results are compelling and the work has clear potential for high impact in multiple fields.
*   **Con:** The experimental comparison to other state-of-the-art methods, especially from the deep RL community, is lacking, making it difficult to fully situate the contribution.
*   **Con:** Some details required for full reproducibility are omitted.

**Decision**

Overall, the strengths of this paper far outweigh its weaknesses. The impressive generality and quality of the results, combined with the novelty of the core technical contributions, make this a strong paper that will be of great interest to the NIPS community. My recommendation is a clear **Accept**. The suggestions for improvement, particularly regarding a more direct comparison with RL methods and the inclusion of more implementation details, would further enhance the quality and impact of this excellent work.
