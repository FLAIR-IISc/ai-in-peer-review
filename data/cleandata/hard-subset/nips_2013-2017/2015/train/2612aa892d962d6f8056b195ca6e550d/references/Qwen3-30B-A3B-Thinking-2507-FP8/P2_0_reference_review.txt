

# Review: "Interactive Control of Diverse Complex Characters with Neural Networks"

## Summary
This paper proposes a novel method for generating neural network controllers that enable interactive, real-time control of diverse physical characters (bipedal, quadrupedal, flying, and swimming) through a combination of trajectory optimization and neural network training. The authors build on Contact-Invariant Optimization (CIO) to generate optimal trajectories, then train a neural network to mimic these trajectories while incorporating noise injection for robustness. The approach differs from prior work [9, 11] by extending to three-dimensional locomotion and demonstrating success across a wide range of character morphologies without specialized controllers for each type.

## Quality
The paper presents a technically sound approach that carefully addresses key challenges in neural network-based control. The authors provide a clear mathematical formulation of their distributed stochastic optimization algorithm (Algorithm 1) and demonstrate its effectiveness through a comprehensive ablation study (Section 8.1). The method's superiority over naive neural network training (two orders of magnitude better performance) and the critical role of noise injection are well-supported by experimental results. The paper acknowledges limitations, including computational requirements (hours of training) and the need for trajectory optimization to generate training data. The authors correctly identify that their method is complementary to reinforcement learning approaches rather than a replacement, and they thoughtfully discuss the tradeoffs between their approach and direct RL methods (Section 1).

## Clarity
The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with logical progression from problem statement to technical details to results. The mathematical formulations are precise, though some sections (particularly Section 3 on trajectory optimization) could benefit from more intuitive explanations. The authors effectively use figures (e.g., Figure 2, Figure 3) to illustrate key concepts. The supplementary video is a strong asset that would greatly enhance understanding of the results, though its absence from the main paper is a minor drawback. The paper would be improved by more detailed explanation of the sensory state representation (Section 2.1) and the connection between the trajectory optimization and neural network training phases.

## Originality
The paper makes a significant contribution by extending previous trajectory-optimization-based methods [9, 11] to three-dimensional locomotion with diverse character types. The key technical innovations include:
1) The principled use of optimal feedback gains for noise injection (Section 2.2)
2) The distributed stochastic optimization approach (Algorithm 1) that avoids complex optimization techniques like ADMM
3) The demonstration of a single controller architecture working across vastly different character morphologies

This represents a meaningful extension of prior work rather than a simple application of known techniques. The authors clearly differentiate their approach from related work (Section 1, Section 8.1), particularly noting that previous methods were limited to two-dimensional characters.

## Significance
The paper addresses a problem of substantial practical importance in animation, gaming, and robotics. The ability to generate controllers that work across diverse character types without specialized hand-crafted controllers represents a significant step toward more generalizable character control systems. The demonstration of successful locomotion for swimming, flying, bipedal, and quadrupedal characters shows the method's broad applicability. The approach could have meaningful impact on robotics, where robustness to perturbations is critical, as the authors note in Section 9. While the paper focuses on locomotion, the method's potential for other behaviors (e.g., climbing, reaching) is noted as future work.

## Strengths
- Strong technical contribution with clear ablation study demonstrating the necessity of key innovations
- Demonstrated success across diverse character types in 3D (a significant extension from prior work)
- Practical implementation with distributed training architecture that scales well
- Clear theoretical justification for the method's components (e.g., optimal feedback gains for noise injection)
- Well-organized and well-written paper with strong experimental validation

## Weaknesses
- Limited comparison with state-of-the-art reinforcement learning approaches (beyond Section 1)
- The neural network architecture is not thoroughly explored beyond a small ablation on size
- The paper doesn't fully address the limitations of the approach (e.g., character types it cannot handle)
- Computational requirements (hours of training) are not compared to other methods
- The paper doesn't discuss how the method could be extended to more complex behaviors beyond locomotion

## Recommendation
This paper makes a significant contribution to the field of neural network-based control for physical systems. The method represents a meaningful advance over previous work in trajectory-optimization-based controllers, particularly in its extension to three-dimensional locomotion across diverse character morphologies. The technical soundness, clear ablation study, and practical implementation make this a strong contribution to NeurIPS.

The paper is well-suited for NeurIPS as it combines novel technical contributions with a clear application in a domain where deep learning can provide tangible benefits. While the paper has minor weaknesses in comparison to state-of-the-art RL methods and detailed exploration of limitations, these do not detract from the paper's substantial contribution.

**Overall Recommendation: Accept**

The paper makes a clear, significant contribution to the field with strong technical execution, thorough validation, and meaningful application. It advances the state of the art in interactive character control and demonstrates how combining trajectory optimization with neural networks can yield robust, generalizable solutions.
