Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes a method to learn interactive, real-time controllers for complex 3D characters by alternating between direct trajectory optimization (Contact-Invariant Optimization, CIO) and supervised neural policy regression. The core idea is to generate a set of task-driven optimal trajectories (and associated local feedback information) with CIO and to train a neural network policy to imitate these trajectories under noisy sensory inputs. To improve robustness, the authors inject Gaussian noise into policy inputs and use a first-order correction a(s+ε) ≈ a + asε, where as are local feedback gains computed efficiently from the trajectory optimizer via a soft-constraint perturbation analysis that reuses the Gauss-Newton Hessian. Training proceeds with an asynchronous, distributed scheme: multiple nodes produce updated trajectories (with resampled noise) while a GPU trains the policy with proximal regularization to stabilize alternating updates. At execution, the policy outputs desired velocities, recurrent-state rates, and contact forces; the next state is obtained by solving a one-step constrained optimization enforcing dynamics and contact constraints (softly), enabling larger time steps.

This approach is closely related to guided policy search methods that combine trajectory optimization and supervised learning (e.g., Levine & Koltun, ICML’14; Mordatch & Todorov, RSS’14) and to work on physics-based character control (SIMBICON; ZMP/capture point methods), but targets a single architecture that generalizes across diverse morphologies and 3D settings. Compared to prior GPS-like work, the paper’s distinct contributions are: (i) generating noise-robust supervised targets using feedback gains derived from direct trajectory optimization, (ii) a simple asynchronous proximal alternation scheme, (iii) dataset generation via branched segments with changing goals, and (iv) a one-step constrained projection for real-time execution.

Strengths
- Addresses challenging 3D control across multiple morphologies (bipeds, quadrupeds, swimmer, flier) with a single policy architecture and training pipeline; results are qualitatively compelling for animation.
- Practical and principled robustness measures: sensory noise during training and first-order action correction using local feedback gains.
- Efficient computation of feedback gains from a direct trajectory optimizer via a soft-constraint perturbation that reuses the Gauss-Newton inverse; likely broadly useful beyond this application.
- Asynchronous distributed training with proximal regularization is simple and scalable; training completes in hours.
- Ablations indicate the necessity of joint optimization and noise injection; architecture sweep provided.
- Real-time execution via a single-step constrained update avoids explicit integration instabilities and is implementation-friendly.

Weaknesses
- Limited quantitative evaluation and comparisons: no head-to-head with guided policy search baselines (e.g., iLQG/LQG-based GPS), model-free continuous control (e.g., TRPO), or MPC on identical tasks with matched costs. Claims of improved stability/realism are largely qualitative.
- Physical realism at test time relies on soft enforcement of dynamics and contact complementarity (allowing residual “root forces” and slight penetrations). This undermines claims of robotics relevance and makes it hard to assess physical fidelity.
- Action space includes contact forces, which are not directly controllable in most physical systems; implications for transfer are not addressed.
- The policy’s “recurrent state” r is under-specified (dimension, semantics, initialization), and its contribution is not analyzed. The network is otherwise feedforward with tanh activations; no motivation for this particular memory design vs standard RNNs/LSTMs.
- Convergence/stability of the alternating, asynchronous scheme is empirical; no sensitivity study on α, η, λ, or on the conditioning of feedback gains. The choice to freeze noise during NN training is expedient but may bias updates.
- Reproducibility could be improved: task costs are only broadly described; exact features φ, state/action dimensions, and solver tolerances are not fully specified; references have incomplete bibliographic data.
- Dataset generation covers only goal changes; no evaluation of robustness to external perturbations, terrain variability, or broader task variations.

Questions and requests for clarification
- What is the dimensionality and role of r? How is it initialized per rollout and why is ṙ a useful control output vs using a standard RNN?
- How sensitive are results to the feedback-gain soft-constraint weight λ? Please report gain norms/condition numbers and performance vs λ.
- During execution, what are the magnitudes of root forces, complementarity violations, and penetrations? Please provide quantitative metrics.
- Can the method succeed if forces are not included in the action (i.e., let the dynamics determine contact forces)? How does this affect stability and realism?
- Provide quantitative comparisons to a GPS baseline (e.g., iLQG-derived gains) and to MPC with the same costs. Also report success rates, trajectory costs, and robustness under disturbances.
- What frame rates do you achieve for different characters and hardware? How does performance scale with dataset size and number of branches?

Assessment by criteria
- Quality: Methodologically sound and well-motivated; the feedback-gain derivation is correct under quadratic approximation and reuses available computations. Empirical evidence supports the role of joint optimization and noise. However, the evaluation lacks strong quantitative baselines and physical fidelity metrics.
- Clarity: Generally readable with a coherent algorithmic narrative. Important details are missing or brief (definition and role of r, exact cost terms, dimensions), and notation can be dense. Several typos/formatting issues (e.g., algorithm step numbering, references).
- Originality: Combines known elements (trajectory optimization + supervised policy learning) in a GPS-like framework. The gain computation from a direct optimizer and noisy target generation are worthwhile incremental contributions; the breadth of 3D application is notable for graphics/control.
- Significance: For animation/interactive control, the ability to train a single policy per character family for diverse 3D behaviors is impactful. For ML/control, the contribution is incremental; significance would increase with rigorous comparisons and stricter physics at execution.

Arguments for acceptance
- Practical and scalable method with clear engineering contributions that produce diverse, stable 3D behaviors.
- Novel and useful technique for extracting stabilizing feedback gains from direct trajectory optimization.
- Clear ablations demonstrating necessity of joint optimization and noise.

Arguments against acceptance
- Insufficient quantitative comparisons to strong baselines and limited physical fidelity at execution time.
- Under-specified components (recurrent state), and lack of analysis on sensitivity/convergence.
- Transfer to robotics and generalization claims are speculative without supporting metrics.

Overall, this is a solid and practically motivated contribution with promising results for interactive character control, but it would benefit from stronger empirical comparisons and clearer specification of key components.
