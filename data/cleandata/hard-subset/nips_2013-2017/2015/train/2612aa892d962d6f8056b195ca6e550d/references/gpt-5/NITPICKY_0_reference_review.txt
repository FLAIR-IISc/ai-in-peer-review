Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes an alternating optimization framework that combines direct trajectory optimization (Contact-Invariant Optimization, CIO) with supervised neural policy learning for interactive, real-time control of complex 3D characters. Key ingredients are: (i) adding a quadratic penalty to encourage the optimizer’s trajectories to be compatible with a parametric policy, (ii) injecting Gaussian noise in sensory inputs and hidden units, (iii) deriving local optimal feedback gains “for free” from the trajectory optimizer via a perturbation method that reuses the Gauss–Newton inverse, and (iv) executing the learned policy online by solving a single-step constrained optimization to match desired velocities/forces while softly enforcing physics.

This is close in spirit to Guided Policy Search / trajectory optimization + supervised learning (e.g., Levine & Koltun, 2014; Mordatch & Todorov, 2014), and model-based control with implicit dynamics (MuJoCo). The paper claims improved scalability to 3D locomotion and a new way to extract feedback gains from a direct optimizer. The application to diverse morphologies (swimmer, bird, biped, quadruped) is appealing.

Strengths
- Ambitious scope: a single pipeline producing interactive controllers for multiple 3D morphologies with the same architecture and hyperparameters.
- Practical training system: asynchronous distributed trajectory optimization + GPU policy fitting; branching dataset generation for interactive goals; sensible noise injection for robustness.
- Technical contribution: perturbation-based computation of feedback gains that reuses CXX−1 from the Gauss–Newton solver, potentially avoiding ill-conditioned LQG post-processing.
- Clear articulation of the alternating objective and the rationale for injecting input noise paired with linear feedback targets a + as ε.
- Implementation uses standard components (MuJoCo, Newton/GAuss–Newton) and could be of interest to practitioners.

Weaknesses and detailed concerns
Quality and empirical support
- Evaluation is largely qualitative and video-dependent; quantitative metrics and comparisons are insufficient. The “two orders of magnitude” improvement in Fig. 2 is referenced but the metric (units, definition, test conditions) is not defined. No statistics (means/variances over seeds), no sample efficiency, and no generalization tests (e.g., to unseen goal distributions).
- Baselines: There is no systematic comparison to standard RL/model-based methods for continuous control (e.g., TRPO [14], GPS variants, iLQG-based MPC with learned policies) on the same tasks. The MPC comparison is qualitative and on a single bird example.
- Real-time execution relies on a T=1 optimization with soft physics constraints and explicitly allows root forces. This undermines claims about physical realism and robotics relevance; contact forces are Lagrange multipliers, not controllable actions, and permitting root forces makes evaluation incomparable to true physics-based control.

Technical clarity and correctness
- State/action definitions are inconsistent with dynamics: s_t = [q_t, r_t, q̇_{t−1}, f_{t−1}] and a_t = [q̇_t, ṙ_t, f_t]. Velocities are not included in the “state” q, yet the EoM (6) use q̇ and q̈. How τ is obtained at execution time is unclear; the one-step objective does not penalize τ but the constraints include τ. The use of previous-step velocities/forces in s_t vs current in a_t is unusual and could introduce off-by-one artifacts.
- Treating contact forces as action variables is physically questionable; on hardware they are consequences of state/torque under contact constraints. CIO’s contact-invariant formulation makes this workable offline, but online execution with soft constraints and desired f potentially yields unphysical solutions.
- Feedback gains derivation: the Woodbury-based expression for X̃_s̄ seems dimensionally plausible, but the algebra as written likely misses a factor of λ in the final form (check λ(CXX + λ sX^T sX)−1 sX^T = CXX−1 sX^T((1/λ)I + sX CXX−1 sX^T)−1). Sensitivity to λ (set to 10^2) and numerical conditioning are not analyzed. No ablation shows the benefit of these gains vs. simple finite-difference Jacobians or no gains.
- Algorithm 1 lacks precision: proximal terms are written as “η2 ||·||2” with unclear scaling; freezing ε during NN training breaks the stated stochastic objective (3)–(4) and the paper does not justify the bias introduced or its effect on convergence.
- The “recurrent memory state” r is central but underspecified: dimension, initialization beyond “null,” and its role in the network (external continuous memory vs. internal recurrent units) are not detailed. The network appears feedforward with r fed back via s, but the paper calls the controller “recurrent” without architectural specifics.
- Many crucial training details are missing for reproducibility: trajectory horizon T, frame count per trial/segment, exact costs and weights in C(X), learning rates, batch sizes, normalization, gradient clipping, line search/trust region for Newton steps, stopping criteria, and the numerical tolerances that enable real-time T=1 solves at 50 ms timesteps.

Originality and significance
- The high-level alternating framework and noise-injected supervised learning from optimized trajectories are not new (GPS family). The novel part is the feedback-gain extraction from CIO and the system integration to 3D characters. Without rigorous quantitative evidence and stronger comparisons, it is hard to assess the substantive advance over prior work.

Clarity and presentation
- References are incomplete and inconsistently formatted (missing years/venues/pages; e.g., [1], [2], many entries). Several typos/grammar issues: “unity these disparate approaches” → unify; “changed changed”; “controller learned”; “patter” → pattern; missing articles throughout. Figures/tables (Fig. 2, 3; Table 1) are referenced but not provided in the text I saw.
- Some notation is sloppy: x vs X, q̇ defined as qt+1 − qt but later used in continuous EoM; missing “des” superscript in one place; ambiguous use of norms without weights/units.

Questions for the authors
- Provide precise definitions and units for the evaluation metric(s) in Fig. 2; include full numerical results and comparisons to TRPO/GPS baselines on identical tasks.
- Clarify the role and dimension of r, and provide full training hyperparameters (learning rate schedule, batch size, optimizer details).
- Analyze sensitivity to λ, α, σε, σγ, η; show effect on stability and performance.
- Quantify physical realism: what fraction of timesteps require nonzero root forces during execution? How often are friction cone and no-penetration constraints violated?
- Report real-time performance (fps), horizon lengths T, per-iteration timings for the T=1 solver, and total dataset size in frames.

Pros
- Ambitious system with diverse 3D results; practical engineering contributions.
- Clever reuse of Gauss–Newton inverse for feedback gains.
- Clear motivation for noise-injected targets and alternating optimization.

Cons
- Insufficient quantitative evaluation and weak baselines.
- Physical/action modeling mismatch (contact forces as actions; soft root forces).
- Several technical/notation issues and missing details that hinder reproducibility.
- Presentation and references need substantial polishing.

Recommendation: Reject. Interesting direction with promising ideas, but the current submission lacks rigorous evaluation, has several technical ambiguities, and requires significant clarification and polishing to meet NIPS standards.

Confidence: High.
