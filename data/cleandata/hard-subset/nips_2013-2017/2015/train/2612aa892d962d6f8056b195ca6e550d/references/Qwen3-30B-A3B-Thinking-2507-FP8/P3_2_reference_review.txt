

# Peer Review: "Interactive Control of Diverse Complex Characters with Neural Networks"

## Summary
This paper proposes a method to automatically generate neural network controllers for diverse character types (bipedal, quadrupedal, flying, swimming) in 3D environments. The approach combines trajectory optimization (specifically Contact-Invariant Optimization, CIO) with neural network training, where the network learns to replicate optimized trajectories while being robust to noise. The key innovation is a distributed stochastic optimization algorithm that alternates between optimizing trajectories and training the neural network, with principled noise injection to improve robustness. The authors claim their method achieves stable 3D locomotion for the first time, unlike prior work limited to 2D.

## Strengths
The paper makes a significant contribution to character control by demonstrating a unified framework that handles diverse morphologies without task-specific hand-crafted controllers. The technical approach is well-motivated: trajectory optimization generates high-quality training data, while the neural network provides real-time control. The distributed stochastic optimization framework (Algorithm 1) effectively handles the joint optimization problem, and the noise injection strategy (Section 2.1) is theoretically grounded with a clear connection to optimal feedback gains. The ablation studies (Section 8.1) convincingly demonstrate the necessity of both joint optimization and noise injection, showing two orders of magnitude better performance than naive approaches.

The experimental results are impressive, showing stable locomotion across multiple character types in 3D (swimming, flying, bipedal, quadrupedal) with a single network architecture. This represents a substantial advancement beyond prior work limited to 2D scenarios. The supplementary video would have been particularly valuable for demonstrating the quality of the learned behaviors.

## Weaknesses
While the paper claims to solve "substantially harder problems" than prior work, it fails to adequately distinguish itself from closely related approaches. The authors reference [9, 11] but don't sufficiently explain how their distributed stochastic optimization differs from ADMM or dual gradient descent approaches. The paper also lacks a comprehensive comparison to other neural network control methods (e.g., [3, 6, 14]), which is critical for establishing the method's relative strengths.

The theoretical foundation for the distributed optimization approach is underdeveloped. While the paper provides empirical convergence results (Section 8.1), it lacks formal analysis of the optimization guarantees or convergence rates. The claim that the method "solves substantially harder problems" would be stronger with quantitative comparisons to prior work on similar 3D locomotion tasks.

The paper also has some technical gaps: the perturbation method for optimal feedback gains (Section 3.2) is described but not thoroughly validated. The relationship between the noise parameters (σε, σγ) and the quality of the learned policies is not systematically explored. The paper mentions using "a single network architecture and parameters" for all controllers, but doesn't analyze how the network size affects performance across different character types.

## Pro and Con Arguments
**Pro:**
- Demonstrates a unified framework for diverse 3D locomotion without task-specific controllers
- Provides a clear, principled approach to handling noise for robust control
- Shows convincing empirical results across multiple character types
- Offers a practical solution to a longstanding challenge in character control

**Con:**
- Insufficient distinction from prior work on trajectory-optimization-based methods
- Limited theoretical foundation for the distributed optimization approach
- Lacks comprehensive comparison to alternative neural network control methods
- Incomplete analysis of hyperparameters and their impact on performance

## Recommendation
This paper makes a valuable contribution to the field of character control by demonstrating a unified approach to 3D locomotion across diverse character types. The technical approach is sound, the results are impressive, and the method has clear practical applications. However, the paper would benefit from stronger theoretical grounding and more thorough comparison to related work.

The significance is high for the animation and robotics communities, as it provides a practical solution to a challenging problem. While the paper doesn't represent a fundamental theoretical advance, it makes a substantial practical contribution that could be widely adopted.

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the comparative analysis and theoretical gaps. The authors should strengthen the discussion of how their approach differs from [9, 11] and provide a more systematic analysis of the noise parameters and their impact on performance.

Overall, this is a strong paper that advances the state of the art in character control, with clear potential for practical applications. It represents a meaningful contribution to the field that would be valuable to the NIPS community.
