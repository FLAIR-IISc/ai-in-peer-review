Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes an approach to learn interactive, real-time controllers for diverse, high-DOF simulated characters by alternating between trajectory optimization (Contact-Invariant Optimization, CIO) and supervised policy learning with deep networks. The key ideas are: (i) generate optimal trajectories for tasks (e.g., locomotion to a goal) using CIO; (ii) learn a neural policy πθ mapping sensory inputs to actions via regression; (iii) inject Gaussian sensory noise and use local linear feedback gains (computed from the trajectory optimization via a perturbation analysis reusing Gauss-Newton structure) to form noise-perturbed training targets a + As ε; (iv) perform block-alternating optimization with quadratic penalties and proximal terms, asynchronously distributed over many CPU nodes and a GPU. At test time, the policy outputs desired velocities and contact forces, and the next state is found by solving a 1-step constrained optimization (T=1 CIO) to enforce dynamics and contacts. The authors demonstrate interactive locomotion for swimming, flying, bipeds, and quadrupeds in 3D, and provide ablations on joint optimization and noise injection.

This line of work is closely related to prior methods that combine trajectory optimization and supervised learning for control, notably Guided Policy Search (Levine & Koltun, ICML’14) and Mordatch & Todorov (RSS’14), as well as model-free policy optimization (e.g., TRPO). The paper cites these, claiming technical improvements (feedback gain computation for direct methods, distributed asynchronous training, and 3D demonstrations).

Strengths
- Tackles a challenging problem (real-time control for diverse, contact-rich characters) using a sensible combination of established tools (trajectory optimization + supervised learning).
- The perturbation-based derivation of local feedback gains that reuses CIO’s Gauss-Newton machinery is practically appealing and computationally efficient.
- Engineering contributions (asynchronous distributed training with proximal stabilization; GPU SGD; policy regularization via noise at inputs and hidden units) are thoughtfully integrated.
- Broad qualitative demonstrations across morphologies with a single network architecture and minimal per-task tuning are compelling from an animation standpoint.
- Ablations indicate that joint optimization and noise injection matter for closed-loop rollout stability.

Weaknesses and evaluation against review criteria

Quality
- Empirical evaluation is largely qualitative. There are no quantitative metrics of control performance (success rate, stability, energy/torque cost, tracking error, recovery under perturbations), nor statistical results across seeds/initial conditions. The lone claim of “two orders of magnitude better” lacks units, definitions, and figures in the submission.
- Baselines are weak. Comparisons are only to ablations (no joint optimization, no noise). There is no comparison to established alternatives: Guided Policy Search (GPS), TRPO, DDPG/SVG, model predictive control with learned dynamics, or classic controllers (e.g., SIMBICON, ZMP/capture point for bipeds). The qualitative note that MPC yields non-cyclic trajectories is insufficient.
- The execution-time controller is not purely a feedforward neural policy: a 1-step constrained optimization (T=1 CIO) is solved every step to enforce dynamics/contacts and match network outputs. This is closer to a short-horizon MPC/implicit integrator than a pure policy. The computational cost and sensitivity to solver parameters are not reported; it is unclear how much of the robustness stems from the optimizer vs. the network.
- Dynamics are enforced with soft constraints, and the authors note possible “root forces.” Allowing unphysical forces undermines claims about physical realism and limits relevance to robotics. It also makes comparisons to physically valid baselines problematic.
- The feedback gain derivation hinges on a local quadratic approximation with a tunable λ; there is no analysis of sensitivity, conditioning, or when the linearization is valid given the chosen sensory noise scale. Finite-difference computation of φX for high-dimensional systems can be noisy; no discussion of numerical stability or regularization is provided.

Clarity
- Several crucial details are underspecified: state/action normalization; precise magnitudes of noise relative to state scales; trajectory lengths; success criteria; time horizons; convergence criteria; the definition of the reported “error” metrics; and computational budgets for both optimization and execution-time control.
- The description of “interactive control” is essentially changing goal positions mid-trajectory; no evaluation with external perturbations or environment variations is shown.
- References are incomplete (missing years/venues/pages), and some notation/phrasing is imprecise.

Originality
- The overall framework is close to GPS and prior work by the authors (Mordatch & Todorov 2014), which also alternate trajectory optimization with supervised policy learning. The main methodological novelty here appears to be: (a) a way to extract softer local feedback gains directly from a direct trajectory optimizer; (b) a particular stochastic alternating scheme with proximal terms; and (c) demonstrations in 3D with contact-rich tasks. These are useful engineering refinements but incremental relative to GPS-style methods. The paper should more clearly articulate conceptual differences and provide head-to-head comparisons to establish novelty and benefits.

Significance
- For character animation, the approach is promising and could be impactful. For NIPS, the lack of rigorous quantitative evaluation and strong baselines makes it hard to assess scientific contribution beyond a polished system demonstration. Without physically valid execution (no root forces) and quantitative robustness, the claims for robotics/neuroscience relevance are not substantiated.

Questions for the authors
1. Provide quantitative metrics (success rate, tracking error, torque/energy, foot slip/contact violations, CoM/ZMP stability) across many episodes and seeds, including robustness to external pushes and model perturbations.
2. Clarify execution-time cost: wall-clock time per step, solver tolerance, and how performance degrades if the T=1 optimization is removed (pure policy rollout) or tightened (harder dynamics).
3. Compare against GPS (with the same cost and simulator) and a strong model-free baseline (e.g., TRPO) on at least one task, reporting sample complexity and performance.
4. Are root forces disabled in final evaluations? If not, quantify their magnitude; if yes, report feasibility/infeasibility rates.
5. Detail the scaling/normalization of s, a, noise σ ε, and λ; analyze sensitivity to λ and conditioning of the computed gains.
6. How large are the training datasets (T, number of frames), and what is the generalization to unseen goals and initial conditions?

Minor comments
- Tighten and complete citations; fix typos; define all variables on first use; include units.
- Clarify whether r is necessary (what does the recurrent state learn?) and whether a feedforward policy performs similarly.

Pros
- Sensible and well-engineered combination of established methods.
- Practical algorithm for extracting feedback gains from direct trajectory optimization.
- Diverse 3D demonstrations with a unified architecture.
- Asynchronous training design likely reproducible.

Cons
- Lack of rigorous quantitative evaluation and strong baselines.
- Dependence on soft dynamics (root forces) undermines physical claims.
- Execution relies on per-step optimization; “pure policy” benefits are unclear.
- Novelty over GPS-style methods not convincingly established.

Recommendation: Weak reject. The paper shows impressive demos and contains practical ideas, but falls short on methodological validation against established baselines and on quantitative, physically grounded evaluation necessary for NIPS. Strengthening experiments, clarifying novelty vs. GPS, and removing reliance on non-physical forces would substantially improve the case for acceptance.
