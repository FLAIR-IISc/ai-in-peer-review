This paper presents a method for learning neural network controllers for complex characters by combining trajectory optimization (specifically, Contact-Invariant Optimization – CIO) with supervised learning. The core idea is to leverage CIO to generate training data (state-action pairs) and then train a neural network to mimic the resulting policy, enabling real-time control. The authors address the challenges of high dimensionality, instability, and limited data by injecting noise during training and utilizing a distributed optimization architecture. While the concept is promising and the results demonstrate successful locomotion in 3D—a notable step beyond previous 2D work—the paper suffers from a surprising number of shortcomings in execution and presentation.

**Summary of Strengths:**

*   **Addresses an important problem:** Automating the design of complex controllers is a long-standing goal in robotics, animation, and biomechanics.
*   **Demonstrated 3D locomotion:** Achieving stable and realistic locomotion in 3D space is a significant accomplishment, particularly given the complexity of the task.
*   **Combines established techniques:** The combination of trajectory optimization and neural networks is a sensible approach.
*   **Addresses data scarcity:** The noise injection strategy is a reasonable attempt to improve generalization.

**Summary of Weaknesses:**

*   **Lack of rigorous comparison:** The claim of superiority over “direct methods” (RL, etc.) is asserted without a thorough empirical comparison. A mere “impression” is insufficient.
*   **Implementation details are glossed over:** Many critical implementation details are omitted or described vaguely. For example, the precise formulation of the cost function C(X) (Section 3), crucial for understanding the learned behavior, is only stated generally. What specific features are included in φt(X)?  How are they weighted? The description is insufficient for replication.
*   **Inconsistent notation:**  The use of 'a' to denote both the action and, when subscripted, the feedback gains (as) is confusing and creates ambiguity. Similarly, X denotes both a trajectory *and* a set of trajectories (X1,...,XN).
*   **Justification for design choices is weak:** The rationale for specific parameters (e.g., noise variances σ2ε, σ2γ, penalty weight α, regularization rate η) is not adequately explained. Are these values determined empirically, or based on theoretical considerations?  Why these values specifically?
*   **Limited ablation studies:** While a limited comparison to a naive regression approach is included, a more comprehensive ablation study of the various components (noise injection, proximal regularization, network architecture) would be invaluable.
*   **Section 3.2’s perturbation method's claims lack support**: The statement that the perturbation method produces "softer" and "better-conditioned" gains needs to be substantiated with some analysis or quantitative results.  What metric defines “better-conditioned”?
*   **Overly verbose introduction:** The introduction is lengthy and contains several sentences that state the obvious. It could be significantly streamlined.
*   **Minor Formatting Issues:** There are several minor formatting inconsistencies (e.g., inconsistent spacing in equations, inconsistent use of boldface).

**Detailed Critique:**

*   **Section 2.2:** The quadratic penalty term in equation (3) seems unnecessarily complex. Why not use a simpler L1 or L2 penalty? The justification for this specific form is unclear.
*   **Section 3.1:** The Gauss-Newton Hessian approximation is mentioned but not explained sufficiently for readers unfamiliar with optimization techniques.
*   **Section 3.2:** The claim of reusing computation is overstated. While avoiding recomputation of *some* terms, the perturbation method still requires solving a modified optimization problem.
*   **Section 4:** The justification for adding noise to the hidden activations is weak. The reference to Wager et al. [19] is relevant, but the connection to the specific problem being addressed is not clearly established.
*   **Section 5:** The description of the training trajectory generation process is vague. How are the initial poses and goal locations sampled? What criteria are used to ensure diversity?
*   **Section 6:** The details of the distributed training architecture are superficial. What communication protocols are used? How is data consistency maintained?
*   **Section 7:** The explanation of using the optimized trajectory as an implicit integrator lacks rigor.

**Arguments for/against acceptance:**

**Against:**

*   The lack of rigorous comparison to state-of-the-art methods (e.g., RL) casts doubt on the significance of the results.
*   The numerous implementation details that are glossed over make it difficult to assess the reproducibility of the work.
*   The paper suffers from inconsistencies in notation and weak justifications for design choices.
*   The claims made in Section 3.2 regarding the perturbation method are not adequately supported.

**For:**

*   The demonstration of 3D locomotion is a significant technical achievement.
*   The combination of trajectory optimization and neural networks is a sensible approach.
*   The noise injection strategy shows promise for improving generalization.

**Overall Assessment:**

While the paper presents a potentially valuable contribution, the numerous shortcomings in execution and presentation outweigh the strengths. The lack of rigorous comparison, incomplete implementation details, and inconsistencies in notation raise serious concerns about the validity and reproducibility of the results. The paper needs substantial revision before it can be considered for acceptance.

**Recommendation:** **Reject**.  Significant revisions addressing the concerns outlined above are necessary. A crucial step would be including a comprehensive comparative evaluation against relevant baseline methods. The paper, as it stands, does not meet the quality standards for NIPS.
