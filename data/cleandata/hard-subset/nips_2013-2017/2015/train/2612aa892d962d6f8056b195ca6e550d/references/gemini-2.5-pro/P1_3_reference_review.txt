**Summary of the Paper**

This paper presents a method for training a single neural network policy to control a wide variety of physically simulated 3D characters for interactive locomotion tasks. The core idea is to combine offline trajectory optimization with online policy learning in a joint optimization framework. Specifically, the authors use Contact-Invariant Optimization (CIO) to generate optimal trajectories that fulfill task objectives (e.g., moving to a goal) while respecting physical constraints. A neural network is then trained via supervised learning to mimic the behavior of the optimizer.

The key contributions that enable this method to succeed on complex 3D characters, where prior work was limited to 2D, are threefold:
1.  **Joint Optimization:** Instead of a one-way pipeline (optimize then learn), the paper proposes an alternating optimization scheme. The trajectory optimizer's cost function includes a term to match the current neural network policy, and the network is continuously retrained on the updated trajectories. This co-adaptation is crucial for stability.
2.  **Principled Noise Injection:** To improve robustness and prevent overfitting, the authors inject noise into the network's sensory inputs during training. Critically, they do not train the network to simply reproduce the original action from a noisy state. Instead, they compute the optimal feedback gains from the trajectory optimizer to determine the *correct* compensatory action for the perturbed state. This effectively teaches the network a local linear policy around the optimal trajectory, making it robust to small deviations during execution.
3.  **Distributed Architecture:** A practical contribution that makes this computationally intensive method feasible. Trajectory optimization is distributed across multiple CPU nodes, while the neural network regression is centralized on a GPU, with an asynchronous update scheme regularized by proximal terms.

The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach by training a single, general-purpose controller architecture to produce stable and realistic gaits for bipeds, quadrupeds, a swimmer, and a flyer, all without morphology-specific engineering.

**Strengths**

1.  **Significance and Impact:** The paper tackles a long-standing and challenging problem in computer graphics, robotics, and reinforcement learning: the automatic synthesis of robust, real-time controllers for complex, high-dimensional systems. The ability to generate stable locomotion for diverse 3D morphologies from a single, unified framework is a significant advancement over prior art, which was either limited to 2D characters or required substantial task-specific engineering. The results, as described, are highly impressive.

2.  **Technical Quality and Originality:** The method of using optimal feedback gains to generate corrected training targets for noisy inputs (Section 3.2) is a highly novel and intelligent contribution. It provides a principled way to imbue the learned policy with the local robustness of the underlying optimizer, directly addressing the critical problem of error accumulation in closed-loop control. This is a substantial improvement over naive data augmentation or simply training a denoising autoencoder. The joint optimization framework, while conceptually related to earlier work [9, 11], is well-adapted to this challenging stochastic setting.

3.  **Thorough Experimental Validation:** The authors provide strong empirical support for their claims. The application to a wide range of characters (bipeds of different scales, quadrupeds, swimmer, flyer) convincingly demonstrates the method's generality. The ablation studies in Section 8.1 are particularly valuable, clearly showing that both the joint optimization ("no joint" case) and the noise injection scheme ("no noise" case) are essential for good performance. The performance drop of two orders of magnitude in the "no joint" case is a striking testament to the power of the co-adaptation approach.

4.  **Generality of the Approach:** A major strength is that the controller architecture and training algorithm are not tailored to any specific morphology. The emergence of natural, cyclic gaits (walking, trotting, flapping, swimming) without explicit encoding is a powerful demonstration of the framework's ability to discover solutions from first principles (physics and task objectives).

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Clarity and Reproducibility:** While generally well-written, the paper is quite dense. Section 3.2, which describes the novel method for computing optimal feedback gains, is particularly compressed. The derivation is mathematically elegant but could benefit from more intuition and explanation. For a reader not deeply familiar with trajectory optimization and the Woodbury identity, this core contribution might be difficult to fully grasp. Reproducing this work would be challenging given the complexity of the overall system and the custom distributed implementation.

2.  **Computational Cost:** The training process is extremely computationally expensive, requiring hours on a distributed cluster of ~30 machines plus a GPU. The authors note that "we invest more computing resources in generating the data than in learning from it." While the results are worth the cost, this high barrier to entry may limit the method's adoption and further exploration by other researchers. A discussion of the computational trade-offs would be welcome.

3.  **Comparison to Reinforcement Learning:** The paper appropriately positions itself relative to trajectory optimization-based methods but offers only a brief, qualitative comparison to modern deep reinforcement learning (RL) approaches like TRPO [14]. While the authors' intuition that their method produces more "purposeful" behaviors than "chaotic" RL policies is likely correct for the time of writing, the field of deep RL has advanced rapidly. A more detailed discussion or even a simple quantitative comparison on one of the characters would have strengthened the paper's claims.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Presents a significant step forward in automated character control, successfully scaling to complex 3D characters where prior similar methods could not.
*   Introduces a novel and technically sound method for robust policy learning by combining noise injection with optimal feedback gains.
*   The results are impressive, demonstrating the framework's generality across vastly different character morphologies (walking, flying, swimming).
*   The ablation studies provide clear and compelling evidence for the importance of the paper's core technical contributions.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**
*   The high computational requirements present a significant barrier to reproducibility and broader use.
*   The clarity of the key technical derivation in Section 3.2 could be improved.
*   The comparison to alternative methods, particularly modern deep RL, is limited.

**Recommendation:**

This is a strong paper with significant technical novelty and impressive results on a difficult problem. The strengths—particularly the novel method for learning robust policies and the demonstrated generality—far outweigh the weaknesses. The work clearly advances the state of the art in learning-based control for animation and robotics. I strongly recommend **acceptance**.
