## NIPS Paper Review: Interactive Control of Diverse Complex Characters with Neural Networks

**Summary:**

This paper presents a method for learning neural network control policies for complex, 3D articulated characters through a combination of trajectory optimization (specifically, Contact-Invariant Optimization - CIO) and supervised learning. The core idea is to leverage CIO to generate training data (diverse trajectories with optimal control) and then train a neural network to mimic these trajectories, enabling real-time, interactive control.  The authors address the challenges of scaling to high-dimensional control problems – a space where reinforcement learning has historically struggled – by introducing noise injection during training and a distributed stochastic optimization scheme. They demonstrate successful application to a range of character morphologies (bipedal, quadrupedal, swimming, flying) without character-specific engineering.

**Relation to Previous Work:**

The paper correctly positions itself within a lineage of work attempting to automate controller design. It acknowledges the challenges faced by traditional methods (manual tuning, motion capture reliance) and the limitations of purely reinforcement learning-based approaches in high-dimensional spaces. The work builds on recent efforts combining trajectory optimization and neural networks [9, 11], but explicitly aims to tackle more complex 3D locomotion scenarios. It also touches upon historical approaches to control, such as state machines and simplified models for specific character types (biped, quadruped), and frames this work as a unification attempt. The authors appropriately cite relevant work in neural network training techniques (denoising autoencoders, regularization, initialization strategies) and physics simulation (MuJoCo).

**Strengths:**

* **Addresses a Challenging Problem:**  Automating the creation of stable and realistic controllers for complex, articulated characters is a long-standing and difficult problem.  This work tackles it head-on, specifically addressing the scalability issues that plague many existing approaches.
* **Strong Empirical Results:** The demonstration of successful control across a diverse range of character morphologies (swimming, flying, bipedal, quadrupedal) is compelling. The supplementary video appears to showcase realistic and stable locomotion.  The ablation studies (Section 8.1) provide valuable insights into the importance of joint optimization and noise injection.
* **Novel Combination of Techniques:**  The coupling of CIO trajectory optimization with neural network learning is a significant contribution. It intelligently leverages the strengths of both approaches – CIO provides high-quality data, and the neural network enables real-time control.
* **Principled Noise Injection:** The use of optimal feedback gains to guide noise injection (Section 2.1) is a clever approach that appears to improve robustness and generalization.
* **Scalable Implementation:** The distributed stochastic optimization scheme, combined with a GPU implementation, demonstrates a practical pathway to tackling these computationally intensive problems.
* **Clear Writing:** The paper is generally well-written and organized, making the core ideas accessible.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Comparative Evaluation:** While the ablation studies are helpful, a more systematic comparison with alternative control methods (e.g., direct reinforcement learning approaches like DDPG or TRPO, or model-predictive control) would strengthen the paper. The authors mention their impression that direct methods yield “chaotic” behaviors (Introduction), but lack concrete evidence to support this claim.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper does not extensively explore the sensitivity of the approach to hyperparameter settings. While values are provided (Section 8), it's unclear how robust the results are to changes in these parameters.
* **Focus on Locomotion:** The work focuses primarily on locomotion tasks.  While this is a good starting point, it would be beneficial to see the method applied to a broader range of behaviors (e.g., manipulation, interaction with objects) to demonstrate its generality.
* **Complexity of Implementation:** Despite the scalable implementation, the overall system seems quite complex, requiring expertise in both trajectory optimization and neural network training.  Simplifying the pipeline or providing more readily available code would enhance usability.
* **Cost Function Details:** While the broad strokes of the cost function are mentioned, more detail regarding the weighting of different cost terms (e.g., task completion vs. physical realism) would be helpful.



**Arguments For/Against Acceptance:**

**For Acceptance:**

* Solves a challenging problem with a novel and effective approach.
* Demonstrates strong empirical results across diverse character morphologies.
*  Combines the strengths of trajectory optimization and neural networks.
* Offers a scalable implementation.
*  Clear and well-written.

**Against Acceptance:**

* Limited comparative evaluation with alternative control methods.
*  Hyperparameter sensitivity not thoroughly explored.
* Primarily focused on locomotion.
* Implementation complexity could be a barrier to adoption.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that presents a significant contribution to the field of character control. The combination of trajectory optimization and neural networks is a compelling approach, and the authors have demonstrated its effectiveness on a diverse range of challenging tasks.  While the paper could benefit from a more thorough comparative evaluation and exploration of hyperparameter sensitivity, the strengths clearly outweigh the weaknesses. 

**Recommendation:** **Accept** with minor revisions. I suggest the authors strengthen the comparative evaluation with a few key baseline methods (e.g. DDPG or a basic MPC controller) and provide a more detailed discussion of the cost function weighting. Addressing these points will further solidify the paper’s contribution and impact.
