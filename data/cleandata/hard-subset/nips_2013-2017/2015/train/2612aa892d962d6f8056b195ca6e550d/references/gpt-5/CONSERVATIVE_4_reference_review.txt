Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes an alternating optimization framework that couples direct trajectory optimization (Contact-Invariant Optimization, CIO) with supervised learning of a neural network policy for interactive, real-time control of diverse 3D characters (walking, quadruped, swimming, flying). The key ingredients are: (i) learning a policy that outputs desired pose velocities, recurrent-state velocities, and contact forces from a sensory vector; (ii) injecting Gaussian noise into sensory inputs and hidden activations, with targets corrected via locally optimal feedback gains derived from the trajectory optimizer; (iii) a distributed, asynchronous training loop with proximal regularization alternating between re-optimizing trajectories with a penalty toward the current network and fitting the network to the noisy, gain-corrected actions; and (iv) online execution by solving a short-horizon (T=1) constrained step that matches the network’s desired velocities/forces under soft dynamics and contact constraints. The work is positioned relative to Levine & Koltun (ICML’14) and Mordatch & Todorov (RSS’14), arguing improved difficulty (3D locomotion), a practical feedback-gain computation for direct methods, and a scalable distributed training architecture.

Strengths
- Addresses an important and difficult problem (real-time control of complex bodies) with a systematic synthesis pipeline.
- Sensible use of established tools: direct trajectory optimization, supervised policy learning, noise injection, and proximal regularization in an alternating scheme.
- The feedback-gain derivation for direct trajectory optimization is a useful practical contribution, reusing Gauss-Newton quantities to produce better-conditioned gains with a tunable softness parameter.
- Asynchronous distributed implementation details are pragmatic and potentially useful for practitioners.
- Ablations (no joint training, no noise) qualitatively support the claim that joint optimization and noise/gain targets matter.

Weaknesses
- Lack of rigorous quantitative evaluation. Results are largely qualitative (“view video”). The paper reports “two orders of magnitude better” without a clearly defined metric, units, or statistical reporting. There are no success rates, robustness curves (e.g., under perturbations), energy/effort metrics, contact stability metrics, or timing benchmarks for “real-time.”
- Inadequate comparison to established baselines. The method is not compared against strong baselines such as iLQG/LQR-MPC with contact, TRPO or other policy-gradient/actor-critic methods for continuous control, or even CIO+MPC at test time. The MPC comparison is anecdotal and qualitative; no numbers or task success comparisons are provided.
- Ambiguity in physical realism: dynamics and complementarity are enforced softly, and the authors acknowledge non-physical root forces during execution. This undermines the claim of “physically consistent” control and makes it hard to interpret realism claims.
- Insufficient methodological detail for reproduction: cost terms and weights in CIO, precise feature set φ, dimensionality of s and a, size and role of the recurrent state r, trajectory lengths, and dataset size are not fully specified. The recurrent component is not clearly defined (the network is feedforward; r is updated externally), making it unclear how memory is actually learned/used.
- Alternating optimization lacks convergence analysis and is run with heavy asynchrony and frozen noise; claims of convergence are not substantiated with plots or criteria. The penalty formulation (instead of ADMM or augmented Lagrangian) is plausible but its behavior is not analyzed.
- The “feedback target” innovation would benefit from a direct empirical test: with/without gain-corrected targets (keeping sensory noise) and comparison to an LQG-based gain computation. Currently the ablation conflates several components (noise off disables both sensory and hidden noise).
- The online controller still solves an optimization at every step (T=1), but timing/latency is not reported. Using 50 ms steps and soft constraints may hide contact issues; stability guarantees are not discussed.
- References are incomplete/malformed; related work coverage is uneven (recent continuous control RL methods are acknowledged but not empirically engaged).

Quality
Technically competent overall; components are standard and integrated sensibly. However, claims outpace evidence: without quantitative, reproducible metrics and strong baselines, it is hard to assess whether the approach advances state of the art versus prior trajectory-optimization–guided policy learning. The feedback-gain derivation is interesting, but its practical impact is not isolated with experiments.

Clarity
Readable and generally well organized, but important implementation and evaluation details are missing (costs, network I/O shapes, r definition, dataset size, metrics). Figures referenced in the text (e.g., convergence in Section 8.1) are not present, and numerical results are sparse. References need fixing.

Originality
Moderate. The overall approach is an incremental combination of established techniques (CIO + supervised policy learning + noise). The gain-computation trick for direct methods and the particular distributed training loop add some novelty, but the conceptual contribution relative to Levine & Koltun (2014) and Mordatch & Todorov (2014) seems limited.

Significance
Potentially useful for graphics/animation practitioners, but the absence of rigorous comparisons and metrics limits the scientific impact. The reliance on soft dynamics and root forces also weakens relevance to robotics.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide quantitative evaluations: task success rates, distance/orientation errors, energy, contact violations, robustness to external pushes, and runtime/latency for online control. Include means/variances over seeds.
- Add strong baselines: CIO+MPC, iLQG/iLQR-MPC, TRPO/modern RL on the same MuJoCo models; report apples-to-apples metrics.
- Isolate the contribution of gain-corrected targets by ablating: sensory noise only vs sensory+gain targets vs no noise; compare to LQG-derived gains.
- Detail all costs and weights, the exact feature vector φ, state/action dimensions, r’s size and update, trajectory durations, and dataset size. Clarify whether the policy truly benefits from “memory.”
- Remove or quantify root forces; alternatively, report physical realism metrics (contact complementarity residuals).
- Report real-time performance (ms per step) and stability under variable time steps.
- Improve references and clarity; add a convergence plot for the alternating optimization.

Pros
- Addresses an important control problem with a pragmatic synthesis pipeline.
- Practical feedback-gain computation for direct trajectory optimization.
- Diverse qualitative demonstrations across morphologies.

Cons
- Lacks rigorous quantitative evaluation and strong baselines.
- Uses soft physics with admitted root forces, weakening realism claims.
- Incomplete methodological details; unclear recurrent component.
- Novelty primarily incremental relative to prior trajectory-optimization–guided policy learning.

Recommendation
Lean reject. The paper is promising and may be impactful with more thorough evaluation, clearer methodology, and stronger comparisons. As it stands, evidence is insufficient to support the claims at NeurIPS standards.
