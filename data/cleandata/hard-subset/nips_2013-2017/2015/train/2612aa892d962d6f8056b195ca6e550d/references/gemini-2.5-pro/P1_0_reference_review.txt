### Paper Review

**Summary:**
This paper presents a method for automatically synthesizing interactive, real-time controllers for a diverse range of complex, physically-simulated 3D characters. The core idea is to combine the strengths of offline trajectory optimization for generating high-quality, physically realistic motion with the real-time capabilities of neural network policies. The authors propose a joint, alternating optimization framework where a dataset of optimal trajectories and the parameters of a neural network controller are optimized together. A key contribution is a principled method for injecting noise during training to create robust policies that are stable in a closed-loop setting. This is achieved by adding noise to the network's sensory inputs and training it to predict the optimal action for the perturbed state, which is computed using optimal feedback gains. The paper proposes a novel and efficient method to extract these feedback gains directly from the Contact-Invariant Optimization (CIO) process. The framework is demonstrated on an impressive variety of characters, including bipeds, quadrupeds, a swimmer, and a flyer, all using the same general algorithm to learn complex locomotion behaviors like walking, trotting, and flapping, which emerge without being explicitly specified.

**Strengths and Weaknesses:**

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound and presents a complete and well-executed piece of work. The proposed alternating optimization algorithm, which combines trajectory optimization with neural network regression, is well-motivated. The claim that this joint optimization is crucial for performance is strongly supported by an ablation study (Figure 2, "no joint" case) that shows training on a static dataset fails completely. Similarly, the contribution of their noise-injection technique is validated by an experiment showing that training without noise leads to overfitting and poor test-time performance. The method for extracting "soft" optimal feedback gains from a direct trajectory optimizer (Section 3.2) is a clever technical contribution that appears both novel and useful. The results are compelling and demonstrate a significant advance in the state of the art.

**Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The high-level overview in Section 2 provides a clear roadmap of the method. However, several key details could be clarified to improve understanding and reproducibility:
1.  **State and Action Representation:** The policy `πθ` maps `st = [ qt rt q̇t−1 f t−1 ]` to `at = [ q̇t ṙt f t ]`. The role and structure of the recurrent memory state `r` are not well-defined. Is it simply a vector passed through the network, or does it have a specific structure like an LSTM/GRU cell? Clarifying its function would be helpful.
2.  **Policy Execution (Section 7):** The real-time execution step involves solving a single-timestep optimization problem to find the next state `x1` that best matches the network's desired velocities and forces, subject to physics constraints. The paper claims this runs in real-time, but this seems computationally demanding for every frame (50ms). A more detailed explanation of why this step is necessary (e.g., to ground the network's output in physical reality) and how it achieves real-time rates would strengthen the paper. Does a simple forward integration of the network's output `q̇_des` lead to instability?
3.  **Cost Functions:** The paper mentions that task costs involve reaching a goal and minimizing effort, but more detail on the specific cost formulations `c(φ)` used in the trajectory optimization would improve reproducibility.

**Originality:** The work builds upon a recent line of research that combines trajectory optimization with function approximation [9, 11]. However, it makes several novel contributions. The most significant is the successful application and scaling of this approach to a diverse set of complex 3D characters, a substantial step beyond previous work in 2D. The proposed method for extracting well-conditioned feedback gains from a direct trajectory optimizer is a novel contribution to the field of optimal control. Furthermore, the principled use of these gains to create training targets for noisy inputs is a sophisticated approach to data augmentation for learning robust control policies. The paper correctly positions itself relative to prior work and adequately references related literature.

**Significance:** The significance of this work is very high. It addresses the long-standing and difficult problem of creating general-purpose, interactive controllers for physically-based characters. The ability to generate controllers for bipeds, quadrupeds, swimmers, and flyers using a single, unified algorithm is a major achievement that could have a substantial impact on computer animation, robotics, and biomechanics research. The results, demonstrating the emergence of natural gaits and behaviors, suggest that this is a powerful paradigm for behavior synthesis. The demonstrated robustness and real-time performance make the approach practical for applications like video games and interactive simulations. Other researchers are very likely to build upon these ideas.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
1.  **State-of-the-Art Results:** The method successfully generates high-quality, interactive controllers for an unprecedented variety of complex 3D characters, significantly advancing the state of the art.
2.  **Novel Technical Contributions:** The paper introduces a novel method for extracting feedback gains from direct trajectory optimization and a principled way to use them for robust policy learning.
3.  **Strong Empirical Validation:** The claims are well-supported by rigorous ablation studies that clearly demonstrate the importance of the key components of the proposed method (joint optimization and noise injection).
4.  **High Impact Potential:** The work addresses a problem of major interest to several fields and provides a general framework that is likely to inspire and enable a wide range of future research and applications.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
1.  **Lack of Clarity on Key Details:** As noted above, certain aspects of the method (the role of `r`, the real-time execution step) could be explained in greater detail.
2.  **Weak Comparison to Alternatives:** The comparison with direct reinforcement learning methods is qualitative and based on the authors' "impression." While likely correct, a more direct or quantitative comparison would make the paper stronger.

**Recommendation:**
This is an excellent paper that presents a significant advance in learning-based control for physically simulated characters. The strengths—in particular, the impressive and diverse results, novel technical ideas, and strong empirical validation—far outweigh the minor weaknesses in clarity. This work is of high quality and significance and is a clear fit for NIPS. I strongly recommend acceptance.
