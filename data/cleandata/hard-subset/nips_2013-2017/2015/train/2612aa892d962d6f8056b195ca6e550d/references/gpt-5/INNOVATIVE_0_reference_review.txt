Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes an approach for learning real-time, interactive controllers for complex 3D characters by alternating between (i) direct trajectory optimization (Contact-Invariant Optimization, CIO) to synthesize locally optimal state-action trajectories and (ii) supervised regression to train a neural policy that reproduces those actions from sensory inputs. Key ingredients include: (1) injecting Gaussian sensory noise during training and using local feedback gains (∂a/∂s) to generate target actions for perturbed inputs via first-order expansions; (2) a novel, efficient method to compute well-conditioned feedback gains from a direct trajectory optimizer via a soft-constraint perturbation and Woodbury identity, avoiding a separate ill-conditioned LQG pass; (3) an asynchronous, distributed alternating optimization with proximal regularization that scales to many trajectories and characters; and (4) a T=1 dynamics-consistent optimization at test time to realize the policy’s desired velocities/forces, enabling stable real-time control at 50 ms steps.

Empirically, a single 3-layer tanh network (3×250) trained in this manner produces diverse, cyclic, stable behaviors for swimmers, a bird, bipeds, and quadrupeds in full 3D, with interactive goal changes handled by branching goals during trajectory generation. Ablations indicate that joint alternating training and noise/gain injection are both crucial; static supervised learning and no-noise training exhibit large performance drops. The method is qualitatively contrasted with MPC, arguing that the joint learning regularizes the solution toward predictable cyclic gaits.

This line is closely related to guided policy search / trajectory-optimization–supervised-learning hybrids (e.g., Levine & Koltun ICML’14; Mordatch & Todorov RSS’14). The present work advances that direction by scaling to 3D locomotion across multiple morphologies, proposing a practical feedback-gain computation for direct trajectory optimization, and demonstrating an asynchronous distributed implementation.

Strengths
- Conceptual boldness and unification: A single neural policy architecture, trained by alternating with CIO, yields interactive control across very different morphologies (flying, swimming, bipeds/quadrupeds) in 3D, without state machines, gait encodings, or motion capture. This is a compelling step toward general-purpose physics-based controllers.
- Technical novelty: The method to extract stabilizing feedback gains directly from a Gauss-Newton CIO pass via a soft-constraint perturbation is elegant, computationally efficient, and addresses a real pain point (ill-conditioned LQG gains post hoc). Using these gains to create consistent supervision under noisy inputs is a principled twist on denoising and should be of broad interest.
- Practical scaling: The asynchronous distributed scheme with proximal penalties is simple and effective; investing compute in trajectory generation rather than learning is a pragmatic and underutilized design choice.
- Empirical breadth: Demonstrations across multiple complex bodies in full 3D are rare and valuable; the behaviors are realistic and cyclic without explicit cyclic priors. The ablations convincingly show the need for the joint optimization and noise.
- Clarity of the high-level algorithm: The block-alternating scheme and the runtime controller are described clearly, and the key hyperparameters are reported.

Weaknesses and limitations
- Limited quantitative evaluation: Most results are qualitative. Apart from an ablation on a biped and a small architecture sweep, there are no standardized metrics (success rates, disturbance recovery, energy/effort, goal-tracking error distributions), nor timing benchmarks for the T=1 optimizer at test time.
- Baseline coverage: The comparison to MPC is qualitative. There is no empirical comparison to contemporary model-free or guided policy search methods (e.g., TRPO, deterministic policy gradients, GPS variants) on any shared task. While authors discuss this, a minimal baseline would strengthen claims.
- Physical realism caveat: Dynamics constraints are enforced softly and the authors note possible root forces during simulation. This weakens claims of physical realism and limits direct applicability to robotics; the policy outputs contact forces directly, which are not realizable on torque-controlled hardware.
- Clarity gaps: The recurrent state r is introduced but the recurrence mechanism is not described in detail (is the network recurrent or is r purely a learned latent updated by the policy?). The sensory/action definitions use q̇t−1 and q̇t; it would help to justify this interleaving and discuss sensitivity. Some notation is terse, and the proximal terms’ role could be clarified.
- Generalization and robustness: While noise-injection and gains target robustness, there’s little quantitative analysis of generalization to unseen goals, terrain variations, or model perturbations beyond the training distribution. Sensitivity to λ (gain softness), α (penalty), and η (proximal rate) is not explored.
- Reproducibility: CIO cost feature details, soft constraint formulations, and exact execution-time optimization setup are not fully specified; without code, reproduction may be difficult.

Questions for the authors
- What is the structure of r and the policy network? Is there actual recurrence (e.g., RNN) or is r a learned latent that the feedforward net updates? How critical is r?
- How fast is the T=1 optimization per step for each character? What are worst-case latencies?
- Do learned policies remain stable if the test-time optimizer is removed (i.e., pure forward Euler with torques predicted by the net)? This would help separate the contribution of the policy vs. the implicit integrator.
- Can you quantify the amount of root force used at test time, and can it be eliminated with tighter constraints while retaining stability?
- How sensitive are the results to λ in the feedback-gain computation? Any heuristics for setting it?

Suggestions
- Add quantitative metrics across tasks (e.g., goal error, fall rate, perturbation recovery, energy) and a lightweight baseline (e.g., TRPO on simplified versions, or GPS with LQR-based gains) to anchor the gains of the proposed ingredients.
- Report timing for the runtime optimizer and sensitivity to timestep size.
- Provide more detail on r, cost features, and constraint penalties; releasing code or configs would amplify impact.
- Explore harder generalization tests (terrain variations, mass/inertia perturbations).
- Consider evaluating with hard constraints/no root forces to strengthen claims of physical realism.

Significance and originality
While the overall paradigm builds on guided policy search ideas, the paper contributes a notably practical and scalable instantiation for 3D character control, with a clever feedback-gain extraction for direct trajectory optimization and convincing breadth of behaviors from a single architecture. In my view, the conceptual unification and the feedback-gain machinery constitute meaningful advances that others will build on for both graphics and control.

Recommendation: Accept (leaning strong)
This is a bold, well-motivated paper that advances the state of the art in neural control for complex physics-based characters. Despite some evaluation gaps and reliance on soft constraints, the novelty and demonstrated scope outweigh the weaknesses. I expect this work to influence subsequent research in guided policy learning, robust control from trajectory optimization, and interactive character animation.
