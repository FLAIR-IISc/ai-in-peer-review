### Summary

This paper presents a novel framework for automatically synthesizing interactive, real-time controllers for a wide variety of complex, physically-simulated 3D characters. The core idea is a joint optimization process that couples an offline, high-quality trajectory optimizer (Contact-Invariant Optimization, or CIO) with a neural network policy. The optimizer generates optimal trajectories that serve as training data for the network, but critically, the optimization process is regularized to produce trajectories that are learnable by the current policy. In turn, the neural network learns to mimic the optimizer's behavior, effectively distilling its complex, non-real-time planning capabilities into a fast, reactive policy. Key technical contributions include a principled method for injecting noise during training by using optimal feedback gains derived from the trajectory optimizer to create robust and stable policies, and a demonstration of the framework's generality by applying the exact same method to generate locomotion for bipeds, quadrupeds, swimmers, and flyers without any task-specific modifications.

The work builds upon the "guided policy search" paradigm, particularly related to prior work by Levine & Koltun and Mordatch & Todorov, which also combined trajectory optimization with policy learning. However, this paper makes a significant leap by successfully scaling these ideas to complex, unstable 3D characters, a substantially harder problem than the 2D characters addressed in previous work.

### Strengths

1.  **High Novelty and Ambition:** The paper tackles a grand challenge in control and graphics: the automatic creation of a *universal* controller synthesis method. The proposed solution, a symbiotic optimization between a planner and a policy, is an elegant and powerful concept. It moves beyond the standard paradigm of training on a static dataset of "expert" demonstrations by creating a dynamic process where the expert and the learner improve together. This is a bold and significant research direction.

2.  **Principled Robustification:** The method for handling noise during training (Section 2.1) is a major strength. Instead of simply adding noise to states and training the network to predict the original action (as in a standard denoising autoencoder), the authors use the optimal feedback gains from the trajectory optimizer to compute the *new optimal action* for the perturbed state. This directly teaches the network how to locally stabilize its trajectory and recover from small perturbations, a critical feature for closed-loop control where errors can otherwise compound catastrophically. This is a clever and well-motivated technical contribution.

3.  **Impressive Generality and Results:** The most compelling evidence for the method's power is its application to a diverse set of morphologies—biped, quadruped, swimmer, bird—using a single, unified algorithm. The emergence of natural-looking gaits (walking, trotting, flapping) without any explicit encoding of cyclic patterns or contact schedules is a remarkable achievement. This demonstrates a significant step towards the goal of creating controllers automatically from just a physical model and a high-level task description, which has been a long-standing goal in the field.

4.  **Clear Conceptual Framework:** The paper clearly articulates the challenges of learning controllers (data scarcity, closed-loop instability) and presents its alternating optimization scheme as a coherent solution. The idea of using the neural network's learnability as a regularizer for the trajectory optimization is insightful and effectively bridges the gap between offline planning and online execution.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Limited Comparison to Alternative Methods:** The paper's primary weakness is the lack of a quantitative comparison to state-of-the-art direct reinforcement learning methods (e.g., TRPO, PPO, SAC). The authors offer a qualitative impression that direct RL methods produce "somewhat chaotic behaviors," while their method yields more "realistic and purposeful" ones. While this may be true, a direct comparison on at least one of the characters would have been invaluable for contextualizing the performance and trade-offs of this approach. Without it, it is difficult to judge whether the significant complexity and computational cost of the proposed framework is justified compared to simpler, end-to-end RL approaches.

2.  **Heuristic Optimization Scheme:** The proposed block-alternating optimization algorithm (Algorithm 1) is heuristic and lacks convergence guarantees. The authors acknowledge this, noting that it may have a slower convergence rate than more complex methods like ADMM. While the empirical results are strong, the theoretical underpinnings of the optimization process are not fully developed.

3.  **Ambiguity in Policy Execution:** The policy execution step (Section 7) is slightly unclear. It states that at runtime, they solve a T=1 optimization problem at each timestep to determine the next state, subject to physics constraints. This sounds more like a form of Model Predictive Control (MPC) that uses the network's output as a target, rather than a simple forward pass of the policy. This seems computationally demanding for real-time rates and raises questions about how much of the "work" at runtime is being done by the network versus this per-step optimization. A clarification of the computational cost and the exact role of the network at test time would be beneficial.

### Recommendation: Accept

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   The paper introduces a highly novel and ambitious framework for a very challenging problem.
*   The core technical ideas, particularly the joint optimization and the principled use of feedback gains for robustification, are significant conceptual contributions that are likely to influence future work in control, robotics, and animation.
*   The empirical results are extremely impressive, showcasing a level of generality across diverse 3D morphologies that is rarely seen.
*   The work clearly advances the state-of-the-art in learning-based character control.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   The experimental evaluation is incomplete due to the absence of quantitative comparisons with modern deep RL baselines.
*   The optimization algorithm is heuristic and lacks theoretical guarantees.
*   Some implementation details, especially regarding the real-time execution, could be clearer.

**Justification:**
While the paper is not without flaws, particularly in its experimental comparison, its strengths in novelty, ambition, and conceptual contribution are overwhelming. The approach of synergistically combining the strengths of trajectory optimization and neural network policies is a powerful direction for the field. The paper presents a bold system that achieves remarkable results on a set of very difficult tasks. This is precisely the type of forward-looking, high-impact research that NIPS should champion. The weaknesses, while valid, primarily point towards avenues for future work rather than fatal flaws in the core contribution. I am confident this work will be of great interest to the community and will inspire follow-up research.
