

# Review of "Interactive Control of Diverse Complex Characters with Neural Networks"

This paper proposes a method for learning neural network policies for interactive control of diverse 3D characters using a combination of trajectory optimization and neural network training. The authors aim to automate the design of controllers for complex locomotion behaviors across different character morphologies (bipedal, quadrupedal, swimming, flying) without task-specific hand-crafted controllers. The approach alternates between trajectory optimization (using Contact-Invariant Optimization) and neural network regression with stochastic noise injection.

**Summary and Relation to Previous Work:** The paper builds on established work in trajectory optimization (Mordatch et al., 2012; Levine & Koltun, 2014) and neural network control (Huh & Todorov, 2013; Mnih et al., 2013). While the authors claim their method extends previous work to 3D environments, the core components (trajectory optimization, neural network policy, noise injection) are largely established techniques. The paper cites relevant prior work but fails to adequately distinguish their contributions from existing methods in the literature, particularly regarding comparisons to state-of-the-art reinforcement learning approaches.

**Quality:** The paper demonstrates promising results on several locomotion tasks across different character types, showing that their method can learn controllers that maintain stability in 3D environments where previous 2D approaches failed. However, the validation is incomplete. The authors compare to a "static training" baseline but fail to compare against established reinforcement learning methods (PPO, SAC, etc.) or model-based control approaches (MPC), which would be the standard baselines for this problem. The ablation study showing the importance of noise injection is limited to a single bipedal walking task. The paper lacks thorough analysis of failure cases, robustness to perturbations, or comparison to alternative noise injection methods. The claim that their method "yields stable and realistic locomotion in three-dimensional space" is supported only by qualitative video examples, not quantitative metrics.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-organized and clearly explains the proposed method. The mathematical formulation is precise, and the distributed training architecture is described in sufficient detail. However, the paper lacks sufficient detail on hyperparameters (beyond a few values in Section 8), which would be necessary for reproducibility. The comparison to related work is incomplete, particularly regarding how their method differs from recent work on neural network control with trajectory optimization (Mordatch & Todorov, 2014).

**Originality:** The paper does not present a fundamentally new approach but rather combines existing techniques (trajectory optimization, neural networks, noise injection) with a distributed training scheme. While the authors claim their method "solves substantially harder problems" than previous work, the paper does not clearly demonstrate how their approach differs from Mordatch and Todorov's earlier work on combining trajectory optimization with neural networks. The extension to 3D is mentioned as an advancement, but the technical details of how this extension was achieved are not sufficiently explained.

**Significance:** The paper addresses an important problem in character animation and robotics. However, the significance is limited because the method relies heavily on trajectory optimization (which is computationally expensive and offline) to generate training data, undermining the claim of "real-time interactive control." The training process takes hours on cloud infrastructure, while the inference is claimed to be real-time but without concrete latency measurements. For practical applications, the high computational cost of training may outweigh the benefits. The paper also lacks discussion of how this method would scale to more complex tasks beyond locomotion (e.g., manipulation, object interaction).

**Strengths:** The paper successfully demonstrates the method on diverse character types without task-specific hand-crafted controllers, showing the potential for a unified control framework. The noise injection approach is reasonably motivated and shows improved robustness in their experiments. The distributed training architecture is well-designed for handling the computational load.

**Weaknesses:** The paper's most significant weakness is the lack of rigorous comparison against established baselines. The authors compare only to a naive static training approach, not to state-of-the-art reinforcement learning or model-based control methods. The paper does not address how their method would perform on more complex tasks beyond locomotion, nor does it provide sufficient evidence that the neural network controllers generalize to unseen character morphologies or perturbations. The computational cost of training is not justified in the context of real-world applications.

**Conclusion:** While the paper demonstrates an interesting application of combining trajectory optimization with neural networks for character control, it falls short of providing sufficient evidence that this is a significant advancement over existing approaches. The method relies on expensive offline trajectory optimization to generate training data, which contradicts the claim of enabling "interactive real-time control" without substantial computational overhead. The paper lacks rigorous validation against established baselines and fails to provide convincing evidence that their approach offers a fundamental improvement over prior work.

The paper would benefit from a more thorough comparison to state-of-the-art reinforcement learning methods, detailed analysis of computational costs, and clearer justification for why this approach is superior to simpler alternatives. Without these, the contribution appears incremental rather than transformative.

**Recommendation:** Reject. The paper presents an interesting application of established techniques but fails to demonstrate sufficient novelty or rigorous validation against appropriate baselines to warrant publication at NIPS. The claims about performance improvements are not adequately supported, and the computational cost of training undermines the practical significance of the method.
