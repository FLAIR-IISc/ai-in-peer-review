Summary and relation to prior work:
This paper proposes a framework for learning interactive, real-time control policies for complex, 3D characters by alternating between trajectory optimization and supervised neural policy learning. The core idea is to (i) generate task-conditioned optimal trajectories with Contact-Invariant Optimization (CIO), (ii) compute local feedback gains around these trajectories via a perturbation method that reuses CIO computations, and (iii) train a neural network to map noisy sensory inputs to actions that approximate the locally optimal feedback policy. The training proceeds via block-alternating minimization with proximal regularization and noise injection in both inputs and hidden activations. At test time, the learned policy produces desired velocities, memory updates, and contact forces; the next state is found by solving a T=1 optimization problem subject to (soft) dynamics constraints. The approach is demonstrated on diverse 3D morphologies (swimmer, bird, multiple bipeds, quadrupeds), with some ablations showing that joint optimization and noise injection materially improve performance over static training.

The paper is closely related to Guided Policy Search (GPS) and trajectory-optimization-to-policy pipelines (e.g., Levine & Koltun, ICML’14; Mordatch & Todorov, RSS’14), which also alternate between local trajectory optimization and supervised policy regression, often with ADMM/BADMM or dual gradient descent to enforce consistency constraints. The present work distinguishes itself by: (a) a perturbation-based method to extract feedback gains from a direct trajectory optimizer without a separate LQG pass, (b) a particular stochastic penalty formulation amenable to asynchronous, distributed training, and (c) demonstrations on a wider variety of 3D characters with cyclic gaits emerging without hand-crafted state machines. The paper also situates itself relative to MPC and deep RL (e.g., TRPO), though quantitative comparisons are limited.

Strengths:
- Addresses a challenging setting (continuous, high-DoF, contact dynamics) and provides real-time policies that appear qualitatively robust and diverse across morphologies.
- Sensible and well-motivated alternating optimization framework leveraging mature tools (CIO + NN regression), making the approach practical and scalable with a simple distributed architecture.
- The perturbation-based feedback gain computation that reuses the CIO Gauss-Newton system is elegant and practically valuable; it seems to yield better-conditioned gains than a naive LQG pass.
- Careful use of noise (sensor and hidden activations) and proximal regularization improves robustness and stabilizes asynchronous training.
- Some ablations (no joint optimization, no noise, varying network sizes) support the design choices.
- The execution-time T=1 optimization acts as an implicit integrator, which can improve stability over explicit rollouts.

Weaknesses:
- Quantitative evaluation is very limited. Most results are qualitative videos and a single error curve and table. There are no standard metrics (success rates, constraint violations, energy/torque usage, foot slip, phase stability, disturbance rejection, time-to-goal), nor statistical reporting across seeds.
- Comparison to strong baselines is insufficient. Given the overlap with GPS (2013–2015), TRPO (2015), and model-based MPC variants, a fair empirical comparison (or at least matched-cost comparisons on shared tasks) is needed. The MPC comparison is qualitative and limited to one character.
- The execution relies on soft dynamics constraints with possible “root forces” at runtime. This undermines physical realism and makes it hard to assess transfer to robotics. A torque-only or strictly physically feasible variant would be much more convincing.
- “Interactive control” is instantiated only as mid-trajectory goal changes in position/orientation. There is no evaluation of perturbation recovery (pushes), sensor/model mismatch, or obstacles.
- Some derivations are sketched and could be clearer. The feedback gain computation is compelling, but the assumptions and sensitivity to λ are not thoroughly analyzed; stability guarantees are not discussed. Notation occasionally drifts (e.g., overloaded symbols, missing indices).
- The dependence on CIO and finite-difference Jacobians/Hessians can be computationally heavy; compute budgets are reported, but the method’s scalability with increasing DoFs and contact complexity is not rigorously profiled.
- References are incomplete/inconsistently formatted; related work on GPS variants, local linear models, and contemporary continuous-control deep RL is under-cited or not compared experimentally.

Quality:
Technically sound at a high level, with a plausible algorithmic pipeline and an interesting feedback-gain reuse. However, the experimental section does not meet NeurIPS standards for quantitative rigor, and the use of soft constraints with root forces at execution weakens claims about physical realism.

Clarity:
Generally well-written with a clear high-level flow. Some mathematical details and notation could be tightened, and the cost terms used in CIO should be specified precisely. Reference formatting needs attention.

Originality:
Incremental but non-trivial. The combination is close to GPS-style methods, but the feedback gain extraction and the breadth of 3D demonstrations add novelty.

Significance:
Potentially impactful for character animation and as a bridge between trajectory optimization and policy learning. The lack of strong quantitative comparisons and physically strict execution reduces broader significance, especially for robotics.

Pros (arguments for acceptance):
- Addresses an important, difficult problem; compelling demos across diverse morphologies.
- Practical algorithm combining strengths of trajectory optimization and neural policy learning.
- Novel, useful method to extract feedback gains from direct trajectory optimization.
- Robustness techniques (noise, proximal terms, asynchronous training) that practitioners can adopt.

Cons (arguments against acceptance):
- Insufficient quantitative evaluation and ablations; largely qualitative evidence.
- Weak baselines; no head-to-head with GPS/TRPO on common benchmarks.
- Execution uses soft dynamics with root forces, limiting claims of physical realism/transfer.
- Limited notion of “interaction” and robustness testing.
- Some methodological details and theoretical properties (stability, sensitivity to λ) underdeveloped.

Questions for authors / suggestions for improvement:
- Provide quantitative metrics (task success, tracking error, energy/torque, constraint violation, foot slippage, contact consistency) with statistics over seeds.
- Compare against GPS (e.g., BADMM-based), TRPO, and a strong MPC baseline with matched costs on at least one shared task (e.g., 3D biped to waypoints).
- Remove root forces at test time; enforce hard dynamics and torque actuation, or report the magnitude of root forces and demonstrate their reduction to negligible levels.
- Evaluate robustness: push-recovery tests, sensor/model noise, terrain variations, obstacles.
- Analyze sensitivity to λ in the feedback-gain computation; report condition numbers and the effect on policy robustness.
- Detail CIO costs and features, and the exact sensor/action spaces; release hyperparameters and code snippets if possible.
- Profile compute: wall-clock per CIO solve, gradient/Hessian evaluations, scaling with DoF and number of contacts.

Recommendation: Weak reject. The approach is promising and potentially impactful, but the current version lacks the quantitative rigor and baseline comparisons expected at NeurIPS and relies on execution-time soft constraints that weaken claims. Strengthening the empirical evaluation and tightening the physical realism at test time would make this much more compelling.
