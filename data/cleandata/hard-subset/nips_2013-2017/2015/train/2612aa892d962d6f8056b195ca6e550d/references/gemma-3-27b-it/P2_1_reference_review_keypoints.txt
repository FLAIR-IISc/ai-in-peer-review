- Proposes combining Contact-Invariant Optimization (CIO) trajectory optimization with supervised learning to train neural network controllers for complex 3D characters for real-time interactive control.
- Uses CIO to generate optimal trajectories and trains networks to mimic them, addressing noisy data and overfitting via noise injection and distributed stochastic optimization.
- Demonstrates the method on diverse morphologies (swimmers, flyers, bipeds, quadrupeds) producing stable, realistic locomotion without hand-crafted controllers or motion capture.
- Positions the work within trajectory optimization, neural control, and physically-based animation literature, emphasizing supervised learning guided by trajectory optimization rather than direct RL.
- Achieves scalability to complex 3D characters beyond prior methods largely limited to 2D.
- Employs a single network architecture to control diverse character morphologies, reducing need for specialized controllers.
- Learned controllers exhibit stable, non-chaotic behaviors compared to some purely data-driven approaches.
- Principled noise injection combined with computed optimal feedback gains improves robustness and generalization.
- Training paradigm efficiently allocates computation: CIO handles heavy optimization while the neural network serves as a learned function approximator, supported by distributed training.
- Paper is clearly written with helpful algorithmic details and an informative ablation study.
- Lacks comprehensive comparisons to other state-of-the-art control methods (e.g., MPC, PPO, SAC), making some claims about alternatives unsubstantiated.
- Hyperparameter choices (noise variances, penalty weights) seem arbitrary and lack sensitivity analysis or systematic tuning discussion.
- Offers limited interpretability of learned behaviors; internal network representations and reasons for emergent behaviors remain unexplored.
- Method depends heavily on CIO quality; potential CIO failure modes and their effects on learned controllers are not fully discussed.
- Computational cost is substantial (hours and distributed resources), which may limit reproducibility and accessibility.
- For acceptance: significant advance, generalizable across morphologies, principled robustness techniques, and strong ablation evidence.
- Against acceptance: limited comparative evaluation, insufficient justification for hyperparameter selection, and dependence on CIO as a preprocessing step.
- Overall assessment: strong, novel, technically sound contribution with impressive results; reviewer recommends acceptance (rating 8/10).
