

**Review of "Interactive Control of Diverse Complex Characters with Neural Networks"**

**Summary**  
This paper proposes a method to learn neural network controllers for diverse 3D characters (bipedal, quadrupedal, swimming, flying) using a combination of trajectory optimization (Contact-Invariant Optimization, CIO) and neural network regression. The core idea extends prior work [9,11] by scaling to 3D locomotion, addressing data scarcity via noise injection, and enabling interactive control. Unlike prior methods limited to 2D or task-specific controllers, this approach uses a single network architecture across character morphologies, generating stable, realistic behaviors without manual tuning. The work relates to NIPS themes in reinforcement learning (e.g., [14,10]) and neural control [3,6], but advances by integrating trajectory optimization with neural policies for real-time applications.

**Strengths**  
1. **Technical Contribution**: The paper successfully extends prior trajectory-optimization-based methods to 3D locomotion, a significant leap from previous 2D work [9,11]. The distributed stochastic optimization framework (Algorithm 1) efficiently handles large-scale training, and the noise-injection strategy (Section 2.1) effectively prevents overfitting and stabilizes rollouts—a critical insight for closed-loop control.  
2. **Empirical Validation**: Comprehensive experiments across diverse character types (swimmers, birds, bipeds, quadrupeds) demonstrate robustness and generalizability. Ablation studies (Section 8.1) convincingly show the necessity of joint optimization and noise injection (e.g., no-joint optimization fails by two orders of magnitude).  
3. **Practical Impact**: The method produces interactive, task-compliant behaviors without motion capture or hand-designed controllers, directly addressing a key bottleneck in character animation and robotics. The distributed training architecture (Section 6) is scalable and well-motivated.  
4. **Clarity**: The paper is well-structured, with clear problem formulation (Section 2) and sufficient technical detail for reproducibility. The supplementary video (cited in Section 8) would further strengthen the claims.

**Weaknesses**  
1. **Computational Cost**: The method requires hours of training (Section 6) and cloud resources, which may limit practical adoption compared to simpler controllers (e.g., MPC [8]). The paper lacks a cost-benefit analysis (e.g., training time vs. real-time performance) for real-world robotics.  
2. **Comparison to RL Baselines**: While the paper contrasts with "direct methods" [3,6,10], it omits comparisons to modern RL algorithms (e.g., SAC [14], PPO [13]) that also aim for real-time control. This weakens the significance claim, as RL approaches might offer comparable or better performance with less manual tuning.  
3. **Technical Depth**: The perturbation method for feedback gains (Section 3.2) is novel but lacks intuitive explanation. For readers unfamiliar with trajectory optimization, the derivation (e.g., Woodbury identity) may obscure the practical contribution.  
4. **Limitations**: The method assumes accurate physics models (MuJoCo [16]), but real-world applications face model inaccuracies. The paper does not test robustness to large perturbations (e.g., sudden obstacles), a common challenge in robotics.

**Recommendation and Suggestions**  
This paper makes a valuable contribution to interactive control, particularly in scaling to 3D character diversity. However, the lack of RL comparisons and unaddressed computational costs prevent it from reaching the highest tier of NeurIPS acceptance. To strengthen the paper:  
1. Add a comparison to state-of-the-art RL baselines (e.g., SAC, PPO) on the biped task, focusing on training efficiency and real-time performance.  
2. Discuss trade-offs between training time (hours) and real-world applicability (e.g., "For robotics, can this be accelerated via domain randomization?").  
3. Simplify the feedback-gain derivation (e.g., include a high-level intuition in Section 3.2) and clarify limitations in Section 9.  
4. Explicitly address how the method handles sensor noise during execution (beyond the training noise injection).

**Pro/Con Acceptance**  
*Pro*: Novel extension to 3D locomotion, robust empirical validation, and a scalable training framework.  
*Con*: Limited comparison to RL, high computational cost, and insufficient discussion of real-world constraints.  

**Decision**  
**Reject**. While the work is technically sound and demonstrates a significant step toward unified character control, the lack of comparison to modern RL methods and unaddressed computational barriers reduce its impact for NeurIPS. With the suggested revisions—particularly adding RL baselines and cost analysis—the paper could become a strong candidate for future conferences. The core idea is promising, but the current version does not sufficiently establish its novelty or practical significance relative to the broader field.
