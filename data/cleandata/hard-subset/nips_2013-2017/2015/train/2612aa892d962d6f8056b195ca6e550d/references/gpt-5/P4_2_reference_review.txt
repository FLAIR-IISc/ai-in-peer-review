Summary and relation to prior work
The paper presents a method for learning interactive, real-time control policies for diverse, physically simulated characters by coupling direct trajectory optimization with supervised neural network training. The approach alternates between (i) optimizing trajectories via Contact-Invariant Optimization (CIO) with soft dynamics and contact constraints, and (ii) fitting a neural network policy to reproduce the optimized actions from noisy sensory inputs. A key component is injecting Gaussian noise in the policy inputs and training on first-order corrected action targets a + As ε, where As are locally optimal feedback gains derived efficiently from the CIO solution via a perturbation/soft-constraint method. Training proceeds asynchronously and distributively: many CPU workers optimize trajectories with proximal regularization, while a GPU performs batched SGD to fit the policy. At test time, the learned policy outputs desired velocity, recurrent-state update, and contact forces; the system advances one step by solving a small inverse-dynamics optimization (T=1) that enforces dynamics and contact constraints.

Empirically, the method yields stable and realistic goal-directed locomotion across morphologies (3D swimmers, a bird, bipeds, and quadrupeds), with cyclic gaits emerging without hand-crafted contact schedules or state machines. Ablations show that joint optimization and noise-injection materially improve rollout stability; different network widths/depths are also compared. The approach is positioned relative to: trajectory-optimization-plus-learning (e.g., Levine & Koltun 2014; Mordatch & Todorov 2014), model-free RL (e.g., TRPO and other continuous-control methods), classical animation/robotics controllers (state machines, ZMP/capture point, CPGs), and MPC.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The formulation of a block-alternating optimization with proximal terms is standard and appropriate for asynchronous updates. The CIO subproblems use Gauss-Newton approximations and finite-difference Jacobians, consistent with prior work. The derivation of local feedback gains via a soft constraint on sensory state (using the Woodbury identity and reusing the CXX inverse) is plausible and computationally efficient, and explicitly addresses issues observed when applying LQG post hoc. The policy training objective with noisy inputs and first-order corrected targets is well-motivated, as is injecting noise in hidden activations.
- Assumptions/limitations: Dynamics and contacts are enforced softly; the execution step may include nonphysical root forces, which limits direct transfer to hardware. Stability and convergence claims are empirical; no theoretical guarantees are given for the alternating, stochastic, asynchronous scheme, though proximal terms are used to stabilize. The policy outputs contact forces directly, which are not actuated in real robots; this is reasonable within the inverse dynamics formulation but complicates applicability beyond simulation. The recurrent state r is treated as part of the plant state with ṙ predicted by the network, but its role and dimensionality are not specified.
- Empirical evaluation: The paper provides strong qualitative demonstrations across varied morphologies and shows ablations indicating the necessity of joint optimization and noise. However, quantitative metrics (success rates, trajectory tracking error, energy/effort, gait regularity, robustness to perturbations, latency) and systematic comparisons against MPC or model-free RL baselines are limited or absent in the provided text.

Clarity
The paper is generally well-structured and readable. The optimization objectives and algorithmic steps are clearly stated, and the role of noise and feedback gains in supervision is explained. Areas that would benefit from more detail:
- Sensory features s, the dimensionality and interpretation of the recurrent state r, and how r differs from hidden network state (the network appears feedforward, with r maintained externally).
- Exact cost terms used in CIO (beyond high-level descriptions), and the settings/units for penalties α, λ, η with sensitivity analyses.
- Execution-time performance numbers (e.g., per-step solve time for the T=1 optimization at 50 ms timesteps).
- Quantitative plots/tables referenced (e.g., Figure 2, Table 1, Figure 3) to substantiate claims.
Some references are incomplete; ensuring accurate and complete citations would improve clarity.

Originality
The paper extends prior work that combines trajectory optimization with supervised policy learning by:
- Training policies for diverse 3D characters that exhibit stable cyclic locomotion without contact schedules.
- Using a perturbation-based method to extract well-conditioned local feedback gains directly from CIO computations.
- Employing an asynchronous, proximal-regularized alternating optimization with principled noise-injection in inputs and hidden units tailored to closed-loop stability.
While the overall paradigm resembles prior guided policy search/trajectory learning approaches, the technical refinements and the demonstrated 3D scope constitute a meaningful increment in capability.

Significance
Interactive control of complex, contact-rich 3D characters is important for graphics, robotics, and neuroscience. If the method reliably produces stable, realistic behaviors across varied morphologies with modest runtime costs, it is impactful. The absence of thorough quantitative comparisons and hard-constraint physics reduces the strength of the scientific evidence, but the breadth of demonstrations suggests practical value and potential for follow-on work (e.g., hardware transfer with torque-level outputs).

Pros
- Coherent integration of CIO and neural policy learning with principled noise and feedback-target supervision.
- Efficient, reusable computation for local feedback gains that improves target quality and conditioning.
- Broad qualitative validation across multiple 3D morphologies and gaits without task-specific architectures.
- Asynchronous distributed training design that is practical and scalable.
- Clear ablations showing the importance of joint optimization and noise.

Cons
- Limited quantitative evaluation and baseline comparisons (MPC, TRPO/guided policy search) beyond qualitative observations.
- Soft dynamics/contact constraints and root forces at execution undermine physical fidelity and applicability to real robots.
- Incomplete specification of features, recurrent state, costs, and hyperparameter sensitivities; referenced figures/tables not provided here.
- No analysis of robustness to disturbances or model errors, or generalization to unseen tasks.
- The policy relies on a per-step inverse-dynamics optimization at test time; runtime and stability details are not fully reported.

Suggestions for improvement
- Add quantitative metrics: success/fall rates, path/orientation error, energy/torque usage, gait periodicity, and responsiveness to goal changes; include robustness tests with external perturbations and sensor noise.
- Provide runtime benchmarks for the T=1 execution step on standard hardware and clarify real-time constraints.
- Compare against MPC and at least one strong model-free baseline (e.g., TRPO) on matched tasks and costs.
- Clarify the role and size of the recurrent state r; report ablations with/without r.
- Report sensitivity analyses for α, λ, η, σε, σγ; justify λ for feedback gain softness.
- Where possible, enforce hard contacts (or eliminate root forces) and report the impact on control quality.
- Improve references and include complete bibliographic details.

Overall, the paper presents a technically sound and practically useful approach with solid qualitative results and incremental methodological contributions. Strengthening the quantitative evaluation and clarifying implementation details would significantly increase its scientific rigor and impact.
