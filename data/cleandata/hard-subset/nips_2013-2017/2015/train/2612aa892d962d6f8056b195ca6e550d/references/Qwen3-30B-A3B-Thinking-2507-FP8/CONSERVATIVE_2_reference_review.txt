

# Review of "Interactive Control of Diverse Complex Characters with Neural Networks"

This paper proposes a method for generating neural network controllers for interactive character control by combining trajectory optimization (Contact-Invariant Optimization) with neural network regression. The core contribution is a distributed stochastic optimization approach that jointly optimizes trajectories and neural network policies, with specific techniques for noise injection and feedback gains. The authors claim their method can learn controllers for diverse 3D characters (swimming, flying, bipedal, quadrupedal) that outperform naive neural network training on static trajectory data.

**Strengths:**
The paper provides a clear technical description of the method, including the optimization procedure and noise injection techniques. The ablation studies (comparing with static training and without noise) provide some evidence of the importance of the proposed techniques. The method is applied to a variety of character types in 3D space, demonstrating some level of generality. The distributed training architecture is well-motivated for handling the computational demands of the problem.

**Weaknesses:**
The claimed novelty is questionable. The approach is a straightforward combination of existing techniques (trajectory optimization + neural networks) rather than a fundamentally new method. Previous work by the authors [12] and others [9, 11] have explored similar ideas, and the paper does not sufficiently differentiate its contributions. The paper's claim that "previous work was applied to only two-dimensional characters" is misleading—significant prior work has addressed 3D locomotion (e.g., [13, 21] in robotics and [12] itself).

The evidence provided is insufficient to support the claims of "substantially harder problems" being solved. The paper lacks rigorous comparisons with state-of-the-art methods in the control community (e.g., model-based MPC, reinforcement learning methods like PPO [14] or SAC [18]). The comparison to "naive neural network training" is inadequate as a baseline—this is not a standard approach in the control community.

The evaluation is limited to locomotion tasks without demonstrating the method's ability to handle more complex tasks or providing meaningful comparisons with other approaches. The paper claims "real-time" control but fails to provide detailed timing information or compare execution speed with other controllers. The evaluation metrics are superficial, focusing on visual appeal rather than quantitative performance measures that would allow for proper scientific comparison.

The paper lacks thorough ablation studies on key parameters (e.g., noise levels, network architecture) and doesn't analyze failure modes. The claim that the method "yields stable and realistic locomotion" is based on visual inspection rather than rigorous stability metrics. The paper does not address whether the method can handle perturbations, noise, or modeling errors as claimed in the abstract.

The methodological soundness is questionable. The approach of using trajectory optimization to generate training data for neural networks is common in control (e.g., [9, 11, 14]), but the paper does not provide convincing evidence that this particular implementation offers significant advantages. The distributed optimization procedure (Algorithm 1) appears to be a simple adaptation of existing techniques rather than a novel contribution.

**Significance and Originality:**
The paper does not significantly advance the state of the art. The combination of trajectory optimization and neural networks is not new, and the claimed improvements over previous work are not rigorously demonstrated. The paper does not provide unique data, unique theoretical insights, or a demonstrable advance in the field. The evaluation does not convincingly show that this approach outperforms established methods.

**Overall Assessment:**
While the paper presents a technically sound approach that combines known techniques in a somewhat novel way, the evidence provided is insufficient to support the claims of significant advancement. The method appears to be incremental rather than groundbreaking, and the evaluation lacks the rigor required to justify the claims. The paper does not demonstrate how this approach fundamentally improves upon established methods in control or robotics.

As a reviewer who prefers established methods and is skeptical of unproven approaches, I find this paper to be an incremental extension of prior work without sufficient evidence of meaningful advancement. The authors have not provided thorough validation against established baselines or convincing evidence that their method solves substantially harder problems than previous approaches. Without more rigorous evaluation and clearer differentiation from existing work, the paper does not meet the high standards expected at NIPS.

**Recommendation: Reject**
