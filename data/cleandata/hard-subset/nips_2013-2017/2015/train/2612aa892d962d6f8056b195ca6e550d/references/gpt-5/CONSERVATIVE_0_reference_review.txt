Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper proposes an alternating optimization framework that couples direct trajectory optimization (Contact-Invariant Optimization, CIO) with supervised neural network policy learning for interactive control of complex 3D characters (swimmer, bird, bipeds, quadrupeds). The key idea is to generate optimal trajectories under soft dynamics/contact constraints, then train a feedforward policy πθ to reproduce the actions and a latent recurrent state r along those trajectories. To improve robustness, the authors inject Gaussian noise into the sensory inputs and use first-order expansions a(s+ε) ≈ a + Asε, where local feedback gains As are computed by reusing Hessian-related quantities from the trajectory optimizer via a perturbation/penalty trick. The global objective replaces the policy-consistency constraint with a quadratic penalty, and is optimized by block-alternating updates: per-trajectory re-optimization (with proximal regularization) and a single-epoch batched SGD update for θ. Training is distributed (many CPU nodes for CIO, one GPU for NN). At test time, the policy outputs desired q̇, ṙ, and contact forces, and the next state is obtained by solving a 1-step optimization that softly enforces dynamics and contacts.

This line of work is closely related to Guided Policy Search and trajectory-optimization-based policy learning (e.g., Levine & Koltun 2014; Mordatch & Todorov 2014/RSS), and to TRPO (Schulman et al. 2015). Model-based continuous control approaches such as PILCO (Deisenroth & Rasmussen 2011) and iLQG/LQR variants are also relevant comparators. The authors claim to scale to harder 3D tasks than prior policy-from-optimizer methods, attributing gains to noise-injection, their feedback-gain construction, and distributed training.

Strengths:
- Ambitious engineering effort demonstrating interactive control across diverse 3D morphologies from a common pipeline, seemingly without task-specific state machines or mocap.
- Practical trick to extract/use local feedback gains from direct trajectory optimization by reusing Gauss–Newton structure; conceptually appealing and computationally efficient.
- Sensible use of input and activation noise to reduce overfitting and stabilize closed-loop rollouts; ablations (no joint optimization and no noise) indicate their importance.
- Asynchronous, proximal-regularized alternating updates are simple and scalable, and the training infrastructure is clearly described.

Weaknesses and evaluation against criteria:

Quality:
- Validation is largely qualitative (video) with minimal quantitative evaluation. There are no standardized metrics (success rate, goal error, energy/torque, contact violation magnitudes, falls, robustness to perturbations) nor statistical summaries across seeds/trials.
- Baselines are missing or weak. There is no quantitative comparison to established methods: MPC with identical costs/horizon, GPS/TRPO on at least one morphology/goal-tracking task, or even to training without the feedback-gain targets. The MPC comparison is qualitative and anecdotal.
- Physical realism is compromised by the use of soft dynamics/contact constraints and allowance of root forces both in CIO and during 1-step execution. The magnitude/frequency of such residual forces is not reported, casting doubt on the claimed physical plausibility and limiting relevance to robotics.
- The recurrent memory r is under-specified (dimensionality, initialization, role). The policy is feedforward with an integrator on ṙ, which is an unusual “latent memory” formulation; there is no comparison to standard RNN/LSTM policies nor analysis of necessity/benefit.
- The proposed penalty reformulation (Eq. 4) and block-alternating stochastic optimization lack convergence guarantees; “observed convergence” is not supported by robust diagnostics. Choice/sensitivity of α, η, λ, σε, σγ are not analyzed.
- The feedback-gain derivation depends on λ; there is no empirical sensitivity study, stability analysis, or ablation demonstrating the incremental benefit of As versus plain noisy targets.

Clarity:
- High-level exposition is clear, but important details are missing: exact cost terms and weights (task, dynamics, contact), definition of feature vector φ, dimensionality and semantics of r, and numerical tolerances. Without these, reproduction is difficult.
- Some notational inconsistencies/typos (e.g., repeated “changed changed”; citation formatting) detract from polish.

Originality:
- The overall approach is a combination of known components: direct trajectory optimization, supervised policy learning from optimizer traces, noise injection. The specific construction of feedback gains from CIO via a soft-constraint perturbation reusing the Gauss–Newton system is the most novel element. The asynchronous proximal alternating scheme is a pragmatic variant of existing alternating frameworks.
- Claims of novelty versus Levine & Koltun (2014) and Mordatch & Todorov (2014) hinge on scaling to 3D morphologies; however, without head-to-head baselines on matched tasks, the incremental contribution is difficult to assess.

Significance:
- If substantiated with rigorous, quantitative comparisons, the ability to produce stable 3D controllers without task-specific design would be impactful for graphics/control. As it stands, the heavy reliance on soft constraints and missing baselines tempered by anecdotal evidence limits confidence and likely adoption.
- The engineering recipe (noise, proximal alternation, feedback-gain targets) could be useful to practitioners, but the paper does not isolate which components are essential beyond two simple ablations.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Provide quantitative metrics and statistical evaluations across seeds: tracking error, energy/torque, contact violations, success/fall rates, and robustness to force/observation perturbations.
- Report magnitude distributions of residual/root forces; ideally eliminate them at test time or show results with hard dynamics constraints.
- Add head-to-head baselines: MPC (same costs/horizon), GPS/TRPO for at least one morphology, and ablations on As (with/without), r (with/without), and the T=1 execution optimization (with/without).
- Fully specify costs, φ, r dimensionality, and hyperparameters; include sensitivity analyses (α, η, λ, σε, σγ).
- Clarify the role of the “recurrent memory” and justify the choice of a feedforward net plus integrator versus standard RNNs.

Pros:
- Clear, practical algorithm; strong engineering; diverse demos.
- Sensible robustness techniques; useful feedback-gain extraction trick.

Cons:
- Insufficient quantitative evaluation and missing baselines.
- Soft dynamics and root forces undermine physical validity.
- Key components (r, λ choice, sensitivity) under-specified; limited theoretical support.

Recommendation: Reject (leaning toward borderline if substantial quantitative evidence and stronger baselines are added). Confidence: high.
