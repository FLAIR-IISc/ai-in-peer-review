## Peer Review: Interactive Control of Diverse Complex Characters with Neural Networks

**Summary:**

This paper presents a novel approach to learning controllers for complex, physically simulated characters. The core idea is to combine trajectory optimization (specifically Contact-Invariant Optimization, CIO) with neural network learning.  CIO is used to generate a dataset of optimal trajectories, and a neural network is trained to mimic the control policy derived from these trajectories. Crucially, the authors introduce noise injection and a distributed optimization scheme to improve robustness, generalization, and scalability to high-dimensional, 3D locomotion tasks – a challenge previously unmet by related work focusing primarily on 2D characters. The resulting network allows for real-time, interactive control of diverse characters (swimming, flying, bipeds, quadrupeds) without requiring hand-designed controllers or motion capture data.

**Relation to Prior Work:**

The authors correctly position their work within the broader context of control, reinforcement learning, and optimal control. They acknowledge the limitations of traditional methods (manual design, task-specificity) and the challenges of applying RL to high-dimensional continuous control problems.  They build upon recent work combining trajectory optimization with neural networks [9, 11], but demonstrate superior performance on significantly more complex 3D locomotion tasks.  The authors also acknowledge related work in physics-based control (e.g., state machines, simplified models for bipedal/quadrupedal locomotion [13, 18, 21]) and highlight their aim to unify these disparate approaches.  The inclusion of references to related work on noise injection for robustness and regularization [4, 5, 6, 17, 20] is appropriate.

**Strengths:**

* **Addresses a challenging problem:** Learning robust and generalizable controllers for high-dimensional, physically simulated characters is a longstanding challenge in robotics, animation, and biomechanics.
* **Novel combination of techniques:** The synergy between CIO and neural network learning is compelling. Leveraging the offline optimization power of CIO to generate data for training a real-time control network is a clever approach.
* **Scalability to 3D:** Successfully extending this approach to 3D locomotion is a significant achievement, overcoming limitations of prior work.
* **Generality across character morphologies:**  The ability to control diverse characters (swimming, flying, bipeds, quadrupeds) with a single network architecture is impressive and highlights the generality of the method.
* **Robustness via noise injection:**  The principled approach to noise injection, incorporating optimal feedback gains, is well-motivated and demonstrably improves performance.
* **Detailed implementation details:** The authors provide a thorough description of their distributed training architecture and optimization procedures, aiding reproducibility.
* **Clear experimental validation:** The ablation studies (comparison to static training, noise injection) convincingly demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed techniques.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Computational Cost:** The paper acknowledges the substantial computational resources required for trajectory optimization. While the distributed architecture helps, the "heavy lifting" being done by the optimizer remains a significant limitation for accessibility.  A more detailed breakdown of computational costs (e.g., optimization time per character, GPU hours for training) would be valuable.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** While the authors mention specific parameter values used, there's limited discussion of sensitivity to these parameters.  Understanding the robustness of the method to variations in hyperparameters (e.g., noise levels, penalty weights) would strengthen the results.
* **Limited comparison to direct methods:** The authors state an "impression" that direct methods yield "somewhat chaotic behaviors," but a systematic comparison is only mentioned as a future direction.  Including a more rigorous comparison (even on a limited scale) would be beneficial.
* **Simplified sensory input:** While the chosen sensory representation is reasonable as a starting point, it might limit the controller's ability to handle more complex scenarios. Exploring more informative sensory inputs could potentially improve performance.
* **Lack of user study:**  The interactive control is demonstrated visually, but there's no quantitative evaluation of user experience or control precision. A user study would provide valuable insights into the practical usability of the system.

**Quality:**  The paper is technically sound and the claims are well-supported by experimental results. The methodology is clearly explained and the ablation studies provide strong evidence for the effectiveness of the proposed techniques.

**Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The descriptions of the algorithms and implementation details are thorough. However, some sections (e.g., the derivation of optimal feedback gains) could benefit from further simplification or clarification.

**Originality:** The combination of CIO with neural network learning, coupled with the principled noise injection scheme and distributed optimization, constitutes a novel and significant contribution.

**Significance:** This work has the potential to significantly advance the field of character control, offering a promising alternative to traditional methods. The ability to learn generalizable controllers for diverse characters could have broad applications in robotics, animation, and biomechanics.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* Solves a challenging and important problem.
* Presents a novel and effective approach.
* Demonstrates strong results on complex 3D locomotion tasks.
* Offers a scalable and generalizable solution.
* Thoroughly validates the approach with ablation studies.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* Significant computational cost.
* Limited comparative evaluation against direct methods.
* Lack of user study.




**Overall Assessment:**

I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions. The paper presents a strong and original contribution to the field of character control. The strengths of the work outweigh the weaknesses, and the potential impact is significant. The authors should address the weaknesses by providing a more detailed discussion of computational costs, hyperparameter sensitivity, and potentially including a preliminary comparison to direct methods. A brief discussion of future work including a user study would also be valuable.
