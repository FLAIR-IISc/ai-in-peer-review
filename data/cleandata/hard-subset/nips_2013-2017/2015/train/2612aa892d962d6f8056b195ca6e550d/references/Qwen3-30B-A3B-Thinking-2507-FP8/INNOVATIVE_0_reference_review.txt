

# Review of "Interactive Control of Diverse Complex Characters with Neural Networks"

This paper presents a novel approach to generating neural network controllers for interactive, real-time control of diverse complex characters (bipedal, quadrupedal, flying, swimming) in 3D environments. The core innovation lies in combining trajectory optimization (specifically Contact-Invariant Optimization [12]) with neural network learning to create controllers that work across multiple character morphologies without specialized architectures. While previous work [9, 11] achieved similar results in 2D, this paper demonstrates stable 3D locomotion with realistic behaviors, representing a significant technical advancement.

The authors introduce a distributed stochastic optimization framework that jointly optimizes trajectories and neural network parameters. Key technical contributions include:
1. Using trajectory optimization to generate training data with optimal feedback gains
2. Injecting noise during training to improve robustness to perturbations
3. Creating a unified controller architecture that works across diverse character types
4. Implementing a practical distributed training approach using cloud computing

The method's novelty is particularly compelling: instead of using neural networks to directly learn controllers (as in [3, 6, 14]), the authors leverage trajectory optimization to generate high-quality training data that the neural network then learns to reproduce. This approach yields more realistic and purposeful behaviors than direct learning methods, as the authors demonstrate through compelling examples across multiple character types.

The paper makes a strong case for significance: it solves a fundamental challenge in character control by creating universal controllers that work across diverse morphologies and behaviors. Previous approaches required specialized controllers for each character type (e.g., state machines for bipeds vs. quadrupeds vs. flying characters [21, 18, 8]), while this work uses a single network architecture for all character types. The ability to generate controllers for diverse 3D character types with a single architecture represents a substantial step toward more generalizable control policies.

Strengths:
- Significant novelty in solving a challenging problem (3D universal control) where previous work was limited to 2D
- Demonstrated effectiveness across multiple character types with realistic behaviors
- Practical implementation using cloud computing with clear training methodology
- Clear explanation of technical innovations (optimal feedback gains calculation, noise injection)
- Well-structured experimental validation showing the method's superiority over naive approaches

Weaknesses:
- The theoretical foundation could be strengthened with more rigorous analysis of why the joint optimization approach works
- The comparison with other methods could be more comprehensive (e.g., including MPC results beyond Figure 3)
- The relationship to [9, 11] could be clarified beyond the 2D vs. 3D distinction
- The paper doesn't fully address how this approach scales to even more complex behaviors (e.g., manipulation)

The paper excels in its clear presentation of a bold, novel approach to a significant problem. While the theoretical underpinnings could be more developed, the practical results are compelling and the method represents a meaningful advancement in the field. The authors successfully demonstrate that their approach produces stable, realistic locomotion across diverse character morphologies in 3D space, a capability not previously achieved.

This work stands out in its ability to bridge trajectory optimization and neural network learning to create controllers that are both physically realistic and interactive. The paper's focus on solving a challenging, practical problem with a novel technical approach aligns perfectly with NeurIPS' emphasis on bold, innovative contributions.

I believe this paper makes a significant contribution to the field. The novelty of the approach is substantial, the results are compelling, and the method has clear potential for broader applications in animation, gaming, and robotics. The minor weaknesses in theoretical justification and comparative evaluation do not detract from the paper's core contribution, which is a significant technical advance in character control.

Given NeurIPS' emphasis on novelty and bold approaches over methodological perfection, I recommend acceptance. The paper introduces a new direction in character control that could influence future work in animation, robotics, and embodied AI. The authors have successfully demonstrated a method that solves a problem previously considered intractable for complex 3D characters, and their technical approach is both innovative and practical.

To further strengthen the paper, I would recommend:
1. Adding a more detailed comparison with other state-of-the-art methods
2. Providing more theoretical justification for the joint optimization framework
3. Clarifying the relationship to [9, 11] beyond the 2D vs. 3D distinction

However, these are refinements rather than fundamental issues. The paper's core contribution is strong enough to warrant acceptance as is.
