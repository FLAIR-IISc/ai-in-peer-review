Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes a method to learn interactive, real-time controllers for complex 3D characters by combining direct trajectory optimization with supervised neural network policy learning. The key idea is to: (i) generate optimal trajectories with Contact-Invariant Optimization (CIO), (ii) compute local optimal feedback gains around these trajectories via a perturbation-based method that reuses the Gauss-Newton Hessian from CIO, and (iii) train a neural policy to imitate the locally optimal actions under injected sensory noise, using targets a + as ε. The global objective alternates between re-optimizing trajectories with a quadratic penalty that encourages agreement with the current policy and proximal regularization, and regressing the policy with noise on inputs and hidden activations. Execution uses a one-step constrained optimization (T=1) to enforce dynamics while following the policy’s desired velocities/forces, allowing larger timesteps.

This builds on CIO for synthesizing realistic motions, and on recent work combining trajectory optimization with policy learning [Levine & Koltun 2014; Mordatch & Todorov 2014], but extends to more complex 3D tasks and diverse morphologies (flying, swimming, bipeds, quadrupeds). It contrasts with direct RL methods (e.g., DQN, TRPO) and classical animation control architectures (state machines, simplified models) by seeking a unified pipeline and more stable, purposeful behaviors.

Strengths
- Clear overall formulation of alternating optimization between trajectory synthesis and policy regression, with principled noise injection tied to local feedback.
- Novel and practical method to derive stabilizing feedback gains from a direct trajectory optimizer via a soft-constraint perturbation that reuses the Gauss-Newton system, mitigating ill-conditioning seen with a separate LQG pass.
- Asynchronous distributed training architecture with proximal regularization enabling scalable data generation and training; practical runtime details are provided.
- Demonstrated generality across multiple 3D morphologies and contact-rich behaviors using a single network architecture and training procedure, with interactive control.
- Ablations indicate the importance of joint optimization and noise; comparison to MPC suggests regularization toward cyclic, less chaotic behaviors.

Weaknesses
- Empirical evaluation is largely qualitative; quantitative metrics (task success, stability, energy/torque, robustness to perturbations, tracking error over time) are minimal and rely on figures/videos.
- Baseline comparisons are limited. Claims relative to direct RL and to LQG are plausible but not supported by controlled experiments; the MPC comparison is illustrative but narrow.
- Cost terms are only sketched; precise components and weights (beyond a few hyperparameters) are not fully specified, which hinders reproducibility and analysis of what drives learned style.
- The execution model uses soft dynamics constraints with possible root forces and a large timestep; this may undermine physical realism and limits conclusions for robotics.
- Convergence properties of the asynchronous alternating scheme are empirical; no diagnostics on policy fit error, divergence episodes, or sensitivity to λ, α, η are reported.

Quality
Technical soundness: The alternating optimization and CIO components are standard and correctly described. The derivation of feedback gains via augmenting the cost with a soft constraint and applying the Woodbury identity to reuse CXX−1 is sound, given the Gauss-Newton approximation. The noise-augmented supervision a + as ε is well motivated to train closed-loop-stable policies. However, evaluation lacks rigorous quantitative validation and baselines to substantiate claims of improved stability/realism beyond ablations. The real-time execution step is consistent with the training formulation but relies on soft dynamics; implications for true physical feasibility are not evaluated.

Clarity
The paper is generally well written and organized, with an explicit algorithm, architecture, and implementation details (network depth/width, noise scales, compute setup). Some notation/typos remain (e.g., argmin∗, repeated words), and crucial details of the cost c(·) are deferred. The sensory/action representation is defined but could benefit from clearer articulation of included features and normalization. Figures/tables referenced (ablation results) are not fully described in the text.

Originality
The overall approach is a novel and effective combination of known ingredients, with two original contributions: (i) the perturbation-based feedback gain computation within a direct trajectory optimization framework, and (ii) an alternating, noise-aware imitation scheme that scales to diverse 3D characters. While adjacent to [9,11], the technical additions and demonstrated scope appear meaningfully beyond prior 2D results.

Significance
For ML-for-control and graphics, the work advances the practical synthesis of robust, interactive controllers from optimization-generated data, reducing reliance on handcrafted state machines. The demonstrated breadth across morphologies is valuable. Broader impact is limited by evaluation gaps and the use of soft dynamics; stronger quantitative and baseline comparisons would increase significance for the NeurIPS audience and for robotics.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide quantitative metrics: success rate to goal under disturbances, deviation over long rollouts, torque/energy, gait stability measures, and measure of contact consistency.
- Add baselines: TRPO or other continuous-control RL on the same tasks; LQG-based gain extraction vs the proposed method; MPC with various horizons; motion imitation approaches where applicable.
- Detail the cost terms and weighting; report sensitivity analyses for λ (feedback softness), α (policy penalty), η (proximal rate), and timestep.
- Report policy training/fit error across iterations and convergence curves; include robustness tests (sensor noise, model mismatch).
- Discuss execution-time constraint enforcement rigor (root forces) and consequences; optionally present a “hard-constraint” variant for comparison.

Pros for acceptance
- Technically sound integration of trajectory optimization and policy learning with a novel, efficient feedback gain computation.
- Demonstrated interactive control across diverse 3D characters without task-specific architectures.
- Practical, scalable training system; clear ablation evidence for key design choices.

Cons for acceptance
- Limited quantitative evaluation and baseline comparisons; reliance on qualitative demos.
- Use of soft dynamics and root forces at execution raises questions about physical fidelity and transferability.
- Incomplete specification of costs and sensitivity analyses reduces reproducibility and insight.

Recommendation
Lean accept. The method is technically solid, addresses an important problem, and shows broad applicability, but the paper would benefit from stronger quantitative evaluation and baselines to fully substantiate its claims.
