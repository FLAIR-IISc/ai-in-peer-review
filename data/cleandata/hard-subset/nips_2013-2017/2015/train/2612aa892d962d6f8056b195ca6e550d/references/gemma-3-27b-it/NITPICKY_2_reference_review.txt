This paper presents a method for learning neural network controllers for complex characters by combining trajectory optimization (specifically, Contact-Invariant Optimization – CIO) with supervised learning. The core idea is to leverage CIO to generate training data and then use a neural network to learn a policy that can reproduce the optimized behaviors in real-time. The authors address challenges of high dimensionality and instability by injecting noise and using a distributed optimization scheme. They demonstrate the approach on diverse characters, including swimming, flying, and legged robots.

While the paper tackles an important and challenging problem – interactive control of complex characters – and presents a reasonably well-executed approach, I find myself leaning towards rejection due to a multitude of minor, yet persistent, shortcomings that detract from the overall rigor and clarity. The presentation, while generally understandable, suffers from a lack of precision in several areas, and a seemingly cavalier attitude towards justifying design choices.

**Strengths:**

*   **Problem Significance:** The goal of automating control design for complex characters is undeniably important with applications in robotics, animation, and biomechanics.
*   **Demonstrated Capability:** The results, as showcased in the supplementary video (which is crucial for assessing the work), demonstrate the ability to generate plausible locomotion behaviors for a variety of character morphologies. The claim of achieving stable 3D locomotion where previous work was limited to 2D is a notable achievement.
*   **Combined Approach:** The synergy between trajectory optimization and neural network learning is a sensible approach, allowing for both high-quality behaviors and real-time control.
*   **Distributed Implementation:** The description of the distributed training architecture is relatively clear and highlights the computational demands of this type of work.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Lack of Quantitative Comparison:** The claim that networks trained with direct methods produce “somewhat chaotic behaviors” is asserted without any quantitative backing. A systematic comparison, as acknowledged by the authors, is desperately needed. The absence of such a comparison weakens the justification for the trajectory-optimization-based approach.
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The choice of hyperparameters (σε, σγ, α, λ, η) is presented without adequate justification. The authors state these values were used in *all* experiments, which raises concerns about sensitivity and potential sub-optimality for specific characters. A brief ablation study exploring the impact of these parameters would significantly strengthen the paper.
*   **Overly Concise Dynamics:** The state representation `st = [ qt rt q̇t−1 f t−1 ]` feels insufficient, especially considering the complexity of the characters. Ignoring previous actions seems particularly problematic. The justification for this specific formulation is weak.
*   **Ambiguous “Soft Constraints”:** The implementation of physical realism via “soft constraints” is glossed over. The details of these constraints – their exact formulation and weighting – are crucial for reproducibility and understanding the robustness of the system.
*   **Inconsistent Notation:** There are instances of inconsistent notation. For example, ‘as’ is introduced as the matrix of optimal feedback gains, but is later used seemingly interchangeably with other derivatives.
*   **Minor Technical Details:** The discussion of the Hessian approximation in section 3.1 feels incomplete. The implications of truncating the Hessian are not fully explored.
*    **Algorithm 1 Clarification:** Step 3 of Algorithm 1 references "neural network regression" yet the associated text and section 4 describes stochastic gradient descent. These are not synonymous and the nuance should be clarified.
*   **Reference Formatting:** Some references are incomplete or missing essential information. For example, reference [1] is simply "P. Chen, SIAM J. Numerical Analysis,, , ,"

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Demonstrates a working system for interactive control of diverse characters.
*   Successfully extends previous work on trajectory optimization and neural network control to higher-dimensional spaces.
*   Highlights the computational challenges of this type of research.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   Lack of quantitative evaluation and comparison to alternative methods.
*   Poor justification for key design choices (hyperparameters, state representation).
*   Numerous minor technical inconsistencies and omissions.
*   Overreliance on qualitative assessments and anecdotal evidence.



**Overall:**

While the ideas presented are interesting and the results are promising, the paper suffers from a lack of thoroughness and rigor. The numerous minor flaws, combined with the absence of a strong quantitative evaluation, lead me to recommend **rejection**. The paper has the potential to be a valuable contribution, but it requires substantial revisions to address the concerns outlined above. Specifically, a rigorous comparison to baseline methods, a sensitivity analysis of key hyperparameters, and a more detailed explanation of the technical implementation are essential for improving the quality of the work. The authors' seeming dismissal of the importance of these details is particularly concerning.
