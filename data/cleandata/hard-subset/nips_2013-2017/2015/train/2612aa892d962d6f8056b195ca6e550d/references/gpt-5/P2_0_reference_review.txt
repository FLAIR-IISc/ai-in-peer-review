Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes a framework for learning interactive, real-time control policies for complex physics-based characters by combining direct trajectory optimization (Contact-Invariant Optimization, CIO) with supervised training of deep neural networks. The core idea is to (i) synthesize diverse optimal trajectories for multiple tasks and initial conditions; (ii) fit a neural policy that maps a compact sensory state to desired velocity, contact forces, and a learned internal “memory” state; and (iii) jointly refine trajectories and policy via an alternating, distributed stochastic optimization with proximal regularization. Two technical ingredients are emphasized: (a) injecting Gaussian noise in sensory inputs and neural activations during training, with targets adjusted using locally optimal feedback gains, to improve closed-loop robustness; and (b) a new way to compute these gains from a direct optimizer using a soft-constraint perturbation and Woodbury identity, reusing the Gauss-Newton inverse already computed by CIO. At execution time, the policy outputs are enforced via a T=1 optimization step with softly enforced dynamics constraints, enabling stable real-time control at 50 ms steps.

The approach is positioned relative to trajectory-optimization-plus-learning (e.g., guided policy search–style methods [Levine & Koltun ICML’14; Mordatch & Todorov RSS’14]) and model-free RL (e.g., TRPO, DQN). The novelty claimed is scaling to diverse 3D locomotion, swimming, and flying with a single policy architecture, and an asynchronous distributed training pipeline. Qualitative comparisons with MPC are included; ablations demonstrate benefits of joint optimization and noise injection.

Strengths
- Addresses a hard and impactful problem: robust, interactive control of high-DOF 3D characters across morphologies and tasks, with promising qualitative results (cyclic gaits, flapping, swimming).
- Sound integration of CIO with policy learning via an alternating scheme with proximal regularization; the distributed, asynchronous implementation is practical and well-described.
- The perturbation-based feedback gain computation that reuses the CIO Hessian is elegant, computationally efficient, and likely of independent interest for stabilizing trajectories from direct methods.
- Thoughtful regularization choices: sensory and hidden-unit noise, spectral-radius initialization; the design explicitly targets closed-loop stability, not just per-step regression.
- Clear ablation showing that (i) joint optimization beats static supervised learning on fixed optimal trajectories, and (ii) noise prevents overfitting and divergence.

Weaknesses
- Limited quantitative evaluation and baselines. Beyond an ablation and a small architecture sweep, there is no rigorous comparison to closely related guided policy search (e.g., Levine & Koltun 2014) or strong model-free baselines (TRPO) on matched tasks. The MPC comparison is qualitative and anecdotal.
- Physics fidelity at test time is partially compromised: the execution step uses soft dynamics constraints and allows root forces, which can mask instabilities and limit relevance to robotics. There is no metric quantifying constraint violations or contact realism during execution.
- Ambiguity around the “recurrent” state r: its dimensionality, role, and learning dynamics are not clearly specified. The network appears feedforward with an externally integrated memory variable; clarity and justification are needed (e.g., why not an RNN?).
- The policy outputs desired velocities and contact forces directly. In physical settings, contact forces are constrained by friction cones and feasibility; relying on a subsequent optimization to reconcile them may hinder transferability. Empirical sensitivity to mismatch between policy outputs and feasible dynamics is not analyzed.
- Missing quantitative robustness analysis: while noise injection is used, there is no systematic study of robustness to perturbations, model errors, or sensor delays. Generalization to out-of-distribution goals or terrains is not reported.
- Some important details are underspecified for reproducibility: exact feature definitions and normalizations; cost terms and weights; the dimension of r; timing and computational footprint at execution; convergence behavior of the alternating optimization.

Quality
The technical components are mostly sound. The feedback gain derivation is plausible and well-motivated, leveraging the available Gauss-Newton inverse; the alternation with proximal terms is reasonable for asynchrony. Claims about improved stability and realism are supported by ablations and qualitative demonstrations, but broader claims (3D stability, superiority to prior methods) would be stronger with quantitative metrics (task completion, falls, energy, constraint violations) and matched baselines. The paper is honest about scope and limitations, but lacks some critical comparisons (e.g., LQG-based gains vs proposed gains).

Clarity
Generally well-written and organized, with a clear high-level algorithm and training architecture. However:
- Precisely specify s and a for all character types; detail the size and initialization of r, and why this design is chosen over recurrent networks.
- Provide exact cost functions (including weights) and feature definitions φ to enable reproduction.
- Clarify how contact forces in a are handled when infeasible, and report the magnitude/frequency of soft constraint violations during execution.
- Include more quantitative plots (e.g., training/test error curves, constraint violations, trajectory variability).

Originality
Moderate to high. Combining CIO with policy learning has clear antecedents, but the specific stochastic alternation, the way noise is injected with targets adjusted by locally optimal gains, and the perturbation-based gain computation inside a direct optimizer add novelty. The breadth of 3D demonstrations from one pipeline is a compelling contribution for the NIPS community bridging RL/control and graphics.

Significance
For graphics/animation, the ability to produce interactive, realistic controllers across morphologies without handcrafting is valuable. For RL/control, the work reinforces the efficacy of trajectory-optimization-guided policy learning and contributes a practical method to extract stabilizing gains from direct methods. The impact would be higher with stronger quantitative evidence and comparisons, and with constraints tightened to be more physically plausible for robotics.

Questions for the authors
- What is the dimension of r, how is it initialized, and how sensitive is performance to this choice? Why not use an RNN?
- How large are dynamics/contact constraint violations at test time? Can you report statistics and ablate the effect of allowing root forces?
- How does your feedback-gain method compare quantitatively to an LQG pass (stability, conditioning, performance)?
- Can you include matched comparisons to GPS/TRPO on at least a subset of tasks?
- What is the real-time compute cost per control step on CPU/GPU?

Pros for acceptance
- Addresses an important, challenging problem with a unified method and strong qualitative breadth.
- Novel and practical technique for computing stabilizing feedback gains from direct trajectory optimization.
- Clear engineering contribution: distributed training pipeline enabling large-scale data generation and policy learning.
- Ablations substantiate key design choices (joint optimization, noise).

Cons for acceptance
- Missing strong baselines and quantitative metrics; reliance on qualitative video.
- Soft dynamics/contacts at test time weaken physical realism and applicability to robotics.
- Some design choices (memory state r, action parameterization) are underspecified and not justified experimentally.

Overall recommendation
Borderline accept. The paper advances the trajectory-optimization-plus-learning paradigm with a clever gain computation, demonstrates impressive qualitative results in 3D, and presents a practical training pipeline. Strengthening quantitative evaluation and clarifying key design choices would materially improve the work. Confidence: medium.
