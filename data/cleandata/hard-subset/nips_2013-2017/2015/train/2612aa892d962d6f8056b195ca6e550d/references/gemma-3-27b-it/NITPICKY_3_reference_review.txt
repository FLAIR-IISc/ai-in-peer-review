This paper presents a method for learning neural network control policies for complex characters by combining trajectory optimization (specifically Contact-Invariant Optimization, CIO) with supervised learning. The core idea is to use CIO to generate training data, then train a neural network to mimic the resulting control actions, enabling real-time interactive control. The authors demonstrate this approach on diverse character morphologies, including swimming, flying, bipedal, and quadrupedal creatures. 

While the paper addresses an important and challenging problem – scalable control of complex characters – and demonstrates promising results, a multitude of minor issues detract from its overall quality and ultimately lead me to recommend rejection. The sheer volume of these issues, even if individually small, raises concerns about the rigor and attention to detail applied throughout the work.

**Strengths:**

* **Novel Combination:** The combination of CIO and neural network learning is a sensible approach to address the challenges of both offline trajectory optimization and real-time control.
* **Broad Applicability:** The demonstrated application to diverse character types suggests the potential for a general control framework.
* **Interactive Control:** The ability to achieve interactive control is a significant achievement, particularly for complex, 3D characters.
* **Addressing Challenges:** The authors acknowledge and attempt to address key challenges like data scarcity and overfitting through noise injection.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Lack of Rigorous Comparison:** The claim of outperforming “direct methods” (e.g., reinforcement learning) is made without a systematic comparison. The statement "our impression is that networks trained with direct methods give rise to successful yet somewhat chaotic behaviors" is anecdotal and unsubstantiated. A proper comparison, even a limited one, is crucial.
* **Insufficient Ablation Study:** While some ablation studies are presented (section 8.1), they are not comprehensive enough. For instance, the impact of different noise levels (σγ and σε) is not explored systematically. Why were those specific values chosen?
* **Unclear Hyperparameter Choices:** Many hyperparameters are mentioned (α, λ, η, network architecture) without clear justification for their selection. What sensitivity analysis was performed? The values are simply stated, leaving the reader wondering if the results are sensitive to these choices.
* **Ambiguous Terminology:** The use of "sensory state" and "action" is sometimes inconsistent. It is unclear whether ‘action’ refers to the network output or the control signal applied to the physics engine. Precise definitions are needed.
* **Redundancy and Verbosity:** The introduction is overly long and repetitive. Several sentences state the obvious (e.g., "large datasets are available" for computer vision) and do not contribute to the paper's argument.
* **Minor Technical Inaccuracies/Omissions:**
    * In section 2.1, equation (2) lacks clarity. *as* is introduced as the “matrix of optimal feedback gains,” but it's unclear if this is a full matrix or a diagonal approximation. The context suggests the latter, but it should be stated explicitly.
    * The claim in section 3.2 that the perturbation method avoids ill-conditioning is not convincingly demonstrated.
    *  Equation (8) presents a recursion for X*, but doesn’t detail how the initial X is initialized, which is crucial for Newton’s method.
* **Formatting/Style Issues:** Numerous minor formatting inconsistencies are present (e.g., inconsistent use of boldface, variable spacing). Although minor, they detract from the paper's professionalism.
* **Missing Detail in Algorithm 1:** Algorithm 1 mentions “optimal feedback gains” but does not specify how these are used in neural network regression, leaving this step ambiguous.
* **Limited Discussion of Failure Cases:** The paper focuses on successes, but doesn't discuss scenarios where the method *fails* or struggles. What types of characters or behaviors are particularly challenging?

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The work tackles a relevant and challenging problem.
* The combination of CIO and neural networks is a promising approach.
* The results demonstrate the feasibility of learning controllers for diverse character morphologies.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

* The lack of rigorous comparison to existing methods (particularly reinforcement learning) weakens the claims of superiority.
* The numerous minor flaws in technical detail, clarity, and presentation demonstrate a lack of meticulousness.
* Insufficient justification for hyperparameter choices and limited ablation studies raise concerns about the robustness and generalizability of the results.
* The verbose and repetitive writing style detracts from the paper's overall impact.



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite the novelty of the approach and the promising results, the paper's numerous shortcomings prevent me from recommending acceptance. The lack of rigorous evaluation, unclear presentation, and multitude of minor flaws suggest that the work is not yet ready for publication in a venue as selective as NIPS. The authors should address these issues through a more thorough and detailed revision before resubmission. Specifically, a comprehensive comparison to other control methods, a systematic ablation study, and a more polished presentation are essential.
