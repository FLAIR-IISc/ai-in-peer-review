## Review of "Interactive Control of Diverse Complex Characters with Neural Networks"

**Summary:**

This paper presents a method for learning neural network control policies for complex, 3D articulated characters. The core idea is to combine trajectory optimization (specifically Contact-Invariant Optimization - CIO) with neural network regression.  CIO is used to generate a dataset of optimal trajectories, and a neural network is then trained to mimic these trajectories, enabling real-time, interactive control. The authors address challenges related to data acquisition (synthesizing training data via optimization), training in high-dimensional spaces, and generalization through the use of noise injection and a distributed training architecture.  They demonstrate the approach on a variety of characters including swimming, flying, bipedal and quadrupedal creatures.

**Relation to Previous Work:**

The paper correctly situates itself within the broader context of neural network control and trajectory optimization. It acknowledges prior work in reinforcement learning and optimal control, noting their difficulties scaling to complex, high-dimensional problems.  It also acknowledges related work leveraging neural networks to learn from trajectory optimization (Levine & Koltun, Mordatch & Todorov), differentiating itself by tackling more challenging 3D locomotion tasks. The authors appropriately mention related work in physics-based control, highlighting the limitations of task-specific architectures.

**Strengths:**

* **Addresses a challenging problem:**  Interactive control of complex characters is a notoriously difficult task. This work offers a compelling approach to automating the controller design process.
* **Scalability & Generalization:** The combination of CIO for data generation and a neural network for real-time control is a sensible strategy for scaling to high-dimensional problems. The noise injection technique is well-motivated and likely contributes to better generalization, as demonstrated by the ablation studies.
* **Unified Framework:** The method appears to be remarkably general, successfully controlling a diverse range of characters (swimming, flying, walking) with the *same* network architecture and parameters. This is a significant achievement, moving towards a more unified control framework.
* **Detailed Implementation & Analysis:**  The paper provides a good level of detail regarding the implementation, including the distributed training architecture, optimization algorithms, and parameter settings. The ablation studies (Fig. 2, Table 1) are helpful in understanding the contribution of different components.
* **Strong Empirical Results:** The demonstration of stable and realistic locomotion in 3D is impressive, especially considering the complexity of the characters. The supplementary video likely showcases these results effectively.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Comparison to Direct Methods:**  While the authors acknowledge the existence of direct methods for training neural network controllers (e.g., reinforcement learning), they only offer an "impression" that these methods yield more chaotic behaviors. A more systematic, quantitative comparison would significantly strengthen the paper.  It's crucial to demonstrate *why* their approach outperforms alternatives.
* **Computational Cost:** The authors acknowledge that generating the training data (via CIO) is more computationally expensive than training the neural network.  While this is not necessarily a fatal flaw, it limits the practical applicability of the method, especially for scenarios requiring frequent re-training or adaptation.  A discussion of potential strategies for reducing this cost would be beneficial.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper lists the values of algorithmic constants used, but doesn’t discuss sensitivity to these hyperparameters. It’s important to understand how robust the method is to changes in these settings.
* **Lack of User Study/Interactive Evaluation:** The paper demonstrates *automated* control, but doesn’t explicitly show the “interactive” aspect. A user study or demonstration of how a user can effectively manipulate the character in real-time would further highlight the practical value of this work.



**Qualitative Evaluation:**

* **Quality:** Technically sound and well-supported. The theoretical justification for noise injection and the detailed analysis of the optimization process are convincing.  It feels like a complete piece of work, given the scope.
* **Clarity:**  Generally well-written and organized. The notation is mostly clear, although some familiarity with trajectory optimization is helpful.  More explanation on the “recurrent memory state” (r) and its role in the policy would be beneficial.
* **Originality:**  The combination of CIO and neural network regression, applied to complex 3D locomotion, is a novel contribution.  The perturbation method for calculating optimal feedback gains is also a clever idea. 
* **Significance:**  This work has the potential to significantly impact the field of character animation and robotics. A unified control framework applicable to diverse morphologies is a valuable contribution. The method could be used to create more realistic and engaging virtual characters, or to develop robust controllers for physical robots.



**Arguments For/Against Acceptance:**

**For:**

* Addresses a difficult and important problem.
* Demonstrates strong empirical results with a compelling approach.
* Presents a novel and well-motivated method.
* Offers a potentially unified control framework.

**Against:**

* Limited comparison to alternative methods.
* High computational cost.
* Lack of a dedicated user study.




**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper is a **strong accept**. While there are some weaknesses, the strengths of the work outweigh them. The authors tackle a challenging problem, propose a novel and well-supported solution, and demonstrate impressive results. Addressing the suggested improvements (especially a more thorough comparison to other methods) would further strengthen the paper. I am confident that this work will be of significant interest to the NIPS community.
