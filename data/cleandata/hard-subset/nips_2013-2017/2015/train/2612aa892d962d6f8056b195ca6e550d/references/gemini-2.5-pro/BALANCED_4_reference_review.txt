### Summary

This paper presents a method for automatically synthesizing neural network control policies for a diverse set of complex, physically-simulated 3D characters. The goal is to create a single, unified framework that can produce robust, interactive controllers for various morphologies (bipeds, quadrupeds, swimmers, fliers) without task-specific engineering.

The core of the proposed method is a joint optimization problem over both a set of reference trajectories and the parameters of a neural network policy. This problem is solved via an alternating optimization scheme:
1.  **Trajectory Optimization:** Multiple optimal trajectories are generated for various tasks (e.g., locomotion to different goals) using Contact-Invariant Optimization (CIO), a powerful direct trajectory optimization method.
2.  **Policy Regression:** A neural network is trained via supervised learning to mimic the state-action pairs from these optimized trajectories.

The key novelty lies in a principled approach to making this "learning from an expert" framework robust enough for closed-loop control. The authors introduce a stochastic training procedure where Gaussian noise is added to the sensory inputs of the policy. Crucially, the target actions are simultaneously corrected using optimal feedback gains derived directly from the trajectory optimizer's Hessian. This teaches the network not just to follow a trajectory, but also how to react to perturbations. The entire system is implemented in a distributed architecture to handle the computational load.

This work builds upon prior research combining trajectory optimization and function approximation (e.g., Levine & Koltun, 2014; Mordatch & Todorov, 2014) but makes a significant leap by demonstrating success on a much wider and more challenging class of 3D characters, enabled by the aforementioned technical innovations.

### Strengths

*   **Exceptional Results and Generality:** The most significant strength of this paper is the impressive quality and diversity of the resulting controllers. The ability to use a single algorithm, with fixed hyperparameters, to generate stable and natural-looking gaits (walking, trotting, swimming, flying) for vastly different 3D character morphologies is a substantial achievement. The emergent cyclic behaviors, which were not explicitly programmed, are particularly compelling.
*   **Novel and Sound Technical Contributions:** The method for robustifying the policy is a key contribution. Injecting noise is a common technique, but the proposed method of correcting the target action using optimal feedback gains (`a + a_s ε`) is novel and principled. The technique for efficiently extracting these feedback gains from a direct trajectory optimizer (CIO) by reusing the computed Hessian is clever and could be of independent interest to the control community.
*   **Strong Empirical Validation:** The authors provide a convincing ablation study (Figure 2) that clearly demonstrates the necessity of their main contributions. Training on a static dataset (`no joint`) or training without noise (`no noise`) both lead to policies that fail catastrophically at execution time. This provides strong evidence that the joint, stochastic optimization is critical to the method's success.
*   **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is very well-written and easy to follow. The problem formulation is laid out clearly, and the motivation for each component of the algorithm is well-explained. Algorithm 1 provides a concise overview of the overall training loop.

### Weaknesses

*   **Details of Policy Execution:** Section 7, which describes how the policy is executed in real-time, is somewhat unclear. It states that the next state is found by solving a single-step optimization problem subject to the full dynamics constraints (Eq. 6). This implies that the final controller is not a simple feed-forward pass of the neural network, but rather a hybrid system where the network provides targets for a real-time optimizer. This is an important detail that affects the conceptual understanding and computational cost of the final controller. More information on the solver, runtime, and robustness of this single-step optimization would significantly strengthen the paper.
*   **Comparison to Alternatives:** The paper qualitatively contrasts its results with those from "direct" reinforcement learning methods, suggesting the latter are more "chaotic." This is a strong claim that is not substantiated with a quantitative or even a direct qualitative comparison. Including a baseline comparison against a contemporary continuous control RL algorithm (e.g., TRPO, which the authors cite) on one of the characters would have made the paper's claims about the quality of its motions much more compelling.
*   **Convergence Properties:** The paper states that the alternating optimization algorithm converges in practice, but provides no theoretical analysis of its convergence properties. While this is a very difficult non-convex problem, a brief discussion of the role of the proximal regularization terms in stabilizing the optimization would be welcome.

### Constructive Suggestions for Improvement

1.  **Clarify Policy Execution (Section 7):** Please provide more details on the single-step optimization used at runtime. Is this a convex problem? What solver is used? What is its average computational cost per timestep, and how does this support the claim of "real-time rates"? A more detailed description is crucial for reproducibility and for a complete understanding of the final control system.
2.  **Elaborate on the Recurrent State:** The recurrent state `r` is part of the state-action definition but is not discussed in detail. It would be helpful to specify its dimensionality and briefly explain its role. For instance, does it help the policy learn history-dependent behaviors like gait cycles?
3.  **Provide Cost Function Details:** The specific cost functions `C(X)` used in the trajectory optimization are central to the emergent behaviors. While the paper describes the goals at a high level, providing the precise mathematical formulations (e.g., in an appendix) would greatly benefit researchers wishing to build upon this work.
4.  **Temper Claims or Strengthen Comparisons:** I would suggest either toning down the qualitative claims regarding the inferiority of direct RL methods or, ideally for future work, providing a direct experimental comparison to demonstrate the benefits of the proposed approach in terms of motion quality and realism.

### Recommendation

Despite the minor weaknesses, this is an excellent paper presenting a significant advance in automated controller synthesis. The generality of the method and the quality of the results are outstanding. The core technical ideas are novel, well-motivated, and empirically validated. This work is of high quality and is likely to have a substantial impact on the fields of character animation, robotics, and reinforcement learning. I strongly recommend acceptance.
