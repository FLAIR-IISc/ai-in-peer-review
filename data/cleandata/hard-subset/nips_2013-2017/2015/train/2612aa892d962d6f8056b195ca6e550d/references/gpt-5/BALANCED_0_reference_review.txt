Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes an alternating optimization framework that learns interactive, real-time controllers for diverse 3D articulated characters by combining trajectory optimization (Contact-Invariant Optimization, CIO) with supervised neural network policy learning. The core idea is to (i) generate optimal trajectories for task objectives via CIO, (ii) compute local feedback gains around these trajectories, and (iii) train a neural network policy to map noisy sensory inputs to actions that match locally optimal responses (a + Asε). The training alternates between re-optimizing trajectories with a proximal penalty that encourages agreement with the current policy and refitting the policy to the (noisy) optimized trajectories. Noise is injected both at the inputs and into hidden activations to improve robustness. A distributed asynchronous system is used to scale trajectory optimization across machines and to train the policy on a GPU. At execution time, the policy outputs desired state rates and contact forces, and the next state is obtained by solving a short-horizon (T=1) dynamics-consistent optimization, enabling real-time control. The method is demonstrated on swimming, flying, bipedal, and quadrupedal characters, with ablations showing the importance of joint optimization and noise.

This work sits at the intersection of model-based trajectory optimization and function approximation for policy learning, akin to Guided Policy Search/trajectory optimization with supervised learning [Levine & Koltun, ICML’14] and follow-ups, and to prior CIO-based complex behavior synthesis [Mordatch et al. TOG’12; RSS’12]. It also relates to deep RL for continuous control (e.g., TRPO [Schulman et al. 2015]) and to MPC-based control. The paper’s stated novelty is scaling to diverse 3D locomotion/flying/swimming characters with a single architecture, introducing a practical way to compute feedback gains from direct trajectory optimization, and an asynchronous distributed training loop with proximal regularization.

Strengths
- Clear and appealing problem: robust, real-time, interactive control across morphologies without hand-engineered state machines or motion capture.
- Solid integration of known components in a coherent system: CIO for trajectory generation, denoising-style regression with feedback-targets, noise injection, and a pragmatic distributed implementation.
- Technical contribution in computing local feedback gains directly from the Gauss-Newton structure of CIO (via a soft-constrained perturbation and Woodbury identity), reusing C−1XX and avoiding a separate LQG pass.
- Demonstrations on diverse 3D settings (swimmer, bird, multiple bipeds, quadrupeds), with emergent cyclic gaits.
- Useful ablations indicating the benefits of joint alternation and noise.

Weaknesses
- Quantitative evaluation is limited. Beyond ablations, there are few standardized metrics (success rates, energy, slip, stability margins, perturbation recovery) and no head-to-head comparisons with strong baselines (GPS/iLQR variants, TRPO/DDPG, pure MPC). Claims of “substantially harder problems” and improved predictability over MPC would be more convincing with quantitative and visual comparisons across tasks.
- Execution relies on a T=1 optimization with soft dynamics constraints, possibly including “root forces.” This undermines realism/physicality and makes it difficult to attribute performance to the learned policy vs. the online optimizer. The action space (desired velocities and contact forces) is unusual relative to torque control, and mapping to torques is only implicitly handled via the optimization, with unclear guarantees.
- Details on the recurrent state r, its dimensionality, and how memory improves control are sparse. It is unclear whether the demonstrated behaviors depend on recurrence, and no comparison to a purely feedforward policy is provided.
- Sensitivity and robustness are underexplored: stability under unmodeled disturbances, domain randomization, contact model mismatch, and larger goal changes are not systematically evaluated.
- Methodological clarity gaps: training hyperparameters, stopping criteria, data volume, and convergence diagnostics are not well specified. The feedback gain derivation is local; stability and conditioning as a function of λ are not analyzed empirically.
- References are incomplete and some formatting is inconsistent; related work coverage in deep RL/control could be expanded, and comparisons to contemporary guided policy search methods are thin.
- Scope creep toward graphics: the evaluation focuses on visual behaviors; for NeurIPS, stronger quantitative RL/control evidence is expected.

Quality
Partially convincing. The system engineering is thoughtful, and the feedback-gain computation is a neat addition. However, the reliance on soft constraints/root forces and lack of robust quantitative comparisons weaken the technical validation. It is unclear how the approach fares against GPS/TRPO when evaluated on the same tasks under common metrics.

Clarity
Generally well written with a clear high-level algorithm. Some crucial details are missing or under-specified: the role/size of r, precise action-to-torque mapping at test time, training schedules, and evaluation metrics. Figures/tables referenced (e.g., Fig. 2–3, Table 1) would be essential.

Originality
Moderate. The overall recipe (trajectory optimization + supervised policy learning with noise) is close to prior GPS-style work. The new pieces are the feedback-gain computation from direct trajectory optimization, the specific alternating/proximal asynchronous setup, and the breadth of 3D character demonstrations.

Significance
Potentially impactful for physics-based animation and as a step toward robust model-based policy learning. For NeurIPS, the contribution would be more significant with stronger quantitative evidence, torque-based control without root forces, and comparisons showing clear advantages over established baselines.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide standardized quantitative metrics across tasks (tracking error, energy/torque usage, contact violations, foot slip, falls per minute, recovery after pushes). Report success rates and variance across seeds.
- Compare against: (i) GPS/iLQR with supervised policy learning, (ii) TRPO (and/or more recent model-free baselines), (iii) pure MPC with similar cost and horizon. Include runtime and sample complexity.
- Remove root forces at test time; enforce hard dynamics/contact constraints or demonstrate the impact of softening. Consider torque-only actions to clarify the policy’s role.
- Analyze the effect of λ on feedback gains (magnitude/conditioning) and policy robustness; report stability properties and sensitivity to noise.
- Clarify the recurrent state: dimensionality, ablation to feedforward, and tasks where memory helps.
- Detail training hyperparameters and dataset size; report convergence statistics of the alternating procedure.
- Expand related work discussion, ensure complete citations, and position contributions more precisely relative to GPS and model-free deep RL.

Arguments for acceptance
- Cohesive system that achieves diverse 3D interactive control with minimal hand-engineering.
- Practical and elegant technique to derive feedback gains from CIO.
- Positive ablations and scalable distributed implementation.

Arguments against acceptance
- Insufficient quantitative evaluation and lack of strong baselines.
- Dependence on soft dynamics/root forces and T=1 optimization muddles the contribution of the learned policy.
- Limited analysis of robustness, stability, and the role of recurrence; several clarity gaps.

Recommendation
Reject (borderline). The paper presents a promising and well-engineered approach with interesting technical ideas and demonstrations. However, for NeurIPS standards, it needs substantially stronger quantitative evaluation, clearer isolation of the learned policy’s contribution (under realistic dynamics), and comparisons to established baselines. Addressing these points would likely elevate this work to a strong submission.
