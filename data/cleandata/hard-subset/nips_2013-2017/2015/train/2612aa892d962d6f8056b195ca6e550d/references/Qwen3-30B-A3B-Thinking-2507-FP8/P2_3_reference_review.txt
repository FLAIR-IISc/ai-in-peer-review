

# Review: "Interactive Control of Diverse Complex Characters with Neural Networks"

## Summary and Relation to Previous Work

This paper proposes a novel approach to training neural network controllers for interactive, real-time control of diverse physical characters using a combination of trajectory optimization and neural network regression. The authors address the challenge of creating controllers that can handle complex 3D dynamics, multiple character morphologies, and real-time interaction - problems where previous work was largely limited to 2D environments. While related to recent work by Levine et al. [9] and Mordatch et al. [11], the authors make significant technical improvements that enable their method to succeed in substantially harder 3D problems.

The core innovation is a distributed stochastic optimization framework that jointly optimizes trajectory data and neural network parameters. The authors introduce noise injection during training to improve robustness and develop a method for computing optimal feedback gains from direct trajectory optimization, avoiding the need for inefficient LQG passes. The approach demonstrates successful locomotion control across diverse 3D characters (bipedal, quadrupedal, swimming, and flying) using a single neural network architecture without specialized initialization.

## Strengths

**Technical Soundness and Innovation (Quality):** The paper presents a well-structured, technically sound approach. The distributed stochastic optimization algorithm (Algorithm 1) elegantly combines trajectory optimization and neural network regression. The authors' method for computing optimal feedback gains during trajectory optimization (Section 3.2) is particularly clever and avoids the inefficiencies of previous LQG-based approaches. The ablation studies (Section 8.1) convincingly demonstrate the necessity of both joint optimization and noise injection for robust performance.

**Significant Advancement (Significance):** The paper makes a substantial contribution to the field by demonstrating successful control of diverse 3D characters with a single neural network architecture - a problem where previous trajectory-optimization-based methods [9, 11] were limited to 2D. The results (shown in the supplementary video) are impressive, with the network learning natural-looking behaviors for swimming, flying, bipedal, and quadrupedal locomotion without hand-crafted controllers or motion capture. This represents a significant step toward the goal of "universal synthesis methods applicable to arbitrary behaviors, body morphologies."

**Clarity and Organization:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized, with clear mathematical formulations and a logical flow from problem statement to implementation to results. The distributed training architecture (Section 6) is described with sufficient technical detail for reproducibility, and the supplementary video provides essential context for the qualitative results.

## Weaknesses

**Limited Comparison to Modern RL Methods:** While the paper correctly notes that "traditional methods for designing such controllers are time-consuming and largely manual," it only briefly mentions RL methods [3, 6, 14] without a thorough comparison to state-of-the-art approaches. The paper claims their method "yields more realistic and purposeful behaviors" than direct RL methods, but lacks quantitative comparison to modern deep RL methods like SAC, PPO, or DDPG on the same tasks. This is a significant gap for NIPS, where RL is a major focus.

**Limited Quantitative Evaluation:** The evaluation relies heavily on qualitative results (supplementary video) and a single quantitative metric (the 2-orders-of-magnitude improvement in test performance over the baseline). The paper would be stronger with additional quantitative metrics (e.g., task completion rate, energy efficiency, stability metrics) that could better demonstrate the method's advantages.

**Underdeveloped Discussion of Limitations:** The paper does not sufficiently address potential limitations. For example, how does the method scale to even more complex characters or environments? What are the failure cases? How does it handle significant model mismatches between the simulation and real world? A more thorough discussion of limitations would strengthen the paper's scientific contribution.

## Recommendation

I recommend **acceptance** of this paper. The work makes a significant technical contribution by demonstrating a robust method for training neural network controllers that can interactively control diverse 3D characters. The technical innovations in the distributed stochastic optimization framework and feedback gain calculation are substantial, and the results are impressive for the challenging problem domain.

The paper's main weaknesses (limited comparison to modern RL methods and lack of additional quantitative metrics) are important but not sufficient to warrant rejection. These could be addressed in the camera-ready version with additional experiments and discussion.

## Pro and Con Acceptance Arguments

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
- Demonstrates a significant technical advancement over previous trajectory-optimization-based methods for character control
- Solves a challenging problem (3D diverse character control) that previous work could not address
- Provides a well-structured, technically sound method with clear implementation details
- Shows impressive qualitative results across diverse character types
- Makes valuable contributions to the combination of trajectory optimization and neural networks

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
- Limited comparison to modern reinforcement learning methods (which are a major focus at NIPS)
- Insufficient quantitative evaluation beyond the ablation study
- Limited discussion of potential limitations and failure cases

The paper's strengths substantially outweigh its weaknesses, and it represents a meaningful contribution to the field of character control with clear potential for broader applications in robotics and biomechanics. The authors have made a compelling case for their method's advantages over previous approaches, and with minor additional work (particularly in comparison to modern RL methods), this would be an outstanding contribution to NIPS.
