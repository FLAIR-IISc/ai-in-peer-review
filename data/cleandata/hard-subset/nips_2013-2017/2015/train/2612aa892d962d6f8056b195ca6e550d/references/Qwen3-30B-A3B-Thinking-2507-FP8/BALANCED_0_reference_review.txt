

**Summary**  
This paper proposes a method for learning neural network policies that enable interactive, real-time control of diverse 3D characters (bipedal, quadrupedal, swimming, flying) using a joint optimization framework combining trajectory optimization (CIO) and stochastic neural networks. The core idea is to train a policy π_θ to mimic optimal trajectories generated by CIO, with noise injection during training to improve robustness. The approach avoids task-specific hand-crafting, instead leveraging trajectory optimization to synthesize training data and a neural network to enable online control. The work extends prior trajectory-optimization-based methods (e.g., [9, 11]) to 3D locomotion, which was previously limited to 2D. It relates to NIPS work on neural control [6, 10, 14] but addresses the challenge of *data scarcity* in physical control via trajectory synthesis and noise-based regularization.  

**Strengths**  
1. **Technical Soundness & Clarity**: The joint optimization framework (Algorithm 1) is well-motivated and rigorously derived. The ablation studies (Section 8.1) convincingly demonstrate the necessity of joint optimization (100× better test performance than static training) and noise injection (prevents overfitting). The trajectory optimization (Section 3) and noise injection (Section 2.1) are technically precise, with clear connections to prior work (e.g., LQG, denoising autoencoders).  
2. **Breadth & Impact**: The method successfully controls *diverse* 3D characters (swimming, flying, bipedal, quadrupedal) with a *single* architecture, showcasing generalizability. The results (e.g., cyclical flapping for birds, trot gaits for quadrupeds) are compelling and visually demonstrated in the supplementary video. This addresses a key gap in prior work [13, 21] that required task-specific controllers.  
3. **Practical Implementation**: The distributed training architecture (Section 6) is scalable and well-documented, with realistic resource estimates (2.5 hours on AWS). The execution phase (Section 7) avoids offline data storage, enabling true real-time interaction.  

**Weaknesses**  
1. **Novelty Gap**: While the paper claims to solve "substantially harder problems" than [9, 11] (3D vs. 2D), it does not adequately distinguish its *technical contributions* from prior trajectory-optimization + NN work. The noise injection (Section 2.1) and distributed optimization (Algorithm 1) build directly on [9, 11] and [4, 19], but the paper lacks a clear *new* algorithmic insight (e.g., how the feedback gains in Section 3.2 fundamentally improve over [9]). The 3D extension is significant but not novel in methodology.  
2. **Insufficient Baselines & Significance**: The comparison to MPC (Section 8.1) is superficial; MPC is not a standard baseline for *real-time* neural control (e.g., SAC, PPO, or [14] would be more relevant). The claim that the method "yields more realistic and purposeful behaviors" than direct RL methods (Section 1) lacks empirical support (e.g., no comparison to SAC on locomotion tasks). Without these, the significance claim is overstated.  
3. **Methodological Gaps**: The network architecture choice (3 layers, 250 units) is justified empirically but not theoretically (Section 8.1). The noise scales (σ_ε, σ_γ) are tuned per task without analysis of sensitivity. The paper also omits discussion of *why* trajectory optimization (vs. RL) is better suited for this problem beyond "data scarcity," missing a chance to clarify the problem space.  

**Recommendations for Improvement**  
1. **Sharpen Novelty Claims**: Explicitly contrast the *algorithmic* differences from [9, 11] (e.g., the perturbation method for feedback gains in Section 3.2 vs. LQG in prior work). Highlight how the *joint optimization* framework enables 3D stability where prior methods failed.  
2. **Add Rigorous Baselines**: Compare against modern RL methods (e.g., SAC [14]) on the same locomotion tasks. Include quantitative metrics (e.g., success rate, energy efficiency) beyond qualitative behavior.  
3. **Clarify Significance**: Discuss *why* trajectory optimization + NN is preferable to end-to-end RL for *interactive* control (e.g., data efficiency, stability guarantees). Address whether the method scales to non-locomotion tasks (e.g., climbing [12]).  
4. **Improve Reproducibility**: Report noise scale sensitivity and architecture ablation details (e.g., "Why 3 layers?"). Specify if the 2.5-hour training time is feasible for robotics applications.  

**Decision**  
**Weak Reject**. The paper makes a solid contribution to character control with a well-executed framework, but its novelty is marginal relative to prior trajectory-optimization work, and the significance claim is undermined by insufficient baselines. The technical quality is high, but the *advancement* over existing methods is not sufficiently demonstrated. With the recommended revisions (especially adding RL baselines and clarifying novelty), this could be a strong NIPS paper. As is, it falls short of the "demonstrable advance" standard for NIPS.
