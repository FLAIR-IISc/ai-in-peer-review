Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes an alternating trajectory-optimization–plus–neural policy learning framework for interactive control of complex 3D characters. The key idea is to use Contact-Invariant Optimization (CIO) to synthesize optimal trajectories for a variety of tasks and initial conditions, then train a neural network policy to imitate and generalize these trajectories for real-time control. To address distribution shift and closed-loop stability, the authors inject Gaussian noise into sensory inputs and adjust target actions using locally optimal feedback gains computed efficiently as a byproduct of the trajectory optimization via a perturbation method. They perform distributed, asynchronous alternating optimization with proximal regularization to stabilize training. At test time, the learned policy outputs desired velocities, memory updates, and contact forces, and a short-horizon (T=1) inverse-dynamics optimization step advances the state under soft dynamics and contact constraints. Results are shown for multiple 3D morphologies (bipeds, quadrupeds, swimmer, bird), with ablations for joint optimization and noise injection, and a qualitative comparison to MPC.

This direction closely relates to guided policy search and trajectory-centric RL (e.g., Levine & Koltun 2014; Mordatch & Todorov 2014), model-based control with learned policies, and graphics control via trajectory optimization. The paper’s main claimed advances over prior work are: scaling to 3D characters and locomotion; a practical distributed alternating scheme; a feedback-gain target construction leveraging the CIO Gauss-Newton structure; and robustness via noise injection at inputs and hidden units.

Strengths
- Addresses a challenging and impactful problem: reliable, interactive locomotion for diverse, high-DOF 3D characters.
- Sensible and well-motivated integration of trajectory optimization with policy learning; the alternating scheme is practical and uses cloud/GPU resources effectively.
- The perturbation-based feedback gain computation is elegant, reuses the CIO linear system, and provides a tunable smoothness parameter. This is a technically interesting contribution.
- Robustness measures (input and hidden noise) are justified and empirically supported by ablations showing reduced divergence at execution time.
- Demonstrates breadth across morphologies and tasks without per-character controller design; same NN architecture and hyperparameters are reused.
- Clear description of distributed training setup and compute budget; reproducibility of the overall training pipeline seems feasible in principle.

Weaknesses and detailed comments

Quality
- Evaluation is mostly qualitative. Apart from the ablation plots and architecture table, there are few quantitative metrics. Important metrics are missing: success rate to target, average time-to-target, energy/torque costs, fall rate, robustness to perturbations, sensitivity to sensor noise, and comparison of learned policy rollouts over long horizons.
- Physical realism is compromised by design choices. The action includes desired contact forces, and dynamics constraints are enforced softly. The authors acknowledge root forces may appear in simulation. This undermines the claim of physically realistic control and complicates comparison to torque-controlled baselines. A stronger evaluation would (a) use torque-only actuation with hard contact constraints or demonstrate negligible violation magnitudes, and (b) report the magnitude/frequency of root forces or constraint violations at test time.
- The runtime controller still solves a T=1 optimization subproblem each step. This blurs the line between “pure” policy execution and MPC. It would help to report the computational cost per step and performance when skipping the optimization (direct Euler integration of policy outputs), to isolate the policy’s contribution.
- The feedback-gain target construction is plausible, but its numerical properties are not well characterized. Please provide sensitivity to λ, conditioning of the linear solves, and a comparison to standard LQG-based gains beyond anecdotal remarks.
- The “recurrent memory” variable r is used in the state and action, but the network is described as a feedforward MLP. Clarify how r is updated and fed back, how long-term dependencies are handled, and whether the memory improves performance (with ablation).

Clarity
- The paper is generally well written, but several crucial details are missing:
  - Exact cost terms and feature vector φ for each domain, with weights; without this, reproducing CIO and assessing realism is difficult.
  - Full specifications for the sensory state s (dimension, normalization, observation noise) and action a per character.
  - Quantitative convergence diagnostics of the alternating scheme (beyond a single figure reference), and the role of proximal terms η.
  - Clearer description of the distributed pipeline timing (how often noise is resampled, typical number of CIO iterations per trajectory per epoch).
- Some references are incomplete or missing bibliographic details.

Originality
- The overall approach builds on established ideas in trajectory-optimal policy learning (e.g., guided policy search, CIO-based synthesis) and noise-robust training. The novel parts appear to be the specific perturbation-based gain computation integrated with CIO, and the practical distributed alternating setup applied to 3D locomotion with a uniform NN architecture. These are worthwhile, though incremental, contributions. More explicit contrasts to GPS/ADMM variants and to recent continuous-control RL would strengthen the positioning.

Significance
- If robust and physically plausible, a unified controller that works across diverse 3D morphologies is impactful for graphics and model-based control. However, the lack of quantitative evaluation and reliance on soft constraints and force outputs weakens the evidence that this advances the state of the art in physically realistic control. Stronger baselines (e.g., MPC with the same cost, GPS/TRPO/DDPG variants where applicable) and hard-constraint torque-only results would substantially increase significance.

Questions for the authors
- Can you report quantitative metrics (success rate, energy, perturbation robustness) across tasks and compare to MPC and a model-free policy baseline?
- What is the per-step runtime of the T=1 optimization and the overall control loop? Can your controller run at >100 Hz for complex characters?
- How often do soft constraints or root forces activate at test time? What are their magnitudes?
- How does performance vary with λ in the feedback-gain construction and with sensory/hidden noise scales?
- What is the contribution of the memory state r? Have you tried removing it or replacing it with an RNN?

Pros
- Practical, well-motivated integration of CIO and NN policies
- Interesting, efficient feedback-gain computation
- Broad qualitative results across challenging 3D domains
- Ablations that support key design choices

Cons
- Limited quantitative evaluation and baselines
- Use of soft dynamics/contacts and direct force outputs reduces physical realism
- Ambiguity about the role of runtime optimization vs learned policy
- Missing methodological details needed for reproduction

Recommendation
Borderline accept. The paper tackles an important problem with a thoughtful and practical method, and the feedback-gain construction is a nice technical contribution. However, the current evaluation and reliance on soft constraints and force outputs limit confidence in the claimed realism and generality. With stronger quantitative results, clearer methodological details, and torque-only evaluations, this would be a clear accept.
