- Proposes alternating between Contact-Invariant Optimization (CIO) trajectory optimization and supervised learning of recurrent neural policies for real-time, contact-rich control.  
- Policy maps sensory state (pose, previous velocity/forces, recurrent memory) to desired pose velocity, contact forces, and memory update.  
- Introduces a method to extract local feedback gains from a direct trajectory optimizer using a soft-constraint perturbation and reuse of Gauss-Newton Hessians.  
- Trains with injected input and hidden noise so learned policies emulate optimal feedback under perturbations, improving closed-loop robustness.  
- Uses an asynchronous, proximal alternating scheme and distributed data generation with GPU-accelerated SGD for scalable training.  
- At runtime, executes the learned policy with a one-step constrained CIO (T=1) to update state and enable stable large-timestep execution.  
- Demonstrates qualitative success on diverse 3D characters (swimmer, bird, bipeds, quadrupeds) with emergent cyclic gaits and interactive goal tracking.  
- Ablation studies indicate joint alternation and noise injection are important for test-time robustness.  
- Advances prior guided-policy-search and trajectory-optimization methods by scaling to contact-rich 3D tasks and avoiding a separate LQG pass for gains.  
- Technical derivations (feedback gain via soft constraints and Woodbury identity) are plausible though not fully detailed in the paper.  
- Empirical evaluation is compelling qualitatively but lacks quantitative metrics (success rates, errors, energy/torque, robustness tests, runtime).  
- Missing comparisons to strong policy-learning baselines (e.g., TRPO) and MPC, limiting claims of ML state-of-the-art.  
- Physical fidelity is reduced by soft enforcement of dynamics and allowing root forces, limiting applicability to real robots.  
- The recurrent state r is under-specified and its contribution is not fully ablated or explained.  
- Execution details (constraints used in real time, measured runtimes/latency) and some notational specifics are missing.  
- No theoretical convergence analysis for the asynchronous proximal alternation is provided—only empirical evidence.  
- Strengths: clear integration of CIO and neural policies, practical feedback-gain computation, scalable implementation, and thoughtful noise/branching data generation.  
- Weaknesses: limited quantitative evaluation, lack of baseline comparisons, reduced real-world applicability due to action design, and underspecified recurrent-state treatment.  
- Suggested improvements: add quantitative metrics and runtime, compare to MPC/RL baselines, clarify/ablate recurrent state, discuss hardware feasibility and soft-constraint implications, and provide convergence/sensitivity plots.  
- Recommendation: borderline accept for NeurIPS, leaning toward weak accept; solid engineering and contributions but needs stronger quantitative evidence.
